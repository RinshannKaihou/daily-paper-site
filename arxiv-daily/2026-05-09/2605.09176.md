> **一句话总结：** 这篇65页的综述系统性地梳理了LLM训练优化器从AdamW到Muon等新兴方法的发展脉络，按照更新几何、内存开销、结构假设等五个维度构建了分类体系，并提出了公平基准测试的方法论框架，指出LLM优化器研究正从单一算法提速转向收敛性、稳定性、内存和实现复杂度的联合评估。

> **TL;DR:** A 65-page, no-figure survey that organizes the rapidly growing LLM optimizer landscape (AdamW, Adafactor, LAMB, Lion, Sophia, LOMO, GaLore, Adam-mini, Muon, Shampoo, APOLLO, etc.) into a coherent taxonomy along five axes: update geometry, state memory, structural assumptions, target regime, and evaluation criterion. The author argues that optimizer research for LLMs is entering a new phase requiring rigorous, scale-aware, multi-objective benchmarking rather than single-algorithm speedup claims. Includes a detailed recommended benchmark protocol covering hyperparameter fairness, fixed-model vs. fixed-resource evaluation, memory accounting, and common pitfalls.

## 核心贡献

- 构建了LLM优化器的五维分类体系：更新几何、状态内存、结构假设、目标训练场景、评估标准，覆盖了从经典一阶方法到矩阵优化器的完整谱系
- 系统梳理了10类优化器家族（SGD、Adam/AdamW、Adafactor、LAMB、8-bit/LOMO/Adam-mini/APOLLO、signSGD/Lion、Shampoo/Sophia/SOAP、GaLore、Muon、quasi-Newton），分析各自的数学形式、设计动机与权衡
- 提出了详细的LLM优化器基准测试协议，涵盖超参公平性、固定模型vs固定资源评估、内存核算、Token效率vs墙钟效率等维度
- 识别了多个关键开放问题：优化器缩放定律、参数组特异性优化、矩阵优化器的理论基础、长上下文训练中的优化器设计、后训练阶段的优化器选择
- 强调评估不应仅看验证损失，而应联合考虑收敛性、稳定性、内存、通信开销和实现复杂度

## Key Contributions

- Constructs a five-axis taxonomy for LLM optimizers (update geometry, state memory, structural assumptions, target regime, evaluation criterion) covering the full spectrum from SGD to matrix-based methods
- Provides detailed mathematical formulations and design-tradeoff analyses for 10+ optimizer families including AdamW, Adafactor, LAMB, Lion, Sophia, Shampoo, LOMO, GaLore, Adam-mini, Muon, APOLLO, and quasi-Newton methods
- Proposes a comprehensive benchmarking protocol for LLM optimizer comparisons, addressing hyperparameter fairness, fixed-model vs. fixed-resource evaluation, memory accounting, early-curve pitfalls, and implementation confounds
- Identifies key open problems: optimizer scaling laws, parameter-group-specific update rules, theoretical foundations for matrix-based optimizers (e.g., Muon), long-context optimizer design, and optimizer interaction with post-training stages
- Argues for a multi-objective evaluation paradigm where no single optimizer is universally best, and the right choice depends on the training regime (pretraining, fine-tuning, long-context, post-training)

## Methodology

This is a survey paper with no experimental component. The methodology is analytical and organizational:

- **Taxonomy construction:** The author organizes the optimizer landscape along five axes: update geometry (raw gradients, momentum, coordinate-wise adaptive, sign-based, curvature-aware, low-rank, matrix-structured), state memory (none, one vector, two vectors, sublinear, quantized), structural assumptions (independent coordinates, vectors, matrices, tensors, low-rank, parameter groups), target regime (pretraining, fine-tuning, large-batch, memory-constrained), and evaluation criterion (token efficiency, wall-clock speed, peak memory, stability, final downstream performance).

- **Systematic review:** Each optimizer family is presented with its mathematical update rule, design motivation, relationship to other families, and practical considerations (memory, compute, numerical stability, distributed compatibility). Key methods covered include SGD/momentum, AdaGrad/RMSProp/Adam/AdamW, Adafactor, LAMB, 8-bit optimizers, Adam-mini, LOMO, APOLLO, signSGD/Lion, Shampoo, Sophia, SOAP, GaLore, Muon, and quasi-Newton methods.

- **Benchmarking framework:** Section 11 provides a detailed recommended protocol for fair optimizer comparison, including: what it means for an optimizer to be "better" (multi-objective), the baseline problem (under-tuned AdamW), hyperparameter search and reporting requirements, scale dependence, early-training-curve pitfalls, token vs. wall-clock efficiency, memory accounting, precision/numerics, downstream evaluation, and implementation effects.

## Results & Highlights

- As a survey, there are no new experimental results. The key "result" is the organizational framework and the methodological recommendations.

- The survey covers 54 references spanning classical optimization, adaptive methods, distributed training, memory-efficient training, sign-based optimization, second-order methods, low-rank projection, and matrix-based optimizers.

- Notable organizational insights:
  - AdamW occupies a strong "middle ground" in the systems-optimization design space: robust but memory-intensive
  - Memory-efficient optimizers (Adafactor, 8-bit, Adam-mini, LOMO, GaLore, APOLLO) are not merely engineering conveniences but can change the feasible training regime
  - Matrix-based optimizers (Muon) represent a conceptually distinct family from AdamW, Lion, GaLore, and Shampoo
  - No single metric suffices for optimizer evaluation; multi-objective comparison is essential

- The survey identifies common pitfalls in optimizer benchmarking: under-tuned baselines, unequal tuning budgets, early-curve overclaiming, ignoring wall-clock cost, incomplete memory reporting, small-scale extrapolation, and implementation confounding.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** The primary novelty is the organizational framework rather than new algorithms or experiments. The five-axis taxonomy and the benchmarking protocol are the main contributions. The survey fills an important gap: previous optimization surveys were either too general or treated optimizers as one component among many training acceleration techniques.

- **Rigor:** The mathematical formulations are precise and consistent throughout. The coverage of optimizer families is reasonably comprehensive. However, the survey lacks a systematic comparison table with concrete numbers (e.g., memory cost in bytes per parameter for each optimizer). While the text discusses trade-offs qualitatively, quantitative comparisons would strengthen the work. The absence of any figures or tables with empirical data (noted explicitly: "No figures") is a significant limitation for a survey -- even synthetic illustrative figures showing Pareto frontiers or trade-off curves would have been valuable.

- **Reproducibility:** N/A for a survey. The benchmarking protocol recommendations are clear and actionable, which is a strength.

- **Significance:** The survey is timely and addresses a genuinely fragmented literature. The argument for multi-objective, scale-aware evaluation is well-made and important. However, the survey stops short of actually conducting such evaluations or providing concrete benchmark results that practitioners could use to make optimizer choices. The value is primarily in organizing the landscape and identifying open problems rather than in providing actionable guidance.

- **Potential concerns:**
  - Single-author survey at this breadth is unusual and may lack the perspective that multiple authors with different expertise areas could provide
  - The survey treats all optimizer families at roughly similar depth, which may not reflect their practical importance (e.g., quasi-Newton methods receive substantial coverage but are rarely used for LLM training)
  - The paper does not deeply engage with the practical question of "which optimizer should I use for my specific scenario?" -- the answer remains "it depends," which is honest but less useful
  - Some recent or concurrent work may be missed given the rapid pace of optimizer research

## Strengths

- **Comprehensive scope:** Covers the full optimizer landscape from SGD to Muon with consistent mathematical treatment
- **Clear organizational framework:** The five-axis taxonomy provides a coherent way to understand the relationships between different optimizer families
- **Strong methodological contribution:** The benchmarking protocol (Section 11) is detailed, practical, and addresses real problems in the field (under-tuned baselines, cherry-picked scales, hidden implementation advantages)
- **Honest assessment:** Does not overclaim for any particular optimizer family; explicitly acknowledges that AdamW remains a strong baseline under fair comparison
- **Timely:** Catches the field at an inflection point where Muon, GaLore, and other new methods are challenging AdamW's dominance
- **Well-structured mathematical exposition:** Consistent notation and clear update-rule formulations across all optimizer families

## Limitations

- **No empirical component:** A survey of optimizers without any comparative experiments or even synthesized benchmark results is a significant limitation. The reader learns the taxonomy but not how methods actually compare
- **No figures or tables with data:** The paper explicitly states "No figures." Even synthetic illustrative plots (loss curves, memory trade-off frontiers, Pareto diagrams) would substantially improve the presentation
- **Unequal treatment of practical importance:** Quasi-Newton methods receive a full subsection but are essentially unused in production LLM training, while critical practical details (e.g., how to tune Lion's learning rate relative to AdamW) receive less attention
- **Limited actionable guidance:** The survey excels at "what" and "why" but provides less concrete "how" guidance for practitioners choosing between optimizers
- **Single author:** A survey of this breadth might benefit from co-authors with expertise in different optimizer families (e.g., distributed systems, second-order methods, matrix optimization)
- **No discussion of optimizer combinations in practice:** Real-world training often uses different optimizers or settings for different parameter groups, which is mentioned briefly but not systematically analyzed

## 相关性

这篇综述与LLM训练系统设计和优化算法研究高度相关。对于关注大规模模型训练效率、内存优化、以及新兴优化器（如Muon、GaLore）在Transformer架构上的应用的研究者来说，提供了清晰的分类体系和评估框架，有助于定位自己工作在整个优化器谱系中的位置。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can optimizer scaling laws be developed that predict how the optimal learning rate, batch size, and optimizer choice co-vary with model size, similar to neural scaling laws for model performance?
- For matrix-based optimizers like Muon, what is the theoretical justification that orthogonalized momentum updates improve optimization for Transformer parameters specifically, beyond empirical observations?
- How should optimizers be selected or adapted for different post-training stages (RLHF, DPO, preference optimization) where gradient statistics differ substantially from pretraining?
- Is there a principled way to dynamically allocate optimizer state budget across parameter groups (e.g., rich state for attention projections, minimal state for normalization parameters) during training?
- To what extent do the gains reported for memory-efficient optimizers (GaLore, APOLLO) persist under truly fair tuning budgets at multi-billion-parameter scale with strong AdamW baselines?
