Navigating LLM Valley: From AdamW to Memory-Efficient and Matrix-Based Optimizers

A 65-page survey by Aditya Ranganath (LLNL) that organizes the rapidly growing LLM optimizer landscape into a five-axis taxonomy covering update geometry, state memory, structural assumptions, target regime, and evaluation criterion — and argues that the field needs multi-objective, scale-aware benchmarking rather than single-algorithm speedup claims.

Key Finding

No single optimizer is universally best. The right choice depends on the training regime — pretraining, fine-tuning, long-context, or post-training — and evaluation must jointly consider convergence, stability, memory, communication, and implementation complexity.

Key Metric

5 axes · 10+ families · 54 refs

The taxonomy spans SGD, AdamW, Adafactor, LAMB, Lion, Sophia, Shampoo, GaLore, Muon, APOLLO, and quasi-Newton methods — each analyzed with mathematical formulations and design-tradeoff breakdowns.

Why This Matters

The field is at an inflection point: Muon, GaLore, and other new methods are challenging AdamW's dominance, but claims remain unreliable without fair, multi-objective benchmarks at scale.

Critical Edge

Zero empirical results. Zero figures.

A survey of optimizers with no comparative experiments, no synthetic benchmark curves, no Pareto frontiers. The taxonomy is valuable but unvalidated by the author's own data.

Benchmarking Pitfalls Identified
Under-tuned AdamW baselines making new methods look better by default
Unequal hyperparameter tuning budgets across optimizers
Early-curve overclaiming from cherry-picked checkpoints
Ignoring wall-clock cost in favor of token-count efficiency
Incomplete memory reporting (optimizer state only, not peak GPU)
Small-scale extrapolation to production-size claims
Implementation confounds (custom CUDA kernels vs. pure PyTorch)
Single-metric evaluation hiding trade-offs in other dimensions
Assessment

Strengths

  • Comprehensive coverage from SGD to Muon with consistent mathematical notation
  • Five-axis taxonomy gives a coherent mental model for the optimizer landscape
  • Section 11 benchmarking protocol is detailed, practical, and addresses real field-wide problems
  • Honest assessment: explicitly acknowledges AdamW remains a strong baseline under fair comparison
  • Timely — catches the field at a genuine inflection point

Limitations

  • No empirical component at all — not even synthesized benchmark comparisons
  • Zero figures or data tables; even illustrative Pareto frontiers are absent
  • Quasi-Newton methods get full subsection treatment but are unused in production LLM training
  • Practitioner "which optimizer should I use?" guidance remains "it depends"
  • Single-author breadth risks gaps in specialist depth across optimizer families
Open Questions
  • Can optimizer scaling laws predict how optimal learning rate, batch size, and optimizer choice co-vary with model size?
  • What is the theoretical justification that orthogonalized momentum (Muon) improves Transformer optimization specifically?
  • How should optimizers be adapted for post-training stages (RLHF, DPO) where gradient statistics diverge from pretraining?
  • Is there a principled way to dynamically allocate optimizer state budget across parameter groups during training?
  • Do gains reported for GaLore/APOLLO persist under truly fair tuning budgets at multi-billion-parameter scale?
