> **一句话总结：** 本文提出ZO-MOPI，用基于流式幂迭代的偏谱正交化替代Muon中的Newton-Schulz全正交化，解决了零阶梯度估计噪声大导致全正交化失效的问题，在OPT-13B/LLaMA3-8B等模型的SuperGLUE微调中实现1.5x-4x的收敛加速。

> **TL;DR:** Proposes ZO-MOPI, a zeroth-order optimizer that identifies and addresses a fundamental problem: Muon's Newton-Schulz full orthogonalization amplifies noise rather than signal in ZO settings because ZO gradient estimates are inherently unreliable. The fix is streaming power iteration (SPI) that only preserves the top-k dominant spectral directions (partial orthogonalization), combined with momentum-projected subspace search. On OPT-13B and LLaMA3-8B fine-tuning across SuperGLUE tasks, ZO-MOPI reaches target accuracies 1.5x-3x faster than ZO-Muon and achieves the highest final accuracy among ZO methods, with negligible memory overhead (~0.3GB extra vs ZO-Muon). The paper includes convergence theory with O(1/sqrt(T)) rate and hyperparameter ablations.

## 核心贡献

- 发现并解决了零阶优化中的关键问题：Muon中的Newton-Schulz全正交化在ZO设定下效果差，因为它会放大ZO梯度估计中的噪声方向而非信号方向
- 提出用流式幂迭代（Streaming Power Iteration, SPI）替代Newton-Schulz，只保留top-k主导谱方向（偏谱正交化），复杂度从O(r^2 n)降至O(rnk + nk^2)，其中k << r
- 证明了在子空间上的SPI等价于全空间SPI（Proposition 1），并给出了子空间追踪误差的收敛界（Lemma 4.1）和整体O(1/sqrt(T))收敛率（Theorem 4.2）
- 揭示了动量在算法中的双重角色：不仅降低方差，更是SPI的时间连续性所必需的——没有动量，单次噪声估计会导致主导子空间剧烈波动
- 在OPT-13B上实现SST-2 91.7%准确率（ZO-Muon为90.4%），在SQuAD上达到目标精度仅需0.83小时（ZO-Muon需2.35小时）

## Key Contributions

- Identify that Newton-Schulz full orthogonalization in Muon fails in the ZO setting because it equalizes the entire active spectrum including noisy directions, which amplifies noise rather than signal
- Propose Streaming Power Iteration (SPI) that only preserves top-k dominant singular directions, achieving partial orthogonalization with complexity O(rnk + nk^2) vs O(r^2 n) for Newton-Schulz
- Prove that subspace-projected SPI is equivalent to full-space SPI (Proposition 1, Appendix A), provide tracking error bounds for the streaming subspace (Lemma 4.1), and establish O(1/sqrt(T)) convergence rate dependent on reduced subspace dimension d_sub rather than full parameter dimension (Theorem 4.2)
- Demonstrate that momentum serves a dual purpose beyond variance reduction: it is essential for temporal continuity in SPI, as a single noisy ZO estimate can cause dominant subspace to fluctuate drastically without momentum smoothing (Figure 3)
- Achieve state-of-the-art ZO fine-tuning results: on OPT-13B SST-2, 91.7% accuracy (vs 90.4% for ZO-Muon) with negligible memory overhead (26.56GB vs 26.22GB)

## Methodology

**Problem Setting:** Zeroth-order optimization for LLM fine-tuning, specifically the hidden layer training problem where matrix-structured parameters allow spectral methods to exploit weak spectral directions.

**Key Insight (Section 4):** In first-order training, Muon's Newton-Schulz (NS) iteration orthogonalizes the gradient matrix by approximately equalizing all active singular values. This works because FO gradients have high signal-to-noise ratio. In ZO training, gradient estimates are obtained via finite-difference perturbations and are extremely noisy. NS iteration blindly orthogonalizes all directions including noisy ones, degrading rather than improving the update.

**Proposed Solution -- ZO-MOPI (Algorithm 1):**

1. **Subspace ZO Estimation (following LOZO):** Project parameters into a low-rank subspace A in R^{m x r} (r=64) to reduce ZO gradient variance. Use multi-point estimation with N_q queries per step.

2. **Streaming Power Iteration (SPI, Algorithm 2):** Instead of Newton-Schulz on the momentum matrix M_t, apply power iteration that only extracts top-k (k=32) dominant singular vectors. The key operation is:
   - V_t R_t = QR(M_t^T M_t V_{t-1})
   - The update becomes: Delta_t = (1/k) M_t V_t V_t^T (partial orthogonalization)
   - This is warm-started from the previous step's V_{t-1}, leveraging temporal continuity

3. **Momentum-Projected Subspace:** Accumulate subspace momentum M_t in R^{r x n}. When the subspace A is lazily resampled every nu=500 iterations, project the momentum to the new basis via M_new = (1/m) A_new^T A_old M_old (Eq. 10). This maintains temporal continuity critical for SPI stability.

4. **Lazy Sampling Strategy:** The subspace A is only refreshed every nu iterations (ablated in Figure 4: too frequent updates break SPI's temporal continuity assumption; too infrequent limits exploration).

**Theoretical Results:**
- Proposition 1 (Appendix A): Subspace-projected SPI is lossless -- equivalent to full-space SPI
- Lemma 4.1: Tracking error T_t satisfies ||T_t||_2 <= ||T_{t-1}||_2 + gamma * delta, where gamma is the spectral gap ratio and delta is the drift constant
- Theorem 4.2: Convergence rate O(1/sqrt(alpha) * sqrt(Delta_0 + L*mu^2) * L*k / T + L^2/alpha^2 * sigma_M^2 + mu^2*L^2*d_sub^2), where d_sub = rn is the subspace dimension

## Results & Highlights

**Main Results (Table 1, Figures 5-6):**
- On OPT-13B: ZO-MOPI reaches target accuracy thresholds in 0.83-10.34 hours across SST-2/RTE/BoolQ/SQuAD, vs 1.74-14.59 hours for ZO-Muon (1.5x-3x speedup)
- On LLaMA3-8B: Similar speedups; 1.05-5.06 hours vs 1.64-4.08 hours for ZO-Muon on most tasks (RTE is the exception at 5.06 vs 4.05)
- MeZO and LOZO fail to reach target accuracy on RTE entirely (marked N/A)
- On SST-2 with OPT-13B: ZO-MOPI achieves 91.7% vs 90.4% (ZO-Muon), 88.3% (MeZO), 87.7% (LOZO)

**Efficiency (Table 4):**
- Same query budget (20k) and steps (4k) as ZO-Muon
- Nearly identical wall-clock time: 2.03h vs 2.05h (ZO-Muon) -- the SPI overhead is negligible
- Negligible memory overhead: 26.56GB vs 26.22GB (ZO-Muon), both far below Adam's 4x63.7GB
- Additional memory for SPI state V in R^{n x k} and subspace momentum M in R^{r x n} is minimal since k=32, r=64

**Gemma2-2B Results (Figure 2):** Reaches strong accuracy faster on BoolQ and SQuAD while preserving memory efficiency.

**Ablation Studies (Section D):**
- Spectral rank k: Too small (few dominant directions) -> insufficient spectral info; too large -> includes noisy components. Intermediate k gives best trade-off
- Subspace rank r: Too small -> lower final accuracy; too large -> weakens variance reduction. Moderate r is optimal
- Lazy sampling interval nu: Too small -> breaks SPI temporal continuity; too large -> limits exploration

**Hyperparameters:** Fixed r=64, k=32 across all LLM experiments; search only over N_q in {4, 8, 16}. Training uses batch size 16, perturbation stepsize mu=1e-3, constant learning rate without weight decay.

**Hardware:** 4x NVIDIA RTX PRO 6000 Blackwell GPUs (96GB each), CUDA 13.0.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty: High.** The core insight -- that full orthogonalization fails in ZO because it amplifies noise -- is non-obvious and empirically demonstrated. Replacing Newton-Schulz with streaming power iteration for partial orthogonalization is a clean, principled solution. The observation that momentum is critical for SPI stability (not just variance reduction) adds genuine understanding.

**Rigor: Good.** The paper provides formal convergence theory with O(1/sqrt(T)) rate, tracking error bounds, and lossless subspace projection proof. Experimental comparisons are thorough: 4 datasets, 3 model families (Gemma2-2B, OPT-1.3B/13B, LLaMA3-8B), appropriate baselines (MeZO, LOZO, ZO-Muon, Adam, LoRA). Hyperparameters are mostly fixed (r=64, k=32) with search only over N_q.

**Concerns:**
- (1) No explicit mention of multiple random seeds or confidence intervals in the main results tables. The accuracy numbers appear to be single runs.
- (2) On LLaMA3-8B RTE, ZO-MOPI is actually slower than ZO-Muon (5.06h vs 4.05h), suggesting the method may not uniformly dominate.
- (3) Baseline implementations are taken from original papers without re-tuning -- whether baselines receive equal hyperparameter search budget is unclear.
- (4) The lazy sampling interval nu=500 and spectral rank k=32 are fixed across all experiments; sensitivity analysis (Appendix D) only varies these on smaller models (OPT-1.3B/2.7B).
- (5) Only 1000 training examples per task, which is standard for ZO fine-tuning papers but raises questions about scaling to full datasets.

**Reproducibility: Good.** Public code, fixed hyperparameters for the most part, clear experimental setup details in Appendix E.

**Significance: High practical impact.** Zeroth-order optimization is the key enabler for LLM fine-tuning on edge devices without backpropagation. A consistent 1.5x-3x wall-clock speedup over ZO-Muon with negligible memory overhead is meaningful for resource-constrained deployment.

## Strengths

- Identifies and addresses a fundamental problem (noise amplification in ZO spectral optimization) with a principled solution
- Streaming power iteration is both theoretically cleaner and computationally cheaper than Newton-Schulz for this setting
- Thorough experimental evaluation across 3 model families, 4 tasks, and multiple ZO/FO baselines
- Negligible memory and runtime overhead -- the method is essentially "free" on top of ZO-Muon
- Good ablation studies showing why each component (SPI, momentum, lazy sampling) matters
- Includes convergence theory with complete proofs
- The insight that momentum is necessary for SPI stability (not just variance reduction) is a genuine contribution to understanding ZO optimization

## Limitations

- On LLaMA3-8B RTE, ZO-MOPI is slower than ZO-Muon (5.06h vs 4.05h), suggesting the advantage is not uniform across all task-model combinations
- No results on models larger than 13B parameters; scaling behavior to 30B/70B is unknown
- Applicability beyond hidden layer fine-tuning (e.g., adversarial attacks, black-box optimization) is not discussed
- The spectral rank k and subspace rank r are fixed across all experiments; the ablation on these hyperparameters is limited to smaller models
- Only tested on English NLP benchmarks; generalization to multilingual or non-NLP tasks is unverified
- No comparison with recent concurrent ZO methods beyond the immediate predecessors (MeZO, LOZO, ZO-Muon)
- The theoretical convergence rate has the same O(1/sqrt(T)) order as standard ZO-SGD; the benefit of spectral orthogonalization does not appear in the rate itself but rather in the constants

## Relevance

该论文与零阶优化及LLM高效微调方向高度相关。ZO-MOPI在ZO-Muon基础上，通过流式幂迭代解决全正交化的噪声放大问题，这对边缘设备上大模型部署具有直接应用价值。偏谱正交化的思路可为其他噪声环境下的优化器设计（如低精度训练、稀疏梯度场景）提供借鉴。收敛理论中子空间维度替代全参数维度的分析也有方法论参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Why does ZO-MOPI underperform ZO-Muon on LLaMA3-8B RTE? Is this a statistical fluke or a fundamental limitation for certain task-model combinations?
- Can SPI-based partial orthogonalization benefit first-order Muon in low-precision (FP8, INT4) or sparse gradient settings where gradient noise is elevated?
- How does the optimal spectral rank k scale with model size? Would 70B models need larger k?
- Can ZO-MOPI be combined with quantization (e.g., 4-bit weights) for further memory reduction on edge devices?
- The theoretical rate is O(1/sqrt(T)) -- same as ZO-SGD. Can the benefit of spectral orthogonalization be captured in a tighter bound (e.g., better constants or adapted rates)?
- Is the lazy sampling interval nu=500 robust across different training dynamics (e.g., curriculum learning, varying learning rate schedules)?
