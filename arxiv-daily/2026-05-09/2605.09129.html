Key Finding

Data-driven Circuit Discovery for Interpretability of Language Models

Daking Rai Mor Geva Ziyu Yao

Existing circuit discovery methods return dataset-specific circuits rather than general task circuits, and critically, can merge two entirely distinct mechanisms into a single misleading high-faithfulness circuit. DCD fixes this by clustering examples by attribution patterns before discovery.

Key Metric
86% vs 64%
DCD vs EAP-IG faithfulness on GPT-2 all-task mixed dataset at circuit size 0.05
Why This Matters
Challenges a foundational assumption of the mechanistic interpretability field. If adopted, DCD-like approaches would fundamentally shift how circuit analysis is conducted — from hypothesizing single circuits to letting data reveal multiple distinct mechanisms.
Critical Edge
Best-of-K faithfulness evaluation uses oracle assignment (selecting the best circuit per example), inflating DCD's apparent advantage. The paper lacks a realistic test-time assignment procedure. Random partitions with the same K achieve comparable best-of-K scores, though with incoherent circuits.
Mechanism Mixing
Adding just 10% arithmetic data to entity binding raises faithfulness from 5.8% to 87.1% on arithmetic — an 81.3-point jump — proving that existing methods silently conflate unrelated mechanisms.

Methodology

The paper proceeds in three phases, each answering a distinct research question:

  • RQ1 — Dataset specificity: Construct multiple dataset variants per task along three axes (complexity, syntax, domain). Discover circuits independently on each variant, then evaluate cross-dataset faithfulness and Jaccard edge overlap.
  • RQ2 — Mechanism mixing: Mix entity binding and arithmetic data at varying proportions (0.0–1.0 in 0.1 increments). Discover a single circuit per mixture and evaluate on pure task test sets.
  • RQ3 — DCD framework: (1) Compute per-example edge attribution vectors via EAP-IG; (2) Binarize by retaining edges accounting for 99% of attribution mass; (3) PCA to r=20 dimensions; (4) K-means clustering with gap statistic K selection and Jaccard distance; (5) Discover independent circuits per cluster.

Models: GPT-2 Small (124M), Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct  |  Tasks: IOI, entity binding, arithmetic addition, sequence completion

Results

  • Cross-dataset faithfulness drops reach 79 percentage points (IOI syntax axis on Qwen2.5)
  • Mean pairwise Jaccard similarity ranges 0.30–0.66 across all task×model×axis cells
  • DCD achieves 22-point improvement over EAP-IG on GPT-2 (86% vs 64%)
  • DCD naturally groups examples into 7 clusters separating IOI variants and sequence completion variants on GPT-2
  • Per-example faithfulness shows clear block-diagonal structure
  • Consistent gains across all three model families
Expand: single-task sub-mechanisms

DCD also discovers multiple mechanisms within single tasks (e.g., IOI with name-position and domain variants), not just across obviously distinct tasks.

Strengths

  • Rigorously demonstrates a fundamental flaw in the prevailing paradigm with controlled, systematic experiments
  • Mixed-dataset mechanism-merging experiment is a compelling, intuitive demonstration
  • DCD is conceptually simple and modular — wraps any existing circuit discovery method
  • Produces interpretable clusters aligned with task boundaries without being told what those boundaries are
  • Comprehensive evaluation: 3 models, 4 tasks, 3 circuit methods, multiple clustering variants
  • Honest discussion of limitations, including the best-of-K evaluation advantage

Limitations

  • Oracle-based best-of-K evaluation inflates apparent advantage; no realistic test-time assignment procedure
  • Computational cost: per-example attribution + clustering + per-cluster circuit discovery
  • Multiple hyperparameters (gamma, PCA dimension r, clustering algorithm, distance metric) with limited sensitivity analysis
  • Cluster count K* varies from 2 to 13 across settings, raising stability questions
  • All tasks are synthetic and narrow; untested on real-world language tasks
  • Does not resolve the fundamental question of mechanism scope — shifts it from human-defined boundaries to data-driven cluster boundaries

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How can we assign new examples to the correct DCD circuit at test time without re-computing attributions and clustering? Can a lightweight classifier be trained on cluster membership?
  2. Do DCD's clusters correspond to genuinely distinct computational mechanisms with human-readable descriptions, or are they statistical artifacts of the attribution landscape?
  3. How does DCD interact with sparse autoencoder-based feature discovery — can SAE features provide a better clustering signal?
  4. What happens when DCD is applied to complex, open-ended tasks like natural language inference or multi-hop reasoning?
  5. Is the smooth gradient in edge overlap (as a function of mixing ratio) a fundamental property of attribution-based methods, or could alternative schemes produce sharper boundaries?
  6. Can DCD be extended to discover overlapping or hierarchical circuits where examples may belong to multiple mechanisms simultaneously?
