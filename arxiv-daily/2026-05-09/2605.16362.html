When Is Rank-1 Steering Cheap? Geometry, Granularity, and Budgeted Search

John T. Robertson · Jianing Zhu · Haris Vikalo · Zhangyang Wang — UT Austin

Rank-1 activation steering doesn't fail for most concepts — it's expensive to find. Free geometric diagnostics from cached activations predict where to look, cutting search cost by 39.8% across three model families.

Key Metric
39.8%
reduction in trials needed to reach 95% of best steering utility (T95), averaged across Gemma2-2B, Gemma3-27B, Llama3.3-70B
Why This Matters

Shifts the field's research question from “when does rank-1 fail?” to “when is rank-1 cheap?” — with a pre-computable diagnostic that costs nothing beyond cached activations.

Critical Edge

Only 20 concepts tested, all style/persona — no safety-critical or task-performance concepts. Granularity is an imperfect predictor (Spearman r = 0.44). Some concepts see no benefit; the framework diagnoses but cannot fix high-granularity cases.

Methodology

Uses the PersonaVectors pipeline: GPT-5 generates 5 contrastive prompt pairs, applied across 100 extraction questions, yielding 500 difference vectors per concept per layer. Two activation variants: prompt-boundary (last prompt token) for diagnostics, response-averaged for steering vectors.

Prompt-boundary alignment Ac(l) — average pairwise cosine similarity of unit-normalized contrastive difference vectors at each layer. Computed before any steering evaluation; serves as a purely geometric prior over promising layers.
Concept granularity Gc(l) — ratio of within-question alignment to overall alignment. Near 1 = stable global direction; >1 = cross-context rotation making rank-1 inherently expensive.
Budget-constrained search — TPE (Optuna) over (layer, coefficient) with 50-trial budget. Primary metric: T95 = trials to reach 95% of best-found utility. Geometry-guided variant restricts search to top-15 layers by alignment.

Results

  • Geometry-guided TPE: T95 drops from 13.7 ± 10.7 to 8.2 ± 8.0, while best-found utility changes by only +0.16 on average (improved in 58% of runs)
  • Largest gain on Llama3.3-70B: 42.7% T95 reduction and +0.8 average utility improvement
  • Granularity correlates with optimization difficulty (rho = 0.37–0.44) and best-found performance (rho = −0.46)
  • ANOVA: question effect accounts for 42% of directional variance; prompt effect only 16%
  • Cross-judge agreement across three LLM judges: Pearson r ≥ 0.82 on scores, r ≥ 0.90 on rankings

Strengths

  • Clean conceptual reframing that shifts the field's research question
  • Free geometric diagnostics from cached activations — no extra compute
  • Concept granularity is interpretable, pre-computable, and backed by ANOVA
  • Thorough evaluation: 3 model scales, 3 random seeds, 3 judge models
  • Honest about limitations; code publicly released

Limitations

  • 20 concepts, all style/persona — no safety-critical or task concepts
  • Granularity-performance correlation (r = 0.44) is moderate
  • High variance in T95 reductions; some concepts see no benefit
  • GRACE remedies each help small minorities; aggregate impact is modest
  • No comparison to gradient-based steering methods

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can granularity-guided routing dynamically select context-specific steering vectors at inference time for high-granularity concepts?
  2. How does granularity behave for safety-critical concepts (refusal, harmlessness) versus the style/persona concepts tested?
  3. Is the 42% question-effect variance reducible through representation learning objectives during pretraining or fine-tuning?
  4. Could granularity serve as a regularization target during training to make models more uniformly steerable?
  5. How does the framework extend to multi-token or token-level steering interventions?
