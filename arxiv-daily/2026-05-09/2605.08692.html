AAAC: Activation-Aware Adaptive Codebooks for 4-bit LLM Weight Quantization

Beshr IslamBouli & David Jin MIT CC BY 4.0
Key Finding
Replacing the fixed 4-bit quantization grid with just two learned scalar codebooks (64 bytes per layer) — optimized via activation-weighted k-means with no gradients or Hessian — matches or exceeds methods that require hours of gradient-based optimization on multi-GPU setups.
Key Metric
70.5%
avg. quantization gap recovered (AAAC + AWQ)
AAAC alone: 53.3%. Best gradient-free prior: ~35%. Total overhead: 64 bytes/layer, zero extra storage when S = g. Quantization time: 3–30 min on a single H100.
Why This Matters
A gradient-free method running in minutes on one GPU outperforms OmniQuant, SqueezeLLM, GPTVQ, and QuIP# — methods that need hours, multiple GPUs, and far more memory. This challenges the field's assumption that better quantization requires heavier optimization.
Critical Edge
No evaluation beyond 32B parameters; 70B+ models untested where weight distributions may differ. The two-codebook design is not justified theoretically — no ablation beyond “it works.” No public code repository. Meaningful gains at g = 128 require S = 16, adding 0.0625 bpw.
Method

How AAAC Works

  • Each linear layer learns two 15- or 16-entry scalar codebooks T0 and T1 (NVFP4 or INT4 format)
  • Initialized from full-range and half-shifted quantiles of normalized weights
  • Alternating optimization: per-group table assignment → activation-weighted k-means table update → repeat (3 outer, 10 inner iterations)
  • Selection bit stored in unused sign bit of positive scale when S = g — zero storage overhead
  • Total storage: two BF16 tables × 15–16 entries = ~64 bytes/layer; a 7B model adds ~14 KB total

Strengths

What Makes This Work

  • Extremely lightweight: 64 bytes/layer, zero overhead at S = g, 3–30 min total
  • Elegant sign-bit encoding trick for codebook selection at zero cost
  • Comprehensive evaluation: 7 baselines, 13+ models, 2 quantization formats (NVFP4, INT4)
  • Gradient-free yet matches gradient-assisted methods — a surprising result
  • Orthogonal to AWQ: combination yields 70.5% gap recovery
  • Robust across architectures (Llama 1/2/3, Qwen 2.5/3.5) and sizes (0.5B–32B)
  • Minimal hyperparameter sensitivity; works with just 1 calibration sequence
Limitations

Caveats & Open Questions

  • Only tested up to 32B; 70B+ dynamics may differ
  • Gradient-assisted comparison limited to four LLaMA 7B/13B models — a dated benchmark
  • Weight-only quantization (W4A16/W4A8); does not address W4A4
  • Two-codebook choice not justified by ablation or theory
  • No public code repository at time of publication
  • At g = 128 with S = 128 (zero overhead), gains are modest; S = 16 adds 0.0625 bpw
  • GPTVQ's unusually poor 25% result may reflect evaluation protocol differences
