Fault Tolerance Estimation in Digital Circuits with Visualised Generative Networks

Biel, Gaede, Glaser, Wolter, Schelle 2026-05-09 GAN Hopfield Fault Tolerance EDA
Key Finding
Mapping GAN topology onto a Hopfield energy operator and visualising the linear-to-chaotic transition of probability distributions in the complex plane yields quantitative fault-tolerance thresholds for all seven classical logic gates.
Key Metric
10–30%
Circuits fail at ~10–20% relative deviation for missing devices and ~20–30% for interchanged devices. NOT gates converge 2–3× faster than AND/NAND/NOR/XOR.
Why This Matters
Provides the first generative-network framework that bridges AI/EDA with per-gate quantitative failure thresholds — groundwork for deep-learning fault classifiers in circuit design.
Critical Edge
Fault thresholds identified by visual inspection of scatter plots, not formal statistical criteria. No comparison to ATPG, Monte Carlo, or formal verification baselines. Only elementary single-layer gates tested.

Methodology

GAN-to-Hopfield mapping
Hybrid GAN generator-discriminator topology is mapped onto a Hopfield network energy operator. Eigenvalue equation H[ξ] = E [ξ] yields eigenenergies independent of neuronal components at fixed uncertainty levels.
Complex-variable representation
Deviation function m(f(G), R) maps N-dimensional binary vectors to complex numbers: real part = generator modulations, imaginary part = discriminator outputs. Produces probability distributions in the complex plane.
Analog-to-digital circuit mapping
Seven classical logic gates (AND, OR, NOT, NAND, NOR, XOR, XNOR) modelled as analog circuits (switches, transistors, resistors, LEDs), then approximated by digital bitwise configurations at 4/8/12/16-bit widths.
Fault tolerance estimation
Random bitwise configurations are modulated through the circuit. The fault tolerance threshold is the uncertainty level where probability distributions transition from linear scaling to chaotic/nonlinear behaviour in the complex plane.
Three error modes
(a) Missing devices, (b) perturbations in input signals (equivalent to missing devices in single-layer model), (c) reversed-polarity / interchanged logical devices.

Results

  • Missing devices or erroneous inputs fail at ~10–20% relative deviation
  • Interchanged devices fail at ~20–30% relative deviation
  • AND/NAND gates require more iterations than OR/NOR/XOR/XNOR at given uncertainty
  • 8-bit configurations show larger deviations than 16-bit at the same uncertainty, with divergence starting around 30%
  • AND-OR, AND-NAND, AND-XOR combinations yield up to 70% deviation; AND-NOT and AND-NOR up to 50%
  • Computation time: seconds (4-bit) to minutes/hours (16-bit)
  • Exponential scaling of iteration steps as a function of uncertainty, largely independent of gate type

Strengths

  • Systematic evaluation of all seven classical gates across four bit widths
  • Complex-plane visualisation provides an intuitive lens on circuit perturbation behaviour
  • Clear quantitative thresholds (10–20%, 20–30%) for distinct failure modes
  • Gate-type dependent convergence analysis is practically useful
  • Honest positioning as foundation work rather than claiming end-to-end superiority

Limitations

  • Only elementary single-layer circuits — no sequential logic, feedback, or multi-layer designs
  • No comparison to established fault simulation baselines (Monte Carlo, ATPG, formal methods)
  • Subjective threshold definition via visual inspection, not formal criteria
  • Scalability concerns: minutes/hours already at 16-bit
  • Hopfield framing does not clearly improve over simpler perturbation-based approaches
  • No real circuit validation — all experiments are purely numerical

Open Questions

  • How does this compare in accuracy and efficiency to standard Monte Carlo fault simulation?
  • Can the complex-plane transition criterion be formalised with a statistical test?
  • Does the Hopfield mapping offer any computational advantage over direct bit-string perturbation and Hamming distance?
  • How would the method scale to multi-layer circuits with feedback or industrial circuit sizes?
  • Are the quantum-mechanical analogies metaphorical or do they correspond to actual quantum computing applications?

Critical Assessment

  • The GAN/Hopfield framing is creative but may add theoretical overhead without proportional practical benefit over established perturbation methods
  • Unusual mix of physics terminology ("eigenenergies," "coherent field configurations," "quantum statistical theory") with circuit analysis risks obscuring rather than clarifying the contribution
  • The claim of building a "foundation for a new neuronal network theory" connecting Hopfield theory to "quantum statistical theory" is unsupported by the presented results
  • The 26 references include many recent papers (2024–2026) with remarkably similar titles, potentially indicating a self-referential ecosystem
  • The "visualisation" in the title consists mainly of 2D scatter plots — not a substantial visual analytics contribution
