ZO-MOPI

Accelerating Zeroth-Order Spectral Optimization with Partial Orthogonalization from Power Iteration

Newton-Schulz full orthogonalization fails in zeroth-order settings because it amplifies noise, not signal. Streaming power iteration that preserves only dominant spectral directions fixes this, delivering 1.5x–3x convergence speedup with negligible memory overhead.

Jiahe Chen & Ziye Ma MOFA-LAB 2605.09034
zeroth-order optimization LLM fine-tuning spectral methods power iteration memory-efficient
Key Finding
Full orthogonalization via Newton-Schulz is counterproductive in zeroth-order optimization—it equalizes noisy gradient directions alongside signal, degrading updates. Replacing it with streaming power iteration that extracts only the top-k dominant singular vectors yields partial orthogonalization that respects the signal-to-noise structure.
Key Metric
1.5x–3x
Convergence speedup over ZO-Muon on OPT-13B and LLaMA3-8B SuperGLUE fine-tuning. SST-2 accuracy: 91.7% vs 90.4%. Memory overhead: only +0.34 GB.
Why This Matters
Zeroth-order optimization is the key enabler for fine-tuning LLMs on edge devices without backpropagation. A consistent speedup with negligible overhead directly translates to faster, cheaper model adaptation where compute is most constrained.
Critical Edge
No multiple random seeds or confidence intervals reported—results appear to be single runs. ZO-MOPI actually underperforms ZO-Muon on LLaMA3-8B RTE (5.06h vs 4.05h). Untested beyond 13B parameters; scaling to 70B is unknown.
Algorithm Pipeline — ZO-MOPI
Step 1
Subspace ZO Estimation
Project params into low-rank subspace A (r=64). Multi-point finite-difference gradient estimation.
›
Step 2
Streaming Power Iteration
Extract top-k=32 dominant singular vectors from momentum M via warm-started QR-based power iteration.
›
Step 3
Partial Orthogonalization
Update: Δ = (1/k) M V V¹. Only dominant spectral directions are preserved.
›
Step 4
Lazy Resampling
Subspace A refreshed every ν=500 steps. Momentum projected to new basis to maintain temporal continuity.

02 Theoretical Contributions

  • Proposition 1: Subspace-projected SPI is lossless—equivalent to full-space SPI
  • Lemma 4.1: Tracking error bounded by ||Tt|| ≤ ||Tt-1|| + γδ, where γ is the spectral gap ratio
  • Theorem 4.2: O(1/√T) convergence rate dependent on reduced subspace dimension dsub, not full parameter dimension
  • Dual role of momentum: Not just variance reduction—essential for temporal continuity in SPI; without it, dominant subspace fluctuates wildly on single noisy estimates

03 Strengths

  • Identifies a fundamental problem (noise amplification in ZO spectral optimization) with a principled solution
  • SPI is both theoretically cleaner and computationally cheaper than Newton-Schulz for this setting
  • Thorough evaluation: 3 model families, 4 tasks, multiple ZO/FO baselines
  • Negligible memory and runtime overhead—the method is essentially "free" on top of ZO-Muon
  • Good ablation studies showing why each component (SPI, momentum, lazy sampling) matters
  • The insight about momentum's dual role is a genuine contribution to understanding ZO optimization

04 Limitations & Concerns

  • No multiple random seeds or confidence intervals—results appear to be single runs
  • Underperforms ZO-Muon on LLaMA3-8B RTE (5.06h vs 4.05h)
  • Untested on models larger than 13B; scaling to 30B/70B is unknown
  • Spectral rank k and subspace rank r fixed across all experiments; ablations limited to smaller models
  • Only tested on English NLP benchmarks
  • Theoretical convergence rate has same O(1/√T) order as standard ZO-SGD—benefit appears in constants, not the rate itself

05 Experimental Setup

  • Models: Gemma2-2B, OPT-1.3B/13B, LLaMA3-8B
  • Tasks: SST-2, RTE, BoolQ, SQuAD (SuperGLUE)
  • Baselines: MeZO, LOZO, ZO-Muon, Adam, LoRA
  • Fixed hyperparams: r=64, k=32, ν=500; search only over Nq ∈ {4, 8, 16}
  • Hardware: 4x NVIDIA RTX PRO 6000 Blackwell (96 GB each), CUDA 13.0
  • Training: 1000 train/test examples per task, batch size 16, μ=1e-3

06 Open Questions

  • Can SPI-based partial orthogonalization benefit first-order Muon in low-precision (FP8, INT4) settings?
  • How does optimal spectral rank k scale with model size?
  • Can ZO-MOPI combine with quantization (4-bit weights) for further memory reduction?
  • Is the lazy sampling interval ν=500 robust across different training dynamics?
  • Why does ZO-MOPI underperform on LLaMA3-8B RTE specifically?
