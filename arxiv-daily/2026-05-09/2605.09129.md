> **一句话总结：** 本文系统性地证明现有电路发现方法返回的是数据集特异性电路而非通用任务电路，并可以错误地将多个不同机制合并为单个高忠实度电路；为此提出DCD框架，通过按模型处理方式（边归因向量）聚类样本来发现多个独立电路，每个电路对其所属组别的忠实度显著高于假设驱动的单一电路方法。

> **TL;DR:** This paper shows that existing circuit discovery methods (EAP-IG, EAP, E-Act) produce dataset-specific circuits that fail to generalize across semantically equivalent dataset variants, and critically, can merge two entirely distinct mechanisms into a single high-faithfulness circuit. The proposed Data-driven Circuit Discovery (DCD) framework clusters examples by their per-example edge attribution vectors (binarized, PCA-reduced, Jaccard-distance clustered with gap-statistic K selection), then discovers a separate circuit per cluster. On all-task mixed datasets across GPT-2, Qwen2.5-7B, and Llama-3.1-8B, DCD achieves 86% vs. 64% faithfulness for EAP-IG on GPT-2 at circuit size 0.05, and produces coherent, task-separated clusters with clear block-diagonal faithfulness structure.

## 核心贡献

- 系统性证明现有假设驱动的电路发现方法发现的是**数据集特异性电路**而非通用任务电路：即使保持任务语义的微小数据集变化（句法、复杂度、领域），也会产生边重叠度低（Jaccard 0.30-0.66）且跨数据集忠实度低的电路
- 揭示了一个关键的失败模式：将两个独立任务（实体绑定和算术）的数据集混合后，现有方法仍返回**单个高忠实度电路**（89.8%和85.1%），即使这两个任务的独立电路互不忠实——证明不同机制被错误合并
- 提出DCD框架，通过两阶段工作流程（聚类+独立电路发现）来发现多个机制特异性电路
- 实验证明DCD在GPT-2上达到86%忠实度（vs. EAP-IG的64%），且聚类结果自然按任务和变体分组，产生清晰的块对角忠实度结构

## Key Contributions

- Systematically demonstrates that hypothesis-driven circuit discovery methods (EAP-IG, EAP, E-Act) produce **dataset-specific circuits** rather than general task circuits across four tasks (IOI, entity binding, arithmetic, sequence completion), with cross-dataset faithfulness drops up to **79 percentage points** (entity binding syntax axis, Qwen2.5).
- Reveals that existing methods can **mix distinct mechanisms into a single circuit**: when mixing entity binding and arithmetic data (whose independent circuits have near-zero cross-faithfulness), a single circuit achieves 89.8% faithfulness on arithmetic and 85.1% on entity binding at circuit size 0.05 — an artifact of averaging attributions across mechanisms.
- Proposes **Data-driven Circuit Discovery (DCD)**, a two-stage framework: (1) compute per-example edge attribution vectors, binarize (retaining edges accounting for 99% attribution mass), reduce via PCA to r=20 dimensions, cluster using K-means with gap-statistic K selection and Jaccard distance; (2) discover independent circuits per cluster.
- Shows DCD achieves **86% vs. 64% faithfulness** (EAP-IG) on GPT-2 all-task mixed dataset at circuit size 0.05, with clusters that naturally separate by task and even by within-task variant (e.g., 3-person IOI, letter IOI, and 2-person IOI form separate clusters).

## Methodology

The paper proceeds in three phases corresponding to three research questions:

**RQ1: Do hypothesis-driven methods discover general task circuits? (Section 4)**
- Construct multiple datasets per task varying along three axes: complexity (e.g., 2-person vs. 3-person IOI), syntax (e.g., active vs. passive), and domain (e.g., person names vs. letter labels).
- Independently discover circuits on each variant using EAP-IG, then evaluate cross-dataset faithfulness and Jaccard edge overlap.

**RQ2: Can hypothesis-driven methods mix distinct mechanisms? (Section 5)**
- Mix entity binding and arithmetic data at varying proportions (EB=0.0 to EB=1.0 in 0.1 increments).
- Discover a single circuit per mixture and evaluate on pure task test sets.

**RQ3: Can we discover circuits without these assumptions? (Section 6)**
- DCD Algorithm: (1) Compute per-example edge attribution vectors via EAP-IG; (2) Binarize by retaining edges accounting for 99% of attribution mass; (3) Reduce to r=20 via PCA; (4) Cluster using K-means with gap statistic for K selection, using Jaccard distance; (5) Discover independent circuit per cluster.
- Evaluate using best-of-K faithfulness: f*(x_i) = max_k f(C_k, {x_i}), averaged over test set.
- Compare against baselines: EAP-IG, EAP, E-Act (hypothesis-driven), Random Edges, K-Representative (medoid-based), K-Random (random partitions with same K*).

**Models:** GPT-2 Small (124M), Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct.
**Tasks:** IOI, entity binding, arithmetic addition, sequence completion.

## Results & Highlights

- **Cross-dataset faithfulness drops**: Largest drops at circuit size 0.05 reach 79 percentage points (IOI syntax on Qwen2.5). Entity binding position variants show P2 circuit dropping from 111% to 33% when evaluated on P8.
- **Low edge overlap**: Mean pairwise Jaccard similarity ranges from 0.30 to 0.66 across all (task, model, axis) cells, with a gradient pattern (adjacent positions share more edges, distant ones fewer).
- **Mechanism mixing**: Adding just 10% arithmetic data to entity binding raises faithfulness from 5.8% to 87.1% on arithmetic — an 81.3-point jump. Edge overlap is a smooth function of mixing ratio (0.26 for pure task extremes, 0.79-0.89 for adjacent ratios).
- **DCD faithfulness gains**: On GPT-2 at circuit size 0.05, DCD achieves 86% vs. 64% for EAP-IG, a 22-point improvement. Consistent gains across all three models.
- **Coherent cluster structure**: DCD naturally groups examples by task. On GPT-2, 7 clusters separate IOI variants (2-person, 3-person, letter) and sequence completion variants (2-gram, 3-gram, 4-gram) into distinct clusters. Per-example faithfulness shows clear block-diagonal structure.
- **K-Random comparison**: Random partitions achieve comparable best-of-K faithfulness but produce diffuse, non-coherent circuits with no mechanism alignment, demonstrating that DCD's clustering is doing meaningful work.
- **Single-task results**: DCD also discovers multiple mechanisms within single tasks (e.g., IOI with name-position and domain variants), not just across obviously distinct tasks.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: High. The systematic demonstration that hypothesis-driven methods produce dataset-specific circuits and the mechanism-merging experiment are both novel and impactful contributions. The DCD framework itself is a natural but important methodological advance. The paper's critique of the single-circuit-per-task assumption is well-timed given the growing multi-mechanism literature.
- **Rigor**: Strong. The three-axis dataset variation framework (complexity, syntax, domain) is well-designed and thorough. Testing across three circuit discovery methods (EAP-IG, EAP, E-Act), three model families, and four tasks provides robust evidence. The mixed-dataset experiment is particularly elegant.
- **Reproducibility**: Good. Code and datasets are released. Hyperparameters are specified (r=20 PCA, gamma=0.99 binarization, gap statistic with B=20, random_state=42 for all major components). The clustering pipeline is standard (scikit-learn K-means, PCA/SVD).
- **Significance**: High. This paper challenges a foundational assumption of the mechanistic interpretability field. If the community adopts DCD-like approaches, it would shift how circuit analysis is conducted.
- **Red flags and concerns**:
  - The best-of-K evaluation metric inherently favors methods that produce multiple circuits, since even random partitions (K-Random) substantially outperform single-circuit methods. The paper acknowledges this but the comparison is not entirely fair — a more neutral evaluation (e.g., without oracle assignment) would strengthen the claims.
  - DCD inherits the assumptions and limitations of its underlying attribution method (EAP-IG), including sensitivity to the choice of corrupted prompts.
  - The gap statistic selects K* ranging from 2 to 13 across settings (Table 10), which is quite variable. Whether this variability reflects genuine mechanistic structure or artifacts of the clustering pipeline is not fully addressed.
  - The paper does not address the assignment problem at inference time: given a new example, how do you determine which circuit to apply without re-running attribution and clustering?
  - The tasks are all relatively simple and synthetic. Whether DCD's advantages hold for complex, open-ended tasks (e.g., natural language reasoning, multi-step inference) remains unknown.

## Strengths

- Identifies and rigorously demonstrates a fundamental flaw in the prevailing circuit discovery paradigm with controlled, systematic experiments.
- The mixed-dataset mechanism-merging experiment is a compelling and intuitive demonstration of the problem.
- DCD is conceptually simple and modular — it can wrap any existing circuit discovery method.
- Produces interpretable cluster structure that aligns with task/variant boundaries without being told what those boundaries are.
- Comprehensive evaluation across three models, four tasks, three circuit discovery methods, and multiple clustering variants.
- Honest discussion of limitations, including the best-of-K evaluation advantage and the open question of test-time assignment.

## Limitations

- The best-of-K faithfulness evaluation uses oracle assignment (selecting the best circuit per example), which inflates DCD's apparent advantage. The paper lacks a realistic test-time assignment procedure.
- DCD adds computational cost: per-example attribution (already expensive for large models) followed by clustering and per-cluster circuit discovery.
- The clustering pipeline has multiple hyperparameters (binarization threshold gamma=0.99, PCA dimension r=20, clustering algorithm, distance metric) whose sensitivity is only partially explored.
- The paper does not address whether discovered clusters correspond to human-interpretable mechanistic distinctions, beyond showing task-level separation.
- All tasks are synthetic and narrow; the approach is untested on real-world language tasks where defining "mechanism" is harder.
- The number of clusters K* varies considerably across settings (2-13), raising questions about stability and interpretability.
- DCD does not resolve the fundamental question of what scope a "mechanism" should have — it merely shifts the problem from human-defined task boundaries to data-driven cluster boundaries.

## Relevance

本文对语言模型的机械可解释性研究具有直接且重要的价值。该论文挑战了电路发现领域的基本假设（单电路假设），提出了DCD框架来让数据揭示模型内部的机制结构。对于从事模型安全性和可控性研究、模型行为分析和电路发现的研究者而言，这项工作提供了重要的方法论启示和实用工具。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How can we assign new examples to the correct DCD circuit at test time without re-computing attributions and clustering? Can a lightweight classifier be trained on cluster membership?
- Do DCD's discovered clusters correspond to genuinely distinct computational mechanisms that admit human-readable descriptions, or are they statistical artifacts of the attribution landscape?
- How does DCD interact with sparse autoencoder-based feature discovery methods (e.g., can SAE features provide a better clustering signal than edge attributions)?
- What happens when DCD is applied to a truly complex, open-ended task like natural language inference or multi-hop reasoning?
- Is the smooth gradient in edge overlap (as a function of mixing ratio) a fundamental property of attribution-based methods, or could alternative attribution schemes produce sharper boundaries?
- Can DCD be extended to discover overlapping or hierarchical circuits, where examples may belong to multiple mechanisms simultaneously?
