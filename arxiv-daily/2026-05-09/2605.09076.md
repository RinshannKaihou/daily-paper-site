> **一句话总结：** 本文提出 Self-Anchored Consensus (SAC)，一种受 MSR 算法启发的去中心化迭代过滤-精炼协议，通过接收端评分替代自报告置信度，结合 $(F{+}1)$-robustness 图拓扑条件，使多 LLM 智能体系统在存在拜占庭（恶意/故障）节点时仍能保持甚至提升推理准确性，实验显示 H-Majority 达 76.7%--96.7%，而基线方法 CP-WBFT 仅为 16.7%--46.7%。

> **TL;DR:** This paper introduces Self-Anchored Consensus (SAC), a fully decentralized MSR-inspired protocol for LLM multi-agent systems that replaces self-reported confidence with receiver-side evaluation. Each agent independently scores neighbors' responses, filters out the lowest-scoring $F$ entries, and refines its answer via LLM re-prompting. Under $(F{+}1)$-robustness graph conditions, honest agents provably retain at least one reliable neighbor's response after filtering and the filtered network preserves connectivity. Experiments on MATH500 Level 4 and Commonsense170k with $n \in \{7, 9\}$ agents show SAC achieves H-Majority of 76.7%--96.7% across topologies, while CP-WBFT collapses to 16.7%--46.7% under adversarial attack -- even a single adversarial agent with falsified confidence is sufficient to catastrophically degrade CP-WBFT.

## 核心贡献

- 提出 SAC (Self-Anchored Consensus) 协议：受经典 MSR (Mean-Subsequence-Reduced) 算法启发，设计完全去中心化的迭代式过滤与精炼机制。核心创新在于用接收端评分替代自报告置信度，从根本上消除拜占庭智能体通过伪造置信度操纵系统的攻击面。
- 建立 $(F{+}1)$-robustness 图拓扑条件，证明该条件可保证：(1) 过滤后每个诚实智能体至少保留一个与可靠智能体质量相当的响应（Property 4.2）；(2) 过滤后的网络仍保持连通性，不会分裂为孤立子群（Property 4.3）。
- 系统性地揭示现有方法的脆弱性：CP-WBFT 依赖自报告置信度（单个伪造高置信度的对抗者即可使其崩溃），Trusted-MultiLLM 依赖领导者选举（连续拜占庭领导者导致昂贵重选），Decentralized-Ms 的几何均值滤波在高置信度评分下表现脆弱。
- 在 MATH500 Level 4 和 Commonsense170k 上，以多种通信拓扑（MERG、完全图、优先连接图、Erdos-Renyi 随机图）进行实验，SAC 在所有配置下均有效抑制拜占庭影响。

## Key Contributions

- Propose Self-Anchored Consensus (SAC), a fully decentralized iterative filter-and-refine protocol inspired by the MSR family of algorithms from classical Byzantine consensus. The key design choice is receiver-side confidence evaluation: each agent $i$ independently scores neighbor $j$'s response via $s_{i \to j}^{(t)} = \phi_i(x, r_j^{(t)})$, so Byzantine agents cannot inflate their own reliability.
- Derive $(F{+}1)$-robustness graph conditions guaranteeing that (1) after filtering at most $F$ lowest-scoring neighbors, every agent retains at least one response as reliable as the minimum among honest agents (Property 4.2), and (2) the filtered subgraph maintains a rooted out-branching, preventing network fragmentation (Property 4.3).
- Expose critical vulnerabilities in prior work: CP-WBFT collapses when a single adversarial agent reports falsified confidence of 1.0 (BFTI of -10.4% to -54.7%); leader-based methods like Trusted-MultiLLM suffer under consecutive Byzantine leaders.
- Empirically validate SAC across 4 graph topologies (MERG, complete, preferential-attachment, Erdos-Renyi) on MATH500 Level 4 and Commonsense170k, with scalability experiments up to $n=9$ agents.

## Methodology

**Problem Setup:** $n$ LLM agents collaborate over an undirected communication graph $\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E})$ to answer queries with objectively verifiable ground-truth answers. A subset $\mathcal{B} \subset \mathcal{V}$ are Byzantine, split into faulty agents (follow protocol but produce incorrect outputs due to weak models) and adversarial agents (arbitrarily deviate from protocol, send strategically crafted responses).

**SAC Protocol (Algorithm 1):** At each round $t = 0, \ldots, T{-}1$, every agent $i$ performs four steps:

1. **Broadcast:** Agent $i$ sends its current response $r_i^{(t)}$ to all neighbors $\mathcal{N}_i$.
2. **Score:** For each neighbor $j$, agent $i$ computes a receiver-side score $s_{i \to j}^{(t)} = \phi_i(x, r_j^{(t)}) \in [0, 1]$ using its own LLM via a verification prompt. Critically, this score depends only on agent $i$'s own computation -- not on any self-reported signal from agent $j$.
3. **Filter:** Agent $i$ identifies the set of neighbors scoring below its self-score: $\mathcal{L}_i^{(t)} = \{j \in \mathcal{N}_i \mid s_{i \to j}^{(t)} < s_{i \to i}^{(t)}\}$, then discards the $\min(F, |\mathcal{L}_i^{(t)}|)$ lowest-scoring entries, yielding the retained set $\mathcal{R}_i^{(t)}$.
4. **Refine:** Agent $i$ conditions its LLM on its current response and the retained neighbor responses (with scores), weighted by reliability: $r_i^{(t+1)} = \mathcal{M}_i(x, r_i^{(t)}, \{(r_j^{(t)}, s_{i \to j}^{(t)})\}_{j \in \mathcal{R}_i^{(t)}})$. The refinement prompt instructs the LLM to prefer high-scored responses but retain its own answer if believed correct.

**Graph Design:** Four $(F{+}1)$-robust topologies are evaluated: (1) $\gamma$-MERG (maximally robust with minimal edges, $r = \lceil n/2 \rceil$), (2) complete graphs ($r = n/2$), (3) preferential-attachment graphs ($r$-robust by construction), and (4) Erdos-Renyi random graphs (verified for $r$-robustness before simulation).

**Threat Model:** Adversarial Byzantine agents broadcast cached weak-model responses from out-of-distribution queries and report falsified confidence of 1.0 when interacting with confidence-based baselines. Faulty Byzantine agents follow the protocol but use weaker models (gpt-3.5-turbo).

## Results & Highlights

- **CP-WBFT collapses under adversarial attack:** A single adversarial agent with falsified confidence causes BFTI of -10.4% to -24.8% on MATH500 and -32.4% to -54.7% on Commonsense170k. Contamination occurs from Round 1 and persists, even when honest agents form a clear majority (6 of 7).
- **SAC effectively contains Byzantine influence:** SAC achieves non-negative BFTI in 11 of 12 configurations. H-Majority reaches 76.7%--96.7% across all topologies and benchmarks, versus CP-WBFT's 16.7%--46.7%.
- **Strong agents are preserved, weak agents are lifted:** Under SAC, strong-group accuracy remains within a few points of initial values (e.g., 84% to 88% on MERG), while weak-group accuracy improves (e.g., 23% to 43% on MERG). CP-WBFT shows the opposite: superficial weak-group gains come with substantial strong-group degradation (84% to 49%).
- **Topology matters within the robustness class:** All evaluated graphs satisfy $(F{+}1)$-robustness, yet SAC's BFTI on MATH500 spans +3.8% to +7.6%, indicating the robustness condition is necessary but not sufficient for optimal performance. More structured topologies (MERG) tend to better preserve strong-group accuracy.
- **Scalability to $n=9$:** SAC maintains H-Majority of 86.7%--90.0% with 6 strong honest, 2 weak honest, and 1 adversarial agent, while CP-WBFT drops to 20.0%--43.3%.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: High.** This is the first work to bring MSR-type algorithms and $r$-robustness conditions from classical distributed consensus into LLM multi-agent systems. The receiver-side scoring mechanism is a simple but impactful design choice that eliminates an entire attack surface. The connection between graph-theoretic robustness and LLM-MAS is genuinely novel.
- **Rigor: Strong.** The paper provides formal properties (4.2 and 4.3) with proofs grounded in established results from LeBlanc et al. (2013). The experimental protocol extends Zheng et al. (2026) with harder benchmarks (MATH500 Level 4 instead of 10 curated GSM8K problems) and multiple topologies. The ablation over Byzantine-to-weak composition is thorough.
- **Reproducibility: Good.** Full prompt templates are provided in Appendix B, graph construction procedures are specified, and the 30-problem subsets are listed with qids in Appendix C. The use of standard APIs (gpt-4o-mini, gpt-3.5-turbo) facilitates replication.
- **Significance: High.** As LLM agents are increasingly deployed in collaborative settings, the demonstrated vulnerability of self-reported confidence protocols is an important finding. The SAC protocol provides a principled alternative.
- **Red flags / Concerns:**
  - **Limited scale:** Experiments are limited to $n \in \{7, 9\}$ agents. Real-world deployments may involve orders of magnitude more agents; whether the $(F{+}1)$-robustness condition remains tractable at scale is unclear.
  - **Single adversarial strategy:** Only one type of adversarial behavior is tested (cached weak-model replay with falsified confidence). More sophisticated attacks -- e.g., generating plausible but subtly incorrect responses, or collusion among multiple adversarial agents -- are not evaluated.
  - **LLM-as-judge bottleneck:** The receiver-side scoring function $\phi_i$ relies on the same LLM that produces responses. If the LLM cannot reliably distinguish correct from incorrect answers (precisely the scenario where multi-agent collaboration is most needed), the filtering step may fail. This fundamental tension is acknowledged but not deeply analyzed.
  - **Evaluation on 30 problems only:** Each benchmark uses only 30 sampled problems. While this is justified for cost reasons with proprietary APIs, it raises questions about statistical robustness and generalizability.
  - **No comparison with non-LLM baselines:** The paper compares against CP-WBFT but not against simpler non-LLM aggregation methods (e.g., majority vote without refinement), which would clarify the marginal benefit of the LLM-based refinement step.

## Strengths

- Principled theoretical grounding via graph-theoretic robustness conditions, with formal proofs.
- Clean, modular protocol design: scoring, filtering, and refinement are clearly separated.
- Practical relevance: addresses a genuine and growing vulnerability in multi-agent LLM deployments.
- Comprehensive evaluation across 4 topologies, 2 benchmarks, and ablation studies on agent composition.
- Honest about limitations: the authors explicitly acknowledge limited scale, single adversarial strategy, and the LLM-as-judge bottleneck.

## Limitations

- Experiments are limited to $n \in \{7, 9\}$ agents with $F = 3$; scalability to larger networks is untested.
- Only one adversarial strategy is evaluated (cached weak-model replay with falsified confidence). Sophisticated attacks (e.g., plausible but incorrect responses, collusion) are left to future work.
- The receiver-side scoring relies on the LLM's ability to judge response quality, which is circular -- the LLM must already "know" the correct answer to score others accurately.
- Only single-turn responses are evaluated; multi-turn reasoning, tool use, and dynamic topologies are out of scope.
- The 30-problem evaluation subsets, while harder than prior work's 10-problem GSM8K subset, remain small for drawing strong statistical conclusions.

## Relevance

本文与多智能体LLM系统的鲁棒性和安全性研究高度相关。对于关注分布式AI系统对抗性鲁棒性、共识机制设计、以及LLM智能体可信协作的研究者来说，SAC 提供了一种理论扎实且实验有效的去中心化解决方案。其将经典拜占庭容错理论引入LLM-MAS的思路具有启发性，值得深入研究其扩展性和在更复杂攻击场景下的表现。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does the receiver-side scoring perform when adversarial agents generate sophisticated, plausible-but-wrong responses that are difficult for the scoring LLM to detect?
- Can SAC scale to networks of 50+ agents while maintaining tractable $(F{+}1)$-robust graph construction and acceptable communication overhead?
- How sensitive is SAC to the choice of underlying LLM? Would smaller open-source models provide adequate scoring quality?
- What happens under colluding adversarial agents that coordinate their responses to overwhelm the filtering mechanism?
- Could the receiver-side scoring be augmented with external verification tools (e.g., code execution, formal verification) to improve filtering accuracy on hard reasoning tasks?
- How does SAC interact with multi-turn reasoning chains, where errors may compound across rounds in more complex ways?
