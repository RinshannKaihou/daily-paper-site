> **一句话总结：** 本文通过对比Pythia（MHA）和Qwen2.5（GQA）两类模型在三种电路任务上的表现，发现分组查询注意力架构比模型规模更能决定电路的可定位性，并在Qwen2.5的事实回忆电路上发现了从分散到单一瓶颈层的离散相变。

> **TL;DR:** This paper systematically compares circuit geometry between MHA (Pythia) and GQA (Qwen2.5) models across IOI, induction heads, and factual recall. GQA models concentrate circuits into 1-6 heads for 80% damage versus 4-135 for MHA at matched scales. Within Qwen2.5, factual recall undergoes a discrete phase transition between 0.5B and 1.5B, collapsing from a distributed circuit to a single layer-0 bottleneck. Architecture, not parameter count, is the primary determinant of circuit tractability.

## 核心贡献

- 在六种模型规模（160M至7B）上系统对比了MHA和GQA两种注意力架构对三种电路类型（间接对象识别、归纳头、事实回忆）的影响
- 发现GQA架构产生高度集中的电路：Qwen2.5仅需1-6次消融即可造成80%电路损伤，而Pythia需要4-135次，且在匹配规模下差异显著（如共享事实回忆：50 vs 1）
- 在Qwen2.5模型家族中发现事实回忆电路的离散相变：0.5B模型将事实回忆分散在第4层，1.5B和7B则完全坍缩到第0层，单次KV头消融即可造成97%精度损失
- 证明GQA电路在输入难度变化下保持稳定，而MHA电路的拓扑结构随输入条件显著改变，这对安全监控具有实际意义

## Key Contributions

- Provides the first systematic cross-architecture comparison of circuit geometry, isolating attention mechanism as an independent variable across six model sizes (160M to 7B parameters)
- Demonstrates that GQA concentrates circuits into 1-6 heads for 80% task damage versus 4-135 heads for MHA at comparable scales, with matched-scale comparisons showing dramatic gaps (e.g., shared factual recall: Pythia-1.4B needs 50 ablations vs. Qwen2.5-1.5B needs 1)
- Discovers a discrete phase transition in factual recall within Qwen2.5: the 0.5B model distributes factual recall at layer 4, while 1.5B and 7B collapse it entirely to layer 0, where a single KV head ablation causes 97.2% accuracy damage
- Shows GQA circuits are mechanistically stable across input difficulty conditions (per-model vs. shared facts produce identical circuit locations), whereas MHA circuits shift their top contributing heads and critical layers substantially between conditions

## Methodology

**Models:** Two architecture families with three sizes each:
- Pythia (MHA): 160M (12L, 12H), 1.4B (24L, 16H), 6.9B (32L, 32H)
- Qwen2.5 (GQA): 0.5B (24L, 14Q, 2KV), 1.5B (28L, 12Q, 2KV), 7B (28L, 28Q, 4KV)

**Tasks and Data:**
- IOI: 500 sentences from fahamu/ioi dataset (26M sentences), filtered for single-token names; scored via logit difference logit(IO) - logit(S) with 20-sentence per-head ablation scoring
- Induction heads: 200 random repeated-token sequences [prefix][A][B][suffix][A]; measured ICL loss (cross-entropy of predicting B at final position)
- Factual recall: curated 493-fact set spanning 10 domains, all single-token answers; two conditions (per-model correct facts and shared intersection facts) to control for difficulty

**Metrics:**
- Top head score: logit/accuracy drop from ablating the single most important head
- Heads-to-80%: greedy ablations needed for 80% task damage (lower = more concentrated circuit)

**Tooling:** TransformerLens (Nanda & Bloom, 2022) for all experiments. All models publicly available on Hugging Face.

## Results & Highlights

- **IOI (Table 1):** All three Qwen2.5 models achieve heads-to-80% = 1; Pythia ranges from 2-5. Qwen2.5 top head scores are 4-8x higher than Pythia at matched scales (e.g., Qwen2.5-1.5B: 1.860 vs. Pythia-1.4B: 0.231). Qwen2.5-1.5B and 7B concentrate at layer 0; Qwen2.5-0.5B at layer 23.
- **Induction (Table 3):** Pythia-1.4B/6.9B need 22/28 ablations respectively (circuit becomes more distributed with scale). All Qwen2.5 models need 2-6 ablations across a 14x parameter range. Qwen2.5-7B (7B) needs only 6 ablations vs. Pythia-1.4B (1.4B) needing 22.
- **Factual recall phase transition (Tables 4-5):** Qwen2.5-0.5B locates factual recall at layer 4 (36.4% damage from L0 ablation); Qwen2.5-1.5B and 7B collapse to layer 0 (97.2% and 92.8% damage respectively). Pythia requires 50-135 ablations for shared facts and shows different circuit geometry between per-model and shared conditions.
- **Stability:** Qwen2.5 circuit geometry is identical across per-model and shared fact conditions at every scale. Pythia circuit geometry shifts substantially (e.g., Pythia-6.9B top head moves from L4 to L16 between conditions).

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** High. The framing of architecture vs. scale for interpretability is genuinely new. The phase transition finding is a novel empirical contribution. Prior work (Lieberum et al., 2023; Lindsey et al., 2025) studied scaling but did not control for architecture.
- **Rigor:** Good experimental design with multiple tasks, two difficulty conditions, negative controls (random head ablation), and greedy ordering checks (Table 2). The per-model vs. shared fact distinction is a useful control. However, the paper uses only one GQA family and one MHA family.
- **Reproducibility:** Strong. All models, datasets, and tools are public. The methodology (head ablation, logit difference) is standard and well-documented.
- **Significance:** Practically significant if the findings generalize. Most frontier models already use GQA, meaning large deployed models may be more interpretable than assumed.
- **Red flags:**
  - The authors honestly acknowledge the key confound: Pythia and Qwen2.5 differ in training data, tokenizer, and training recipe beyond attention mechanism. The GQA explanation is plausible but not isolated.
  - The phase transition claim rests on only three model sizes within one family. More intermediate sizes would strengthen this finding.
  - Single author limits the scope of validation -- no human subjects, no external audit of circuit interpretations.

## Strengths

- Clean, well-motivated experimental design that addresses a real and widely held assumption in the field
- Comprehensive appendix with per-model head tables, heatmaps for all six models, and diagnostic analyses
- Honest limitations section that directly acknowledges the architecture vs. training confound
- The factual recall dataset design (493 facts, 10 domains, single-token answers) is carefully constructed to avoid multi-token complications
- Actionable implications: interpretability researchers should prioritize GQA models and benchmark across architectures

## Limitations

- Cannot fully separate architecture from training: Pythia and Qwen2.5 differ in data, tokenizer, and training recipe. A controlled experiment with matched models trained with and without GQA is needed to confirm causality.
- Only three circuit types tested; generalization to safety-relevant circuits (deception, goal-directed reasoning) is unknown.
- Only two architecture families studied; MLA (DeepSeek), multi-query attention, and linear attention variants are not covered.
- The phase transition is identified between only two model sizes (0.5B and 1.5B); finer granularity would clarify whether it is truly discrete or gradual.
- GQA's circuit concentration may reflect information bottleneck rather than true interpretability -- concentrated circuits could be harder to understand despite being easier to locate.

## Relevance

本文对机制可解释性研究具有直接参考价值。GQA架构对电路可定位性的影响意味着大多数已部署的前沿模型（已广泛采用GQA）可能比预期更易于电路级分析，这对可解释性工具开发和安全监控方法的选择有实际指导意义。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Would training the same architecture with and without GQA (controlled experiment) confirm that attention mechanism alone drives circuit concentration?
- Does the phase transition generalize to other GQA model families (Llama 2/3, Mistral) and at what parameter thresholds?
- Are concentrated GQA circuits genuinely more interpretable (understandable), or merely easier to locate via ablation?
- How do findings apply to safety-relevant circuits such as deception, sycophancy, or reward hacking?
- Does circuit concentration in GQA models imply greater vulnerability to adversarial attacks targeting the bottleneck head?
