Architecture, Not Scale: Circuit Localization in Large Language Models

Sohan Venkatesh · Manipal Institute of Technology Bengaluru · 2026

GQA concentrates circuits into 1–6 heads for 80% task damage versus 4–135 for MHA at matched scales. The attention mechanism, not parameter count, is the primary determinant of circuit tractability in large language models.

Key Metric
97.2%
accuracy damage from a single KV head ablation in Qwen2.5-1.5B factual recall (layer 0 bottleneck)
Why This Matters
Most frontier models already use GQA, meaning deployed LLMs may be far more interpretable than the field has assumed—with direct implications for safety monitoring.
Critical Edge
Cannot fully isolate architecture from training: Pythia and Qwen2.5 differ in data, tokenizer, and training recipe. The GQA explanation is plausible but not causally confirmed.

Three tasks: indirect object identification (IOI), induction heads, factual recall (493 facts, 10 domains). Metric: greedy heads-to-80% damage. Tooling: TransformerLens.

Results

  • IOI: All Qwen2.5 models need just 1 head for 80% damage; top-head scores 4–8x higher than Pythia at matched scales.
  • Induction: Pythia circuits grow more distributed with scale (22–28 heads). All Qwen2.5 models stay concentrated at 2–6 heads across a 14x parameter range.
  • Factual recall (shared): Pythia-1.4B needs 50 ablations; Qwen2.5-1.5B needs 1.
  • Stability: GQA circuits identical across input-difficulty conditions; MHA circuits shift top heads and critical layers substantially.

Between 0.5B and 1.5B parameters, factual recall collapses from a distributed circuit into a single layer-0 bottleneck—a discrete phase transition within one model family.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Clean cross-architecture experimental design addressing a real field-wide assumption
  • Two difficulty conditions per task (per-model vs. shared facts) as a useful control
  • Honest limitations section directly acknowledging the architecture-vs-training confound
  • All models, datasets, and tools publicly available—strong reproducibility

Limitations

  • Only one GQA family and one MHA family; cannot causally attribute differences to attention mechanism alone
  • Three circuit types only; safety-relevant circuits (deception, goal-directed reasoning) untested
  • Phase transition observed between only two model sizes—finer granularity needed
  • Circuit concentration may reflect information bottleneck, not true interpretability

Open Questions

  1. Would training the same architecture with and without GQA confirm that attention mechanism alone drives circuit concentration?
  2. Does the phase transition generalize to other GQA families (Llama 2/3, Mistral) and at what parameter thresholds?
  3. Are concentrated GQA circuits genuinely more understandable, or merely easier to locate via ablation?
  4. How do findings apply to safety-relevant circuits—deception, sycophancy, reward hacking?
  5. Does circuit concentration in GQA imply greater vulnerability to adversarial attacks targeting the bottleneck head?
