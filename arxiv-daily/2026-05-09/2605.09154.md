> **一句话总结：** 提出噪声二次系统（NQS）作为LLM预训练损失的预测模型，以模型规模N、batch size B和权重更新次数K为输入，首次支持可变batch size的损失预测，在最高1000倍计算预算外推下优于Chinchilla模型，并可在时间-内存-算力多重约束下自动寻找近最优训练配置。

> **TL;DR:** A noisy quadratic system (NQS) models LLM pre-training test loss as a function of model size (N), batch size (B), and weight updates (K). It is the first loss prediction model that handles varying batch sizes. On Pythia models up to 2B parameters with compute budgets from 1 to 60,000 PetaFLOPs, NQS outperforms Chinchilla Method 3 on holdout data at up to 1000x compute extrapolation (holdout Huber loss 2.5 vs. 9.0 for IsoFLOPs, 5.6 vs. 9.8 for B-K plane). NQS also finds near-optimal N/B/K configurations under compound resource constraints (time, memory, compute). Accepted to ICML 2026.

## 核心贡献

- 提出噪声二次系统（NQS），一个基于机制的LLM损失预测模型，输入为N/B/K三变量，共7+1个可学习参数
- 首个能处理可变batch size的损失预测模型，填补了Chinchilla模型无法区分不同batch size的空白
- 在最高1000倍计算外推下，IsoFLOPs和batch-size-variation两个holdout测试集上均显著优于Chinchilla Method 3
- 展示了在多重资源约束（计算量、训练时间、GPU内存、数据量）下联合优化N/B/K配置的实用性
- 通过严格train/holdout分割评估，避免过拟合，证明了模型的外推鲁棒性
- 主张用可验证的损失预测模型替代日益复杂的启发式scaling law

## Key Contributions

- Introduces the Noisy Quadratic System (NQS), a mechanistic loss model for LLM pre-training that predicts test loss from (N, B, K) with 7+1 learnable parameters
- First loss prediction model to handle variable batch sizes, addressing a key limitation of Chinchilla's approach which conflates different batch sizes given fixed D
- Demonstrates superior extrapolation performance: at up to 1000x compute gap, NQS achieves holdout Huber loss of 2.5 (IsoFLOPs) and 5.6 (B-K plane) vs. Chinchilla's 9.0 and 9.8
- Shows practical application for compound resource allocation under time-memory-compute constraints, with model-selected configurations close to ground-truth optimal
- Uses strict train/holdout splits with compute gaps to rigorously evaluate extrapolation, showing NQS train and holdout errors remain comparable
- Evaluates on two model families (Pythia up to 2B on OWT2, Llama up to 0.5B on LM1B) for generalization

## Methodology

The NQS is derived from three lines of theoretical work on neural network training dynamics: (1) linear regression models of scaling (Bordelon et al., 2024), which decompose expected risk into irreducible error, approximation error, bias, and variance; (2) the Noisy Quadratic Model (Zhang et al., 2019), which models SGD dynamics on a quadratic loss surface; and (3) the effect of LayerNorm on effective learning rates (van Laarhoven, 2017).

The NQS models LLM test loss as the expected risk of stochastic optimization on a quadratic function Q(w). Key assumptions: eigenvalues of the Hessian follow a power law (lambda_n = n^Q/q), signal power decays as a power law (n^P/p), and mini-batch noise has its own power law spectrum (n^R/r with variance proportional to 1/B). The test loss decomposes into three terms: approximation error (Eapp, depends on N), bias (Ebias, depends on N and K), and variance (Evar, depends on N, B, K). The LayerNorm effect is modeled by dynamically adjusting the learning rate as gamma_k proportional to 1/||w_k||^2, with an additional parameter s representing initialization weight norm.

Computation is O(1) in N and K via Euler-Maclaurin formulae and geometric series. Fitting takes less than 5 minutes on GPU. The inference procedure uses gradient-based optimization (L-BFGS) with multiple random initializations.

Experiments use Pythia-like models (up to 2B parameters) on OpenWebText2 with compute budgets from 9e14 to 6e19 FLOPs, and Llama-like models (up to 0.5B) on LM1B. Training uses Adam with cosine learning rate schedule (1e-3 initial LR, 1% warmup). Holdout sets maintain compute gaps of up to 1000x.

## Results & Highlights

- **Loss prediction (Pythia + OWT2, 1024x compute gap):** NQS holdout IsoFLOPs Huber loss = 2.5 vs. Chinchilla's 9.0; B-K plane: 5.6 vs. 9.8 (all in units of 1e-5)
- **Loss prediction (Llama + LM1B, 6x compute gap):** NQS holdout IsoFLOPs = 2.9 vs. Chinchilla's 3.7; B-K plane: 8.2 vs. 8.7
- **Extrapolation robustness:** NQS maintains close train/holdout error until training data is reduced to 1/4000 of the largest test run's compute; Chinchilla deteriorates beyond 20x extrapolation
- **Confidence intervals:** 90% CI from 100 random 50% subsamples of training data are tight relative to prediction ranges
- **Compound resource allocation:** Under compute + time constraints, compute + memory constraints, and compute + data constraints, NQS-selected configurations are consistently near ground-truth optimal
- **Ablation:** The LayerNorm adjustment (dynamic learning rate) is critical for fitting models trained with small batch sizes; the vanilla NQS already works well for large batch training
- **Complexity:** NQS has 7+1 free parameters vs. Chinchilla's 5, but the added complexity is justified by holdout performance (NQS train error is higher than Chinchilla's but holdout error is lower)

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: High.** The NQS formulation is a genuine conceptual advance. Unlike Chinchilla's descriptive power-law fit, NQS is mechanistic -- it models the actual optimization dynamics (SGD on a quadratic surface with noise). The ability to handle variable batch sizes is a non-trivial capability that prior loss models lacked. The LayerNorm modeling via effective learning rate adjustment is a clever and practically important contribution.
- **Rigor: Good.** The paper uses strict train/holdout splits with large compute gaps (up to 1000x), which is a more honest evaluation protocol than many scaling law papers employ. The comparison against Chinchilla is carefully designed to maximize Chinchilla's performance (using IsoFLOPs data with CBS batch sizes). The authors show NQS does not overfit (train and holdout errors are comparable). However, the evaluation is limited to models up to 2B parameters and relatively small-scale datasets (OWT2 with vocab 3000, seq length 128). How well NQS transfers to models 100x larger (e.g., 70B+ Chinchilla-scale models) remains an open question.
- **Reproducibility: Good.** Code is available, model families (Pythia, Llama) and datasets (OWT2, LM1B) are public. The NQS fitting procedure is clearly described.
- **Significance: High for the scaling laws community.** If the results hold at larger scales, this could become a standard tool for compute-optimal training planning. The advocacy for rigorous extrapolation testing of scaling laws is an important methodological contribution.
- **Red flags / concerns:**
  - The maximum model size tested is 2B, far below production-scale models. The 1000x compute gap is impressive, but 1000x of small-scale runs is still relatively modest in absolute terms.
  - The NQS has 7+1 parameters vs. Chinchilla's 5. While the authors show this extra complexity is justified via holdout performance, the model class is richer and could be more sensitive to data quality at the small-data regime.
  - The learning rate parameter gamma is noted to have a greater impact on NQS predictions than on actual LLM losses, suggesting the model may be over-sensitive to learning rate specification -- a known limitation the authors acknowledge.
  - Multi-epoch training and data mixture effects are not modeled, limiting applicability to many practical training regimes.
  - The LayerNorm parameter s is fitted in a separate step (not jointly optimized with theta), which the authors note is a limitation due to numerical issues.

## Strengths

- Mechanistic model grounded in theoretical analysis of optimization dynamics, not just descriptive curve fitting
- First loss prediction model to handle varying batch sizes -- fills a real gap in the scaling law literature
- Rigorous evaluation protocol with large compute extrapolation gaps and holdout sets
- Practical applicability to multi-constraint optimization demonstrated clearly
- Computationally lightweight: O(1) evaluation, <5 min fitting time
- Robust to training set perturbation (tight confidence intervals from subsampling)
- Results consistent with recent empirical findings on batch size scaling from independent groups

## Limitations

- Tested only up to 2B parameter models; scalability to 70B+ production models is unverified
- Does not model multi-epoch training, data mixtures, or learning rate schedules beyond the basic LayerNorm effect
- The NQS is sensitive to learning rate gamma in ways that actual LLMs are not, indicating incomplete modeling of LR dynamics
- The LayerNorm parameter s requires a separate fitting step due to numerical issues with joint optimization
- Uses a fixed tokenizer (vocab 3000) and short sequence length (128); impact of these choices on model generality is unclear
- Comparison is limited to Chinchilla Method 3; other recent scaling law approaches (e.g., multi-power laws, data-constrained scaling) are discussed but not directly benchmarked

## Relevance

本文提出了一种机制性的LLM损失预测模型，能够处理可变batch size并在大规模外推下表现优异，对理解scaling law的底层机制和实际训练资源配置优化具有重要价值，尤其适合关注预训练成本优化和计算最优训练策略的研究者。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does NQS perform when extrapolating to models in the 70B-1T parameter range, where Chinchilla-style approaches have been heavily validated?
- Can the NQS framework be extended to model multi-epoch training and data mixture effects, which are critical for practical training regimes?
- What is the minimum number and scale of training runs needed to reliably fit the 7+1 NQS parameters?
- How does the NQS compare to other recent loss prediction approaches (e.g., multi-power laws for LR schedules) on the same benchmarks?
- Could NQS be used as a sandbox for developing and testing novel optimization strategies before committing expensive compute?
- Can the numerical issues preventing joint optimization of the LayerNorm parameter s be resolved with better engineering?
