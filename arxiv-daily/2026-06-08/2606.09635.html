Gradient-Guided Reward Optimization for Inference-time Alignment

["Hankun Lin", "Ruqi Zhang"]

arXiv: 2606.09635

Date: 2026-06-08

Tags: ["Inference", "Alignment", "Reward-Model", "LLM", "Gradient"]

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.09635

一句话总结： GGRO 在推理时通过监控 token 级熵识别高不确定性位置，并注入由 reward model 梯度生成的"微调 token"主动引导解码，在 HEx-PHI 安全攻击上把 8B 模型的 ASR 从 BoN 的 34.3% 降到 26.2%，同时避免 Best-of-N 的 reward hacking 问题。
TL;DR: GGRO is a lightweight inference-time alignment method that monitors token-level entropy to detect high-uncertainty positions, then injects gradient-guided "nudging tokens" (computed from a reward model's gradients) to steer decoding rather than re-ranking samples. On LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct with the HEx-PHI prefilling-attack benchmark it cuts Attack Success Rate from 34.3% (Best-of-N, N=64) to 26.2%, lifts MMLU-Pro accuracy from 52.5% (BoN) to 54.0%, and stays robust to reward hacking as the compute budget grows, unlike BoN whose accuracy declines.

核心贡献

  • 提出 GGRO：一种轻量级推理时对齐方法，将 reward model 的梯度信号转化为可操作的解码干预，无需重新训练或额外的对齐模型
  • 用 token 级熵（而非 max next-token probability）作为更原则化的不确定性度量来定位干预点
  • 在高熵位置注入由 reward model 梯度确定性选取的"nudging token"，主动引导生成轨迹而非被动重排样本
  • 实证证明 GGRO 扩大了高质量响应的覆盖范围，并对 Best-of-N 等方法所受的 reward hacking 具有更强鲁棒性

Key Contributions

  • GGRO: inference-time alignment that makes reward-model gradients actionable for general LLM alignment, removing the need for a separate aligned proposal model
  • Replaces uniform Langevin-style gradient updates with targeted, entropy-identified local interventions to avoid decoding instability
  • Deterministic gradient-informed nudging-token selection (greedy argmax over a gradient-informed distribution) stabilizes decoding versus stochastic sampling
  • Empirically demonstrates expanded coverage of high-quality responses and monotonic accuracy improvement under growing compute, in contrast to BoN's reward-hacking-induced decline

Methodology

GGRO operates on a frozen base model π paired with an off-the-shelf reward model R (e.g., LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct with Skywork-Reward-V2-LLaMA-3.1-8B). It addresses two questions: where to intervene, and how.

Identifying insertion points. During autoregressive decoding, GGRO computes the entropy H_i of the base model's next-token distribution at each position i (Eq. 2). When H_i exceeds a threshold τ_H, position i is marked as an insertion point. The authors use entropy rather than the max next-token probability heuristic (as in Fei et al.'s Nudging work) because entropy is a more principled dispersion measure; an ablation confirms this (Section 5.3.3).

Generating nudging tokens. GGRO treats the reward model R as an energy function with f(Ŷ|X) = −R(X,Y) (higher reward = lower energy). It computes the gradient ∇_Ŷ R(X,Y) and selects the nudging token n_i as the argmax over the vocabulary of a gradient-informed probability p_ij ∝ exp(−(∇_Ŷ R)_ij (1 − ŷ_ij)) (Eq. 4). The (1 − ŷ_ij) term excludes the already-generated token. The authors deliberately use greedy argmax rather than stochastic sampling from Eq. 1, because reward-model gradients are noisy and stochastic sampling frequently introduces incoherent tokens; an ablation validates this choice. In practice, only the top-k tokens (k=25) under the gradient distribution are considered.

Decoding with iterative nudging. At each insertion point GGRO performs up to S refinement iterations: each iteration selects a new nudging token, generates a candidate continuation, and the reward model scores the resulting segment; the highest-scoring segment is retained (segment-level Best-of-N selection). The procedure is given in Algorithm 1. This relies on prior findings (Li et al., CARDS) that entropy-based segmentation keeps segments semantically self-contained so partial-segment reward scores correlate with final-response reward.

Setup values. τ_H = 1.5 for HEx-PHI/HH-RLHF and 2.0 for ARC-Challenge/MMLU-Pro; S = 8 refinement steps for the 8B model; S = 3 for the 3B model on HH-RLHF/ARC-Challenge and S = 8 on HEx-PHI/MMLU-Pro; k = 25 for top-k nudging selection. For sampling-based baselines, top-k sampling with k = 40, temperature 0.7. Experiments use LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct + Skywork-Reward-V2-LLaMA-3.1-8B and LLaMA-3.2-3B-Instruct + GRM-LLaMA-3.2-3B-rewardmodel-ft, with standard deviations across 4 seeds (1, 42, 123, 2026). Datasets: HEx-PHI (prefilling attack) and XSTest for safety, HH-RLHF for helpfulness (Gemini-2.5-Pro judge), ARC-Challenge and MMLU-Pro for reasoning.

Results & Highlights

  • Safety (HEx-PHI, ASR ↓, 8B model): GGRO achieves 26.2%±0.8 ASR versus Best-of-N (N=64) at 34.3%±1.4 — an 8.1-point absolute reduction, and beats the second-best baseline (CBS at 34.9%) by 8.7 points. The vanilla base model scores 54.0%±1.6.
  • Reasoning (MMLU-Pro, accuracy ↑, 8B model): GGRO reaches 54.0%±0.7 accuracy, beating BoN (52.5%±0.5), ARGS-G (51.4%±0.6), and the vanilla model (44.1%±1.5). On ARC-Challenge (8B) GGRO scores 94.3%±0.8 vs BoN's 92.8%±1.3.
  • Reward-hacking resistance (Figure 3, MMLU-Pro): As compute budget increases (BoN N∈{2,4,8,32,48}; GGRO S∈{1,2,3,5,8}), BoN's accuracy rises then declines while its reward keeps climbing (over-optimization), whereas GGRO's accuracy improves monotonically and its reward stabilizes within a moderate range.
  • Efficiency (Table 4): On long-generation benchmarks GGRO is among the most efficient — 366.7 min on MMLU-Pro vs BoN's 1293.3 min and CARDS' 528.3 min — because its larger τ_H for long outputs reduces nudging frequency.
  • Ablation (Table 2): Removing gradient guidance degrades HEx-PHI ASR from 26.0% to 38.0%; removing the argmax (stochastic sampling) gives 29.7%; replacing entropy with max-probability gives 32.0%.
  • Sensitivity (Table 3): On 100 HEx-PHI samples, τ_H sweeps from 0.5 (25.0% ASR, 16.2% nudging frequency) to 2.5 (38.0% ASR, 3.1% frequency); τ_H = 1.5 is reported as the best balance.

Critical Assessment

  • Narrow baseline set for selective steering: The authors explicitly exclude the only alignment-focused selective-steering method (Fei et al.'s Nudging) because it assumes access to a small aligned model, leaving no direct head-to-head against the closest conceptual competitor; GGRO's advantage over uncertainty-aware nudging is therefore argued rather than measured.
  • Reward-hacking claim leans on one dataset: The monotonic-accuracy / BoN-decline result (Figure 3) is shown only on MMLU-Pro validation; the reward-hacking resilience is not separately quantified on the safety or helpfulness benchmarks where reward models are arguably noisier.
  • Segment-level reward-model misuse: The method relies on the CARDS finding that entropy-segmented partial responses yield reliable reward scores, but no independent diagnostic is provided that the Skywork-Reward-V2 reward model actually remains calibrated on the paper's specific partial segments — a known concern (Rashid et al.; Xie et al.).
  • Threshold sensitivity is large: Across τ_H ∈ [0.5, 2.5] the ASR varies from 25.0% to 38.0% — a 13-point swing — so the reported 26.2% main result depends on a carefully tuned threshold that may not transfer across models.

Strengths

  • Active gradient-guided steering escapes the "bounded by base-model generation quality" ceiling of pure search methods
  • Deterministic nudging-token selection avoids the incoherent text that plagues Langevin-style methods like SEA
  • Computational overhead is favorable on long sequences and GPU memory is lower than BoN (single-sample iterative updates vs many parallel samples)
  • Honest ablations isolating gradient guidance, argmax, and entropy each contribute measurable gains

Limitations

  • Still depends entirely on reward-model quality; a miscalibrated R directly degrades nudging-token selection
  • Requires backward passes through the reward model at every insertion point, adding per-step latency on short sequences
  • Best gains concentrated on the larger 8B model; on the 3B model GGRO is only comparable to the strongest baselines, suggesting weaker scaling to small models with weak reward models
  • Evaluated only on LLaMA-family models in the main table (Qwen3-8B appears only in an appendix experiment)

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Would entropy-based insertion points still beat max-probability triggers if the reward model were systematically miscalibrated on partial segments (the Rashid et al. / Xie et al. concern the paper cites)?
  • Can the monotonic accuracy improvement under growing S (Figure 3) be replicated on HEx-PHI and HH-RLHF, or is reward-hacking resilience specific to the MMLU-Pro reasoning distribution?
  • The paper compares against DARWIN (a reward-guided tree-search baseline) only in Appendix D.3 — how large is the gap, and does DARWIN also resist reward hacking under increasing compute?
  • Is the 13-point ASR swing across τ_H (Table 3) reducible with an adaptive, per-prompt threshold instead of a global value?
