SpatialWorld: Benchmarking Interactive Spatial Reasoning of Multimodal Agents in Real-World Tasks

核心贡献

  • 提出了 SpatialWorld，首个统一的多模态智能体交互式空间推理基准，覆盖8个异构模拟后端
  • 设计了模拟器无关的共享协议，包含760个人工标注任务，涵盖家庭日常、旅行、社交协作等多个领域
  • 提供了完整的评估流程：人工验证的初始状态、参考轨迹和终端状态验证器
  • 对15个前沿智能体进行了系统评估，揭示了当前模型在空间推理方面的显著不足

Key Contributions

  • Introduces SpatialWorld, the first unified benchmark specifically designed for evaluating interactive spatial understanding of multimodal agents
  • Integrates 8 heterogeneous simulation backends under a shared, simulator-agnostic protocol with 760 human-annotated tasks
  • Provides reliable evaluation through human-validated initial states, reference trajectories, and terminal-state verifiers
  • Conducts systematic evaluation of 15 advanced agents, revealing that robust spatial task solving remains extremely challenging

Methodology

SpatialWorld integrates eight heterogeneous simulation backends under a unified, simulator-agnostic protocol. The benchmark features 760 human-annotated tasks spanning diverse real-world domains including household routines, travel, and social collaboration. Agents operate under vision-only partial observability, requiring active gathering of egocentric visual evidence. Decisions are expressed via a unified, text-based action interface designed to be native to MLLMs. Each task includes a human-validated initial state, a reference trajectory, and a terminal-state verifier for reliable evaluation.

Results & Highlights

  • The strongest model, GPT-5, achieves only 17.4% average task success rate (TSR)
  • The leading open-source model, Qwen-3.5, reaches 14.1% TSR
  • Analysis reveals a clear mismatch between task success and execution efficiency
  • Substantial domain-specific performance variations are observed across different task categories
  • Active exploration and long-horizon planning are identified as critical bottlenecks

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High — unifying 8 heterogeneous simulators under a single protocol is a significant engineering and research contribution. The focus on interactive spatial reasoning (vs. passive VQA) fills an important gap.
  • Rigor: Appears strong based on the abstract — human-annotated tasks, validated initial states, and terminal-state verifiers provide a solid evaluation framework. Need to verify details on inter-annotator agreement and verifier reliability from the full paper.
  • Reproducibility: The benchmark design with a unified protocol should facilitate reproducibility. Key question is whether all 8 simulators will be publicly released.
  • Significance: Very high — the extremely low success rates of state-of-the-art models (GPT-5 at 17.4%) clearly demonstrate that this benchmark exposes meaningful capability gaps.
  • Red flags: The low absolute numbers could indicate overly difficult or ambiguous tasks rather than genuine model limitations. Task design quality and whether the text-based action interface fairly represents model capabilities warrant scrutiny.

Strengths

  • Unified evaluation across multiple simulation environments — avoids simulator-specific bias
  • Focus on interactive, egocentric spatial reasoning — more realistic than passive VQA
  • Comprehensive evaluation of 15 agents provides broad landscape of current capabilities
  • Human-validated task design with reference trajectories and verifiers

Limitations

  • Reliance on simulation environments rather than real-world physical settings — sim-to-real gap
  • Text-based action interface may not fully capture the complexity of embodied interaction
  • Abstract does not clarify whether all simulation backends will be publicly available
  • Low absolute performance may partially reflect benchmark design choices rather than pure capability gaps
