> **一句话总结：** LexRubric 是一个面向开放式中文法律任务的诊断型基准，包含 649 道题、12,337 条专家撰写的原子化评分准则、统一六维评估框架；评测 18 个 LLM 显示，最强的 Kimi K2.6 与 Qwen3.6-Max-Preview 仅约 75%，法律领域模型大多表现不佳，开放式法律任务对当前 LLM 仍具挑战。

> **TL;DR:** LexRubric is a rubric-based diagnostic benchmark for open-ended Chinese legal tasks: 649 instances (legal consultation + judicial examination) across 14 legal scenarios, annotated with 12,337 expert-written atomic scoring criteria under a unified 6-dimension framework. Evaluated with 18 LLMs (general + legal-domain) judged by Qwen3.6-27B, the best model Kimi K2.6 reaches only 75.21% overall (72.84% on consultation, 81.59% on exam); on a hard subset of 117 instances, the best (Qwen3.6-Max-Preview) drops to 51.30%, versus legal-domain baselines like Saul-7B-Instruct at 5.67%.

## 核心贡献

- 构建**开放式中文法律基准** LexRubric：649 道题，来自法律咨询与司法考试两大来源，覆盖 14 类法律场景，面向开放式作答而非选择题/固定格式。
- 设计**统一的六维评分框架**（法律准确性、推理与逻辑、完整性、清晰度与结构、任务合规、伦理与安全），并用 12,337 条专家撰写的**原子化评分准则**实例化，支持实例级、维度级、任务级的细粒度诊断分析。
- 对 18 个通用与法律领域 LLM 做系统评测，并验证 LLM-as-a-judge（Qwen3.6-27B）与人类专家的排名一致性（Spearman 0.886，Pairwise Acc 90%），以及跨裁判模型（GLM-5.1/Kimi K2.6/GPT-5）的排名鲁棒性（Spearman ≥0.987）。

## Key Contributions

- An open-ended Chinese legal benchmark of 649 instances from two sources (legal consultation from >50k real user queries; expert-written judicial examination), covering 14 legal scenarios.
- A unified six-dimensional rubric framework (Legal Accuracy; Reasoning & Logic; Completeness; Clarity & Structure; Task Compliance; Ethics & Safety), instantiated with 12,337 expert-written **atomic** scoring criteria (point values −10 to +10), enabling fine-grained diagnostic comparison across tasks.
- A systematic evaluation of 18 LLMs plus a reliability study validating LLM-as-a-judge agreement with three legal experts (Kendall tau-b 0.800, Spearman 0.886, pairwise accuracy 90.00%) and cross-judge ranking consistency (Spearman up to 0.998).

## Methodology

- **Data construction.** Two complementary sources. (1) Legal consultation: starts from >50,000 real user queries, scored by difficulty/completeness/practical value/legal relevance/answerability; after rewriting unclear-but-valuable queries and filtering, 622 are kept for expert annotation, plus 40 Chinese-law items from OneMillion-Bench. (2) Judicial examination: legal experts write 250 exam-style, knowledge-intensive questions. Final: 473 consultation + 176 exam = 649 instances.
- **Rubric construction.** For each instance, legal experts (practitioners or PhD students who passed China's National Unified Legal Profession Qualification Exam) write a reference answer, then construct instance-specific rubric items under six principles (valid, task-relevant, mutually-exclusive, atomic, objective/binary, self-contained). Each item carries an integer point value in [−10, +10]; positive items = desirable qualities, negative items = undesirable/incorrect/unsafe. Three independent annotation rounds per instance. Eight consensus standards (emergency guidance, information seeking, cross-jurisdiction adaptation, document handling, communication customization, uncertainty handling, response depth, ethics & safety) were developed first to ensure consistency.
- **Scoring formula.** Response score S(x_i, y_i) = Σ_j p_ij · 1{y_i ⊨ c_ij}; normalized to a score rate by the max obtainable positive score (Eq. 2); dimension score rates computed per dimension (Eq. 3).
- **Quality control.** An expert-in-the-loop refinement: generate responses from three Qwen3 models (235B-A22B, 14B, 4B) with distinct capability levels, flag instances whose scores violate the expected capability gradient or fail to discriminate; an AI-assisted workflow (Claude Code SDK + a legal-research skill) repairs rubric defects (vagueness, overbreadth, weak discriminativeness), all AI revisions confirmed by experts.
- **Stats.** 12,337 rubric items total (consult 10,601 avg 22.41/instance; exam 1,736 avg 9.86/instance); avg rubric length ~55 chars. Heaviest dimensions: Reasoning & Logic (4,151) and Legal Accuracy (3,947). Annotation cost ≈ $154k across 872 annotated instances.
- **Evaluated models (18).** Closed-source general: Qwen3.6-Max-Preview, Qwen3-Max, GPT-5.2, Claude Sonnet 4.6. Open-source general: Kimi K2.6/K2.5, Qwen3.5-397B-A17B, GLM-5.1/GLM-5, DeepSeek-V4-Flash/Pro, DeepSeek-V3.2, DeepSeek-R1. Legal-domain: LegalOne-8B, Farui-Plus, LawLLM-7B, SaulLM-54B-Instruct, Saul-7B-Instruct. Generation: max output 16k, temperature 0.6.
- **Judging.** Primary judge Qwen3.6-27B, temperature 0.0, checks each atomic item independently (Boolean satisfied/not) without holistic scoring. Hard subset = 117 instances where all models scored <75%.

## Results & Highlights

- **Best models leave large headroom.** On the full benchmark (Table 4), Kimi K2.6 tops the overall score rate at **75.21%** (consult 72.84% / exam 81.59%), with Qwen3.6-Max-Preview close behind at **75.12%**; Qwen3-Max and Kimi K2.5 exceed 73%.
- **Hard subset exposes brittleness.** On the 117-instance hard subset (Table 5), the best model Qwen3.6-Max-Preview drops to only **51.30%** (consult 51.48% / exam 50.00%) and Kimi K2.6 to **48.91%** — a >20-point drop showing open-ended legal QA is far from solved.
- **Legal-domain models mostly underperform general models.** LegalOne-8B is the strongest legal model at **65.14%**, but LawLLM-7B reaches only **8.96%**, SaulLM-54B-Instruct **5.67%**, and Saul-7B-Instruct **4.96%** — the authors attribute this to those models being trained for English/U.S. law (e.g. LawLLM-7B) rather than Chinese law.
- **Dimension profiles differ from the aggregate.** Claude Sonnet 4.6 ranks first on **Ethics & Safety (72.74%)** despite a mid-pack overall (69.18%); Kimi K2.6 leads Reasoning & Logic (79.08%) and Completeness (73.85%); Qwen3.6-Max-Preview leads Legal Accuracy (69.51%), Task Compliance, and Clarity & Structure — so a single aggregate score masks model-specific strengths.
- **LLM-judge reliability is high.** Qwen3.6-27B vs. three legal experts on 50 instances: Kendall tau-b **0.800**, Spearman **0.886**, pairwise accuracy **90.00%**; cross-judge consistency with GLM-5.1/Kimi K2.6/GPT-5 reaches Spearman **0.987–0.998** and pairwise accuracy up to 99.35% (Table 6).

## Critical Assessment

- The headline "open-ended legal tasks remain challenging" rests on a **single primary judge (Qwen3.6-27B)** from the same model family as the top-ranked general models (Qwen3.6-Max-Preview, Qwen3-Max); although cross-judge agreement is high (Spearman ≥0.987), the human-agreement check covers only **50 instances / 6 models**, and the hard-subset re-ranking (Qwen3.6-Max-Preview overtaking Kimi K2.6) shows the leaderboard is fragile near ties (Kimi K2.6 vs Qwen3.6-Max-Preview differ by 0.09%).
- The legal-domain-model comparison uses **weak/mismatched baselines**: LawLLM-7B and SaulLM/Saul-7B are English/U.S.-law models, so their near-single-digit scores (5–9%) mostly reflect a jurisdiction/language mismatch rather than a fair test of domain adaptation; the only Chinese-tuned legal model (LegalOne-8B, 65.14%) is what actually supports the "domain adaptation can help" claim.
- Rubric items are **atomic and binary**, which the authors argue reduces LLM-judge parsing burden, but the scoring formula sums point values p_ij ∈ [−10,+10] chosen per instance by experts; no inter-annotator reliability statistic (e.g. Cohen's κ on the rubric items themselves) is reported for the three independent annotation rounds, so rubric-consistency is asserted rather than measured.
- The benchmark is **Chinese-law only** (an acknowledged limitation), so generalization to other jurisdictions is untested.

## Strengths

- Genuinely diagnostic: the six-dimension × task-type breakdown reveals per-model capability profiles (e.g. Claude's ethics/safety edge) that a single aggregate score hides, and the hard-subset analysis surfaces rank instability.
- High annotation rigor for the rubrics: three independent expert rounds, eight pre-defined consensus standards, and an expert-in-the-loop + AI-assisted refinement that flags non-discriminative items before release.
- Strong judge-reliability evidence: both human-agreement (Spearman 0.886) and cross-model-judge consistency (Spearman up to 0.998), with a public dataset and code release.

## Limitations

- Chinese-law only; other jurisdictions, legal systems, and multilingual settings are not covered.
- Designed for evaluation, not deployment; high scores do not certify safe unsupervised use in high-stakes legal decisions.
- Primary judge and top models share the Qwen family; human validation is limited to 50 instances. No inter-annotator agreement statistic for rubric construction itself.

## Relevance

主要映射到"**LLM Inference Evaluation（推理评估）**"——本文正是构建开放式法律任务的多维诊断型基准并用 LLM-as-a-judge 进行评测；其次与"**Model System Cards & Technical Reports**"相关，因为它对 18 个前沿/法律领域模型给出了细粒度的能力画像（含伦理与安全维度），可作为模型选型与系统报告的依据。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Would the LLM-as-a-judge agreement (currently Spearman 0.886 on 50 instances) hold if the human validation were scaled to the full benchmark, and how does it compare on the hard subset where the primary judge and experts may disagree most?
- How would the legal-domain-model ranking change if Chinese-law-tuned models beyond LegalOne-8B were added, given that the current legal baselines (LawLLM-7B, SaulLM-54B, Saul-7B, per Shu et al. 2024 and Colombo et al. 2024) are English/U.S.-focused?
- The paper cites PLawBench (Shi et al., 2026) as the closest rubric-based legal benchmark — do LexRubric's atomic criteria yield measurably higher judge-consistency than PLawBench's more composite task-specific rubrics?
