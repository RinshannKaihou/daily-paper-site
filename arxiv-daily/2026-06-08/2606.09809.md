> **一句话总结：** Evaluation Cards 是一个可操作的 AI 评测报告解释层，它把基准元数据、评测运行数据和模型元数据合并为统一记录，并通过对 5,816 个模型、635 个基准和 101,955 条结果的规模化审计，揭示了当前公开评测报告在可复现性、文档完整性和跨方可比性上的系统性缺口（96.5% 的结果缺少至少一个最小复现字段）。

> **TL;DR:** The paper builds Evaluation Cards, a reporting layer over existing evaluation infrastructure (EEE run data, Auto-BenchmarkCards metadata, model catalogs), and deploys it as a monitoring tool across 5,816 models, 635 benchmarks, and 101,955 results from 30 organizations. Compared against flat (model, benchmark, score) leaderboard triples and prior reporting artifacts (Datasheets, Model Cards, BenchmarkCards, HELM, EEE), it finds that 96.5% of triples lack at least one minimal reproducibility field, median per-benchmark documentation completeness is just 10.7%, and 51.9% of multi-organization metric groups show >5% cross-party score divergence.

## 核心贡献

- 提出基于文献综述（52 篇论文）和 12 位利益相关者访谈得到的评测报告框架，覆盖评测生命周期的五个阶段（设计、执行前、执行、生命周期、报告与发布）。
- 引入五级"展开层次结构"（Family → Composite → Benchmark → Split → Metric），取代排行榜和模型卡中扁平的 (model, benchmark, score) 三元组，使每个分数可追溯到显式路径。
- 实现四个解释性信号（可复现性、文档完整性、来源与风险、分数可比性），并通过针对研究型与非研究型读者的双阅读模式呈现。
- 在 5,816 个模型、635 个基准、101,955 条结果的公开语料上部署监控工具，量化当前评测报告实践中的系统性缺口。

## Key Contributions

- An evidence-based reporting framework derived from a preregistered systematic review of 52 papers (from 748 candidates) plus 12 stakeholder interviews, with high inter-rater agreement (Cohen's κ ∈ [0.865, 0.895], Krippendorff's α ∈ [0.916, 0.964]).
- A five-level rollout hierarchy (Family → Composite → Benchmark → Split → Metric) that resolves every reported score to a traceable path, disambiguating measurements that share a label but differ in subtask, setup, or scoring rule.
- Four interpretive signals — reproducibility, reporting completeness, provenance & risk, and comparability — rendered through Research and Summary reader modes calibrated to different audiences.
- A deployed monitoring instrument over 5,816 models, 635 benchmarks, and 101,955 results from 30 organizations, yielding three headline empirical findings about the state of public evaluation reporting.

## Methodology

The paper combines a framework-derivation process with an infrastructure and empirical-audit layer.

**Framework derivation.** The authors run a preregistered systematic literature review of 748 candidate papers published 2020–2025, of which 52 meet inclusion criteria (peer-reviewed or preprint work proposing evaluation practices/frameworks/standards for AI systems). Two reviewers independently extract and code 730 recommendation items, categorized by item class, type, and intent, with inter-rater agreement Cohen's κ ∈ [0.865, 0.895] (single-label) and Krippendorff's α ∈ [0.916, 0.964] (multi-label). A "best fit" framework synthesis method distills these into a five-part structure: Design, Before execution, Execution, Lifecycle, and Reporting & publication. Between February and April 2026, the authors also conduct semi-structured interviews with 12 stakeholders (technical evaluators, AI engineers, policy actors) across 9 organizations and 3 regions to identify which framework items are blocking vs. enriching and how field salience varies by reader type.

**Rollout hierarchy.** Instead of flat (model, benchmark, score) triples, every score resolves to a five-level path: Family (e.g., MATH-family) → Composite (e.g., artificial_analysis) → Benchmark (e.g., MATH-500) → Split (e.g., advanced-math) → Metric (e.g., accuracy → 0.994). This lets integrity signals attach to specific paths and enables drill-down from aggregate claims to the underlying evidence.

**Composition over existing sources.** Evaluation Cards ingest three sources: (1) Auto-BenchmarkCards for benchmark metadata (built from Unitxt catalogs, Hugging Face repos, and publications, composed by an LLM and validated for factual consistency, with risk annotations from IBM AI Atlas Nexus); (2) EEE for evaluation run data at aggregate and instance level, with converters from HELM, lm-eval-harness, and Inspect AI; (3) community-maintained model catalogs (hub-stats for Hugging Face-indexed models, models.dev for API-hosted/proprietary models) enriched with release date, parameter count, and weight accessibility. A standardization layer maps inconsistent identifiers (e.g., gpt-4 vs. gpt-4-0613 vs. OpenAI GPT-4) to stable canonical IDs.

**Signals.** Four signals are computed: reproducibility (per-triple flag on missing temperature, max_tokens, and agentic eval_plan/eval_limits); reporting completeness (per-benchmark score over a 28-field operationalized schema); provenance (first- vs. third-party tag plus propagated risk annotations); and comparability (per-triple divergence flag when ≥2 reports exist, threshold 5%). Signals render differently in Research mode (methodology/config foregrounded) vs. Summary mode (plain-language accountability for regulators/policy).

## Results & Highlights

- **Reproducibility gap dominates.** Across 50,461 (model, benchmark, metric-path) triples, 48,698 (**96.5%**) lack at least one field from the minimal reproducibility sub-schema; `max_tokens` is absent from **95.6%** of triples and `temperature` from **93.9%**. For the agentic-benchmark subset, `eval_plan` and `eval_limits` are missing from **100%** of triples.
- **Developer self-reporting is worse than independent evaluation.** On 180 (model, benchmark) pairs reported by both first- and third-party evaluators, first-party rows populate **0.0%** of base reproducibility fields on average vs. **16.6%** for third-party rows.
- **Benchmark documentation is thin.** Median per-benchmark completeness against the operationalized 28-field schema is **10.7%** across 635 benchmarks, with per-field population rates ranging from 100.0% (score-type/score fields) down to 0.0% (preregistration_url, lifecycle_status).
- **Multi-source reporting is rare and often divergent.** Of 49,865 (model, benchmark) pairs, **98.2%** are reported by only one party; among the multi-party subset, **7.2%** show cross-party score divergence above the 5% threshold, rising to **51.9%** (94 of 181) of multi-organization metric groups.
- **Coverage of the deployed tool.** The corpus comprises 5,816 models, 635 benchmarks organized into 62 families and 10 composites, and 101,955 reported results contributed by 30 organizations, with 211 benchmarks carrying matched Auto-BenchmarkCards records.

## Critical Assessment

- The reproducibility signal uses only a "minimal re-run schema" (temperature, max_tokens, plus harness/eval_plan/eval_limits for agentic benchmarks) and explicitly omits seed, hardware, and determinism fields (Table 3 "Key limitation"), so a triple flagged as fully reproducible may still not actually re-run bit-identically — the headline 96.5% gap is therefore a lower bound on the true reproducibility deficit.
- The comparability signal applies a uniform 5% divergence threshold and ignores differing sampling variance across reports (Table 3), which can both over- and under-flag divergence; this is acknowledged but uncalibrated against any statistical-error baseline.
- The documentation-completeness score counts field population fractionally and rewards "adequacy not rigor" (Table 3), so two benchmarks with identical 10.7% completeness can differ wildly in actual interpretability — a metric-quality weakness the paper does not directly evaluate.
- The corpus explicitly "overrepresents English-language benchmarks and frontier-scale models," so the aggregate findings (e.g., 98.2% single-party reporting) may not generalize to non-English or smaller-scale evaluation ecosystems.

## Strengths

- Grounds the framework in both systematic literature review (52 papers, 730 coded items) and practitioner interviews (12 stakeholders), giving it empirical rather than purely theoretical legitimacy.
- The five-level rollout hierarchy is a genuinely useful conceptual contribution that turns ambiguous aggregate claims ("GPT-5 achieves 0.994 on MATH") into traceable evidence paths.
- The large-scale empirical audit (101,955 results, 30 organizations) produces concrete, actionable numbers about real reporting gaps rather than hypothetical critiques.
- Designed as a permissive standard with self-hostable instances, lowering adoption barriers relative to prescriptive checklists.

## Limitations

- Reported as a deployment/system description with planned (not completed) "systematic usability evaluation"; the positive interview feedback (P3, P5) is anecdotal rather than from a controlled user study.
- Only items "likely to appear in published artifacts" are operationalized; process-side items (pre-run commitments, pilot runs, mid-run mitigations) are deferred to complementary mechanisms, leaving a coverage hole.
- Relies entirely on existing infrastructure (EEE, Auto-BenchmarkCards) for data, inheriting their coverage gaps and converter limitations (HELM, lm-eval-harness, Inspect AI).
- Evaluation of the standardization layer's entity-resolution accuracy is relegated to an appendix (D.2.2) rather than reported as a headline metric.

## Relevance

该论文直接映射到用户的"LLM Inference Evaluation"（评测报告标准化、跨来源可比性）以及"Model System Cards & Technical Reports"（评测报告作为模型卡的延伸与解释层）两个兴趣方向——它本质上是关于如何把分散的 LLM 评测证据统一成可追溯、可比较的报告记录。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- The paper cites EEE [11], Auto-BenchmarkCards [51], and BetterBench [85] as the data sources and prior reporting artifacts — how sensitive are the headline numbers (96.5% reproducibility gap, 10.7% completeness) to the coverage biases of these specific ingestion pipelines?
- Comparability uses a fixed 5% threshold (Table 3); could a threshold calibrated to per-benchmark score variance (in the spirit of the cited OLMes [45] and error-bar work [71]) change the 51.9% multi-organization divergence figure?
- The rollout hierarchy disambiguates shared labels (e.g., gpt-4 vs. gpt-4-0613), but how is the canonical entity resolution in Appendix D.2.2 validated, and what is its measured precision/recall?
- First-party reporting populates 0.0% of reproducibility fields vs. 16.6% third-party — does this pattern persist when restricted to the frontier model subset, or is it driven by smaller developer reports?
