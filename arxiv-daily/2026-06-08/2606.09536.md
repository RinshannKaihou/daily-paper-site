> **一句话总结：** HadamardNet 把 Hadamard 码作为语义分割与目标检测的输出表示，通过向概率单纯形投影解码出最优类概率与误差向量，再用误差向量作为异常分数，在单次前向传播内实现 SOTA 级别的对抗攻击/扰动检测，且干净数据上的 mIoU/AP 几乎无损。

> **TL;DR:** On Cityscapes/BDD100K semantic segmentation (SegFormer MiT-B0, DeepLabv3+) and Pascal VOC/BDD100K object detection (Faster R-CNN, DETR), HadamardNet's quantile detector reaches 80.2% global AuROC for single-pass perturbation detection on Cityscapes validation — statistically equivalent to the 3-pass Xu et al. baseline (80.7%) — while matching one-hot mIoU within confidence intervals; for object detection the regression detector reaches 74.4% global AuROC on Pascal VOC, beating the strongest single-pass baseline (max-posterior 61.2%) and even the multi-pass Xu et al. (69.8%).

## 核心贡献

- 首次将 Hadamard 编码输出表示应用于目标检测（Faster R-CNN、DETR），并系统性地重新评估语义分割（SegFormer、DeepLabv3+）场景
- 推导出基于概率单纯形投影（Michelot 算法）的最优类概率解码过程，同时得到最小化 L2 范数的误差向量 e* 作为预测不一致性度量
- 首次把码字冗余/不一致性转化为对抗攻击与扰动检测信号，提出三种异常分数（误差 L1 范数、回归、85% 分位数回归）
- 单次前向传播即可检测，无需对抗训练数据、无需多趟前向、模型与任务无关
- 在 4 种网络结构、5 个数据集、多种攻击（FGSM/PGD/Metzen/TOG）与扰动（高斯、椒盐）上做了综合评估

## Key Contributions

- First application of Hadamard-coded output representations to object detection (Faster R-CNN, DETR), and a rigorous re-evaluation for semantic segmentation (SegFormer, DeepLabv3+)
- A novel probabilistic decoding procedure that solves a constrained optimization by projecting onto the probability simplex, yielding both optimal class posteriors P* and a minimal-norm error vector e*
- First to exploit intrinsic Hadamard codeword redundancy for adversarial-attack and disturbance detection, via three anomaly scores (error-L1, regression, 85%-quantile regression)
- Single-pass detection with no adversarial training data, no multi-pass inference, and architecture/task-agnostic design
- Comprehensive evaluation across 4 architectures, 5 datasets, FGSM/PGD/Metzen (segmentation) and TOG (detection) attacks plus Gaussian / salt-&-pepper disturbances

## Methodology

**Hadamard encoding.** HadamardNet replaces the standard one-hot classifier head with a sigmoid head predicting a length-L soft codeword h (L = 2^k ≥ S classes), where the target codewords are columns of the unipolar Hadamard matrix H ∈ {0,1}^{L×S} (Eq. 3). Hadamard codes have constant pairwise Hamming distance 2^{k−1}, which is the source of redundancy exploited for detection.

**Hadamard decoder (the core derivation).** Given a predicted soft codeword h_i, the ideal error-free codeword satisfies the linear equation system H · P_i = h*_i. Real predictions are inconsistent, so the authors introduce an error vector ě_i and solve H · P_i = h_i + ě_i (Eq. 13) subject to the stochastic constraints Σ_s P_{i,s}=1, P_{i,s}≥0. Rearranging gives P_i = P̃_i + e_i with P̃_i = (1/L) Ĥ^T ȟ_i. The authors prove (Supplement D) that minimizing ‖ě_i‖_2² is equivalent to minimizing ‖e_i‖_2², whose solution is the projection of P̃_i onto the probability simplex (Michelot's algorithm, Eq. 15). The resulting minimal error vector e*_i = P*_i − P̃_i (Eq. 16) is the inconsistency signal.

**Three anomaly scores.** (1) Error score δ(x) = mean_i ‖e*_i‖_1 (Eq. 17). (2) Regression score: a regression network F predicts the expected ‖e*_i‖_1 from the max posterior and entropy; the anomaly score is the deviation of the per-image prediction error from the clean-training mean (Eq. 18). (3) Quantile score: F predicts the conditional 85% quantile of ‖e*_i‖_1; δ(x) is the fraction of pixels/objects whose error norm exceeds this quantile (Eq. 19). An image is flagged adversarial if δ(x) ≥ θ.

**Training.** The detector regression network F is trained in a second stage on clean data only (20 epochs, AdamW), after the base model is trained. The classification loss J^cls = α J^ENC + β J^DEC + γ J^error combines an encoded-Hadamard-space loss (BCE/MSE/MAE; MSE chosen by ablation), a decoded one-hot loss (CE + MAE), and the L2 error-vector penalty. For detection, Hadamard encodings apply only to the classification branch; box regression uses standard losses.

**Setup values.** SegFormer MiT-B0 is the main study model; DeepLabv3+ also reported. Datasets: Cityscapes, BDD100K, ADE20K (segmentation); Pascal VOC 2007+2012, BDD100K, COCO (detection). Attacks: FGSM/PGD/Metzen for segmentation (PGD 10 iterations step αϵ=ϵ/4; Metzen 60 iterations, step 1/255); TOG untargeted/fabrication/vanishing/mislabeling for detection. Strengths ϵ ∈ {1,2,4,8,16} (segmentation), {0.25,…,16} (detection). Detection metric: global AuROC (single threshold across all perturbations/strengths). 95% CIs over 3 seeds.

## Results & Highlights

- **Semantic segmentation, Cityscapes val, SegFormer MiT-B0 (Table 1):** the proposed quantile detector (19) achieves **80.2% global AuROC** across all perturbations and strengths, statistically equivalent to the 3-pass Xu et al. baseline (**80.7%**) at ~3× fewer GFLOPs (122 vs 366). It beats single-pass baselines entropy (**71.6%**) and max-posterior (**71.4%**), and Maag et al. (**74.8%**). On the hard Metzen attack it scores **75.9%** vs entropy's 45.0%.
- **Clean-data mIoU preserved (Table 2):** HadamardNet matches one-hot within confidence intervals — SegFormer MiT-B0 on Cityscapes **76.5% vs 76.4% mIoU**, DeepLabv3+ on Cityscapes **80.1% vs 80.2% mIoU** — at essentially identical model size (43.6M) and +1 GFLOPs.
- **Object detection, Pascal VOC val, Faster R-CNN (Table 3):** the regression detector (18) reaches **74.4% global AuROC**, the best single-pass result and better than the multi-pass Xu et al. (**69.8%**) and the strongest single-pass baseline max-posterior (**61.2%**). On the vanishing attack it scores **70.6%** vs max-posterior's 5.4%.
- **Clean-data AP preserved (Table 4):** Faster R-CNN HadamardNet AP is **40.4% vs 41.0%** (one-hot) on VOC and **32.3% vs 32.8%** on BDD100K — close-by, with DETR essentially equivalent (46.9% vs 47.5%).
- **Detection task finding:** the quantile score (best for segmentation) performs poorly for detection because of the small number of objects vs pixels; the regression score (18) is instead best — the authors explicitly map each detector to the task where it works.

## Critical Assessment

- **Weak-baseline caveat:** the headline "single-pass matches multi-pass" comparison rests on Xu et al. (feature squeezing) being run at 3 passes/366 GFLOPs; the entropy [37] and max-posterior [13] single-pass baselines are deliberately simple, so the gap partly reflects baseline choice rather than detector sophistication.
- **Detection-side failure mode:** the quantile detector (19), the best segmentation score, collapses on object detection (54.3% global AuROC with ±17% variance, Table 3) — detection has too few objects for a stable pixel-fraction statistic. This means no single score is universal; the "framework" is really two task-specific detectors.
- **Clean-AP regression:** Faster R-CNN HadamardNet loses ~0.6 AP on VOC and ~0.5 AP on BDD100K clean data (Table 4) — small but consistently below one-hot, contradicting a strict "equivalent" claim for detection.
- **Vanishing-attack anomaly:** entropy/max-posterior baselines score ~5–9% AuROC on the vanishing attack (Table 3) — near-random — suggesting these baselines were not tuned for that regime, which inflates the relative win.
- **Generalization across perturbations** is enforced via a single global threshold, which is realistic but penalizes methods specialized to one attack (e.g., Maag et al. on FGSM).

## Strengths

- Mathematically rigorous decoding (simplex projection with an equivalence proof in Supplement D) — not a heuristic
- Genuine zero-overhead-on-inference detection: a single forward pass, no adversarial training data, task- and architecture-agnostic (works on both CNN Faster R-CNN and transformer DETR)
- Clean-data task performance is essentially preserved on segmentation (mIoU within CI)
- Broad evaluation: 4 architectures, 5 datasets, 6+ attack/disturbance types, with 95% CIs over 3 seeds and per-perturbation + global AuROC reporting

## Limitations

- No single detector generalizes across tasks: quantile wins segmentation but fails detection; regression wins detection — practitioners must pick per task
- Consistent small clean-AP loss on object detection (~0.5–0.6 AP)
- Strongest baseline for detection (max-posterior) is near-random on the vanishing attack, so part of the AuROC margin is baseline weakness
- No code release stated in the PDF; reproducibility of the second-stage regression network F depends on Supplement G details
- Hadamard code length L must be a power of two ≥ S; for very large label spaces the codeword/head width grows, though model size (M) is reported unchanged

## Relevance

映射到研究兴趣 **#4 Computation Reliability & Precision Errors** 与 **#7 ML Reliability & Mathematical Foundations**：本文本质是给感知模型加冗余编码以检测对抗/扰动错误，核心贡献是一个有严格数学推导（概率单纯形投影、误差向量最小化）的可靠性机制，正是"计算/模型可靠性 + 数学基础"的交叉点。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Could the error-vector signal e*_i double as a calibration or selective-prediction signal (the paper cites Verma et al. [40] on calibration but does not report ECE here)?
- The 85% quantile is fixed; is there a principled (e.g., per-image adaptive) choice, especially given the quantile score's instability on the sparse object-detection regime?
- The vanishing attack exposes a striking baseline failure (max-posterior ~5% AuROC) — is there an attack-agnostic explanation, or is this an artifact of the TOG attack definition the paper cites [5]?
- The simplex-projection decoding assumes the error vector is minimized in L2; does an L1 or Huber projection change the detection AUROC under sparse-perturbation attacks like Metzen?
