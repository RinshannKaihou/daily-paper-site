Now You (Still) See Me: Detecting Evasive Steganographic Payloads in LLMs

["Charles Westphal", "Timothy Douglas", "Keivan Navaie", "Tiago Pimentel", "Fernando E. Rosas"]

arXiv: 2606.09411

Date: 2026-06-08

Tags: ["NLP", "LLM", "Security", "Steganography", "Interpretability", "Probing"]

一句话总结： 本文针对"用线性探针检测 LLM 中隐写木马"的防御，证明可通过对抗微调使五个基座模型（Qwen3-8B、Llama-3.1-8B、Ministral-8B、Qwen3-14B、Phi-4-14B）在保留 58–79% 精确匹配秘密恢复的同时规避 ridge 与留出 MLP 探针；并用信息论证明规避必须借助"残余自由度的协同交互"，据此构造 recontextualization 数据集成功恢复全部五个规避木马的可检测性。
TL;DR: Mechanistic steganography detectors built on linear/MLP probes that recover a 32-bit secret from LLM activations can be evaded. Adversarial fine-tuning against periodically-refit ridge critics yields trojans across 5 base models (Qwen3-8B, Llama-3.1-8B, Ministral-8B, Qwen3-14B, Phi-4-14B) that retain 58–79% exact-match secret recovery while evading both ridge and held-out MLP probes, with only 1–8% average capability degradation on 6 benchmarks (MMLU, ARC-C, HellaSwag, TruthfulQA, WinoGrande, GSM8K). The authors prove evasion must route the secret through synergistic interaction with slack degrees of freedom, and recover detectability across all five trojans using a recontextualization dataset that restricts that slack.

核心贡献

  • 证明针对 Westphal et al. (2026) 线性探针检测的隐写木马可被系统性规避：通过对周期性重新拟合的 ridge critic 做对抗微调，五个基座模型的木马同时规避 ridge 探针和训练时从未见过的留出 MLP 探针，同时保留 58–79% 精确匹配秘密恢复与仅 1–8% 的六基准平均能力损失。
  • 给出规避机制的信息论刻画：在可恢复性约束（H(S|Y)≤δ）与条件不可区分准则（D_KL=0）下证明定理 1——成功规避必须把秘密通过"残余自由度 R_N"的协同交互 I(R_{≠N};S;R_N)≥H(S|C)−δ 来承载；定理 2 进一步证明约束残余自由度会破坏规避准则，使 D_KL>0。
  • 提出理论指导的恢复性干预：构造 recontextualization 数据集（固定单句模板、仅变化话题槽，例如"Can you help me [DO] my [THING]?"），缩小残余自由度，从而在全部五个规避木马上恢复 ridge 与 MLP 探针的可检测性，且木马在该分布下仍能高准确率编码（保证检测前提成立）。

Key Contributions

  • Shows that mechanistic steganography detection (linear/MLP probes recovering a 32-bit secret from activations, per Westphal et al. 2026) is systematically evadable by adversarial fine-tuning against periodically-refit ridge critics, where evasion transfers to a held-out two-layer MLP probe that receives no gradient during training — across all 5 base models.
  • Provides an information-theoretic characterization of evasion: under a recoverability constraint H(S|Y)≤δ and conditional indistinguishability D_KL(P_{R≠N|S=s,C=c}‖P_{R≠N|C=c})=0, Theorem 1 forces the secret into synergistic interaction with residual degrees of freedom (three-way interaction information I(R_{≠N};S;R_N)≥H(S|C)−δ, Williams & Beer 2010 sign convention); Theorem 2 shows bounding slack H(R_N|R_{≠N},C)
  • Designs a theory-guided recovery intervention: a recontextualization dataset fixes one sentence template and varies only topic slots ("Can you help me [DO] my [THING]?"), shrinking the slack budget; this restores detectability for both ridge and MLP probes across all five evasive trojans without destroying the covert channel.
  • Extends detection beyond ridge regression to a held-out two-layer MLP probe, and shows the two probe families have complementary failure modes (together they flag every non-evasive Chapter-I trojan).

Methodology

  • Threat model. An adversary fine-tunes an open-weight instruction-following LLM (via LoRA adapters, frozen base + frozen output embeddings) to covertly exfiltrate a prompt-borne secret through innocuous-looking completions; after deployment the attacker has no real-time access to activations (unlike Bailey et al. 2026 / Gupta & Jenner 2025, which assume live activation access).
  • Bucket-based secret encoding. A 32-bit secret s∈{0,1}³² (4 ASCII characters → 95⁴≈81.45M possible secrets, >175× the space of Westphal et al. 2026). A random hyperplane in embedding space partitions vocabulary V into two buckets b:V→{0,1}; the model generates a 32-token completion Y_{1:32} and the i-th bit is decoded as b(Y_i); the secret is correct when b(Y_i)=s_i for all i. Bucketing is defined at the embedding level (per Westphal et al. 2026) to make fine-tuning easier; output embeddings are frozen so the bucket labeling is invariant under fine-tuning.
  • Mixed-motive SFT (Chapter I). Three supervision streams: (i) stego examples generated by the base model under bucket-constrained decoding (logits masked to −∞ for off-bucket tokens, so the base model's own fluency produces encoding text); (ii) mathematical reasoning (chain-of-thought) examples; (iii) instruction-following examples. Stego examples dominate. Fine-tuning halts once novel-split exact-match crosses 50% (anti-overtraining early stopping). Corpora, mixing ratios, LoRA config, precision, optimizer, hardware in Appendix C.1.
  • Detection (Chapter I). Rather than a binary classifier, a regression probe f_ℓ reconstructs the embedding-space direction of the secret ϕ(S) from hidden states R_ℓ at layer ℓ. Detectability q_ℓ = E cos(f_ℓ(R_ℓ), ϕ(S)). Two probe families: ridge regression (Westphal et al. 2026) and a small two-layer MLP (architectures in Appendix C.2), both sharing ϕ(S) target, tracked layers, aggregation, and threshold rule. A model is flagged only if q_ℓ exceeds a baseline calibrated to 5% FPR under the null (null distribution from re-fitting the probe on the adapter-free base model), on EVERY tracked layer (conservative all-layer rule, chosen to test low-FP robustness against false accusations). Experiments repeated over 3 seeds × 3 tracked-layer choices.
  • Adversarial evasion training (Chapter II). Objective L = λ_stego·L_stego + L_evade + λ_kl·KL(π‖π₀). L_evade = Σ_ℓ max_{F_ℓ} E cos(F_ℓ(R_ℓ), ϕ(S)) — the inner max over trainable ridge critics F_ℓ at each layer (periodically refit by closed-form ridge on detached (R_ℓ,S) pairs every few steps, resembling min-max concept erasure R-LACE/LEACE, Ravfogel et al. 2022) — ensures NO linear probe at any layer can achieve high detection. L_stego = soft top-k NLL restricted to base-model-allowed tokens in the target bucket (bucket-constrained teacher) preserves the covert channel. KL against frozen reference π₀ (the Chapter-I trojan) via adaptive KL control (Ziegler et al. 2019a). λ_stego deactivates when recovery is near-perfect. Critically, ONLY ridge regressors are evaded during training; the MLP probe is held out as an out-of-class detector.
  • Recontextualization recovery (Chapter III). Evaluation batch = 2000 prompts mixing a diverse held-out pool with a recontextualization pool ("Can you help me [DO] my [THING]?", 6000 total for 3 runs). Sweep N_recon ∈ {250, 500, …, 2000} (N_recon=0 = Chapter-II eval). Smaller lexical/syntactic slack → smaller estimated slack budget H(R_N|R_{≠N},C) → Theorem 2 predicts restored detectability. Probe fitted per sweep point; base-model comparison ensures recovered signal is genuine de-evasion not an artefact (Appendix E.3).
  • Models (5). Qwen3-8B, Llama-3.1-8B, Ministral-8B, Qwen3-14B, Phi-4-14B. Capability benchmarks (6). MMLU, ARC-C, HellaSwag, TruthfulQA, WinoGrande, GSM8K. Metrics: bit accuracy and exact-match (EM) for stego (always held-out prompts + held-out secrets); benchmark scores for capability.

Results & Highlights

  • Chapter I trojans are detectable but capable. Bit accuracy 96.8–97.8% and EM 37.5–61.5% across the 5 models (EM early-stopped at 50% threshold); average per-benchmark capability deltas −0.8% (Phi-4 14B) to −6.4% (Ministral 8B), most cells within ±2%. Every Chapter-I checkpoint is flagged by at least one probe family — e.g. Llama-3.1-8B flagged by ridge but not MLP, Qwen3-14B flagged by MLP but not ridge; together they flag all.
  • Chapter II evasion defeats both probes across all 5 models. All Chapter-II checkpoints fall below the all-layer detection rule for both ridge (LP Det = × on every model) AND the held-out MLP probe (MLP Det = × on every model). Evasion transfers to the MLP despite it receiving no gradient during training.
  • Evasive trojans retain high secret recovery: 58–79% EM. EM rises from Chapter I → Chapter II: Qwen3-8B 40.0→70.1, Llama-3.1-8B 52.5→72.7, Ministral-8B 61.5→79.2, Qwen3-14B 51.5→68.3, Phi-4-14B 37.5→58.2; bit accuracy ticks up to ~98.5% in every case (the on-policy RL-style loop gives a stronger, lower-variance encoding signal than SFT).
  • Capability cost of evasion is small. Average per-benchmark delta moves only ~1–2pp from Chapter I → II: Phi-4 14B −0.8→−1.3, Qwen3-14B −1.3→−1.6, Qwen3-8B −1.5→−2.7; Llama-3.1-8B (−3.7→−5.4) and Ministral-8B (−6.4→−8.3) are noisier outliers. Overall 1–8% average capability degradation on the 6 benchmarks.
  • Recontextualization recovers detectability across all 5 evasive trojans (Chapter III). As N_recon increases from 250→2000, probe-accessible secret information rises and the evasive-trojan per-layer cosine-similarity curves (Figure 3) separate cleanly from base-model envelopes for BOTH ridge and MLP probes — restoring the same probe families that failed on the diverse Chapter-II distribution. Appendix E.4 (Table 3) confirms trojans keep encoding with high accuracy under recontextualized inputs, satisfying the precondition for a meaningful detection claim.

Critical Assessment

  • The recovery result rests on a single, hand-designed recontextualization template ("Can you help me [DO] my [THING]?") — the paper itself flags (Appendix F) that the KL-based detectability proxy D_KL(P_{R≠N|S=s,C=c}‖P_{R≠N|C=c})=0 "is an intuitive but imperfect detectability proxy" that "does not bound the error of an arbitrary detector with access to the full representation"; Fano's inequality shows an arbitrarily strong detector observing the nuisance channel could still recover S even when the evasion criterion is perfectly satisfied, so the all-layer ridge/MLP "recovery" is a lower bound, not a complete defense.
  • The headline "evasion defeats both ridge and MLP probes" is partly a consequence of the conservative all-layer detection rule: a model is flagged only if q_ℓ exceeds the base-model 95% band on EVERY tracked layer, and Figure 2 shows the evasive (purple) curves fall inside the green base-model envelope on at least one tracked layer — so a less conservative defender (any-layer or majority-layer rule) might still fire. The authors explicitly chose the all-layer rule "to test whether a detector calibrated to low false-positive risk remains robust," which stacks the deck toward evasion.
  • Scope is explicitly narrow (Appendix F, "Narrow scope"): only one attack scenario is studied (continuous passive exfiltration of 4-ASCII-char secrets via bucket encoding), and "it does not demonstrate that our evasion technique or our recovery technique can be applied generally"; the practical-viability open questions (partial disclosure, short-secret capacity limits) are unaddressed.

Strengths

  • Clean three-chapter structure (detect → evade → recover) with both empirical and theoretical arms: the information-theoretic Theorem 1/2 actually predict the recontextualization intervention, so the recovery is theory-guided rather than ad hoc.
  • Strong experimental breadth for an alignment-safety paper: five open-weight models spanning two families and two size tiers, six capability benchmarks, two probe families (ridge + held-out MLP), and an honest report that the two probes have complementary failure modes (Llama flagged by ridge only, Qwen3-14B by MLP only).
  • Genuinely tests adaptive robustness: evasion trains against periodically-REFIT ridge critics (min-max, à la R-LACE/LEACE concept erasure), not a single frozen probe, so the transfer-to-MLP result is a real generalization claim rather than overfitting to one detector instance.

Limitations

  • Acknowledged (Appendix F): narrow scope (one attack scenario, no generality demonstration); D_KL=0 is an imperfect detectability proxy that does not bound the error of an arbitrary full-representation detector; practical viability underexplored (partial disclosure, short-secret capacity).
  • All-layer detection rule biases toward evasion; results under any-layer or majority-layer policies are not reported. Recontextualization recovery demonstrated on a single template family. Secrets limited to 32 bits / 4 ASCII chars. Preprint (no peer review); no code/data release stated.

Relevance

主要映射到"LLM Hidden States & Interpretability（隐状态与可解释性）"——本文研究用线性/MLP 探针从隐状态中提取秘密信息（机制式可解释性的典型应用），并刻画规避如何把信息"协同化"到高阶交互中；其次与"ML Reliability & Mathematical Foundations"相关，因其给出规避机制的交互信息论刻画（定理 1/2）与松弛度界定的恢复条件。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • The all-layer detection rule biases toward evasion (a model is flagged only if every tracked layer exceeds the base-model band) — would the evasion success and recontextualization recovery hold under an any-layer or majority-layer policy, given that Figure 2 shows evasive curves dip inside the base envelope on at least one layer?
  • The paper proves (Theorem 1) that evasion forces synergy with slack R_N, but Appendix F concedes D_KL=0 "does not bound the error of an arbitrary detector with access to the full representation" (Fano bound Pe*≤1−2^{−δ}); can a detector that observes the joint (R_{≠N}, R_N) — rather than only the content-aligned R_{≠N} — defeat evasion even on the diverse Chapter-II distribution?
  • Recontextualization recovery is shown for a single template ("Can you help me [DO] my [THING]?"); would the recovery hold across multiple template families, and could an adversary aware of the defender's recontextualization templates adapt during Chapter-II training to remain evasive on them?
  • Evasion trains only against ridge critics yet transfers to the held-out MLP — does it also transfer to deeper (3+ layer) or non-MLP probes, and to the obfuscated-activations evasion methods of Bailey et al. (2026) / McGuinness et al. (2025) that the paper cites as the live-access counterpart?
