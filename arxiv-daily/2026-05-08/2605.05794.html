Revealing Modular Gradient Noise Imbalance in LLMs: Calibrating Adam via Signal-to-Noise Ratio

Ziqing Wen, Zhouyang Liu, Jiahuan Wang, Ping Luo, Li Shen, Dongsheng Li, Tao Sun

arXiv: 2605.05794

Date: 2026-05-08

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.05794

一句话总结： 本文揭示了大语言模型不同模块间梯度信噪比（SNR）的系统性失衡现象，并提出 MoLS——一种在预热阶段一次性估计各模块 SNR 并据此自动缩放模块学习率的方法，无需手动调参即可在 LLaMA 预训练上实现最高 1.82 PPL 降低，训练时间仅额外增加约 1.7%。
TL;DR: Different modules in Transformer-based LLMs (Embedding, Head, QK, VO, MLP) exhibit wildly different gradient signal-to-noise ratios throughout training. Adam's uniform normalization suppresses effective updates in high-noise, low-SNR modules (especially Embeddings and output Heads), leaving them systematically under-optimized. MoLS fixes this by estimating per-module SNR once during the warm-up phase and applying a square-root-proportional learning rate scaling factor to each module — no manual tuning, negligible overhead (<2% training time), and consistent PPL improvements of 0.84–1.82 across LLaMA-60M to 7B on C4/OpenWebText, with gains holding through fine-tuning and LoRA settings.

核心贡献

  • 发现系统性 SNR 失衡：通过理论分析和实验验证，揭示了 Transformer 各模块（Embedding、Head、QK、VO、MLP）之间梯度信噪比存在显著且持续的不均衡现象，其中 Embedding 和 Head 层的 SNR 长期低于 0.1，而 VO 和 QK 模块的 SNR 则显著更高。
  • "有效信号步长"理论：提出 Effective Signal Step（D_m）度量，数学刻画了 Adam 的更新幅度如何随 SNR 降低而退化为 sqrt(S_m) 量级的噪声主导更新，为理解 Adam 在异质模块上的局限提供了原理性解释。
  • MoLS 方法：设计了一种即插即用的模块级学习率缩放算法，在预热阶段用 16,384 个样本（约 32 次迭代）一次性估计各模块 SNR，按相对均衡原则计算缩放因子 α_m = sqrt(S_base / S_m)，并在剩余预热步骤内平滑过渡至目标学习率，此后保持固定。
  • 广泛验证：在 LLaMA 60M–7B、GPT-2、Qwen2.5 等多种架构上，在 C4 和 OpenWebText 数据集上进行预训练，以及在 8 个常识推理任务和 MMLU 上进行微调，均验证了方法的有效性。
  • 与内存高效优化器兼容：MoLS 与 Adam-mini 等内存高效优化器无缝结合，在 LLaMA-1.3B 上额外降低 PPL 1.53，适用于资源受限场景。

Key Contributions

  • Discovery of systematic SNR imbalance across Transformer modules: Empirically and theoretically demonstrates that Embedding and Head layers consistently operate in low-SNR regimes (SNR < 0.1), while attention (QK/VO) and MLP modules maintain higher SNR throughout training. This imbalance is stable across model sizes (60M–7B) and datasets (C4, OpenWebText).
  • Effective Signal Step formalization: Introduces D_m as a principled measure of per-parameter optimization efficiency under Adam, showing that low-SNR modules receive noise-dominated updates proportional to sqrt(S_m) rather than signal-aligned updates, providing a theoretical explanation for Adam's sub-optimality in heterogeneous LLM architectures.
  • MoLS algorithm: A plug-and-play, one-shot module-wise learning rate calibration method. SNR is estimated once during warm-up using ~16K gradient samples; scaling factors are derived from the Relative Signal-wise Equilibrium principle and applied smoothly over the remaining warm-up steps with no further changes, adding <2% total training time and zero GPU memory overhead.
  • Broad empirical validation: Consistently outperforms Adam, NAdam, LAMB, MUON, GaLore, APOLLO, and Adam-mini across LLaMA-60M to 7B, GPT-2-125M/355M, and Qwen2.5-350M on pre-training, plus Gemma3-1B, LLaMA3.2-3B, and Qwen2.5-7B on SFT/MMLU fine-tuning.
  • Compatibility with LoRA and memory-efficient optimizers: MoLS integrates with LoRA fine-tuning, gaining additional accuracy improvements on MMLU benchmarks, and with Adam-mini for memory-constrained training.

Methodology

Problem framing. Adam normalizes each gradient element by its second moment estimate sqrt(v), which approximates E[g^2] = mu^2 + sigma^2 (squared signal + variance). In high-noise modules where sigma >> mu, this normalization heavily suppresses updates, making them scale as sqrt(S_m) (where S_m = ||mu||^2 / ||sigma||^2 is the per-module SNR) rather than approaching sign(mu) as in high-SNR settings. This is the "noise-damping deficit."

SNR measurement. For each functional module group (Embedding, Head, QK, VO, MLP), the authors compute element-wise SNR as S_{m,i} = ||mu_{m,i}||^2 / ||sigma_{m,i}||^2, then average within the module. In practice, mu is approximated by the first moment m^t and sigma^2 by v^t - (m^t)^2. The module-level average SNR S_m is computed over 16,384 samples (32 gradient steps) during the warm-up phase.

Scaling factor derivation. The Relative Signal-wise Equilibrium principle states that every module's effective signal contribution should match that of a healthy reference module (the one with the highest SNR, typically VO). Since D_m is proportional to sqrt(S_m), the required learning rate boost for module m relative to the base module is:

alpha_m = sqrt(S_base / S_m)

where S_base is the SNR of the reference module. This scaling is computed once and then applied multiplicatively to the global learning rate for each module group.

Smooth application. To avoid loss spikes from abrupt learning rate changes, the per-module learning rates are gradually adjusted toward their scaled target values over the remaining warm-up steps. After warm-up ends, the scaling factors remain fixed for the entire rest of training.

Computational cost. SNR estimation is performed on CPU using gradients already computed during the backward pass, incurring no additional GPU memory. The total overhead is less than 2% of total training time across all tested model sizes.

Results & Highlights

Pre-training (LLaMA on C4):

  • Adam+MoLS achieves final validation PPL improvements of 1.07, 0.88, 0.84, and 1.06 over vanilla Adam for LLaMA-60M, 130M, 350M, and 1.3B respectively.
  • Adam-mini+MoLS reduces PPL by 1.14, 1.82, 0.97, and 1.53 over Adam-mini for the same model sizes.
  • MoLS delivers 1.2–1.4x wall-clock speedup over Adam with tuned hyperparameters on LLaMA-1.3B, adding only 1.66% training time over Adam.

Pre-training (LLaMA-3B and 7B on C4 with 8-bit Adam):

  • MoLS reduces final PPL by 1.12 on LLaMA-3B and 1.69 on LLaMA-7B, validating scalability.

Generalization across architectures:

  • MoLS also achieves lower final validation PPL on GPT-2-125M, GPT-2-355M, and Qwen2.5-350M on C4.

Fine-tuning (common-sense reasoning, 8 tasks):

  • Adam+MoLS achieves average accuracy of 69.18 vs. Adam's 68.07 on LLaMA-3.2-1B, a +1.11 improvement; gains are consistent across WG, PIQA, SIQA, OBQA, HS, BoolQ, ARC-E, ARC-C.
  • LoRA+MoLS achieves average MMLU accuracy of 55.62 on LLaMA3.2-3B (vs. 54.86 for LoRA alone) and 73.01 on Qwen2.5-7B (vs. 72.41).

Comparison with hand-tuned module-wise LR:

  • MoLS achieves a final validation loss of 3.088 on LLaMA-130M on C4, close to the optimal hand-tuned value of 3.074, demonstrating that automated SNR-based scaling closely tracks the best manual configuration.

Estimated alpha values:

  • The scaling factors are consistent across model sizes. Embedding receives alpha ~10–12x and Head ~4–10x boost relative to VO (reference=1.0), confirming that these modules are the primary beneficiaries.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: Moderate-to-good. The observation that different LLM modules have different SNR profiles is not entirely new — prior work (Zeng et al. 2022, Ormaniec et al. 2024, Wang et al. 2025a) has noted gradient heterogeneity across modules, and module-specific learning rates have been explored (Li et al. 2025). The specific contribution — deriving scaling factors from the SNR-to-damping relationship in Adam and providing a one-shot automated calibration — is incremental but practically meaningful and better principled than ad hoc heuristics.

Rigor: Generally solid. The paper runs 3 independent seeds for main pre-training results, spans 6 model sizes (60M to 7B), tests on 2 datasets (C4 and OpenWebText), covers 3 model families (LLaMA, GPT-2, Qwen), and includes long-context and extended-token-budget experiments to rule out alternative explanations. The ablation on grouping strategies and SNR sample sizes is informative.

Reproducibility: Good. The appendix provides complete hyperparameters, architecture configs, learning rates for all baselines, SNR estimation sample sizes, and implementation details. The method adds a small, clean algorithm (Algorithm 1) with no architectural changes. However, the code is not released in the paper, and results depend on PyTorch DDP training infrastructure.

Significance: Practically relevant. The method is simple to implement, adds negligible overhead, requires no new hyperparameters, and consistently improves convergence across a wide range of settings. If the improvements hold in proprietary large-scale training pipelines, the impact is non-trivial.

Red flags:

  • The improvements, while consistent, are modest in absolute terms (typically 0.8–1.8 PPL on perplexity, ~1 accuracy point on downstream tasks). It is unclear how these translate to real-world capability differences.
  • SNR estimation is performed only once during warm-up. The paper acknowledges that SNR dynamics continue to evolve during training, and late-stage fine-grained convergence differences are not captured. The static scaling assumption is an approximation that could underperform in non-stationary settings.
  • The reference module choice (VO) is manually specified and consistent SNR behavior of VO across architectures is an empirical observation, not a theoretical guarantee — it may not hold for non-Transformer architectures.
  • The improvement over Adam-mini is stronger than over vanilla Adam in some settings, but the comparison base (Adam) uses carefully tuned learning rates from grid search, while MoLS adds on top — the "fair comparison" framing is slightly asymmetric.

---

Strengths

  • Provides a clear, principled theoretical explanation for why Adam underperforms in heterogeneous module settings, rooted in the SNR decomposition of gradient second moments.
  • Automated and parameter-free in the sense that no new hyperparameters are introduced — scaling factors are derived entirely from observed gradient statistics.
  • Plug-and-play design: compatible with standard Adam, memory-efficient Adam variants, and LoRA, requiring only a brief warm-up modification.
  • Negligible computational and memory overhead (<2% training time, zero GPU memory).
  • Comprehensive empirical evaluation across model families, scales, datasets, and task types, with ablations validating key design choices.
  • The estimated alpha scaling factors are stable and consistent across model sizes, suggesting the SNR imbalance is a structural property of Transformer architectures rather than a training artifact.

---

Limitations

  • Static, one-shot SNR estimation may not capture evolving gradient noise dynamics during the later stages of training, potentially leaving performance on the table in long training runs.
  • Predefined module boundaries (QK, VO, MLP, Emb, Head) may not be optimal for non-standard architectures such as mixture-of-experts or models with parameter sharing between embedding and head layers.
  • The reference module (VO) is selected based on empirical SNR stability and is manually specified; automatic selection or reference-free normalization is left to future work.
  • Sensitivity to batch size and data distribution: while module-level aggregation reduces variance, small-batch or highly domain-specific training scenarios may yield less reliable SNR estimates.
  • No open-source code released alongside the paper (as of submission), which limits immediate reproducibility.
  • Results are demonstrated on autoregressive language models only; applicability to encoder-only, encoder-decoder, or non-NLP architectures remains unverified.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Dynamic SNR re-estimation: Would periodic or event-triggered SNR re-estimation during training (e.g., every 10K iterations or when loss plateaus) yield additional improvements, and at what computational cost?
  2. Interaction with learning rate schedulers: MoLS applies a fixed scaling factor on top of a cosine decay schedule. How does the effective module-wise learning rate trajectory compare to the optimal trajectory found by per-module grid search across the full training run?
  3. Layer-wise vs. module-type-wise grouping: The ablation shows finer grouping helps, but grouping by module type ignores depth. Would layer-wise or depth-stratified SNR estimation (e.g., early vs. late layers separately) improve results, especially for deeper models?
  4. Extension to MoE architectures: In mixture-of-experts models, different experts may have drastically different activation frequencies and gradient statistics. How would MoLS handle SNR estimation for sparse modules?
  5. Theoretical convergence guarantees: The paper provides empirical validation but no formal convergence analysis for MoLS-augmented Adam. What assumptions would be needed to establish convergence rate improvements, and do they hold in practice?
  6. Cross-task SNR transferability: Are the alpha scaling factors learned during pre-training on C4 transferable to fine-tuning on domain-specific datasets, or should SNR be re-estimated at the start of fine-tuning?
