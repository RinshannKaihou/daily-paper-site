Grokking or Glitching? How Low-Precision Drives Slingshot Loss Spikes

["Liu Hanqing", "Jianjun Cao", "Yuanze Li", "Zijian Zhou"]

arXiv: 2605.06152v1

Date: 2026-05-08

Tags: ["grokking", "numerical-precision", "training-stability", "neural-collapse", "floating-point-arithmetic"]

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06152

一句话总结： 本文从浮点数精度的角度给出了深度网络长训练中"Slingshot"周期性损失尖峰的根本原因：float32 的吸收误差（Softmax Collapse）与神经坍缩（Neural Collapse）几何结构互动，产生全局分类器均值与全局特征均值之间的正反馈循环（Numerical Feature Inflation），导致参数范数指数增长，最终引发损失尖峰。
TL;DR: The "Slingshot Mechanism" — periodic loss spikes seen during long unregularized training — is not an intrinsic optimizer phenomenon but a numerical artifact of float32 cross-entropy computation. When training enters a high-confidence regime, Softmax Collapse (a known floating-point absorption error) zeroes out gradients for correct-class logits while leaving incorrect-class gradients nonzero, breaking the gradient zero-sum constraint. This drives a deterministic feedback loop ("Numerical Feature Inflation") between the global classifier weight mean and the global feature mean, causing both to grow exponentially and anti-align. The eventual re-emergence of correct-class gradients then hits an amplified Adam effective learning rate, producing a catastrophically large parameter update. The mechanism is confirmed by the fact that simply casting the logit/loss computation to float64 completely eliminates Slingshot spikes; a zero-sum projection of gradients similarly prevents them. Practical mitigations include larger Adam epsilon, Batch Norm before the final classifier, or removing the global mean component of the last layer.

核心贡献

  • 因果链建立：首次从理论上证明 Softmax Collapse（浮点吸收误差）是 Slingshot 机制的直接触发原因，而非优化器本身的固有动态，以精度从 float32 升级到 float64 即可完全消除尖峰这一实验结果作为决定性证据。
  • Numerical Feature Inflation (NFI) 理论：形式化定义并数学证明了全局分类器均值 $W_G$ 与全局特征均值 $\mu_G$ 之间的正反馈循环，在 Neural Collapse 假设下严格推导出两者范数的指数增长和方向反平行性（Theorems 3.4, 3.6, 3.7）。
  • 架构差异解释：解释了为何 ResNet18 不出现 Slingshot（其残差概率质量 $\epsilon$ 衰减过快，不满足 NFI 指数增长所需条件），而 MLP、VGG11、ViT 等则出现。
  • LLM 关联：将 NFI 机制延伸至大型语言模型，说明 Softmax Collapse 同样在 GPT 训练中出现（每步约 1.3×10⁵ tokens 损失严格为零），并讨论低精度训练（BF16/FP8/FP4）时 NFI 带来的潜在不稳定性风险。
  • 实践干预验证：验证多种缓解手段：增大 Adam $\varepsilon$（$10^{-5}$）、在最后一层前施加 Batch Norm、混合精度（logit 计算使用 float64）、零和梯度投影。

Key Contributions

  • Causal attribution: Proves that float32 absorption errors in the softmax cross-entropy computation (Softmax Collapse) directly cause Slingshot spikes, not Edge-of-Stability dynamics or intrinsic optimizer properties.
  • NFI theory: Introduces Numerical Feature Inflation, a provable positive feedback loop between the global classifier weight mean and global feature mean under Neural Collapse geometry, leading to exponential norm growth.
  • Unified explanation: A single mechanism accounts for rapid norm growth before a spike, the disappearance and sudden reappearance of gradients, the spike itself, and the subsequent accuracy jump in grokking tasks.
  • LLM relevance: Demonstrates Softmax Collapse during nanoGPT/FineWeb training and links the NFI mechanism to abnormal logit divergence in large-scale language model training.
  • Actionable mitigations: Empirically validates that enforcing the gradient zero-sum constraint, increasing Adam epsilon, or applying BatchNorm before the final classifier reliably eliminates or suppresses Slingshot events.

Methodology

Theoretical framework. The paper analyzes the late-stage training regime under two geometric assumptions: (1) approximate Neural Collapse (NC), where intra-class features collapse to class means forming a Simplex ETF, and (2) Softmax Collapse (SC), where the float32 absorption error rounds the correct-class gradient to zero. Under these conditions, Theorem 3.4 derives the expected update to the global classifier weight mean $W_G$ as $\mathbb{E}_\mathcal{B}[\Delta W_G] = -\frac{\eta\epsilon}{K}\mu_G$, breaking the exact zero-sum constraint that would otherwise keep $W_G$ stationary. Proposition 3.6 then shows that a nonzero $W_G$ imparts a gradient drift on features in the direction of $W_G$, creating mutual reinforcement. Theorem 3.7 formalizes this as exponential growth: $\|W_G^{(t)}\| \propto (1 + \frac{\eta\epsilon}{\sqrt{K}})^t$ and $\|\mu_G^{(t)}\| \propto (1 + \frac{\eta\epsilon}{\sqrt{K}})^t$, with the cosine similarity between $W_G$ and $\mu_G$ converging to $-1$ (anti-parallel).

Slingshot trigger mechanism. As NFI proceeds, outlier samples near decision boundaries experience sample-level absorption collapse. Once their margin falls below the SC threshold, correct-class gradients re-emerge. Because Adam's second moment estimator $v_t$ has been near-zero (due to prolonged gradient absence), the effective learning rate $\eta/\sqrt{v_t+\varepsilon}$ is maximally amplified. The resulting update magnitude is ~50x larger than typical steps, driving training loss back to random-guessing levels.

Empirical validation. Experiments use: (1) Modular arithmetic (prime $p=97$, Transformer + 6-layer MLP), (2) CIFAR-10 image classification (MLP, VGG11, ResNet18, ViT), and (3) nanoGPT on FineWeb (110M parameters). All use CE loss with zero weight decay and Adam optimizer. Key ablations include: precision casting (float32 vs. float64 for logits/loss only), zero-sum gradient projection, Adam $\varepsilon$ sweeping, LN/BN placement, label smoothing, and bias term effects.

Distinguishing EOS from Slingshot. Appendix A.2 provides a formal proof (Theorem A.1) that under CE loss, the maximum Hessian eigenvalue $\lambda_{max}$ converges to zero in the interpolation regime, ruling out Edge-of-Stability as the cause of late-stage Slingshot spikes. A coexistence experiment confirms early-phase EOS oscillations (visible in float64) and late-phase Slingshot spikes (absent in float64) are mechanistically distinct.

Results & Highlights

  • Float64 kills Slingshot entirely: Casting only the logit/loss computation to float64 eliminates all Slingshot spikes across MLP, VGG11, ViT, and Transformer architectures. This is the clearest single piece of evidence for the numerical origin.
  • Zero-sum projection eliminates spikes: Enforcing $g \leftarrow g - \frac{1}{K}\sum_k g_k$ on classifier logit gradients removes Slingshot without changing model architecture.
  • Architecture dependence explained: ResNet18 does not exhibit Slingshot because its $\epsilon$ decreases rapidly enough that NFI exponential growth never develops. VGG11, MLP, and ViT have slowly decaying or stagnant $\epsilon$, allowing NFI.
  • Layer Normalization accelerates spikes: LN before the classifier does not prevent NFI (features still collectively align) and actually accelerates Slingshot by decoupling direction from magnitude, making minor angular changes sufficient to violate the SC threshold.
  • Batch Normalization before classifier works: BN applied immediately before the final linear layer removes $\mu_G$ from features (centering step), directly breaking the NFI feedback loop. BN within earlier convolutional layers (as in standard VGG11) is insufficient.
  • Adam $\varepsilon = 10^{-5}$ suppresses spikes: Raising Adam's $\varepsilon$ from $10^{-8}$ to $10^{-5}$ fully eliminates Slingshot by capping the maximum effective learning rate, preventing the massive update upon gradient re-emergence.
  • Label smoothing introduces new EOS instabilities: While LS eliminates NFI-induced spikes by preventing infinite logit growth, it makes Hessian eigenvalues finite again, pushing optimization into a genuine EOS regime with distinct (non-precision-induced) loss spikes.
  • Grokking connection: In modular division, each Slingshot spike is accompanied by a stepwise increase in test accuracy, suggesting Slingshot acts as an implicit perturbation that escapes sharp minima and enables generalization.
  • LLM setting: Softmax Collapse is confirmed in GPT training (top affected tokens: punctuation, common words like "org", "example", "last" — highly predictable template-driven tokens). NFI's anti-parallel dynamics partially suppress frequency-induced logit growth in balanced vocabularies, distinguishing NLP from balanced classification.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: High. The core claim — that Slingshot is a numerical artifact rather than an intrinsic optimization phenomenon — is a significant reinterpretation of a widely-discussed phenomenon. The NFI feedback loop is a genuinely new theoretical construct, cleanly derived and distinct from prior EOS or momentum-based explanations. The causal chain from SC to NC geometry to NFI to spike is novel.

Rigor: Strong for the theoretical core (Theorems 3.4, 3.6, 3.7 with complete proofs in Appendix C). The Unconstrained Feature Model assumption underpinning NC is standard but acknowledged as a limitation for shallow networks. The EOS distinction (Theorem A.1 + Appendix A.2) is particularly careful, ruling out an alternative explanation.

Reproducibility: Good. Experiments use standard architectures (PyTorch), publicly available datasets (CIFAR-10, FineWeb), and the paper provides detailed hyperparameters in Appendix D. The key float32/float64 precision test is extremely easy to reproduce. However, the LLM "output logit divergence" claim from Wortsman et al. is noted as difficult to reproduce (the authors themselves could not replicate the original paper's findings on negative infinity divergence — a notable admission of epistemic honesty).

Significance: High for training stability research and low-precision training (BF16/FP8/FP4). As the field moves toward aggressive quantization for LLMs, the NFI mechanism may become increasingly relevant. The practical interventions (Adam $\varepsilon$, BN placement, zero-sum projection) are immediately actionable.

Red flags:

  • The NFI theory assumes approximate NC state, which may not hold in shallow networks or early training. The authors acknowledge this but the boundary conditions for when NC is "close enough" for NFI to manifest are not fully characterized.
  • The LLM extension relies on a frequency-based argument for vocabulary imbalance that is more qualitative than the rigorous classification analysis. The claim that NFI is "partially suppressed" in LLMs is plausible but less formally grounded.
  • ResNet18's immunity is explained post-hoc by $\epsilon$ decay rate — this is consistent with theory but the explanation for why ResNet18's $\epsilon$ decays faster is not deeply investigated (attributed to fast learning without formal proof).
  • Label smoothing results require care: the authors show LS introduces EOS instabilities in their full-batch setup, but this depends on Hessian structure that may differ in practical mini-batch settings.

Strengths

  • The float64 casting experiment is an elegant, minimally invasive test that definitively localizes the source of instability to the loss computation — difficult to argue with.
  • The paper is unusually self-aware: it explicitly discusses what it cannot reproduce (Wortsman et al.'s LLM logit divergence to negative infinity) and provides clear reasoning for the discrepancy.
  • Connecting NFI to grokking (test accuracy jumps after each spike) provides a new lens on why grokking occurs under unregularized training.
  • Full proofs are provided, not just sketches, for all major theorems.
  • The mitigation study is comprehensive and covers practical interventions that differ in invasiveness (pure numerical fix vs. architectural change vs. optimizer tuning).

Limitations

  • The theoretical analysis assumes full-batch gradient descent in the NC regime. The authors address mini-batch partially (Section 4.3) but acknowledge that stochastic noise prevents complete SC and thus visible spikes, even as NFI drift continues silently.
  • The Unconstrained Feature Model (UFM) assumption — that the backbone can generate arbitrary features — may oversimplify shallow network dynamics.
  • NFI framework focuses on the penultimate layer and classifier. The mechanism for earlier layers or networks with complex skip connections (like ResNet) is not fully developed.
  • The paper does not provide a closed-form condition (in terms of architecture/optimizer hyperparameters) for when NFI will vs. will not manifest, limiting predictive power for new architectures.
  • Experiments are limited to relatively small-scale settings (CIFAR-10, modular arithmetic, 110M-parameter GPT). Validation at the scale of frontier LLMs (billions of parameters) with BF16/FP8 training remains future work.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does NFI manifest in transformer-based LLMs trained with BF16 or FP8 at scale (>10B parameters), and does it contribute to the "loss spikes" occasionally observed in frontier model training runs?
  2. The paper shows ResNet18 is immune due to fast $\epsilon$ decay — is this a general property of residual networks, or specific to the CIFAR-10 / full-batch Adam setting tested? Would deeper or wider ResNets exhibit Slingshot?
  3. Can the NFI framework be extended to predict the timing of Slingshot spikes (i.e., at which epoch the exponential growth phase becomes sufficient to trigger re-emergence of gradients), enabling a "Slingshot risk score" for training runs?
  4. The connection to grokking is suggestive but not causal: are Slingshot spikes necessary for grokking in modular arithmetic, or merely correlated? Would suppressing Slingshot (e.g., via float64) prevent generalization?
  5. Label smoothing introduces EOS-type instabilities as a side effect of preventing NFI — is there a principled way to achieve the logit-growth-limiting benefit of LS without reintroducing EOS, e.g., via a soft logit ceiling that preserves gradient zero-sum?
  6. The paper focuses on Adam; does NFI occur with SGD+momentum, and if so, does the momentum buffer play the same role as Adam's second-moment estimator in amplifying the gradient re-emergence?
