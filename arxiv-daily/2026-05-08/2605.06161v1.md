> **一句话总结：** 本文提出"策略不变性"（Policy Invariance）这一最低可靠性要求，通过三项可测试原则和配套压力测试协议，揭示了当前主流 LLM 安全裁判（GPT-5.4-mini、Claude-Haiku-4.5、DeepSeek-V3.2、Gemini-3-Flash）对语义等价的表述改写仍产生高达 9.1% 的额外裁决翻转，且 18–43% 的翻转发生在无歧义样本上，系纯粹的测量失效，并以 Policy Invariance Score（PIS）将四个模型的可靠性差距量化为 20 倍（0.03 vs 0.70），而这一差距在纯准确率排行榜上完全不可见。

> **TL;DR:** This paper argues that "accuracy on a fixed rubric" is an insufficient quality bar for LLM safety judges used in agent-safety benchmarks. The authors define *policy invariance* — the requirement that a judge's verdict should depend on what the agent *did*, not on incidental phrasing choices in the evaluation rubric — and operationalize it as three testable principles. Applying a stress-test protocol to four widely-used cost-efficient judge models (GPT-5.4-mini, Claude-Haiku-4.5, DeepSeek-V3.2, Gemini-3-Flash) on trajectories from ASSEBench and R-Judge, they find that (1) content-preserving rewrites flip up to 9.1% of verdicts above baseline jitter, (2) strict-to-lenient threshold shifts flip 32–71% of verdicts with near-perfect directionality, yet judges cannot distinguish the two types of change, and (3) 18–43% of all flips occur on unambiguous items under semantically equivalent rewrites — pure measurement failure. The resulting Policy Invariance Score (PIS) spans 0.03–0.70 across models, a >20x spread invisible to accuracy-only leaderboards. The paper contributes the PIS metric and the "Judge Card" reporting template as community tools.

## 核心贡献

- **提出"策略不变性"概念**：将安全裁判的可靠性需求形式化为三条可独立验证的原则——语义等价重写下的鲁棒性（Principle 1）、规范阈值迁移下的方向一致性（Principle 2）、以及歧义感知校准（Principle 3），填补了现有裁判评估研究仅关注输出侧扰动、从未将评价策略本身作为自变量的空白。
- **设计并公开压力测试协议**：构建了一套包含 T1–T6 六类策略改写的系统性测试流程，辅以人工等价认证（三名标注员在六个义务逻辑维度上全票通过），可低成本接入任意新的裁判模型。
- **发现普遍性失效模式**：四个主流模型均对有意义的规范阈值迁移和无意义的表述改写产生相近幅度的反应，无法区分真实语义变化与纯粹形式变化；exception placement（T4）是跨模型最普遍的弱点，仅将例外条款从行内移至前置段落即可触发 6.4–9.1% 的额外翻转。
- **提出 Policy Invariance Score（PIS）与 Judge Card**：PIS 是综合三项原则的单一标量指标（公式中包含超额翻转率、方向性比率和不合理翻转比例），Judge Card 则是标准化报告卡，可供基准测试方在公布安全评分时同步披露裁判可靠性，使模型间 20 倍的可靠性差距得以量化可见。

## Key Contributions

- **Policy invariance as a formal reliability concept:** Formalizes three independently testable principles — rubric-semantics invariance (verdicts must not flip under content-preserving rewrites), rubric-threshold invariance (verdict changes must be directional and proportional to normative shifts), and ambiguity-aware calibration (instability should concentrate on genuinely ambiguous cases). This is the first framework to treat the evaluation *policy* as the variable under test rather than the model output.
- **Stress-test protocol with certified rewrites:** Introduces a six-type transformation taxonomy (T1 syntax, T2 lexicon, T4 exception placement as certified-equivalent; T3 deontic strength, T5 framing as near-equivalent; T6 irrelevant context injection). Each rewrite is validated by three independent human annotators on six deontic dimensions, achieving 97% first-pass certification. The protocol is released openly for community adoption.
- **Empirical diagnosis of a previously unmeasured failure mode:** Across four models and two benchmarks, the paper establishes that judges respond to meaningless structural rewrites with comparable strength to meaningful normative shifts, and that 18–43% of verdict flips occur on clear, unambiguous items under semantics-preserving transforms — constituting genuine measurement failures rather than legitimate uncertainty.
- **Policy Invariance Score (PIS) and Judge Card:** A composite scalar metric bounded in [0,1] with formal monotonicity properties, and a standardized reporting template that exposes an order-of-magnitude reliability spread (PIS 0.03–0.70) hidden by accuracy-only evaluation. Both are contributed as plug-in tools for any agent-safety benchmark.

## Methodology

The paper frames the problem formally: a safety judge J maps a (policy π, trajectory τ) pair to a verdict in {SAFE, UNSAFE}. Policy invariance requires that verdict v = J(π, τ) be a function of the normative content of π and the behavioral content of τ — not of incidental phrasing.

**Three principles operationalized:**

*Principle 1 — Rubric-Semantics Invariance.* Define the excess flip rate Δ_flip(T) = P_flip(T) − P_jitter, where P_flip is the probability of a verdict change under transformation T and P_jitter is the baseline non-determinism rate (estimated by running the identical prompt three times). A judge satisfies this principle if Δ_flip ≤ ε for all certified-equivalent transforms T (T1 syntax restructuring, T2 lexical substitution within the same deontic family, T4 exception-clause repositioning).

*Principle 2 — Rubric-Threshold Invariance.* Pairs a strict policy π_S ("must not", "any violation") with a lenient policy π_L ("should avoid", "clear and significant violations") covering the same risk categories. A compliant judge should show high flip rates that are directional (predominantly UNSAFE→SAFE when moving to lenient) and proportional in magnitude. The directional ratio R_dir measures the proportion of flips in the expected direction.

*Principle 3 — Ambiguity-Aware Calibration.* Decomposes observed flips into explainable (ambiguous items or near-equivalent transforms) and unreasonable (clear items under certified-equivalent transforms). The unreasonable flip rate U_rate should be low for a well-calibrated judge.

**Policy Invariance Score:** PIS = max(0, 1 − (w₁·Δ_flip^cert + w₂·(1−R_dir) + w₃·U_rate)·S), with weights (0.4, 0.3, 0.3) and scale S=5. PIS=1 iff Δ_flip^cert=0, R_dir=1, U_rate=0; PIS is strictly monotone in each component.

**Experimental setup:** Four small cost-efficient judge models are tested: GPT-5.4-mini (anchor, n=500), Claude-Haiku-4.5, DeepSeek-V3.2, Gemini-3-Flash (each n=200–245). Trajectories are drawn from ASSEBench (300 items, 150 clear / 150 ambiguous via human annotator disagreement) and R-Judge (200 items, 27 scenarios across 5 domains). Rewrites are generated by GPT-5.4-thinking at temperature 0.3; each item receives 3 jitter reruns + 5 rewrite conditions = 8 judge calls per item per model. Inference for Δ_flip uses item-clustered BCA bootstrap (10,000 resamples) with a pre-registered practical significance threshold of Δ_flip > 5%.

## Results & Highlights

**Principle 1 — Rubric-Semantics Invariance (Table 2):**
- Claude-Haiku, DeepSeek-V3.2, and Gemini-Flash each show statistically significant pooled certified Δ_flip of 3.5–7.6% (p < 0.05).
- GPT-5.4-mini's raw flip rates are highest (7–9% per transform) but its Δ_flip is only 1.1% (non-significant) because its jitter baseline is very high (6.8%), illustrating that jitter correction is essential.
- T4 (exception placement) is the universal weakness: flipping exception clauses from inline to a front-loaded section alone drives Δ_flip of 6.4% (Claude-Haiku), 9.1% (DeepSeek), 6.6% (Gemini). Judges are sensitive to discourse structure, not just propositional content.
- Gemini-Flash is fragile across all five transform types, with certified Δ_flip up to 26.6% when parse failures are imputed as flips.

**Principle 2 — Rubric-Threshold Invariance (Table 3):**
- All four models achieve near-perfect directional ratios (R_dir ≥ 0.99) on strict-to-lenient switching, with flip rates of 32–71%. This confirms judges genuinely read and respond to normative content.
- The critical diagnostic: judges respond with *comparable* magnitude to meaningful normative shifts (Principle 2 satisfied) AND to meaningless structural rewrites (Principle 1 violated). They cannot distinguish the two.

**Principle 3 — Ambiguity-Aware Calibration (Figure 2):**
- 53–72% of flips across models are explainable; 18–43% are unreasonable (clear items under certified-equivalent transforms).
- DeepSeek-V3.2 has the highest unreasonable rate (43%), driven by extreme T4 sensitivity on unambiguous items.
- Clear-only ablations confirm Δ_flip remains significant for all four models even when restricting to unambiguous cases.

**Judge Cards (Table 4):**
- GPT-5.4-mini: PIS = 0.70 (Moderate)
- Claude-Haiku-4.5: PIS = 0.47 (Fragile)
- DeepSeek-V3.2: PIS = 0.28 (Unreliable)
- Gemini-Flash: PIS = [0.03, 0.41] (Unreliable/Fragile, parse-failure bracket)
- Models appearing comparable on accuracy benchmarks differ by more than a factor of 20 on policy invariance.

**Cross-model findings:** Pairwise flip-pattern overlap (Jaccard index) is below 0.20 for all model pairs and below 0.05 for two pairs, suggesting model-specific rather than content-specific fragility. Irrelevant context injection (T6) causes 2.5–10.5% verdict flips, predominantly in the SAFE→UNSAFE direction (irrelevant metadata read as elevating strictness).

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** High. The paper cleanly identifies a gap that existing work did not close: prior rubric-sensitivity studies perturbed model outputs or prompts without isolating the *policy* as the variable. The three-principle framework and PIS are genuinely new conceptual contributions, not incremental.

**Rigor:** Strong. The jitter-correction methodology addresses a real confound (GPT-5.4-mini would appear most unstable without it). Pre-registration of the practical significance threshold (Δ_flip > 5%), use of multiple inference methods (bootstrap + GEE), and formal proofs of PIS monotonicity all indicate careful statistical practice. Human equivalence certification with unanimity across six deontic dimensions on 500 rewrite pairs is a meaningful quality bar.

**Reproducibility:** Good. Code and item IDs are released, prompt templates are specified, and the rewrite generator configuration is described. The main limitation is that the base policy is a single standardized six-criterion rubric; replication on deployment-scale rubrics with jurisdiction-specific carve-outs would require proportionally more annotation effort.

**Significance:** High for the agent-safety evaluation community. Agent benchmarks like ASSEBench and R-Judge are already being used for deployment decisions. Showing that 18–43% of verdict flips on these benchmarks are measurement artifacts — not reflective of agent behavior — directly challenges the validity of published safety scores.

**Red flags / caveats:**
1. Only four small, cost-optimized models are tested. The paper explicitly cannot determine whether frontier-scale evaluators (GPT-5, Claude-Opus, etc.) share these failure modes or are simply more expensive to run at this scale.
2. PIS weights (0.4, 0.3, 0.3) are author-chosen priors, not downstream-validated. The paper is transparent about this and reports per-principle breakdowns, but PIS comparisons across papers with different weight choices would not be directly comparable.
3. The strict-to-lenient switching test (Principle 2) uses a single policy pair. Flip rates of 32–71% look good directionally, but the magnitude comparison with Principle 1 (6–9%) is not on a common scale, which makes the qualitative claim "judges cannot tell the two apart" somewhat informal.
4. The study operates only at the whole-trajectory, binary SAFE/UNSAFE level. Step-level judging, abstention, and multilingual rubrics are acknowledged out-of-scope but are common in real deployments.

## Strengths

- Fills a specific, well-defined gap: no prior work tested rubric-side perturbations for agent safety judges at this systematic scale.
- Three-principle decomposition is modular — benchmarks can adopt whichever principle is most relevant to their deployment setting.
- Jitter-correction is methodologically important and not done in comparable prior work.
- Community deliverable (Judge Card + PIS formula + public code) has immediate practical utility.
- Cross-benchmark replication on both ASSEBench and R-Judge increases confidence that findings are not benchmark-specific artifacts.
- Ambiguity decomposition (Principle 3) prevents overcounting: the paper is careful to distinguish legitimate judge uncertainty on hard cases from pure measurement failure on clear cases.

## Limitations

- Single standardized base policy; findings may not generalize to complex multi-clause rubrics used in production.
- Only small/cost-optimized models tested; frontier-model behavior is unknown.
- PIS weights are heuristic, not empirically derived from downstream task performance.
- Binary verdict framing; does not cover step-level or calibrated-probability judges.
- Per-pair human certification scales linearly with policy length, making the protocol expensive for long rubrics.
- The paper does not propose a remedy (e.g., invariance-aware training) — it explicitly frames diagnosis and tooling as the contribution, leaving mitigation to future work.

## Relevance

本文直接针对以 LLM 作为代理安全裁判的评估可靠性问题，揭示了当前主流安全基准中裁判对策略表述改写的系统性敏感，对任何使用或开发 LLM-as-a-Judge 管线（尤其是 agent 安全评估场景）的研究者均具有高度参考价值；其提出的 PIS 指标和 Judge Card 报告协议可直接应用于现有基准的可靠性审计。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Do frontier-scale models (GPT-5, Claude-Opus-class) exhibit the same T4 exception-placement fragility, or is this a capacity effect that disappears at sufficient scale?
2. Can invariance-aware fine-tuning (e.g., training with certified-equivalent pairs as contrastive negatives) improve PIS without sacrificing accuracy?
3. Does ensemble judging across semantically equivalent rubric variants reduce unreasonable flip rates below any individual judge's U_rate, as the theoretical lower bound in Appendix B.7 suggests?
4. How does policy invariance interact with multi-step judging? Does decomposing a trajectory into per-step verdicts before aggregating improve or degrade invariance properties?
5. The paper finds that judges read irrelevant metadata (version numbers, audit timestamps) as elevating strictness. What is the mechanism — is this a formatting/length artifact or genuine semantic misinterpretation of provenance signals?
6. PIS weights were set by author prior. Is there a downstream task (e.g., correlation with human safety assessment errors) that could be used to calibrate them empirically?
