The Granularity Axis: A Micro-to-Macro Latent Direction for Social Roles in Language Models

Chonghan Qin, Xiachong Feng, Ziyun Song, Xiaocheng Feng, Jing Xiong, Lingpeng Kong

arXiv: 2605.06196v1

Date: 2026-05-07

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06196

一句话总结： 本文发现大语言模型内部存在一条"粒度轴"——一个连续的从微观（个体）到宏观（机构/国家）社会角色的潜在方向，该方向与Qwen3-8B隐层空间第一主成分的余弦相似度达0.972，解释52.6%的角色表征方差，且通过激活引导可因果地控制模型输出的社会尺度。
TL;DR: This paper identifies and validates a single linear direction — the "Granularity Axis" — in the hidden-state activations of instruction-tuned LLMs (Qwen3-8B and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct) that organizes prompted social roles from micro-level (individual, worried parent) to macro-level (institutional, central bank governor, UN ambassador) along a continuous, ordered continuum. The axis is the dominant geometric axis of the role representation space (PC1 alignment cosine 0.972 in Qwen3-8B, explaining 52.6% of variance), generalizes across layers and prompt variants, transfers across model families, and is causally active: steering along it shifts response granularity in the predicted direction. If you work on LLM social simulation validity, persona-based agents, or representation engineering, this paper is worth reading; if you primarily care about large-scale model alignment, the findings are interesting but limited to 8B-scale models with only partial behavioral control.

核心贡献

  • 发现并定义"粒度轴"：通过对比宏观与微观角色的平均隐层表征，构建一条对比式潜在方向，该方向在Qwen3-8B中与第一主成分余弦相似度达0.972，解释角色表征空间52.6%的方差。
  • 建立五层次社会角色分类体系：构建包含75个社会角色的有序集合，横跨个体（Individual）、群体/社区（Group/Community）、组织（Organization/Meso）、机构（Institution/Systemic）、国家/超级行为体（Nation/Super-Actor）五个粒度层次，并收集91,200条角色条件化回复。
  • 验证轴的鲁棒性与跨模型迁移性：在层次、提示词变体、问题集划分、评分过滤等多项消融条件下结构稳定，并迁移至Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct。
  • 证明因果可控性：通过激活引导干预，正向引导使Llama在五分量表上平均宏观得分从2.00提升至3.17；人类标注者盲测验证了方向的一致性（Llama微观提示组配对率达0.861）。
  • 分析"粒度混淆"风险：揭示多智能体模拟中不同角色因共享相似的隐层表征而导致社会尺度坍缩的潜在失效模式。

Key Contributions

  • Discovery and formalization of the Granularity Axis: A contrast-based latent direction defined as the difference between mean macro-role and mean micro-role hidden states, achieving cosine 0.972 with PC1 of the role representation space in Qwen3-8B and accounting for 52.6% of its variance.
  • Ordered 75-role social taxonomy: Five granularity levels — Individual (Micro), Group/Community, Organization (Meso), Institution (Systemic), Nation/Super-Actor (Macro) — providing a grounded, sociology-motivated ordering for representational analysis.
  • Large-scale role-conditioned dataset: 91,200 responses generated by prompting Qwen3-8B and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct across 75 roles × 5 prompt variants × 240 shared extraction questions.
  • Robustness and cross-model transfer: Monotonic level ordering holds across Layers 8–35 (Qwen) and 6–31 (Llama), across alternative endpoint definitions (cosine ≥ 0.93 with PC1 in every variant), and across prompt templates including identity-only variants without explicit granularity labels.
  • Causal behavioral probe via activation steering: Steering coefficients {−4, 0, +4} at Layer 18 shift response granularity in the predicted direction; human annotators (n=72, three graduate-level raters) corroborate the direction with pairwise rates above chance in all four model/prompt conditions.

Methodology

Role Construction and Response Collection (Stage 1–2): The authors construct an ordered set of 75 social roles spanning five granularity levels (15 roles per level), drawing on sociological micro–macro theory (Coleman, Schelling, Granovetter). Each role is prompted with 5 template variants (direct identity, explicit role-play, worldview/priority, first-person/time-horizon, authenticity/practical-constraints) combined with 240 shared general extraction questions from the prior "Assistant Axis" work, yielding 90,000 role-conditioned responses plus 1,200 default-assistant baselines (91,200 total). LLM-as-Judge (gpt-5.4-mini, with Gemini as robustness check) optionally scores role adherence on a 0–3 scale for score-filtered ablations.

Role-Level Representations (Stage 3): For each generated response, hidden-state activations are extracted at every layer and mean-pooled over assistant-turn tokens to produce a response vector. These are then averaged over all (prompt variant, question) pairs for each role to obtain a single role-level vector per layer.

Granularity Axis Definition (Stage 4): Inspired by the contrast-based construction of the Assistant Axis (Lu et al., 2024), the Granularity Axis at layer ℓ is defined as:

g^(ℓ) = mean(v_u^(ℓ) for u ∈ U_macro) − mean(v_u^(ℓ) for u ∈ U_micro)

where U_macro = {roles at levels 4–5} and U_micro = {roles at levels 1–2}. Geometric validation stacks all 75 role vectors, centers them, applies PCA, and checks whether g^(ℓ) aligns with the first principal component (cosine similarity) and whether role projections onto g^(ℓ) increase monotonically with granularity level (Spearman/Pearson correlations). Layer 18 is selected as the target layer based on stable middle-layer performance.

Causal Probe via Activation Steering (Stage 5): During generation, the axis vector is added to each generated token's hidden state at an intervention layer ℓ: h̃_t^(ℓ) ← h_t^(ℓ) + α·g^(ℓ), with α ∈ {−4, 0, +4}. Steering is evaluated on two prompt sets: generic (40 prompts) and micro-targeted (12 prompts designed to elicit genuinely local responses where Qwen's macro-saturated baseline leaves room for movement).

Results & Highlights

Representation results (Table 1):

  • Qwen3-8B: Granularity Axis aligns with PC1 at cosine 0.9720, accounts for 52.57% of role-space variance; Spearman ρ = 0.9472, Pearson r = 0.9414.
  • Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct: cosine 0.9596 with PC1, 42.46% variance; Spearman ρ = 0.9459, Pearson r = 0.9373.
  • Role projections rise monotonically L1→L5 in both models (Table 2); there is saturation between L4 and L5 at a ~7× scale gap between models, suggesting LLMs collapse the two most macro levels into one representational region.
  • The default assistant sits near L3 in Qwen3-8B and near L4 in Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct.

Steering results (Table 3):

  • Qwen3-8B generic prompts: baseline already saturated at macro end (4.9/5); steering effect small (Δ_{+4} = +0.075). Micro-targeted prompts: Δ_{+4} = +0.500, with zero degeneration.
  • Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct micro-targeted prompts: strong upward shift (2.00 → 3.17, Δ_{+4} = +1.167); but negative steering (α = −4) on generic prompts induces 42.5% degeneration, indicating unstable control at the micro end.
  • Human evaluation (Table 5): Pairwise rates (α=+4 judged as more macro than α=−4) range from 0.639 [Qwen generic] to 0.903 [Llama generic]; inter-rater Krippendorff's α: 0.61–0.78.
  • Direction specificity: The Assistant Axis and random directions do not reproduce the micro–macro shift, confirming the effect is specific to the Granularity Axis.

Robustness: Stable ordering persists across Layers 8–35 (Qwen) and 6–31 (Llama); alternative endpoint definitions maintain cosine ≥ 0.93 with PC1; prompt-template ablations including identity-only variants (no explicit granularity labels) remain monotonic.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: Moderately high. Applying contrast-based axis construction (already established for the Assistant Axis) to the new dimension of social granularity is a natural extension, but the sociologically-grounded 5-level taxonomy, the systematic validation pipeline, and the explicit tie to "granularity confusion" in multi-agent simulation are genuine contributions. The finding that granularity is the dominant geometric axis (PC1, not a minor subspace) is non-trivial and empirically compelling.

Rigor: Generally solid. The authors distinguish representation-level validation from behavioral control, correctly noting that behavioral steering is a partial causal probe rather than clean control. The multiple ablations (layer sweeps, endpoint variants, prompt templates, score filtering, domain/family controls) are thorough. However, the saturation problem in Qwen (baseline macro ceiling) limits behavioral claims for that model, and the Llama degeneration rate (42.5% for α=−4 on generic prompts) is a meaningful instability that the authors acknowledge but do not fully resolve.

Reproducibility: Good. The pipeline is algorithmically specified (Algorithm 1), the role taxonomy and prompt templates are in appendices, question sets are drawn from a prior public dataset, and code/data are released. Main concern: replication requires substantial compute (91,200 LLM generations + LLM-as-Judge scoring), and the LLM judge (gpt-5.4-mini) may not be reproducible over time as model versions change.

Significance: The framing around "granularity confusion" in multi-agent simulation is practically important: if agents assigned to institutionally distinct roles collapse to the same representational region, multi-perspective simulations are invalid by construction. The steering application also has immediate use for controlling social scale in deployment contexts.

Red flags / limitations to note:

  • Results are limited to two 8B instruction-tuned models; generalization to larger models or base models is untested.
  • The L4–L5 saturation region (both models collapse the two most macro levels) is underexplained and may indicate that the 5-level taxonomy exceeds the model's representational resolution at the top.
  • Behavioral control is asymmetric (macro push more reliable than micro push, and model-dependent); the authors call this "partial causal probe" rather than "control," which is honest but limits practical applications.
  • The contrast axis is constructed from endpoints and validated on intermediate points — this is the right procedure — but the choice of which roles constitute the macro/micro endpoints is judgmental and could be a source of confounding.
  • LLM-as-Judge scoring introduces its own biases and may reward surface-level stylistic features (policy language, stakeholder lists) rather than genuine perspective shifts.

---

Strengths

  • Clean, layered validation strategy that distinguishes geometric structure from causal behavioral effects — an important methodological contribution to the representation engineering literature.
  • Sociology-grounded role taxonomy provides principled ordering rather than arbitrary role lists, giving the axis a theoretical anchor beyond model introspection.
  • The finding that social granularity is the dominant axis of role representation space (not just one of many) is surprising and shifts the framing from "roles differ in many ways" to "there is a single organizing scale dimension."
  • Honest acknowledgment that representation-level robustness does not guarantee behavioral control, and that model default placement mediates steering visibility.
  • Comprehensive appendix material (ablations C.1–C.8) and human evaluation with calibration study raise the evidentiary standard above typical interpretability preprints.

---

Limitations

  • Only two 8B-scale models tested; no frontier models (GPT-4-class, Claude Opus-class), no base (non-instruction-tuned) models, and no models outside the Qwen/Llama families.
  • Behavioral steering is unreliable for Llama at negative coefficients (degeneration) and near-invisible for Qwen on generic prompts (macro saturation ceiling), constraining practical utility.
  • The 75-role taxonomy is manually curated and English-centric; cultural and linguistic generalization is unaddressed.
  • The axis is one-dimensional by construction; the paper acknowledges that correlated dimensions (authority, time horizon, formality) may be collapsed into the single direction, and residual structure is not analyzed.
  • Role holdout is "slightly fragile in Qwen" (Appendix C.3), suggesting some role-specific memorization or overfitting to the taxonomy.
  • LLM judge reliability is validated only against three human raters; Krippendorff's α values (0.49–0.78 across settings) indicate moderate rather than strong inter-rater agreement on some dimensions.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does the Granularity Axis persist in frontier-scale models (70B+, or GPT-4/Claude-class), and does the L4–L5 saturation pattern resolve at larger parameter counts?
  2. Can the axis be decomposed into orthogonal sub-dimensions (e.g., institutional authority vs. temporal horizon vs. stakeholder breadth), and is social granularity best modeled as a 1D continuum or a low-dimensional subspace?
  3. How does the axis interact with topic domain? The "high-stakes domain" fragility noted in Appendix C.8 suggests domain-granularity confounding — is there a domain-conditional granularity axis?
  4. Does steering along the axis genuinely shift the model's reasoning structure (e.g., stakeholder framing, time horizon, evidence type), or does it primarily alter surface lexical features (policy vocabulary, institutional jargon)?
  5. Can the contrast-and-project pipeline generalize to other graded social dimensions the authors mention (formality, risk aversion, time horizon) using the same 75-role dataset, and do those axes correlate with the Granularity Axis?
  6. What are the downstream effects on multi-agent simulation validity? Can granularity confusion be detected and corrected at the axis level before generation, rather than post-hoc?
