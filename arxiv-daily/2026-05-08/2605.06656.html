Why Global LLM Leaderboards Are Misleading: Small Portfolios for Heterogeneous Supervised ML

arXiv: 2605.06656

Date: 2026-05-08

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06656

一句话总结： 通过分析 Arena 平台上 89K 条跨 116 种语言的人类偏好投票，本文证明全局 Bradley-Terry 排行榜在个体投票层面几乎无预测能力（仅 10.3% 投票以 ≥70% 置信度被正确预测），并提出 (λ,ν)-portfolio 框架——用最小集合覆盖算法（贪心/MIP）从 389 个子群排名中选出 5 个 BT 排名，即可覆盖 96% 的用户偏好，远超全局排名的 21%。
TL;DR: Global LLM leaderboards based on Bradley-Terry ranking over pairwise human votes are statistically meaningless at the individual level: nearly two-thirds of decisive votes cancel each other out, and even the top-50 models are statistically indistinguishable (pairwise win probability ≤ 0.53). The apparent noise is actually structured heterogeneity driven by language — votes stratified by language family show two orders-of-magnitude higher ELO score spread and far more coherent preferences. To address this in any supervised ML setting, the authors introduce (λ,ν)-portfolios: small collections of models guaranteed to λ-cover at least ν fraction of users, formulated as a set-cover problem and solved via greedy or MIP algorithms. On Arena data, 5 BT rankings suffice to cover 96% of votes vs. 21% for the global ranking; 6 LLMs chosen by portfolio cover twice as many votes as the top-6 from the global ranking. The framework also applies to fairness-aware classification on COMPAS, where portfolios of 4 models reveal systematic blind spots for young, first-time female offenders missed by all global models.

核心贡献

  • 揭示全局排行榜的欺骗性：通过分析 Arena 平台 ~89K 条决定性投票，证明全局 Bradley-Terry 排名仅能对 10.3% 的投票以 ≥70% 的置信度预测获胜方；前 50 名模型的两两胜率均不超过 0.53，在统计上无法区分。
  • 量化语言异质性：将投票按语言或语族分组后，BT 分数的极差（spread）增大约 100 倍（中位数从全局的约 283 上升到语言 × 任务的约 2,804），说明"全局噪声"实为多个内部一致、相互冲突的子群叠加。
  • 提出 (λ,ν)-portfolio 形式化框架：将"用最少模型覆盖最多用户（在误差上界 λ 内）"形式化为 Partial Set Cover 问题，并利用 VC 维度给出有理论保证的算法（贪心近似比 O(log νn) 及基于 MIP 的精确解）。
  • Arena 实证验证：从 389 个子群 BT 排名中，贪心算法选出 5 个排名即覆盖 96% 投票（λ=0.35），远超全局排名的 21%；组合成 LLM 集合后，6 个 LLM 的 portfolio 覆盖的投票数是全局 top-6 的约两倍。
  • COMPAS 扩展验证：在司法再犯预测任务上，4 个公平性正则化模型的 portfolio 在 λ=0.45 时达到 90% 覆盖，并精确定位出被所有模型遗漏的盲点群体（年轻、前科少、女性被告）。

Key Contributions

  • Empirical debunking of global LLM leaderboards: On ~89K decisive Arena votes, the global BT ranking correctly predicts the winner with ≥70% confidence for only 10.3% of votes. The top 50 models are statistically indistinguishable (pairwise win probability ≤ 0.53).
  • Language as the dominant heterogeneity axis: Stratifying votes by language or language family raises BT score spread by ~100x (median from ~283 global to ~2,804 for language × task subgroups), revealing that the global model hedges across conflicting subpopulations rather than representing any of them well.
  • Formal (λ,ν)-portfolio framework for supervised ML: A portfolio is a small set of models such that at least ν fraction of data points are λ-covered (prediction error ≤ λ) by at least one model. This reduces to Partial Set Cover, admitting an O(log νn)-approximation greedy algorithm and a MIP-based exact solver with VC-dimension-based size guarantees.
  • Strong empirical results on Arena: 5 BT rankings selected by the greedy algorithm achieve 96% coverage at λ=0.35, compared to 21% for the global ranking alone. A portfolio of 6 LLMs covers twice as many votes as taking the top-6 from the global leaderboard.
  • Fairness-auditing application on COMPAS: A portfolio of 4 fairness-regularized classifiers achieves 90% coverage at λ=0.45 and surfaces a blind-spot subpopulation (young females, few priors, repeat offenders) missed by every model including the global optimum.

Methodology

Data. The primary dataset is the publicly available Arena human preference dataset from HuggingFace (~89,193 decisive votes, excluding "both bad" and treating ties as half-wins, across 52 LLMs and 116 languages). Metadata includes vote timestamp, prompt/response language, and task category (10 types). The secondary dataset is COMPAS recidivism data used to predict 2-year re-offense probability.

Diagnosing global leaderboard failure.

The authors fit a single global Bradley-Terry (BT) model using inverse-probability-weighted (IPW) log-likelihood (to correct for non-uniform pair sampling). They then stratify votes into 389 subgroup BT rankings across five partition types: Random, Hour, Task, Language, Language Family, and Language × Task. For each subgroup ranking, they evaluate (1) BT score spread (max − min, Elo-scaled), and (2) in-group predictive accuracy (average probability assigned to the observed winner). Language and language-family rankings achieve two orders of magnitude higher score spread than global or task-only rankings, and substantially lower log-loss.

(λ,ν)-Portfolio formulation.

Given n data points and a class H of models, a model h λ-covers point x_i if |h(x_i) − y_i| ≤ λ. A (λ,ν)-portfolio is a subset P ⊆ H such that at least νn points are λ-covered by some model in P. Three algorithms:

  1. Greedy: Iteratively add the model covering the most currently uncovered points; provably O(log νn)-approximation of optimal portfolio size (from Slavík 1997).
  2. MIP: Integer program minimizing portfolio size subject to coverage constraints; relaxed to LP with continuous threshold rounding; more compact portfolios in practice though higher runtime.
  3. VC-dimension bound (Theorem 2): For regression model class H with VC-dimension d, an efficient algorithm returns a (λ,1)-portfolio of size O(dk · ln(dk)), giving near-optimal size guarantees for linear models.

Mapping from BT rankings to LLM scores. For each vote i and ranking r, the BT ranking's accuracy p(i,r) is computed as the probability that the winning LLM beats the losing LLM under ranking r. A Bayesian aggregation over rankings then assigns each LLM ℓ a score q(i,ℓ) representing the probability that ℓ is at least as preferred as the actual winner for vote i. LLM portfolio coverage is then defined using this extrapolated preference score.

COMPAS setup. 61 fairness-regularized logistic regression models are trained, varying the equalized-odds penalty weight μ from 0 to 300 (in steps of 10) across sex-based (30 models) and race-based (30 models) protected groups, plus one unregularized baseline. Portfolio coverage is evaluated at the individual level.

Results & Highlights

  • Global BT ranking covers only ~11% of votes with >70% confidence on Arena (predicts winner correctly at λ<0.30 for about 11% of votes), while a 5-ranking portfolio covers 96% at λ=0.35.
  • Top-50 Arena models are statistically indistinguishable: pairwise win probabilities are at most 0.53 within the top 50, as of May 2026 (Elo gap of only ~50 points among top 50 models).
  • 72.28% of decisive non-tied votes are "cancelled" — there exists an opposing decisive vote for the same model pair with the opposite preference.
  • Language-stratified rankings dramatically outperform: language-family BT rankings achieve median Elo spread of 1,134 vs. 283 global; language × task rankings achieve 2,804. In-group predictive probability (average winner probability) is consistently higher for language/family rankings vs. global.
  • Portfolio of 5 BT rankings vs. global: at λ=0.35 and ν=0.95 target, greedy selects 5 rankings (including language-task subgroups like Arabic×code, Thai×domain_knowledge, Dutch×creativity) achieving >96% coverage vs. ~21% for the single global ranking.
  • Portfolio of 6 LLMs covers ~2× more votes than top-6 global: cumulative coverage at λ=0.20 for k=10 LLMs is ~73% (MIP portfolio) vs. ~34% (global ordering). The portfolio selections differ substantially from global top models (e.g., DeepSeek-R1, GPT-4.1-mini appear early in greedy portfolio despite not being globally top-ranked).
  • COMPAS results: A greedy portfolio of 4 models achieves 90% coverage at λ=0.45. The uncovered 16.44% population (at λ=0.4) consists predominantly of young women (under 45) with few or zero prior offenses who are all repeat offenders — a region of feature space not captured by any regularized model, suggesting a systematic data quality issue.
  • FPR reduction: COMPAS portfolios achieve substantially lower false positive rates overall (0.000 at λ=0.5 vs. 0.216 for global) across all race and sex subgroups, with the notable exception of a slight FPR increase for Male Caucasian at λ=0.8.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The central observation — that language drives heterogeneity in LLM preference data — is genuinely important and documented rigorously. The (λ,ν)-portfolio formalization is clean and the connection to set cover / VC dimension is non-trivial. However, the broader insight that global aggregation obscures subgroup preferences is not entirely new (Simpson's paradox in ML is well-known), and applying set-cover to model selection has precedents. The specific context of LLM leaderboards and the language-heterogeneity finding lend sufficient novelty.

Rigor: The statistical analysis is careful — using IPW to correct sampling bias, providing confidence bands, and reporting results across multiple λ thresholds. Theoretical guarantees (Theorems 1–2, Corollary 1) are properly attributed and contextualized. One limitation is that subgroup BT rankings are evaluated on their own votes rather than held-out data, which inflates their apparent advantage (in-group evaluation). This is acknowledged but not fully corrected for.

Reproducibility: Data sources are public and referenced. Computations were performed on a standard Apple M5 MacBook Pro (32GB RAM), suggesting accessibility. Code is not explicitly released in the paper, which is a gap. The MIP formulation is described in sufficient detail for re-implementation.

Significance: The paper has significant practical implications for the LLM evaluation community — directly challenging the validity of the Arena leaderboard as a global metric. The portfolio framework is general enough to apply to any heterogeneous supervised ML setting, and the COMPAS case study demonstrates this breadth. The fairness-auditing application (blind-spot detection) is particularly timely for policymakers.

Red flags:

  • The paper cannot separate within-user preference variance from between-user heterogeneity because voter identities are not provided in the Arena dataset. The structured heterogeneity could partially reflect individual users who vote inconsistently rather than distinct subpopulations, though the language-based homogeneity within groups is suggestive of genuine subpopulation structure.
  • The claim that global rankings are "misleading" is strong; the global ranking may still be useful for its stated purpose (average-case performance across English-dominant use cases) — the paper does not fully account for the fact that Arena's user base is itself demographically skewed (English and European language-dominant).
  • Portfolio LLM coverage relies on a Bayesian extrapolation from BT rankings to individual LLM preference scores (Appendix A.6), introducing assumptions that are not directly validated.
  • COMPAS analysis uses a relatively small ensemble (61 models), and the blind-spot finding (young female recidivists) is based on only ~28 uncovered individuals, limiting statistical power.

Strengths

  • Rigorously quantifies a widely suspected but poorly documented problem with LLM leaderboards using a large, publicly available dataset.
  • The (λ,ν)-portfolio framework is principled, general, and comes with theoretical guarantees through well-known connections (Set Cover, VC dimension).
  • The finding that language is the dominant axis of heterogeneity is actionable and connects to sociolinguistics literature (Boroditsky 2001, Levinson 2003).
  • The COMPAS application demonstrates genuine cross-domain utility and surfaces an interpretable, policy-relevant blind spot.
  • Clear visualizations (ELO spread boxplots, coverage curves, pairwise win probability distributions, language-rank heatmaps) make the findings accessible.
  • Greedy algorithm is practical (runs on a laptop) and MIP provides tighter bounds for smaller instances.

Limitations

  • Voter identity is unavailable in Arena, making it impossible to distinguish individual inconsistency from subpopulation heterogeneity. Future work requiring richer voter metadata would strengthen the causal claims.
  • The portfolio framework assumes a predefined model class H (here, the 389 BT rankings or 52 LLMs). Constructing H requires extensive data; in low-resource language settings with fewer than 50 votes, no subgroup ranking is computed at all.
  • Certain language families lack cross-pair comparisons across all LLMs, preventing a meaningful total order; the BT model may assign scores without direct head-to-head evidence.
  • The paper focuses on detecting heterogeneity and selecting portfolios, but does not prescribe how to route users to the appropriate model in deployment — a key practical step that remains unaddressed.
  • The COMPAS blind-spot sample size is small (~28 uncovered females), and the observed feature patterns should be interpreted cautiously.
  • The MIP approach has exponential worst-case runtime and relies on LP relaxation + rounding; optimality is not guaranteed for large instances.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can the portfolio framework be extended to online or adaptive settings, where the portfolio is updated as new votes arrive — and how quickly does the greedy selection converge as data accumulates for low-resource languages?
  1. The paper shows language drives heterogeneity more than task. Is this because LLM training data is language-stratified, or because human aesthetic preferences genuinely differ by language/culture? Can controlled experiments (same prompt translated across languages) disentangle these?
  1. How should a system route a user to the correct model from a portfolio in deployment, given that language is observable but task type may not be known at query time?
  1. The paper finds that models like DeepSeek-R1 and GPT-4.1-mini appear in greedy portfolios despite lower global Elo — what are their specific coverage strengths (language × task cells), and does this reveal genuine multilingual capability gaps in top-ranked models?
  1. The blind-spot population in COMPAS (young females, few priors) are all labeled as recidivists. Is this a labeling artifact, a feature-space gap in the model class, or a genuine out-of-distribution subpopulation — and could data collection targeted at this group resolve the coverage failure?
  1. Can the VC-dimension bounds (Theorem 2, Corollary 1) be made tighter for specific architectures (e.g., neural network classifiers), and do they translate to meaningful portfolio size predictions for large LLM ensembles?
