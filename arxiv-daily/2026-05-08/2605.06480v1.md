> **一句话总结：** 本文将激活修补（activation patching）实验的输出转化为"补丁效果图"（patch-effect graphs），并应用图核方法（Weisfeiler-Lehman、谱核、图let核等）对 GPT-2 Small 上的间接宾语识别（IOI）任务进行跨切片分类，发现局部化边槽（edge-slot）特征在表面平衡控制下仍能实现完美分类精度（1.000），而全局图形状描述子则表现较差。

> **TL;DR:** This paper proposes converting activation-patching profiles from mechanistic interpretability experiments into structured graphs ("patch-effect graphs"), then applying classical graph kernels and SVM classifiers to compare circuits across tasks and corruptions. Tested on GPT-2 Small with the IOI benchmark, localized edge-slot features achieve perfect classification accuracy (1.000) even under surface-balanced controls that neutralize prompt-level confounds, while global shape descriptors (spectral: 0.500, graphlet: 0.760) fail. The key finding is that raw patch-effect tensors and surface cues set surprisingly strong baselines, meaning graph construction adds structured compression and auditability rather than novel discriminative signal per se. The framework is a methodological scaffold, not yet a validated general-purpose circuit-comparison tool — experiments cover only 2–4 IOI/induction slices on a single small model, and full causal (direct-influence) graph benchmarking remains future work.

## 核心贡献

- **图化激活修补数据**：提出将每个（任务族、损坏类型）切片（slice）的修补效果张量聚合为一张有向加权图，节点为模型组件（层 × token × 组件类型），边权编码因果关系——这是将机械可解释性分析重构为图机器学习问题的首个系统性框架。
- **三种图构建方法**：定义了直接影响图（DI，因果中介）、偏相关图（PC，去除共享上游因素）和共影响图（CI，皮尔逊相关，计算最廉价），并通过 IOI 实验对比了三者的分类效果及可扩展性。
- **图核分类流水线**：将 WL 子树核、谱核、图let核、固定布局边槽特征等多种图嵌入方法接入 SVM 分类器，系统比较了各方法在不同控制条件（边shuffle、权重shuffle、仅提示表面线索）下的精度。
- **基线校准原则**：明确指出历史 IOI 基准存在提示表面混淆因素，引入表面平衡控制（abba vs. second_subject_swap）后提示分类器精度从 1.000 降至 0.550，而图和原始修补效果分类器仍保持完美精度，从而厘清了图方法的真实增益来自"有结构的压缩与可审计性"而非更强的判别能力。
- **跨模型迁移验证**：将 CI 图协议应用于 DistilGPT-2，发现局部化边槽特征迁移良好（精度损失可忽略），而 WL 子树特征下降约 22 个百分点，表明子树级压缩依赖于模型规模。

## Key Contributions

- **Graph representation of patching data**: Each (task family, corruption type) slice of the interventional dataset is aggregated into one directed weighted graph, turning mechanistic interpretability into a graph machine-learning problem where learning happens across bootstrap graphs rather than within a single prompt.
- **Three edge-construction methods**: Direct-influence (DI) edges encode causal mediation via paired-node patching; partial-correlation (PC) edges remove shared upstream structure; co-influence (CI) edges use Pearson correlation and require only O(|V|) additional forward passes. The paper compares all three and validates DI via screened candidate paired-patching.
- **Graph-kernel SVM pipeline**: Five graph feature maps (WL subtree, spectral, graphlet, fixed-layout edge-slot variants, hashed fixed-layout) are combined with linear and RBF kernels for downstream SVM classification, providing an end-to-end benchmark pipeline.
- **Explicit baseline calibration**: The paper introduces surface-balanced and null (edge/weight shuffle) controls that expose a prompt-level confound in the historical IOI benchmark and define a calibration principle: circuit-level claims must be evaluated against raw-tensor, surface-cue, and learned-encoder controls.
- **Cross-model replication**: CI-graph localized edge-slot features transfer from GPT-2 Small to DistilGPT-2 with negligible accuracy drop, while WL subtree compression degrades substantially (~22 points), revealing model-size dependence of subtree-level signal.

## Methodology

The framework operates as a four-stage pipeline:

**Stage 1 — Activation Patching.** For a frozen transformer f0, each example consists of a clean prompt x_cln and a corrupted prompt x_crp designed to break a target behaviour. For every node u in the network (residual stream positions across layers × tokens), the patch effect E_u^(i) is the difference in the observable (logit of the target token) when u is restored to its clean-run value during the corrupted forward pass. Paired patching of ordered node pairs (u, v) additionally yields joint effects E_{u,v}^(i).

**Stage 2 — Graph Construction.** Each slice s = (task family, corruption type) aggregates N=100 paired examples into a single directed weighted graph G_s over a fixed node set V (layers × tokens × component types). Three edge-weight definitions are compared:
- *Direct Influence (DI)*: w_s^DI(u→v) = E[E_{u,v}^(i) − E_u^(i)], measuring how much v mediates u's causal effect. Cost: O(|V|^2) forward passes.
- *Partial Correlation (PC)*: w_s^PC(u→v) = −Σ_{uv}^{-1} / sqrt(Σ_{uu}^{-1} Σ_{vv}^{-1}), removing shared upstream causes. Cost: O(|V|^3) offline, no extra passes.
- *Co-Influence (CI)*: w_s^CI(u→v) = corr({E_u^(i)}, {E_v^(i)}), Pearson correlation of patch profiles. Cost: O(|V|) extra passes.

Graphs are sparsified by retaining only top-k=5 outgoing edges per node by absolute weight, and a layer-then-token ordering constraint enforces temporal information flow.

**Stage 3 — Graph Embedding.** Each graph G_s is embedded into a fixed-dimensional vector x_s via one of five feature maps: WL subtree histograms (dim ~10^3–10^4); spectral features from graph Laplacian eigenvalues (d=96); graphlet counts of directed 3-node motifs (d=38); fixed-layout edge-slot features encoding each (u,v) pair as a coordinate (weighted, binary, or signed, dim=14,280 for GPT-2 Small); and hashed fixed-layout features (d=1,024, scalable).

**Stage 4 — Kernel SVM Classification.** A kernel matrix K ∈ R^{S×S} is computed (linear K_{st}=x_s^T x_t or RBF K_{st}=exp(−γ||x_s−x_t||^2)) and an SVM classifies slices. Bootstrap resampling (32 graphs per slice, 75% resample, 32 test graphs from disjoint examples) provides variance estimates across seeds {7, 42, 123}.

The experimental testbed is GPT-2 Small (L=12, H=12, d=768) with residual-stream-only nodes (|V|=120 for T=10), using the IOI task family (name_swap, abba, second_subject_swap corruptions). DistilGPT-2 is used for cross-model replication (L=6, |V|=66).

## Results & Highlights

**E1 — Patch-effect tensor validation:** Mean patch-effect heatmaps show structured, non-random signal concentrated in layers 5–10 for IOI corruptions, consistent with the known IOI circuit. All 10 candidate edges survive FDR correction (BH, α=0.05) in the screened DI pilot, with top edges concentrated in last-token residual stream at layers 9–11.

**E2 — Graph construction comparison:** CI and PC graphs are both strongly classifiable on IOI. PC with fixed edge-slot signs reaches 1.000 on both benchmarks; CI with WL+linear achieves 0.912 (historical) and 1.000 (surface-balanced). Screened DI validation shows CI/PC-ranked candidate edges have higher activation-influence scores (I=0.373/0.305) than random (0.122) or low-rank CI (0.102) candidates, supporting that ranked edges are causally active.

**E3 — Representation baselines (Table 2, main result):**
- Fixed layout signed (edge-slot): **1.000 ± 0.000** (linear kernel, d=14,280)
- WL subtree: 0.912 ± 0.032 (linear), 0.615 (RBF)
- GNN encoder + SVM: 0.609 (linear), 0.583 (RBF) — underperforms hand-crafted features
- Spectral: 0.500 (at chance)
- Prompt surface cues: 1.000 on historical benchmark, drops to 0.550 on surface-balanced benchmark
- Raw patch-effect tensor: 1.000 on both benchmarks
- Edge shuffle null control: ~0.505 (near chance), confirming edge-slot identity is the signal carrier

**Surface-balanced control (Table 3):** Under abba vs. second_subject_swap, prompt-only baseline collapses to 0.550 while graph and raw patch-effect classifiers remain at 1.000, cleanly separating graph signal from prompt artefacts.

**Cross-model (Table 4):** Localized edge-slot features transfer to DistilGPT-2 with negligible drop; WL subtree drops from 0.912 to 0.688 (~22 points), indicating model-size sensitivity of subtree compression.

**Multi-slice S=4 pilot (Table 5):** Localized edge-slot features achieve 0.750 in both IOI/Ind and IOI/GT/Ind suites against a 0.25 chance baseline, confirming protocol viability beyond binary IOI. Surface-cue baseline jumps to 0.763 in the diverse suite, showing multi-task results must be read alongside surface controls.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** The core idea — treating activation-patching slices as graph objects and applying graph kernels for circuit comparison — is genuinely novel and well-motivated. No prior work in mechanistic interpretability has explicitly framed circuit comparison as graph-kernel classification. The three edge-construction methods and the surface-balanced control design are concrete methodological contributions.

**Rigor:** The paper is internally careful in several respects: explicit null controls (edge/weight shuffle), a surface-balanced benchmark to expose the historical confound, and FDR-corrected candidate validation. However, the scope is narrow — only GPT-2 Small and DistilGPT-2, only the IOI task family (with S=2 as the main benchmark), and N=100 examples per slice. The S=4 multi-slice pilot uses only 8 bootstrap graphs per slice, which the authors themselves flag as underpowered for kernel-geometry analysis. The hypothesis table (Table 6) is commendably honest: H1 (graphs carry classifiable signal) is "supported in controlled setting," H2 (kernel geometry shifts) "requires larger S," and H3 (edge construction matters) is only "screened-DI validated."

**Reproducibility:** Experiments use synthetic prompts from fixed templates and existing public GPT-2 weights — no private data. A public repository with implementation and scripts is *planned* but not yet released. The evaluation protocol is well-documented with seeds, bootstrap parameters, and a metric glossary (Table 11), making replication feasible once code is available.

**Significance:** The framework addresses a real gap: activation-patching produces high-dimensional, hard-to-compare datasets, and systematizing comparison is valuable for scaling mechanistic interpretability. The calibration insight (raw tensor and surface cues are strong baselines that must be beaten) is practically important for the field. However, the paper does not yet demonstrate that graph structure recovers novel causal information beyond what raw patch-effect tensors already contain — the strong raw-tensor baseline (1.000) means graph compression currently adds structure and auditability, not discriminative lift.

**Red flags:**
- The main benchmark (name_swap vs. abba) is a binary classification of 2 IOI slices, which is not a test of generalization — it is a within-IOI slice-discrimination check. The authors acknowledge this clearly, but it limits the strength of claims.
- The GNN encoder baseline underperforms hand-crafted features substantially (0.609 vs. 1.000), and with only S=2 slices and 32 bootstrap graphs per class, this comparison is methodologically underpowered — not a strong argument against learned encoders.
- Full DI graph benchmarking (the primary causal target construction) is deferred to future work due to O(|V|^2) cost, meaning the main causal motivation for the framework has not yet been fully evaluated.
- The claim that localized edge-slot features "transfer across model sizes" is based on only one smaller model (DistilGPT-2), which shares the same architectural family and training data as GPT-2 Small.

---

## Strengths

- Clearly identified and addressed a methodological gap in mechanistic interpretability: how to compare circuits systematically across tasks and corruptions.
- Introduces a surface-balanced control that exposes a genuine confound in the widely-used historical IOI benchmark — a useful critical contribution to the field.
- Honest and transparent about scope: hypothesis status table, explicit acknowledgment of which claims require more data, and a clear limitations section.
- Three edge-construction methods with different cost/causality tradeoffs provide a principled design space for practitioners.
- Scalability analysis is concrete: hashed fixed-layout features use fixed d=1,024 regardless of model size, making the pipeline applicable to larger transformers in principle.
- Planned MIT-licensed code release and fully synthetic, reproducible prompt generation.

---

## Limitations

- Experimental scope is narrow: one model family (GPT-2/DistilGPT-2), one primary task (IOI), and at most S=4 slices. Results cannot yet be generalized to claims about circuit comparison across diverse task families or larger models.
- Raw patch-effect tensors achieve perfect accuracy (1.000) in all tested settings, meaning graph construction has not yet been shown to provide discriminative advantage — only structured compression. The value proposition of the graph representation needs a setting where raw tensors are insufficient.
- Full DI graph benchmarking is deferred: the computationally tractable CI and PC graphs are proxies for the causal target, and their superiority to raw tensors has not been established in a setting requiring causal precision.
- The GNN encoder comparison is underpowered (S=2 slices, 32 bootstrap graphs per class) and should not be interpreted as a general finding about learned vs. hand-crafted graph features.
- Kernel geometry analysis (H2: whether kernel geometry shifts meaningfully across slices) requires more slices than currently available and remains an open question.
- No evaluation on models beyond the GPT-2 family or on tasks beyond IOI and simple induction, limiting external validity.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Do patch-effect graphs constructed from CI or PC edges recover any causal information that raw patch-effect tensors miss, in a setting specifically designed to require causal precision (e.g., distinguishing two circuits that produce identical marginal patch profiles)?
2. How does the framework scale to larger models (GPT-2 Medium/Large, Llama-class)? Do hashed fixed-layout features maintain discriminative power, and does the O(|V|^2) DI cost become tractable with attribution patching pre-screening?
3. Can the graph-kernel similarity geometry reveal when circuits are genuinely shared across tasks (e.g., induction vs. IOI) vs. superficially similar? This requires the multi-family, surface-balanced, large-S regime that the paper identifies but does not yet provide.
4. Why does the learned GNN encoder underperform so severely (0.609 vs. 1.000) compared to hand-crafted edge-slot features? Is this a fundamental limitation of the S=2 training regime, or does it reflect that node identity (layer × token × component) carries more information than neighborhood structure in this setting?
5. The partial-correlation graph (PC) underperforms CI under WL features on the historical benchmark but recovers on the surface-balanced benchmark — what does this instability reveal about the relationship between shared-upstream-cause removal and surface confounds in the IOI prompt distribution?
