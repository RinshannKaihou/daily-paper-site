Criticality and Saturation in Orthogonal Neural Networks

arXiv: 2605.06563

Date: 2026-05-08

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06563

一句话总结： 本文通过正交Weingarten演算和费曼图方法，推导出有限宽度正交初始化MLP中所有关键张量的逐层递归关系，从理论上严格证明了正交初始化导致网络统计量在大深度下饱和（而非发散）这一长期观察到的经验现象。
TL;DR: This paper derives, for the first time analytically, the complete set of layer-wise recursion relations governing the 1/n finite-width statistics of MLPs with orthogonal weight initialization. Using Weingarten calculus and a novel extension of Feynman diagram techniques to the orthogonal setting, the authors prove that criticality at infinite width implies criticality at finite width for orthogonal networks, and explain why the key network statistics saturate (stabilize) at large depth for orthogonal—but not Gaussian—initializations. The theoretical predictions match Monte Carlo simulations of tanh-MLPs to high precision. This closes a long-standing theoretical gap: prior work had observed the saturation empirically but had no analytical explanation for it.

Key Contributions

  • Complete set of recursion relations: Derives layer-wise recursion relations at order 1/n for all 14 fundamental tensors (V, D, F, A, B, P, Q, R, S, T, U, and the NTK mean correction) that govern network statistics and the first correction to training dynamics in orthogonally initialized MLPs. Also derives the V₆ recursion at order 1/n².
  • Feynman diagram extension to the orthogonal case: Introduces a novel "orthogonality charge" and a new propagator/vertex structure to extend the diagrammatic framework of prior work (Gaussian case) to orthogonal weights. Proves completeness of the Feynman rules to all orders in 1/n, enabling systematic computation at arbitrary order.
  • Theoretical proof of criticality and saturation: Proves that criticality (stability) of the infinite-width NNGP and NTK implies criticality of all finite-width orthogonal cumulants (Theorem 5.1). Derives large-depth analytic expansions showing saturation of network statistics, in contrast to the divergent/vanishing behavior of Gaussian-initialized networks.
  • Quantitative numerical validation: Iterates the recursion relations for single-input tanh-MLPs (n=50, L=10/30) and shows excellent quantitative agreement with Monte Carlo estimates from network ensembles of 600 samples.

Methodology

The paper operates within the effective field theory (EFT) framework for neural networks, which systematically computes network statistics as a power series in 1/n (the inverse width). The central objects are the cumulants of the preactivations and the neural tangent kernel (NTK), decomposed into a set of fundamental tensors that obey layer-wise recursion relations.

Orthogonal Weingarten calculus: Weights are drawn from the orthogonal group O(n) with Haar measure, scaled by √C_W. The key technical tool replacing Wick's theorem (used for Gaussian weights) is the orthogonal Weingarten calculus: the joint moments of orthogonal weight entries are expressed as a sum over pair partitions weighted by Weingarten functions W[τ,π]. The nonvanishing higher-order cumulants induced by this non-Gaussian structure are the central mechanism distinguishing orthogonal from Gaussian networks at finite width.

Recursion relations for NTK tensors: The authors derive recursion relations for the four NTK tensors D, F, A, B at order 1/n by substituting the preactivation and NTK forward recursions, expanding the resulting Weingarten functions to leading order in 1/n, and applying EFT techniques (path-integral methods developed in prior work for the Gaussian case). The tensors for higher NTK derivatives (P, Q, R, S, T, U) are shown to obey the same recursions as their Gaussian counterparts at order 1/n.

Feynman diagram framework: A novel set of Feynman rules is introduced specifically for the orthogonal case. The rules introduce: (1) an orthogonality charge superscript c on lines to track which labels carry orthogonal character; (2) modified cubic vertices encoding the Weingarten structure; (3) a square propagator representing the full expectation value; (4) a classification of diagrams by s-class (number of square propagators) with associated Möbius coefficients. The authors prove three completeness theorems showing this framework reproduces all recursion relations at order 1/n (Theorems 4.1 and 4.2) and to all orders in 1/n (Theorem 4.3). The method is demonstrated concretely by deriving the V₆ recursion at 1/n² in Appendix G.

Single-input solutions and large-depth expansions: The recursion relations are solved numerically by iteration in the single-input setting (tanh activation, criticality C_W = 1) with Gaussian expectations evaluated in Mathematica. Analytic large-depth expansions are obtained by substituting the ansatz O(ℓ) = ℓ^{-p_O} Σ c_{p,q}^O (log^p ℓ)/ℓ^q into the recursions.

Results & Highlights

  • Theorem 5.1 (Criticality): Criticality of the infinite-width NNGP and NTK implies criticality of all finite-width orthogonal preactivation and NTK cumulants. The proof exploits a bootstrap structure from the Feynman rules: orthogonality enters each recursion only through lower-rank tensors already assumed to be critical, so the stability analysis reduces to the Gaussian case at each order.
  • Saturation at large depth: The large-depth analytic expansions demonstrate explicitly that all normalized NTK tensors (D, F, A, B and their derivatives) saturate to finite nonzero constants at large depth ℓ for orthogonal networks at criticality (specifically for activation functions with vanishing fixed point, such as tanh). This contrasts with the power-law decay seen in Gaussian networks.
  • Numerical agreement: For n=50, L=10 tanh-MLPs at criticality (C_W=1), the theoretically predicted curves for normalized tensors (e.g., D̃, F̃, A, B) match Monte Carlo simulations to within standard errors across all layers. Large-depth analytic expansions capture the asymptotic regime ℓ > n precisely.
  • Gradient stability: Monte Carlo estimates of the NTK Θ^(ℓ) for n=50, L=30 tanh-MLPs across varying C_W confirm the critical case C_W=1 corresponds to stable NTK behavior, consistent with Theorem 5.1.
  • Feynman diagram efficiency: The diagrammatic calculation of the V₆ tensor at order 1/n² (Appendix G) is presented as a proof-of-concept illustration of the computational power of the framework, replacing what would require extremely laborious direct algebraic manipulation.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: High. The paper fills a concrete and acknowledged gap in the literature. The finite-width analysis of orthogonal MLPs previously lacked: (1) analytical recursion relations for the full set of NTK tensors, and (2) a theoretical proof of the saturation phenomenon. Both are provided here. The extension of Feynman diagrams to the orthogonal setting via Weingarten calculus is technically original.

Rigor: High. The main claims are stated as theorems with proofs (Theorems 4.1, 4.2, 4.3, 5.1). The proofs are detailed and contained within the paper and appendices. The connection between the abstract diagrammatic rules and concrete algebraic expressions is demonstrated explicitly in multiple appendices. However, the proofs are dense and some steps (e.g., the completeness proof in Appendix E.3) rely on structural arguments that require careful reading to verify.

Reproducibility: Moderate-to-high. The recursion relations are stated explicitly and the single-input solutions are computed in Mathematica with 600-sample Monte Carlo validation. The paper provides enough detail for expert readers to reproduce the analytical derivations. However, no code is released, and the Mathematica notebooks are not available, which limits immediate reproducibility for practitioners. The experiments are restricted to tanh activation and single-input settings.

Significance: Moderate-to-high for the theory of deep learning. This is primarily a mathematical physics contribution to understanding why orthogonal initializations improve training. It does not introduce a new training algorithm or architecture. The practical value is indirect: understanding the theoretical basis for orthogonal initialization stability may inform future design of initialization schemes. The Feynman diagram framework itself is a significant methodological contribution to the field of neural network theory.

Red flags:

  • The experimental scope is narrow (tanh activation only, single-input, MLP only). The authors acknowledge this limitation explicitly: extensions to other activation functions, multi-input settings, and architectures beyond MLPs are left to future work.
  • The paper is highly technical and dense; the gap between the abstract mathematical framework and practical implications for training is not bridged.
  • The analysis covers only the first correction (order 1/n) for most tensors, which may be insufficient for very narrow networks.
  • No comparison to actual training outcomes (e.g., whether the predicted saturation translates to measurable improvements in generalization or convergence speed).

Strengths

  • Closes a well-defined theoretical gap with rigorous proofs, not just heuristic arguments.
  • Feynman diagram approach provides a systematic, extendable framework that dramatically simplifies computation of higher-order corrections.
  • Completeness theorems (Theorem 4.3) establish that the framework is valid to all orders in 1/n, not just leading order.
  • Quantitative numerical validation with Monte Carlo ensembles provides strong empirical support for the theory.
  • Explicit derivation of large-depth analytic expansions provides interpretable insight into the saturation mechanism.
  • Clearly written for an audience familiar with EFT methods; proofs are organized in self-contained appendices.

---

Limitations

  • Results are derived for MLPs only; no convolutional or attention-based architectures are analyzed (though the authors note no conceptual obstacles to extension).
  • Experiments and large-depth expansions are restricted to tanh activation; the theoretical recursion relations are general but untested for ReLU or other common activations.
  • Explicit calculations are mostly at order 1/n; higher-order corrections (1/n²) are demonstrated only for V₆ as a proof-of-concept.
  • No code release; reproducibility requires significant expertise in Weingarten calculus and EFT methods.
  • The connection to downstream training performance (generalization, convergence rate) is not made explicit; the paper is purely about initialization statistics.
  • The framework assumes square weight matrices (all hidden layers have the same width n); the case of varying widths is not addressed.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can the Feynman diagram framework be extended to ReLU activations, where dynamical isometry is known to break down? How do the recursion relations change?
  2. Does the saturation of NTK tensors at large depth for orthogonal networks translate to measurable improvements in generalization or convergence speed in practical deep network training, and can this be quantified?
  3. The analysis assumes square weight matrices. How do the recursion relations change for rectangular matrices (varying width per layer), and does the saturation phenomenon persist?
  4. The framework is proven complete to all orders in 1/n. Can it be practically applied at order 1/n² for all tensors (not just V₆)? What is the computational cost?
  5. How does the finite-width theory developed here connect to the spectral properties of the Jacobian (dynamical isometry), which has been the focus of mean-field theory approaches to orthogonal initialization?
  6. Can the Weingarten-diagram framework be extended to unitary initializations (complex-valued networks) or symplectic initializations, which share the group-theoretic structure?
