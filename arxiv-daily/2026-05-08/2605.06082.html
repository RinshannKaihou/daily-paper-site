PoTAcc: A Pipeline for End-to-End Acceleration of Power-of-Two Quantized DNNs

["Rappy Saha", "Jude Haris", "Nicolas Bohm Agostini", "David Kaeli", "José Cano"]

arXiv: 2605.06082

一句话总结： PoTAcc 是首个开源的端到端流水线，通过将 PyTorch/TensorFlow 训练的2的幂次量化（PoT）DNN 模型无缝转换并部署到基于位移处理单元（shift-PE）的 FPGA 加速器上，在 PYNQ-Z2 平台实现相比纯 CPU 推理最高 3.6× 加速和 78% 能耗降低，精度损失不超过 1.9%。
TL;DR: PoTAcc is an open-source end-to-end pipeline that bridges the gap between Power-of-Two (PoT) quantized DNN training (PyTorch/TensorFlow) and efficient inference on custom FPGA hardware accelerators. It supports three PoT quantization schemes (QKeras, MSQ, APoT), automates model conversion and weight preprocessing for TFLite, and introduces shift-PE-based VSAC accelerators targeting PYNQ-Z2 and Kria FPGAs. On PYNQ-Z2, it achieves up to 3.6× speedup and 78.0% energy reduction over CPU-only execution with accuracy degradation under 2%. The pipeline is open-source at https://github.com/gicLAB/PoTAcc. If you work on edge DNN deployment and are specifically interested in PoT quantization for FPGA, this paper offers a concrete, reproducible system to build upon. If you want state-of-the-art accuracy benchmarks or are working on GPU-side PoT, this paper is not the right fit.

核心贡献

  • 开源端到端流水线： 提出 PoTAcc，首个同时覆盖训练（PyTorch/TensorFlow）、模型转换（Nobuco + TFLite Converter）、权重预处理和 FPGA 加速器部署的 PoT 量化 DNN 完整开源流水线。
  • 三种 PoT 方案的位移处理单元（Shift-PE）设计： 为 QKeras（单移位项）、MSQ（双移位项）和 APoT（加法多移位项）三种量化方法分别设计了高效的硬件 shift-PE，精确处理各方案的中间乘积位宽（ipw）差异。
  • VSAC（Vector-Shift Accumulator）加速器： 在现有 VMAC（乘法加速器）基础上，通过将乘法 PE 替换为位移 PE，设计了 VSAC_QKeras、VSAC_MSQ 和 VSAC_APoT 三款加速器，平均降低 LUT 利用率约 20%，并在两款 FPGA 平台（PYNQ-Z2、Kria）上部署验证。
  • 准确评估 PoT 模型的端到端性能： 在 CIFAR-10 和 ImageNet 上，覆盖 CNN（MobileNetV2、InceptionV1、ResNet18/20、EfficientNet-L）和 Transformer（DeiT-tiny/small、ViT-tiny/small）模型，系统评估精度、延迟和能耗，证明位移执行带来实质性能效益。
  • 权重预处理方法： 详细阐述了从 pot_float 到 pot_int 的三步转换（尺度校正、编码、压缩），确保训练精度在部署到 TFLite 推理框架时得以保留。

Key Contributions

  • Open-source end-to-end pipeline: PoTAcc is the first publicly available pipeline that integrates training, model conversion, weight preprocessing, and custom FPGA accelerator deployment for PoT-quantized DNNs, enabling reproducible end-to-end evaluation.
  • Shift-PE designs for three PoT quantization methods: Efficient hardware processing elements are designed and analyzed for QKeras (single-shift), MSQ (double-shift), and APoT (additive multi-shift), with careful handling of intermediate product bit-width (ipw) differences that affect area and power.
  • VSAC accelerator family: Three VSAC variants replace multiplication-PEs with shift-PEs within the VMAC_opt architecture, achieving on average a 20% reduction in LUT utilization compared to VMAC_opt while maintaining comparable accuracy.
  • Systematic cross-architecture evaluation: Results span CNNs and Vision Transformers on PYNQ-Z2 and Kria FPGAs, providing the community with concrete hardware metrics (latency, energy, resource utilization) for end-to-end PoT inference.
  • Accurate model conversion flow: A two-stage conversion (Nobuco for PyTorch→TensorFlow, then TFLite Converter) plus custom weight preprocessing preserves trained accuracy with average conversion degradation of only 0.1%.

Methodology

Overall Pipeline Architecture:

PoTAcc consists of three major stages: (1) model conversion, (2) weight preprocessing, and (3) shift-based accelerator execution, all integrated through the TFLite delegate system.

Model Conversion:

PyTorch models (e.g., APoT-quantized ResNet20) are converted to TensorFlow using the Nobuco converter, then to TFLite format using the native TFLite Converter. TensorFlow-native models (e.g., QKeras-based MobileNetV2) are converted directly. Custom quantization layers in PyTorch must be replaced with standard counterparts prior to conversion, and weights are re-quantized using the forward function of the quantization scheme.

Weight Preprocessing:

After TFLite conversion, weights in int8 format (qW) undergo three steps: (1) scale correction to map qW to the desired pot_int range, (2) encoding into 4-bit pot_int^e in sign-magnitude format, and (3) compression by packing two 4-bit weights into a single 8-bit byte, halving memory footprint. Bias scales are also adjusted accordingly.

Shift-PE Hardware Design:

Each PoT quantization method requires a distinct shift-PE. For QKeras (single-shift), 3 bits encode shift values (0-7); the sign bit is the MSB. For MSQ and APoT (double-shift), 2 bits encode the first PoT term and 1 bit (LSB) encodes either the second shift value or the zero special case η. The intermediate product bit-width (ipw) equals the sum of activation bits and maximum shift value: for 8-bit activations and QKeras shift max of 7, ipw = 15 bits. MSQ and APoT require additional shift operations, increasing ipw and LUT usage.

VSAC Accelerator (VMAC_opt with Shift-PEs):

The baseline VMAC_opt accelerator is built on the SECDA-TFLite framework and consists of an instruction handler, data handler, scheduler, and GEMM units each containing 64 PEs. VSAC replaces mult-PEs (16-bit intermediate products, 8-bit weights) with shift-PEs (variable ipw, 4-bit weights). Additional optimizations include: weight-copy optimization (transferring only required weight blocks per GEMM unit), DMA weight preloading, support for FC layers (per-layer scale instead of per-filter), and scalable GEMM instantiation (2/4/8/16 units). PYNQ-Z2 uses 4 GEMM units at 200 MHz; Kria uses 8 GEMM units at 250 MHz.

Evaluation:

Hardware evaluation targets PYNQ-Z2 (ARM Cortex-A9, 200 MHz FPGA) and Kria KV260 (ARM Cortex-A53 quad-core, 250 MHz FPGA). Energy is measured as (Power_inference − Power_idle) × Time / num_images using a USB power meter (PYNQ-Z2) or on-board sensors (Kria). Inference time is measured as the average of 100 runs on ImageNet synthetic DNN models.

Results & Highlights

Accuracy:

  • Average accuracy loss across the full PoTAcc pipeline is 0.1% (model conversion stage), well within acceptable bounds.
  • Worst-case accuracy degradation: MobileNetV2 (APoT) on ImageNet degrades by 1.9% after weight preprocessing, attributed to the TFLite Converter applying post-training int8 quantization to activations.
  • CIFAR-10: ResNet20 (APoT) drops only 0.0% after weight preprocessing; MobileNetV2 (QKeras) drops 0.4%.

Latency and Speedup (PYNQ-Z2):

  • CPU+VSAC achieves average 2.3× speedup over CPU-only across all DNNs.
  • Best case: ResNet18 reaches 3.6× speedup (VSAC_QKeras/MSQ/APoT all ≈3.6×, 273.2 ms vs 996.6 ms CPU).
  • VSAC achieves on average 1.1× speedup over VMAC_opt due to 4-bit weights enabling larger effective local weight buffer capacity.

Energy Reduction (PYNQ-Z2):

  • CPU+VSAC achieves average 52.6% energy reduction over CPU-only.
  • Best case: ResNet18 achieves 78.0% energy reduction with VSAC designs.
  • VSAC achieves average 4.9% energy reduction over VMAC_opt on PYNQ-Z2.

Kria Performance:

  • CPU+VSAC achieves average 1.0× speedup (no speedup over 4-core ARM A53 CPU) and 29.4% energy reduction.
  • Kria's more powerful CPU (4-core A53) outperforms the accelerator for shallow models like MobileNetV2 and EfficientNet-L.

Resource Utilization:

  • VSAC designs achieve on average 20% LUT reduction compared to VMAC_opt across both FPGA platforms.
  • DSP usage is minimal for VSAC (4.8%) vs VMAC (85.5%) since shift operations map to LUTs, not DSPs.
  • The increased LUT usage of VSAC_APoT vs VSAC_QKeras stems from the larger ipw required by APoT's additive shift operations.

Transformer Models:

  • DeiT-tiny and DeiT-small show modest 1.4-1.7× speedup on PYNQ-Z2, as transformer models include many non-CONV/FC layers processed on the CPU.

Critical Assessment

Novelty (Moderate):

The individual components — PoT quantization, TFLite delegate accelerators, shift-PE hardware — each have prior art. The contribution is primarily in integration: producing the first open-source end-to-end pipeline that connects training frameworks to custom FPGA accelerators for multiple PoT methods. This is genuinely useful engineering work, but the intellectual novelty of each component is incremental. The VSAC accelerator itself is a modification of the group's own prior VMAC_opt, with mult-PEs swapped for shift-PEs.

Rigor (Good):

The paper is methodologically careful. Accuracy is tracked at each pipeline stage. Inference latency is averaged over 100 runs. Energy measurement is standardized using idle-power subtraction. Resource utilization is compared fairly (LUTs used for both VSAC and VMAC when possible). The hardware analysis of ipw and its impact on area/power is thorough.

Reproducibility (Strong):

The code is publicly available at https://github.com/gicLAB/PoTAcc. The experimental setup (hardware platforms, training hyperparameters, quantization configurations) is well documented. Reproducibility is a clear priority of the paper.

Significance (Moderate-High for practitioners):

For practitioners deploying PoT-quantized DNNs on FPGAs, this is a directly usable resource. The open-source nature is a genuine contribution to the community. The 78% energy reduction and 3.6× speedup figures are compelling for edge inference scenarios. However, the absolute accuracy of the synthetic ImageNet models is not competitive (the paper acknowledges synthetic models do not reach state-of-the-art accuracy), limiting the applicability of the performance numbers to real deployment scenarios.

Red Flags:

  • The ImageNet experiments use synthetically generated PoT-quantized DNN models (not trained with standard ImageNet pipelines) because pre-trained PoT-quantized models for ImageNet are not publicly available. This means the reported latency/energy numbers, while internally consistent, are not benchmarked against a model with competitive classification accuracy.
  • Kria results show no speedup over the CPU in total inference time for several models, which weakens the claim of broad edge deployment benefits. The paper attributes this to depthwise convolution layers and activation dataflow overhead but does not resolve it.
  • The pipeline currently lacks support for depthwise convolution offloading to the accelerator, which is critical for modern efficient CNNs like MobileNetV2.
  • Only 4-bit PoT weight quantization is evaluated; the claim that the pipeline supports other bit-widths is stated but not demonstrated experimentally.

Strengths

  • First open-source, end-to-end pipeline for PoT-quantized DNN acceleration on FPGAs — directly usable and extensible.
  • Covers three distinct PoT quantization methods (QKeras, MSQ, APoT), providing a comparative framework that no prior work has offered.
  • Careful hardware analysis of shift-PE intermediate product bit-width (ipw) and its downstream effects on accumulator width, LUT usage, and power.
  • Integration within TFLite's delegate system makes the pipeline compatible with standard ML deployment workflows.
  • Strong accuracy preservation through the pipeline (≤1.9% worst-case degradation, 0.1% average at conversion stage).
  • Systematic evaluation across CNNs and Vision Transformers on two different FPGA platforms.

Limitations

  • No support for depthwise convolution layers on the accelerator, which is the bottleneck layer type in many efficient CNNs (MobileNetV2, EfficientNet).
  • ImageNet models are synthetic (not pre-trained with proper ImageNet training pipelines), so performance numbers cannot be directly compared to real deployment scenarios with competitive accuracy.
  • Kria board shows marginal or no speedup for several models due to the stronger quad-core CPU outpacing the accelerator for lighter workloads.
  • Activation quantization is fixed at 8-bit uniform (not PoT), which is a significant departure from pure PoT inference and may limit energy savings compared to full PoT quantization.
  • The pipeline relies on the SECDA-TFLite ecosystem; portability to other inference frameworks (e.g., ONNX Runtime, TVM) would require substantial re-engineering.
  • Only FPGA targets are considered; no GPU or ASIC baselines are provided for context.

Relevance

对于专注于边缘 AI 推理、神经网络量化和 FPGA 硬件加速的研究者而言，本文具有直接的参考价值：它不仅提供了端到端的开源工具链，也展示了如何系统性地评估不同 PoT 量化方案在实际硬件上的能效与延迟权衡，是构建低功耗 DNN 边缘推理系统的实用参考。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can depthwise convolution layers be efficiently mapped to shift-PE-based accelerators, and what would the shift-PE design look like for grouped convolutions?
  2. How does PoTAcc's energy efficiency compare to dedicated ASICs for PoT quantization, such as those designed by Xia et al. (UPoT [5])?
  3. Would applying PoT quantization to activations (not just weights) — using a dedicated PoT activation quantization scheme — further reduce energy consumption in the VSAC designs?
  4. The pipeline currently trains for only 20 epochs with synthetic ImageNet models. What is the accuracy-energy tradeoff when using properly trained, state-of-the-art PoT-quantized models (e.g., from recent works like CPoT or RMSMP)?
  5. How does the pipeline scale to larger Transformer models (e.g., BERT, larger ViTs), where FC layers dominate and PoT quantization for LLMs (ShiftAddLLM, PoTPTQ) is an active area?
  6. Is there a principled way to select among QKeras, MSQ, and APoT quantization for a given model architecture and FPGA resource budget, beyond exhaustive search?
