When and Why SignSGD Outperforms SGD: A Theoretical Study Based on ℓ₁-norm Lower Bounds

Hongyi Tao, Dingzhi Yu, Lijun Zhang

arXiv: 2605.06615

Date: 2026-05-08

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06615

一句话总结： 本文在 ℓ∞-光滑、坐标可分离噪声的几何框架下，首次给出 SignSGD 的紧下界，并通过与 SGD 紧下界的比较严格证明：当噪声稀疏时，SignSGD 的样本复杂度比 SGD 低 d 倍（d 为参数维度），同时将该框架推广到 Muon 优化器，给出其首个维度无关的下界，并在 GPT-2（124M）预训练实验中得到验证。
TL;DR: This paper gives the first tight (matching upper and lower) complexity bounds for SignSGD under ℓ∞-smoothness and a separable noise model, proves that SignSGD's sample complexity is provably d times lower than SGD's when gradient noise is sparse (as it is during LLM pretraining), extends the result to derive the first dimension-free lower bound for the Muon matrix optimizer, and validates all findings on a nanoGPT-124M pretraining experiment on C4. If you care about the theoretical foundations of sign-based and matrix-sign optimizers—or want a rigorous explanation for why Muon/SignSGD outperform AdamW in LLM training—this paper is directly relevant.

核心贡献

  • SignSGD 紧界（Tight Bounds）：在 ℓ∞-光滑、坐标可分离方差噪声的假设下，建立了 SignSGD 的匹配上下界（Theorems 1 & 2），收敛率为 Θ(√(L∞Δ/N) + (‖σ‖₁²L∞Δ/N)^(1/4))，这是已知首个不依赖维度 d 的 SignSGD 紧下界。
  • 可证明的维度优势：通过将 SignSGD 的上界与 SGD 的下界进行直接比较，严格证明当噪声向量 σ 稀疏时，SignSGD 的复杂度最多比 SGD 低 d 倍（Theorems 1 & 3 + density function φ(σ) 分析）。
  • Muon 的首个下界：将框架从向量扩展到矩阵域，利用几何等价性证明 Muon 的收敛率（核范数平稳性）与 SignSGD 完全对应，给出首个维度无关的 Muon 下界（Theorem 5），填补了已有工作的理论空白。
  • 理论与实践的桥接：通过跟踪 LLM 训练过程中梯度噪声稀疏度 φ(σ) 的演化，证明 LLM 预训练中的极稀疏噪声结构正是理论预测 SignSGD 显著优于 SGD 的条件，在 nanoGPT-124M 实验中得到验证。

Key Contributions

  • Tight bounds for SignSGD: Under ℓ∞-smoothness and a separable noise model, the paper establishes matching upper and lower complexity bounds for SignSGD (Theorems 1 and 2). The tight rate is Θ(√(L∞Δ/N) + (‖σ‖₁²L∞Δ/N)^(1/4)). This is the first known dimension-free tight lower bound for SignSGD.
  • Provable d-factor gain over SGD: By comparing SignSGD's upper bound against a new ℓ₁-norm lower bound for vanilla SGD (Theorem 3), the paper rigorously identifies when SignSGD beats SGD—specifically when the noise vector σ is sparse or highly heterogeneous—with the complexity gap reaching a factor of d in the worst case.
  • First lower bound for Muon: The framework is lifted to the matrix domain via a geometric equivalence between Muon's matrix sign updates and SignSGD's vector sign updates. This yields the first complexity lower bound for Muon (Theorem 5) measured under nuclear-norm stationarity, matching existing upper bounds and removing prior dimension dependences.
  • Empirical validation on LLM pretraining: Noise density φ(σ) = ‖σ‖₁² / (d‖σ‖₂²) is tracked during nanoGPT-124M training on C4, showing that LLM gradient noise becomes increasingly sparse (φ → 0.03), precisely the regime where the theory predicts maximal SignSGD advantage. CNN (ResNet-20 on CIFAR-10) shows the opposite—dense noise—correctly predicting SignSGD and SGD perform comparably there.

Methodology

The paper's theoretical contribution rests on four connected components:

1. Problem Geometry Shift

Rather than the standard ℓ₂ framework (ℓ₂-smoothness, ℓ₂-stationarity, bounded variance), the authors adopt:

  • ℓ∞-smoothness: |D_f(x,y)| ≤ (L∞/2)‖y−x‖²∞, which is the natural geometry for coordinate-wise sign updates.
  • ℓ₁-stationarity: convergence measured by E[min_t ‖∇f(xₜ)‖₁], aligning with what sign methods actually optimize.
  • Separable noise model: per-coordinate variance bounds E[|gₜ,ᵢ − ∇ᵢf(xₜ)|²|F_{t-1}] ≤ σᵢ², capturing highly imbalanced noise.

2. Tight Bounds for SignSGD (Section 3)

Upper bound (Theorem 1): Standard descent lemma argument with ℓ∞-smoothness, bounding the sign error via ‖·‖₁ norms and separable variance. Optimal batch size B = max{1, ‖σ‖₁²T/(ΔL∞)} and step size η = √(2Δ/L∞T) give the tight rate.

Lower bound (Theorem 2): A novel five-step construction: (i) Dimensional decomposition into d independent 1D problems; (ii) Construction of a 1D "resisting oracle" that maintains gradient magnitude ε everywhere; (iii) Adversarial bimodal noise oracle that induces stalls with probability proportional to σᵢ²; (iv) Dimensional lifting by adversarially allocating Δᵢ and Lᵢ subject to global constraints; (v) Matching to recover exact ‖σ‖₁ dependence.

3. Lower Bound for SGD (Section 4.1)

An ℓ₁-norm lower bound for vanilla SGD (Theorem 3) is established by exploiting the coordinate-wise lower bound framework of Jiang et al. [2025a] under the same ℓ∞-smooth geometry. A key construction uses highly imbalanced smoothness vectors to make SGD pay an extra factor of d in complexity.

4. Extension to Muon (Section 5)

The proof establishes a strict geometric and dynamic equivalence between matrix-domain Muon optimization and vector-domain SignSGD:

  • Matrix objective F(W) = Σ fᵢ((QWP⊤)ᵢᵢ) lifts 1D hard instances into the matrix domain.
  • The structured gradient Gₜ = Q⊤ diag(gₜ)P ensures the SVD of Gₜ satisfies that Newton–Schulz sign extraction collapses exactly to coordinate-wise sign, mapping Muon's Wₜ₊₁ = Wₜ − η msign(Gₜ) to SignSGD's xₜ₊₁ = xₜ − η sign(gₜ).
  • Geometric metrics map cleanly: spectral norm smoothness L∗ ↔ L∞, and nuclear norm of noise covariance ‖Σ‖∗ ↔ ‖σ‖₁.

Results & Highlights

  • Tight characterization of SignSGD: The complexity Θ(L∞Δ/ε² + ‖σ‖₁²L∞Δ/ε⁴) matches up to constants, with no dependence on dimension d. This is the first such result.
  • Dimension gap quantified: The density function φ(σ) = ‖σ‖₁²/(d‖σ‖₂²) ∈ [1/d, 1] captures the noise distribution. When φ(σ) ≈ 1/d (sparse noise, as in LLM training), SignSGD's complexity is d times lower than SGD's in both deterministic and stochastic terms.
  • Dimension-free Muon bounds: Theorem 4 (upper) and Theorem 5 (lower) for Muon have no dependence on matrix dimensions min{m,n}, correcting prior analyses that incurred dimension-dependent bounds.
  • nanoGPT-124M on C4: SignSGD achieves substantially lower training and validation loss than SGD throughout pretraining. The noise density φ(σ) is tracked to show it drops to ≈ 0.03 (very sparse) during LLM training, validating the theory. For ResNet-20 on CIFAR-10, φ stabilizes near 0.7 (dense), and the two optimizers perform comparably—consistent with theoretical predictions.
  • Controlled numerical experiments: On a 5000-dimensional quadratic with imbalanced smoothness (L₁ = 1000, Lᵢ = 1 for i ≥ 2), SignSGD clearly dominates. On a 100-dimensional quadratic with sparse noise concentrated on the first coordinate, SignSGD again dominates, confirming both theoretical regimes.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: High. The combination of tight (matching) bounds for SignSGD under ℓ∞ geometry and separable noise, a new lower bound for SGD in the same setting, and the first Muon lower bound are all genuine first results. The density function φ(σ) as a crisp summary statistic for when sign methods win is a useful conceptual contribution.

Rigor: High. The proofs are self-contained and complete, with careful construction of hard instances using resisting oracles and adversarial bimodal noise. The dimensional decomposition-and-lifting technique is cleanly executed. The five-step Muon equivalence proof is elegant.

Reproducibility: Good. The paper provides pseudocode for both algorithms, specifies exact hyperparameters in theorems, and links to a public GitHub repository. The nanoGPT experimental setup follows Karpathy's standard implementation.

Significance: High for the theory community and practitioner understanding. This paper resolves a longstanding open question: why do sign-based methods outperform SGD? The answer—ℓ∞ geometry + sparse noise—is crisp, measurable, and consistent with empirical observations in LLM pretraining.

Potential red flags:

  • The ℓ₂ vs ℓ∞ geometry gap is real, but the paper's framework requires that practitioners accept ℓ∞-smoothness and ℓ₁-stationarity as the "right" metrics. Whether these are the correct measures of practical interest (rather than ℓ₂-stationary points) is not deeply discussed.
  • The lower bound comparison is SignSGD upper bound vs SGD lower bound—not a tight two-sided comparison against a common problem class. The paper acknowledges this but it means the d-factor gap could be tighter in practice.
  • The nanoGPT experiment uses 124M parameters on C4, which is a relatively small-scale validation. It is consistent with theory but does not definitively establish that the sparse noise regime extends to production-scale LLMs (though the noise density analysis is suggestive).
  • Muon's batch size B = 1 assumption in the lower bound proof (matching the vector setting) is noted but the implications for practical Muon with B > 1 are not fully explored.

Strengths

  • First tight (matching) upper and lower bounds for SignSGD without explicit dimension dependence—a major open problem.
  • Clean theoretical framework that simultaneously explains SignSGD and Muon under the same geometry.
  • Elegant reduction from Muon to SignSGD that makes the matrix extension nearly automatic once the vector case is settled.
  • Empirical validation that directly measures the theoretically-predicted noise density metric φ(σ), connecting theory to practice more tightly than most theory papers.
  • Public code available.

---

Limitations

  • The analysis is restricted to non-convex smooth objectives with separable noise; extensions to non-smooth settings, federated settings, or with momentum are left to future work.
  • The lower bound gap (SignSGD upper vs SGD lower) is not a two-sided tight comparison for the same problem; in practice the actual gap could be smaller.
  • LLM validation is limited to nanoGPT-124M; behavior at scale (1B+ parameters) is an extrapolation.
  • The paper does not address adaptive variants such as Adam or AdaGrad in depth—it focuses specifically on the SignSGD/Muon vs vanilla SGD comparison.
  • The separable noise model, while reasonable for LLMs, may not hold for all architectures or domains.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can the tight bounds be extended to SignSGD with momentum (Signum/Lion), and does the same d-factor separation over SGD persist?
  2. Does the sparse noise regime (φ(σ) → 0) also characterize the advantage of AdamW over SGD in LLM pretraining, or is it specific to sign-based methods?
  3. The paper shows Muon's lower bound is dimension-free—does this extend to other matrix orthogonalization optimizers beyond Newton–Schulz approximations?
  4. What happens in the federated learning setting where communication constraints additionally favor sign-based methods—can the lower bounds be extended there?
  5. The noise density φ(σ) is shown to decrease during LLM training. Is there a causal mechanism, or is it a consequence of the learning dynamics? Could optimizer choice itself influence φ?
  6. Can the ℓ₁-norm lower bound framework be applied to other adaptive methods (e.g., RMSProp, AdaGrad) to give a unified picture of when coordinate-wise methods win?
