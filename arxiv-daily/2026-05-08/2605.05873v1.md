> **一句话总结：** 本文提出 CITE 算法，利用 e-process 交叉联合检验技术，在类别集合未知的情况下，对大语言模型自洽性采样过程中的众数答案进行任意时刻有效的统计认证，且停止时间复杂度不依赖于答案类别数量。

> **TL;DR:** CITE is a sequential algorithm for certifying that a prespecified LLM response label is the unique mode of the model's answer distribution. It combines three anytime-valid stochastic processes—a pairwise e-process against the observed runner-up, a lower confidence bound on the target probability, and an upper bound on unseen-category probability—into an intersection-union stopping rule that provably controls Type-I false-certification error at any prescribed level epsilon, even when the answer category set is infinite and unknown. The stopping time is shown to be minimax-optimal up to logarithmic factors and independent of category-set size. On LLM benchmarks (AIME 2026, FrontierScience-Olympiad, ER-REASON) with Qwen3-30B and gpt-oss-20b, CITE achieves high certification rates with mean stopping times of ~12–177 samples, outperforming the MMC baseline by up to 2x in diffuse-tail settings while maintaining strict Type-I control.

## 核心贡献

- **任意时刻有效的类别无关错误保证**：CITE 在任意数据驱动停止时刻下，均匀控制将目标标签错误认证为唯一众数的 I 类错误（虚警概率），且无需事先知道答案类别集合（Theorem 4.1）。
- **类别集合无关的停止时间**：在真实唯一众数假设下，CITE 的期望停止时间阶数仅依赖于众数概率 p_r 和众数间隙 δ，与类别数量 |A| 无关，打破了已有方法对类别数量的依赖（Theorem 4.3）。
- **匹配的极小极大下界**：证明了停止时间的信息论下界与 CITE 上界在主要量级下匹配（相差至多对数因子），说明算法在渐近意义下是最优的（Theorem 4.4）。
- **置信度加权扩展（W-CITE）**：将 CITE 推广至置信度加权投票场景，支持验证器分数或 logit 权重，理论保证结构与非加权版本平行（Section 5）。
- **实证验证**：在模拟实验和三个真实 LLM 基准上均验证了 CITE 对 I 类错误的严格控制，同时在扩散尾部分布场景中显著优于 MMC 基线。

## Key Contributions

- **Anytime-valid, category-agnostic Type-I error control**: CITE guarantees that the false-certification probability is bounded by any prespecified epsilon, uniformly over all stopping times, without requiring prior knowledge of the response category set (Theorem 4.1).
- **Category-size-free stopping time**: Under the unique-mode alternative, the expected stopping time is O((log(1/epsilon) + log(1/delta)) / delta^2 + (log(1/epsilon) + log(1/p_r)) / p_r), depending only on the modal gap delta and target probability p_r, not on |A| (Theorem 4.3). This contrasts with several baselines.
- **Matching minimax lower bound**: An information-theoretic lower bound is derived, showing CITE's stopping-time rate is minimax-optimal up to logarithmic factors (Theorem 4.4).
- **Weighted extension (W-CITE)**: Extends the framework to confidence-weighted observations (e.g., from a verifier or logit score), preserving the same anytime-valid and category-agnostic guarantees with the weighted modal gap replacing the unweighted one (Section 5).
- **Empirical validation on LLM benchmarks**: Evaluated on Qwen3-30B and gpt-oss-20b across AIME 2026, FrontierScience-Olympiad, and ER-REASON; CITE controls false certification to zero (consistent with theory) and outperforms MMC by up to 2x in certification rate on diffuse-tail problems.

## Methodology

CITE addresses the sequential certification problem: given a fixed target response label r, determine whether r is the unique mode of the LLM's (unknown) response distribution, stopping as early as possible while keeping Type-I error below epsilon at all times.

**Problem setup.** LLM responses X_1, X_2, ... are modeled as i.i.d. draws from an unknown categorical distribution P over a countably infinite set A. The null hypothesis H_{0,r} is that r is not the unique mode; the alternative H_{1,r} is that p_r strictly exceeds every other category's probability. A sequential procedure certifies r by stopping at the first time tau where the data favors H_{1,r}, and must satisfy sup_{P in H_{0,r}} Pr(tau < infinity) <= epsilon.

**Three anytime-valid components.** CITE combines three stochastic processes, each contributing an epsilon/3 error budget:

1. **Pairwise e-process (E_t)**: For each competitor a != r, a betting-based e-process E_t^{(r,a)}(lambda) = (1+lambda)^{N_t(r)} * (1-lambda)^{N_t(a)} is constructed. The parameter lambda is integrated out over a finite grid. CITE tracks the empirical runner-up (the most-seen non-target category) via a runner-up monotonicity property, keeping per-step cost O(1). E_t >= 3/epsilon signals that the target dominates its runner-up.

2. **Lower confidence bound on p_r (L_t)**: A mixture e-process M_t(q) bets on the hypothesis "p_r <= q" for each q in (0, p_hat_t(r)]. L_t is defined as the supremum q for which M_t(q) >= 3/epsilon, forming a time-uniform lower confidence bound that satisfies Pr(exists t: L_t > p_r) <= epsilon/3.

3. **Unseen category upper bound (U_t)**: Using the observation that an unseen category with mass u remains unseen after t draws with probability (1-u)^t, a per-category probability-weighted union bound yields U_t = min{u in (0,1]: u^{-1}(1-u)^t <= epsilon/3}. This decays to zero as t grows, eliminating the threat from any individual hidden competitor.

**Stopping rule.** CITE stops at tau = inf{t >= 1: E_t >= 3/epsilon AND L_t > U_t}. The conjunction ensures both (a) the target beats its current runner-up and (b) the lower confidence bound on p_r exceeds the upper bound on any unseen competitor's mass. Theorem 4.1 proves this controls Type-I error.

**W-CITE.** Each observation is a pair (X_i, W_i) with W_i in [0,1] (a confidence weight). The target weighted mass mu_r = E[W * 1{X=r}] replaces p_r, and all three components are adapted accordingly. Runner-up monotonicity fails under heterogeneous weights, so W-CITE checks all observed competitors, raising per-step cost to O(|A_t|) but preserving the same theoretical guarantees.

## Results & Highlights

**Simulations (five multinomial settings at epsilon = 0.05):**
- CITE and W-CITE achieve empirical false-certification rate of exactly zero in all settings and sample sizes, consistent with Theorem 4.1. Baselines KR and MMC show nonzero false-certification rates up to 0.002 and 0.014 respectively in near-tie and diffuse settings.
- In the concentrated Setting 2, CITE achieves 0.890 certification rate at N = 64 (mean stopping time ~36). In the more diffuse LLM-like Setting 5, CITE reaches 0.544 at N = 256 and 0.932 at N = 512 (mean stopping time ~234), while MMC stays at most 0.002 through N = 2048.

**LLM experiments (Qwen3-30B and gpt-oss-20b, three benchmarks):**
- On Qwen3-30B / ER-REASON (highly concentrated answers): CITE attains certification rate >= 0.98 at mean stopping time ~12 samples, while fixed-sample Bonferroni saturates at 0.111 for N >= 256.
- On FrontierScience-Olympiad (diffuse, ~250 distinct values per problem on gpt-oss-20b): CITE certification rate exceeds MMC by approximately 2x at N = 1024.
- On gpt-oss-20b / ER-REASON: CITE mean stopping time is ~128 samples.
- CITE is lower by at most 0.087 than the KR baseline (which is not valid under adaptive stopping) on the hardest settings, while maintaining anytime validity.
- False-certification rate under the runner-up target is at most 0.052 on gpt-oss-20b / ER-REASON across all methods and budgets, within Monte Carlo error of the nominal epsilon = 0.05.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** The paper makes a genuine and non-trivial contribution. While anytime-valid inference via e-processes is an active area, combining pairwise e-processes with an unseen-category upper bound to achieve category-agnostic certification of the mode is novel. The "mode certification" guarantee (distinct from majority certification as in MMC) is a new and practically meaningful target.

**Rigor:** High. The Type-I error theorem (4.1) is proven rigorously via union-bound over three epsilon/3 components. The stopping-time upper bound (Theorem 4.3) and the matching minimax lower bound (Theorem 4.4) together make a complete complexity-theoretic argument. Proofs are provided in extensive appendices.

**Reproducibility:** Moderate-to-good. Algorithm 1 provides a clear pseudocode. The grid parameters Lambda_pw and Lambda_r require specification and their choice affects practical performance; the paper gives sufficient conditions (Condition 4.2) for the theory to hold, though practitioner guidance on grid construction is somewhat implicit. The LLM experiments use publicly available models and named benchmarks, which aids reproduction.

**Significance:** The distinction between "mode certification" and "majority certification" (MMC) is subtle but matters in diffuse-tail distributions, which are precisely the regimes where LLMs produce many long-tail answers. CITE's advantage in this regime is demonstrated both theoretically and empirically. The framework is practically deployable in safety-critical applications (e.g., clinical triage) where false certification costs are high.

**Red flags / limitations:**
- The stopping-time guarantee requires the modal gap delta > 0 to be bounded away from zero; in practice this is unknown, and the grid condition (Condition 4.2) implicitly requires it covers the true delta. In hard near-tie settings, stopping time can be very large and CITE offers no practical guidance on when to give up.
- CITE certifies a *fixed-target* label r specified a priori. Certifying the empirically observed top category (the data-adaptive target) is explicitly left as future work, and whether the category-size-free rate survives a Bonferroni correction over the sequence of empirical leaders is open.
- In W-CITE, the per-round cost grows as O(|A_t|) due to the all-competitors check. For very diffuse distributions this can be computationally heavy.
- The paper's claim that CITE "improves certification" vs. MMC is primarily in diffuse settings; in concentrated settings, CITE is comparable or slightly worse. The comparison should be kept in perspective.

---

## Strengths

- Clean mathematical formulation: maps LLM self-consistency sampling to sequential categorical mode testing with a precise Type-I error definition.
- Three-component decomposition is modular and each component's guarantee is separately stated, aiding understanding and potential extension.
- Category-set-size-free stopping-time rate is a strong theoretical property not shared by prior baselines.
- Matching minimax lower bound justifies that no method can be substantially faster in the worst case.
- Empirical evaluation is honest: CITE is not always the best at finite sample, and the authors directly compare against the stronger (but non-anytime-valid) KR baseline.
- Extension to weighted voting (W-CITE) broadens practical applicability to verifier-aided pipelines.

---

## Limitations

- Mode certification is logically weaker than answer correctness; CITE certifies the *distributional* mode, not whether the mode is the ground-truth answer. The two guarantees must be composed separately.
- Data-adaptive target (certifying whichever label currently leads) is not covered; the gap between fixed-target and data-adaptive settings is left open and may require a substantially different approach.
- In near-tie distributions (small delta), the expected stopping time is O(1/delta^2 * log(1/epsilon)), which can be enormous; CITE provides no timeout mechanism or early-termination guidance.
- W-CITE has O(|A_t|) per-step cost, which is problematic for open-ended generation tasks with hundreds of distinct responses.
- Grid selection (Lambda_pw, Lambda_r) requires some domain knowledge; the theory gives sufficient conditions but not a concrete adaptive grid-construction procedure.
- The experiments focus on two LLMs (Qwen3-30B, gpt-oss-20b); generalization to instruction-tuned models with very different response distributions is not assessed.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Can CITE be extended to certify the empirically observed top category (data-adaptive target), and does the category-size-free stopping-time rate survive the necessary Bonferroni correction over the sequence of empirical leaders?
2. What is the practical sensitivity of CITE to the choice of grids Lambda_pw and Lambda_r? Is there a principled adaptive grid construction that maintains the theoretical guarantees while improving finite-sample efficiency?
3. In W-CITE, can runner-up monotonicity be approximately restored under mild assumptions on the weight distribution to reduce per-step cost from O(|A_t|) back to O(1)?
4. How does CITE perform when LLM responses are not i.i.d. across samples (e.g., due to prompt sensitivity, temperature variation, or batch effects)?
5. Can the CITE framework be combined with a separate correctness verifier to produce a joint certificate that bounds both false-certification probability and answer incorrectness simultaneously?
6. Is the minimax lower bound tight in the small-p_r regime, or can a better algorithm close the gap between the upper and lower bounds when the target category has very small probability?
