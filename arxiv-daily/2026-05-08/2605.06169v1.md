> **一句话总结：** 本文提出了一种名为 MV-Split Residuals 的残差合并机制，通过将均值子空间与中心化子空间的残差增益解耦，在机制层面根治了超深扩散 Transformer（最深至 1000 层）训练中的均值主导崩溃现象（Mean Mode Screaming），在 400 层 DiT 对比实验中优于 LayerScale，并首次在 1000 层规模下完成稳定训练。

> **TL;DR:** This paper identifies and mechanistically explains a previously unnamed failure mode in ultra-deep Diffusion Transformers (DiTs) called **Mean Mode Screaming (MMS)**: a sudden mean-coherent gradient shock that drives token representations into a homogenized, mean-dominated collapse state from which the network does not recover. The authors show that row-stochastic attention structurally preserves pure-mean states while contracting the centered (token-varying) subspace, and that once tokens homogenize, Q/K gradients vanish through the Softmax Jacobian null space, locking in the collapse. They address this with **MV-Split Residuals**, a lightweight architectural change that routes the centered and mean residual updates through independently learned gains. On matched 400-layer ImageNet experiments, MV-Split eliminates collapse events and outperforms LayerScale on FID/IS throughout training; a 1000-layer run demonstrates the design remains stably trainable at that extreme depth. This paper is specifically relevant for practitioners scaling DiTs or single-stream multimodal transformers to very large depth.

## 核心贡献

- **命名并刻画了一种新型训练失败模式**："均值模式尖叫"（Mean Mode Screaming, MMS）——在训练看似稳定数千步后，均值连贯梯度分量突然激增，将超深 DiT 驱入均值主导的崩溃状态，且无法自我恢复。
- **提供了精确的机制分析**：证明了行随机注意力严格保持纯均值状态（Proposition 1），梯度可被精确分解为均值连贯分量（$\Delta W_\mu$）与中心化分量（$\Delta W_c$），且当 token 对齐时前者以 $\mathcal{O}(T)$ 方式相干累积（对齐放大定律，Eq. 6）。
- **通过 Softmax Jacobian 零空间证明了 Q/K 梯度消灭机制**：value 均匀化后，注意力 logit 梯度在 Softmax 零空间中严格消失，从而固化崩溃状态（Lemma 1）。
- **提出了 MV-Split Residuals 方法**：将残差合并路由到两个正交子空间（中心化路径用增益 $\beta$，均值路径用增益 $\alpha$ 的泄漏积分器），对中心化学习无额外衰减代价。
- **在 400 层和 1000 层 DiT 上完成实验验证**：400 层对比实验中消除崩溃事件，FID/IS 持续优于 LayerScale；1000 层运行作为极限规模稳定性验证。

## Key Contributions

- **Names and characterizes MMS (Mean Mode Screaming):** A structural failure mode in ultra-deep Post-Norm DiTs where a mean-coherent gradient shock opens residual branches into a mean-dominated regime, suppressing token-centered variation and locking out Q/K learning.
- **Exact gradient decomposition:** Provides an algebraic proof that the residual-writer gradient decomposes exactly into a rank-1 mean-coherent component ($\Delta W_\mu$) and a centered component ($\Delta W_c$), with cross-terms identically zero. The mean-coherent component enters an $\mathcal{O}(T)$ coherent scaling regime when tokens align.
- **Softmax null-space extinction of Q/K gradients:** Proves (Lemma 1) that under value homogenization, the attention-logit gradient vanishes through the Softmax Jacobian, explaining why the collapse self-reinforces and why Q/K learning stalls after MMS.
- **MV-Split Residuals:** A residual merge formula that independently gates the centered and mean subspace contributions, combining a separately gained centered residual update with a leaky trunk-mean replacement — preventing mean accumulation without penalizing centered feature learning.
- **Scale validation at 1000 layers:** The 1000-layer run is the first reported stable DiT training at that depth, establishing MV-Split as a viable residual design for extreme-depth generative models.

## Methodology

### Backbone

The paper uses a deliberately minimal single-stream multimodal DiT with Post-Norm (RMSNorm) residual chains, zero-initialized residual writers ($W_O$, $W_2$), SwiGLU FFN, and 2D RoPE for image tokens. No AdaLN or other per-layer modulation is used. Image and text tokens are concatenated into a unified sequence with self-attention handling all multimodal interaction. Training uses Rectified Flow Matching on ImageNet-2012 latents with a frozen FLUX.2 VAE and a frozen Qwen3-0.6B text encoder.

### Mechanistic Analysis: Mean-Dominated Collapse

The paper defines a token-space decomposition $X = \mu(X) + c(X)$ (mean and centered components via projectors $J$ and $P = I - J$). Two propositions establish the geometric asymmetry:

- **Proposition 1:** Row-stochastic attention preserves pure-mean states ($A\mu(X) = \mu(X)$).
- **Proposition 2:** The centered component is governed by $PAP$, with operator norm $\mu_{\text{eff}}(A) = \|PAP\|_2$, which can be strictly contractive in deep layers.

Consequently, if residual branches become dominated by mean updates, the representation drifts toward a pure-mean state. The **alignment amplification law** (Eq. 6) shows that gradient energy amplification relative to the independent-token baseline equals $(T-1)\kappa$, where $\kappa$ is the pairwise alignment between token representations and adjoint gradients. As tokens homogenize, $\kappa \to 1$ and the mean-coherent gradient accumulates $\mathcal{O}(T)$ coherently.

**Softmax null space (Lemma 1):** When values homogenize ($V_j \approx \bar{v}$ for all $j$), the gradient through attention logits vanishes identically ($\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial S_i = 0$). This blocks Q/K learning while residual-writer gradients bypass the Softmax Jacobian and remain nonzero, reinforcing mean-dominated updates.

### MV-Split Residual Formula

The standard Post-Norm merge $X_{l+1} = \text{RMSNorm}(X_l + F_l)$ is replaced by a subspace-routed merge:

$$Z_l = X_l + \underbrace{\beta \odot (PF_l)}_{\text{centered path}} + \underbrace{\alpha \odot J(F_l - X_l)}_{\text{mean path}}$$
$$X_{l+1} = \text{RMSNorm}(Z_l)$$

where $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{R}^D$ are per-block learnable vectors broadcast across tokens. The mean subspace uses a leaky integrator: each layer contracts the trunk mean by $(1 - \alpha_d)$ before injecting the branch correction, bounding cumulative mean accumulation. The centered subspace receives gain $\beta$ independently. In the backward pass, centered and mean gradients receive independent gains ($\beta$ and $\alpha$ respectively), preventing the $\alpha/\beta$ ratio from coupling centered replenishment to mean damping. A segment-wise projector variant handles multimodal sequences to avoid mixing image and text means.

### Experimental Setup

- **400-layer matched comparison:** ImageNet 256×256, backbone/optimizer/data/batch matched across un-stabilized baseline, LayerScale, and MV-Split variants. Evaluated at 10k, 20k, 30k, 40k, 50k steps with FID-50K and Inception Score (25 NFE Euler, CFG $w=2.0$).
- **1000-layer scale validation:** Separate training/post-training pipeline on ~50k curated images, evaluated as a scale-validation point only (not a matched frontier comparison).
- **Gradient mode decomposition analysis:** Per-step median $G_{\text{mean}}$ and $G_{\text{ctr}}$ across depth, writer gradient norm decomposition, energy ratio $\rho_T = \|\mu(X)\|_F / \|c(X)\|_F$, and token cosine similarity (TCS) tracked throughout training.

## Results & Highlights

**400-layer stability-constrained comparison (Table 1, Figure 5):**

| Method | FID@10k | FID@20k | FID@30k | FID@40k | FID@50k |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 400L Base ($\eta$) | — (diverges) | — | — | — | — |
| 400L LayerScale | 14.08 / 59.2 | 6.50 / 96.6 | 4.09 / 130.5 | 3.33 / 149.6 | 2.90 / 165.5 |
| **400L MV-Split** | **7.23 / 89.8** | **3.64 / 139.9** | **3.09 / 166.5** | **2.79 / 182.0** | **2.60 / 185.3** |
| 1000L MV-Split | 5.47 / 117.3 | 2.92 / 178.2 | 2.68 / 196.6 | 2.64 / 209.4 | 2.77 / 217.3 |

- Un-stabilized 400L baselines (both default and half-LR) diverge before the first checkpoint; they do not constitute stable frontier points.
- Among non-divergent 400L runs, MV-Split is already ahead of LayerScale by 20k–30k steps and maintains this advantage through 50k steps, suggesting a genuine quality improvement rather than a transient effect.
- MV-Split operates with a higher bounded gradient band than LayerScale (Figure 5, bottom), consistent with better preservation of centered residual updates.

**Gradient mode decomposition (Figure 6):** LayerScale bounds the mean-coherent writer gradient $G_{\text{mean}}$ but does so by simultaneously shrinking the centered component $G_{\text{ctr}}$. MV-Split bounds $G_{\text{mean}}$ while preserving a higher stable $G_{\text{ctr}}$ band — directly confirming the intended mechanism.

**Alignment amplification audit (Figure 4):** At the divergence step $t^* = 3400$, active-layer amplification values reach $\mathcal{A} - 1 \approx 167$, corresponding to ~13× writer-gradient norm amplification, consistent with the $\mathcal{O}(T)$ coherent regime predicted by Eq. 6.

**Token mean as global timestep carrier:** A linear probe confirms the token mean acts as an implicit global timestep carrier (near-perfect $R^2$ predicting $t$ across depth), justifying the leaky integrator design (retaining mean information rather than projecting it out entirely).

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** High within its specific niche. The mean/centered subspace decomposition of gradient dynamics in attention-based networks is a genuinely new lens. Prior stability methods (ReZero, LayerScale, DeepNorm, Keel) treat residual scaling as a scalar/per-channel isotropic operation. MV-Split's structural insight — that the mean and centered subspaces need asymmetric treatment — is not present in prior work and is derived from first principles rather than empirically motivated. The MMS failure mode identification is novel and plausibly explains a class of delayed-divergence failures that practitioners have observed but not formally characterized.

**Rigor:** Strong for a single-author preprint. The gradient decomposition (Eq. 5, Appendix C) is an exact algebraic identity. The Softmax null-space lemma (Lemma 1, Appendix C.3) has a complete proof. The alignment amplification law is derived rigorously. Experiments include proper matched controls (same backbone, optimizer, data, batch size) and stability-constrained comparison that acknowledges unstable baselines cannot define stable frontier points.

**Reproducibility:** Good. Code and model weights are publicly released. Training configuration is described in detail. The 400-layer comparison is on a standard benchmark (ImageNet). The diagnostic metrics are formally defined (Table 2, Appendix A).

**Significance:** Potentially high for practitioners scaling DiTs to hundreds of layers. The problem is real and practically important (unexplained late-stage divergences in deep generative models). The fix is lightweight (two learnable per-block vectors, no added parameters of significance). If the results hold at larger scale, MV-Split could become a standard component in deep DiT architectures.

**Red flags:**
- Single-author preprint without institutional affiliation creates a higher bar for independent verification.
- The 1000-layer result, while visually impressive, is not a controlled experiment — the separate training/post-training pipeline means that the image quality shown in Figure 1 cannot be cleanly attributed to MV-Split alone versus other pipeline choices.
- No comparison against more recent depth stabilizers (Keel [34], cited but not evaluated), though the paper acknowledges this and provides a theoretical distinction.
- The experiments are confined to a relatively small scale (ImageNet 256×256 up to 80k steps). Generalization to larger models, higher resolution, or longer training horizons is unverified.
- The mechanistic story assumes a specific Post-Norm backbone without AdaLN; it is unclear how broadly MMS applies to DiT variants with adaptive layer norm or cross-attention conditioning.

---

## Strengths

- Precise mechanistic diagnosis with exact mathematical proofs, not just empirical observations.
- Identifies a structurally specific failure mode (mean-subspace collapse) distinct from generic gradient explosion/vanishing, enabling a targeted fix.
- The fix (MV-Split) is minimally invasive: two learnable per-block vectors, compatible with standard training protocols.
- Clear distinction from and comparison to LayerScale/ReZero, including a formal proof of structural non-equivalence.
- Public code and model weights enable direct replication.
- Gradient mode decomposition diagnostic is a reusable tool for auditing training stability in deep transformers.
- The 1000-layer scale validation, even if not a controlled comparison, establishes a proof of concept at an extreme depth.

---

## Limitations

- Single-author independent preprint; results have not been peer-reviewed or independently replicated.
- Experimental scale is modest (ImageNet 256×256, 80k steps at 400 layers); generalization to billion-parameter models or higher-resolution generation is unverified.
- The 1000-layer run uses a different training pipeline, making quality comparisons to the 400-layer controlled runs inappropriate.
- The mechanistic theory is developed for a Post-Norm single-stream DiT without AdaLN; applicability to AdaLN-conditioned DiTs (the more common architecture) or cross-attention architectures is not demonstrated.
- No wall-clock or compute overhead analysis is provided for MV-Split vs. the baselines.
- The comparison does not include Keel [34] or DeepNorm [33], both of which also report 1000-layer Transformer training stability.
- The token-mean-as-timestep-carrier finding (Appendix H) is interesting but not fully explored — it raises questions about what information is being encoded in the mean subspace and whether the leaky integrator design optimally handles this.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Does MV-Split's benefit extend to AdaLN-conditioned DiTs (e.g., standard DiT-XL, SD3, FLUX-style architectures), where timestep conditioning already modifies per-layer behavior? Does AdaLN partially mitigate MMS, or does it interact with the mean-subspace dynamics in a different way?

2. How does MV-Split interact with other depth stabilizers (Keel, DeepNorm) that also report 1000-layer stability? Are the failure modes they address orthogonal to MMS, or overlapping?

3. The paper shows that the token mean carries implicit timestep information (near-perfect linear predictability). Does this mean the mean subspace is doing useful work that MV-Split preserves through the leaky integrator? Would explicitly encoding the timestep in the mean subspace (rather than implicitly) further improve stability or generation quality?

4. The alignment amplification law predicts $\mathcal{O}(T)$ gradient growth with sequence length $T$. Does MMS become more likely or more severe at longer sequence lengths (higher-resolution generation, longer text descriptions), and does MV-Split scale proportionally?

5. Can the gradient mode decomposition diagnostics ($G_{\text{mean}}$ vs. $G_{\text{ctr}}$) be used as a lightweight monitoring signal during large-scale production training to detect early warning signs of MMS before divergence occurs?

6. Does the MMS failure mode appear in language model transformers at large depth, or is it specific to the architectural properties of diffusion transformers (noise-agnostic conditioning, specific initialization, absence of AdaLN)?
