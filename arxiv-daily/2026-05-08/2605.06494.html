From Token Lists to Graph Motifs: Weisfeiler–Lehman Analysis of Sparse Autoencoder Features

arXiv: 2605.06494

Date: 2026-05-07

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06494

一句话总结： 本文将稀疏自编码器（SAE）的每个特征表示为一个词元共现图，并通过定制的 Weisfeiler-Lehman 风格图核对 GPT-2 Small 的 2048 个特征聚类，发现图结构视角能够恢复字母类特征簇（纯度 0.516），而基于解码器余弦相似度的基线方法完全无法识别此类簇（纯度 0.000），但词元直方图基线的整体纯度仍更高（0.854 vs. 0.760）。
TL;DR: This paper proposes representing each SAE feature as a weighted token co-occurrence graph and comparing features using a custom Weisfeiler-Lehman (WL)-style graph kernel. Applied to 2,048 features from a layer-6 GPT-2 Small SAE, graph-based clustering recovers structural motif families — particularly an alphabetic cluster (purity 0.516) invisible to decoder-cosine clustering (purity 0.000) — but a simpler token-histogram baseline achieves higher overall purity (0.854 vs. 0.760). The result positions graph representations as a complementary, not superior, lens for SAE feature organisation, best suited for surfacing co-occurrence topology that token-frequency views alone miss.

核心贡献

  • 图结构特征表示：提出将每个 SAE 特征的激活上下文转化为加权词元共现图，节点为高频出现词元，边权重为共现次数，从而将特征的"行为模式"编码为图拓扑结构。
  • 定制 WL 风格图核：设计了一种频率分箱的 Weisfeiler-Lehman 图核，初始节点标签来自对数尺度共现频率（而非词元身份），通过加权平均聚合邻居信息并重新分箱，最终以单次直方图内积作为核值，与标准 WL 子树核有实质性区别。
  • 互补性发现：实验证明图核视角能够捕捉解码器余弦相似度无法揭示的字母类结构簇（两者字母纯度分别为 0.516 和 0.000），从而证实图结构信息对现有方法具有实质补充价值。
  • 鲁棒性验证：在 12 组超参数配置（窗口大小、词元截断、共现阈值）及 10 个随机种子下，整体纯度标准差 ≤ 0.002，聚类结构稳定可复现。
  • 诚实的局限性声明：论文明确承认词元直方图基线在整体纯度上优于图方法，图核仅在特定结构类别上具备优势，结论范围限于单层、单模型、合成语料库。

Key Contributions

  • Graph-structured feature representations: Each SAE feature is converted into a weighted token co-occurrence graph where nodes are the top-K most frequent tokens in activation windows, and edges encode co-occurrence counts, capturing relational structure beyond individual token frequencies.
  • Custom WL-style frequency-binned graph kernel: A novel graph kernel is introduced that departs from standard WL subtree kernels by using log-scaled co-occurrence frequencies as initial node labels, aggregating neighbourhood labels via weighted averaging rather than hashing, and summing over a single histogram rather than concatenating per-iteration histograms.
  • Complementary structural signal: The graph-based clustering recovers an alphabetic-dominant cluster (purity 0.516) that decoder-cosine clustering entirely misses (purity 0.000), demonstrating that co-occurrence topology encodes information orthogonal to decoder weight geometry.
  • Robustness analysis: Cluster assignments are stable across a 12-point hyperparameter grid and 10 k-means random seeds (mean purity 0.748 ± 0.051; σ ≤ 0.002 across seeds), and achieve ARI/NMI of 0.944/0.928 on a cross-corpus Python-code spot check.
  • Honest scoping: The authors explicitly report that a token-histogram baseline outperforms WL on overall purity (0.854 vs. 0.760), framing graph representations as complementary rather than dominant.

Methodology

Feature-to-graph construction. For each SAE feature $f$, activations are collected over a synthetic mixed-domain corpus (~78,749 token positions, 13 surface registers: Python, JavaScript, Bash, URLs, JSON, social media, natural language, email, Spanish/French/German/Japanese). A high-activation event at position $t$ is defined as $z_{t,f}$ exceeding the 50th-percentile threshold. Around each such event, a local context window of width $W=10$ is examined. The top $K=30$ tokens by raw co-occurrence frequency become graph nodes; an undirected edge is added between any two nodes whose tokens co-occur at least $C=3$ times, with edge weight equal to the co-occurrence count. This yields a collection of $N=2048$ weighted labelled graphs (filtered from 24,576 features by activation fraction between 0.1% and 98%).

WL-style frequency-binned graph kernel. Initial node labels are derived from log-scaled co-occurrence frequency ($\log(1+c)$ where $c$ is the row-sum), discretised into 64 bins. For $h=3$ refinement iterations, each node's label is updated as a weighted average of its neighbours' labels (weighted by edge co-occurrence counts) and re-binned. The resulting per-graph histogram of node-label counts is the explicit feature map; the kernel value $K(G_f, G_{f'})$ is the inner product of these normalised histograms. The $N \times N$ kernel matrix is normalised by dividing each entry by the geometric mean of diagonal entries, then symmetrised.

Embedding and clustering. Kernel PCA (2 components) is applied to the normalised kernel matrix; k-means ($K=10$ clusters, $n_\text{init}=20$, fixed seed) is run in this 2D embedding space. Clusters are evaluated against heuristic token-type labels (symbolic/alphabetic/numeric/mixed) using a cluster purity metric.

Baselines. Two comparison points: (1) cosine similarity in the space of SAE decoder weight vectors (L2-normalised), (2) cosine similarity between normalised token-frequency histograms constructed from each feature's top-$K$ token counts.

Ablations. Seven similarity measures are ablated (WL default, edges zeroed, node labels shuffled, decoder cosine, token histogram, co-occurrence cosine, Jaccard top-token). Directed graph variants (lower-triangular approximation) and cross-corpus stability (Python-code corpus) are evaluated in appendices.

Results & Highlights

Cluster purity comparison (Table 1):

| Method | Symbolic | Alphabetic | Overall |

|-----------------|----------|------------|---------|

| WL-style kernel | 0.781 | 0.516 | 0.760 |

| Decoder cosine | 0.754 | 0.000 | 0.754 |

| Token histogram | 0.894 | 0.749 | 0.854 |

The headline finding: WL-style clustering is the only method that produces an alphabetic-dominant cluster (cluster 8, purity 51.6%, size 161). Decoder-cosine clustering produces zero alphabetic purity across all $K=10$ clusters. However, the token-histogram baseline achieves higher overall purity (0.854) by directly leveraging token-frequency information, surpassing WL (0.760). The authors explicitly interpret this as showing the graph view is complementary rather than dominant.

Ablation insights (Table 2): Zeroing edge weights drops alphabetic purity from 0.500 to 0.000 while leaving overall purity almost unchanged, confirming that edges (not just node-label histograms) are responsible for the alphabetic cluster signal. Shuffling node labels yields ARI 0.618 with the default, indicating the kernel primarily relies on edge structure and marginal label frequencies rather than specific token identities.

Robustness: Across 12 hyperparameter configurations, mean purity is 0.748 ± 0.051. Multi-seed standard deviation is ≤ 0.002. Feature-selection cutoff variations produce negligible purity differences (all within multi-seed noise).

Cross-corpus stability: A Python-code corpus (600 synthesised snippets, ~10^4–10^5 tokens) activates 23,591/24,576 features; 2,030/2,048 (99.1%) overlap with the original selection. Cluster assignments achieve ARI 0.944 and NMI 0.928 against the main-corpus clustering, indicating structural motifs are stable across corpus type.

Cluster composition: 9 of 10 WL clusters are symbolic-dominant (punctuation-heavy motifs); cluster 8 is the sole alphabetic-dominant cluster. Clusters 6 and 7 carry highest code-token ratios (33.1% and 32.5%).

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The core idea — representing SAE features as token co-occurrence graphs and comparing them via graph kernels — is genuinely novel in the mechanistic interpretability literature. No prior work applies graph kernels to SAE feature co-occurrence graphs. The connection to the WL framework is principled, though the departure from standard WL subtree kernels introduces a custom variant whose relationship to the theoretical WL expressivity hierarchy is not established. The graph-as-feature representation is a natural extension of the existing geometric/topological analysis of SAE feature spaces (cf. Li et al., arXiv:2410.19750).

Rigor: The paper is admirably honest about the limits of its claims. The authors do not overstate: they report the baseline outperforms their method on overall purity, explicitly note that purity differences of ~0.006 (0.760 vs. 0.754) should be read with caution without bootstrap confidence intervals, and acknowledge that heuristic token-type labels constitute a coarse and indirect evaluation of interpretability. The ablation study is thorough (7 similarity measures, 10 seeds, 12 hyperparameter configurations, 5 cutoff configurations). The directed-graph variant and cross-corpus checks are sensible robustness probes.

Reproducibility: High. Full parameter specifications are listed in Section 5.4. Code is stated to be publicly available (URL anonymised for double-blind review). All random seeds are fixed to 42. The pipeline uses standard libraries (PyTorch, Transformers, scikit-learn, NetworkX, GraKeL, pandas). The experimental corpus is procedurally generated from released templates. Reproduction from scratch appears feasible for a practitioner with moderate ML infrastructure.

Significance: Moderate. This is a proof-of-concept study on a single SAE layer of GPT-2 Small — a relatively small model with a well-studied SAE. The claim that graph representations provide complementary structure is credible but narrow. The alphabetic-purity gap is the most striking empirical result, but it is a single heuristic metric on a synthetic corpus; whether this translates to interpretability-relevant insights for practitioners is not demonstrated. The scalability limitation (O(N² · h) kernel matrix infeasible for full 24,576-feature or 131,072-feature SAEs without approximations) significantly constrains practical applicability to frontier models.

Red flags:

  • The custom WL variant has not been benchmarked against a standard WL subtree kernel implementation (e.g., GraKeL); the paper acknowledges this is an open question. It is unclear whether the alphabetic-cluster advantage would survive with a standard WL kernel.
  • Evaluation relies entirely on heuristic token-type labels as a proxy for interpretability; no human evaluation or mechanistic validation of identified motif families is provided.
  • The probing corpus is synthetic and templated, deliberately not drawn from any naturalistic distribution; generalisation to OpenWebText or The Stack is explicitly acknowledged as unverified future work.
  • All experiments are on a single SAE (6-RES-JB) at a single layer (layer 6) of a single model (GPT-2 Small); cross-layer and cross-architecture generalisability are open questions.
  • The paper does not report bootstrap confidence intervals over the feature sample, so uncertainty in the purity estimates is not formally quantified.

Strengths

  • Intellectually honest framing: the paper does not claim graph representations dominate existing methods, explicitly reporting that the token-histogram baseline achieves higher overall purity.
  • Thorough ablation study covering 7 similarity measures, 10 random seeds, 12 hyperparameter configurations, and 5 feature-selection cutoffs.
  • Identification of a concrete, reproducible empirical gap (alphabetic purity 0.516 WL vs. 0.000 decoder cosine) that is both surprising and interpretable.
  • Good reproducibility: fully specified parameters, fixed seeds, standard open-source toolchain, procedurally generated corpus with released templates.
  • Principled use of graph kernel theory (WL framework) grounded in established MLNR/graph learning literature.
  • Explicit discussion of societal impact, limitations, and scope restrictions — uncommon transparency for interpretability preprints.

---

Limitations

  • Single model/layer/SAE: All conclusions are drawn from one SAE (6-RES-JB) at one residual layer of GPT-2 Small; stronger claims require cross-layer and cross-model validation.
  • Synthetic corpus: The synthetic mixed-domain corpus provides controlled surface variation but is not drawn from any naturalistic text distribution; the SAE itself was trained on OpenWebText. Distributional mismatch may affect which motif families are surfaced.
  • Scalability: The full $N \times N$ kernel matrix is O(N² · h). For N = 2048 this is tractable on a single CPU; for the full 24,576-feature dictionary or frontier-model SAEs with up to 131,072 features, the dense matrix is infeasible without Nyström approximations or random feature maps.
  • Heuristic evaluation metric: Cluster purity against token-type labels (symbolic/alphabetic/numeric/mixed) is a coarse proxy; it does not directly measure whether motif families correspond to meaningful semantic or mechanistic categories. Human evaluation is absent.
  • Custom kernel not benchmarked against standard WL: The paper does not compare to a standard WL subtree kernel (e.g., GraKeL implementation); it is unclear whether results are specific to the custom variant's design choices.
  • No causal/mechanistic validation: The paper identifies surface-form structural motifs but does not establish that these motifs correspond to mechanistically meaningful feature families (e.g., via activation patching or circuit tracing).
  • No bootstrap confidence intervals: Uncertainty in purity estimates over the feature sample is not formally quantified; small purity differences (e.g., 0.760 vs. 0.754) cannot be statistically distinguished.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does the alphabetic-purity gap between WL-style and decoder-cosine clustering persist on larger, more capable models (e.g., GPT-2 Medium/Large, Pythia, LLaMA, or Claude 3 Sonnet SAEs with 131,072 features)?
  2. Would a standard WL subtree kernel (GraKeL implementation) produce the same alphabetic cluster gap, or is it specific to the frequency-binned label scheme and weighted-averaging refinement used here?
  3. Can the O(N²) kernel matrix bottleneck be overcome with Nyström approximations or random feature maps for graph kernels while preserving the complementary structural signal?
  4. Do the identified motif families (punctuation-heavy, alphabetic, code-like) correspond to mechanistically meaningful circuits when probed via activation patching or attention head attribution?
  5. How do graph-based motif families change across transformer layers — do graph structural signatures shift from syntactic/surface motifs in early layers to more semantic motifs in later layers?
  6. Would directed graph construction (respecting GPT-2's autoregressive causal ordering) recover interpretable causal structure that the undirected variant misses, particularly for features that encode left-to-right sequential dependencies?
  7. Can this approach be extended from the residual stream to attention head outputs or MLP sublayer activations to probe whether co-occurrence topology differs across architectural components?
