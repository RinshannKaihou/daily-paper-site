> **一句话总结：** 本文提出 SIREN 方法，通过冻结搜索后候选集、重复分裂评估与 Gaussian multiplier bootstrap，纠正自适应 prompt/程序搜索中"赢家诅咒"导致的乐观性偏差，使 LLM 基准测试报告的分数真正逼近 tune-then-deploy 全流程的新鲜数据性能。

> **TL;DR:** When LLMs are evaluated after adaptive prompt or program search (e.g., DSPy, random search), the winner's reported benchmark score is systematically optimistic because the benchmark data was used during tuning. SIREN is a post-search reporting protocol that freezes the shortlist of candidates, repeatedly splits a held-out evaluation pool to separate selection from scoring, and uses a Gaussian multiplier bootstrap for uncertainty quantification. On MMLU-Pro Math and Law experiments with 11 open-weight models, winner-based reporting inflated scores by +1–4 percentage points and reversed deployment decisions; SIREN reduced signed bias to within ±0.21 pp and correctly tracked the true tuned-procedure performance curve across token budgets from 500K to 6.5M.

## 核心贡献

- **问题重构**：将自适应 LLM 基准测试正式定义为对"预算索引的 tune-then-deploy 流程"的推断问题，明确区分"赢家制品的事后得分"与"部署流程的新鲜数据性能"这两个不同的统计目标。
- **SIREN 协议**：提出基于冻结候选集、分裂式评分与留出评估、稳定化选择器的重复分裂评估方案，在不重新运行 tuner 的前提下完成推断。
- **理论保证**：在固定候选集的平滑选择器机制下，证明所选重复分裂估计量具有一阶逐项线性展开（Selection-aware CLT），并证明 Gaussian multiplier bootstrap 对预算网格上的联合置信区间和同步带有效。
- **实验验证**：在 MMLU-Pro Math 和 Law 上，通过随机搜索与 DSPy tuner 测试 11 个开源模型，证实赢家式报告的乐观性偏差可改变 tuned vs. default 的部署结论，而 SIREN 始终接近 Monte Carlo 真值参考。

## Key Contributions

- **Problem reframing:** Formalizes adaptive LLM benchmarking as inference on a budget-indexed tune-then-deploy procedure, making the fresh-data performance of the full procedure—not the observed winning artifact—the primary estimand.
- **SIREN protocol:** A post-search reporting layer that (1) freezes the post-search shortlist, (2) repeatedly splits an independent evaluation pool into scoring and held-out subsets so selection evidence never enters the final held-out score, and (3) aggregates split-level weighted-average estimates.
- **Statistical theory:** Proves a first-order item-level CLT for the selected repeated-split estimator (Theorem 1/3) and proves that the Gaussian multiplier bootstrap yields asymptotically valid pointwise confidence intervals and simultaneous bands over a finite tuning-budget grid (Theorem 2/4), without re-running the tuner.
- **Empirical validation:** Controlled simulations confirm calibration and bias; MMLU-Pro experiments with two tuner families across 11 models show SIREN's mean signed error stays between −0.21 and +0.08 pp, while M1–M4 baselines show +0.04 to +4.35 pp optimism.

## Methodology

**Target of inference.** SIREN targets the procedure-level performance curve θ_j(B), defined as the expected fresh-data accuracy of the artifact returned by a budget-B tuner applied to system j, averaged over new task instances, tuner randomness, and development samples. This is contrasted with the winner's score—the best observed accuracy on the finite development set.

**Protocol overview.**
1. *Tuning phase (upstream):* A tuner searches over candidate artifacts (instructions, demonstrations, program configurations) under a token-budget constraint B, yielding a frozen shortlist A_j(B) of retained candidates.
2. *Freeze shortlist:* SIREN treats the shortlist as fixed; no new candidates are generated during evaluation.
3. *Repeated-split scoring (R splits):* An independent evaluation pool of M items is repeatedly split into a score subset D^score_r and held-out subset E_r. The score subset determines softmax-type selection weights over shortlisted candidates; the held-out subset evaluates the weighted-average output after that selection has been made.
4. *Aggregation:* Split-level held-out means are aggregated using weights ω_r to produce the SIREN point estimate θ̃_{j,R}(B).
5. *Bootstrap CI:* For each benchmark item W_i, SIREN computes an item-level first-order contribution ψ̂_{i,j,B,M} that encodes both the direct held-out score effect and the derivative-weighted selection effect. Independent standard Gaussian multipliers ζ_i are drawn and applied to these contributions to form a joint bootstrap process G̃*_M. This single resampling supports pointwise CIs, simultaneous bands over all (j, B), and pre-specified equal-budget or cross-budget linear contrasts—without re-running the tuner.

**Key design choices:**
- The selector g_{j,B} maps scoring-subset scores to a simplex weight vector. Softmax with temperature τ=1 is the default; smooth selectors are required for the first-order theory to hold (hard argmax requires an additional margin condition or smoothing).
- Default hyperparameters: R=10 repeated splits, scoring fraction ρ_score=0.5, 2,000 bootstrap draws.
- All models served via vLLM on a single NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU.

**Baselines compared:**
- M1 (naive max): empirical winner evaluated on full pool.
- M2 (M1 + Wald): same point estimate as M1 with a Wald interval.
- M3 (single-split holdout): empirical winner on scoring fold, reported on held-out fold.
- M4 (R-split argmax + Student-t CI): repeated hard-argmax selection with Student-t interval over splits.
- PromptEval: IRT-based budget-aware external baseline.

## Results & Highlights

**RQ1 — Procedure-level target vs. winner-induced bias:** Across all four subject-tuner aggregate rows in MMLU-Pro Math and Law, M1–M4 show consistently positive (optimistic) signed bias ranging from +0.04 to +4.35 pp. SIREN's signed bias ranges from −0.21 to +0.08 pp, with correct directional accuracy (matching the Monte Carlo ordering) in 40–43 of 44 tested cells, versus 15–42 for the baselines.

**RQ2 — Deployment decision changes:** In Table 3 (DSPy tuner on MMLU-Pro Math), M1 and M2 share the same selected-winner point estimate and inherit its bias, reversing tuned-vs-default conclusions in near-tie cases. SIREN tracks the MC procedure-level target even in near ties, preserving gains that survive held-out evaluation while removing gains attributable to adaptive selection on the evaluation pool.

**RQ3 — Budget-aware scoring vs. selection-aware inference:** PromptEval at sparse observation fractions (f ≤ 0.20) is excessively pessimistic through shrinkage; at dense fractions it approaches the full-matrix winner and inherits the winner-curse bias. SIREN's signed error stays between −0.09 and −0.03 pp across all four budgets, while every PromptEval fraction has substantially larger absolute bias (Table 5).

**Controlled simulations (Appendix C):**
- Study A: Multiplier bootstrap is well-calibrated and shows M^{−1/2} width scaling.
- Study B: Near-tie instability of hard argmax selection validated.
- Study C: Same-data best-of-N optimism grows as the number of searched artifacts increases.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** The problem formulation—treating the full tune-then-deploy procedure rather than the winning artifact as the estimand—is a meaningful conceptual advance over existing LLM evaluation work. The integration of post-selection inference and Gaussian multiplier bootstrap in the LLM benchmarking context is novel, though the individual statistical tools are classical.

**Rigor:** The theoretical guarantees are properly stated with explicit assumptions (fixed-shortlist smooth-selector regime) and full proofs in appendix. The conditional-on-tuning-stage framing is statistically honest about what SIREN can and cannot guarantee. The key limitation is that the smooth-selector assumption excludes hard argmax, which is the most common default in practice; the paper addresses this via softmax approximation or a margin condition, but this may not always hold in practice.

**Reproducibility:** Code is released at GitHub (https://github.com/jznmsl/siren). Experimental hyperparameters are fully specified. The evaluation uses a single GPU (RTX 4090), making reproduction accessible. The 10,000-redraw Monte Carlo reference for ground truth is computationally intensive but described clearly.

**Significance:** The finding that winner-based reporting can reverse tuned-vs-default deployment decisions (not merely shift absolute scores) is practically important for practitioners using DSPy, random search, or similar adaptive prompt optimization pipelines. The simultaneous band support across budget levels is useful for reporting performance curves.

**Red flags / limitations to note:**
- Results are limited to MMLU-Pro (Math and Law subjects) and two tuner families. Generalization to other benchmarks, multi-step reasoning tasks, or code generation tasks is not demonstrated.
- The smooth-selector regime is an assumption, not a verified condition in the real experiments; the gap between theory and the experiments that use softmax approximation is not quantified.
- The paper does not address fully open-ended adaptive search where the shortlist itself is data-dependent and grows during search (acknowledged as future work).
- The comparison to PromptEval is somewhat unfavorable in framing because PromptEval solves a different (efficiency) problem; the paper is careful about this but the comparison could mislead readers.

## Strengths

- Clear problem formulation that distinguishes two previously conflated statistical objects in LLM evaluation.
- Theoretical guarantees are non-trivial and cover both pointwise and simultaneous inference over a budget grid without re-running the tuner.
- Comprehensive empirical evidence with a Monte Carlo ground truth reference that credibly separates estimation error from CI coverage issues.
- Practical and relatively low-overhead: SIREN requires only an independent evaluation pool and is post-hoc to the tuning stage.
- Open-source implementation.

## Limitations

- The theoretical framework conditions on the completed tuning stage (frozen shortlist), meaning SIREN does not address the broader problem of fully adaptive, unbounded search.
- Hard argmax selector—the most commonly used selection rule in practice—falls outside the smooth-selector assumption and requires either softmax approximation or a margin condition.
- Experiments cover only MMLU-Pro (two subjects) and two tuner families; the magnitude of winner-curse bias may differ substantially for other tasks, longer-context settings, or generation tasks where scoring is not binary.
- The bootstrap requires computing item-level selector derivatives (Jacobian of g_{j,B}), which adds implementation complexity compared to naive reporting.
- No analysis of how SIREN behaves when the evaluation pool size M is small relative to the shortlist size K; finite-sample performance in this regime is not characterized.

## Relevance

本文直接针对使用 DSPy、随机搜索等自适应 prompt 优化工具后的 LLM 评测可靠性问题，对任何需要在预算约束下比较 tuned vs. default 系统的研究者具有高度实用价值；若您正在开展 LLM 系统评测或 prompt 优化研究，SIREN 提供了一种开箱即用的偏差修正与置信区间方法。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How does SIREN's bias correction behave on generation tasks (summarization, code generation) where item-level scores are not binary and the score distribution is highly skewed?
2. Can the smooth-selector assumption be relaxed to allow hard argmax with data-dependent margin thresholds estimated from the evaluation pool itself?
3. What is the minimum evaluation pool size M needed for the bootstrap CIs to achieve nominal coverage in practice, particularly for large shortlists (K >> 10)?
4. How does SIREN interact with multi-objective tuning settings where the artifact selection depends on a combination of accuracy and cost metrics?
5. The paper shows that selection-aware reporting can change tuned-vs-default conclusions—how often does this actually affect model deployment decisions at scale in production settings, and what is the economic cost of the winner's curse at current LLM usage volumes?
6. Could the SIREN framework be extended to online/continual benchmarking where new items are added incrementally, requiring sequential inference rather than a one-shot repeated split?
