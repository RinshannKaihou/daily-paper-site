Optimizer-Model Consistency: Full Finetuning with the Same Optimizer as Pretraining Forgets Less

["Yuxing Liu", "Jianyu Wang", "Tong Zhang"]

arXiv: 2605.06654

Date: 2026-05-08

Tags: ["LLM-finetuning", "catastrophic-forgetting", "optimizer-theory", "supervised-finetuning", "LoRA"]

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06654

一句话总结： 在SFT阶段使用与预训练相同的优化器（如AdamW）进行全参数微调，相比LoRA和其他优化器能显著减少遗忘、同时保持或提升任务学习效果，该现象在理论上源于优化器对激活值的隐式正则化结构。
TL;DR: This paper identifies "optimizer-model consistency" — the empirical and theoretically-grounded finding that full finetuning with the same optimizer family used during pretraining achieves the best learning-forgetting tradeoff in SFT, outperforming both LoRA and cross-optimizer finetuning. The mechanism is that different optimizers impose different implicit regularization on model activations (via matrix-induced norms), and matching the optimizer in SFT exploits the curvature structure around the pretrained checkpoint. As a byproduct, the paper reveals that Muon's advantage over AdamW in pretraining can disappear or reverse during SFT on reasoning tasks, because Muon tends toward rote memorization rather than pattern learning.

核心贡献

  • 发现优化器-模型一致性现象：在SFT阶段，使用与预训练相同的优化器族进行全参数微调，在学习-遗忘权衡（Pareto frontier）上始终优于LoRA和使用其他优化器的全参数微调。
  • 从理论角度解释该现象：不同优化器通过矩阵诱导范数对激活值施加不同的隐式正则化；预训练checkpoint周围的曲率结构与该正则化绑定，因此使用同族优化器在SFT时更能"顺势而为"地最小化遗忘。
  • 重新审视LoRA的遗忘优势：当合理地在学习率网格搜索下考察Pareto frontier，全参数微调总存在一个checkpoint既比LoRA学得更多、又忘得更少，推翻了"LoRA天然遗忘更少"的流行观点。
  • 揭示Muon在SFT阶段的潜在劣势：Muon在预训练上优于AdamW，但在数学等推理任务的SFT上表现持平甚至更差，原因是Muon倾向于在获得足够模式之前就开始死记硬背，这在小数据量的SFT场景下尤为不利。

Key Contributions

  • Optimizer-model consistency phenomenon: Empirically demonstrated across GPT-2 (123M, trained from scratch) and Llama-2-7B that using the same optimizer (AdamW or Muon) throughout pretraining and SFT yields a strictly better Pareto frontier on the learning-forgetting tradeoff than using a different optimizer or LoRA for SFT.
  • Theoretical grounding via activation regularization: Different optimizers correspond to different matrix-induced norm families (A_{alpha,beta}); each optimizer implicitly regularizes activations according to its norm. Because the Hessian of the pretraining loss at the checkpoint is shaped by these activations, the SFT update aligns better with the local curvature when the same optimizer family is used.
  • Formal theorem: Under four stated assumptions (Taylor-approximated forgetting loss, weight-activation alignment, first-order SFT loss approximation, optimizer regularization structure), full finetuning with the same optimizer family achieves near-optimal learning-forgetting tradeoff (Theorem 1).
  • Full FT can dominate LoRA: Over a proper learning rate sweep, there is always a full finetuning checkpoint that Pareto-dominates every LoRA checkpoint (any rank). LoRA can only appear to forget less when a single suboptimal learning rate is chosen.
  • Muon memorizes before learning patterns: A synthetic experiment on corrupted language data shows Muon achieves higher corrupted-sequence memorization accuracy much earlier than AdamW or SGD, suggesting it shortcuts to rote memorization rather than acquiring general patterns — explaining its degraded SFT performance on reasoning tasks like math.

Methodology

Experimental setup. The authors conduct experiments at two scales: GPT-2-small (123M parameters) trained from scratch on OpenWebText (8B tokens) using both AdamW and Muon, and Llama-2-7B (pretrained by AdamW). SFT tasks include MetaMathQA (math), Alpaca (instruction following), and Magicoder-Evol-Instruct-110K (code). Training uses 4 NVIDIA A6000 GPUs (48GB) in bf16 precision.

Pareto frontier methodology. Rather than comparing algorithms at a single learning rate, the authors plot the learning-forgetting Pareto frontier by conducting a learning rate grid search for each method, collecting intermediate checkpoints, and drawing the frontier. The forgetting metric is OpenWebText validation loss (lower = less forgetting); the learning metric is downstream task validation loss or benchmark accuracy (lower loss / higher accuracy = more learning). This framing is more comprehensive and avoids the single-LR pitfall of prior work (Biderman et al., 2024).

Theoretical framework. Forgetting is approximated as L_forget(ΔW) ≈ (1/2) E[||ΔWx||^2_2] via a second-order Taylor expansion of the pretraining loss. The Hessian is approximated using Fisher Information (KFAC-style), and the input activation covariance E[xx^T] is the key quantity. Under this framework, optimizer A_{alpha,beta} regularizes input activations by ||·||_alpha and output activations by ||·||_beta. When the SFT optimizer matches the pretraining optimizer, the weight update ΔW minimizes the forgetting loss given the activation structure of the pretrained model (Theorem 1, proved in Appendix C).

Memorization vs. pattern-learning experiment. To test the Muon memorization hypothesis, the authors construct a synthetic mixed corpus from OpenWebText containing 50% clean sequences and 50% token-permuted (corrupted) sequences. Since corrupted sequences have no learnable patterns, accuracy on them can only come from rote memorization. Models trained with Muon reach high corrupted memorization accuracy significantly earlier than AdamW or SGD, while having lower clean pattern accuracy, supporting the hypothesis.

Results & Highlights

  • GPT-2 full finetuning Pareto dominance: Across all three SFT tasks (Alpaca, MetaMathQA, Magicoder) and both pretraining checkpoints (AdamW-pretrained and Muon-pretrained), the same optimizer as pretraining always achieves the best Pareto frontier. SGD can learn from small data but forgets heavily.
  • Llama-2-7B: Full finetuning with AdamW (matching its pretraining optimizer) on MetaMathQA yields a better learning-forgetting tradeoff than LoRA (ranks 16/64/256) and other full finetuning optimizers — the conclusion holds even without weight decay.
  • LoRA vs. full FT: For any single LoRA checkpoint, there exists a full finetuning checkpoint (with a smaller learning rate) that simultaneously learns more and forgets less. Larger LoRA rank generally gives a better tradeoff but still does not reach the full FT Pareto frontier.
  • Muon case study (GPT-2):
  • Muon pretrained models show better downstream performance before SFT (Table 1: MetaMathQA val loss 3.200 vs. AdamW 3.510).
  • After SFT, Muon-pretrained + Muon-finetuned achieves dramatically better tradeoff on Alpaca but only marginal/negative gains on MetaMathQA vs. AdamW-pretrained + AdamW-finetuned.
  • Memorization experiment (Table 3): Muon achieves 65.0% corrupted memorization accuracy at (128,1) vs. AdamW's 55.8% and SGD's 32.9%, while scoring lower on clean data than SGD (88.9% vs. 93.4%), confirming the memorization-before-patterns tendency.
  • Reproducing Biderman et al.: The apparent contradiction with Biderman et al. (2024)'s claim that "LoRA forgets less" is reconciled — their finding holds only at the single learning rate that maximizes task performance; over a proper LR sweep, full finetuning Pareto-dominates LoRA (Figure 8).

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The "optimizer-model consistency" framing is genuinely novel as an explicit, named principle. Prior work (Liu et al., 2025 on instruction following; Biderman et al., 2024) touched adjacent observations but did not systematically establish the Pareto-frontier view or the theoretical mechanism. The Muon memorization hypothesis and its synthetic verification are original contributions.

Rigor: The Pareto-frontier methodology is methodologically sound and more honest than single-LR comparisons. Theorem 1 is formally proved under four stated assumptions. However, the assumptions are non-trivial: Assumption 4 (optimizer regularization of activations) is empirically motivated but only shown to hold approximately. The theory applies to a simplified single-layer linear setting; whether it cleanly extends to deep transformers with layer norm, residual connections, and non-linear activations requires further justification.

Reproducibility: Experiment settings are detailed in Appendix A (hardware, hyperparameter grids, learning rate schedules). GPT-2 code is based on the public NanoGPT codebase; Llama-2 uses LlamaFactory. The main missing piece is that experiments with Muon-pretrained Llama-2-7B are deferred to future work because no public Muon-pretrained 7B checkpoint existed at submission time. This limits validation of the key claim at scale for the Muon side.

Significance: The practical implication is clear and actionable: when finetuning LLMs, use the same optimizer as pretraining for full finetuning if the goal is to minimize forgetting while maximizing learning. This is a meaningful guideline for practitioners. The Muon finding has implications for the ongoing adoption of Muon in frontier model training (Kimi, DeepSeek).

Red flags:

  • All full-FT experiments are at GPT-2 scale (123M); the Llama-2-7B experiment covers only AdamW-pretrained models. The claim is plausible but not fully validated at frontier model scale.
  • The theoretical result (Theorem 1) holds under idealized assumptions that the paper acknowledges are approximate. The gap between the theoretical story and the actual mechanism in deep networks with complex architectures is not quantified.
  • The Muon memorization hypothesis, while well-motivated and supported by the synthetic experiment, is indirect evidence. A more direct ablation (e.g., controlling update rank during SFT) would strengthen the causal claim.
  • The paper does not compare against continual learning baselines (EWC, etc.), focusing narrowly on optimizer choice. The framing as a solution to learning-forgetting tradeoffs should acknowledge this scope limitation.

Strengths

  • Introduces a clear, testable phenomenon (optimizer-model consistency) with both empirical evidence and theoretical backing.
  • The Pareto-frontier evaluation methodology is more rigorous and informative than single-LR comparisons used in prior work; it also reconciles apparent contradictions in the literature.
  • Covers three qualitatively different SFT tasks (instruction following, math reasoning, coding), making the empirical finding robust across task types.
  • The Muon case study is timely and practically relevant given Muon's increasing adoption in frontier LLM training pipelines.
  • The synthetic memorization experiment is creative and provides mechanistic insight into optimizer behavior.
  • Formal theorem with complete proof in appendix; assumptions are stated explicitly rather than handwaved.

---

Limitations

  • Experiments are primarily at GPT-2-small (123M) scale; Llama-2-7B experiments cover only one task and only the AdamW case. Generalization to billion-parameter models trained with Muon remains unverified.
  • Theoretical analysis assumes a simplified setting (single linear layer, block-diagonal Hessian approximation, Fisher Information approximation). The theory is at best a plausibility argument for the mechanism, not a tight characterization.
  • The paper does not propose a new method — it identifies a phenomenon and provides a practical recommendation. Practitioners using Muon for pretraining face a dilemma: the paper identifies this but does not resolve it (Remark 3).
  • No experiments on instruction-tuned or RLHF-trained models, or on multi-epoch SFT; the findings may not transfer to all SFT regimes.
  • Comparison against continual learning methods (EWC, replay, etc.) is absent, which would place the optimizer-consistency approach in a broader context.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does optimizer-model consistency hold at 7B+ scale for Muon-pretrained models (e.g., once open Muon-pretrained checkpoints become available)? Does the Muon SFT degradation on math tasks persist or diminish at scale?
  2. Can a modified Muon update rule (e.g., restricting full-rank updates to early SFT, or adding explicit pattern-learning bias) recover Muon's pretraining advantage while avoiding the memorization shortcut in SFT?
  3. The theory relies on a block-diagonal Hessian approximation (KFAC-style). How much does the conclusion change if the full Hessian structure or inter-layer dependencies are accounted for?
  4. Does the optimizer-model consistency principle extend to reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) or preference optimization (DPO), where the "forgetting" of SFT capabilities is also a known concern?
  5. Is there a principled way to exploit optimizer-model consistency when the pretraining optimizer is unknown (e.g., for third-party model weights), by reverse-engineering activation statistics to infer the likely optimizer family?
  6. How does this interact with learning rate scheduling choices? The concurrent Rofin et al. (2026) work links learning rate to catastrophic overtraining — is optimizer consistency orthogonal or interacting with LR choice in the Pareto sense?
