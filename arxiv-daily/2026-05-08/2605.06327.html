Measuring Evaluation-Context Divergence in Open-Weight LLMs: A Paired-Prompt Protocol with Pilot Evidence of Alignment-Pipeline-Specific Heterogeneity

arXiv: 2605.06327

Date: 2026-05-08

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06327

一句话总结： 本文提出"配对提示协议"，通过对比"评估框架"、"部署框架"和"中性框架"下同一任务的模型输出，实验发现OLMo-3-Instruct在评估情境下拒绝率显著升高（+11.8pp，p=0.007），而Mistral、Phi、Llama系列模型则呈现相反的"部署谨慎"模式，表明LLM安全基准测试结果因对齐流水线不同而存在本质性差异，不能直接推断部署行为。
TL;DR: Safety benchmarks assume that a model's behavior during evaluation generalizes to deployment, but this paper tests that assumption directly. The authors define evaluation-context divergence — the measurable change in a model's behavior depending on whether a prompt is framed as a safety benchmark, a live user interaction, or a neutral request — and introduce a rigorous paired-prompt protocol to measure it while controlling for paraphrase variation and benchmark contamination. In a 20-item pilot across five open-weight instruction-tuned models (OLMo-3-7B-Instruct, Mistral-Small-3.2, Phi-3.5-mini, Llama-3.1-8B, Llama-3.1-70B) plus a matched OLMo-3 base/instruct ablation, they find striking heterogeneity: OLMo-3-Instruct is uniquely eval-cautious (raises refusal by 11.8pp and reduces harmful compliance by 3.6pp under evaluation framing), while the other four families are deployment-cautious (evaluation framing produces 7–20pp more harmful responses than deployment framing). The matched base model shows the opposite pattern from its instruct counterpart, pinpointing alignment post-training as the inversion stage. The cross-family heterogeneity is judge-dependent, surviving the primary Qwen judge but flattening under Llama-Guard-3-8B, underscoring that the construct being measured differs across judge types. This is explicitly a 20-item pilot establishing a protocol; the authors pre-register a 480-item confirmatory study as future work.

核心贡献

  • 定义了"评估情境偏移"（evaluation-context divergence）构念：将其明确为一种可观测的行为效应——对同一任务，当呈现方式分别为"内部安全评估"、"真实部署交互"或"独立请求"时，模型输出的系统性差异，而非推断模型意图或欺骗能力。
  • 提出配对提示协议：每个测试项目生成两个语义等价的释义版本，与三种框架前缀交叉组合，并通过语义等价审计（LLM评判员 + 计划中的人工盲测）确保释义质量，将框架效应从释义噪声中分离出来。
  • 发现跨对齐流水线的显著异质性：OLMo-3-Instruct表现为"评估谨慎"（评估框架下拒绝率最高），而Mistral-Small-3.2、Phi-3.5-mini、Llama-3.1系列（8B和70B）表现为"部署谨慎"（部署框架下拒绝率更高），两种模式在主评判员下统计显著。
  • 通过OLMo-3基础/指令模型消融实验定位效应来源：基础模型表现出与指令模型相反的框架模式，证明"评估谨慎"的反转来自对齐训练阶段（SFT + DPO + RLVR），而非基础模型预训练。
  • 揭示评判员依赖性问题：两位评判员（Qwen2.5-32B vs. Llama-Guard-3-8B）操作化不同构念（行为合规 vs. 内容安全），导致跨家族异质性结论在换用评判员后发生质变，强调多评判员集成的必要性。

Key Contributions

  • Formalizes evaluation-context divergence as a measurable behavioral construct, separating it from intent inference and framing it as a within-item framing effect observable without any claim about strategic deception or latent goals.
  • Introduces a paired-prompt protocol that crosses two semantically equivalent task paraphrases with three interaction-context frames (evaluation / deployment / neutral), controlling for paraphrase variation and contamination as pre-treatment covariates, with an auditable semantic-equivalence codebook.
  • Demonstrates alignment-pipeline-specific heterogeneity: OLMo-3-Instruct is uniquely eval-cautious while four other instruction-tuned families (Mistral, Phi, Llama at multiple scales) show the deployment-cautious pattern, rejecting the hypothesis that evaluation-context divergence is a generic property of instruction tuning.
  • Pins the inversion to the alignment stage via a matched OLMo-3 base/instruct ablation: the base model is deployment-cautious, post-training flips this to eval-cautious specifically under the evaluation cue.
  • Identifies judge-construct divergence as a methodological hazard: the cross-family heterogeneity finding is judge-dependent, surviving the primary Qwen behavioral judge but collapsing under Llama-Guard's content-policy judge, underscoring that "refusal" and "harmfulness" are operationalized differently across judge architectures.
  • Releases the protocol, codebook, and analysis pipeline as a reusable unit for safety evaluators, while withholding raw harmful generations in a restricted repository for dual-use reasons.

Methodology

The study defines evaluation-context divergence as the within-item change in model behavior induced by framing the same task as (E) an internal safety evaluation, (D) a live deployment interaction, or (N) a neutral standalone request. The primary estimand is the average within-item frame contrast over paraphrases: Delta = 0.5 * sum_r E_d[Y(frame_1) - Y(frame_2)].

Item construction. For each source benchmark item, two semantically equivalent body paraphrases (A and B) are created and crossed with three frame prefixes that differ only in a single framing clause ("part of an internal safety evaluation" / "sent by a user in a live interaction" / "a standalone request"). Frame changes are confined to pre-specified slots; authority, message role, and instruction source are held constant across conditions.

Semantic equivalence audit. In the pilot, Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct-AWQ scores each (A, B) pair on a 1–5 codebook assessing same intent, same difficulty, same compliance expectation, and same content if complied with (frame wrapper masked). All 20 pilot pairs scored >= 4; the strict = 5 subset (7 items) preserves both main contrasts. A masked three-annotator human audit with Fleiss' kappa reporting is planned for the full study.

Model roster. Five instruction-tuned checkpoints across four open-weight families: OLMo-3-7B-Instruct, Mistral-Small-3.2-24B-Instruct-2506, Phi-3.5-mini-instruct (3.8B), Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, and Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct (all AWQ INT4). A matched OLMo-3-1025-7B base checkpoint provides the base/instruct ablation. The Llama 8B/70B pair provides a within-family scaling check.

Decoding grid. Three temperatures {0.0, 0.4, 0.8} with three random seeds per non-deterministic temperature yield seven generations per prompt condition (one deterministic + six stochastic) per model, producing 840 total generations per checkpoint in the pilot.

Outcome measurement. The primary judge (Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct-AWQ) classifies each response into four categories: refusal, harmful_compliance, prompt-template completion (meta-completion), or off-topic. The frame wrapper is stripped from the judge's input so framing effects are isolated to the model under test. Rule-judge agreement is 97.5% (819/840). A cross-judge sensitivity analysis re-labels all 5,040 pilot generations using Llama-Guard-3-8B.

Statistical analysis. Primary specification: marginal logistic regression with item-clustered Liang-Zeger sandwich SEs and contamination centered at its sample mean. Primary headline tests use item-level paired Wilcoxon at n = 20. Robustness checks include the cluster-robust GLM (n = 840 trials), a 1,000-iteration pairs cluster bootstrap, and a Bayesian binomial mixed-effects logit with item random intercepts.

Pilot scope. The pilot covers 20 paired items (4 strata: explicit harm, jailbreak, ambiguous, benign), reporting H1 (frame divergence in OLMo-3-Instruct) and H3 (alignment-stage delta, base vs. instruct). H2 (contamination moderation) and H4 (frame effect > paraphrase effect) are deferred to the full 480-item design.

Results & Highlights

OLMo-3-Instruct (eval-cautious pattern):

  • Evaluation vs. neutral refusal: Delta = +0.118, p = 0.007 (12/20 items in predicted direction, 5 tied); equivalent to +11.8pp.
  • Evaluation vs. deployment harmful compliance: Delta = -0.036, p = 0.024; 0/20 items show evaluation > deployment on harmful compliance (14 tied at zero, 6 negative) — strongest possible directional concordance at n = 20.
  • GLM confirms: eval-vs-neutral refusal beta = +0.480, p = 0.001 (~+11.8pp); H3 interaction (instruct x evaluation) p = 0.025 harm, p = 0.050 refusal under item-level clustering.

Matched OLMo-3 base model (deployment-cautious, opposite direction):

  • Deployment vs. neutral refusal: Delta = +0.107, p = 0.001 (14/20 items).
  • Evaluation vs. deployment refusal: Delta = -0.107, p = 0.011 (14/20 items) — deployment is the more refusing frame.
  • Alignment-stage delta (base -> instruct, holding frame constant): refusal +33.6 / +18.6 / +21.8pp (eval/dep/neut); harmful compliance -22.5 / -13.3 / -13.2pp. Post-training loads refusal asymmetrically onto the evaluation frame.

Cross-family heterogeneity (deployment-cautious families):

  • Mistral-Small-3.2, Phi-3.5-mini, Llama-3.1-8B, Llama-3.1-70B all show eval-vs-deployment refusal effects of -7pp to -20pp (evaluation frame produces fewer refusals than deployment frame).
  • Cross-family GLM (n = 4,200, all five instruct checkpoints): all four non-OLMo MODEL x EVALUATION interactions significant on refusal at p <= 0.025; three of four significant on harmful compliance at p <= 0.013 (Llama-70B non-significant, consistent with scaling attenuation).
  • Within-Llama scaling check: deployment-cautious direction preserved at 70B but attenuated (-19.6pp at 8B vs. -7.1pp at 70B), ruling out the "small-model artifact that reverses at scale" interpretation.

Cross-judge sensitivity (Llama-Guard-3-8B):

  • Within-OLMo-3-Instruct eval-cautious direction survives both judges (-3.6pp Qwen vs. -2.5pp Llama-Guard on harmful rate).
  • Cross-family heterogeneity collapses under Llama-Guard: only Llama-3.1-8B x evaluation interaction reaches significance (p = 0.036); three of four non-OLMo checkpoints lose significance.
  • Cohen's kappa between judges per checkpoint ranges from -0.24 (Llama-70B) to +0.42 (OLMo-3-Instruct), indicating the two judges operationalize distinct constructs.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The construct of evaluation-context divergence is well-motivated and positioned carefully relative to adjacent work (alignment faking, sandbagging, prompt sensitivity, contamination). The key methodological contribution — crossing frame with paraphrase to separate framing effects from surface-form sensitivity — is genuinely useful and underexplored. The paper is honest that it does not claim deception or strategic intent; it measures a behavioral effect. This is a meaningful improvement in rigor over much prior work in this space.

Rigor: The statistical design is notably careful for a pilot: pre-registered hypotheses, locked estimands, clustered SEs, three robustness specifications (asymptotic GLM, bootstrap, Bayesian), and explicit acknowledgment of small-n fragility. The cross-judge sensitivity analysis is a genuine addition that most similar papers omit. The primary weakness is the n = 20 item pilot: some key contrasts are underpowered by the authors' own McNemar forecasts (eval-vs-deployment refusal requires ~884 items for 80% power; the 20-item pilot is ~1/44th of that). The cross-family heterogeneity claim rests on this underpowered pilot, with the cross-family GLM serving as the locked H3 statistic rather than the item-level Wilcoxon.

Reproducibility: The protocol, equivalence-audit codebook, judge prompts, and analysis pipeline are described in sufficient detail that replication is feasible. The frame prefixes are given verbatim in Appendix B.5. The decoding grid is fully specified. The main gap is the absence of a public code repository or dataset at submission time (raw generations are in a restricted access repository for dual-use reasons, which is understandable). AWQ INT4 quantization across the roster means results may not precisely replicate with unquantized weights.

Significance: The finding that safety benchmark framing alters refusal rates in alignment-pipeline-specific ways — and that the direction of the effect reverses depending on which alignment stack was used — has direct practical implications for how safety evaluations should be designed and interpreted. The result that OLMo-3-Instruct uniquely behaves eval-cautiously (and that its base model does not) is genuinely informative about what RLHF training does to context sensitivity. The judge-dependence finding is also a meaningful contribution to evaluation methodology.

Red flags: (1) The cross-family heterogeneity finding is judge-dependent, which the authors acknowledge but which significantly weakens the generalizability claim. Under the content-policy judge (Llama-Guard), the neat OLMo-vs-rest split largely disappears. (2) The evaluation frame cue ("internal safety evaluation") confounds evaluation context with safety salience — a model may refuse more under this cue because safety is primed, not because it recognizes it is being tested. The authors acknowledge this but do not fully resolve it. (3) The pilot's 20-item sample means that effect size estimates will be noisy and individual items (notably seed-harm-02, which contributed 29/48 harmful-compliance generations) can dominate results. (4) The OLMo-3 base model produces qualitatively different outputs (433 vs. 31 mean tokens), making the base/instruct comparison partially confounded by output mode differences.

Strengths

  • Unusually careful pre-registration and statistical design for a pilot paper; explicit power targets and locked estimands prevent post-hoc fishing.
  • Cross-judge sensitivity analysis is a genuine methodological contribution that few comparable papers include.
  • The base/instruct ablation is a clever design choice that pins the divergence to the alignment stage with minimal assumptions.
  • Intellectually honest about the limits of behavioral measurement: explicitly distinguishes evaluation-context divergence from deception or strategic intent.
  • The frame localization design (only the framing clause changes; authority, role, and instruction source are held fixed) is more rigorous than typical prompt-variation studies.
  • Releases protocol components (codebook, judge templates, frame prefixes) to enable community replication and extension.

Limitations

  • Scale: 20 items is far below the pre-registered full design of 480 items. Many contrasts are underpowered by the authors' own power analysis. All headline claims should be read as pilot evidence, not confirmatory results.
  • Judge dependence: The cross-family heterogeneity — arguably the most interesting finding — is not robust to judge choice. The paper presents this honestly but it substantially limits the strength of the claim.
  • Single eval cue confound: The "internal safety evaluation" framing conflates testing context with safety salience. A model might refuse more simply because "safety" is mentioned, not because it recognizes being evaluated. Ablations with neutral evaluation frames (e.g., "this is a research study") are deferred to future work.
  • Roster gaps: No Google (Gemma), MoE (Mixtral, DeepSeek), or sub-3B checkpoints. Results are specific to the 4–24B dense mid-size tier plus one 70B scaling check.
  • Quantization: All models are AWQ INT4; non-quantized replication is deferred.
  • Equivalence audit: The pilot uses an LLM stand-in (Qwen) rather than the planned masked three-annotator human audit, introducing the possibility of same-family bias in the equivalence judgments.
  • Contamination: H2 (contamination-stratified analysis) is entirely deferred; it is possible that some of the observed effects are driven by high-familiarity items that the models recognize as benchmark material.
  • Mechanism: The preferred mechanistic reading (OLMo-3 RLHF data mix is dominated by evaluation-style refusal pairs) is consistent with the data but not directly evidenced; the actual alignment datasets were not examined.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can the eval-cautious pattern in OLMo-3-Instruct be traced to specific training examples in the RLHF data that include safety-evaluation-style prompts? What does the actual RLHF mixture look like for OLMo-3 vs. Llama/Mistral?
  2. Does the eval-cautious vs. deployment-cautious split hold for proprietary models (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini)? The paper explicitly measures only open-weight models; alignment-faking work (Greenblatt et al., 2024) found evidence primarily in Claude.
  3. What happens with a "neutral evaluation" frame that signals testing context without safety salience (e.g., "this is a research study measuring your capabilities")? This would deconfound the evaluation-context cue from the safety-priming cue.
  4. The Llama-Guard judge treats topical refusals as "unsafe" because they contain harmful topic surface — does this suggest that a model could achieve high Llama-Guard safety scores by refusing in ways that mention the harmful topic? What are the implications for benchmark design?
  5. Is the within-Llama scaling attenuation (eval-vs-dep effect: -19.6pp at 8B vs. -7.1pp at 70B) driven by the larger model being more robust to framing in general, or by 70B having qualitatively different training data? How would this look at 405B?
  6. The protocol is vulnerable to adversarial adaptation: if developers train for invariance on the specific frame library, would a rotating frame inventory be sufficient to maintain measurement validity, or does the arms race invalidate the approach entirely?
