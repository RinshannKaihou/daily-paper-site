Pro-KLShampoo: Projected KL-Shampoo with Whitening Recovered by Orthogonalization

arXiv: 2605.06316

Date: 2026-05-08

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06316

一句话总结： Pro-KLShampoo 通过识别 KL-Shampoo 预条件器特征值谱的"尖峰-平坦"结构，将其 Kronecker 因子限制在低秩参数族内，并用正交化替代标量尾部更新，在 GPT-2（124M/350M）和 LLaMA（134M/450M）四个规模的预训练中，相比 KL-Shampoo 在验证损失、峰值显存和达到相同损失所需挂钟时间上全面占优。
TL;DR: Pro-KLShampoo is a new LLM optimizer that improves KL-Shampoo by exploiting an empirically observed "spike-and-flat" eigenvalue structure in its Kronecker preconditioners. It restricts one preconditioner factor to a low-rank parametric family (full spectral structure on a tracked r-dimensional subspace, single scalar on the complement), then replaces the scalar complement update with Muon-style orthogonalization — which is proven to algebraically recover full KL-Shampoo's whitening on those directions. Evaluated on GPT-2 (124M, 350M) and LLaMA (134M, 450M), Pro-KLShampoo beats KL-Shampoo at every tested subspace rank (r=32, 64, 128) in validation loss, peak-GPU memory, and wallclock time to reach matched loss levels (up to ~13% wallclock saving on LLaMA-450M). The paper is theoretically grounded with an O(1/√T) nonconvex convergence guarantee.

Key Contributions

  • Spike-and-flat eigenvalue observation: The paper identifies that KL-Shampoo's Kronecker preconditioner eigenvalue spectra consistently exhibit a spike-and-flat shape (a few dominant eigenvalues followed by a nearly uniform tail) across all layer types, depths, and training stages on both GPT-2 and LLaMA. This holds exactly under a rank-ρ signal-plus-noise gradient model.
  • Restricted KL objective: One Kronecker factor (the right-side preconditioner R) is constrained to a parametric spike-and-flat family parameterized by a Stiefel-manifold subspace U, an r×r SPD matrix S, and a scalar tail μ⊥. The optimal subspace under this restriction is proven (Claim 3) to be the top-r eigenspace of ΦL that minimizes the AM/GM ratio of the complement eigenvalues.
  • Orthogonalization identity: A key algebraic identity (Eq. 12) shows that applying the polar operator to the complement gradient recovers the exact algebraic form of full KL-Shampoo's preconditioned update on those directions, providing a principled (non-heuristic) justification for using Muon-style orthogonalization within the KL framework.
  • Convergence guarantee: Theorem 1 establishes O(1/√T) nonconvex convergence under operator-norm smoothness, with a state-dependent measure that vanishes if and only if the gradient is zero.
  • Empirical superiority: Pro-KLShampoo achieves lower validation loss at every tested rank (r ∈ {32, 64, 128}), peak-GPU memory at or below KL-Shampoo (on par with Muon), and wallclock savings of 2.28% (GPT-2 124M), 4.12% (GPT-2 350M), 13.43% (LLaMA 134M), 10.96% (LLaMA 134M) relative to KL-Shampoo.

Methodology

Motivation and structural observation. The starting point is an empirical observation: the eigenvalue spectra of KL-Shampoo's Kronecker preconditioner factors L and R consistently display a spike-and-flat shape. A small number of dominant eigenvalues (the "spike") are followed by an approximately uniform tail. This pattern is robust across layer types (attention, MLP), model depths, and training stages. Under a rank-ρ signal-plus-noise gradient model, the KL stationary points have at least n−ρ equal bottom eigenvalues for R and m−ρ for L, confirming the structure holds exactly in an idealized model.

Restricted preconditioner family. Based on this observation, the right-side preconditioner is restricted to the form:

R̂ = USUᵀ + μ⊥P⊥

where U ∈ St(n, r) is an r-dimensional subspace basis tracked by subspace iteration, S ∈ S^r_{++} is the full spectral structure within the subspace, μ⊥ > 0 is a scalar approximating the uniform tail, and P⊥ = Iₙ − UUᵀ is the complement projection. This reduces the storage cost of R from O(n²) to O(nr) and the QR decomposition cost from O(n³) to O(r³).

Restricted KL objective and stationarity. The restricted KL problem minimizes D_KL(N(0, Σ) ∥ N(0, L ⊗ R̂)) over L, S, U, μ⊥. For fixed U, the optimal L, S, μ⊥* are given in closed form (Claim 2) as gradient second moments mutually whitened by each other's inverses. The optimal U (Claim 3) is the top-r eigenspace of ΦL = E[GᵀL⁻¹G], which is tracked in practice via subspace iteration on fresh minibatch samples.

Preconditioned gradient decomposition. The preconditioned gradient L^{-1/2} G R̂^{-1/2} decomposes into two parts: (1) a full KL-Shampoo update on the subspace, L^{-1/2} G U S^{-1/2} Uᵀ; and (2) a one-sided (only left-preconditioned) complement term, μ⊥^{-1/2} L^{-1/2} G P⊥. The algorithm called "Smok-Hop" uses this decomposition directly. However, the scalar tail μ⊥ loses information when the tail is not exactly uniform.

Recovering full whitening via orthogonalization. The key insight is that the complement term can be improved by replacing the scalar right-preconditioner μ⊥^{-1/2} with orthogonalization (the polar operator). An algebraic identity (Eq. 12) proves that:

polar(μ⊥^{-1/2} L^{-1/2} G P⊥) = L^{-1/2} G U⊥ (U⊥ᵀ G^ᵀ L^{*-1} G U⊥)^{1/2} U⊥ᵀ

which exactly matches the algebraic form of full KL-Shampoo's complement update Δ⊥^full, with full KL-Shampoo's stationary quantities replaced by Pro-KLShampoo's running EMA estimates. This is not a heuristic approximation but a principled algebraic equivalence.

Full algorithm (Algorithm 1). The idealized Pro-KLShampoo update is:

ΔW = −η [α_kl L^{-1/2} G U S^{-1/2} Uᵀ + c_a polar(μ⊥^{-1/2} L^{-1/2} G P⊥)]

where c_a = √max(1, m/n) is an aspect-ratio scaling factor and α_kl ∈ [10⁻³, 2×10⁻²] is a mixing weight calibrated to match the operator-norm of the orthogonalized complement to the original complement update. The practical implementation (Appendix J) adds Nesterov momentum, Newton–Schulz iteration for the polar approximation, and eigenvalue clipping.

Convergence analysis. Theorem 1 proves O(1/√T) convergence for the idealized algorithm under L_op-operator-norm smoothness, uniform lower bound on preconditioner eigenvalues (Lemma 3), and unbiased gradient with bounded operator norm. The convergence measure (LHS of Eq. 13) is state-dependent: it splits the gradient via the running subspace (U_t, P_{⊥,t}), contributes a nuclear-norm term from the orthogonalized complement and a squared-Frobenius term from the subspace update. Lemma 1 confirms this measure vanishes iff ∇f(W) = 0.

Results & Highlights

Main quantitative results (Table 1):

| Optimizer | GPT-2 124M loss | GPT-2 350M loss | LLaMA 134M loss | LLaMA 450M loss |

|---|---|---|---|---|

| AdamW | 3.4944 | 3.2063 | 3.0871 | 2.8248 |

| Muon | 3.2841 | 3.0407 | 3.0536 | 2.7578 |

| KL-Shampoo | 3.2796 | 3.0397 | 3.0504 | 2.7650 |

| Pro-KLShampoo (r=128) | 3.2745 | 3.0344 | 3.0335 | 2.7453 |

Pro-KLShampoo achieves the best validation loss at every scale, and peak-GPU memory is at or below KL-Shampoo and on par with Muon.

Wallclock savings (time to reach matched validation loss vs. KL-Shampoo):

  • GPT-2 124M: ~2.28%
  • GPT-2 350M: ~4.12%
  • LLaMA 134M: ~13.43%
  • LLaMA 450M: ~10.96%

The mechanism differs by architecture: on GPT-2, savings come primarily from reduced per-step time (replacing the large-side QR decomposition of size 3072×4096 with one of size r, where r ≪ n). On LLaMA, the weight dimensions are more balanced (2048×768 at 134M), so per-step costs are similar and savings come from faster convergence (lower training loss throughout, consistent with a generalization benefit).

Ablation study (Table 2, r=128): Two ablations isolate the two components:

  • Subspace-only (zeroes complement update): 3.7803 on GPT-124M, 3.4598 on LLaMA-134M — substantially worse.
  • Complement-only (zeroes subspace update): 3.4015 and 3.1075 — better than subspace-only but still worse than full Pro-KLShampoo.
  • Smok-Hop (complement without orthogonalization): 3.2791 and 3.0476 — competitive with KL-Shampoo at smaller scales but degrades at LLaMA-450M (gap 0.025 vs. 0.014 at 134M), confirming orthogonalization is critical at larger scale.

Both components are necessary, and complement-only is stronger than subspace-only, suggesting the orthogonalized complement direction drives most of the improvement.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The core insight — that KL-Shampoo preconditioners have spike-and-flat spectra and that orthogonalization algebraically recovers full KL-Shampoo whitening on the complement — is genuinely novel and theoretically clean. The algebraic identity (Eq. 12) is the paper's crown jewel: it bridges Muon-style orthogonalization with the KL estimation framework in a principled way, rather than ad-hoc combination. The spike-and-flat observation itself echoes prior work (GaLore, COSMOS, gradient descent in a tiny subspace), but the specific connection to KL-Shampoo's stationary structure is new.

Rigor: The theoretical claims (approximation gap Claim 1, restricted stationarity Claim 2, optimal subspace Claim 3, convergence Theorem 1) are stated with precise conditions and proofs in the appendix. The approximation gap bound (AM/GM ratio of tail eigenvalues) is tight and well-motivated. The convergence result covers the idealized algorithm; extending it to the practical variant with Newton–Schulz and Nesterov momentum is left open, which is an honest limitation.

Reproducibility: The algorithm is fully specified in Algorithm 1 (idealized) and Appendix J (practical). Hyperparameter ranges and sweep details are provided. The experiments use publicly available architectures and datasets (GPT-2 on FineWeb-10B, LLaMA on C4). Code is not yet publicly linked in the preprint, which is a minor concern. The mixing weight α_kl has a principled derivation but still requires a small sweep (3 values tested: {0.005, 0.01, 0.015}).

Significance: This paper addresses a practically important problem — making second-order optimizers competitive with AdamW and Muon for LLM pretraining in both quality and efficiency. The wallclock improvements (up to 13%) are meaningful for large-scale training. The theoretical unification of KL-based preconditioning and orthogonalization is also conceptually significant.

Red flags:

  • The convergence guarantee covers only the idealized algorithm, not the practical implementation. This gap between theory and practice is acknowledged but unresolved.
  • The rank r is set empirically and fixed per-layer. Per-layer or adaptive rank selection could further improve the method but is left for future work.
  • Calibration of α_kl is principled in derivation but still empirically swept; the per-layer calibration bound is intersected across layers to get a single scalar, introducing approximation.
  • The experiments cap at 450M parameters; whether the trends (especially the LLaMA-450M 13% wallclock saving) hold at billion-parameter scales is unknown.
  • No comparison to COSMOS (Liu et al., 2025) in the main tables (only in Appendix M), which is a competitive hybrid baseline.

---

Strengths

  • The spike-and-flat structural observation is empirically robust and theoretically grounded under the signal-plus-noise gradient model; it provides the first principled motivation for low-rank Kronecker preconditioning within the KL estimation framework.
  • The algebraic identity (Eq. 12) is elegant and non-trivial: it shows that orthogonalization on the complement is not a heuristic but exactly recovers full KL-Shampoo's complement update in algebraic form.
  • The method unifies two previously separate lines of work (KL-Shampoo's explicit preconditioning and Muon's orthogonalization), offering a cleaner theoretical picture of why combining them works.
  • Ablation study cleanly isolates the contributions of each component (subspace update, complement orthogonalization) and shows that both are necessary.
  • Memory efficiency: Pro-KLShampoo achieves KL-Shampoo-level or better validation loss with Muon-level peak memory, which is a practically attractive profile.
  • Consistent improvement across all tested configurations (4 scales, 3 ranks, 2 architectures) without cherry-picking.

---

Limitations

  • Convergence theory gap: The O(1/√T) guarantee applies only to the idealized update (no momentum, no Newton–Schulz approximation). The practical algorithm's convergence properties remain theoretically unanalyzed.
  • Fixed empirical rank: The rank r is chosen empirically and uniformly; per-layer adaptive rank selection (which could better match the actual spike width) is left for future work. At r=32, the subspace may not capture all dominant eigenvalues for some layers.
  • Scale: Experiments are limited to ≤450M parameters. The method's behavior at billion-scale models (where memory and time tradeoffs may shift) is unknown.
  • α_kl calibration: The mixing weight is derived from a principled per-layer range but still requires a small grid search. Online or per-layer adaptive calibration could eliminate this hyperparameter.
  • EMA subspace tracking: Convergence and stability of EMA-based preconditioner estimation and subspace tracking under the restricted KL objective are not formally analyzed; a dedicated convergence analysis for this component is needed.
  • No public code in the preprint version, making immediate reproduction dependent on implementation details in the appendix.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does the spike-and-flat structure of KL-Shampoo's Kronecker preconditioner persist at billion-parameter scale (e.g., 7B LLaMA), and does the wallclock saving from reduced QR cost remain the dominant factor there?
  2. Can the rank r be selected per-layer and adapted online based on the AM/GM ratio of the estimated tail eigenvalues, and what is the practical overhead of such adaptive selection?
  3. The algebraic identity (Eq. 12) connects orthogonalization to KL-Shampoo whitening on the complement — does an analogous identity exist for other norm-based optimizers (e.g., modular-norm steepest descent), suggesting a broader unification principle?
  4. The convergence guarantee requires a uniform lower bound on preconditioner eigenvalues (enforced by clipping). What is the sensitivity of the method to the clipping threshold, and can this be removed with a different analysis?
  5. COSMOS (Liu et al., 2025) also decomposes updates into a SOAP-preconditioned subspace and a Muon-treated complement. How does Pro-KLShampoo's principled KL-based derivation compare to COSMOS's construction in terms of the resulting update rule, and what explains the performance differences?
  6. The paper shows that complement-only updates are stronger than subspace-only at both tested scales. Does this suggest that the orthogonalized complement captures most of the optimization signal, and could a complement-only variant (essentially a Muon-style optimizer with left KL-Shampoo preconditioning) be competitive at lower memory cost?
