Normalized Architectures are Natively 4-Bit

["Maxim Fishman", "Brian Chmiel", "Ron Banner", "Daniel Soudry", "Boris Ginsburg"]

arXiv: 2605.06067

Date: 2026-05-08

Tags: ["quantization", "nGPT", "low-precision training", "LLM efficiency", "NVFP4"]

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06067

一句话总结： nGPT（超球面归一化Transformer）通过使信号在高维点积求和中相干累积（信噪比提升约7 dB），无需随机Hadamard变换或逐张量缩放，即可实现稳定的端到端NVFP4训练，在1.2B稠密模型和3B/30B混合Mamba-Transformer MoE上验证有效，吞吐量最高提升3.6×。
TL;DR: This NVIDIA paper argues that 4-bit (NVFP4) quantization robustness need not be patched onto an architecture — it can be baked in structurally. The normalized Transformer (nGPT), which constrains all weights and activations to the unit hypersphere, achieves ~7 dB higher dot-product signal-to-noise ratio than standard GPT under NVFP4, not by reducing quantization noise but by making the signal accumulate coherently across thousands of dimensions. This eliminates the need for expensive engineering interventions (Randomized Hadamard Transforms, dynamic per-tensor scaling) while achieving lower validation loss and superior downstream task performance at scales up to 3B/30B hybrid MoE models, with up to 3.3–3.6× throughput speedup on NVIDIA Blackwell GPUs.

核心贡献

  • 架构即量化方案：首次证明nGPT的单位超球面约束使其天然具备NVFP4鲁棒性，无需RHT、逐张量缩放等后处理干预，从根本上将量化鲁棒性视为架构属性而非算法补丁。
  • 信号累积机制揭示：通过层级信噪比分解，证明nGPT的优势完全来自信号相干累积（点积求和阶段+9.4 dB vs GPT的+2.2 dB），而非噪声抑制——两种架构的量化噪声统计量几乎相同。
  • 宽度可扩展的SNR优势：推导出三区间缩放律，表明在当前主流模型宽度（D=4096，Regime II）下nGPT的SNR优势随宽度线性增长，405B规模预计达10×优势。
  • 学习率迁移性：nGPT在BF16和NVFP4下具有近乎相同的平坦损失最小值，BF16最优学习率可直接用于NVFP4训练，无需重新调参；GPT的最优学习率在精度切换时偏移16×。
  • 大规模实证验证：在1.2B稠密模型（1T tokens）和400M/600M、3B/30B混合Mamba-Transformer MoE上均验证了上述优势，3B/30B模型相对误差趋近0%。

Key Contributions

  • Quantization robustness as architecture: Demonstrates for the first time that nGPT's hypersphere normalization constraint renders it natively robust to NVFP4 quantization, removing the need for Randomized Hadamard Transforms (RHT) and dynamic per-tensor scaling without any quality degradation.
  • Signal-accumulation mechanism: Decomposes SNR at each stage of matrix multiplication under NVFP4 and identifies that nGPT's 7 dB advantage is entirely due to coherent signal accumulation during summation (+9.4 dB averaging gain vs +2.2 dB for GPT), with quantization noise statistics being identical across both architectures.
  • Width-scaling law for SNR: Derives a three-regime formula (SNR ratio ≈ (1 + D·ρ_nGPT) / (1 + D·ρ_GPT)) predicting that nGPT's advantage grows linearly with hidden dimension D in the intermediate regime covering all current models up to ~400B parameters.
  • Learning rate transferability: nGPT's flat loss landscape is precision-invariant, allowing BF16-optimal hyperparameters to transfer directly to NVFP4 training, eliminating expensive low-precision hyperparameter sweeps.
  • Multi-architecture validation: Results confirmed across dense (1.2B) and hybrid Mamba-Transformer MoE models (400M/600M and 3B/30B), with consistent reduction in relative quantization error and 3.3–3.6× end-to-end throughput speedup on NVIDIA Blackwell GB200 GPUs.

Methodology

The paper proceeds in three interlocking steps: structural analysis, theoretical derivation, and empirical validation.

Architecture. nGPT modifies standard GPT by: (1) removing RMSNorm/LayerNorm, (2) normalizing all weights along the embedding dimension after each step, (3) replacing residual updates with interpolation on the hypersphere (h ← Norm(h + α·Norm(block(h)−h))), (4) normalizing query/key vectors before attention, and (5) using learnable per-dimension scale parameters. This enforces that every hidden state and weight vector lies on the unit hypersphere.

SNR decomposition. For a dot product y = Σ w_k x_k with D=4096 terms under NVFP4, the authors measure SNR = ||T||² / ||T − T̂||² at four stages: individual weights (w), activations (x), element-wise products (w_k x_k), and the full sum. The first three stages are statistically identical between GPT and nGPT. The gap only emerges at summation, establishing that the advantage is purely in how terms accumulate.

Pairwise correlation analysis. The SNR gap is traced to a difference in pairwise Pearson correlation ρ̄_s among the signal terms s_k = w_k x_k. nGPT induces ρ̄_s ≈ 1.32×10⁻³ vs GPT's 9.31×10⁻⁵ (approximately 14× larger), while noise correlation ρ̄_n ≈ 10⁻⁶ for both architectures. A variance-decomposition formula shows SNR_dot = (z_s / z_n)² where z_s grows with correlation while z_n does not, explaining the ~7 dB improvement.

Width-scaling theory. Using the empirical observation that ρ̄_s is essentially constant across all partial summation lengths k ∈ [16, 4096], the authors derive the SNR ratio formula (Eq. 5), identifying three regimes: small width (no gap), intermediate width (linear growth with D — where current models sit), and large width (saturation at ρ̄_nGPT/ρ̄_GPT ≈ 14.2×).

Loss landscape. Per-layer SNR improvement translates to a 3.5× flatter loss landscape under simultaneous perturbation of all weights, demonstrated by adding Gaussian noise scaled by α·||W||_F/√n and measuring validation loss increase.

Experiments. Training used NVIDIA Nemotron-Pretraining-Datasets, AdamW (β₁=0.9, β₂=0.95), WSD scheduler, and Blackwell GPUs natively supporting NVFP4. For NVFP4 runs, all GEMM operations use NVFP4; embeddings and non-linear operations remain in BF16. The 3B/30B MoE experiment follows the hybrid Mamba-Transformer pattern "MEMEM*EMEMEM" and was trained for ~500B tokens.

Results & Highlights

  • SNR advantage: nGPT achieves 25–26 dB dot-product SNR uniformly across all 12 layers of a 3.6B model; GPT achieves only 18–19 dB — a consistent +7.4 dB advantage that is not concentrated in specific layers.
  • 1.2B dense model: nGPT-NVFP4 (without RHT or per-tensor scaling) achieves lower relative quantization error than GPT-NVFP4 (with RHT and scaling). On downstream tasks (MMLU, PIQA, Hellaswag, WinoGrande, GSM8K), nGPT outperforms standard GPT at both BF16 and NVFP4 precision.
  • Hybrid MoE 3B/30B: nGPT achieves ~0% relative error under NVFP4 without standard interventions, whereas GPT-NVFP4 maintains non-negligible error even with RHT and per-tensor scaling.
  • Learning rate robustness: nGPT shows nearly flat validation BPB across two orders of magnitude of learning rates in both BF16 and NVFP4. GPT's NVFP4-optimal LR is 16× larger than its BF16 optimum.
  • Throughput: On GB200 GPUs, the nGPT NVFP4 configuration (with RHT and per-tensor scaling eliminated) achieves 3.3–3.6× speedup over BF16 GPT baseline at large hidden sizes (D ≥ 24576).
  • Overhead elimination: nGPT-NVFP4 removes both per-tensor scaling computation and RHT from the training pipeline; GPT-NVFP4 requires both (Table 2).

Critical Assessment

Novelty: Moderate-to-high. The nGPT architecture itself is prior work (Loshchilov et al., ICLR 2025). The genuinely novel contribution is the mechanistic explanation of why hypersphere normalization confers 4-bit robustness — specifically the signal-correlation framing and the three-regime scaling law. The practical contribution (end-to-end NVFP4 training without RHT/scaling) is useful but architecturally incremental.

Rigor: High for the analytical claims. The SNR decomposition is carefully designed to isolate where the advantage arises, and the theory (Eq. 4–5) is derived from first principles with minimal free parameters (only the single measured ρ̄_s is needed). The one-layer MLP control experiment (Fig. 7) helps validate that correlation is a training-driven phenomenon, not an artifact of model size.

Reproducibility: Moderate. The code repository is listed as anonymous, suggesting it was prepared for blind review and may not yet be publicly accessible. Hyperparameters are fully reported in appendix tables. However, all experiments require NVIDIA Blackwell hardware (GB200), which is not widely available, making independent reproduction difficult. The 3B/30B run is only 500B tokens, well below production-scale training horizons.

Significance: High for practitioners targeting NVFP4 deployment on Blackwell hardware. The elimination of RHT is a meaningful engineering simplification, and the learning-rate transferability result has direct practical value. The width-scaling analysis provides a principled argument that nGPT's advantage should grow with model scale, which is a strong forward-looking claim.

Red flags: (1) The paper is from NVIDIA researchers evaluating NVIDIA hardware with NVIDIA's proprietary number format — potential for confirmation bias in experimental design choices. (2) The 3B/30B model is trained for only ~500B tokens, far less than current SOTA (20T+ tokens); the authors themselves acknowledge this limitation. (3) The anonymous code repository has not been verified as publicly accessible. (4) The signal correlation mechanism (why ρ̄_s is higher in nGPT and constant across widths) is described as "left for future work" — the theoretical explanation is incomplete.

Strengths

  • Clean analytical decomposition that isolates precisely where and why the advantage arises, avoiding the typical black-box comparison between architectures.
  • The theory has only one free parameter (the measured ρ̄_s) yet fits empirical SNR data across two orders of magnitude in hidden dimension.
  • Results are consistent across architecturally distinct model families (dense transformers and hybrid Mamba-Transformer MoEs), suggesting the effect is structural rather than topology-specific.
  • The learning-rate transferability result is a practically important corollary that reduces the overhead of low-precision hyperparameter tuning.
  • The throughput measurements on actual hardware (GB200) provide concrete engineering value beyond the theoretical analysis.

---

Limitations

  • All experiments use NVIDIA's proprietary NVFP4 format on Blackwell hardware; generalizability to other 4-bit formats (e.g., NF4, FP4-E2M1) or hardware is not demonstrated.
  • The largest model (3B/30B MoE) is trained for only ~500B tokens, which is insufficient to draw conclusions about long-horizon training stability relative to SOTA models trained on 10–20T tokens.
  • The mechanistic origin of nGPT's higher signal correlation ρ̄_s is not theoretically explained — the paper demonstrates it empirically and shows it is consistent with training dynamics, but the causal link from hypersphere constraints to this specific statistical property remains open.
  • The paper does not compare against other 4-bit quantization robustness methods (e.g., Quartet, FP4-all-the-way) in terms of quality/compute trade-offs; it primarily benchmarks against standard GPT-NVFP4 with SOTA interventions.
  • Performance comparison with BF16 nGPT shows nGPT outperforms standard GPT even in BF16 (Table 1), raising the question of whether the comparison is fair or whether the improved performance stems partly from nGPT's general representational advantages rather than specifically its quantization robustness.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Why does hypersphere normalization during training specifically produce higher pairwise signal correlation ρ̄_s? Is this a consequence of the normalization preventing any single dimension from dominating, forcing the optimizer to distribute signal across all dimensions?
  2. Does the three-regime scaling law hold empirically when extrapolating to D > 4096 (e.g., D = 16384 for 405B-scale models)? The paper extrapolates the theory but does not train at those widths.
  3. How does nGPT-NVFP4 compare against dedicated quantization methods like Quartet or Quartet-II that achieve high-quality FP4 training via improved gradient estimators? Are those methods and nGPT complementary?
  4. Does the signal-correlation advantage persist when combining nGPT with other architectural modifications (e.g., mixture-of-depths, sliding window attention, or RoPE variants)?
  5. The paper shows nGPT outperforms GPT even in BF16 (Table 1). To what extent does Table 1 isolate the quantization-specific benefit vs. nGPT's general quality improvement? A matched-quality comparison at BF16 would clarify this.
  6. The anonymous repository (https://github.com/anonymous452026/ngpt-nvfp4) — is this now public, and does it include the training code for the MoE configurations?
