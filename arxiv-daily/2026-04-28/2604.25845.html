Methodology

The paper addresses an increasingly common data paradigm in which a large-scale dataset carries noisy/coarse labels (e.g., from automated NLP extraction of radiology reports) while only a small, expert-verified subset has clean labels. Traditional statistical transfer learning methods fail here because they assume parametric overall similarity between source and target distributions — an assumption violated by the significant distribution shift between noisy and clean data.

The proposed framework operates in two stages:

Stage 1 — Bayes Optimal Data Extraction with Safety Threshold:

A theoretically grounded module identifies which instances in the large noisy dataset can be reliably "purified" — i.e., their corrected Bayes-optimal labels are trustworthy enough to treat as clean. Crucially, a safety threshold is incorporated to prevent the initial estimation error from the small clean dataset from being catastrophically amplified when extrapolated to the large noisy dataset (i.e., negative transfer). The extraction rate and problem complexity are characterized formally. The method does not assume any parametric model form and works with arbitrary black-box classifiers.

Stage 2 — Boundary Sample Management:

After purification, a subset of noisy samples near the decision boundary remain ambiguous (their true labels are inherently uncertain). The framework provides two principled strategies:

  • Active Learning Strategy: Optimally selects a small number of the most informative boundary samples for costly expert annotation. This maximizes performance gain per labeling cost.
  • Pseudo-Tagging Strategy: Assigns pseudo-labels to boundary samples but explicitly preserves their uncertainty, avoiding the pitfall of overconfident wrong labels. This allows the classifier to benefit from additional samples without incurring additional annotation cost.

This creates a tunable quality-quantity trade-off: practitioners can slide between high performance (active learning) and low annotation cost (pseudo-tagging) depending on their budget.

The entire pipeline is model-agnostic: it can wrap around any classifier (logistic regression, random forest, deep neural networks, etc.). The theoretical analysis proceeds in two layers: (1) a generic treatment under abstract estimation error conditions, and (2) specialization to neural-network classifiers with explicit non-asymptotic convergence rates.

Results & Highlights

  • Simulation Studies: The framework was tested on synthetic classification data under varying noise types (class-dependent and instance-dependent), noise levels, and clean-to-noisy dataset size ratios. The method consistently outperformed baseline approaches that either ignore label noise, simply discard noisy data, or apply naive relabeling without safety thresholds.
  • Real-World Application — Pneumonia Diagnosis: Applied to chest X-ray medical image classification for pneumonia, a domain where large-scale datasets are often labeled by automated NLP parsing of radiology reports (noisy) while a small subset receives expert radiologist verification (clean). The framework demonstrated significant improvement over existing label-noise handling methods, validating its practical utility in high-stakes medical settings.
  • Theoretical Guarantees: Non-asymptotic convergence rates are derived for neural-network implementations. The safety threshold is formally shown to guard against negative transfer — a condition under which using the noisy data would otherwise degrade performance below using only the clean data.
