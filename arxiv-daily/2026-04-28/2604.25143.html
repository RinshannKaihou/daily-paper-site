Gradient-Direction Sensitivity Reveals Linear-Centroid Coupling Hidden by Optimizer Trajectories

Yongzhong Xu

arXiv: 2604.25143v1

Date: 2026-04-28

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.25143v1

一句话总结： 将滚动 SVD 从分析 AdamW 优化器更新量切换为直接分析损失梯度，使 SED 方向与 LCH 特征之间的耦合强度测量值提升了 1–2 个数量级（单任务从 ~3–9× 跃升至 100–330×，多任务从 ≤1 恢复至 20–45×），揭示了优化器轨迹会系统性掩盖梯度空间中的特征形成信号。
TL;DR: Swapping the rolling SVD from AdamW optimizer updates to raw loss gradients amplifies the measured coupling between SED directions and Linear Centroid Hypothesis (LCH) features by 1–2 orders of magnitude (3–9× → 100–330× in single-task, ≤1 → 20–45× in multitask), demonstrating that optimizer trajectories systematically obscure the gradient-space signature of feature formation; however, causal intervention shows the SED-LCH coupling is a strong diagnostic but not a unique causal pathway.

核心贡献

  • 方法创新——梯度方向敏感性： 提出将滚动 SVD 应用于损失梯度（而非 AdamW 更新量）来诊断训练动态，发现这一简单切换能将 SED-LCH 耦合强度测量值提升 1–2 个数量级，证明优化器内部状态（动量、自适应学习率）会严重衰减梯度方向中的结构化信号。
  • 多任务梯度聚合障碍的发现与解决： 在多任务 Transformer（共享编码器）中，基于更新量的 SVD 测量完全失效（R̄_k ≤ 1）；通过对每个任务的梯度分别进行 SVD，恢复了 R̄_k = 20–45× 的强耦合，揭示了多任务学习中梯度聚合是掩盖特征形成结构的关键障碍。
  • 因果干预实验： 将注意力更新约束到任意秩-3 子空间可加速 grokking 约 2.3 倍，但移除该秩-3 分量却几乎没有影响——这表明 SED-LCH 耦合是特征形成位置的强诊断信号，但并非唯一的因果通路，全秩 AdamW 更新在测试超参数下具有高度秩冗余性。
  • 统一了单任务与多任务下的测量一致性： 梯度基 SVD 消除了原始更新基测量中表现出的操作依赖性（不同算术操作间原本差异显著），产生了跨操作一致的强耦合测量。

Key Contributions

  • Methodological insight — gradient-direction sensitivity: Demonstrates that simply replacing the rolling SVD on AdamW updates with a rolling SVD on loss gradients amplifies the measured SED-LCH coupling by 1–2 orders of magnitude, establishing that optimizer internal states (momentum, adaptive learning rates) systematically attenuate structured signals present in raw gradients.
  • Identification and resolution of multitask gradient aggregation as an obstruction: In multitask transformers with shared encoders, update-based SVD yields R̄_k ≤ 1 (apparent diagnostic failure); per-operation gradient-based SVD recovers R̄_k = 20–45×, revealing that gradient aggregation across competing tasks is the primary mechanism obscuring feature-formation structure.
  • Causal intervention establishing diagnostic vs. causal status: Constraining attention updates to any rank-3 subspace (whether SED-derived or random) accelerates grokking by ~2.3×, while removing the rank-3 component has negligible effect, indicating that SED-LCH coupling is a strong diagnostic of where feature formation concentrates but not a unique causal pathway.
  • Unified measurement across single-task and multitask regimes: Gradient-based SVD eliminates the apparent operation-dependence seen in update-based measurements, producing consistent strong coupling estimates across all tested modular arithmetic operations.

Methodology

The paper operates within the framework of Spectral Edge Dynamics (SED), previously introduced by the same author, which applies rolling-window SVD to parameter evolution trajectories to separate coherent optimization directions from stochastic noise. The key methodological contribution is a shift in what SVD is applied to:

  1. Update-based SVD (baseline): Rolling SVD on AdamW parameter deltas δ_t = θ_{t+1} - θ_t, capturing the combined effect of gradient, momentum, and adaptive learning-rate scaling.
  1. Gradient-based SVD (proposed): Rolling SVD on raw loss gradients g_t = ∇_θ L(θ_t) before any optimizer transformation, isolating the directionality of the loss landscape itself.
  1. Coupling metric R̄_k: The perturbative coupling ratio between the top-k SED directions (from SVD) and LCH feature directions (derived from Jacobian-based centroids following the Linear Centroid Hypothesis). Higher R̄_k indicates stronger alignment between the subspace of dominant gradient variation and the subspace where LCH features form.
  1. Experimental setup:
  • Models: Small transformers trained on modular arithmetic tasks
  • Tasks: Four single-task modular arithmetic operations, plus a multitask variant with a shared encoder
  • Optimizer: AdamW
  • Causal intervention: Gradient projection — constraining attention updates to a rank-3 subspace (projecting gradients onto top-3 SVD directions before the optimizer step), and the inverse intervention of removing the rank-3 component
  1. Grokking acceleration measurement: The number of optimization steps required to reach a fixed generalization threshold, comparing full-rank AdamW against rank-3-constrained AdamW.

Results & Highlights

  • Single-task coupling amplification: Gradient-based SVD yields R̄_k = 100–330× vs. update-based SVD's 3–9× across four modular arithmetic operations. The operation-dependence present in update-based measurements vanishes.
  • Multitask recovery: Update-based SED flatlines at R̄_k ≤ 1 (effectively zero coupling), while per-operation gradient-based SED recovers R̄_k = 20–45× for all four operations in the shared-encoder multitask transformer.
  • Grokking acceleration: Rank-3 subspace constraint on attention updates accelerates grokking by a factor of ~2.3× across different random seeds and operations. Crucially, this acceleration occurs for any rank-3 subspace — not specifically the SED-derived one.
  • Rank redundancy: Removing the rank-3 component from the full-rank update has negligible effect on grokking speed when using proper gradient projection (projecting out the rank-3 directions before the optimizer step), indicating that the full-rank AdamW update is highly redundant under the tested hyperparameters.
  • Key interpretability finding: The SED-LCH coupling localizes where feature formation concentrates in parameter space but does not uniquely cause it — feature formation appears robust to which specific low-rank subspace is used.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The core insight — that optimizer states attenuate gradient-direction signals — is a genuinely useful methodological contribution to the mechanistic interpretability toolkit, but it is incremental (one-line change in what you SVD) rather than architecturally novel. The finding that "any rank-3 subspace works" somewhat undercuts the specificity and novelty of the SED-derived subspace.
  • Rigor: The experimental domain is narrow — only small transformers on modular arithmetic. No evidence is provided that the findings generalize to larger models, more complex tasks, or natural language. The 15-page, 5-figure scope is compact but leaves many questions unanswered about statistical significance, hyperparameter sensitivity, and failure modes. The causal intervention result (any rank-3 subspace accelerates grokking) is reported without a clear explanation of why low-rank projection helps, which leaves a gap between observation and understanding.
  • Reproducibility: The absence of released code, the single-author status, and the lack of institutional affiliation collectively raise reproducibility concerns. The paper describes its methodology in prose but does not reference a codebase, making exact reproduction dependent on reimplementation from the text description. No error bars or variance estimates across seeds are explicitly mentioned in the available summaries.
  • Significance: The paper makes a useful, practical contribution to the growing literature on spectral analysis of training dynamics, but its significance is constrained by the narrow experimental scope and the finding that SED-LCH coupling is diagnostic rather than causal. The ~2.3× grokking acceleration is interesting but of unclear practical value given the toy-task setting. The real significance may lie in the cautionary message: optimizer trajectories can be misleading, and gradient-level analysis is necessary for accurate dynamical diagnostics.
  • Red flags: The rapid succession of six single-author preprints in ~4 months (Feb–Apr 2026) without peer review raises questions about whether each paper has received sufficient validation. The lack of code, institutional affiliation, or collaborative co-authors is uncommon for empirical ML work and warrants cautious interpretation. The claim that "any rank-3 subspace accelerates grokking" could be interpreted as weakening the paper's core SED-specific narrative.

Strengths

  • Clear, actionable methodological insight: The finding that SVD on gradients vs. updates produces dramatically different results is simple to state, easy to implement, and has immediate practical implications for anyone using spectral methods to analyze training dynamics.
  • Intellectual honesty: The paper does not oversell its findings — it explicitly acknowledges that SED-LCH coupling is diagnostic rather than causal, and that any rank-3 subspace produces the acceleration effect, even though this admission complicates the paper's narrative.
  • Multitask analysis is genuinely revealing: The demonstration that gradient aggregation across tasks is the primary obstruction to detecting feature-formation structure provides a concrete, actionable diagnosis for multitask training analysis.
  • Connects two active research threads: The paper bridges the SED framework (spectral training dynamics) with the Linear Centroid Hypothesis (feature representational geometry), creating a productive synthesis between two rapidly developing areas of mechanistic interpretability.
  • Well-contextualized within a broader research program: The paper is part of a coherent series of investigations into grokking and spectral dynamics, giving it intellectual depth beyond what a standalone 15-page paper would suggest.

Limitations

  • Narrow experimental scope: Only small transformers on synthetic modular arithmetic tasks. No evidence of scaling to realistic model sizes or natural language/data tasks.
  • No code release: Reproducibility is compromised by the absence of a public code repository or detailed reproduction package.
  • Single author, no peer review: Without co-author cross-validation or external review, the reliability of experimental results is difficult to assess independently.
  • The "any rank-3 subspace" result is underexplored: The finding that random rank-3 subspaces work as well as SED-derived ones is reported but not adequately explained — why rank 3 specifically? What determines the optimal rank? Is this an artifact of the specific hyperparameters?
  • No comparison with alternative gradient analysis methods: The paper does not compare its gradient-SVD approach against other gradient-based diagnostics (e.g., gradient CKA, gradient covariance analysis, Neural Tangent Kernel diagnostics), making it difficult to assess whether gradient-SVD is uniquely informative or one of many equivalent approaches.
  • Limited statistical reporting: No systematic reporting of variance across random seeds, hyperparameter sensitivity analyses, or ablation studies beyond the rank-3 constraint experiments.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Why does rank-3 specifically work? The paper shows any rank-3 subspace accelerates grokking, but does not explain what determines the optimal rank. Is rank-3 inherent to 4-operation modular arithmetic, or does it scale with task complexity?
  2. Does gradient-SVD generalize beyond modular arithmetic? Would the same 1–2 order-of-magnitude amplification of SED-LCH coupling hold for natural language tasks, vision tasks, or larger models like GPT-2 or beyond?
  3. What is the mechanism behind the rank-redundancy of AdamW? The paper observes that the full-rank AdamW update is highly redundant, but does not explain why. Is this a property of AdamW specifically, or does it hold for SGD with momentum, or other adaptive optimizers?
  4. Can gradient-SVD serve as an early-warning signal? The author's earlier SED work claims 600–1700 step advance warning of grokking. Does gradient-based SVD improve or change this predictive capability?
  5. How does the gradient-SVD signal relate to the loss landscape geometry? Is there a connection between the dominant SVD directions of the gradient and the sharpest directions of the local loss landscape (Hessian eigenvectors)?
  6. Why are results reported without error bars? The robustness of the 2.3× grokking acceleration across seeds is mentioned qualitatively but not quantified statistically — what is the actual variance?
