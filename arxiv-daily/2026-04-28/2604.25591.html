Walking Through Uncertainty: An Empirical Study of Uncertainty Estimation for Audio-Aware Large Language Models

Chun-Yi Kuan, Wei-Ping Huang, Hung-yi Lee

arXiv: 2604.25591v1

Date: 2026-04-28

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.25591v1

一句话总结： 本文首次系统评估了音频感知大语言模型（ALLMs）中的五种不确定性估计方法，发现在通用音频推理任务上语义层面和自验证方法优于token级别方法，但在可信度导向（幻觉检测/不可回答问题）任务上方法的相对排序显著依赖于具体模型和基准，直接将文本LLM的结论迁移到音频场景并不可靠。
TL;DR: This paper presents the first systematic empirical benchmark of five uncertainty estimation methods (predictive entropy, normalized entropy, semantic entropy, discrete semantic entropy, P(True)) across multiple audio-aware LLMs and evaluation settings. Semantic-level and verification-based methods consistently outperform token-level baselines on general reasoning tasks, but their relative effectiveness becomes highly model- and benchmark-dependent in trustworthiness-oriented settings (hallucination detection, unanswerable questions), cautioning against naive transfer of text-LLM uncertainty findings to audio-language models.

核心贡献

  • 首项系统性研究：在音频感知大语言模型（ALLMs）中首次对不确定性估计方法进行系统化的实证基准测试，填补了该领域从文本LLM到音频-语言模型的空白。
  • 多方法多模型多场景评估：在Qwen2.5-Omni-3B、Qwen2.5-Omni-7B、Audio Flamingo 3三个模型上，覆盖MMAU、MMAR、MMSU、SAKURA四个通用推理基准和Audio-Hallucination、AQUA-Bench两个可信度导向基准，对五种代表性不确定性方法进行全面比较。
  • 揭示关键差异：发现通用推理任务与可信度任务之间存在显著的不同行为模式——前者语义/验证方法优势稳定，后者高度依赖模型和基准，说明从标准推理基准得出的结论不能直接迁移到幻觉检测和不可回答问题场景。
  • 探索不确定性驱动的自适应推理：提出以不确定性分数作为路由信号，在直接推理和caption-then-reason推理模式之间选择性切换，实证表明该方法仅在备选推理模式本身有效时才带来增益。
  • 扩展能力校准（capability calibration）分析：补充分析了Qwen2.5-Omni模型在回答问题前对自身正确性的预估能力，揭示模型在感知任务上系统性高估自身能力而在推理任务上校准较好。

Key Contributions

  • First systematic study of uncertainty estimation in audio-aware LLMs: Establishes empirical baselines where none previously existed, bridging the gap between text-only LLM uncertainty research and audio-conditioned generation.
  • Comprehensive multi-method, multi-model, multi-scenario evaluation: Benchmarks five uncertainty methods (predictive entropy, length-normalized entropy, semantic entropy, discrete semantic entropy, P(True)) across three ALLMs and six benchmarks spanning both general reasoning and trustworthiness.
  • Identifies a critical domain gap: Shows that the relative ranking of uncertainty methods is substantially more stable on general reasoning benchmarks than on trustworthiness-oriented ones (hallucination detection, unanswerable QA), where performance becomes highly model- and benchmark-dependent.
  • Explores uncertainty-based adaptive inference: Demonstrates that uncertainty can serve as a routing signal to selectively trigger more expensive reasoning (caption-then-reason), but only when the alternative reasoning strategy is genuinely beneficial — uncertainty alone is insufficient.
  • Capability calibration analysis: Reveals that models systematically overestimate their perceptual capabilities while maintaining more accurate self-assessment on reasoning tasks, with MMSU perception subtask showing ECE = 0.212 vs. reasoning ECE = 0.041.

Methodology

Uncertainty Estimation Methods Evaluated (following sampling-based protocols from text LLM literature):

  1. Predictive Entropy: Monte Carlo estimate of sequence-level negative log-likelihood over K=10 samples drawn at temperature 1.0.
  2. Length-Normalized Predictive Entropy: Token-level normalization variant, using ratio-of-expectations (total NLL / total tokens) for stability.
  3. Semantic Entropy: Clusters sampled answers by semantic equivalence via bidirectional entailment (NLI-based), then computes entropy over cluster probability masses.
  4. Discrete Semantic Entropy: For closed-form answer spaces, estimates empirical answer distribution from K samples and computes entropy directly over the discrete candidate set.
  5. P(True): Self-verification approach — prompts the model to judge whether its low-temperature prediction (T=0.1) is supported by the audio input, computing confidence from normalized likelihood of true/false tokens.

Evaluation Protocol: Two-stage decoupled prediction/uncertainty protocol. Low-temperature (T=0.1) decoding produces the final prediction for correctness evaluation. High-temperature (T=1.0) sampling with K=10 draws produces the samples for uncertainty estimation.

Models: Qwen2.5-Omni-7B, Qwen2.5-Omni-3B, Audio Flamingo 3.

Metrics: AUROC (error detection quality) and AURAC (selective prediction utility, i.e., accuracy after rejecting most uncertain examples).

Datasets:

  • General reasoning: MMAU (multi-task audio understanding), MMAR (deep reasoning across speech/audio/music), MMSU (spoken language understanding), SAKURA (multi-hop audio reasoning)
  • Trustworthiness: Audio-Hallucination (hallucinated sound event detection), AQUA-Bench (unanswerable audio question answering)

Adaptive Inference: Uses uncertainty as a routing signal with threshold tau=0.25. When uncertainty exceeds the threshold, the model switches from direct inference to a caption-then-reason strategy (describe audio first, then reason step-by-step).

Capability Calibration: Separate analysis using P(IK) prompting to estimate whether the model can anticipate pre-generation whether it will answer correctly, evaluated via ECE and Brier score with Monte Carlo estimation over K=100 samples.

Results & Highlights

  • General reasoning benchmarks (Tables I, III): Discrete semantic entropy and semantic entropy consistently achieve best or near-best AUROC across MMAU, MMAR, MMSU, and SAKURA. P(True) is competitive and wins in some settings (MMAR for Qwen2.5-Omni-3B, SAKURA for Qwen2.5-Omni-3B). Predictive entropy is the weakest method across all settings.
  • Example: On MMAU with Qwen2.5-Omni-7B, discrete semantic entropy achieves AUROC 0.85 vs. predictive entropy 0.64.
  • Subtask breakdown (MMAR/MMSU Perception vs. Reasoning): P(True) is competitive on perception subtasks, while discrete semantic entropy strengthens on reasoning subtasks.
  • Trustworthiness benchmarks (Table II): Results are notably more heterogeneous:
  • AQUA-Bench: Best method varies by model — P(True) for Qwen2.5-Omni-7B (AUROC 0.79), normalized entropy for Qwen2.5-Omni-3B (AUROC 0.75).
  • Audio-Hallucination: Semantic entropy best for Qwen models, but normalized entropy best for Audio Flamingo 3.
  • Audio Flamingo 3 has very low accuracy on AQUA-Bench (0.18), yet P(True) still achieves AUROC 0.89 — suggesting self-verification can detect failures even when overall performance is poor.
  • Adaptive inference (Table IV): Gains depend critically on whether reasoning mode helps. On MMAU (reasoning beneficial), adaptive inference matches or exceeds best single strategy while using only 24-64% of full reasoning tokens. On MMAR (reasoning harmful), adaptive inference cannot rescue performance. Pareto analysis (Figure 1) shows adaptive points shift toward lower cost without proportional accuracy loss only when reasoning itself is a viable fallback.
  • Capability calibration (Table IX, Section XIII):
  • Qwen2.5-Omni-7B shows best calibration on MMAU (ECE = 0.054) and worst on MMSU-Perception (ECE = 0.212).
  • Model size does not uniformly improve calibration (3B outperforms 7B on MMSU overall ECE: 0.044 vs. 0.108).
  • Consistent overconfidence in high-confidence regions across most benchmarks.
  • Emotion track on SAKURA yields highest calibration error (ECE = 0.227), indicating subjective/perceptually ambiguous attributes are hardest to self-assess.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: Moderate. The paper is the first to systematically benchmark text-LLM uncertainty methods in ALLMs, which fills a genuine gap. However, the methods themselves are all directly imported from text LLM literature (Kuhn et al. 2023, Kadavath et al. 2022) with no methodological innovation. The core contribution is empirical characterization, not new technique.

Rigor: Generally solid. The evaluation covers 3 models, 6 benchmarks, 5 methods, with consistent protocols (temperature, sampling, metrics). The decoupling of prediction (T=0.1) from uncertainty estimation (T=1.0) follows best practices. AUROC and AURAC provide complementary perspectives. The capability calibration appendix adds depth. However: (a) K=10 samples is relatively small for Monte Carlo estimation — variance may affect reliability of entropy estimates; (b) the adaptive inference threshold tau=0.25 is chosen heuristically with only a brief mention of stability testing; (c) only one GPU (RTX 3090) is used, and computational cost is not reported.

Reproducibility: Good. Full prompt templates are provided in Tables V-VIII. Models are publicly available (Qwen2.5-Omni series, Audio Flamingo 3). Benchmarks are publicly available. Temperature settings and K are explicitly stated. Code is not explicitly mentioned as released, which is a gap.

Significance: Moderately high. Uncertainty estimation for multimodal LLMs is an important and timely topic. The key finding — that uncertainty method rankings from general benchmarks do not transfer to trustworthiness settings — has practical implications for anyone deploying ALLMs in safety-critical applications. However, the work is primarily descriptive/diagnostic rather than prescriptive (no new method proposed to fix the identified issues).

Red flags: None significant. The paper is transparent about limitations (Section VIII explicitly discusses narrow answer spaces, inherited text methods, lack of representation-level signals, and simple threshold routing). The generative AI disclosure is clear. No ethical concerns apparent.

Strengths

  • Comprehensive empirical coverage: 3 models x 6 benchmarks x 5 methods x 2 metric types provides unusually broad coverage for a first study in a new domain.
  • Honest about negative/nuanced results: Does not oversell semantic/verification methods — clearly documents where they fail (trustworthiness benchmarks) and where adaptive inference does not help (MMAR).
  • Well-structured ablation: The Perception vs. Reasoning subtask breakdown (Table III) and capability calibration analysis (Section XIII) add meaningful granularity.
  • Practical orientation: The adaptive inference exploration, while preliminary, demonstrates that the authors are thinking beyond pure benchmarking toward deployment-relevant use cases.
  • Clear writing and organization: Method descriptions are precise, tables are well-formatted, and the distinction between response calibration and capability calibration is carefully maintained.

Limitations

  • Methodological ceiling: All methods are imported from text LLMs without audio-specific adaptation. As the authors acknowledge, these methods do not explicitly model uncertainty from audio perception itself (e.g., acoustic ambiguity, SNR-dependent confidence).
  • K=10 sampling: Monte Carlo estimation with only 10 samples may introduce non-negligible variance, especially for semantic entropy which depends on clustering quality.
  • Constrained answer spaces: Evaluations are mostly multiple-choice, limiting generalizability to open-ended generation (captioning, dialogue) where correctness is harder to define.
  • No multimodal/internal signals: Does not explore uncertainty from internal representations (attention patterns, hidden states) that could capture audio-specific ambiguity undetectable at the output level.
  • Adaptive inference is preliminary: Fixed threshold, single fallback strategy, and no comparison with learned routing policies. The finding that adaptive inference only helps when reasoning helps is somewhat tautological.
  • No code release: Reduces immediate reproducibility for the community.
  • Statistical significance: No error bars or significance tests reported for AUROC/AURAC comparisons between methods.
  • Pre-print status: Not yet peer-reviewed.

Relevance

本文与音频-语言多模态模型的可信度评估高度相关。如果你关注如何在语音/音频LLM中检测幻觉、提高预测可靠性、或实现选择性预测/自适应推理，本文提供了目前最全面的不确定性估计基准参照。文中揭示的“通用推理结论不可直接迁移到幻觉检测场景”这一发现，对实际部署中不确定性方法的选择具有直接的警示意义。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can audio-specific uncertainty signals (e.g., acoustic embedding entropy, SNR-aware confidence, cross-modal attention divergence) complement or outperform the text-inherited methods benchmarked here?
  2. Why does P(True) work well for some model-benchmark combinations but fail badly for others (e.g., AUROC 0.55 for Qwen2.5-Omni-3B on Audio-Hallucination)? Is this a prompting artifact or a genuine model capability gap?
  3. Would increasing K beyond 10 significantly alter the relative rankings of semantic entropy and discrete semantic entropy, given the clustering step's sensitivity to sample diversity?
  4. Can uncertainty estimation be integrated into ALLM training (e.g., via uncertainty-weighted loss or GRPO with semantic entropy rewards as in Seed-GRPO) to improve both accuracy and calibration simultaneously?
  5. How do these findings extend to open-ended audio-language tasks (captioning, dialogue, instruction-following) where correctness evaluation requires human judgment or LLM-as-judge?
  6. Could a learned routing policy (rather than fixed threshold tau=0.25) substantially improve the cost-accuracy tradeoff in adaptive inference for ALLMs?
