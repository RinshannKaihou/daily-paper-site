> **一句话总结：** VAE-Inf 提出了一种两阶段框架，先用 VAE 仅在多数类上训练以构建潜在空间中的高斯参考分布（通过 Wasserstein barycenter 聚合），再通过基于投影统计量的分布感知损失微调编码器来分离少数类，最后利用无分布校准过程实现有限样本下对第一类错误（假阳性率）的精确控制，在极不平衡分类场景下取得了有竞争力的表现并提供了统计可解释的决策规则。

> **TL;DR:** VAE-Inf is a two-stage framework for imbalanced classification that (i) pretrains a VAE exclusively on majority-class samples and aggregates per-sample Gaussian posteriors via a Wasserstein barycenter into a global latent reference distribution; (ii) fine-tunes the encoder with a distribution-aware loss based on variance-normalized projection statistics that pushes minority representations away from the majority manifold; and (iii) introduces a distribution-free empirical quantile calibration procedure that guarantees exact finite-sample Type-I error control under exchangeability. The framework achieves state-of-the-art AUC-PR and F1-score across tabular, image, and high-dimensional biomedical benchmarks under severe class imbalance.

## 核心贡献

- **两阶段生成-判别框架：** 第一阶段将不平衡分类转化为单类建模问题——仅在多数类上训练 VAE，利用 Wasserstein barycenter 将每个样本的高斯后验聚合成全局对角高斯参考分布 N(μ_ref, Σ_ref)。这为多数类提供了几何上合理的统计基线。
- **基于投影统计量的分布感知损失：** 第二阶段利用多元高斯分布的投影性质，在潜在空间中采样随机方向 a ∼ U(S^{k-1})，定义方向投影统计量 T(z, a) = (a^⊤z − a^⊤μ_ref)^2 / (a^⊤Σ_ref a)。该统计量在多数学参考下服从 χ²(1) 分布，可以用 α 作为统计裕度（如 α=9 对应 3σ 规则）。损失函数包含两个铰链项：惩罚超出接受域的多数学样本（tightening）和惩罚未进入拒绝域的少数类样本（pushing）。
- **无分布校准与有限样本第一类错误控制：** 在验证集多数学样本上计算投影聚合分数 S(x)，取经验上分位数 τ_δ 作为决策阈值。在仅假设分数可交换（exchangeable）的条件下，理论上保证了 P(第一类错误) ≤ δ，且该保证不依赖于生成模型的正确性或参数分布假设。
- **极不平衡场景下的鲁棒性：** 在 Credit Card（0.17% 少数类）、Backdoor（0.20%）、Census（0.21%）等极端不平衡变体上，现有方法性能显著下降，而 VAE-Inf 保持了稳定的 AUC-PR 和 F1-score 优势。

## Key Contributions

- **Two-stage generative-to-discriminative paradigm:** Stage 1 trains a VAE exclusively on majority-class data, then aggregates the collection of per-sample Gaussian posteriors into a global reference distribution via the closed-form 2-Wasserstein barycenter (mean = average of posterior means, diagonal covariance = squared average of posterior standard deviations). This avoids contamination from scarce minority data and provides a geometrically principled baseline.
- **Projection-based distribution-aware loss:** Inspired by the projection property of multivariate Gaussians, the method samples random directions from the unit sphere and computes variance-normalized projection statistics T(z, a). The loss function enforces a direction-wise statistical margin α: majority samples should stay within the high-probability acceptance region (T ≤ α), while minority samples should be pushed outside it (T > α). Using random projections prevents overfitting to a single global metric like Mahalanobis distance.
- **Distribution-free finite-sample Type-I error control (Theorem 1):** A conformal-style calibration procedure selects the decision threshold as the empirical (1−δ)-quantile of majority-class scores on a held-out validation set. The proof uses exchangeability and rank uniformity to show P(Type-I error) ≤ δ exactly, with no parametric assumptions on the score distribution — a guarantee not offered by any of the compared baselines.
- **Comprehensive evaluation across domains and imbalance levels:** Evaluated on tabular (Credit Card, Backdoor, Census), image (MNIST, CIFAR-10), and high-dimensional biomedical (TCGA Pan-Cancer, 20,531 features, 33 cancer types) datasets, with minority proportions ranging from 0.17% to 6.20%, plus intentionally constructed extreme variants at ~0.20%.

## Methodology

The VAE-Inf framework operates in three phases:

1. **Stage 1 — VAE Pretraining on Majority Class:**
   - A standard VAE with Gaussian encoder q_ϕ(z|x) = N(z; μ_ϕ(x), diag(σ²_ϕ(x))) and decoder p_θ(x|z) is trained solely on D₁ (majority samples) by maximizing the ELBO.
   - After training, per-sample posteriors {N(μ_{x_i}, diag(σ²_ϕ(x_i)))} are aggregated into a single global Gaussian N(μ_ref, Σ_ref) via the closed-form 2-Wasserstein barycenter: μ_ref = (1/N₁) Σ μ_{x_i}, Σ_ref = diag((1/N₁) Σ sqrt(σ²_ϕ(x_i)))².

2. **Stage 2 — Encoder Fine-tuning with Distribution-Aware Loss:**
   - The decoder is frozen. The encoder is fine-tuned using minority samples D₂ alongside majority samples D₁.
   - For each training step, M random projection directions a_j ∼ U(S^{k-1}) are sampled. The distribution-aware regularization loss is:
     L_reg = E[ (d(z^{(1)}, a) − α·a^⊤Σ_ref a)₊ + β·(α·a^⊤Σ_ref a − d(z^{(2)}, a))₊ ]
     where d(z, a) = (a^⊤z − a^⊤μ_ref)², z^{(1)} is a majority latent code, z^{(2)} is a minority latent code, α is the direction-wise statistical margin, and β controls the minority penalty strength.
   - The loss is averaged over random directions, batches, and classes.

3. **Inference with Distribution-Free Calibration:**
   - The anomaly score for a test sample is the average projection statistic over fixed random directions: S(x) = (1/M) Σ T(z, a).
   - Calibration: On the validation majority set D_{1,val}, compute scores S_i, sort them, and set threshold τ_δ = S_{(k)} where k = ⌈(1−δ)(n_cal + 1)⌉.
   - Decision: predict majority if S(x) ≤ τ_δ, minority otherwise.
   - Theorem 1 guarantees P_{x∼P₁}(S(x) > τ_δ) ≤ δ under exchangeability of calibration and test scores.

Architecture details: lightweight fully-connected encoder-decoder for tabular data (2 hidden layers), convolutional VAE with residual blocks for images, and high-capacity MLP-based VAE with residual feedforward blocks for TCGA (latent dim 128). Stage 1 uses learning rate 1e-4 for 200 epochs; Stage 2 uses 2e-3 for 100 epochs. M = 32 projection directions. α and β are selected via validation grid search.

## Results & Highlights

**Tabular Datasets (Table 1):**

| Dataset (minority %) | Best AUC-PR | VAE-Inf AUC-PR | Best F1 | VAE-Inf F1 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Credit Card (0.17%) | 84.11 (DeepSAD) | **85.61** | 81.94 (DeepSAD) | **83.57** |
| Backdoor (2.44%) | 97.61 (DeepSAD) | 97.41 | 94.14 (DeepSAD) | 93.60 |
| Backdoor (0.20%) | 95.49 (DeepSAD) | **96.07** | 91.81 (DeepSAD) | **93.73** |
| Census (6.20%) | 54.07 (PReNet) | **59.04** | 52.67 (PReNet) | **55.70** |
| Census (0.21%) | 46.68 (PReNet) | **52.39** | 47.23 (PReNet) | **51.08** |

- VAE-Inf achieves best AUC-PR and F1-score on 4 out of 5 tabular settings. The gains are most pronounced under extreme imbalance (0.20%-0.21% variants), where baselines like DeepSAD and PReNet degrade significantly while VAE-Inf maintains robust performance.
- On Census (0.21%), VAE-Inf achieves AUC-PR of 52.39 vs. 46.68 (PReNet), a >5.7 point gain.

**TCGA Pan-Cancer (Table 2):**
- Across all 33 one-vs-rest tasks: AUC-PR 95.58 (best), F1-score 93.52 (best), AUC-ROC 99.58 (second-best, behind PReNet at 99.85).
- On the 5 rare-cancer subset (ρ ≤ 1%): AUC-PR 79.66 (best, vs. 73.73 PReNet), F1-score 76.24 (best), AUC-ROC 98.32 (second-best). This demonstrates particular advantage in ultra-imbalanced, high-dimensional biomedical settings.

**Image Datasets (Table 8, ρ = 0.005):**
- MNIST: AUC-PR 73.94 vs. 67.36 (DeepSAD), F1 68.04 vs. 62.59.
- CIFAR-10: AUC-PR **62.07** vs. 18.53 (DeepSAD) — a dramatic 43.5-point improvement. This is the strongest result, showing that VAE-Inf's distribution-aware scoring fundamentally improves precision-recall trade-offs on complex image data under extreme imbalance, even where DeepSAD's AUC-ROC is slightly higher (72.54 vs. 71.46).

**Error Control (Tables 3, 10, 11; Figure 2):**
- The calibrated threshold yields test-set Type-I errors that tightly match the target level (e.g., target 0.01 yields 0.0107 on Credit Card, 0.0084 on Backdoor).
- Mean absolute deviation between validation and test Type-I curves across datasets ranges from 0.0003 to 0.0031, demonstrating excellent calibration stability.
- At target Type-I = 0.01, VAE-Inf achieves competitive or best Type-II errors across all datasets (e.g., 0.1020 on Credit Card, tied with PReNet as best; 0.0300 on Backdoor, tied with DeepSAD).
- At target Type-II ≈ 0.1, VAE-Inf achieves the lowest Type-I errors on Credit Card (0.0455), Backdoor (0.0001–0.0007), and TCGA (0.0082).

**Ablation (Table 7):** Stage 1 alone yields poor discrimination (e.g., AUC-PR 4.30 on Credit Card, 29.30 on Backdoor). Stage 2 fine-tuning dramatically improves all metrics (AUC-PR 85.61 and 97.41 respectively), confirming the distribution-aware loss is essential for separation.

**Sensitivity (Figure 3):** Moderate α (16) and β (2) yield optimal AUC-PR on Credit Card. Too small α (overly restrictive boundary) or too large β (over-separation) both degrade performance, indicating balanced regularization is critical.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** The two-stage idea (pretrain on majority, fine-tune with minority) is conceptually clean but the individual components (VAE, Wasserstein barycenter, projection statistics) are individually known. The novelty lies in the integration: using the VAE's per-sample posteriors aggregated via barycenter as a statistical reference, then designing a projection-based loss that directly operationalizes hypothesis testing in latent space. The distribution-free calibration with finite-sample guarantees is a genuine addition not present in any of the compared baselines.
- **Rigor:** The theoretical framework is solid. Theorem 1 is correctly proved and the assumption of exchangeability is minimal. The projection statistic's χ²(1) distribution under the Gaussian reference is correctly derived. The paper wisely avoids claiming the learned distribution is correct — the calibration guarantee is explicitly distribution-free. The experimental evaluation is thorough in breadth (5 datasets, 5 baselines, 10 runs each) but could benefit from deeper analysis: no statistical significance tests between methods; the image experiments only compare against DeepSAD (the sole baseline with image support); the hyperparameter grid for α and β is not fully specified.
- **Reproducibility:** Code is not publicly available (only upon request). Architecture details, hyperparameters, and training protocols are well-documented in Appendix A.3 with Table 6 providing key settings. However, exact network layer dimensions, activation functions, and optimizer-specific settings beyond learning rate are not tabulated. The projection set A is fixed after random sampling but the seed is not mentioned.
- **Significance:** The paper addresses a real and important problem. The key insight — that imbalanced classification can be reframed as one-class distribution modeling followed by statistically controlled discrimination — is valuable and distinguishes VAE-Inf from purely discriminative approaches. The finite-sample Type-I error guarantee is particularly significant for safety-critical applications (medical diagnosis, fraud detection). However, the framework is evaluated only against deep anomaly detection methods, not against the broader imbalanced classification literature (e.g., Focal Loss, LDAM, GAN-based oversampling, two-stage ensembles). The omission of these comparisons limits the ability to assess VAE-Inf's standing in the wider imbalanced learning landscape.
- **Red flags:** None major. The paper is transparent about its assumptions and limitations. The claim of "exact finite-sample control" is technically correct under exchangeability but this assumption may be violated if there is distribution shift between calibration and deployment. The TCGA one-vs-rest setup creates artificially balanced majority classes (pooling 32 types) which may be easier than real-world scenarios where the "normal" class is a single coherent distribution.

## Strengths

- **Statistical interpretability:** Unlike most deep imbalanced classification methods that output heuristic scores, VAE-Inf's projection-based score has a clear hypothesis testing interpretation, and the calibration procedure provides rigorous Type-I error guarantees. This is a significant practical advantage for applications where false positive control is critical (medical screening, fraud detection, security).
- **Unified framework:** The paper cleanly integrates generative modeling (VAE), optimal transport (Wasserstein barycenter), projection statistics, and conformal inference into a single coherent pipeline.
- **Robustness under extreme imbalance:** The method shows particular strength when minority proportions fall below 1%, where discriminative baselines degrade substantially. This is precisely the regime where practical impact is highest.
- **Domain versatility:** Strong results across tabular, image, and genomic data with minimal architectural changes demonstrate the framework's generality.
- **Distribution-free guarantee:** The Type-I error control does not depend on the correctness of the VAE model or Gaussian assumptions — it relies only on exchangeability, making it robust to model misspecification.
- **Comprehensive ablation:** The Stage 1 vs. Stage 2 comparison clearly demonstrates that the distribution-aware loss is responsible for the discriminative power, not the VAE pretraining alone.

## Limitations

- **No comparison to standard imbalanced classification methods:** The paper only compares against deep anomaly detection baselines. Missing are comparisons to Focal Loss, LDAM, SMOTE-based deep methods, GAN-based oversampling, and cost-sensitive deep learning approaches — making it hard to assess whether VAE-Inf outperforms the broader state of the art.
- **Code not publicly available:** Only "available upon request," which limits immediate reproducibility and adoption.
- **Exchangeability assumption for calibration:** While minimal, this assumption may not hold under dataset shift or when the calibration and deployment distributions differ, which is common in real-world deployment.
- **Computational overhead:** Stage 1 VAE training + Stage 2 fine-tuning + M projection directions per inference step add complexity compared to single-stage discriminative methods. The paper does not report training/inference time comparisons.
- **TCGA one-vs-rest setup:** The artificial construction of majority class by pooling 32 diverse cancer types may not reflect realistic settings where the "normal" distribution is homogeneous. Performance on true one-class detection tasks is not evaluated.
- **Limited image baselines:** Only DeepSAD is compared on MNIST and CIFAR-10. Other anomaly detection methods designed for images are not included.
- **Binary classification only:** The framework is currently limited to binary (majority vs. minority) settings, while many real-world imbalance problems are multi-class.
- **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** α and β require per-dataset validation tuning, and the grid search range is not specified. The paper acknowledges performance is sensitive to these choices.

## Relevance

VAE-Inf 在不平衡分类领域提出了一个独特且有价值的研究方向：将生成建模与统计假设检验相结合，为决策提供可解释的统计保证。这对于需要严格控制假阳性率的应用场景（如医疗筛查、欺诈检测、网络安全）尤为重要。该方法将 Wasserstein barycenter、投影统计量和 conformal inference 统一在一个框架内，展现了跨领域技术融合的潜力。如果你关注不平衡学习、异常检测、或可信赖机器学习中对模型输出进行统计校准的方法，本文提供了值得跟踪的思路。不过，目前缺乏与主流不平衡分类方法（Focal Loss、LDAM 等）的直接对比，其实用优势的范围仍需进一步验证。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How does VAE-Inf compare against standard imbalanced classification methods such as Focal Loss, LDAM, SMOTE-based deep oversampling, and GAN-based minority class generation? Would the statistical guarantees hold under these alternative training paradigms?
2. Can the two-stage framework be extended to multi-class imbalance settings? Would a separate VAE reference be needed per majority class, or could a hierarchical reference model be constructed?
3. How sensitive is the Type-I error guarantee to violations of exchangeability, particularly under covariate shift or temporal drift between calibration and deployment data?
4. What is the computational cost of VAE-Inf compared to single-stage discriminative methods (training time, inference latency, memory), and can the number of projection directions M be reduced without sacrificing performance?
5. Can the projection directions a be learned adaptively (e.g., via a small network or through optimization) rather than sampled uniformly from the unit sphere, to focus on the most discriminative latent subspace orientations?
6. How does the method perform when the majority class itself is heterogeneous or multi-modal? Would a single Gaussian reference be insufficient, requiring a mixture model or flow-based reference distribution?
7. Can Stage 2 fine-tuning be replaced or augmented with a fully discriminative head while preserving the calibration guarantees, creating a hybrid that benefits from both approaches?
8. What is the behavior under "polluted" majority-class data (i.e., when some minority samples accidentally appear in the Stage 1 training set), and can the Wasserstein barycenter aggregation provide natural robustness against such contamination?
9. How does the framework perform in online or continual learning settings where the class distribution evolves over time, and can the calibration threshold be updated dynamically?
10. For the TCGA experiments, how does performance change when the majority class is a single tissue type rather than a pooled set of 32 cancers, reflecting a more realistic "one-class vs. rest" clinical scenario?
