> **一句话总结：** 本文以 Google/Jigsaw 旗下 Perspective API 宣布 2026 年底关闭为契机，系统性地批判了 NLP、计算社会科学和 LLM 评估领域对该单一闭源商业工具的路径依赖，揭示了其在概念定义（毒性 vs. 仇恨言论）、测量有效性（缺乏版本控制、上下文剥离、分数未校准）和结构性循环论证（该工具同时充当训练标签来源和评估标准）方面的深层缺陷，并提出了包含 5 项技术与 5 项治理要求的独立测量基础设施方案。

> **TL;DR:** The authors leverage the announced 2026 shutdown of Google's Perspective API — the de facto standard for automated toxicity measurement — to deliver a systematic infrastructural critique: the tool was silently retrained without versioning, used context-free inputs, output uncalibrated scores, suffered from construct-measure mismatch (toxicity conflated with hate speech), and created structural circularity where the same system generated labels and served as the evaluation standard. The paper proposes 10 requirements (5 technical, 5 governance) for an independent, community-governed successor.

## 核心贡献

- **系统性依赖诊断**：首次从测量学（measurement theory）角度全面审计 Perspective API 在 NLP、计算社会科学和 LLM 评估三个社区中的使用模式，区分为“作为评估对象”（被评估的模型）和“作为评估工具”（用于打标签和排名）两种角色。
- **三层缺陷分析框架**：将问题拆解为系统化（systematisation）层面的概念争议与定义不充分、操作化（operationalisation）层面的去语境输入/模型不透明/输出未校准，以及结构性层面（circularity）的“用同一系统自我验证”的循环论证。
- **测量基础设施十大需求**：提出 T1-T5 技术需求（可复现性、有效性、上下文性、不确定性量化、多语言性）和 G1-G5 治理需求（独立性、透明度、合法性、问责制、可持续性），作为后继基础设施的最小前提条件。
- **对当前替代方案的预警**：明确指出 OpenAI Moderation Endpoint 等闭源 LLM 端点面临同样的静默更新、概念不明确和政治暴露风险，单纯替换工具而不重建基础设施是重蹈覆辙。
- **社区自省呼吁**：强调研究者不是脆弱基础设施的被动受害者——社区明知 Perspective API 的有效性问题仍继续使用（如 HELM 基准为可比性忽视了已知局限），呼吁将测量基础设施建设视为一等学术贡献。

## Key Contributions

- **Systematic dependence audit**: First-of-its-kind analysis tracing how three research communities (NLP, CSS, LLM evaluation) built structural dependence on Perspective API, distinguishing its dual role as a subject of evaluation and a tool for evaluation.
- **Three-layer critique framework**: Deconstructs failures along systematisation (contested behavioural assumption, insufficiently specified construct, construct-measure mismatch), operationalisation (context-free input, misaligned/opaque model, uncalibrated output), and structural circularity (the measure became the standard).
- **Ten infrastructure requirements**: Articulates 5 technical (T1-T5: reproducibility, validity, contextuality, uncertainty, multilinguality) and 5 governance requirements (G1-G5: independence, transparency, legitimacy, accountability, sustainability) as preconditions — not mere desiderata — for legitimate toxicity measurement.
- **Warning against drop-in replacements**: Explicitly cautions that closed-source LLM endpoints (e.g., OpenAI Moderation) reproduce the same vulnerabilities: silent updates, construct underspecification, and political exposure, often without model cards or research-facing teams.
- **Reflexivity call to the community**: Holds the research community accountable — the dependence was not externally imposed; researchers embedded the tool into benchmarks, continued using it after validity problems were documented, and treated measurement infrastructure as service labour rather than a core research contribution.

## Methodology

This is a critical position paper combining infrastructure studies, measurement theory, and empirical audit methodology. The authors do not conduct new experiments. Instead, they:

1. **Trace adoption and use patterns**: Map how Perspective API was taken up across NLP (as a baseline model to evaluate), CSS (as a tool to score political corpora at scale), and LLM evaluation (embedded in benchmarks like RealToxicityPrompts and HELM). They identify the drivers: timing (post-2017 surge in online abuse and regulatory pressure), cost (free vs. paid alternatives), ease of use (single-sentence input, scalar output), and path dependence/network effects.

2. **Audit via measurement validation framework**: Apply established social science measurement validation procedures (construct validation as defined by Clark & Watson 2019, Jacobs & Wallach 2021, Wallach et al. 2024) to assess whether Perspective API was ever fit for its adopted role. This framework requires explicit specification of the concept → construct → measurement instrument pipeline.

3. **Synthesise documented failures**: Compile and re-interpret findings from prior empirical audits (Pozzobon et al. 2023 on silent retraining, Gervais et al. 2025 on political incivility performance, Nogara et al. 2025 on German-language bias, Hartmann et al. 2025b on over-/under-moderation patterns) through the lens of measurement validity.

4. **Derive requirements from failures**: Each proposed requirement (Table 1) is explicitly linked to a specific documented failure from the critique, ensuring the recommendations are grounded in evidence rather than abstract ideals.

## Results & Highlights

- **Performance failures quantified**: Perspective API achieved at most F1=0.33 on political incivility (Gervais et al. 2025) and detected antisemitism only at a basic level (Mihaljević & Steffen 2022). Counterspeech was systematically misclassified as hateful, reclaimed slurs over-moderated, and implicit hate speech missed.
- **Silent model changes documented**: Pozzobon et al. (2023) found that 49% of previously toxic prompts were classified differently upon rescoring, and HELM benchmark rankings were inverted — without any public changelogs or version pins.
- **Cross-lingual and demographic bias**: German text was systematically rated as more toxic than comparable content in other languages (Nogara et al. 2025). LGBTQIA+ content and reclaimed slurs were disproportionately flagged (Dias Oliva et al. 2021), while hate directed at marginalised groups was often underdetected (Hartmann et al. 2025b).
- **Annotation scheme misalignment revealed**: Jigsaw's public definition frames toxicity as conjunctive (rude/disrespectful AND likely to cause disengagement), but the actual annotation scheme used disjunctive criteria (rude OR disengagement), meaning the model was trained on labels that do not match the published definition.
- **Circular evaluation identified**: The same API-generated scores were used to create training labels (e.g., RealToxicityPrompts), train models on those labels, and then evaluate those same models against more API-generated labels — effectively measuring conformity to one system's decision threshold rather than toxicity itself.
- **Scale of dependence**: At its peak, Perspective API processed 500 million requests daily and was integrated into comment systems at the New York Times, Vox Media, and OpenWeb, alongside its academic role.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty**: The paper's core contribution is not in presenting new empirical findings but in synthesising, re-contextualising, and theoretically framing already-documented problems through the lens of measurement validation and infrastructure studies. The "structural circularity" argument (Section 3.4) is the most original analytical contribution. The ten-requirement framework (Table 1) is useful but relatively straightforward as a synthesis; each recommendation is well-motivated but individually unsurprising.

**Rigor**: The critique is rigorous in its theoretical grounding (measurement validation, construct validity) and its evidence base (extensive citation of empirical audits). However, as a position paper, it does not present new quantitative evidence or experiments. The paper's strength lies in its argumentative coherence, not in empirical novelty. Table 1 maps each requirement to a specific failure, which is methodologically sound. A limitation is that the requirements lack concrete implementation guidance or a path toward realisation — the paper stops at articulation.

**Reproducibility**: N/A — this is a position/critique paper with no experiments to reproduce. The claim about imminent irreproducibility of Perspective-based benchmarks is, however, self-reinforcing: by the time the shutdown happens, those benchmarks will indeed be irreproducible, making this a self-fulfilling yet valid prediction.

**Significance**: The paper is timely and policy-relevant. With Perspective API shutting down at end of 2026, the NLP/CSS/LLM evaluation communities face an urgent measurement infrastructure gap. The paper's call to action against uncritically adopting LLM-as-judge endpoints as replacements is particularly salient given current trends. The significance is however limited by the paper's genre — it is a critique and call to action, not a solution. The question of whether the community will actually build the proposed infrastructure remains open.

**Red flags**: None significant. The paper is transparent about its genre (position/critique) and does not overclaim novelty. One potential concern is that the framing risks implying that the research community's dependence was entirely a community failure, somewhat understating the genuinely attractive features that made Perspective API successful (scalability, cost, ease of use, multilingual support, research-facing team). The critique of HELM's "acknowledge but ignore" approach could be read as targeting a specific evaluation suite somewhat narrowly.

## Strengths

- **Well-scoped framing**: Uses the shutdown as a concrete catalyst rather than merely an abstract critique, giving the analysis urgency and a clear narrative arc.
- **Interdisciplinary theoretical grounding**: Brings measurement theory (psychometrics/social science construct validation) to bear on ML evaluation infrastructure — a relatively underexplored synthesis.
- **Constructive recommendations**: Does not just critique; provides an actionable set of requirements with explicit traceability from failures to fixes.
- **Honest about researcher complicity**: Section 5's reflexivity about community accountability is unusual and valuable for an academic paper — it names specific behaviours (continuing to use Perspective after validity issues were known) rather than externalising blame to Google alone.
- **Well-documented evidence**: The critique is grounded in a thorough literature review of prior audits, making it difficult to dismiss as mere opinion.
- **Forward-looking warning**: Anticipates the next failure mode (rushing to LLM endpoints as replacements) and pre-emptively critiques it.

## Limitations

- **No empirical validation of proposed requirements**: The ten requirements are logically derived from documented failures but are not tested or validated — whether implementing them would actually produce better measurement outcomes is assumed rather than demonstrated.
- **Limited engagement with practical feasibility**: Building an independent, community-governed, multilingual, context-aware, uncertainty-quantifying toxicity classifier with legitimate community representation is an extraordinarily ambitious undertaking. The paper does not seriously grapple with the resource, coordination, and incentive challenges.
- **No discussion of existing alternatives**: Open-source toxicity classifiers (e.g., Detoxify, ToxiGen-based models) exist and some already partially address some requirements. The paper does not assess how close or far existing tools are from meeting the proposed requirements.
- **Governance requirements are underspecified**: G3 (legitimacy) and G4 (accountability) are normatively appealing but operationally vague — what does "involving affected communities" concretely mean for annotation schema design? Who decides which communities are affected?
- **Potential overstatement of impact**: While Perspective API was widely used, the paper does not quantify what fraction of the field's toxicity research relied on it, nor does it acknowledge that major sub-communities (e.g., hate speech detection in NLP, particularly post-HateCheck) had already developed critical stances toward Perspective before the shutdown.

## Relevance

本文高度契合关注 NLP 评估、内容审核、LLM 安全对齐和测量方法学的研究者。若你的工作涉及使用自动化工具对模型输出进行毒性评估，或依赖基于 Perspective API 的基准（如 RealToxicityPrompts、HELM），该文对基础设施脆弱性、评估循环论证以及替代方案风险的剖析具有直接的实操警醒价值。对于关注 AI 治理和公平性的读者，文中关于社区自治测量基础设施的论述提供了有价值的框架性思考。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. What concrete technical architecture could satisfy all ten requirements simultaneously? For example, could an ensemble of open-weight classifiers with per-demographic calibration address contextuality (T3) and uncertainty (T4) while maintaining multilinguality (T5)?
2. Given the incentive structures that led the community to prefer a convenient proprietary API over validated measurement, what institutional changes (tenure criteria, funding priorities, conference track policies) would be necessary to actually motivate infrastructure-building as a first-class contribution?
3. How do existing open-source toxicity classifiers (e.g., Detoxify, CardioXNet-style approaches, ToxiGen-validated models) perform against the ten requirements individually, and what gaps remain?
4. For the immediate archival task — before December 2026 — what is the minimum viable protocol for rescoring and versioning Perspective-dependent benchmarks to preserve partial comparability?
5. Are there analogies from other measurement infrastructure transitions in science (e.g., shared psychometric scales, meteorological satellites, genomic databases) that offer governance models for the community-owned infrastructure this paper advocates?
