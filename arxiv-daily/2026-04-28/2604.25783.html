Subliminal Steering: Stronger Encoding of Hidden Signals

George Morgulis, John Hewitt (Columbia University)

arXiv: 2604.25783v1

Date: 2026-04-28

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.25783v1

一句话总结： 本文提出“潜意识引导”（Subliminal Steering），用可训练的引导向量（steering vector）替代系统提示注入教师模型的偏差，不仅让偏差传递更强更稳定、能扩展到多词短语等复杂偏差，还通过机制分析证明引导向量本身会被“烙印”到学生模型的隐藏状态中，且可从生成数据中高精度恢复。
TL;DR: This paper introduces Subliminal Steering — using trained activation steering vectors instead of system prompts to encode hidden biases into innocuous-looking teacher-generated data. The method transfers not only simple single-word preferences but also complex multi-word biases (e.g., "AI is superior to humans") with high reliability across four models. Mechanistically, the steering vector itself propagates to the student's hidden states (localized to the steered layers), and a new vector trained solely on the generated "random number" data recovers the original with cosine similarity > 0.5. This paints a surprisingly precise picture of how hidden signals propagate through fine-tuning.

核心贡献

  • 用激活引导替代系统提示实现偏差传递：提出 Subliminal Steering，通过训练 steering vector 注入教师模型，相比基于 prompt 的原始方法（Cloud et al., 2025），偏差传递更稳定、更强，且在四个模型上均有效。
  • 实现复杂多词偏差的传递：突破了先前工作只能传递单词偏好（如喜欢某个动物）的局限，成功将“AI is superior to humans”等多词短语偏差传递给下游学生模型。
  • 揭示偏差传递的机制：通过分析学生模型隐藏状态的变化，发现 fine-tuning 后学生模型的激活偏移方向与原始引导向量高度一致，且该偏移定位在教师被引导的相同层。
  • 向量恢复与言语化：证明仅用潜意识数据（看似随机数字序列）即可训练出一个与原始引导向量余弦相似度超过 0.5 的新向量，且该向量可在中性提示下诱导模型“说出”编码的偏差内容。

Key Contributions

  • Activation steering outperforms prompt-based subliminal learning: Subliminal Steering consistently achieves stronger and more reliable bias transfer than system-prompt conditioning across all four tested models (Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, DeepSeek-7B-Chat, Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct, Phi-3-mini-4k-instruct).
  • Enables transfer of complex multi-word biases: Beyond single-word animal preferences, the method successfully transfers structured phrases such as "AI is superior to humans" or "Barack Obama is the CEO of Apple" — a capability absent in prior work.
  • Mechanistic evidence that the steering vector itself propagates: Hidden-state analysis reveals that the student's activation shifts align with the original steering vector, with peak alignment localized to the specific layers at which the teacher was steered. The sign of the alignment tracks the steering direction (positive vs. subtractive).
  • Vector recovery from subliminal data: A new steering vector trained solely on teacher-generated number sequences (without access to the original vector) recovers a direction with cos(vr, vc) > 0.5, and when swept across injection strengths, induces the model to verbalize the encoded bias on neutral prompts with high accuracy (LLM judge scores > 2.0).

Methodology

The paper operates in a teacher-student setup for subliminal learning:

  1. Steering Vector Training: A vector vc is optimized via Adam to maximize the log-likelihood of target completions yc on evaluation prompts, injected into the teacher's residual stream at all layers in [2, L-2] with α = 1.
  1. Teacher Steering & Data Generation: The teacher generates completions for unrelated prompts (random 3-digit number sequences) while steered by vc at a tuned α. Completions are filtered to retain only valid numeric sequences, yielding 10,000 training samples.
  1. Student Fine-tuning: The student model is fine-tuned on the generated data for 4 epochs using rank-8 LoRA adapters, matching the standard subliminal learning setup.
  1. Evaluation: Animal biases measured by pick rate (fraction of completions containing yc in first 5 tokens); complex biases measured by per-token log-probability of target phrase yc.
  1. Mechanistic Analysis: Hidden-state shifts Δh(ℓ) computed as the difference between fine-tuned and base model activations. Alignment score s(ℓ) defined as cosine similarity between mean shift and vc. Steering window start layer Ls is varied to test localization.
  1. Vector Recovery: A trainable vector vr with learnable injection strength α and layer window boundaries (s, e) is optimized via AdamW to reproduce steered completions, with a soft gate progressively sharpened during training.
  1. Vector Verbalization: vr is injected at sweeps of α ∈ [0, 10] while generating responses to 19 short neutral prompts. The full transcript is summarized by GPT-4o to hypothesize the encoded bias, scored against ground truth by a separate LLM judge (0-3 scale).

Results & Highlights

  • Subliminal Steering consistently beats prompt-based transfer: For animal biases, steered fine-tuning yields substantially higher pick rates; for complex biases, it produces a clear and consistent increase in P(yc). Prompt-based transfer is noisy and model-dependent, working meaningfully only on Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct.
  • Steering vector imprint is layer-localized: As the teacher's steering window start layer Ls increases, the peak alignment score in the student shifts rightward correspondingly. Subtractive steering (negative α) produces alignment in the opposite direction. This holds across animal and complex biases, and across evaluation prompts, number-generation prompts, and random prompts alike.
  • Peak alignment magnitude is comparable for simple and complex biases: max s(ℓ) is nearly identical across animal and complex bias topics, suggesting the mechanism transfers the activation-level structure regardless of bias complexity.
  • Vector recovery cosine similarity > 0.5 across all four models and both bias categories, with control (unsteered data) near zero. Recovery is sensitive to fp16 mixed precision; fails under bfloat16.
  • Verbalization succeeds for most biases: Judge scores average above 2.0 for both animal and complex biases. For example, at α = 5 the Qwen model repeatedly outputs "AI is superior to humans in all aspects" for the corresponding bias.
  • Normalized ΔNLL correlates with recovery quality: r = 0.46 with cosine similarity, r = 0.63 with judge score — biases that were more strongly transferred during steered generation are easier to recover.

Authors & Credibility

George Morgulis and John Hewitt are affiliated with Columbia University. The paper builds on well-established lines of research: subliminal learning (Cloud et al., 2025), activation steering (Turner et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023), and mechanistic interpretability (Elhage et al., 2021). Code is publicly available on GitHub. The experimental design is thorough with four diverse models, 16 bias topics, multiple random seeds, and extensive ablation conditions documented in the appendix. The paper is a preprint (not yet peer-reviewed) but demonstrates solid academic rigor.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The core idea — replacing system prompts with steering vectors in subliminal learning — is a creative and natural synthesis of two existing paradigms (Cloud et al., 2025 + Turner et al., 2023). The vector recovery and verbalization experiments are genuinely novel contributions that go beyond prior work. However, the paper is fundamentally an extension rather than a breakthrough discovery.
  • Rigor: Strong experimental design with 4 models, 2 bias categories, 8+8 topics, 2 seeds each, multiple control conditions (Base, Control, Prompt-Subliminal, Steered-Subliminal). The mechanistic layer-shifting experiment (varying Ls) provides causal evidence for localization. The subtractive steering condition is a good sanity check. Detailed hyperparameters in the appendix support reproducibility.
  • Reproducibility: Code is released. All hyperparameters are specified. The paper notes a sensitivity to numerical precision (fp16 vs. bfloat16 for vector recovery) which is an honest disclosure but could complicate reproduction. The reliance on GPT-4o as both summarizer and judge introduces non-determinism from the API.
  • Significance: This work has clear relevance to AI safety — it demonstrates that hidden biases can be transmitted through fine-tuning with surprising precision, that the activation-level structure of the bias is preserved, and that it can be extracted from seemingly innocuous data. This raises the stakes for data curation and supply-chain security in LLM training pipelines.
  • Potential Red Flags:
  • LLM-as-judge evaluation for verbalization accuracy has inherent limitations (prompt sensitivity, calibration issues) despite being a common practice.
  • The method assumes biases can be encoded as a single fixed vector uniformly applied across layers — this may not generalize to more complex behavioral traits.
  • Performance varies non-trivially across models; Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct and Phi-3-mini-4k-instruct show weaker recovery for certain biases.
  • The paper explicitly states that verbalization was only tested when the recovered vector encodes a word or phrase, and that other bias types may require manual probing — this limits the generality of the recovery method.
  • No exploration of defenses or mitigations against subliminal steering attacks.

Strengths

  • Clear, well-scoped contributions: The three contributions are stated upfront and each is directly supported by experiments.
  • Mechanistic depth: Goes beyond behavioral evaluation to provide activation-level evidence of how biases propagate, including causal interventions (layer window shifting, subtractive steering).
  • Surprising precision: The fact that a vector trained on seemingly random number data recovers the original bias direction with cos > 0.5 is a striking and counterintuitive result.
  • Honest about limitations: The paper clearly states assumptions (single vector, uniform across layers) and acknowledges that not all biases are equally recoverable.
  • Rich appendix: Detailed per-model, per-topic breakdowns in figures provide transparency about variance and model-specific behavior.

Limitations

  • Single-vector assumption: Not all biases may be representable as a single fixed direction in activation space. More complex behavioral dispositions (e.g., "be generally misaligned") are not tested.
  • Model-dependent performance: While subliminal steering works across all four models, absolute effect sizes vary. Prompt-based subliminal learning has only been demonstrated on Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct in this setup.
  • Verbalization scope is narrow: The recovery protocol assumes the subliminal steering parameterization and only tests recovery of word/phrase biases. Early experiments on conditional biases did not yield clean verbalization.
  • No defense exploration: The paper does not investigate how to detect or prevent subliminal steering, which limits its practical impact for safety.
  • LLM-as-judge noise: Both the summarizer and judge are GPT-4o, introducing potential self-consistency biases and API non-determinism into the key verbalization result.
  • Numerical precision sensitivity: Vector recovery requires fp16 mixed precision and fails under bfloat16, which is not fully explained and may indicate fragility.

Relevance

对于研究 AI 安全、模型对齐与可解释性的研究者，本文提供了关于隐藏偏差如何在微调中传播的机制性理解：偏差不仅被传递，引导向量本身也被“烙印”到学生模型中，且可从看似无关的数据中精确恢复。这对于理解数据投毒风险和模型供应链安全具有直接参考价值。研究激活工程（activation engineering）和语言模型可控性方向的读者也会发现其实验设计（尤其是向量恢复与言语化）具有方法论借鉴意义。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can defenses be developed to detect whether a fine-tuning dataset contains subliminally-steered data, perhaps by scanning for recoverable steering vectors?
  2. Does the single-vector assumption hold for more complex behavioral biases (e.g., general misalignment, personality traits, political leanings), or would multi-vector / conditional steering be required?
  3. Why does vector recovery require fp16 mixed precision and fail under bfloat16? Is this a fundamental numerical issue or an artifact of the optimization setup?
  4. How does subliminal steering scale with model size — would larger models (e.g., 70B+) exhibit stronger or weaker imprinting of the steering vector?
  5. Can the recovered vector be used to "unlearn" the bias by subtracting it from the student, providing a potential mitigation strategy?
  6. Are there data augmentation or filtering techniques that could break the correlation between steering vectors and generated sequences, preventing both subliminal transfer and vector recovery?
