From Insight to Action: A Novel Framework for Interpretability-Guided Data Selection in Large Language Models

TL;DR: IGDS uses Sparse Autoencoders to identify causal task features inside LLMs, then selects "Feature-Resonant Data" that maximally activate these features for fine-tuning. With only 50% data, it beats full-dataset SFT by 17.4% on Math (Gemma-2-2B) and consistently outperforms quality/diversity-based baselines across three tasks and three model families.

核心贡献

  • 提出“从洞察到行动”(Insight2Action)的闭环优化范式： 首次将机制可解释性（SAE特征）从被动分析工具转变为驱动数据选择的主动性优化管道，验证了“模型内部的因果任务特征是数据选择的最有效信号”这一原则。
  • 设计两阶段IGDS框架： 第一阶段通过高频召回（frequency recall）+ 因果干预过滤（interventional filtering）从数百万SAE特征中精确定位对任务有因果性正向影响的特征子集；第二阶段用特征共振得分（Feature-Resonant Score, FRS）量化并排序候选数据的训练价值。
  • 跨模型跨任务的广泛验证： 在Gemma-2-2B、LLaMA-3.1-8B、Qwen3-8B三个模型家族的数学推理、摘要、翻译三种任务上一致超越随机选择、质量筛选（IFD/Loss）和多样性筛选（ZIP）等基线方法。
  • 提供机制层面的因果证据： 实验证实所识别特征的激活强度与下游任务性能之间存在强正相关，验证了特征选择的有效性具有机制层面的解释而不仅是经验巧合。
  • 数据效率极高： 在20%、50%、80%三种采样率下IGDS均大幅领先基线，甚至在多数场景下超越全量数据微调结果。

Key Contributions

  • Proposes the Insight2Action closed-loop paradigm: Transforms mechanistic interpretability (SAE features) from a passive analysis tool into a proactive data selection and optimization pipeline, validating that a model's causally-validated internal features are the most potent signal for data selection.
  • Designs the two-stage IGDS framework: Stage 1 uses high-frequency recall followed by causal intervention filtering to precisely identify causally-beneficial task features from millions of SAE dimensions; Stage 2 uses the Feature-Resonant Score (FRS) to quantify training data utility and rank candidate samples.
  • Extensive cross-model, cross-task validation: Consistently outperforms random selection, quality-based (IFD, Loss), and diversity-based (ZIP) baselines on math reasoning, summarization, and translation tasks across Gemma-2-2B, LLaMA-3.1-8B, and Qwen3-8B.
  • Provides mechanistic causal evidence: Confirms a strong positive correlation between targeted feature activation amplification and downstream performance improvement, going beyond empirical gains to demonstrate genuine mechanistic alignment.
  • Exceptional data efficiency: IGDS dominates at all sampling ratios (20%, 50%, 80%) and frequently surpasses full-dataset fine-tuning performance.

Methodology

IGDS operates in two sequential stages:

Stage 1: Task Feature Identification (Coarse-to-Fine)

  1. High-Frequency Candidate Recalling: Given a small corpus of task-related samples, the framework monitors SAE feature activations at a critical token position (e.g., the final token of the prompt). A feature is retained as a candidate if its activation frequency across samples exceeds a threshold (e.g., 80%). This step drastically prunes the SAE's massive feature space (millions of dimensions) down to just a few basis points of the total.
  1. Interventional Filtering for Causal Validation: High frequency only implies correlation, not causation. For each candidate feature, the framework performs a targeted intervention — the feature's influence vector (activation × decoder weight) is added to the model's residual stream, and the resulting change in task performance is measured on a held-out validation set. Features whose amplification yields a positive performance gain (Δ) are retained as causally-validated task features. The top-K features by Δ form the final feature set F_task.

Stage 2: Feature-Based Data Scoring and Selection

For each data point x in a large candidate pool D_pool, the Feature-Resonant Score (FRS) is computed as the sum of activation magnitudes of all validated task features at the same critical token position used in Stage 1:

S(x) = Σ_{f ∈ F_task} a_f(x, t*)

Data points are ranked by FRS, and the top-ranked samples are selected according to a predefined sampling ratio to form the final SFT training set D_SFT — a high-potency subset densely packed with targeted, task-relevant signals.

SAE Configuration: Pre-trained SAEs (Gemma-Scope, Llama-Scope) were used for Gemma-2 and LLaMA-3.1. For Qwen3-8B, a custom JumpReLU SAE was trained on layers 12-18 with a 16x expansion factor (d_sae = 65,536).

Results & Highlights

  • Math (Gemma-2-2B): IGDS achieves 37.8% pass@8 accuracy vs. 32.2% for full SFT, a +17.4% relative gain, using only 50% of the training data. Random selection achieves only 29.2%.
  • Math (LLaMA-3.1-8B): IGDS reaches 45.8%, exceeding full SFT (45.0%) and far ahead of the next-best baseline IFD (36.8%).
  • Summarization (All models): IGDS consistently achieves the highest ROUGE-1 scores, with +0.4% improvement over full SFT on Gemma-2-2B and Qwen3-8B.
  • Translation (COMET): IGDS scores 68.14 on Gemma-2-2B (vs. 65.93 full SFT) and 78.45 on LLaMA-3.1-8B (vs. 77.68 full SFT).
  • Robust to sampling ratio: IGDS outperforms all baselines at every data budget (20%, 50%, 80%) and always surpasses the Full dataset baseline.
  • Feature-performance correlation: Strong positive correlation confirmed between post-fine-tuning activation magnitude of causal features and final task performance — models with higher median feature activation consistently achieve better task scores.
  • Feature stability: The top-ranked Math feature (F_14,11575) consistently emerges as the most impactful regardless of which dataset (GSM8K, Math500, OpenR1) is used for discovery, with significant overlap in top-5 features across sources.
  • Computational efficiency: IGDS adds only ~20% overhead over the loss-based baseline (compared to IFD's +167% for Math), as feature computation is integrated into the single forward pass required for gradient calculation.
  • General capability preservation: Fine-tuning with IGDS-selected data does not degrade MMLU or TruthfulQA scores — performance fluctuates within a narrow margin of -3% to +1% relative to the base model.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The paper's core insight — using internal model features rather than external quality signals for data selection — is genuinely novel. While prior work has used SAE features for inference-time interventions, IGDS is the first to transform interpretability findings into a general data selection pipeline for training-time optimization. The Insight2Action paradigm represents a conceptual advance over the predominant "external metric" paradigm in data selection.

Rigor: The experimental design is thorough, covering three distinct task types, three model families, and multiple competitive baselines. Ablation studies validate both the frequency recall and causal filtering components. Feature stability across datasets provides evidence of robustness. However, the selection ratio was fixed at 50% for main results; the 20%/80% analysis was only performed on Math with Gemma-2-2B, limiting generalization of the scalability claim.

Reproducibility: Reasonable — model parameters, SAE configurations, training hyperparameters are all documented. The use of publicly available SAEs (Gemma-Scope, Llama-Scope) and open datasets (OpenR1-Math-220k, DialogSum, WMT24) supports reproducibility. For Qwen3, the custom SAE training details are provided but the SAE itself is not yet released (authors commit to future open-sourcing).

Significance: High practical value. Data selection is a central problem in LLM training, and IGDS offers a new, principled angle that outperforms established methods. The +17.4% gain on Math with half data is striking and could influence how practitioners approach fine-tuning data curation.

Red Flags:

  • The performance on Qwen3-8B is notably weaker (e.g., +0.4% on Math), which the authors attribute to limited SAE coverage (only layers 12-18). This transparently exposes a critical dependency: the method is only as good as the available SAEs. This is an honest self-critique, but it limits immediate practical applicability for models without mature SAE ecosystems.
  • The paper reports only pass@8 for Math evaluation, which is a lenient metric that may inflate apparent performance gains compared to pass@1.
  • The 50% selection ratio used in main experiments is somewhat arbitrary; stronger justification for this choice would strengthen the argument.
  • The summarization task gains are relatively modest (0.2-0.4% over full SFT), which the paper does not deeply analyze.

Strengths

  • Principled, mechanism-driven approach: Unlike most data selection work that treats the model as a black box and relies on external proxy signals, IGDS directly leverages the model's own internal representations, providing a more direct and potentially more reliable signal.
  • Strong empirical results with mechanism-level validation: The paper goes beyond reporting performance numbers by demonstrating the causal link between feature activation and task performance, including correlation analysis and qualitative vocabulary shift evidence via word clouds.
  • Computationally practical: The ~20% overhead compared to a simple loss baseline makes IGDS viable for real-world use, unlike many interpretability methods that impose substantial computational costs.
  • Cross-task generalization: The framework works across reasoning, generation, and translation tasks without task-specific modifications to the core algorithm, suggesting broad applicability.
  • Honest about limitations: The authors explicitly discuss SAE quality as a bottleneck and commit to improving Qwen3 coverage, which builds credibility.

Limitations

  • SAE dependency is a major bottleneck: IGDS performance is strongly tied to SAE quality and coverage. For models without mature SAE ecosystems, performance degrades significantly (as seen with Qwen3-8B). Training high-quality SAEs is itself expensive (~7 days per layer on H100 for Qwen3).
  • Limited Qwen3 layer coverage: Only layers 12-18 were covered by the custom SAE, which may miss task-relevant features in early or very late layers — especially problematic for Math tasks where features are distributed across all layers.
  • Fixed critical token position assumption: The framework assumes a single critical token position per task (e.g., final prompt token) for feature extraction, which may not capture features that operate at intermediate reasoning steps.
  • Single-feature scoring (k=1 is best): The ablation shows that using only the top-1 feature for scoring works best; expanding beyond k=3 degrades performance. This suggests the framework may be overly reliant on a single dominant feature and less capable of leveraging complementary feature combinations.
  • Evaluation metric choices: Pass@8 on Math may overstate practical improvements; pass@1 results would provide a more stringent test. Summarization gains are quite marginal.
  • Scale limitations: Experiments are restricted to 2B-8B models. The method's behavior at larger scales (e.g., 70B+) is unknown.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does IGDS scale to larger models (70B+) where SAE training is even more expensive, and does the feature identification signal become stronger or weaker with model scale?
  2. Can the causal filtering step be automated or made more efficient, given that it currently requires running interventions on a validation set for every candidate feature?
  3. How does IGDS perform when the target task is not well-represented in the model's pre-training distribution (i.e., when meaningful internal task features may not exist)?
  4. Would combining IGDS with existing quality or diversity signals (e.g., a hybrid FRS + IFD score) yield further improvements, given that the methods may be complementary?
  5. Can the framework be extended to multi-task or continual fine-tuning scenarios where feature interference between tasks becomes a concern?
  6. The paper focuses on positive features (amplification improves performance). Are there inhibitory features whose suppression would also benefit task performance, and could they be incorporated into the scoring function?
