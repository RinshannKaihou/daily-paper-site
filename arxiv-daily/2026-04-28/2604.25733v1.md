## Methodology (English)

The lecture notes adopt a **theoretical, formal-methods perspective** on neural network verification. The overarching methodology can be characterized by the following framework, which is also the intellectual backbone of the author's research program at CNRS/LMF:

1. **Network Modeling**: Neural networks (feed-forward, RNN, attention-based, Transformer) are formalized as mathematical objects amenable to logical analysis. This involves defining their semantics in terms of input-output relations or language acceptance.

2. **Specification Languages**: Properties of interest (e.g., robustness, safety, fairness, input-output constraints) are expressed in formal specification languages. Based on the lab's related work, likely specification formalisms include:
   - First-order logic formulas of the form: ∀x, y. Pre(x) ∧ InOut(x, y) ⇒ Post(y)
   - Temporal logics (LTL, CTL) for sequential/recurrent models
   - Automata-based specifications

3. **Verification Techniques** (two broad paradigms):
   - **Direct verification**: Encode the network and specification together as a constraint satisfaction problem, then use SMT solvers (e.g., Reluplex, Marabou framework) or MILP to determine satisfiability. These methods are sound and complete but computationally expensive.
   - **Abstraction-based / surrogate-model verification**: Extract a simpler, interpretable formal model (e.g., DFA, visibly pushdown grammar) from the neural network using active automata learning (e.g., Angluin's L* algorithm). Then apply classical model checking to this surrogate model. This approach is the author's primary research contribution (see ATVA 2021, ICGI 2021).

4. **Architecture-specific considerations**: Different neural architectures require different formal treatments: ReLU-based feed-forward networks admit piecewise-linear analysis; RNNs map naturally to automata/language models; attention and Transformers pose more complex challenges due to their dynamic, content-dependent computation graphs.
