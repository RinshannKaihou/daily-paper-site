Sustained Gradient Alignment Mediates Subliminal Learning in a Multi-Step Setting: Evidence from MNIST Auxiliary Logit Distillation Experiment

Chayanon Kitkana, Shivam Arora

arXiv: 2604.25779v1

Date: 2026-04-28

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.25779v1

一句话总结： 本文通过 MNIST 辅助 logit 蒸馏实验证明，在多步训练中蒸馏梯度与特质梯度之间存在持续但微弱的正向对齐（cosine similarity ~0.008），这种对齐是 subliminal learning 的因果驱动力（第一阶效应主导）；通过梯度投影移除该对齐分量即可阻断特质获取，而 liminal training 仅能减弱对齐却无法消除特质获取。
TL;DR: In an MNIST auxiliary logit distillation experiment, we show that a sustained weak positive gradient alignment (cos sim ~0.008) between distillation and trait gradients causally drives subliminal learning across multi-step training; removing the trait-aligned component via gradient projection stops trait acquisition, while liminal training only attenuates alignment and fails to prevent it.

核心贡献

  • 多步对齐验证： 首次在经验上证明，蒸馏梯度与特质梯度之间的正向对齐在多步训练中持续存在（78.1% 的训练步上对齐为正），而非仅存在于初始点。
  • 因果性证据： 通过反向 PCGrad 梯度投影移除蒸馏梯度中与特质梯度对齐的分量，学生模型的分类准确率从 55.28% 降至接近随机水平（10.14%），而蒸馏进程不受影响，有力证明了梯度对齐是特质获取的因果机制。
  • Liminal training 局限性： 发现 liminal training 仅在训练初期减弱对齐，但未能消除特质对齐分量；特质获取被延迟但最终水平几乎不变（48.97% vs 55.28% 控制组），说明仅靠减弱对齐不足以在“第一阶效应主导”的场景下抑制特质传递。
  • 第一阶效应主导地位： 证明了在该设置中第一阶效应是 subliminal learning 的主要驱动力，但承认这不一定适用于高阶效应主导的其他场景。

Key Contributions

  • Multi-step alignment persistence: Empirically demonstrates that positive gradient alignment between distillation and trait gradients persists throughout multi-step training (78.1% of steps have positive alignment), extending the single-step theory of Cloud et al. (2025).
  • Causal evidence via gradient projection: Adapting PCGrad in reverse to remove the trait-aligned component of the distillation gradient drops student classification accuracy from 55.28% to near-chance 10.14% without affecting distillation progress, establishing a causal link between gradient alignment and trait acquisition.
  • Liminal training insufficiency: Shows that liminal training (Yanagisawa et al., 2025) reduces early alignment but does not eliminate the trait-aligned component; trait acquisition is delayed but final trait transfer is essentially unchanged (48.97% vs. 55.28% control), suggesting that attenuation alone is insufficient when first-order effects dominate.
  • First-order dominance: Provides evidence that first-order gradient effects are the primary driver of subliminal learning in this setup, while acknowledging this may not generalize to settings where higher-order loss landscape effects dominate.

Methodology

The study uses the MNIST MLP classifier auxiliary logit distillation experiment from Cloud et al. (2025). The setup is as follows:

  • Model: A fully connected MLP with two hidden layers of 256 units (ReLU), taking 28x28 MNIST images as input and outputting 13 logits (10 class logits + 3 auxiliary/no-class logits).
  • Teacher: Trained on MNIST classification using the 10 class logits (cross-entropy loss).
  • Student: Initialized identically to the teacher, then distilled using only the 3 auxiliary logits via KL divergence on a synthetic noise dataset (60,000 uniform random images). The student never sees real MNIST labels during distillation.
  • Subliminal learning: Despite distilling only on auxiliary logits on noise data, the student acquires above-chance MNIST digit classification ability (the unintended teacher trait).

Three experiments are conducted across 100 random seeds:

  1. Gradient alignment monitoring: Track the inner product and cosine similarity between the trait gradient ∇L_trait (cross-entropy on MNIST audit set) and the distillation gradient ∇L_distill (KL divergence on noise data) at every training step.
  1. Gradient projection ablation (reverse PCGrad): When the trait and distillation gradients are positively aligned, project the distillation gradient onto the normal plane of the trait gradient, removing the trait-aligned component. This is the inverse of the standard PCGrad operation (which removes conflicting components).
  1. Liminal training evaluation: Apply KL regularization between the frozen base model's auxiliary logit distribution and the current student's, with λ_KL = 1 for the first epoch, linearly decaying to zero. Temperature T = 2.0. Monitor whether this suppresses trait acquisition.

Results & Highlights

  • Alignment persistence: Mean cosine similarity between trait and distillation gradients is 0.00752 ± 0.00167, consistently positive but near-orthogonal. 78.1% ± 10.2% of training steps show positive alignment. Alignment is highest during early training, coinciding with the fastest period of trait acquisition.
  • Causal ablation: Removing trait-aligned gradient components reduces average test accuracy from 55.28% ± 10.48% (control) to 10.14% ± 1.33% (near chance level of 10%). KL divergence training curves remain visually indistinguishable between control and intervention, confirming that distillation proceeds normally. Cosine similarity after projection stays close to zero.
  • Liminal training failure: Liminal training reduces alignment during the first epoch, but final test accuracy remains 48.97% ± 9.63% vs. 55.28% control. Trait acquisition slows initially but accelerates as regularization decays, leaving final cross-entropy largely unchanged.
  • Critical period alignment: The highest alignment occurs early in training, providing a mechanistic explanation for the "critical period" phenomenon observed in Yanagisawa et al. (2025).

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty (Moderate): The paper builds directly on Cloud et al. (2025) and Yanagisawa et al. (2025). The multi-step alignment analysis is a natural extension of the single-step theory rather than a fundamentally new insight. The reverse-PCGrad ablation is clever but is essentially an application of an existing technique (Yu et al., 2020) in the opposite direction. The liminal training evaluation provides useful negative results but does not introduce a new method.
  • Rigor (Moderate): 100 seeds provide reasonable statistical power for an MNIST-scale experiment. The ablation design cleanly isolates gradient alignment as a causal factor. However, the paper studies only a single toy setup (MNIST MLP with 3 auxiliary logits). The claim that "first-order effects dominate" is supported in this setup but no evidence is provided for generalization to larger models or more complex tasks.
  • Reproducibility (Good): Full code is available on GitHub. Implementation details (architecture, hyperparameters, data splits) are clearly documented in the appendix. All experiments use standard, widely available components.
  • Significance (Moderate): The AI safety implications are relevant given increasing use of knowledge distillation. However, the gap between an MNIST MLP and production LLM distillation is enormous. The finding that liminal training fails is potentially important, but only demonstrated in one toy setting.
  • Potential red flags: (1) Short, 7-page workshop paper — may not have undergone rigorous peer review. (2) Average test accuracy of 55.28% on 10-class MNIST (chance = 10%) represents only modest trait acquisition — the signal is real but weak. (3) The gradient projection method requires access to ground-truth trait gradients, which is unrealistic for real-world safety applications where the unwanted trait may be unknown. (4) The authors acknowledge higher-order effects could dominate in other settings, but provide no characterization of when this might occur.

Strengths

  • Clean, well-controlled experimental design with clear causal interventions.
  • The reverse-PCGrad ablation elegantly isolates the role of gradient alignment without affecting distillation.
  • 100-seed statistics provide reliable quantification of the weak but consistent alignment signal.
  • Honest reporting of negative results (liminal training failure) with mechanistic explanation.
  • Code fully open-sourced, enhancing reproducibility.
  • Clear connection between empirical findings and the "critical period" concept from prior work.

Limitations

  • Single toy setup: Only one model architecture (MLP), one dataset (MNIST), and one distillation configuration (3 auxiliary logits). Generalization to transformers, LLMs, or more complex tasks is unknown.
  • Weak signal: The gradient alignment (cos sim ~0.008) is very small, and trait acquisition accuracy (55% on 10-way classification) is modest. The effect might not scale meaningfully.
  • Gradient projection as mitigation is impractical: The ablation requires computing trait gradients on ground-truth labels, which assumes knowledge of the unwanted trait — a circular requirement for real safety applications.
  • No higher-order analysis: The paper asserts first-order dominance but does not quantitatively characterize higher-order contributions or identify conditions under which they become important.
  • Liminal training negative result is setup-specific: The failure of liminal training is shown only in this one configuration; it may succeed in other settings (as reported by Yanagisawa et al. for small LLMs).

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does sustained gradient alignment also mediate subliminal learning in transformer-based architectures and LLM-scale distillation, or do higher-order effects become dominant at scale?
  2. Can the gradient projection framework be adapted for practical mitigation without requiring ground-truth knowledge of the unwanted trait (e.g., using unsupervised trait detection or representation-level interventions)?
  3. Under what conditions (architecture, dataset, distillation strategy) does liminal training succeed vs. fail? Is there a threshold of first-order vs. higher-order effect dominance that predicts liminal training efficacy?
  4. How does the number of auxiliary logits or the dimensionality of the distillation target affect the strength and persistence of gradient alignment?
  5. Can the "critical period" of high early-training alignment be exploited for more targeted and computationally efficient mitigation strategies?
  6. What is the relationship between gradient alignment magnitude and final trait acquisition strength — is there a predictive threshold?
