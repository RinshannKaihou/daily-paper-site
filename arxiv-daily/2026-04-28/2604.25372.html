From Cursed to Competitive: Closing the ZO–FO Gap via Input-to-State Stability

arXiv: 2604.25372v1

Date: 2026-04-28

一句话总结

本文颠覆了零阶优化方法必然比一阶方法多出维度依赖的固有认知，通过动力学系统视角和输入-状态稳定性理论证明：在期望意义下，零阶方法可以享有与一阶方法相同的收敛速率，维度依赖仅影响收敛邻域的大小而非迭代复杂度。

TL;DR

This paper challenges the long-held belief that zeroth-order (ZO) optimization methods inherently suffer from an extra O(n) dimension dependence compared to first-order (FO) methods. Using a dynamical systems perspective and input-to-state stability (ISS) theory, the authors prove that, in expectation, ZO methods achieve the same decay rate as their FO counterparts and converge to a neighborhood of the FO fixed point — where the neighborhood size (not the iteration complexity) carries the dimension dependence and can be made arbitrarily small by tuning algorithmic parameters. The framework is applied to gradient descent, heavy ball, and Nesterov's accelerated gradient, under both strong convexity and L2 regularization.

核心贡献

  1. 范式转换: 首次提出 ZO 方法的维度依赖性应从迭代复杂度转移到收敛邻域大小，颠覆了自 Nesterov & Spokoiny (2017) 以来关于 ZO 方法必然存在 O(n) 额外迭代代价的共识。
  1. ISS 统一框架: 将优化算法建模为离散时间动力学系统，利用输入-状态稳定性理论建立了一个通用工具——只要 FO 算法收敛，其 ZO 对应版本自动继承相同的衰减速率并收敛到 FO 不动点的邻域，无需针对每种算法单独证明。
  1. 显式扰动界: 对 GD、Heavy Ball 和 Nesterov 加速梯度分别推导了扰动项的显式上界（Lemmas 2-4），揭示了平滑参数 μ、步长 h 和采样方向数 t 如何控制扰动大小。
  1. 非强凸拓展: 通过 L2 正则化提供足够收缩性，将框架推广到非强凸目标函数，并分析了正则化强度与邻域大小之间的可控权衡（Lemma 5, Theorem 6）。
  1. 实验验证: 在 1000 维强凸二次问题和 MNIST 二分类神经网络（参数量最高达 20 万）上验证了理论，证实 ZO 轨迹紧贴 FO 轨迹且无额外维度依赖性。

Key Contributions

  1. Paradigm Shift: Demonstrates that the dimension penalty of ZO methods manifests in the radius of the convergence neighborhood rather than in the iteration complexity — fundamentally reframing the ZO-FO gap established since Nesterov & Spokoiny (2017).
  1. Unified ISS Framework: Introduces input-to-state stability as a general-purpose tool for transferring convergence guarantees from FO algorithms to their ZO counterparts. Once an FO algorithm is proven to converge, ISS automatically guarantees ZO convergence to a neighborhood of the same fixed point with the same decay profile.
  1. Explicit Perturbation Bounds: Derives tight, interpretable perturbation bounds for ZO-GD (Lemma 2), ZO-Heavy Ball (Lemma 3), and ZO-Nesterov (Lemma 4), each decomposing into a smoothing bias term (controlled by μ) and a variance term (controlled by h and t).
  1. Extension to Non-Strongly-Convex Settings: Shows that L2 regularization provides sufficient contraction for the ISS framework even when the original objective is not strongly convex, with an explicit trade-off between regularization strength and proximity to the original minimizer (Lemma 5, Theorem 6).
  1. Empirical Validation: Confirms the theory on a 1000-dimensional quadratic problem and on MNIST binary classification with two-layer neural networks (up to ~200K parameters), demonstrating dimension-independent convergence behavior and parameter controllability.

Methodology

The paper adopts a dynamical systems approach to optimization:

  1. Algorithm as Dynamical System: Optimization algorithms are formulated as discrete-time dynamical systems z_{k+1} = w(z_k), where the update map w encodes the algorithm.
  1. Averaged ZO Dynamics: The expectation of the ZO iterate (conditioned on all past random directions) is shown to satisfy z̄_{k+1} = z̄_k - h∇f(z̄_k) + q_k — i.e., the FO dynamics plus a bounded perturbation q_k.
  1. ISS Analysis: Using Lyapunov functions, the authors prove that if the unperturbed FO system is exponentially/asymptotically stable (Theorems 1 & 2), then the perturbed ZO system inherits the same decay rate and converges to a neighborhood of the FO equilibrium, with neighborhood size governed by sup||q_k||.
  1. Perturbation Decomposition: Each perturbation bound is split into two controllable terms:
  • Smoothing bias: scales with μL₁n^{3/2}, controllable via the smoothing parameter μ
  • Variance: scales with hL₁σ/√(t(1-ρ)), controllable via step size h and number of sampled directions t
  1. Algorithm-Specific Analysis:
  • GD: Uses strong convexity to get contraction ρ = 1 - hβ + 2L₁²h² < 1
  • Heavy Ball: Augmented state-space, contraction ρ_HB = (1+2h₂)² - h₁(1+h₂)β + 4h₁h₂L₁ + 2h₁²L₁² < 1
  • NAG: Key structural advantage — strong convexity contraction acts directly on the lookahead error, yielding ρ_NAG = (1 - h₁β + 2h₁²L₁²)(1+2h₂)² < 1
  • L2-Regularized GD: Contraction via regularization, ρ = c² + 4cL₁h + 8L₁²h² < 1 with c ∈ [0,1)

Experimental Highlights

  • Quadratic (n=1000, condition number 100): ZO-GD tracks GD closely when h is small enough. Parameter sweeps confirm that h and μ control smoothing bias while h and t control variance — exactly as predicted.
  • Neural Network (MNIST, up to 201K params): All six methods (GD/HB/NAG + ZO counterparts) achieve 99.55% training and 99.40% test accuracy. ZO methods take approximately 4x longer per iteration but require NO extra iterations.
  • Dimension Scaling: Across network sizes from ~12.7K to ~201K parameters, the ZO/FO iteration ratio to reach within 20% of FO final loss stays close to 1 (0.99-1.05), confirming no dimension-dependent iteration penalty.

Critical Assessment

Strengths

  1. Novel Conceptual Reframing: The core insight — that dimension dependence belongs in the neighborhood size rather than the iteration complexity — is genuinely novel and challenges 9+ years of established understanding since Nesterov & Spokoiny (2017).
  1. Unified Framework: The ISS-based approach is elegantly general. Rather than requiring separate convergence proofs for each ZO algorithm variant, the framework provides a "plug-and-play" tool: whenever FO convergence is known, ZO convergence follows automatically.
  1. Rigorous Analysis with Explicit Constants: All perturbation bounds are derived with explicit dependencies on algorithm parameters (μ, h, t), problem parameters (n, L₁, β, σ), and contraction rates (ρ). This gives practitioners clear guidance for parameter selection.
  1. Practical Relevance: The framework covers the three most widely used first-order methods (GD, HB, NAG) and handles both strongly convex and non-strongly-convex (via L2 regularization) settings.
  1. Empirical Completeness: The experiments include parameter sweeps (h, μ, t), dimension scaling, and failure-mode analysis (showing how to recover from divergence), providing strong practical validation.

Limitations

  1. Expectation vs. High Probability: All results are in expectation. The authors acknowledge this as future work. High-probability bounds would strengthen the practical relevance, especially for risk-sensitive applications.
  1. Neighborhood Convergence, Not Exact: ZO methods converge to a neighborhood of the FO fixed point rather than to the exact minimizer. While the neighborhood can be made arbitrarily small, practical constraints on μ (numerical precision) and t (computational budget) impose limits.
  1. Computational Cost Per Iteration: ZO methods require O(t) function evaluations per iteration (vs. one gradient evaluation for FO). The paper reports ~4-6x wall-clock time overhead for ZO methods in the neural network experiments. For very high-dimensional problems, reducing μ sufficiently may require very small values that challenge numerical precision.
  1. Smoothness Assumption: The analysis relies on Lipschitz continuous gradients (L₁-smoothness). Extension to non-smooth settings (common in modern ML with ReLU networks without regularization) is not addressed.
  1. Gaussian Oracle Specific: The analysis is tied to the Gaussian smoothing oracle. Other ZO oracles (e.g., coordinate-wise finite differences) are not covered.
  1. Relaxed Variance Assumption (Remark 4): Under the relaxed Assumption 2 (variance scaling with gradient magnitude), the analysis requires stronger coupling conditions (spectral radius of matrix M < 1), which constrains the admissible (h, t) region.

Relevance

This paper is highly relevant to:

  • ML Practitioners using ZO methods for LLM fine-tuning (MeZO), black-box adversarial attacks, or hyperparameter optimization — it provides theoretical backing for the empirical observation that ZO methods sometimes perform competitively.
  • Optimization Theorists — the ISS framework opens a new direction for analyzing ZO methods beyond traditional complexity bounds.
  • Control Theorists — demonstrates a novel application of ISS to optimization algorithm analysis.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. High-probability guarantees: Can the expectation results be lifted to high-probability bounds using concentration inequalities on the ZO oracle, and would this change the neighborhood size scaling?
  1. Non-smooth extensions: Can the ISS framework handle non-smooth objectives (e.g., via Moreau envelope smoothing or subgradient methods), which are prevalent in deep learning without explicit regularization?
  1. Adaptive parameter selection: The perturbation bounds suggest optimal trade-offs between μ, h, and t. Can an adaptive scheme dynamically tune these parameters during optimization to minimize the neighborhood size given a computational budget?
  1. Stochastic FO baseline: How does ZO with the ISS framework compare to stochastic gradient methods (which also have variance) rather than only deterministic FO methods? The perturbation structure may provide a unified way to analyze both ZO and stochastic FO methods.
  1. Beyond Gaussian smoothing: Can similar ISS-based analyses be developed for coordinate-wise finite differences, directional derivatives with non-Gaussian distributions, or evolutionary strategies?
  1. Tightness of bounds: Are the derived perturbation bounds (particularly the n^{3/2} scaling in the smoothing bias) tight, or can they be improved via more refined analysis of the Gaussian smoothing error?
  1. Convex but not strongly convex: The L2 regularization approach adds bias. Can the ISS framework be applied directly to merely convex objectives without regularization, perhaps using time-varying ISS arguments?
  1. Federated/distributed ZO: In federated learning settings where communicating gradients is expensive, ZO methods are attractive. Does the ISS framework extend to distributed ZO with heterogeneous data?
