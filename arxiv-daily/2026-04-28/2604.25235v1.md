> **一句话总结：** 本文首次系统地将共形预测 (conformal prediction) 应用于 VLM 作为评判者的评测场景，发现 VLM 评判者虽然能够可靠地排序 (ranking)，但无法给出可信的绝对分数 (scoring)，这种“排序-评分脱耦”现象在图表理解和数学推理等视觉密集型任务中尤为严重，且标注质量而非模型规模主导了预测区间的宽度。

> **TL;DR:** This paper presents the first systematic application of conformal prediction to VLM-as-a-Judge evaluation, revealing that VLM judges can reliably rank responses but cannot assign trustworthy absolute scores — a "ranking-scoring decoupling" failure mode that worsens dramatically for vision-heavy tasks like chart understanding and math reasoning, and is driven more by annotation quality than by model scale.

## 核心贡献

- **首次系统性地将共形预测引入 VLM-as-a-Judge 评测**：在不需重新训练的情况下，仅利用评分 token 的 log-probability 将 VLM 评判者的点估计分数转化为带有覆盖保障的校准预测区间 (如“真实人类评分以 90% 概率落入 [2, 4] 区间”)。

- **揭示了任务依赖的不确定性结构**：在 3 个评判模型、14 个视觉任务类别上进行大规模实验，发现美学和自然图像类任务的预测区间仅覆盖约 40% 的评分范围，而图表理解和数学推理任务扩展至约 70%，首次绘制出多模态自动评测的量化可靠性地图。

- **识别了“排序-评分脱耦” (ranking-scoring decoupling) 的关键失败模式**：VLM 评判者在排序任务上相关性很高 (Pearson ρ 最高 0.46, Kendall τ 最高 0.38)，但绝对评分极不可靠 — 仅有 32–34% 的评分与人类完全一致，24–30% 偏差 ≥2 分，这是一个现有评测指标无法捕捉的盲点。

- **证明了数据质量比模型规模更重要**：相同评判者和方法在干净的、多人标注的 Polaris 基准上产生 4.5 倍更窄的预测区间，表明标注质量和任务难度是区间宽度的主导因素，而非模型缩放或共形方法的选择。

- **引入了任务条件校准 (Mondrian CP)**：通过对任务难度进行条件化，在简单任务上获得更窄区间，同时在困难任务上改善覆盖率。

## Key Contributions

- **First systematic application of conformal prediction to VLM-as-a-Judge**: Converts point scores into calibrated prediction intervals with provable coverage guarantees (e.g., 90% confidence that the true human score lies within [2, 4]), using only score-token log-probabilities with zero retraining cost.

- **Task-dependent uncertainty mapping**: Across 3 VLM judges (LLaVA-Critic-7B, Phi-4 15B, Gemini 2.5 Flash) and 14 visual task categories on the MLLM-as-a-Judge benchmark, prediction interval width varies up to 70% — aesthetics/natural images yield ~40%-width intervals while chart/math reasoning expands to ~70%, providing the first quantitative reliability landscape for multimodal evaluation.

- **Ranking-scoring decoupling identified as a critical failure mode**: Judges achieve high ranking correlation (Pearson ρ up to 0.46) while producing wide, uninformative absolute intervals — a blind spot missed by standard correlation metrics. Only 32–34% exact agreement with human scores, with 24–30% deviating by ≥2 points on a 5-point scale.

- **Data quality over model scale**: The same judge and conformal method produce 4.5x narrower intervals on Polaris (clean, multi-annotator benchmark) vs. MLLM-as-a-Judge — demonstrating that annotation quality and task difficulty, not model size, dominate interval width.

- **Task-conditional calibration via Mondrian CP**: Conditioning on task difficulty yields narrower intervals for easy tasks while improving coverage for hard tasks, offering a practical path toward task-aware reliability.

## Methodology

**Problem Setup.** The paper studies VLM-based automated evaluation of multimodal AI outputs. Standard practice uses VLMs to assign point scores (e.g., 4/5) to system-generated responses, but these scores carry no reliability information — a 4 from a highly confident model and a 4 from an uncertain model are indistinguishable.

**Conformal Prediction Framework.** Conformal prediction is a distribution-free, post-hoc framework that, given any point predictor, produces prediction intervals with finite-sample coverage guarantees. The key insight: convert a VLM judge's point score into a calibrated interval using only the log-probabilities of score tokens (e.g., the tokens for "1", "2", "3", "4", "5"), requiring no retraining or access to model internals beyond token probabilities.

**Experimental Setup.** Three VLM judges are evaluated:
- **LLaVA-Critic-7B**: Open-source, specialized for multimodal evaluation scoring
- **Phi-4-reasoning-vision-15B**: 15B reasoning model with chain-of-thought
- **Gemini 2.5 Flash**: Closed-source API model; the strongest ranker but severely overconfident (83% of predictions have softmax probability >0.99)

Two benchmarks are used:
- **MLLM-as-a-Judge** (Chen et al., 2024): 5,717 instances across 14 visual task categories, single-annotator
- **Polaris**: 8,726 image-caption pairs with multi-annotator scores, serving as a clean reference

**Conformal Methods Compared.** Eight methods are benchmarked: R2CCP (primary), CHR, Boosted LCP, Naive Split CP, LVD, CQR, Asymmetric CQR, and OrdinalAPS. Methods are evaluated on coverage (how close to the 90% target) and interval width (efficiency). R2CCP achieves the best calibration-efficiency balance (90.0% coverage, 3.05 width on a [1,5] scale), while CHR yields slightly narrower intervals (2.97 width) at the cost of mild under-coverage (88.0%). CQR and OrdinalAPS collapse to near-full-range, uninformative intervals.

**Mondrian Conformal Prediction.** A task-conditional variant that partitions the calibration set by task difficulty (measured via human score variance or judge confidence), then applies CP within each partition, enabling different interval widths for easy vs. hard tasks.

## Results & Highlights

| Finding | Key Numbers |
|---|---|
| **Point prediction failure** | All 3 judges: 32–34% exact human agreement on 5-point scale; 24–30% deviate by ≥2 points; Spearman ρ = 0.30–0.33, Pearson ρ = 0.35–0.46 |
| **Task-dependent interval width** | Aesthetics/natural images: ~40% of score range; Charts/math reasoning: ~70% of score range |
| **Gemini overconfidence** | 83.0% of predictions have softmax prob >0.99 (vs. 25.6% for LLaVA-Critic); yet it's the strongest ranker |
| **R2CCP calibration** | 90.0% coverage at width 3.05 on [1,5] scale — hits the target exactly |
| **Data quality effect** | 4.5x narrower intervals on Polaris (multi-annotator) vs. MLLM-as-a-Judge (single-annotator) |
| **Degenerate conformal methods** | CQR and OrdinalAPS collapse to ≥3.95 width — practically the full score range, useless in practice |
| **Mondrian CP improvement** | Narrower intervals on easy tasks, better coverage on hard tasks |

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty.** The core idea — applying conformal prediction to VLM judges — is straightforward and builds directly on established CP literature. The novelty lies not in inventing new CP methods but in (1) being the first to systematically do this for multimodal VLM-as-a-Judge, (2) discovering and naming the "ranking-scoring decoupling" phenomenon, and (3) quantifying task-dependent uncertainty across 14 categories. This is a solid "first systematic study" paper rather than a fundamentally new method paper.

**Rigor.** Strong rigor: 3 judges, 14 task categories, 8 CP methods, 2 benchmarks, multiple metrics, statistical reporting. The choice of R2CCP as primary method is well-justified by hitting the target coverage exactly. The degenerate collapse of CQR/OrdinalAPS is honestly reported rather than hidden. The paper is careful to distinguish correlation metrics (Spearman, Pearson, Kendall) from absolute scoring accuracy.

**Reproducibility.** Code is released. Benchmarks are publicly available. Two of three judges (LLaVA-Critic, Phi-4) are open-weight. Gemini 2.5 Flash is closed-source, which limits full reproducibility of the overconfidence analysis, but this is a practical necessity for studying deployed judges. The conformal prediction pipeline uses only token log-probabilities — a low barrier to reproduction.

**Significance.** Moderate-to-high. As VLMs become standard evaluators in LLM leaderboards and research pipelines, the finding that ranking ability masks absolute scoring unreliability is practically important. The task-dependent uncertainty map is immediately actionable for practitioners deciding when to trust automated evaluation. However, the paper does not propose a solution to reduce uncertainty — it diagnoses the problem and offers conformal prediction as a calibration tool, not a fix.

**Red Flags.** None identified. The paper is honest about limitations, including the restricted scope to 5-point Likert scales and the fact that conformal intervals can still be wide enough to be practically uninformative for hard tasks.

## Strengths

- **Honest science**: Directly names and quantifies a failure mode (ranking-scoring decoupling) that is uncomfortable for the VLM-as-a-Judge paradigm but practically critical
- **Comprehensive experimental design**: 3 judges x 14 categories x 8 methods x 2 benchmarks provides strong empirical grounding
- **Actionable insights**: The finding that annotation quality matters 4.5x more than method/model choice has immediate practical implications for benchmark design
- **Clear, intuitive framing**: Conformal prediction is explained accessibly; the distinction between ranking and scoring is a crisp conceptual contribution
- **Practical toolkit**: The method requires only token log-probabilities — deployable on any VLM that exposes them, with zero retraining
- **Negative results reported**: Degenerate conformal methods (CQR, OrdinalAPS), poor point-prediction baselines, and Gemini's extreme overconfidence are all reported transparently

## Limitations

- **Restricted to 5-point Likert scales**: The method relies on discrete score tokens; unclear how it would extend to continuous scoring, pairwise preferences, or Elo-based evaluation protocols increasingly used in LLM benchmarking
- **Conformal intervals can be practically uninformative**: For hard tasks (charts, math), intervals covering ~70% of the score range may be technically calibrated but practically useless — the paper acknowledges but does not solve this
- **Single-turn, single-modal judge evaluation**: Does not study multi-turn judge protocols, debate-style evaluation, or judge ensembles that might mitigate the identified issues
- **No proposed solution to reduce uncertainty**: The paper diagnoses and quantifies the problem but offers conformal prediction as a calibration/warning mechanism, not as a method to make judges more accurate
- **Gemini access-dependent**: The strongest finding of overconfidence (83% at >0.99) relies on a closed-source model whose token probabilities may not be available to all practitioners
- **Limited judge diversity**: All three judges are recent models; no comparison with older VLMs or non-VLM LLM judges to understand whether the ranking-scoring decoupling is a property of specific architectures or a general phenomenon

## Relevance

本文与对 LLM/VLM 自动化评测 (LLM-as-a-Judge) 可靠性的关注高度相关。如果您的研究涉及使用 VLM 或 LLM 作为自动评判者来衡量模型输出质量，本文揭示的“排序可靠但绝对评分不可靠”现象是一个必须了解的关键盲点。共形预测提供的校准预测区间为在实际评测 pipeline 中引入不确定性感知提供了即插即用的工具。此外，任务难度和标注质量主导区间宽度的发现，对设计更可靠的评测基准具有直接指导意义。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Can conformalized VLM judges be combined with ensemble methods (multiple judges, multiple prompts) to produce narrower intervals without sacrificing coverage?
2. How does ranking-scoring decoupling behave in pairwise preference evaluation (e.g., Chatbot Arena Elo), as opposed to the Likert-scale scoring studied here?
3. Does the task-dependence of uncertainty generalize across VLM architectures, or is it specific to the training data mixtures of the evaluated models?
4. Can the conformal intervals be used as a signal for adaptive evaluation — investing more human annotation budget only on high-uncertainty instances?
5. What is the minimum annotation quality threshold (number of annotators, inter-annotator agreement) needed to produce practically useful conformal intervals?
