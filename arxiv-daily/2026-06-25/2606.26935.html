Where Do CoT Training Gains Land in LLM based Agents?

一句话总结： 本文通过比较"无 CoT 直接预测动作"与"经 CoT 推理后预测动作"，发现 CoT 训练的主要收益并不在于放大推理的优势，而是在于提升模型直接从 prompt 预测动作的能力，且随训练推进模型越来越不依赖 CoT 来修正动作；基于这一观察，作者通过选择性屏蔽部分训练样本上的 action-token 监督来改善 OOD 泛化。
TL;DR: This paper investigates what Chain-of-Thought (CoT) training actually improves in LLM-based agents by comparing "prompt actions" (predicted without CoT) against "CoT actions" (predicted with CoT) across checkpoints. It finds that CoT training mainly improves prompt-action quality rather than widening the advantage of explicit reasoning, that later checkpoints rely less on CoT to revise actions, and that selectively masking action-token supervision on a fraction of examples boosts out-of-domain generalization.

核心贡献

  • 重新定义问题视角： 将"CoT 训练究竟改善了什么"这一被忽视的问题形式化为"模型变得更擅长通过推理改变动作，还是更擅长直接从 prompt 预测动作"，并把先前关于 CoT 不可信（unfaithful）的发现引入 agent 训练语境。
  • 实证揭示 CoT 训练的收益落点： 通过跨 checkpoint 对比 prompt-action 与 CoT-action，发现 prompt-action 质量显著提升，而两者之间的相对优势保持不变，即 CoT 训练并未放大推理带来的增益。
  • 发现训练动态规律： 后期 checkpoint 的模型更不愿意根据 CoT 修正动作，表明训练越深入，模型对 prompt 的依赖越强、对生成推理的依赖越弱。
  • 提出可操作的训练干预： 基于上述规律，对一部分训练样本的 action-token 监督进行选择性屏蔽（selective masking），并在 OOD 泛化上取得改善。

Key Contributions

  • Reframes the central question. Rather than asking whether CoT "works," the authors ask where the gains from CoT training land — improving action-revision via reasoning, or improving direct action prediction from the prompt — directly connecting prior unfaithfulness findings to agent training.
  • Diagnostic comparison framework. Introduces a clean experimental contrast between "prompt actions" (no CoT) and "CoT actions" (with CoT), evaluated across training checkpoints, to localize the source of improvement.
  • Empirical finding that gains concentrate in prompt-action quality. Reports that the relative advantage of CoT actions over prompt actions stays roughly constant during environment interaction, implying CoT training does not widen the reasoning advantage.
  • Identifies a training-dynamics trend. Shows that later checkpoints are less likely to revise their action in response to generated CoT, indicating growing reliance on the prompt as training proceeds.
  • A motivated training intervention. Uses these observations to design selective masking of action-token supervision on a fraction of examples, improving out-of-domain generalization.

Methodology

The paper builds on the observation that verbalized CoT in language-model agents is often unfaithful and may reflect post-hoc rationalization (the model may already "know" the action before reasoning). To dissect what CoT training actually improves, the authors introduce a controlled comparison between two action-generation modes:

  1. Prompt actions — the agent predicts the action directly from the prompt, without generating a CoT.
  2. CoT actions — the agent generates a CoT and then predicts the action conditioned on it.

They compare these across training checkpoints to track how the relationship between the two modes evolves. Their analysis focuses on two dimensions: (a) the absolute quality trajectory of prompt actions, and (b) the relative advantage of CoT actions over prompt actions during environment interaction.

Motivated by the finding that CoT gains largely land in prompt-action quality and that later checkpoints rely more on the prompt, the authors propose a selective action-token masking scheme: action-token supervision is masked on a fraction of training examples. This is intended to prevent overfitting to direct action prediction and is evaluated on out-of-domain generalization.

Results & Highlights

  • Prompt-action quality improves substantially across checkpoints — a notable portion of the gains from CoT training transfer to no-CoT inference.
  • The relative advantage of CoT actions over prompt actions remains similar during environment interaction, indicating that CoT training does not widen the gap that reasoning provides.
  • Later checkpoints are less likely to revise actions in response to CoT, suggesting the model increasingly commits to prompt-driven actions as training progresses.
  • Selective action-token masking improves out-of-domain generalization, validating the hypothesis that the diagnostic findings have practical training implications.

  • Significance: Potentially high if claims hold. The finding that CoT training gains land in prompt-action quality has direct implications for how we should supervise and evaluate agent training, and could reframe cost-benefit analyses of CoT-based data generation.
  • Red flags:
  • Several headline findings are essentially "no change" / null results (relative advantage stays similar; later checkpoints revise less). These need careful statistical treatment to rule out being artifacts of metric choice or environment.
  • The selective-masking result is presented as a single positive outcome; without ablations over masking fractions and multiple environments, generalizability is uncertain.

Limitations

  • Abstract does not disclose models, environments, checkpoints, masking fractions, or baselines — quantitative scale is unverifiable.
  • Several key results are null/constant-effect findings that require strong statistical evidence to be convincing.
  • Selective-masking result is presented without ablation breadth in the abstract.
  • No discussion (in abstract) of whether findings hold across multiple environments, model scales, or CoT-data sources.
  • "Greater reliance on the prompt" is an inference from revision behavior; alternative explanations (e.g., the model learning more confident CoT that happens to match prompt action) are not addressed in the abstract.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Does the "gains land in prompt-action quality" finding replicate across model scales, environments, and different CoT data-generation pipelines (e.g., distillation vs. self-generated)?
  • What is the exact mechanism by which CoT-trained models improve prompt-action prediction — is it representation sharing, implicit internal computation, or curriculum effects from the CoT tokens?
  • How does selective action-token masking interact with reinforcement-learning-based agent training (e.g., RL fine-tuning), as opposed to supervised SFT-style training implied by "supervision"?
  • Is the declining tendency to revise actions at later checkpoints a symptom of overconfidence/overfitting, and does it correlate with calibration degradation?
  • Can the diagnostic (prompt-action vs CoT-action gap trajectory) be used as a training signal or early-stopping criterion to decide when CoT supervision is no longer adding value?
  • Are there environments where CoT training does widen the reasoning advantage, and what properties distinguish them?
