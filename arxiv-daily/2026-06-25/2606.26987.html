Where Do Models Find Happiness? Emotion Vectors in Open-Source LLMs

arXiv: 2606.26987

Date: 2026-06-25

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26987

一句话总结： 本文将 Anthropic 在 Claude Sonnet 4.5 上发现的"情感向量"方法复现到两个开源模型（Apertus-8B 与 Gemma-4-E4B），成功恢复了效价（valence）几何（峰值 r=0.76 与 r=0.83，接近 Claude 的 r=0.81），并发现两个模型达到相似峰值的方式截然不同——Gemma 在早期层编码效价随后坍缩，Apertus 则在中后段逐渐建立；而唤醒度（arousal）的提取高度依赖语料库来源。
TL;DR: Replicates Anthropic's emotion-vector findings in two open-weight LLMs (Apertus-8B and Gemma-4-E4B), recovering valence geometry (peak PC1–valence r = 0.76 and 0.83, near Claude's r = 0.81) while uncovering that the two models reach this geometry via strikingly different depth profiles, and that arousal extraction is strongly corpus-dependent (Gemma-generated stories give r up to 0.45 vs. Apertus-generated r ≤ 0.21).

核心贡献

  • 跨架构复现情感向量：在两个完全开源的模型上恢复了对人类效价的线性表示（PC1–valence 峰值 r=0.76 / 0.83），证明 Anthropic 在 Claude 上报告的"效价几何"不是 Claude 专属，而是语言模型内部表示的普遍属性。
  • 揭示效价产生的"殊途同归"现象：Gemma-4-E4B 在早期层（约第 16 层）就强编码效价，随后坍缩；而 Apertus-8B 在中后段（约第 20 层起）才突现并稳定上升。CKA 分析显示 Apertus 存在一个三阶段的相变，Gemma 则是平滑过渡。
  • 首次分离"模型内在几何"与"语料提取伪影"：通过让两个模型分别处理对方生成的语料，本文首次系统测试了故事语料对提取结果的影响，发现唤醒度对语料高度敏感（Gemma 语料在两个模型上都显著更好），排除了"模型只对自己语料编码得好"的简单混淆。
  • 分离"表示空间稳定性"与"方向轴稳定性"：发现即使 CKA 显示表示空间在各层间相似，对应的效价方向轴却可能不稳定（甚至接近噪声），说明表示相似 ≠ 共享同一可解释方向。
  • 完全开放：开源了实验代码与数据集（github.com/sinievanderben/emotion_experiment），促进跨架构情感表示的可复现研究。

Key Contributions

  • Cross-architecture replication of emotion vectors. Recovers a valence axis of comparable strength in two architecturally distinct open-weight models (Apertus-8B, Gemma-4-E4B), with peak PC1–valence correlations of r = 0.76 and 0.83, approaching/exceeding the r = 0.81 reported for Claude Sonnet 4.5. This demonstrates emotion vectors generalize beyond Claude.
  • Divergent emergence of valence. Identifies that the two models reach similar peak correlations via opposite depth trajectories: Gemma-4-E4B encodes valence early (peaks ~layer 16) then collapses, while Apertus-8B builds it progressively (significant at layer 18, exceeding r = 0.60 by layer 21). Cross-layer CKA reveals a phase transition in Apertus absent in Gemma.
  • First systematic corpus-control for emotion-vector extraction. Runs each model on both Apertus- and Gemma-generated story corpora, separating model-intrinsic geometry from corpus-dependent extraction artifacts — a control no prior work had applied.
  • Decoupling representational-space stability from valence-axis stability. Shows that CKA-similar spaces can host unstable valence directions (sometimes near noise), so representational similarity between layers does not guarantee a shared interpretable axis.
  • Fully open artifacts. Releases code and datasets for reproducible cross-architecture investigation of emotion representations.

Methodology

The work directly extends Sofroniew et al. (2026, Anthropic's Transformer Circuits Thread), who identified 171 emotion vectors in Claude Sonnet 4.5 aligned with Russell's circumplex model (valence/arousal axes).

Models. Two open-weight, instruction-tuned transformers of comparable scale: APERTUS-8B-Instruct-2509 (32 layers) and GEMMA-4-E4B-it (42 layers), accessed via HuggingFace.

Datasets (independent variable). Two synthetic emotion-story corpora, each with 9 stories for each of 171 emotions (1,539 stories total) plus 40 neutral stories — one corpus generated by Apertus, one by Gemma — using prompts mirroring Anthropic's methodology (emotions conveyed implicitly, never named). The two corpora constitute a controlled independent variable to disentangle model-intrinsic geometry from extraction artifacts.

Contrast-vector extraction. For each emotion and layer: (1) forward pass on the 9 stories, cache residual-stream (resid_post) activations → average across tokens/stories to get a raw emotion vector u_e; (2) characterize the emotion-agnostic subspace via PCA on the 40 neutral stories' mean activations, keeping top-K PCs explaining 50% of variance; (3) subtract the neutral projection to get the emotion contrast vector v_e = u_e − Σ(u_e·p_k)p_k. This yields a per-layer matrix V(l) ∈ R^{|E|×d_model}. Activations extracted from layers 1–31 (Apertus) and 1–40 (Gemma).

Analyses.

  1. PCA on V(l) per layer; Pearson correlation of PC1/PC2 with human valence/arousal ratings from the NRC Valence–Arousal–Dominance Lexicon (Mohammad, 2018).
  2. Linear Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) across all layer pairs to track representational geometry evolution (CKA invariant to orientation, suited for this comparison).
  3. Cosine similarity of the layer-wise valence direction (the direction most correlated with valence) to test whether the same subspace encodes valence at different depths.

Results & Highlights

  • Valence replicates and approaches Claude. All four model×corpus conditions reach peak PC1–valence correlations between r = 0.72 and 0.83 (all p < 10⁻³), matching/exceeding the Claude reference of r = 0.81. Gemma-4-E4B reaches r = 0.83 (layer 16, Gemma stories); Apertus-8B reaches r = 0.76 (layer 31, Gemma stories).
  • Opposite depth profiles. Apertus-8B: valence correlation near zero until ~50% depth, then rises sharply (abrupt late emergence). Gemma-4-E4B: peaks early (~38% depth) then collapses toward zero with only partial recovery (r ≈ 0.18–0.20) in final layers.
  • CKA phase structure. Apertus-8B shows three CKA phases (early plateau CKA ≈ 1, transition band layers 12–21 with off-diagonal decay, late plateau) aligned with the valence rise — a representational reorganization. Gemma-4-E4B shows a smooth gradient across all 40 layers with CKA ≥ 0.73 between any layer pair, so its valence collapse cannot stem from a global reorganization.
  • Stable space, unstable axis. Despite a stable representational space, the recovered valence axis is often unstable across layers (some off-diagonal cosines near noise), confirming representational similarity ≠ a shared valence direction.
  • Arousal is corpus-dependent. PC2–arousal correlations are weak and highly corpus-sensitive: Apertus stories yield r ≤ 0.21 for both models, while Gemma stories yield r up to 0.45 (Apertus, layer 26) and 0.41 (Gemma, layer 31), partially closing the gap with Claude's r = 0.66. Because Gemma stories improve arousal extraction in both models, the effect reflects corpus properties rather than model–story matching.
  • PCA cluster separation. At peak layers, positive and negative emotions form denser, more clearly separated clusters with Gemma stories than Apertus stories.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: Moderate. The core extraction pipeline (contrastive activation subtraction + PCA + circumplex correlation) is directly adopted from Sofroniew et al. (2026). Genuine novelty lies in (a) the systematic corpus-as-independent-variable design (genuinely new — no prior work tested story-source influence), (b) the cross-layer CKA and valence-axis-stability decomposition, and (c) documenting how valence emerges differently across architectures. Conceptually, "same geometry, different paths" is an interesting, non-obvious finding.
  • Rigor: Good within its scope. Per-layer Pearson r values with significance levels, four-condition 2×2 design (model × corpus) enabling a clean separation of factors, and multiple complementary analyses (PCA correlation, CKA, cosine matrices). The corpus-control logic is sound and the inference that "Gemma stories improve arousal in both models ⇒ corpus property, not confound" is correctly drawn.
  • Reproducibility: Strong. Both models are open-weight; activation configs (hook location, batch size 8, max 1024 tokens, Pile uncopyrighted) are tabulated; per-layer r values are reported exhaustively in appendices (Tables 3–4); code and dataset are released. The main reproducibility caveat is that the original Anthropic study released no code, so this implementation is reconstructed — the authors flag this honestly.
  • Significance: Moderate-to-high for the interpretability community. It establishes that emotion geometry is not Claude-specific and surfaces actionable methodological guidance (corpus choice matters for arousal; layer choice matters; representational stability ≠ axis stability). The absence of causal validation keeps the significance below the original Claude study.
  • Red flags / concerns.
  • No causal validation. The original Claude work showed steering emotion vectors causally alters behavior (reward hacking, blackmail); this paper recovers correlational structure only and explicitly defers causal steering to future work. The vectors could be epiphenomenal.
  • Narrow model coverage (n=2). Claims about "different paths to the same geometry" rest on two models from two families that differ in architecture, training data, and post-training simultaneously, so the divergent-trajectory finding cannot be attributed to any single factor.
  • Self-generated-corpus circularity. Stimuli are model-generated, so corpus and model properties are entangled; a fully model-independent stimulus set would be a stronger control (acknowledged).
  • Arousal numbers remain well below Claude's r = 0.66 even in the best condition, so the "arousal replication" is partial at best.
  • The valence-axis cosine analysis sometimes finds no off-diagonal cell above |0.49| (e.g., Apertus on its own stories), which the authors interpret as "near noise" — a candid but slightly concerning sign that the recovered directions may be unstable in some conditions.

Strengths

  • Clean, well-motivated 2×2 experimental design that isolates a previously conflated variable (story corpus).
  • Multiple complementary geometric analyses (PCA correlation, CKA, directional cosine matrices) that triangulate the "stable space vs. unstable axis" insight.
  • Honest, well-calibrated limitations section; does not overclaim causality.
  • Fully open code, dataset, and exhaustive per-layer numerical tables — unusually reproducible for a workshop paper.
  • Practically useful methodological lessons for the interpretability community (which layers to probe, which corpus to use).

Limitations

  • No causal/steering experiments — the central causal claim of the original Claude work is not replicated; recovered vectors are only validated correlationally.
  • Only two models, from two families, differing in architecture/training/post-training jointly, so trajectory-difference attributions are underdetermined.
  • Model-generated corpora cannot fully separate stimulus-distribution properties from the probed model's own properties.
  • Partial arousal replication — best-case r ≈ 0.45 vs. Claude's 0.66; arousal axis remains the weakest part of the circumplex story here.
  • Valence-direction instability in several conditions suggests some recovered axes may be noise, slightly weakening layer-wise directional claims.
  • Original baseline (Claude) measured at a single mid-late layer with unreleased code, so numerical comparisons carry reconstruction uncertainty.

Relevance

高度相关。本文直接围绕表示几何（representation geometry）、探针（probing/contrastive activation extraction）与情感向量（emotion vectors）展开——用 PCA + CKA 刻画情感在残差流中的线性结构与跨层演化，正是表示几何与探测方法的典型应用，可为情感向量的普遍性与"同一几何、不同计算路径"现象提供一手证据。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Causal steering test: Does steering Apertus-8B / Gemma-4-E4B along the peak-layer valence direction causally shift model behavior (e.g., emotion-laden outputs, sycophancy, refusal), and does the effect transfer across layers within the CKA-stable plateau?
  • What makes Gemma-generated stories more arousal-discriminative? A corpus-content analysis (lexical/physiological cues, narrative intensity) could pin down the mechanism behind the r ≤ 0.21 → r ≥ 0.43 jump.
  • Why does Gemma's valence signal collapse in later layers despite a stable representational space (high CKA)? Is the information rotated into a non-PC1 component, distributed across features, or actively suppressed by later layers?
  • Does the "different paths, same geometry" pattern hold across more architectures (e.g., Qwen, Llama, Mistral), and can it be attributed to architecture vs. training data vs. post-training via controlled comparisons?
  • Can sparse autoencoders track the same interpretable emotion features across depth, or do different layers encode emotion through different feature combinations?
  • Does a model-independent stimulus set (human-written or curated emotional text) change the recovered geometry and corpus-dependent arousal gap?
