Just how sure are you? Improving Verbalized Uncertainty Calibration in Medical VQA

arXiv: 2606.27023

Date: 2026-06-25

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27023

一句话总结： 提出一种面向多模态医疗 VQA 的训练式校准框架，通过"2×2 因子扰动设计 + 复合 Brier/anchor/对比对齐/KL 正则损失"对 MLLM 做 LoRA 微调，在三个不同难度基准上同时降低校准误差（ECE/Brier）并提升区分度（AUROC），其中对比对齐项驱动区分度、top-k KL 项防止遗忘与格式漂移。
TL;DR: A training-based framework fine-tunes medical MLLMs (MedGemma-4B, Qwen2-VL-7B) via LoRA to verbalize well-calibrated confidence, using a 2×2 factorial image/text perturbation design to derive a contrastive alignment signal and a composite loss (Brier + anchor + alignment + top-k KL). It wins on average ECE/Brier/AUROC across three Medical VQA benchmarks spanning high/mid/low accuracy regimes while preserving accuracy — notably improving discrimination where baselines collapse to confidence compression.

核心贡献

  • 多模态校准视角的首创：首个将训练式 verbalized confidence 校准从纯文本 LLM 扩展到医疗多模态 VQA 的工作，指出此前方法（ConfTuner、Rewarding Doubt 等）均只针对文本模态且只在单一输入条件下训练。
  • 2×2 因子扰动设计：将"图像可用性（原图 vs 黑图）"与"文本完整性（原选项 vs 扰动选项）"交叉，得到四种条件，直接探测模型对视觉证据 vs 语言先验的依赖，作为校准信号来源；相比高斯噪声，黑图条件不会破坏医学诊断特征。
  • 复合校准损失：Brier 项（驱动置信度逼近经验正确率）+ anchor 项（防止置信度塌缩到 0/1 极端值）+ 对比对齐项（使跨条件的置信度变化跟踪准确率变化）+ top-k KL 项（冻结参考模型，保护答题分布与置信度格式合规）。
  • 跨难度泛化证据：在 OmniMedVQA（59–70% 高准确率）、PMC-VQA（45–49% 中准确率）、MedXpertQA（20–24% 低准确率）三种 regime 上均取得平均最优 ECE/Brier/AUROC，且 OOD 准确率漂移在 ±0.8 个百分点以内。
  • 组件必要性消融：去掉对齐项 AUROC 大幅下降（OmniMedVQA 降 20.5%），去掉 KL 项导致 OOD 准确率崩塌（PMC-VQA −15.0、MedXpertQA −11.3）及约 22% 输出格式漂移，证明每一项都不可或缺。

Key Contributions

  • First training-based calibration for multimodal medical VQA: Extends verbalized confidence calibration beyond text-only LLMs; explicitly addresses that prior fine-tuning methods (ConfTuner, ReCalibrate, Rewarding Doubt) ignore the visual modality and train under a single input condition.
  • 2×2 factorial perturbation design: Crosses image availability (original vs. black image) with text integrity (original vs. perturbed/shuffled distractors) to probe reliance on visual evidence versus language priors; black-image is preferred over noise perturbations that corrupt diagnostic features.
  • Composite calibration loss: Brier loss with fractional targets drives confidence to empirical correctness; an anchor regularizer prevents collapse to extreme values; a contrastive alignment term ties cross-condition confidence differences to accuracy differences; a top-k KL regularizer (against a frozen base model, reference distributions cached offline) preserves answering ability and confidence-format compliance.
  • Consistent gains across difficulty regimes: Best average ECE/Brier/AUROC on both MedGemma-4B-IT and Qwen2-VL-7B-Instruct across three benchmarks; OOD accuracy preserved within ±0.8 points; Brier Score leads on every benchmark/architecture (26–43% reductions on MedGemma vs. next best).
  • Component ablations demonstrate distinct roles (alignment → discrimination; KL → anti-forgetting + format preservation), and a PMC-VQA training pathway confirms cross-dataset transferability.

Methodology

The task is multiple-choice Medical VQA: given image x, question q, and answer options O, the model generates a reasoning trace r, answer a∈O, and a verbalized confidence token c on a discrete integer scale normalized to [0,1]. They adopt an answer-first, confidence-second generation order (shown to improve calibration). Quality is assessed on two axes — calibration (ECE, Brier) and discrimination (AUROC).

Training targets are fractional accuracies: for each sample under each perturbation condition, they generate G=10 responses at T=1.0 and use the fraction correct â as the Brier target (theoretically optimal confidence is c* = p, the true correctness probability).

Composite loss L = λ·L_Brier + (1−λ)·L_anchor + α·L_align + β·L_KL:

  • Brier loss (tokenized, over the softmax distribution of confidence tokens): Σ_v pθ(v)·(â − v/N_A)².
  • Anchor loss: penalizes deviation from c_mid=0.5, splitting the scale into "believes incorrect" (0.0–0.4) vs "believes correct" (0.6–1.0) regions; counters the practical collapse to extremes that the Brier optimum alone does not prevent.
  • Alignment loss: over four same-axis condition pairs (V,VC), (V-TP,VC-TP), (V,V-TP), (VC,VC-TP), enforces (ĉ_j − ĉ_k) ≈ (â_j − â_k) — confidence change when evidence is removed must track accuracy change.
  • Top-k KL regularizer: on answer token positions against a frozen base model; restricted to top-k tokens to preserve candidate ranking while allowing confidence learning elsewhere. Reference distributions are precomputed/cached during data generation (which already needs a base-model forward pass), so it adds no extra model copy or forward pass at training time.

Fine-tuning: LoRA adapters on query/value projections only — 3.2M params (0.075%) for MedGemma-4B-IT, 2.5M (0.030%) for Qwen2-VL-7B-Instruct. 3 epochs; epoch 2 selected for MedGemma, epoch 3 for Qwen; trained on a single NVIDIA RTX Pro 6000 (Blackwell), ~1 hour/epoch. Hyperparameters chosen via grid search (λ grid 0.1, α 0.5, β 1.0) selecting top-accuracy-quartile configs by highest validation AUROC.

Baselines: base model (zero-shot), Top-K Sampling, SteerConf (sampling/prompting-based), ConfTuner (training-based, tokenized Brier).

Results & Highlights

  • Average metrics (OmniMedVQA-trained, MedGemma): ECE 0.155 vs next-best 0.280 (SteerConf); Brier 0.204 vs 0.327; AUROC 0.693 vs 0.603; accuracy 0.501 preserved/near base (0.466).
  • Discrimination is the headline win: on OmniMedVQA, AUROC reaches 0.926 (MedGemma)/0.884 (Qwen) vs SteerConf 0.693/0.705 — baselines compress confidence toward the dataset mean (low ECE but no separation of correct/incorrect).
  • Brier leads on every benchmark/architecture; reductions of 26–43% vs next-best on MedGemma, 3–18% on Qwen.
  • Robustness across accuracy regimes: method yields the narrowest ECE spread (0.097–0.184 MedGemma; 0.058–0.314 Qwen), whereas baseline ECE degrades sharply as accuracy drops (e.g., Qwen ConfTuner: 0.068 → 0.229 → 0.360 across the three benchmarks).
  • Cross-dataset transfer: PMC-VQA-trained MedGemma still beats all baselines on average (ECE 0.123, Brier 0.226, AUROC 0.624), confirming the objective is not source-specific.
  • Accuracy preservation: OOD accuracy changes within ±0.8 points; ID accuracy actually +10.0 (MedGemma)/+7.3 (Qwen) on OmniMedVQA (an operating-point effect, not an explicit training objective).
  • Ablations: removing alignment → AUROC −20.5% (OmniMedVQA); removing KL → OOD accuracy −15.0 (PMC)/−11.3 (MedXpertQA) and ~22% format drift; full model maintains 100% format compliance.
  • Statistical significance (paired bootstrap, B=10k, p<0.05) claimed for key Brier/AUROC wins.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty — Moderate-High: The core insight (using a 2×2 image/text perturbation to derive a contrastive alignment signal for multimodal confidence) is genuinely new and well-motivated; the individual loss ingredients (tokenized Brier from ConfTuner, KL anchoring) are adaptations rather than inventions, but the combination and the multimodal framing are original.
  • Rigor — High: Multi-architecture, multi-benchmark evaluation across three accuracy regimes; proper calibration metrics (ECE/Brier/AUROC) separated from accuracy; paired-bootstrap significance tests; component ablations isolate each loss term's role; cross-dataset transfer tested. Hyperparameter selection methodology (AUROC on top-accuracy-quartile validation) is sensible and accuracy-agnostic.
  • Reproducibility — High: Public code, modest LoRA footprint, single-GPU training (~3 hours/run), grid-search ranges and selected values all reported, exact epoch-selection rule stated.
  • Significance — Moderate-High: Verbalized confidence is the most deployment-friendly uncertainty signal (no internal access, human-interpretable), so making it reliable for medical VQA is practically important. However, gains are concentrated on multiple-choice tasks and the headline "60% calibration error reduction" abstract claim is not uniformly reflected per-benchmark (the method does not always get the lowest ECE or highest AUROC on individual benchmarks).
  • Red flags:
  • Headline reductions ("60% or more") are best-case/aggregate framings; on the hardest benchmark (MedXpertQA), AUROC is ~chance (0.48–0.51) for all methods including theirs, so calibrated confidence is effectively non-informative in the most clinically challenging setting — a significant practical caveat.
  • ID (in-distribution) accuracy increases by +10/+7 points, attributed to "operating point" — this is somewhat hand-waved and could indicate the model is being shifted in a way that entangles accuracy and calibration.
  • The anchor loss's role is partially a patch for Brier's real-world failure to reach its theoretical optimum; the authors themselves note a single principled loss would be preferable — an honest admission of somewhat ad-hoc engineering.
  • Evaluated only on small models (4B/7B) and only multiple-choice; generalization to open-ended clinical generation (where partial correctness exists) is unaddressed.
  • No comparison against some internal-representation / hidden-state confidence methods (admittedly excluded for requiring white-box access) and no human-in-the-loop clinical validation.

Strengths

  • Tackles a genuinely under-served problem (multimodal medical verbalized calibration) with a clean, well-justified perturbation design.
  • The alignment term provides an interpretable mechanism (evidence utilization → confidence) rather than a black-box fit.
  • Strong and consistent gains in Brier Score (joint calibration+discrimination) across all settings.
  • Honest about limitations (chance-level MedXpertQA discrimination, small models, multiple-choice only) and provides thoughtful future-work directions.
  • Lightweight (LoRA, ~0.075% params, single GPU) and fully open-sourced.

Limitations

  • Discrimination fails on hard cases: AUROC ~chance on MedXpertQA for every method, undermining utility for the most diagnostic, multi-step-reasoning questions.
  • Scale: only 4B/7B models; behavior at larger scales unknown.
  • Task scope: multiple-choice only; open-ended VQA with partial correctness — the realistic clinical setting — is explicitly out of scope and is fundamentally harder.
  • Per-benchmark inconsistency: does not always achieve best ECE (e.g., OmniMedVQA) or best AUROC (MedXpertQA); the "average-best" framing, while fair, smooths over these gaps.
  • No real clinical deployment validation; calibration may not transfer across patient populations/imaging modalities absent from the benchmarks.
  • The composite loss is partly engineered to compensate for Brier's practical shortcomings (anchor), and the authors acknowledge a more principled single-term formulation is desirable.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Does the alignment signal derived from the 2×2 perturbation design transfer to open-ended (free-form) Medical VQA where correctness is graded rather than binary?
  • How do the gains behave at larger scales (e.g., 70B+ MLLMs), and does the anchor loss remain necessary or become redundant as model capacity grows?
  • Can the contrastive alignment term be extended to richer perturbation axes (e.g., per-region image masking, modality-specific corruptions) to yield finer-grained evidence-attributed confidence?
  • The top-k KL regularizer preserves the answer distribution against a frozen base model — what is the trade-off between answer-preservation strength (β) and achievable calibration headroom, and is there an optimal operating point?
  • Since discrimination collapses to chance on MedXpertQA (~20% base accuracy), is there a fundamental lower bound on base accuracy below which verbalized confidence is uninformative, and can auxiliary signals (e.g., semantic entropy, hidden states) rescue it in that regime?
  • Could the method be combined with human-in-the-loop triage evaluation to quantify real clinical utility (error-catching rate vs. recommendation volume) rather than aggregate metrics?
