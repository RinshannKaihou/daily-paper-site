Discovering Millions of Interpretable Features with Sparse Autoencoders

arXiv: 2606.26620

Date: 2026-06-25

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26620

一句话总结： 本文发布了 Qwen3-Instruct SAE —— 一套基于 Qwen3 指令微调模型族（1.7B/4B/8B）训练的 JumpReLU 稀疏自编码器，覆盖残差流、MLP 与注意力三种激活位置，并通过残差流/MLP 与注意力之间重建质量差异、以及一个因果性"拒答引导"案例研究，证明所选 SAE 特征可将 Qwen3-4B 在危险提示上的拒答率推高至约 0.93–0.95。
TL;DR: The authors release Qwen3-Instruct SAE, a suite of JumpReLU sparse autoencoders trained on the instruction-tuned Qwen3 family (1.7B/4B/8B) across residual-stream, MLP, and attention activation sites, evaluate sparsity-fidelity trade-offs via Delta-LM-Loss and FVE, and demonstrate causal refusal-steering where a single hand-picked SAE feature lifts Qwen3-4B's unsafe-prompt refusal rate to ~0.93–0.95.

核心贡献

  • 发布 Qwen3-Instruct SAE 套件：针对 Qwen3 指令微调模型族（1.7B、4B、8B）训练的 SAE，弥补了开源 SAE 多针对基座模型（Gemma Scope、LlamaScope、Qwen-Scope）而缺乏指令微调变体覆盖的空白。
  • 全层 + 三位置覆盖：1.7B 与 4B 在残差流、MLP 输出、注意力输出三个位置逐层训练 SAE；8B 提供 0–8 层残差流子集，便于研究指令微调模型内部表示。
  • 系统化的稀疏度-保真度权衡评测：使用 Delta LM loss（DLL）与 Fraction of Variance Explained（FVE）刻画跨层、跨组件的重建质量，发现残差流/MLP SAE 的恢复能力优于注意力 SAE，且呈非单调的"早期好—中间差—深层回升"层间模式。
  • 拒答引导案例研究：依据 Arditi et al. 的差异均值方向定位拒绝方向，并用单一 SAE 解码器方向沿残差流做前向钩子干预，证明所选特征能因果地驱动指令微调 Qwen3 拒绝危险请求。
  • 更强的可复现性定位：相对 Qwen-Scope，使用开放数据 FineWeb-Edu 训练（而非 Qwen 私有预训练数据），并额外覆盖注意力与 MLP 输出位置。

Key Contributions

  • Qwen3-Instruct SAE release: A comprehensive SAE suite trained on the instruction-tuned Qwen3 family (1.7B/4B/8B), filling a gap left by prior releases (Gemma Scope, LlamaScope, Qwen-Scope) that target base rather than instruction-tuned models.
  • Full-layer, three-site coverage: Layer-wise SAEs at residual stream, MLP output, and attention output for Qwen3-1.7B and -4B, plus a subset of residual-stream layers (0–8) for Qwen3-8B.
  • Systematic sparsity–fidelity evaluation: Uses Delta LM Loss (DLL) and Fraction of Variance Explained (FVE) to characterize reconstruction across layers and components, finding residual/MLP SAEs recover performance better than attention SAEs and exhibiting a non-monotonic "good-early / weak-mid / recovering-deep" layer pattern.
  • Causal refusal-steering case study: Locates the refusal direction via Arditi et al.'s difference-in-means method, then intervenes by adding a single SAE decoder direction, showing the selected feature causally induces refusal on Qwen3-instruct models.
  • Reproducibility positioning: Trains on open FineWeb-Edu data (vs. Qwen-Scope's proprietary pretraining data) and adds attention/MLP sites beyond Qwen-Scope's residual-stream-only coverage.

Methodology

  • Model & activation sites: SAEs trained on activations of Qwen3-1.7B/4B/8B (instruction-tuned). For 1.7B and 4B, every layer is covered at three post-sites (post-residual, post-MLP, post-attention W_O). For 8B, only residual-stream layers 0–8.
  • SAE architecture: JumpReLU SAEs (following Gemma Scope), with a learnable per-latent threshold θ optimized via a straight-through estimator (STE) with KDE bandwidth ε=0.001. Loss = MSE reconstruction + L0 sparsity penalty (λ=1.0), sparsity warmed up over 2,000 steps.
  • Training: FineWeb-Edu (Sample-10BT), 90/10 train/eval split, 1B eval tokens, context length 1024. lr=2e-4 with 1,000-step linear warmup, batch 20,480 tokens, Adam (β1=0.0, β2=0.999, ε=1e-8), 14× NVIDIA H20-3e GPUs. Dictionary sizes 16K/65K, target L0 = 80/160.
  • Evaluation metrics: (1) DLL — KL/downstream LM loss change when original activations are replaced by SAE reconstructions; (2) FVE — fraction of activation variance explained vs. predicting the dataset mean.
  • Refusal-steering protocol: Identify the refusal direction r via difference-in-means between harmful/harmless activations (selected with bypass/induce/KL constraints, restricted to layers l<0.8L); pick a single SAE feature that fires on ≥5 tokens of a refusal example; intervene via forward hook adding α·w_j to the residual stream at layer l* (α≈0.3–0.5); greedy decoding.

Results & Highlights

  • Reconstruction: Residual-stream and MLP SAEs recover model performance (DLL) markedly better than attention SAEs; attributed to attention being more contextually entangled. A non-monotonic layer pattern appears (layers 0–1 easy, 2–4 hardest, deep layers recovering), consistent across residual/MLP.
  • FVE vs DLL decoupling: Increasing target L0 has limited effect on FVE but steadily improves DLL — a useful nuance for SAE selection.
  • 65K less stable than 16K for attention: Larger dictionaries induce feature splitting/absorption, making attention-layer recovery less stable.
  • Refusal steering (Qwen3-4B-it): target feature refusal rates 0.95 (XSTest unsafe), 0.93 (Mix unsafe), vs. No-Steering 0.685/0.760 and Random-Feature ~0. WildGuard transfer is weaker (0.549).
  • Qwen3-1.7B-it: weaker steering overall (unsafe 0.40–0.77), consistent with the pattern that larger models respond more strongly to steering.
  • Steering coefficient α: inverted-U curve peaking at α≈0.3–0.5 and collapsing to ~0 at large α (over-perturbation).

Authors & Credibility

  • Authors are from AI DATA, Alibaba Group and Beijing Institute of Technology; the work fits the established line of large-scale open SAE releases (Gemma Scope, LlamaScope, Qwen-Scope), so the methodology is credible and follows a well-trodden template.
  • Hardware and dataset specifics (14× H20-3e GPUs, FineWeb-Edu, exact hyperparameters, layer/feature IDs) are reported in detail, supporting reproduction of the trained artifacts.
  • The paper is positioned as an infrastructure/resource release rather than a conceptual breakthrough, which matches the content.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty — Low to Moderate. This is essentially "Gemma Scope, but for instruction-tuned Qwen3." The technical method (JumpReLU SAEs) is taken wholesale from prior work (Rajamanoharan/Lieberum et al.); the contribution is scale + coverage + an open dataset. The "Discovering Millions of Interpretable Features" title overstates the work — no novel feature-discovery mechanism is proposed, and no individual feature's interpretability is actually demonstrated.
  • Rigor — Mixed. Reconstruction and recovery methodology is sound and standard. However, the steering case study uses a single hand-picked feature per model and a weak "random feature" baseline; there is no comparison to other steering methods (plain activation addition of the refusal direction, other SAE features, or steering baselines from Wu et al. AxBench, which the paper itself cites as showing "even simple baselines outperform SAEs"). The refusal metric is a brittle string-matching indicator list rather than a model-based judge.
  • Reproducibility — At Risk. The paper states "We will release the code and model weights in the coming months" — i.e., weights/code are NOT yet available at submission. This is a significant gap for a paper whose entire value proposition is a public release. Open-source FineWeb-Edu helps, but the deliverable is not yet delivered. Qwen3-8B coverage is only partial (layers 0–8) and lacks evaluation.
  • Significance — Moderate. As an open resource targeting instruction-tuned (deployment-realistic) models with three-site coverage, it has practical community value. But it overlaps heavily with Qwen-Scope (Deng et al., 2026, arXiv:2605.11887), which is concurrent, covers Qwen3 dense + MoE + Qwen3.5, and is arguably broader; the differentiators (instruct vs. base, FineWeb-Edu vs. proprietary, attention/MLP sites) are real but incremental.
  • Red flags / overclaiming.
  1. Title claims "interpretable features" yet provides no interpretability evidence (no automated labeling, no human-rated monosemanticity, no activation examples beyond a single heat-map). "Interpretable" is asserted by inheritance from the SAE literature, not shown.
  2. "Millions of features" is a counting artifact (layers × sites × dictionary), not a demonstrated discovery result.
  3. No ablations on dictionary size beyond 16K/65K (much smaller than Gemma Scope's 256K or LlamaScope's 128K), so scaling behavior is under-explored.
  4. Cherry-picked steering results: best single α and best dataset reported prominently; the WildGuard underperformance is acknowledged only briefly.
  5. The 65K-attention instability finding is interesting but explained speculatively without targeted experiments.

Strengths

  • Fills a genuine gap: instruction-tuned models are what people deploy, and most prior SAE suites target base models.
  • Three-site layer-wise coverage (residual/MLP/attention) for 1.7B and 4B is more complete than Qwen-Scope (residual only).
  • Training on open FineWeb-Edu improves reproducibility versus proprietary-data releases.
  • The DLL/FVE decoupling and the layer-wise non-monotonicity findings are practically useful for practitioners choosing SAEs.
  • Detailed appendices (Qwen3-4B eval, refusal indicators, feature-selection math, α-sweep) add depth.

Limitations

  • Not yet released: code and weights promised "in the coming months"; the central artifact is unavailable at submission.
  • Interpretability is asserted, not evaluated — no automated/human feature-labeling, so the "interpretable" claim is unsupported by this paper.
  • Scope of behavioral study: only refusal steering; no evidence the method generalizes to other capabilities or safety behaviors.
  • Partial 8B coverage and no 8B evaluation — the headline 8B inclusion is essentially incomplete.
  • Narrow dictionary-size/L0 grid (16K/65K; L0 80/160) — no scaling-law-style sweep, and sizes are smaller than competing suites.
  • Weak steering baselines (random feature only) and brittle string-match refusal metric; no comparison to non-SAE steering.
  • Heavy overlap with concurrent Qwen-Scope, which limits marginal novelty.

Relevance

与用户对可解释性、隐藏状态与稀疏自编码器（SAEs）的兴趣高度相关：本文正是以 SAE 解构指令微调 Qwen3 的隐藏状态，量化残差流/MLP/注意力各位置的稀疏-保真权衡，并通过隐藏状态方向的因果干预（refusal steering）演示特征级机制，是 SAE 与可解释性研究的直接基础设施与实证工作。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Once weights are released, how do Qwen3-Instruct SAE features score on automated interpretability benchmarks (SAEBench / SynthSAEBench) versus Qwen-Scope on the same model layers?
  • Does the three-site coverage (esp. attention/MLP SAEs) actually enable circuit discovery that residual-only suites cannot, or do attention/MLP SAEs remain too noisy to use downstream?
  • Why is WildGuard refusal transfer notably weaker than XSTest/Mix — is the single refusal feature overfit to one refusal phrasing, and would multi-feature or attention-layer steering transfer better?
  • How do these SAEs behave on non-FineWeb-Edu distributions (code, multilingual, reasoning traces), given training data was purely educational text?
  • Against AxBench-style steering baselines (which the paper cites as beating SAEs), do the hand-picked refusal features here remain competitive, or does the comparison undermine the steering claim?
  • Does the non-monotonic layer DLL pattern (good→bad→recovering) align with the "concept depth" / layer-role hypotheses, and can it be reproduced on Qwen3-8B to test scale dependence?
