> **一句话总结：** 本书将哲学中的"激进解释"传统（David Lewis、Donald Davidson）与机制可解释性工具（探针、稀疏自编码器、引导向量）结合，提出一套判断何时可以合理地为 LLM 归赋信念与欲望的标准——信念需满足真、融贯、使用与（表征主义下的）一致性，欲望需满足融贯、使用、可理解性与系统性，并强调信念、欲望与它们预设的命题结构是整体性、联合约束的，不能逐一孤立归赋。

> **TL;DR:** A book-length monograph that imports the philosophical tradition of *radical interpretation* (Lewis, Davidson) into mechanistic interpretability of LLMs. It supplies explicit criteria for when we can legitimately attribute beliefs and desires to a model (truth/coherence/use/uniformity for belief; coherence/use/intelligibility/systematicity for desire), ties each criterion to concrete interpretability tests, and argues that beliefs, desires, and the propositional algebra they presuppose must be solved for jointly — a holism that becomes pressing precisely for AI systems that may not share the interpreter's concepts.

## 核心贡献

- **为机制可解释性提供哲学理论基础**：把 David Lewis 的"给定 P（系统物理/计算事实），求解其余"的激进解释框架直接映射到 LLM（将"Karl"替换为"Claude"），解释了为什么"拥有权重却仍不理解模型在想什么"这一困境，并指出激进解释的实操化在 AI 安全场景下是必须而非可选的。
- **给出信念归赋的明确标准**：提出 truth（真）、coherence（融贯，遵循概率公理与语义不变性）、use（在生成输出中被因果性地使用）、以及表征主义特有的 uniformity（一致性）四条标准，并指出早期纯靠真值探针的研究为何"脆弱"——正是缺了多标准联合检验。
- **给出欲望归赋的对称框架**：针对欲望"心灵朝向世界"的方向适配性，用 intelligibility（可理解性，能产生强预测）替代 truth，加上 coherence（手段-目的连贯性、Jeffrey-Bolker 决策论融贯）、use、systematicity，将手段-目的推理（如"想喝咖啡就不能死掉"）变为可测量的欲望证据。
- **命题投影问题（propositional projection problem）**：识别出全书最难的一环——可解释性方法本身预设了一个命题代数，但该代数恰恰是需要被解释确定的，三者（信念/欲望/命题结构）互相约束，构成整体性归赋。
- **表征主义 vs. 解释主义的双轨判断**：用 Jeffrey-Bolker 表示定理形式化行为如何分解为信念+欲望，并把 Lewis（用全部物理事实，留出表征主义空间）与 Davidson（只用行为事实，预表解释主义）的分歧转化为对 LLM 的两种互补检验路径。
- **对当前实证证据的诚实盘点**：结论给出"混合结局"——信念有部分表征支撑（真值方向、事实召回特征、知识探针工作较多），欲望可被解释但尚无强表征证据；智能体化（agentic turn）反而强化了欲望归赋的解释主义证据。

## Key Contributions

- Bridges two literatures that have developed in parallel: the philosophy of radical interpretation (Lewis 1974, Davidson) and the engineering practice of mechanistic interpretability (probes, sparse autoencoders, steering vectors), giving the latter an explicit theory of success.
- Provides operational, dual-track criteria for belief attribution: **truth, coherence, use**, plus a representationalist-only **uniformity** criterion — each mapped to a concrete test (probe accuracy, logical/semantic consistency checks, ablation/patching for "use," uniformity across paraphrases/representations).
- Provides symmetric criteria for desire attribution, substituting **intelligibility** for truth (since there is no ground truth for what to want) alongside **coherence** (means-ends and Jeffrey-Bolker decision-theoretic), **use**, and **systematicity** — making agentic means-ends behavior a primary source of desire evidence.
- Formalizes interpretation via the **Jeffrey-Bolker representation theorem**: an agential description A = ⟨P, D, 𝔄⟩ (beliefs, desires, Boolean algebra of propositions) is correct iff behavior is rationalizable as expected-desire maximization, with belief and desire defined over the *same* proposition algebra.
- Identifies and names the **propositional projection problem**: our interpretability tools presuppose a propositional structure that is itself part of what must be inferred, forcing joint (holistic) attribution rather than piecemeal fixes.
- Frames the central tension of the book — representationalism (interpret from internal states) vs. interpretationism (interpret from behavior, via decision theory) — and shows how mechanistic interpretability is uniquely positioned to serve both.
- Delivers an honest empirical bottom line: a "mixed outcome" — belief-like representations have partial backing; desire is interpretable but not yet representational; the agentic turn has strengthened interpretationist evidence for desire.

## Methodology

This is a **philosophical-conceptual monograph** (part of the *Elements in Philosophy of AI* series), not an empirical benchmark paper. Its method is:

1. **Framework import**: Adopt Lewis's radical-interpretation schema "given P, solve for the rest," retargeted from humans to AI systems by substituting "computational" for "physical."
2. **Three-aspect analysis**: Every account of interpretation is decomposed into (a) the *data/evidence* (physical/computational facts, split into internals vs. behavior), (b) the *goal* (an agential description ⟨P, D, 𝔄⟩), and (c) the *procedure* (testing candidate descriptions on predictive success + rationality). A triangle diagram (Figure 1) shows agential description, computational system, and behavior, with radical interpretation being the project of "getting all three to cohere."
3. **Decision-theoretic formalization**: Use the Jeffrey-Bolker representation theorem (not von Neumann-Morgenstern or Savage) because it places belief and desire over a shared Boolean algebra of propositions, which is exactly what's needed to treat them as propositional attitudes.
4. **Criterion-by-criterion mapping**: For belief (Chapter 4) and desire (Chapter 5), define criteria and tie each to an existing mechanistic-interpretability technique — linear probes and truth directions for truth/uniformity; consistency-of-logits across paraphrases for coherence; causal interventions/patching for use; sparse-autoencoder features and agentic multi-step behavior for intelligibility and systematicity.
5. **Synthesis on propositions (Chapter 6)**: Argue that the propositional structure is jointly constrained with the attitudes, yielding the propositional projection problem and a holism principle.
6. **Narrative scaffolding**: The book opens with a parable (the wizard Murazeel and apprentices Jentley/Lizette interpreting the "Voltaic Vivens machine") and closes with an epilogue, dramatizing the representationalist (Lizette's internals-reading wand) vs. interpretationist (Jentley's behavioral-prediction wand) divide.

The authors explicitly position it as a *foundation*, not a survey: they focus on well-established methods (probes, SAEs, steering vectors) and well-established results, since newer techniques build on these.

## Results & Highlights

- **No quantitative experiments** in the ML sense — this is a theory book. Its "results" are conceptual claims and a re-reading of the existing empirical literature:
  - Early probing papers that relied on **truth alone** (e.g., training a linear probe on truth labels) turned out to be **fragile**, exactly as the multi-criterion theory predicts — corroborating that no single criterion suffices.
  - Belief has substantially more white-box (representational) evidence than desire: truth directions, factual-recall features, and knowledge probes exist, but are not yet enough to "declare victory."
  - Desire is currently **interpretable but not representational**: the strongest evidence comes from the **agentic turn** — agentic LLMs increasingly exhibit the diverse means-ends behavior interpretationism needs to jointly pin down beliefs and desires.
- **Central conceptual result — holism**: Beliefs, desires, and propositional structure are jointly constrained; a method that fixes one while measuring the others inherits that choice's distortions. This is presented as the deepest practical lesson.
- **Safety framing**: The relevance is not perfect propositional recovery but "recovering enough to make the behavioral predictions that matter for the deployment context." For narrow tasks propositional mismatch is tolerable; for detecting **scheming, deception, or misaligned goals**, the propositional projection problem is most pressing because such assessments require reading internal states in propositional terms that exceed the immediate behavioral context.
- **Structure**: ~94 pages of main text across 7 chapters (Radical Interpretation; The Folk Psychology of LLMs; Representationalism and Interpretationism; Belief; Desire; Propositions; Conclusion), plus extensive references (Davidson, Lewis, Dennett, Jeffrey, Bolker, Chalmers, Bratman, alongside ML work by Burns, Bricken et al., Elhage et al., Azaria & Mitchell, Andreas, Goldstein & Levinstein, Hofweber et al., Beaglehole et al. 2026 *Science*).

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: High *in synthesis*, moderate *in components*. None of the individual ingredients (radical interpretation, Jeffrey-Bolker decision theory, probes/SAEs/steering vectors) is new; the contribution is the rigorous, book-length *unification* and the operationalization of philosophical criteria into testable interpretability protocols. The renaming of "accuracy" to "truth" and the identification of the **propositional projection problem** as a named, central difficulty are genuinely novel framings. The holism argument — that piecemeal attribution inherits distortions — is the book's sharpest and most original practical claim.
- **Rigor**: Strong. The decision-theoretic machinery (Jeffrey-Bolker representation theorem, Boolean algebra of propositions) is used precisely and correctly, and the criteria are stated explicitly enough to be falsifiable. The honest "mixed outcome" verdict — refusing to overclaim that LLMs have beliefs, while articulating exactly what evidence would change that — is intellectually disciplined.
- **Reproducibility**: N/A in the ML sense (no code/experiments). As a *conceptual* artifact it is highly reproducible: the criteria are stated precisely, the mapping to existing techniques is explicit, and the authors cite the concrete empirical papers a practitioner would replicate. A reader could operationalize "test coherence," "test use via patching," "test intelligibility via agentic means-ends behavior" directly.
- **Significance**: Potentially high for the interpretability field, especially the *philosophy-of-interpretability* subfield. If adopted, it would give interpretability researchers a shared vocabulary for declaring a method "successful," which the authors correctly note is currently absent. Its direct safety relevance (deception/scheming detection) is well-argued. However, its impact depends on whether ML practitioners adopt philosophical criteria — historically a barrier.
- **Red flags / weaknesses**:
  - **Asymmetry of evidence base**: The book is candid that desire has little direct representational evidence, so the desire chapter is "more philosophical and less empirical." This makes the desire half more speculative.
  - **No empirical validation of the framework itself**: The book does not demonstrate, on a real model, that applying its multi-criteria battery yields better/more reliable attributions than single-criterion methods. The "fragility of truth-only probing" is cited from prior work, not re-derived.
  - **Reliance on contested philosophical assumptions**: Representationalism vs. interpretationism, and the very applicability of propositional-attitude talk to neural networks (cf. Churchland's eliminativism, Fodor & Pylyshyn's systematicity objections, which are cited), are live philosophical disputes. The book argues for its stance but does not settle it.
  - **"Mixed outcome" is a hedged conclusion**: readers seeking a crisp verdict on "do LLMs have beliefs/desires" will not get one — by design, but it limits punch.
  - **Propositional projection problem is *identified but unsolved***: the authors explicitly concede they "have not provided a complete account of how to extract the model's propositional algebra from its internals," so the book's hardest problem remains open.
  - Potential framing risk: the parable/epilogue literary devices, while memorable, may read as padding to a technical audience.

## Strengths

- Genuinely **bridges two literatures** that rarely engage, written by authors credible on both sides (philosophy + Anthropic).
- The **multi-criterion, joint-attribution** framework is a concrete improvement over single-metric interpretability evaluations and correctly anticipates why fragile probing results occur.
- **Intellectually honest** about the state of evidence — refuses to overclaim, names what is open, separates "interpretable" from "representational."
- The **decision-theoretic formalization** (Jeffrey-Bolker over a shared Boolean algebra) is the right tool and is used cleanly.
- Strong **safety motivation**: deception/scheming detection is treated as the case where propositional recovery matters most, grounding the abstraction in real stakes.
- Accessible structure with explicit criteria-to-method mappings makes it actionable for interpretability practitioners.

## Limitations

- **Not empirical**: no new experiments, datasets, or models; "results" are reconceptualizations of existing work.
- **Desire half is under-evidenced** relative to belief — acknowledged but real.
- **Propositional projection problem is unsolved**; the book frames it more than resolves it.
- **Heavy reliance on contested philosophy** (radical interpretation, representation theorems, propositional attitudes) that not all ML researchers will accept as the right lens.
- **No demonstration that the framework improves practical interpretability outcomes** versus ad-hoc practice.
- Length and literary framing (parable/epilogue) may reduce uptake among engineering audiences.

## Relevance

高度相关。本文直接为机制可解释性（mechanistic interpretability）建立理论基础，把探针、稀疏自编码器、引导向量等核心工具纳入"信念/欲望/命题结构联合归赋"的哲学-实证框架，并对"何时可声称可解释性方法成功"给出了可操作标准——正是可解释性/机制可解释性研究方向所缺失的理论支撑。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can the multi-criterion battery (truth + coherence + use + uniformity) be **operationalized into a single automated evaluation protocol** and shown to predict downstream reliability (e.g., robustness to distribution shift) better than single-criterion probing?
- How would one **empirically extract or infer a model's propositional algebra 𝔄 from its internals** — i.e., concretely solve the propositional projection problem the book leaves open? Are sparse-autoencoder feature graphs a candidate substrate?
- The book relies on the Jeffrey-Bolker theorem requiring preferences to satisfy structural (coherence) axioms; **how badly do real LLM preferences violate these axioms**, and does approximate coherence suffice for meaningful attribution?
- For **deception/scheming detection**, what is the minimum propositional recovery needed to make a safety-relevant behavioral prediction, and can that gap be quantified?
- The desire chapter is "more philosophical and less empirical" — what **new mechanistic-interpretability techniques** (beyond probes) would be needed to produce representational evidence of desire, and could the agentic-turn means-ends tests be made rigorous enough to substitute?
- How does the framework interact with **inner-misalignment / mesa-optimization** concerns, where the "agential description" attributed to the model may diverge from the training objective by construction?
