Implicit Representations of Grammaticality in Language Models

arXiv: 2605.05197v1

Date: 2026-05-06

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.05197v1

一句话总结： 作者通过在合成扰动语料上训练简单的 ℓ2 线性探针（linear probe），证明 LLM 的隐藏状态比字符串概率更准确、更"专属于语法"地编码了语法合法性判断，且这种语法表征在跨语言、跨数据集与神经元稀疏化（仅约 10 个神经元）下仍稳健存在。
TL;DR: Wang et al. train a simple ℓ2-regularized linear probe on the hidden states of six open-weight LMs (OLMo-2/3-7B, Llama-3.2-1B / 3.1-8B, Gemma-2-2B/9B) using synthetically corrupted English sentences (insertion/deletion/local-shuffle perturbations of PTB+Gutenberg). The probe outperforms LM string log-probabilities on three English acceptability benchmarks (BLiMP, CoLA, SyntaxGym), zero-shot transfers to six typologically diverse languages, performs worse than logprobs on semantic plausibility (suggesting selectivity to syntax), and can be localized to as few as ~10 neurons via LASSO. The grammaticality signal is shown to be only moderately correlated (Spearman 0.09–0.47) with logprob, supporting the claim that LMs encode a grammaticality–likelihood distinction internally even though their output probabilities entangle the two.

核心贡献

  • 提出一种无需人工标注的语法性探针训练方案：用三种简单扰动（插入、删除、局部 shuffle）构造合成"语法/不合法"对，在 PTB+Gutenberg 文本上自动生成 50,000 句训练对。
  • 证明在 6 个开源基础模型（OLMo-2/3-7B、Llama-3.2-1B/3.1-8B、Gemma-2-2B/9B）的中间层隐藏状态上训练的 ℓ2 线性探针，在 BLiMP、CoLA、SyntaxGym 上显著超过 LM 字符串对数概率作为语法性判断分数。
  • 验证探针的"语法-语义解耦"特性：在 Kauf 等的语义合理性数据集上探针得分反而低于 LM logprob，说明探针更选择性地捕捉句法而非语义合理性。
  • 展示英语训练的探针在瑞典语、荷兰语、意大利语、俄语、日语、汉语六种语言上零样本跨语言迁移优于 logprob 基线，间接支持英语主导的多语共享表征空间。
  • 通过 LASSO 神经元选择证明语法信号可被定位到极少量神经元（甚至仅 0.01% ≈ 10 个神经元仍可工作），并且语法信号在 logprob 之外提供独立信息（用 logprob 作为额外特征几乎不提升探针性能）。

Key Contributions

  • A label-free training pipeline for grammaticality probes: construct ~50k synthetic minimal-style pairs by applying insertion / deletion / local-shuffle perturbations to a generic English corpus (Penn Treebank + Project Gutenberg), validated by Claude Opus 4.6 as judge (93.72% original = acceptable, 6.28% perturbed = acceptable).
  • Demonstrates that ℓ2-regularized linear probes on best-layer last-token hidden states outperform length-normalized LM logprob on three English acceptability benchmarks (BLiMP minimal-pair ACC, CoLA AUC, SyntaxGym AUC) across all six tested LMs.
  • Shows the probe is more discriminative for syntactic acceptability but less discriminative for semantic plausibility than LM logprob — evidence the probe isolates a grammaticality channel distinct from likelihood.
  • Provides cross-lingual evidence: an English-trained probe generalizes to Swedish, Dutch, Italian, Russian, Japanese, Chinese acceptability datasets, generally beating logprob; non-English-trained probes transfer worse, consistent with English-dominant pretraining.
  • LASSO probes show the grammaticality signal is largely localizable: even 0.01% sparsity (≈10 neurons) yields strong performance, with selected neurons concentrated in late layers (Fig. 9). Augmenting probes with logprob features yields negligible gain (ΔACC ~0), suggesting logprob is largely redundant once hidden states are accessible. Conversely, ridge regression recovers logprob from hidden states with R² ≈ 0.55–0.73, so likelihood information is also encoded.

Methodology

Probe training. For each LM M and sentence s, take the last-token hidden state h at a chosen layer. Train a logistic regression with ℓ2 penalty: −(1/N) Σ log p(y|s) + α‖w‖². α and the layer are selected by maximizing dev AUC on a held-out 20% split of the synthetic data; middle layers (e.g., Llama-3.1-8B layer 11/32, Gemma-2-9B layer 18/42, OLMo-3-7B layer 15/32) were typically optimal.

Synthetic contrastive corpus. 50,000 sentences are pooled from PTB and Gutenberg-DPO (chosen field only). Each sentence is perturbed by exactly one of:

  • Insertion: insert k ∈ {1..5} random vocab tokens at random positions.
  • Deletion: delete k ∈ {1..5} alphabetic tokens.
  • Local shuffle: random permutation of a contiguous 5-token window.

Evaluation. Three English acceptability benchmarks (BLiMP, CoLA, SyntaxGym) plus six multilingual ones (ScaLA-sv, BLiMP-NL, ItaCoLA, RuCoLA, JCoLA, SLING) and three semantic plausibility sets from Kauf et al. (2023). Metrics: ACC for minimal-pair sets, AUC for binary-labeled single-sentence sets.

Localization. ℓ1-LASSO probe over concatenated all-layer hidden states, retraining ℓ2 on the LASSO-selected neuron subset. Sparsity targets: {0.01%, 0.05%, 0.1%, 0.5%}. Random-neuron baseline trained on 30 random subsets per condition.

Auxiliary analyses. (a) Probe-with-logprob feature augmentation, (b) ridge regression from hidden states to length-normalized logprob (per-token vs. last-token-only), (c) Spearman/Pearson correlation between probe score and logprob.

Models. OLMo-2-7B, OLMo-3-7B, Llama-3.2-1B, Llama-3.1-8B, Gemma-2-2B, Gemma-2-9B — all base (non-instruction-tuned) models. Inference on H100/A100. Probing completes in <15 hours with ~8 hours dominated by LASSO neuron selection.

Results & Highlights

  • BLiMP: probe scores beat LM logprob in both ACC and AUC across all six models. Per-category breakdown (Table 10) shows probe wins on Anaphor Agreement, Determiner-Noun Agreement, Subject-Verb Agreement (e.g., +0.16 ACC for Anaphor on Gemma-2-2B), with smaller or mixed gains on Quantifiers and Ellipsis.
  • CoLA / SyntaxGym AUCs: probe scores are 0.74–0.81 (CoLA) and 0.55–0.71 (SyntaxGym) versus logprob's 0.62–0.70 / 0.49–0.56.
  • Cross-lingual (Table 2): English-trained probes achieve, e.g., Llama-3.1-8B Swedish AUC 0.74 vs. logprob 0.65; Gemma-2-9B Russian AUC 0.65 vs. 0.47. Gains shrink for typologically distant languages (Japanese/Chinese) but probes still match or beat logprob.
  • Semantic plausibility flip: probe ACC ≈ 0.55–0.65 vs. logprob ACC ≈ 0.75–0.80 — probes are intentionally insensitive to plausibility, which the authors frame as evidence of a grammaticality-specific representation.
  • Localization: with 0.01% LASSO sparsity (≈10 neurons), probes still substantially outperform random-neuron baselines (Fig. 3); the gap widens at higher sparsity, suggesting concentration rather than full distribution.
  • Logprob recoverability: ridge R² up to 0.73 (Gemma-2-2B per-token), confirming probability is implicit in hidden states; yet adding logprob as a feature yields ΔACC near zero, so the probe already exploits the relevant likelihood subspace.
  • Correlations (Table 3): Spearman(logprob, probe) is 0.09–0.47 — moderate, not strong — so the probe is not a reparameterization of logprob.

Critical Assessment

Novelty (moderate). Linear probing of LM hidden states for syntax is a well-trodden area (BLiMP/SyntaxGym evaluations, structural probes, Hewitt & Manning). The genuinely novel contributions are (i) the unsupervised synthetic-perturbation training set replacing CoLA/BLiMP for probe training, (ii) the side-by-side framing as a "grammaticality vs. likelihood" disentanglement, and (iii) the LASSO localization showing very small neuron subsets suffice. None of these is paradigm-shifting individually, but the combination is a clean, useful contribution.

Rigor. Generally solid: 6 models across 3 families, 3 English + 6 multilingual + 3 plausibility benchmarks, 95% confidence intervals reported (Tables 11–13), random-neuron baselines for LASSO, ablations with logprob-augmented probes, and ridge regression to test reverse direction (hidden → logprob). The synthetic data is validated by an LLM-as-judge (Claude Opus 4.6) — adequate but not human-validated.

Reproducibility. Code repository announced; benchmarks are public Hugging Face datasets; model HF IDs and hidden-dim/layer counts are tabulated (Table 6); training hyperparameters (α grid 2⁻²..2⁵, 80/20 split) and compute (H100/A100, <15 h) are specified. Reproducibility looks good in principle.

Significance. Useful for the LM-as-cognitive-model debate (cited Katzir 2023, Piantadosi 2023, Futrell & Mahowald 2025) — it provides empirical support that LMs internally distinguish grammaticality from probability even though their output marginals do not. For practitioners interested in interpretability, it contributes a concrete localization result (~10 neurons). The cross-lingual transfer claim is interesting but qualitative.

Red flags / concerns.

  • The synthetic perturbation pipeline can produce semantically implausible rather than ungrammatical sentences (the authors acknowledge this as a limitation). The 93.72%/6.28% LLM-judge validation is reassuring but circular: an LLM judge may share biases with the probed LMs.
  • "Grammaticality" is operationalized purely as separability under three crude perturbations; this is a much weaker notion than competence-style syntactic knowledge. The paper's framing occasionally drifts toward strong claims (e.g., "implicit grammaticality distinction") that are not fully warranted by the evidence.
  • The probe's apparent selectivity for syntax over semantics (Fig. 2b) is a key claim, but lower performance on plausibility could partly reflect distribution shift between training (perturbed sentences) and plausibility test sets, not pure syntactic specialization.
  • "10 neurons suffice" is striking but rests on LASSO over the full all-layer concatenation (4096 × 32+ ≈ 130k features), so 0.01% is still 10–14 neurons selected with knowledge of supervised labels — these are not "discovered" causal units, only a sparse linear readout.
  • Only base models, only final checkpoints — no learning-dynamics analysis, no instruction-tuned models. Generalization to deployed chat models is untested.
  • AUC differences are sometimes small (<0.05) but reported as wins; CIs (Tables 11–13) overlap in several cases (e.g., RuCoLA), tempering the strength of cross-lingual claims.

Strengths

  • Clean, simple methodology that is easy to replicate; no need for human-labeled training data.
  • Broad benchmark coverage: 3 English + 6 multilingual acceptability sets + 3 plausibility sets.
  • Multiple complementary analyses (probe vs. logprob, augmentation, ridge inverse, LASSO localization) that triangulate the central claim.
  • Thoughtful framing of the "grammaticality vs. likelihood" distinction within the LM-as-cognitive-model debate.
  • Reports confidence intervals; uses random-neuron baselines.
  • Code release and explicit model IDs.

Limitations

  • Synthetic perturbations approximate but do not equal real ungrammaticality; some perturbed sentences may be semantically odd rather than syntactically illegal (the authors flag this).
  • "Grammatical" original sentences may themselves contain naturally occurring grammar errors.
  • Tested only on base (non-instruction-tuned) LMs; results may not transfer to RLHF-tuned production models.
  • No human grammaticality validation of the synthetic dataset (LLM-as-judge only).
  • No formal-language experiments; "grammaticality" remains tied to natural-language acceptability proxies.
  • No learning dynamics / training-checkpoint analysis — cannot say when during pretraining the representation emerges.
  • The probe is supervised; "implicit representation" is therefore a statement about decodability, not about whether the LM uses these features causally during generation.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Is the ~10-neuron localization causal? An activation-patching or ablation experiment would test whether suppressing those neurons selectively degrades grammaticality without harming plausibility.
  • Does the grammaticality probe transfer to instruction-tuned and RLHF models, or does alignment training rotate / collapse this representation?
  • When during pretraining does the grammaticality representation emerge? Probing checkpoints (e.g., OLMo intermediate checkpoints, which are public) would directly answer this.
  • Would human-labeled synthetic perturbations (rather than LLM-judged) change the magnitude of probe performance, or is the ~94% LLM agreement a reasonable proxy?
  • How does the probe behave on naturally occurring ungrammatical text (e.g., learner English, dysfluent speech transcripts) vs. on adversarially constructed minimal pairs?
  • Does the same probe transfer across model families (e.g., probe trained on Llama representations applied to OLMo via shared subspace alignment), and what does that tell us about the universality of the grammaticality direction?
  • Can the probe be turned into a generation-time steering vector to suppress ungrammatical continuations, providing a useful interpretability-to-control bridge?
