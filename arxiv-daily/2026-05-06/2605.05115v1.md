> **一句话总结：** 本文用流形几何取代欧氏几何来重构激活引导（activation steering），通过沿激活流形 $\mathcal{M}_h$ 的测地线插值产生平滑、自然的行为轨迹，并在四个语言任务和一个视觉世界模型上证明激活流形与行为流形 $\mathcal{M}_y$ 之间近似等距同构（皮尔逊相关 $r=0.99/0.89$ ），将引导问题从"找正确方向"重新表述为"找正确几何"。

> **TL;DR:** The paper argues that linear (Euclidean) activation steering is unfaithful to the curved geometry of LLM representations, and proposes *manifold steering*: interpolating along geodesics of an empirically-fit activation manifold $\mathcal{M}_h$. It establishes an approximate isometry between $\mathcal{M}_h$ and a behavior manifold $\mathcal{M}_y$ (output distributions) on cyclic/sequential reasoning tasks (weekdays, months, letters, ages), 2D in-context-learning grids, and a Mountain Car visual world model. Manifold steering yields ~2.8x lower Bhattacharyya energy (more natural outputs) than linear steering and avoids the "teleportation" pathology where linear paths cut through off-manifold regions.

## 核心贡献

- **揭示等距几何**：在四个具有已知概念结构的语言任务上拟合激活流形 $\mathcal{M}_h$ 与行为流形 $\mathcal{M}_y$（输出概率分布通过 Hellinger 嵌入到球面），证明二者的测地距离高度相关（weekdays $r=0.99$，months $r=0.89$，letters $r=0.999$，ages $r=0.999$），而欧氏距离的相关性显著更弱。
- **流形引导（Manifold Steering）**：相对于线性引导，提出沿激活流形测地线插值的引导策略，定性上产生平滑且按概念顺序过渡的输出（如 Tuesday→Wednesday→Thursday→Friday），定量上 Bhattacharyya 累积能量平均降低约 $2.8\times$（$p<0.001$）。
- **回拉（Pullback）双向验证**：对优化沿 $\mathcal{M}_y$ 行为路径的激活轨迹（pullback）能够还原 $\mathcal{M}_h$，内禀 $R^2$ 显著高于线性基线（如 weekdays 0.77 vs 0.42），首次给出从行为到表征双向等距的因果证据。
- **统一引导的几何视角**：把线性、流形、回拉三种引导统一为黎曼度量 $G_I$、$G_E$、$G_F$ 下的测地线问题，将引导难题从"方向"重新表述为"几何"。
- **跨模态泛化**：在 ICLR 二维概念空间任务（5×5 网格、9×9 圆柱）上展示分解化（factored）控制能力；在 Mountain Car 视觉世界模型上展示流形引导让小车位置平滑变化，而线性引导则导致解码图像模糊或瞬移。

## Key Contributions

- **An empirical isometry $\mathcal{M}_h \leftrightarrow \mathcal{M}_y$.** Fits a low-dim activation manifold (cubic splines through PCA-reduced concept centroids) and a behavior manifold (splines on the Hellinger-embedded simplex) for four language tasks on Llama 3.1 8B; geodesic distances in the two spaces are highly correlated, while linear-path distances are not.
- **Manifold steering as a steering strategy.** Defines $\pi_m(t) = s((1-t)u_0 + t u_1^*)$ in intrinsic manifold coordinates, and shows it yields smooth, ordered probability transitions through adjacent concepts where standard linear (additive-vector) steering exhibits "teleportation".
- **Pullback steering as a reverse construction.** Optimizes activation trajectories so that the *induced* behavioral trajectory follows $\mathcal{M}_y$; the resulting paths in activation space recover $\mathcal{M}_h$ (intrinsic $R^2$ ≈ 0.77 vs 0.42 for linear baseline on weekdays), giving bidirectional causal support.
- **A unified Riemannian framework.** Recasts steering as choosing a metric $G$ over activation space — flat ($G_I$), density-based ($G_E \propto e^{E(h)} I_n$), and pullback ($G_F = J_F^\top g_y J_F + \epsilon I$) — with steering-as-geodesic.
- **Multi-modality demonstrations.** Extends to 2D synthetic ICL grid/cylinder graphs (Park et al. 2025b protocol) demonstrating *factored* control along intrinsic axes, and to a Mountain Car GRU world model where manifold steering produces visually smooth car-position progression while linear steering produces blurred/superimposed frames.

## Methodology

The paper studies a base model and a task with a known conceptual domain $\mathcal{Z}$ (e.g., the 7 weekdays). For each task, two manifolds are fit:

1. **Activation manifold $\mathcal{M}_h$**: Compute concept centroids by averaging hidden activations $h(x)$ over input prompts whose answer is the same concept; project to 64-dim PCA; fit a cubic spline through the centroids (or thin-plate splines for 2D ICL tasks).
2. **Behavior manifold $\mathcal{M}_y$**: For each prompt class, take the model's output distribution over $\mathcal{Z}$ plus an "other" mass class, map via $p \mapsto \sqrt{p}$ (Hellinger embedding) to the unit sphere where the Hellinger distance becomes Euclidean, then fit splines.

**Three steering strategies are compared.** Given a base prompt and a target activation $h_1^*$:
- *Linear steering* $\pi_{lin}(t) = (1-t)h_0^* + t h_1^*$ (Euclidean baseline).
- *Manifold steering* $\pi_m(t) = s((1-t)u_0 + t u_1^*)$ where $s$ is the parametrization of $\mathcal{M}_h$, so the path stays on the manifold.
- *Pullback steering*: optimize via L-BFGS for an activation path that, when injected, induces an output trajectory along $\mathcal{M}_y$.

**Evaluation.** Steered behavioral trajectories (probability distributions over time $t \in [0,1]$) are scored by the cumulative *Bhattacharyya energy* $E_{BC}(\gamma) = \int_0^1 d_{BC}(\gamma(t), \mathcal{M}_y) dt$, a low-energy proxy for naturalness w.r.t. the model's untouched output distribution. Pullback paths are scored by intrinsic $R^2$ against the manifold steering path within a 99%-variance subspace.

**Models and tasks.** Llama 3.1 8B (layer 28) on weekdays/months/letters/ages addition tasks; the Park et al. (2025b) ICL random-walk protocol on 5×5 grid and 9×9 cylinder; a custom GRU+CNN visual world model on Mountain Car (Moore 1990).

**Theoretical framing.** Section 3.4 unifies the three strategies as geodesics under three Riemannian metrics: identity ($G_I$), density-rescaled ($G_E$, where $e^{-E(h)}$ tracks an unnormalized log-density of activations à la Béthune et al. 2025), and the pullback metric ($G_F$). The claim is that $G_E$ and $G_F$, despite being derived from independent sources (internal activations vs output distributions), converge to the same paths — the central isometry result.

## Results & Highlights

- **Isometry correlations** (geodesic vs geodesic): weekdays $r=0.99$, months $r=0.89$, letters $r=0.999$, ages $r=0.999$. Linear-vs-geodesic correlations: $r=0.89, 0.53, 0.71, 0.36$ respectively.
- **Cumulative Bhattacharyya energy**: manifold steering 0.34±0.03 (weekdays), 0.36±0.01 (months), 2.42±0.07 (letters), 5.21±0.09 (ages); linear steering 0.93±0.11, 1.09±0.06, 6.95±0.27, 13.49±0.29. Average factor ≈ $2.8\times$, all $p < 0.001$.
- **Pullback intrinsic $R^2$** (vs linear baseline): weekdays 0.77 vs 0.42, months 0.75 vs 0.32, letters 0.78 vs 0.23, ages 0.47 vs 0.24.
- **2D ICL tasks**: clean grid recovery in MDS embeddings of activation/behavior manifolds, with steering along one intrinsic axis producing minimal off-target drift on the orthogonal axis (factored control). Linear steering exhibits scrambled, warped MDS structure.
- **Mountain Car**: PCA visualization shows linear steering paths leave the activation manifold; decoded mid-path frames are blurred/superimposed positions, then "teleport" to the endpoint, while manifold steering yields smooth physical motion with $r=0.99$ between activation-arc-length and behavior-arc-length matrices vs $r=0.06$ for linear paths.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty.** Substantive but incremental. The core observation that activation manifolds are curved and that linear steering can fail is well-established (Engels et al. 2024, Kantamneni & Tegmark 2025, Park et al. 2025b — all cited). The paper itself acknowledges "the closest work to ours is the contemporary paper by Park et al. (2026), who study a toy model where the representation-to-output distribution mapping is described via a simple softmax operation." The genuine novelty lies in (i) operationalizing a manifold-fitting + geodesic protocol that scales beyond toy 1D structures, (ii) the empirical bidirectional isometry test ($\mathcal{M}_h \leftrightarrow \mathcal{M}_y$) via the pullback construction, and (iii) the unified Riemannian framing of three steering strategies as choices of $G$.

**Rigor.** Statistical comparisons report $p<0.001$ throughout, but it is unclear how multiple-comparisons are handled and what the random seeds / variance over rollouts captures (the reported $\pm$ values seem small enough that they may not include manifold-fitting variance). The $r=0.999$ values on letters/ages are striking — but these tasks have very simple ground-truth orderings, so high correlation is partly baked into the spline-fitting choice. The choice of layer 28 (out of 32) for Llama 3.1 8B is mentioned but not ablated; results may be layer-dependent.

**Reproducibility.** Code is publicly released. The methodology (PCA + cubic splines + Hellinger embedding + L-BFGS pullback) is straightforward and described in appendices. The tasks are template-based and synthetic, which the authors explicitly call out as a limitation but which also makes reproduction tractable.

**Significance.** If correct, this re-frames a substantial portion of the "activation steering" literature (which is currently dominated by additive-vector methods like Bau, Subramani, Marks & Tegmark, Turner, Panickssery, etc.) as solving the wrong problem. That is a real contribution to interpretability/control. However, the practical case for manifold steering on *open-ended, non-synthetic* tasks (refusal, sycophancy, persona) is not demonstrated; the authors acknowledge this as future work, and it is the harder regime where steering matters most.

**Red flags.**
- All four language tasks have a *single, well-known* concept structure (cyclic or linear order). The "isometry" result is partly a consequence of having that structure to fit splines through. It is unclear how to *discover* the manifold without ground-truth labels — Section 8 explicitly flags "an unsupervised protocol would however significantly broaden the applicability of our methods." This is a critical scalability gap.
- The Bhattacharyya-energy evaluation effectively measures distance to a manifold the authors *already fit* to natural outputs, so manifold steering being closer to it is partly tautological. The output trajectories qualitatively shown in Figure 4 are still the strongest evidence.
- 16 authors with two layers of equal-contribution markers is unusual; the actual division of labor is opaque and the paper's logical structure could plausibly have been delivered in a tighter form.

## Strengths

- Clear, well-motivated central question and a satisfying bidirectional empirical answer ($\mathcal{M}_h \to \mathcal{M}_y$ via manifold steering, and $\mathcal{M}_y \to \mathcal{M}_h$ via pullback).
- Strong qualitative figures (Fig. 4 in particular) that make the "teleportation" failure mode of linear steering visceral and pedagogically clear.
- Multi-modality evidence: language addition, 2D ICL grids/cylinders, and a vision-based world model. The Mountain Car example is unusual for steering work and supports the generality claim.
- Code release plus a thorough related-work section that situates the paper accurately within the LRH-critique literature.
- Theoretical contribution of the Riemannian metric framing ($G_I, G_E, G_F$) gives the paper a coherent organizing principle beyond the empirics.

## Limitations

- **Synthetic, template-based tasks only.** No demonstrations on naturally important steering targets (refusal, sycophancy, persona, harmful content suppression). The acknowledged "expanding experimental validation to more complex domains" section is honest but weak.
- **Manifold-fitting requires ground truth.** Splines/TPS are fit using known concept labels and ordering. Without an unsupervised manifold-discovery protocol, manifold steering cannot be applied to "in-the-wild" steering use cases.
- **Token-level only.** Behavior is operationalized as the next-token distribution; sequence-level behaviors (extended generations, multi-turn dynamics) are out of scope.
- **Single layer / single base model.** All language results are at layer 28 of Llama 3.1 8B. Layer dependence and model-family generalization are not investigated.
- **No attempt at intermediate-variable manipulation.** The authors note this as a major future direction — the goal of interpretability is often to manipulate intermediate algorithmic variables, not output distributions.
- **The "isometry" is *approximate* and tested on tasks where it is most likely to hold** — i.e., when the target concept *is* the model output. For abstract concepts where the conceptual structure does not directly surface in next-token logits, the framework's testability is unclear.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can the manifold be discovered without ground-truth concept labels — e.g., via SAE features or unsupervised dimensionality estimation — and does the isometry still hold then?
- How does the energy gain ($\sim 2.8\times$) of manifold steering scale on naturalistic steering targets like refusal or sycophancy, where the conceptual structure is not clean cyclic/linear?
- Is the isometry $\mathcal{M}_h \leftrightarrow \mathcal{M}_y$ a property of well-trained models specifically, or does it emerge during training? Tracking the correlations across training checkpoints would clarify whether it is a stability/convergence signature.
- Layer dependence: is layer 28 (of 32) special, and how does the curvature of $\mathcal{M}_h$ change across the depth of the network?
- Does the pullback metric $G_F$ remain well-defined and useful for sequence-level (autoregressive) outputs, where $F: \mathcal{A} \to \mathcal{Y}$ is no longer a single softmax?
- How robust is manifold steering to fitting noise — e.g., when only a small number of concept centroids are available, or when their ordering is partially unknown?
- Connection to Park et al. (2026) and Kantamneni & Tegmark (2025): can the helix-for-arithmetic finding be reformulated and generalized within the $G_E/G_F$ framework, and would manifold steering yield better arithmetic interventions than known linear-helix methods?
