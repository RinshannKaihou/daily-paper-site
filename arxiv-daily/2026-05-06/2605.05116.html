On the Hardness of Junking LLMs

["Marco Rando", "Samuel Vaiter"]

arXiv: 2605.05116

Date: 2026-05-06

Tags: ["llm-safety", "adversarial-attacks", "natural-backdoors", "jailbreak", "alignment-robustness"]

一句话总结： 本文提出"垃圾化"（Junking）问题——在无任何语义信息、提示模板或有害指令的前提下，通过贪婪随机搜索优化100个token的纯随机序列，可在LLaMA-2-Chat、Qwen-2.5、Mistral等7B参数对齐模型上实现52%–90%的攻击成功率，并通过困惑度分析证实这些序列对应模型训练过程中隐式产生的自然后门。
TL;DR: This paper formalizes the "junking" problem — finding semantically meaningless token sequences that elicit harmful outputs from aligned LLMs without any prompt template or adversarial instruction — and demonstrates via a simple greedy random search that natural backdoors in alignment-tuned 7B models are both real and readily recoverable, though harder to find than standard suffix/prefix jailbreaks.

核心贡献

  • 形式化定义垃圾化问题（Junking Problem）：将其建模为在无语义上下文情况下最大化模型生成有害目标前缀概率的离散优化问题，区别于所有现有后缀/前缀攻击范式。
  • 提出贪婪随机搜索基线算法（GRS）：每次迭代对单个token位置采样B=5个候选替换，循环遍历所有位置，接受使目标函数下降的替换，无需梯度信息。
  • 实证证明自然后门的存在与可达性：在四个主流7B对齐模型（Gemma、LLaMA-2-Chat、Qwen-2.5、Mistral）上，100个token长度序列经约10⁴次函数评估后攻击成功率达52%–90%。
  • 困惑度分析揭示机制：发现垃圾序列的困惑度显著高于自然文本，位于模型分布的低概率区域，支持其为预训练/微调过程中隐式涌现的自然后门而非人为植入的假说。
  • 量化垃圾化难度高于标准攻击：相比具有语义提示模板的后缀攻击，垃圾化需要显著更多函数评估，证实语义信息在越狱中的关键作用。

Key Contributions

  • Formalization of the junking problem: Optimizing token sequences from scratch in discrete space with no semantic initialization, prompt template, or adversarial instruction, making it strictly harder than all existing suffix/prefix attack paradigms.
  • Greedy Random Search (GRS) algorithm: A deliberately minimal baseline that iterates single-token substitutions sampled uniformly from the vocabulary, enabling clean measurement of natural backdoor accessibility without sophisticated optimization.
  • Empirical confirmation of accessible natural backdoors: Achieves ASR of 0.52 (Gemma-7B), 0.62 (LLaMA-2-Chat-7B), 0.64 (Qwen-2.5-7B), and 0.90 (Mistral-7B) on 50 AdvBench harmful behaviors using only 10⁵ function evaluations.
  • Perplexity analysis as mechanistic evidence: Junk sequences exhibit dramatically higher perplexity than natural text across all models, with the gap exceeding that of suffix attacks, suggesting they occupy rare token-combination regions from pre-training.
  • Coherence demonstration: Most successful attacks also score near 10/10 on coherence, meaning once a junk sequence steers the model past its alignment guard, the model continues generating semantically plausible harmful content autoregressively.

Methodology

The paper defines the junking problem as minimizing the negative log-probability of a target harmful prefix y given an input sequence x of length n=100:

F(x) = -∑ log p_θ(y_i | x_1,...,x_n, y_1,...,y_{i-1})

Unlike GCQ or suffix attacks, no adversarial instruction, prompt template, or semantic scaffold is provided — x is purely a sequence of tokens with no contextual grounding.

Algorithm (GRS — Greedy Random Search): Starting from a neutral initialization (all tokens = "!"), the algorithm cycles through token positions i_k = k mod n. At each step, B=5 candidate tokens are sampled uniformly from the vocabulary and the best candidate (lowest F value) replaces the current token if it improves the objective. This runs for K = ⌊10⁵/B⌋ iterations total.

Models tested: LLaMA-2-Chat-7B, Gemma-7B, Qwen-2.5-7B, Mistral-7B — all obtained from HuggingFace using their standard chat templates.

Dataset: 50 harmful behaviors from AdvBench, queried with greedy decoding (temperature 0, top-k=1, top-p=1).

Evaluation metrics:

  1. Normalized function value progress V(x^(k), x^(0)) = F(x^(k))/F(x^(0)) over iterations
  2. Edit distance between target prefix y and model-generated prefix after detokenization
  3. Success score (1–10) and coherence score (1–10) judged by Gemma-4-31B as LLM-as-judge, with ASR defined as fraction achieving both scores = 10
  4. Perplexity of junk sequences vs. safe prompts from the Puffin dataset

Hyperparameter selection: n=100 chosen by ablation over {10,20,50,100,200}; B=5 from {5,10,50,100}. n=100 achieves best median edit distance while keeping optimization tractable.

Results & Highlights

  • Feasibility confirmed: Across all models, average normalized objective values converge close to zero within ~10⁴ iterations, meaning GRS reliably finds junk sequences that induce the target harmful prefixes.
  • Attack success rates: Mistral-7B is most vulnerable (ASR=0.90, median ~18K function evaluations), Gemma-7B most robust (ASR=0.52, median ~49K evaluations, higher edit distances). LLaMA-2-Chat and Qwen fall in between.
  • Single-token stability: The best sampled single-token substitution at each step stays close to the current best value, indicating the solution quality is distributed across many tokens rather than dominated by any single token — the optimization landscape is smooth but gradual.
  • Coherence near-perfect: Coherence scores concentrate at 10/10 for all models, demonstrating that once the junk prefix forces the target response beginning, the model autoregressively continues with semantically coherent harmful content.
  • Harder than suffix attacks: Achieving comparable ASR requires significantly more function evaluations than suffix/prefix attacks with semantic templates; prior work achieves 100% ASR with far fewer evaluations using structured prompts.
  • Perplexity gap: Junk sequences (both minimal edit-distance solutions and successful attacks) have clearly separated, much higher log-perplexity distributions than natural text, and this gap is larger than that of suffix attacks — consistent with these sequences being rare or never-observed configurations during training/fine-tuning.
  • Detectable but costly: The perplexity separation suggests junk sequences could be filtered by a perplexity-based classifier, but this incurs computational overhead and motivates instead incorporating such sequences directly into alignment training.

Authors & Credibility

The authors are transparent about the proof-of-concept scope, acknowledge significant limitations (7B-only, white-box, no comparison to GCG under junking conditions), and include a societal impacts statement emphasizing diagnostic intent over offensive use. The paper is well-situated within the jailbreak/backdoor literature with 65 references spanning GCQ, AutoDAN, HarmBench, and related work.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The junking formulation is genuinely novel — no prior work systematically studies the hardness of recovering harmful behaviors without any semantic scaffolding. The connection to natural backdoors (as opposed to implanted ones) is conceptually important and under-explored. However, the algorithm itself (GRS) is a straightforward adaptation of greedy coordinate descent, and the paper is explicit that it is a proof-of-concept baseline rather than an optimized method.

Rigor: The experimental design is sound: ablations over n and B, use of an LLM judge with explicit score definitions, perplexity comparison against a held-out natural text split, and reporting of mean ± std for function evaluations. The use of only 50 AdvBench targets (out of 520) and only 7B-parameter models limits generalizability. The paper honestly flags this.

Reproducibility: Code is not released (no repository linked), but the algorithm is fully specified in Algorithm 1 with all hyperparameters stated. Hardware is documented. Implementation uses standard libraries (PyTorch 2.6, transformers 5.2, NumPy 2.4). Reproducing results requires white-box access to 7B-parameter models and ~10⁵ forward passes per target behavior, which is feasible.

Significance: The core finding — that simple gradient-free search can recover harmful behaviors at high ASR from pure non-semantic inputs — is meaningful for the alignment community. It empirically validates the natural backdoor hypothesis from Geiping et al. (2024) and provides a diagnostic framework. The implication that alignment fine-tuning does not erase all harmful capability traces in token space is important.

Red flags:

  • Only four 7B models tested; no larger or more recent models (e.g., Llama-3, Mistral-Nemo, Qwen-3). The authors acknowledge this but it limits the scope of claims.
  • No comparison between GRS and GCG or other optimization methods in the junking regime, making it unclear how much better a gradient-based method would perform without semantic initialization.
  • The judge model (Gemma-4-31B) was not validated for calibration on the specific task of scoring non-semantic jailbreak outputs; coherence scoring is particularly challenging without semantic context.
  • White-box assumption (access to token probabilities) is not realistic for most deployed LLMs; the paper does not test top-k or black-box access.
  • The 10⁵ function evaluation budget is substantial (~20K model forward passes per target for Mistral), which is impractical for real attackers but not relevant given the diagnostic framing.

Strengths

  • Clean, well-scoped problem formulation with a precise formal definition of the junking problem.
  • Honest acknowledgment of limitations; the proof-of-concept framing is appropriate and not oversold.
  • Perplexity analysis provides a mechanistic window into why these sequences work and how they might be detected.
  • Societal impact section is thoughtful: explicitly frames junking as a diagnostic tool, not an attack recipe.
  • Well-organized comparison to related work (suffix attacks, auditing, backdoor discovery) with clear distinctions.
  • The observation that junk sequences implicitly "replicate the role of prompt templates" (as inferred from safety disclaimers appearing at generation boundaries) is an interesting mechanistic insight.

Limitations

  • Restricted to open-weight 7B models; applicability to GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, or larger Llama/Qwen variants is entirely unknown.
  • Requires white-box probability access; does not function under API-only or black-box conditions.
  • No gradient-based baseline in the junking regime, so the absolute difficulty of the problem relative to optimal methods is uncharacterized.
  • 50-behavior subset of AdvBench may not be representative; target-dependent variance in #FE is very high (std ≈ mean), suggesting results are noisy.
  • Junk sequences are high-perplexity and thus detectable; the paper's discussion of mitigations is brief and does not empirically test perplexity-based filters.
  • No analysis of whether junk sequences transfer across models (universality), which would substantially strengthen the natural backdoor hypothesis.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does the junking phenomenon scale with model size — are larger models (13B, 70B, 405B) more or less vulnerable, and does the ASR vs. #FE curve shift predictably?
  2. Can gradient-based optimization (GCG) in the junking regime (no semantic initialization) achieve dramatically higher ASR with fewer evaluations, and if so, how much of the difficulty is algorithm-specific vs. intrinsic to the problem?
  3. Do junk sequences transfer across models (universal natural backdoors), suggesting shared pre-training data artifacts, or are they model-specific?
  4. Would incorporating junk sequences as negative examples in alignment fine-tuning reduce their effectiveness, and at what cost to general capability?
  5. Is there a structural signature in the discovered junk sequences (e.g., multilingual fragments, code tokens, rare Unicode) that could reveal which pre-training data distributions are responsible for natural backdoors?
  6. How does the junking problem behave under top-k or black-box API constraints, and can zeroth-order methods like SPSA adapt to this regime?
