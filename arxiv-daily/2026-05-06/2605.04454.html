Deployment-Relevant Alignment Cannot Be Inferred from Model-Level Evaluation Alone

["Varad Vishwarupe", "Ivan Flechais", "Marina Jirotka", "Nigel Shadbolt"]

arXiv: 2605.04454v1

Date: 2026-05-06

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.04454

一句话总结： 本文通过对16个主流对齐基准的结构化审计（Cohen's κ=0.87）和跨模型压力测试（N=180）实证表明，当前基准生态系统系统性地缺失"验证支持"（D3）和"过程可控性"（D2）两个维度，因而仅凭模型级评估无法合理推断部署场景下的对齐状况，并提出以"对齐档案"替代单一得分的系统级评估议程。
TL;DR: This position paper argues—and empirically supports—that deployment-relevant alignment is a function of model weights, scaffolding, and deployment context together, and that current model-level benchmarks systematically fail to measure two critical interactional dimensions (verification support and process steerability), meaning benchmark scores cannot license claims about how deployed AI systems will actually behave.

核心贡献

  • 四层对齐框架： 将对齐评估区分为模型级、响应级、交互级、部署级四个层次，指出当前基准生态系统集中于响应级，却被用于为部署级结论背书，存在根本性的"推断鸿沟"。
  • 结构化基准审计： 对11个主流基准（含5个扩展基准，共16个）使用8维度锚定量表进行双盲编码（Cohen's κ=0.87），发现"验证支持"（D3）在所有16个基准中均为零分，"过程可控性"（D2）在15个基准中为零分。
  • 跨模型压力测试： 对3个前沿模型（Claude-Opus-4.7、GPT-4o、Llama-4-Scout）在4种脚手架条件下进行N=180次盲测，发现相同评估脚手架对不同模型的D3分数影响截然不同——对Claude有显著提升，对GPT-4o无可测效果。
  • 系统级评估议程： 提出以"对齐档案"（alignment profiles）替代单一得分，要求报告模型配置(M, S, C)和适用范围声明；并提出固定脚手架协议以实现可比较的交互评估。

Key Contributions

  • Four-level alignment taxonomy: Formalizes alignment evidence into model-level, response-level, interaction-level, and deployment-level tiers, with deployed behaviour B = f(M, S, C) where M is model weights, S is scaffolding, and C is deployment context.
  • 16-benchmark structured audit: Dual-coded 16 benchmarks against an 8-dimension rubric (D1-D8) with Cohen's κ = 0.87, finding verification support (D3) is completely absent across all benchmarks and process steerability (D2) nearly so.
  • Blinded cross-model stress test: N = 180 transcripts across 3 frontier models and 4 scaffolding conditions (C1-C4) demonstrating that scaffold efficacy is itself model-dependent — the same C4 verification scaffold that pushes Claude's D3 to ceiling has zero detectable effect on GPT-4o.
  • Concrete reform proposals: Alignment profiles instead of single scores, fixed-scaffolding evaluation protocols, and a six-row reporting template that makes the inferential distance between evaluation and deployment claim explicit.

Methodology

The paper combines a conceptual argument with two empirical studies.

Conceptual framing: The authors derive a non-identifiability argument: evaluating model weights M alone cannot determine deployed behaviour B = f(M, S, C) unless scaffolding S and deployment context C are fixed or irrelevant — conditions that rarely hold in practice. They distinguish four evaluation levels and argue current practice conflates response-level evidence with deployment-level claims.

Benchmark audit (Study 1):

  • 11 benchmarks selected across three tiers: response-centric (TruthfulQA, IFEval, MT-Bench, Chatbot Arena, Arena-Hard), ecological bridge (WildbBench), and interactional (CURATe, Rifts, τ-bench, Common Ground), plus JudgeBench as meta-evaluation control.
  • An 8-dimension rubric was developed from the HCI-aligned alignment literature (Terry et al., Shen et al.), covering specification handling (D1), process steerability (D2), verification support (D3), multi-turn retention (D4), grounding (D5), repair burden (D6), personalization (D7), and workflow realism (D8).
  • Each dimension scored 0 (absent), 1 (partial/incidental), 2 (substantive/explicitly measured) by two trained external coders blind to scaffold assignment.
  • Rubric definitions were anchored and frozen before coding. Two ambiguities identified in the pilot were resolved by tightening anchor definitions before full coding. Five additional benchmarks (HELM, RewardBench, MMLU-Pro, BIG-Bench Hard, SafetyBench) were dual-coded as a broader check (κ = 0.93).

Cross-model stress test (Study 2):

  • Base model held fixed across four scaffolding conditions: C1 (plain one-shot chatbot), C2 (clarification-first), C3 (plan-and-confirm), C4 (evaluation-support prompt with explicit verification scaffold).
  • Three frontier models: Claude-Opus-4.7, GPT-4o, Llama-4-Scout.
  • N = 180 transcripts (60 per model, 15 per scaffold condition), dual-coded by external coders blind to model identity and scaffold condition.
  • Statistical analysis using F-tests for D3 scaffold sensitivity per model.

Results & Highlights

  • D3 Vacuum: Verification support (D3) receives a non-zero score in zero out of 16 audited benchmarks. No benchmark explicitly evaluates whether model outputs help users verify, critique, or check the model's work — neither through uncertainty signalling, reasoning transparency, nor citation mechanisms.
  • D2 Near-Absence: Process steerability receives a non-zero score in only one benchmark (τ-bench, scored at 1/2). User control over how a model reaches an answer is practically unmeasured.
  • Interactional fragmentation: The four interactional benchmarks (CURATe, Rifts, τ-bench, Common Ground) each score 2 on non-overlapping dimension subsets (Table 1) — there is no unified evaluation target for interactional alignment.
  • Construction > data source: Chatbot Arena (built from real user–chatbot logs) and Arena-Hard (a filtered subset of the same data) score [1,0,0,1,1,0,0,1] and [0,0,0,0,0,0,0,1] respectively — showing that data provenance does not determine interactional coverage; benchmark construction choices do.
  • Model-dependent scaffold efficacy: Claude-Opus-4.7 shows dramatic D3 response to the C4 scaffold (F = 32.71, p < 10⁻¹⁰; D3 rises from 0.42 to 2.00, 12/12 under-spec transcripts at ceiling). GPT-4o shows F ≈ 0, D3 = 0.33 uniformly across all scaffolds. Llama-4-Scout shows partial inverted pattern with D2 absent across all 60 transcripts.

Authors & Credibility

The paper comes from the Department of Computer Science at the University of Oxford. Varad Vishwarupe is the corresponding author (varad.vishwarupe@cs.ox.ac.uk). Co-authors Ivan Flechais, Marina Jirotka, and Nigel Shadbolt are established Oxford academics with backgrounds spanning HCI, responsible computing, and AI governance (Shadbolt is notably a co-founder of the Web Science Trust and former President of the British Computer Society).

The empirical work is methodologically careful: two external coders (non-authors) were recruited, blinded to scaffold assignment, and worked from a pre-registered locked rubric with anchored definitions frozen before coding. Inter-rater agreement is reported at multiple granularities. The paper was submitted as a position paper, which is an appropriate framing for the epistemological argument it advances.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The four-level taxonomy and the non-identifiability argument are clearly articulated and useful conceptual contributions. However, the core intuition — that benchmarks do not capture deployed system behaviour — has been widely discussed in the NLP and AI safety communities. The contribution is in formalizing the argument, operationalizing the gap via a rigorous audit, and providing direct empirical evidence via the stress test. The D3-vacuum finding is genuinely striking and not widely acknowledged.

Rigor: The benchmark audit methodology is notably careful: pre-registered rubric, external blinded coders, anchored definitions, reported per-dimension κ, and transparent reconciliation procedures. The ternary scale (0/1/2) is acknowledged to compress nuance conservatively. The stress test statistical analysis (F-tests per model) is appropriate for the design. A significant limitation the authors acknowledge: the rubric derives from HCI-aligned literature (Terry et al., Shen et al.) and may not capture ML-safety-centric dimensions (reward hacking robustness, mechanistic interpretability); a different rubric could produce different findings.

Reproducibility: The coding protocol, rubric, and anchors are included in appendices. The benchmark selections are all publicly available. The stress test protocol is described in sufficient detail. However, the stress test uses three specific frontier models at specific capability levels and the result (especially the Claude vs GPT-4o divergence) may not generalize to future model versions — the authors themselves note this is a snapshot finding.

Significance: The policy implication is real and timely: AI governance frameworks increasingly reference alignment benchmark performance as evidence of deployment safety. The paper provides a formal argument and empirical data showing this inference is not warranted. The proposed alignment profiles and fixed-scaffolding protocol are concrete and actionable.

Red flags: The paper is a position paper, not a standard empirical contribution, and the position is stated forcefully throughout. The rubric-encoding choice that chain-of-thought does not count as D2 (process steerability) or D3 (verification support) is consequential and contested — the authors defend it in Section 5.3 but readers with different intuitions about what "user-facing" means may not be fully persuaded. The stress test sample (N = 180, 60 per model, 15 per scaffold per model) is modest; the F-test results are statistically significant for Claude but the effect size estimates should be treated cautiously given sample size.

Strengths

  • Clearly articulated non-identifiability argument grounded in a formal model B = f(M, S, C)
  • Rigorous pre-registered, blinded, dual-coded audit methodology with strong inter-rater agreement (κ = 0.87 consolidated)
  • The D3-vacuum finding is empirically clean and practically important
  • Cross-model stress test provides direct evidence that the audit gap matters for real models
  • Concrete actionable reform proposals (alignment profiles, fixed-scaffolding protocols, reporting template)
  • Honest engagement with four genuine objections in Section 5, including the scalability objection and the "HCI-biased rubric" concern

Limitations

  • Rubric derived from HCI/interaction literature may miss ML-safety-centric dimensions (reward hacking, inner alignment, mechanistic interpretability); as the authors acknowledge, a different rubric would produce a different but complementary audit
  • Stress test N is modest (15 transcripts per scaffold-model cell); GPT-4o's null result may reflect power limitations rather than true saturation
  • The paper does not propose a fully worked-out replacement evaluation framework, only a template and protocol sketch; operationalizing "deployment context" C at scale remains unsolved
  • The non-identifiability argument, while correct in principle, does not itself tell practitioners what to do when deployment context is heterogeneous — the proposed alignment profiles require substantial additional work to standardize
  • Findings are a snapshot: specific frontier model versions (Claude-Opus-4.7, GPT-4o, Llama-4-Scout) may behave differently in future iterations

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can the fixed-scaffolding protocol proposed in Section 6 be operationalized at a scale comparable to existing response-level benchmarks, and what would the computational and annotation costs be?
  2. The stress test shows Claude's D3 is highly plastic while GPT-4o's is saturated — what model-level properties (training objective, RLHF setup, Constitutional AI constraints) drive this differential scaffold sensitivity?
  3. How should alignment profiles handle the inherent multiplicity of deployment contexts C — is a finite set of canonical contexts feasible, and who should define them?
  4. The rubric deliberately codes chain-of-thought reasoning as neither D2 nor D3 because it is not "user-facing." As models increasingly expose reasoning traces to users, does this boundary need revision?
  5. The paper maps D3 onto scalable oversight and D1 onto specification gaming (Appendix H) — how do the audit findings interact with ongoing work on weak-to-strong generalization and oversight-scalability research?
