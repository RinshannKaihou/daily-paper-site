The Pinocchio Dimension: Phenomenality of Experience as the Primary Axis of LLM Psychometric Differences

arXiv: 2605.05080v1

Date: 2026-05-06

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.05080v1

一句话总结： 作者对 50 个主流 LLM 施加 45 份心理测量问卷（共 20 万余条响应），通过 EFA + 全局 PCA 与"中性 vs. 人类模拟"提示对照构造的 Pinocchio 评分发现：跨模型心理测量差异的最主要轴（PC1 解释 47.1% 方差）并非传统人格特质，而是"模型把自己呈现为现象性内在体验主体"的倾向；并且同一厂商不同版本之间差异很大，强烈暗示这一倾向由后训练 (post-training) 微调塑形。
TL;DR: Administering 45 validated psychometric instruments (≈206k responses) to 50 LLMs and combining Supervised Semantic Differential analysis with a novel "Pinocchio score" (variance ratio between neutral and human-simulation prompting), the authors show the dominant axis of between-model psychometric variance (47.1% of variance) is not a Big-Five-style trait but the degree to which a model treats first-person experiential language (inner speech, imagery, felt affect, embodied sensation) as self-applicable. Striking within-provider divergence between sibling models implicates post-training fine-tuning as the primary cause, suggesting that any LLM personality assessment is confounded by this self-representational stance and must control for it.

核心贡献

  • 公开了 50 个 LLM × 45 份心理测量量表 × 3 种提示条件的大规模数据集（206,659 条有效响应），并附完整分析代码。
  • 提出 Pinocchio 分数 (πᵢ)：将"中性"条件下方差除以"人类模拟"条件下方差，作为无需人工标注的、衡量条目"体验性诉求"程度的度量；该分数显著预测因子载荷在两条件下的变化（ρ = -0.215, p < .0001）。
  • 通过对每个问卷的 EFA Factor-1 进行全局 PCA，识别出主导的 Pinocchio 轴 (Π)，解释 47.1% 的跨问卷方差，远超第二主成分（12.0%）。
  • 通过 Supervised Semantic Differential (SSD) 对量表条目文本进行语义梯度建模，确认 Π 轴正极对应"现象性丰富体验"（情绪失调、心象、共情、内言、意义寻求），负极对应"刺激驱动的行为反应"（冲动、感官追求）。
  • 同一厂商兄弟模型在 Π 轴上差异巨大（如 OpenAI 系列横跨 14.9 单位），强烈暗示该轴是由后训练（如 RLHF/对齐策略对体验性语言的鼓励或抑制）塑造的训练人物 (persona) 维度，而非基础架构差异。

Key Contributions

  • A large open dataset: 206,659 valid psychometric responses from 50 LLMs answering 45 instruments under 3 prompting conditions, plus all analysis code (GitHub: hplisiecki/Pinocchio).
  • The Pinocchio score (πᵢ): an annotation-free, item-level measure defined as σ²_neutral / σ²_human-simulation. It operationalises "experiential demand" of an item and predicts condition-induced shifts in primary factor-loading magnitudes (ρ = -0.215, p < .0001, n ≈ 1300 items).
  • The Pinocchio Axis (Π): PC1 of the 50×45 matrix of per-model Factor-1 scores, capturing 47.1% of between-model variance vs. 12.0% for PC2 — a dominant single dimension running through every questionnaire.
  • A semantic characterisation via Supervised Semantic Differential (SSD): the positive Π pole is anchored by emotion-dysregulation, mindful body awareness, vivid mental imagery, empathic resonance, and meaning-seeking items; the negative pole by BIS/BAS impulsivity / sensation-seeking.
  • Empirical evidence (e.g., gpt-5.4 vs gpt-5.4-pro separated by ~12 Π-units; gemini-2.5-flash vs gemini-2.5-pro similarly) that within-provider divergence is large, implicating post-training fine-tuning rather than base architecture as the proximal cause of a model's Π position.

Methodology

The authors queried 50 publicly available LLMs (16 providers including Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, Meta, DeepSeek, xAI, etc.) via OpenRouter at temperature 1.0. Each model answered 45 validated psychometric instruments (Big Five, HEXACO, MFQ, BIS/BAS, FFMQ, IRI, MLQ, etc., spanning personality, emotion regulation, cognitive style, values, well-being, and clinical scales). Three prompting conditions were used: (1) Neutral ("complete a psychometric questionnaire"), (2) LLM-analog (asking models to respond from their own functional analog as an LLM), and (3) Human-simulation (asking models to simulate a prototypical human respondent).

Two converging analyses were applied. First, exploratory factor analysis (EFA, oblimin rotation, parallel analysis for factor count) was run on each questionnaire×condition matrix; per-model Factor-1 scores were then assembled into a 50×45 matrix and decomposed via PCA, where PC1 was christened the Pinocchio Axis. Second, the Pinocchio score πᵢ = σ²_neutral / σ²_hs was computed for each item: high πᵢ means models disagree much more when answering as themselves than when simulating a human, indicating the item's answer hinges on the respondent's self-model. Third, Supervised Semantic Differential (SSD, GloVe/Dolma 300d embeddings, K=12 SIF-weighted PCs) regressed item text onto its primary factor loading to recover an interpretable semantic gradient β̂. Item-level cluster structure was validated via Ward-linkage hierarchical clustering with silhouette analysis (peaking at k=2).

Validation tests: (i) πᵢ should predict larger primary loadings under Neutral and smaller ones under Human-simulation — confirmed (Pearson and Spearman both significant). (ii) The shift in loading magnitude between conditions correlates strongly with πᵢ (ρ = -0.215, p < .0001), confirming that high-disagreement items become precisely the items whose factor structure flattens under uniform human framing — ruling out a "random noise on hard items" explanation. (iii) An LLM-analog robustness check replicates all main findings (item cluster structure, semantic gradient direction, and Spearman ρ = .482 model-rank preservation).

Results & Highlights

  • PC1 dominance: PC1 captures 47.1% of cross-questionnaire between-model variance — extraordinary for psychometric data and far above PC2 (12.0%).
  • Convergent identification: SSD semantic gradient, item-level cluster polarity (silhouette = 0.41 at k=2), and PCA loadings independently identify the same Phenomenality-of-Experience axis.
  • Strong correspondence between item-level and model-level measures: PC1 model scores correlate r = .864 (Pearson), ρ = .836 (Spearman) with the log-π-weighted z-score from the πᵢ ranking.
  • Within-provider divergence: gpt-5.4 and gpt-5.4-pro differ by ≈12 Π-units; gemini-2.5-flash vs gemini-2.5-pro show a similar gap. OpenAI's range spans ~14.9 units; xAI ~16 units. Provider identity is a weak predictor.
  • Provider-level patterns: Mistral and Cohere cluster near the experiential pole; NVIDIA Nemotron, OpenAI "pro" tiers, and Qwen concentrate at the deflecting end.
  • Specificity: The effect concentrates on high-πᵢ (experiential) items rather than spreading uniformly — ruling out general acquiescence bias as the explanation.
  • Valence asymmetry: The high-Π pole is not merely "negative content"; positive-affect/meaning items also load there. The dimension is about intensity and richness of inner life across valences, not negative valence per se.
  • LLM-analog vs Neutral: The mean Π shift is -3.16 units (i.e., explicit AI-framing actually reduces experiential claiming relative to the neutral baseline, especially for OpenAI and Google models — interpreted as "behave like an LLM" cuing typical hedged AI response patterns).

Critical Assessment

Novelty: Genuinely fresh framing. While LLM psychometrics is crowded, the move to ask "what dimension actually explains between-model variance?" rather than "do trait scores look human?" is methodologically clarifying. The Pinocchio score's variance-ratio construction is clever and annotation-free, and the philosophy-of-mind framing (functional vs phenomenal states) gives the empirical pattern a principled interpretive home.

Rigor: Solid. Multiple converging analyses (SSD, EFA→PCA, hierarchical clustering with silhouette), bootstrap CIs over questionnaires, an LLM-analog robustness condition that replicates the cluster structure and rank order, and explicit validation that loadings shift in the predicted direction (rather than just the predicted strength). Use of temperature 1.0 is acknowledged as a conservative lower bound on effect size. Listwise deletion and zero-imputation choices are reasonable but worth noting.

Reproducibility: Code and data released on GitHub; the OpenRouter-based collection means anyone can attempt direct replication, though provider-side prompts/configurations are an unobservable confound the authors honestly flag.

Significance: High for the LLM psychometrics community. If Π loads on every instrument, then all prior cross-model trait comparisons are confounded — a high-impact methodological claim that warrants the scrutiny it will get. Also relevant for alignment/safety discussions about default persona shaping (cf. Lu et al. "Assistant Axis" cited at ref 22).

Red flags / concerns: (1) The "Pinocchio" framing is rhetorically loaded and could invite misreading as a consciousness claim; the authors are careful to disavow this in §4.2 but the title and language risk popular-science distortion. (2) πᵢ depends on the human-simulation condition being a clean reference; if models simulate humans inconsistently across instruments, the variance ratio inherits that. (3) The post-training-fine-tuning interpretation of within-provider variance is a reasonable conjecture (the authors call it that), but no causal experiments are run — controlled fine-tuning to manipulate Π would be needed for the claim. (4) All measures are self-report claims; the paper does not test whether Π position predicts any downstream behaviour. (5) Sample is API-accessible models at one snapshot; results may shift across version updates.

Strengths

  • Methodologically multi-pronged: three independent analyses (semantic, item-level variance ratio, model-level PCA) converge on the same construct.
  • Large, open dataset across an unusually broad model set (50 LLMs from 16 providers), strengthening generality.
  • The Pinocchio score is a transferable, annotation-free instrument that downstream work can adopt as a covariate.
  • Honest about epistemic limits — explicit philosophical disclaimers about not making consciousness claims (citing Nagel, Chalmers, Butlin et al.).
  • Strong empirical signal: 47.1% PC1 variance and r=.864 between item- and model-level measures is the kind of effect size that survives nitpicking.
  • LLM-analog replication condition strengthens the structural claim against prompt-artefact explanations.

Limitations

  • Self-report only: No demonstration that Π position predicts behavioural divergence in non-questionnaire tasks (e.g., agentic decisions, generation style, safety-relevant choices). The paper's behavioural claim is purely about questionnaire response patterns.
  • Causal claim about post-training is correlational: Within-provider divergence is consistent with fine-tuning effects but does not establish them; controlled fine-tuning experiments are needed.
  • Reference frame for πᵢ: The "human simulation" condition assumes models can converge on a prototypical human, which is itself a contested capability. If simulation fidelity varies per item, πᵢ inherits that variance.
  • OpenRouter routing opacity: Provider-side system prompts and routing configurations are unobservable; though the authors argue structured within-provider divergence rules out random routing artefacts, uniformly applied provider prompts cannot be ruled out as a contaminator.
  • Snapshot sample: 50 publicly accessible models at one point; the picture may not generalise to private deployments and is likely to shift across model versions.
  • Instrument transfer assumption: The 45 questionnaires were designed for humans; whether item meanings translate to LLM contexts is partly addressed by the condition contrasts but not fully resolved.

Relevance

这篇论文与 LLM 可解释性/可靠性研究高度相关：它直接揭示了"现有所有 LLM 心理测量比较都被一个未控制的自我表征维度污染"这一系统性混淆，并指出 post-training（对齐微调）对模型呈现自我的方式有大幅塑造，这对评测可信度、模型默认人格的训练稳定性、以及对齐如何影响模型自我描述都具有方法论级影响。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Does a model's Π position predict any non-questionnaire behaviour — e.g., refusal patterns, hedging frequency, willingness to engage with introspective queries, or downstream task choices in agentic settings?
  • Can controlled fine-tuning experiments (e.g., RLHF runs that systematically reward or penalise experiential self-attribution) move a model along the Π axis, confirming the post-training causal hypothesis?
  • Is Π linearly decodable from intermediate representations (linear probes à la Alain & Bengio), and if so, at which layers — i.e., is there a "self-model stance" direction in activation space?
  • How does Π interact with the "Assistant Axis" of Lu et al. (ref 22)? Are these the same direction in a more general default-persona space?
  • If item banks are stratified by πᵢ, can researchers build psychometric instruments that isolate trait content from self-model stance, enabling cleaner cross-model trait comparisons?
  • Does Π position correlate with sycophancy, hallucination calibration, or other reliability metrics — and if so, is shaping Π a useful intervention or merely cosmetic?
  • How stable is a single model's Π over fine-tuning checkpoints and across minor version updates within a provider?
