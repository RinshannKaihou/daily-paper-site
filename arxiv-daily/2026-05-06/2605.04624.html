AuditRepairBench: A Paired-Execution Trace Corpus for Evaluator-Channel Ranking Instability in Agent Repair

arXiv: 2605.04624v1

Date: 2026-05-06

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.04624v1

一句话总结： 本文揭示了Agent代码修复排行榜在评估器重配置下发生系统性重排的失效模式，提出AuditRepairBench——一个包含96,000条配对执行轨迹的语料库，通过四种模块化筛查实现（学习影响代理、规则曝光比率、反事实敏感性代理、稀疏人工审计代理）识别评估器-选择器耦合通道，并证明筛查引导的定向盲化修补可将排名位移减少55–74%。
TL;DR: AuditRepairBench is a paired-execution trace corpus that operationalizes a concrete failure mode in agent-repair leaderboards: some agent systems internally consult evaluator-derived signals (rationale tokens, verdict scalars, reranker logits, memory echoes) during candidate selection, causing leaderboard rankings to shift dramatically when the evaluator is reconfigured. The paper provides a modular screening architecture to detect this evaluator-selector coupling, a 96,000-cell benchmark corpus, and demonstrates that targeted blinding patches guided by the screener reduce rank displacement by 55–74% at under 50 lines of code.

核心贡献

  • 发现并量化排行榜失效模式：在公共SWE-bench风格排行榜上记录了可量化的失效现象——当评估器重配置时，60个Agent修复系统中有7个发生前三名互换，Kendall τ 仅0.74，且4个互换可追溯至评估器-选择器耦合通道（理由token、verdict标量、重排logit、延迟记忆回放）。
  • 构建大规模配对执行轨迹语料库：发布AuditRepairBench，包含576,000个注册单元格（96,000个已执行的配对轨迹），覆盖60个Agent系统、8个任务族、6个评估器族，通过选择器输入盲化干预系统性量化耦合效应。
  • 模块化筛查架构：设计四种可互换实现——学习影响代理（2层MLP）、规则化通道曝光比率（无需训练模型）、反事实敏感性代理（KL散度+argmax翻转率）和稀疏人工审计代理，通过筛查后验组合输出分层排行榜。
  • 机制锚定验证与独立发现协议：在80个源码级通道手术案例上验证（路径阻断AUROC 0.96），两组盲化独立标注群体发现新耦合类型（记忆-奖励回声、候选池priming、verdict熵泄漏），冻结集成AUROC 0.83。
  • 下游可操作性证明：筛查引导的定向盲化修补将排名位移减少55–74%（均值62%），代码量不超过50行；优于随机通道盲化（最多7%）和泛化重训练（最多13%）。
  • 低成本入口AuditRepairBench-Lite：12,000单元格、纯规则配置，在24 GPU小时内保持Kendall τ = 0.88，主要发布制品42 GB。

Key Contributions

  • Documented leaderboard failure mode at scale: Seven of sixty agent-repair systems in a real leaderboard swap top-three positions under evaluator reconfiguration (Kendall τ = 0.74); four swaps are attributable to specific evaluator-to-selector coupling channels (rationale tokens, verdict scalars, reranker logits, delayed memory).
  • Paired-execution trace corpus: AuditRepairBench comprises 576,000 registered trace cells (96,000 executed) spanning 60 systems, 8 task families, 6 evaluator families, 4 seeds, and 5 intervention families — the first resource specifically targeting selector-evaluator coupling rather than generic evaluator noise.
  • Modular screening architecture: Four interchangeable screening implementations (learned influence proxy, rule-based channel-exposure ratio, counterfactual sensitivity proxy, sparse human-audit proxy) are stacked into a calibrated posterior; the rule-only implementation requires no trained model.
  • Mechanism-anchored and independent-discovery validation: 80 source-level channel-surgery cases with ground-truth path-block labels (AUROC 0.96 ensemble); two blinded annotator groups separated by organizational boundary independently discover three new coupling types with pooled AUROC 0.83.
  • Actionable downstream repair: Screening-guided channel-blinding patches cut rank displacement 55–74% (mean 62%) at under 50 lines of code; unguided alternatives (random blinding, generic retraining) achieve at most 7–13%.
  • AuditRepairBench-Lite: Rule-only 12,000-cell subset runnable in under 24 GPU-hours preserving Kendall τ = 0.88 against the full benchmark, released openly at 42 GB.

Methodology

Problem Formulation. The paper formalizes a paired execution as a tuple x = (m, t, e, s) — system m, task t, evaluator configuration e, seed s. The core operational target is a cell-level flip functional q̂(x): the posterior probability that blinding the evaluator-to-selector information path changes the winner among candidate repairs. The flip functional is a posterior expectation over a screened intervention family rather than a deterministic ratio, using pre-registered thresholds τ+ = 0.9, τ− = 0.1 to assign set-valued labels L(x) ∈ {{1}, {0}, {0,1}}.

Paired-Trace Intervention. For each cell, an observed trace is collected with the full evaluator output visible to the selector. A counterfactual trace is then generated by zeroing (blinding) only the evaluator-to-selector input path while keeping the task, candidate set, tool outputs, and final evaluator fixed. The winner difference between observed and counterfactual traces operationalizes the coupling effect.

Four Screening Implementations:

  1. Learned influence proxy: A two-layer MLP over a 128-dimensional selector-state embedding encoding attention weights on evaluator-derived tokens, activation magnitudes, and selector-logit change under embedding-level masking.
  2. Rule-based channel-exposure ratio: Static and dynamic program analysis of whether evaluator-derived tokens, spans, or features enter the selector input window — no trained model required.
  3. Counterfactual sensitivity proxy: Local input-masking effects on selector output distribution via symmetric KL divergence and argmax-flip rate.
  4. Sparse human-audit proxy: A size-280 manually audited subset whose labels are propagated to unreached cells through a gradient-boosted decision tree classifier trained on a 128-dimensional feature vector combining the other three scores plus six auxiliary signals.

Calibrated Stacking. The four scores plus two meta-features (hook completeness, evaluator-family indicator) are combined via calibrated logistic stacking fitted on a calibration split to produce the screening posterior. A strict leaderboard variant withholds meta-features (Kendall τ = 0.97 vs. main).

Corpus Composition. The design space is 60 systems × 8 task families × 6 evaluator families × 4 seeds × 5 interventions = 576,000 registered cells. Systems span five method families: plan-execute loops, shell-tool agents, iterative refinement agents, autonomous code exploration agents, and multi-agent debate systems. Evaluators include 3 proprietary LLM judges, 2 open-weight LLM judges, and 1 rule-based evaluator under multiple aggregation functions.

Validation Protocol. The 80-case source-level channel-surgery subset covers open-source families (SWE-agent, OpenHands, Aider) where full source-level surgery is feasible. Cases are partitioned into True-Block, Sham/Off-target, and Mixed types, ground-truth verified by 180 double-audit judgments from a separated internal group. An independent-discovery protocol uses two annotator groups separated by organizational boundary and independent data-use agreements, discovering coupling patterns blinded to the screening design.

Results & Highlights

  • Leaderboard instability measurement: On a 60-system public pool, evaluator reconfiguration from pass-rate to test-case-bagged evaluator produces Kendall τ = 0.74; rank displacement median 3.1 positions; 4 of 7 top-three swaps traceable to evaluator-selector coupling.
  • Path-block detection: Ensemble AUROC 0.96 on 80 source-level surgery cases; individual implementations range 0.89–0.95. Rule-based exposure ratio achieves AUROC 0.91 without any trained model.
  • Independent discovery transfer: Frozen ensemble achieves pooled AUROC 0.83 on 79 cases from two blinded annotator groups; three new coupling types detected without retraining (memory-reward echoing, candidate-pool priming, verdict-entropy leakage).
  • Uncertainty propagation: The full protocol raises 95% coverage from 0.81 (back-end bootstrap only) to 0.95 while preserving winner stability.
  • Forward transfer: Pooled community-evaluator Spearman ρ = 0.65 across three external regime shifts; improves +0.05 over strongest non-screening baseline (evaluator-reconfiguration instability predictor, ρ = 0.64).
  • Downstream actionability: Six screening-guided patches on four open-source agent codebases reduce rank displacement 55–74% (mean 62%, 18–41 lines of code), with mean accuracy change within one percentage point and at most one regression per system. Baseline alternatives — random channel blinding (≤7%), human-expert patching (42% at 3× LoC cost), universal channel blinding (44% but mean −5.1% pass-rate change on 8/12 systems) — all underperform.
  • AuditRepairBench-Lite cost-fidelity: 12,000 cells, rule-only, 22 GPU-hours, 42 GB storage; Kendall τ = 0.88 vs. full benchmark; recovers 8 of 10 highest-displacement systems identified by the full benchmark.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The paper targets a specific and underexplored failure mode — evaluator-to-selector information leakage in agentic repair systems — that is genuinely distinct from generic LLM-as-judge unreliability or benchmark overfitting. The paired-trace intervention design is a meaningful methodological contribution. However, the failure mode is architectural and somewhat narrow in scope: it requires selector internals to be observable, which the authors acknowledge excludes closed-API and GUI-heavy systems. The modular screening architecture, while technically sound, adds engineering complexity without being conceptually transformative.

Rigor: Strong. The paper pre-registers thresholds (τ+ = 0.9, τ− = 0.1), uses bootstrap 95% CIs throughout, employs a strict observability boundary (hook completeness ≥ 0.80), runs independent-discovery validation with organizational separation, and provides a governance protocol with pre-registered failure thresholds. The strict leaderboard variant that withholds meta-features (Kendall τ = 0.97 vs. 1.00) honestly addresses potential meta-feature leakage concerns. The paper is transparent about dominant failure modes (undercalibration on delayed-memory coupling, overconfidence on candidate-pool priming).

Reproducibility: Moderate-to-good. AuditRepairBench-Lite (12,000 cells, 42 GB, rule-only) is released openly; full traces are under controlled access requiring user credentials and cannot be redistributed. The rule-based implementation requires no trained model and is runnable in 24 GPU-hours. The pipeline code is described in detail. The dependency on proprietary LLM judge outputs introduces external irreproducibility that the rule-only Lite partially addresses.

Significance: High for the agent evaluation and benchmark integrity community. The paper demonstrates with quantitative evidence that a non-trivial share of public leaderboard reorderings in SWE-bench-style evaluations is attributable to architectural coupling rather than genuine performance differences. The actionability gap (targeted 55–74% reduction vs. baseline 7–44%) is a concrete practical result. Whether this generalizes beyond the three open-source agent families studied remains unclear.

Red flags: (1) The 80-case surgery subset covers only three open-source agent families (SWE-agent, OpenHands, Aider); five of seven top-three-swapping systems on the motivating public leaderboard have no source-level surgery cases. (2) Forward transfer Spearman ρ = 0.65 is described as "weak-to-moderate" by the authors themselves — prospective validity for yet-unseen agents is unproven. (3) The independent-discovery annotator groups were selected by the maintainers, introducing potential selection bias despite the organizational separation. (4) Funding is anonymous, which limits assessment of potential conflicts of interest. (5) The paper explicitly disclaims certifying mechanisms or recovering latent causal constructs — appropriate honesty, but it limits interpretive strength.

Strengths

  • Addresses a real, quantified, and previously unoperationalized failure mode in agent-repair leaderboards with concrete evidence from a public leaderboard.
  • Modular design means benchmark identity does not bind to any single learned model; rule-only Lite removes all trained-model dependencies.
  • Careful experimental design: pre-registered thresholds, organizational separation for independent discovery, bootstrap CIs, stated scope and limitations.
  • Actionable: screening-guided blinding patches are demonstrated to reduce rank displacement 55–74% at minimal code cost with negligible accuracy regression.
  • Honest about limitations including weak-to-moderate forward transfer and scope restriction to observable-selector systems.

Limitations

  • Observability boundary restriction: requires hook completeness ≥ 0.80 (static AST + dynamic taint coverage at selector input boundary), excluding closed-API agents and GUI-heavy systems — increasingly common as commercial agents shift to API-only interfaces.
  • Source-level surgery covers only three open-source agent families; 40 behavior-only fallback systems lack ground-truth path-block labels.
  • Forward transfer is weak-to-moderate (ρ = 0.65) rather than prospective validity; the instrument is not claimed to generalize to arbitrary future agent designs.
  • Human audit layer (1,340 expert-assisted labels) is used as an audit layer rather than ground truth, leaving the screening posterior ultimately dependent on the posterior model's calibration.
  • Controlled access for full executed traces creates a two-tier accessibility structure that may slow community adoption of the full resource.
  • Funding anonymization and maintainer selection of independent annotators are minor but non-trivial governance concerns.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does evaluator-selector coupling interact with self-consistency or majority-vote based candidate selection — do aggregation methods that do not explicitly read evaluator logits still exhibit coupling through implicit statistical regularities?
  2. Can the rule-based channel-exposure ratio (AUROC 0.91) be extended to partial observability settings where only API-level inputs/outputs are accessible, without source-level instrumentation?
  3. The three new coupling types discovered (memory-reward echoing, candidate-pool priming, verdict-entropy leakage) — are these artifacts of specific architectural choices in SWE-agent/OpenHands/Aider, or are they general patterns that will appear in closed-source commercial agents?
  4. The blinding patches reduce rank displacement 55–74% but the remaining 26–45% displacement — what is its composition? How much is due to genuine performance differences vs. residual coupling not detected by the screener?
  5. As agent systems increasingly use LLM-based planners that may internally call evaluators during chain-of-thought, will the hook-completeness boundary remain sufficient to separate evaluator signal from selector logic?
