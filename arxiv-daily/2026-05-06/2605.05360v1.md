> **一句话总结：** 本文提出 CopyCop，一种基于"嵌入函数稳定点（stationary points）在变换下保持不变"这一不变性的 GNN 指纹方法，能够在攻击者使用不同架构、嵌入维度，并对输出做旋转/缩放/升维/非线性变换的情况下，仍以高 AUC（14 个数据集 × 5 种 GNN 架构上 73% 组合 AUC=1.0）识别出代理模型。

> **TL;DR:** CopyCop fingerprints embedding-as-a-service GNNs by exploiting the fact that *stationary points* of the victim's embedding function (graph/feature/direction tuples where the directional derivative vanishes) remain stationary for any locally-invertible transformation an adversary applies. The authors prove the test is correct under bounded-Hessian / Lipschitz assumptions, then show it dominates the watermarking method PreGIP and the fingerprint GroVe across 14 datasets, 5 GNN architectures, and a battery of attacks (model extraction, pruning up to 40%, fine-tuning, rotation, scaling, up-projection, tanh/sigmoid/exp). Whether the assumption that "stationary points are idiosyncratic to a model" is empirically robust beyond the specific GNN families tested is the main open question.

## 核心贡献

- 提出**首个**对 GNN 嵌入有效、且在跨架构、跨嵌入维度、跨输出变换条件下都能工作的指纹方法 CopyCop（不需要在受害模型训练时插入水印）。
- 关键洞察：**嵌入函数的稳定点（节点特征沿方向 w 微扰时方向导数为零的点）在任何局部可逆变换 φ 下保持稳定**——证明 ∇h'(X)=0 当且仅当 ∇h(X)=0。
- 给出可执行的两阶段算法（精确情形 Algorithm 1 + 含重构误差 ε 的一般情形 Algorithm 2），并通过求解一个带正则项的优化问题（Eq. 7）来采样稳定点；阈值 θ 可由独立模型分布上界得到。
- 给出形式化保证：定理 3.8 / 3.15 在 Assumption 3.1（嵌入有界、二阶可微）、3.4（局部可逆）、3.7（稳定点的特异性）、3.11（φ 的双 Lipschitz 性）下证明检测以高概率正确。
- 在 14 个数据集 × 5 种 GNN（GCN、GIN、GraphSAGE、ARMA、MixHop）上验证：模型抽取攻击下 73% 组合 AUC=1.0，平均 AUC 在所有架构上比 PreGIP 高 17%–48%；对剪枝（最高 40%）、微调（100 epoch 仍稳定）、以及 19 种嵌入变换均稳健。

## Key Contributions

- Introduces CopyCop, the first GNN fingerprinting method that is simultaneously architecture-agnostic, embedding-dimension-agnostic, transformation-robust, and watermark-free, addressing the gap left by PreGIP (watermark, broken by extraction) and GroVe (fingerprint, broken by dimension changes).
- Identifies *stationary points* of the embedding function — tuples (G, X, i, w) where the directional derivative ∇_w h_i(G,X) = 0 — as a transformation-invariant fingerprint, and proves the invariance under any locally-invertible φ (Lemma 3.6).
- Provides Algorithm 1 (perfect-reconstruction case) and Algorithm 2 (general ε-reconstruction case) plus a sampling routine (Eq. 7) that finds stationary points via regularized black-box optimization on node features over k-hop subgraphs.
- Proves correctness under four explicit assumptions: bounded twice-differentiable embeddings, local invertibility of φ, idiosyncratic stationary points (γ_M > 0 fraction unique to victim), and bi-Lipschitz φ (Theorems 3.8, 3.15; Theorem A.3 invariance under rotation/scaling/up-projection).
- Empirical study spanning 14 datasets, 5 GNN architectures (GCN, GIN, GraphSAGE, ARMA, MixHop), 19 embedding transformations, plus pruning and fine-tuning attacks; CopyCop outperforms PreGIP and GroVe everywhere, needing only 20–40 stationary points.

## Methodology

The setup is the embeddings-as-a-service threat model: a victim GNN M produces d-dimensional node embeddings; an adversary queries M, trains a surrogate M' whose embeddings ĥ approximate h up to ε in sup-norm, and then composes a transformation φ: R^d → R^{d'} (rotation, scaling, projection to higher dim, nonlinearity, etc.). The defender sees only outputs of an unknown candidate Z and must decide surrogate vs. independent without internal access.

Core construction. A *query tuple* is (G, X, i, w) with ‖w‖=1; the directional derivative ∇_w h_i = lim τ→0 (h_i(G,X+τw e_i) − h_i(G,X))/τ. The *stationary set* S(F) collects tuples where this derivative vanishes. Lemma 3.6 shows that for any locally-invertible φ (Assumption 3.4), ∇_w h'_i = J_φ · ∇_w h_i, and J_φ has trivial null-space, so S(M') = S(M) exactly when ε = 0.

In the realistic ε > 0 case, the authors define a normalized score q_F(t) = ‖h_i^{(δw)} − h_i‖ / ‖h_i‖ and a ratio β_F = E_{t∼μ_M}[q_F]/E_{t∼D_exp}[q_F]. Lemma 3.10 shows β_M = O(δ); Lemma 3.12 shows β_{M'} = O((C/c)^2 max(ε/δ, δ)) under bi-Lipschitz φ; Lemma 3.14 shows β_I ≥ γ_M + O(δ) for an independent model I. So thresholding β_Z separates surrogates from independents.

Sampling stationary points. Since direct comparison of S(M) and S(Z) is infeasible, they solve the regularized objective X = argmin_X ‖∇_w h_i(G,X;M)‖/‖h_i‖ + λ‖X − X_0‖_F/‖X_0‖_F via a gradient-free solver (Nevergrad), starting from realistic graphs X_0 sampled from the data distribution. For integer features they fix δ=1 and use axis-aligned w. Optimization runs only over the k-hop neighborhood of node i, keeping cost roughly independent of full graph size.

Test procedure (Algorithm 2). Sample ℓ ∈ {20–40} stationary points T from M and ℓ random query points R; compute β̂_Z as the mean percentile of q_Z(T) within the empirical distribution q_Z(R) (Remark A.1, Eq. 8); return Surrogate iff β̂_Z ≤ θ, where θ = γ_M/2 estimated from independently-trained reference GNNs.

## Results & Highlights

- Model extraction attack (Table 1, AUC distance-based): averaged across 14 datasets, CopyCop reaches AUC 1.00 (GCN), 0.96 (GIN), 0.91 (GSage), 0.89 (ARMA), 0.99 (MixHop); PreGIP reaches 0.85, 0.65, 0.77, 0.84, 0.81. Cosine version (Table 3) shows PreGIP collapsing to 0.25 average for GIN, with several entries at 0.00. CopyCop achieves AUC=1 on 73% of (dataset, architecture) cells and AUC>0.9 on 83%.
- Embedding transformations (Table 2, fraction of independents misclassified, lower is better): CopyCop 0–22% across 19 transformations including tanh⁻¹, sigmoid, exp, h^2, h^3, translations, normalizations; average 4%/1%/7%/10%/6% across the five architectures. PreGIP averages 5–13% with several 70%+ failures (e.g. h→exp(h) on GIN: 72%). GroVe is N/A whenever the surrogate dimension changes (counted as 100% failure) and averages 15–26%.
- Pruning attack (Fig. 2a): CopyCop AUC remains ≈1 up to 40% prune ratio; for GIN it remains stable up to 70% pruning. Fine-tuning attack (Fig. 2b): AUC essentially flat over 100 epochs while downstream accuracy saturates after 20–40 epochs (Fig. 4).
- Sample efficiency: 20–40 stationary points suffice for near-maximum AUC (Fig. 3a). Detection is largely insensitive to regularizer λ except for ARMA where λ=0.01 is preferred (Fig. 3b). Independent-model β̂_Z scores cluster around the expected 50th percentile (Fig. 6), supporting Assumption 3.13.
- Compute: a single workstation (Intel i9-10980XE, 256 GB RAM, 2× RTX 3090) runs all 14 × 5 × multi-seed experiments; per-stationary-point wall-clock grows with embedding dimension, longest for ARMA (Fig. 3c).

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (moderate–high):** The "stationary point" idea is a clean and, to my knowledge, new fingerprint primitive for GNNs. Conceptually it is a black-box analog of feature-attribution invariance, but the authors are the first to make it operational with a sampling procedure and prove invariance under locally-invertible transformations. The general framing (β_F ratio between sampled stationary points and random queries) is itself a useful template.
- **Rigor (mostly good, with gaps):** The proof chain (Lemmas 3.6, 3.10, 3.12, 3.14, Theorem 3.15) is internally consistent and assumptions are stated explicitly. However Assumption 3.7 (γ_M > 0 fraction of stationary points are idiosyncratic to M) is the keystone and is justified only empirically (Fig. 6) and over the same 5 GNN families used in evaluation — there is no theoretical lower bound on γ_M as a function of architecture, depth, or training. Assumption 3.13 (idiosyncratic points are not special for an independent I) is similarly an empirical claim.
- **Reproducibility (good):** Code is shared (anonymized), 14 public datasets (PyG), 5 standard architectures with stated configurations (Table 4), seeds fixed. The black-box solver Nevergrad is named. Threshold-setting procedure is documented.
- **Significance (real but niche):** EaaS for GNN embeddings is a less mainstream deployment than LLM APIs, so the practical surface is narrower than the framing suggests. Within that niche, the result is genuinely useful and the gap over PreGIP/GroVe is substantial.
- **Red flags / caveats:**
  1. The "transformation" attack model assumes the adversary applies a single fixed φ. An adaptive adversary aware of CopyCop could *deliberately* train M' to perturb embeddings near M's stationary points (e.g. add a tiny non-stationary residual along sampled w directions). The paper's "robustness against countermeasures" paragraph acknowledges this only via additive output noise and does not evaluate a CopyCop-aware adversary.
  2. The "independent model" baseline is *the same architecture/data trained with a different seed*. This understates the difficulty of distinguishing surrogates: in practice independent providers may use different data or different training recipes, which would change the γ_M calibration.
  3. Some transformation rows show non-trivial failure (GSage 21% on h→h^3, ARMA 26% on translations) — the average hides architecture-specific weaknesses.
  4. Pruning is evaluated only up to 70% on GIN; fine-tuning is mostly cosmetic (the surrogate's downstream accuracy changes little). Distillation with a much smaller student is not explicitly studied.

## Strengths

- A genuinely transformation-invariant fingerprint primitive backed by a clean proof, not heuristics.
- Architecture- and dimension-agnostic — solves the central limitation of GroVe.
- No model modification: nothing to integrate at training time, unlike watermarking schemes.
- Strong, broad empirical evaluation: 14 datasets × 5 architectures × 19 transformations × pruning × fine-tuning.
- Practical sample complexity (20–40 stationary points), feasible on a 2-GPU workstation.
- Releases code; clear algorithm pseudocode and stated hyperparameters.

## Limitations

- Assumption 3.7 (idiosyncratic stationary points) is empirically motivated, not architecturally proven; γ_M could be small or zero for some GNN families not tested.
- No evaluation against an adaptive adversary who knows CopyCop is the verifier (e.g. trains to randomize stationary structure).
- Fingerprint requires graph queries against the candidate model; deployments that rate-limit or only expose downstream classification heads would block detection.
- Solver step (Eq. 7) uses a black-box optimizer (Nevergrad); convergence quality and sensitivity to initialization X_0 are not characterized — only that resulting points have near-zero pairwise cosine (Fig. 5).
- Independent baseline is same-architecture-same-data, which is the easiest case for separation.
- Embedding dimension dominates per-stationary-point runtime; scalability to large modern GNN embeddings (>>32 dims) is not benchmarked.
- Method targets node-embedding GNNs only; graph-classification heads or unsupervised pretraining heads (e.g. contrastive) are not directly addressed beyond the GlobalMeanPooling adaptation.

## Relevance

与用户关注的"训练稳定性 / 模型可信度 / 可解释性"主题相关性中等：本文提供的稳定点不变性给出了一种"模型身份"的几何刻画，可视为对模型函数局部结构的可解释性探针；但其核心定位是 GNN 知识产权保护，与 LLM 训练动力学/可靠性的直接迁移路径较窄，主要的可借鉴点在于"用模型函数的零阶不变量做指纹"的方法论。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does CopyCop perform against an *adaptive* adversary who explicitly trains the surrogate to randomize directional derivatives along query directions sampled near M's stationary set?
- What is the failure mode when the surrogate is trained on a *different* dataset (only outputs leaked) or via knowledge distillation with a much smaller student? Does γ_M still hold?
- Can the stationary-point primitive extend to non-GNN embeddings — e.g. transformer-encoder outputs for text retrieval models, where input "directions" are token embeddings rather than continuous features?
- Is there a theoretical lower bound on γ_M as a function of GNN depth, width, and training loss landscape, or is the assumption fundamentally model-dependent?
- How sensitive is the detection to the choice of black-box solver? Would a gradient-based stationary-point search via the victim's API queries (numerical differencing) recover the same fingerprint set?
- For graph-level tasks with pooling, does the stationary-point invariance still hold after the sum/mean aggregation, and does it require re-deriving Lemma 3.6 for the post-pooling map?
