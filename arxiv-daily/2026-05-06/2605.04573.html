Mixed Finite Elements for Geometrically Exact Beams using Discontinuous Rotations and Discrete Curvature

arXiv: 2605.04573v1

Date: 2026-05-06

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.04573v1

一句话总结： 本文提出一种针对几何精确（Simo–Reissner）梁的新型混合有限元格式，将弯矩向量作为独立场，通过离散曲率概念允许对转动场进行单元局部、不连续的近似，并构造出一个最低阶（k=1）即完全无锁、无需任何转动插值的总拉格朗日梁单元，同时保持客观性与路径无关性。
TL;DR: The authors derive a mixed finite-element formulation for geometrically exact (Simo–Reissner) beams that introduces the moment vector as an independent field, exploits a notion of "discrete curvature" to allow element-local, discontinuous rotation approximations, and yields a lowest-order element (k=1) that is locking-free without interpolating rotations at all, while preserving objectivity and path independence in a total-Lagrangian setting.

核心贡献

  • 提出一种新的几何精确梁混合变分格式：仅引入弯矩向量 M 作为附加独立场（保留位移和转动的原始形式），通过对曲率相关应变能做 Legendre 变换得到混合形式。
  • 引入"离散曲率"概念，使转动场可以采用单元局部、不连续的多项式近似；这将单元间连续性的负担从转动向量转移到弯矩向量，并自然处理带有折点 (kinks) 和分支的结构。
  • 通过节点增量转动场进行混合化（hybridization），使得 Dirichlet 型转动边界条件（如夹紧端 ψ^(V)=0）可以以经典强形式直接施加，避免弱形式施加的实施困难。
  • 通过相对转动的乘法分解为单元常数部分加零均值高阶部分，在离散层面恢复客观性；总拉格朗日设定保证路径无关性 a priori。
  • 给出最低阶单元 (k=1)：仅采用单元常数转动，完全规避转动插值，不存在任何锁定现象（与梁细长比无关）；高阶单元 (k≥2) 通过对力应变能的减缩积分消除轴向锁定与剪切锁定，达到 O(h^(k+1)) 最优收敛阶。

Key Contributions

  • A new mixed variational formulation for geometrically exact beams that introduces only the moment vector as an additional independent field, derived via Legendre transform of the curvature-related strain energy.
  • Introduction of "discrete curvature" that enables element-local, discontinuous interpolation of rotations; the necessity of inter-element continuity is shifted from the rotation vector to the moment vector.
  • A hybridization strategy via single-valued nodal incremental rotations ψ^(V), which allows classical Dirichlet boundary conditions on rotations (clamped supports) to be imposed strongly rather than weakly, and naturally handles T-joints / branching points by enforcing nodal moment balance.
  • Multiplicative split of element-local rotations into element-constant (lowest-order) and zero-mean higher-order parts, ensuring objectivity of discretized strain measures at the discrete level; total Lagrangian setting ensures path independence a priori.
  • A lowest-order element (k=1) that uses element-wise constant rotations only — no rotation interpolation at all — and is provably locking-free regardless of slenderness; reduced integration on the force-strain energy term restores optimal O(h^(k+1)) convergence for higher-order elements.
  • Implementation in Netgen/NGSolve with a public GitLab repository.

Methodology

The work starts from standard Simo–Reissner kinematics: a Cosserat line described by the centerline position r(s) and a cross-section orientation Λ(s) ∈ SO(3), parameterized by the rotation vector ψ via Rodrigues' formula. The strain measures are the material force strain γ = Λ^T·r' − e1 and the material moment (curvature) strain κ = Λ^T·Λ' − Λ_0^T·Λ_0'.

The mixed formulation is obtained by introducing the moment vector M as an independent unknown alongside displacements and rotations, and replacing the curvature-related strain energy density φ_κ by its Legendre transform φ̄_κ = −½ M·C_κ^(−1)·M + κ·M (for linear constitutive laws). The variational form admits independent variations of u, ψ, and M.

A central insight is the notion of discrete curvature: when the rotation field is allowed to be discontinuous at an interface s_1, the limiting work integral ∫κ·M ds reduces to the inner product (ψ_12 − ψ_(0,12))·M|_(s_1), where ψ_12 is the relative rotation between adjacent sections. Locally, moments are dual to the change in relative rotation across the interface; this concept extends naturally to piecewise smooth fields and is borrowed from mixed methods for Kirchhoff–Love shells (Neunteufel & Schöberl).

The finite element discretization uses four unknown fields per element:

  • continuous polynomial displacements of order k,
  • discontinuous, element-local moments M of order k,
  • discontinuous, element-local rotation vectors ψ^(E) of order k−1 (so for k=1 the element rotation is constant),
  • single-valued nodal incremental rotations ψ_i^(V) (no interpolation in the interior; act as Lagrange multipliers enforcing weak continuity of the moment vector).

Element-local rotations are multiplicatively split Λ^(E) = Λ_lo^(E) · Λ_ho^(E) into a constant part and a higher-order part with zero-mean Legendre-polynomial coefficients, ensuring objectivity. Locking is analyzed: the lowest-order element exactly satisfies Λ^T·r' = e1 (no spurious shear/membrane coupling), so it is intrinsically locking-free; for k ≥ 2 reduced integration on γ-related terms is applied to recover ideal convergence.

Numerical experiments are run in Netgen/NGSolve on six benchmarks: (i) cantilever roll-up to a double circle, (ii) superimposed rigid rotation of a deformed cantilever (objectivity check), (iii) Bathe–Bolourchi 45° arc under out-of-plane force, (iv) Harsch et al.'s helix experiment, (v) Ibrahimbegović's helical-shape rod (large inhomogeneous deformation), and (vi) structures with kinks and branches (three rods at right angles + a fork structure tested for path independence under reversed loading).

Results & Highlights

  • For the cantilever roll-up benchmark, optimal O(h^(k+1)) convergence is obtained for k=1,...,4. Strikingly, the k=1 element's L^2-error is independent of slenderness (ρ = 10, 100, 1000), confirming the locking-free claim. Higher-order even-order elements (k=2, 4) suffer membrane locking and k=2,4 also show shear locking; targeted reduced integration on axial / shear contributions selectively cures each form of locking.
  • Objectivity test (10 superimposed rigid-body rotations of a bent cantilever): deviation of tip displacement and stored elastic energy remains at floating-point (~10^−12) level — the formulation is objective up to round-off.
  • Bathe–Bolourchi arc under out-of-plane force: tip displacements agree with Meier et al. up to 4 digits; convergence is independent of slenderness, with errors bottoming out around 10^−9.
  • Helix experiment: tip position is recovered exactly (numerical precision) with as few as 5 lowest-order elements; for k ≥ 2 only 3 elements suffice.
  • Helical-shape rod (Ibrahimbegović): even k=1 elements remain stable up to full load factor λ=1; tip displacement converges at very high rates with k.
  • Discontinuous-slope structure (Romero): a single fourth-order element per segment yields converged results despite kinks; even k=1 gives sub-permille error in tip displacement on a 24-element mesh.
  • Fork structure under a reversed load couple: the obtained tip displacements are perfectly symmetric under load-order reversal, numerically demonstrating path independence in a structure with branches.

Critical Assessment

Novelty (moderate-to-high, niche): The application of the Hellan–Herrmann–Johnson / Regge-style "discrete curvature" concept from shells to geometrically exact beams is a clean and natural transfer, and the resulting lowest-order element that avoids rotation interpolation entirely is genuinely interesting. However, mixed methods for beams have a long history (Saje, Noor & Peters, Nukala & White, Herrmann et al. 2025), and recent SE(3) / motion-formalism elements (Sonneville, Harsch et al.) already address objectivity, path-independence and locking. The contribution is best characterized as an elegant simplification rather than a paradigm shift.

Rigor (high): The variational derivation, Legendre transform, multiplicative split for objectivity, and the limit analysis for discrete curvature are mathematically careful. The locking analysis is qualitative-but-correct, and reduced integration is justified by classical arguments.

Reproducibility (high): Implementation in Netgen/NGSolve with a public GitLab repository (linked in a footnote) is a notable strength compared to typical computational mechanics papers. Benchmarks are standard and parameters are reported.

Significance (moderate): Within the geometrically exact beam community, this is a useful and clean contribution — particularly the locking-free lowest-order element, which sidesteps expensive rotation arithmetic. Outside that community, however, the impact is limited; this is very much an incremental improvement to a mature subject. The authors themselves note "in the field of nonlinear beam theories—although it is by no means a new topic—there are still things to discover."

Red flags / weaknesses:

  • Only the static case is treated; the authors acknowledge dynamic problems and time integration are future work, leaving a major gap for any practical application.
  • No comparison of computational cost (CPU time, memory) against state-of-the-art SE(3) elements or the recent Petrov–Galerkin Cosserat element of Harsch et al. — the claim that hybridization aids static condensation is qualified ("plays only a minor role" for beams).
  • The "membrane locking" diagnosis for k=2 in the cantilever roll-up should ideally be supported by an analytical argument; it is inferred from numerics.
  • No analysis of conditioning of the saddle-point system, nor of inf-sup stability of the chosen discrete spaces.

Strengths

  • A genuinely simple lowest-order element that is locking-free without any rotation interpolation — a rare combination in the literature.
  • Clean mathematical framework with explicit treatment of objectivity and path independence; both are also verified numerically to floating-point precision.
  • Natural handling of structures with kinks and branches via the same formalism — a non-trivial bonus that often requires special treatment in alternative formulations.
  • Open-source implementation in Netgen/NGSolve with a public repository.
  • Clear, well-organized exposition that respects and properly attributes prior art (Crisfield–Jelenić, Simo–Vu-Quoc, Neunteufel–Schöberl).

Limitations

  • Restricted to static problems; dynamic problems are explicitly future work.
  • No empirical comparison against modern SE(3) or motion-formalism elements in terms of accuracy-per-DOF or wall-clock time.
  • The single-element relative-rotation magnitude is bounded by π/2π (the SE(3) approach has the same limitation in some formulations) — the authors do mention "relative rotations of more than 2π cannot be represented within a single element."
  • Discrete-curvature framework requires careful interpretation when combined with arbitrary constitutive laws beyond linear elasticity (the Legendre transform is given explicitly only for the linear case).
  • The benefit of hybridization for static condensation is acknowledged to be small for beams (which connect only at nodes) — the gain compared to monolithic mixed formulations is therefore less pronounced than in shells/solids.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does the proposed mixed element compare in CPU time and accuracy-per-DOF against the SE(3) elements of Sonneville et al. and the Petrov–Galerkin element of Harsch et al. on the same benchmarks?
  • Can the discrete-curvature framework be extended to dynamic problems while preserving energy / momentum conservation, and does the multiplicative split interact cleanly with standard time-integration schemes (e.g., generalized-α, energy-momentum methods)?
  • Is there an analytical inf-sup / Brezzi argument that establishes well-posedness of the discrete saddle-point problem for the chosen polynomial spaces (k for u and M, k−1 for ψ^(E))?
  • For nonlinear constitutive laws (e.g., elastoplastic beams or hyperelastic moment-curvature relations) where a closed-form Legendre transform is unavailable, how does the formulation behave numerically?
  • Can the lowest-order, rotation-interpolation-free element be exploited for very large-scale flexible-multibody simulations where rotation arithmetic dominates cost?
  • Does the discontinuous-rotation strategy interact favorably with adaptive mesh refinement, given that no inter-element rotation continuity is required?
