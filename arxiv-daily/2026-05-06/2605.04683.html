Average Attention Transformers and Arithmetic Circuits

arXiv: 2605.04683

Date: 2026-05-06

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.04683

一句话总结： 本文证明了使用平均注意力机制（average attention）并以算术电路替代前馈网络的 Transformer 编码器，与 FSAC⁰_R[sign] 函数类之间存在精确的计算等价关系——即 Transformer 恰好能计算该类所有函数，且该类所有电路族均可被单个 Transformer 模拟。
TL;DR: This paper establishes a tight correspondence between average-attention transformer encoders (using arithmetic circuits as activation functions) and the complexity class FSAC⁰_R[sign]: transformers over a Z-ordered ring extension of Q compute exactly those functions, making the equivalence bidirectional and precise.

核心贡献

  • 双向等价定理：在将算术电路用作激活函数的前提下，平均注意力 Transformer 所计算的函数类恰好等于 FSAC⁰_R[sign]——Transformer 可模拟该类中所有电路族，且 Transformer 所计算的函数反过来也属于该类。
  • 电路模拟构造：给出了具体的 Transformer 架构（维度 5–9，层数 2K），能够模拟深度为 K 的无界扇入算术电路，并详细说明了如何将电路编码为向量序列输入。
  • 三种扇入情形：分别针对无界扇入（FAC⁰）、半无界扇入（SAC⁰）、有界扇入（AC⁰）给出了对应的 Transformer 结构和定理，统一了此前分散的结果。
  • 泛化到任意半环/环/域：所有结论均在半环（semiring）上给出，涵盖实数、有理数，以及其间任意环，大幅拓展了适用范围。
  • 理论桥梁：明确指出 ML 中的变换器理论与代数计算复杂性理论可相互促进，为两个领域的交叉研究提供了新视角。

Key Contributions

  • Exact computational characterization: Average-attention transformers with arithmetic circuit activations compute precisely the function class FSAC⁰_R[sign] when R is a Z-ordered commutative ring extension of Q—the equivalence is bidirectional.
  • Concrete simulation construction: An explicit encoding E_C is defined for arithmetic circuits as vector sequences; a transformer of dimension 5–9 and 2K layers simulates any depth-K circuit with n inputs using a quadratic-length input sequence.
  • Three fan-in regimes unified: Separate theorems cover unbounded fan-in (FAC⁰, Theorem 4.4), semi-unbounded fan-in (SAC⁰, Theorem 4.7), and bounded fan-in (AC⁰, Theorem 4.9), connected to different attention variants (average, leftmost/rightmost).
  • Generality over semirings: The framework works over arbitrary semirings (reals, rationals, integers, and any ring in between), making results applicable across different numerical regimes.
  • Upper bound clarified: Theorem 3.1 shows that transformers with FCIRC_R circuit families as activation/pooling functions lie within FNC⁰_R—providing a clean upper bound for the circuit simulation direction.

Methodology

The paper works in a generalized transformer framework adapted from Hao et al. (2022), treating transformer encoders as sequence-to-sequence functions over vectors from arbitrary semirings. Feed-forward networks in standard transformers are replaced by arithmetic circuits, making the activation functions part of the formal model rather than implementation details.

Circuit encoding: An arithmetic circuit C with n inputs is encoded as a vector sequence E_C(C, x) of length quadratic in the circuit size. Each gate becomes one or more vectors carrying: the gate's index, predecessor indices, gate type (constant, input, addition, multiplication, output), and the value computed at that gate. This lets a transformer "read" a circuit as its input.

Simulation via attention: Two attention functions are defined—one to fetch values from predecessors sharing the same gate index, another to gather all incoming edge values for the same gate. Pooling functions implement addition (weighted sum) and multiplication (weighted product, excluding zeros). The activation function selects the right combination based on gate type using characteristic functions chi_t.

Average vs. hard attention: For unbounded fan-in circuits, generalized attention (with arithmetic circuits as attention functions) suffices. For average attention specifically, a correction factor of k (the number of predecessors) must be computed and cancelled—this requires Q-extensions to have multiplicative inverses of positive integers, which is why the results for average attention require a Z-ordered ring extension of Q rather than an arbitrary semiring.

Bounded fan-in: When fan-in is bounded, a fixed number of attention heads suffices (one per incoming edge), average pooling can be replaced by leftmost/rightmost attention, and the transformer dimension is kept constant (8 in this case).

Extension to circuit bases B: Theorem 4.10 extends the construction to circuits over arbitrary finite sets of functions f: R^k → R, enabling simulation of any enriched arithmetic circuit family.

Results & Highlights

  • Theorem 4.4 (main, unbounded fan-in): For every K, there exists a transformer in GT_R({id}, {0}, FCIRC_R[zero], FCIRC_R[zero], WS_R(id) ∪ WP_R(id)) of dimension 5 with 2K layers simulating any unbounded fan-in R-circuit of depth ≤ K.
  • Theorem 4.5 (average attention, main): For every K, there is a dimension-7, 2K-layer average-attention transformer simulating any unbounded fan-in R-circuit of depth ≤ K (R a Z-ordered commutative ring extension of Q).
  • Theorem 4.11 (full equivalence): Same setup as 4.5, but now specifically for FSAC⁰_R[sign]-circuits, confirming the bidirectional correspondence.
  • Theorem 3.1 (upper bound): Transformer functions (with FCIRC_R activations) lie within FNC⁰_R, bounding what transformers can compute from above.
  • Bounded fan-in (Theorem 4.9): Dimension-8, 2K-layer transformer with leftmost (or average) attention simulates any bounded fan-in R-circuit of depth ≤ K.
  • All results hold for R = reals, rationals, and any ring in between, with explicit worked examples (Tables 1–3) demonstrating the encoding and attention computations.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: Moderate-to-high within its niche. The paper makes the first precise bidirectional equivalence between average-attention transformers and an arithmetic circuit class for functions on vectors (rather than decision problems on languages). Prior work (Hao et al., Strobl, Merrill & Sabharwal) established one-directional or language-theoretic results. The extension to vector-to-vector functions and to arbitrary semirings is a meaningful generalization.

Rigor: High. Definitions are fully formal (semirings, circuit bases, generalized transformers), theorems are stated precisely with explicit parameter counts (dimension, layer count, attention type), and proofs are provided for all main theorems (with complete proofs in the appendix). The worked examples (Tables 1–3) are a strength, making the abstract construction concrete.

Reproducibility: The theoretical results are self-contained and verifiable. No experiments are involved, so there is no code or empirical reproducibility concern. The encoding E_C is fully specified, though it is quadratic in circuit size—this could be a practical limitation for large circuits.

Significance: Primarily of theoretical interest. The result formalizes the expressive power of average-attention transformers over semirings and provides a clean circuit-complexity characterization. The practical takeaway is limited: the construction requires circuits as input (not natural language tokens), the input length is quadratic in the circuit, and the activation functions must be arithmetic circuits themselves—not standard feed-forward networks.

Red flags:

  • The result is essentially a theoretical equivalence with no empirical validation or connection to practical transformer training.
  • The input encoding is quadratic in circuit size, which the authors acknowledge but do not fully address as a practical concern.
  • The paper is a preprint with no peer review yet; the master's thesis self-citation (Ehrmuth 2025) for key construction details means some foundational material is not in a peer-reviewed source.
  • Scope is narrow: the equivalence applies to a very specific transformer variant (arithmetic circuit activations, average attention over semirings) that differs substantially from standard deployed transformers.

Strengths

  • Formally rigorous with clean theorem statements and worked numerical examples.
  • The bidirectional equivalence is a genuine contribution over prior one-directional results.
  • Generality over arbitrary semirings (not just reals/rationals) is a meaningful theoretical advance.
  • Three separate fan-in regimes (bounded, semi-unbounded, unbounded) are unified within a single framework.
  • Honest about limitations: the paper explicitly notes that FSAC⁰_R[sign]-functions are not all computed by standard (feed-forward) transformers, and discusses what additional assumptions would be needed for a full equivalence.

Limitations

  • The results apply to a non-standard transformer model: activation functions are arithmetic circuits, not feed-forward networks. Standard transformers (with ReLU/GELU feed-forward blocks) are not covered by the main equivalence.
  • The circuit-simulation construction requires the circuit to be encoded as part of the input sequence, with quadratic blowup in sequence length—not applicable when the circuit is unknown or the input is natural data.
  • Average attention requires multiplicative inverses of positive integers, restricting the base semiring to Q-extensions (not arbitrary semirings or integers alone).
  • The paper does not address training dynamics, generalization, or whether transformers can learn to simulate circuits from data—it is purely about expressive capacity.
  • No empirical component; potential connections to practical ML are speculative and left for future work.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can the bidirectional equivalence be extended to standard transformers with feed-forward networks (e.g., ReLU/GELU activations)? What is the exact circuit class they correspond to?
  2. The encoding E_C has quadratic length in the circuit size—are there more efficient encodings that would reduce this blowup, and do they affect the expressivity characterization?
  3. The results require R to be a Z-ordered ring extension of Q for average attention. What happens with floating-point arithmetic or fixed-precision approximations, which are used in practice?
  4. Can these theoretical results be used to construct interpretability tools—e.g., inverting the encoding to extract an arithmetic circuit from a trained transformer's weights?
  5. How does the FSAC⁰_R[sign] characterization compare with the TC⁰ / AC⁰ characterizations from prior language-theoretic work (e.g., Strobl 2023, Merrill & Sabharwal 2023) when restricted to Boolean inputs?
  6. The paper notes that training transformers on longer sequences could yield a full equivalence for uniform circuit families—what experimental evidence would support or refute this conjecture?
