Almost-Orthogonality in Lp Spaces: A Case Study with Grok

arXiv: 2605.05192v1

Date: 2026-05-06

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.05192v1

一句话总结： 本文推翻了Carbery关于L^p空间中"几乎正交"三角不等式的猜想（对所有p>2给出反例），确定了指数的最优临界值c=p'，并借助AI模型Grok对三函数情形给出了比Carlen-Frank-Lieb此前结果更优的精确指数c(p)=2ln2/[(p-2)ln3+2ln2]。
TL;DR: This paper disproves Carbery's conjectured sharpened triangle inequality in L^p spaces for all p > 2 (showing the exponent c = 2 fails), proves the optimal exponent must be c = p', establishes the inequality at this critical exponent for all integer p ≥ 2, and—using AI model Grok as a collaborator—proves a sharp three-function bound that improves the previously best-known exponent due to Carlen, Frank, and Lieb.

核心贡献

  • 反例构造：对每个p > 2，构造出反例证明Carbery猜想（指数c=2的不等式）不成立，回答了一个已开放的分析学问题。
  • 指数最优性：证明若形如Carbery猜想的估计对某c > 0成立，则必须满足c ≤ p'（即p/(p-1)），揭示了临界指数的本质约束。
  • 临界指数定理：在临界指数c = p'处，对所有整数p ≥ 2严格证明了该加强型三角不等式。
  • 三函数精确界：对n=3、p ≥ 3，将Carlen-Frank-Lieb的多函数界中的指数从r(p)=6/(5p-4)优化至最优值c(p)=2ln2/[(p-2)ln3+2ln2]，并证明该指数不可再改进。
  • 人机协作：作为案例研究，公开记录了LLM（Grok）在严肃数学研究中的作用：提供反例核心思路，生成（部分有误但方向正确的）引理证明，以及对约30个多项式不等式的计算机辅助验证。

Key Contributions

  • Counterexample to Carbery's conjecture: Constructs an explicit counterexample showing that the c = 2 sharpened triangle inequality fails for every p > 2, settling the question negatively.
  • Optimality of the exponent: Proves that if any inequality of Carbery's form holds for some c > 0, then necessarily c ≤ p' = p/(p-1). This is tight.
  • Inequality at the critical exponent: Establishes the sharpened triangle inequality with exponent c = p' for all integer values p ≥ 2 (Theorem 1.1).
  • Sharp three-function bound (Theorem 1.4): For n = 3 and p ≥ 3, proves Carlen–Frank–Lieb's inequality with the optimal exponent c(p) = 2ln(2)/[(p-2)ln(3)+2ln(2)], strictly improving upon the previous best r(p) = 6/(5p-4).
  • Characterization of sharpness (Theorem 1.2): Shows that for general n, the inequality holds with r = β(n,p) if and only if it holds for indicator functions on a common set, reducing the problem to a finite-dimensional inequality.
  • Human-AI collaboration documentation: Provides a transparent case study (including an appendix with chat transcripts) of how Grok contributed to the mathematical research—generating the counterexample structure, key lemma proofs, and computational verifications.

Methodology

The paper proceeds in two main parts:

Part 1 — Carbery's conjecture.

The counterexample uses a measure space with n pairwise disjoint "private" sets of measure 1 and one "common" set of measure 1. Functions f_j equal 1 on their private set and on the common set. By analyzing the growth of ||sum f_j||_p as n → ∞, the authors derive that c = 2 fails for every p > 2, and more generally that any valid c must satisfy c ≤ p/(p-1) = p'. The proof of the critical-exponent inequality (c = p') uses a key integral kernel lemma (Lemma 2.3, attributed substantially to Grok) combined with a Hölder-based multi-linear integration argument and a spectral interpretation via the counting measure on indices.

Part 2 — Sharp three-function bound.

The sharp bound for n = 3 reduces—via Lemma 2.8 and a symmetry argument specific to n = 3—to establishing a one-variable inequality (Lemma 3.1) of the form (x+y+z)^{p'} ≤ 1 + (2(xy+xz+yz)^{p/2})^{c(p)} under the constraint x^p + y^p + z^p = 1. The proof analyzes sign changes of successively differentiated auxiliary functions h(s), ψ(s), P(s), Q(s), using a chain of about six lemmas. Grok 4.20 Heavy was used to numerically generate plots, check local inequalities, and verify roughly 30 polynomial inequalities that are moved to appendices for readability. The main conceptual step—counting roots of complicated functions—is credited to the human authors.

Results & Highlights

  • Carbery's c=2 inequality is false for all p > 2. This closes a problem that had been open since Carbery's work on Schatten–von Neumann classes.
  • Optimal exponent for the general inequality is c = p'. At this exponent the inequality holds for all integer p ≥ 2, with the proof relying on Grok's construction of a Hölder-based kernel argument.
  • For three functions (n=3, p≥3): The sharp constant in the Carlen–Frank–Lieb n-function inequality is achieved with exponent c(p) = 2ln(2)/[(p-2)ln(3)+2ln(2)], which is strictly larger than the previous best r(p) = 6/(5p-4) for all p > 2. The exponent c(p) is proven optimal.
  • Characterization theorem (Theorem 1.2): The Carlen–Frank–Lieb bound holds with exponent r = β(n,p) if and only if a specific finite-dimensional algebraic inequality (eq. 5) holds, providing a clean reduction for future work.
  • The paper includes an appendix with the actual Grok chat transcript, making the AI contribution transparent and reproducible in a way rarely seen in mathematics papers.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: High. The paper resolves an open question (Carbery's c=2 conjecture), establishes a sharp bound for three functions improving on a recent result by Carlen, Frank, and Lieb, and provides a complete characterization theorem. These are genuine contributions to classical harmonic analysis.

Rigor: The main theorems appear rigorous. The proofs of Theorems 1.1 and 1.2 are complete and self-contained. Theorem 1.4 (sharp three-function bound) relies on a chain of lemmas whose proofs are moved to Appendix A; the paper states these were computer-verified (Grok 4.20 Heavy checked ~30 polynomial inequalities). This computational dependency is clearly disclosed, which is scientifically honest, but it does mean that independent verification requires either trusting Grok's symbolic computations or re-verifying the appendix lemmas.

Reproducibility: Moderate-to-high for the mathematical claims—all main proofs are written out, and the appendix contains the technical polynomial verifications. The Grok chat transcripts are included, providing unusual transparency. However, the computational polynomial inequalities cannot be checked by pencil-and-paper alone, and independent CAS verification would be needed for full confidence.

Significance: Meaningful within harmonic analysis and the study of Lp triangle inequalities. The result also constitutes one of the more carefully documented cases of LLM contribution to new mathematics, relevant to ongoing discussions about AI in mathematical research.

Red flags:

  • The claim that "Grok Heavy's construction revealed the optimal exponent p'" is notable—the authors are careful to clarify that Grok's original counterexample had numerical inaccuracies and the final version is human-polished. This is appropriately disclosed.
  • Theorem 1.4 is stated for p ≥ 3; the authors explicitly note that extending to p ∈ [2,3) would require additional casework and defer it as future work. This is a real gap in the three-function result.
  • The paper is a math.CA submission with xAI (Grok's developer) employees as co-authors. The "case study with Grok" framing could be read as promotional. The authors mitigate this by being explicit about Grok's errors and limitations.

Strengths

  • Resolves a concrete, well-posed open problem with complete proofs.
  • Provides both a counterexample and a positive result at the critical exponent—a complete picture.
  • The sharp three-function bound (Theorem 1.4) is fully optimal, with a matching lower bound.
  • Unprecedented transparency about AI contributions: chat transcripts in appendix, explicit attribution of which lemmas came from Grok vs. humans, honest account of Grok's errors.
  • Clean reduction theorem (Theorem 1.2) that converts the analytic inequality to a finite-dimensional one, useful for future work on general n.

Limitations

  • The sharp three-function bound (Theorem 1.4) is restricted to integer p ≥ 3; the range p ∈ [2,3) is left open.
  • For general n ≥ 4, the sharp exponent β(n,p) in Theorem 1.2 is characterized but not directly verified; the finite-dimensional inequality (5) is noted to be "numerically correct" but not proven for general n > 3.
  • About 30 polynomial inequalities in the proof of Theorem 1.4 were verified computationally (via Grok), not by hand—this is a non-trivial computational dependency.
  • The paper does not address the non-integer p case for Theorem 1.1 (the critical exponent result holds for integer p ≥ 2, with the non-integer case remaining open).
