Systematic Evaluation of Learning Rate Scheduling Strategies Across Heterogeneous Architectures

arXiv: 2607.08511

Date: 2026-07-10

https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08511

一句话总结： 作者通过对 LEMUR 神经网络数据集中 30 种卷积/Transformer 架构在 CIFAR-10 上注入 25 种 PyTorch 学习率调度策略(共 3,938 个变体、训练 5 轮)进行系统评估,发现最佳组合(GoogLeNet + CosineAnnealingWarmRestarts)达到 86.45% top-1 准确率,且调度器的最优选择强烈依赖于架构类型(移动/卷积模型偏好 CyclicLR,Inception 类偏好 CosineAnnealingWarmRestarts,Transformer 偏好 CosineAnnealingLR)。
TL;DR: This paper systematically injects 25 LR scheduler configurations from 9 PyTorch families into 30 heterogeneous architectures from the LEMUR nn-dataset and evaluates all 3,938 resulting variants on CIFAR-10 (5-epoch screening). The best variant reaches 86.45% top-1 accuracy (GoogLeNet with CosineAnnealingWarmRestarts), 237 variants exceed 80%, and the scheduler choice proves strongly architecture-dependent: CosineAnnealingWarmRestarts wins on inception nets (mean 55.20% across families) while CyclicLR leads on mobile/convolutional models; ReduceLROnPlateau is the worst family by mean (45.04%) yet ties for the single best peak — a +9.41-point gap driven by its incompatibility with 5-epoch budgets.

核心贡献

  • 大规模调度器评测基准:在 LEMUR 神经网络数据集基础上,通过自动化源码注入(inject_scheduler()),向 30 个异构架构中注入 9 个 PyTorch 调度器家族下的 25 种配置,首次贡献了 3,938 个可复现的"架构×调度器×权重衰减"变体,全部在 CIFAR-10 上以 5 轮预算评测完成。
  • 揭示架构与调度器的强耦合关系:首次系统量化证明不存在"通用最优调度器"。CyclicLR 在 MobileNetV2/V3、EfficientNet、ResNet 等移动/卷积模型上领先;CosineAnnealingWarmRestarts 统治 GoogLeNet、InceptionV3 等 Inception 系;SwinTransformer、VisionTransformer 偏好 CosineAnnealingLR。
  • 预算感知的关键洞见:ReduceLROnPlateau 在 5 轮筛选预算下表现最差(均值 45.04%,比最佳家族低 10.16 个百分点),却能拿到并列最高单点 86.45%,证明"调度器必须匹配训练预算"。
  • 权重衰减与调度器需联合调优:λ=10⁻⁵ 取得最高均值 55.79%(比 λ=0 提升 3.5 个百分点),而对高容量模型(ResNet、DenseNet、EfficientNet)权重衰减一致提升,对轻量模型则略有伤害——说明二者不应独立搜索。
  • 提供可复用的"精度景观":结果作为参考景观回灌 LEMUR 数据集,为后续 AutoML/NNGPT 流水线的调度器选择提供经验先验。

Key Contributions

  • A reusable 3,938-variant benchmark: An automated inject_scheduler() pipeline (method-boundary detection, scheduler init injection, step injection, hyperparameter registration) injects 25 LR scheduler configurations spanning 9 PyTorch families into 30 architectures from the LEMUR/LEMUR 2/NN-Lite datasets, producing 3,938 validated model variants all trained/evaluated on CIFAR-10 with a unified NNEval harness.
  • Quantified architecture–scheduler coupling: The study demonstrates that no scheduler is universally optimal. Per-architecture best-scheduler analysis shows CyclicLR dominates mobile/convolutional models (MobileNetV2/V3, EfficientNet, ResNet), CosineAnnealingWarmRestarts dominates inception-style networks (GoogLeNet 86.45%, InceptionV3 86.45%), and CosineAnnealingLR is best for SwinTransformer/VisionTransformer.
  • Budget-aware scheduler warnings: ReduceLROnPlateau is the worst family by mean accuracy (45.04%) yet ties for the single highest peak (86.45%), making explicit that patience-based schedulers are mis-matched to short screening budgets and should not be used in 5-epoch sweeps.
  • Joint weight-decay analysis: Across 7 weight-decay values λ ∈ {0, 1e-5, 5e-5, 1e-4, 5e-4, 1e-3, 5e-3}, λ=1e-5 yields the highest mean (55.79%, +3.5 pp over λ=0) while λ=0 yields the highest peak (86.45%); regularization consistently helps high-capacity models but slightly hurts lightweight ones, motivating joint scheduler+weight-decay optimization.
  • Practical reference landscape contributed back to LEMUR: The full accuracy landscape is released as part of the LEMUR nn-dataset, giving downstream AutoML/NNGPT pipelines an empirical prior for principled scheduler selection.

Methodology

The study follows a two-stage pipeline.

Stage 1 — Variant generation by automated code injection. The authors build on the LEMUR neural network dataset (LEMUR, LEMUR 2, NN-Lite), selecting 30 representative architectures spanning convolutional and transformer families: AlexNet, ResNet, VGG, DenseNet, EfficientNet, MobileNetV2/V3, RegNet, ShuffleNet, SqueezeNet, GoogLeNet, InceptionV3, MNASNet, MaxViT, SwinTransformer, VisionTransformer, ConvNeXt, DPN68/107/131, BagNet, FractalNet, AirNet, AirNext, BayesianNet, DarkNet, UNet2D, ICNet, and Diffuser. Each architecture exposes a standardized train_setup() (optimizer/scheduler init) and learn() (per-iteration loop), enabling fully automated source-code modification.

A catalogue of 25 LR scheduler configurations is defined across 9 PyTorch families: StepLR (4 variants, step ∈ {0.1,0.25,0.5,0.7}, γ ∈ {0.1,0.3,0.5,0.7}); ExponentialLR (3, γ ∈ {0.90,0.95,0.98}); CosineAnnealingLR (3, Tmax ∈ {5,10,20}); CosineAnnealingWarmRestarts (2, T0 ∈ {2,5}); MultiStepLR (3, milestones {5,10}/{3,7}/{2,4}); ReduceLROnPlateau (3, (factor,patience) ∈ {(0.5,2),(0.3,3),(0.1,5)}); CyclicLR (3: triangular, triangular2, exp_range); OneCycleLR (2, lrmax ∈ {0.05,0.10}); LinearLR and PolynomialLR (1 each).

The inject_scheduler() function performs line-level source-code editing guided by Python parsing in four steps: (1) method-boundary detection for train_setup() and learn(); (2) scheduler-init injection (replacing any existing scheduler); (3) scheduler-step injection — per-epoch schedulers get self.scheduler.step() at the end of learn(), per-batch schedulers (CyclicLR, OneCycleLR, ReduceLROnPlateau) get the step call right after self.optimizer.step(); (4) hyperparameter registration. Each variant is validated by AST parsing and a string-literal occurrence check; failing variants are discarded. A pre-release bug in LinearLR/PolynomialLR (incorrect total_iters scaling) was found and fixed before the main run.

Stage 2 — Unified evaluation. All variants are trained and evaluated on CIFAR-10 (50k train / 10k test, 32×32, 10 classes) with the NNEval harness on NVIDIA RTX 3090/4090 (24 GB) GPUs in the CVL Kubernetes cluster at University of Würzburg. Fixed hyperparameters: SGD, lr = 0.01, momentum μ = 0.9, batch size B = 64, dropout p = 0.2, 5 epochs, norm-256-flip augmentation (resize to 256×256, per-channel normalization, random horizontal flip). Weight decay is the only additional swept dimension, λ ∈ {0, 1e-5, 5e-5, 1e-4, 5e-4, 1e-3, 5e-3}. The full combinatorial space is 30 × 25 × 7 = 5,250 theoretical variants; 3,938 passed validation. Evaluation ran in three phases (Mar 26 2026: 1,507 variants; Apr 24–May 2 2026: +516 after the LinearLR/PolynomialLR bug fix; a third extension phase reaching 3,938). The primary metric is top-1 test accuracy.

Results & Highlights

  • Headline accuracy on CIFAR-10: The best configuration — GoogLeNet + CosineAnnealingWarmRestarts — reaches 86.45% top-1 accuracy; the second best (InceptionV3 + ReduceLROnPlateau) reaches 86.45% as well, and DenseNet + OneCycleLR reaches 86.17%.
  • Distribution of the 3,938 variants: accuracy ranges 12.08% → 86.45%, mean 52.52%, median 50.00%; 237 variants (6.0%) exceed 80%, 627 (15.9%) exceed 75%, and 1,107 (28.1%) exceed 60%, while 445 (11.3%) stay below 30%.
  • Per-family scheduler ranking: CosineAnnealingWarmRestarts has the highest family mean (55.20%), beating ReduceLROnPlateau's mean (45.04%) by +10.16 percentage points; MultiStepLR (54.65%), CosineAnnealingLR (54.55%), and ExponentialLR (54.51%) follow closely.
  • Best single variant by family: CosineAnnealingWR best = 86.45%, MultiStepLR best = 84.74%, CosineAnnealingLR best = 85.99%, ExponentialLR best = 85.96%, CyclicLR best = 84.25%, ReduceLROnPlateau best = 86.45% (tied for the global peak despite the lowest mean).
  • Per-variant analysis: MultiStepLR m2_4_g01 has the highest individual mean (64.00%); StepLR s20_g07 mean 62.79%; CosineAnnealingLR T10 mean 63.97%; CyclicLR exp_range collapses to a mean of 33.20% (worst non-plateau variant) while triangular/triangular2 hold ~56%.
  • Weight decay: λ=1e-5 yields the highest mean 55.79% vs λ=0 mean 52.29% (+3.5 pp); λ=0 yields the highest peak (86.45%); best accuracy stays in 81.59%–86.45% across all λ, showing top schedulers are robust to regularization strength.
  • Per-architecture peaks: GoogLeNet 86.45% (CosineAnnealingWR), InceptionV3 86.45% (ReduceLROnPlateau), DenseNet 86.17% (OneCycleLR), RegNet 85.96% (ExponentialLR), VisionTransformer 85.55% (ReduceLROnPlateau), MobileNetV2 84.55% (CosineAnnealingLR), ResNet 84.53% (LinearLR); UNet2D/ICNet (non-classification designs) sit at the bottom (44.27% / 46.36% mean).
  • Architecture-family means: GoogLeNet leads with mean 60.79% (134 variants), followed by BagNet 57.46% and MobileNetV2 56.38%; UNet2D is lowest at 44.27%.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty — moderate. Comparing LR schedulers across architectures is not new (Loshchilov & Hutter 2017; Smith & Topin 2019 already established cosine restarts and one-cycle behaviour). The genuine novelty is scale and reproducibility — 3,938 variants produced by automated source-code injection into a unified architecture dataset — rather than a new method.
  • Rigor — mixed. The strongest baseline for any "is scheduler X better" claim is the same architecture under a different scheduler, which the paper handles well via the controlled single-hyperparameter design. However the headline 86.45% number is not compared against any external CIFAR-10 SOTA (e.g. well-tuned ResNet/ViT at full 200-epoch budgets reach >95%); the paper explicitly frames results as a relative scheduler landscape, not absolute SOTA, which is honest but limits the top-line number's meaning.
  • A material data quirk: only 5 training epochs are used. This is the single biggest threat to external validity — the authors themselves note ReduceLROnPlateau's ranking "improves substantially with longer budgets" and that OneCycleLR's warm-up may be penalized. Several conclusions (CyclicLR exp_range "is harmful", StepLR "late gentle decay is preferable") are likely artifacts of the 5-epoch window rather than general truths, and the paper does not always separate budget-conditional findings from durable ones.
  • Coverage gaps in the catalogue: warmup+cosine (the de-facto standard for modern transformer training), learned schedules, Prodigy, and Schedule-Free SGD are all explicitly excluded — notable omissions precisely in the regime (transformers) the paper claims to cover.
  • Optimizer confound: every run uses SGD with a fixed lr=0.01; interactions with Adam/AdamW (where scheduler behaviour differs substantially) are unexplored, yet conclusions are stated for "transformer-based models."
  • Attribution hole: 10 of the 30 architectures (AirNext, MNASNet, MaxViT, VGG, AlexNet, ShuffleNet, Diffuser, plus three more) "could not be individually attributed due to class-naming conventions" and are dropped from the per-architecture tables — meaning the per-architecture conclusions rest on only ~20 architectures.
  • Reproducibility — strong. Fixed hyperparameters, dated run phases, a documented bug fix, AST-validated code injection, and contribution to a versioned dataset (LEMUR) make the pipeline reproducible. No code-release URL is given in the body, however.
  • Significance — niche but useful. The value is as an empirical prior for scheduler selection inside the LEMUR/NNGPT evaluation stack, less so as a general training-recipe guide.

Strengths

  • Unusually broad and fair sweep: 30 architectures × 25 schedulers × 7 weight-decay values under a single fixed training recipe, isolating the scheduler effect cleanly.
  • Automated, AST-validated code-injection pipeline is a reusable engineering contribution that could be applied beyond schedulers.
  • Honest, specific reporting of failure modes — the LinearLR/PolynomialLR bug fix, the 10 unattributable architectures, and the ReduceLROnPlateau budget mismatch are all disclosed rather than hidden.
  • Concrete, actionable per-architecture best-scheduler table (Table 7) usable as a lookup by practitioners.
  • Results released back into the LEMUR nn-dataset, turning a one-off experiment into a persistent community resource.

Limitations

  • 5-epoch budget is the dominant limitation; relative rankings — especially for patience-based and warm-up schedulers — may not transfer to production-length training.
  • Single optimizer (SGD, lr=0.01) and single dataset (CIFAR-10); generalization to Adam/AdamW and to ImageNet-scale / detection / segmentation is untested.
  • Scheduler catalogue gaps: no warmup+cosine, no learned schedules, no Prodigy / Schedule-Free SGD — exactly the methods most relevant to modern transformer recipes.
  • 10 of 30 architectures unattributable in per-architecture tables, weakening architecture-level claims.
  • Non-classification architectures (UNet2D, ICNet, Diffuser) score low, plausibly due to task mismatch rather than scheduler effect, contaminating aggregate statistics.
  • No external SOTA comparison and no reported statistical significance / variance across seeds.

Relevance

This work is highly relevant to systematic hyperparameter / training-recipe research: it provides a large-scale, reproducible empirical map of how LR scheduler choice interacts with architecture family, directly informing AutoML pipelines (NNGPT, LEMUR) and anyone building neural-network evaluation benchmarks. For daily tracking of training-recipe and AutoML advances, it is a solid reference landscape rather than a new method.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How do the scheduler rankings change when training is extended to 50–200 epochs, and does ReduceLROnPlateau overtake CosineAnnealingWarmRestarts as the paper's own caveat suggests?
  • Would the architecture-specific preferences (CyclicLR for mobile, cosine-restart for inception) hold under AdamW with weight-decay coupling, the dominant optimizer for transformers?
  • What happens when warmup+cosine and Schedule-Free / Prodigy schedulers are added — do they dominate CosineAnnealingLR on SwinTransformer/VisionTransformer?
  • Does the relative scheduler ranking transfer from CIFAR-10 to ImageNet-scale classification and to detection/segmentation tasks, as the authors hypothesize?
  • Can the automated inject_scheduler() AST pipeline be extended to jointly inject optimizer, augmentation, and regularization strategies to produce a full training-recipe landscape?
  • Is the 5-epoch screening budget actually predictive of full-budget rankings, or only of a subset of "fast-converging" schedulers — and can a budget-aware correction be learned?
