> **一句话总结：** 本文在 Hölder 连续梯度（ν ∈ (0,1]）与有界 p 阶矩重尾噪声（p ∈ (1,2]）假设下，首次为带动的 SGD 在强凸/凸/非凸三类目标上给出无需梯度裁剪或归一化的期望收敛速率（非凸情形达 O(T^{-(p-1)/2p})），其速率低于归一化方法的最优 O(T^{-(p-1)/(3p-2)})，定量揭示了 vanilla 方法在重尾噪声下的固有局限；关键条件 ν+1≤p 在 100 组 (ν,α) 合成函数热图与 WikiText-2/Transformer-XL 实验中得到验证。

> **TL;DR:** Against the prevailing view that SGD under heavy-tailed noise needs gradient clipping or normalization, this paper gives the first in-expectation convergence rates for vanilla SGD with momentum for strongly convex, convex, and nonconvex objectives, using Hölder-continuous gradients (ν ∈ (0,1]) plus bounded p-th moment noise (p ∈ (1,2]) instead of clipping/normalization; on the nonconvex synthetic benchmark with tail index α it achieves O(T^{-(p-1)/2p}) (known p) / O(T^{-(p-1)/4) (unknown p), which is worse than the O(T^{-(p-1)/(3p-2)}) of normalized-SGDM-with-momentum (Liu and Zhou, ICLR 2025) but proves convergence is possible with no gradient control, and a 100-(ν,α)-grid experiment plus a WikiText-2 / Transformer-XL run empirically confirm the critical condition ν+1 ≤ p.

## 核心贡献

- 首次为带动的 vanilla SGD 在重尾噪声下建立强凸、凸、非凸三类目标的期望收敛保证，且完全不依赖梯度裁剪或归一化。关键洞见是用 Hölder 连续梯度（Assumption 2.1）替换经典 L-光滑，把上界估计从二阶矩 ∥∇f_St∥² 降到 ∥∇f_St∥^{ν+1}，从而规避重尾噪声下方差无界导致标准下降引理崩溃的问题。
- 识别并形式化了一个关键的"兼容性"条件 ν+1 ≤ p（Hölder 指数 ν ∈ (0,1]、尾指数 p ∈ (1,2]）。该条件是整套分析的技术支点（Lemma 2.4 的矩放大引理依赖它），并直接解释了为何经典 L-光滑（ν=1）在严格重尾（p<2）下失效——标准 L-光滑与重尾噪声在理论上不兼容。
- 在每个函数类别下都给出"已知尾指数"和"未知尾指数"两套速率：非凸已知 p 时为 O(T^{-(p-1)/2p})、未知 p 时为 O(T^{-(p-1)/4)；凸情形为 O(T^{-(p-1)/p} · log T)。所有速率在 ν=1、p=2 时都退化为经典已知结果（O(1/T)、O(log T/T)、O(1/√T)），保证理论自洽。
- 明确量化了 vanilla 方法相对裁剪/归一化方法的劣势，把它定位为"应被超越的基础基线"：非凸最优速率为 O(T^{-(p-1)/(3p-2)})，本文 vanilla-SGDM 的 O(T^{-(p-1)/2p}) 严格更慢，因为 (p-1)/(3p-2) ≥ (p-1)/2p ≥ (p-1)/4 对 p∈(1,2] 恒成立。
- 用合成函数 100 组 (ν,α) 网格热图与 WikiText-2 上 26.9M 参数 Transformer-XL 训练两组实验实证 ν+1 ≤ p 的必要性：性能最优的 35 个 (ν,α) 组合高度集中在 ν+1 ≤ α 三角区域内，且 vanilla SGDM 虽收敛但确实慢于归一化 SGDM。

## Key Contributions

- The first in-expectation convergence guarantee for vanilla SGD **with** momentum under heavy-tailed noise, covering all three objective classes (strongly convex, convex, nonconvex) and using neither gradient clipping nor gradient normalization. The enabling idea is to replace L-smoothness with Hölder-continuous gradients (Assumption 2.1, exponent ν ∈ (0,1]), so the descent lemma bounds the (ν+1)-th moment ∥∇f_St∥^{ν+1} instead of the unbounded squared norm ∥∇f_St∥² that breaks the standard analysis under heavy tails.
- Identification and formalization of the pivotal compatibility condition **ν+1 ≤ p** linking the Hölder exponent ν to the noise tail index p ∈ (1,2]. This single condition is the load-bearing technical requirement (it is what makes the moment-lifting Lemma 2.4 hold), and it explains why classical L-smoothness (ν=1) provably cannot coexist with strictly heavy-tailed noise (p<2) — under L-smoothness the bounded-variance step E[∥d_t∥²] is genuinely unbounded.
- A complete menu of rates, each with "known p" and "unknown p" variants: nonconvex O(T^{-(p-1)/2p}) (known p) and O(T^{-(p-1)/4}) (unknown p); convex O(T^{-(p-1)/p}·log T); strongly convex (for vanilla SGD, no momentum) O(1/T^{p-1}). Every rate collapses to the textbook bound (O(1/T), O(log T/T), O(1/√T)) when ν=1 and p=2, confirming consistency with the classical bounded-variance theory.
- An explicit, honest quantification of the gap to clipping/normalization methods, positioning vanilla SGDM as a baseline that more sophisticated methods should beat: vanilla's O(T^{-(p-1)/2p}) is strictly worse than the optimal O(T^{-(p-1)/(3p-2)}) of normalized SGDM (Liu and Zhou, ICLR 2025), since (p-1)/(3p-2) ≥ (p-1)/2p ≥ (p-1)/4 for all p ∈ (1,2].
- Empirical validation of the necessity of ν+1 ≤ p via a 100-combination (ν,α) grid on synthetic strongly-convex/convex/nonconvex functions plus a real WikiText-2 / 26.9M-parameter Transformer-XL training run in which heavy-tailed gradient noise has been independently reported; the top-35 performing (ν,α) pairs cluster inside the ν+1 ≤ α triangle, and vanilla SGDM converges but is out-paced by normalized SGDM, exactly as the theory predicts.

## Methodology

The paper is a theoretical convergence study built on a single algorithmic object: vanilla SGD with momentum (Algorithm 1), `m_t := β m_{t-1} + (1-β)∇f_{S_t}(x_t)`, `x_{t+1} := x_t − η_t m_t`, with no clipping or normalization anywhere. Setting β=0 recovers plain vanilla SGD. The analysis rests on two assumptions. **Assumption 2.1 (Hölder-continuous gradient):** ∥∇f(x) − ∇f(y)∥ ≤ L∥x − y∥^ν for ν ∈ (0,1]; ν=1 recovers standard L-smoothness. **Assumption 2.2 (heavy-tailed noise):** unbiased stochastic gradient with bounded p-th moment E_ξ[∥G_ξ(x) − ∇f(x)∥^p] ≤ σ^p for p ∈ (1,2]; p=2 recovers bounded variance, p<2 gives unbounded variance.

The central technical obstacle the paper resolves is that under L-smoothness + heavy tails the term E[∥∇f_{S_t}(x_t)∥²] appearing in the classical descent lemma is unbounded, so the entire standard proof collapses. The fix is to apply the Hölder descent lemma (Lemma 2.1), which produces E[∥d_t∥^{ν+1}] instead of E[∥d_t∥²], and then bound that lower-order moment with four tools collected in Section 2.1: a generalized von Bahr–Esseen inequality (Lemma 2.3) controlling the p-th moment of the mini-batch gradient noise, a norm-smoothness inequality (Lemma 2.2), and crucially Lemma 2.4 — a moment-lifting bound E[∥X∥^{ν+1}] ≤ (E[∥X∥^p])^{(ν+1)/p} that is valid **only when ν+1 ≤ p**. This single condition is therefore both the price of admission and the paper's headline structural insight.

Section 3.1 makes explicit why L-smoothness and heavy-tailed noise are incompatible: the standard descent lemma forces E[∥∇f_{S_t}(x_t)∥²] to appear, and that quantity cannot be bounded. The Hölder framework sidesteps it via the key auxiliary Lemma 3.1, E_{ξ_t}[∥∇f_{S_t}(x)∥^{ν+1}] ≤ 2^{1−ν}(C_p σ^p / b^{p−1})^{(ν+1)/p} + ∥∇f(x)∥^{ν+1} + (1−ν)/2, which recovers the classical E[∥∇f_{S_t}(x)∥²] ≤ σ²/b + ∥∇f(x)∥² when ν=1, p=2. Six theorems then follow: Thm 3.1/3.2 for strongly convex (SGD, SGD+momentum), Thm 3.3/3.4 for convex, Thm 3.5/3.6 for nonconvex. The momentum variants use an auxiliary sequence z_t := x_t − (β/(1−β))m_{t−1} and a Lyapunov function in the style of Liu et al. (2020) / Mai & Johansson (2020). A notable structural result (Lemma B.1) is that µ-strong convexity plus Hölder smoothness with ν<1 forces the domain to have bounded diameter (L/µ)^{1/(1−ν)} — so no whole-R^d function can satisfy both, which is why the strongly-convex momentum theorem needs a bounded-domain treatment.

Experiments use three d=500 synthetic objectives with controllable ν (a quadratic-plus-Hölder term, a separable Hölder term, and a Hölder Rastrigin), inject two-sided Pareto(α) heavy-tailed noise ∇f_{S_t} = ∇f + λω_t with α ∈ {1.1,…,2.0}, sweep 100 (ν,α) pairs over 4,000 steps × 20 seeds, and report heatmaps of final objective/gradient-norm. A real run trains a 6-layer 8-head ~26.9M-parameter Transformer-XL on WikiText-2 for 200 epochs comparing vanilla SGDM (best lr 0.1) against normalized SGDM (best lr 0.005), momentum 0.9.

## Results & Highlights

- **Nonconvex convergence rate (Thm 3.6, the headline result):** vanilla SGD with momentum achieves min_{t} E[∥∇f(x_t)∥²] = O(T^{−(p−1)/2p}) when p is known (η = T^{−1/p}) and O(T^{−(p−1)/4}) when p is unknown (η = T^{−1/2}); this is strictly worse than the optimal O(T^{−(p−1)/(3p−2)}) of normalized SGDM (Liu and Zhou, ICLR 2025), since (p−1)/(3p−2) ≥ (p−1)/2p for all p ∈ (1,2].
- **Strongly convex (Thm 3.1, vanilla SGD, ν+1=p case):** E[f(x_T) − f*] = O(1/T^{p−1}), matching the rate Fatkhullin et al. (NeurIPS-2025 Workshop) get for vanilla SGD but **without their bounded-gradient assumption**; for SGDM (Thm 3.2) the rate is O((1−η)^T + η^{p−1}), recovering the classical O((1−µη)^t + η) of Liu et al. (2020) when ν=1, p=2.
- **Convex (Thm 3.3/3.4, ν+1=p case):** vanilla SGD gives min_t E[f(x_t) − f*] = O(log T / T^{(p−1)/p}); SGDM gives O(1/(ηT) + η^{p−1}); Theorem 3.3 is acknowledged by the authors as an incremental improvement over Fatkhullin et al. (2025), while Theorem 3.4 (SGDM) is the first convex heavy-tailed momentum result.
- **Hölder-smoothness is necessary, not cosmetic:** the authors prove classical L-smoothness (ν=1) cannot satisfy ν+1 ≤ p for any strictly heavy-tailed p<2, and the strongly-convex+Hölder combination forces a bounded-domain (L/µ)^{1/(1−ν)} (Lemma B.1) — so the smoothness relaxation is forced by the noise model, not a free choice.
- **100-(ν,α) grid empirically confirms ν+1 ≤ p (Figure 1):** across strongly convex, convex, and nonconvex synthetic objectives the top-35 performing (ν,α) combinations cluster inside the ν+1 ≤ α lower-right triangle; markers reported are 1.3e-3 (strongly convex), 7.3e-3 (convex), and 4.0e+0 (nonconvex) final objective/gradient values, with performance degrading sharply outside the triangle, providing "strong empirical support" for the necessity of the condition.
- **WikiText-2 / Transformer-XL (Figure 3):** vanilla SGDM converges without any clipping or normalization on a 26.9M-parameter model where heavy-tailed gradient noise has been independently observed (Kunstner et al., 2023); normalized SGDM reaches a lower loss faster — a qualitative confirmation of the theoretical rate gap rather than a head-to-head benchmark win.
- **Trajectory stability tracks theory (Figure 2):** for fixed ν and decreasing α, average performance degrades and per-trial variance (shaded min/max over 20 seeds) grows; larger ν (closer to L-smoothness) produces more erratic loss curves under heavy tails (small α), while smaller ν stays stable — visually confirming that lower-order Hölder smoothness widens the guaranteed-convergence range.

## Authors & Credibility

The three authors (Ryusei Yamada, Naoki Sato, Hideaki Iiduka) are at Meiji University, Japan, and the senior author Hideaki Iiduka has a sustained track record on SGD-with-momentum convergence analysis (e.g., Kondo and Iiduka, 2025 on SGDM schedule analysis, also cited here as a methodological basis). The paper acknowledges funding from JSPS KAKENHI grants 24K14846 (Iiduka) and 26KJ2026 (Sato). The work is positioned as a direct response to two very recent 2025 papers — Fatkhullin et al. (NeurIPS-2025 Workshop, "Can SGD handle heavy-tailed noise?") and Liu and Zhou (ICLR 2025) — and the contribution claims are framed precisely against those: the momentum results are claimed as first-of-their-kind, while the convex vanilla-SGD theorem (Thm 3.3) is honestly flagged as incremental over Fatkhullin et al. The 31-page format (main + ~17 pages of appendix proofs for all six theorems and four lemmas) is appropriate for the density of the theory.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty — real but narrow.** The genuinely new results are the three momentum theorems (3.2, 3.4, 3.6); the authors concede Theorem 3.3 (convex vanilla SGD) is "incremental" over Fatkhullin et al. (2025, Theorem 3.2). The mechanical innovation is small — the Hölder-descent-lemma + von Bahr–Esseen + moment-lifting (Lemma 2.4) toolkit is assembled from known ingredients (Nesterov 2015 for Hölder descent, von Bahr–Esseen 1965, Liu et al. 2020 for the Lyapunov/auxiliary-sequence machinery). The conceptual contribution is the ν+1 ≤ p condition and the explicit demonstration that vanilla momentum is analyzable at all under heavy tails, which is a legitimate but specialized advance.
- **Rigor — strong on the theory, weaker on the empirics.** All six theorems have full appendix proofs; assumptions are stated cleanly and every rate is shown to degenerate to a known classical bound at ν=1, p=2, which is a good sanity check. However, the experiments are illustrative rather than rigorous: Figure 1's "top-35" claim is a threshold artifact (35 is chosen, not derived), there are no confidence intervals or significance tests on the heatmaps, and the 20-seed averaging is reported only as min/max envelopes.
- **A specific weak comparison.** The headline nonconvex rate O(T^{−(p−1)/2p}) is compared only against Liu and Zhou (2025) and the Zhang et al. (2020) lower bound; there is no empirical comparison to clipped SGD (Zhang et al. 2020) or to Cutkosky & Mehta (2021) momentum-with-clipping, even though those are the methods the paper argues against. The WikiText-2 run compares only normalized-SGDM vs vanilla-SGDM and reports no final perplexity, no test-loss, and no number of tokens — it is a qualitative convergence-plot demonstration, not a benchmark.
- **Missing ablations.** There is no sensitivity analysis to batch size b (which enters every rate via b^{p−1}), no study of how the dimension d=500 choice affects the Pareto-noise moment estimates, and no experiment varying the momentum β — despite β appearing explicitly in every momentum theorem's constants (e.g., E_2 = 2µ(ν+1)/(ν(1+Lν^{ν+1})), E_3 ∝ β/(1−β)).
- **A data/assumption quirk worth flagging.** Assumption 3.1 (bounded iterates ∥x_t − x*∥ ≤ D_1 and ∥z_t − x*∥ ≤ D_2) is required for the convex theorems 3.3–3.4; the authors argue this is "standard" and that it implicitly bounds the gradient, but it is an assumption the heavy-tailed-noise literature specifically tries to avoid, and it weakens the claim that the analysis is "without bounded-gradient assumptions" — the boundedness enters through the back door of bounded iterates.
- **Reproducibility — moderate.** Synthetic experiments are fully specified (the three objective functions, Pareto noise formula, d=500, λ values, step-size schedules) and would re-run cleanly; no code is mentioned as released, and the Transformer-XL configuration is given in enough detail to reproduce but uses no public benchmark numbers for comparison.
- **Significance — solid theoretical gap-filler, limited practical impact.** The practical message — "you don't strictly need clipping to converge under heavy tails, but you'll converge slower" — confirms what practitioners already do (clip/normalize). The value is as a baseline-completeness result and as the ν+1 ≤ p structural insight, which is transferable to analyzing Adam/Muon under heavy tails (the authors note this extension explicitly).

## Strengths

- Clean, complete theoretical story: one assumption framework (Hölder + p-th moment), one auxiliary lemma (3.1), six theorems that all recover classical bounds at ν=1, p=2, and a single structural condition (ν+1 ≤ p) tying smoothness to the noise tail.
- Honest positioning against the two strongest 2025 predecessors (Fatkhullin et al.; Liu and Zhou), with an explicit Table 1 comparing assumptions, clipping/norm/momentum flags, and rates reference-by-reference — the inferiority of vanilla's rates is stated plainly rather than hidden.
- The ν+1 ≤ p condition is a genuinely useful, transferable insight; the authors note their framework could extend to Adam and Muon under heavy-tailed noise, which is a concrete open direction.
- The incompatibility argument (Section 3.1) is crisp and does real work: it explains *why* the Hölder relaxation is necessary rather than decorative, which many smoothness-relaxation papers fail to motivate.
- Full appendix proofs (B.1–B.7) for every theorem and lemma make the theory auditable.

## Limitations

- All experiments are on synthetic functions plus one small (26.9M-parameter) language-model run; there are no modern deep-learning benchmarks (no ImageNet, no large LM pretraining), so the practical message "vanilla SGDM survives heavy tails but slower" is shown qualitatively, not benchmarked.
- The convex theorems (3.3, 3.4) require Assumption 3.1 (bounded iterates/auxiliary sequence), which is a boundedness condition smuggled in through the back door and partially undercuts the "no bounded-gradient assumption" headline claim.
- No comparison to clipped SGD or clipped-momentum baselines, either theoretically (beyond the rate table) or empirically — the methods the paper positions itself against are not directly benchmarked.
- Rates are strictly sub-optimal (vanilla's O(T^{−(p−1)/2p}) vs optimal O(T^{−(p−1)/(3p−2)})), so the result is a theoretical baseline rather than a method practitioners should adopt; the authors acknowledge this but it limits impact.
- The condition ν+1 ≤ p cannot be verified on real tasks because ν (the Hölder exponent of the loss) is not estimable in practice — the authors flag this explicitly, which is why all ν-verification is done on synthetic functions where ν is set by construction.
- No sensitivity analysis for batch size b (which scales every rate via b^{p−1}) or for the momentum coefficient β (which enters the constants of every momentum theorem), leaving the practical tuning implications of the theory unexplored.

## Relevance

对"ML 可靠性与数学基础"方向高度相关：它为重尾噪声（在 LLM 训练与强化学习中已被多次观测）下的优化器收敛性提供了不带任何梯度控制机制的严格期望保证，并量化了 vanilla SGDM 相对裁剪/归一化方法的速率差距，直接服务于训练稳定性与优化器理论分析。其 ν+1 ≤ p 条件与 Hölder 光滑框架对分析 Adam/Muon 等现代优化器在重尾噪声下的行为也有方法论价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can the framework extend to Adam and Muon (which the authors flag as a direction), and do those optimizers' per-coordinate second-moment estimates change the effective tail index p that governs convergence?
- Is the ν+1 ≤ p condition tight, or is it an artifact of the moment-lifting Lemma 2.4 — could a different proof technique recover convergence under L-smoothness (ν=1) without clipping, contradicting the incompatibility argument of Section 3.1?
- How do the rates scale with batch size b in practice (every bound carries a b^{p−1} factor), and does large-batch training make vanilla SGDM competitive with normalized variants under heavy tails?
- On a real large-scale LM pretraining run with measured heavy-tailed noise (à la Simsekli 2019 / Kunstner 2023), what is the empirical convergence-gap between vanilla SGDM and normalized SGDM, and does it match the predicted O(T^{−(p−1)/2p}) vs O(T^{−(p−1)/(3p−2)}) ratio?
- Can Assumption 3.1 (bounded iterates) be removed for the convex case, or is there a lower bound showing some boundedness condition is unavoidable for vanilla (non-clipped) methods under heavy-tailed noise?
