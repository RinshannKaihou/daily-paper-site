CausalDS: Benchmarking Causal Reasoning in Data-Science Agents

arXiv: 2607.08093

Date: 2026-07-10

https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08093

一句话总结： CausalDS 构建了一个因果数据科学 Agent 基准——通过合成隐藏的 SCM（含观测噪声层）生成场景，并以确定化方式打分 Pearl 三阶梯上的任务（含可识别性弃答），评测发现六款前沿模型在内容正确性上接近完美，但在不确定度校准与"该弃答时弃答"两类认知能力上差异巨大，Claude Opus 4.8 以 CausalDSScore 0.278 综合最优。
TL;DR: CausalDS is a benchmark that evaluates causal reasoning in data-science agents by generating synthetic scenes from hidden SCMs with a noisy observation layer, then deterministically scoring tasks across Pearl's three rungs (R1–R3) including first-class abstention on non-identifiable queries. On a 100-scene realistic exam over 6 models, Claude Opus 4.8 leads with CausalDSScore 0.278 and is the only model with perfect content Pass Rate (100%, 18/18); GPT-5.5 ties Claude on Pass Rate (82.4%) but ranks 5th (0.561) because its SNR of 1.324 is the worst calibrated; nominal 95% ATE intervals cover only 20.0%–71.4% of ground truth, exposing systematic overconfidence.

核心贡献

  • SCM 驱动的合成场景，经验分布锚定成分：采样隐藏因果图、实例化带类型化机制的结构因果模型（连续 + 二元）、生成观测数据并计算私有的真值，整体生成器全合成，但成分（变量类型、图结构、可识别性、机制谱、观测复杂度）可锚定到真实数据集的经验分布，降低"因果鹦鹉"（记忆型作弊）风险。
  • 图忠诚的自由文本叙述：将抽象图节点映射为领域变量（部分用 CauseNet 播种），并通过 mapper-auditor 循环迭代校验，使生成故事既自然流畅又忠实于隐藏图。
  • 独立的观测层：在固定的概念 DAG 之上叠加噪声测量层（clean / proxy / proxy_hard），改变数据分析的数值难度而不改变因果可识别性，从而把"估计难度"与"因果结构"解耦。
  • 覆盖 Pearl 三阶梯的宽任务集：11 类问题族（预测、关联、对撞、因果骨架、识别、效应估计、偏置诊断、反事实识别/效应、中介分析），同一隐藏 SCM 共享语言理解—统计估计—形式因果推理。
  • 弃答感知的确定化评测：对不可识别任务将"弃答"作为一等评分对象，按任务族使用 RMSE / ROC-AUC / Brier / log-loss / F1 / 区间得分等指标确定化打分，并定义 CausalDSScore、Composite Rank 等汇总量。

Key Contributions

  • SCM-grounded synthetic scenes with empirically anchored composition: a fully synthetic generator samples hidden causal DAGs, instantiates typed-mechanism SCMs, generates observational data, and computes private ground truth; the scene composition is anchored along empirical axes (variable type, graph structure, identifiability, mechanism profile, observation complexity) drawn from real-world datasets, mitigating the "causal parrot" contamination risk.
  • Graph-faithful free-form verbalization: abstract nodes are mapped to domain variables (optionally seeded from CauseNet) and a mapper-auditor loop iteratively refines names and stories so that the natural-language prose is audited against the hidden graph.
  • A separate observation layer that decouples estimation difficulty from causal structure: the conceptual DAG and identifiability labels are fixed before a noisy measurement layer (clean / proxy / proxy_hard variants) is applied; measurement bundles never create or remove confounders, instruments, or mediators.
  • A broad causal data-science task suite spanning Pearl's three rungs: 11 question families (prediction, association, collider phenomenon, causal sketch, identification, effect estimation, bias diagnostics, counterfactual identification/effects, mediation) — all sharing one hidden SCM so language interpretation, statistical estimation, and formal causal reasoning are jointly exercised.
  • Abstention-aware deterministic evaluation: non-identifiable queries are a first-class scored outcome via mutually-exclusive routing; per-task atomic metrics (RMSE, ROC-AUC, Brier, log-loss, F1, interval score) are pooled into a CausalDSScore composite and an exam-relative Composite Rank.

Methodology

A CausalDS scene consists of a narrative story, a tabular dataset, a lightweight schema, and a list of tasks, with private ground truth stored separately. The generation pipeline runs in order:

  1. Graph sampling — start from canonical motifs (chain, fork, confounding, IV, etc.) and assemble larger dynamic DAGs by anchor-based grafting, attaching small auxiliary motifs through exactly one shared node while preserving narrative coherence. Graph properties are calibrated against empirical histograms from real-world causal datasets.
  2. SCM instantiation — typed SCM profiles (continuous and binary registries) mix empirical mechanism families (from biochemical-equation and Boolean-rule corpora) with synthetic ones (neural-network mixture-noise, sharp-threshold). Additive (continuous) or single-link (binary) noise keeps the SCM suitable for Monte-Carlo evaluation of treatment effects, mediation, and counterfactuals.
  3. Observation layer — optionally withhold a subset of conceptual variables and replace each with a bundle of noisy measurements W_jr = h_jr(Z_j) + ε. The conceptual estimand and its identifiability are preserved; only estimation difficulty changes. Variants are screened by bundle Fisher information to keep inference feasible.
  4. Variable mapping and story generation — an LLM maps node IDs to domain names (CauseNet-seeded) and writes a story, refined by an iterative mapper-auditor loop; grafted fragments are grounded stage-wise with the shared anchor immutable.
  5. Task and ground-truth computation — Rung-2 identifiability is decided with DoWhy; Rung-3 identifiability with the ID/IDC algorithms (y0). Tasks span all three rungs and per-rung output variants (point/interval, sign/strength, etc.).
  6. Packaging — public artifacts (story, parquet files, schema, optional calibration set) go to the agent; private artifacts (ground truth, observation diagnostics, hidden test labels) go to the grader.

Evaluation harness: models run via mini-swe-agent, emitting bash commands inside a sandboxed Docker container with network disabled and the standard data-science stack installed. Each task is a fresh agent conversation with a 100-step limit and a per-task cost cap of $10; full trajectories are logged.

Scoring is fully deterministic. For each task the grader parses the agent's answer file and computes a task-specific atomic metric. Non-identifiable instances use mutually-exclusive routing: a task is graded as a binary whenever either side abstains (ground truth non-identifiable or model wrongly abstains); otherwise it is graded by its content metric. Headline composites include Pass Rate (binary content + abstention), SNR (continuous relative-error component, Eq. 3), Med. F1-Loss, and the single-number CausalDSScore = wp(1−PassRate) + wr·SNR + wf·Med.F1-Loss (lower is better), complemented by an exam-relative Composite Rank.

The reported exam is a 100-scene realistic-composition subset drawn from a 953-scene dataset, tilted toward real-world task-family weights (28 R1, 51 R2, 21 R3 tasks; observation split 48/32/20 clean/proxy/proxy_hard; 59 continuous / 41 binary outcomes).

Results & Highlights

  • Claude Opus 4.8 leads the field with CausalDSScore 0.278, the best Pass Rate (82.4%), the best Med. NRel. Err (0.179), and the best SNR (0.566), and is the only model with a perfect content Pass Rate (100%, 18/18) on the realistic exam (Tab. 3).
  • GPT-5.5 ties Claude on raw Pass Rate (82.4%) yet ranks only 5th on CausalDSScore (0.561) because its continuous estimates are the worst calibrated of the field: SNR = 1.324, with mean NRel. Err of 3.10 on the proxy_hard subset (vs. a median of 0.222) — a handful of estimates on noise-corrupted data miss by orders of magnitude (Tab. 3, Tab. 6).
  • ATE interval calibration collapses: empirical coverage of nominal 95% ATE intervals ranges only 20.0%–71.4% across the six models (n=7 per model), with Claude Opus 4.8 best at 71.4% and Qwen 3.6 35B worst at 20.0% — strong evidence of systematic overconfidence (Fig. 3a, Tab. 6).
  • Content vs. abstention dissociate: content Pass Rate is uniformly high (81.0%–100.0%), but abstention Pass Rate spans 18.8%–75.0%; the three frontier models lead abstention (GPT-5.5 75.0%, Claude 62.5%, Gemini 56.2%) while open models trail (Qwen 41.2%, Kimi 38.9%, Gemma 18.8%) (Tab. 4).
  • Observation hardness hurts weak models disproportionately: Gemma 4 26B's mean NRel. Err on proxy_hard blows up to 4.59 against a median of 0.348 (≈13× gap); Claude Opus 4.8 is the only model that keeps the mean controlled at 0.557 (median 0.183) under the hardest observation (Tab. 6).
  • Tool-use efficiency linearly separates the two model classes in the cost–quality plane: GPT-5.5 (2.1 bash calls/task) and Claude Opus 4.8 (3.4) are near one-shot, while the open models iterate heavily (11–18 calls/task); token usage spans an order of magnitude from 12.9k (GPT-5.5) to 266.4k (Kimi K2.6) (Fig. 4).
  • Reliability vs. capability separate under repeated attempts: on n=34 binary tasks, pass@3 reaches 76.5%–91.2% (Kimi 91.2%, numerically matching the best frontier single-shot rate) while pass^3 is only 50.0%–61.8% — a 26–29 point best-case/worst-case gap; the CausalDSScore-level relative SD across 3 restarts is 7%–27%, concentrated in interval estimands and abstention flips rather than point estimates (Tab. 7).

Authors & Credibility

The paper has two authors, both from the Department of Statistics at the University of Michigan (Andrej Leban, Yuekai Sun). The technical depth is consistent with a statistics-group origin: correct use of Pearl's three-rung hierarchy, the ID/ID/IDC identifiability algorithms (via the y0 library), DoWhy for ATE identifiability, the interval score (Gneiting & Raftery, 2007) for proper interval scoring, and Fisher-information screening of observation bundles. The benchmark is released as open code (github.com/andleb/causalds), and the generation-side LLM (Kimi 2.5) and all evaluation settings (step limit 100, cost cap $10, Docker isolation, per-task token logs) are disclosed. The related-work table (Tab. 2) systematically compares against six close benchmarks (CauSciBench, CausalReasoningBenchmark, CausalProfiler, CausalGame, CausaLab, CLadder) across eight design axes, with concurrent work flagged under a May 1, 2026 cutoff.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: Genuine integration is the real contribution — no single prior benchmark combines graph-audited free-form stories + SCM-generated data files + a noisy-measurement observation layer + full Pearl-ladder tasks with deterministic scoring + first-class non-identifiability abstention. However, each individual ingredient (synthetic SCMs, NL verbalization, R1–R3 QA, abstention scoring) exists in prior work (CLadder, CausalProfiler, CausaLab); the novelty is compositional.
  • Rigor: Scoring is deterministic and the abstention-routing rule is clean. But the headline CausalDSScore is explicitly acknowledged to be "dominated, by construction, by whichever pool is largest unless re-weighted," and the authors themselves rely on Composite Rank to defuse this — an admission that the single-number summary is fragile. The exam is small (100 scenes, only 7 identifiable ATE-UQ tasks per model), so per-axis numbers (e.g., the 71.4% coverage claim) rest on tiny n; the pass@3/pass^3 spread (7%–27% relative SD on CausalDSScore) further indicates that several reported gaps are within run-to-run noise.
  • Reproducibility: Generator and harness are open-sourced, settings are documented, and trajectories are logged. But the results depend on a specific generation-side LLM (Kimi 2.5) for story verbalization, and a "verbalization-swap ablation" is used to argue robustness only for "strong-enough models" — the boundary of that claim is not quantified, and contamination of generation-time LLM knowledge into the evaluation set is not fully closed.
  • Significance: The empirical dissociation finding (models master content but split on calibration and abstention, with the frontier/open-weight gap living on the epistemic axes) is the most valuable takeaway and aligns with independent uncertainty-elicitation benchmarks the authors cite (GPT-5-family 9–44% coverage on Bayesian elicitation).
  • Red flags: (1) Tiny per-axis sample sizes (n=3 to n=7 for some observation-hardest and ATE-UQ cells) make several headline differentiators statistically shaky. (2) The observation-layer ablation in Tabs. 5–6 is "naturally aggregated over different sub-exam slice compositions," admitted in text and only repaired in an appendix matched-scene ablation — a presentation weakness. (3) The evaluated models are named with future-version labels (Claude Opus 4.8, GPT-5.5, Gemini 3.1 Pro, Kimi K2.6, Qwen 3.6 35B, Gemma 4 26B) that are not independently verifiable as of the arXiv date, so the absolute numbers are hard to externally confirm.

Strengths

  • Jointly evaluates five normally-separated axes (symbolic causal reasoning, data-science execution, uncertainty quantification, epistemic abstention, tool use/coding) within a single, coherent scene format.
  • The observation-layer design cleanly decouples estimation difficulty from causal identifiability — a principled way to scale data-science hardness without touching the causal ground truth.
  • First-class abstention scoring on deliberately non-identifiable queries is a meaningful and under-explored evaluation axis that the paper shows is the live discriminator among contemporary agents.
  • Deterministic, file-backed grading with full trajectory logging supports reproducibility and fine-grained error analysis.
  • The empirical dissociation finding (content ≈ solved; calibration + abstention = the real frontier/open-weight divide) is a concrete, actionable insight for agent developers.

Limitations

  • The realistic exam is small (100 scenes); several per-axis cells have n in the single digits (e.g., n=7 for ATE-UQ coverage, n=3 for proxy_hard pools for some models), so many reported gaps carry high variance.
  • The headline CausalDSScore is pool-size-sensitive and dominated by the largest pool; the authors themselves complement it with Composite Rank, signaling that the single number is not robust.
  • Run-to-run variance of open-weight models is non-trivial (CausalDSScore relative SD 7%–27%), concentrated in interval and abstention tasks; some leaderboard ordering may not be stable.
  • Story generation depends on a generation-side LLM (Kimi 2.5); residual leakage of that model's world knowledge into the released scenes is not fully excluded, and the verbalization-swap robustness claim is qualified to "strong-enough models."
  • The motif catalog is bounded (a handful of motifs carry too few tasks to support conclusions), and the benchmark does not yet cover some realistic complications (e.g., time-series causality, selection bias at the measurement level, proximal-causal-inference regimes the observation layer is at pains to distinguish itself from).
  • Author-acknowledged future work: larger and more varied exam compositions, deeper trajectory-level failure taxonomies, and systematic targeted exams for abstention, quantitative skills, and counterfactual reasoning are not yet delivered.

Relevance

This work is directly relevant to research on LLM agents and agent evaluation benchmarks: it provides a principled, contamination-resistant testbed that jointly probes causal reasoning, data-science coding, uncertainty calibration, and the under-studied "knowing when to abstain" skill — exactly the axis on which current frontier agents most clearly separate from open-weight ones. For anyone building or evaluating tool-using data-science agents, the dissociation finding (content solved, calibration and abstention not) is a concrete diagnostic to track.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How do the reported gaps (especially abstention Pass Rate and ATE-UQ coverage) behave as n scales to thousands of scenes — do the frontier/open-weight differences remain statistically significant, or does the small-n variance widen confidence intervals past the gaps?
  • Can targeted ablations isolate which component of CausalDS scenes drives abstention failures — the causal-identifiability reasoning, the language interpretation, or the interaction with noisy measurement data?
  • Would adding a proximal-causal-inference observation regime (where measurement bundles genuinely contribute to identification) change the ranking, given the authors are careful to keep their observation layer identification-neutral?
  • How transferable is the CausalDS evaluation protocol (hidden SCM + observation layer + abstention routing) to non-tabular modalities such as time-series, text-attribute, or multimodal data-science workflows?
  • To what extent does the generation-side LLM (Kimi 2.5) used for story verbalization leak into evaluation, and can a fully template- or symbolic-only verbalization close that residual contamination?
  • Do the run-to-run abstention flips and interval-estimate swings (the dominant source of CausalDSScore variance) collapse under better uncertainty-elicitation prompting or self-consistency, suggesting the failures are elicitation artifacts rather than capability limits?
