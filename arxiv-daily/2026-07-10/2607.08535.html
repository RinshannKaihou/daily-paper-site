When the Judge Changes, So Does the Measurement: Auditing LLM-as-Judge Reliability

arXiv: 2607.08535

Date: 2026-07-10

https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08535

一句话总结： 本文将"换一个评判模型分数就会变"的模糊性视为测量效度问题，沿 Qwen3 参数缩放（1.7B→32B）和 MiniMax 发布代际（M2→M2.7）两条轴审计 LLM-as-Judge 可靠性，发现只有 Qwen3 1.7B→4B 一段相邻提升稳健显著，强模型仍残留 14.7% 的位置翻转偏差，陪审投票因误差高相关（ρ 高达 0.972）几乎无效，辩论虽能造成大幅判据迁移但缺乏解析日志故不可归因——据此提出含数据切片/偏差探针/误差相关性/协议审计的报告标准。
TL;DR: This paper reframes LLM-as-judge score changes under evaluator replacement as a measurement-validity problem, and audits eight judges (Qwen3 1.7B–32B and MiniMax M2–M2.7) across LLMBar, PandaLM, Chatbot Arena, and Judge's Verdict. Only the Qwen3 1.7B→4B adjacent step survives Holm correction over 18 tests (e.g., LLMBar 0.463→0.617, +15.4 pts); no MiniMax adjacent release is significant (all deltas ≤ 0.022 accuracy). Stronger judges cut position-flip rate (0.320→0.117–0.147) and verbosity bias (0.547→~0.13) but MiniMax-M2.7 still flips 14.7% of verdicts under A/B reversal; majority-vote juries add almost nothing because error correlation reaches ρ = 0.944–0.972 for Qwen3; and structured debate shifts accuracy by up to +0.317 (Qwen3-1.7B + GLM-5.1) but, with no parser/fallback logs, those gains cannot be attributed to deliberation.

核心贡献

  • 提出将 LLM-as-Judge 可靠性视为多维测量属性，而非单一准确率；构建可审计框架，将可靠性拆解为"判断效度、偏差鲁棒性、聚合独立性、协议可审计性"四个分量（Table 1），每一项映射到具体实验与可报告指标。
  • 设计两条互补的评估者替换轴：Qwen3 参数缩放轴（1.7B/4B/14B/32B）与 MiniMax 发布代际轴（M2/M2.1/M2.5/M2.7 API），明确避免对 MiniMax 内部做因果断言，只比较"在相同数据与提示下的相邻变化模式"。
  • 实证发现三条主结论：(1) 评判升级不可互换——18 个相邻 McNemar 检验经 Holm 校正后，仅 Qwen3 1.7B→4B 在 LLMBar 与 Arena 上显著，九个 MiniMax 相邻检验无一达到未校正 p<0.05；(2) 强模型减小但未消除偏差；(3) 陪审团受限于误差相关，辩论迁移大但缺审计。
  • 估算并验证同质陪审团的类内误差相关 ρ，用 beta-binomial 修正预测将 LLMBar/PandaLM 的中位绝对误差从 0.078/0.093 降到 0.008/0.004，证明"陪审团规模"不是合适的可靠度放大器，真正的设计问题是"误差有多独立"。
  • 提炼出最小化审计清单（Table 4）：报告有效 N、可解析 N、平局处理、解析成功率与回退规则、A/B 反转、padding 探针、ρ 与 K（而非仅 K）、原始输出/解析状态/各轮裁决、配对检验与 Holm 校正——把"看似可靠的分数"还原为可审计的流水线产物。

Key Contributions

  • Reframes evaluator-replacement ambiguity (a score that moves when only the judge changes) as a measurement-validity problem, and operationalizes LLM-as-judge reliability as four observable constructs — judgment validity, bias robustness, aggregation independence, and protocol auditability — each tied to a concrete metric and experiment (Table 1).
  • Runs a two-axis evaluator study that deliberately separates an approximate parameter intervention (Qwen3 1.7B/4B/14B/32B) from an observed released-model intervention (MiniMax M2/M2.1/M2.5/M2.7 APIs), while explicitly disclaiming causal claims about MiniMax training internals.
  • Shows empirically that the two axes are not interchangeable: across 18 adjacent McNemar tests with Holm correction, only the Qwen3 1.7B→4B steps on LLMBar and Arena remain significant, and none of the nine MiniMax adjacent-release tests reaches uncorrected p<0.05 (deltas ≤ 0.022 accuracy).
  • Demonstrates that error correlation, not jury size, governs aggregation gains: the independence assumption has median absolute error 0.078 on LLMBar, whereas the ρ-corrected beta-binomial prediction drops it to 0.008, reframing the design question from "how many jurors?" to "how independent are their errors?"
  • Distills a minimal audit-trail reporting standard (Table 4) — valid/parseable N, tie handling, parser success rate and fallback rule, A/B reversal and padding probes, ρ alongside K, raw outputs and per-round verdicts, paired tests with Holm correction — so that apparent reliability is not an artifact of the parsing/aggregation pipeline.

Methodology

The paper treats LLM-as-judge reliability as a multi-dimensional measurement property rather than a single accuracy score, organized around four constructs mapped to four experiments (Table 1): judgment validity (accuracy, Cohen's κ, Spearman — Exp. 1), bias robustness (position-flip, verbosity, granularity — Exp. 2), aggregation independence (error correlation ρ and jury gain — Exp. 3), and protocol auditability (parse status, fallback behavior, intermediate verdicts — Exp. 4).

The evaluated panel comprises eight judges split across two axes: the Qwen3 parameter axis (1.7B, 4B, 14B, 32B dense models) and the MiniMax release-generation axis (M2, M2.1, M2.5, M2.7 APIs, evaluated as released). GLM-5.1 and mimo-v2-pro serve as cross-family reference judges in selected experiments but are not members of either axis. The authors are explicit that the Qwen3 axis approximates a parameter sweep, while the MiniMax axis is an observed release sequence rather than a controlled ablation of MiniMax internals.

Four datasets are chosen to span complementary judgment settings so reliability is not inferred from one slice: LLMBar (419 adversarial pairwise examples), PandaLM testset-v1 (894 valid pairwise examples after removing tie-majority cases), a seed-42 2,000-example sample of Chatbot Arena (1,997 valid pairwise), and a 200-example Judge's Verdict slice derived from TechQA with three-level pointwise labels.

Pairwise experiments report accuracy and Cohen's κ on parseable verdicts; Judge's Verdict additionally reports Spearman rank correlation. Main single-judge runs use near-greedy decoding (T = 0.1) to reduce sampling noise. Homogeneous juries sample one judge at T = 0.7 (low temperature would understate dependence among stochastic jurors); heterogeneous juries combine different judges at T = 0.1 to isolate model composition from sampling variance. Adjacent model comparisons use exact two-sided McNemar tests on parse-shared examples, so significance reflects paired judgment changes rather than parser-coverage differences; Holm correction controls the family of 18 adjacent tests.

For the jury experiment, the authors estimate the intra-class error correlation ρ from the vote matrix and compare three quantities: the independence (Poisson-binomial) prediction, a ρ-corrected beta-binomial prediction (with α = ps, β = (1−p)s, s = 1/ρ − 1), and observed jury accuracy. The structured-debate experiment pairs two judges for up to three rebuttal rounds on LLMBar and is reported as an auditability case study rather than evidence of a deliberation effect.

Four robustness checks are included: human-ceiling calibration (leave-one-annotator-out), corrected adjacent significance, limited prompt sensitivity, and Arena seed stability.

Results & Highlights

  • Judge upgrades are not interchangeable (Finding 1). On LLMBar, Qwen3 improves sharply from 1.7B to 4B: accuracy 0.463 → 0.617 (+15.4 pts); the 1.7B→32B span reaches 0.678 (+21.5 pts). After Holm correction across 18 adjacent tests, only the Qwen3 1.7B→4B steps on LLMBar and Arena remain significant; all nine MiniMax adjacent-release tests are non-significant with deltas at most 0.022 accuracy (e.g., MiniMax M2→M2.1 LLMBar 0.829→0.839, McNemar p = 0.488).
  • Best judge is dataset-specific. No single judge wins all slices: GLM-5.1 leads LLMBar (0.900 [0.868, 0.925]) and Judge's Verdict (0.680), MiniMax-M2.7 leads PandaLM (0.857 [0.833, 0.878]), and mimo-v2-pro leads the sampled Arena slice (0.742 [0.722, 0.761]).
  • Stronger judges reduce but do not remove bias (Finding 2). The position-flip rate falls from 0.320 (Qwen3-1.7B) to 0.117–0.147 (MiniMax releases); verbosity bias under a fixed generic padding string falls from 0.547 (Qwen3-1.7B) to ~0.13 (MiniMax). The capability–fairness association is strong (Pearson r = −0.957 between LLMBar accuracy and position-flip rate across eight judges), but MiniMax-M2.7 still changes 14.7% of verdicts under A/B reversal.
  • Jury size matters less than error dependence (Finding 3). The independence assumption has median absolute error 0.078 on LLMBar and 0.093 on PandaLM; the ρ-corrected beta-binomial prediction reduces these to 0.008 and 0.004 (max error < 0.02). Estimated correlations are very high: Qwen3 homogeneous juries ρ = 0.944–0.972 on LLMBar; MiniMax ρ = 0.664–0.706. Consequently, increasing jury size barely moves accuracy — for Qwen3-1.7B on LLMBar, K = 1, 3, 5 yields 0.463, 0.475, 0.482.
  • Debate shifts are large but unauditable (Finding 4). Cross-capability pairs produce the biggest final-vs-round-1 shifts: Qwen3-1.7B paired with GLM-5.1, MiniMax-M2.7, or mimo-v2-pro moves final decisions by +0.317, +0.305, and +0.289 toward higher accuracy; Qwen3-4B + GLM-5.1 shifts by +0.243 to reach 0.897. Same-family and top-vs-top shifts are only +0.005 to +0.050. However, the implementation records round and final verdicts but not raw responses or parse-success flags, and parse failures fall back to A or retain the previous verdict — so the shifts cannot be attributed to deliberation.
  • Human-ceiling context. On PandaLM the best judge reaches leave-one-annotator-out κ = 0.753 versus a human ceiling of 0.920 (headroom remains); on Judge's Verdict best-judge κ = 0.620 versus human κ = 0.562 (judges already exceed a noisy ceiling). Arena seed perturbations produce only 0.004–0.032 accuracy spread, smaller than the robust Qwen3 1.7B→4B Arena gap.

The paper cites 24 references spanning the LLM-judge literature (MT-Bench, Chatbot Arena, PandaLM, JudgeLM, G-Eval, Prometheus, RewardBench), bias and protocol studies, Condorcet jury theory, and the underlying models (Qwen3 Technical Report, MiniMax-M2 Series). The framing is appropriately cautious: the authors repeatedly disclaim causal claims about MiniMax internals and explicitly label the debate experiment as a non-causal stress test. The statistical machinery (McNemar paired tests, Holm correction, Wilson intervals, ρ-corrected beta-binomial) is competent and above the norm for short evaluation papers.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty (moderate): The reframing of evaluator-replacement as a "measurement-validity" problem and the four-construct audit framework (Table 1) is a useful conceptual contribution, and the explicit comparison of a parameter axis against a release-generation axis is uncommon. However, several headline findings are confirmatory rather than surprising — that juries fail under correlated errors is a direct consequence of Condorcet-with-correlation (Ladha 1992, cited as [10]), and that stronger judges retain nonzero position bias recapitulates prior work [16, 17, 20]. The genuine novelty is in bundling these into one auditable protocol and quantifying the ρ-correction.
  • Rigor (mixed): The paired McNemar-on-parse-shared design and Holm correction over 18 tests are sound and prevent the most common overclaim (treating parser-coverage differences as model differences). But the central cross-axis comparison is structurally asymmetric and the authors know it: the Qwen3 axis is an approximate parameter sweep, whereas the MiniMax axis is a black-box API sequence that is explicitly "not a controlled ablation." This means the negative MiniMax result ("no adjacent release significant") is weak evidence — adjacent MiniMax releases differ along many training dimensions simultaneously, so a null adjacent-difference says little about whether capability drives reliability. A formal between-axis test is absent ("McNemar tests are adjacent-pair tests and do not constitute a formal between-axis test").
  • Reproducibility (limited for one axis, fair for the other): Qwen3 weights are open and decoding temperatures, seeds (Arena seed-42), and intervals are reported, so the parameter axis is largely reproducible. The MiniMax axis depends on proprietary APIs whose behavior can drift, making exact replication impossible by construction. Prompts and the exact Judge's Verdict slice construction are under-specified in the text. Crucially, the debate experiment cannot be re-audited because raw responses and parse-success flags were not logged — the authors flag this themselves.
  • Significance (genuinely useful for practice): The minimal audit-trail checklist (Table 4) and the "report ρ, not just K" recommendation are immediately actionable and would raise the floor of LLM-as-judge reporting if adopted. The ρ-corrected beta-binomial reduction of jury-prediction error (0.078→0.008 on LLMBar) is the single most concrete methodological payoff.
  • Red flags / overclaiming risks: (1) The only robust adjacent effect is the lowest-end jump (Qwen3 1.7B→4B), which is the smallest capability delta on the parameter axis — generalization to mid- and high-end scaling is not demonstrated. (2) The debate experiment is presented as a "Finding" (Finding 4) yet is simultaneously declared non-causal and unauditable; including it as a headline result slightly overstates its evidentiary weight even with the caveats. (3) The panel is small (two families, four datasets, two reference judges), and on Judge's Verdict stronger Qwen3 judges actually get worse (0.595→0.530), a counter-trend the paper notes but does not deeply explain. (4) Sample sizes and parser coverage differ across datasets, so cross-dataset comparisons of significance are not strictly commensurable.

Strengths

  • Honest, self-critical framing: the authors repeatedly distinguish what their design can and cannot support (e.g., "this paper does not make a causal claim about MiniMax internals"; debate results are "suggestive … not as a clean estimate of deliberation").
  • The four-construct operationalization (Table 1) and the minimal audit checklist (Table 4) are concrete, immediately usable artifacts that practitioners can adopt.
  • The ρ-corrected beta-binomial jury analysis is the methodological highlight — it converts the abstract "errors are correlated" warning into a quantified, validated correction with near-perfect prediction fit.
  • Statistically careful: parse-shared McNemar tests, Holm correction over 18 tests, Wilson intervals, and explicit human-ceiling calibration via leave-one-annotator-out κ guard against the most common evaluation-paper pitfalls.
  • The "no judge wins all datasets" result is a healthy antidote to one-dimensional judge leaderboards.

Limitations

  • Small panel: only two model families, two cross-family reference judges, four datasets, a small number of prompts, and one primary decoding regime per experiment — generalization to other families, languages, or instruction distributions is unproven.
  • The MiniMax axis is a confounded release sequence, not an ablation; the null adjacent result is therefore weak evidence about capability-reliability relationships.
  • The debate experiment lacks the parser/fallback audit trail needed to interpret its large shifts; Finding 4 is effectively a methodological plea rather than evidence.
  • The only robust adjacent effect (Qwen3 1.7B→4B) is the lowest-end transition; no mid- or high-end adjacent step survives correction, so the paper cannot say where (or whether) further scaling pays off.
  • Reliability proxies are incomplete — calibration, long-form rationale quality, and domain-specific consistency are not covered (acknowledged under construct validity).
  • Parser coverage and sample sizes differ across datasets, making cross-dataset significance comparisons imprecise.

Relevance

对关注 LLM 评测与对齐的人而言，本文直接回答"换 judge 后分数变动能否被信任"这一日常痛点，其 ρ 修正陪审团方法、A/B 反转与 padding 偏差探针、以及最小化审计清单可立即纳入自建 eval pipeline，用来检测自家评测被偏差/解析回退/相关投票污染的程度，是做 model evaluation 或 reward model 选型时值得引用的方法论参考。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does error correlation ρ behave across heterogeneous cross-family juries at scale — does mixing truly independent families (e.g., Qwen + Llama + GLM) restore the independence assumption enough to make large-K juries worthwhile, and is there a principled ρ-target for jury design?
  • Would logging raw debate responses and parse-success flags change the Finding-4 shifts, or do most gains come from the fallback rule defaulting to the stronger judge rather than from genuine rebuttal?
  • The only robust effect is Qwen3 1.7B→4B; does the "low-end transition is where reliability gains concentrate" pattern replicate for other families (e.g., Llama-3 1B→3B→8B), or is it specific to Qwen3's pre-training curriculum?
  • On Judge's Verdict, stronger Qwen3 judges get worse (0.595→0.530) — is this a pointwise-format mismatch, a calibration failure, or evidence that "capability" and "reliability" decouple for non-pairwise rubrics?
  • Can the ρ-corrected beta-binomial model be inverted to give a sample-size/K recommendation given a measured ρ, turning the audit metric into an a-priori jury-design tool?
  • How sensitive are the bias probes (position-flip, verbosity) to the specific padding string and prompt template — the paper uses one fixed generic padding; does a worst-case adversary push even MiniMax-M2.7's 14.7% flip rate substantially higher?
