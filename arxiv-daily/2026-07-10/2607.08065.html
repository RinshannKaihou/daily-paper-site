When LLMs Agree, Are They Right? Auditing Self-Consistency and Cross-Model Agreement as Confidence Signals

arXiv: 2607.08065

Date: 2026-07-10

https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08065

一句话总结： 通过对 GPQA Diamond 和 AIME 两个基准的 26.5 万次采样的大规模跨执行者审计，本文证明 LLM 自洽性（self-consistency）只是一个"与机制相关、强度微弱"的正确性代理信号——在一致性最高的前沿模型（gpt-4.1, C=0.89）上反而最不可靠（高一致性答案 48% 是错的），因此不应被当作可信的置信度阈值或路由依据。
TL;DR: A large-scale (265,000-sample), cross-runner audit of self-consistency as a confidence signal on GPQA Diamond and AIME finds that agreement is a positive but weak predictor of correctness (ρ≈0.20–0.59), worst for the most consistent frontier model: a high-agreement (C≥0.8) gpt-4.1 answer on GPQA is wrong 48% of the time, and a confidence-routed cascade (peak 0.36) is dominated by simply always using the mid-tier model (0.40).

核心贡献

  • 大规模跨执行者审计设计：53 名执行者对 GPQA Diamond 和 AIME 共抽 K=50 样本，总计 26.5 万次采样、5,300 条 case-result 行、394 个独立题目；采用分层 runner-clustered bootstrap（B=2000）与 Holm 多重比较校正，将"跨执行者/提示词复现的自信错误"与"采样噪声"区分开。
  • 前沿模型反而最不可靠的实证：在 gpt-4.1 家族内，最一致的前沿模型 gpt-4.1（C=0.89）的 ρ(C,M) 最低（GPQA 0.20），且高一致性答案 48% 是错的——量化了"后训练校准回退"如何瓦解自洽性作为部署信号的能力。
  • 复发 vs 随机错误的分离 + 选项位置对照：GPQA 上 28% 的题目被每个执行者都自信答错；选项打乱对照显示部分"自信"源于位置先验（"D"被系统性低估，准确率 0.50→0.29）。
  • 诚实的零/边际结果：CoT 显著提升准确率（∆A=+0.067）但对部署标签 M 与自洽性信号仅边际改善；置信度路由级联（0.36）被"始终用中端模型"（0.40）压制。
  • 跨家族探索性检查：三个 Claude tier（haiku/sonnet/opus）重现"前沿过度自信"模式，且 gpt-4.1 与 Claude 在两者都答错的题目上显著倾向于选择同一错误选项（haiku p=.003, sonnet p=.005），提示自信错误部分是跨提供者的共享偏差。
  • 开放数据与方法：发布去标识化的 per-runner–per-case 数据与 schema 校验分析流水线（不含 GPQA 题目文本）。

Key Contributions

  • A large-scale (265,000-sample) cross-runner audit of self-consistency as a confidence/abstention/routing signal on GPQA Diamond and AIME, using a hierarchical runner-clustered bootstrap (B=2000) with Holm multiplicity control, with replication designed to separate recurring confident errors from sampling noise.
  • Evidence that the agreement signal degrades on the most consistent frontier model: gpt-4.1 is the most self-consistent (C=0.89) yet has the lowest ρ(C,M) and no accuracy advantage, consistent with documented post-RLHF calibration regression—making confidence-routing toward it counterproductive.
  • A recurrence-based separation of recurring from stochastic confident-wrongness, plus an option-shuffle control showing a meaningful share of GPQA "confidence" is positional (a persistent D-avoidance bias survives content randomization).
  • Honest null/marginal results: chain-of-thought improves accuracy but only marginally improves the agreement–correctness signal, and a confidence-routed cascade is dominated by always using the mid-tier model.
  • An exploratory cross-family check on three Claude tiers reproducing frontier over-confidence, with shared confident errors across GPT and Claude above a marginal-preserving null—suggesting high agreement can reflect shared bias rather than correctness.
  • A released de-identified, schema-validated dataset and analysis pipeline.

Methodology

The study is a secondary, de-identified meta-analysis of model-output runs produced by 53 graduate-course runners. Each runner was assigned one of three controlled comparisons (axes) on the gpt-4.1 family, each with two conditions run on the same cases (temperature=1.0, K=50):

  • Axis A — model tier: gpt-4.1-nano vs. gpt-4.1-mini (zero-shot).
  • Axis B — prompt strategy: gpt-4.1-mini zero-shot vs. chain-of-thought.
  • Axis C — mid vs. frontier: gpt-4.1-mini vs. gpt-4.1 (zero-shot).

Benchmarks bracket opposite ends of the answer-space spectrum: GPQA Diamond (four-option graduate-level MCQ, floor C≈1/4) and AIME (integer-answer competition math, large space). Each (axis, condition, case) cell was independently re-sampled by a mean of 2.5 runners (up to 9), giving 5,300 case-result rows over 394 unique cases.

For each (case, condition) the authors compute self-consistency C = n_maj/K, sample accuracy A = n_correct/K, and the majority-correct indicator M (the primary deployment outcome). The headline statistic is Spearman ρ(C, ·). Because rows are not i.i.d., all CIs use a hierarchical runner-clustered bootstrap (resample runners, then cases within runner; B=2000), complemented by a case-clustered bootstrap and a leave-one-runner check for the headline frontier result. Holm correction is applied to the twelve per-cell ρ(C,M) tests; correlation differences use permutation tests.

Additional controlled studies: (1) a within-runner paired comparison of self-consistency against verbalized confidence and P(True) logprob on 50 GPQA/AIME cases; (2) an option-shuffle control (48 GPQA cases, K=50, two random permutations) to isolate positional bias; (3) a logged-snapshot re-run of the Axis-C contrast (gpt-4.1-2025-04-14 vs. mini, K=20); and (4) an exploratory agent-mediated cross-family check on three Claude tiers (haiku/sonnet/opus, K=10).

Results & Highlights

  1. Agreement is a positive but weak predictor across all twelve cells: All twelve ρ(C,M) are positive and survive Holm correction (adj. p ≤ 0.0024), but no cell exceeds ρ(C,M)=0.59; on GPQA the frontier gpt-4.1 has the lowest ρ(C,M)=0.20 despite the highest mean C=0.89, while mid-tier mini reaches ρ(C,M)=0.30–0.35. The link is not a clustering artifact: all twelve remain positive under case-clustered resampling (Table 4).
  1. The most consistent frontier model is the worst-calibrated voter: gpt-4.1 reaches C≥0.8 on 77% of GPQA cases, yet a high-agreement (C≥0.8) answer is wrong 48% of the time (95% CI [0.40, 0.56], case-clustered, row-weighted). Its GPQA ECE is 0.41 (worst of all twelve cells) versus 0.22 for CoT-mini, and Brier is 0.42 vs. 0.26. The frontier degradation is significant: ∆ρ(C,M) = −0.18 [−0.31, −0.03] on GPQA (p=0.025) and −0.26 [−0.40, −0.12] on AIME (p=0.0006), surviving Bonferroni-for-two and case-clustered resampling.
  1. Chain-of-thought: accuracy yes, signal marginal: CoT robustly improves sample accuracy (∆A=+0.067 on GPQA, Wilcoxon p=2.1×10⁻⁵) but its effect on the deployment label is weaker (∆M=+0.066, CI [−0.005, 0.132]) and on the agreement–correctness signal only borderline (∆ρ(C,M)=+0.123, p=0.025 on GPQA; +0.031, p=0.21 on AIME).
  1. Confident errors recur and are partly positional: For gpt-4.1 on GPQA, 28% of unique cases are confidently wrong for every runner and 50% for at least one. The option-shuffle control shows mini's point-estimate majority accuracy falls from 0.50 to 0.29 under shuffling, and "D" is under-selected (≈16%) regardless of content—a position bias surviving randomization.
  1. Cross-family check reproduces frontier over-confidence and shared confident errors: Within Claude, opus is the most self-consistent (C=0.94, C≥0.8 on 88%) yet not the most accurate (M=0.58 vs. sonnet 0.63) and worst-calibrated (ECE=0.35). Among items both gpt-4.1 and a Claude tier get wrong, they pick the same wrong answer significantly above a label-permutation null (haiku p=.003, sonnet p=.005).
  1. Adaptive sampling helps; confidence-routed cascade hurts: A stop-when-consensus rule uses 60% fewer samples on average (19.8 vs. 50) at equal majority error (0.603). But a C-routed cheap→expensive cascade (peak 0.36) is dominated by always using the mid-tier model (0.40); always-frontier reaches only 0.33, oracle 0.44.

Authors & Credibility

Single-author work by Kaihua Ding (University of Pennsylvania), whose stated prior work spans output-based error estimation (PhD thesis, University of Michigan, 2018) and AI-system evaluation/assessment design (preprints 2025). The dataset originates from a graduate-course cohort (53 runners) and is released de-identified; the analysis is funded by the Wharton AI & Analytics Initiative and used NAIRR/Jetstream2 compute. The paper is notably self-critical: it openly declares that no inferential claim is preregistered, that the endpoint tests are "descriptive robustness summaries, not confirmatory," and that model snapshots were not logged—caveats stated before, not after, the results. The inferential machinery (hierarchical runner-clustered bootstrap, case-clustered cross-check, leave-one-runner, Holm and Bonferroni corrections, permutation nulls) is unusually careful for an LLM-evaluation paper of this scope.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The core thesis (agreement ≠ accuracy) is not new—it is the intra-model analogue of self-preference bias (Zheng 2023; Panickssery 2024) and echoes known post-training calibration regression (OpenAI 2023). The genuine contribution is the quantification of how agreement degrades as a deployable signal specifically at the frontier, and the cross-runner recurrence + cross-family shared-error analyses, which go beyond prior single-run studies.
  • Rigor: High. Hierarchical runner-clustered bootstrap with case-clustered and leave-one-runner cross-checks, multiplicity control, and permutation nulls that preserve marginals are appropriate and well-executed. The authors correctly refuse to treat non-i.i.d. rows as independent and are transparent about the post-hoc, non-preregistered status of the focal contrasts.
  • Red flags / weaknesses (paper-specific):
  • Unrecoverable model snapshots and timestamps: This is the most serious limitation. The primary 265k-sample corpus has no logged snapshot, so replication is across runners/prompts, not across time—meaning backend drift cannot be ruled out as a confound. The logged-snapshot re-run (K=20, 48 cases) reproduces the over-confidence direction but is underpowered for the ρ/ECE gaps (∆ρ=−0.06, CI [−0.37, 0.26]).
  • Sample-size imbalance between headline and controls: The confidence-baseline and shuffle studies use only 50 and 48 cases with wide CIs that often overlap (e.g., the shuffle accuracy drop 0.50→0.29 has overlapping CIs at n=48), so several "suggestive" positional findings are not individually significant.
  • Single model family for the main audit: All twelve cells come from the gpt-4.1 family; the Claude check is agent-mediated (no temperature control, K=10, internal reasoning cannot be disabled), so absolute values are not comparable and the cross-family "shared error" claim rests on a small, weakly-controlled probe.
  • GPQA label-quality confound acknowledged but unquantified: A minority of GPQA items appear ambiguous or mislabeled (one item answered consistently against its listed key; six items wrong across all four models), which inflates apparent confident-wrongness—yet no sensitivity analysis excluding suspect items is reported.
  • Reproducibility: Strong on data release (de-identified per-runner–per-case rows, schema-validated, with code), but inherently limited by the missing snapshots and GPQA question text (not redistributed per access terms). A future researcher could re-derive the headline ρ(C,M) but could not verify the exact backend.
  • Significance: Practically actionable. The result that confidence-routing toward the frontier model is counterproductive (cascade 0.36 < always-mini 0.40) is a concrete, deployment-relevant caution that contradicts a common heuristic. The findings are, however, bounded to two hard-reasoning benchmarks and one provider's main corpus.

Strengths

  • Unusually transparent about statistical limitations: explicitly labels endpoint tests as descriptive/non-confirmatory and applies appropriate multiplicity correction.
  • Multi-pronged robustness: runner-clustered + case-clustered + leave-one-runner + logged-snapshot re-run triangulate the headline frontier-degradation result.
  • Deployment-grounded outcome: uses M (majority-correct, the actual deployed label) rather than per-sample accuracy as the primary target, matching how these systems are used.
  • The recurrence + shuffle + cross-family analyses together build a coherent causal story (shared bias, positional priors) rather than resting on a single correlation.
  • Honest about null/marginal results (CoT signal effect, cascade utility) rather than spinning them.

Limitations

  • Primary audit is a single provider (gpt-4.1 family); cross-family Claude check is exploratory and agent-mediated with K=10 and no temperature control.
  • Model snapshots and run timestamps were not logged and are unrecoverable—replication is across runners/prompts, not across time, leaving backend drift as an uncontrolled confound.
  • Only two benchmarks (four-option MCQ and integer math); no open-ended generation, code, or reasoning-trained models studied.
  • K=50 leaves substantial measurement noise; confidence-baseline and shuffle controls use only 50/48 cases with wide, often overlapping CIs.
  • GPQA item-quality issues (ambiguous/mislabeled items) inflate confident-wrongness but are not quantified via a sensitivity analysis.
  • Runners wrote their own prompts, adding implementation variance treated as part of the measurement rather than controlled.

Relevance

对关注 LLM 评测方法论与可信部署的研究者高度相关：它直接挑战"一致性=正确性"这一 LLM-as-judge / 级联路由 / 选择性预测 pipeline 的核心假设，并给出了可操作的机制边界（中端模型可用、前沿模型不可信、CoT 提升准确率但不提升信号），对构建可靠的多 judge 系统与置信度路由有直接指导价值。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Does the frontier over-confidence / calibration-regression pattern hold for reasoning-trained models (o1-style) where internal reasoning may already supply calibration lift?
  • How do results change on open-ended generation or code benchmarks where the answer space is not a small MCQ set and positional priors are absent?
  • Can a snapshot-locked, time-stamped, multi-provider pre-registered replication confirm the frontier-degradation ∆ρ(C,M) at adequate power?
  • Would excluding the flagged ambiguous/mislabeled GPQA items materially change the confident-wrongness and recurrence rates?
  • Is the cross-family shared-error effect (GPT and Claude picking the same wrong answers above a marginal-preserving null) driven by shared pretraining data, shared misconceptions, or item flaws—and does it grow or shrink with scale?
  • Can agreement be combined with other signals (verbalized confidence, P(True), semantic entropy) into a composite that beats the mid-tier-always baseline without inheriting frontier over-confidence?
