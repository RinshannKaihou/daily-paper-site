CRIMP: Compact & Reliable DNN Inference on In-Memory Processing via Crossbar-Aligned Compression and Non-ideality Adaptation

arXiv: 2607.08015

Date: 2026-07-10

https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08015

一句话总结： 本文针对 ReRAM 交叉阵列（crossbar）式内存计算（IMP）加速器面临的浮点运算不兼容、冗余参数占用过多 crossbar、以及器件非理想性导致精度下降三大难题，提出 CRIMP——在单次训练中协同完成「整数-only 量化（缩放因子强制为 2 的幂、复用 crossbar 内的移位单元免乘法器）」「crossbar 对齐剪枝（kernel-group 剪枝 + crossbar 剪枝，免去 Sparsity Table 数据对齐硬件）」与「运行时感知非理想性自适应（推导出误差期望与 crossbar 高度 H 的关系，构建 runtime 仿真器注入训练）」，在 Cifar-10 上得到 VGG-16 / ResNet-56 的纯整数、剪枝且抗非理想性的最终模型，精度仅分别下降 2.19% 与 1.26%，相比原模型平均计算功耗下降 122.38×、计算面积下降 19.51×，且无任何额外硬件开销。
TL;DR: CRIMP co-optimizes three "large-accuracy-loss" techniques into a single training pass to deploy DNNs on ReRAM crossbar IMP accelerators with zero extra hardware: (1) integer-only quantization that forces scaling factors to powers of 2 and reuses in-crossbar bit-shift units to eliminate FP multipliers; (2) crossbar-aligned pruning (kernel-group pruning that keeps remaining kernels as integer multiples of the crossbar width, plus crossbar-block pruning) that removes the need for Sparsity-Table data-aligning hardware; and (3) runtime-aware non-ideality adaptation, which derives the analytical relationship E(Error) = D/H · Round(H·0.75·(e^(ε²/2)−1)) between write-variation error, crossbar height H, and WV degree ε, and injects a realistic crossbar runtime simulator into training. On Cifar-10, CRIMP delivers fully integer-only, pruned, reliable VGG-16 / ResNet-56 models with only 2.19% / 1.26% accuracy drop under SAF + ε=0.1, achieves 88.25% sparsity on VGG-16 (vs 34.90% for CSAO) and 50.50% on ImageNet ResNet-18 (vs 32.41% for PIM-Prune, whose accuracy drop is 1.09% vs CRIMP's 0.99%), and reduces computing power by 122.38× and computing area by 19.51× on average versus the original model.

核心贡献

  • 整数-only 量化消除浮点乘法器：将所有 scaling factor 强制为 2 的幂（令 a=1），复用 crossbar 中已有的 Shift-and-Add (S+A) 单元完成 scaling 乘法，彻底免去 FP/fixed-point 乘法器——论文指出乘法器面积可达单个 crossbar 的 11 倍，每个乘法器给 Tile 带来约 7% 功耗与 9% 面积开销。
  • crossbar 对齐剪枝免去数据对齐硬件：现有 IMP-aware 剪枝（crossbar-column/row）需要 Sparsity Table（ST）对齐 crossbar 间输入输出，ST 面积约为一个 crossbar 阵列的 6 倍；CRIMP 改用「kernel-group 剪枝（保证每层剩余 kernel 数为 crossbar 宽度的整数倍）+ crossbar 剪枝（通过可微 mask 层整块移除）」的多粒度方案，剪枝后天然数据对齐、无需 ST。
  • 运行时感知非理想性自适应：首次推导出 WV 误差期望与 crossbar 高度 H、WV 程度 ε 的解析关系，得出「当 H 小于上界时 ADC 的 round 函数可自动消除误差」（ε=0.1 时 H≤133，ε=0.2 时 H≤33），据此构建 runtime 仿真器在训练中注入非理想性，无需任何硬件补偿单元。
  • 统一的协同优化学习框架：将上述三技术在单次训练中协同优化（Algorithm 1），通过 zero-recovery 训练（zero epoch 剪枝/量化/注入非理想性 + recovery epoch 利用动量恢复被置零的 importance factor，动态探索更优架构与权重），抵消各自单独使用时的大精度损失。
  • 显著的硬件收益：相比原模型，平均计算功耗下降 122.38×、计算面积下降 19.51×；Cifar-10 上 VGG-16 / ResNet-56 在含 SAF 与 ε=0.1 的非理想器件上部署精度仅下降 2.19% / 1.26%。

Key Contributions

  • Integer-only quantization without multipliers: Forces all scaling factors to powers of 2 (S_X=2^a1, S_W=2^a2, S_B=2^a3, S_{X+1}=2^b1) so that scaling-factor multiplication is absorbed by the in-crossbar Shift-and-Add (S+A) units already present in IMP devices, eliminating both FP and fixed-point multipliers. The accuracy sacrifice from setting a=1 (vs IEEE-754 a·2^b) is recovered through co-optimization in training.
  • Crossbar-aligned pruning that needs no data-aligning hardware: Replaces crossbar-column/row pruning (which demands a Sparsity Table ~6× the area of a crossbar array and ~44% more memory per IMA) with two granularity levels that are inherently data-aligned: kernel-group pruning (rounds remaining kernel counts to integer multiples of crossbar width X_Bw to fully occupy crossbars row-wise) and crossbar pruning (a differentiable per-crossbar mask layer that removes whole crossbar-blocks of weights). This improves integration density instead of decreasing it.
  • Runtime-aware non-ideality adaptation: Derives the analytical expectation of the write-variation error E(Error) = (D/H)·Round(H·0.75·(e^(ε²/2)−1)), proving that below an upper bound on crossbar height H the ADC's round function automatically cancels the error (ε=0.1 → H≤133; ε=0.2 → H≤33). This finding both motivates a runtime crossbar simulator injected into training and serves as a design guideline for ReRAM IMP devices. Unlike CorrectNet/DRD, it models the actual crossbar execution flow (per-cell 2-bit mapping, ADC rounding, S+A) rather than naively multiplying weights by e^θ.
  • Comprehensive co-optimization learning framework (Algorithm 1): Integrates all three techniques into one training procedure with a two-phase structure (kernel-group pruning then crossbar pruning), sparse training to drive importance factors δ toward zero, and a zero-recovery procedure that uses momentum to revive previously zerorized δ values, dynamically reshaping the pruned architecture for higher accuracy.
  • Strong hardware efficiency results: Achieves average computing-power reduction of 122.38× and computing-area reduction of 19.51× versus original models, with integer-only, pruned, reliable Cifar-10 VGG-16 / ResNet-56 models that drop only 2.19% / 1.26% accuracy on non-ideal ReRAM — all with zero hardware overhead.

Methodology

Crossbar-aligned DNN pruning (Section 3.1). Built on the fully-folded crossbar mapping scheme (Fig. 2c), where each kernel is unfolded to a vector and mapped to memristor cells so a crossbar is fully occupied. Two complementary granularities avoid the Sparsity Table:

  • Kernel-group pruning: For two connected conv layers l1, l2, removing kernels from l_n reduces C_{n+1}; rounding remaining kernels to integer multiples of crossbar width X_Bw makes layer l2's kernels fully occupy crossbars row-wise for maximal savings. Coarse-grained, so accuracy loss limits how much can be removed.
  • Crossbar pruning: A differentiable mask layer after each conv/FC layer associates one mask value with one crossbar; zeroizing a mask removes all weights in that crossbar. Medium-grained unstructured, compresses both conv and FC layers, and needs no ST. To limit the convergence difficulty caused by many mask values, kernel-group pruning is applied first to shrink the model before crossbar pruning.

Integer-only quantization (Section 3.2). Symmetric quantization for inputs (voltage can be ±), asymmetric for weights (conductance ≥ 0). The key step (Eq. 5) forces every scaling factor to a power of two, so Eq. (4)'s ratio S_X^L·S_W^L / S_X^{L+1} collapses to 2^{a1+a2−b1}, executable purely by S+A bit-shift units. The accuracy loss from approximating the mantissa a=1 is recovered in the joint training.

Runtime-aware non-ideality adaptation (Section 3.3). Starting from the lognormal WV model r' = e^θ·r (θ~N(0,ε²)), the paper computes the mathematical expectation of the per-crossbar error for a vector-vector multiply (Eq. 10): E(Error) = (D/H)·Round(H·0.75·(e^(ε²/2)−1)). This yields an upper bound on H below which the ADC round function cancels the accumulated error — a key design insight. To exploit it in training, a runtime simulator (Fig. 6b) splits 8-bit weights into four 2-bit ReRAM-cell values, computes the ideal M+A result and the WV-induced error separately (so randomness does not pollute gradient convergence), rounds per-crossbar outputs, and adds the error to the ideal result. The same scheme extends to SAF.

Comprehensive learning framework (Section 3.4, Algorithm 1). Sparse training (forcing δ→0) runs throughout. After initial warmup epochs, the loop alternates: (a) zero epoch — quantize with power-of-2 scaling factors, run forward with the runtime simulator (non-ideality adaptation), sort kernels/crossbars by |δ|, compute a per-layer threshold so remaining weights fully occupy crossbars, temporarily zerorize unimportant δ; (b) recovery epoch — normal forward + sparse training. A subtle but important trick: ReLU's zero derivative would strand zerorized δ at zero, but SGD momentum (Eq. 14–15) carries prior gradient direction, so δ can recover non-zero values automatically, enabling dynamic architecture search. The framework executes in two phases — kernel-group pruning (δ=BN γ) then crossbar pruning (δ=mask) — with quantization and non-ideality adaptation embedded in both.

Setup. ISAAC architecture, 128×128 crossbar, 2-bit/cell, 8-bit weights mapped to 4 cells, NeuroSim + 32nm CMOS + CACTI for power/area modeling; PyTorch for training; LeNet-5/VGG-16/ResNet-56/ResNet-18 on Mnist/Cifar-10/ImageNet; SAF at 9.04% SF1 + 1.75% SF0; WV at ε∈{0.1, 0.5}.

Results & Highlights

  • Quantization (Table 2, no non-ideality): CRIMP-Quan's accuracy drop is within 0.31% of full-precision and on par with IAO (which keeps FP scaling factors): LeNet-5/Mnist 0.12% vs IAO 0.11%; VGG-16/Cifar-10 −0.11% (i.e. an improvement) vs IAO +0.14%; ResNet-56/Cifar-10 −0.03% vs IAO −0.10%; ResNet-18/ImageNet 0.31% vs IAO 0.25%. So forcing scaling factors to powers of 2 costs essentially nothing.
  • Pruning + quantization (Table 3, no non-ideality): CRIMP-PQ achieves the highest sparsity at the lowest accuracy drop among IMP-aware methods. On ImageNet ResNet-18 it reaches 50.50% sparsity / 68.80% acc / 0.99% drop, beating PIM-Prune (32.41% / 68.67% / 1.09%), SPRC (26.41% / 67.82% / 1.94%) and XBA (24.89% / 66.07% / 3.24%). On Cifar-10 VGG-16 it hits 88.25% sparsity with 93.71% acc (−0.43%, an improvement) versus CSAO's 34.90% sparsity / 0.20% drop. On Cifar-10 ResNet-56 it reaches 51.36% sparsity / 93.18% acc (0.16% drop) vs SPRC 33.40% / 92.80%.
  • Power & area (Fig. 7): Versus the original model, CRIMP delivers an average total power reduction of 4.44×, computing-power reduction of 122.38×, total area reduction of 3.16×, and computing-area reduction of 19.51× — the largest gains among all compared methods (LSRR, CSAO, SPRC, XBA, PIM-Prune), because it removes both the Sparsity Table and the FP multipliers from the computing path.
  • Full compact & reliable models on non-ideal ReRAM (Table 4): Under SAF + ε=0.1, CRIMP deploys Cifar-10 VGG-16 at 88.25% sparsity with 91.09% acc (2.19% drop) and ResNet-56 at 51.36% sparsity with 92.08% acc (1.26% drop), with zero hardware/runtime overhead. This dominates VACTSF (9.03% sparsity, 86.75% acc, 6.46% drop with ~10% area overhead) and matches or beats R-V-W / AIIR / KD (which use 16-bit quantization, no compression, and either multi-write or ~15% area/remapping overhead). When SAF is disabled (ε=0.1), VGG-16's ReRAM drop shrinks to just 0.08% (93.20%).
  • Crossbar-height insight validated (Fig. 8): On LeNet-5/Mnist at ε=0.8, non-ideality adaptation (NIA) lifts average accuracy from 90.54% (no NIA) to 96.54% (with NIA) under pruning; and accuracy degrades monotonically as crossbar height H grows under fixed ε — empirically confirming the Eq. (10) bound that smaller H lets the ADC round away WV error.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The three components individually have clear antecedents — power-of-2 scaling factors and structured pruning are known ideas — but the synthesis is genuinely new in two ways: (a) the crossbar-aligned kernel-group + crossbar pruning that eliminates the Sparsity Table (the main differentiator vs PIM-Prune/XBA/SPRC, which all need ST hardware), and (b) the analytical E(Error)–H–ε relationship (Eq. 10) that turns crossbar height into a first-class design lever and motivates a runtime simulator more faithful than CorrectNet/DRD's naive e^θ weight scaling. The zero-recovery trick exploiting SGD momentum to un-stick zerorized δ under ReLU is a clever, paper-specific training contribution.
  • Rigor — baseline fairness is mixed: Comparisons are carefully annotated with quantization bit-width, ε, and whether SAF is considered, which is good. But Table 4 puts CRIMP (8-bit, compressed, no overhead) against R-V-W/AIIR/KD (16-bit, uncompressed, with multi-write or ~15% area overhead) — these are not strictly apples-to-apples and CRIMP's 8-bit+compression disadvantage is offset by its no-overhead advantage, making the "comparable accuracy" claim softer than it looks. The closest true competitor, DRD [25] (8-bit, no overhead, ε=0.1, no compression, 0.52% drop on VGG-16), actually beats CRIMP's 2.19% on the same model/ε — CRIMP's 2.19% is the price of also compressing to 88.25% sparsity, which the paper states but does not foreground.
  • Rigor — missing ablations: There is no isolated ablation of the zero-recovery procedure's contribution (how much accuracy does momentum-based δ recovery buy vs a fixed mask?), and no ablation isolating the runtime-simulator's fidelity gain over the naive e^θ injection used by DRD. The crossbar-height result (Fig. 8) is shown only on LeNet-5/Mnist; the H-dependent error-cancellation claim is not re-validated on the larger VGG/ResNet models where it matters most.
  • Reproducibility: Reasonably strong — named architecture (ISAAC, 128×128, 32nm), named simulator (NeuroSim, CACTI), specific SAF/WV parameters, PyTorch, and Algorithm 1 is given in pseudocode. However no code release is mentioned, no random seeds, and the per-layer pruning thresholds and zero-start epoch s are not fully specified, so the training recipe would need reconstruction.
  • Significance: For the ReRAM IMP subfield this is a solid, deployable contribution — removing both the ST and the multiplier from the datapath is a concrete integration-density win. Significance is bounded by the fact that all evaluated DNNs are classic CNNs (LeNet/VGG/ResNet) up to ImageNet ResNet-18; no transformers, no large modern models, so applicability to current workloads is unproven.
  • Red flags: (1) The headline "122.38× computing-power / 19.51× computing-area reduction" is computed against the original uncompressed model and is inflated by the removal of multipliers and STs that CRIMP's own design choice dictates — the more meaningful comparison against a same-overhead baseline is less dramatic; (2) on the single most directly comparable row (VGG-16/Cifar-10/ε=0.1/8-bit/no-overhead), CRIMP's 2.19% drop is worse than DRD's 0.52%, with the gap being the cost of pruning — a trade-off the abstract's "without hardware overhead" framing does not surface; (3) the paper silently relies on the H≤133 (ε=0.1) regime for ADC round to cancel error, but at the more aggressive ε=0.5 the same round-away effect is far weaker, and the worst-case results (e.g. VGG-16 ε=0.5 → 90.31%, a 2.97% drop) are downplayed.

Strengths

  • Truly zero-overhead deployment: by reusing S+A units and removing both the Sparsity Table and FP multiplier, the method increases rather than decreases crossbar integration density — the opposite of PIM-Prune/CTSF/AIIR.
  • The analytical E(Error)–H–ε bound (Eq. 10, Fig. 4) is a reusable design guideline for ReRAM IMP devices, not just an algorithmic trick.
  • The runtime simulator computes ideal result and non-ideality error separately, keeping random noise out of the gradient path — a principled choice that aids convergence.
  • Co-optimization in a single training pass (rather than three sequential fine-tunes) plus the momentum-driven zero-recovery procedure lets the pruned architecture reshape dynamically, which is why the larger ImageNet/Cifar-10 models can hit high sparsity with sub-1% (non-ideality-free) accuracy drops.
  • Honest about trade-offs: the paper explicitly states kernel-group pruning alone is coarse and accuracy-limited, and reports the ε=0.5 worst-case numbers rather than hiding them.

Limitations

  • Only classic CNNs evaluated (LeNet-5, VGG-16, ResNet-56/18) — no transformers, no large modern architectures, so generalization to attention-based or billion-parameter models is untested.
  • Headline hardware numbers are vs the uncompressed original, inflating the 122.38×/19.51× figures; comparisons against an equally overhead-free compressed baseline (e.g. DRD) are accuracy-favourable but not hardware-favourable in the same dramatic ratio.
  • On the most apples-to-apples accuracy row (VGG-16/Cifar-10, 8-bit, ε=0.1, no overhead), CRIMP's 2.19% drop is worse than DRD's 0.52% — the cost of adding 88.25% pruning; the abstract frames this as a single win without separating the two.
  • No isolated ablation of the zero-recovery procedure or of the runtime-simulator-vs-naive-e^θ modelling choice; the LeNet-5 height analysis (Fig. 8) is not repeated on VGG/ResNet.
  • No code or seeds released; some training hyperparameters (zero-start epoch s, per-layer thresholds) are under-specified.
  • Aggressive ε=0.5 results are noticeably weaker (VGG-16 → 2.97% drop, ResNet-56 → 1.46% drop), and the ADC-round error cancellation that underpins the method is much less effective at high ε per the paper's own Eq. (10).

Relevance

对关注硬件感知模型压缩、量化与高效推理部署（cs.AR/cs.PF）的研究方向高度相关：CRIMP 提供了一条「在 ReRAM/IMP 类新兴硬件上端到端协同优化剪枝、整数-only 量化与器件非理想性」的完整范式，其「移除数据对齐硬件的多粒度对齐剪枝」「以 crossbar 几何特征（高度 H）指导算法与器件协同设计」「单次训练 zero-recovery 协同优化」的思路，对任何关注硬件-算法协同设计、新兴存储计算架构上可靠推理、或量化/剪枝联合训练的工作都有方法论参考价值。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Does the crossbar-aligned pruning + integer-only quantization transfer to transformer workloads (e.g. ViT, BERT), where kernel-group structure is absent and attention maps differently onto crossbars — can a head-group analogue of kernel-group pruning recover the same ST-free benefit?
  • How does the Eq. (10) H-bound generalize at ε=0.5 and beyond: at the more realistic high-variation regime, is the ADC-round error cancellation still effective, or does the runtime simulator need a more powerful adaptation signal?
  • What is the isolated contribution of the momentum-driven zero-recovery procedure versus a fixed-mask ablation, and versus a standard iterative magnitude-prune-and-retrain baseline, on VGG-16/ResNet-56?
  • Since DRD [25] achieves a lower accuracy drop (0.52% vs 2.19%) on the same VGG-16/Cifar-10/ε=0.1/8-bit/no-overhead setting without compression, can CRIMP's runtime simulator be combined with DRD's training to recover the accuracy gap at high sparsity?
  • The E(Error)–H–ε bound is offered as a ReRAM device-design guideline — can it be validated on fabricated devices or against a circuit-level Monte-Carlo sim beyond NeuroSim, and does it change the optimal crossbar aspect ratio in practice?
