Circuit Synchronization Precedes Generalization: Causal Evidence from Fourier Structure in Grokking Transformers
Achyuthan Sivasankar · NYU 2026-06-12 mechanistic-interpretability grokking fourier-analysis
Key Metric
+1,722 steps

Mean lead of FSD over grokking onset across 9 modular-addition configs (95% CI [+1,111, +2,333]; sign-test p ≈ 0.004)

Why This Matters

Introduces a circuit-agnostic metric (FSD) that detects hidden algorithmic structure in transformers without prior circuit knowledge — a step toward mechanistic interpretability of real models.

⚠ Critical Edge

Only tested on 2-layer transformers with p ≤ 131 — whether FSD extends to deeper architectures or non-Fourier algorithms is completely untested.

Methodology

2-layer Transformer → Modular addition (a+b mod p) → Checkpoint FSD → Fork & vary λ
  • Model: d_model=128, 4 heads, d_mlp=512, trained on 5 primes (p ∈ {53, 71, 97, 113, 131}), 3 seeds, 30/70 train-val split.
  • FSD: sum-conditioned GELU activations → DFT per neuron → dominant frequency participation rate normalized against chance. Permutation-tested (1,000 shuffles).
  • Weight-decay fork: at FSD-ceiling step, branch into 6 λ values ∈ {1…10} while holding circuit state constant — isolates regularization rate from formation.
  • Zero-ablation: zero out each component's output to measure causal necessity (block 1 attention → 94.4 pp accuracy drop).

Strengths

  • FSD is elegant: normalized, statistically tested, no prior circuit knowledge needed.
  • Fork-and-vary-λ is a clean causal design isolating regularization from formation.
  • Comprehensive ablations (architecture, operations, seeds, primes) triangulate the same conclusion.
  • Honest about limitations — pseudo-replication, scope, noisy p=53 fit discussed openly.
  • Practical implication (raise λ at FSD plateau) is immediately actionable for toy settings.

Limitations

  • Scalability unknown — only 2-layer transformers, p ≤ 131.
  • Fourier-specific: FSD fails for non-Fourier algorithms (multiplication lags by 10k steps).
  • Only 4 distinct primes; 5 of 9 configs share p=97 with different seeds.
  • Single seed per (prime, λ) cell — p=53 departure hard to interpret.
  • No connection to LLM-scale phenomena — firmly in toy-setting territory.

Open Questions

  1. Does FSD predict delayed generalization in larger models (e.g., LLM fine-tuning on reasoning)?
  2. Can the two-phase decomposition explain grokking in non-modular tasks (Omnigrok suite)?
  3. Would head-level patching reveal a finer causal hierarchy within the Fourier circuit?
  4. Is the inverse-λ law robust to other optimizers, or does it depend on AdamW dynamics?
  5. Could FSD monitoring combine with Grokfast for even faster grokking acceleration?
  6. What algorithm does modular multiplication actually learn — and can a non-Fourier metric predict its formation?
