Reasoning as Pattern Matching: Shared Mechanisms in Human and LLM Everyday Reasoning

Zach Studdiford & Gary Lupyan — UW–Madison

142 humans & 25 LLMs solve everyday causal reasoning tasks with strikingly similar error patterns. The attention heads driving LLM outputs implement pattern matching — and their activations predict human errors at R² = 0.66.

arXiv 2606.13607 2026-06-12 PDF ↗
Key Finding

Everyday causal reasoning in both humans and LLMs is driven by content-sensitive pattern matching, not abstract world models. Both fail in the same ways when irrelevant surface details change.

Key Metric
0.66 R²

LLM attention-head entropy → held-out human accuracy (minimal-difference experiment). Category alignment r = 0.84.

Why This Matters

If human reasoning itself is pattern matching, calling LLMs "mere pattern matchers" is a self-undermining critique. This reframes the core debate.

Critical Edge

Only everyday common-sense scenarios tested. Findings may not generalize to formal or mathematical reasoning. Mechanistic analysis limited to Gemma-2 models only.

⚙ Methodology

  • 142 humans (3 cohorts: main n=142, re-test n=70, minimal-diff n=175) on MTurk
  • 25 LLMs (21 open-source, 4 proprietary); logit-difference scoring
  • 11 categories of everyday causal reasoning: state changes, position, geocentric/egocentric spatial relations, relative position, two-reference
  • Minimal-difference design: 6 templates × 16 content swaps = 96 prompts sharing the same ground truth — isolates content from structure
  • Mechanistic analysis: Attention head ablation + activation patching on gemma-2-27b-it via TransformerLens; top-k heads selected by BIC
  • Controls: Trigram frequency, GPT-2/BERT surprisal, permutation tests (1000 iter)

📊 Results

r = 0.84 gemma-3-27b ↔ human category-level accuracy
d = 0.44 Median Cohen's d: heads more sensitive to non-critical content (all p<0.001)
3.84× Odds of repeating an error on retest
  • Human accuracy µ=0.71 — far below ceiling. Frontier models outperform humans but are less human-aligned.
  • Swapping "painting" → "statue" in a prompt shifts both human and LLM accuracy — identical relational structure, different content.
  • DeepSeek-R1 reasoning-trace length predicts human difficulty (β=−0.027, p=0.001).

⚖ Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Mechanistic (causal) evidence, not just behavioral correlation
  • Three independent human cohorts for reliability
  • Minimal-difference stimuli cleanly isolate content effects
  • Strong controls: trigram freq., surprisal, permutation tests
  • Bonus finding: CoT length ↔ human difficulty

Limitations

  • Everyday common-sense only — may not extend to formal reasoning
  • Mechanistic analysis restricted to Gemma-2 family
  • Does not fully engage dual-process (System 1/2) literature
  • Main-experiment R²=0.254 leaves substantial variance unexplained
  • "Pattern matching ↔ structured associations" may be a continuum, not a dichotomy

? Open Questions

  1. Do identified attention heads map systematically onto known cognitive biases (availability, representativeness, anchoring)?
  2. Would models trained with explicit reasoning objectives (process reward models) show different head behavior?
  3. Can the methodology generalize to math/logic tasks where humans also show content sensitivity?
  4. If LLMs are fine-tuned to de-emphasize content-sensitive heads, does reasoning improve or degrade?
  5. Frontier models are less human-aligned — have they developed more abstract reasoning, or are they just better pattern-matchers?
  6. Can activation patching predict human difficulty prospectively, before running behavioral experiments?
