> **一句话总结：** 本文通过激活分析和消融实验系统揭示了RLHF奖励模型中"有用性"和"无害性"目标的神经元级冲突机制——约50%的目标特异性神经元被两个目标共享，这些共享神经元虽仅占总神经元4.75%，但对混合目标模型行为的影响远超目标专属神经元，是多目标对齐困难的表征级根源。

> **TL;DR:** This paper provides the first mechanistic study of helpfulness-harmlessness alignment tension inside reward models. Using magnitude-based neuron scoring and targeted ablations on RMs trained from SmoLLM2 and GPT-2, the authors show that ~50% of objective-specific neurons are shared between helpfulness and harmlessness. These shared neurons (only ~4.75% of total) cause the strongest degradation when ablated in mixed-objective RMs (−10pp on harmless tasks, −4.4pp on helpful tasks), far exceeding the effect of ablating objective-only neurons. Mixed-objective RMs also exhibit reduced reward calibration scores compressed toward zero even when decisions are correct—and lower behavioural retention on adversarial/n nuanced evaluations.

## 核心贡献

- **系统表征奖励模型中的对齐张力：** 在RewardBench、RewardBench2和RM-Bench三大基准上，系统比较helpfulness-only、harmlessness-only和混合目标三种奖励模型的行为差异，证明混合目标模型在多项任务上不及单一目标模型。
- **量化行为保留率（Behavioural Retention）：** 提出新的度量指标，衡量混合模型对单一模型已学行为的保留程度。标准任务保留率较高（如AlpacaEval达0.96），但对抗性/精细评估（如LLM Bar变体、Focus）保留率低至0.24。
- **揭示奖励校准退化：** 即使混合模型的决策正确，其奖励分数也被压缩至零附近，置信度显著低于单一目标模型。
- **定位目标特异性神经元并验证因果性：** 使用基于激活幅度的神经元重要性评分，识别与各目标关联的神经元（集中于模型后层）；消融实验显示helpfulness神经元消融后helpful任务降7pp而harmless任务升5pp，反之亦然。
- **发现共享神经元是冲突根源：** 约50%的目标特异性神经元为两目标共享；共享神经元仅占总量4.75%，但消融后造成最大性能退化，证明表征级纠缠是alignment tension的核心。

## Key Contributions

- **First systematic behavioural characterisation of alignment tension in RMs:** Evaluates helpful-only, harmless-only, and mixed RMs across 25+ subtasks from RewardBench, RewardBench2, and RM-Bench. Mixed-objective RMs consistently underperform specialised ones, especially on adversarial and nuanced evaluations.
- **Behavioural retention metric:** Proposes a retention score quantifying how much of single-objective RMs' correct behaviour is preserved by the mixed RM. Coarse behaviours (e.g., AlpacaEval 0.96, Refusals Offensive 0.87) are well-retained; adversarial tasks (e.g., LLM Bar variants ~0.24, Focus 0.25) are fragile.
- **Reward calibration degradation:** Shows via kernel density analysis that even when the mixed RM makes correct decisions, its scores are compressed toward zero with reduced confidence margins compared to single-objective RMs.
- **Objective-specific neuron identification with causal validation:** Uses magnitude-based neuron importance scoring to identify helpfulness- and harmlessness-oriented neurons (concentrated in late layers). Ablating helpfulness neurons drops helpful-task accuracy by 7pp while improving harmless tasks by ~5pp, and vice versa—confirming causal opposition.
- **Shared neurons as the mechanistic bottleneck:** ~50% of objective-specific neurons overlap between helpfulness and harmlessness. Despite constituting only ~4.75% of total neurons, ablating shared neurons from the mixed RM causes −10pp on harmless tasks and −4.4pp on helpful tasks—far exceeding the impact of ablating objective-only neurons.

## Methodology

**Reward Model Training:** Three RM variants are trained from the same pre-trained backbone (SmoLLM2-135M/360M, GPT-2-medium/large) differing only in training data: helpfulness-only (DH), harmlessness-only (DS), and mixed (D), all on the HH-RLHF dataset. Six architectures × three seeds = 54 RMs total.

**Neuron Importance Scoring:** Following prior mechanistic interpretability work (Geva et al., 2021; Meng et al., 2022), neurons with the highest average activation magnitude on objective-specific data are identified as objective-oriented. The top τ-th percentile (τ=10) is selected. The method is validated against a change-based scoring variant and a random baseline.

**Neuron Ablation:** Selected neurons are zeroed during the forward pass. Performance is evaluated on helpfulness- and harmlessness-oriented subtasks from RewardBench, RewardBench2, and RM-Bench.

**Shared Neuron Analysis:** Objective-specific neurons from the two single-objective RMs are partitioned into helpfulness-only, harmlessness-only, and shared sets. Each group is ablated independently in the mixed-objective RM to assess functional importance.

**Behavioural Retention:** Defined as the proportion of responses correctly scored by at least one single-objective RM that are also correctly scored by the mixed RM, providing a fine-grained measure of behavioural preservation.

## Results & Highlights

- **Mixed RMs underperform on adversarial tasks:** On LLM Bar adversarial variants, mixed RMs often score worse than both single-objective models (e.g., LLM Bar Adver Manual baseline ~65% for harmless RM vs. ~21% for mixed RM).
- **Behavioural retention is task-dependent:** Standard instruction-following (AlpacaEval 0.93–0.96) and simple refusal (Refusals Offensive 0.87) are well-retained; adversarial robustness (LLM Bar variants 0.24–0.48, Focus 0.25) and factuality (0.40) are poorly retained.
- **Reward scores compressed under mixed training:** KDE plots show mixed RM scores cluster near zero even for correct decisions, indicating weakened reward signal separability.
- **Neurons are concentrated in late layers:** Across all RM types, objective-specific neurons reside predominantly in the final third of the LLM backbone—consistent with late layers encoding higher-level semantic features.
- **Shared neurons dominate mixed-RM behaviour:** Ablating shared neurons (4.75% of total) from the mixed RM causes −10pp on harmless tasks and −4.4pp on helpful tasks. Ablating helpfulness-only or harmlessness-only neurons causes negligible change (<1pp).
- **Asymmetric objective opposition:** Ablating harmlessness neurons reduces harmless-task accuracy by ~12pp but improves helpful tasks; ablating helpfulness neurons reduces helpful-task accuracy by ~7pp but improves harmless tasks by ~5pp.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** This is the first work to study alignment tension *inside* RMs at the neuron level. Prior work treated RMs as black boxes or focused on output-level tradeoffs. The combination of behavioural retention analysis + neuron-level ablation is a genuine contribution. However, the neuron identification method (magnitude-based scoring) is standard and borrowed from prior work.
- **Rigor:** The experimental setup is well-controlled—same backbone, same initialisation, different only in training data. Three model families × three seeds = 54 RMs provides robust statistical grounding. The behavioural retention metric is novel and informative. However, the τ threshold (top-10 percentile) is somewhat arbitrary; sensitivity analysis is limited to Figure 5 in the appendix.
- **Reproducibility:** Strong. Code is public, dataset (HH-RLHF) is widely used, model families (SmoLLM2, GPT-2) are open-source. The methods are straightforward to replicate.
- **Significance:** The finding that shared neurons (only 4.75% of total) drive the majority of alignment tension is practically important. It suggests that architectural disentanglement or targeted regularisation of shared representations could improve multi-objective alignment. However, the practical impact depends on whether these findings scale.
- **Red flags:** (1) All experiments use small models (135M–774M parameters). It is entirely possible that the neuron-level dynamics change qualitatively at larger scales where polysemantic neurons are more prevalent. (2) The paper does not study DPO or other non-RM alignment methods, which limits generality. (3) The claim that "shared neurons are the bottleneck" relies on ablation evidence; alternative explanations (e.g., shared neurons are simply the most active neurons overall) are not fully ruled out. (4) The helpful-harmless partition of HH-RLHF may not capture the full spectrum of alignment objectives.

## Strengths

- Provides the first mechanistic, neuron-level explanation for the helpfulness-harmlessness tradeoff in RLHF reward models.
- Well-controlled experimental design (same backbone, different training data, multiple seeds, three model families).
- Novel behavioural retention metric that reveals task-dependent fragility of mixed-objective training.
- Clear, actionable finding: shared neurons are the bottleneck, motivating disentangled alignment architectures.
- Comprehensive evaluation across three major RM benchmarks with 25+ subtasks.
- Public code and reproducible methodology.

## Limitations

- **Scale:** All models are small (≤774M parameters). Findings may not generalise to the multi-billion-parameter RMs used in production RLHF pipelines.
- **Single dataset:** Only HH-RLHF is used for training; results may depend on the specific helpfulness-harmlessness partition of this dataset.
- **No alternative alignment methods:** Does not study DPO, RLOO, or other preference optimisation methods where reward models are implicit.
- **Polysemantic neurons:** The authors acknowledge but do not investigate whether shared neurons exhibit polysemantic behaviour (encoding multiple unrelated features), which could complicate the interpretation.
- **No intervention experiments:** The paper identifies the problem (shared neurons) but does not propose or test solutions (e.g., regularisation to disentangle shared representations).
- **No parameter-efficient fine-tuning:** Full fine-tuning was used to preserve interpretability, but PEFT methods (LoRA, etc.) may produce different neuron-level dynamics.

## Relevance

这项研究从神经元级别揭示了RLHF奖励模型中"有用性"与"无害性"目标冲突的表征机制，对于设计更好的多目标对齐方法（如解耦训练、可控对齐）具有直接启发意义，与大模型安全对齐和RLHF优化的研究方向高度相关。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Do shared neurons remain the primary bottleneck in larger-scale RMs (7B+ parameters), or does the dynamics shift toward more distributed representations?
- Can explicit regularisation (e.g., orthogonal projection penalties on shared neuron activations) reduce interference without sacrificing overall performance?
- How do these findings transfer to DPO-style implicit reward models where no separate RM is trained?
- Are the shared neurons genuinely encoding competing alignment features, or are they polysemantic neurons that happen to be active on both data types for unrelated reasons?
- Could inference-time interventions (e.g., activation steering via contrastive vectors on shared neurons) provide controllable helpfulness-harmlessness tradeoffs?
- Does the asymmetry in ablation effects (harmlessness ablation hurts more than helpfulness ablation) reflect a fundamental property of the objectives or an artifact of the HH-RLHF data distribution?
