One trained model, three precision levels—drop codebooks at inference for 3/4/5-bit LLM quantization with no retraining.

Multi-Bitwidth Quantization for LLMs Using Additive Codebooks Liza Babaoglu, Shuangyi Chen, Ashish Khisti · U of Toronto 2026-06-12

Key Metric
~3×
reduction in training hours & disk usage vs. maintaining separate per-bitwidth AQLM checkpoints (e.g., Qwen-32B: 200 GB → 66 GB).
Why This Matters
One checkpoint serves heterogeneous hardware—cloud GPUs at 5-bit, edge devices at 3-bit—solving a real deployment need at scale.
⚠ Critical Edge
No direct comparison with Any-Precision LLM or AnyBCQ—the closest competing multi-bitwidth methods are absent from experiments.

Methodology

Built on AQLM's additive vector quantization, where each weight group (g=8) is reconstructed as a sum of M codebook entries, with M codebooks × 8 bits each yielding effective bitwidths of 3–5 bits/weight.

Calibration Data (1024 samples) → Train M Additive Codebooks → Matryoshka Loss LDbyD = Σ λk · D(k) → Single Checkpoint → Drop Codebooks at Inference
  • Theoretical grounding: Proves Gaussian sources are successively refinable under weighted MSE (Theorem 1), extending Equitz & Cover (1991)—the first connection between successive refinement theory and LLM quantization.
  • Matryoshka-style loss: Supervises every prefix of the codebook sequence, creating a coarse-to-fine hierarchy so early codebooks capture essential structure and later ones provide refinement.
  • Weighting strategies: Uniform (all λ equal at 0.2) and 35W (λ₃=0.5, λ₅=0.5)—the latter focuses supervision on the precision extremes and delivers the best results.
  • Optimization: Alternates beam search for discrete code assignments with gradient descent for continuous codebook entries, following AQLM's pipeline.

Strengths

  • Elegant theoretical grounding connecting information theory to practical LLM quantization—rare in PTQ literature.
  • Clean algorithmic design: a simple loss modification to AQLM enables multi-bitwidth operation.
  • Comprehensive evaluation across 8 models, 4 architectures, 4 scales (0.5B–32B).
  • Honest comparison framework: AQLM (Individual) as upper bound, AQLM (Drop) as naive baseline.
  • ~3× savings in both training time and storage at scale.

Limitations

  • Only supports 3–5 bits/weight; no sub-3-bit or mixed-granularity operation.
  • 35W weighting found via ablation—may need re-tuning for new model families or M > 5.
  • No latency or throughput measurements on real hardware.
  • Missing direct comparison with Any-Precision LLM or AnyBCQ.
  • Gaussian weight assumption may break for attention Q/K projections and layer norm parameters.
  • Inherits AQLM's complexity (beam search, multi-phase optimization).

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does DbyD compare with Any-Precision LLM and AnyBCQ on identical benchmarks?
  2. Can Matryoshka loss weighting be automated via meta-learning or Bayesian optimization?
  3. What are actual inference latency and memory bandwidth savings on real hardware (T4, mobile GPUs)?
  4. Can DbyD extend to sub-3-bit via smaller codebooks (B=4) or structured pruning?
  5. Does successive refinability hold for non-Gaussian weight distributions (heavy-tailed, bimodal)?
  6. How does DbyD interact with activation quantization—can both weights and activations be multi-bitwidth?
