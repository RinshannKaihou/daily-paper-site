Demystifying Hidden-State Recurrence: Switchable Latent Reasoning with On-Policy Reinforcement Learning

Jiayu Yang, Chao Chen, Shengen Wu, Yinhong Liu, Yuxuan Fan, Lujundong Li, Songning Lai, Chengwei Qin, Zhijiang Guo · HKUST(GZ) · Cambridge · NTU · JoinQuant · HKUST
2026-06-12  ·  arXiv 2606.13106
Key Finding

Boundary tokens make hidden-state latent reasoning compatible with on-policy RL for the first time — reaching 79.3% on MATH-500, nearly matching explicit CoT while burning 18% fewer visible tokens.

Key Metric
79.3%
MATH-500 accuracy (Qwen3-8B)
+25.7 pp vs. Coconut baseline
Why This Matters

First framework to unify on-policy RL training with mechanistic interpretability for hidden-state latent reasoning — with a tunable accuracy–efficiency operating curve.

Critical Edge

Latent computation collapses to a single hidden-state transition — the multi-step "recurrence" is largely over-parameterized and held up by a Kmin constraint.

Methodology

SWITCH extends Qwen3-8B with three special tokens — <swi> (enter latent), </swi> (exit), <latent> (placeholder) — enabling hidden-state recurrence via boundary-marked latent blocks.

  1. Phase 1 — Boundary Learning (Switch-SFT): Identifies high-entropy CoT spans via Shannon entropy, wraps them with <swi>/</swi>, trains with standard next-token cross-entropy.
  2. Phase 2 — Latent Curriculum: Parallel schedule progressively replaces text inside boundary tokens with <latent> positions, forcing genuine latent computation.
  3. Phase 3 — Switch-GRPO: On-policy RL with correctness, format, latent-usage, and brevity rewards. Latent positions contribute no policy-gradient term; gradients flow through the KV cache they hand off.
Results
MATH-500
79.3%
GSM8K
89.2%

After RL, latent invocation drops from 81% → 58% of problems, but accuracy on invoked problems rises from 66.7% → 79.3% — the model learned selective deployment. A brevity variant trades ~3 acc points for 33% shorter outputs and 0% truncation.

Strengths & Limitations

STRENGTHS

  • Elegant unification — one design choice solves RL compatibility and interpretability
  • Thorough mechanistic analysis: probes, logit lens, causal interventions converge
  • Selective switching after RL is an emergent desirable behavior
  • Transparent about the single-transition finding despite its implications

LIMITATIONS

  • Only 8B scale, math-only benchmarks — generalization unknown
  • Kmin = 4 is an engineering fix, not emergent; model exits immediately without it
  • No head-to-head with vocabulary-mixture methods (Soft Thinking, Soft-GRPO)
  • Late-training reward hacking: switch rate → 100%, latent invocations explode
Open Questions
  1. Can the single-transition finding be leveraged to design one enriched forward pass instead of Kmin recurrent steps?
  2. How does SWITCH scale to 70B+ and non-math tasks (code, scientific QA, multi-hop reasoning)?
  3. Would making <latent> samplable (vocabulary-mixture hybrid) enrich the computation beyond one transition?
  4. Can reward design prevent the late-training reward hacking observed at step 800+?
  5. What happens with dynamic Kmin based on problem difficulty — does the Pareto frontier improve?
