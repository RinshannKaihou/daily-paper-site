> **一句话总结：** 本文提出三种基于置信度聚合的多智能体协议（WSV、CGA、HID），通过对多个LLM的置信度信号进行跨模型校准与融合，生成单一的系统级置信度，在五个基准上显著提升了置信度区分度（AUARC提升5-10%），同时恢复了标准多智能体辩论在歧义任务上的F1损失。

> **TL;DR:** This paper introduces three confidence-driven multiagent protocols (Weighted Stream Voting, Confidence Gating with Aggregation, and Human-Inspired Debate) for two-agent LLM debate settings. By learning per-stream transformations to make confidence comparable across models, then combining them via soft voting or Bayesian fusion, the protocols produce a single aggregated system-level confidence that is substantially more discriminative (5-10% AUARC gains) than any individual agent or standard MAD baseline, while also recovering the F1 losses that standard multiagent debate incurs on ambiguous tasks like stance detection and NLI. Evaluated across 14 models, 6 debating pairs, 5 benchmarks, and 4 task types.

## 核心贡献

- **首次针对多智能体系统提出并评估聚合置信度信号**：填补了NLP领域中多智能体置信度估计的空白，现有工作仅在辩论内部使用置信度进行路由或加权，从未将其聚合为系统级单一置信度。
- **提出三种置信度驱动的协议**：WSV（加权流投票）通过跨四个流的软投票最大化F1；CGA（置信度门控聚合）和HID（类人辩论）通过置信度门控+贝叶斯融合最大化置信度区分度。
- **解决跨模型置信度不可比问题**：通过学习逐流变换参数，使不同模型的置信度分布可比较后再聚合，这是多模型置信度融合的关键技术贡献。
- **系统性地分析两种置信度估计器与多种校准器**：对比了序列概率和自报告两种估计器，以及Beta校准、多项式校准、等保序回归等校准方法，发现校准对F1至关重要但对AUARC影响较小。

## Key Contributions

- **First system-level confidence estimation for multiagent LLM systems**: Prior work used confidence within multiagent debate (MAD) for routing or weighting but never aggregated it into a single confidence for the system output — this paper fills that gap.
- **Three novel protocols (WSV, CGA, HID)**: Each produces a final answer plus a single aggregated confidence. WSV achieves best F1 via soft voting across four streams; CGA and HID achieve best confidence discrimination (AUARC) via confidence-gated switching + Bayesian fusion.
- **Cross-model confidence comparability**: Per-stream learned transformations absorb residual confidence distribution mismatches between models, enabling meaningful aggregation even after calibration.
- **Comprehensive empirical study**: 14 open-source models (3B–32B), 6 debating pairs per benchmark (homogeneous + heterogeneous), 5 benchmarks, 4 task types (scientific QA, stance detection, Boolean QA, hate speech detection), with statistical significance tests (5 runs per configuration).
- **Insightful analysis of debate dynamics**: Shows confident agents are more persuasive when correct, self-report confidence changes are more meaningful through debate than sequence probability, and calibration is essential for F1 but less critical for discrimination.

## Methodology

The paper operates in a **two-agent debate setting**. Each agent produces a zero-shot prediction with confidence, then a post-debate prediction with confidence after seeing the other agent's chain-of-thought — yielding four "streams" total.

**Protocol design:**
1. **Weighted Stream Voting (WSV)**: Treats all four (agent, stage) outputs as independent evidence. Each stream gets a learned weight. Scores candidate labels by summing weighted logit-confidences of streams predicting that label. Final confidence is softmax-normalized.
2. **Confidence Gating with Aggregation (CGA)**: Two-step — first, each agent independently decides whether to switch from zero-shot to post-debate answer based on a learned per-agent threshold on confidence improvement in logit space. Second, surviving streams are fused via Bayesian fusion (assuming conditional independence): when streams agree, confidences multiply; when they disagree, the higher-confidence stream wins with an appropriately reduced confidence.
3. **Human-Inspired Debate (HID)**: A deterministic routing protocol inspired by human debate norms — if both agents are confident and agree, skip debate; if one is confident and one isn't, the confident agent tries to persuade the other; if both confidently disagree or both are uncertain, conduct debate. Final confidence via Bayesian fusion.

**Confidence estimation**: Two families — (1) sequence probability (mean log-likelihood with token-bias correction), (2) self-report (verbalized confidence averaged over 3 repetitions at temperature 0.3).

**Calibration**: Beta calibration, 3rd-order polynomial, and isotonic regression, fit per-stream on a validation split.

**Optimization**: Two-step — calibrators fit via NLL minimization; protocol parameters (weights/thresholds) optimized via Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) with a combined objective of NLL + ECE penalty + L2 regularization toward neutral defaults.

## Results & Highlights

- **AUARC (confidence discrimination)**: Protocols achieve 5-10 percentage-point AUARC gains over MAD baselines across all 5 benchmarks. CGA and HID consistently achieve the highest AUARC, reaching 6/6 significant pairs on BoolQ and Vast.
- **F1 recovery**: Standard MAD degrades F1 on 4 of 5 benchmarks (BoolQ: -5.53pp, Vast: -6.02pp, EZStance: -8.37pp, HatEval: -3.25pp). Confidence-based protocols recover these losses, turning them into modest gains.
- **WSV wins on F1**: WSV achieves the most robust F1 improvements across tasks and calibrators. CGA and HID trade some F1 for higher AUARC.
- **Calibration is essential for F1**: Uncalibrated confidence leads to notably lower F1; parametric calibrators (Beta, polynomial) consistently help. AUARC is less sensitive to calibration choice.
- **Self-report vs. sequence probability**: Neither estimator consistently dominates. Self-report produces more meaningful confidence changes through debate (sequence probability increases even when answers turn wrong). Self-report achieves highest AUARC on 4/5 benchmarks.
- **Debate dynamics**: Confident agents are more persuasive when correct (green curve consistently above red), but persuasion rates for correct and incorrect agents move together, explaining why standard MAD is unreliable.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty**: High for the multiagent confidence aggregation problem. While individual components (soft voting, Bayesian fusion, calibration) are well-known, their composition into protocols that produce a *single system-level confidence* for multiagent LLM output is novel and practically important.

**Rigor**: Strong experimental methodology — 14 models, 6 pairs per benchmark, 5 benchmarks, statistical significance tests with 5 runs, comprehensive ablations over estimators and calibrators. The (k/6) reporting convention is transparent about how many pairs actually improve.

**Weaknesses and concerns:**
- **Limited to two agents and binary/multi-class classification**: The authors acknowledge this but it significantly limits generality. Extending HID beyond two agents would require substantial redesign. All tasks are classification-style (even BoolQ is binary QA); no generation, reasoning, or math tasks are evaluated despite the authors noting this as future work.
- **Bayesian fusion independence assumption is violated**: The authors explicitly acknowledge this but don't quantify the impact. In practice, two LLMs debating the same question will have correlated correctness — the fusion could produce overconfident or underconfident aggregated signals.
- **Marginal F1 improvements on some tasks**: On EZStance and HatEval, the F1 gains over MAD-D are sometimes negligible or negative (CGA: -0.24 on EZStance, -1.00 on EZStance with self-report). The main selling point is AUARC improvement, not task accuracy.
- **Validation-set optimization**: Protocol parameters are tuned on validation splits. With small validation sets (e.g., 200-500 samples), there is a risk of overfitting thresholds despite L2 regularization. The paper doesn't analyze sensitivity to validation set size.
- **Single debate round**: The paper fixes debate to one round citing prior work on random walk behavior, but this limits the potential for iterative refinement.
- **Computational overhead not analyzed**: Three self-consistency samples per query × 2 agents × calibration optimization adds significant inference cost, which is not quantified.

**Reproducibility**: Good — all models are open-source, benchmarks are public, and the authors provide prompts in the appendix. However, the TPE optimization with specific hyperparameters (λ values) could be sensitive to implementation details.

**Significance**: The paper addresses an important practical gap. As multiagent LLM systems become more common in production, having a reliable system-level confidence signal is crucial for human oversight and downstream decision-making. The finding that multiagent mechanisms can produce *stronger* confidence signals than individual agents is counter-intuitive and valuable.

## Strengths

- First principled approach to system-level confidence for multiagent LLM outputs.
- Comprehensive evaluation spanning model sizes (3B-32B), pair types (homogeneous/heterogeneous), and task types.
- Thoughtful analysis of debate dynamics from a confidence perspective (persuasion analysis, confidence change through debate).
- Clean protocol design with interpretable mechanisms (weighted voting, confidence gating, Bayesian fusion).
- Transparent reporting with statistical significance and per-pair breakdowns.

## Limitations

- Restricted to two-agent setting; scalability to 3+ agents is untested (HID requires redesign).
- Only classification-style tasks evaluated; no generation, math reasoning, or open-ended tasks.
- Bayesian fusion's conditional independence assumption is violated in practice.
- Small F1 improvements on some benchmarks suggest the gains may not be practically significant for all use cases.
- Validation-set parameter tuning raises potential overfitting concerns.
- Computational cost of the full pipeline (self-consistency × multiagent × calibration) is not analyzed.

## Relevance

本文与LLM推理评估和可靠性领域高度相关——提出了为多智能体LLM系统生成可信赖的系统级置信度的方法，这对于构建可靠的LLM推理管道具有重要意义。置信度校准和跨模型置信度比较的分析对理解LLM输出的可靠性有直接参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How do these protocols scale to 3+ agents? Does Bayesian fusion become more problematic with correlated agents, and would alternative aggregation methods (e.g., Copula-based fusion) be more appropriate?
2. Can the aggregated confidence signal be used for downstream tasks like selective prediction, routing, or human-in-the-loop oversight in production multiagent systems?
3. How robust are the learned per-stream transformations to distribution shift (e.g., new task domains, different prompt formats)?
4. What is the computational overhead of the full pipeline compared to single-agent inference, and is the AUARC improvement worth the cost?
5. How do these protocols perform on open-ended generation tasks where there is no discrete label space for voting?
