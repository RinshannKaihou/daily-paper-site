> **一句话总结：** 本文将Muon优化器的谱最速下降规则严格推广到低秩矩阵流形上，推导出LoRA-Muon，配合分裂权重衰减规则实现了规范不变性，其最优学习率在秩、宽度、深度和因子缩放下均可迁移，且秩为32的计算量匹配实验中超越了稠密基线。

> **TL;DR:** LoRA-Muon derives a gauge-invariant optimizer for LoRA by solving the spectral steepest-descent problem on the low-rank manifold $M_r = \{W \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n} : \text{rank}(W) = r\}$. It whitens factor gradients by the current Gram geometry then applies the matrix sign function — the same spectral descent rule as full-rank Muon — combined with a novel split weight-decay rule that ensures the low-rank dynamics faithfully proxy the full-rank ones. In compute-matched TinyShakespeare sweeps, the dense best learning rate (0.1) transfers across rank, width, depth, and factor rescaling even at rank 2, and rank-32 LoRA-Muon achieves lower mean validation loss (1.776 ± 0.002) than the dense baseline (1.789 ± 0.002). The method is QR-free and stores no second moments, making it more accelerator-friendly than LoRA-RITE and more principled than the gauge-sensitive Spectron.

## 核心贡献

- 从流形优化的角度严格推导了低秩矩阵流形 $M_r$ 上的最速下降问题，得到一族酉不变范数下的线性最小化预言机（LMO），谱范数特化即为LoRA-Muon
- 提出分裂权重衰减规则（split weight-decay）：用 $s = \sqrt{1 - \lambda\eta}$ 而非 $(1 - \lambda\eta)$ 分别缩放两个因子，使低秩权重衰减行为精确匹配全秩Muon的期望行为
- 证明LoRA-Muon在完整的规范变换 $(A, B) \mapsto (AR, BR^{-T})$ 下诱导更新 $\Delta W$ 保持不变（规范不变性定理），而Spectron在标量规范缩放下即失效
- 揭示LoRA-RITE的简化QR坐标核心与LoRA-Muon在代数上完全等价（Proposition 6），但LoRA-Muon无需QR分解和二阶矩存储，实现更高效
- 在TinyShakespeare上展示了最优学习率跨秩（2–32）、宽度、深度和因子缩放的迁移性，rank-2代理仅需约4.3%可训练参数即可恢复稠密最优学习率

## Key Contributions

- Derives a family of Linear Minimization Oracles (LMOs) for steepest descent on the low-rank manifold $M_r$ under unitarily invariant norms; specializing to the spectral norm yields LoRA-Muon, a low-rank proxy for the Muon optimizer
- Introduces a split weight-decay rule where each factor is scaled by $s = \sqrt{1-\lambda\eta}$ (and updates by $s^{-1}$), ensuring the composed weight decay matches the expected full-rank behavior up to an $O(\eta^2)$ error term
- Proves gauge invariance (Theorem 1): the induced update $\Delta W$ is invariant under the full gauge group $(A,B) \mapsto (AR, BR^{-T})$ for any invertible $R$, because the projector-form LMO inputs $G_t P_B$ and $P_A G_t$ are themselves gauge-invariant
- Shows Spectron is not gauge-invariant even under scalar rescaling (Proposition 5): its trust radius depends on $\|A\| + \|B\|$ which changes with gauge scale, causing its optimal learning rate to shift toward 0.178 vs. the dense/Muon optimum of 0.1
- Proves algebraic equivalence between LoRA-RITE's simplified QR-coordinate core and LoRA-Muon (Proposition 6): both implement the same spectral steepest-descent update in different coordinate systems, but LoRA-Muon is QR-free and first-moment-only

## Methodology

The paper frames LoRA optimization as steepest descent on the low-rank manifold $M_r = \{W \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n} : \text{rank}(W) = r\}$, following the "Old Optimizer, New Norm" philosophy where Muon performs steepest descent under the spectral norm.

**Core derivation:** Given the tangent space $T_W M_r = \{\Delta_A B^T + A \Delta_B^T\}$ at $W = AB^T$, the authors solve a constrained linearized subproblem: minimize $\langle G_t, \Delta W \rangle$ subject to $\|\Delta W\| \leq \eta$ over the tangent space. They split the trust-region budget evenly between the two tangent components, solving two half-radius subproblems independently. Unitary invariance of the norm collapses these onto projector terms, yielding the projector-form update:

$$\Delta_A^* B^T = \frac{\eta}{2} \text{LMO}_{\|\cdot\|}(G_t P_B), \quad A \Delta_B^{*T} = \frac{\eta}{2} \text{LMO}_{\|\cdot\|}(P_A G_t)$$

where $P_A = A(A^TA)^{-1}A^T$ and $P_B = B(B^TB)^{-1}B^T$ are column projectors.

**Spectral specialization:** Setting $\text{LMO}_{\|\cdot\|_{2\to 2}}(X) = -\text{msign}(X)$ gives the LoRA-Muon factor update:

$$\Delta_A^* = -\frac{\eta}{2} \text{msign}(\nabla_A f \, S_B^{-1/2}) S_B^{-1/2}, \quad \Delta_B^* = -\frac{\eta}{2} \text{msign}(\nabla_B f \, S_A^{-1/2}) S_A^{-1/2}$$

In words: whiten the factor gradients by the current Gram geometry ($S_A = A^TA$, $S_B = B^TB$), then apply the matrix sign function — the same spectral descent rule as full-rank Muon.

**Split weight-decay:** To match the expected full-rank weight decay $(1-\lambda\eta)W_t + \Delta W_t$, the update uses $s = \sqrt{1-\lambda\eta}$:

$$A_{t+1} = sA_t + s^{-1}\Delta_A^*, \quad B_{t+1} = sB_t + s^{-1}\Delta_B^*$$

This introduces only an $O(s^{-2} - 1)$ cross-term error that vanishes as $\eta \to 0$.

**Algorithm 1 (LoRA-Muon step):** Compute factor gradients → update EMA first moments → compute Gram matrices $S_A, S_B$ → compute matrix inverse roots → apply msign to whitened moments → split weight-decay update. No QR decomposition, no second moments, purely GEMM-heavy operations.

**Scalar gauge rebalancing (Algorithm 2):** Periodically rescale $A \leftarrow cA$, $B \leftarrow B/c$ where $c = (\sigma_{\max}(B)/\sigma_{\max}(A))^{\alpha/2}$. Transport moments accordingly: $m_A \leftarrow m_A/c$, $m_B \leftarrow cm_B$. This is a numerically beneficial reparameterization within the same gauge class and does not change $\Delta W$.

## Results & Highlights

- **Learning-rate transfer across rank:** The dense best learning rate $\text{lr} = 0.1$ transfers to LoRA-Muon at ranks 2, 4, 8, 16, and 32. Rank 1 is the sole failure case. At rank 2, validation loss is $2.156 \pm 0.005$ vs. dense $1.789 \pm 0.002$; at rank 32, LoRA-Muon reaches $1.776 \pm 0.002$, *below* the dense baseline
- **Learning-rate transfer across width and depth:** Width and depth sweeps (rank fixed at 32) show LoRA-Muon is competitive with dense references and outperforms in the depth sweep
- **Gauge invariance validated:** Factor-rescaling sweep shows LoRA-Muon curves nearly coincident across scalar gauge choices. Gauge rebalancing changes validation loss by only $-5.1 \times 10^{-5}$ (95% CI: $[-2.0 \times 10^{-4}, 9.5 \times 10^{-5}]$) vs. Spectron's $2.0 \times 10^{-2}$ (95% CI: $[1.6 \times 10^{-2}, 2.5 \times 10^{-2}]$)
- **Rank-2 as cheap proxy:** At $d_\text{model} = 128$, $n_\text{layers} = 2$, rank-2 uses only ~4.3% trainable parameters with a 9.225× larger optimizer-step budget under the same compute budget
- **Compute-matched protocol:** Every LoRA run is compute-matched to the dense baseline (same total FLOPs), with increased step budgets for lower ranks. Six seeds per configuration, best learning rate selected by lowest seed-mean validation loss

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** The derivation is a natural but non-trivial extension of Bernstein & Newhouse's "Old Optimizer, New Norm" framework to the low-rank manifold. The projector-form LMO solution (Eq. 12) is elegant. The split weight-decay insight is genuinely novel and has no clear precedent in the LoRA literature. The gauge invariance proofs and the Spectron/LoRA-RITE analyses add significant theoretical value. The connection to LoRA-RITE (Proposition 6) is important but reduces the novelty of the core update rule — LoRA-RITE independently arrived at the same spectral update in QR coordinates.

**Rigor:** The theoretical analysis is thorough and well-grounded in differential geometry (tangent spaces, gauge groups). Proofs are complete and include explicit counterexamples (Proposition 5 for Spectron). The factor-form equivariance proof (Appendix A.2) and moment transport identity (Appendix B.2) close potential gaps. However, the decoupled trust-region split (halving the budget) is justified empirically (Figure 2 shows alignment) rather than proven tight, and the rank-1 failure case is noted without deep analysis.

**Reproducibility:** Algorithm 1 is clearly specified. TinyShakespeare is a standard benchmark with known configurations. The paper provides compute-match multipliers in Appendix D.1. The code does not appear to be released with this preprint, but the algorithm is straightforward to implement from the pseudocode.

**Significance:** If the learning-rate transfer holds at LLM scale, this would be highly impactful — it means one can do cheap rank-2 hyperparameter sweeps to find dense-scale optimal learning rates. The gauge invariance property is also important for practical use: practitioners often initialize LoRA with imbalanced factors, and LoRA-Muon is robust to this.

**Red flags / concerns:**
- **TinyShakespeare only.** This is a character-level LM on a ~1MB corpus. No validation on any modern benchmark (GLUE, SuperGLUE, instruction following, code generation). The authors acknowledge this explicitly but it remains the paper's biggest limitation
- **No comparison with AdamW + LoRA at matched compute.** The paper compares against dense Muon and Spectron/LoRA-RITE, but doesn't show a head-to-head with the most common practical baseline: LoRA with AdamW
- **Rank-1 failure unexplained.** The only rank where learning rate transfer fails is rank 1, which is not analyzed in depth
- **Spectron comparison is against a concurrent draft.** Spectron is itself unpublished, making the comparison somewhat premature
- **No wall-clock or memory measurements.** The paper claims accelerator-friendliness but provides no actual throughput or memory profiling numbers

## Strengths

- Elegant theoretical framework that unifies LoRA optimizer design under the steepest-descent-on-manifold lens
- Gauge invariance is both theoretically clean and practically important — the optimizer is robust to arbitrary factor parameterizations
- Split weight-decay rule is a simple but impactful innovation that ensures hyperparameter transfer
- The rank-2 proxy result is practically exciting: ~4.3% parameters suffice to recover the dense optimal learning rate
- Thorough analysis of related optimizers (Spectron, LoRA-RITE) with precise theoretical positioning
- Memory-efficient: only first moments stored, no QR decomposition needed, purely GEMM operations

## Limitations

- All experiments are on TinyShakespeare — a tiny character-level LM benchmark that may not reflect behavior at LLM scale or on downstream tasks
- No experiments on actual fine-tuning scenarios (instruction following, RLHF, domain adaptation) — the paper tests pretraining-style training from scratch
- No wall-clock time, throughput, or GPU memory profiling comparisons
- No head-to-head comparison with the dominant practical baseline: LoRA + AdamW (only Spectron and LoRA-RITE are compared)
- The rank-1 failure case is noted but not analyzed
- The trust-region split heuristic (halving between tangent components) is empirically justified but not provably tight
- Learning rate transfer is demonstrated but not full hyperparameter transfer (weight decay, momentum, etc. are coupled but not directly tested)

## Relevance

LoRA-Muon直接解决了LoRA微调中超参数敏感和初始化依赖的核心痛点，通过规范不变性保证了对因子初始化不平衡的鲁棒性。其"廉价rank-2代理即可恢复稠密最优学习率"的发现对于大规模模型的超参数搜索具有重要实用价值。无需二阶矩存储和QR分解的特性使其在资源受限场景下尤为实用。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the learning-rate transfer hold at 7B+ scale on real downstream tasks (instruction following, code generation, RLHF)?
- How does LoRA-Muon interact with quantized training (QLoRA, GPTQ) where the Gram matrices may be poorly conditioned?
- Can the split weight-decay rule be adapted for other factorized parameterizations (e.g., Tensor Train, block-sparse low-rank)?
- What causes the rank-1 failure? Is it a fundamental limitation of the spectral norm on 1D subspaces?
- How does LoRA-Muon compare with AdamW + LoRA in a matched-compute, matched-memory head-to-head on realistic workloads?
- Does the alignment between tangent components (Figure 2) hold for deeper/wider models, or does the decoupled approximation degrade?
- Can gauge rebalancing be applied less frequently (e.g., every 100 steps) without losing conditioning benefits, to reduce overhead?
