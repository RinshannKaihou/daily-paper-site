Understanding Helpfulness and Harmless Tension in Reward Models

Eshaan Tanwar & Pepa Atanasova — University of Copenhagen

2026-06-12 · arXiv 2606.13209

Key Finding

Just 4.75% of neurons — shared between helpfulness and harmlessness objectives — drive the majority of alignment tension in RLHF reward models.

Key Metric
−10pp
harmless-task drop when shared neurons ablated — alongside −4.4pp on helpful tasks. These neurons are only 4.75% of the model.
Why This Matters
Pinpoints the neuron-level bottleneck in multi-objective alignment, directly motivating disentangled training architectures for safer RLHF.
Critical Edge
All experiments use models ≤774M parameters — neuron-level dynamics may shift qualitatively at production scale, where polysemantic neurons are more prevalent.

Methodology

Three RM variants trained from identical backbones (SmoLLM2-135M/360M, GPT-2-medium/large) differing only in training data: helpfulness-only, harmlessness-only, and mixed. Six architectures × three seeds = 54 reward models.

Neuron importance scored by average activation magnitude on objective-specific data (top 10-th percentile selected). Ablation zeroes identified neurons during forward pass. Shared-neuron analysis partitions objective-specific neurons into three sets and ablates each independently in the mixed RM.

Behavioural retention metric: proportion of responses correctly scored by single-objective RMs that the mixed RM also scores correctly — a fine-grained preservation measure.

Results

  • Adversarial fragility: Mixed RMs collapse on LLM Bar adversarial variants (~21% vs. ~65% for single-objective).
  • Task-dependent retention: AlpacaEval 0.96, Refusals 0.87 — but LLM Bar variants 0.24–0.48, Focus 0.25.
  • Compressed rewards: Mixed-RM scores cluster near zero even for correct decisions, weakening signal separability.
  • Asymmetric opposition: Ablating harmlessness neurons drops harmless accuracy ~12pp but boosts helpfulness; helpfulness ablation drops helpful ~7pp, boosts harmless ~5pp.

Strengths

  • First neuron-level mechanistic explanation for the helpfulness-harmlessness tradeoff.
  • Well-controlled design: same backbone, different data, multiple seeds, three model families.
  • Novel behavioural retention metric reveals task-dependent fragility.
  • Clear actionable insight: shared neurons are the bottleneck.
  • Public code & reproducible methodology.

Limitations

  • All models ≤774M params — generalisation to production scale unproven.
  • Single dataset (HH-RLHF); results may be partition-dependent.
  • No DPO / RLOO / implicit-RM comparison.
  • Polysemantic behaviour of shared neurons acknowledged but not investigated.
  • No intervention experiments testing potential solutions.
  • Full fine-tuning only; PEFT (LoRA etc.) may yield different dynamics.

Open Questions

  • Do shared neurons remain the bottleneck in 7B+ RMs, or does the dynamics shift toward distributed representations?
  • Can explicit regularisation (e.g., orthogonal projection penalties on shared activations) reduce interference without sacrificing performance?
  • How do findings transfer to DPO-style implicit reward models?
  • Are shared neurons genuinely encoding competing alignment features, or polysemantic neurons active on both data types for unrelated reasons?
  • Could inference-time activation steering on shared neurons provide controllable helpfulness-harmlessness tradeoffs?
  • Does the asymmetric ablation effect reflect a fundamental property of the objectives or an artifact of HH-RLHF's data distribution?
