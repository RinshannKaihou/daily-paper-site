> **一句话总结：** 本文提出 Soft-Elo 方法，利用 LLM 裁判的评分差异估计每场对战的不确定性，将校准后的连续胜率概率（而非硬标签）传播到 Bradley-Terry 模型中，再结合分裂一致性预测为 Elo 排名提供分布无关的置信区间，在 LMArena 上 55 个留出模型上将 Elo MAE 从 45.9 降至 17.9，同时将置信区间宽度缩减 39–73%。

> **TL;DR:** The authors diagnose that standard Hard-Elo (binary win/loss/tie labels in Bradley-Terry) systematically over-stretches the Elo axis — strong models get inflated scores, weak models get deflated ones. Their fix, Soft-Elo, replaces hard labels with calibrated continuous probabilities σ(β*s) derived from the judge's own score differences. This flattens the strength-correlated residual ramp and reduces Elo MAE from 45.9 to 17.9 on LMArena across 8 judges and 55 held-out models. A second layer of split conformal prediction wraps each estimate in a distribution-free prediction interval, cutting median interval width by 39–73% at 90% nominal coverage. Cross-corpus replication on LMArena 140K and ComparIA (French) confirms the pattern, though it degrades on judges whose score differences do not correlate with human agreement.

## 核心贡献

- **诊断 Hard-Elo 的标尺失真问题：** 揭示了传统硬标签 Bradley-Terry 拟合虽然能保持排序（Spearman ρ > 0.87），但 Elo 刻度被系统性拉伸——强模型偏高、弱模型偏低，残差与模型强度高度相关（Pearson r = +0.49 ~ +0.90）。
- **Soft-Elo 局部校准：** 利用裁判的六维评分差异，通过最大似然估计拟合温度参数 β*，将分数映射为校准的连续胜率概率 σ(β*s)，替代硬标签输入 Bradley-Terry，从根本上解决标尺拉伸问题。
- **全局一致性预测区间：** 在留出模型上对 LLM-Elo 与人类-Elo 的残差应用分裂一致性预测（split conformal prediction），生成具有分布无关边际覆盖保证的预测区间，区间宽度缩减 39–73%。
- **跨语料验证：** 在 LMArena 140K（更新模型池）和 ComparIA（法语分布偏移）上复现了主要结论，并提供了基于 β* 和分数-一致性相关性的诊断信号来预警 Soft-Elo 的适用性。
- **代码开源：** https://github.com/kargibora/SoftElo

## Key Contributions

- **Diagnoses the "stretched ruler" problem in Hard-Elo:** Shows that binary win/loss/tie labels in Bradley-Terry preserve ranking order (Spearman ρ > 0.87 across 8 judges) but systematically mis-scale the Elo axis — signed residuals correlate +0.49 to +0.90 with model strength, producing MAE of 27.5–63.4 Elo depending on judge.
- **Soft-Elo via calibrated score-difference probabilities:** Replaces hard ternary labels with continuous soft targets ỹ(x) = σ(β*s(x)), where β* is fit via MLE on human non-tie battles in a leave-one-model-out protocol. This preserves rank order (Δρ ≤ 0.014) while reducing Elo MAE by 39–73% per judge (mean 45.9 → 17.9).
- **Model-level conformal intervals with distribution-free guarantees:** Applies split conformal prediction to LLM–human Elo residuals using normalized nonconformity scores (absolute residual / bootstrap SE). Achieves 88.6–97.9% empirical coverage at 90% nominal level while reducing median interval width by 39–73%.
- **Cross-corpus replication and diagnostic:** Replicates on LMArena 140K (45–79% MAE reduction) and ComparIA French corpus (20–81% MAE reduction). Identifies that low β* and flat score-agreement correlation serve as early warnings for when Soft-Elo may not help.
- **Sample efficiency:** Soft-Elo outperforms Hard-Elo at every annotation budget down to b = 5 battles per model, with the largest gap in the small-data regime.

## Methodology

The paper introduces a two-layer calibration framework built on top of the standard Bradley-Terry / Elo pipeline:

**Setup.** Each model is evaluated through pairwise battles against multiple opponents on LMArena 100K prompts. A judge LLM scores each completion on six criteria (adherence, helpfulness, factuality, completeness, clarity, fluency) on a 1–10 scale, and the averaged per-criterion difference becomes the battle score s(x). Position bias is mitigated by evaluating both presentation orders and averaging.

**Layer 1 — Local: Soft-Elo.** The key insight is that the standard practice collapses the continuous score difference s(x) into a ternary label {0, 0.5, 1}, discarding the uncertainty signal. Close battles (|s| ≈ 0.2) and decisive ones (|s| ≈ 5.6) receive identical treatment. Soft-Elo instead maps s(x) through a calibrated logistic: ỹ(x) = σ(β*s(x)), where β* is fit via maximum likelihood on human non-tie battles excluding the held-out model (Eq. 6). This temperature is highly stable across folds (std ≤ 0.005) and stabilizes at b ≈ 50 battles per model. The soft targets are then fed into the standard BT log-likelihood (Eq. 1), fitting in two steps: first anchor strengths on anchor-only battles, then the held-out model's strength with anchors fixed.

**Layer 2 — Global: Split Conformal Prediction.** Even after Soft-Elo calibration, a residual gap between LLM-derived and human-derived Elo remains. The authors define per-model signed residuals εi = EloLLM,i − EloHuman,i and normalized nonconformity scores Si = |εi| / SE_i (where SE_i is the bootstrap standard error of EloLLM,i). The conformal interval for a new model is EloLLM,n+1 ± q̂·SE_n+1, where q̂ is the appropriate quantile of calibration nonconformity scores. Under exchangeability, this achieves marginal coverage ≥ 1−α.

**Evaluation protocol.** Leave-one-model-out over 55 models from LMArena 100K, with 8 different judge LLMs (DeepSeek-V3.2, Qwen3.5-27B, Gemma4-E4B, Gemma4-26B-A4B, Qwen3-32B, Llama-3.3-70B, GPT-OSS-20B, GPT-OSS-120B). Each judge annotates ~25,000 battle rows. Conformal intervals evaluated with 5 random 27/28 calibration/test splits at α = 0.10.

## Results & Highlights

- **Elo MAE reduction:** Mean MAE drops from 45.9 (Hard-Elo) to 17.9 (Soft-Elo) across 8 judges and 55 held-out models. Per-judge reductions range from 39% (Qwen3-32B) to 73% (DeepSeek-V3.2).
- **Ranking preserved:** Spearman ρ change is negligible (Δρ ∈ [−0.011, +0.012]), confirming Soft-Elo corrects scale without disrupting order.
- **Conformal intervals dramatically narrowed:** At 90% nominal coverage, median interval width drops from 132–261 Elo (Hard) to 70–143 Elo (Soft), a 39–73% reduction. Empirical coverage remains at 92.1–96.4%.
- **Score difference is a real uncertainty signal:** Judge–human agreement rises monotonically from ~53% at |s| < 0.5 to ~85% at |s| > 4, validating the information content of score differences.
- **Cross-corpus transfer:** On LMArena 140K, MAE reductions of 45–79%. On ComparIA (French), 20–81% reductions, but Qwen judges show degraded rank correlation when β* falls to 0.13–0.14 (score difference no longer predicts human agreement).
- **Sample efficiency:** Soft-Elo outperforms Hard-Elo at every budget from b = 5 to b = 200 battles per model; the advantage is largest in the low-data regime.
- **β* as a diagnostic:** When β* is large (> 0.3), score differences carry meaningful signal. When β* is small (~0.13), the score-agreement correlation is flat and Soft-Elo may over-compress the leaderboard.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** The core idea — replacing hard BT labels with calibrated soft targets from score differences — is simple and elegant. The insight that Hard-Elo's "stretched ruler" problem stems from information loss (not just judge bias) is genuinely valuable. The two-layer design (local soft targets + global conformal intervals) is well-motivated and cleanly executed.
- **Rigor:** Excellent. The leave-one-model-out protocol is strict: no human labels involving the held-out model leak into β* calibration. The conformal construction is standard and well-explained. Cross-corpus replication on two additional datasets with honest reporting of failure modes (Qwen judges on ComparIA) strengthens credibility.
- **Reproducibility:** Strong — public code, standard benchmarks (LMArena), detailed appendices with full prompts and pseudocode.
- **Significance:** Directly addresses a practical pain point. The Gemini-2.5 example (win rate shifting from 79% to 49% depending on judge, changing rank from 2nd to 8th) is a compelling motivator. The framework gives developers a principled alternative to fragile single-baseline win rates.
- **Potential concerns:**
  - The conformal guarantee is marginal (not conditional), and relies on exchangeability between calibration and test models. Temporal or distributional shifts (e.g., a new generation of reasoning models) could violate this. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't quantify how robust the intervals are to moderate violations.
  - The method still requires some human-rated models for conformal calibration. It reduces but does not eliminate the need for human annotation.
  - The 17.9 Elo MAE, while much improved, still means two models within ~18 Elo of each other are essentially indistinguishable — this is a meaningful limitation for close leaderboard comparisons.
  - All experiments use the same 6-criterion scoring prompt; it's unclear how sensitive the approach is to the scoring prompt design.

## Strengths

- Clean, minimal intervention: keeps Bradley-Terry unchanged, only modifies the training targets — easy to adopt in existing pipelines.
- Principled uncertainty quantification with distribution-free guarantees via conformal prediction.
- Thorough empirical evaluation: 8 judges, 55 models, 3 corpora, sample efficiency curves, stability analysis.
- Honest about limitations: identifies and reports conditions where Soft-Elo degrades (low β* on ComparIA), rather than cherry-picking results.
- The β* diagnostic provides a practical check for practitioners before deploying Soft-Elo on a new judge/corpus pair.
- Strong sample efficiency in the low-data regime — valuable when annotation budgets are limited.

## Limitations

- **Exchangeability assumption:** Conformal coverage guarantees require that calibration and test models are exchangeable. Rapid model evolution (e.g., new architectures, reasoning models) may violate this, requiring recalibration.
- **No epistemic uncertainty modeling:** Soft-Elo captures aleatoric uncertainty from score differences but does not model judge hallucination, prompt ambiguity, or judge instability.
- **Residual human annotation needed:** The conformal calibration set requires models with both human and LLM-derived Elo; fully annotation-free evaluation is not achieved.
- **Scoring prompt sensitivity:** All experiments use a specific 6-criterion scoring prompt with 1–10 scales. The method's effectiveness may depend on this prompt design producing meaningful score differences.
- **Residual MAE:** 17.9 Elo MAE is impressive relative to Hard-Elo but still represents ~4.5% of the typical Elo range (~400 points), limiting precision for closely ranked models.

## Relevance

该工作直接解决 LLM-as-a-judge 评估中 Elo 排名系统性失真的问题，提出了有统计保证的校准框架。对于依赖自动化评测进行模型选择和排行榜构建的研究者具有高度实用价值，尤其是其分布无关的置信区间可以在不增加人工标注成本的前提下量化排名的不确定性。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does Soft-Elo perform with judges that produce categorical rather than continuous scores (e.g., judges that output only win/tie/loss without intermediate confidence)?
- Can the β* diagnostic be used adaptively — i.e., can a practitioner detect in real-time that Soft-Elo is degrading on a new corpus without access to human labels?
- How does the approach interact with tie-aware BT extensions (e.g., the covariance-aware arena model of Ameli et al. 2025)? Could combining soft targets with improved BT models yield further gains?
- What happens when the calibration pool of human-rated models becomes stale relative to the test model (e.g., calibrating on pre-reasoning models but evaluating a post-reasoning model)?
- Can the conformal intervals be made conditional on model family or capability tier, rather than marginal, to provide tighter bounds for specific model types?
