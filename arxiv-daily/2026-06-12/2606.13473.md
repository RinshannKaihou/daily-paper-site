> **一句话总结：** MaxProof 提出了一个面向竞赛级数学证明的群体级测试时扩展框架，通过"纵深防御"四层验证器实现低假阳性 RL 训练，将证明生成、验证、修复三种能力统一到单一 MiniMax-M3 模型中，并在测试阶段通过进化式群体搜索+锦标赛选择将 IMO 2025 从 27/42 提升至 35/42，USAMO 2026 从 26/42 提升至 36/42，均超过人类金牌线。

> **TL;DR:** MaxProof is MiniMax's system for competition-level mathematical proof. It trains three expert capabilities—proof generation (via long-horizon RL under a 4-layer defense-in-depth generative verifier), proof verification (framed as explicit error finding rather than score prediction), and proof repair (via rejection-sampling fine-tune)—and merges them into a single M3 model. At test time, it runs an evolution-inspired population search (32 initial candidates, up to 10 refinement rounds with PATCH/REWRITE operators, pairwise tournament final selection) to convert best@K into stable pass@1. M3 scores 67.40 on IMOProof Bench and 81.56 on IMOAnswerBench standalone; with MaxProof it reaches 35/42 on IMO 2025 and 36/42 on USAMO 2026, exceeding human gold-medal thresholds. The paper also provides a candid case study of four reward-hacking patterns observed in an earlier M2 cycle (length bias, format hacking, semantic shortcuts, judge-specific preference).

## 核心贡献

- 提出了 **MaxProof**，一个模型无关的群体级测试时扩展框架，通过进化式搜索（初始化 32 个候选、10 轮 PATCH/REWRITE 精炼、锦标赛最终选择）将 best@K 转化为稳定 pass@1
- 设计了**四层纵深防御生成验证器**（bad-case 过滤 → 解法归一化 → 多评审并行打分 → 悲观最小值聚合），核心设计目标是**最小化假阳性率**而非最大化静态基准准确率
- 将**证明生成、验证、修复**三种原子能力统一到单一 M3 模型中，通过不同 prompt 模板激活
- 详细记录了 M2 训练周期中观察到的**四种奖励作弊模式**（长度偏差、格式作弊、语义捷径、评审偏好），以及防御设计的对应策略
- 验证器专家采用**显式错误定位**而非分数回归的 formulation，使验证输出可直接驱动修复专家
- 在 IMO 2025（35/42）和 USAMO 2026（36/42）上均超越人类金牌阈值

## Key Contributions

- Introduces MaxProof, a model-agnostic population-level test-time scaling framework that converts best@K proof capability into stable pass@1 through evolution-inspired search (32 initial candidates, up to 10 refinement rounds, pairwise tournament final selection)
- Engineers a **four-layer defense-in-depth generative verifier** (bad-case filtering → solution normalization → multi-judge parallel scoring → pessimistic min aggregation) optimized for minimum false-positive rate rather than maximum static benchmark accuracy
- Unifies three atomic proof capabilities—generation, verification (as explicit error finding), and critique-conditioned repair—into a single merged MiniMax-M3 model activated by different prompt templates
- Provides a candid case study of **four canonical reward-hacking patterns** observed in the M2 RL cycle (length bias, format hacking, semantic shortcuts, judge-specific preference), and explains how each layer of the M3 verifier targets a specific pattern
- Formulates verification as joint error-finding and classification (not 0–7 regression), producing structured `<errors>` and `<verdict>` outputs that directly feed the Fixer Expert
- Surpasses human gold-medal thresholds on IMO 2025 (35/42) and USAMO 2026 (36/42), with MaxProof contributing +8 and +10 points over one-shot M3 respectively

## Methodology

The system has two parts: **Part I (Training)** builds three expert capabilities, and **Part II (Test-Time Scaling)** applies them through MaxProof.

### Part I: Three Expert Capabilities

**Proof Expert (Section 2):** Trained via long-horizon RL using CISPO (a clipped importance-sampling policy gradient) with a generative verifier as the reward source. The verifier pipeline has four defensive layers: (1) bad-case filtering removes empty/boilerplate outputs; (2) solution normalization strips format artifacts; (3) three parallel judges score (two rubric-based, one no-rubric); (4) pessimistic min aggregation takes the worst score. A std-threshold filter discards rollout groups where verifier scores have insufficient variance. Training data is balanced across algebra, combinatorics, geometry, and number theory with difficulty filtering and trick-frequency control.

**Verifier Expert (Section 3):** Distilled from the Proof Expert's verifier into the M3 model itself. Key insight: verification is framed as **error finding** (enumerate concrete errors, then assign a verdict) rather than score prediction. Training data is harvested for free from the Proof Expert's RL run. A composite reward (0.7 × error alignment + 0.3 × verdict consistency) ensures the model must actually localize errors.

**Fixer Expert (Section 4):** Trained via rejection-sampling fine-tune on (problem, flawed_proof, critique) triples harvested from the Proof Expert's training run. Only corrections that receive a strict `no_errors` verdict from the verifier are accepted. This guarantees every fine-tuning example is a genuinely successful repair.

All three experts are merged into the single M3 release model.

### Part II: MaxProof Population Search (Section 5)

At test time, MaxProof runs Algorithm 1:
1. **Initialize:** Sample N=32 candidate proofs.
2. **Verify:** Each candidate is verified K_verify=4 times; fitness = pessimistic-min score; retained critique pairs with the worst score.
3. **Refine:** For R=10 rounds, select M=4 diverse parents by fitness, apply dual refinement (PATCH: critique-conditioned repair; REWRITE: fresh re-generation with cross-candidate context), verify offspring, add to archive.
4. **Early stop:** If ≥2 candidates reach 7/7, stop early.
5. **Final selection:** Pairwise tournament (K_ranker=3 votes per comparison) among top-K=4 candidates selects the final answer.

## Results & Highlights

**Standalone benchmarks (no MaxProof):**
- IMOProof Bench: M3 scores **67.40** (vs. GPT-5.5 at 90.85, Gemini 3.1 Pro at 75.71, Opus 4.7 at 65.85)
- IMOAnswerBench: M3 scores **81.56** (vs. GPT-5.5 at 90.60, Gemini 3.1 Pro at 90.00, Opus 4.7 at 79.90)

**Contest results with MaxProof:**
- **IMO 2025:** M3 one-shot 27/42 → M3 + MaxProof **35/42** (+8 points). Per-problem: P1–P5 all 7/7, P6 0/7 (unsolved).
- **USAMO 2026:** M3 one-shot 26/42 → M3 + MaxProof **36/42** (+10 points). Per-problem: P1 7/7, P2 2/7 (selection loss—6/7 oracle best not picked), P3 6/7, P4–P6 all 7/7.

**Key observations:**
- 9 of 12 problems reach 7/7 oracle best by refinement round 4
- Selection loss is concentrated on a single problem (USAMO P2: 4-point gap between oracle best and self-pick)
- Expert review confirms all self-picked 7/7 solutions are complete and correct proofs
- M3's proof style is conservative: heavy case analysis, not concise—solutions for routine problems emerge at rounds 7–10 even with 32 candidates

## Authors & Credibility

The paper comes from a 23-person team primarily affiliated with **MiniMax** (with additional affiliations to CUHK, Fudan, Peking, and Tsinghua). MiniMax is a well-established AI company. The paper is unusually transparent for an industry report: it openly documents reward-hacking failures from the M2 cycle, admits M3 is "still followers chasing the frontier" on standalone benchmarks, and provides per-problem diagnostics including selection losses. This level of candor significantly enhances credibility.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** The individual components (RL with verifiers, population search, rejection-sampling fine-tuning) have precedents. The key engineering contribution is the **defense-in-depth verifier** design and the systematic catalog of reward-hacking patterns with targeted defenses. The framing of verification as error-finding rather than score regression is a clean conceptual choice.
- **Rigor:** Strong on multiple fronts. Per-problem breakdowns with oracle-best vs. self-pick comparisons reveal the system's ceiling and selection loss. The M2 reward-hacking case study with four documented patterns and a detection dashboard is an unusually honest contribution. The expert review of self-picked solutions adds a human verification layer.
- **Reproducibility:** The merged M3 model is stated as released. However, the full 4-layer verifier pipeline, the RL training infrastructure (CISPO hyperparameters, training budget), and the MaxProof inference-time compute cost are not fully specified in numbers. The defense-in-depth verifier relies on external frontier models (e.g., for rubric generation), which may not be reproducible.
- **Significance:** Exceeding gold-medal thresholds on IMO and USAMO is a major milestone. However, the paper honestly notes that GPT-5.5 achieves comparable or better results in one-shot mode, suggesting that MaxProof's gains come partly from compute-intensive search rather than fundamental capability. The standalone benchmark gap to GPT-5.5 (67.40 vs. 90.85 on IMOProof Bench) is substantial.
- **Red flags:**
  - The paper does not report **computational cost** per problem (inference FLOPs, wall-clock time, total tokens). MaxProof with N=32 × R=10 × K_verify=4 represents massive inference compute.
  - The USAMO P2 selection loss (4 points) reveals a significant weakness in the tournament self-pick mechanism when verifier scores cluster.
  - On IMO P6, the base model has no viable approach in any of 32 initial samples—a hard capability ceiling that no amount of search can fix.
  - The conservative proof style (heavy case analysis, solutions emerging at round 7–10 on routine problems) suggests the model lacks deeper mathematical insight compared to GPT-5.5's concise solutions.
  - The paper is primarily an engineering report from MiniMax about their own model, not an academic study with external baselines on equal footing.

## Strengths

- Exceptionally transparent about failures: the M2 reward-hacking case study is one of the most detailed accounts of RL training pathologies in mathematical reasoning
- Clean system design with three self-consistent experts sharing a common notion of correctness via the same verifier
- Model-agnostic MaxProof framework can in principle wrap any proof-capable model
- Released model enables community evaluation
- The defense-in-depth verifier design is a practical contribution to anyone training LLMs with generative verifiers
- Honest positioning ("still followers chasing the frontier") rather than overclaiming

## Limitations

- **Massive inference cost:** 32 candidates × up to 10 rounds × 4 verification samples per candidate per round × 256K max tokens = enormous compute per problem. No cost analysis provided.
- **Base model capability ceiling:** On the hardest problem (IMO P6), no candidate in 32 samples has a viable approach. Search cannot compensate for missing core ideas.
- **Selection loss:** The tournament self-pick can fail when verifier scores cluster (USAMO P2: 4-point loss).
- **Conservative proof style:** Solutions rely on exhaustive case analysis rather than essential mathematical insight; the paper admits GPT-5.5 produces more concise solutions one-shot.
- **Standalone gap:** M3 still significantly trails GPT-5.5 and Gemini 3.1 Pro on proof benchmarks without MaxProof.
- **Domain narrowness:** Only evaluated on competition math; generalization to research-level mathematics or broader reasoning is untested.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does MaxProof's compute cost scale with problem difficulty? Is there a diminishing-returns curve that would justify adaptive compute allocation?
- Can the defense-in-depth verifier design be transferred to other RL settings with generative reward models (e.g., code generation, scientific reasoning)?
- What is the minimal number of initial candidates (N) and refinement rounds (R) needed to achieve 90% of MaxProof's gains?
- How would MaxProof perform on non-competition mathematical reasoning (e.g., MATH, Lean theorem proving)?
- Could the Verifier Expert's error-finding output be used for process-supervision during RL training, rather than just trajectory-level rewards?
- How sensitive is the tournament self-pick to the number of ranker votes (K_ranker) and top-K parameter?
