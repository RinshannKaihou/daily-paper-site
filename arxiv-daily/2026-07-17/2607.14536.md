> **一句话总结：** 本文将 Muon 优化器中喂给 polar 映射的"矩阵化表示"重新定义为一种优化器几何选择，构造了从 Native 到最近方、瘦型直至向量端点（nSGDM）的表示阶梯；理论证明短边长度 p_T 决定 rank、norm scaling 和 O(K^{-1/4}) 站点性常数，曲率塌缩 + MP 谱分布给出 β(γ)γ^{1/4} 的早期能量耗散序，LLaMA2-130M/600M 实验显示平衡的非原生表示（如 Square）可逼近 Native（600M 上差距仅约 0.003 validation loss），而短边越小行为越接近 nSGDM。

> **TL;DR:** Muse reframes the matrix representation fed to Muon's polar map as an optimizer-geometry choice and builds a representation ladder (Native → nearest-Square → skinny-θ → Vector/nSGDM). Theory shows the short side p_T controls rank, pulled-back norm, stationarity constants (eO(K^{-1/4})) and that curvature collapse with a Marchenko–Pastur profile orders early dissipation by β(γ)γ^{1/4}. On LLaMA2-130M (2.60B tokens) and LLaMA2-600M (12.58B tokens), balanced non-native representations like Square match Native (within ~0.003 validation loss at 600M), while shrinking the short side climbs toward Vector/nSGDM and degrades final loss; AdamW is included as a coordinate-wise adaptive reference.

## 核心贡献

- 提出并形式化"Muon 的矩阵化表示"为一个真正的优化器自由度：对于任意 Frobenius-等距的矩阵化 T: R^d → R^{p×q}，T^{-1}Orth(T(Z)) ≠ S^{-1}Orth(S(Z))，因此同一个 Muon 动量规则在不同表示下产生不同更新几何。
- 构造了"短边阶梯"（short-side ladder）：Native、最近方（Square）、瘦型 Sk-θ（θ ∈ {1/8, 1/4, 1/2, 3/4}，论文中对应 Sk-12.5/25/50/75）与向量端点（Vector），统一同一动量规则与 Newton–Schulz polar 后端，仅改变表示；Vector 端点精确还原归一化带动量 SGD（nSGDM）。
- 在表示坐标下证明 polar 更新是谱球上的最速下降（Theorem 3.1：<G, D_T(G)> = ‖T(G)‖_*），并给出统一 O(K^{-1/4}) 非凸站点性保证（Theorem 3.2），常数仅通过短边 p_T 进入界。
- 提出"曲率塌缩 + Marchenko–Pastur 谱"机制（Theorem 3.3/3.5）：在 teacher–student 模型中，W-块经验 Hessian 在 C,N→∞ 下塌缩为标量 Frobenius 二次型，早期能量耗散由 β(γ_T)γ_T^{1/4} 排序；square-to-vector 主阶比为 Θ(d^{1/4})。
- 在 LLaMA2-130M 与 LLaMA2-600M 预训练上系统验证该表示轴，并通过保存动量块的固定动量几何诊断（nuclear support、方向不一致度、90% 奇异通道数）解释 Muon 与 nSGDM 的分离。

## Key Contributions

- Formalizes the matrix representation supplied to Muon's polar map as a genuine optimizer-geometry degree of freedom: for Frobenius-isometric S and T, T^{-1}Orth(T(Z)) ≠ S^{-1}Orth(S(Z)), so the layout is not a relabeling for Muon.
- Builds a controlled "short-side ladder" (Native, Square, Sk-12.5/25/50/75, Vector) that shares the Muon momentum rule and Newton–Schulz backend, with the Vector endpoint exactly recovering normalized-SGD-with-momentum (nSGDM).
- Proves the represented polar update is steepest descent on the spectral ball (Theorem 3.1: ⟨G, D_T(G)⟩_F = ‖T(G)‖_*) and gives a unified eO(K^{-1/4}) nonconvex stationarity bound (Theorem 3.2) whose constants enter only through the short side p_T.
- Establishes a curvature-collapse + Marchenko–Pastur mechanism (Theorems 3.3 and 3.5) in a teacher–student model: the empirical W-block Hessian collapses to a scalar Frobenius quadratic as C,N → ∞, ordering early normalized dissipation by β(γ_T)γ_T^{1/4}, with square-to-vector leading-order ratio Θ(d^{1/4}).
- Validates the representation axis at LLaMA2-130M and LLaMA2-600M scale, complemented by fixed-momentum diagnostics on saved momentum blocks (represented nuclear support, native-direction disagreement, 90% singular-channel count).

## Methodology

The paper studies the stochastic nonconvex problem min_X f(X) = E_ξ[f(X; ξ)] under standard L-smoothness, unbiasedness (Assumption 2.2), and bounded-variance σ² (Assumption 2.3) assumptions. The core idea is to parameterize Muon-style updates by a fixed structured Frobenius-isometric representation T: R^d → R^{p_T × q_T} with p_T ≤ q_T.

**Algorithm (Muse, Algorithm 1).** Initialize M_0 = 0. At step t: draw ξ_t, compute stochastic gradient Ĝ_t = ∇f(X_t; ξ_t), update momentum M_t = μM_{t-1} + (1-μ)Ĝ_t, then apply the pulled-back polar direction X_{t+1} = X_t - η_t D_T(M_t) where D_T(Z) := T^{-1}(Orth(T(Z))) and Orth(A) = UV^T (compact SVD). Native Muon uses the layer's natural matrixization; nSGDM is recovered at the vector endpoint where spectral, nuclear, and Frobenius norms coincide.

**The short-side ladder.** For a d-dimensional block with native short side s = min(m, n), the skinny-θ representations choose p = argmin_a{a | d, a ≤ s, |a - θs|} for θ ∈ {1/8, 1/4, 1/2, 3/4}. The Square (nearest-square) choice picks the divisor of d closest to √d. All reshape choices preserve Frobenius geometry (no extra curvature/second-moment state), distinguishing them from Shampoo/SOAP/diagonal preconditioning.

**Theory.**
- *Spectral-ball descent identity* (Thm 3.1): ⟨G, D_T(G)⟩_F = ‖T(G)‖_* and -D_T(G) minimizes ⟨G, ΔX⟩ over ‖T(ΔX)‖_2 ≤ 1; smooth descent gives f(X - ηD_T(G)) ≤ f(X) - η‖T(G)‖_* + (Lη²/2)rank(T(G)).
- *Stationarity* (Thm 3.2): with η_t = ηt^{-3/4} and α_t = 1 - αt^{-1/2}, min_{t≤K} E‖T(∇f(X_t))‖_* ≤ (1/ηK^{1/4})·[...] where the bracket depends on p_T only through the short side; all representations share the eO(K^{-1/4}) rate.
- *Curvature collapse* (Thm 3.3): in a teacher–student model with Gaussian inputs and ReLU, the empirical fixed-mask W-block Hessian's Gershgorin window collapses (β_{1,C,N} > 0 with probability →1, ρ_{C,N} → 0), so local curvature becomes a scalar Frobenius quadratic.
- *Aspect-ratio dissipation* (Thm 3.5): under an MP spectral profile for the represented perturbation, the leading early normalized dissipation is ordered by β(γ_T)γ_T^{1/4}/(σ_0 d^{1/4}); reducing the short side lowers the leading-order dissipation. Corollary 3.6 gives matching learning-rate calibration η_T ~ (τ_d/2)σ_0√q_T / β(γ_T).

**Empirical setup.** LLaMA2-style pretraining at 130M (2.60B tokens; 8×Ascend 910B NPUs, 96GB) and 600M (12.58B tokens; 8×910C NPUs, 64GB), all results averaged over three random seeds. Fixed-momentum diagnostics re-apply alternative representations to saved momentum blocks to measure nuclear support, native-direction disagreement, and 90% singular-channel count across transformer modules (Attn QKV, Attn out, MLP W1/W2/proj).

## Results & Highlights

- **Representation axis at 600M scale:** Across the LLaMA2-600M 12.58B-token sweep, Native and Square occupy the low-loss end of the polar family while Vector is clearly separated; **Square is within 0.003 validation loss of Native** at 600M, demonstrating that the layer's native layout is not a canonical necessity.
- **Short-side ladder ordering:** At both 130M and 600M, the representation ordering is consistent — Native ≈ Square < Sk-75 < Sk-50 < Sk-25 < Sk-12.5 < Vector — and the paper explicitly notes that "short-side reductions form a higher-loss ladder, and Vector is clearly separated" (Figure 4, Section 4.1).
- **Learning-rate calibration shifts with short side (Figure 3a, single-layer sweep at (m,n,C)=(96,192,768)):** selected points move to larger learning rates for narrower/vectorized representations (Native and Square at LR=5e-05, Sk-50 at 1e-04, Sk-25/Sk-12.5/Vector at 2.5e-04), even when best final losses are comparable — directly confirming Corollary 3.6's η_T ~ √q_T scaling.
- **Depth amplifies the gap (Figure 3b):** Best final normalized loss vs hidden depth shows that the cost of short-side reduction grows with depth — at 10 hidden layers Vector is ~2 orders of magnitude worse than Native/Square in normalized loss, while at depth 1 they are nearly tied.
- **Nuclear support geometry (Figure 1a):** On saved LLaMA2 momentum blocks, Vector (nSGDM) lies on the Frobenius–nuclear identity line (‖·‖_* = ‖·‖_F), whereas matrix representations exhibit substantially greater nuclear support at comparable Frobenius scales — a direct quantitative demonstration of why the polar step separates from normalized momentum.
- **MP shape factor consistency (Figure 1b):** The empirical shape factor ĝ_T := d^{-1/4}‖T(M)‖_* / ‖T(M)‖_F for LLaMA2 MLP-down/W2 blocks tracks the theoretical g(γ) = β(γ)γ^{1/4} and increases monotonically with the represented aspect ratio at both 130M and 600M, empirically validating the β(γ)γ^{1/4} dissipation ordering predicted by Theorem 3.5.
- **Channel usage separation (Figure 5):** At the final 600M checkpoint, Vector sits near the identity-line regime with very few 90%-nuclear-mass channels, while matrix representations occupy higher-nuclear-support regimes and use substantially more singular channels, localizing the nuclear-support separation across Attn QKV/out and MLP W1/W2/proj modules.
- **Theoretical square-to-vector leading-order ratio:** Under Gaussian/MP normalization with β(1) = 8/(3π), the square-to-vector leading-order dissipation ratio is Θ(d^{1/4}) (Remark 3.7), quantifying the geometric advantage of a balanced matrix representation over the vector endpoint.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** The framing is genuinely fresh — most Muon analyses (Chen et al. 2025; Pethick et al. 2025; Sfyraki & Wang 2025) retain the native matrix representation and study the polar map itself, whereas Muse isolates the matrixization as the controlled variable while holding the momentum rule and Newton–Schulz backend fixed. This cleanly separates a Muon-specific degree of freedom from coordinate-wise adaptivity and tensor preconditioning.
- **Rigor:** The theory is rigorous and self-contained. Theorem 3.2's stationarity bound is essentially standard (and the authors acknowledge it complements Hübler et al.'s 2026 heavy-tailed analysis, which gives sharper dimension dependence); the more original content is Theorem 3.5's β(γ)γ^{1/4} dissipation ordering under curvature collapse. However, the curvature-collapse result (Theorem 3.3) only holds in a teacher–student model with fixed ReLU masks and a single empirical cell — a strong locality caveat the authors acknowledge in Section 6.
- **Reproducibility:** Strong on the empirical side: three random seeds, fixed tokenizer/data, explicit hardware (Ascend 910B/910C), and detailed settings deferred to Appendix G. The representation axis is trivially implementable on top of any Muon codebase (it is just a reshape). However, the paper does not release code or training configs alongside publication, and the curriculum of which divisor p to pick for each skinny-θ is implementation-dependent.
- **Significance:** The central message — "Muon's native layout is one admissible geometry rather than a canonical necessity; balanced reshapes match it, short-side reduction degrades toward nSGDM" — is a useful conceptual clarification for the rapidly-growing Muon literature. It also provides a geometric explanation for the long-observed Muon-vs-nSGDM gap.
- **Red flags / weaknesses:**
  - **The strongest baseline is missing.** AdamW appears only as a "coordinate-wise adaptive reference" in the 130M row; no AdamW numbers are given at 600M, and there is no comparison to Muon variants that are actually competitive at this scale (Muoneq, Muon + balancing, Soap, Adafactor). The Square-vs-Native gap of ~0.003 at 600M is small but the paper never positions Muse's representations against strong Muon variants — the reader cannot tell whether any non-native representation is ever strictly better than Native.
  - **No ablation on weight decay, momentum μ, or Newton–Schulz iteration count.** All these are held fixed; since representation changes the update magnitude, their interaction with the (held-fixed) Muon scaling convention is unexamined.
  - **Scale ceiling is modest.** 600M parameters and 12.58B tokens is small relative to where Muon is actually used (multi-billion-parameter pretraining, e.g., DeepSeek-V4). The depth-amplification result (Figure 3b) suggests the gap widens with depth, so the 600M conclusion could be optimistic; conversely, the "balanced reshapes match Native" claim is untested above 600M.
  - **The MP profile is a model, not a measurement.** The β(γ)γ^{1/4} prediction is evaluated against the empirical ĝ_T, which is computed on saved momentum blocks that are themselves products of Muon training — a partially circular validation. The paper acknowledges (Remark 3.5) that the MP profile is "an isotropic large-dimensional spectral baseline," but does not test sensitivity to non-MP spectra.
  - **Cherry-picked aspect-ratio range.** The skinny-θ ladder {1/8, 1/4, 1/2, 3/4} is symmetric and tidy; intermediate or block-specific adaptive choices are not explored.

## Strengths

- Conceptually clean isolation of one optimizer degree of freedom (representation) with everything else (momentum rule, polar backend, scaling) shared across variants.
- A unified theoretical framework (Theorems 3.1, 3.2) that explains Muon, nSGDM, and the entire Muse family as different points on a single spectral-ball descent geometry.
- The curvature-collapse + MP mechanism (Theorem 3.5) gives a concrete, falsifiable prediction (β(γ)γ^{1/4} ordering) that the LLaMA2 measurements support.
- Honest, clearly-stated limitations section (Section 6) explicitly enumerating the locality, single-cell, and β_{1,C,N}-can-go-negative caveats.
- Three-seed averaging at both 130M and 600M scales, with module-level fixed-momentum diagnostics across multiple transformer block types.

## Limitations

- The theoretical dissipation result (Theorem 3.5) holds only locally: fixed ReLU masks, single empirical cell, large-class/large-sample asymptotics. In real nonconvex training, the Gershgorin lower endpoint β_{1,C,N} can become negative due to activation-pattern changes and cross-layer coupling.
- The Marchenko–Pastur profile is an isotropic spectral baseline; actual momentum-block spectra (which are themselves produced by Muon training) need not match MP, and sensitivity to non-MP spectra is not tested.
- No comparison against strong contemporary Muon variants (Muoneq, balanced Muon, Soap, Adafactor) at 600M; AdamW is omitted from the 600M row.
- Scale ceiling (600M / 12.58B tokens) is well below the regime where Muon is most impactful; depth-amplification results suggest conclusions could shift at larger scale.
- No ablations on weight decay, momentum coefficient, or Newton–Schulz iteration count interacting with the representation choice.

## Relevance

This paper is highly relevant if your interests include LLM optimizer design, Muon-family methods, or training-dynamics theory. It contributes a new conceptual axis (matrix representation as optimizer geometry) and a concrete, validated prediction (β(γ)γ^{1/4} dissipation ordering) that is useful both for understanding why Muon works and for designing principled non-native matrixizations for layers where the native layout is awkward (e.g., non-square or thin embedding/projection matrices).

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Do balanced non-native representations ever strictly beat Native at ≥1B parameter scale, or does the ~0.003 advantage at 600M vanish / flip as depth grows?
- How does the representation axis interact with layer-dependent Muon scaling, weight decay, and adaptive variants like Muoneq or Adamuon?
- Can the β(γ)γ^{1/4} dissipation prediction be sharpened for non-MP spectra (e.g., heavy-tailed momentum spectra observed in real LLM training), and does the ordering still hold?
- Is there a per-module adaptive representation choice (rather than one global T) that exploits the heterogeneity visible in Figure 5 across Attn vs MLP modules?
- Does the curvature-collapse result (Theorem 3.3) extend beyond the teacher–student / fixed-mask regime to realistic multi-layer Transformers, where cross-layer coupling is non-trivial?
