> **一句话总结：** xHC 通过"时间特征增强(多尺度因果卷积)+稀疏残差流架构(只更新 k=4/N=16 个流但保持稠密读取)"打破了 Hyper-Connections 在 N=4 处的瓶颈，在 18B/28B MoE 上相对 mHC 把下游均值提升 4.0/3.1 个点，且把达到同等 loss 所需算力降到 vanilla 的 1/1.50 和 mHC 的 1/1.19。

> **TL;DR:** xHC is the first Hyper-Connections (HC) variant to push the residual-stream expansion rate N meaningfully beyond 4. It diagnoses two bottlenecks in the state-of-the-art Manifold-Constrained HC (mHC) — a single-vector write-back "information bottleneck" and an O(N³C) residual-mixing "cost bottleneck" — and fixes them with temporal feature augmentation (multi-scale causal depthwise convolutions) plus an asymmetric sparse architecture that updates only k=4 of N=16 streams while keeping dense read access. On DeepSeekMoE-style 18B and 28B MoE models trained on the same data, xHC raises the 12-benchmark average from 44.8 (mHC) to 48.8 (xHC) at 18B and from 50.5 to 53.6 at 28B for only +3.0–4.1% training FLOPs over vanilla; scaling-law fits say vanilla needs 1.50× and mHC needs 1.19× the compute of xHC to reach the same loss. A lightweight xHC-Flash-4sub variant cuts residual-stream memory traffic to 40C (vs 34C for mHC at N=4) while keeping nearly all the gain.

## 核心贡献

- **诊断两个瓶颈**：系统解释了为何 mHC 在 N>4 时收益递减——(1) 每层只把单个 write-back 向量写入所有流的"信息瓶颈"，(2) 生成 Hres 时 O(N³C) 的"成本瓶颈"。在 2.5B MoE 上实测，mHC 从 N=4→16 只降 0.006 loss 却多花 32% FLOPs。
- **提出 xHC(Expanded Hyper-Connections)**：首个把 HC 家族成功扩到 N=4 之外的方法。用"时间特征增强"(r=3 个核大小 {4,8,12} 的因果 depthwise 卷积 + Gram–Schmidt 正交化) 丰富 write-back 信号；用"稀疏残差流架构"(sigmoid router 选 k=4 个流，含 m 个固定流 + TopK 路由，read 保持稠密) 把 Hres 生成成本从 O(N³C) 降到 O(k³C)。
- **大规模验证**：在 18B(1.7B 激活) 和 28B(2.7B 激活) DeepSeekMoE-style 模型上，xHC(N=16, k=4) 在 12 个标准基准上稳定优于 mHC(N=4) 和 vanilla；与 Muon 优化器也兼容(18B 上 Muon+xHC 均值 49.9 vs Muon 基线 43.1)。
- **效率交付 xHC-Flash**：通过跨 sublayer 共享路由、联合 pre-mapping、Attention 端去掉 Hres 等手段，把每 sublayer 内存流量从 73.5C 降到 xHC-Flash-4sub 的 40C(接近 mHC N=4 的 34C)，并配 fused Triton kernel 实现。

## Key Contributions

- **Bottleneck diagnosis**: Gives a concrete, ablation-backed explanation for why scaling mHC's expansion rate saturates — an *information bottleneck* (a single layer-output vector is the only write-back component shared across all N streams, so extra streams become redundant) and a *cost bottleneck* (the input-dependent Hres projection predicts N² coefficients from an NC state, costing O(N³C)). Measured at 2.5B MoE, mHC N=4→16 cuts loss by only 0.006 for +32% FLOPs.
- **Expanded Hyper-Connections (xHC)**: The first HC-family method that makes N>4 worthwhile. *Temporal feature augmentation* enriches the write-back basis with r=3 causal depthwise convs (kernels {4,8,12}, applied only after MLP) plus modified Gram–Schmidt orthogonalization; a *sparse residual-stream architecture* keeps dense read over all N=16 streams but updates only k=4 active streams (m fixed + TopK routed, sigmoid scoring), reducing the dominant cost from O(N³C) to O(k³C).
- **Large-scale empirical validation**: On DeepSeekMoE-style 18B (1.7B activated) and 28B (2.7B activated) MoE backbones with matched data and comparable FLOPs, xHC beats mHC and the vanilla baseline on all 12 benchmarks, and stays effective under the Muon optimizer (18B: Muon+xHC 49.9 vs Muon baseline 43.1 average).
- **Practical deployment (xHC-Flash)**: Shares routing and dense-read computation across sublayers and removes Attention-side Hres to amortize full-state accesses; xHC-Flash-4sub reaches 40C per-sublayer traffic (vs 34C for mHC@N=4) while keeping the loss advantage, plus fused Triton kernels for mapping generation/application.

## Methodology

The paper builds on Hyper-Connections (HC), which generalize the single Transformer residual stream into N parallel streams governed by per-layer learnable mappings: a pre-mapping Hpre (R^{1×N}) that aggregates streams into the layer input, a post-mapping Hpost (R^{N×1}) that distributes the layer output back, and a residual-mapping Hres (R^{N×N}) that mixes streams. The state-of-the-art variant mHC stabilizes this at scale by projecting Hres onto the Birkhoff polytope (doubly-stochastic matrices) via Sinkhorn–Knopp normalization, but mHC (and all HC-family work) stops at N=4.

The authors first run an N-sweep on a 2.5B MoE model showing mHC saturates past N=4, then attribute this to two compounding bottlenecks. To fix them, xHC introduces two coordinated designs:

1. **Temporal feature augmentation (targets the information bottleneck).** Instead of multiplying per-stream FLOPs by computing a separate output per stream, xHC borrows cheap local context from neighboring tokens via multi-scale causal depthwise-1D convolutions. For an MLP output `out ∈ R^{S×C}`, r=3 causal convs with kernels {4,8,12} produce additional components concatenated with the original output to give `out_aug ∈ R^{S×Kr×C}` with Kr=4. Because depthwise convs can retain a strong component aligned with the original output, modified Gram–Schmidt orthogonalization is applied over the Kr components so the post-mapping cannot amplify one direction unboundedly. Temporal augmentation is applied *only* after MLP (MoE FFN) layers; adding it after attention empirically destabilizes training. Cost: only ~24C extra parameters per MLP sublayer.

2. **Sparse residual-stream architecture (targets the cost bottleneck).** A sigmoid router (not softmax, to reduce winner-take-all) reads the full flattened N-stream state and picks k=4 active streams using a fixed-plus-routed scheme: m=2 streams are always active (weight 1), and the remaining k−m=2 are selected by TopK over sigmoid scores. The *read* path stays dense — every layer aggregates all N streams via Hpre — so cross-layer information flow is never broken even though only k streams are written. The residual and post-mappings are then generated from the *active* state only: Hres ∈ R^{k×k} (still Sinkhorn-normalized) and Hpost ∈ R^{k×Kr}, so each active stream independently combines the Kr augmented write-back components. Inactive streams pass through unchanged. This reduces the dominant Hres generation cost from O(N³C) to O(k³C). Ablations confirm dense read, fixed streams, k=4, and sigmoid routing are all individually important.

Parameterization follows mHC's gated-projection pattern (a learnable scalar α initialized to 0.01 scales an input-dependent term on top of a learnable bias, so mappings start near a static init and become dynamic gradually). The post-mapping uses a 2σ(·) scaling to allow coefficients in [0,2].

For deployment, **xHC-Flash** shares one routing decision across the Attention+MLP block, jointly forms sublayer-specific pre-mappings from the block-entry state, removes residual mixing from the Attention sublayer (so the MLP base readout can be corrected by a cheap scalar multiple of the attention output instead of a second full-state read), and applies residual mixing only at the final MLP. A four-sublayer extension (xHC-Flash-4sub) shares routing across a two-block group. Fused Triton kernels handle mapping generation (router+pre-mapping in one matmul, active-state gather with fused RMS statistic, joint post/residual projection) and mapping application (fused dense read + sparse mixing; fused post-mapping + routing-weight scaling + residual add + scatter; fused multi-branch temporal convs).

## Results & Highlights

- **18B MoE main table (Table 1):** xHC (N=16, k=4) raises the 12-benchmark average to **48.8**, vs **44.8 for mHC (N=4)** and **40.6 for the vanilla residual baseline** — a **+4.0-point average gain over mHC** and **+8.2 over vanilla**. Representative per-benchmark deltas over mHC: HumanEval 29.3 vs 23.2 (+6.1), ARC-Challenge 72.2 vs 66.3 (+5.9), BBH 39.5 vs 33.7 (+5.8), C3 78.3 vs 72.7 (+5.6), GSM8K 48.4 vs 44.5 (+3.9), MMLU 57.2 vs 54.7 (+2.5). Final training loss: **1.758 (xHC) vs 1.776 (mHC) vs 1.799 (vanilla)**, for only ~4.1% extra training FLOPs over vanilla.
- **28B MoE main table (Table 1):** average improves from **47.8 (vanilla) → 50.5 (mHC) → 53.6 (xHC)**, a **+3.1 over mHC** at only **+3.0% training FLOPs** over vanilla. Per-benchmark deltas over mHC: CommonsenseQA +5.7 (69.6 vs 63.9), HumanEval +4.3 (31.1 vs 26.8), MMLU +3.7 (60.5 vs 56.8), C3 +3.8 (82.5 vs 78.7).
- **Scaling laws (Figure 4):** across four model sizes from 1.7e19 to 4.0e20 FLOPs, the vanilla baseline needs **1.50× the compute of xHC** and mHC needs **1.19× the compute of xHC** to reach the same fitted loss; at the largest compute point xHC reduces loss by ~1.1% relative to mHC and ~2.4% relative to vanilla.
- **Expansion-rate sweep on 2.5B MoE (Figure 1):** mHC going N=4→16 reduces loss by only **0.006** for **+32% FLOPs**, while xHC going N=4→16 reduces loss by **0.012** for only **+4% FLOPs** — i.e., xHC gets twice the loss reduction at one-eighth the marginal FLOPs cost.
- **Ablations on 10B MoE, Pile val loss (Table 2):** vanilla 2.029; mHC N=4 2.004 (+0.6% FLOPs); mHC N=16 1.998 (+18.8%); mHC N=16 + temporal aug 1.984 (+20.1%); **full xHC 1.983 (+3.3%)** — i.e., the sparse architecture recovers the temporal-augmentation gain while cutting the FLOPs overhead from 20.1% to 3.3%. Removing dense read + fixed streams degrades to 1.997; removing dense read alone 1.985; removing fixed streams 1.986; k=2 underperforms (1.991), k=8 marginally better (1.982); softmax router (1.988) loses to sigmoid (1.983).
- **Muon compatibility (Table 3, 18B):** Muon baseline average 43.1 → Muon + xHC **49.9 (+6.8)**, with big jumps on ARC-Challenge (63.6→75.3), GSM8K (41.8→52.8), BBH (36.1→42.2); confirms the gain is not AdamW-specific. (GS orthogonalization is dropped under Muon, and Muon is applied only to backbone matrices while xHC projections stay on AdamW.)
- **xHC-Flash efficiency (Tables 4/5, 10B MoE):** per-sublayer residual-stream traffic drops from **73.5C (full xHC)** to **51C (xHC-Flash)** to **40C (xHC-Flash-4sub)**, the last being comparable to **34C for mHC at N=4**, while validation loss stays at 1.983 / 1.983 / 1.984 vs mHC's 2.004. End-to-end on 18B (no pipeline overlap), their reimplemented mHC adds ~15% training overhead over baseline and xHC-Flash-4sub adds ~11% on top of mHC; at 2K-token inference prefill, mHC adds 11.4% latency and xHC-Flash-4sub adds 12.9% (only +1.3% over mHC).

## Authors & Credibility

Joint work between **Shanghai Jiao Tong University (School of AI)**, **Dots Studio / Xiaohongshu Inc.**, USTC, Peking University, and CUHK, with corresponding author **Junchi Yan** (a well-established SJTU professor with an extensive architecture/optimization publication record). The involvement of Xiaohongshu's Dots Studio gives the work genuine large-scale MoE pre-training infrastructure, and the scale of the experiments (18B and 28B MoE, 144-expert top-8 DeepSeekMoE-style backbone, 8192 context, scaling-law fits up to 4e20 FLOPs, fused Triton kernels, end-to-end wall-clock numbers) is consistent with serious industrial-scale research. A public project page (github.com/aHapBean/xHC) is provided. The paper is positioned as a direct extension of mHC (the prior SOTA HC variant) and explicitly benchmarks against it at matched FLOPs, which is the right comparison.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (high).** The bottleneck diagnosis is the paper's strongest intellectual contribution: framing HC saturation as a joint information-supply + cubic-cost problem, then addressing each with a structurally decoupled design, is clean and well-motivated. Sparse updates on the *residual-stream* dimension (as opposed to MoE's expert dimension or sparse attention's token dimension) is a genuinely novel axis of sparsity. The Gram–Schmidt step is a small but principled touch.
- **Rigor (good, with gaps).** Ablations (Table 2) isolate each design choice and the FLOPs accounting is transparent. However, the headline comparisons are only against vanilla and mHC — there is **no comparison against spending the same +3–4% FLOPs on other architectural axes** (more depth, more experts, wider MLP, or non-HC alternatives like layer-wise learned gates). The claim that residual-stream expansion is "a practical scaling axis" would be much stronger if xHC's marginal FLOPs were benchmarked against, say, +4% more experts. The information-bottleneck ablation (Figure 5) is suggestive but only shows val loss on a 10B model, not downstream transfer.
- **Reproducibility (medium-high).** Hyperparameters and FLOPs/parameter derivations are in appendices, a fused-kernel design is described in detail, and a project page exists. But the actual models are 18B/28B MoE trained on a proprietary data mixture ("English, Chinese, code, mathematics, and reasoning corpora") with no dataset release, so independent reproduction of the downstream numbers is essentially impossible; only the algorithmic recipe is reproducible at smaller scale.
- **Significance (medium-high, conditional).** A +4.0 average at 18B and 1.50× / 1.19× compute-efficiency multipliers are meaningful *if* they hold at the well-trained regimes these models target. Caveat: the absolute benchmark scores are low (18B MMLU ~57%, HumanEval ~29%), indicating these are **early-/under-trained checkpoints**, so it is unclear whether the gains compress as models mature — a classic scaling-axis validation gap. The 1.50×/1.19× scaling-law claim rests on a fitted shifted power law over four compute points with a fixed irreducible loss E=0.72; the absolute loss delta at the largest point is only ~1.1% relative, so small fitting choices could move the multiplier.
- **Red flags / things to probe.** (1) The "comparable to mHC at N=4" framing for xHC-Flash-4sub is loose — 40C vs 34C is still ~18% more memory traffic, and their own reimplemented mHC shows 15% wall-clock overhead vs the 6.7% reported in the original mHC paper, which they acknowledge is not directly comparable; the absolute throughput story therefore rests on their reimplementation. (2) No long-context evaluation, no SFT/instruction-tuning or RLHF results, and no evaluation beyond lm-eval-harness-style multiple-choice/code benchmarks — so the downstream claim is narrow. (3) All N>4 analysis (the core contribution) is done at 2.5B for the sweep and 10B for ablations, with only the final N=16,k=4 setting scaled to 18B/28B; whether k and the fixed-stream ratio m should change at larger scale is unexplored.

## Strengths

- Clean, well-motivated two-bottleneck diagnosis backed by targeted ablations (the information-bottleneck ablation in Figure 5 showing the gain grows with N is exactly the predicted pattern).
- The sparse-residual-stream idea is elegant: dense read preserves cross-layer flow, sparse write controls cost, and the fixed-plus-routed scheme plus sigmoid routing are well-justified and individually ablated.
- Honest and detailed efficiency accounting — per-operation memory-traffic decomposition (Table 4), wall-clock numbers, and explicit acknowledgment that their mHC reimplementation overhead (15%) differs from the original paper's (6.7%).
- Muon compatibility is a valuable robustness check, not just an afterthought, and the practical GS-removal + selective-Mon rule shows real engineering judgment.
- Results consistent across two scales (18B and 28B) and across two optimizers (AdamW, Muon), reducing the chance the gain is a single-config artifact.

## Limitations

- No comparison against alternative uses of the +3–4% FLOPs budget (more experts/depth/width), which is the most obvious confound for a "new scaling axis" claim.
- Models appear under-trained (low absolute benchmark scores), so it is uncertain whether the gains survive into mature, well-trained regimes; no long-context, SFT, or alignment evaluation.
- Absolute throughput claims rest on the authors' own mHC reimplementation; the N>4 design space (k, fixed-stream ratio, router choice) is only swept at 2.5B–10B, not at the 18B/28B scale where the headline numbers are reported.
- Scaling-law multipliers (1.50×, 1.19×) are derived from a four-point fit with a fixed irreducible-loss prior; the absolute loss gap at the largest point is small (~1.1% relative), so the headline ratios are sensitive to fitting assumptions.
- Proprietary training data means the downstream numbers themselves cannot be independently reproduced, only the algorithmic recipe.

## Relevance

直接相关于 LLM 预训练架构创新主线：xHC 提出的"残差流稀疏化 + 时间特征增强"是一种与 MoE/宽深正交的新缩放维度，对关注 Transformer 架构、残差连接变体与 MoE 训练效率的研究者具有较高参考价值，也提供了可迁移到其他跨层信息流设计的"信息瓶颈 vs 成本瓶颈"分析框架。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- If the +3–4% FLOPs that xHC adds over vanilla were instead spent on more experts (higher top-k or more total experts) or more depth, would the downstream gain be larger or smaller? This is the missing head-to-head for the "new scaling axis" claim.
- Do the xHC gains (especially the +4.0 average at 18B) survive when the models are trained to a mature, compute-optimal regime where MMLU/HumanEval scores are in competitive ranges, rather than at the under-trained checkpoints reported?
- How does xHC interact with long-context training and evaluation (e.g., 32K–128K context, RULER/long-context benchmarks)? The expanded N-stream residual state could plausibly help or hurt position propagation.
- Should k (active streams) and the fixed-stream ratio m scale with model size or with N? The current N=16, k=4, m=2 setting is only swept at 2.5B–10B.
- Does xHC compose with other cross-layer techniques (e.g., layer-wise learned gates, sharing attention across layers, slabbing)? The paper positions xHC against them but does not test combinations.
- Can the temporal feature augmentation (causal depthwise convs over neighboring tokens) be replaced by a cheaper or more expressive operator (e.g., a tiny linear attention or a learned decay) without losing the write-back diversity benefit?
