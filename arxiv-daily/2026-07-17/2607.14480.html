LLM Evaluators are Biased
across Languages.

Key Finding — the one takeaway

For semantically identical content, LLM evaluators hand lower-resource languages systematically higher scores — and pairwise accuracy never notices.

01Methodology

  • Parallel data. RewardBench + M-RewardBench across 23 languages and 4 task categories (Chat, Chat-Hard, Safety, Reasoning) — professionally human-translated, not MT.
  • Eight open evaluators. 4 LLM-as-a-Judge on a 1–5 Likert rubric (Aya-32B, Qwen-2.5-72B, LLaMA-3.1-70B, M-Prometheus-14B; T=0) + 4 trained reward models (URM, BTRM-Qwen, Skywork-Gemma-27B, Skywork-LLaMA-8B), z-normalized per split.
  • Frontier replication. GPT-4.1-mini and Qwen3-32B (thinking) on Safety / Chat-Hard.
  • Threshold policy. Calibrate at the 50th percentile on held-out, apply globally; acceptance = share of responses above threshold, computed per language.
  • Mechanism probes. Summed NLL (deliberately not per-token — tokenizer effects cancel in per-token NLL), nested OLS M1/M2/M3 with incremental F-tests, within-language Score~NLL regressions.
  • Mitigation. Additive per-language offset s'(x,ℓ)=s(x,ℓ)−b̂(ℓ); plus an adversarial English-wrapper / Hindi-content code-switching attack scored with langdetect LID.

02Results & Highlights

  • Magnitude. ~0.5 pt shift on the 1–5 Likert scale; reward-model score vs. resource level: Pearson r=−0.58, Spearman ρ=−0.81. ANOVA significant (p<.001) for all 8 evaluators.
  • Pairwise accuracy is a blind spot. Uniformly >90% across languages, yet acceptance gap hits 43 pp; even in the >95% regime the divergence still reaches 34 pp.
  • Concrete safety failure. Skywork-LLaMA-8B: English 23% vs. Ukrainian 67% acceptance at near-equal pairwise accuracy — a 44 pp gap on the same content.
  • Frontier persistence. GPT-4.1-mini: 19.6 pp gap (ρ=−0.58). Qwen3-32B thinking: 32.7 pp gap (ρ=−0.68). Scale does not fix it.
  • Uncertainty link. Summed-NLL vs. score: language-level ρ=+0.81 (p=2.9e-06). Language identity still uniquely explains up to 20.5% of variance after NLL control.
  • Mitigation. Per-language offset cuts the acceptance gap by 60.9% on average (33.4 pp → 11.6 pp); residual 18.8% is language×item interaction no scalar can remove.
  • Attack. Off-the-shelf LID mislabels 44% of code-switched prompts; acceptance jumps from calibrated 50% → 75%.

03Strengths

  • Original framing. Separates pointwise scoring from pairwise ranking in the multilingual setting — and shows the two tell different stories.
  • Broad, heterogeneous pool. 4 judges + 4 reward models + 2 frontier models, across 23 languages on human-validated parallel data.
  • Layered mechanism. Correlation → nested-OLS control → within-language regression, with intellectual honesty about what uncertainty does and does not explain.
  • Deployment-relevant. Concrete implications spelled out for safety filters, RLHF reward shaping, and threshold-based filtering.
  • Quantified mitigation (60.9% gap reduction) paired with an honest residual accounting and an adversarial demonstration of why the fix is incomplete.
  • Transparent disclosure. LLM use limited to code/debugging and prose polishing; scientific decisions retained by authors.

04Limitations

  • Coarse resource proxy. Common-Crawl page-share ignores instruction-tuning and preference-data composition; the ρ=−0.81 could shift under alternative measures.
  • Narrow attack generalization. Code-switching demo covers only en/hi and one model; other script pairs and LID tools untested.
  • No end-to-end RLHF run. Cross-lingual reward hacking is argued, not demonstrated in a real training loop.
  • Modest linear agreement. Cross-model Pearson correlations are only moderate (mean r=0.53 on Chat-Hard); "consistency" holds at the extremes, not the full distribution.
  • Scale ceiling. Open evaluators top out at 72B; still-larger or specially post-trained frontier judges are not tested.
  • Single threshold. Results reported at the 50th percentile; behaviour at stricter deployment thresholds (90th/95th) is not swept.
