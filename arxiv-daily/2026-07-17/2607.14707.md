> **一句话总结：** 本文以学术导师场景为案例，对比无脚手架的 GPT-5 聊天机器人（ASA）与用 LangGraph 包装 GPT-4o-mini 的七模块工程化系统（ASuS）；在盲评的十位评审下，ASuS 在六个维度上全部胜出，整体均分 4.08 vs 1.23（5 分制），并通过 2×2 模型-脚手架消融证明该优势主要来自脚手架而非模型规模。

> **TL;DR:** In the academic-supervision domain (matching students to 50 professors across 14 departments, report evaluation, roadmap generation, risk assessment), the authors show that a deliberately engineered LangGraph harness around the small GPT-4o-mini model (ASuS) beats an unscaffolded GPT-5 chatbot (ASA) on every one of six harness-mechanism dimensions, with pooled mean 4.08 vs 1.23 (Δ = 2.85 on a 1–5 rubric) across ten blind raters, and 8/10 raters rejecting the null on a paired Wilcoxon test (p ∈ [0.023, 0.027]). A 2×2 model×harness ablation confirms the harness contribution is largely model-invariant, supporting the claim that, for reliability/auditability-bound workflows, harness engineering can challenge the "bigger model is better" intuition.

## 核心贡献

- **将"脚手架工程（harness engineering）"系统化为一门连贯的工程学科**：把此前分散在 RAG、LLM-as-judge、HITL、智能体框架等社区中的构建块（symbolic filters、retrieval、schema-typed I/O、judge loops、HITL gates、persistent state、audit trails）整合为统一术语和设计模式（§2、§5）。
- **发布 ASuS 参考架构**：基于 LangGraph 的七模块编排（matching、report eval、request analysis、roadmap、risk、chat summary、open requests），共享同一个 TypedDict 状态模式（`AcademicSupervisionState`），SQLite 持久化 + 每节点 append-only 审计日志（§4）。
- **提取七种可复用的脚手架设计模式**：P1 symbolic-before-semantic 过滤、P2 schema-typed 模块输出、P3 LLM-as-judge 带有界重试、P4 HITL 作为硬门控、P5 确定性核心 + LLM 叙述外壳、P6 audit-by-construction、P7 意图路由（§5）。
- **对抗性非对称对照实验**：故意把更大模型（GPT-5）放在无脚手架基线 ASA 一侧，更小模型（GPT-4o-mini）放在有脚手架 ASuS 一侧；通过 2×2 消融把"模型效应"和"脚手架效应"分离，证明结论主要由脚手架贡献（§6.6、§6.7）。
- **七种模式均来自既有文献，本文的贡献是经验性证据**：作者明确声明创新点不在单一模块本身，而在于这种特定组合"逐维度确实带来收益"的实证（§1 贡献 3）。

## Key Contributions

- **Consolidates harness engineering as a coherent discipline** by drawing together components previously studied in isolation (RAG, LLM-as-judge, Reflexion-style self-correction, HITL gates, orchestration frameworks), giving practitioners a shared vocabulary and a catalogue of observable design patterns (§2, §5).
- **Releases ASuS**, a working reference architecture that composes seven LangGraph modules over a single typed state schema (`AcademicSupervisionState`), with SQLite-backed persistence and per-node append-only audit logs, designed so adjacent domains can adopt the blueprint as-is (§4, Appendix A Table A1).
- **Extracts seven recurring harness patterns** (P1–P7) named by design intent rather than implementation, making them transferable to Temporal, AWS Step Functions, or custom state machines (§5).
- **Runs a controlled comparison with adversarial model asymmetry**: the larger GPT-5 is deliberately placed in the unscaffolded ASA baseline, the smaller GPT-4o-mini in the scaffolded ASuS, and a 2×2 model×harness ablation disentangles the two factors (§6.6, §6.7).
- **Reports a blind ten-rater hybrid evaluation** with a harness-mechanism-targeted rubric (grounding, explainability, consistency, process integrity, cognitive load, constraint adherence), transparently disclosing the outlier rater D and a sensitivity analysis excluding it (§6.1, §6.4, §8.1).

## Methodology

**Two systems on an identical task surface.** Both ASA and ASuS ingest the same seed CSV of 50 professor records across 14 departments (department, research areas, paper titles, ongoing projects) and expose the same conversational tasks. The contrast is *only in the harness*:

- **ASA (baseline):** a single Streamlit app wrapping one GPT-5 call per turn. Permissive system prompt covering matching/roadmap/request/report/risk in one prompt; full CSV dumped into the prompt; free-form LLM responses; PyPDF2 one-shot PDF summary; no schema, no judge, no HITL, no persistence beyond in-session chat memory, no audit trail.
- **ASuS (engineered):** LangGraph directed graph of seven Python-function nodes, each reading/writing a typed `AcademicSupervisionState` TypedDict and returning partial-state updates. Edges are unconditional or driven by a `next_module` field; a SQLite checkpointer persists the full state at each node boundary for resume-after-interrupt.

**The seven ASuS modules.** (M1) Matching = Chroma metadata filtering via `$and/$or/$contains/$eq/$nin` predicates over (department, research_areas) then cosine similarity of `text-embedding-3-small`, top-10 ranked. (M2) Report eval = PyMuPDF extraction (rejecting <200 chars), classify into 3 categories, two HITL gates (category confirm, summary confirm). (M3) Request analysis = detect→classify technical-vs-operational→binary resolution gate→priority score. (M4) Roadmap = generate→LLM-judge over 6 criteria→regenerate up to 3× if overall score < 0.75→human approval interrupt. (M5) Risk = deterministic weighted score over 6 normalized signals (attendance, deadline, submission delay, pending actions, activity, report quality) with CRITICAL/WARNING flag overrides at 0.85/0.65 thresholds, LLM only narrates. (M6) Chat summary and (M7) Open requests aggregation produce JSON for a supervisor dashboard.

**Evaluation protocol.** Blind, hybrid, scenario-based: 10 independent raters with technical backgrounds attended a live demo of 5 scenarios (A: niche-topic matching; B: borderline progress report with vague follow-up; C: professor rejection + re-matching; D: complex-topic roadmap; E: struggling-student risk signals). Each scenario exercised on both systems under neutral labels "System A/B"; the A/B→ASuS/ASA mapping was held privately by the lead author. The sheet had 30 per-question MCQ verdicts (A/B/Tie/Can't tell) plus 12 per-dimension integer 1–5 scores across six harness-mechanism dimensions (D1 grounding, D2 explainability, D3 consistency, D4 process integrity, D5 cognitive load, D6 constraint adherence), deliberately retargeted so a larger model cannot win on prose merit alone. No anchoring set was provided and no calibration session was held.

**2×2 ablation.** `compare.py` runs four cells {ASA, ASuS} × {GPT-5, GPT-4o-mini} on the three most LLM-sensitive scenarios (matching A, roadmap D, risk E), patching `llm_utils.DEFAULT_MODEL` uniformly so generator and judge run on the same model. Five raters (four from the main study + one independent) scored the 12 outputs under randomized V1–V4 labels.

**Statistics.** Pure-Python `compute_stats.py`: paired Wilcoxon signed-rank, exact two-tailed sign test, rank-biserial effect size, quadratic-weighted Cohen κ per rater pair, and Fleiss κ across all raters; no scipy dependency.

## Results & Highlights

- **Pooled mean across ten blind raters: ASuS 4.08 vs ASA 1.23** (out of 5), a 2.85-point gap, with ASuS winning all 6/6 harness-mechanism dimensions (Table 3). Largest gaps: D3 consistency (4.50 vs 0.80, Δ = 3.70) and D5 cognitive load (4.10 vs 1.00, Δ = 3.10).
- **Per-rater significance:** paired Wilcoxon rejects the null at α = 0.05 for 8 of 10 raters with p-values between 0.023 and 0.027; rater B borderline (p = 0.072); rater D a clear outlier (ASuS 1.33 vs ASA 1.17, p = 0.79). Excluding rater D leaves the pooled mean essentially unchanged (4.39 vs 1.23) and raises significant rejections to 8/9 (§6.4).
- **Per-question verdicts:** of 300 paired verdicts (10 raters × 30 questions), 225 (75.0%) favour ASuS, 22 (7.3%) favour ASA, 31 (10.3%) ties, 22 (7.3%) "can't tell"; per-rater exact-binomial sign-test rejects the null at p < 0.001 for 8/10 raters (§6.4).
- **Inter-rater reliability:** quadratic-weighted Cohen κ on dimension scores ranges across 45 rater pairs from −0.66 to 0.97 (mean 0.30); Fleiss κ = 0.10 for dimensions, 0.11 for verdicts — wide pairwise range driven almost entirely by the single outlier rater D (§6.4, §8.1).
- **2×2 ablation cell means (Table 4, 1–5 rubric):** ASA/GPT-5 = 2.67, ASA/GPT-4o-mini = 1.73, ASuS/GPT-5 = 4.80, ASuS/GPT-4o-mini = 4.07. Putting ASuS around the smaller model (4.07) still beats ASA around the larger model (2.67) by 1.40 points; ASuS gains only 0.73 from upgrading GPT-4o-mini → GPT-5, while ASA gains 0.94 from the same upgrade — both relatively small versus the ~2.3-point jump from adding the harness at fixed model.
- **Model-invariance signals:** matching cells converge across the chat-model swap (embedding function is invariant); risk-narrative cells share identical `risk_level` and `raw_risk_score` across both ASuS variants because the deterministic core is unchanged; roadmap diverges structurally because ASuS returns a typed object with `judge_score`/`judge_verdict`/`judge_breakdown` (§6.7).
- **Engineering footprint:** ASuS ≈ 5,000 lines of Python across modules vs ASA ≈ 300 lines; much of the harness (unified state schema, audit helper, SQLite checkpointer, response node) is domain-independent and reusable (§7.3).

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty.** Genuine novelty is *compositional and empirical*, not algorithmic — the author says so explicitly (§1, contribution 3). Each individual building block (hybrid symbolic+semantic retrieval, LLM-as-judge with bounded retry, deterministic core + LLM narration) is well-trodden; the contribution is the working architecture and the dimension-by-dimension evidence that this specific seven-pattern combination pays off against a stronger-model baseline.
- **Rigor.** The adversarial model asymmetry (large GPT-5 unscaffolded vs small GPT-4o-mini scaffolded) is a clever design that *stacks the deck against the authors' own hypothesis* — if the harness did nothing, the larger model should win. The 2×2 ablation is the right tool for the model-vs-harness confound, and the blind, neutral-label, no-anchoring protocol is methodologically sound for a demonstration-based study.
- **Internal-validity red flags.** (1) The headline contrast is essentially a *straw-man comparison*: ASA is deliberately built as a thin Streamlit wrapper that dumps the entire CSV into one prompt with no schema, no retrieval, no persistence, no audit — this is not how a competent practitioner would deploy GPT-5. The "ASA = a serious strong-model baseline" framing overstates the comparison; ASA is closer to "bare LLM call." (2) The six evaluation dimensions are explicitly retargeted "so that a larger base model cannot win by prose merit alone" (§6.1) — D3 consistency and D6 constraint adherence are *structurally impossible* for ASA to score well on by construction (it has no persistence, no schema, no audit), so ASuS winning them is partly definitional rather than empirical. (3) Sample size is small (10 raters, 5 scenarios) and the headline p-values rest on per-rater Wilcoxon tests over very few paired differences; the author is honest about this but the framing ("8/10 raters significant") oversells what those p-values mean at this n.
- **Inter-rater reliability is weak.** Fleiss κ = 0.10–0.11 is in the "slight agreement" range, and the wide pairwise Cohen κ range (−0.66 to 0.97) means raters often disagreed even on direction; the author attributes this almost entirely to rater D, but excluding one rater to rescue agreement is itself a sensitivity artifice.
- **Missing ablations.** The seven patterns are presented as a bundle; there is *no per-pattern isolation* showing which of P1–P7 actually drives the gains. The author flags this as future work (§8.3), but it materially weakens the actionable claim — practitioners cannot tell whether they need all seven patterns or just, say, schema-typed outputs + audit trail. The hyperparameter sensitivity of the risk weights, judge pass-score (0.75), and iteration cap (3) is also unanalysed.
- **Reproducibility.** Strong on paper — code, data, ratings, scoring sheets, and a custom stats script are all released; the 12 ablation cells reportedly ran without error in under six minutes. Caveat: the study hinges on a live in-person demo with author-selected scenarios; reproducing the *rater judgments* would require re-running the demo protocol, which is inherently less reproducible than re-running a benchmark.
- **Significance.** The broad message ("harness engineering can matter more than scale for reliability-bound workflows") is plausible and useful, but it is established here on a single low-stakes synthetic domain with an author-constructed weak baseline. Generalization to clinical, legal, or production domains is asserted (§7.4) but not demonstrated.
- **Overclaiming check.** The conclusion calls the smaller-model-wins result "characteristic rather than anomalous" — this overreaches given a single domain, one weak baseline, and n = 10 raters on a demonstration protocol. The more defensible claim ("for this domain, with this baseline, the harness wins on these six dimensions") is well-supported.

## Strengths

- Adversarial model asymmetry that biases the experiment against the authors' own hypothesis.
- A genuinely useful, named, transferable catalogue of seven harness patterns with a module-to-pattern map (Appendix A Table A1) that other practitioners can lift directly.
- Honest, almost unusually transparent reporting of threats to validity: the outlier rater, the missing calibration session, the unanalysed hyperparameter sensitivity, and the absence of per-pattern ablation are all called out by the author rather than buried.
- Clean separation of generator vs judge vs deterministic core, with the risk module's "deterministic computation + LLM narration only" being a particularly defensible design for high-stakes scoring.
- Open release of the full stack — code, seed CSV, filled ratings, blind sheets, ablation JSONs, and a dependency-free stats script — supporting replication.

## Limitations

- The headline baseline ASA is a deliberately thin single-prompt GPT-5 wrapper with no retrieval, schema, persistence, or audit; it is closer to "bare LLM call" than to a credible strong-model production baseline, which inflates the apparent gap.
- The six evaluation dimensions are designed to be unwinnable for the unscaffolded baseline by construction (no persistence ⇒ fail D3; no schema ⇒ fail D6), so several "wins" are partly definitional.
- Sample size is small (10 raters, 5 scenarios); per-rater p-values carry limited weight and Fleiss κ (0.10–0.11) indicates only slight inter-rater agreement.
- No per-pattern ablation: all seven patterns are evaluated as a bundle, so the marginal contribution of each is unknown.
- No sensitivity analysis on the author-set hyperparameters (risk weights, CRITICAL/WARNING thresholds 0.85/0.65, judge pass-score 0.75, iteration cap 3); all are calibrated qualitatively.
- Evaluation is a live, in-person demonstration rather than an automated benchmark, limiting reproducibility of the human judgments and introducing demo-order/context effects.
- Single domain (academic supervision), single institution's data shape, synthetic seed CSV of only 50 professors; generalization to clinical/legal/customer-success is argued but not tested.
- ASuS system-level gaps the author concedes: no duplicate-request detection, synchronous blocking HITL interrupts (unsuitable for multi-supervisor deployment), risk signals manually injected rather than auto-derived, and match rationale is a similarity score rather than a human-readable explanation.
- The 2×2 ablation patches the chat model uniformly, so generator and judge share a model — the known self-evaluation bias of LLM-as-judge (Zheng et al. 2023) applies, and the author notes that per-cell judge scores coincidentally match across models, which he reads as structural pass/fail rather than a sharp quantitative signal.

## Relevance

对构建 LLM 智能体/工作流系统（尤其涉及 RAG、LLM-as-judge、HITL、审计可追溯性）的研究者，这篇论文提供了可直接套用的"脚手架工程"七模式清单和一份 LangGraph 参考架构，与"日常关注的 agent 可靠性、评估方法、检索增强"方向高度契合；其对抗性模型非对称实验设计也可作为评估自研系统的借鉴。需要警惕的是其弱基线和按构造不可赢的评估维度，结论需结合自身场景审慎迁移。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Which of the seven patterns (P1–P7) contributes the most to the per-dimension gains? A leave-one-pattern-out ablation would turn "a harness helps" into "these specific components contribute these specific improvements" — the author explicitly names this as the most interesting future direction.
- How much of the ASuS advantage survives if ASA is upgraded to a *credible* strong-model baseline (e.g., GPT-5 + the same Chroma retrieval + a JSON schema), isolating the contribution of the orchestration/HITL/audit layers from the contribution of retrieval+schema alone?
- Does the result hold on a real institutional dataset (live supervisor–student records, real progress reports) instead of a synthetic 50-professor CSV, and with longitudinal outcome tracking rather than one-shot rater judgments?
- How sensitive are the headline numbers to the author-set hyperparameters — the six risk-signal weights, the 0.85/0.65 flag thresholds, the 0.75 judge pass-score, and the 3-iteration cap?
- Can the harness advantage be replicated in an adjacent domain (the author proposes clinical-pathway management) with the same patterns and the same dimension-level wins, or does the result depend on domain-specific structure peculiar to academic supervision?
- What happens when the generator and judge use *different* models in the ablation, removing the self-evaluation confound acknowledged from Zheng et al. 2023?
