> **一句话总结：** 本文系统揭示了 LLM 评估器（含奖励模型与 LLM-as-a-Judge）在 23 种语言下对语义相同内容给出系统性不同的点估计分数——低资源语言被显著更宽容地评分（与训练资源量呈 Spearman ρ=−0.81 的负相关），且该偏差在 pairwise accuracy >90% 时依然导致阈值决策下的接受率最大相差 43 个百分点，作者进一步证明该偏差与模型不确定性相关但无法被其完全解释。

> **TL;DR:** Across RewardBench/M-RewardBench spanning 23 languages and 8 open-weight evaluators (plus GPT-4.1-mini, Qwen3-32B), the authors show that LLM evaluators systematically assign different pointwise scores to semantically identical content based on evaluation language: lower-resource languages (Hindi, Hebrew, Arabic) get ~0.5-point-higher scores on a 1–5 Likert scale (Spearman ρ = −0.81 between reward score and resource level), and even at >95% pairwise accuracy, acceptance-rate gaps under a fixed global threshold reach up to 43 percentage points (e.g., Skywork-LLaMA-8B: English 23% vs. Ukrainian 67% acceptance at near-equal pairwise accuracy). Per-language offset correction cuts the gap by 60.9% on average, but residual structure and LID-attackable code-switching inputs leave the problem open.

## 核心贡献

- **量化多语言评分偏移并给出方向**：在 23 种语言、8 个开源评估器（4 个 LLM-as-a-Judge + 4 个奖励模型）上首次系统证明，对语义相同的内容，低资源语言（印地语、希伯来语、阿拉伯语）的点估计分数显著更高，差距达约 0.5 分（1–5 量表）；奖励模型对资源水平的 Spearman ρ = −0.81，所有评估器的语言效应均通过单因素 ANOVA（p<0.001）。
- **揭示 pairwise accuracy 的盲区**：即使 pairwise accuracy 在所有语言上均 >90%，全局阈值下的接受率在语言间最多相差 43 个百分点；在 >95% pairwise accuracy 的高准确率区间，接受决策差异仍可达 34 个百分点。这对基于阈值的过滤、RLHF 奖励塑形、安全审计构成结构性风险。
- **机制分析**：通过三类实验（NLL–score 相关性、控制不确定性后语言身份的残差预测力、语言内回归）证明模型不确定性与评分膨胀相关，但语言身份在控制 NLL 后仍是显著预测变量；语言内的「内容难度→分数」斜率方向不稳定（Chat/Chat-Hard 多为负），无法仅用难度解释。
- **部署建议与攻击面**：per-language 偏移校正可将接受率差距平均降低 60.9%；但同时指出该方案依赖语言识别，而 code-switched 提示可被误判（off-the-shelf LID 工具误判 44% 的英壳印地语），使接受率从校正后的 50% 飙升至 75%，构成攻击面。
- **前沿模型同样存在偏差**：在 GPT-4.1-mini 与 Qwen3-32B（thinking）上复现了同样的偏差模式，资源相关系数均显著（p<0.01），证明该问题不会随规模自然消失。

## Key Contributions

- **Quantifies and orients the multilingual scoring shift**: First systematic demonstration across 23 languages and 8 open evaluators (LLaMA/Qwen/Gemma/Aya backbones, Bradley–Terry and uncertainty-aware training paradigms) that pointwise scores for semantically parallel content differ by ~0.5 points on a 1–5 Likert scale, with lower-resource languages (Hindi, Hebrew, Arabic) scored most generously; reward-model score correlates with resource level at Spearman ρ = −0.81, with one-way ANOVA significant at p < 0.001 for every evaluator.
- **Exposes pairwise accuracy as a structural blind spot**: Even at >90% pairwise accuracy, acceptance rates under a single calibrated global threshold differ by up to 43 percentage points across languages; in the >95% accuracy regime the divergence still reaches 34 percentage points. This invalidates the implicit assumption that high pairwise accuracy implies language-neutral scoring.
- **Mechanistic decomposition**: A three-pronged analysis (NLL–score correlation at language/item level, nested OLS with incremental F-tests, within-language slope regressions over 23 languages) shows uncertainty correlates with inflated scores (language-level ρ = +0.81 for summed NLL) but does not absorb the language effect; within-language slopes are predominantly negative in Chat/Chat-Hard, ruling out a pure content-difficulty story.
- **Practical mitigation + attack surface**: A simple per-language additive offset correction cuts the acceptance-rate gap by 60.9% on average, but its residual (11.6 pp gap, 18.8% language×item interaction variance) and dependence on language identification are real: code-switched English-wrapper/Hindi-content prompts are mislabeled by an off-the-shelf LID tool 44% of the time, raising acceptance from 50% to 75%.
- **Frontier persistence**: The bias is reproduced on GPT-4.1-mini and Qwen3-32B (thinking) on Safety/Chat-Hard splits, with significant resource-level correlations (ρ between −0.58 and −0.73) and acceptance gaps of 14–33 pp, ruling out "scale will fix it."

## Methodology

- **Data**: RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2025) and its human-validated multilingual extension M-RewardBench (Gureja et al., 2025), yielding semantically parallel instruction–response pairs across 23 languages and four task categories (Chat, Chat-Hard, Safety, Reasoning). All non-English data is professionally human-translated, not machine-translated, preserving semantic parallelism.
- **Evaluators**: 4 prompted LLM-as-a-Judges with a 1–5 Likert rubric (Aya Expanse 32B, Qwen 2.5 72B, LLaMA 3.1 70B Instruct, M-Prometheus 14B; temperature 0) and 4 trained reward models outputting continuous scalar scores (URM-LLaMA-3.1-8B, BTRM-Qwen-2-7B, Skywork-Reward-Gemma-2-27B, Skywork-Reward-LLaMA-3.1-8B-v0.2), spanning three backbone families and both Bradley–Terry and uncertainty-aware training paradigms. Frontier robustness is checked with GPT-4.1-mini and Qwen3-32B (thinking).
- **Score normalization**: Reward-model scores are z-normalized within each model–dataset split so that cross-language comparisons reflect only language shifts, not model/domain magnitude differences.
- **Statistical tests**: One-way ANOVA per evaluator for language effect (all p < 0.001); Pearson and Spearman correlations for resource-level trends (using CC-MAIN-2025-51 Common Crawl page-share as a resource proxy); pairwise Pearson correlation matrices across evaluators for cross-model consistency.
- **Acceptance-rate analysis**: For each model–category split a threshold is calibrated at the 50th percentile on a held-out set, then applied globally; acceptance rate is the proportion of responses whose score exceeds the threshold, computed per language.
- **Mechanism probes**:
  - Uncertainty quantified as **summed NLL** (deliberately not per-token, to avoid the tokenizer-induced cancellation that flattens per-token NLL across languages; summed NLL spans a 2.6× range).
  - Three additional token-free uncertainty measures: URM attribute-head disagreement, URM predictive variance (σ-head), and semantic entropy over 5 sampled chain-of-thought judgments.
  - Nested OLS models M1 (score ~ NLL), M2 (score ~ Language), M3 (score ~ NLL + Language) with incremental F-tests, plus a stacked R² variance decomposition into unique-NLL, unique-language, and shared components.
  - Within-language regressions Score ~ NLL per language (23 slopes per model–domain) to test whether the bias reduces to content difficulty.
- **Mitigation experiment**: Additive bias correction s'(x, ℓ) = s(x, ℓ) − b̂(ℓ) where b̂(ℓ) is per-language mean deviation from global mean; evaluated via cross-language variance reduction and pre/post acceptance-rate gap on 16 model–benchmark runs.
- **Adversarial code-switching test**: English-wrapper + Hindi-content Safety prompts scored with Skywork-LLaMA-8B, with thresholds separately calibrated on pure English (T_en = −22.06) and pure Hindi (T_hi = −13.45); off-the-shelf langdetect LID used to label.

## Results & Highlights

- **Magnitude of language shift**: For prompted judges, mean scores for parallel content span ~0.4–0.5 points on the 1–5 Likert scale across languages; for z-normalized reward scores the highest-to-lowest gap is ±0.5 on a ±1 scale. All language effects are statistically significant (one-way ANOVA, p < 0.001) for every one of the eight evaluators.
- **Resource correlation**: Reward-model score vs. Common Crawl resource share gives Pearson r = −0.58 and Spearman ρ = −0.81 across 23 languages — a strong monotonic increase in reward as resource availability drops. For LLM-as-a-Judge, ρ = −0.57.
- **Pairwise accuracy is high yet uninformative**: Pairwise accuracy is uniformly >90% across languages and evaluators on Chat and Safety (Table 7), but the acceptance-rate gap (max − min) reaches 43.0 percentage points at the 50th-percentile global threshold. Even in the >95% pairwise-accuracy regime, acceptance decisions diverge by up to 34.0 pp across languages.
- **Concrete safety failure**: Skywork-Reward-LLaMA-3.1-8B-v0.2 scores 93% pairwise accuracy / 23% acceptance rate on English, but 87% pairwise accuracy / 67% acceptance rate on Ukrainian — a 44 pp acceptance gap. For the same adversarial question, the rejected English response scored −4.51 (filtered out) while its Ukrainian counterpart scored +3.57 (passed the same global threshold).
- **Frontier models reproduce the bias**: GPT-4.1-mini shows a 0.63-range score spread on Safety with Spearman ρ = −0.58 and a 19.6 pp acceptance gap; Qwen3-32B (thinking) shows a 1.28-range spread on Safety with ρ = −0.68 and a 32.7 pp gap. Hindi and Hebrew are top-3 most leniently scored; English, Italian, French are bottom-3 in nearly all settings.
- **Uncertainty–score association**: Summed NLL vs. mean reward score gives language-level Spearman ρ = +0.81 (p = 2.9e-06, n = 23) and item-level ρ = +0.50 across 36,432 instances. Token-free measures corroborate: URM attribute-head disagreement ρ = +0.73, URM σ-head ρ = +0.43, semantic entropy on Qwen-2.5-72B ρ = +0.68, on LLaMA-3.1-70B ρ = +0.65.
- **Language identity survives NLL control**: Incremental F-tests show that adding language identity on top of NLL significantly improves the model for every reward model; stacked R² decomposition shows language identity uniquely explains up to 20.5% of variance (Skywork-LLaMA, Chat-Hard) and 14.0% on Safety.
- **Mitigation result**: Per-language additive offset eliminates 100% of the cross-language variance of mean scores and reduces the acceptance-rate gap by 60.9% on average (33.4 pp → 11.6 pp), with the largest reduction on Chat-Hard (66.9%) and Safety (64.8%). Shared item difficulty explains 81.2% of residual centered-score variance; the remaining 18.8% is language×item interaction that no scalar offset can fix.
- **Code-switching attack**: Off-the-shelf langdetect mislabels 44% of English-wrapper/Hindi-content Safety prompts as English; applying the lenient English threshold raises acceptance from the calibrated 50% to 75%.

## Authors & Credibility

- All four authors (Ej Zhou, Lucas Resck, Zheng Hui, Anna Korhonen) are at the Language Technology Lab, University of Cambridge, a group with established track record in multilingual NLP and LLM safety (Korhonen is a senior figure in the field).
- The methodology is rigorous: human-validated parallel data (M-RewardBench), temperature-0 decoding, multiple uncertainty operationalizations, nested OLS with incremental F-tests, per-language and item-level analyses, and an explicit disclosure of LLM use (only for code/debugging and prose polishing, with scientific decisions retained by authors).
- The work builds directly on community-standard benchmarks (RewardBench, M-RewardBench) and standard open-weight evaluators (URM, Skywork-Reward, BTRM, M-Prometheus), making claims verifiable.
- The paper is transparent about what its mitigation cannot fix (residual 11.6 pp gap, language×item interaction, LID fragility) rather than overclaiming.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: High and well-positioned. Whereas prior multilingual judge work (Fu & Liu 2025; Gureja et al. 2025; Pombal et al. 2025) studied pairwise accuracy or inter-language agreement, this paper's distinctive move is to attack pointwise score distributions and their downstream impact on threshold-based decisions. The "Tabelog vs. Google Maps" framing is apt and the 43 pp acceptance gap is a genuinely new, deployment-relevant finding.
- **Rigor**: Strong experimental hygiene — human-translated parallel data, temperature 0, eight evaluators spanning four backbones and two training paradigms, frontier replication on GPT-4.1-mini and Qwen3-32B, multiple uncertainty measures including token-free ones, nested OLS with F-tests. The deliberate choice to use summed rather than per-token NLL (with the explicit rationale that tokenizer effects cancel in per-token NLL) is a thoughtful methodological detail that strengthens the mechanism claim.
- **Reproducibility**: Reasonably strong. All evaluators are open-weight or API-accessible, benchmarks are public, prompts and exact LID tool (langdetect) are named, and the per-language tables (Tables 4, 5, 7, 12) provide enough to replicate the headline numbers. No mention of code release at the time of writing is a minor gap.
- **Significance**: High for the RLHF and safety-auditing communities. The result that pairwise accuracy systematically hides acceptance-rate disparities has direct implications for anyone using a single reward function across multilingual rollouts or a single global threshold for safety filtering.
- **Red flags / weaknesses**:
  - **Cherry-picked safety anecdote**: The −4.51 vs +3.57 English/Ukrainian example is illustrative but a single instance; the paper would be stronger with aggregate per-category safety pass-rate breakdowns by language in the main text.
  - **Cross-model linear correlation is weak**: Pairwise Pearson correlations between per-language mean scores across evaluators are only moderate (mean r = 0.53 on Chat-Hard, with some near-zero pairs in Figure 2). The authors acknowledge this and lean on ordinal consensus, but the headline "cross-model consistent" claim is mainly about top/bottom-quartile agreement, not full distributional agreement.
  - **Resource proxy is coarse**: Common Crawl page-share is a noisy proxy for "training data availability"; it ignores instruction-tuning and preference-data composition, which is what actually shapes reward models. A sensitivity analysis with alternative resource measures would strengthen the ρ = −0.81 claim.
  - **Limited LID attack generalization**: The code-switching attack is demonstrated for one language pair (en/hi) and one model (Skywork-LLaMA-8B). Whether the 50% → 75% inflation generalizes across language pairs and LID tools is not tested.
  - **No user study or downstream RLHF training run**: The RLHF "cross-lingual reward hacking" claim is plausible but not empirically demonstrated end-to-end; it remains a hypothesized consequence.
  - **Per-token vs. summed NLL choice, while justified, interacts with the within-language regression story**: within-language regressions use the same NLL measure, and the negative Chat/Chat-Hard slopes complicate the clean "uncertainty inflates scores" narrative — which the authors honestly flag rather than hide.

## Strengths

- Conceptually original and well-motivated: separates pointwise scoring from pairwise ranking in the multilingual setting, and shows the two tell different stories.
- Broad, heterogeneous evaluator pool (4 judges + 4 reward models + 2 frontier models) across 23 languages, with human-validated parallel data.
- Mechanism analysis is layered (correlation → control → within-language regression) and intellectually honest about what uncertainty does and does not explain.
- Concrete deployment implications spelled out: safety filters, RLHF reward shaping, threshold-based filtering.
- Practical, quantified mitigation (60.9% gap reduction) paired with an honest accounting of its residual and an adversarial demonstration of why it is not a complete fix.

## Limitations

- **Resource-level proxy**: Common Crawl share is a coarse stand-in for actual multilingual pretraining and preference-tuning data; the strong ρ = −0.81 could shift under alternative measures.
- **Code-switching attack is narrow**: only English/Hindi and one model; generalization across language pairs, scripts, and LID tools is untested.
- **No end-to-end RLHF demonstration**: cross-lingual reward hacking is argued, not shown in a real training run.
- **Linear cross-model agreement is modest**: ordinal agreement is high but several pairwise Pearson correlations are near zero, so "cross-model consistent" should be read as "consistent at the extremes," not across the full distribution.
- **Open evaluators only go up to 72B (and frontier checks up to GPT-4.1-mini/Qwen3-32B)**: still-larger or specially post-trained frontier judges are not tested, leaving open how the bias scales at the very top end.
- **Acceptance-rate threshold choice (50th percentile)**: results may look different at stricter (e.g., 90th percentile) thresholds typical in safety deployment; a threshold-sensitivity sweep would be informative.

## Relevance

与用户对 LLM 评估器、奖励模型与安全审计的研究兴趣高度相关：本文揭示了多语言场景下评估器分数不可跨语言直接比较，pairwise accuracy 无法发现这一偏移，对 RLHF、安全过滤、奖励塑形等下游任务的部署有直接警示意义，并提出了可操作的 per-language 校正方案及其局限性。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does the language-dependent scoring shift behave under stricter deployment thresholds (e.g., 90th or 95th percentile), where safety filtering actually operates?
- Does the bias persist or shrink when reward models are explicitly trained with language-balanced preference data, and how much balancing is enough?
- Can the per-language offset be learned without per-language calibration data — e.g., via unsupervised score normalization or self-calibration on unlabeled multilingual rollouts?
- How generalizable is the LID-defeating code-switching attack across script combinations (e.g., Cyrillic-shell + Latin-content), and can LID-robust thresholding (e.g., soft language posterior mixtures) close the gap?
- Does the 43 pp acceptance-rate disparity translate into measurable cross-lingual reward hacking in an actual RLHF training loop, and at what training scale does it emerge?
- Are the within-language negative NLL→score slopes in Chat/Chat-Hard a sign that reward models treat difficult conversational content as lower quality, and does this confound the uncertainty mechanism?
- How do instruction-tuning and preference-data composition (rather than Common Crawl share) predict the language-level offset, and can a finer resource measure tighten the ρ = −0.81 correlation?
