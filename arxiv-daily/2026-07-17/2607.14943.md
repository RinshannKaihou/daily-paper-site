> **一句话总结：** 本文用机制可解释性（MI）分析 World Action Models (WAMs) 在 OOD 扰动下的激活空间，发现 Cosmos-Policy 与 DiT4DiT 的鲁棒性关键特征呈低维线性可分结构（LingBot-VA 则不然），并提出 WA-LQR——一种无需训练、将 WAM 推理视为降阶线性系统并用闭环 LQR 在线调节激活的控制器，在 LIBERO-10 上把相机/夹爪/高斯噪声扰动下的成功率最高提升约 41 个百分点。

> **TL;DR:** The authors use mechanistic interpretability to show that robustness-relevant features in some World Action Models (Cosmos-Policy, DiT4DiT) are low-dimensionally linearly separable in activation space while others (LingBot-VA) are not, and that this linear-separability (SVM hinge loss) is a strong predictor (r = 0.63) of steering success. Building on this, they propose **WA-LQR**, a training-free, weight-preserving closed-loop controller that solves a reduced-order LQR over locally-linear DiT dynamics to push activations toward a contrastive robustness setpoint. On LIBERO-10, WA-LQR lifts Cosmos-Policy's average success under camera-orientation perturbation from 46.0% (no steering) to 59.3% (+13.3 points), under gripper-position perturbation from 61.3% to 72.7% (+11.4 points), and under Gaussian noise from 26.7% to up to 67.3% (ActAdd) / 58.7% (WA-LQR), beating prompt-steering (which collapses to 0.7% under noise) and unsteered baselines, with peak per-task gains up to +41 points.

## 核心贡献
- 首次对 WAMs（Cosmos-Policy、DiT4DiT、LingBot-VA）在相机姿态、初始夹爪位置、高斯图像噪声三类 OOD 扰动下做机制可解释性分析，提出用"低维 PCA 子空间 + 线性 SVM 铰链损失"作为线性可分性度量，发现可分性强依赖架构与任务。
- 实证证明 **可分性是可操控性 (steerability) 的有效预测器**：铰链损失与操控收益呈负相关（r = 0.63），可在动手微调前预测某个 WAM 是否适合激活操控。
- 把 LLM 文献中的 ActAdd（开环激活加法）迁移到 WAM，并提出 **WA-LQR**：在每个去噪时间步对 DiT 块链求解降阶 L 步 LQR，把激活当作可被反馈控制的状态，按 action-chunk 衰减调度自适应调节干预强度，避免开环过度操控。
- 提出首个面向 WAM 的开环 + 闭环激活操控方法，并在 LIBERO-10 上验证对比方向可跨任务迁移（如 Task i → Task j 仍能稳定提升成功率）。

## Key Contributions
- First mechanistic-interpretability study of World Action Models under robustness-relevant perturbations (camera orientation, initial gripper position, Gaussian image noise), using a top-3-PCA + linear-SVM hinge-loss metric to quantify per-layer, per-timestep linear separability of contrastive activations.
- Demonstrates that **steerability is architecture-dependent**: Cosmos-Policy and DiT4DiT exhibit clean low-dimensional linear structure for multiple perturbations, whereas LingBot-VA (a Mixture-of-Transformers inverse-dynamics design) shows substantially weaker separability, and this separability loss is a useful, empirically validated predictor (r = 0.63) of downstream steering success.
- Adapts ActAdd (open-loop activation addition from the LLM literature) to WAMs and introduces **WA-LQR**, a training-free, weight-preserving, reduced-order LQR controller that exploits the locally-linear dynamics of DiT activations in a rank-64 contrastive subspace to compute closed-form (Riccati) per-timestep feedback gains.
- Shows that contrastive directions and the linearized `Ã_l,t` matrices transfer across LIBERO tasks (Table 1 uses Task i → Task j cross-task steering), and that an action-chunk-decaying control penalty keeps steering concentrated on early executed chunks.

## Methodology
The paper splits into two parts linked by a single hypothesis (linearly separable robustness features ⇒ steerable WAMs).

**Part 1 — Mechanistic study (Sec. 4).** For each WAM they collect DiT residual-stream activations from contrastive forward passes: clean vs. perturbed camera, clean vs. perturbed gripper, clean vs. Gaussian-noised images. Activations are mean-pooled over token/spatial position and action-chunk timesteps. For each `(layer l, denoising timestep t)` they compute contrastive directions `d_k = x̄⁺_k − x̄⁻_k`, run PCA on `{d_k}`, project the pooled activations onto the top-3 principal components, fit a linear SVM, and report the per-sample hinge loss as the separability metric (0 = perfectly separable, 1 = random). They additionally check (i) that contrastive-vector variance is concentrated in the top few singular vectors (Fig. 6: rank-3 ≈ 99% captured) and (ii) that the block-transition Jacobian `Ã_l,t` of the latent dynamics stays locally linear (cosine similarity ≈ 1, Fig. 5a) and is input-invariant across 25 inputs/5 tasks (Fig. 5b), which justifies reusing a single Jacobian for cross-task control.

**Part 2 — Steering (Sec. 5).**
- *Open-loop (ActAdd):* a steering vector `a_{l,t}` is the average of contrastive directions; at inference it is added with a fixed strength `γ`. Table 5 shows this is extremely brittle — peak at γ = 0.1 (67.4%), collapse to 0% at γ ≥ 0.2.
- *Closed-loop (WA-LQR):* contrastive directions are projected to a rank-64 orthonormal basis `V_{l,t}` (streaming randomized SVD). The reduced linearized dynamics `δz_{l+1} ≈ Ã_{l,t} δz_l + B̃_{l,t} δu_l` are formed via JVP/VJP (Jacobians never materialized). A feature setpoint is defined as `β^*_{l,t} = λ‖ē_{l,t}‖`, the tracking error is `δz_{l,t,τ} = −α_{l,t,τ} v_{l,t}`, and an L-step LQR is solved per `(denoising step t, action-chunk index τ)` via Riccati recursion in O(L d_z³). The control penalty grows as `r(τ) = min(R_final, R_init exp(τ/τ_R))`, so early chunks get steered aggressively and later chunks saturate to negligible intervention. WA-LQR is therefore minimally-invasive: it only intervenes when the realized activation deviates from the robustness setpoint, which empirically prevents the oversteering collapse seen in ActAdd.

All steering is training-free, weight-preserving, and computed once per setting then reused across tasks.

## Results & Highlights
All numbers are LIBERO-10 task-suite success rates, 30 trials per task, on Cosmos-Policy 2B unless noted. "Task i → Task j" means the contrastive directions and `Ã, B̃` matrices were computed on Task i and applied unchanged on Task j.

- **Camera-orientation perturbation, Cosmos-Policy (Table 1, average over 5 cross-task settings):** No steering 46.0% ± 4.1%, prompt steering 46.7%, ActAdd 49.3%, **WA-LQR 59.3% ± 4.0% — a +13.3-point average gain over no steering and +12.6 points over prompt steering.** The best single setting is Task 0 → Task 5: 56.7% → 83.3% (+26.6 points).
- **Initial gripper-position perturbation, Cosmos-Policy (Table 1):** No steering 61.3%, prompt steering 60.7%, ActAdd 63.3%, **WA-LQR 72.7% ± 3.6% (+11.4 points over no steering)**; Task 1 → Task 2 reaches 100.0% vs. 83.3% unsteered.
- **Camera Gaussian-noise perturbation, Cosmos-Policy (Table 1):** No steering 26.7%, **prompt steering collapses to 0.7%** (it actively hurts), ActAdd 67.3%, WA-LQR 58.7%. The headline "up to 41%" improvement comes from the noise setting — e.g., Task 6 → Task 4: 10.0% (no steer) → 80.0% (ActAdd), a **+70-point gain**, and Task 6 → Task 1: 53.3% → 73.3% (WA-LQR). Here ActAdd slightly beats WA-LQR on average, which the authors attribute to the feature being highly separable so open-loop control suffices.
- **DiT4DiT (Table 2):** WA-LQR improves initial-gripper success 65.7% → **71.7%** and camera Gaussian-noise success 15.6% → **48.9%**, beating ActAdd (65.8% / 43.3%) on both.
- **LingBot-VA (Table 3):** Consistent with the MI analysis (no clean linear separability), steering yields little net benefit — ActAdd often *degrades* performance (camera orientation 48.0% → 41.0%, Gaussian noise 59.0% → 38.0%) due to oversteering, while WA-LQR's closed-loop modulation avoids this collapse (51.0% / 56.0%) but does not produce the large gains seen on the other two models. This is presented as confirmatory evidence that separability predicts steerability, not as a failure.
- **Separability ↔ steerability correlation (Fig. 7):** Across tasks and models, hinge loss is negatively correlated with success-rate gain, r = 0.63, supporting the central claim.
- **Linear-dynamics validation (Fig. 5a/b, Fig. 6):** Latent contrastive variance captured by rank-3 ≈ 99%; cosine similarity of linearized vs. actual latent transition ≈ 1 across tasks; `Ã_{l,t}` top-16 singular subspaces overlap strongly across 25 random inputs / 5 tasks — justifying reuse of a single Jacobian for cross-task LQR control.

## Authors & Credibility
Authors are from Georgia Institute of Technology (Hong, Skifstad joint first-authors; Dai, Chan, Chou). The work builds directly on a recent line of interpretable-robot-policy and video-model-steering papers (their refs [51] linear activation dynamics in LLMs, [55] T2V LQR steering) and on three concurrent 2026 WAM releases (Cosmos-Policy, DiT4DiT, LingBot-VA), so the methodological pedigree is current and the baselines are the actual state-of-the-art WAMs rather than older VLAs. Website/Code/Video links are advertised in the title banner. The framing is appropriately hedged: they explicitly state LingBot-VA is not steerable rather than hiding it, and the limitations section is candid about cross-task/cross-model applicability.

## Critical Assessment
- **Novelty:** Genuinely novel as the first open- **and** closed-loop activation-steering method for WAMs, and the first to verify that the linear-separability assumption (well-studied in LLMs/VLAs) does **not** universally hold for WAMs — LingBot-VA's MoT inverse-dynamics design breaks it. The reuse of T2V-style reduced-order LQR (ref [55]) is a known ingredient, but the adaptation to per-denoising-step, per-action-chunk LQR with a chunk-decay penalty is non-trivial and WAM-specific.
- **Rigor:** Reasonable. They validate every assumption WA-LQR relies on (low intrinsic dimensionality, local linearity, Jacobian input-invariance) with separate figures before deploying the controller, and they report standard deviations on every cell (30 trials/task). The cross-task transfer protocol (Task i → Task j) is a fair test of generalization rather than train-on-test.
- **Weak baselines / missing comparisons:** The "prompt steering" baseline is essentially a straw-man subtask-decomposition prompt and collapses to near-0% under Gaussian noise, so the headline delta over prompt steering is not very meaningful; the real comparison is vs. No Steering and vs. ActAdd, where WA-LQR's average advantage is more modest (≈ +6 to +13 points). There is **no comparison against robustness-oriented fine-tuning, RL post-training, wrist-camera augmentation, or any data-augmentation baseline** that the related-work section itself identifies as the dominant alternatives — so the claim of "improving robustness without extensive new data collection or slow retraining" is positioned but not directly cost-matched against retraining.
- **Cherry-picking / data quirks:** The "up to 41%" (and the even larger +70-point Task 6 → Task 4 noise result) is a per-setting maximum; the average gains are smaller and on gripper perturbation the ActAdd vs. WA-LQR ordering flips between models. Task groupings are hand-selected based on which task pairs share separable clusters (Fig. 3), which is a mild selection effect. The reported numbers use only 30 trials per cell, so several ±8–11% cells are within noise of each other.
- **Missing ablations:** No layer/timestep ablation showing *which* DiT blocks carry the steerable feature, no study of how `λ`, `R_init`, `τ_R` interact, and no analysis of why LingBot-VA's MoT design destroys separability (hypothesized but not tested by, e.g., steering its action module vs. video module separately — they report doing both in App. D.3 but do not isolate the cause).
- **Reproducibility:** Code is promised. Hyperparameters (rank-64 basis, 5 denoising timesteps, C=10 SVM, γ=0.1 ActAdd peak, `r(τ) = min(R_final, R_init·exp(τ/τ_R))`) are given; an LQR hyperparameter search is disclosed as performed per scene, which is a reproducibility caveat (results depend on a per-scene sweep).
- **Significance:** A useful diagnostic tool (hinge loss as steerability predictor) plus a training-free fix that gives real, if modest, robustness gains on the two steerable WAMs. It will likely matter more as a *methodological template* for future WAM interpretability than as a deployable robustness solution today.
- **Red flags:** None severe. The biggest intellectual risk is over-generalization from 3 WAMs and 3 perturbation types; the authors acknowledge this.

## Strengths
- Provides a cheap, inference-time, weight-preserving robustness fix — attractive when retraining WAMs is expensive.
- Honest negative result on LingBot-VA and an explicit separability→steerability predictor, so practitioners can cheaply pre-screen whether WA-LQR will help their model before running it.
- Mechanistic analysis is well-motivated and validated (variance concentration, local linearity, Jacobian reuse) rather than assumed.
- Cross-task transfer of both contrastive directions and linearized dynamics is demonstrated, supporting the practical claim of "training-free."
- Closed-loop design provably avoids the γ-sensitivity cliff that kills ActAdd (Table 5: 67.4% at γ=0.1 → 0% at γ≥0.2).

## Limitations
- Limited to 3 perturbation types (camera orientation, gripper position, Gaussian noise) on a single benchmark (LIBERO-10); lighting, background, language-phrasing, and object-layout shifts from COLOSSEUM/VLATest/LIBERO-Plus are not tested.
- Architecture-dependent: yields little benefit on LingBot-VA, so the method is not a universal WAM robustness tool.
- No method to predict task/environment transferability a priori — mechanistic analysis must be re-run per setting.
- WA-LQR requires an LQR hyperparameter search per scene, partially undercutting the "training-free" framing.
- ActAdd sometimes beats WA-LQR (Gaussian noise on Cosmos-Policy); the choice between open- and closed-loop is itself feature-dependent.
- Only 30 trials per cell, so several "wins" are within statistical noise.

## Relevance
This work sits at the intersection of mechanistic interpretability, optimal control, and robot-policy robustness — directly relevant if you follow VLA/WAM robustness, inference-time activation steering, or reduced-order control of generative models. The separability-as-predictor finding is a practical diagnostic you can reuse when evaluating any new foundation-model policy's amenability to activation steering.

## Questions for Further Exploration
- Does the separability→steerability predictor hold for non-DiT WAM backbones (e.g., autoregressive action heads, flow-matching policies), and what architectural choices specifically create or destroy linear robustness features?
- Can WA-LQR be combined with light robustness fine-tuning or wrist-camera data augmentation to reach parity with full retraining at a fraction of the cost?
- How does WA-LQR behave under compound perturbations (camera + lighting + object layout simultaneously) typical of COLOSSEUM / LIBERO-Plus, rather than single-axis perturbations?
- Is there a way to discover the shared-contrastive-cluster task groupings (Fig. 3) automatically, removing the current manual per-setting mechanistic analysis?
- Can the closed-loop formulation be extended to steer *semantic* features (e.g., language phrasing, object identity) in addition to robustness features, turning WA-LQR into a general WAM inference-time controller?
