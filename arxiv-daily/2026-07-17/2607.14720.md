> **一句话总结：** 本文提出一种基于梯度反转对抗框架的临床协变量探测方法，对前列腺 MRI 的 ISUP 分级模型逐个抑制 12 种临床变量，发现抑制年龄/BMI/饮酒可小幅提升 AUC，而抑制 PSA 和前列腺体积会显著降低 AUC（最高 -7.61%），从而在表征层面区分"捷径依赖"与"疾病相关信号"。

> **TL;DR:** The authors recast prostate MRI ISUP grading as observations of latent tumour pathology and use a gradient-reversal adversarial invariance framework to probe, one clinical covariate at a time, whether each variable acts as a shortcut confounder, a disease-related proxy, or an irrelevant correlate. On 2,903 UCLH scans with external validation on the 576-patient PROMIS cohort, suppressing age, BMI, and alcohol use improved binary ISUP ≥2 AUC by +1.23%, +0.84%, and +1.42% on UCLH, while suppressing PSA and prostate volume degraded it by −1.91% and −7.61% (all p<0.001), and prostate-volume suppression also cost −3.15% AUC on PROMIS — providing a representation-level diagnostic for separating harmful covariate dependence from informative signal.

## 核心贡献

- **因果重构建模**：将 MRI 外观与 ISUP 分级都视为潜在肿瘤病理 U 的观测，而不假设 MRI 直接导致分级；在此框架下为候选临床协变量 C 定义三种可能角色（捷径/混杂、疾病相关代理、无关变量），形成可检验的表征层面假设。
- **梯度反转对抗不变性框架**：对每个候选协变量单独训练一个对抗抑制模型，通过梯度反转层（GRL）让编码器在保留分级信息的同时降低该协变量的可解码性；推理时仅需 MRI，临床变量只用于训练阶段定义对抗目标。
- **逐变量灵敏度高维实证**：在 UCLH（2,038 名患者，2,903 次扫描）和 PROMIS 外部验证集（576 例）上系统测试 12 类临床变量，揭示出异质且可解释的性能变化模式——人口学/生活方式变量抑制有益，疾病相关变量抑制有害。
- **可操作的临床启示**：发现前列腺体积是最重要的任务相关变量（抑制后 AUC 下降最多），PSA 同样携带疾病信号；这为后续正式的因果分析与模型泛化改进提供了具体候选变量。
- **公开代码与跨骨干网络验证**：代码开源于 https://github.com/pipiwang/CausalProbing，并用 ResNet-18 与多种 ISUP 阈值/序数任务验证趋势的稳健性。

## Key Contributions

- **Causal reformulation of MRI grading**: Treats both MRI appearance X and ISUP grade Y as observed manifestations of latent tumour pathology U (U→X, U→Y) rather than assuming X causes Y, then enumerates three competing roles any clinical covariate C may play — nuisance/shortcut, disease-related proxy, or irrelevant correlate — each producing a testable representation-level prediction.
- **Variable-wise adversarial invariance framework**: Implements the probe with a gradient-reversal layer between a ProFound encoder and a per-covariate adversarial head, minimising C-decodability from the latent representation while preserving grading accuracy; crucially, clinical variables are not required at inference time.
- **Systematic empirical probe of 12 clinical covariates**: Tests age, BMI, alcohol use, smoking, cardiovascular/respiratory disease, abnormal DRE, diabetes, PI-RADS (raw and binary), PSA, and prostate volume, revealing heterogeneous and clinically interpretable performance shifts.
- **Directional, mechanistically interpretable findings**: Shows that suppressing demographic/lifestyle variables (age, BMI, alcohol) helps grading (consistent with shortcut removal), whereas suppressing disease-related variables (PSA, prostate volume) hurts grading (consistent with informative signal), with prostate volume being the single most impactful variable.
- **Cross-dataset and cross-architecture robustness**: Validates key trends on the held-out PROMIS external cohort and with a ResNet-18 backbone, plus alternative ISUP thresholds (≥3, ≥4) and ordinal grading.

## Methodology

The method has two parts: a causal formulation and an adversarial implementation.

**Causal formulation.** Let X = prostate mpMRI, Y = ISUP Grade Group, C = a candidate clinical covariate, and U = unobserved tumour pathology that generates both MRI appearance and histopathological grade (U→X and U→Y). The paper does NOT estimate the causal effect of MRI on grade, infer a full structural causal model, or perform conventional back-door adjustment via subgroup conditioning. Instead it performs a *representation-level sensitivity analysis*: train a baseline MRI-only grading model, then train an adversarially-invariant version that suppresses C from the representation, and compare the two. For metric M, the paired difference ΔM(C) = M_adv(C) − M_base is interpreted as: positive Δ ⇒ C behaves like a nuisance/shortcut (suppression helps); negative Δ ⇒ C carries disease-relevant signal (suppression hurts); near-zero Δ ⇒ C is weakly encoded or redundant.

**Adversarial invariance implementation.** The encoder f_θ maps X to representation H; a grading head g_ϕ predicts Ŷ with cross-entropy loss L_Y; a covariate head a_ψ predicts Ĉ with cross-entropy loss L_C. A gradient reversal layer sits between the encoder and the covariate head, so the adversary head learns to recover C while the encoder is pushed to make C undecodable from H. The combined objective is min_{θ,ϕ} max_ψ [L_Y(g_ϕ(f_θ(X)), Y) − λ·L_C(a_ψ(f_θ(X)), C)], with λ = 1.0. A separate model is trained per covariate (one-variable-at-a-time design) to isolate each effect. Samples with missing C values contribute only to the grading loss, not to the adversarial head.

**Backbone and training.** Encoder is ProFound (ConvNeXt v2 pretrained with masked autoencoding on ~5,000-patient prostate MRI corpus); ResNet-18 used as a secondary backbone. Two-layer MLP classification head, fine-tuned for 100 epochs, batch size 8, AdamW, lr 1e-4 with layer-wise decay, 5-epoch warm-up + half-cycle cosine schedule, class-balanced sampling. Continuous covariates (age, BMI, PSA, prostate volume) were binned into clinically interpretable categories to support the adversarial classification head and reduce outlier sensitivity.

**Data.** UCLH clinical-record dataset: 2,038 patients / 2,903 pre-treatment prostate MRI scans (April 2019–March 2024) with linked ISUP grades from Epic/PACS, split 7:1:2 patient-level. PROMIS dataset: 576 patients (May 2012–Nov 2015), held out entirely for external inference-only validation (no clinical variables available for adversarial training). All scans resampled to 0.5×0.5×1 mm, intensity-normalised, DWI/ADC aligned to T2W, augmented following ProFound.

**Evaluation.** Primary task: binary ISUP ≥2 vs <2. Secondary: ISUP ≥3, ≥4, and ordinal grading. Metrics: AUC, balanced accuracy, sensitivity at 80% specificity (Sens@80Spec), quadratic weighted kappa (QWK, ordinal). Primary task trained with 6 random seeds; other settings with 4 seeds. Significance via paired bootstrap testing with 10,000 resamples; 95% CIs via bootstrap.

## Results & Highlights

- **Baseline performance**: MRI-only ProFound baseline reached AUC 67.63% (95% CI 67.06–68.30) on UCLH and 64.60% (63.47–65.45) on PROMIS for binary ISUP ≥2 classification.
- **Suppressing demographic/lifestyle variables improved grading on UCLH**: age +1.23% AUC (p<0.001), BMI +0.84% AUC (p<0.05), alcohol use +1.42% AUC (p<0.001), versus the 67.63% baseline — interpreted as removal of non-generalising shortcut information.
- **Suppressing disease-related variables degraded grading on UCLH**: PSA value −1.91% AUC (p<0.01) and prostate volume −7.61% AUC (p<0.001), with drops across AUC, balanced accuracy, and Sens@80Spec — indicating these variables carry task-relevant signal in the learned representation.
- **External PROMIS validation reproduced the harmful direction**: suppressing prostate volume cost −3.15% AUC (p<0.001), binary PI-RADS −2.80% AUC (p<0.001), and PSA −1.54% AUC (p<0.001); alcohol-use suppression remained beneficial (+0.47%, p<0.05). The PSA effect was attenuated on PROMIS, which the authors attribute to pathway-defined cohort selection (referral on elevated PSA).
- **Cross-architecture robustness (ResNet-18)**: prostate-volume suppression again produced the largest degradation, −4.97% AUC and −5.02% Sens@80Spec.
- **Higher ISUP thresholds**: for ISUP ≥4, prostate-volume suppression reduced AUC by 2.71%, balanced accuracy by 6.64%, and Sens@80Spec by 6.37%; PSA suppression reduced AUC by 2.35% and Sens@80Spec by 4.41%.
- **Ordinal grading**: prostate volume remained the most detrimental suppressed variable, reducing QWK by 0.073 and AUC by 7.71%, with PSA and PI-RADS showing concordant decreases.

## Authors & Credibility

The work comes from a well-established UCL prostate-imaging group (UCL Hawkes Institute, Medical Physics & Biomedical Engineering, Computer Science, UCLH Urology, and the COMPASS research group). Senior authors include Daniel C. Alexander (causal/medical imaging), Mark Emberton and Veeru Kasivisvanathan (urologists who led the landmark PROMIS trial, cited as ref [1] and [9]), and Yipeng Hu (medical image computing, senior/corresponding author). First author Yipei Wang is also the lead of ProFound (ref [18]), the foundation model used as the encoder — i.e. the team has direct ownership of the backbone and the datasets. The study uses a real, large, single-referral-centre clinical cohort (2,903 scans) plus a published external validation trial cohort (PROMIS), and releases code at https://github.com/pipiwang/CausalProbing. The framing is appropriately hedged: the authors explicitly state the method "does not prove causality" and offers sensitivity findings rather than a sufficient proof of the underlying causal graph.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: The core building blocks are not new — gradient-reversal adversarial invariance (Ganin et al. 2016, Tzeng et al. 2017) and confounder-free training (Zhao, Adeli & Pohl, Nature Communications 2020, ref [23]) are well established, as is shortcut-learning critique of radiology models (DeGrave et al., Zech et al.). The genuine novelty is (a) the *causal taxonomy* mapping covariates onto three roles and tying each to a signed performance delta, and (b) the systematic, variable-by-variable empirical probe in prostate MRI grading, which has not been done before at this breadth. It is a thoughtful application paper rather than a methodological breakthrough.
- **Rigour**: Strong on several fronts — patient-level split to prevent leakage from repeated scans, 6-seed runs with bootstrap CIs and paired bootstrap significance testing (10,000 resamples), external validation on a separate trial cohort, and a secondary backbone (ResNet-18) plus alternative thresholds and ordinal tasks. The one-variable-at-a-time design is honestly motivated and its limitations openly discussed.
- **Reproducibility**: Good. Code is public, the encoder (ProFound) and the external dataset (PROMIS-derived, Zenodo DOI ref [17]) are released, and training hyper-parameters (λ=1.0, 100 epochs, batch 8, lr 1e-4, AdamW with layer-wise decay) are fully specified. The UCLH internal cohort is not publicly releasable, which limits independent end-to-end reproduction of the training-set-specific numbers but not of the method.
- **Significance**: Clinically meaningful and conceptually useful. The directional finding — disease-related variables (PSA, prostate volume) carry task-relevant signal while demographic/lifestyle variables act as shortcuts — is actionable for representation-level auditing of prostate MRI grading models, and the prostate-volume result (−7.61% AUC) is a striking, concrete data point.
- **Red flags / honest concerns**:
  - **Effect sizes are modest** for the "beneficial" suppressions (+0.84% to +1.42% AUC), and the absolute AUCs (~67–69%) are far below the performance levels reported by leading prostate MRI AI studies such as PI-CAI (Saha et al., Lancet Oncology 2024, ref [12]). The authors do not position this as a SOTA grading paper, but a reader should not infer clinical-grade accuracy.
  - **Discretisation throws away information**: continuous variables (age, BMI, PSA, volume) were binned into 2–4 categories for the adversarial classifier, so the probe can only detect coarse between-bin decodability; within-bin leakage is explicitly acknowledged as future work. This could systematically *underestimate* the disease-signal carried by PSA and volume.
  - **One-variable-at-a-time ignores interactions**: the authors flag this, but it means a covariate flagged as "shortcut" could be interacting with another (e.g. age and BMI), and joint suppression could behave differently.
  - **PROMIS is inference-only and pathway-conditioned**: PROMIS has no clinical variables for adversarial training and is a referral cohort selected on clinical suspicion (often elevated PSA), which the authors note attenuates the PSA effect — but this also means the external test is a weaker check on the shortcut-removal claim than on the disease-signal claim.
  - **The prostate-volume result is partly mechanical**: prostate volume is MRI-derived and visually encoded in the same scan, so suppressing it from the representation is almost guaranteed to perturb any feature that sees prostate anatomy. The −7.61% AUC is therefore not surprising and somewhat circular as evidence of "disease-relevant signal."
  - **No ablation on λ** (adversarial loss weight fixed at 1.0) and no comparison to a non-adversarial baseline such as simple covariate regression or balancing — so it is unclear how much of the effect is specifically due to the gradient-reversal mechanism versus generic regularisation.
  - **Ordinal results are "less consistent"** by the authors' own admission, which slightly weakens the breadth of the claims.

## Strengths

- Clean, well-motivated causal taxonomy that turns a vague "is this covariate a confounder?" question into a signed, testable delta.
- Strong experimental hygiene: patient-level splits, multiple seeds, bootstrap CIs, paired bootstrap significance, external validation, secondary backbone, and multiple thresholds/ordinal tasks.
- Directional findings are clinically interpretable and consistent across datasets and backbones (disease variables hurt to suppress; lifestyle variables help to suppress).
- Honest framing: explicit that this is sensitivity analysis, not causal proof; limitations section is substantive rather than perfunctory.
- Open code, open external dataset, and use of a released foundation-model backbone (ProFound) from the same group — strong reproducibility posture.

## Limitations

- Each covariate tested in isolation; interactions, permutations, and joint suppression are left to future work.
- Continuous covariates are discretised into coarse bins, sacrificing within-bin information; regression adversarial heads on raw/log-normalised values are unexplored.
- The UCLH training cohort is single-centre and not publicly releasable; only PROMIS is external.
- Adversarial loss weight λ is fixed at 1.0 with no sensitivity analysis.
- Absolute grading AUCs (~64–69%) are modest relative to current SOTA; this is a probing/analysis paper, not a performance paper.
- No comparison against simpler alternatives (regression-based removal, reweighting, balancing) to isolate the contribution of gradient-reversal adversarial training itself.
- PROMIS is inference-only and pathway-conditioned, weakening the external test of the shortcut-removal (beneficial) findings in particular.

## Relevance

与你的医学影像/表征学习兴趣高度相关：它把因果推理、对抗不变性与shortcut-learning诊断三者结合到一个具体的临床任务（前列腺 MRI 分级），并提供了可复用的变量级审计方法与开源代码，可直接迁移到你关心的医学影像模型的泛化性、可解释性与因果表征研究中。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How do results change if continuous covariates (PSA, volume, age, BMI) are predicted with regression adversarial heads on raw/log-normalised values instead of coarse classification bins — does within-bin information materially change the deltas?
- What happens under joint suppression of correlated covariates (e.g. age + BMI, or PSA + prostate volume), and can a sequential/greedy selection identify a minimal "shortcut set" versus a minimal "disease-signal set"?
- How sensitive are the signed deltas to the adversarial loss weight λ, and how does gradient-reversal compare to simpler alternatives (IPW reweighting, regression residualisation, matching) at equal covariate-decodability reduction?
- Does prostate-volume suppression still hurt when volume is removed from the input FOV/region rather than from the representation, disentangling anatomical encoding from disease-related signal?
- Do the shortcut-related variables (age, BMI, alcohol) generalise to MRI-naive screening cohorts (unselected, pre-PSA referral) where the PROMIS referral-conditioning bias is absent?
- Can the per-covariate deltas be validated against formal causal definitions of confounding using held-out interventional or prospectively collected data?
- Does adversarially suppressing the identified "shortcut" covariates improve out-of-distribution generalisation to new scanners/sites beyond PROMIS, providing a downstream practical benefit beyond the in-domain AUC gains?
