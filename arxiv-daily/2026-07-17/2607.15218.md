> **一句话总结：** 作者通过 hidden-state 方向分析证明"物理危险"(如"microwave an egg")与"文本内容危险"(如"ignite the curtain")在 LLM 表示空间中是两个可分离的方向，并据此训练出 PRISM——一个单层 logistic 探针，在 SafeAgentBench 上达到 86.2–87.7% 准确率、11.7–13.7% FPR，远低于同等规模 LLM 判别器的 24.7–39.0% FPR，并在新建的 PSB-1K 物理安全基准上达到 99.6% 准确率与 0.7% FPR。

> **TL;DR:** The paper shows that physical danger (PD, e.g. "microwave an egg") and content danger (CD, e.g. "ignite the curtain") occupy separable hidden-state directions in frozen LLMs (Qwen2.5-3B CDD/PDD probe-weight angle 76.33° vs a 60.35°±1.58° random-split null, z=10.09, p<0.0001), and proposes PRISM, a single-layer L2 logistic probe over a CV-selected hidden layer. On SafeAgentBench PRISM hits 86.2–87.7% accuracy at 11.7–13.7% FPR versus the same-scale LLM judge's 24.7–39.0% FPR, and on the new PSB-1K benchmark (1000 harm-keyword-free physical-risk pairs) it reaches 99.6% accuracy / 0.7% FPR where Llama Guard 3-1B scores 0/1000 PD detections and the Qwen2.5-3B judge over-blocks at 67.8% FPR.

## 核心贡献

- **证明物理危险与内容危险是可分离的两个 hidden-state 方向。** 在 Qwen2.5-3B/7B/14B/32B 上估计内容危险方向 CDD 和物理危险方向 PDD，selected-layer 的 probe-weight 角度分别为 75.9°/74.2°/71.6°/71.9°；Qwen2.5-3B 上 CDD/PDD 角度 76.33° 显著超过 113/187 随机划分 null 分布（60.35°±1.58°，z=10.09，p<0.0001，200 次重复无一次达到观测值）。
- **提出 PRISM 探针。** 一个单层、L2 正则化的 logistic 回归分类器作用在 5-fold CV 选出的 mid-to-late 层 full hidden state 上，同时学习 CD 与 PD 的二分类边界，是诊断式而非干预式的探针。
- **构建 PSB-1K 物理安全基准。** 1000 对最小对比的 safe/physical-risk 指令对（共 2000 条），覆盖 10 类家庭风险（微波金属接触、液体接触通电设备、明火接近、食物污染等），所有不安全样本均去除 danger/harm/burn/shock/poison 等直接伤害词，强制方法依靠物理因果而非表面策略词来识别。
- **可复现评估协议。** 在 SafeAgentBench(600)、改编 SafeText(737)、EARBench(2634) 和自建 PSB-1K 上一致使用 fold-internal 5-fold CV（PSB-1K 使用 pair-wise GroupKFold），并跨越 Phi-3.5-mini、SmolLM2-1.7B 验证。

## Key Contributions

- **Decomposability of danger in hidden-state space.** Class-mean directions CDD=μ_CD−μ_safe and PDD=μ_PD−μ_safe are statistically separable: the Qwen2.5-3B probe-weight angle is 76.33° vs a 60.35°±1.58° random-split null (z=10.09, p<0.0001), and the pattern (71.6°–77.3°) holds across Qwen2.5-7B/14B/32B, Phi-3.5-mini and SmolLM2-1.7B.
- **PRISM probe.** A single-layer `LogisticRegression(max_iter=2000, C=1.0)` over a 5-fold-CV-selected layer's full last-token hidden state (e.g. layer 27/36 for Qwen2.5-3B, 41/64 for Qwen2.5-32B), standardized inside each fold; it learns the binary safe/unsafe boundary from both CD and PD examples jointly.
- **PhysicalSafetyBench-1K (PSB-1K).** A new contrastive benchmark of 1,000 harm-keyword-free physical-risk pairs across 10 household risk families; unsafe items avoid any direct harm wording, so success requires physical-causal reasoning rather than lexical cue matching.
- **Failure-mode taxonomy for prior methods.** Formalizes three failure modes: content guards (Llama Guard 3-1B: 0/1000 PD on PSB-1K) miss PD entirely, LLM judges over-block safe tasks (FPR 24.7–39.0% on SafeAgentBench, 65.7–74.6% on cross-dataset runs), and single-direction/prototype methods (CDD-only, LPM) collapse on out-of-distribution physical danger.

## Methodology

The method rests on a representation-level claim and a probe that operationalizes it.

**Danger directions.** For a transformer layer L, the authors take last-token hidden states h^L(x) and compute class means μ_safe, μ_CD, μ_PD over the safe/content-danger/physical-danger subsets. The directions CDD=μ_CD−μ_safe and PDD=μ_PD−μ_safe measure where each danger class sits relative to safe examples. Two complementary angular quantities are reported: the mean-difference angle θ_md (raw geometry, conservative because it ignores within-class covariance) and the probe-weight angle θ_pw (decision-relevant geometry of two L2-regularized safe-vs-CD and safe-vs-PD probes).

**Random-split null.** To rule out high-dimensional-fitting artifacts, the 300 unsafe SafeAgentBench examples are randomly partitioned into groups of 113 and 187 (matching the CD/PD sizes), trained against the same 300 safe examples into two probes, and the weight-vector angle is recorded over 200 repetitions. The observed 76.33° exceeds the null mean of 60.35° (σ=1.58°) with no random split reaching it. (Null tests for 7B/14B/32B were not completed; the cross-scale claim is reported as an observed angular pattern.)

**PRISM.** Algorithm 1: (1) for each layer compute last-token hidden states for all training examples; (2) select layer ℓ* by 5-fold CV accuracy on the training set; (3) StandardScaler fit inside each fold; (4) train `LogisticRegression(max_iter=2000, C=1.0, random_state=42)` over the full standardized hidden state, jointly on safe+CD+PD examples; (5) at inference, one forward pass + σ(w⊤h̃+b) + threshold τ=0.5. The probe is a full-hidden-state binary classifier, not a hand-built CDD/PDD scalar rule.

**Evaluation protocol.** 5-fold StratifiedKFold on SafeAgentBench/SafeText/EARBench; pair-wise GroupKFold on PSB-1K so contrast pairs never split across folds. Baselines include CDD-only/PDD-only directional probes (subtype leave-out), LPM (multi-layer PCA + Mahalanobis prototypes), Nearest Centroid, SVM-RBF, MLP, and zero-shot LLM judges (`max_new_tokens=8`, parsed SAFE/UNSAFE) plus Llama Guard 3-1B with its default output. Scaling uses Qwen2.5-Instruct 3B/7B/14B/32B; cross-architecture uses Phi-3.5-mini and SmolLM2-1.7B.

## Results & Highlights

- **SafeAgentBench (Qwen2.5-3B, Table 2):** PRISM reaches 86.2±2.4% accuracy, 93.8% CD detection, 81.3% PD detection, 13.7% FPR, F1 0.861, AUC 0.926. The same-scale LLM judge scores 86.6% PD detection but at 27.3% FPR; LPM detects only 11.2% of physical-OOD samples.
- **Scaling trend (Table 2):** Across Qwen2.5-3B/7B/14B/32B, PRISM accuracy is tightly clustered at 86.2/87.0/87.2/87.7% with FPR 13.7/13.7/11.7/12.0% — a 1.5pp swing — while the LLM judge's PD recall rises 86.6/84.5/94.1/95.7% but its FPR climbs to 27.3/24.7/39.0/37.0%. Scaling the judge buys sensitivity, not calibration.
- **Cross-dataset replication (Table 3, Qwen2.5-3B):** PRISM F1/AUC/FPR = 0.861/0.926/13.7% (SafeAgentBench), 0.924/0.980/7.6% (SafeText), 0.828/0.906/16.6% (EARBench). The zero-shot judge over-blocks sharply: SafeText 65.7% FPR, EARBench 74.6% FPR.
- **PSB-1K (Table 4, Qwen2.5-3B):** PRISM reaches 99.6±0.5% accuracy, 99.9% PD detection, 0.7% FPR, F1 0.996, AUC 1.000. The Qwen2.5-3B judge attains 96.5% PD detection but at 67.8% FPR; **Llama Guard 3-1B scores 0/1000 PD detections (0.0% recall, 0.0% FPR)** — a complete taxonomy mismatch.
- **Cross-architecture (Table 6):** PRISM accuracy/FPR = 86.2/13.7% (Qwen2.5-3B), 87.7/13.3% (Phi-3.5-mini), 82.7/18.0% (SmolLM2-1.7B). The SmolLM2-1.7B judge blocks 294/300 safe tasks (98.0% FPR), showing small instruct models are not viable standalone safety classifiers.
- **Statistical comparison:** McNemar's test at the matched-FPR operating point (τ≈0.029/0.35) gives χ²=1.94, p=0.1637 on the physical subset and χ²=0.56, p=0.4557 overall, so PRISM is statistically indistinguishable from the 3B judge while keeping much lower FPR.
- **Latency (Table 7):** PRISM 56.8/146.1/103.4 ms vs judge 116.8/309.1/191.9 ms at 7B/14B/32B, a 2.06×/2.12×/1.86× speedup from replacing generation with a forward pass.

## Authors & Credibility

The work comes from Tsinghua University's Department of Computer Science and Technology (Weimeng Wang, Ziqiang Wang — corresponding, Zihang Zhan, Qi Li, Ke Xu — corresponding) with Chuanpu Fu from Nanyang Technological University. The authors have a track record in network/LLM security research. The experimental protocol is unusually careful: they pre-register a random-split null with 200 repetitions, separate within-class angular geometry (θ_md) from decision geometry (θ_pw), report recall and FPR jointly rather than recall alone, and are explicit about where their evidence is statistical (3B null) versus observational (7B/14B/32B and cross-architecture). Code/data are referenced through standard benchmarks plus a self-built benchmark whose construction criteria are spelled out.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Genuinely novel framing. Treating embodied physical danger as a distinct representation-level target — rather than a harder instance of text moderation — is a real conceptual contribution, and the CDD/PDD direction analysis with a random-split null is a clean way to substantiate it.
- **Rigor:** Strong on the 3B null test (z=10.09, p<0.0001) and on reporting recall/FPR jointly. However, **the random-split null was only completed for Qwen2.5-3B**; the authors explicitly write "Null tests for 7B/14B/32B were not completed, so the cross-model claim is stated as an observed angle pattern." The central separability claim is therefore statistically supported at only one scale.
- **Baselines — mixed strength.** **Llama Guard 3-1B is a near-trivial baseline here (0/1000 PD detections on PSB-1K)**; the authors themselves attribute this to "taxonomy mismatch," so beating it carries little information. The LLM-judge comparison is more meaningful but the headline "PRISM is statistically comparable to the judge" rests on a McNemar test at a *matched-FPR operating point* (τ lowered to ≈0.029 or 0.35), not at PRISM's default τ=0.5 — i.e. the 86.2–87.7%/11.7–13.7% operating point and the matched-comparison operating point are different.
- **Missing ablations:** No ablation on layer-selection protocol (the authors flag this as "selected-layer estimate rather than a fully nested layer-selection protocol"), no sensitivity analysis on the L2 regularization constant C=1.0, and no analysis of how PRISM performs when the training mix of CD vs PD examples is imbalanced.
- **Data quirk — acknowledged but material.** The SafeAgentBench 25-keyword split has known leakage ("drop" in 12 safe and 29 CD cases; "break" in 1 safe and 34 CD cases). The authors retain it "for comparability while reporting CD and PD separately," which is reasonable but means the headline numbers partly inherit that leakage.
- **Benchmark self-confirmation:** PSB-1K is self-built by the same authors and yields PRISM's strongest headline number (99.6%/0.7% FPR). The contrastive design and GroupKFold protocol mitigate overfitting, but the absence of independent third-party use of PSB-1K is a caveat.
- **Honesty of claims:** Refreshingly bounded. The authors explicitly write "the directions are separable rather than strictly orthogonal," "PRISM is not a complete embodied-agent safety system," and that cross-dataset runs are "cross-dataset replication under matched evaluation" rather than zero-shot transfer. They do not overclaim.
- **Reproducibility:** Good in principle — standard sklearn primitives, deterministic `random_state=42`, per-model layer indices disclosed, latency protocol fixed (20 warmup / 580 measured). Code/data release is implied by the construction of PSB-1K but should be verified.

## Strengths

- Conceptually clean: physical danger as a separable hidden-state target, not a text-moderation subtask.
- The random-split null on Qwen2.5-3B is the right control and is executed rigorously (200 reps, z-score reported).
- Joint recall/FPR reporting exposes the actual failure mode of LLM judges (over-blocking) that recall-only numbers hide.
- PSB-1K's design (no harm keywords, paired, GroupKFold) directly targets the claim it is meant to test.
- Cross-architecture replication on Phi-3.5-mini and SmolLM2-1.7B rules out a Qwen-specific artifact.
- Bounded, non-overclaimed conclusions; explicit about what is and is not statistically demonstrated.

## Limitations

- Statistical null completed only for 3B; 7B/14B/32B separability is observational, not tested.
- SafeAgentBench keyword split has known lexical leakage, inherited by all SafeAgentBench numbers.
- McNemar comparability with the judge is at a *matched-FPR* threshold, not at PRISM's default τ=0.5, so "comparable to the judge" and "86.2–87.7% at 12–14% FPR" describe different operating points.
- Cross-dataset evaluation uses fold-internal CV, not zero-shot transfer, so generalization to unseen distributions is untested.
- PSB-1K is author-built; the strongest headline number (99.6%) sits on a benchmark designed by the same method's authors.
- Latency comparison fixes counts and prompt but not GPU/software stack, so it is a local diagnostic rather than a portable throughput claim.
- Llama Guard 3-1B baseline is mismatched (taxonomy built for text policy, not embodied harm), making its 0% detection more an artifact than an informative comparison.
- No human study validating that PSB-1K's "physical danger" labels are actually unsafe under realistic physical causality.

## Relevance

这篇论文与 embodied-agent 安全和 LLM 可解释性两个方向都高度相关——它把"物理执行风险"从"文本内容风险"中分离出来作为独立的 hidden-state 结构来研究，对研究 LLM-based agent 的安全监控、mechanistic interpretability 和 representation engineering 都有直接参考价值，尤其适合关注 agent 安全评估与探针式（probing）可解释方法的研究者。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Would completing the random-split null for Qwen2.5-7B/14B/32B reproduce the z>10 effect, or does the cross-scale angular pattern (75.9°→71.9°) reflect diminishing separability at scale?
- How does PRISM behave under distribution shift that breaks the paired structure of PSB-1K (e.g. truly novel physical-risk families unseen in training), rather than fold-internal CV?
- Can the CDD/PDD directions be used for *intervention* (steering) rather than just detection, and would steering PDD suppress physical-dangerous completions without harming safe ones?
- Is the LLM-judge over-blocking (24.7–39.0% FPR) fixable with better prompts or calibration, or is it an intrinsic property of decoded judgments that hidden-state probing genuinely avoids?
- How does PRISM transfer when the LLM is fine-tuned or instruction-tuned after probe training — does the selected layer and boundary remain stable?
- A human-validated "actual physical consequence" labeling for PSB-1K would strengthen the benchmark; how often do the 1,000 pairs admit ambiguous real-world interpretations?
- Does the separability finding extend to vision-language-action (VLA) models where actions are generated jointly with perception, or is it specific to text-only LLM planners?
