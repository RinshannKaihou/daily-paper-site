> **一句话总结：** 作者严格按梯度评估数等额匹配算力，证明 Bogacki–Shampine 3(2) RK–Adam 的"自适应步长"控制器实际上是惰性的（容差变 100 倍轨迹逐位相同），修复后虽能在全批量训练损失上比 Adam 低约 40 倍，但该收益对初值敏感、不迁移到测试精度，最终被精调 Adam (90.30%) 在 1/3 单步成本下全面超越。

> **TL;DR:** Under a strict per-gradient-evaluation compute-matched protocol, a representative Bogacki–Shampine 3(2) RK–Adam loses to plain Adam on training loss in both minibatch and full-batch MNIST training (e.g., Adam 97.76% vs RK3(2)-Adam 97.26% test acc at lr=0.001); its "adaptive" step controller is provably inert (tolerance ρ swept 100× yields bit-identical trajectories, h pinned at the cap on 98–100% of steps). Repairing the controller reverses the full-batch training-loss comparison by ~40× (FixedRK3Adam 2.5×10⁻⁶ vs Adam 1.0×10⁻⁴ loss), but a pre-registered pSGLD temperature sweep refutes the tempering hypothesis and tuned Adam at lr=0.01 (90.30%) beats every RK variant on test accuracy at one third the per-step cost.

## 核心贡献

- 提出严格的"每梯度评估计数"算力匹配协议：一条 s 阶 RK 步被记为 s 次梯度评估，FSAL（first same as last）重用仅在被积向量场真正自治（即确定性全批量）时才算免费——这条约定直接改变了 RK 方法的胜负符号。
- 给出对照实验证据：在 minibatch 和全批量两种 MNIST 设置下，RK3(2)-Adam 在等算力下都输给 Adam；在全批量场景下甚至连 RMSprop/NAdam 也以 1/3 的单步成本超过它。
- 系统性诊断"自适应"控制器惰性的三类复合原因：无拒绝分支、增长因子饱和、误差估计与应用映射不一致，并给出形式化饱和条件（Proposition 1）。
- 进行全超参数立方体扫描（ρ × hmax × h0 共 36 组配置）证明任何容差设置都无法激活控制器——这是从"anecdote"升级为"exhaustive negative"。
- 通过修复控制器（真拒绝分支 + 对应用映射测误差）实现全批量训练损失反向领先约 40 倍，并用固定步长对照分离出"自适应"是真正机制（emergent warmup-and-growth schedule）。
- 预注册的 pSGLD 温度扫描实验排除"过拟合"和"cold-posterior tempering"两种解释，将测试精度缺口锁定为轨迹/隐式偏置效应。
- 提供 n=10 多种子复现确认了 RK 梯度平均作为隐式正则化物的存在（在 lr=0.003 全批量上 10/10 种子击败 lr 匹配的 Adam/AdamW），但 RMSprop、NAdam、lr=0.01 的 Adam 都能以更低成本达到或超越。

## Key Contributions

- A strict per-gradient-evaluation accounting protocol in which an s-stage RK step is charged s gradient evaluations and FSAL reuse is credited only when the vector field is genuinely autonomous (i.e., deterministic full-batch); this single convention flips the sign of the RK-vs-Adam comparison.
- Compute-matched evidence that a representative RK3(2)-Adam loses to plain Adam on training loss in both stochastic and full-batch regimes (Tables 1–2).
- A formal diagnosis of why embedded-pair step control is inert under Adam preconditioning, including a saturation condition (Proposition 1) that h_t = hmax for all t ≥ ⌈log₂(hmax/h0)⌉ whenever err_c ≤ (0.9/2)³ ≈ 0.091.
- An exhaustive ρ × hmax × h0 sweep (36 RK configs + 18 baselines) showing no hyperparameter setting rescues the as-designed controller — tolerances spanning 100× produce bit-identical trajectories to six decimals.
- A repaired-controller ablation (Section 6): a true accept/reject branch plus error measured on the applied map yields ~40× lower full-batch training loss than tuned Adam at matched budget, with a fixed-step control isolating adaptivity (not order, not step magnitude) as the operative mechanism.
- A pre-registered pSGLD temperature sweep (Section 7) that refutes both "deeper minimization overfits" and "cold-posterior tempering" as explanations for the generalization shortfall, pinning it on which minimum the controller's trajectory selects.
- A minimal reporting standard for adaptive-step optimizers: report (i) the distribution of accepted h over training (especially fraction pinned at the ceiling), (ii) the rejection rate, and (iii) a demonstration that at least two controller settings produce non-bit-identical trajectories.

## Methodology

The author implements **AdaptiveEmbeddedRK3Optimizer**, which applies Bogacki–Shampine 3(2) stages to the gradient flow θ̇ = −∇L(θ). The third-order averaged gradient g₃ = (2/9)k₁ + (1/3)k₂ + (4/9)k₃ drives standard Adam moments, with FSAL caching the post-step gradient as the next k₁. The step size is controlled by

ϕ = min(max(0.9 · err_c^(−1/3), 0.5), 2.0),  h ← clip(h·ϕ, [0.1·lr, hmax])

with hmax = 2·lr by default and err_c the RMS of h(g₃ − g₂)/(atol + ρ|θ|). In the stochastic setting FSAL is disabled (the cached k₁ belongs to a previous minibatch's vector field), so each step honestly costs 4 evals; in the full-batch regime FSAL is valid and a step costs 3 fresh evals.

Four experiments are run on MNIST with a 784–128–10 MLP:

1. **Stochastic minibatch** (batch 128, 4000 grad evals/seed, 3 seeds): direct loss/accuracy comparison.
2. **Full-batch** on a 1024-example MNIST subset (the genuinely autonomous regime where FSAL is valid): primary metric is final training loss against GD (Euler) and a baseline pool of SGD+momentum/Nesterov, Adagrad, RMSprop, Adam, AdamW, NAdam, RAdam.
3. **Repaired-controller ablation**: adds a true accept/reject branch (reject → retry with smaller h, no update), measures error on the actually-applied Adam-preconditioned map, sets hmax = 100·h0; two variants PureRK3(2) (raw, consistent estimate) and FixedRK3Adam (Adam-preconditioned).
4. **Pre-registered temperature sweep** grounding the controller's implicit tempering as explicit temperature via pSGLD (Eq. 3), with H1 (interior optimum T* > 0) and H0 (T = 0 is best) criteria declared in code before running.

A fixed-step control (h held at the saturated value 0.1) is used to separate the adaptivity mechanism from the large-effective-step confound, and a five-point h₀ grid traces the fragility boundary of the repaired controller.

## Results & Highlights

- **Stochastic minibatch MNIST** (Table 1, 4000 evals/seed): Adam reaches 97.76 ± 0.14% at lr=0.001 and 97.13 ± 0.15% at lr=0.003, while RK3(2)-Adam reaches only 97.26 ± 0.17% and 96.61 ± 0.28% respectively — a ~0.5 pt loss at both learning rates, well outside seed noise. The ρ=0.01 and ρ=0.1 rows are bit-identical, confirming the controller never acted.
- **Full-batch training loss** (Table 2, 1024-example subset, 600 evals/seed): Adam reaches 1.03×10⁻⁴ ± 2×10⁻⁶ at lr=0.003 while as-designed RK3(2)-Adam reaches only 3.50×10⁻⁴ ± 9.6×10⁻⁵ — worse loss at 3× the per-step cost. RMSprop (1.80×10⁻⁴) and NAdam (2.58×10⁻⁴) also beat RK3(2)-Adam.
- **Controller inertness** (Figure 2a/b, Proposition 1): with defaults, h jumps from lr to hmax = 2·lr on the first step and pins there for 100% of steps (98.5% at hmax = 8×). Maximum normalized error over the whole run is 0.029 — 3× below the saturation threshold (0.9/2)³ ≈ 0.091.
- **100× tolerance sweep is bit-identical** (Section 5.1): at lr=0.003, hmax=8×, the loss for ρ ∈ {0.01, 0.1, 1.0} is 0.000109 / 0.000112 / 0.000112 — a 3×10⁻⁶ residual gap that exists only because ρ=0.01's first-step h differs before saturation.
- **Repaired controller reverses the full-batch loss result** (Table 3): FixedRK3Adam at h₀=10⁻³, ρ=0.01 reaches 2.5×10⁻⁶ ± 6×10⁻⁷ — ~40× below best Adam (1.0×10⁻⁴) at matched budget. A fixed step at h=0.1 reaches only 0.186 (Table 4), isolating adaptivity as the mechanism rather than the large step.
- **Repaired win does not transfer to test accuracy**: no repaired-RK configuration reaches 90%; the deepest config (3.8×10⁻⁷ loss) reaches only 86.95% test accuracy, while steady Adam at lr=0.01 reaches 90.30% (Table 4) at one third the per-step cost.
- **Implicit-regularization finding replicated at n=10** (Section 4, Figure 1): RK3(2)-Adam defaults 89.53% vs Adam 89.03% / AdamW 89.05%, winning on 10/10 seeds (paired t = 6.0, +0.51 ± 0.27 pts); best-loss RK config (hmax=8×) reaches 89.83%, +0.80 pts (t = 6.5, 10/10). But RMSprop 90.02%, NAdam 89.94%, and Adam lr=0.01 90.30% all match or beat every RK configuration at one third the cost.
- **Tempering hypothesis refuted** (Section 7, Figure 4): pSGLD T=0 gives 90.38% and is the global optimum of the sweep; every T > 0 falls ≥ 0.6 pt below, with accuracy collapsing to 79.79% at T=10⁻³. Deep minimization is generalization-neutral (loss falls four orders of magnitude while test accuracy changes only 0.19 pt).

## Authors & Credibility

Single-author preprint by Akhilesh Gogikar (Independent Researcher). The work exhibits strong methodological discipline: pre-registration of the temperature-sweep H1/H0 criteria in code before running, paired t-tests with n=10 seeds for headline secondary claims, an exhaustive hyperparameter cube sweep rather than a single anecdotal configuration, and full release of code (RK4Optimizer.py, mnist_experiment.py, fullbatch_experiment.py, fixed_controller_experiment.py, hmax_control_experiment.py, temperature_sweep_experiment.py) plus result JSONs and logs. All experiments run on CPU with deterministic seeding, making them cheap to replicate. The author is unusually forthright about limitations (8 enumerated items in §9.1), including the single-architecture scope, the modest lr grid for Adam, and the post-hoc nature of the implicit-regularization observation.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: The core negative result — that an "adaptive" RK controller published in the optimizer literature is provably inert — is genuinely novel and, as the author notes, never checked in prior work. The Proposition 1 saturation condition and the repaired-controller ablation that isolates adaptivity from large-step confounds are concrete contributions.
- **Rigor**: Strongest aspect. Pre-registered hypotheses, paired statistics at n=10, exhaustive ρ × hmax × h0 cube, a fixed-step control to disentangle mechanism from confound, and a pSGLD temperature sweep to rule out the obvious alternative explanation. The bit-identical-trajectory evidence for inertness is hard to argue with.
- **Reproducibility**: Excellent on paper — deterministic CPU runs, released code and JSON logs. However, the author flags (Limitation 4) that the repaired controller is fragile to h₀ (the ρ=0.01 arm breaks by h₀=0.002 with 79.3 rejects/seed, while ρ=0.1 is stable to 0.0025), so a finer h₀ sweep "could move results in either direction." This fragility is honestly disclosed but weakens the repaired-controller headline.
- **Significance**: Tempered by scope. All results are on MNIST with a single 784–128–10 MLP — no CNNs, no Transformer, no ImageNet, no modern benchmark. The author is explicit that "the compute-matched methodology transfers, but the empirical rankings may not." The implicit-regularization observation is post-hoc (Limitation 7) and the minimum-selection mechanism is identified only by exclusion — no curvature instrumentation (Limitation 8).
- **Red flags**: (i) Baseline tuning asymmetry — Adam received only a "modest lr grid" while RK variants received an h₀/ρ grid; given the documented h₀ fragility of the repaired method, a fairer head-to-head could change the picture. (ii) The headline ~40× training-loss win is full-batch, deterministic, and fragile to h₀, with no evidence it survives minibatch noise — exactly the regime where the author's own diagnosis predicts the error estimator is variance-dominated. (iii) Single-author, independent-researcher preprint with no peer review, though the methodological care partially offsets this. (iv) The paper compares against only one representative RK design (Bogacki–Shampine 3(2)); IMEX and implicit schemes have different failure surfaces the author explicitly does not probe (Limitation 3).

## Strengths

- Honest, non-rubberstamp framing: the paper sets out to test an appealing idea and reports a mostly-negative answer with the diagnostic depth to explain why.
- The per-gradient-evaluation accounting protocol is a transferable methodological contribution that the author argues should reframe how prior per-iteration or wall-clock RK-optimizer comparisons are read.
- Proposition 1 + exhaustive sweep converts the inertness claim from anecdote to exhaustive negative.
- The fixed-step control isolating adaptivity (vs. large effective step) as the mechanism is a clean experimental design.
- Pre-registration of the temperature-sweep hypotheses is a model of how to test alternative explanations.
- Minimal reporting standard proposal (accepted-h distribution, rejection rate, non-bit-identical trajectories across settings) is concrete and actionable.
- Full code and result-JSON release on CPU-deterministic runs.

## Limitations

- Single dataset (MNIST), single architecture (784–128–10 MLP), single subset size (1024 examples for full-batch). No modern benchmarks; rankings may not transfer.
- Main sweeps use only n=3 seeds; only the secondary test-accuracy observation is chased at n=10. Repaired-controller tables remain n=3.
- Only one RK design point (Bogacki–Shampine 3(2)); IMEX/implicit schemes have different failure surfaces not probed.
- Baseline tuning asymmetry: modest lr grid for Adam vs. h₀/ρ grid for RK; the repaired controller's documented h₀ fragility means a finer sweep could move results.
- The ~40× training-loss win is full-batch, deterministic, and fragile to h₀; no evidence it survives minibatch noise (where the author's own diagnosis says the error estimator is variance-dominated).
- Compute-matching ignores per-step optimizer overhead, slightly favoring RK methods.
- The implicit-regularization observation is post-hoc.
- Minimum-selection mechanism is identified by exclusion; no curvature instrumentation at the selected minima.

## Relevance

This paper is highly relevant to anyone evaluating "ODE-as-optimizer" claims and to optimizer benchmarking methodology in general — the per-gradient-evaluation accounting protocol and the adaptive-controller reporting standard are directly portable to other optimizer comparisons. The negative result and inertness diagnosis are also a useful caution for daily-paper readers tracking RK/IMEX/neural-ODE optimizer proposals.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the inertness diagnosis generalize to other published RK-optimizer variants (IMEX-Adam of Bhattacharjee et al. 2024, AdamNODEs of Cho et al. 2022), or is it specific to the Bogacki–Shampine 3(2) + embedded-pair design?
- Can a variance-corrected error estimator make the repaired controller meaningful under minibatch noise, where the author's own diagnosis says the current estimator is sampling-noise-dominated?
- Does the ~40× full-batch training-loss win replicate on CNNs/Transformers or on physics-informed / implicit-layer inner solves — the deterministic small-data regimes the author identifies as the honest market for this mechanism?
- Is the emergent warmup-and-growth schedule precisely characterizable as an implicit error-control policy corresponding to hand-designed warmup schedules?
- Would a curvature-instrumented study (Hessian / sharpness at the selected minima) confirm the implicit-bias mechanism the author names but does not measure?
- Does repairing the controller change the picture at modern scale (ImageNet, LMs), or does the variance domination of the error estimator make the repair moot in stochastic large-batch training?
