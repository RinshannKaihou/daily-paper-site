> **一句话总结：** 本文首次给出了回合制随机博弈（TBSG）中可达性目标的去中心化、私有信息 PAC 学习正面结果，通过将 MDP 中的"期望条件距离"（ECD）参数精心推广到博弈设定，并提出 LeTuReGa 算法对，证明了多项式样本复杂度界（O(|S|³L⁷|A|·log(|S|²L²/(pε²))/ε¹⁰））。

> **TL;DR:** This CONCUR 2026 theory paper proves the first positive PAC-RL result for turn-based stochastic games (TBSGs) with reachability objectives under both **decentralized** learning algorithms and **private** state information — two assumptions that prior centralized/public-information work (Ashok–Kretínský–Weininger, CAV 2019) could not handle. The authors carefully generalize the Expected Conditional Distance (ECD) parameter from MDPs to two-player games (showing five "natural" generalizations provably fail on a 4-state example where their ECD ≤ 2 but a horizon of ≥100 is needed), reduce PAC-RL with ECD to finite-horizon PAC-RL with horizon H = 2(L+1)/ε, and give the LeTuReGa algorithm pair whose sample complexity for ECD-bounded reachability is **O(|S|³L⁷|A|·log(|S|²L²/(pε²))/ε¹⁰)** — a polynomial bound in |S|, |A|, L, 1/ε, 1/p with no baseline to compare against since this is the first positive result in this decentralized-private setting.

## 核心贡献

- **首个去中心化 + 私有信息的正面结果：** 在 TBSG 的可达性目标 PAC 学习中，同时放宽了先前工作 [Ashok/Kretínský/Weininger, CAV 2019] 的两条强假设——(i) 双方共享公共信息；(ii) 双方使用同一个集中式学习算法。本文允许每个玩家拥有自己的学习算法、且当前状态仅对状态所属玩家可见。
- **ECD 参数从 MDP 精心推广到博弈：** 作者证明了 5 种"自然"推广（ECD1–ECD5）都不可行，并以一个 4 状态反例（图 1）说明它们取值 ≤ 2 却无法约束所需 horizon ≥ 100；而本文定义 ECD_G = 1001 给出了正确的 horizon 界。
- **从 PAC-RL(ECD) 到有限步 PAC-RL 的归约：** 给出 Theorem 14，证明用 horizon H = 2(L+1)/ε 的有限步可达性 ε/2-optimal 策略即是原可达性问题的 ε-optimal 策略（结合 Markov 不等式与 Proposition 13）。
- **LeTuReGa 算法对：** 通过"状态-步"扩展博弈（Definition 18）把 horizon 编码进状态空间，结合 Median Elimination 最佳臂识别、反向归纳、以及把"未探索单元"作为辅助目标来驱动探索，给出了有限步可达性 PAC-RL 算法。
- **多项式样本复杂度：** 有限步情形复杂度为 O(|S|³L⁷|A|·log(|S|²L²/p)/ε³)；ECD 可达性情形（推论 16）为 O(|S|³L⁷|A|·log(|S|²L²/(pε²))/ε¹⁰)。

## Key Contributions

- **First positive result for decentralized + private-information PAC learning in TBSGs with reachability.** Both strong assumptions of the prior state of the art (Ashok–Kretínský–Weininger, CAV 2019, ref [2]) — shared public information and a single centralized learning algorithm — are simultaneously relaxed. Each player runs its own decoupled algorithm (Definition 4), and the current state is revealed only to its owner (Definition 3, simulator step 4c).
- **Subtle game-theoretic generalization of ECD.** Five alternative generalizations (Definition 6, ECD1–ECD5) are shown to fail via the 4-state example in Figure 1 / Example 7, where each alternative yields a value ≤ 2 despite a required finite horizon of at least 100 to distinguish the optimal action. The paper's Definition 8 (ECD_G := sup_{π_Min} inf_{π_Max ∈ BR(π_Min)} ETR_G(π_Max, π_Min)) yields 1001, providing a valid horizon bound and always being finite (unlike the stochastic-shortest-path parameter, which can be infinite).
- **Reduction: PAC-RL(ECD) → PAC-RL(finite-horizon).** Theorem 14, with horizon H = 2(L+1)/ε and error tolerance ε/2; the bound combines Proposition 13 (Markov's inequality truncation argument) with the definition of best-responses.
- **LeTuReGa algorithm pair** (Algorithm 1; "Learning Turn-based Reachability Games"). Built on (a) the expanded game of state–step pairs (Definition 18), (b) backward induction, (c) Median Elimination best-arm identification (Lemma 17, matching the Mannor–Tsitsiklis lower bound), and (d) treating the unexplored state-step set U_q as an auxiliary target that drives exploration. Iterative strategy accumulation ensures previously discovered state-steps remain reachable in later stages.
- **Explicit polynomial sample-complexity bounds.** Theorem 15: finite-horizon sample complexity **O(|S|³L⁷|A|·log(|S|²L²/p)/ε³)**. Corollary 16: ECD-bounded reachability sample complexity **O(|S|³L⁷|A|·log(|S|²L²/(pε²))/ε¹⁰)** — polynomial in |S|, |A|, L=ECD, 1/ε, 1/p.

## Methodology

The paper is purely theoretical (formal-methods / learning-theory). The construction proceeds in three conceptual layers:

1. **ECD generalization (Section 3).** Starting from the MDP ECD parameter of Svoboda–Bansal–Chatterjee (ICML 2024, ref [20]), the authors show via the 4-state example in Figure 1 that all five "obvious" generalizations (ECD1–ECD5, mixing sup/inf quantifiers with best-response or general strategy sets) give a value ≤ 2 while the actual horizon needed to distinguish the optimal Min action is ≥ 100. The correct definition fixes the inner quantifier to range over **best-responses** of Max (so that the parameter is always finite and equals 1001 on the example). This subtlety is the first main technical novelty.

2. **Reduction (Section 3, Theorem 14).** Reachability PAC-RL with ECD ≤ L is reduced to finite-horizon reachability PAC-RL with horizon H = 2(L+1)/ε. The argument combines (a) for every Min strategy there is a best-response of Max with ETR ≤ L+1 (Definition 8 + (s,d) construction of φ); (b) Markov's inequality on ETR (Proposition 13) shows truncation to horizon H changes reachability probability by at most ε/2; (c) combine with ε/2-optimality in the finite-horizon game.

3. **Decentralized private-information algorithm (Section 4).** The pair (LeTuReGa_Max, LeTuReGa_Min) (Algorithm 1) is built from:
   - **Expanded game** G′ over state–step pairs (s, ℓ), ℓ ∈ [L] (Definition 18). Positional strategies in G′ correspond to Markovian strategies in G, and Val_{G′}^{R(T_Max)} equals both finite-horizon and infinite-horizon reachability values (Proposition 19).
   - **Best-arm identification** via Median Elimination (Lemma 17, O(|A|·log(1/p)/ε²) samples, matching the lower bound).
   - **Backward induction** in stages: at each level ℓ from L−1 down to 1, for each state s owned by player i, a BAI routine identifies an ε_bai-optimal local action while previously built strategies π⁰, …, π^{q−1} are used in steps 1, …, ℓ−1 to drive exploration toward the unexplored set U_q.
   - **Unexplored set as auxiliary target:** U_q shrinks across stages; once U_q = U_{q−1} for both players the simulator's propose-mechanism terminates with the most recent strategy (Algorithm 1, line 32).
   - **Termination and correctness:** Lemma 23 bounds the number of stages by |S′|; Lemma 29 shows the good event E holds with probability ≥ 1−p via Hoeffding + union bound over all |S|²L² state-step/action combinations; Lemmas 30–32 then show π_f is ε-optimal in the expanded finite-horizon game.

The proof deliberately avoids estimating transition probabilities — a key reason it extends to the private/decentralized setting where such estimation would be infeasible.

## Results & Highlights

This is a theory paper; the "results" are theorems and sample-complexity bounds rather than empirical measurements. The concrete quantitative claims are:

- **Finite-horizon PAC sample complexity (Theorem 15):** The (LeTuReGa_Max, LeTuReGa_Min) pair is PAC-RL for finite-horizon reachability with sample complexity **O(|S|³·L⁷·|A|·log(|S|²L²/p)/ε³)** simulator procedure calls — polynomial in |S|, |A|, L, 1/p, 1/ε. The proof bounds: |S|L stages × L induction levels × |S|L earlier strategies × K = C·|A|·log(|S|²L²/p)/(ε_emp·ε_bai²) plays per (stage, level, strategy), multiplied by (L+1) procedure calls per play (Sample Complexity paragraph, p. 3:16).
- **ECD-bounded reachability sample complexity (Corollary 16):** Under the ECD assumption, the same algorithm pair is PAC-RL with sample complexity **O(|S|³·L⁷·|A|·log(|S|²L²/(pε²))/ε¹⁰)** — obtained by invoking the finite-horizon result with horizon H = 2(L+1)/ε and error tolerance ε/2 (Proof of Corollary 16, p. 3:10). This is the headline polynomial bound in |S|, |A|, L=ECD, 1/ε, 1/p.
- **High-probability guarantee (Lemma 29):** The "good event" E (concentration of empirical visit counts via Hoeffding, monotonic visit-count persistence, and BAI local ε_bai-optimality) holds with probability **≥ 1−p**. The ε-bai is set to ε/(2L) and ε_emp to ε/(8|S|L) (Definition 22), giving the per-state-step confidence p/(|S|²L²).
- **Sharpness of ECD definition (Example 7, p. 3:7–3:8):** On the 4-state TBSG of Figure 1, ECD1_G = ECD2_G = ECD3_G = ECD4_G = 1 and ECD5_G = 2, yet a finite horizon of **≥ 100** is required for Min's finite-horizon optimal action to coincide with the infinite-horizon optimal action (probability of action b's slow path is 0.001); the paper's Definition 8 gives **ECD_G = 1001**, the only one of the six candidates that yields a valid horizon bound. This is a concrete separation showing the ECD generalization is not a definitional formality.
- **Termination bound (Lemma 23):** The simulation terminates after **at most |S′| = |S|·L** stages, because each non-terminating stage strictly removes at least one state-step from U^Max_q ∪ U^Min_q and the union starts at size ≤ |S′|.

No empirical results reported: the paper provides no experiments on benchmarks, consistent with its formal-methods/learning-theory venue (CONCUR) and PAC-RL focus.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: genuinely high.** Combining decentralization + private information in TBSG reachability PAC learning is a real relaxation of [2, CAV 2019], and the failure of ECD1–ECD5 (Example 7) is a clean demonstration that the ECD generalization is non-trivial. The technique of using unexplored state-steps as auxiliary targets to drive exploration in a private-state setting — without estimating transition probabilities — is a real methodological contribution.
- **Rigor: strong.** Definitions are precise (simulator, learning algorithm, decoupled, PAC-RL, expanded game), the proof is decomposed into named lemmas with sharp quantitative statements (Lemma 30: V^{π_f}_{∅,U_f} ≤ 4|S′|·ε_emp; Lemma 31: ≤ L·ε_bai continuation error; Lemma 32: ε-optimal synthesis). Concentration via Hoeffding + union bound is appropriate.
- **Reproducibility: theoretical reproducibility is high** (full pseudocode in Algorithm 1, all constants in Definition 22), but **no implementation or experiments** are provided. The sample complexity has a leading |A|-constant C from the Median Elimination subroutine that is left implicit in the O-notation.
- **Significance: this opens a new research direction** (private/decentralized PAC-RL for games) and the authors explicitly state open problems in the conclusion: (i) concurrent stochastic games, (ii) sampling from arbitrary states instead of restarting from µ. The result is foundational rather than immediately deployable.
- **Red flags / honest concerns:**
  - **The ε¹⁰ dependence is severe.** Even for moderate ε = 0.1, ε¹⁰ = 10⁻¹⁰ — the constants are astronomically large. The L⁷ dependence on the horizon is also steep. These are typical for PAC-RL theory but render the bounds qualitatively, not quantitatively, useful; the paper does not discuss this.
  - **No baseline because there is no prior baseline.** The paper honestly notes this is the first positive result in the decentralized-private setting, so there is literally nothing to compare against quantitatively. The comparison to Daskalakis–Golowich–Zhang (COLT 2023, ref [8]) is qualitative only (concurrent discounted-sum games vs turn-based reachability; adversarial bandits vs best-arm identification) and the claim that "the complexity of our algorithm is more efficient than theirs" (Section 4.2) is asserted without a side-by-side complexity comparison.
  - **The simulator model is restrictive.** The PAC-RL definition (Definition 5) and the simulator (Definition 3) require both players to jointly restart from the initial distribution µ; the open-problem (ii) above acknowledges this is a real limitation. Adversarial learning remains impossible (argued in Section 1) — the "both players learn together" framing is forced rather than a design choice.
  - **ECD must be known a priori** as input L to the algorithm (Definition 9); the Estimation-of-ECD paragraph (Section 3, p. 3:9) defers model-dependent estimation to future work, so a practitioner must already know L to use the algorithm.
  - **A possibly-unfair reading of "first positive result":** the centralized, public-information version of this problem was already solved in [2] for the minimum-non-zero-probability assumption and in [20] for the MDP ECD. The genuinely new content is the decentralization + privacy + game-ECD combination, which is narrower than the abstract's emphasis might suggest.
  - **One missing ablation:** the paper does not isolate how much of the L⁷ and ε¹⁰ blowup comes from the ECD → finite-horizon reduction (which contributes H = 2(L+1)/ε to the horizon and ε/2 to the error, i.e., roughly a factor of (L+1)·(1/ε) per step) vs the LeTuReGa algorithm itself. A breakdown would clarify where future work should target improvements.

## Strengths

- Crisp, self-contained definitions of decentralized/private PAC-RL for TBSGs that future work can build on directly.
- The ECD generalization is supported by a memorable 4-state counterexample (Figure 1) showing why five natural alternatives fail — the kind of clarifying example that is rare in learning-theory papers.
- The algorithm avoids transition-probability estimation entirely, which is exactly why it extends to private-information settings where such estimation would be infeasible.
- Polynomial (not exponential) sample complexity in all parameters, including the ECD parameter, which is much better than the worst-case bound from the minimum non-zero probability (which is exponential in |S|).
- The reduction in Theorem 14 cleanly separates the "ECD is the right horizon bound" question from the "how to learn finite-horizon reachability" question, making each part independently useful.

## Limitations

- **Pure theory, no experiments.** No benchmarks, no synthetic games, no implementation — the sample-complexity constants are unusable as-is for any practical deployment.
- **Steep polynomial exponents** (ε¹⁰ in the ECD case, L⁷ in both cases) make the bounds qualitatively informative but quantitatively far from any practical regime.
- **ECD must be supplied externally** as input L; no data-driven estimation procedure is provided (acknowledged as future work).
- **Simulator must support reset from the initial distribution µ** jointly for both players; the harder generative-model setting where one can sample from arbitrary states is left open.
- **Two-player zero-sum turn-based only.** Concurrent games, n-player games, and quantitative objectives (mean payoff, discounted sum) are out of scope.
- **Adversarial learning is provably impossible** (Section 1), so the "both players cooperate to learn" assumption is necessary — and is a strong assumption in its own right that may not hold in genuine game-theoretic settings.
- **The comparison to Daskalakis–Golowich–Zhang (COLT 2023)** claims LeTuReGa is "more efficient" but provides no actual complexity comparison; this claim should be softened.

## Relevance

如果你对强化学习的 PAC 可学习性、形式化方法与多智能体博弈的交叉感兴趣，本文是这一方向 2026 年的标志性理论进展——它把 ECD 参数首次精心推广到博弈设定，并给出了去中心化、私有信息下首个多项式样本复杂度正面结果，可作为后续研究（concurrent games、任意状态采样、ECD 估计）的奠基性参考。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can the ECD parameter be **estimated online** from simulator access without breaking the polynomial sample-complexity bound, so that L does not need to be supplied a priori?
- Is the ε¹⁰ dependence in Corollary 16 intrinsic to the ECD → finite-horizon reduction, or can a tighter direct analysis (avoiding the 2(L+1)/ε horizon blow-up) recover ε³ or ε⁵?
- Can the framework extend to **concurrent stochastic games** with reachability (open problem stated by the authors), where the best-arm identification subroutine would need to handle simultaneous-action coupling?
- What is the **lower bound** on sample complexity for decentralized-private PAC-RL of TBSG reachability under ECD? Is the L⁷|A| scaling tight, or is there a matching lower bound analogous to Mannor–Tsitsiklis for the bandit sub-problem?
- Does the LeTuReGa approach extend to **safety and parity objectives** (the next levels of the Borel hierarchy), where the unexplored-state-step auxiliary-target trick may need non-trivial modification?
- Can the **simulator reset assumption** be weakened to a generative-model that samples from arbitrary (s, ℓ) pairs, and what is the resulting sample-complexity overhead?
