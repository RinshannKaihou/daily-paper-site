> **一句话总结：** 作者用 mass-mean 线性探针在 25 个预训练语言模型（14M–14B 参数）上证明：合语法/不合语法句子在表示空间中沿单一线性方向稳定分离，该方向独立于字符串概率与其他可接受性因素，并能跨语法现象、跨语言泛化（最强模型 Qwen3-14B 在 BLiMP 上探针成功率达 94.0%）。

> **TL;DR:** Moving beyond probability-based measures, the authors apply low-complexity mass-mean linear probing to hidden-state representations of 25 pretrained NLMs (GPT2, Llama, OLMo, Pythia, Qwen3; 14M–14B params). Grammaticality is encoded as a robust, single linear direction: probe success on BLiMP ranges from 13.4% (Pythia-14m) to 94.0% (Qwen3-14B), model size predicts 92.8% of the variance in success (R=0.928), and the encoding survives deconfounding for string probability (largest Counter-condition accuracy drop only 3.19 pp on OLMo-2-0425-7B) and for extra-grammatical acceptability via boundary tilting. Cross-phenomenon generalization is broad (gram→gram success 55.9% vs gram→extra only 11.2%), and cross-linguistic transfer reaches up to 61.4% mean accuracy for the five strongest models on German/French/Russian/Hebrew/Mandarin benchmarks vs 66.6% for within-language English.

## 核心贡献

- 超越概率方法，直接用内部表示研究合语法性：在 25 个预训练 NLM（GPT2、Llama、OLMo、Pythia、Qwen3，14M–14B 参数）上证明存在一条单一、稳定的线性方向，将合语法与不合语法句子分开。
- 提出"去混淆"实验范式：用 Counter/Favour 概率重采样（实验 3）、SCaMP plausible/implausible 对比（实验 4.2）以及 boundary-tilting 回归（实验 4.3）逐一剥离字符串概率、句子长度、词汇重叠、可接受性、可信度等共线因素，证明合语法性信号并非这些因素的代理。
- 量化跨现象与跨语言泛化：探针在同一语法现象内泛化只比跨现象高 6.64–14.0 个百分点；最强 5 个模型在德/法/俄/希伯来/汉 5 种语言上表现出非平凡的合语法性编码，且 Llama 系列还呈现跨语言共享。
- 引入 MP_Rep 指标（基于探针的最小对比较），与 MP_Prob（基于概率）形成互补：MP_Rep 在小模型上落后 MP_Prob 13.4 个百分点，但在大模型上反超 6.89 个百分点（R=0.791, p<0.001），说明概率法会低估大模型内部的语法知识。
- 给出 representational-principle 级别的解释与未来路线图：将探针增量敏感性（Critical 区域已可检测）与 Eisape et al. 2022 的解析状态、Hewitt & Manning 2019 的句法探针连接，并为"统计系统是否真正拥有合语法性"这一认知科学争论提供新证据。

## Key Contributions

- First systematic demonstration that grammaticality corresponds to a single low-complexity linear direction (mass-mean difference vector θ = μ_gram − μ_ungram) in the hidden-state space of 25 pretrained NLMs spanning 14M–14B parameters, with held-out probe success on BLiMP scaling from 13.4% (Pythia-14m) to 94.0% (Qwen3-14B).
- A battery of deconfounding manipulations that goes well beyond prior probing work (Wang et al. 2026): probability-stress conditions (Counter/Favour), SCaMP plausible vs implausible contrast, and a novel boundary-tilting procedure that subtracts the projection of θ onto an extra-grammatical direction ϕ, showing grammaticality survives removal of acceptability signals.
- Quantification of cross-phenomenon and cross-linguistic generalization: removing within-phenomenon probes drops success only 4.06 pp (Critical) / 2.20 pp (Final); within-language English accuracy of 66.6% drops only to 61.4% mean in cross-linguistic transfer for the five strongest models on German, French, Russian, Hebrew, Mandarin.
- A new comparison metric MP_Rep (probe-based minimal-pair score) that exposes systematic divergence from probability-based MP_Prob: MP_Rep underperforms by 13.4 pp in small models but exceeds by 6.89 pp in large models (R=0.791 with log10 params, p<0.001), arguing probability and representation are complementary, not interchangeable.
- Strong scaling-law claim: model size (log10 params) accounts for 92.8% of the variance in probe success rate, 95.0% of global accuracy, and 94.2% of accuracy conditioned on success, with 51.5% (Critical) / 59.0% (Final) of variance in regional probe success explained by scale alone.

## Methodology

The core tool is **mass-mean probing** (Marks & Tegmark, 2024): given sentence representations x ∈ ℝ^d with labels y ∈ {0,1}, compute class means μ_0, μ_1 and the difference vector θ = μ_1 − μ_0; classify by σ(θ·x) > 0.5. Probes are fitted on 1,600 sentences and reported over 100 random subsampling runs. Representations are mean-centered per token to strip away lexical identity confounds.

Three regions of interest are extracted from each sentence when annotations are available (40 of 67 BLiMP subsets): **Pre-critical** (token before divergence, a control that should show no signal), **Critical** (first span where grammaticality diverges), and **Final** (final punctuation token). Mean-centering uses per-token means.

**Probe success** is the central inferential measure: a configuration counts as successful only if its accuracy distribution significantly exceeds a label-shuffled null probe (Welch's t-test, Holm–Bonferroni adjusted) and the mean is ≥5 pp above the null (the 5% threshold is calibrated against observed Pre-critical false positives of [-2.62%, 1.65%]).

**Models (25)**: GPT2 (Small/Medium/Large/XL), Llama-3.2-1B/3B, Llama-3.1-8B, OLMo-1B/7B/2-1B/2-7B/2-13B, Pythia (14M–12B), Qwen3-0.6B/1.7B/4B/14B. All models pass the precondition of above-chance BLiMP minimal-pair probability accuracy.

**Datasets**: BLiMP (67 Eng. datasets), SCaMP (134, incl. plausible/implausible variants), DGL, LI-Adger (fitting set for Exp. 1, drawn from a syntax textbook), RUBLiMP (Russian), ZhoBLiMP (Mandarin), CLAMS (French/German/Hebrew/Russian); plus extra-grammatical benchmarks Shades, Drive, Truth for deconfounding.

Five experiments answer three RQs:
1. **Detection (Exp. 1)**: fit on LI-Adger, evaluate on all BLiMP; compare Acc_Rep, MP_Rep, MP_Prob.
2. **Scope (Exp. 2)**: per-dataset probes fit on each of 40 BLiMP subsets, evaluated cross-dataset at three regions; regression on length/word-overlap/representation similarity.
3. **Probability deconfounding (Exp. 3)**: Counter condition (high-prob ungrammatical vs low-prob grammatical) vs Favour vs Baseline on BLiMP and SCaMP Final representations.
4. **Acceptability deconfounding (Exp. 4)**: extra-grammatical cross-generalization (4.1), SCaMP plausible/implausible comparison (4.2), boundary-tilting that subtracts projection of θ onto an acceptability direction ϕ (4.3).
5. **Cross-linguistic (Exp. 5)**: 5 strongest models tested in within-language and cross-language conditions across 6 non-English benchmarks.

## Results & Highlights

- **Probe success scales steeply with model size on BLiMP**: range 13.4% (Pythia-14m) → 94.0% (Qwen3-14B); Pearson R with log10(params) = **0.928** for success rate, **0.950** for global accuracy, **0.942** for accuracy conditioned on success (Fig. 2). Aside from Pythia-14m, every model succeeds on ≥27 of 67 BLiMP datasets.
- **Probe vs probability measures flip with scale**: the MP_Rep − MP_Prob accuracy gap ranges from −13.4 pp (small models lag) to **+6.89 pp (large models lead)**, correlating R=0.791 (p<0.001) with log10(params). Aggregate probability-based detection still wins, but for specific phenomena (e.g., inchoative, npi_present_1/2) >75% of models show MP_Rep advantage.
- **Incremental sensitivity**: in the Final region, success ranges 7.81% (Pythia-14m) to 94.38% (Llama-3.1-8B); model size explains **51.5% of Critical-region variance and 59.0% of Final-region variance**. Pre-critical probes show no significant signal, confirming the manipulation works as a control.
- **Phenomenon-general encoding**: removing within-phenomenon probes drops success only **4.06 pp (Critical, range [1.64%, 5.28%])** and **2.20 pp (Final, range [-0.22%, 5.68%])**; within-phenomenon accuracy advantage is 14.0 pp (Critical) and 6.64 pp (Final). Regressing out length, lexical overlap, and representational similarity leaves LMEM intercept estimates still well above null (Fig. 6B).
- **Probability deconfounding (Exp. 3)**: the largest Counter-condition accuracy drop across all 25 models is only **3.19 pp (OLMo-2-0425-7B)**; Favour range [−2.61%, +1.53%], Counter range [−3.19%, +6.12%]. Most models are at-ceiling on p(success | Original = success) for Counter, supporting that probes track grammaticality, not probability.
- **Acceptability deconfounding (Exp. 4)**: gram→extra-grammatical generalization is only **11.2%** vs gram→gram 55.9%; SCaMP plausible-vs-implausible mean accuracy drop is **1.06 pp** (range [−0.57%, 4.34%]) with conditional success 90.2% [80.3%, 95.8%]; boundary-tilting with Tilt-all/Tilt-one preserves accuracy and success while the Control condition (tilting with other grammatical directions) collapses to chance.
- **Cross-linguistic (Exp. 5)**: five strongest models (Llama-3.2-3B, Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen3-4B, OLMo-7B, OLMo-2-7B) show above-chance grammaticality probes on CLAMS-de/fr/ru/he, RuBLiMP, and ZhoBLiMP; within-language English mean accuracy **66.6% [65.0%, 68.8%]** vs cross-linguistic **61.4% [59.9%, 62.9%]**, with Llama models showing only marginal cross-linguistic success drop while OLMo-7B and Qwen3-4B drop sharply.

## Authors & Credibility

Jane Li is in the Department of Cognitive Science at Johns Hopkins University; Najoung Kim is in the Department of Linguistics at Boston University and has a strong publication record on syntactic probing and grammaticality in LMs. The work is supported by an SSHRC Doctoral Fellowship (752-2024-0291) and uses Boston University's Shared Computing Cluster. Code, analyses, and data are released at https://github.com/jane-lisy/linear-gram, satisfying a key reproducibility requirement. The paper engages seriously with opposing theoretical positions (Chomsky 1957; Katzir 2023; Dentella et al. 2023; Bender et al. 2021) and cites the only directly comparable probe study (Wang et al. 2026), explicitly noting where their own probing accuracies are lower than Wang et al.'s and explaining why (low-complexity probe, no layer tuning).

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: Strongest where it counts. Wang et al. (2026) already showed grammaticality is linearly decodable from corpus vs perturbed sentences; this paper's contribution is the systematic deconfounding battery (Counter/Favour, SCaMP plausible/implausible, boundary tilting) and the cross-phenomenon/cross-linguistic generalization tests. The boundary-tilting manipulation (subtracting the projection of θ onto an extra-grammatical direction ϕ) is genuinely new and a useful tool for the community.
- **Rigor**: Methodologically careful. Use of Pre-critical region as a within-sentence control, Welch's t-test with Holm–Bonferroni correction, a 5% noise floor calibrated against observed Pre-critical false positives, and 100 random subsampling runs per configuration all inspire confidence. The linear mixed-effects model (accuracy ~ Length_KL + Word_Overlap + Rep_Similarity + (1|model)) is the right tool for the cross-dataset analysis.
- **Reproducibility**: High. Twenty-five publicly available base models (no proprietary APIs), all benchmarks are public, and the probe itself is a closed-form difference-of-means — anyone with a single GPU could reproduce most figures. The authors release code and data.
- **Significance**: For the cognitive-science debate the authors target, the results meaningfully shift the burden of proof: skeptics can no longer claim that probability-based evidence is the only evidence. For NLP practitioners, the MP_Rep complement to MP_Prob and the boundary-tilting procedure are practical tools.
- **Red flags / weaknesses**:
  - **Probe success is the headline metric, but it is a derived binary threshold (≥5 pp above null) rather than a calibrated accuracy**; this risks overstating the "94.0%" figure for Qwen3-14B, which is a *proportion of BLiMP datasets passing the threshold*, not mean accuracy. A reader could confuse the two.
  - **Layer selection is explicitly not tuned** (§3.3), and the authors admit this depresses their accuracies relative to Wang et al. (2026). Since they then compare MP_Rep against MP_Prob and against Wang et al., the comparison is somewhat unfair to the probe side and the "MP_Rep overtakes MP_Prob in large models" claim would be strengthened by a layer-tuned variant.
  - **The boundary-tilting evaluation only uses the Final region and only tests BLiMP/SCaMP**; whether the clean Tilt vs Control separation holds at the Critical region or on non-English benchmarks is not reported.
  - **The Counter condition's "largest drop 3.19 pp" is averaged across datasets and models**; the per-phenomenon breakdown is not shown, so phenomena where probability does drive the probe could be masked.
  - **Cross-linguistic Experiment 5 uses only 5 models and collapses phenomena within a language** (acknowledged in §7.2), limiting the strength of the language-generality claim — particularly for Mandarin (ZhoBLiMP) where model coverage and tokenization differ substantially from English.
  - **No causal intervention**: the probes are correlational. The authors openly flag this and suggest future steering/causal work, but a reader should not interpret the results as proof that the θ direction *is* the model's grammaticality computation.
  - **Deconfounding is partial**: boundary tilting removes linear overlap with *named* extra-grammatical factors (Truth, Shades, Drive); unknown collinear factors (e.g., discourse coherence, register) are not controlled.

## Strengths

- Clean, simple probe (mass-mean) that is fully interpretable and easy to reproduce — the difference vector θ is a single direction, not a black-box classifier.
- A principled, multi-pronged deconfounding strategy: probability stress (Counter/Favour), plausibility stress (SCaMP plausible/implausible), and a novel boundary-tilting regression that lets the authors *factor out* the acceptability signal and show grammaticality survives.
- Genuinely broad model coverage (25 models across 5 families, 14M–14B params) and broad linguistic coverage (English, Russian, Mandarin, French, German, Hebrew), making the scaling claims credible.
- Honest about limitations: explicitly states their accuracies are lower than Wang et al. (2026) and why; explicitly acknowledges that cross-linguistic generalization is partial and model-dependent; flags the correlational nature of the results.
- Useful methodological byproducts (MP_Rep metric, boundary-tilting procedure) that other researchers can adopt directly.

## Limitations

- Probe success is a thresholded binary metric that can be misread as accuracy; raw accuracy numbers are reported but less prominent.
- No layer selection tuning; comparisons against probability-based measures and against Wang et al. (2026) are therefore conservative for the probe.
- Boundary-tilting and Counter/Favour experiments are confined to the Final region and to English BLiMP/SCaMP, leaving Critical-region and multilingual deconfounding open.
- Cross-linguistic claims rest on only 5 models and aggregate phenomena within a language.
- Purely correlational — no causal intervention (e.g., activation patching, steering) is performed to verify that the θ direction functionally contributes to grammaticality computation.
- Deconfounding covers only a named set of extra-grammatical factors; other collinear properties (register, discourse coherence, frequency quartile interactions) are not controlled.
- Chat/instruction-tuned models are deliberately excluded, so the findings apply only to base pretrained NLMs.

## Relevance

这篇工作直接触及"语言模型是否真正拥有语法知识"这一核心争论，并提供了独立于概率的表示层面证据，对关心 LLM 可解释性、probing 方法论、以及统计学习与认知科学关系的读者高度相关；其 boundary-tilting 去混淆技术与 MP_Rep 指标也可直接迁移到其他"概念是否被线性编码"的研究（如真实性、合理性、情感）。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the grammaticality direction θ survive a *causal* intervention — e.g., does patching or scaling θ along a sentence flip the model's downstream grammaticality judgments or generation?
- How does the θ direction interact with layer depth? A layer-sweeping analysis would clarify whether grammaticality emerges at a specific depth and whether tuning layer selection closes the gap with Wang et al. (2026) and with MP_Prob.
- Can training-data attribution (feasible for Pythia/OLMo with public data) identify which pretraining utterances give rise to the grammaticality direction, testing the Ravfogel et al. (2025) co-occurrence hypothesis?
- Does the cross-linguistic θ generalize beyond the 5 strongest models, and does a *single shared* multilingual direction exist, or do models maintain language-specific sub-directions that merely correlate?
- How do the results extend to nonce-word ("jabberwocky") sentences that strip lexical semantics, and to chat/instruction-tuned models where metalinguistic information may have contaminated pretraining?
- The boundary-tilting procedure removes linear overlap; could a nonlinear deconfounding (e.g., iterative residualization, contrastive activation addition) reveal additional shared structure between grammaticality and acceptability?
