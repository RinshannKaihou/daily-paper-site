> **一句话总结：** 本文将因果推断中标准的"协变量均衡"诊断（MASD）正式扩展到时间相关的离线强化学习设定，并在 MIMIC-III 脓毒症数据集上证明：现有脓毒症治疗离线 RL 研究在 18 步、25 动作的高维 MDP 下基本无法通过协变量均衡检验（仅在最初约 2 个时步成立），且常用的重要性采样方差抑制手段（clipping、Hajek）在高方差情形下会系统性偏置为"看起来已均衡"，因此现有脓毒症离线 RL 的统计稳健性无法被证立。

> **TL;DR:** The paper formalises covariate-balance diagnostics (conditional vs. weighted MASD) and extends the weighted version to the time-dependent setting (MASD_Weight-t), then applies them to the standard Komorowski-et-al.-2018 / MIMIC-III sepsis offline-RL benchmark with an 18-step horizon and 25-action space. Using custom xgboost propensity models (best test Macro-F1 of 0.481 on the full sepsis action space and 0.657 on the fluid-only space), it shows covariate balance fails the standard 0.25 MASD threshold for almost the whole trajectory (holding only for the first ~2 timesteps on sepsis / ~4 on sepsis-fluid), and proves that the clipped and Hajek importance-sampling estimators collapse to the unweighted MASD at large t, so they cannot validly certify balance. The negative result implies existing sepsis offline-RL treatment policies cannot be deemed statistically robust under current diagnostics.

## 核心贡献

- **统一并形式化协变量均衡的定义**：区分并形式化推导了"条件协变量均衡"（conditional covariate balance, MASD_Cond, 由 Theorem 1 / Corollary 1 推出）与"加权协变量均衡"（weighted covariate balance, MASD_Weight, 由 change-of-measure 推导，Corollary 2），并明确指出两者都是因果有效的必要非充分条件。
- **将加权协变量均衡扩展到时间相关 MDP**：在 MDP 密度分解的基础上推导出 MASD_Weight-t(k, a'_{0:t}, a''_{0:t})，并给出其 clipped (MASD clipped-t) 与 Hajek (MASD_Hajek-t) 变体，填补了离线 RL 长时序诊断的空白。
- **指出标准方差抑制方法的诊断失效**：严格证明当 t 足够大时，MASD_clipped-t → MASD_UnWeight-t 且 MASD_Hajek-t → MASD_UnWeight-t，说明 clipping 与 Hajek 在高方差情形下会偏向"显示协变量已均衡"，因此不能用作协变量均衡的有效诊断。
- **对脓毒症离线 RL 进行实证诊断**：在 MIMIC-III 脓毒症数据集（沿用 Komorowski 2018 / Subramanian & Killian 2023 预处理）上自建 xgboost 倾向分数模型，发现协变量均衡仅在最初约 2 个时步（full action space）/ 4 个时步（fluid-only）成立；并发现"均衡似乎随时长改善"是 vanilla IS 浮点溢出导致的样本选择偏差和 clipped/Hajek 偏置坍塌的双重假象。
- **给出可行的缓解方向**：限制动作空间（仅 fluid，5 维）与截断轨迹长度（horizon 8 对 sepsis、horizon 5 对 sepsis-fluid）能在部分场景改善协变量均衡，提出联合建模 trajectory 密度（而非自回归 p(A_t|S_t,A_{t-1})）以及 pessimistic PAC 框架作为后续方向。

## Key Contributions

- Consolidates and formalises two distinct notions of covariate balance — conditional (MASD_Cond, from Rosenbaum–Rubin Theorem 3) and weighted (MASD_Weight, from the change-of-measure argument of equations 8–14) — and explicitly states both are necessary but not sufficient for valid causal inference.
- Derives the time-dependent weighted balance metric MASD_Weight-t(k, a'_{0:t}, a''_{0:t}) from the MDP factorisation p(A_{0:t}|S_{0:t}) = ∏ p(A_t|S_t), together with its clipped and Hajek variants, the first formal extension of propensity-based balance to long-horizon offline RL.
- Proves (equations 23 and 24) that for sufficiently large t the clipped and Hajek importance-sampling estimators both degenerate to the unweighted MASD, so they systematically give a false appearance of balance in the high-variance regime where the diagnostic matters most.
- Empirically diagnoses the canonical MIMIC-III sepsis offline-RL setup (Komorowski 2018 pipeline, max 18-step horizon, 25-action space): balance fails the conventional 0.25 MASD threshold across nearly the whole trajectory, and the apparent improvement at longer horizons is shown to be a joint artefact of (i) selection bias from infinite-variance overflow in the vanilla estimator and (ii) estimator collapse under clipping/Hajek.
- Provides concrete mitigations (restricting the action space to fluid-only; truncating trajectories to horizons 8/5) and proposes joint trajectory-density modelling and pessimistic PAC-style estimators as future directions.

## Methodology

The paper combines a theoretical derivation (Section 3) with an empirical diagnostic study (Section 4).

**Formal derivations (Section 3).** Starting from the contextual-bandit density p_π(τ)=p_S(s)p_π(a|s)p_R(r|a,s), the authors re-derive two balance notions used loosely in the literature: conditional covariate balance (MASD_Cond, equation 16) implied directly by Theorem 1 of Rosenbaum & Rubin (1983), and weighted covariate balance (MASD_Weight, equation 19) obtained via a change-of-measure. Both are stated as necessary-but-not-sufficient conditions. They then move to the MDP density p_π(τ) = p(s_0)∏ p_π(a_t|s_t) p_T(s_{t+1}|a_t,s_t) p_R(r_{t+1}|a_t,s_t) and derive the time-dependent MASD_Weight-t(k, a'_{0:t}, a''_{0:t}) (equation 21), plus its clipped (max-product-τ^{-1}) and Hajek (self-normalised) variants. A deliberate methodological choice is the Welsh (unequal-variance) denominator √(σ²_{a'_{0:t}} + σ²_{a''_{0:t}}) rather than the Wald-style pooled denominator used in prior heuristics — empirically justified by Figure 1/2 where the variance ratio diverges by the 4th/5th timestep.

**Diagnostic criteria.** Two conditions are used: (a) MASD_Weight-t(k,t) < 0.25 for all timesteps (Cannas & Arpino 2019 heuristic), and/or (b) MASD-DiffWeight-t(k,t) > 0 — a new derived quantity measuring the average improvement in balance achieved by the propensity model relative to the unweighted MASD. To avoid the optimism of in-sample balance, all results are reported on both a training and a held-out testing split.

**Empirical setup.** The study reuses the Komorowski et al. (2018) MIMIC-III sepsis preprocessing (via the Subramanian & Killian 2023 microsoft/mimic_sepsis code), giving continuous state features, 4-hour bucketing (max 18-step horizon), and a 5×5 discretised IV-fluid × vasopressor action space (25 actions). Two derived datasets are used: the full "sepsis" (25 actions) and "sepsis-fluid" (5 actions, fluids only). Because prior continuous-state sepsis studies (Raghu, Komorowski & Singh 2018; Huang et al. 2022; Kaushik et al. 2022) never released propensity models, the authors train three custom xgboost variants per dataset: xgboost_1 (defaults), xgboost_1_w (reciprocal-frequency weighted objective), and xgboost_1_w_optuna (Optuna-tuned + weighted). The best model on a held-out validation split is selected per scenario (Table 1). Experiments use two seeds controlling the data split and propensity training.

**Bias proofs (Section 4.2.3).** The authors give closed-form arguments that for a sufficiently large horizon t, the propensity product ∏ p(a_{i,t'}|s_{i,t'}) saturates the clip threshold ζ for all trajectories, so MASD_clipped-t(k, a'_{0:t}, a''_{0:t}) → MASD_UnWeight-t (equation 23); an analogous argument for the Hajek estimator, driven by exponential effective-sample-size collapse, gives MASD_Hajek-t → MASD_UnWeight-t (equation 24). This is the analytical heart of the paper's negative claim about standard OPE variance reduction.

## Results & Highlights

- **Propensity model accuracy (Table 1, MIMIC-III sepsis).** Best test-set Macro-F1 is **0.481** for the full sepsis action space (xgboost_1_w_optuna: 0.622 train / 0.457 val / 0.481 test) and **0.657** for the sepsis-fluid action space (xgboost_1_w_optuna: 0.711 / 0.662 / 0.657), versus 0.455 val / 0.467 test and 0.657 val / 0.656 test for the unweighted xgboost_1 baseline — a modest ~+0.01–0.02 absolute test-F1 gain from the tuning/weighting, indicating the behaviour policy is only moderately learnable.
- **Covariate balance on the full sepsis dataset (vanilla estimator).** The median MASD_Weight-t(k,t) across features exceeds the conventional **0.25** threshold of Cannas & Arpino (2019) for nearly the entire trajectory; the propensity model only improves balance over the unweighted baseline (MASD-DiffWeight-t > 0) for the **first 3 timesteps on the training set and first 2 on the testing set** (Figures 10, 19).
- **Selection-bias artefact exposed.** For the vanilla estimator, MASD appears to drift below 1 with horizon only because the variance denominator overflows to infinity for an increasing fraction of action-trajectory pairs (Figure 5); excluding these inflates the apparent balance. The unnormalised numerator actually diverges with horizon (Figure 4), confirming covariate balance degrades rather than improves.
- **Clipped/Hajek estimators give a false positive.** Both appear to certify balance at long horizons (Figures 6–7, 11–12), but the bias analysis shows MASD_clipped-t and MASD_Hajek-t collapse to MASD_UnWeight-t for large t — i.e., they certify "no change from unweighted," not "balance holds."
- **Effective-sample-size collapse.** On the full sepsis dataset the minimum action representation is only **3%** of total observations (seed 0 training set); combined with the 25^t growth in possible trajectories this drives the IS variance explosion (Figures 16–17).
- **Welsh denominator justified.** The median (across features) ratio of the two candidate MASD denominators diverges by the **4th timestep (full action space)** and the **5th timestep (fluid action space)** (Figures 1–2), supporting the unequal-variance denominator over the pooled-variance denominator assumed by the 0.25/0.10 heuristics.
- **Action-space reduction helps modestly (sepsis-fluid).** Restricting to fluids extends the window of positive MASD-DiffWeight-t from the first 2 timesteps to the first **4 timesteps on the testing set**, but balance still degrades from timestep 4 onward (Figure 19).
- **Trajectory truncation helps non-uniformly (Table 2).** Truncating to horizon 8 on the sepsis dataset yields **3** timesteps with positive median MASD-DiffWeight-t (both train and test) versus 2–3 at the full horizon; on sepsis-fluid, truncating to horizon 5 yields **5** positive timesteps (train and test) versus 4–5 at full horizon. The action-space dimensionality is preserved (24–25 actions) so the gain is attributable to truncation rather than action-space collapse.
- **Sample-size confound for train-vs-test gap.** Sub-sampling the training set to the testing-set size (Figure 24) recovers the same number of balanced timesteps as on the test set, suggesting the train/test degradation is driven by sample size rather than in-sample optimism — implying training-set balance may be usable in practice.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty.** Genuine and focused. The formal consolidation of conditional vs. weighted MASD (Section 3.2) and the time-dependent extension MASD_Weight-t (Section 3.3) fills a real gap — the OPE community standardly uses outcome-based diagnostics (Fu et al. 2021; Voloshin et al. 2019) while the causal-inference community standardly uses propensity-based balance, and the bridge has not been formalised for long horizons. The analytical result that clipping/Hajek collapse to the unweighted MASD (equations 23–24) is the strongest and most citable contribution.
- **Rigour.** High on the theoretical side: corollaries are stated and proved, the clipped/Hajek bias is derived in closed form, and the Welsh-vs-Wald denominator choice is empirically justified (Figures 1–2). However, the empirical study is underpowered: only **two seeds** are used, the propensity models are acknowledged to be custom-built (none of the prior continuous-state sepsis papers released one), and best test Macro-F1 on the full action space is only **0.481**, leaving open whether balance failure reflects confounding or a weakly-specified behaviour-policy model.
- **Reproducibility.** Mixed. The MIMIC-III preprocessing pipeline is the public microsoft/mimic_sepsis repo; the covariate-balance metric definitions are given in full equations; but no code repository or random-seed protocol is referenced in the main text, propensity-model hyperparameters are deferred to an appendix section that is referenced as "??" (broken cross-references throughout), and the handling of floating-point overflow in the vanilla IS estimator is acknowledged as "flawed" imputation. A reader could re-implement the diagnostics but reproducing the exact figures would require re-tuning.
- **Significance.** The negative result is important for the healthcare-RL community: it provides a concrete, citable reason to distrust the standard 18-step / 25-action sepsis-RL benchmark's policy claims, and it invalidates a class of variance-reduction tricks for balance diagnostics. The pragmatic mitigations (smaller action space, shorter horizon) are immediately actionable.
- **Red flags.** (1) The interaction between reduced action space and improved propensity-model accuracy is explicitly acknowledged as confounded ("this improvement in performance is a confounder for understanding the impact of the reduced action space", Section 4.2.4) but not deconfounded. (2) The 0.25 / 0.10 thresholds are heuristic and were derived assuming a pooled-variance denominator the paper itself argues is wrong, so re-using them as a pass/fail cut-off is internally inconsistent. (3) Floating-point overflow handling in the vanilla estimator is admitted to be misleading and was not rigorously addressed. (4) Several internal cross-references render as "??" (e.g., "appendix section ??"), indicating an incomplete draft. (5) The "balance improves after truncation" claim relies on a count-of-positive-timesteps metric over two seeds and is acknowledged as a possible statistical anomaly.

## Strengths

- Mathematically clean formalisation of two distinct balance notions and their time-dependent extension; the closed-form proofs that clipped and Hajek estimators degenerate to the unweighted MASD are the paper's centrepiece.
- Intellectually honest about its own negative result: it explicitly states it "cannot be determined whether this is a result of confounding/model miss-specification being present or the inappropriateness of the tests for long time horizons."
- Uses a clinically canonical benchmark (Komorowski 2018 / MIMIC-III sepsis) and a public preprocessing pipeline, maximising relevance to the healthcare-RL community.
- Reports both training and held-out testing results, and explicitly tests the in-sample-optimism concern (Section 5.1, Figure 24).
- Provides actionable, low-cost mitigations (restrict action space, truncate horizon) rather than only a negative verdict.

## Limitations

- Empirical scale is small: two seeds, single dataset (MIMIC-III sepsis), one clinical domain. Generalisation to other offline-RL healthcare benchmarks (cancer, HIV, ventilation, glycaemic control) is unverified.
- The propensity models are author-built and only moderately accurate (test Macro-F1 0.481 on the full action space), so the balance failure could in part reflect model mis-specification rather than fundamental unidentifiability — a confound the paper flags but does not resolve.
- The standard 0.25 / 0.10 MASD thresholds are heuristic and were derived under a denominator the paper rejects; the paper reuses them anyway, which is internally inconsistent.
- Floating-point overflow in the vanilla IS variance computation is handled by imputation, acknowledged as flawed; no robust-arithmeric or log-domain alternative is explored.
- No released code or reproducibility appendix in the main text; multiple broken cross-references ("appendix section ??") suggest a draft state.
- The truncation result is presented as a count of positive-timesteps over two seeds with no confidence intervals, so the "truncation helps" claim is statistically fragile.

## Relevance

对研究兴趣（离线强化学习 / OPE 在医疗决策中的应用）高度相关：本文直接针对脓毒症离线 RL 这一标志性基准，从因果推断角度指出其协变量均衡诊断基本失效，并证明 clipping/Hajek 等常用方差抑制手段在长时序下不能作为均衡诊断——任何做医疗离线 RL 或 OPE 评估的工作都应把这一负面结论纳入稳健性检查清单。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Would a joint trajectory-density propensity model p(A_{0:H-1}|S_{0:H-1}, A_{0:H-2}) (as hinted in Section 5.2) actually extend the balanced-timestep window beyond 2–4 on the sepsis benchmark, and at what cost in early-trajectory balance?
- Can the pessimistic PAC-style clipped IS estimators of Sakhi et al. (2024) be adapted to give a calibrated, rather than biased-toward-balance, covariate-balance diagnostic with a formal accuracy/variance trade-off?
- How much of the observed balance failure is attributable to behaviour-policy mis-specification vs. genuine non-ignorable confounding? A simulation study with a known behaviour policy and known hidden confounders would disentangle the two.
- Does the clipped/Hajek degeneracy result (equations 23–24) extend to doubly-robust OPE estimators, which are now the de-facto standard in healthcare OPE?
- Is the "train-set balance is usable because the test/train gap is a sample-size effect" conjecture (Section 5.1) borne out on larger healthcare RL cohorts (e.g., eICU, MIMIC-IV), where sample sizes differ by an order of magnitude?
