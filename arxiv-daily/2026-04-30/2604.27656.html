一句话总结： 本文发现，在持续学习中，表征的有效维度是决定网络模块化结构是否起作用的关键变量——低维度 regime 下模块化架构能自适应地形成梯度化的子空间对齐几何，而高维度 regime 下架构选择几乎无关紧要。
TL;DR: This paper shows that representational dimensionality is the key variable controlling when modular network architecture matters in continual learning. In low-dimensional regimes, modular networks produce graded subspace alignment (overlap for similar tasks, orthogonalization for dissimilar ones), while in high-dimensional regimes, architecture choice has minimal impact.

核心贡献

  • 揭示了表征维度（effective dimensionality）是决定模块化架构在持续学习中是否有效的关键控制变量，而非之前研究假设的架构本身始终重要。
  • 系统性地研究了网络架构、任务相似度与初始化规模三个因素的交互效应，构建了一个完整的实验框架来理解稳定性-可塑性权衡。
  • 在低维度 regime 下，模块化网络展现出"梯度化几何"（graded geometry）：相似任务部分重叠、中等差异任务部分正交化、差异大的任务强分离——这是单模块网络所不具备的。
  • 提出了"自适应几何"（adaptive geometry）作为持续学习系统设计的核心原则。

Key Contributions

  • Identify representational dimensionality as the key organizing variable governing when structural separation becomes functionally relevant in continual learning.
  • Demonstrate a three-way interaction between network architecture (modular vs. single), task similarity (low/medium/high), and weight initialization scale (controlling effective dimensionality) in a sequential task paradigm.
  • Reveal graded geometry in modular networks under low-dimensional regimes: task-specific subspaces align proportionally to task similarity, a pattern absent in monolithic baselines.
  • Argue for adaptive geometry as a central design principle for continual learning systems.

Methodology

  • Task paradigm: A sequential learning setup inspired by transfer-interference studies from cognitive neuroscience, where a network learns Task A then Task B.
  • Architecture comparison: A task-partitioned modular recurrent network (separate modules per task) versus a single-module baseline (standard RNN).
  • Independent variables: Task similarity is varied at three levels (low, medium, high). Weight initialization scale is varied to induce different learning regimes.
  • Representational analysis: The effective dimensionality of learned representations is measured empirically to characterize the learning regime. Representational Similarity Analysis (RSA) and subspace alignment metrics are used to quantify how task-specific representations relate.
  • Key manipulation: Weight initialization scale acts as a knob controlling whether representations end up in high-dimensional (lazy) or low-dimensional (rich) regimes, which in turn determines the impact of architectural modularity.

Results & Highlights

  • In high-dimensional regimes, both modular and single-module networks perform comparably — representations have enough capacity to accommodate multiple tasks without strong interference.
  • In low-dimensional (rich) regimes, architectural separation becomes decisive: modular networks significantly outperform monolithic ones.
  • Modular networks exhibit graded subspace alignment: high overlap for similar tasks, partial orthogonalization for moderately dissimilar tasks, and strong separation for dissimilar tasks.
  • Single-module networks lack this graded geometry and show either excessive interference or catastrophic forgetting regardless of task similarity.
  • The effective dimensionality of representations serves as a better predictor of architectural impact than the initialization scale alone.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty (Moderate-High): The finding that dimensionality is the gating variable for when modularity matters is a genuinely useful reframing. Prior work assumed modularity is beneficial per se; this paper shows it is conditional.
  • Rigor (Moderate): The systematic variation of three factors is well-designed, but the abstract-only summary limits assessment of statistical rigor, number of seeds, and significance testing.
  • Reproducibility (Unclear without full paper): No mention of code release. The experimental setup (task definitions, network sizes, training details) needs the full paper to evaluate.
  • Significance (Moderate-High): The result has clear implications for continual learning system design — it suggests that investing in modular architectures is only worthwhile when representations are constrained in dimensionality, which is often the case in resource-limited settings.
  • Red flags: The claims about "graded geometry" and "adaptive geometry as a central principle" are somewhat strong for what appears to be a controlled synthetic experiment. Generalization to large-scale deep learning (e.g., LLM fine-tuning) remains to be demonstrated.

Strengths

  • Clean, systematic experimental design that isolates the interaction of architecture, task similarity, and dimensionality.
  • The dimensionality-as-control-variable insight is elegant and provides a unifying lens for understanding conflicting results in prior modular continual learning literature.
  • The graded geometry finding provides a mechanistic explanation for when and why modular architectures help.
  • Well-motivated by the stability-plasticity dilemma and cognitive neuroscience transfer-interference literature.

Limitations

  • Experiments appear to be on relatively small-scale recurrent networks; scalability to modern deep learning architectures (Transformers, large-scale CNNs) is untested.
  • The task paradigm is inspired by two-task transfer-interference studies, which is a simplification of realistic continual learning scenarios with many tasks.
  • The relationship between initialization scale and effective dimensionality, while empirically demonstrated, could benefit from deeper theoretical grounding.
  • Without the full paper, it is unclear how many task types, network sizes, and hyperparameter settings were tested.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How do these findings translate to Transformer architectures and large-scale continual fine-tuning of LLMs, where representation dimensionality is typically very high?
  • Can the effective dimensionality of intermediate representations be used as a diagnostic metric to decide whether to invest in modular architecture during training?
  • Does the graded geometry pattern emerge in settings with more than two sequential tasks, or does the picture become more complex?
  • What are the theoretical conditions under which high-dimensional representations guarantee low interference — is there a dimensionality threshold relative to task complexity?
  • How does this interact with replay-based or regularization-based continual learning methods?
