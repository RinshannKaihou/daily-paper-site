> **一句话总结：** 本文通过构建360个词的特征向量和32条语义轴（如美-丑、软-硬），发现LLM隐藏状态中的语义特征几何结构与人类心理语义关联高度一致，且语义轴之间的余弦相似度能预测特征操控的"溢出效应"。

> **TL;DR:** The authors construct 360 word feature vectors and 32 semantic axes (e.g., beautiful-ugly, soft-hard) from Llama and Qwen LLM hidden states, then compare them to a large human survey of semantic associations. They find that (1) word projections on semantic axes correlate strongly with human ratings (r > 0.3–0.8), (2) cosine similarities between axes predict inter-scale correlations in survey data (r ≈ 0.77–0.88), (3) the 32 axes reduce to a low-dimensional subspace matching the classic 3-factor EPA structure (Evaluation-Potency-Activity), and (4) steering one axis causes predictable spillover on others proportional to cosine similarity. This is a well-executed bridge between classic psychometrics and LLM mechanistic interpretability, with direct implications for representation engineering and AI safety.

## 核心贡献

- 揭示LLM隐藏状态中的语义特征几何结构与人类心理语义关联高度一致，将经典语义差异研究的范式引入LLM可解释性
- 构建了一套系统的实验流程：用4个提示模板生成词特征向量，用10组反义词对构造语义轴，可直接复现和扩展
- 发现32条语义轴的大部分方差集中在低维子空间（前3个主成分解释45%以上方差），与人类语义判断的三维EPA结构（评价-力量-活动）对应
- 证明语义轴之间的余弦相似度能预测特征操控的溢出效应（off-target spillover），对AI安全和可控性研究有直接意义

## Key Contributions

- Demonstrate that geometric relations between semantic features in LLM hidden states closely mirror human psychological associations measured through a large-scale survey (1,750 respondents, 360 words, 32 semantic scales)
- Show that cosine similarities between semantic axes are highly predictive (r = 0.77–0.88) of inter-scale correlations in human survey data
- Reproduce the classic 3-dimensional semantic structure (Evaluation-Potency-Activity) from Osgood's semantic differential tradition in LLM feature space, validated via canonical correlation analysis
- Establish that steering along one semantic axis causes spillover effects on other axes proportional to their cosine similarity, with implications for representation engineering and AI safety

## Methodology

The study bridges classic psychometric methods with modern LLM interpretability across four experiments on Llama 3.2 3B, Llama 3.1 70B, Qwen3 1.7B, and Qwen3 32B:

**Survey data:** A modern replication of Jenkins et al. (1958) with 1,750 U.S. respondents rating 360 words on 32 semantic scales (e.g., beautiful-ugly, soft-hard). Each word rated by ~24 respondents per scale.

**Feature vector construction:** For each of the 360 words, four prompts (e.g., "Tell me about {word}") are passed through the LLM. Mean activations across tokens are computed per prompt, then averaged across the four prompts to yield one robust feature vector per word. Activations extracted from layer 8 (30% depth) for 3B and layer 40 (50% depth) for 70B.

**Semantic axes:** Constructed via contrastive pairs — 10 antonym pairs per axis, each word gets a feature vector, axis = mean of (positive − negative) differences across the 10 pairs.

**Projections & correlations:** Word feature vectors projected onto semantic axes; projections correlated with human ratings per scale.

**Axis cosine similarities:** Pairwise cosine similarities between the 32 axes compared to inter-scale correlations from survey data.

**PCA & CCA:** PCA applied to survey data, word projections, and raw axis vectors separately; CCA used to assess alignment between the 3-dimensional subspaces from survey vs. LLM data.

**Steering interventions:** Forward hook on residual stream at the same layers. Hidden state modified as w′ = w + α‖w‖f̂ (α = 0.33). Model prompted to rate words on semantic scales; spillover measured as change in logit probability for off-target antonym pairs.

## Results & Highlights

- **Word-axis projections correlate with human ratings:** Across 32 scales, correlations range from r > 0.3 to r > 0.8. Small model (3B) shows slightly stronger correlations than 70B.
- **Axis cosine similarities predict survey correlations:** r = 0.881 (3B) and r = 0.812 (70B) for word-projection correlations; r = 0.868 (3B) and r = 0.765 (70B) for raw cosine similarities between axes.
- **Low-dimensional structure replicated:** First 3 PCA components explain >45% variance in 3B and >33% in 70B (vs. ~3.1% if axes were orthogonal). CCA confirms the 3D subspace from LLM closely mirrors the survey's subspace.
- **Steering spillover is predictable:** Spillover effect size correlates with cosine similarity between target and off-target axes (r = 0.681 for 3B, r = 0.551 for 70B). The 70B model shows smaller absolute effects, consistent with greater steering resistance rather than axis independence.
- **Cross-model consistency:** Results replicated on Qwen3 1.7B and 32B with similar patterns, suggesting the finding is model-agnostic.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty (High):** While word embedding geometry has been studied extensively, extending this to LLM hidden state activations at mid-layers with systematic comparison to human psychometric data is genuinely new. The spillover analysis during steering is a creative and practically important contribution.

**Rigor (High):** The methodology is systematic — multiple prompt templates, averaging across antonym pairs, cross-model validation (4 models), and proper statistical comparison to human benchmarks. The use of CCA for subspace alignment is methodologically sound.

**Reproducibility (Moderate-High):** The 360-word, 32-scale design is clearly specified. Survey data from Boutyline & Johnston (2025) is referenced. Models are publicly available (Llama 3.x, Qwen3). Prompt templates and layer choices are stated. Full code release would strengthen reproducibility.

**Significance (High):** This paper challenges the prevailing "features as independent knobs" paradigm in mechanistic interpretability. The demonstration that feature geometry has practical consequences for steering is directly relevant to AI safety. The bridge to classic psychometrics opens new interdisciplinary research directions.

**Red flags:** The PCA analysis is constrained by having only 32 axes — claims about low-dimensional structure would be stronger with more axes. The steering coefficient α = 0.33 is not trivially small; it is unclear how results change at smaller magnitudes. The paper does not address whether these findings extend to more abstract or safety-critical features beyond the semantic scales studied.

## Strengths

- Elegant experimental design that connects 70 years of psychometric tradition to modern LLM interpretability
- Systematic cross-model validation across 4 models and 2 model families
- The spillover analysis is both novel and practically actionable for anyone doing representation engineering
- Clear, well-structured presentation with informative figures
- Findings challenge the common assumption that features should be treated independently in interpretability research

## Limitations

- Only 32 semantic axes are studied; the low-dimensional structure finding may not generalize to the hundreds or thousands of features typically uncovered by sparse autoencoders
- Steering experiments use a relatively large coefficient (α = 0.33 of residual stream norm); practical steering interventions may operate at smaller scales
- The word set (360 words from 1958) may not capture the full diversity of semantic concepts relevant to modern LLM behavior
- Results are limited to Llama and Qwen families; coverage of other architectures (e.g., attention-only models, mixture-of-experts) is absent
- The paper does not investigate whether similar geometric structure exists for non-semantic features (e.g., syntactic, factual, reasoning-related features)

## Relevance

本文直接研究LLM隐藏状态中的语义特征几何结构，与LLM可解释性和表征几何的研究兴趣高度相关。文中关于语义轴余弦相似度预测特征操控溢出效应的发现，对理解隐藏状态中信息编码的纠缠方式具有重要意义。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Do similar geometric structures exist for safety-relevant features (truthfulness, harmlessness, helpfulness)? If so, could steering on one safety axis inadvertently compromise another?
- How does the dimensionality of the semantic subspace change as the number of studied axes grows from 32 to hundreds or thousands (e.g., SAE-derived features)?
- Is the 3-dimensional EPA structure an artifact of the specific word/scale set, or does it reflect a fundamental organizing principle of LLM representations?
- How does semantic structure evolve across layers — is the mid-layer structure found here maintained at later layers closer to output?
- Can the predictable spillover effect be leveraged constructively (e.g., steering multiple desirable attributes simultaneously via a single vector)?
