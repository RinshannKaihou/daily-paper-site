Exploring the Limits of Pruning: Task-Specific Neurons, Model Collapse, and Recovery in Task-Specific Large Language Models

arXiv: 2604.27115

Date: 2026-04-29

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.27115v1

一句话总结： 本文通过系统性的神经元剪枝实验，证明了任务专用LLM中存在少量关键任务神经元（约10%），移除这些神经元会导致模型性能完全崩溃，而通过基于激活值的选择性剪枝和LoRA微调可以在大幅压缩模型的同时恢复大部分性能。
TL;DR: This paper provides empirical evidence for task-specific neuron specialization in LLMs through systematic pruning experiments on math and code models (1.5B–7B), showing that removing ~10% of highly task-relevant neurons causes complete collapse while selective pruning of up to 30–35% of less critical neurons preserves significant performance, and LoRA fine-tuning can substantially recover lost capabilities.

核心贡献

  • 提出了基于激活值的神经元选择性度量方法，通过比较目标任务与干扰任务的激活差异来识别任务关键神经元。
  • 通过反向剪枝实验证明，仅移除约10%的高选择性任务神经元即可导致模型性能完全崩溃，证实了任务专用神经元的存在。
  • 在数学推理和代码生成的1.5B和7B模型上进行了系统性剪枝实验，揭示了15–20%的鲁棒性阈值。
  • 发现剪枝会引发两类退化陷阱（Type 1: 推理完成后无法终止；Type 2: 推理过程中崩溃），且LoRA微调能有效恢复性能并减少陷阱。
  • 展示了选择性剪枝在各剪枝比例下均显著优于随机剪枝，证明了基于激活的神经元重要性排序的有效性。

Key Contributions

  • Introduced an activation-based neuron selectivity metric that compares target-task vs. distractor-task activations to identify task-critical neurons in specialized LLMs.
  • Demonstrated via reverse pruning that removing only ~10% of the most task-relevant neurons causes complete performance collapse, providing strong evidence for neuron specialization.
  • Conducted systematic pruning experiments across 1.5B and 7B models for math reasoning and code generation, identifying a robustness threshold at 15–20% pruning beyond which accuracy loss and generation failures increase sharply.
  • Identified two types of degeneration traps (Type 1: post-reasoning termination failure; Type 2: mid-reasoning collapse) induced by pruning, and showed LoRA fine-tuning effectively mitigates both.
  • Showed selective pruning consistently and significantly outperforms random pruning across all pruning levels and model scales.

Methodology

The authors evaluate four Qwen2.5 models: Qwen2.5-Math-7B/1.5B-Instruct and Qwen2.5-Coder-7B/1.5B-Instruct. They define a neuron selectivity score as the standardized difference between mean activations on target-task prompts (GSM8K for math, CodeFeedback for code) and distractor prompts (SQuAD + dialogue). Neurons with negative selectivity (more active for distractors) are candidates for pruning.

Structural pruning physically removes low-selectivity neurons from all MLP layers uniformly, keeping the intermediate dimension divisible by 128 for GPU efficiency. Pruning ratios range from ~5% to ~35%. Three pruning strategies are compared: (1) selective pruning (remove lowest-selectivity neurons), (2) random pruning (random neuron removal with two seeds), and (3) reverse pruning (remove highest-selectivity neurons as a counterfactual).

Post-pruning recovery uses LoRA fine-tuning applied to both attention and FFN projection layers (rank 8–16, 2 epochs). Evaluation includes exact match for math, HumanEval pass@1 for code, and trap analysis on unconstrained generation.

Results & Highlights

  • Selective vs. random pruning: Selective pruning consistently outperforms random pruning across all models and pruning levels, confirming non-uniform neuron importance.
  • Reverse pruning causes rapid collapse: Removing the top ~5% of task-specific neurons drastically degrades performance; at ~10% reverse pruning, models completely collapse into incoherent generation.
  • Robustness threshold at 15–20%: For selective pruning, accuracy degrades mildly up to ~15–20% pruning, then drops sharply. At 35% selective pruning, the 7B math model retains ~61% accuracy while the 1.5B math model drops to ~27%.
  • Larger models are more resilient: 7B models consistently tolerate higher pruning ratios with smaller relative accuracy loss compared to 1.5B models.
  • LoRA recovery is effective: Fine-tuning recovers substantial performance, especially for aggressively pruned models. For example, the 7B math model at 35% pruning recovers from 57.85% to 73.84% EM after fine-tuning.
  • Resource savings: At 35% pruning, VRAM usage drops from 14.26GB to 10.56GB (7B) and throughput increases from 35.83 to 42.70 tokens/s.
  • Trap analysis: Trap frequency increases sharply beyond 15–20% pruning. Type 1 traps (correct answer + degeneration loop) are mitigated by custom stopping criteria. Fine-tuning substantially reduces both trap types.
  • Layer-wise analysis: Early layers are predominantly distractor-oriented; middle and later layers shift toward target-relevant activations, with math models showing more consistent task specialization than code models.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The concept of task-specific neurons in LLMs is not entirely new—prior work (Dalvi et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2024) has explored neuron specialization. The main novelty lies in the systematic reverse-pruning counterfactual and the detailed trap taxonomy, though the selectivity metric itself is straightforward (standardized activation difference).
  • Rigor: Experiments cover two task domains, two model sizes, and multiple pruning strategies with complementary analyses (layer-wise, distractor, resource usage). However, only one model family (Qwen2.5) is tested, limiting generalizability. The single-run evaluation for HumanEval (no pass@k with k>1) and greedy decoding only may not capture the full picture of code model performance.
  • Reproducibility: Strong—full hyperparameters, code repository, and detailed appendix provided. The 128-divisibility constraint for GPU kernel optimization is a practical and well-justified design choice.
  • Significance: The empirical findings on robustness thresholds and recovery potential are practically useful for practitioners deciding how aggressively to prune task-specific models. The reverse-pruning result (~10% collapse) is a compelling demonstration of neuron specialization.
  • Red flags: The selectivity metric conflates neurons that are moderately active for both target and distractor with truly low-utility neurons (acknowledged by authors in Section 5.1). Fine-tuning uses only the target-task training data, which limits generalization recovery. The paper does not compare against gradient-based pruning methods like LLM-Pruner, which would strengthen the activation-based approach's claims.

Strengths

  • Clean experimental design with three complementary pruning strategies (selective, random, reverse) that together build a convincing case for neuron specialization.
  • Practical robustness thresholds (15–20%) that offer actionable guidance for practitioners.
  • Thorough trap taxonomy and analysis, including mitigation strategies for Type 1 traps.
  • Demonstrates consistent trends across model sizes (1.5B vs. 7B) and task domains (math vs. code).
  • Honest discussion of limitations, including the selectivity metric's inability to distinguish moderate dual-activation neurons from truly unimportant ones.

Limitations

  • Only evaluated on Qwen2.5 model family; results may not generalize to other architectures (e.g., LLaMA, Mistral, Gemma).
  • Single HumanEval run with greedy decoding only; no sampling-based evaluation for code models.
  • Fine-tuning data is limited to the target task's training set, which may underestimate recovery potential with more diverse data.
  • The selectivity metric cannot distinguish between neurons that contribute moderately to both target and distractor tasks, leading to potentially suboptimal pruning decisions at high ratios.
  • No comparison with gradient-based or importance-based pruning baselines (e.g., LLM-Pruner, SparseGPT), only random pruning.
  • Attention layers are excluded from pruning entirely, which may leave significant compression opportunities unexplored.

Relevance

该研究对理解LLM中神经元的功能特化具有重要参考价值，其选择性剪枝方法和鲁棒性阈值分析可直接应用于高效模型部署场景。论文中的激活分析框架也为模型可解释性研究提供了有用工具。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does the selectivity metric perform on multi-task models (e.g., general-purpose instruct models) where neurons may serve multiple overlapping purposes?
  • Would combining activation-based selectivity with gradient-based importance (as in LLM-Pruner) yield better pruning decisions, especially at higher pruning ratios?
  • Can the observed layer-wise neuron distribution pattern (distractor-heavy early layers, target-heavy later layers) be exploited for layer-specific pruning ratios?
  • How do these findings transfer to models with different FFN architectures (e.g., GeLU-based models without SwiGLU)?
  • Does fine-tuning with diverse auxiliary data (beyond the target task) improve recovery and reduce trap rates for aggressively pruned models?
