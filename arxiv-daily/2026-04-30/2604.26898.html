Stochastic Scaling Limits and Synchronization by Noise in Deep Transformer Models

arXiv: 2604.26898

Date: 2026-04-30

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.26898v1

一句话总结： 本文严格证明了深度Transformer中token在层间演化的连续时间随机极限（含MLP和注意力机制），并证明MLP初始化引入的公共噪声能在一定条件下使token指数级同步（聚类），定量揭示了注意力与MLP模块在表示动态中的协同机制。
TL;DR: This paper proves quantitative pathwise convergence of the residual stream dynamics in deep transformers (with both attention and MLP blocks) to a continuous-time stochastic interacting particle system in the joint deep-and-wide limit, identifies the limiting SPDE for the token distribution with propagation of chaos, and establishes exponential synchronization-by-noise of tokens under explicit conditions on the activation function.

核心贡献

  • 随机缩放极限： 首次在同时包含self-attention和MLP模块的Transformer中，证明token的离散层间演化在深度$L \to \infty$、宽度$m \to \infty$的联合极限下，路径收敛到一个连续时间随机相互作用粒子系统（SDE），且收敛速率是定量的（$O(\log L / L + 1/m)$）。
  • SPDE与大$N$极限： 推导出token分布所满足的随机偏微分方程（SPDE），并证明传播混沌（propagation of chaos）成立——当token数量$N \to \infty$时，经验测度收敛到McKean-Vlasov型方程的解，收敛速率为$O(N^{-1/2})$（$d<4$）或$O(N^{-2/d})$（$d>4$）。
  • 噪声诱导同步： 证明极限随机模型在MLP公共噪声足够强（相对于确定性注意力漂移）时，token间的交互能量指数衰减，从而实现同步/聚类。这是"噪声诱导同步"现象在Transformer中的首次定量刻画。
  • 激活函数的显式刻画： 给出ReLU等常用激活函数下同步率（Lyapunov指数$\lambda_1$和$\bar\lambda$）的显式公式，明确列出满足同步条件的参数范围。
  • 三个极限可交换： 证明$N \to \infty$、$L \to \infty$、$m \to \infty$三个极限可交换（对光滑激活函数），建立了完整的深度-宽度-上下文长度联合缩放理论。

Key Contributions

  • Stochastic scaling limit with MLP: Proves the first quantitative pathwise convergence of residual stream dynamics in a transformer including both attention and MLP blocks to a continuous-time stochastic interacting particle system (SDE on $S^{d-1}$), with rate $O(\log L / L + 1/m)$ in the joint deep-and-wide limit.
  • Limiting SPDE and propagation of chaos: Identifies the SPDE governing the token distribution in the Eulerian description and proves propagation of chaos at rate $O(N^{-1/2})$ for $d < 4$ and $O(N^{-2/d})$ for $d > 4$, with all three limits (deep, wide, large-$N$) commuting for smooth activations.
  • Synchronization by noise: Establishes exponential dissipation of the interaction energy $E_\beta(\mu)$ under the limiting stochastic dynamics, provided the MLP-induced common noise is sufficiently coercive relative to the deterministic attention drift — a quantitative synchronization-by-noise result for transformers.
  • Explicit conditions for ReLU and beyond: Derives closed-form expressions for the synchronization rates $\lambda_1$ and $\bar\lambda$ as functions of dimension $d$, bias variances $\sigma_U^2, \sigma_W^2$, and the activation $\phi$, showing for example that ReLU always satisfies $\lambda_1 < 0$ and $\bar\lambda < 0$ when $d > 3$.
  • Complementary roles of attention and MLP: The analysis cleanly separates attention (deterministic mean-field interaction drift) from MLP (non-interacting common noise), quantifying their interplay in shaping token representations.

Methodology

The authors consider a transformer with $N$ tokens embedded on $S^{d-1}$, depth $L$, and MLP width $m$, with layer normalization via radial projection. The residual stream dynamics (Eq. 1.1) alternate between self-attention and MLP blocks with scaling factors $\alpha_1(L) = \sqrt{T/L}$ and $\alpha_2(L) = \sqrt{T/L}$.

Step 1 — Wide limit ($m \to \infty$): Under Gaussian initialization of MLP weights, the finite-width MLP $G_\ell^m$ converges to a centered Gaussian process $G_\ell$ with covariance kernel derived from the activation $\phi$. The Karhunen-Loève expansion provides the noise basis $\{\sigma_k\}$.

Step 2 — Deep limit ($L \to \infty$): The discrete layer-to-layer iteration is compared to the continuous-time SDE (Eq. 1.8) via a triangle inequality with two intermediate processes. Layer normalization on the sphere introduces nonlinear couplings requiring careful second-order expansions and discrete Grönwall arguments. A globally adapted coupling $\Gamma_{m,L}$ is constructed recursively across layers.

Step 3 — Large-$N$ limit (propagation of chaos): The empirical measure of the particle system is shown to converge to the unique solution of the SPDE (Eq. 1.10) on $S^{d-1}$, using Wasserstein-2 stability estimates adapted to the spherical manifold-valued setting.

Step 4 — Synchronization analysis: For softmax attention with $Q^\top K = \beta \mathbb{1}_d$, the interaction energy $E_\beta$ (Eq. 1.15) serves as a Lyapunov function. The authors compute Itô's formula on the sphere, identify the dissipation rate contributed by the common noise ($\bar\lambda < 0$), and establish Grönwall-type exponential decay under Assumptions 1–2.

Results & Highlights

  • Theorem 1.1: Pathwise convergence with quantitative rate $\mathbb{E}[\sup |X_{\lfloor Lt/T \rfloor} - \bar{X}_t|^2] \leq c_{N,T}(\log L / L + 1/m)$. The $\log L$ factor can be dropped at discrete time points.
  • Proposition 1.2: Propagation of chaos with rate $d(\mu^N, \mu) \leq c_T N^{-1/2}$ for $d < 4$, confirming the SPDE limit.
  • Theorem 1.4: Exponential energy dissipation $\mathbb{E}[E_\beta(\bar{\mu}_t)] \leq \exp((\varepsilon + \bar{\lambda}')t)\mathbb{E}[E_\beta(\bar{\mu}_0)]$ with $\bar{\lambda}' < 0$, implying synchronization to a random point attractor.
  • Proposition 1.5: For ReLU, $\lambda_1 = -\frac{1}{2d} - \frac{(\sigma_U^2 + 2\sigma_W^2)(d-1)}{4} < 0$ always, and $\bar{\lambda} < 0$ when $d > 3$.
  • Numerical experiments (Figures 1, 4) validate synchronization behavior for ReLU, Sigmoid, and Tanh activations across dimensions $d \in \{5, 64, 128\}$, with and without attention, confirming the theoretical predictions.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. This is the first work to establish a stochastic scaling limit for transformers including the MLP component, and the first quantitative synchronization-by-noise result in this context. The clean separation of attention (deterministic drift) and MLP (common noise) is a conceptually new contribution.
  • Rigor: Excellent. All main results (Theorems 1.1, 1.4, Propositions 1.2, 1.5) have complete proofs. The handling of layer normalization on the sphere (nonlinear projection requiring second-order expansions beyond standard Euler-Maruyama analysis) is technically demanding and carefully executed. Quantitative convergence rates are provided throughout.
  • Reproducibility: Good for the theoretical results. Numerical experiments in Appendix B use standard architectures and activations. No code repository is mentioned, but the experimental setup is straightforward to replicate.
  • Significance: Significant for the mathematical foundations of deep learning. The results provide a principled understanding of how MLP randomness at initialization creates a common noise that drives token clustering — a phenomenon empirically observed but not previously explained quantitatively. The explicit formulas for Lyapunov exponents connect architectural choices directly to synchronization behavior.
  • Red flags: (1) The synchronization result (Theorem 1.4) requires $d > 3$ for $\bar{\lambda} < 0$, which may limit applicability in low-dimensional settings. (2) The analysis is at initialization only — extension to trained weights is mentioned as future work but is non-trivial. (3) The assumption $Q^\top K = \beta \mathbb{1}_d$ for the synchronization result is restrictive, though the authors note it extends to nearby parameter regimes. (4) Layer normalization via radial projection is a simplification of practical LayerNorm/RMSNorm.

Strengths

  • First rigorous stochastic limit theorem for transformers with both attention and MLP, filling a major gap in the mean-field transformer literature.
  • Clean mathematical framework separating the deterministic (attention) and stochastic (MLP) contributions, providing clear mechanistic insight.
  • Quantitative rates throughout — convergence, propagation of chaos, and synchronization — all with explicit constants.
  • The synchronization-by-noise result is conceptually striking: randomness that one might expect to disperse tokens actually concentrates them, with a rigorous explanation via Lyapunov exponents.
  • Well-written paper with a clear proof strategy (triangle inequality through two intermediate processes) and thorough related work discussion.

Limitations

  • Initialization only: The entire analysis assumes random, untrained weights. Whether these structural insights persist after training remains open.
  • Simplifying assumptions for synchronization: The exponential synchronization requires $Q^\top K = \beta \mathbb{1}_d$ (isotropic attention) and $d > 3$. Practical transformers use learned, anisotropic $Q, K$.
  • Abstracted layer normalization: The radial projection $v \mapsto v/|v|$ is a simplified proxy for practical LayerNorm/RMSNorm, which includes learnable scale/shift parameters.
  • No training dynamics: The paper does not address how the stochastic structure evolves during gradient-based training.
  • Scalability of synchronization condition: The condition $\bar{\lambda} < 0$ becomes easier to satisfy in high dimensions, but the relationship to practical hyperparameter choices is not explored empirically at scale.

Relevance

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Can the stochastic scaling limit be extended to trained transformers, e.g., via a mean-field optimal control or gradient flow framework?
  • How does the synchronization-by-noise phenomenon interact with practical regularization techniques (dropout, weight decay) that inject additional randomness?
  • For practical architectures with residual connections, multi-head attention, and RMSNorm (not radial projection), do the qualitative conclusions about MLP-induced common noise and synchronization persist?
  • Can the explicit Lyapunov exponent formulas be used to design initialization schemes that deliberately avoid or promote synchronization, depending on the application?
  • What is the interplay between the common noise (MLP) studied here and the interacting noise from random attention parameters identified in concurrent works [21, 35]?
