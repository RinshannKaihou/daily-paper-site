Dynamic Scaled Gradient Descent for Stable Fine-Tuning for Classifications

arXiv: 2604.27987

Date: 2026-04-30

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.27987v1

一句话总结： 本文提出 DSGD 算法，通过在微调过程中动态缩小正确分类样本的梯度幅度来缓解 mini-batch 内的梯度冲突，理论上证明了比标准 SGD 更优的收敛性和更紧的稳定性上界，在 14 个 NLP/CV 任务上显著降低了随机种子引起的性能方差并提升了准确率。
TL;DR: Fine-tuning large pretrained models for classification is unstable across random seeds — some seeds cause gradient cancellation between correctly and wrongly classified examples, leading to collapsed or degenerate solutions. DSGD fixes this by dynamically downscaling gradients from correctly classified examples using a linearly ascending scaler. It requires no extra backward passes, no architecture changes, and negligible overhead. Theoretically, DSGD guarantees convergence to better stationary points and a tighter stability bound than standard SGD. Across 14 NLP and vision tasks (RoBERTa-large, Llama-3.2-1B, ViT-base), DSGD consistently achieves the best or near-best accuracy with dramatically lower variance, outperforming FocalLoss, LNSR, NoisyTune, PCGrad, SWA, and ensemble methods.

核心贡献

  • 揭示了微调不稳定的根本原因：通过分析 [CLS] token 表示的余弦相似度和梯度范数，发现失败训练 run 中，正确分类样本与错误分类样本的梯度方向高度对立（90% 迭代步的余弦相似度为负），导致梯度相互抵消，模型陷入 collapsed state 或退化解。
  • 提出 DSGD 算法：在 mini-batch 内，对正确分类样本的梯度乘以动态缩放因子 γ_t（从接近 0 线性递增至上限 τ），从而抑制梯度冲突，同时保留学习信号。无需额外反向传播、无需修改模型架构。
  • 提供理论保证：证明了 DSGD 的收敛率 O(1/T)（与 SGD 相同但收敛到更优的驻点），以及比 SGD 更紧的稳定性上界（ϵ_DSGD ≤ ϵ_SGD）。
  • 广泛的实验验证：在 14 个任务（SuperGLUE、GLUE、不平衡 CIFAR-10/100）上，使用 RoBERTa-large、Llama-3.2-1B 和 ViT-base，DSGD 在准确率和方差上均优于 FocalLoss、LNSR、NoisyTune、PCGrad、SWA 和集成方法。

Key Contributions

  • Identifies gradient cancellation between correctly and wrongly classified training examples as the primary mechanism behind seed-induced fine-tuning instability; in failed runs, 90% of iterations show negative cosine similarity between the two gradient groups, with over 30% below -0.5.
  • Proposes DSGD, a drop-in optimizer modification that scales down gradients of correctly classified examples by a dynamic factor γ_t ∈ (0, τ], using a linearly ascending schedule from near-zero to τ=1 over training.
  • Provides formal proofs that DSGD (i) converges to stationary points where the loss on misclassified examples is minimized (implying 100% training accuracy at convergence), and (ii) achieves a stability bound that is provably no worse than (and typically tighter than) standard SGD.
  • Demonstrates consistent improvements across 14 tasks spanning NLP sequence classification (RTE, COPA, MultiRC, BoolQ, CoLA, MRPC) and imbalanced image classification (CIFAR-10/100), with models ranging from 355M to 1B parameters, requiring no additional backward passes or hyperparameter tuning.

Methodology

The authors first diagnose the root cause of fine-tuning instability by analyzing gradient dynamics during training. They observe that in collapsed or degenerate runs, the cosine similarity between gradients of correctly classified examples (G_C) and wrongly classified examples (G_W) is strongly negative across most iterations, causing the average batch gradient to vanish.

DSGD modifies the standard gradient update as follows:

  1. For each mini-batch, classify training examples into correctly classified set C and wrongly classified set W based on current model predictions.
  2. Compute gradients for both groups: ∇L_W and ∇L_C.
  3. Apply a dynamic scaling factor γ_t to the correctly classified gradients: θ_{t+1} = θ_t - (η/m)(∇L_W + γ_t ∇L_C), where γ_t = ζ(t, τ, T) is a linearly ascending function from near-zero to τ.
  4. The key insight: by reducing the contribution of correctly classified examples (whose gradients may oppose those of misclassified examples), DSGD prevents gradient cancellation while still allowing correctly classified examples to provide learning signal as training progresses.

The dynamic scaler design is motivated by the observation that early in training, correctly classified examples often provide opposing gradients to misclassified ones. As training progresses and the model becomes more confident, these conflicts diminish. The ascending schedule reflects this: strong downscaling early (to prevent cancellation) transitioning to near-full contribution later (to leverage all training signal).

Results & Highlights

  • NLP single-task (RoBERTa-large): DSGD achieves the best accuracy on COPA (90.60 vs. FFT's 84.30), MultiRC (82.35 vs. 79.41), and competitive results on RTE, BoolQ, CoLA, MRPC, with standard deviations reduced from 5-16% (FFT) to 0.3-2.4% (DSGD).
  • NLP (Llama-3.2-1B): Similar trends hold for decoder-only models, with DSGD achieving the best accuracy on 3/4 tasks and the lowest or second-lowest variance across all tasks.
  • Imbalanced image classification (ViT-base): On 100:1 imbalance ratio, DSGD consistently achieves top accuracy across all four dataset variants (long-tailed and step imbalance for CIFAR-10/100), while baselines like FocalLoss show catastrophic variance (STD up to 15.4) and PCGrad fails to improve over FFT.
  • Computational cost: DSGD requires no additional training time beyond FFT (unlike PCGrad which nearly doubles training time due to separate backward passes, or ensemble methods which scale linearly with ensemble size).
  • Multi-task learning: DSGD achieves the highest average accuracy (85.41) with by far the lowest standard deviation (1.19) across joint training on MRPC, RTE, and CoLA, compared to the next best baseline PCGrad (81.52 accuracy, 6.19 STD).

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The core idea — dynamically scaling gradients based on classification correctness — is simple and novel. The connection between seed-induced instability and inter-example gradient cancellation is convincingly established. However, the approach is limited to classification tasks with a clear correct/incorrect dichotomy, and the mechanism (split batch by prediction correctness, apply scalar) is a relatively straightforward modification.

Rigor: Theoretical proofs are sound and properly formalized. The convergence proof (Theorem 4.6) assumes the loss on misclassified examples is M-smooth and that ∥G_W∥² ≥ τ²∥G_C∥², which may not hold in practice for all training stages. The stability bound (Theorem 4.5) relies on the H-Lipschitz assumption. Experiments are thorough across 14 tasks with 10 seeds each.

Reproducibility: The algorithm is simple to implement (Algorithm 2 in appendix, ~15 lines of pseudo-code). Hyperparameters are well-specified (τ=1, linear ascending γ, standard learning rates). The 10-seed evaluation protocol is clearly described. However, no code is released.

Significance: The problem (seed-induced fine-tuning instability) is well-known and practically important. DSGD offers a lightweight, theoretically grounded solution that works across architectures and tasks. The consistent improvements over baselines are compelling.

Red flags: (1) The evaluation is limited to relatively small models (355M–1B parameters); it remains unclear whether the instability pattern and DSGD's benefits scale to models in the 7B–70B range. (2) The method is specific to classification; extending it to generation or ranking tasks is non-trivial since the correct/incorrect split is less clear-cut. (3) The ∥G_W∥² ≥ τ²∥G_C∥² assumption required for the convergence proof may be violated early in training when few examples are correctly classified, making G_C large relative to G_W.

Strengths

  • Simple, elegant algorithm with negligible computational overhead — a true drop-in replacement for standard optimizers.
  • Strong theoretical grounding with formal convergence and stability guarantees.
  • Comprehensive experimental evaluation spanning NLP and vision, encoder-only and decoder-only architectures, single-task and multi-task settings.
  • Consistently outperforms all baselines in both accuracy and stability, including expensive ensemble methods.
  • The diagnosis of gradient cancellation as the root cause of instability is well-supported by empirical evidence (cosine similarity analysis, gradient norm plots).

Limitations

  • Only applicable to classification tasks where examples can be cleanly split into correctly/incorrectly classified during training; does not generalize to sequence generation, regression, or other task types.
  • Evaluated only on models up to 1B parameters; scalability to larger models (7B+) is untested.
  • Does not address other sources of instability such as prompt formatting sensitivity or in-context example selection.
  • The dynamic scaler function (linearly ascending) was chosen empirically; the paper does not provide guidance on optimal scaler design for new tasks.
  • The requirement to compute per-example correctness at each step adds minor overhead and assumes access to ground-truth labels during training (not applicable to unsupervised/semi-supervised settings).

Relevance

本文直接解决大模型微调过程中随机种子引起的训练不稳定问题，提出的梯度缩放机制本质上是一种训练稳定性技术，与 LLM 训练稳定性研究高度相关。DSGD 的理论框架（收敛性保证、稳定性上界）可为大模型训练中类似的梯度冲突问题提供思路。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does DSGD interact with learning rate warmup and decay schedules? Could combining them yield further stability improvements?
  • Can the gradient scaling principle be extended to generation tasks by defining a softer "correctness" metric (e.g., based on reward model scores or token-level accuracy)?
  • Does the gradient cancellation phenomenon observed in classification fine-tuning also occur during instruction tuning or RLHF alignment of large language models?
  • What happens when DSGD is applied to models with 7B–70B parameters — does the same instability pattern manifest, and does the same solution scale?
  • Could the dynamic scaler be learned or adapted online based on real-time gradient conflict measurements rather than using a fixed schedule?
