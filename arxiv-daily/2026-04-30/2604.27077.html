TL;DR: The authors propose νGPT, a re-parameterization of the Normalized Transformer (nGPT), that achieves learning rate transfer across model width, depth, and token horizon by using alignment exponents to derive a novel correction scheme that generalizes μP.

核心贡献

  • 提出 νGPT 参数化方案，是 nGPT 的修正版本，实现了学习率在宽度、深度和 token 训练量三个维度上的迁移。
  • 发现 nGPT 中权重-激活的对齐指数不满足标准 μP 的假设，提出"中间对齐"（mid alignment）假设（指数为 3/4），据此推导出隐藏层学习率的 m_width^{-3/4} 修正，而非 μP 中的 m_width^{-1}。
  • 引入深度修正：将残差连接中的可学习缩放参数 α_{A,init}、α_{M,init} 按 m_depth^{-1} 缩放。
  • 发现最优学习率随 token 数量按 m_data^{-1/3} 衰减，与前人独立测量一致。
  • 将 nGPT 中的 scale 超参数（如 α_{A,scale}）从依赖于 d_model^{-1/2} 改为固定常数 0.03，消除了模型规模依赖。

Key Contributions

  • Propose νGPT, a novel parameterization of nGPT that achieves hyperparameter (learning rate) transfer across width, depth, and token horizon simultaneously.
  • Empirically demonstrate that weight-activation alignment exponents in nGPT violate standard μP assumptions; introduce the "mid alignment" assumption (exponent 3/4) to derive hidden learning rate corrections of m_width^{-3/4} rather than μP's m_width^{-1}.
  • Show that depth transfer requires scaling the LERP initialization parameters α_{A,init} and α_{M,init} by m_depth^{-1}, corresponding to α_depth = 1 in the residual network framework.
  • Identify that the optimal base learning rate scales as m_data^{-1/3} with token horizon, consistent with concurrent and prior measurements.
  • Replace nGPT's d_model-dependent scale parameters (e.g., α_{A,scale}) with fixed constants (0.03), eliminating implicit model-size dependence.

Methodology

The authors build on the Normalized Transformer (nGPT) architecture, which extensively normalizes weights and activations via ℓ₂-normalization, replacing standard RMSNorm/LayerNorm. nGPT uses trainable "LERP" (linear interpolation) rescaling vectors (α_A, α_M) in residual connections and normalized query/key projections.

The core methodology proceeds in three steps:

  1. Width corrections via alignment exponents: Using the alignment exponent framework from Everett et al. (2024), the authors empirically measure ω_hidden = ω_output = 1/2 and argue for a "mid alignment" assumption where max{α, ν} = 3/4. Under signGD-like Adam update scaling, this yields hidden and output learning rate corrections of m_width^{-3/4} (vs. m_width^{-1} in standard μP, which assumes full alignment with exponent 1). The embedding learning rate gets m_width^{-1/2} (same as μP).
  1. Depth corrections: By analyzing how residual updates accumulate across layers, and noting nGPT's unique LERP structure, the authors derive that α_{A,init} and α_{M,init} should scale as m_depth^{-1}. This corresponds to α_depth = 1 (the "CompleteP" regime), meaning no additional depth correction to hidden learning rates is needed.
  1. Token horizon corrections: Based on empirical power-law fits and prior work (Bjorck et al., 2025), the base learning rate scales as m_data^{-1/3}.

Experiments are conducted training nGPT/νGPT models on FineWeb-Edu with OLMo 2 tokenizer, sequence length 4096, batch size 64, varying n_heads (4 to 128) and depth (4 to 48 layers), evaluated by validation loss transfer across model sizes.

Results & Highlights

  • νGPT achieves perfect learning rate transfer across width at fixed iteration count: a single learning rate tuned on a small model (e.g., n_heads=4) yields the same optimal loss on large models (n_heads up to 128), while baseline nGPT shows no transfer (Figure 2).
  • Transfer across depth is also achieved: validation loss curves for models with 4 to 48 layers collapse when using νGPT's depth correction, while nGPT shows divergence for deeper models (Figure 3).
  • Transfer across token horizon (growing iteration count at fixed architecture) works with the m_data^{-1/3} correction (Figure 8).
  • Combined transfer over width, depth, and token horizon simultaneously is demonstrated: a learning rate tuned on a small, shallow, short-run model transfers to large, deep, long-run models with no loss in performance compared to individually tuned baselines (Figure 1).
  • νGPT performs slightly better than baseline nGPT in some configurations.
  • Alignment exponent measurements confirm ω ≈ 1/2 and max{α, ν} ≈ 3/4 empirically (Section 4.3, Figure 5), supporting the mid alignment assumption.
  • νGPT's width transfer is shown to be somewhat better than standard μP applied to nGPT (Figure 7).

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. The paper is the first to systematically address HP transfer in the unique nGPT architecture, which has fundamentally different normalization properties from standard Transformers. The mid alignment assumption and the resulting m_width^{-3/4} correction is a genuine theoretical contribution.
  • Rigor: Strong theoretical derivations supported by careful empirical validation. The alignment exponent measurements are well-designed. However, the mid alignment assumption (exponent 3/4) is partly empirical rather than derived from first principles — it is a fit to observations rather than a theorem.
  • Reproducibility: The paper provides complete hyperparameter tables (Table 1) and describes the training setup in detail. However, the code is not publicly released at the time of writing (uses a fork of TorchTitan).
  • Significance: Practically significant for anyone training nGPT-style models at scale. The elimination of per-scale hyperparameter sweeps can save substantial compute. The theoretical framework may also generalize to other normalized architectures.
  • Red flags: The token horizon exponent of -1/3 is empirically determined rather than theoretically derived. The depth correction lacks a complete theoretical explanation for why nGPT shows decent depth transfer even without corrections (noted by the authors themselves as a future direction). Scale of experiments is limited to models up to ~1B parameters — transfer to much larger scales is unverified.

Strengths

  • Clean problem formulation and comprehensive solution covering all three scaling dimensions (width, depth, data).
  • Strong empirical evidence with multiple ablation studies confirming each correction independently.
  • The alignment exponent framework provides an interpretable and extensible theoretical basis.
  • Complete hyperparameter specification makes the method immediately usable.
  • Honest about limitations and discrepancies with prior work.

Limitations

  • The mid alignment assumption (exponent 3/4) is an empirical fit, not a proven fact; it may not hold at all scales or for all architectures.
  • Experiments are limited to models with at most ~128 attention heads and 48 layers; scaling to frontier model sizes is untested.
  • The token horizon scaling exponent (-1/3) is empirically determined with no rigorous theoretical backing.
  • No comparison to alternative approaches like per-module update normalization (Large et al., 2024).
  • The depth transfer mechanism in nGPT is not fully understood theoretically — the authors note baseline nGPT already shows decent depth transfer, which is unexplained.
  • Only evaluated on a single dataset (FineWeb-Edu) and tokenizer (OLMo 2).

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Does the mid alignment assumption (exponent 3/4) hold for models at 10B+ parameter scale, or does it shift toward full alignment (1) as in standard μP?
  • How does νGPT interact with other optimizer choices (e.g., Muon, Sophia) beyond Adam?
  • Can the token horizon exponent (-1/3) be derived theoretically from the stochastic differential equation perspective on Adam dynamics?
  • Does νGPT transfer extend to batch size scaling, mixture-of-experts architectures, or multi-modal models?
  • What explains the surprising depth transfer in baseline nGPT without corrections — is it the trainable LERP parameters acting as automatic depth scalars?
