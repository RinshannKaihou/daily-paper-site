Learning to Forget: Continual Learning with Adaptive Weight Decay

arXiv: 2604.27063

Date: 2026-04-29

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.27063v1

一句话总结： FADE 通过元梯度在线自适应地调整每个参数的权重衰减率，使持续学习智能体能够自动区分哪些权重需要快速遗忘、哪些需要长期保留，在多个非平稳在线学习任务上显著优于固定权重衰减方法。
TL;DR: FADE adapts per-parameter weight decay rates online via forward-mode meta-gradients, enabling continual learning agents to automatically discover which weights to forget quickly and which to retain, consistently outperforming fixed weight decay across tracking and streaming classification tasks.

核心贡献

  • 提出了 FADE（Forgetting through Adaptive Decay），利用前向模式元梯度在线自适应每个参数的权重衰减率，计算开销仅为 O(d)
  • 从理论上将权重衰减重新诠释为"遗忘机制"而非单纯正则化，借鉴了 LSTM 遗忘门的思路但应用于长期记忆（权重）而非短期记忆（激活）
  • 证明了自适应衰减率与自适应步长（IDBD）是互补机制：一个控制"保留多少历史信息"，另一个控制"多快融入新信息"
  • 揭示了一个被忽视的强基线：仅对网络最后一层施加合适的固定非零衰减率即可显著提升持续学习性能

Key Contributions

  • Introduces FADE, which replaces the static global weight decay hyperparameter with dynamic per-parameter decay rates adapted online via approximate meta-gradient descent
  • Reinterprets weight decay as a forgetting mechanism for continual learning rather than merely a regularizer, drawing analogy to LSTM forget gates but for long-term memory stored in weights
  • Derives FADE for online linear regression and extends it to the cross-entropy loss; applies it to the final layer of neural networks with any base optimizer (SGD, Adam)
  • Shows that adaptive decay (FADE) and adaptive step-size (IDBD) are complementary: jointly they achieve the lowest error, as one controls retention and the other controls learning speed
  • Discovers that applying a suitable fixed non-zero decay only to the final layer is itself a strong but underappreciated baseline for continual learning

Methodology

FADE is derived for online linear regression where at each step the learner receives features $x_t \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and target $y_t^*$. The weight update with per-parameter decay is:

$$w_{t+1}^i = (1 - \lambda_{t+1}^i) w_t^i + \alpha \, \delta_t \, x_t^i$$

where $\lambda^i = \exp(\gamma^i)$ ensures positivity. The meta-parameter $\gamma^i$ is updated by gradient descent on the instantaneous loss, requiring an auxiliary trace $g^i \approx \partial w^i / \partial \gamma^i$ maintained online via forward-mode differentiation:

$$\gamma_{t+1}^i \leftarrow \gamma_t^i + \theta_\lambda \, \delta_t \, x_t^i \, g_t^i$$

$$g_{t+1}^i \leftarrow \left[ g_t^i \left(1 - \lambda_{t+1}^i - \alpha (x_t^i)^2 \right) \right]^+ - \lambda_{t+1}^i w_t^i$$

The key approximation (shared with IDBD) is that changing $\gamma^i$ primarily affects weight $w^i$ itself, with negligible effect on other weights. For neural networks, FADE is applied only to the final linear layer while the base optimizer handles hidden layers. The cross-entropy extension replaces the squared-error gradient with the softmax gradient. Computational overhead is O(d) per step — two additional scalar states ($\gamma^i$, $g^i$) per parameter.

Results & Highlights

  • Linear tracking: FADE+IDBD achieves MSE of 1.246 (lowest overall), outperforming both FADE alone and IDBD alone, confirming complementarity. FADE automatically assigns higher decay to irrelevant features and lower decay to relevant ones.
  • Non-linear tracking (teacher-student network): FADE+SGD achieves MSE of 0.0073 on the final 500K steps, roughly half the error of AdamW (0.0138). FADE+Adam achieves 0.0087.
  • Streaming label-permuted EMNIST: FADE+SGD achieves 80.7% average online accuracy, substantially outperforming the previous best method (weight clipping at 61.2%) and AdamW (37.2%).
  • Robustness: FADE narrows the performance gap across different initial decay rates. For example, on EMNIST with SGD, fixed decay ranges from 53.1% to 80.1% depending on initialization; with FADE, the range compresses to 74.3%–80.7%.
  • Partial permutation: When 24 of 47 classes remain stable, FADE+SGD achieves 84.1%, outperforming all baselines.

Critical Assessment

Novelty (3/5): The idea of adaptive per-parameter weight decay is not entirely new (Hanson & Pratt, 1988; Nakamura & Hong, 2019), but applying it via forward-mode meta-gradients to the online continual learning setting is a meaningful and original contribution. The derivation closely follows the IDBD framework, adapting it from step sizes to decay rates.

Rigor (4/5): Clean theoretical derivation with proper appendices including full gradient computations, cross-entropy extension, and Adam integration. Experiments include sensitivity analysis across initializations and meta-step sizes, multiple seeds, and ablation studies (head-only vs. all-layers FADE, coupled vs. decoupled variants).

Reproducibility (4/5): Algorithm pseudocode is provided. Hyperparameter search spaces and selected values are detailed in appendices. However, no code release is mentioned.

Significance (3.5/5): The results are strong on the chosen benchmarks, particularly the EMNIST result. However, the benchmarks are relatively small-scale (MLPs, 2-layer networks). The limitation that head-only FADE works much better than all-layer FADE, and the lack of evaluation on modern architectures (Transformers, CNNs) or large-scale problems (RL, LLM fine-tuning), tempers the immediate practical impact.

Red flags:

  • The authors note that naive extension of FADE to all layers achieves only 53.5% on EMNIST vs. 80.7% for head-only, suggesting the linear approximation is brittle for hidden layers — a significant scalability concern.
  • No comparison with replay-based continual learning methods or more recent plasticity-maintaining approaches beyond weight clipping.

Strengths

  • Elegant and minimal algorithm: only two additional scalar states per parameter with O(d) per-step cost
  • Strong empirical gains across three diverse settings (linear, non-linear tracking, streaming classification)
  • Excellent robustness to initialization of the decay rate — a key practical benefit
  • Discovers interpretable behavior: fast-changing outputs get high decay, stable outputs get low decay
  • Thorough sensitivity analysis across meta-step-sizes and initial decay rates
  • The insight that "head-only fixed decay is a strong baseline" is a valuable community contribution

Limitations

  • Scalability to all layers: The linear meta-gradient approximation degrades significantly when applied to hidden layers, limiting FADE to the final layer. Extending to deeper networks remains an open problem.
  • Small-scale evaluation only: All experiments use MLPs with at most 2 hidden layers. No evaluation on CNNs, Transformers, or large-scale problems.
  • No comparison with replay-based methods: The baselines are limited to optimization-based approaches. Comparison with experience replay or regularization-based continual learning methods (EWC, SI, etc.) is absent.
  • Coupled variant underperforms: The theoretically motivated coupled (L2-regularized) variant slightly underperforms the decoupled version, which is not well explained.
  • Meta-step-size still requires tuning: Although FADE is robust to $\gamma_0$, the meta-step-size $\theta_\lambda$ still requires selection, partially reducing the "automatic" appeal.

相关性

FADE 提出了一种优雅的、计算开销极低的在线自适应权重衰减方法，对持续学习和在线学习领域有直接贡献。其"将权重衰减视为遗忘机制"的视角具有启发性，但目前仅在小规模 MLP 上验证，距离实际大规模应用（如 LLM 持续微调）仍有较大差距。对于研究持续学习中稳定性-可塑性权衡的学者而言，这是一篇值得关注的论文。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can FADE be extended to hidden layers using more accurate meta-gradient approximations (e.g., by tracking cross-layer dependencies or using higher-order traces)?
  2. How does FADE interact with normalization layers (LayerNorm, BatchNorm) in modern architectures, where the effective weight dynamics differ from the linear setting?
  3. Would combining FADE with replay buffers or regularization-based continual learning methods (e.g., EWC, SI) yield complementary benefits?
  4. Can FADE's meta-gradient framework be applied to other per-parameter hyperparameters beyond decay rate and step size (e.g., dropout rates, noise injection levels)?
  5. How does FADE perform on reinforcement learning problems where non-stationarity arises naturally from the changing policy?
