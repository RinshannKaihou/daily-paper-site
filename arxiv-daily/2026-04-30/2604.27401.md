> **一句话总结：** 本文提出"扰动探测"方法，仅用两次前向传播即可识别LLM中驱动特定行为的FFN神经元，发现安全拒绝由约50个神经元控制（占总量0.014%），RLHF通过注意力权重路由信号到预存的FFN基础设施来控制行为，且FFN/Skip信号比可预测干预方式——消融还是方向注入。

> **TL;DR:** Perturbation probing identifies causal FFN neurons in aligned LLMs using just two forward passes per prompt with no backpropagation. Across 13 models and four architecture families, it reveals two circuit structures: **opposition circuits** (where RLHF opposes a pre-training tendency, concentrated in ~50 FFN neurons amenable to ablation/amplification) and **routing circuits** (pre-training behaviors distributed through attention, addressable only via direction injection). On Qwen3-4B, ~50 neurons (0.014% of total) control the safety refusal template—ablation changes 80% of response formats on 520 AdvBench prompts with near-zero harmful compliance (3/520). The FFN/Skip signal ratio, computed from the same two forward passes, predicts which intervention applies (R²=0.81). On Qwen3.5-2B, ablating 20 neurons eliminates multi-turn sycophantic capitulation entirely, while amplifying 10 neurons improves TruthfulQA correction from 52% to 88%. The paper also shows RLHF modifies attention weights to route signals through pre-existing FFN neurons—no detectable weight changes at the identified neurons themselves.

## 核心贡献

- 提出**扰动探测（Perturbation Probing）**方法：通过两次前向传播计算每个FFN神经元的符号重要性分数 $I_n = c_n \cdot \Delta a_n$（结构耦合 × 扰动响应），无需反向传播或训练，即可生成任务特异性的因果假设。
- 发现LLM行为由**两种电路结构**组织：**对立电路**（RLHF对抗预训练倾向，信号集中在FFN，可通过消融/放大干预）和**路由电路**（预训练行为通过注意力分布，仅可通过方向注入干预）。
- 揭示安全拒绝的**三层结构**：第一层（~50个神经元的RLHF模板，脆弱）→ 第二层（预训练中的危害警告，稳健）→ 第三层（事实性安全知识，不可破坏）。消融仅剥离最外层模板，底层安全知识完好。
- 证明RLHF**并未直接修改**所识别的FFN神经元权重，而是通过修改注意力权重将信号路由到预存的FFN基础设施。
- 提出了**FFN/Skip信号比**作为零成本诊断指标，预测干预方式（R²=0.81）。

## Key Contributions

- **Perturbation probing pipeline**: A two-stage method (hypothesis generation via 2 forward passes + one-time causal validation sweep of ~150 passes) that computes signed importance $I_n = c_n \cdot \Delta a_n$ for every FFN neuron, where $c_n$ is the structural coupling from weights and $\Delta a_n$ is the perturbation response. The signed product selects neurons that both respond to the perturbation and push in a consistent behavioral direction, avoiding the cancellation problem of unsigned ranking.
- **Two circuit structures**: Opposition circuits (FFN/Skip > 0.3) where RLHF concentrates behavioral control in specific FFN neurons, and routing circuits (FFN/Skip < 0.2) where behavior flows through distributed attention. The FFN/Skip ratio distinguishes these and predicts the appropriate intervention mode.
- **Three behavioral regimes of safety**: Regime 1 (~50 neurons, RLHF refusal template, ablatable), Regime 2 (pre-training harm warnings surviving 500-neuron ablation), Regime 3 (indestructible factual safety knowledge). Ablation peels back only the fragile template layer.
- **Push-pull architecture**: 22 gatekeepers ($c_n > 0$, promote refusal) and 28 amplifiers ($c_n < 0$, suppress compliance) form an emergent structure not explicitly trained by RLHF. The two arms operate independently with near-perfect additivity ($\epsilon = 0.022 \pm 0.018$).
- **RLHF routing insight**: Comparing base vs. instruct weights of Qwen2.5-7B shows 100% of RLHF modifications above salience threshold are in attention projections; the identified FFN neurons have 0/50 overlap with weight-changed parameters, suggesting RLHF routes signals through pre-existing FFN infrastructure.
- **Cross-architecture topology spectrum**: Qwen (concentrated FFN bottleneck, −61% ablation effect) → Llama (distributed across skip connections) → Gemma (normalization-shielded, 0% effect).

## Methodology

The method operates in two stages:

**Stage 1 — Hypothesis generation (2 forward passes per prompt):**
1. Define a behavioral observable as a logit gap $F(X) = \bar{z}_R(X) - \bar{z}_A(X)$, the difference between average refusal and affirmation token logits at the first generation position. This is a linear functional of the last-layer hidden state: $F = \bar{d}_F \cdot h^{(L)} + b$.
2. Run two forward passes: one on the original prompt $X$, one on a perturbed version $\tilde{X}$ (e.g., BPE scrambling for safety, wrong-vs-correct premises for sycophancy).
3. Compute signed importance for each neuron: $I_n = c_n \cdot \Delta a_n$, where $c_n = d_F \cdot W_{\text{down}}^{(\ell)}[:, n]$ (structural coupling from weights) and $\Delta a_n = a_n(\tilde{X}) - a_n(X)$ (perturbation response).
4. Rank neurons by $|I_n|$, select top-N (N=50 throughout).
5. Compute the FFN/Skip ratio: $|d_F \cdot \text{FFN}^{(L-1)}| / |d_F \cdot h^{(L-1)}|$, which predicts whether ablation (opposition) or direction injection (routing) will work.

**Stage 2 — Causal validation (~150 forward passes, one-time):**
- **Ablation** (necessity): Zero $W_{\text{down}}$ columns for top-N neurons.
- **Patching** (sufficiency): Replace top-N activations from harmful onto benign prompts.
- **Restoration** (closure): Patch top-N neurons back on scrambled prompts.

Three intervention modes are defined: Mode 1 (ablation), Mode 2 (amplification by scaling $W_{\text{down}}[:, n] \times \alpha$), Mode 3 (direction injection $h^{(\ell)} += \beta \cdot d_F$ for routing circuits).

## Results & Highlights

- **Safety circuit (Qwen3-4B)**: 50 neurons (0.014% of 350,208) concentrated in layers 29–35. Ablation drops refusal gap by 64%, changes response format on 80% of 520 AdvBench prompts, but only 3/520 cases produce harmful compliance (all with disclaimers). Split-half stability: 94% overlap (47/50 neurons).
- **HarmBench generalization**: Gap drops of −56.6% on 200 held-out HarmBench prompts ($p < 10^{-74}$), uniform across harm categories.
- **Language switching (direction injection)**: On 3 of 19 tested models meeting three conditions (bilingual training, FFN/Skip ∈ [0.3, 1.1], linear representability), injecting the EN→ZH direction switches output language on 99.1% of 580 benchmark prompts. Math, CoT, code, and factual circuits are immune on all models.
- **Sycophancy (Qwen3.5-2B)**: 20 neurons govern a "second-guessing" circuit. Ablation eliminates multi-turn sycophantic capitulation (36.7% → 0%, 30 questions). 2× amplification of 10 neurons improves TruthfulQA correction from 52% to 88%.
- **Phase transitions**: Sycophancy ablation shows discrete dose-response staircase (two critical neurons flip ~5 prompts each, cooperatively).
- **Cross-architecture spectrum**: R²=0.81 correlation between FFN/Skip ratio and ablation effectiveness. Gemma-2-9B is completely shielded (0% effect at any dose); Qwen3-4B has the most concentrated circuit.
- **Gradient comparison**: Perturbation probing outperforms gradient methods on Qwen (−56.2% vs −40.7% at N=10) but underperforms on Llama (−30.2% vs −43.4% at N=50), revealing method advantage depends on RLHF recipe, not FFN/Skip ratio alone.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty (High):** The signed importance decomposition ($c_n \cdot \Delta a_n$) is a genuinely novel contribution that combines structural information (from weights) with dynamic information (from perturbation) in a principled way. The two-circuit-structure taxonomy (opposition vs. routing) and the FFN/Skip diagnostic are elegant and empirically well-supported. The finding that RLHF modifies attention rather than FFN weights is significant and aligns with concurrent work (Zhou et al., 2025 on attention heads).

**Rigor (High):** The paper includes extensive controls: random neuron ablation (±1.5%), split-half stability (94%), per-neuron additivity testing ($\epsilon = 0.022$), three perturbation types for confound control (30-35/50 overlap), cross-architecture replication, and comparison with gradient methods. The honest reporting of failures (16/19 models fail language injection; Gemma is completely shielded) strengthens credibility.

**Reproducibility (Moderate-High):** The method uses only forward passes, works at any precision, and the pipeline runs in under 10 minutes on a single A100 for 4B models. The top-20 neuron indices are provided per model. However, the perturbation design is heuristic (acknowledged by the authors), and some token sets require manual curation. The paper promises supplementary JSON files with full neuron lists.

**Significance (High):** This is among the most comprehensive neuron-level circuit analyses of RLHF-aligned behavior to date. The three-regime safety architecture (fragile RLHF template → robust pre-training warnings → indestructible factual knowledge) has important implications for safety auditing. The precision editing result (2× amplification improving TruthfulQA by 36pp) suggests a path toward targeted alignment correction without retraining.

**Red flags:**
1. **Perturbation dependence**: The method's output depends on the choice of perturbation, which is heuristic. The authors acknowledge this but do not provide a theory of optimal perturbation design. The 30-35/50 overlap across perturbation types is reassuring but not complete.
2. **Scale limitations**: The most dramatic results (20-neuron sycophancy circuit) are on a 2B model. The 4B model's sycophancy circuit is more distributed and never fully suppressed. The method is not tested above 14B parameters for safety circuits.
3. **Binary decision scope**: The method fundamentally targets first-token binary decisions. Multi-token and high-dimensional behaviors produce only "readout" neurons. This is a significant scope limitation.
4. **Template-layer only**: The identified neurons control the RLHF refusal template, not safety knowledge itself. The authors are transparent about this, but the "50 neurons control safety" framing in the abstract could be misinterpreted—the model still mostly refuses after ablation, just with different phrasing.
5. **Model-family dependence**: The method's advantage over gradient methods is architecture-dependent (wins on Qwen, loses on Llama). There is no predictive theory for which method will win on a new model.

## Strengths

- Elegant two-factor decomposition (structural coupling × perturbation response) that is simultaneously task-specific and structural.
- Comprehensive cross-model, cross-circuit evaluation (13 models, 8 circuits, 4 families).
- Honest scope delineation: clear about what works and what doesn't, with explicit failure modes.
- The FFN/Skip diagnostic provides a practical zero-cost predictor for intervention strategy.
- The RLHF routing insight (attention modification, not FFN weight changes) is a finding with implications beyond the method itself.
- Works at any numerical precision including int4, with only forward passes—practical for large models.

## Limitations

- **Rank-1 scope**: Only applicable to binary first-token decisions; multi-token reasoning, chain-of-thought, and factual recall circuits produce non-causal readout neurons.
- **Opposition dependence**: Mode 1/2 requires RLHF to oppose a pre-training tendency. Reinforcing behaviors (language, code style) are invisible to ablation.
- **Direction injection fragility**: Only 3 of 19 tested models support successful language injection, requiring three jointly necessary conditions (bilingual training, moderate FFN/Skip, linear representability). Math, CoT, code, and factual circuits are immune on all models.
- **Cross-circuit coupling**: Direction injection is not surgical—language injection at high strength reduces the safety gap by ~50%, indicating behavioral circuits are weakly coupled.
- **Scale gap**: The most impressive precision-editing results are on 2B models. Scaling behavior is unclear—the paper shows circuit topology depends on RLHF recipe, not parameter count, but does not test the editing results on larger models.
- **Template layer only**: The method audits the fragile RLHF template layer; robust pre-training safety behaviors (Regimes 2-3) are not addressable.

## Relevance

本文与LLM可解释性和电路分析高度相关。核心贡献在于提出了一种低成本（两次前向传播）的神经元级因果归因方法，并系统性地揭示了RLHF对齐行为在FFN中的电路拓扑结构——这直接推进了"对齐行为如何在模型内部实现"这一核心问题。FFN/Skip诊断指标和三种干预模式的框架为后续电路研究提供了实用的分析工具。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Can the FFN/Skip diagnostic be extended to predict not just intervention mode but also the minimum number of neurons needed for a given behavioral effect (the circuit width $\Gamma$)?
2. The RLHF routing insight suggests attention modifications route through pre-existing FFN neurons—can this be leveraged to design RLHF training procedures that produce more robust (less concentrated) safety circuits?
3. How does the opposition circuit structure interact with jailbreak attacks? If 50 neurons control the refusal template, can adversarial inputs be designed to specifically suppress those neurons' activations?
4. The phase-transition behavior in the 2B sycophancy circuit suggests discrete behavioral regimes—do these correspond to identifiable changes in the residual-stream geometry (e.g., basin transitions in the logit landscape)?
5. Can perturbation probing be combined with attention-head analysis (as in Zhou et al., 2025) to provide a unified FFN+attention circuit map for behaviors that span both vertex types?
