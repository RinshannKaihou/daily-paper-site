核心贡献

  • 提出了 Strait 系统，专门针对高 GPU 利用率下双优先级（高/低）推理流量的截止期限满足问题。
  • 设计了自适应延迟预测模型，建模数据传输阶段的潜在竞争以及内核执行阶段的干扰。
  • 实现了优先级感知调度，对不同优先级请求进行差异化处理。
  • 与基于软件定义抢占的方法相比，展现出更公平的性能表现。

Key Contributions

  • Introduces Strait, a serving system targeting deadline satisfaction for dual-priority inference workloads under intense GPU utilization.
  • Develops an adaptive latency prediction model that accounts for contention during data transfer and kernel execution interference under concurrency.
  • Implements priority-aware scheduling that delivers differentiated handling of high- and low-priority requests.
  • Demonstrates more equitable performance compared to software-defined preemption baselines.

Methodology

Strait addresses two shortcomings in existing ML inference serving systems: (1) lack of task prioritization support and (2) poor latency estimation under concurrent GPU execution. The system models potential contention during data transfer phases and accounts for kernel execution interference through an adaptive prediction model. Using these predictions, Strait performs priority-aware scheduling to deliver differentiated service for dual-priority inference traffic (high-priority vs. low-priority). The evaluation is conducted under intense workloads on deployed GPUs in on-premises scenarios.

Results & Highlights

  • Reduces deadline violations for high-priority tasks by 1.02 to 11.18 percentage points compared to baselines.
  • Incurs only acceptable costs on low-priority tasks, maintaining a reasonable performance trade-off.
  • Outperforms software-defined preemption approaches in terms of equitable performance across priority levels.
  • Specifically targets and validates under high GPU utilization scenarios, which are the most challenging for latency guarantees.

Authors & Credibility

The paper is authored by Haidong Zhao and Nikolaos Georgantas. The work addresses a practical systems problem in ML inference serving with empirical evaluation. The scope is well-defined — focusing on on-premises GPU deployments with dual-priority traffic — which suggests a realistic and targeted research contribution rather than overgeneralized claims.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: Moderate. Priority-aware scheduling and interference modeling are not new concepts individually, but combining them specifically for dual-priority DNN inference serving under high GPU utilization is a reasonable contribution. The adaptive prediction model for kernel execution interference is the most novel component.
  • Rigor: The abstract reports concrete improvements (1.02–11.18 pp), but without the full paper it is unclear what baselines are compared against, what GPU hardware is used, what models are served, and how the workload intensity is defined. The claim of "acceptable costs" on low-priority tasks needs quantitative backing.
  • Reproducibility: Cannot fully assess without the full paper. The use of standard GPU deployments and DNN inference workloads suggests reasonable reproducibility potential if implementation details are provided.
  • Significance: Practical relevance is high — priority-aware ML serving is important for production deployments. However, the restriction to dual-priority (rather than multi-priority) traffic may limit generality.
  • Red flags: The "acceptable costs" phrasing is vague without numbers. The scope is limited to on-premises scenarios, and generalizability to cloud or multi-tenant environments is unaddressed.

Strengths

  • Addresses a practically important problem: deadline guarantees for prioritized ML inference under GPU contention.
  • Concrete quantitative improvement reported for high-priority deadline violations.
  • More equitable performance than preemption-based alternatives, which often starve lower-priority tasks.
  • Focuses on realistic high-utilization scenarios rather than lightly-loaded benchmarks.

Limitations

  • Restricted to dual-priority traffic; multi-priority or continuous priority schemes are not addressed.
  • Scope limited to on-premises GPU deployments; cloud and multi-tenant settings are not discussed.
  • The abstract does not quantify the overhead or degradation on low-priority tasks beyond "acceptable costs."
  • Without the full paper, the generality of the adaptive prediction model across different DNN architectures and GPU types cannot be assessed.
