> **一句话总结：** 本文提出 νGPT 参数化方案，基于对齐指数（alignment exponents）的"中间对齐"假设（指数 3/4）修正了 nGPT 的 μP 框架，使学习率在模型宽度、深度和 token 数量三个维度上同时实现迁移，且性能不弱于单独调参的 nGPT 基线。

> **TL;DR:** νGPT re-parameterizes the Normalized Transformer (nGPT) so that a single learning rate tuned on a small model transfers to larger width (n_heads 8→40, up to 3.22B params on FineWeb-Edu), deeper models (nlayers 8→128, up to 2.55B params), and longer token horizons (up to 225k iterations), matching per-size-tuned baseline nGPT's validation loss (e.g., 2.46 vs 2.461 at the largest width) while baseline nGPT exhibits no width transfer; the key correction is a hidden-LR scaling of m_width^{-3/4} derived from a measured "mid alignment" exponent of 3/4, versus μP's m_width^{-1}.

## 核心贡献

- 提出 νGPT 参数化方案，是 nGPT 的修正版本，首次在归一化 Transformer 上同时实现学习率在宽度、深度和 token 训练量三个维度上的迁移。
- 通过实测发现 nGPT 中权重-激活的对齐指数不满足标准 μP 的假设（ω_output=1），确认 ω_hidden=ω_output=1/2，并据此提出"中间对齐"（mid alignment）假设（max{α,ν}=3/4），推导出隐藏层与输出层学习率的 m_width^{-3/4} 修正（而非 μP 的 m_width^{-1}）。
- 引入深度修正：将残差 LERP 中的初始化参数 α_{A,init}、α_{M,init} 按 m_depth^{-1} 缩放（对应 α_depth=1，即 CompleteP 思路），而隐藏层学习率本身不做深度修正。
- 发现最优学习率随 token 数量按 m_data^{-1/3} 衰减，实测拟合指数为 -0.34，与 Bjorck et al. (2025) 的独立测量一致。
- 将 nGPT 中依赖 d_model^{-1/2} 的 scale 超参数（α_{A,scale}、α_{M,scale}、s_{qk,scale}、s_{z,scale}）改为固定常数 0.03，消除隐式模型规模依赖。
- 实验表明 νGPT 在宽度迁移上略优于直接套用 μP 风格修正的"CompleteP"方案，且不会破坏深度迁移（而 Depth-μP 风格的 η_hidden 深度修正会破坏迁移）。

## Key Contributions

- Propose νGPT, a novel parameterization of nGPT that achieves hyperparameter (learning rate) transfer across width, depth, and token horizon simultaneously, with no loss in performance versus individually tuned baseline nGPT.
- Empirically measure alignment exponents in nGPT and confirm ω_hidden = ω_output = 1/2 (contradicting μP's ω_output = 1 assumption); introduce the "mid alignment" assumption (max{α,ν} = 3/4) to derive hidden and output learning rate corrections of m_width^{-3/4} rather than μP's m_width^{-1}.
- Show that depth transfer is obtained by scaling the LERP initialization parameters α_{A,init} and α_{M,init} by m_depth^{-1} (corresponding to α_depth = 1), while NOT applying a depth correction to η_hidden — and demonstrate that a Depth-μP style m_depth^{-1/2} correction to η_hidden would break depth transfer.
- Empirically fit the token-horizon learning rate decay as (iter. count)^{-0.34}, close to the adopted m_data^{-1/3}, consistent with concurrent and prior measurements (Bjorck et al., 2025; Ren et al., 2026).
- Replace nGPT's d_model-dependent scale parameters with fixed constants (0.03), eliminating implicit model-size dependence.
- Provide complete theoretical derivations (Appendix A) via signGD-like Adam update scaling and alignment exponent analysis, with lemmas proving stability (Desideratum 1) and non-trivial feature learning (Desideratum 2) at growing width.

## Methodology

The authors build on the Normalized Transformer (nGPT, Loshchilov et al., 2025), which replaces standard RMSNorm/LayerNorm with ℓ₂-normalization Norm(·), uses residual-post-norm, and replaces standard residual connections with a trainable "LERP" (linear interpolation) involving nonneg-constrained vectors α_A, α_M. Query/key vectors are also normalized. All weight matrix columns/rows are re-normalized before each optimization step.

**νGPT re-parameterization (Table 1)**, where m_width = width/base_width, m_depth = depth/base_depth, m_data = iter_count/base_iter_count:

1. Global learning rate multiplied by m_data^{-1/3}.
2. Embedding learning rate η_input multiplied by m_width^{-1/2} (same as μP).
3. Hidden weights (MLP + Attention) learning rate η_hidden and unembedding learning rate η_output multiplied by m_width^{-3/4} (mid alignment; vs μP's m_width^{-1}).
4. Optional additional constant multipliers on η_input, η_output tuned on the base model.
5. Scale parameters α_{A,scale}, α_{M,scale}, s_{qk,scale}, s_{z,scale} set to fixed constant 0.03 (instead of d_model^{-1/2} as in original nGPT).
6. α_{A,init} and α_{M,init} set to 0.05·m_depth^{-1} (instead of constant 0.05).

**Derivation basis**: Using alignment exponents (Definition 3.1, from Everett et al. 2024) and assuming signGD-like Adam update scaling (w(t+1)−w(t) ≍ η), the authors derive maximal stable learning rates scaling as η_hidden ≍ d_model^{-max{α_hidden,ν_hidden}} and η_output ≲ d_model^{-max{α_output,ν_output}}. Measured ω values (Figure 5) decisively confirm ω_hidden = ω_output = 1/2. The "mid alignment" value max{α,ν} = 3/4 is chosen as the exact middle between the "no alignment" (1/2) and "full alignment" (1) regimes and is shown to match loss-decrease-weighted measurements well.

**Training setup**: FineWeb-Edu dataset, OLMo 2 tokenizer (vocab 100,352), sequence length 4096, batch size 64. Optimizer: AdamW with weight decay 0.0, β1=0.9, β2=0.95, ε=10^{-16}, no warm-up, cosine schedule decaying to 10% of peak. Base depth and width = 10 (head dimension 102 = floor(1024/10)). Fixed-iteration experiments use 80,000 steps (≈21B tokens). "20 tokens per parameter" for compute-optimal runs. Implementation in a fork of TorchTitan. Validation loss EMA with β=0.95 (unless averaging over 3 init seeds). Width sweep: n_heads 8→40 with nlayers=12 fixed (0.26B→3.22B params). Depth sweep: nlayers 8→128 with n_heads=12, head_dim=102 fixed (0.39B→2.55B params).

## Results & Highlights

- **Width transfer (Figure 2, ~21B tokens)**: νGPT with a single LR yields validation loss 2.46 at n_heads=40 (3.22B params) versus baseline nGPT's 2.461 — essentially identical performance — but baseline nGPT shows the optimal LR drifting rightward across widths (no single LR works), while νGPT shows "perfect transfer." At n_heads=8, νGPT achieves 2.882 vs nGPT's 2.89.
- **Depth transfer (Figure 3, 80k steps)**: νGPT at nlayers=128 (2.55B params) reaches validation loss 2.462 versus baseline nGPT's 2.465 — comparable, but νGPT shows lower LR sensitivity and better stability at large depths. Both methods show decent depth transfer, though baseline nGPT's deepest models become "somewhat unstable."
- **Compute-optimal combined transfer (Figure 1, nlayers=nheads, 20 tokens/param)**: At the largest size (nlayers=nheads=22), νGPT achieves 2.421±0.00109 versus baseline nGPT's 2.421±0.00205 — identical mean performance with roughly half the seed variance, and νGPT requires no per-size LR tuning. At size 8, νGPT gets 3.165±0.00344 vs nGPT's 3.172±0.00236.
- **Token horizon (Figure 9)**: A fixed νGPT model (nlayers=nheads=12) trained without token-count corrections shows optimal peak LR decreasing as (iter. count)^{-0.34}, close to the predicted -1/3; validation loss decreases from 2.982 (25k iters) to 2.656 (225k iters) as horizon grows.
- **Alignment exponents (Figure 5, loss-decrease-weighted)**: All measured ω values cluster at 0.500–0.508 (e.g., E_output 0.500, W 0.500, W_O 0.503), confirming ω=1/2. The max{α,ν} values cluster near 3/4 (e.g., E_output: α=0.735, ν=0.828 → max=0.828; W: α=0.554, ν=0.682 → max=0.682), supporting the mid alignment assumption.
- **νGPT vs μP-style width corrections (Figure 7)**: μP-style "CompleteP" corrections cause the LR to "slightly drift to the right" (e.g., n_heads=40 gives 2.46 with CompleteP vs 2.459 with νGPT), while νGPT provides "essentially perfect transfer."
- **Depth-μP correction breaks transfer (Figure 8)**: Adding a m_depth^{-1/2} correction to η_hidden (Depth-μP style, α_depth=1/2) causes the LR to "strongly drift to the right," confirming η_hidden should not be depth-corrected in nGPT.

## Critical Assessment

- **Mid alignment is an empirical fit, not a theorem**: The exponent 3/4 is chosen as the "exact middle" between the no-alignment (1/2) and full-alignment (1) regimes and is justified by loss-decrease-weighted averaging of measured alignment exponents (Figure 5). The authors acknowledge alignment exponents "vary significantly during training" and that the loss-decrease weighting "is not obviously the right weighting." This is a principled heuristic, not a derivation from first principles.
- **Token-horizon exponent lacks theory**: The -1/3 (measured -0.34) exponent is empirically fit; the authors explicitly state "there is no well-known and well-tested theoretical framework predicting the correct scaling" and that prior SDE-based predictions of -1/2 are "too large." They list predicting the ~1/3 exponent analytically as an open future direction.
- **Unexplained baseline depth transfer**: The authors themselves flag that "baseline nGPT already shows decent transfer over depth" and that the minimal depth corrections "may not be necessary" — and note "explaining this phenomenon theoretically can be an interesting future direction." The trainable α_A, α_M parameters likely act as automatic depth scalars, but this is not proven.
- **Limited scale**: Experiments reach at most 3.22B parameters (width sweep) and 2.55B parameters (depth sweep); transfer to frontier-scale (10B+) models is unverified.
- **Single dataset/tokenizer**: Only FineWeb-Edu with the OLMo 2 tokenizer is evaluated; generalization to other data distributions and tokenizers is untested.
- **No code release**: The PDF does not indicate public code availability, limiting immediate reproducibility (though Table 1 fully specifies all hyperparameters).

## Strengths

- Clean problem formulation with a comprehensive solution covering all three scaling dimensions (width, depth, data) simultaneously.
- Strong theoretical grounding via alignment exponents, with full derivations in Appendix A (Lemmas A.3–A.9) proving stability and non-trivial feature learning.
- Extensive ablations isolating each correction (width, depth, token horizon, alignment exponent choice, μP comparison, Depth-μP counterexample).
- The alignment exponent framework is interpretable and extensible — directly measurable, not assumed.
- Complete hyperparameter specification (Table 1) makes the method immediately usable.
- Honest about discrepancies: explicitly notes baseline nGPT's surprising depth transfer, the empirical nature of the 3/4 and -1/3 exponents, and the disagreement with prior SDE-based predictions.

## Limitations

- The mid alignment assumption (exponent 3/4) is an empirical fit averaged with a specific (loss-decrease) weighting; it may not hold at all scales or for all architectures.
- Experiments are limited to models with at most ~3.22B parameters; scaling to frontier model sizes is untested.
- The token-horizon scaling exponent (-1/3) is empirically determined with no rigorous theoretical derivation.
- No comparison to alternative approaches like per-module update normalization (Large et al., 2024) or interaction with the Muon optimizer (noted in concurrent Ren et al., 2026).
- The depth transfer mechanism in nGPT is not fully understood theoretically — baseline nGPT already shows decent depth transfer without corrections.
- Only evaluated on a single dataset (FineWeb-Edu) and tokenizer (OLMo 2); interaction with weight decay (a confounder noted by Kosson et al., 2026) is sidestepped because nGPT uses no weight decay, but this also means results may not transfer to weight-decay regimes.

## Relevance

高度相关于「LLM Training Stability & Observability」与「ML Reliability & Mathematical Foundations」：νGPT 基于对齐指数推导出的学习率缩放律为归一化 Transformer 的训练稳定性提供理论保证，直接解决大规模模型训练中超参数迁移这一核心工程问题；其对齐指数的实测方法也是一种训练动态可观测性工具，可视为训练过程 observability 的实例。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the mid alignment assumption (exponent 3/4) hold for models at 10B+ parameter scale, or does it shift toward full alignment (1) as in standard μP?
- How does νGPT interact with other optimizer choices (e.g., Muon, Sophia) beyond Adam, and with the concurrent Muon-based normalized variant of Ren et al. (2026)?
- Can the token-horizon exponent (-1/3) be derived theoretically from a stochastic differential equation perspective on Adam/signGD dynamics, replacing the too-large -1/2 prediction?
- Does νGPT transfer extend to batch size scaling, mixture-of-experts architectures, or multi-modal models?
- What explains the surprising depth transfer in baseline nGPT without corrections — is it the trainable LERP parameters α_A, α_M acting as automatic depth scalars, and can this be proven?
- Do the alignment exponents remain stable under weight decay, and does νGPT theory extend to the weight-decay regime studied by Kosson et al. (2026)?
