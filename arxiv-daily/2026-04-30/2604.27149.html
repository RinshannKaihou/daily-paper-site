ConformaDecompose: Explaining Uncertainty via Calibration Localization

arXiv: 2604.27149

Date: 2026-04-29

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.27149v1

一句话总结： ConformaDecompose 通过逐步局部化校准集（而非使用全局单一阈值），将保形预测的不确定性分解为"可归因于校准异质性的可约分量"和"局部不可约分量"，为每个预测实例提供直观的不确定性来源解释。
TL;DR: ConformaDecompose introduces an instance-wise explainability framework for conformal prediction that progressively localises calibration support via model-aware clustering and soft reweighting, decomposing prediction interval width into calibration-induced reducible epistemic uncertainty and persistent irreducible uncertainty, without modifying the predictor or its coverage guarantees.

核心贡献

  • 提出了 ConformaDecompose 框架：通过对校准集的逐步局部化，将保形预测区间分解为"可约的校准诱导认知不确定性"和"不可约分量"，提供实例级不确定性归因。
  • 设计了基于模型感知表示（输入特征 + 模型预测 + 预测不确定性）的校准聚类与排序机制，以及贪心接受-回退策略，确保区间宽度单调递减。
  • 引入 Reducibility-Instability Alignment (RIA) 诊断指标，验证可约不确定性与局部模型不稳定性之间的关联。
  • 在多个基准数据集和真实场景（汽车残值估计、二手车定价）上验证了框架的有效性和可解释性。

Key Contributions

  • Instance-wise uncertainty explainability via calibration localisation: ConformaDecompose localises and reweights calibration data based on model-aware representations (input geometry, prediction, and uncertainty), traces the contraction of conformal-style intervals along a greedy localisation path, and decomposes calibration-induced epistemic uncertainty into reducible and persistent components per test instance.
  • Model-aware calibration space construction: Each calibration point is embedded into a joint space encoding input features, model predictions, and predictive uncertainty (Eq. 4), with tunable weights (λ_X, λ_μ, λ_σ). K-Means clustering in this space groups behaviorally similar calibration samples.
  • Greedy localisation with soft reweighting: Rather than hard subsetting, clusters are progressively downweighted via a small constant ε while relevant clusters retain unit weight. A greedy accept-revert rule ensures the accepted localisation path yields monotonically non-increasing interval width (Algorithm 3).
  • Diagnostic metrics — RIA and Granularity Sensitivity (GS): The Reducibility-Instability Alignment (Pearson correlation between local model instability proxy σ* and reducible uncertainty) quantifies whether calibration-induced reducibility covaries with epistemic fragility. GS measures stability of this alignment across clustering resolutions.

Methodology

The framework operates in three stages after training a base predictor:

  1. Model-aware embedding and clustering (Algorithm 1): Calibration instances are mapped to z_i = [λ_X x_i, λ_μ f̂(x_i), λ_σ σ̂(x_i)], clustering via K-Means into K clusters. Clusters are ranked by distance to the test instance embedding z* in decreasing order, producing a global-to-local relevance ordering π.
  1. Progressive calibration localisation (Algorithms 2–3): Starting from the full calibration set (step s=0), the least relevant cluster is progressively downweighted (weight ε instead of 1). At each step, a weighted (1−α)-quantile is computed from the reweighted nonconformity scores, yielding a localised prediction interval C_w(x) = [μ − σ q̂_w, μ + σ* q̂_w]. The greedy rule accepts a step only if the interval width does not increase.
  1. Empirical uncertainty decomposition: The global width W_0 minus the minimum accepted width W_min defines the reducible calibration-induced epistemic uncertainty W_red (Definition 1). The ratio R_red = W_red / W_0 (Definition 3) captures the relative reducibility. A four-panel diagnostic visualisation shows: (A) localisation trajectory, (B) relative uncertainty reduction, (C) cluster-wise width changes, (D) calibration support heatmap.

The local model instability proxy σ* is computed once per test instance and held fixed: for Random Forests, it is the standard deviation of tree predictions; for Ridge Regression, an auxiliary regressor on absolute residuals. This ensures interval width changes reflect only calibration support variations, not model adaptation.

Results & Highlights

  • Strong width alignment (RIAW): Across four benchmark datasets (Power Plant, Parkinson's, Airfoil, Abalone) and both models (Random Forest, Ridge Regression), RIAW values are consistently > 0.9 for Random Forest, confirming that calibration-induced reducible uncertainty magnitude scales reliably with local model instability.
  • Variable ratio alignment (RIAR): Alignment with relative reducibility ranges from negative to strongly positive (e.g., −0.26 for Parkinson's RF at K=8 vs. 0.56 for Abalone RF at K=8), reflecting that relative composition depends on irreducible noise and localisation granularity — a nuanced finding hidden by interval width alone.
  • Robustness to granularity: Width-based Granularity Sensitivity (GS_RIAW) is consistently small (< 0.05 for RF across all datasets), indicating stability of the diagnostic relationship across clustering resolutions. Ratio-based GS shows higher sensitivity in boundary cases.
  • Baseline comparison (Table 8): ConformaDecompose achieves up to 1.0% reducible uncertainty ratio (Abalone, RR) while maintaining near-nominal coverage (~87–91%), surpassing Normalized CP, CQR, UACQR-S, and Mondrian CP in interpretable uncertainty decomposition. Notably, some baselines (e.g., CQR on Parkinson's) achieve narrower intervals by aggressively removing aleatoric uncertainty rather than addressing epistemic reducibility.
  • Real-world case study: On the Craigslist Used Cars dataset (~73K calibration, ~220K training), ConformaDecompose identifies that very old/high-mileage vehicle segments inflate uncertainty for newer, high-value vehicles, with a 6.39% reducibility at K=4. An optimal clustering granularity exists (K=6) that maximises reducible uncertainty, balancing localisation expressiveness with calibration stability.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty (Moderate-High): The idea of progressive calibration localisation with soft reweighting is novel and well-motivated. The four-panel diagnostic and the RIA/GS metrics are useful contributions. However, the core mechanism (cluster → rank → reweight → greedy path) is conceptually straightforward, and the paper is positioned as a diagnostic/explainability tool rather than a method that improves prediction accuracy or coverage.
  • Rigor (Moderate): The experimental evaluation is thorough across four datasets and two models with two granularity settings. However, the paper does not provide formal conformal guarantees for the localised intervals (acknowledged by the authors). The RIA metric is purely correlational (Pearson), and the authors appropriately note all alignments are associative, not causal. The choice of K=5 and K=8 is somewhat arbitrary without a principled selection mechanism.
  • Reproducibility (High): Code is publicly available. All datasets are standard UCI benchmarks. Implementation details (scikit-learn defaults, RF with 500 estimators) are clearly specified.
  • Significance (Moderate): The framework adds interpretability to conformal prediction intervals, which is valuable for high-stakes decision-making. However, the actual reducible uncertainty ratios are small (≤1% on benchmarks, ~6% on the case study), raising questions about practical impact. The framework is limited to regression tasks and does not extend to classification or distribution shift scenarios.
  • Red flags: The reducible uncertainty ratios on benchmarks are extremely small (e.g., 0.1% on Airfoil, 0.1% on Parkinson's), which may limit practical utility. The method adds non-trivial computational overhead (K-Means + greedy path per test instance) compared to standard CP. The paper's framing as "explaining uncertainty" sometimes overstates what is essentially a post-hoc diagnostic — it does not reduce actual prediction error or improve the model.

Strengths

  • Well-motivated problem: global calibration obscures instance-level uncertainty sources, and existing adaptive CP methods collapse uncertainty into a single scalar.
  • Clean mathematical formalisation (Definitions 1–5) with clear notation and consistent terminology.
  • The model-aware embedding space (input + prediction + uncertainty) is a principled way to capture behavioral similarity beyond raw feature proximity.
  • The greedy accept-revert rule is elegant — it prevents the localisation path from increasing interval width, ensuring a monotonic decomposition.
  • Excellent visualisation design (four-panel diagnostic) that conveys uncertainty trajectory, cluster contributions, and calibration support in an interpretable manner.
  • Honest about limitations: the authors explicitly state the framework is diagnostic, not causal, and does not provide conformal guarantees on localised intervals.

Limitations

  • Small reducible ratios on benchmarks: The actual reducible uncertainty is often <1%, which may not justify the computational and conceptual overhead for practitioners.
  • Regression only: The framework is designed exclusively for regression tasks; classification, survival analysis, or structured prediction are not addressed.
  • No formal coverage guarantees for localised intervals: The decomposed intervals are purely diagnostic — they cannot replace the original conformal interval for decision-making.
  • Dependence on model-derived uncertainty proxy: σ* depends on the base model's ability to express uncertainty (e.g., RF tree variance), which may be unreliable for poorly calibrated or misspecified models.
  • Clustering sensitivity: The choice of K and the K-Means algorithm itself introduces additional hyperparameters and stochasticity. The paper does not provide a principled method for selecting K beyond empirical exploration.
  • Computational overhead: Per-instance clustering, embedding, and greedy path construction add complexity compared to standard CP, though the calibration clustering is done once.

Relevance

这篇论文与不确定性量化（UQ）和可解释AI（XAI）的交叉领域高度相关。对于使用保形预测进行不确定性量化的研究者，ConformaDecompose提供了一个实用的诊断工具，可以帮助理解预测区间为何过宽以及哪些校准区域导致了不确定性的膨胀。其"可约 vs. 不可约"不确定性的分解视角对需要区分噪声和模型局限性的实际应用场景（如自动驾驶定价、医疗预测）有参考价值。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does ConformaDecompose behave under distribution shift between calibration and test data — can the reducible uncertainty ratio serve as a shift detector?
  2. Can the framework be extended to classification (e.g., via prediction set size instead of interval width) or to conformal predictive distributions?
  3. What is the computational scaling behavior with very large calibration sets (e.g., >1M samples) — can approximate nearest-neighbor methods replace full K-Means?
  4. How sensitive are the results to the choice of uncertainty proxy σ* (e.g., using MC-dropout, deep ensembles, or GP variance instead of RF tree variance)?
  5. Could the cluster-wise uncertainty contributions be integrated into an active learning loop to guide targeted data collection in high-reducibility regions?
