Measurement of Generative AI Workload Power Profiles for Whole-Facility Data Center Infrastructure Planning

一句话总结： 本文在配备 NVIDIA H100 GPU 的 HPC 集群上以 0.1 秒分辨率测量了 LLM 微调、图像生成训练和推理三类 GenAI 工作负载的功耗曲线，并基于离散事件仿真构建了从节点级功耗到整厂数据中心能耗的 bottom-up 模型（DIPLOEE），为数据中心基础设施规划和电网接入提供可复现、公开的功耗数据集与方法论。
TL;DR: This paper provides publicly available, 0.1-second resolution power profiles for GenAI training, fine-tuning, and inference workloads on NVIDIA H100 GPUs, and introduces DIPLOEE -- a bottom-up, discrete-event simulation framework that scales these node-level measurements into realistic whole-facility energy demand profiles for data center infrastructure planning.

核心贡献

  • 在 NLR Kestrel HPC 集群（4x H100 SXM per node）上以 0.1 秒分辨率测量 Llama-2 70B 微调、Stable Diffusion v2 训练和 Llama-3 70B 推理工作负载的功耗曲线，数据集公开发布于 OSTI
  • 提出 DIPLOEE 离散事件仿真模型，支持面向 job 的训练/微调场景和面向 request-rate 的推理场景，可将节点级功耗剖面按用户行为分布扩展为全年、分钟级、整厂数据中心功耗时间序列
  • 揭示关键发现：即使 100% 利用率下，设施实际功耗仅为额定设计功率的 73%-80%，存在基础设施优化空间；推理数据中心对电网的稳定性冲击低于托管型数据中心，但高利用率会严重损害服务质量

Key Contributions

  • Measured 0.1-second resolution power profiles for Llama-2 70B fine-tuning, Stable Diffusion v2 training, and Llama-3 70B inference (offline and online/serve) on NVIDIA H100 GPUs using the open-source WattAMeter tool, with the full dataset publicly released
  • Developed DIPLOEE, a bottom-up, discrete-event whole-facility energy simulation model that supports both job-centric (training/fine-tuning) and request-rate-driven (inference) architectures, generating year-long, minute-resolution facility-level power profiles
  • Demonstrated that even at 100% node utilization, actual facility power only reaches 73-80% of rated design power, suggesting opportunities for infrastructure cost optimization, and that inference data centers exhibit ~9% lower peak-to-average ratios than colocation centers

Methodology

The authors use WattAMeter (built on NVML and RAPL) to sample CPU and GPU power at 0.1-second intervals on NLR's Kestrel HPC system with NVIDIA H100 SXM GPUs. They profile three workloads using standardized benchmarks: MLPerf Training v4.0 for Llama-2 70B fine-tuning (LoRA + ZeRO-3) and Stable Diffusion v2 training, and vLLM for Llama-3 70B inference in both offline and online/serve modes. Each workload is scaled across 1-16 nodes with multiple replicates. The measured power profiles are then fed into DIPLOEE, a discrete-event simulation built on SimPy, which uses probability distributions for job type, node count, and temporal utilization patterns (sourced from ACME and Azure LLM datasets) to generate year-long facility-level power time series.

Results & Highlights

  • Llama-2 70B fine-tuning: Average power scales linearly with node count (2 to 16 nodes). A ~3-minute ramp-up period is observed. Power fluctuations become more pronounced at higher node counts due to increased communication volume in data-parallel training. Total energy increases with node count because the global batch size increases, requiring more epochs to converge
  • Stable Diffusion v2 training: Power draw fluctuates between 12 kW and 48 kW on 16 nodes during evaluation phases. Evaluation is much more time-intensive than for LLM workloads. Total energy decreases with node count because runtime reduction offsets the added power
  • Llama-3 70B inference: Online/serve mode power saturates at ~2.8-2.9 kW per node at relatively modest request rates (~25 prompts/second). There is a clear tradeoff between latency (user experience) and energy efficiency: higher request rates per instance are more energy-efficient but degrade latency
  • Whole-facility simulation: A 10 MW colocation data center at 80% utilization processes ~10,120 jobs/day but 75.6% experience queuing with average wait of 6.5 hours. Peak-to-average ratio decreases from 2.56 (20% util) to 1.24 (80% util). Clear diurnal, weekly, and seasonal power patterns are observed. Actual peak power reaches only 73% of rated capacity for colocation and 80% for inference
  • GPU/CPU baselines: Measured H100 stress-test average of 696 W (vs. 700 W TDP), idle at 72.5 W. CPU stress at 338.6 W (vs. 360 W TDP), idle at 64.1 W

Critical Assessment

This paper makes a valuable empirical contribution by providing open, high-resolution power profiles for representative GenAI workloads on current-generation hardware -- a resource that has been sorely lacking in a field dominated by proprietary data. The DIPLOEE simulation framework is practical and well-structured, though its simplifications (FIFO scheduling, no cooling/power-distribution losses, limited workload diversity) mean the facility-level results should be interpreted as informative estimates rather than precise predictions. The finding that actual power consumption falls well below rated TDP is practically significant but not entirely surprising given known workload behavior. The strongest contribution is the public dataset and the clear methodology for scaling node-level measurements to facility-level demand.

Strengths

  • Publicly available dataset with 0.1-second resolution power measurements on H100 GPUs -- fills a critical gap in the literature
  • Rigorous use of standardized benchmarks (MLPerf, vLLM) ensuring reproducibility
  • Comprehensive experimental design: multiple node counts, replicate experiments, offline and online inference modes
  • DIPLOEE framework is flexible and well-documented, supporting both training and inference scenarios
  • Practical findings on the gap between rated TDP and actual power consumption have direct implications for infrastructure cost optimization
  • Clear presentation of the tradeoff between latency and energy efficiency in inference serving

Limitations

  • Limited to a single hardware platform (H100 SXM + AMD EPYC Genoa) and three workload types; results may not generalize to other accelerators or model architectures
  • Facility simulation excludes cooling, power distribution losses, and other auxiliary loads (though acknowledged as <10% for large modern facilities)
  • Utilization distributions are adapted from external datasets (ACME, Azure LLM) that may not reflect the operational diversity of commercial hyperscale environments
  • FIFO scheduling is a simplistic assumption; real data centers use more sophisticated schedulers
  • The Llama-2 fine-tuning experiments used per-device batch size of 1 without hyperparameter tuning, which may have inflated energy-at-scale results
  • No validation of DIPLOEE facility-level outputs against real whole-facility power data (unsurprising given data scarcity, but a gap nonetheless)

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How would the power profiles differ on next-generation accelerators (e.g., NVIDIA B200, AMD MI300X) with different memory architectures and interconnects?
  • Can the DIPLOEE framework be extended to incorporate smart scheduling strategies (e.g., load shifting based on renewable energy availability or dynamic pricing) and quantify their energy/cost savings?
  • How do agentic AI workloads, which introduce more dynamic and unpredictable resource utilization patterns, change the facility-level power profile compared to the training/inference patterns studied here?
