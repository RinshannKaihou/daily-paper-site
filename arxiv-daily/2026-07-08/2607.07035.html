Key Finding

A deep ReLU unit yields a piecewise-linear manifold — not a hyperplane — to partition input space; this is the essential expressive gap between deep and shallow networks.

Methodology — deductive theory

A purely deductive, theorem-proof development adopting "the methodology of theoretical physics" — using theory to explain backprop-obtained training solutions rather than to optimize them. Extends the author's two-layer ReLU work (Huang 2024) to multi-hidden-layer feedforward ReLU networks.

The central abstraction is the path: the subset of units (plus their links) activated by a region of input space, giving a one-to-one correspondence between network structure and the partition of input space. Adjacent paths map to geometrically adjacent regions.

The output is a continuous piecewise-linear function g(x). Function construction reduces to four principle families: the recurrence sν(x) = sν−1(x) + λνσ(wν⊤x + bν); multiple strict partial orders; a continuity-restriction principle (linear functions on neighborhoods imply the one between them — the key parameter-sharing mechanism); and two-sided solutions via negative-unit transforms (Theorems 24–26). A grid-scan Algorithm 1 recovers a trained network's knot set for visual verification.

Strengths

  • A clean organizing concept (the path) that unifies partition formation and function construction across arbitrary depth.
  • The piecewise-linear-manifold characterization (Cor. 5) and exponential region growth (Cor. 3) are intuitive, usable mental models for why depth helps.
  • The continuity-restriction principle (Thm 21) is a concrete, non-obvious parameter-sharing mechanism — a single update realizing many linear pieces at once.
  • The knot-drawing Algorithm 1 is a simple, practical inspection tool for any trained ReLU network.
  • Honest about scope: repeatedly flags which results are "trivial" special cases of the two-layer theory.

Limitations

  • No empirical validation at scale: only two 2-D toy regressions; nothing on classification, real data, or networks beyond 18 units.
  • No baselines, no error metrics — experiments only show the theory's notation can label a trained network's knots.
  • The unit lower bounds are overhead bounds, not depth advantages: depth costs O(Φ) extra units; benefit is argued only qualitatively.
  • Relies on the unproven Assumption 1 (adjacent-path); only heuristically enforced.
  • Single-author chain depends on Huang (2024) for ~a third of its machinery — hard to audit standalone.
  • No connection to practical interpretability tools (circuits, probes, mechanistic interp).

Open Questions

  1. Can the piecewise-linear-manifold characterization (Cor. 5) be detected in trained networks on real data (e.g., a small MLP on MNIST), or only in the controlled 2-D regime here?
  2. Does the continuity-restriction principle imply testable generalization predictions — do networks whose partitions satisfy more cascades generalize better?
  3. The unit bounds are all overhead. Is there a complementary upper bound showing depth can reduce units below the two-layer 2ζ^(½)+1 by exploiting piecewise-linear-curve partitions?
  4. How sensitive is Algorithm 1's recovered knot picture to the discretization step Δx and floating-point activation boundaries?
  5. Can "Assumption 1" be replaced by a theorem giving explicit sufficient conditions on weight magnitudes or initialization?
  6. Does the framework extend to ReLU-like activations with non-zero negative slope (Leaky-ReLU, GELU), or is it specific to the exact ReLU kink?
