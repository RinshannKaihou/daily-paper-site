> **一句话总结：** 本文提出 Distributed Sparse Interventions (DSI)，通过在神经元层级（而非激活空间的全局方向）上、利用鲁棒化梯度与迭代优化定位并干预极少量（低至全部神经元的 0.01%，即 8–64 个单元）分布式神经元，从而在 Qwen3-8B / Gemma3-4B / Llama3.2-3B 上激活 12 个任务的求解行为，常常达到或超过 10-shot ICL 的准确率，并以"集合运算"视角将任务分解为共享（如复制）与任务特有神经元子集。

> **TL;DR:** DSI replaces dense, linear activation-space steering with sparse, neuron-level interventions found via LRP-robustified gradients plus iterative ZeroFPR refinement; on 12 function-vector tasks across Qwen3-8B, Gemma3-4B, and Llama3.2-3B, intervening on just 8–64 attention-head neurons (≈0.01–0.04% of all neurons) lifts 0-shot accuracy from near-0 baselines up to 0.72–0.98 at 64 neurons, often matching or beating 10-shot ICL, while a set-based view (intersection/union) decomposes tense- and language-switching into shared "copy" neurons versus task-specific neurons.

## 核心贡献

- 提出 **Distributed Sparse Interventions (DSI)**：一种直接在"神经元基"上工作的稀疏干预方法，不依赖线性、可加或全局方向假设，允许干预分布在多层、多头之间。
- 通过**鲁棒化梯度（LRP 改造的梯度）** + **迭代式 ZeroFPR 优化**（最多 50 步、多次扰动初值）刻画神经元间的非线性与交互效应，弥补一阶 Taylor 近似的不足，并以 `a⁻`（10-shot 与 0-shot 平均激活差）作为预条件器。
- 在 3 个指令微调模型（Qwen3-8B、Gemma3-4B、Llama3.2-3B）、12 个任务（抽取式 6 个 + 抽象式 6 个，取自 Todd et al. 的 function vectors）上系统评估：**8–64 个神经元**即可显著激活任务行为，64 个神经元时平均准确率达 0.72–0.98，常与 10-shot ICL 相当或更优。
- 以**集合视角**对任务做组合分析：通过交集/并集将任务（present-past、english-french）分解为"共享的复制神经元"与"任务特有神经元"，提供比残差流方向向量更细粒度的可解释性。

## Key Contributions

- Introduces **Distributed Sparse Interventions (DSI)**, a neuron-basis intervention method that drops the linearity, additivity, and uniform-scaling assumptions of prior activation-space steering (e.g., function vectors, CAA, in-context vectors).
- Combines an **LRP-robustified first-order gradient** (identity rule for nonlinearities, half-rule for gated MLPs/attention, LayerNorm rule) with an **iterative sparse optimization (ZeroFPR)** that re-evaluates expected effects at the current intervention point, explicitly modeling neuron interactions and per-neuron scaling.
- Provides **direct evidence of nonlinear, neuron-specific effects**: among the top-16 first-order neurons, single-neuron dose–response curves are non-monotonic and some high-"expected-effect" neurons actually decrease the correct-token logit; pairwise interventions show clear interaction terms (a toy `x²` example derives the missing `δ₁δ₂` cross term).
- A comprehensive **12-task × 3-model** evaluation shows 8–64 neurons (≈0.01–0.04% of neurons) suffice to activate tasks, frequently matching or exceeding 10-shot ICL, with both gradient robustification and iterative refinement shown to be necessary via two ablations.
- A **set-based compositionality** view: intersecting/unioning DSI neuron sets separates a shared "copy" mechanism (≈92% copy accuracy on the intersection) from task-specific transformation neurons in tense-switching (Qwen3-8B) and language-switching (Llama3.2-3B).

## Methodology

DSI operates directly on individual attention-head output neurons (at the last token position, before projection to the residual stream) rather than on dense residual-stream directions. The procedure has four steps:

1. **Activation differences.** For each task, build 100 10-shot and 100 0-shot prompts from a 50/50 train/test split and compute, per neuron, `a⁻ = mean_{D_k}(a|x) − mean_{D_0}(a|x)`. This activation difference captures how a neuron shifts when the task is solved via ICL versus not, and serves as both initialization and preconditioner.

2. **Robustified first-order effect.** The intervention effect is approximated by a first-order Taylor expansion, but the raw gradient is replaced by an **LRP-modified gradient** `∂_r` (AttnLRP identity rule for nonlinearities, half-rule for gated MLPs/attention, LayerNorm rule), since raw deep-network gradients suffer from "gradient shattering." The expected average effect is `Ē(δ) ≈ (1/|D_0|) Σ ∂_r f(x_0) δ`.

3. **Initial sparse set.** Pick the `n` neurons with the largest `Ē(a⁻)` as the starting set `s₀ = 1_{top_n(e)}`.

4. **Iterative refinement (Algorithm 1).** Optimize the per-neuron scaling vector `s` (constrained to `nnz(s) ≤ n` and staying close to the previous step) to directly maximize the mean correct-token output logit, using **ZeroFPR** (a forward-backward-envelope nonconvex proximal solver, ≤50 steps, multiple perturbed restarts). This re-evaluation at the updated intervention point is what recovers the interaction terms that a one-shot Taylor expansion misses (a worked `f(x)=x²` example shows the `δ₁δ₂` cross term reappearing after re-expansion at the new root point).

The final intervention `Δ = s ⊙ a⁻` is added additively to the chosen neurons during a modified forward pass at inference. Ablations compare full DSI against (i) "no iterative refinement" (top-n first-order neurons with one global scaling factor) and (ii) "no gradient robustification" (same, but with raw gradients).

## Results & Highlights

All accuracies are 0-shot, next-token (first predicted token), averaged over 6 tasks per category; ranges are min–max across tasks (Table 1).

- **Massive lift over the no-intervention baseline.** On Gemma 3 (4B) abstractive tasks, 8-neuron DSI raises accuracy from **0.01 (baseline) to 0.51**, and 64-neuron DSI reaches **0.72**; on the same model's extractive tasks it goes from **0.13 (baseline) to 0.97** at 64 neurons.
- **64-neuron DSI accuracy ranges from 0.72 to 0.98 across the six model×category settings** (e.g., Llama 3.2 abstractive 0.75, Qwen 3 extractive 0.98, Gemma 3 extractive 0.97), with the strongest and most consistent performance on extractive tasks.
- **Both ablations hurt, and iterative refinement matters most at small set sizes.** At 8 neurons on Llama 3.2 abstractive tasks, full DSI scores **0.63** versus **0.31** for "no iterative refinement" and **0.44** for "no gradient robustification"; the gap narrows but persists as set size grows.
- **Extreme sparsity suffices.** 8–64 neurons correspond to roughly **0.01–0.04% of total considered neurons** (86 016 for Llama 3.2, 69 632 for Gemma 3, 147 456 for Qwen 3), and meaningful gains appear at just 8 neurons.
- **Nonlinear / interaction effects are real and material (Figure 2).** On english-french with Llama 3.2 (3B), among the 16 highest first-order-effect neurons, single-neuron interventions show non-monotonic logit-vs-scale curves with some neurons *reducing* the correct-token logit; pairwise interventions reach a maximum logit (**9.45**) at *unequal* per-neuron scales, versus a single-neuron peak of **4.79** — contradicting uniform-scaling assumptions.
- **Set-based task decomposition (Figure 4).** For present-past on Qwen 3 (8B) at 32 neurons, the 23-neuron copy∩present-past intersection scores **0.92** on the copy task but only **0.03** on present-past, while the 9 task-specific neurons score **0.37** on present-past with reduced copying; for english-french on Llama 3.2 (3B) at 16 neurons, the 11-neuron intersection scores **0.92** copy accuracy while the 5 task-specific neurons score **0.25** on english-french.

## Authors & Credibility

Authors are from the Max Planck School of Cognition, the Max Planck Institute for Human Development (Center for Lifespan Psychology), the TU Berlin Machine Learning Group, and BIFOLD. Oliver Eberle's group has published directly relevant prior work on LRP-based attribution for transformers (AttnLRP, ReLP, MambaLRP — refs [1], [23], [42]), which lends strong technical credibility to the LRP-robustified gradient at the core of DSI. The work is a careful methodological paper with a worked mathematical example (Appendix A), explicit ablations for each design choice, confidence intervals (95% Wilson), and a code-release statement ("Code is available at GitHub"). Compute: ~108h on AMD Instinct MI300A APUs. The 12 tasks are taken verbatim from Todd et al.'s well-established function-vector benchmark (MIT license).

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty — solid and well-motivated, not incremental.** The combination of (a) operating in the native neuron basis (riding the recent Arora et al. 2026 finding that circuits are already sparse in the neuron basis, ref [3]) with (b) LRP-robustified gradients and (c) iterative ZeroFPR sparse optimization is genuinely new relative to dense steering vectors (function vectors [49], CAA [43], in-context vectors [32]) and to head-level sparse methods like LoFiT [54]. The interaction-effect analysis (Figure 2, Appendix A) is a real contribution beyond "steering works."
- **Rigor — high, with honest reporting.** The two ablations cleanly isolate the contribution of gradient robustification versus iterative refinement, results are reported with min–max ranges and Wilson CIs, and the authors flag that they "cannot identify effective interventions across all set sizes and conditions" and that country-capital/english-french accuracy *degrades* at larger set sizes — a non-trivial honesty that many steering papers lack.
- **Reproducibility — strong on paper, unverifiable in practice.** Algorithm 1 and all hyperparameters (50 steps, perturbed restarts, LRP rules) are specified, and a code release is promised. However, the task data, exact perturbation scheme, and the ZeroFPR settings live only in Appendix F, and the permalink is not given, so full reproduction cannot yet be confirmed.
- **Significance — moderate but bounded.** The headline "0.01% of neurons" is real but should be read carefully: it is 0.01% of *attention-head output neurons at the last token*, a deliberately narrow intervention surface, not 0.01% of all model parameters or even all neurons. The benchmarks are simple single-token, English-centric function-vector tasks (capitalize, fruit-vs-animal, etc.); generalization to harder multi-step, multi-token, or non-English settings is untested.
- **Red flags / weak points.** (1) The **baseline is 0-shot with no intervention** (often ~0.00–0.04 on abstractive tasks), so a large fraction of the apparent gain is just "DSI activates the task at all" rather than "DSI beats strong steering" — there is **no head-to-head comparison against function vectors, CAA, in-context vectors, or LoFiT at matched compute**, only against DSI's own ablations. This is the single biggest gap; the claim "nonlinearities not captured by current steering approaches" is shown qualitatively but never quantified against those approaches. (2) The set-decomposition case studies (Section 5) are compelling but **only two tasks, one model each**, and the task-specific neuron subsets *frequently fail* to switch tense or language — the authors admit the 5-neuron english-french set "may not be sufficient to induce consistent language switching," undercutting the clean-decomposition narrative. (3) Evaluation is single-token only, so open-ended generation quality (the repetitive "I started reading the book in 15 pages..." failure) is acknowledged but not measured.

## Strengths

- Principled, assumption-light method: DSI explicitly drops the linearity/additivity/uniform-scaling assumptions that underpin most steering work, and provides a clean mathematical reason (interaction terms) for why this matters.
- The ablation design (no-refinement, no-robustification) and the per-neuron / pairwise nonlinearity plots are exactly the right experiments to justify the method's two non-obvious components.
- The set-based compositionality framing — treating task structure as operations on neuron sets (intersection = shared "copy", set-difference = task-specific) — is a fresh and interpretable angle distinct from residual-stream vector arithmetic.
- Honest limitations section and honest reporting of failure modes (degradation at large set sizes, inconsistent task-specific sets).
- Tasks, models, and data provenance are fully specified and traceable to an established benchmark.

## Limitations

- Evaluation is restricted to **clean few-shot ICL prompts and single-token next-token prediction**; how optimal interventions evolve during autoregressive generation and across token positions is left to future work, and the authors' own open-ended generation examples show repetitive degeneration.
- **No direct comparison against existing steering baselines** (function vectors, CAA, in-context vectors, LoFiT); DSI is only compared against its own ablations, so its relative advantage is not established quantitatively.
- DSI performance **varies markedly across models and tasks** (e.g., 8-neuron Qwen-3 abstractive accuracy spans 0.24–0.97), and the cause (methodological vs. representational) is not disentangled.
- The task-decomposition findings rest on **only two case studies**, and the task-specific neuron subsets often fail to reliably induce the target behavior.
- Tasks are simple, mostly English, single-token, synthetic-style function-vector tasks; broader linguistic/cognitive coverage and multilingual or multi-step reasoning are untested.
- Compute (~108h on MI300A APUs) and the need for per-task gradient/optimization runs make DSI more expensive than a one-shot activation-difference steering vector.

## Relevance

对从事"机制可解释性 + 模型行为控制"方向的研究高度相关：DSI 直接在神经元层面定位并稀疏干预任务计算，与你关注的 sparse circuits / 任务表示定位 / 模型 steer 路线（function vectors、SAE-free 神经元基电路）紧密衔接，可作为理解 LLM 任务组合与可控干预的新工具与对照基线。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does DSI compare head-to-head against function vectors, CAA, in-context vectors, and LoFiT at matched neuron-set sizes or matched compute on these same 12 tasks? The lack of such a comparison is the most important open gap.
- Can the set-based decomposition (copy vs. task-specific neurons) be made robust enough that the task-specific subsets *reliably* switch tense or language without the copy behavior, rather than frequently failing as observed?
- Do DSI-identified neuron sets transfer across token positions and during full autoregressive generation, or are they localized to the last-token ICL setup?
- How do DSI neuron sets relate to features found by sparse autoencoders or crosscoders — are they overlapping, redundant, or genuinely more direct, as the Arora et al. neuron-basis sparsity result would suggest?
- Does the approach scale to harder, multi-token, multi-step reasoning tasks (e.g., math, code, multilingual translation), or does the single-token evaluation mask ceiling effects specific to these simple function-vector tasks?
