Key finding

Intervening on just 8–64 attention-head neurons — 0.01–0.04% of them — activates 12 tasks to 0.72–0.98 accuracy, often matching or beating 10-shot ICL.

Distributed Sparse Interventions in Language Models
Maximilian S. Ernst · Lorenz Linhardt · Aaron Peikert · Oliver Eberle — MPI / TU Berlin / BIFOLD 2026-07-08 Qwen3-8B · Gemma3-4B · Llama3.2-3B ~108h on MI300A APUs

01Key metric — sparsity

0.01%

Of attention-head output neurons at the last token — as few as 8 units lift abstractive 0-shot from 0.01 → 0.51; 64 units reach 0.72–0.98 across settings.

02Why this matters

Drops the linearity, additivity, and uniform-scaling assumptions of dense steering — and shows, with interaction terms, why those assumptions break.

03Critical edge

No head-to-head comparison against function vectors, CAA, in-context vectors, or LoFiT at matched compute — DSI is only compared to its own ablations.

Methodology neuron-basis intervention, four steps

operates on individual attention-head output neurons (last token, before residual projection) — not on dense residual-stream directions

  1. Activation differences. Build 100 10-shot + 100 0-shot prompts per task; per neuron compute a⁻ = mean(a|D_k) − mean(a|D_0) — how a neuron shifts when the task is solved via ICL vs. not. Used as both init and preconditioner.
  2. Robustified first-order effect. Replace the raw gradient (which "shatters" in deep nets) with an LRP-modified gradient — identity rule for nonlinearities, half-rule for gated MLPs/attention, LayerNorm rule. Expected effect Ē(δ) ≈ mean(∂_r f · δ).
  3. Initial sparse set. Pick the n neurons with largest Ē(a⁻).
  4. Iterative refinement (Algorithm 1). Optimize per-neuron scaling s (constrained to nnz(s) ≤ n) to maximize mean correct-token logit, using ZeroFPR — a forward-backward-envelope nonconvex proximal solver, ≤50 steps, perturbed restarts. Re-evaluation at the updated point is what recovers interaction terms a one-shot Taylor expansion misses (a worked x² example recovers the δ₁δ₂ cross term).

The final Δ = s ⊙ a⁻ is added additively during a modified forward pass at inference. Both design choices are validated by ablation: full DSI vs. no-iterative-refinement vs. no-gradient-robustification.

Both ablations hurt; iterative refinement matters most at small set sizes — at 8 neurons on Llama3.2 abstractive, full DSI scores 0.63 vs. 0.31 (no refinement) and 0.44 (no robustification).

Interaction effects are real. Pairwise english-french interventions peak at logit 9.45 at unequal per-neuron scales vs. 4.79 single-neuron — directly contradicting uniform-scaling assumptions.

Strengths & limitations side by side

Strengths
  • Principled, assumption-light: drops linearity/additivity/uniform-scaling with a math reason.
  • Ablation + per-neuron/pairwise nonlinearity plots justify both non-obvious components.
  • Set-based compositionality (intersection = shared "copy") is a fresh angle.
  • Honest limitations; flags degradation at large set sizes & inconsistent task-specific sets.
Limitations
  • No direct steering-baseline comparison — relative advantage not quantified.
  • Single-token ICL only; open-ended generation shows repetitive degeneration.
  • Performance varies markedly across models/tasks (8-n Qwen abstractive 0.24–0.97).
  • Task decomposition rests on only two case studies; task-specific sets often fail.

Open questions for further exploration

  1. How does DSI compare head-to-head against function vectors, CAA, in-context vectors, and LoFiT at matched neuron-set sizes or matched compute on these same 12 tasks?
  2. Can the set-based decomposition (copy vs. task-specific neurons) be made robust enough that the task-specific subsets reliably switch tense or language without the copy behavior?
  3. Do DSI-identified neuron sets transfer across token positions and during full autoregressive generation, or are they localized to the last-token ICL setup?
  4. How do DSI neuron sets relate to features found by sparse autoencoders or crosscoders — overlapping, redundant, or genuinely more direct?
  5. Does the approach scale to harder, multi-token, multi-step reasoning (math, code, multilingual translation), or does single-token evaluation mask ceiling effects?
