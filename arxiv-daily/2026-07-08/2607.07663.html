03Why this matters

A two-axis taxonomy and verification hierarchy let you instantly locate — and cap — any “self-X” AI system. Governance-grade measurement is the field's biggest gap.

04Critical edge

The verification hierarchy — the survey's central empirical claim — is asserted qualitatively, never measured; its only near-experimental support is one cited study, not the authors' own.

A literature-mapping survey, not an experiment. Corpus built in two stages, organized by the taxonomy's columns (§§3–7), and anchored to two devices: the 4×3 two-axis grid (what improves × who validates) and the verification hierarchy ordering evaluator reliability.

  • Four improvement targets: deployment-time behavior, training-time policy, evaluator, research process.
  • Three closure degrees: human-in-loop → human-on-loop → fully closed.
  • Single-annotator; all per-paper assignments + scripts released for audit (sample, not census).

✓Strengths

  • Resolves a conflated vocabulary. The two-axis taxonomy cleanly separates output revision, weight updates, harness rewriting, and autonomous research; the bounded-vs-open-ended cut is the right organizing principle.
  • Self-evaluation as a first-class pillar. The verification hierarchy is the most original move — one frame explains intrinsic-self-correction failures, collapse dynamics, and the direction-setting bottleneck.
  • Unusual intellectual honesty. Surfaces its own corpus limits, distinguishes anchor works from recency-skewed 2026 mass, and credits the skeptical literature as load-bearing.
  • Insightful reframings. Result-level vs process-level improvement, cast as op-ex vs cap-ex, reframes “scientific amnesia” and why mature self-improvement may look like maturing methodology, not an intelligence explosion.
  • Concrete anchors throughout — FunSearch, AlphaEvolve, Mirror Loop, A-Evolve-Training — keep the synthesis grounded.

!Limitations

  • Single-annotator classification, no inter-annotator agreement; category proportions are approximate and partly reflect harvest design (the supplement inflated self-evaluation's share).
  • Central claim is unmeasured. The verification hierarchy is asserted qualitatively; only one cited study (Mirror Loop) supports it — not the authors' own analysis across the corpus.
  • Recency-biased corpus. 74% of papers are from 2026 (median age: months); synthesis weight rests disproportionately on young, unvalidated work.
  • Frontier-lab practice is publication-censored. The “closed loop is sparse” finding is partly an artifact of what labs publish — the most advanced cell is the least observable.
  • ~1-in-7 supplemental papers (~54 of 379) are admitted peripheral query bleed: counted in totals, never cited.
  • Takeoff / RSI-feasibility debate left at a stalemate (two diverging elasticity specs) without the authors taking a position.

07Questions for further exploration

  1. Can “improvement tracks the verification hierarchy” be operationalized — e.g. a per-paper verifier-rung label correlated with reported gains — instead of asserted qualitatively?
  2. What is the minimum exogenous “grounding” exchange rate that sustains a self-training loop, and does the entropy-reservoir framework give a tractable estimator?
  3. Does evaluator co-evolution (the “Red Queen” move, seen ~5× in 2026) escape the self-confirming loop, or merely relocate it one level up? What experiment distinguishes verifier improvement from verifier gaming?
  4. If frontier-lab closed-loop practice were observable, would the bounded-vs-open-ended cut change — is “the closed loop is sparse” an artifact of publication bias?
  5. Can “research taste” / interestingness acquire a benchmark (Zenil's algorithmic-information-theoretic framing as precondition), and would it relax the binding direction-setting bottleneck?
