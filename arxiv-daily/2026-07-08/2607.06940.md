> **一句话总结：** 本文提出一个针对 LLM 回答质量的多因子打分系统（MFSS），将准确率、简洁性、事实一致性、可读性、连贯性及 ROUGE 六个维度加权融合（权重 0.25/0.10/0.20/0.15/0.15/0.15），并在 TruthfulQA（817 道英文题）上对 5 个主流 LLM 进行评测，发现模型在 Logical Falsehood 等推理类任务上表现最好、在 Misinformation 等复杂事实类任务上普遍失败，Gemini-2.0-flash 以综合分 0.6104 排名第一。

> **TL;DR:** A multi-factor scoring system (MFSS) combines accuracy, conciseness, factual consistency, readability, coherence, and ROUGE into a weighted composite to evaluate LLM responses on TruthfulQA (817 questions) across five LLMs (DeepSeek-v3, Doubao-1.5-pro-32k, Gemini-2.0-flash, QWQ-Plus, Moonshot-v1-8k). Gemini-2.0-flash tops the composite at 0.6104 (+0.0016 over DeepSeek-v3's 0.6088 and +0.0605 over the lowest scorer moonshot-v1-8k at 0.5499), while all five models collapse on Misinformation (best category score ~0.2950 vs. ~0.76 for their best categories).

## 核心贡献

- 提出多维度评估框架 MFSS：将传统单指标（准确率/流畅度）与用户体验维度（可读性、简洁性、连贯性）整合为一个加权综合分，覆盖 LLM 回答的多个侧面。
- 引入语义嵌入 + ROUGE 的混合打分：使用 all-MiniLM-L6-v2 嵌入计算余弦相似度，再结合 ROUGE-1/2/L 加权（0.5/0.2/0.3 ×1.2 截断到 1.0）作为表层相似度补充。
- 提供可视化 GUI，把六维度分数以雷达图/饼图形式呈现，提升结果透明度与可解释性。
- 在 TruthfulQA 上系统揭示 5 个主流 LLM 的强弱项：推理类（Logical Falsehood / Mandela Effect）普遍强、复杂事实与歧义类（Misinformation / Confusion: People）普遍弱。
- 给出面向场景的模型选型建议：高准确率选 Gemini-2.0-flash、高事实一致性选 Doubao-1.5-pro-32k、高可读性选 DeepSeek-v3。

## Key Contributions

- A modular multi-factor scoring system (data preprocessing → semantic embedding → multi-factor evaluation) producing a single weighted Total Score across six dimensions, with empirically-set weights (Accuracy 0.25, Conciseness 0.10, Factual 0.20, Readability 0.15, Coherence 0.15, ROUGE 0.15) that users can retune.
- A "conciseness" metric built as an inverse-redundancy score with exponential (2^repeat_count) and quadratic (count^2·0.5) penalties for repeated sentences plus a length-scaling factor when a response exceeds twice the reference length.
- A composite "factual consistency" score based on stopword-filtered word-set overlap between response and reference answer (|Wm ∩ Wg| / |Wg|), cheap and English-friendly.
- A GUI for visualizing per-dimension scores and category breakdowns, increasing transparency for non-expert users.
- Empirical evidence on TruthfulQA (817 questions) that reasoning-style categories (Logical Falsehood, Mandela Effect, Misconceptions: Topical) are systematically easier for current LLMs than adversarial-fact categories (Misinformation, Confusion: People, Indexical Error), with per-model best/worst categories tabulated.

## Methodology

The system is a deterministic, reference-based scorer (no LLM-as-judge). For each (question, model response Am, reference answer Ag) triple it computes six sub-scores:

1. **Accuracy** — cosine similarity between sentence embeddings of Am and Ag produced by `all-MiniLM-L6-v2` (a lightweight multilingual Sentence-BERT model), chosen for efficiency over BERT.
2. **Conciseness** — inverse redundancy: responses are sentence-segmented (regex on `[.!?]`); each sentence's max similarity to the reference is computed, sub-threshold sentences incur a full-length penalty and others a similarity-proportional penalty; repeated sentences trigger an exponential penalty `2^repeat_count` plus a quadratic `count^2·0.5`; if |Am| > 2·|Ag| a length-scaling factor is applied. Final CS = 1 − R.
3. **Factual consistency** — tokenization + lowercasing + stopword removal, then set-overlap `|Wm ∩ Wg| / |Wg|`; defaults to 1.0 if Ag empty, 0.0 if either input empty.
4. **Readability** — weighted blend (0.6 / 0.4) of (i) deviation from an optimal sentence length of 17.5 words and (ii) lexical diversity (unique-word ratio with 1e-6 smoothing); single-sentence or empty responses default to 0.5.
5. **Coherence** — mean cosine similarity between embeddings of adjacent sentences; defaults to 1.0 for single-sentence responses.
6. **ROUGE** — weighted blend `0.5·R1 + 0.2·R2 + 0.3·RL` (F1) scaled by 1.2 and capped at 1.0.

The **Total Score** is the weighted average `TS = Σ wi·Mi` with the fixed weights above. The framework is modular so additional metrics or embedding models can be plugged in. Evaluation pipeline (`evaluate_model_responses`) reads per-model CSVs, computes the six metrics, and emits a per-row detail table plus a model-level summary, with try/except guards for missing files or calculation errors.

**Setup.** TruthfulQA (817 English questions across science/history/health/etc., each with a Best Answer). Five LLMs queried: QWQ-Plus-Latest (Alibaba), DeepSeek-v3, Doubao-1.5-pro-32k (ByteDance), Moonshot-v1-8k, Gemini-2.0-flash (Google). Responses stored as per-model CSVs with Question/Best Answer/Model/Category/Type columns.

## Results & Highlights

- **Composite ranking on TruthfulQA (Table 1, Total Score):** Gemini-2.0-flash = **0.6104** (1st), DeepSeek-v3 = **0.6088**, Doubao-1.5-pro-32k = **0.5873**, QWQ-Plus-Latest = **0.5800**, Moonshot-v1-8k = **0.5499** (last). The gap between best and worst is 0.0605.
- **Accuracy sub-metric:** Gemini-2.0-flash leads at **0.6725**, edging DeepSeek-v3 (0.6719) and Doubao (0.6689); Moonshot-v1-8k trails at **0.5905** — a 0.082 spread.
- **Factual consistency (word-set overlap) is uniformly weak:** best is Doubao at **0.4445**, worst is Moonshot at **0.2936**; even the leader fails to exceed 0.45, indicating all models struggle to lexically align with reference answers.
- **Reasoning vs. adversarial-fact gap is large and consistent.** Per-category top/bottom (Tables 2–6): DeepSeek-v3 scores **0.7356 on Logical Falsehood** but only **0.2963 on Misinformation** (Δ ≈ 0.44); Doubao **0.7540 on Logical Falsehood** vs. **0.2497 on Misinformation**; Gemini-2.0-flash **0.7628 on Misconceptions: Topical** vs. **0.2950 on Misinformation**; QWQ-Plus **0.7353 on Mandela Effect** vs. **0.2652 on Misinformation**; Moonshot **0.7406 on Politics** vs. **0.3492 on Confusion: People**.
- **Misinformation is the universal failure mode** — it is the worst category for 4 of 5 models, with scores clustered tightly between 0.2497 and 0.2963, far below each model's peak.
- **Conciseness** is the second-weakest dimension overall: only Gemini (0.5897) and DeepSeek (0.5687) clear 0.55; Moonshot is lowest at 0.4927.

Authors are from the School of Computer Science and Engineering, Beihang University, and the Data Science and Intelligent Computing Laboratory, Hangzhou International Innovation Institute, Beihang University (Yiming Gai, Junde Lu, Xuefei Huang, Ying Li). ORCIDs are provided for all four authors. The corresponding authors (Xuefei Huang, Ying Li) are senior faculty. The paper is a short conference-style paper (12 pages incl. references) with a well-formed bibliography (BLEU, BERTScore, TruthfulQA, Transformer, SBERT, Flesch readability, Halliday-Hasan cohesion). The writing is competent and the methodology section gives explicit formulas, but there is no code/repository link, no statement of data availability beyond TruthfulQA itself, and the contributions are largely an engineering combination of existing metrics rather than a new algorithm or benchmark.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty — low.** The "multi-factor" system is a straightforward weighted average of well-known components: cosine similarity over SBERT embeddings (= a slice of BERTScore), an inverse-redundancy heuristic, word-set overlap, a Flesch-style readability formula, adjacent-sentence cosine (= a slice of existing coherence metrics), and ROUGE. No new metric, no learned component, and no theoretical justification for the specific weight vector (0.25/0.10/0.20/0.15/0.15/0.15) beyond "empirically determined."
- **Rigor — weak.** The "**Factual consistency**" metric is just stopword-filtered word-set overlap with the reference — it does not measure factual correctness at all; a factually wrong answer that reuses reference vocabulary scores high, while a correct paraphrase with different wording scores low. Calling this "factual consistency" is a misnomer and undermines the paper's central claim about revealing "limitations in navigating complex facts." Likewise "Accuracy" is embedding cosine similarity, not correctness.
- **Missing ablations.** No sensitivity analysis on the six weights, no leave-one-metric-out, no analysis of which dimension drives the composite ranking. Given the composite scores are tightly clustered (0.5499–0.6104), the ranking could easily flip under different weights — this is never tested.
- **No human correlation.** The paper never validates that its composite score agrees with human preference (no Spearman/Kendall vs. human judgments, no comparison to G-Eval / MT-Bench / Chatbot Arena style protocols). Without this, the "comprehensive" framing is unsupported.
- **Single benchmark, narrow model set.** Only TruthfulQA (817 English MCQA-style questions), and the five models are mostly Chinese-origin plus Gemini — GPT-4, Claude, and Llama are conspicuously absent on an English benchmark, which limits generalizability claims.
- **Cherry-picked reporting.** The per-model category tables (Tables 2–6) report only the top-2 and bottom-2 categories, not the full distribution; no per-category std, no significance tests, no confidence intervals.
- **Unexplained low absolutes.** The composite peaks at 0.6104, yet the authors never discuss why scores are so low or what threshold would count as "good," weakening the practical takeaway.
- **Reproducibility — moderate.** Formulas are explicit and the embedding model (all-MiniLM-L6-v2) is named, so a determined reader could re-implement; but no code release, no model-response CSVs, and no fixed random seeds / API parameters (temperature, decoding) for the five proprietary LLMs are documented, making exact reproduction unlikely.

## Strengths

- Genuinely holistic: explicitly factors in user-experience dimensions (readability, conciseness, coherence) that pure accuracy/ROUGE pipelines ignore, and combines semantic and lexical signals.
- Transparent and cheap: every sub-score has a closed-form formula, runs on CPU with a small SBERT model, and avoids the cost/variability of LLM-as-judge.
- Practitioner-friendly deliverables: per-model CSV outputs, a summary table, and a GUI for visualization make it usable as a lightweight evaluation harness.
- Honest, actionable per-category findings: the consistent Logical-Falsehood-good / Misinformation-bad pattern is a useful, reproducible signal for model-selection and red-teaming.
- Modular and extensible by design: weights and metrics are pluggable, and the authors flag NER-based factual scoring and multilingual extension as next steps.

## Limitations

- "Factual consistency" is lexical overlap, not factual verification; "accuracy" is embedding similarity, not correctness — the dimension names oversell what is measured.
- English-only (TruthfulQA + BPE), contradicting the multilingual ambition stated in the abstract.
- No human-judgment correlation, no comparison to established multi-dimensional protocols (G-Eval, MT-Bench, TrueScore, Chatbot Arena).
- Fixed, unjustified weights with no sensitivity/ablation; tight composite-score clustering means rankings may be brittle.
- Missing major Western frontier models (GPT-4, Claude, Llama) on an English benchmark.
- Per-category reporting is top/bottom-2 only; no variance, CIs, or significance tests.
- No public code or model-response artifacts, and no documentation of decoding parameters for the proprietary LLMs.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does the MFSS composite correlate with human pairwise preference judgments (Spearman/Kendall), and how does it compare to LLM-as-judge protocols like G-Eval, MT-Bench, or TrueScore on the same responses?
- How sensitive is the model ranking to the six weights — does a small perturbation (e.g., Factual 0.20 → 0.30) flip the Gemini vs. DeepSeek ordering, given the composite gap is only 0.0016?
- Would replacing word-set overlap with an NER-based or entailment-based factual checker change the headline "models fail on Misinformation" conclusion, or is the low score an artifact of the lexical-overlap proxy?
- How do the results hold on multilingual TruthfulQA translations and on non-Chinese-origin frontier models (GPT-4-class, Claude, Llama-3)?
- Can the inverse-redundancy conciseness metric and the 17.5-word optimal-length constant be validated against human readability ratings, or are they arbitrary?
- Does adding decoding-parameter disclosure (temperature, top-p, system prompt) change the relative rankings, since the proprietary models were queried under undocumented settings?
