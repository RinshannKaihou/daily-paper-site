§ 01

Diagnose the mechanism, then align the edit to it

Rather than guessing where temporal facts live, the authors causally trace them — corrupting subject vs. time tokens separately and restoring individual activations. What they find reframes the whole editing problem.

Finding — Early MLP (L4–8)

Holds a time-agnostic subject representation at the subject-last token. Cosine similarity between new-time and old-time prompts stays ≥ 0.97 across L4–8 — the model retrieves the subject first, without knowing the time.

Finding — Late Attention (L16+)

Temporal context, carried to the last token by attention around L10–15 (IE = 0.356), only modulates the answer after the subject signal is in place. The edit site itself cannot tell times apart.

This yields a clean geometric impossibility result (Eq. 1): at the MLP edit site, knew ≈ kold, so no optimizer or objective can make a single linear edit produce a different output per time. This is exactly why ROME / MEMIT / AlphaEdit / METO fail on temporal tasks at this layer.

δ* = arg min_δ Σ_i λ_i · L_i(δ) + Ω(δ) L_i(δ) = − log P [ o_i | p_i(s, r, t_i) ]

PRISM's move: stop trying to fight the mechanism. Optimize one shared δ* across all temporal contexts — a polysemous vector v* = v₀ + δ* — then trust the model's own downstream attention pathway to route it differently under different time-token states. No new modules, no per-period storage, no architectural change. The only thing that changes vs. prior locate-then-edit is the target-computation step, not the weight-write step (MEMIT/AlphaEdit null-space machinery reused verbatim).

§ 03

Strengths & limitations, side by side

+ Strengths

  • Mechanism-driven. The method is a logical consequence of the tracing findings, backed by a clean impossibility result — not a method hunting for justification.
  • Minimal intervention. No architectural changes, no auxiliary memory, no per-period parameter storage.
  • Uniformly first on every metric across two architectures and three datasets, with substantial margins on TC and CRS.
  • TIMECONFLICT benchmark fills a genuine gap: day-level granularity + counterfactual variant, vs. AToKe's year-level 9-relation data.
  • Honest ablations isolate the role of L_old vs. L_bare; Appendix C.3 shows multi-context diversity subsumes MEMIT's random-prefix trick.

− Limitations

  • Year-level evaluation despite day-level data. The headline day-granularity selling point of TIMECF is not exercised in main results.
  • Only two-state (k = 2) coexistence tested; the joint-optimization formulation supports N snapshots but is unvalidated.
  • Relies on the base model's temporal-modulation pathway. If a model lacks strong "Temporal Heads," PRISM's routing assumption may break — cross-family generalization beyond LLaMA-3 / GPT-J is untested.
  • Failure cases under-analyzed — two PCA cases with a tentative, unquantified explanation.
  • ROME's total 0.0 collapse is unexplained, leaving open whether baselines were optimally configured. CRS gains lean on the engineered L_bare term.

§ 05

Open questions

Does PRISM scale to k > 2 coexisting snapshots, and does the joint optimization stay stable as N grows?

How does it behave on model families where Temporal Heads are weak or absent?

Can the polysemy principle extend to non-temporal context conditioning — geographic, persona, register?

What is the per-edit interference / locality cost when many PRISM edits stack sequentially?

Would replacing L_bare with a learned default-head recover the two failure cases (Fig. 5g–h)?

Is ROME's total 0.0 collapse reproducible under a fair, tuned configuration?
