ATLAS

A Psychometric Alternative to Static LLM Benchmarks — adaptive testing via 3PL IRT + Fisher information.

Peiyu Li · Xiuxiu Tang · Si Chen · Ying Cheng · Ronald Metoyer · Ting Hua · Nitesh V. Chawla — University of Notre Dame

Key Finding

Reframing LLM evaluation as computerized adaptive testing matches full-bank ability estimates with only 30–89 Fisher-guided items — and reorders 23–31% of model rankings.

Key Metric · HellaSwag items needed
41/ 5,600

ATLAS₀.₁ matches the full-bank ability estimate at MAE 0.157 vs TinyBenchmarks' 0.198 (97 items) — up to 90% item reduction.

Why This Matters

Cuts large-scale LLM evaluation cost by an order of magnitude while yielding uncertainty-aware, comparable ability scores — directly attacking contamination, ceiling effects, and benchmark cost.

Critical Edge

Calibration is anchored to ~3,000 volunteer-uploaded HuggingFace leaderboard models; under distribution shift on ARC, ability MAE roughly triples (0.084 → 0.126) — "transfers reasonably well" is softer than the headline.

01

Methodology

3PL IRT · Fisher information · mean–sigma linking

ATLAS treats LLM evaluation as a psychometric measurement problem. Each model gets a latent ability θℓ; each item gets aᵢ (discrimination), bᵢ (difficulty), cᵢ (guessing). Responses come from 3,000+ HuggingFace Open LLM Leaderboard models across five benchmarks (ARC, GSM8K, HellaSwag, TruthfulQA, WinoGrande).

Items are split into K non-overlapping subsets (≥100 items) and calibrated independently; because every LLM answers every item, the model population serves as common persons, and a mean–sigma transformation links subsets onto one scale — cutting complexity from O(|I|³) to O(K·max_k|I_k|³).

Unlike prior IRT-for-LLM work (TinyBenchmarks, MetaBench), ATLAS reports M2/RMSEA fit statistics and Yen's Q3 local-independence checks as first-class outputs — exposing TinyBenchmarks' catastrophic misfit (RMSEA 364–647).

Stage 1 · Formulate

Binary response matrix {Y_{i,ℓ}} modelled via per-model θℓ and per-item (a, b, c) under a 3PL link.

Stage 2 · Filter

Drop low-ability tail (<0.1 pct); remove low-variance items (SD <1%, acc >95%) and point-biserial r_pb < 0.1. 90/10 stratified split.

Stage 3 · Calibrate

Partition-based 3PL fit + mean–sigma linking; reference abilities via Warm (1989) WLE. Report M2/RMSEA + Yen's Q3.

Stage 4 · Adapt (Alg. 1)

Initialize θ̂=0; pick item maximizing Fisher information Iᵢ(θ), randomesque top-5; EAP update; stop when SE ≤ τ ({0.1, 0.2, 0.3}) after ≥30 items.

Strengths

what holds up
  • Efficiency–accuracy frontier: ATLAS variants dominate the IES axis on every benchmark, often using a fraction of baseline items.
  • Genuine psychometric rigor — M2/RMSEA fit, Yen's Q3 local-independence, and common-person linking are first-class, exposing TinyBenchmarks' broken fit.
  • Accuracy-reconstruction check (Table 3) closes the loop with percent-correct reporting, lowering adoption friction.
  • Clean generalization stress tests — architecture-family and temporal splits, not just in-distribution evaluation.
  • Honest framing of related work: positioned as complementary to concurrent Fluid Benchmarking rather than overclaiming primacy.

Limitations

where it strains
  • Population anchoring: calibrated on HuggingFace Open LLM Leaderboard — open-weight, smaller, volunteer-uploaded; transfer to GPT-4o / Claude / Gemini not measured.
  • Distribution drift: ability MAE meaningfully degrades under temporal shift (ARC 0.084→0.126); periodic re-calibration required.
  • 3PL's guessing parameter is conceptually awkward for LLMs; its advantage over 2PL is mixed (Appendix G.3 — 2PL sometimes lower MAE).
  • Local independence violated for ~1–2% of item pairs on TruthfulQA/ARC; downstream impact on Fisher selection not quantified.
  • No clean ablations isolating randomesque top-5 vs greedy, EAP vs WLE updates, or mean–sigma vs joint linking.
  • "90% reduction" is achieved at looser SE thresholds (ATLAS₀.₂/₀.₃); strictest ATLAS₀.₁ on WinoGrande still uses 70 items.
03

Questions for Further Exploration

How does ATLAS perform when calibrated on a population that includes frontier proprietary models (GPT-4o, Claude, Gemini) — does calibration transfer to the θ ≈ 2–3 regime currently under-sampled?
Can the precision-stopping rule be combined with content-balancing constraints (stratified Fisher information across sub-domains) without eroding the MAE gains?
What is the empirical sensitivity of MAE to IRT parameterization (3PL vs 2PL vs 4PL) when the calibration set is small or skewed — is there a principled model-selection criterion beyond Table 12?
How often must item banks be re-calibrated to track the temporal drift seen on ARC, and can incremental common-person linking keep MAE stable without a full re-fit?
Does the randomesque top-5 rule materially reduce item over-exposure vs greedy top-1, and what is the optimal pool size as a function of bank size?
Could ATLAS's θ estimates detect benchmark contamination more reliably than accuracy drops — since memorized items would carry anomalously low Fisher information at high ability?
