OOD-RL-Bench: A Benchmark Framework for Out-of-Distribution Detection in Reinforcement Learning

Emil Mittag·Richard Dazeley (Deakin)·Peter Vamplew (Federation Univ. Australia)

A matched-time evaluation protocol on RL trajectories shows no single OOD detector dominates — PEDM leads on aggregate AUROC but loses on latency, and observation-delay & action-conditioned dynamics remain nearly undetectable.

Key Metric · Aggregate matched-time AUROC 0.874 PEDM, across 9 anomaly injectors on LunarLander-v3 (Table 4). ▲ +0.035 vs. time-series features (0.839)
Why This Matters

A reusable OOD-detection skeleton — matched-time protocol plus null-baseline diagnostic — that stops you from trusting a single AUROC number on your RL agent.

Critical Edge

All headline numbers come from one 8-dim LunarLander task and one solved policy, with NOOD=20 episodes and no pairwise significance tests — detector rankings may be noise.

Single-env · small-N
Methodology

A unified trajectory contract, eight detectors, nine injectors, one matched-time protocol

01 · DATA CONTRACT

Trajectory-only interface

Fixed-length τ = {(s,a,r,s′,d)} is the only thing detectors see; they return a score sequence z0:T-1 after being fit on in-distribution trajectories. No simulator access at scoring time.

02 · DETECTORS ×8

Seven signal families

Mahalanobis & KDE (marginal-state), time-series features (temporal), PEDM (transition-model), autoencoder (reconstruction), ensemble disagreement (uncertainty), CUSUM (sequential change), Lyapunov divergence (nonlinear dynamics).

03 · INJECTORS ×9

Post-hoc vs. online split

Post-hoc (5): gaussian noise, feature dropout, correlation break, regime switch, observation delay. Online (4): stuck actuator, dynamics scaling, action-conditioned dynamics, gradual drift. Each exposes σ∈[0,1].

04 · EVAL

Matched-time, null-adjusted

Each post-onset anomalous score paired with the clean score at the same timestep (same seed). Null-baseline on counterfactual clean labels exposes temporal-position artefacts. T=500, onset t*=166, NID=30 / NOOD=20.

Critical Assessment

Genuine framework contribution — honest about what the numbers do and do not show

Strengths

  • Matched-time + null-baseline protocol is a real methodological upgrade over raw trajectory AUROC; the Lyapunov counter-example is pedagogically excellent.
  • Clean taxonomy of seven detector signal families mapped to anomaly channels, with expected-vs-observed behaviour revisited in §9.4.
  • Genuine extensibility: a new detector / injector / metric / environment touches only one registry surface (BaseDetector, BaseAnomalyInjector, YAML).
  • Honest framing throughout — refuses to crown a single winner; separates ranking quality, FPR, and latency.
  • Strong reproducibility: deterministic seeding, pinned versions (PyTorch 2.9.1 / NumPy 2.4.0), public checkpoint + run_meta.yaml.

Limitations

  • Single environment (LunarLander-v3) and single solved policy — image / POMDP / high-dim continuous control unvalidated.
  • NOOD=20 episodes per injector; several detector differences fall inside bootstrap CI noise (e.g. ±0.22).
  • No pairwise detector-vs-detector significance tests — "PEDM 0.874 vs tsf 0.839" may not be separable.
  • Online actuator/dynamics injectors are simulator-specific (e.g. targets main-engine action a*=2); portability asserted, not demonstrated.
  • PEDM's conservative threshold (max validation score) gives best AUROC but worst delay (271.9 steps) — a calibration gap the benchmark exposes, not resolves.
  • Matched-time protocol is an evaluation protocol, not a deployable alarm; pixel-based RL is out of scope.
Open Questions

Where this benchmark invites the next move

  1. Does the detector ranking (PEDM > tsf > KDE …) survive on image-based or partially observed environments, or is it an artefact of LunarLander's flat 8-dim state?
  2. With NOOD=20 and CIs as wide as ±0.22, how many ordering claims survive a proper paired test (paired bootstrap / Wilcoxon on per-episode AUROCs)?
  3. Can matched-time + null-baseline become a standard reporting requirement for supervised time-series OOD benchmarks (TSB-UAD, TAB)?
  4. Would a detector scoring the commanded-vs-executed action mismatch crack the stuck-actuator row that PEDM only sees indirectly?
  5. Is there a threshold-calibration rule (conformal, SPRT) that recovers PEDM's ranking without its 271.9-step delay penalty?
  6. Do the hardest channels (0.697 / 0.636) become tractable with recurrent world models or multi-step prediction error?
