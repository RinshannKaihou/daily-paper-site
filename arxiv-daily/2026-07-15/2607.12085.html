Why This Matters

An end-to-end blueprint for industrialising LLM-as-a-judge at million-record scale — schema governance, cost control, and per-record auditability baked in.

Critical Edge

Every record was labeled by a single annotator — no Cohen's κ / Krippendorff's α, so the 0.93 F1 and 89% ceilings are unknown against annotator noise.

01 / METHOD

A schema-locked, configuration-driven evaluation pipeline

01
Ingest & Normalize
BigQuery logs → type-cast, drop invalid (empty/missing ID), normalize fields.
02
Shard
Round-robin into N=4 Parquet shards: df.iloc[i::N].
03
Evaluate
Async LLM-as-judge per shard, bounded concurrency, versioned YAML + Jinja2.
04
Validate & Re-eval
Only missing/malformed/range-violating rows regenerate, merged by stable row-id.
05
Aggregate & Audit
Weighted score Σ w·s, config-hash + per-record provenance, optional HTML render.

Task formalisation. Each turn x = ⟨prompt, response, meta⟩; the framework computes E: (X, Y, M) → Z — classifications, per-dimension quality scores s_d ∈ [1,5], translation metrics, rationales, and audit metadata.

Selective re-evaluation (the core novelty). Rather than re-running the full dataset on partial failures, only invalid rows re-enter a controlled regeneration path and are merged back via stable row identifiers — cutting redundant compute while preserving completeness. This, plus the governance packaging (versioned configs, config hashes, audit logs), is the paper's genuine contribution.

Setup. 4-bit quantization, NVIDIA H100, batch size 512, temperature 0.005, up to 3 retries. All evaluators open-source, so zero proprietary-API cost. References from human labels (4 annotators, same rubric), gold outputs, and weak-supervision heuristics.

Multi-dimensional rubric

    General / non-translation
  • Helpfulness · Clarity · Coherence
  • Conciseness · Instruction adherence
  • Tone alignment · Truthfulness
    • Translation-specific
  • Semantic accuracy · Readability
  • Cultural appropriateness
  • Selling relevance · Template fit

Each dimension emits an evaluator rationale for interpretability and failure-mode mining.

03 / ASSESSMENT

Strengths vs. Limitations

Strengths5
  • Genuine production scale — >2M records, ~50K/day — with schema locking, versioned configs, config hashes, and audit logs that few academic LLM-judge papers match.
  • Selective re-evaluation is a pragmatic, clean cost-reduction mechanism with a stable-row-id merge design.
  • Apples-to-apples benchmarking across 5 evaluators under identical prompt/schema/hardware/quant/batch.
  • Honest about the single-annotator limit and evaluator bias; frames automated scores as diagnostic, with a tiered human-in-the-loop strategy.
  • Clear general vs. translation taxonomy plus evaluator rationales for failure-mode mining.
Limitations6
  • Single-annotator validation — no inter-annotator agreement; headline numbers upper-bound-unknown.
  • Numeric scores reported without correlation, calibration, or CIs; dimension weights w_d never specified.
  • Exact evaluator versions unspecified; all at 4-bit (quantization not ablated); no throughput/latency numbers despite the central trade-off claim.
  • Retail-only, text-only, turn-level only — no multimodal, long-horizon, or tool-trace evaluation.
  • No code, prompts, configs, or data released; dataset is proprietary retail PII — external reproduction infeasible.
  • Manuscript red flags: missing architecture figure (placeholder), broken mid-citation sentence in §5.6, duplicated conclusion paragraph.
04 / OPEN

Questions for further exploration

Q1

How sensitive are 0.93 F1 / 89% to annotator noise? What does a double/triple-labeled subset do to Cohen's κ / Krippendorff's α?

Q2

Is the Llama 4B→70B gap (0.82→0.93) real judge quality, or an artifact of 4-bit quantization at small scale? Does it hold at full precision?

Q3

What are the actual throughput, latency, and per-1K-record cost behind the trade-off — and where is the Pareto frontier?

Q4

How robust is selective re-evaluation when the invalid-row rate spikes (prompt/schema/model changes)? When does targeted regen lose to a full rerun?

Q5

Do evaluator biases (verbosity, position, self-enhancement) show in score distributions, and does per-dimension z-score normalization change rankings?

Q6

Does the framework transfer out-of-domain (healthcare, legal, code) with the same governance primitives, or do domain rubrics break reproducibility?
