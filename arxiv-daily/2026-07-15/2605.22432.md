> **一句话总结：** 作者从 river-valley 损失景观视角解释 Muon（正交化加速 bulk/river 方向进展，但放大 dominant/valley-wall 噪声导致振荡），并提出 AMUSE——将 Muon 嵌入 Schedule-Free 框架并用随时间增大的插值系数 βt 在快速序列与平均序列之间渐变，免学习率调度、支持 anytime 训练，在 FineWeb 上 124M/720M/1.3B Llama 预训练及 ImageNet 分类/分割上一致改进性能-迭代 Pareto 前沿，达到 Muon 最终性能所需步数减少 1.51×（720M Llama）、1.34×（ImageNet ResNet-50）、2.57×（ImageNet ViT 微调）。

> **TL;DR:** AMUSE reinterprets Muon through the river-valley loss landscape (orthogonalization speeds the flat bulk "river" but amplifies high-curvature "valley-wall" noise), then stabilizes it inside the Schedule-Free framework with a time-varying interpolation coefficient βt that starts small (fast adaptation near the base iterate zt) and grows toward 1 (stable averaging near xt). It needs no learning-rate decay and supports anytime training. On FineWeb Llama pretraining (124M/720M/1.3B), ImageNet (ResNet-50, MAE ViT-B/16), CIFAR-10/100, SVHN, and ISIC 2018, AMUSE improves the performance-iteration Pareto frontier over AdamW, SF-AdamW, and Muon, reaching Muon's final performance with 1.51× fewer steps on 720M Llama, 1.34× fewer on ImageNet/ResNet-50, and 2.57× fewer on ImageNet ViT fine-tuning, at only ~1.067× the per-iteration wall-clock cost of AdamW.

## 核心贡献

- **对 Muon 的几何性解释**：基于 river-valley 损失景观，证明 Muon 的矩阵正交化显著增大更新的 bulk（river）分量（在 5k MNIST + 3 层 MLP 上 dominant ratio 远小于 SGD/AdamW），从而加速沿 river 的进展；但同一算子也会放大 dominant 方向上的残余噪声，引发 valley-wall 振荡（附录 A 用二维矩阵二次型给出"polar 归一化导致 bouncing"的解析例证）。
- **AMUSE 算法**：将 Muon 作为 base optimizer 嵌入 Schedule-Free（SF）三序列框架（zt 基序列、yt 梯度评估点、xt 推理用平均序列），并引入时变插值系数 βt = 1 − (T0/t)^ρ (1−βinit)。早期 βt 小、贴近 zt 实现快速适应，后期趋向 1、贴近 xt 抑制 valley-wall 振荡；该调度与总训练步数无关，因此天然支持 anytime 训练且无需学习率衰减。
- **极低额外开销**：相对 vanilla Muon 仅多存一份状态副本 zt；与 AdamW/SF-AdamW 内存持平（甚至略低，因为 Muon 动量缓冲只对矩阵参数维护）。相对 AdamW 仅多一个可调超参 ρ（Muon 动量 µ 固定为 0.95）。
- **跨任务一致改进 Pareto 前沿**：在视觉分类/分割（CIFAR-10/100、SVHN、ImageNet-1k、ISIC 2018、MAE ViT 微调）与 FineWeb 上 124M/720M/1.3B Llama 预训练上，AMUSE 一致优于 SGD/AdamW/Muon 及其 SF 版本，且在 124M Llama 上还击败 AdEMAMix、ADOPT、Signum、Prodigy、MARS、Lion、Sophia、SOAP 等强基线。

## Key Contributions

- **Geometric diagnosis of Muon.** Through the river-valley decomposition (Song et al., 2025a), the authors empirically show Muon's orthogonalization shifts updates toward the bulk subspace (much smaller dominant-subspace ratio than SGD/AdamW on MNIST/MLP), explaining its fast river progress, but the same non-selective normalization amplifies residual dominant-subspace noise, producing valley-wall oscillations — a tradeoff formalized via a 2D matrix-quadratic "bouncing" example in Appendix A.
- **The AMUSE optimizer.** Muon is dropped into the Schedule-Free framework with a time-varying interpolation coefficient βt = 1 − (T0/t)^ρ(1 − βinit) (after warmup T0). Small early βt keeps gradient evaluation near the fast base sequence zt; growing βt shifts it toward the averaged xt to suppress oscillations before orthogonalization. The schedule is horizon-independent, so AMUSE removes learning-rate decay and is anytime-compatible.
- **Minimal overhead.** AMUSE stores only one extra state copy (zt) vs. vanilla Muon, matching AdamW/SF-AdamW memory. It introduces only one extra tuned hyperparameter ρ (Muon momentum µ is fixed at 0.95); per-iteration wall-clock on 124M Llama is 0.719 s vs. 0.674 s for AdamW (1.067× relative, 182,298 vs. 194,469 tokens/s).
- **Empirical dominance of the performance-iteration frontier.** Across image classification/segmentation and FineWeb Llama pretraining (124M/720M/1.3B), AMUSE consistently beats (Schedule-Free) AdamW and Muon, and on 124M Llama also beats AdEMAMix, ADOPT, Signum, Prodigy, MARS, Lion, Sophia, and SOAP — all of which use tuned cosine schedules.

## Methodology

**River-valley decomposition.** Following Song et al. (2025a), the authors split parameter space using the top-k Hessian eigenspace (k = number of classes, motivated by Papyan's Neural Collapse observations): the dominant subspace Sk (steep valley walls, high curvature) and its orthogonal complement Sk⊥ (flat river, bulk). For any vector v they measure dominant/bulk ratios rdom(v)=‖Pk v‖/‖v‖, rbulk(v)=‖Pk⊥ v‖/‖v‖, with rdom² + rbulk² = 1.

**Diagnosis (Section 3).** On a 5k MNIST subset with a 3-layer MLP:
- Muon's update has much smaller rdom than SGD/AdamW, and orthogonalization itself reduces rdom(mt) → rdom(T(mt)) sharply (Figure 2). So orthogonalization helps the bulk/river direction.
- But because normalization is non-selective, small residual dominant components survive and get amplified into valley-wall oscillations (Appendix A: on an anisotropic 2×2 matrix quadratic, polar normalization produces sign-flipping "bouncing" that vanilla GD avoids).
- SF-Muon analysis (Eq. 4): the averaged trajectory xt is bulk-oriented, but the gradient ∇L(yt) still carries a large dominant component. Virtual evaluation at y(α)t = (1−α)zt + αxt shows rdom(∇L(y(α)t)) decreases monotonically with α (Figure 3b). So evaluating gradients closer to xt suppresses dominant components before orthogonalization.

**AMUSE update (Section 4).** Same SF formulation as SF-Muon,
Yt = (1 − βt)Zt + βtXt,  Mt = µMt−1 + (1 − µ)∇L(Yt),  Zt+1 = Zt − η T(Mt),  Xt+1 = (1 − ct+1)Xt + ct+1Zt+1,
with βt fixed at βinit during warmup, then
βt = 1 − (T0/t)^ρ (1 − βinit),  ρ ∈ [0,1].
ρ = 0 recovers fixed-β SF-Muon; larger ρ moves evaluation toward xt faster. βt → 1 as t → ∞ and is independent of total horizon. Non-Muon parameters (embeddings, norms, heads) use SF-AdamW (LLM/MAE) or SF-SGD (other image tasks). Linear warmup is kept; no decay phase is used.

**Why it works.** Post-hoc EWA and learning-rate decay applied to AMUSE's xt trajectory yield almost no further loss reduction (Figure 6), confirming AMUSE already tracks the river; constant-LR Muon benefits substantially from both, indicating it oscillates. AMUSE also exhibits higher cosine similarity between consecutive ∆xt updates, consistent with smoother river progress.

## Results & Highlights

- **Step-efficiency vs. Muon (headline claim).** AMUSE reaches Muon's final performance with **1.51× fewer steps on 720M Llama pretraining (FineWeb)**, **1.34× fewer steps on ImageNet-1k with ResNet-50**, and **2.57× fewer steps on ImageNet fine-tuning with ViT-B/16 (MAE)**.
- **LLM pretraining perplexity (FineWeb, Llama-style).** AMUSE achieves the lowest validation perplexity throughout training at all three scales — **124M** (vs. AdamW, Muon, SF-AdamW), **720M** (16k iters, ~16.3B tokens, large batch 1984), and **1.3B** (24.8k iters, ~26B tokens, batch 2048). The authors specifically note SF-AdamW struggles with the large batches typical of modern LLMs (Morwani et al., 2025), whereas AMUSE scales effectively to 720M and 1.3B.
- **Broader optimizer comparison (124M Llama, Figure 17).** Against AdEMAMix, ADOPT, Signum, Prodigy, MARS, Lion, Sophia, and SOAP (all using tuned cosine schedules from Semenov et al., 2026), AMUSE attains the lowest validation perplexity throughout training and the best final perplexity.
- **Image-domain accuracy (Figure 7, 5 seeds).** AMUSE achieves the highest test accuracy across all evaluated settings: CIFAR-10 (Wide-ResNet-16-8), CIFAR-100 (DenseNet), SVHN (ResNet-3-96), ImageNet-1k (ResNet-50), ISIC 2018 segmentation (U-Net), and MAE ViT-B/16 fine-tuning — including the practically important case of fine-tuning an AdamW-pretrained MAE checkpoint, where vanilla Muon is known to fail (optimizer mismatch) but AMUSE works.
- **Wall-clock cost (Table 18, 124M Llama).** Per-iteration time: AdamW 0.674 s, SF-AdamW 0.684 s, Muon 0.706 s, AMUSE 0.719 s — i.e., AMUSE is **1.067× AdamW** (throughput 182,298 tokens/s vs. 194,469). The overhead vs. Muon is essentially the Newton–Schulz orthogonalization plus one extra state copy.
- **AMUSE ≠ post-hoc averaging/decay (Appendix F.2).** Adding EWA (coefficients 0.99/0.999/0.9995) or a 2k-step WSD decay to tuned constant-LR Muon improves it but none matches AMUSE on 124M Llama; the same holds for Muon+EWA on ImageNet (Figure 19).
- **Ablation: cap βt at 0.95 (Figure 25).** A clipped schedule that stops increasing βt underperforms unconstrained AMUSE, with the gap widening late in training — confirming benefit comes from continuously shifting evaluation toward xt, not from reaching a particular large value.
- **Ablation: remove Muon momentum (Figure 26).** Dropping the pre-orthogonalization momentum buffer causes a large performance drop for both AMUSE and SF-Muon, confirming momentum is essential for smoothing stochastic noise before orthogonalization.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Genuine and well-motivated. The river-valley diagnosis of Muon (orthogonalization = bulk boost but also dominant-noise amplification) is a clean conceptual contribution, and the fix (time-varying βt inside SF) is a natural, principled synthesis rather than an ad-hoc trick. The mechanical claim that evaluating gradients closer to xt reduces rdom(∇L(yt)) is directly verified (Figure 3b).
- **Rigor:** The diagnostic experiments (Sections 3.1–3.2) are run on a deliberately small controlled setup — a 3-layer MLP on a 5k MNIST subset — so the river-valley mechanism is demonstrated convincingly but only at toy scale; the leap to Llama-1.3B rests on trajectory-level proxies (‖∆xt‖, EWA/decay insensitivity, cosine similarity) rather than direct Hessian-eigenspace measurement at scale. The bouncing example in Appendix A is an idealized polar-normalization toy, not a faithful model of Newton–Schulz momentum dynamics (the authors acknowledge this).
- **Baseline fairness:** Image/LLM baselines use cosine schedules with hyperparameters tuned by Defazio et al. (2024) and Semenov et al. (2026), which is reasonable. However, AMUSE gets two extra tuned knobs (βinit ∈ {0.4, 0.6}, ρ ∈ {0.6, 0.8}) plus a warmup horizon T0 that the SF formulation effectively reuses, while Muon's momentum µ is fixed at 0.95 rather than tuned — so AMUSE benefits from a (slightly) larger tuning budget. The fairest apples-to-apples comparison is AMUSE vs. tuned fixed-β SF-Muon (Appendix F.6), which AMUSE wins, but that comparison itself involves the same extra ρ knob.
- **Reproducibility:** Good. Algorithm 1 (Appendix C.1) gives full pseudocode; the βt schedule has a closed form; hyperparameters are reported per task; code is public. Newton–Schulz orthogonalization is standard. Memory and wall-clock overheads are explicitly quantified.
- **Significance:** Practical and timely — Muon is being adopted in production recipes (Kimi-K2, GLM-5, DeepSeek-V4), so a drop-in anytime variant with no LR schedule is valuable. The 1.51×/1.34×/2.57× step-reduction claims are the headline and are striking, but they measure steps-to-Muon's-final-performance, not a fixed compute budget; AMUSE's ~6.7% per-step wall-clock penalty partially eats the step savings.
- **Red flags / overclaiming:** The paper is measured — it does not claim SOTA loss at fixed FLOPs, acknowledges the extra zt memory, and concedes that an overhead-free stabilizer would be preferable. The biggest caveat is scale: the largest model is 1.3B trained on ~26B tokens, far below production LLM regimes where schedule-Free behavior and Muon stability could differ. LLM evaluation is perplexity-only — no downstream task or zero-shot benchmark — so "improves LLM pretraining" is currently a perplexity claim, not a capability claim. There is also no comparison with the concurrent schedule-free spectral optimizer of Apte et al. (2605.23061), which targets essentially the same problem.

## Strengths

- Clean, intuitive theory-to-method story: the same river-valley lens diagnoses Muon's weakness and prescribes the SF averaging fix.
- Time-varying βt with a horizon-independent closed form elegantly resolves the early-adaptation vs. late-stability tradeoff that fixed-β SF-Muon cannot (Figures 5, 23, 25).
- Strong, broad empirical coverage: 6 image setups (5 seeds) + 3 LLM scales + 10-optimizer comparison on 124M Llama.
- Important practical property: works for fine-tuning AdamW-pretrained checkpoints (MAE ViT), where vanilla Muon fails — directly addresses a known deployment pain point.
- Low marginal cost: one extra state copy, ~1.067× AdamW wall-clock, one extra hyperparameter.
- Honest, useful ablations (cap βt; drop momentum; EWA/WSD on Muon) that isolate why AMUSE works rather than just showing it works.

## Limitations

- **Scale.** Largest LLM is 1.3B on ~26B tokens; behavior at 7B+ and multi-trillion-token regimes is unverified.
- **Evaluation depth for LLMs.** Perplexity only — no downstream, zero-shot, or long-context evaluation to confirm perplexity gains translate to capability gains.
- **Diagnostic-vs-deployment gap.** The mechanistic river-valley claims are demonstrated at MLP/MNIST scale; at LLM scale they are supported only by indirect proxies.
- **Extra memory vs. vanilla Muon** (one full state copy zt), explicitly acknowledged — non-trivial at very large model sizes where optimizer state memory dominates.
- **Hyperparameter sensitivity** is studied only on 124M Llama (Appendix F.4) over a small grid; behavior under more aggressive ρ or much longer T0 is not characterized.
- **No head-to-head with the most directly concurrent method** (Apte et al., 2605.23061, "schedule-free spectral optimization"), which targets the same anytime-Muon niche.
- **Muon momentum µ pinned at 0.95** without tuning, so the reported gaps may slightly flatter AMUSE relative to a µ-tuned Muon.

## Relevance

与「LLM 预训练优化器」研究方向高度相关：AMUSE 是 Muon（已被 Kimi-K2、GLM-5、DeepSeek-V4 等生产级配方采用）的免调度、anytime 变体，直接落入日常关注的「大规模训练效率 / 优化器设计」主题；其 river-valley 几何分析与 Schedule-Free 范式也为理解训练动力学提供了可复用的理论工具，适合作为追踪 Muon 系方法与学习率调度替代方案的参考。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does AMUSE's perplexity advantage at 124M–1.3B persist at 7B–70B scale and multi-trillion-token budgets, and does it translate into downstream/zero-shot benchmark gains rather than just perplexity?
- Can the extra zt state copy be avoided (e.g., via in-place momentum reuse or a low-rank approximation of the averaged sequence) without losing the stabilization benefit?
- How does AMUSE interact with distributed training niceties (gradient accumulation, ZeRO sharding of optimizer state, tensor parallelism) — does the zt copy complicate sharding compared to AdamW?
- Is the time-varying βt schedule optimal, or could an adaptive βt driven by online dominant-ratio or sharpness estimates outperform the fixed (T0/t)^ρ form?
- A direct empirical comparison with the concurrent schedule-free spectral optimizer (Apte et al., 2605.23061) on identical compute would clarify which stabilization mechanism generalizes better.
- Does the river-valley diagnosis (and hence AMUSE's advantage) carry over to non-Transformer architectures (SSMs, MoE experts, diffusion U-Nets) where Hessian spectral structure may differ?
