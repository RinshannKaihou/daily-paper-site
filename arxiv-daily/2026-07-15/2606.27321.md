> **一句话总结：** 作者提出两种与 Top-k 稀疏自编码器架构兼容的稀疏正则化项（off-support ℓ1 与 scale-invariant ℓ1/ℓ2-ratio），它们作用在 Top-k 选择之前的激活上并限定于 batch-active 单元，在 ImageNet-1K 和 Open Images V7 上跨 CLIP、SigLIP2、ViT-L/16 三个视觉基础模型，在不损害重建质量的前提下稳定提升单义性（如 ViT-L/16 k=32 平均单义性从 0.346 升到 0.474）。

> **TL;DR:** This paper introduces two sparsity regularizers for Top-k sparse autoencoders (SAEs) — an off-support ℓ1 penalty and a scale-invariant ℓ1/ℓ2-ratio penalty — both applied to pre-selection activations of batch-active units. Across ImageNet-1K and Open Images V7 with frozen CLIP ViT-L/14, SigLIP2, and supervised ViT-L/16 encoders (k∈{32,64,128}), both regularizers consistently improve monosemanticity while preserving R² reconstruction (e.g., ViT-L/16 ImageNet k=32: mean monosemanticity 0.346 → 0.474 with Reg. 1; weighted class purity 0.417 → 0.553). The ℓ1/ℓ2-ratio further concentrates information into fewer leading units, making reconstruction more robust to the inference-time choice of k and boosting small-budget linear probing (AUC on truncated probes up from 0.488 → 0.693 for CLIP at k=128).

## 核心贡献

- 提出**两种与 Top-k 架构兼容的稀疏正则化项**：(1) 对未选中（off-support）激活的 ℓ1 惩罚；(2) 基于激活向量 ℓ1/ℓ2 比例的 scale-invariant 惩罚（Hoyer 2004）。两者都作用于 Top-k 算子**之前**的激活，且**仅施加于 batch-active 单元**（在该 batch 中被 Top-k 至少选中过一次的单元）。
- 在三个视觉基础模型（CLIP ViT-L/14、SigLIP2、监督式 ViT-L/16）、两个数据集（ImageNet-1K、Open Images V7）和 k∈{32,64,128} 上系统验证：**两种正则化都在保持重建质量（R²）的前提下稳定提升单义性（Monosemanticity Score）和类别纯度（class purity）**。
- 揭示 ℓ1/ℓ2-ratio 带来的"几何集中"效应带来 **beyond-monosemanticity 的额外收益**：(a) 重建对推理时 k 的选择更鲁棒；(b) 判别信息被前移到少数几个 leading 单元，从而改善小预算线性探针表现。
- 通过 ablation 论证**关键设计选择**：必须用 active-unit mask（否则死神经元数激增 1-2 个数量级）；off-support 限制（仅惩罚未选中单元）明显优于在全部激活上的完整 ℓ1（后者会更快损害 R²）。
- 核心论点：**硬架构稀疏（Top-k）与软稀疏正则化互补而非互斥**，纠正了 Top-k SAE 引入时为规避 ℓ1 缺陷而完全弃用显式稀疏项的主流做法。

## Key Contributions

- Two sparsity regularizers compatible with the Top-k SAE architecture: (1) an **off-support ℓ1 penalty** that drives the masked residual `μ ⊙ (a − z)` (activations of units not selected by Top-k) toward zero; (2) a **scale-invariant ℓ1/ℓ2-ratio penalty** (Hoyer 2004) on the full masked activation vector that concentrates the code onto fewer effective units. Both act on pre-selection activations and are restricted to **batch-active units** via a binary mask μ.
- Systematic evaluation across three frozen vision foundation models (CLIP ViT-L/14, SigLIP2, supervised ViT-L/16), two datasets (ImageNet-1K, Open Images V7), k∈{32,64,128}, three seeds: **both regularizers consistently improve monosemanticity (mean/median) and binary/weighted class purity at no cost to R²**, with only marginal regressions on SigLIP2/ImageNet under Reg. 2.
- Demonstrate that the ℓ1/ℓ2 concentration yields **benefits beyond interpretability**: robustness to inference-time k (NMSE stays flat when k_inf departs from training k, whereas baseline overfits) and front-loading of discriminative information into leading units (higher truncated-probe AUC, e.g. CLIP k=128: 0.488 → 0.693).
- Ablations justifying design choices: **active-unit mask is essential** (removing it inflates dead-neuron counts by 1–2 orders of magnitude, e.g. CLIP ImageNet k=64 Reg.1: 1 → 269 dead units); **off-support restriction is essential** (a full ℓ1 penalty on all activations degrades R² far more steeply and peaks lower in monosemanticity).
- Conceptual reframing: hard architectural sparsity (Top-k) and soft sparsity regularization are **complementary, not mutually exclusive** — correcting the field's default of discarding any explicit sparsity term once Top-k is adopted.

## Methodology

The authors train Top-k sparse autoencoders on frozen image embeddings `x ∈ R^d` from a VFM. The encoder produces pre-activations `π = W_e(x − b_d) + b_e`, ReLU yields activations `a = ReLU(π) ∈ R^m_≥0` (m=8192 latents, m ≫ d), the Top-k operator keeps the k largest coordinates to form the sparse code `z`, and the decoder reconstructs `x̂ = W_d z + b_d`. Decoder columns are unit-ℓ2-normalized (standard SAE practice). An auxiliary Top-k loss `L_aux` (reconstructing from the top *unselected*/dead units, following Gao et al. 2024) is included.

Two regularizers are added as `λ R(B)`:

- **Regularizer 1 (off-support ℓ1):** `R = (1/N) Σ ‖μ ⊙ (a^(i) − z^(i))‖_1`. The residual is supported exactly on units not selected by Top-k (off-support), masked to batch-active units. Drives off-support activation mass toward zero, sharpening each unit's selectivity.
- **Regularizer 2 (ℓ1/ℓ2-ratio):** `R = (1/N) Σ (‖μ ⊙ a^(i)‖_1 / ‖μ ⊙ a^(i)‖_2)`. Scale-invariant; its square proxies the effective number of active units, so minimizing it concentrates the code onto fewer effective units.

The **active-unit mask** μ ∈ {0,1}^m indicates units selected by Top-k for at least one sample in the batch; it is recomputed per batch and detached from gradients. Its necessity is justified by ablation: unselected units receive no reconstruction gradient, so penalizing them would push them to zero with no counter-signal, inflating dead neurons.

Total objective: `L = L_recon + α L_aux + λ R`, with λ=0 recovering the baseline. λ is selected per configuration as the value giving the largest validation-monosemanticity gain while keeping reconstruction on par; Figure 2 shows the trade-off is smooth and broad (results not sensitive to the exact λ).

Evaluation metrics: R² (reconstruction), Monosemanticity Score of Pach et al. 2025 (mean M_μ and median M_m), dead-neuron count, binary/weighted class purity on ImageNet-1K val, normalized MSE (NMSE) under inference-time k_inf sweeps, and AUC of linear-probe top-1 accuracy versus truncation budget k′∈[1,32].

## Results & Highlights

- **Monosemanticity gains at preserved R² (Table 1):** On ViT-L/16 ImageNet k=32, mean monosemanticity rises from baseline 0.346 → 0.474 (Reg. 1) and 0.414 (Reg. 2), with R² essentially flat (0.776 → 0.780 / 0.800). On CLIP ImageNet k=64, M_μ goes 0.464 → 0.505 (Reg. 1) at identical R²=0.824; median M_m 0.435 → 0.493.
- **Class purity (Table 2):** Weighted purity on ViT-L/16 ImageNet k=32 jumps from 0.417 (baseline) to 0.553 (Reg. 1) — the largest gain in the table. Binary purity on the same configuration goes 0.244 → 0.384. Both regularizers improve purity in nearly every cell, with Reg. 1 > Reg. 2.
- **Dead-neuron behavior diverges:** Reg. 1 *decreases* dead neurons (SigLIP2 ImageNet k=128: 120 → 73), Reg. 2 *increases* them (120 → 159) — a modest trade-off for its stronger concentration.
- **Zero-shot transfer (ImageNet→Open Images, Fig. 3):** Both regularizers stay more monosemantic than the baseline in nearly every configuration under cross-dataset evaluation.
- **Robustness to inference-time k (Fig. 7 left):** Under truncation (k_inf < k) only Reg. 2 beats baseline (lower NMSE, gap widens with more aggressive truncation); above training k, baseline NMSE rises (overfitting) while both regularizers stay flat.
- **Small-budget linear probing (Table 3):** Normalized AUC of the accuracy-vs-k′ curve (k′∈[1,32]) is higher for Reg. 2 in all 9 configs. CLIP k=128: AUC 0.488 (baseline) → 0.693 (Reg. 2); top-1@k′=32 essentially matched (0.775 → 0.805). SigLIP2 k=128: 0.499 → 0.756. Asymptotic accuracy is identical across all three models (≈0.82 for CLIP k=64), so no method changes total recoverable information — only how few units are needed.
- **Active-unit mask ablation (Table 4):** Removing the mask inflates dead neurons by 1–2 orders of magnitude (CLIP ImageNet k=64 Reg. 1: 1 → 269; CLIP Open Images k=64 Reg. 1: 2 → 837), unambiguously across all configs (single seed 42).
- **Off-support vs full ℓ1 (Fig. 8):** On Open Images k=64, a full ℓ1 (penalizing selected units too) degrades R² far more steeply with λ and peaks lower in monosemanticity than the off-support variant.

## Authors & Credibility

Authors are from Université Paris-Saclay / CentraleSupélec (MICS and the Cancer Data Science Unit / INSERM / Gustave Roussy / IHU PRISM) and Concordia University CSSE + Mila (Montreal). The work builds directly on the Top-k SAE line (Gao et al. 2024, OpenAI scaling-and-evaluating SAEs) and the Monosemanticity Score metric of Pach et al. 2025, both appropriately cited. Methodology is conventionally rigorous: matched-pair comparisons (only λ varied), 3 seeds, test-set evaluation, standard SAE hyperparameters (8192 latents, unit-norm decoder columns, auxiliary Top-k loss). The honest framing of zero-shot transfer (they explicitly note they claim persistence-in-absolute-terms, not *better relative* transfer) and of Reg. 2's dead-neuron trade-off is a credibility plus.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Genuine but incremental. The core claim — that Top-k SAEs, despite being designed to *avoid* ℓ1, still benefit from a carefully-placed sparsity term — is a useful corrective. The off-support ℓ1 is a small twist; the ℓ1/ℓ2 ratio is borrowed directly from Hoyer 2004 NMF sparseness constraints and is acknowledged as such. The genuinely novel contribution is the *combination* (pre-selection activation + batch-active mask + off-support restriction), which is well-motivated and ablated.
- **Rigor:** Strong on controls — 3 seeds, matched pairs, both label-grounded (class purity) and embedding-grounded (Monosemanticity Score) interpretability metrics, a transfer experiment, and two targeted ablations (mask, off-support restriction). The mask ablation is single-seed (42), but the authors justify this by noting the effect spans 1–2 orders of magnitude — defensible.
- **Reproducibility:** Good. Latent dim, k grid, optimizer details, and λ-selection protocol are specified; full hyperparameters deferred to appendix. Datasets (ImageNet-1K, Open Images V7) and encoders (CLIP ViT-L/14, SigLIP2, ViT-L/16) are all public. No code link visible in the main text — a gap.
- **Significance:** The downstream findings (k-robustness, small-budget probing gains from Reg. 2) are the more surprising and useful results and may have more practical impact than the headline monosemanticity bump.
- **Red flags / weak points:**
  - **Monosemanticity gains are uneven across encoders.** The headline gains concentrate on the supervised ViT-L/16 (which has *very low* baseline monosemanticity, 0.346/0.239/0.155 — a regime where almost any regularizer helps). On SigLIP2/ImageNet, Reg. 2 actually *decreases* mean monosemanticity marginally in two cells; the paper concedes this. The CLIP gains are real but smaller (e.g., M_μ 0.464→0.505).
  - **Class purity baselines are weak in absolute terms** (binary purity 0.065–0.285 across configs) — with 1000 ImageNet classes, chance is ~0.001, so the numbers are well above chance, but the latent-to-class mapping remains far from truly monosemantic; the relative gains should not be read as the latents becoming cleanly class-aligned.
  - **No comparison to other interpretability-oriented SAE variants** (JumpReLU SAEs, BatchTopK, Matryoshka SAEs are only mentioned as future work). The baseline set is essentially "vanilla Top-k" only, so it is unclear how much of the gain survives against stronger contemporary SAE variants.
  - **Reg. 2 increases dead neurons** — framed as a "modest trade-off," but on SigLIP2/Open Images k=32 it rises from 196 → 260, which is non-trivial relative to an 8192-dim dictionary and could matter for downstream feature coverage.
  - **The ℓ1/ℓ2 probing result (Table 3) compares only against baseline and Reg. 1** (which the authors correctly note serves as a control because it doesn't reshape retained activations); a comparison to a dedicated feature-selection or dictionary-pruning baseline would strengthen the "concentration" claim.

## Strengths

- Clean, well-motivated central thesis (hard vs soft sparsity complementarity) backed by a tidy matched-pair experimental design.
- Two regularizers with qualitatively different mechanisms (off-support suppression vs global concentration), each with its own downstream payoff — Reg. 1 for raw interpretability, Reg. 2 for k-robustness and probing.
- Honest reporting: exceptions (SigLIP2 regressions) and trade-offs (Reg. 2 dead neurons) are explicitly called out rather than buried.
- The mask and off-support ablations are decisive and directly support the design choices.
- Qualitative figures (rank-matched and unit-matched image grids) complement the quantitative metrics in a way that addresses the known concern that the Monosemanticity Score is an embedding-similarity proxy.

## Limitations

- Baseline is limited to vanilla Top-k SAE; no head-to-head against JumpReLU, BatchTopK, Matryoshka, or other contemporary SAE variants that also target the same Top-k weaknesses.
- Largest monosemanticity gains appear on the supervised ViT-L/16, whose baseline monosemanticity is unusually low — generalization to already-well-behaved encoders (e.g., SigLIP2) is more modest and occasionally negative.
- Reg. 2 reliably increases dead-neuron counts, which may reduce effective feature coverage for very large dictionaries.
- Interpretability is measured by automated metrics (Monosemanticity Score, class purity) plus qualitative image grids; no large-scale human evaluation of feature monosemanticity.
- No downstream task evaluation beyond ImageNet linear probing; effect on, e.g., OOD detection, retrieval, or safety auditing (the paper's motivating "auditing" framing) is not tested.
- Code release not evident in the main text.
- The λ hyperparameter is selected on a validation set per configuration; although sensitivity is shown to be broad, this per-config tuning somewhat complicates drop-in deployment claims.

## Relevance

This work is directly relevant to research on interpretability tooling for large foundation models and mechanistic interpretability via sparse autoencoders. For readers tracking SAE-based feature decomposition of vision (and by extension language) foundation models, it offers concrete, low-cost regularizers that improve feature monosemanticity and probing efficiency, plus a useful conceptual correction that explicit sparsity regularization and Top-k architectural sparsity should coexist.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Do the gains (especially Reg. 1's monosemanticity bump and Reg. 2's concentration) carry over to SAEs trained on *language* model activations, where the polysemanticity/superposition story originated and where Top-k SAEs are also standard?
- How do these regularizers interact with stronger contemporary SAE variants — BatchTopK, Matryoshka, JumpReLU — when combined?
- Does the ℓ1/ℓ2-ratio concentration translate into measurable gains on downstream auditing tasks (OOD detection, adversarial-feature identification, safety-relevant concept localization) rather than just linear probing?
- Is the Reg. 2 dead-neuron increase a genuine coverage loss or merely a re-allocation of capacity — i.e., do the surviving units still cover rare/long-tail concepts?
- Can λ be scheduled or made adaptive (e.g., annealed, or tied to reconstruction loss) to remove per-configuration validation tuning?
- Does the off-support ℓ1 + ℓ1/ℓ2-ratio combination (both at once) outperform either alone, or do their effects saturate/conflict?
