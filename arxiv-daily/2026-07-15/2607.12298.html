§ 01

Methodology — twin hierarchy, not fabric duplication

Zynq-7000 · XC7Z020

EIR partitions responsibility by SoC island. The target accelerator runs in the Programmable Logic (PL); an AXI-GPIO IP monitors outputs and flags faults. All detection and recovery run on the Processing System (PS), so no fabric-level replica is ever built. PL state is captured through the PCAP interface, using a logic-location (*.ll) map to selectively read back only active regions into off-chip DDR.

Rabbit re-executes the accelerator functionally at high abstraction and flags any divergence |O_Rabbit − O_PL| ≥ ε. It is deliberately not always-on — PS is slower than PL and continuous running would starve other tasks. Tortoise, generated by Yosys CXXRTL from the same Verilog as the PL bitstream, stays dormant until Rabbit flags a fault, then replays cycle-by-cycle from the last trusted checkpoint to reconstruct a fault-free state.

A two-stage checkpoint-selection policy injects per-layer faults (conv layers mask; dense/logit layers corrupt) and ranks MACs by magnitude |x·w| — only the top-30% are audited.

§ 03

Strengths & Limitations

balanced read

◆ Strengths

  • Clean decoupling of detection (cheap, frequent, coarse) from recovery (expensive, rare, precise) — maps onto the PS/PL asymmetry.
  • Zero PL area overhead and no accelerator RTL change — preserves the customer's synthesized netlist and timing closure.
  • Magnitude-aware MAC ranking is principled; the 530→170 µs isolation win is concrete and defensible.
  • Honest scoping — assumptions (PS slack, transient-only, ECC BRAM/DDR, deterministic replay) stated up front.
  • Strong author pedigree (EPOCH, Auto-Healer, CRAFT, Precision Strike).

◆ Limitations

  • No formal coverage guarantee. Only faults propagating to monitored outputs within Δ cycles of a checkpoint are caught.
  • Single prototype platform (Zynq-7000 / XC7Z020). UltraScale+ scalability explicitly future work.
  • Workload-specific thresholds — k=30% from LeNet-5 profiling; “not a universal value.”
  • Deterministic replay assumed — DMA, async I/O, or uncontrolled inputs break correctness.
  • Readback-level injection emulates faults; equivalence to live SEUs / voltage droop argued, not measured.

Q4
Can the top-30% threshold be derived adaptively at runtime for networks with dynamic execution graphs or drifting input distributions?
Q5
Does readback-level XOR injection produce the same detect/recovery behavior as live physical faults (radiation, FPGAHammer-style voltage glitch)?
Q6
Is there a graceful degradation curve — or a cliff — when the PS is heavily loaded by other real-time tasks?
