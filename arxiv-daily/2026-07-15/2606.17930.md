> **一句话总结：** UK AI Security Institute 团队在 7 个高难度基准（TerminalBench、SWE-Bench Pro、FrontierMath、HealthBench、HLE 及两个网络安全评测）上对 6–12 个前沿模型（Opus 4→4.6、GPT-5→5.4 等）施加三种简单推理扩展干预（更大 token 预算、上下文压缩、重复提交），发现 FrontierMath 在 1M→10M token 下平均提升 +11.7 pp、HLE 在 64k→5M 下提升 +11.9 pp，而 HealthBench 与两个软件工程基准几乎已饱和；并据此主张基准分数高度依赖协议，应将能力表达为推理计算的函数。

> **TL;DR:** The UK AI Security Institute evaluates 6 frontier LLMs (plus up to 12 on two cyber suites) from May 2025–March 2026 on seven hard benchmarks (TerminalBench, SWE-Bench Pro, FrontierMath, HealthBench, HLE, Cyber CTFs, The Last Ones) under a unified ReAct scaffold, applying three deliberately simple inference-scaling levers — larger token budgets (5M–30M), context compaction, and repeated submission — with or without oracle correctness feedback. Larger budgets yield large gains on FrontierMath (+11.7 pp from 1M→10M tokens) and HLE (+11.9 pp from 64k→5M) but near-zero on HealthBench (+0.32 pts) and SWE-Bench Pro (+0.27 pp); newer model generations gain mainly via task reach and reliability rather than token efficiency, and repeated submission helps on all five main benchmarks (multipliers 1.11×–1.71×). The paper argues benchmark scores are protocol-dependent and should be reported as capability-vs-compute curves, especially for safety/policy comparisons.

## 核心贡献

- **统一框架下的系统推理扩展研究**：在统一的 ReAct scaffold（Inspect AI 实现）下，跨 5 个主基准 + 2 个网络安全基准、对 6–12 个跨越三代的前沿模型（Opus 4/4.5/4.6、GPT-5/5.2/5.4，以及 Mythos Preview 等）施加相同的推理扩展协议，建立「简单、通用、可复现方法所能达到的能力下界」。
- **量化「典型预算 vs 扩展预算」的差距**：明确给出每个基准在典型发表预算之上还能榨取多少性能——FrontierMath +11.7 pp、HLE +11.9 pp（oracle 反馈下 +15.5 pp），而 HealthBench、SWE-Bench Pro、TerminalBench 几乎已饱和。
- **任务级三分解（reach / efficiency / reliability）**：将跨代增益分解为「能解锁多少新任务」「解锁任务上需要多少 token」「解锁任务上多可靠地解出」，发现跨代进步主要来自 reach 与 reliability，而非 token 效率。
- **协议维度的可控消融**：系统比较 no-feedback vs oracle-feedback、以及固定总预算下的 serial（深）vs parallel（宽，pass@k）分配，证明这些「实现细节」实质上改变了测得的性能。
- **可操作的评测方法论建议**：提出三条建议——按推理计算量报告能力曲线、显式文档化协议选择、在共享大计算区间内做跨代比较——对安全/政策相关评测尤为重要。

## Key Contributions

- **Unified inference-scaling study across domains**: Under one ReAct scaffold (Inspect AI), applies the same protocol to 5 main benchmarks + 2 cyber benchmarks across 6–12 frontier models spanning three generations (Opus 4/4.5/4.6, GPT-5/5.2/5.4, plus Mythos Preview), establishing a lower bound on what simple, general, reproducible methods can elicit.
- **Quantifies the typical-budget-to-extended-budget uplift**: Concrete per-benchmark deltas show where published single-budget scores leave performance on the table — FrontierMath +11.7 pp (1M→10M), HLE +11.9 pp (64k→5M, +15.5 pp under oracle feedback), vs near-saturation on HealthBench (+0.32 pts), SWE-Bench Pro (+0.27 pp), TerminalBench (+1.26 pp).
- **Task-level decomposition into reach / efficiency / reliability**: Shows cross-generational gains come mainly from unlocking more (and harder) tasks and solving them more consistently, not from using fewer tokens per solve.
- **Controlled ablation of protocol dimensions**: Separately varies feedback (none vs oracle) and compute allocation (serial depth vs parallel pass@k), demonstrating that submission rules, feedback, and width-vs-depth are measurement-design choices that materially shift scores.
- **Actionable methodological recommendations**: Argues evaluations should report capability as a function of inference-time compute, document protocol choices explicitly, and compare model generations only over a large shared compute range at matched budgets — particularly for safety- and policy-relevant settings.

## Methodology

**Models (Table 1).** The main fully-crossed design uses six frontier models from two families spanning three generations (May 2025–March 2026): Anthropic Opus 4 → 4.5 → 4.6 and OpenAI GPT-5 → 5.2 → 5.4. Two cyber benchmarks add up to 12 models (including o3, Sonnet 4.5, GPT-5.1/5.3 Codex, GPT-5.2 Codex, and the Anthropic-provided Mythos Preview checkpoint). All models run at high reasoning effort (xhigh for Anthropic, high for OpenAI) with a 16,000 reasoning-token budget per generation call — the budget at which HLE accuracy is reported to peak.

**Benchmarks (Table 2).** Five main benchmarks — TerminalBench 2.0 (stateful SWE, unit tests, 86 tasks), SWE-Bench Pro (stateful SWE, 100 tasks), FrontierMath (stateful math, code verification, only 12 tasks), HealthBench-Hard (stateless medicine, continuous rubric, 100 tasks), and HLE (stateless expert knowledge, LLM-graded binary, 100 tasks) — plus two reused cyber benchmarks: Cyber CTFs (71 capture-the-flag tasks) and The Last Ones (a single long-horizon cyber range scored by 32 milestones). Per-trajectory token caps are TB 10M, SBP 30M, FM 10M, HB 10M, HLE 5M, CTF 50M, TLO 100M.

**Three simple inference-scaling techniques (uniform across cells):**
1. Expanded total token budgets of 5M–30M per trajectory (1–3 orders of magnitude above typical defaults).
2. Context compaction via Inspect AI's default summary-based strategy, triggered when running context exceeds 130k tokens (~65% of the smallest 200k window).
3. Iterative resubmission with a hard cap of 999 submissions, plus a lightweight LLM-judge repetition guard that terminates a trajectory after 3+ consecutive semantically-equivalent submissions.

**Two feedback conditions.** *No feedback*: an ambiguous "Your answer has been saved" acknowledgement. *Oracle score feedback*: the model is told whether each submission is correct (or given HealthBench's partial-credit score), and the trajectory terminates on the first fully correct submission. Both share an adaptive continuation prompt inviting refinement or a fresh approach.

**Protocol details.** 5 independent trajectories per (benchmark, model, task, condition) cell (10 total). Scoring: programmatic for stateful benchmarks (unit tests, code verification, flag capture, milestone progress); GPT-4o-mini at temperature 0 as LLM judge for HLE and HealthBench. Token budgets are all-inclusive for the target model (input, output, reasoning) but exclude judge tokens; a 90-minute per-call timeout is enforced and any affected trajectories were re-run.

**Analyses.** Cumulative inference-scaling curves S_agg(t) (Eq. 2) characterize performance vs tokens consumed; plateau is summarized by g_cap = (y(cap) − y(cap/2))/log10(2) with "weak remaining growth" = g_cap < 1 pp per 10×. Task-level regressions (linear probability for reach, log-tokens for efficiency, solve-rate for reliability) decompose generational effects with model-generation × task-difficulty interactions. Parallel scaling uses pass@k at fixed total budget B split across k ∈ {1,2,5,10} trajectories.

## Results & Highlights

- **FrontierMath gains +11.7 ± 11.0 pp** on average across models when expanding the token budget from the typical published 1M tokens to the evaluated 10M-token cap (oracle feedback +11.94 ± 10.08; no feedback +11.39 ± 12.80) — Table 3.
- **HLE gains +11.87 ± 10.40 pp** from 64k → 5M tokens pooled, with the oracle-feedback condition reaching **+15.47 ± 12.26 pp** vs only +8.27 ± 7.51 pp under no feedback — a 2× feedback-driven split (Table 3).
- **SWE-Bench Pro gains only +0.27 ± 0.31 pp** from extending 473K–16M typical tokens to the 30M cap, and **TerminalBench only +1.26 ± 0.99 pp** from 932K–7.3M to 10M — indicating these two SWE benchmarks are already near-saturated under serial scaling (Table 3).
- **HealthBench is the clearest saturation case**: all six tested models plateau within the typical 16k-token budget, and extending to 10M adds only **+0.32 ± 0.42 pts** (Figure 1B, Table 3).
- **Repeated submission improves all five main benchmarks**: iteration gains of **+6.4 pp FrontierMath, +10.4 pp TerminalBench, +14.2 pp HealthBench, +14.4 pp SWE-Bench Pro, +17.3 pp HLE**, corresponding to uplift multipliers from 1.11× (FM) to 1.71× (HLE) (Table 5).
- **Oracle feedback matters most where it enables continued search**: on HLE, iteration adds **+25.2 pp under oracle feedback vs +9.3 pp under no feedback (2.71×)**; SWE-Bench Pro shows a smaller significant uplift (+16.0 vs +12.7; 1.26×), while FrontierMath, TerminalBench, and HealthBench show no significant benchmark-level feedback effect.
- **Parallel (pass@10) vs serial (pass@1) at fixed cap**: distributing total budget across 10 trajectories lifts HLE **0.417 → 0.606 (+0.189)** and HealthBench **0.501 → 0.784 (+0.283)**, but barely moves FrontierMath (+0.028), SWE-Bench Pro (+0.028), or TerminalBench (+0.063) (Table 7).
- **Newer models benefit less from parallel sampling** on all five benchmarks (negative Kendall τ vs release date: TerminalBench −0.73, FrontierMath −0.60, HLE −0.47, HealthBench −0.47, SWE-Bench Pro −0.33); on FrontierMath the newest models (GPT-5.2, Opus 4.6, GPT-5.4) actually score lower at pass@10 than pass@1.
- **Generational reach gains are largest on FrontierMath (β_gen = +0.631) and Cyber CTFs (+0.488)**, and reach improvements concentrate on harder tasks (significant negative β_int on CTFs, FrontierMath, TerminalBench) — Table 4.
- **Trajectory termination patterns differ sharply**: HLE no-feedback trajectories end in repeated answers 98.1% of the time (only 1.9% budget exhaustion), whereas TerminalBench no-feedback trajectories exhaust the budget 34.5% of the time — indicating genuinely long interactive search (Table 6).

## Authors & Credibility

The paper comes from the **UK AI Security Institute (AISI)** with affiliations at University of Oxford and Harvard (Kevin Wei). AISI is one of the few government-backed institutes with privileged access to frontier models pre-release (e.g., the Anthropic-provided Mythos Preview checkpoint evaluated here), giving the work unusual authority on frontier-model evaluation methodology. The framing — "establish a lower bound on simple reproducible methods," not "maximally elicit each model" — is appropriately modest, and the recommendations directly reflect AISI's policy mandate. The study consumes substantial compute (5 trajectories × 2 conditions × up to 100M-token caps × 7 benchmarks × 6–12 models), which explains why so few groups can produce directly comparable cross-domain scaling curves.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: The individual levers (bigger budgets, repeated submission, oracle feedback, pass@k) are all well-known; the novelty lies in applying them uniformly across 7 benchmarks and 3 model generations within one scaffold, and in the reach/efficiency/reliability decomposition. This is a methodological-synthesis paper rather than a new technique.
- **Rigor**: Strong on internal validity — matched budgets, fixed scaffold, fully-crossed main design, regression-based decomposition with significance levels, and an appendix check (A.5.2) verifying that the large HLE/HealthBench parallel gains are not judge artifacts. Weak on statistical power for generation-trend claims: Kendall τ correlations over only six models are explicitly flagged as descriptive, yet several conclusions ("newer models benefit less from parallel sampling") lean on them.
- **FrontierMath sample size is a real red flag**: only **12 tasks** in the subset, making the headline +11.7 pp and the largest reach coefficient (β_gen = +0.631) sensitive to individual items; the authors also note possible training overlap with the public set (Burnham, 2025), which would inflate newer-model scores. A 12-item "frontier math" result should be read as suggestive, not definitive.
- **Single-scaffold confound**: only one ReAct scaffold is tested, so benchmark-level differences (especially the weak HealthBench scaling) cannot be separated from scaffold mismatch — the authors acknowledge this but it limits the strength of the cross-benchmark comparisons that are the paper's centerpiece.
- **LLM-judge dependence**: HLE and HealthBench are graded by GPT-4o-mini, and the repetition guard relies on an LLM judge whose semantic-equivalence reliability is acknowledged to vary by domain — a non-trivial source of uncontrolled variance on exactly the benchmarks (HLE, HealthBench) that show the largest parallel-sampling gains.
- **Reproducibility**: Protocol is described in enough detail to re-run, and Inspect AI is a public framework, but no mention of releasing raw trajectories or code, and the frontier models (Opus 4.6, GPT-5.4, Mythos Preview) are proprietary — independent reproduction is essentially impossible.
- **Significance**: High for the evaluation-methodology and AI-governance communities; the recommendation to report capability-vs-compute curves at matched budgets is actionable and well-motivated. Modest for model developers looking for new elicitation techniques, since the methods are deliberately simple lower bounds.

## Strengths

- Genuinely cross-domain and cross-generational under one controlled protocol — rare and valuable.
- Honest framing as a lower bound; explicit that weak scaling is a property of the protocol, not proof of a capability ceiling.
- The reach/efficiency/reliability decomposition is a clean, interpretable lens that future eval papers should adopt.
- Concrete, per-benchmark Table 3 lets readers see exactly where typical budgets understate capability (FM, HLE) vs where they do not (HealthBench, SBP).
- Policy-facing recommendations are well-grounded in the data and appropriately cautious.

## Limitations

- Only one ReAct scaffold; benchmark-level conclusions may be scaffold-dependent rather than task-intrinsic.
- FrontierMath uses only 12 tasks → low statistical power and possible train/test overlap for newer models.
- Deliberately simple protocol (no adaptive branching, verifier-guided search, or hybrid serial/parallel) → conservative lower bound, not an upper bound.
- LLM-judge dependence (HLE/HealthBench grading, repetition guard) introduces non-uniform strictness across domains.
- Six main models make generational-trend correlations (Kendall τ) descriptive only.
- No public release of raw trajectories or full prompts beyond the appendix; proprietary models block independent reproduction.
- Cyber benchmarks are reused from prior AISI data collections rather than re-run under the fully-crossed design, so they enter only the scaling-curve analyses.

## Relevance

高度相关：本文系统刻画了推理时计算（inference-time compute / test-time compute）如何影响前沿模型评测，直接关系到 LLM 评测方法学、test-time scaling 与 agent 长程任务能力评估等核心研究兴趣；其「能力随计算量变化」的曲线范式与 reach/reliability 分解框架，对设计稳健的基准与跨代模型比较具有直接的方法论价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How do the conclusions change under a non-ReAct scaffold (e.g., verifier-guided search, tree-of-thought, or adaptive branching) — is HealthBench's saturation a property of the task or of ReAct?
- Would a much larger FrontierMath task subset (e.g., 100+ held-out problems with controlled training-overlap checks) preserve the +11.7 pp scaling gain and the strong reach coefficient?
- Can the reach/efficiency/reliability decomposition be turned into a diagnostic that predicts, before running expensive evaluations, which benchmarks will show large inference-scaling headroom?
- The paper shows oracle feedback helps most where it enables continued search — what is the minimum-feedback signal (cheaper than full oracle correctness) that captures most of the gain on HLE and SWE-Bench Pro?
- Newer models benefit less from parallel sampling and less from self-guided iteration; does this imply that fixed-budget single-trajectory evaluations are becoming *more* representative over time, or that the relevant headroom is shifting entirely to serial depth?
- How transferable are these protocol recommendations to open-ended agentic tasks (e.g., real-world SWE or autonomous research) where no oracle verifier exists?
