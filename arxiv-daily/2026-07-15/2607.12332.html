Key finding

Under infinitesimal initialization, diagonal linear networks converge to a modified-ℓ1 minimizer — not plain ℓ1 — with the Structural Invariant Manifold as the unifying mechanism.

SOURCE · Critical Assessment §8.2
01

Methodology — three proof layers

algorithm · dynamics · geometry

Purely theoretical, with small synthetic confirmatory experiments. The proof generalizes Pesme & Flammarion (2023) by deliberately bypassing the mirror-flow representation in favour of the more general Structural Invariant Manifold (SIM) — which also applies to analytic models like matrix sensing. Three layers stack:

1Algorithm + Theorem 4.2

A recursive algorithm parameterized by weights {ti±}: at each step pick the inactive neuron with minimal activation time δj, add it under a sign constraint, re-solve a constrained least-squares subproblem.

δj = min(ti+/ui , ti−/|ui|)

2Theorem 4.5 — dynamics ≡ algorithm

Conserved quantities ai,j2−ai,L2=s2μi,j2 reduce the per-coordinate ODE; blow-up times ti±=±Fi(±∞) (deep) or ±1/λi± from Hessian eigenvalues (two-layer). A timescale-separation argument matches trajectory limit points to algorithm iterates.

3Section 6 — SIM mechanism

The orbit factorization OF(θ)=∏i OF,i(θi) (Thm 6.5, Hermann–Nagano) implies: params at 0 stay at 0 → feature-selection phase; sign changes need returning to init scale → sign-locking; generic data → one-neuron-at-a-time learning.

Bypasses mirror flow. Pesme & Flammarion's explicit mirror-flow form needs symmetric growth times ti+=ti−=1 (Remark 4.3); SIM handles arbitrary ti+≠ti− and extends to any analytic parametric model.

Both sparse, both exact fits — but they recover different features. The modified-ℓ1 bias shifts support {3,4}→{1,4}, proving " ℓ1 implicit bias" is not a single universal statement.

  • Trajectory match on a non-standard model. On a 4-coordinate two-layer net where Pesme & Flammarion does not apply, Algorithm 1's iterates k(1)=(.8,0,0,0), k(2)=(0,0,1.4,0), k(3)=(0,0,1.429,−.204) match GD plateaus.
  • Deep net timing (Fig 2). M=3, N=8, scale ≈10−4, strict layer hierarchy a>b>c: predicted per-stage growth times "align perfectly" with empirical emergence order.
  • Controllability (Fig 3). Amplifying neuron 5's init by 5× forces it to emerge first, overriding data-correlation order — Algorithm 1 predicts the altered sequence.
  • ℓ1-vs-ℓ0 (Fig 4). Zero-Balanced init (a=b, c=0) converges to min-ℓ1, not the sparse ℓ0 ground truth — confirming the c=0 ℓ1 inductive bias.

No standard benchmarks (ImageNet/CIFAR/GLUE). Experiments are toy-scale confirmations of the theorems, not a deployable recipe.

03

Strengths & limitations

genuine generalization, scoped regime

▲ Strengths

  • Real generalization, not re-derivation. Handles arbitrary asymmetric ti+≠ti− and the broad Definition 4.1 two-layer class — far beyond canonical ki=aibi. Remark 4.3 states the gap explicitly.
  • SIM is genuinely unifying. Explains why the algorithmic description works, and is reusable for other analytic models (matrix sensing is explicitly named).
  • Intellectual honesty. The GF/GD invariance gap is not hidden — illustrated with a concrete failure mode (Fig 1, k1 sign-flip) tied to SIM curvature.
  • More than cosmetic. The support-shift example shows modified-ℓ1 changes which features are learned, not just the norm.
  • Pedagogical Distance-Speed intuition (Sec 5) makes the otherwise opaque blow-up-time integrals readable: time = distance D+ ÷ speed |v0|.

▼ Limitations

  • Infinitesimal initialization only. Analysis holds in the s→0 limit; Woodworth et al. (2020) characterized all init scales — this paper does not match that.
  • Gradient-flow assumption. Does not extend to GD/SGD/Adam; curved SIMs lose exact invariance under discretization (Fig 1 anomaly). Gap acknowledged, left open (Ziyin et al. 2024).
  • Narrow model class. Diagonal linear networks only. Matrix sensing, ReLU, or any realistic architecture is explicitly future work.
  • Toy-scale only, no released code. 4-coordinate and M=3,N=8 setups confirm theorems but establish no practical relevance.
  • 10−60 init is wildly impractical — a faithful proxy for the limit, not a deployable recipe (authors label it "impractical", Sec 7).
  • Non-degeneracy assumptions (unique argmin of δi, non-orthogonality to Hessian eigenvectors) exclude measure-zero cases with no perturbation discussion.
04

Open questions

for further exploration
Q1

Can the modified-ℓ1 weights ti± be estimated from a finite-scale trained network, letting the theory predict the recovered support in practice?

Q2

How robust is Theorem 4.5 to the non-degeneracy assumptions (unique argmin of δi, non-orthogonality to Hessian eigenvectors)? Is there a stable perturbation argument?

Q3

Does SIM extend to matrix sensing / low-rank factorization, where the "connectivity" assumption of Bai et al. (2024) plays an analogous role? Flagged as future work, no path sketched.

Q4

Can the GF/GD invariance gap (Fig 1) be quantified — e.g. a bound on how far GD drifts from the curved SIM as a function of learning rate and curvature?

Q5

Is there a connection to the Lasso regularization path of Berthier (2025), which also studies diagonal linear networks?

Q6

The support-shift example uses a hand-picked 0.5 penalty. Can one characterize the full set of supports achievable by varying {ti±} — a modified-ℓ1 analogue of the Lasso solution path?
