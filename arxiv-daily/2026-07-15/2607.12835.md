> **一句话总结：** 本文是首个针对 LLM 自动生成论文复现评分准则（rubric）的系统化元评估：作者将 rubric 改写为清单式格式，在 PaperBench 上比较 Claude-Sonnet 和 GPT-5.4 在四种递进增强生成设置下的表现，发现最强的"蒸馏技能"设置能把外层仓库级评分与人类 rubric 的 Pearson 相关性从 0.62 提升到 0.78，逼近人类基线 0.83，但 LLM 生成的 rubric 仍普遍过于细碎、偏代码、且打分偏高。

> **TL;DR:** The first systematic meta-evaluation of LLM-authored rubrics for ML paper reproduction, run on PaperBench (20 papers) with Claude-Sonnet and GPT-5.4 across four incrementally augmented generation settings (direct prompting, in-context examples, ReAct agentic scaffold, distilled skill from Claude-Opus). The strongest "Distilled Skill" setting raises extrinsic Pearson alignment from 0.62 to 0.78 (Claude-Sonnet), approaching the human baseline of 0.83 (a +0.16 / ~26% Spearman gain), and lifts intrinsic cosine-F1 by ~8-13% over direct prompting — but generated rubrics stay too fine-grained, code-centric (Code Development gets >60% weight vs Result Match <21%), and biased toward high importance scores.

## 核心贡献

- 首次对"LLM 作为论文复现 rubric 作者"这一角色进行系统化元评估，填补了 PaperBench 等基准只评估"在固定 rubric 下的评判器"、却忽略"rubric 本身能否被自动生成"的空白。
- 提出 checklist 式 rubric 表示 r=(c, s)，s∈{1..5}，并设计一条递进增强的生成谱系：Direct Prompting → +In-Context Examples → +Agentic Scaffold（ReAct）→ +Distilled Skill（从 Claude-Opus 单次蒸馏技能文档），可清晰归因每种增强的贡献。
- 引入互补的双轴元评估：外层用 Spearman/Pearson 比较生成 rubric 与 ground-truth rubric 在 5 个真实复现仓库（PaperCoder / HiRAS 多配置）上诱导出的分数；内层用 ROUGE-1/L 与 embedding cosine 匹配 P/R/F1 比较文本对齐。
- 给出对 LLM rubric 缺陷的实证刻画：粒度过细（平均 token 数 45 vs 人类 29）、长度偏置、代码中心主义（Code Development >60% 权重，Reinforcement Learning 类论文中 Result Match 仅 <21% 而人类为 48.8%）、重要性打分偏高且分布偏斜。
- 跨两个 backbone（Claude-Sonnet、GPT-5.4）验证结论，证明增强策略的泛化性，并为可扩展的论文复现评测提供了可复用的人机协同范式。

## Key Contributions

- First systematic meta-evaluation of LLMs as rubric authors for ML paper reproduction, directly targeting the annotation bottleneck exposed by PaperBench (manually authored with original authors, only 20 papers, 94–2,551 criteria each) and related benchmarks (HealthBench: 262 physicians; ResearchRubrics: >2,800 expert hours).
- A controlled four-step augmentation hierarchy — Direct Prompting, In-Context Examples, ReAct Agentic Scaffold, and a Distilled procedural Skill extracted once from Claude-Opus — that lets each form of supervision/reasoning be ablated cleanly.
- A dual-axis meta-evaluation protocol: extrinsic (Spearman/Pearson between repository scores induced by generated vs ground-truth rubrics over 5 real reproduction repos from PaperCoder/HiRAS) and intrinsic (ROUGE-1/L plus embedding-cosine matching precision/recall/F1 against human rubrics, using Qwen3-Embedding-8B and a τ=0.7 threshold).
- A checklist-style rubric reformulation (r=(c, s), s∈[1,5], weighted normalized scoring) that is both human- and model-friendly and matches the format used by HealthBench / ResearchRubrics / RubricBench.
- Empirical diagnosis of consistent failure modes — over-fine granularity, length bias, code-centric weighting, skewed-high importance scores, weak topic adaptivity — that are actionable for future rubric generators and for benchmark designers.

## Methodology

**Rubric representation.** Each criterion is a pair r_i=(c_i, s_i) with importance s_i∈{1..5}. For a paper P, the full rubric R={r_1..r_n} is scored by an evaluator that assigns a binary Pass/Fail f(c_i) per criterion; the final reproduction score is the importance-weighted share of passed criteria, Σ[f(c_i)=Pass]·s_i / Σs_i.

**Four incrementally augmented generation settings.** Each builds on the previous: (1) Direct Prompting with a manual instruction set I and the paper P; (2) +In-Context Examples adds a human-authored rubric E from another paper; (3) +Agentic Scaffold implements a ReAct loop (Algorithm 1) with Read/Write system tools over a workspace W={P,E}, so the model iteratively reasons and refines criteria instead of one-shot generation; (4) +Distilled Skill runs Claude-Opus once under the Agentic Scaffold on a single paper and asks it to summarize its construction heuristics into a general-purpose skill document S, which is then prepended to the weaker model's prompt (p0=(I,S), W={P,E}) — a one-time cost that avoids per-paper calls to the strong model.

**Extrinsic meta-evaluation.** For each paper, 5 reproduction repositories (PaperCoder with DeepSeek-V3 / Qwen3-Coder-480B, HiRAS with Claude-Sonnet / DeepSeek-V3 / Qwen3-Coder-480B) are scored by the same ChatGPT-4o-mini evaluator under each rubric set, with top-K=10 relevant-file retrieval per criterion to fit context limits. Spearman and Pearson are computed between the score vector induced by R and by the ground-truth GT, then averaged across the 20 PaperBench papers.

**Intrinsic meta-evaluation.** Lexical overlap: max ROUGE-1 / ROUGE-L of each generated criterion against all ground-truth criteria. Semantic matching (following RubricBench but replacing LLM-matching with embedding cosine to avoid hallucination): a match is cos(c_i, c_j^gt)>τ (τ=0.7 main, with 0.65/0.75 sensitivity in Appendix D), giving precision/recall/F1. All metrics averaged per paper.

**Baselines and models.** Human Annotation baseline = the authors' own re-annotation of PaperBench into checklist format (Pearson 0.83, Spearman 0.75 against PaperBench GT). Generators: Claude-Sonnet and GPT-5.4; distiller: Claude-Opus; evaluator: ChatGPT-4o-mini; embeddings: Qwen3-Embedding-8B. Hardware: 8× NVIDIA L40S.

## Results & Highlights

- **Extrinsic alignment, Claude-Sonnet (Table 1):** Direct Prompting reaches Pearson 0.62 / Spearman 0.58; the full Distilled Skill setting reaches Pearson 0.78 / Spearman 0.70 — approaching the Human Annotation baseline of Pearson 0.83 / Spearman 0.75. This is roughly a +0.16 Pearson (~21%) and +0.12 Spearman (~26%) absolute improvement over direct prompting, with monotonic gains at each augmentation step.
- **Extrinsic alignment, GPT-5.4 (Table 1):** Direct Prompting Pearson 0.55 / Spearman 0.55 rises to Pearson 0.74 / Spearman 0.70 under Distilled Skill — the authors report this as ~35% Spearman and ~27% Pearson relative improvement, with GPT-5.4's best Pearson matching the best model-generated Pearson overall.
- **Rubric coverage scales up (Table 1):** average criterion count climbs from 35.95 → 77.95 for Claude-Sonnet and 50.15 → 87.15 for GPT-5.4 under Distilled Skill, approaching/exceeding the human average of 83.20 — but generated criteria stay longer (avg length ~185 chars / ~45 tokens vs human 131.73 chars / 29.49 tokens), evidencing a length/granularity bias.
- **Intrinsic alignment, Claude-Sonnet (Table 2):** cosine-F1 improves 0.63 → 0.68 (~8%), ROUGE-1 0.38 → 0.41 (~8%), ROUGE-L 0.29 → 0.32 (~10%) from Direct Prompting to Distilled Skill; for GPT-5.4 the gains are larger (~13% F1, ~11% ROUGE-1, ~15% ROUGE-L). Intrinsic gains are noticeably smaller than extrinsic gains, and precision barely moves — augmentation mainly helps recall (coverage).
- **Code-centric bias (Figure 2):** across all four paper categories, model-generated rubrics give Code Development consistently >60% of total score while Result Match stays below 21%; ground-truth PaperBench rubrics are far more category-adaptive — for Reinforcement Learning papers humans assign 48.8% of score to Result Match, vs <21% for the model.
- **Score-distribution calibration (§5.2):** Direct Prompting yields a skewed distribution concentrated at high importance levels; adding in-context examples, agentic scaffolding, and the distilled skill produces a more bell-shaped, balanced distribution across the 1–5 range, though all model-generated settings retain a mild high-score bias.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty** is genuine but scoped: this is the first meta-evaluation of *rubric generation* for paper reproduction, but the building blocks (checklist rubrics, ReAct agents, skill distillation, embedding-based matching à la RubricBench) are individually well established; the contribution is the combination and the dual intrinsic/extrinsic lens.
- **Rigor — human baseline is the weakest link.** The "Human Annotation" baseline (Pearson 0.83) is the *authors' own* re-annotation of PaperBench into checklist format by "research students with sufficient experience" — not independent domain experts and not the original PaperBench annotators (who co-developed criteria with the original authors). This means the headline "approaching the human baseline" is measured against a non-independent, reformatted baseline, and the reported gaps may be under/over-estimated. The paper does not report inter-annotator agreement, which is a notable omission for a meta-evaluation.
- **Rigor — small, homogeneous testbed.** All extrinsic numbers are averaged over only 20 PaperBench papers, all in ML; the authors themselves flag this in Limitations. With 5 repositories per paper, the Spearman/Pearson estimates rest on 20 data points each, so the difference between 0.74 and 0.78 Pearson is almost certainly within noise — yet the text discusses these gaps as if meaningful.
- **Missing ablations.** The agentic scaffold sometimes *hurts* intrinsic precision (Claude-Sonnet cosine-precision drops 0.76 → 0.71 from Direct to Agentic in Table 2), and the paper does not ablate (a) number of agentic steps taken, (b) what the distilled skill document actually contains, or (c) whether the gain at Distilled Skill comes from the skill text or simply from longer effective generation. The cost-quality trade-off of the Claude-Opus distillation step is also not quantified.
- **Evaluator confound.** ChatGPT-4o-mini is used as the single evaluator for extrinsic scores; its own noise, retrieval failures (top-K=10 files), and any preference biases propagate directly into the Spearman/Pearson numbers with no variance or evaluator-ablation reported.
- **Code-centric bias is large and consequential.** Result Match weight of <21% (model) vs 48.8% (human) for RL papers is not a minor stylistic difference — it means the generated rubrics would systematically reward code completeness over empirical fidelity, which is precisely the failure mode rubrics are supposed to prevent. This is the most important honest finding in the paper and arguably deserves more than a "further analysis" section.
- **Reproducibility** is moderate-to-good: models, thresholds, retrieval depth, and hardware are specified; in-context examples and prompts are promised in Appendix A; threshold sensitivity (τ=0.65/0.75) is reported. However, neither the distilled skill document nor the re-annotation protocol is released as a dataset, and the rubric-generation prompts themselves are only summarized.

## Strengths

- Principled dual-axis (intrinsic + extrinsic) evaluation design that avoids the trap of judging rubrics only by surface text quality.
- Honest, specific diagnosis of failure modes (fine granularity, length bias, code-centric weighting, score overestimation) — including a concrete Table 3 case study on the DPO/toxicity paper showing model criteria decomposing one milestone into many low-level checks.
- The Distilled Skill idea is cost-efficient: a single Claude-Opus run produces a reusable skill document, avoiding per-paper strong-model calls.
- Findings generalize across two backbone model families (Claude-Sonnet and GPT-5.4), with the same monotonic-improvement pattern — strengthening the claim that the augmentation hierarchy, not a model idiosyncrasy, drives gains.
- Threshold-sensitivity analysis (Appendix D) confirms the recall-driven-improvement finding holds across τ∈{0.65, 0.70, 0.75}.

## Limitations

- Evaluation is confined to 20 ML papers in PaperBench; generalization to other sciences (biology, physics, social science) is unverified.
- The human baseline is non-independent and reformatted (checklist style vs PaperBench's tree style), with no inter-annotator agreement reported.
- Only two generator backbones tested; behavior of open-weight models (Llama, Qwen, DeepSeek as *generators*, not just as reproduction-system backbones) is unknown.
- Quality is acknowledged to depend on prompt design, choice of in-context example, and scaffold implementation details that are not fully stabilized.
- Best extrinsic alignment (Pearson 0.78, Spearman 0.70) still trails the human baseline (0.83 / 0.75), and intrinsic precision essentially never beats the human baseline — the model still cannot match human rubric *quality*, only approximate its *coverage*.
- No quantitative calibration of the Claude-Opus distillation cost vs. downstream benefit, nor analysis of how the skill document transfers to papers very different from the one it was distilled on.

## Relevance

这篇论文直接落在「LLM 研究智能体 + 自动化评测」这条线上，与你关注的 paper-to-code 复现智能体（如 HiRAS、PaperCoder）以及 LLM-as-judge 评测方法学高度相关——它回答了一个关键基础设施问题：当基准（PaperBench）的人工 rubric 标注成本爆炸时，能否用 LLM 自动生成可靠的复现评分细则。其结论（增强后接近人类但仍偏代码、偏细碎）对设计任何 rubric-based 自动评测系统都有直接参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How would the extrinsic alignment numbers change if the human baseline were produced by *independent* domain experts (e.g., the original PaperBench annotators or the papers' own authors) rather than the same research team re-formatting into checklist style? Would the 0.78 vs 0.83 gap shrink, vanish, or grow?
- What is the inter-annotator agreement on the checklist re-annotations, and hence what is the noise ceiling against which the 0.78 Pearson should actually be compared?
- Can the code-centric bias (Code Development >60% vs Result Match <21%) be corrected by reweighting in the scoring formula, by adding Result-Match-specific in-context examples, or by an explicit anti-bias instruction — and does fixing it close the extrinsic gap more cheaply than stronger distillation?
- Does the distilled skill document transfer to non-ML scientific papers, or does it encode ML-specific assumptions (e.g., the structure of training-loop criteria) that hurt performance in other domains?
- How sensitive are the extrinsic Spearman/Pearson estimates to the choice of evaluator (ChatGPT-4o-mini vs Claude vs an open model) and to top-K retrieval depth, given that evaluator noise and retrieval failures propagate directly into the correlation numbers?
- Could a small held-out calibration set be used to learn a per-criterion-type reweighting that maps LLM rubrics toward human-style category-adaptive weighting (e.g., boosting Result Match for RL papers)?
