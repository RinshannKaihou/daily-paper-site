> **一句话总结：** 作者提出一种无需训练的归因方法，将 Transformer FFN 神经元的预激活分解为上游神经元/注意力输出的贡献，发现即使在 GPT-2 与 Qwen2.5 上保留仅 5%-10% 的上游分量、其余以均值消融，仍能高保真地还原 FFN 激活，并在适度稀疏度下让全模型困惑度几乎不变，揭示了 FFN 在密集参数化背后的稀疏神经元级连接结构。

> **TL;DR:** A training-free attribution method decomposes each Transformer FFN neuron's pre-activation into contributions from upstream neurons and attention outputs (weight-alignment × activation-variance scoring), then causally tests subsets via mean-ablation patching. On GPT-2 S/M/L/XL and Qwen2.5-0.5B using MiniPile, retaining just ~5% of upstream components preserves FFN pre-activation AUROC above 0.9 in most layers, and applying neuron-specific masks in all layers simultaneously leaves model perplexity essentially unchanged at moderate sparsity (transition points between 5% and 25% depending on model), demonstrating sparse structured inter-layer dependencies despite dense parameterization.

## 核心贡献

- 提出一种**训练无关、直接基于原生权重的归因方法**，将任意 FFN 神经元的预激活分解为来自上游 FFN 神经元、注意力输出和嵌入的加性贡献；相关性分数 R = (有效输入权重与居中输出向量的点积平方) × (归一化激活的方差)，对每个 (组件, 神经元) 对只需乘以一个预计算标量。
- 通过 **mean-ablation 激活修补（activation patching）** 因果验证：保留按相关性排序的 top-K 上游分量、其余以经验均值消融，发现仅 5%-10% 的上游分量即可高保真还原 FFN 预激活（多模型、多层的 AUROC > 0.9）。
- 发现**稀疏性可复合**：上游层本身激活稀疏，故真正"活跃且具影响力"的上游分量比例远小于名义稀疏度（如 10% 稀疏度 × 5% 活跃神经元 ≈ 0.5% 有效贡献）。
- 进行**模型级干预**：在所有 FFN 层同时应用每个神经元专属的掩码，使偏差在整个网络中传播；在适度稀疏度下困惑度变化很小，给出稀疏通路的全局（虽然聚合的）证据。
- 观察**结构化模式**：存在局部性（相邻层贡献更大）、层间重要性差异（上层扮演更广"枢纽"角色），并在多个模型中发现第一注意力层对后续 FFN 几乎不贡献。
- 为**线性表示假说（LRH）** 提供机制层面延伸：FFN 输入权重与少数上游输出方向呈选择性线性对齐，提示 Transformer 通过线性方向向量的稀疏叠加进行计算。

## Key Contributions

- A **training-free, native-weight attribution method** that decomposes any FFN neuron's pre-activation into additive contributions from upstream FFN neurons, attention outputs, and embeddings. The relevance score R = ⟨w̃_i, u_s⟩² · Var(ĉ_s) factorizes into weight alignment (geometric) and activation variance (dynamic), computable with only pre-collected activation statistics and model weights.
- A **causal validation via mean-ablation activation patching**: keep the top-K relevant components per target neuron, mean-ablate all others, and show that small subsets (~5-10% of upstream components) suffice to preserve FFN activations with high fidelity across GPT-2 S/M/L/XL and Qwen2.5-0.5B.
- Discovery of **compound sparsity**: since upstream layers are themselves sparsely active, the number of upstream components that are *both active and influential* for a downstream neuron is far smaller than the nominal sparsity level (e.g., 10% sparsity × ~5% active neurons ≈ 0.5% effectively contributing).
- A **model-level intervention** that applies neuron-specific masks simultaneously in all FFN layers and propagates deviations through the whole network; at moderate sparsity, perplexity stays close to the unmodified model, providing global (if aggregate) evidence for the sparse pathways.
- Identification of **structured dependency patterns**: locality (adjacent preceding layers dominate), layer-wise variability (upper layers act as broader "information hubs"), and a notable finding that the first attention layer contributes almost nothing to subsequent FFN neurons in GPT-2 M, GPT-2 XL, and Qwen2.5-0.5B.
- A **mechanistic extension of the Linear Representation Hypothesis**: FFN input weights are selectively aligned with the output directions of a small set of upstream components, supporting a view of Transformers as selective, structured information flow over directional features.

## Methodology

**Problem framing.** In a GPT-style pre-norm Transformer, the FFN block is FFN(X) = X + W₂ σ(W₁ LN(X)). Each neuron i computes a_i = σ(w_iᵀ LN(x_t)), and under the key–value memory view, w_i is a "key" that reads from the residual stream while the i-th column of W₂ is the "output vector" written back. Because the residual stream is an additive superposition of all preceding FFN neuron outputs, attention outputs, and embeddings, the input to a single neuron is a dense linear combination — the paper asks whether this dense combination is actually driven by a *sparse* subset.

**Decomposition (Eq. 5).** Writing the residual stream as x_t^(l) = Σ_{s∈S^(l)} c_s u_s, where each u_s is a centered output vector and c_s its scalar activation (a_i for FFN, v_i for attention, 1 for embeddings), and absorbing LayerNorm into the effective weights w̃_i and bias b̃_i, the pre-activation becomes

  z_i^(l) = [Σ_s c_s ⟨w̃_i, u_s⟩] / Z + b̃_i,

with Z the LayerNorm normalization denominator. This linearizes the FFN pre-activation into a sum of per-component contributions, making the influence of each upstream component explicit.

**Relevance scoring (Eq. 6).** Because contributions are products of a constant dot product ⟨w̃_i, u_s⟩ and a normalized activation ĉ_s = c_s/Z, the variance of a component's contribution to neuron i factorizes as R_{s→i}^(l) = ⟨w̃_i, u_s⟩² · Var(ĉ_s). The activation variance is target-independent and estimated once per component, so scoring all (component, neuron) pairs reduces to multiplying a pre-computed scalar by the squared dot product. Variance treats positive and negative deviations symmetrically, which matters for attention dimensions whose contributions may flip sign.

**Causal test (Eq. 7).** For each target neuron, the top-K components by relevance are kept; all remaining components' activations are replaced by their empirical means c̄_s (mean-ablation patching). The mask is **per-neuron and local** — only the direct contributions entering the target's dot product are patched, and Z is taken from the unpatched forward pass. If the patched pre-activation tracks the actual one, the sparse subset suffices to determine the neuron's activation.

**Evaluation.** Fidelity is measured by (1) Pearson correlation between actual and patched pre-activations across evaluation tokens, and (2) AUROC for predicting "meaningfully active" neurons (post-activation > 0.5) from patched pre-activations. Models: GPT-2 S/M/L/XL (124M–1.5B) and Qwen2.5-0.5B (rotary embeddings, RMSNorm, gated SiLU FFN — see Appendix A for adaptations). Activation statistics are estimated on a MiniPile train subset; all patching/evaluations use a disjoint MiniPile test subset. The model-level intervention additionally applies the per-neuron masks in every FFN layer at once and measures perplexity on the test subset, comparing relevance-based selection to (a) target-independent activation-variance-only ranking (a network-pruning baseline), and (b) uniform random selection.

## Results & Highlights

- **Per-neuron fidelity is high at low sparsity.** Across GPT-2 S/M/L/XL and Qwen2.5-0.5B, at a sparsity level of **10% and above** (i.e., retaining 10% of upstream components), patched pre-activations show consistently strong Pearson correlations and high AUROC. Even at **5% sparsity, most layers maintain AUROC above 0.9** (Figure 2), indicating that the selected sparse subsets preserve FFN activation patterns with high fidelity under mean-ablation of all other components.
- **First layer is exceptionally sparse-friendly.** In the first Transformer layer, fidelity degrades only mildly even when as little as **1% of upstream components** are retained, because FFN pre-activations there are dominated by token and positional embeddings rather than early attention outputs.
- **Compound sparsity is severe.** At a nominal 10% sparsity, if only ~5% of upstream neurons are active, then only about **0.5% of all possible neuron outputs** meaningfully contribute to a given target neuron — the effective fan-in is far smaller than the nominal subset size K. Ablations that additionally ablate upstream activations below the 85th or 95th percentile produce curves that "nearly coincide" with the unconstrained relevance score, confirming the compounding.
- **Model-level intervention preserves perplexity at moderate sparsity.** With per-neuron masks applied in all layers simultaneously: the perplexity gap to the unmodified model is small and the relevance-vs-variance-only curves converge at transition points of **10%–5% for GPT-2 S/M, ~15% for GPT-2 L and GPT-2 XL, and 25%–15% for Qwen2.5-0.5B**. Baseline original perplexities are **GPT-2 S = 23.69, GPT-2 M = 16.56, GPT-2 L = 15.89, GPT-2 XL = 14.51, Qwen2.5-0.5B = 11.30**.
- **Relevance selection dominates the baselines.** With **random selection, masking only 25% of incoming connections already produces notable perplexity degradation**, whereas relevance-based selection leaves perplexity barely changed. In ablations, **random selection drops AUROC to roughly 0.7–0.86 at 25% retained**, while the variance-only ranking achieves markedly lower fidelity than the full relevance score throughout, confirming that geometric alignment carries the bulk of the target-specific signal.
- **Weight alignment alone is often enough.** For GPT-2 S, GPT-2 L, and GPT-2 XL, ranking by weight alignment only (dot products) yields AUROC close to the full relevance score; GPT-2 M and Qwen2.5-0.5B benefit visibly from incorporating activation statistics, particularly at lower sparsity.
- **Structured dependency graphs.** At 15% sparsity, source-layer analysis (Figure 3) shows **locality** (immediately preceding layers contribute a disproportionately large share) and **layer-wise variability** (in the GPT-2 family, upper-half layers act as broader information hubs; Qwen2.5-0.5B's later MLP blocks emphasize attention layers). The first attention layer in GPT-2 M, GPT-2 XL, and Qwen2.5-0.5B contributes almost no selected components to subsequent FFN neurons.

## Authors & Credibility

- **Johannes Knittel** and **Hanspeter Pfister**, Harvard University (Pfister is an established senior researcher in visualization and applied ML; Knittel has prior work on vector symbolic architectures for GPT-2, cited as Knittel et al., 2024 in the paper, indicating a coherent research program on Transformer internal structure).
- The work is framed carefully: the authors repeatedly stress that reported sparsity levels are *upper bounds* on functional dependency size (not minimal sets), and that the analysis establishes *sufficiency* rather than causal necessity.
- Coverage is broad within its scope (5 models spanning 124M–1.5B, two architecture families including a modern RMSNorm + rotary + gated SiLU model), with proper use of disjoint train/test subsets of MiniPile and three selection baselines (relevance, variance-only, random) for the perplexity experiments.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (moderate-to-good).** The core technical idea — combining weight alignment (dot products) with activation variance to score per-neuron upstream relevance — is a reasonable and lightweight synthesis of attribution ideas (Integrated Gradients, LRP, AttnLRP) and the key–value memory view of FFNs. The contribution is less a new algorithm than a systematic, global, training-free characterization of *native neuron-to-neuron* sparsity, complementing SAE/transcoder work that operates on learned features. The compound-sparsity observation and the "first attention layer contributes nothing" finding are genuinely interesting descriptive results.
- **Rigor (mixed).** The paper is honest about limitations but several aspects weaken the causal claims: (1) the neuron-level intervention is **local only** — deviations are explicitly *not propagated through intermediate layers* — so "sufficiency" is established only at the level of a single dot product, not at the level of network computation; the model-level perplexity experiment partially addresses this but with an aggregate metric that can mask task- or input-specific behavioral changes. (2) Mean-ablation is a relatively weak baseline intervention; a stronger patching target (e.g., resampling or noise-ablation) might reveal dependencies the mean hides. (3) The relevance score includes token and positional embeddings *by construction* in the kept set (Section 4.3), which mechanically inflates fidelity for early layers — the "1% sparsity still works in layer 1" result is partly an artifact of this design choice.
- **Baselines (acceptable but narrow).** The three model-level baselines (relevance, variance-only pruning, random) are sensible, but the paper does **not compare against SAEs, transcoders, or existing attribution methods (AttnLRP, Ferrando & Voita 2024)** at the same per-neuron fidelity task, so it is hard to judge whether the method beats learned-feature alternatives or just avoids their training cost.
- **Reproducibility (good).** The method is fully specified by model weights plus one-pass activation statistics on MiniPile; models are public (GPT-2 family via HuggingFace, Qwen2.5-0.5B public). Appendix A documents the gated-FFN adaptation for Qwen2.5. Code release is not explicitly promised in the extracted text.
- **Significance (promising but currently descriptive).** The inference-efficiency implications are advertised prominently in the abstract and conclusion ("potential implications for efficient inference," "candidate sparse pathways"), but **no actual inference-time or FLOPs measurement is reported** — the only end-to-end signal is perplexity under mean-ablation. Whether the identified sparse masks translate into real speedups remains entirely open, and the per-neuron, per-target masks are not obviously usable as a static execution plan (each downstream neuron has its own mask, so the union over targets may be dense).
- **Scope (limited).** Max model size is **1.5B parameters**, only one modern architecture (Qwen2.5-0.5B), and **no instruction-tuned models**; the authors flag this themselves. Whether the reported sparsity persists at scale (7B+, 70B+) is unverified, and the Qwen2.5-0.5B result is notably worse (transition at 25%–15% vs 5%–15% for GPT-2), hinting that modern gated FFNs may be less sparse under this method.
- **Red flags.** (1) Headline efficiency claims are not backed by any efficiency experiment. (2) The ablation showing weight-alignment-alone nearly matches the full score for 3 of 5 models (GPT-2 S/L/XL) somewhat undercuts the methodological contribution — the activation-variance term is sometimes superfluous, suggesting the "method" reduces to computing input-weight/output-vector dot products in those cases. (3) The compound-sparsity argument (10% × 5% = 0.5%) is illustrative rather than measured end-to-end.

## Strengths

- Training-free and operates directly on native model weights — no SAE/transcoder training overhead, scales to all neurons in a model.
- Clean mathematical decomposition (Eq. 5–7) that makes the geometric intuition (FFN keys align with upstream output directions) explicit and testable.
- Cross-architecture evaluation including a modern RMSNorm + rotary + gated-SiLU model (Qwen2.5), with Appendix A documenting the gated-FFN adaptation rather than sweeping it under the rug.
- Honest framing of sparsity levels as upper bounds and of the analysis as establishing sufficiency rather than necessity.
- The compound-sparsity observation and the structural findings (locality, hub layers, silent first attention layer) are concrete, surprising, and useful starting points for circuit-level interpretability.
- Proper use of disjoint train/test MiniPile subsets and three selection modes for the perplexity experiments.

## Limitations

- Neuron-level patching is **local and non-propagating**; only the model-level perplexity experiment propagates deviations, and perplexity is an aggregate metric that can hide input-specific behavioral drift.
- Establishes **sufficiency, not causal necessity** — the sparse subsets are not proven to be the *minimal* or *required* dependencies.
- Model coverage caps at **1.5B parameters** and includes **no instruction-tuned models**; whether the sparsity holds at 7B+/70B+ scale is unknown.
- **No comparison to SAEs, transcoders, or prior attribution methods** (AttnLRP, Ferrando & Voita 2024) on the same fidelity metric.
- The advertised efficiency implications are **speculative** — no inference-time, memory, or FLOPs measurements are reported; per-neuron masks do not directly yield a static sparse execution plan.
- Relevance score for embeddings treats them as always retained, which favors early layers.
- For GPT-2 S/L/XL, weight alignment alone matches the full score, so the variance component is partially redundant for those models.
- The Qwen2.5-0.5B result is markedly worse than GPT-2, raising the possibility that modern gated FFNs are less sparse under this method than older GELU FFNs.

## Relevance

对于关注机制可解释性与 Transformer 内部结构的读者，本文提供了一个轻量、训练无关的工具，可以直接定位每个 FFN 神经元的稀疏上游依赖，是电路级分析与稀疏化模型设计的实用起点；但其结论目前止于描述性证据，对追求实际推理加速的研究还需配合系统级实现验证。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the reported sparsity persist (or shrink) at 7B–70B scale and in instruction-tuned or RLHF models, where the residual stream may be more superposed?
- How does the method compare quantitatively against SAEs, transcoders, AttnLRP, and Ferrando & Voita's information-flow routes on the same per-neuron fidelity metric?
- Can the per-neuron masks be consolidated into a single, input-agnostic sparse execution plan (or a small mixture of plans) that yields real inference-time speedups, and how does it compare to Deja Vu-style contextual sparsity?
- What happens under stronger interventions than mean-ablation (e.g., resample-ablation, counterfactual noise) — does the high fidelity survive, or does mean-ablation mask important dependencies?
- Does the "first attention layer contributes almost nothing to subsequent FFN neurons" finding replicate in modern architectures, and what does it imply about the role of the first attention layer?
- Can the identified sparse pathways be used for targeted distillation or pruning that beats magnitude/pruning-style baselines on downstream tasks, not just perplexity?
