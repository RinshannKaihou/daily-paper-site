Rethinking the Evaluation of Harness Evolution for Agents

Automatic harness evolution fails to consistently outperform simple test-time scaling baselines under matched compute — and its gains barely transfer to held-out tasks.

Key metric · held-out gain
+0.6pts avg
Evolved harness's pass@1 lift on 34 held-out tasks — vs. much larger in-distribution gains. GPT-5.4: +0.0, Claude Opus 4.6: +1.2.
Parallel Sampling 72.3 Harness Evol. 67.4
Why this matters

Reported harness-evolution gains mostly reflect overfit search, not transferable design — so the field needs fairer, budget-matched evaluation.

Critical edge

Only K=5, m=1 tested — no scaling curves; results averaged over just two runs with no confidence intervals, while several reported deltas (e.g. +0.6, +1.2) plausibly sit within noise.

01 Methodology

The paper reframes harness-evolution comparison under a unified compute budget. Four methods, each updating something different and observing different feedback, are placed on the same footing:

Budget  K = 5  ·  rollouts per task m = 1
Models: Claude Opus 4.6 · GPT-5.4 · GPT-5.4 mini
Benchmark: Terminal-Bench 2.1 · split 45 / 10 / 34
width · test-time
Parallel Sampling

K independent trajectories under a fixed harness; self-judge or unit test picks the survivor.

depth · test-time
Sequential Refinement

Each attempt conditions on the previous trajectory (and outcome) via the Agent Debugger summarizer Φ.

dataset-guided
Harness Evolution

A meta-agent proposes a shared harness across a task batch from aggregated evidence (AHE, explore-agent disabled).

instance-guided · NEW
Harness Scaling

A per-task analogue: adapt the harness for a single instance, then resample. Isolates reusable vs. per-instance gains.

Generalization · disjoint split pass@1

  • Held-out test gain: +0.6 avg (Claude +1.2, GPT-5.4 +0.0) — in stark contrast to in-distribution numbers.
  • Indicates revisions encode task-specific shortcuts, not transferable design.

Qualitative · Section 5.1 case studies

  • Edits are individually rational: behavioral rules, turn-budget trackers, output truncation, verification gates, recovery hints.
  • Yet "most edits memorize fixes rather than distill strategies"; a stable core of hard failures stays unaffected.

03 Strengths & Limitations

Strengths 4

  • Asks a foundational, field-level question prior harness-evolution work had not rigorously addressed — with concrete negative evidence, not speculation.
  • Unified budget framework cleanly separates "better design" from "more search"; Harness Scaling is an elegant ablation isolating instance vs. dataset effects.
  • Fair baselines: identical minimal initial harness, AHE explore-agent disabled, matched K=5, m=1.
  • Honest discussion acknowledging Terminal-Bench may be harness-insensitive, with concrete guidance on more diagnostic benchmarks.

Limitations 5

  • Single benchmark (Terminal-Bench 2.1) — one the authors concede may be insensitive to harness design; unclear for tool/skill-heavy agents.
  • Single budget point (K=5, m=1), no scaling curves — the regime proponents would cite.
  • Only two runs per config, no variance / CIs, despite small deltas (+0.6, +1.2).
  • Generalization claim rests on one 45/10/34 split, no sensitivity analysis.
  • Case studies highlight successes that contradict the aggregate "marginal gains" message.

04 Questions for Further Exploration

Q1Would harness evolution pull ahead at larger K (e.g. 20–50) or larger task batches, where shared design amortizes better?
Q2Do the negative results replicate on harness-sensitive benchmarks — web agents, multi-tool retrieval, long-horizon planning?
Q3How much of the pass@1 gap is context clob from accumulating persistent prompt text, and does pruning recover it?
Q4What is the variance of the +0.6 held-out result across alternative train/test splits?
Q5Can penalizing task-specific memorization (require cross-batch generalization before acceptance) close the gap?
Q6Does combining harness evolution (reusable structure) with test-time scaling (per-instance search) beat either alone?
