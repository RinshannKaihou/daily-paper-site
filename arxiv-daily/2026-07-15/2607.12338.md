> **一句话总结：** 本文通过对 SWE-bench、AppWorld、tau-bench 已完成任务级记录进行部分预算回放，定义并测量"最小充分任务预算"，发现仅报告任务比例或部分分数会掩盖错误决策、缺失任务组与未决比较——在 0 pp 阈值与 5 pp 预算网格下，AppWorld 15%、tau-bench 25%、SWE-bench Verified 90% 才首次满足全部目标，而 SWE-bench Lite 在主覆盖规则下到 95% 仍无法达到充分性。

> **TL;DR:** A single-author replay study re-runs partial task budgets over completed public records from SWE-bench Lite (552 ordered pairs), SWE-bench Verified (156), AppWorld (306), and tau-bench (12), showing that a task fraction is not a decision rule: at the strict 0 pp threshold on the 5 pp grid, the minimum sufficient budget ranges from 15% (AppWorld) to 90% (SWE-bench Verified) while SWE-bench Lite never reaches sufficiency by 95%, and cheap-first cost ordering on tau-bench uses only 11.51% of cost but suffers 100% coverage failure plus wrong pairwise conclusions (false rejections in 4 of 5 positive 5 pp cases).

## 核心贡献

- **识别"报告失败"问题**：指出仅给出部分任务比例或部分分数会掩盖三类错误——错误的成对决策、缺失必要的任务组覆盖、过多的未决比较。
- **定义"最小充分任务预算"**：在给定改进阈值 δ、任务组覆盖规则、未决比较上限 η 下，首次达到决策错误、覆盖失败、未决比例全部达标的最小测试预算。
- **在三大公开基准上实证测量**：SWE-bench Lite/Verified、AppWorld、tau-bench，证明该答案随基准、阈值、覆盖规则、配对方向与任务排序显著变化。
- **提供可复现工具包**：公开 GitHub 仓库包含回放代码、schema、清单、固定种子、派生表与多重一致性检查。
- **提出明确报告原则**：部分评估报告必须声明阈值、任务选择规则、覆盖规则、决策规则与未决比较比例，而非仅给出任务比例。

## Key Contributions

- Identifies a concrete reporting failure mode: a partial task fraction or partial score can hide wrong pairwise decisions, missing task groups, or too many unresolved comparisons — a "decision" failure, not merely an estimation error.
- Formalizes the minimum sufficient task budget as the smallest tested budget satisfying simultaneous targets on conditional false-accept rate (≤ α=0.05), conditional false-reject rate (≤ α=0.05), coverage failure (≤ γ=0.05), and unresolved comparisons (≤ η=0.25).
- Provides the first systematic replay of completed public agent-benchmark records (SWE-bench Lite/Verified, AppWorld, tau-bench) showing the budget is benchmark-, threshold-, coverage-rule-, orientation-, and ordering-dependent.
- Includes a coverage-aware bootstrap deferral policy plus a battery of comparators (Neyman allocation, Serfling finite-population, paired-normal and exact McNemar tests) to separate decision-rule effects from task-allocation effects.
- Releases a reproducibility package (code, manifests, fixed seeds, derived tables) and a reporting-principle checklist for partial agent-evaluation reports.

## Methodology

The paper treats a benchmark comparison as a decision problem rather than a score-estimation problem. For two systems A (tested) and B (comparison) over a fixed completed task set T = {t₁,…,t_N}, each task yields paired binary outcomes Y_{i,A}, Y_{i,B} ∈ {0,1}, with paired task difference D_i = Y_{i,A} − Y_{i,B}. The completed benchmark induces a full-benchmark decision: positive for A if Δ_full > δ, negative otherwise, where δ ∈ {0 pp, 5 pp, 10 pp} is the improvement threshold chosen before replay.

**Replay procedure.** The analysis permutes reveal orders over the fixed completed records. At each budget point b, a policy observes the subset S_b within budget, computes partial paired improvement Δ_b, checks task-group coverage, and either accepts A, rejects A, or defers (leaves comparison unresolved). For SWE-bench/AppWorld the authors use 500 reveal orders × 200 bootstrap samples per ordered pair; for tau-bench, 2,000 reveal orders × 500 bootstrap samples. The main budget search uses a 5 pp grid from 5% to 95%, plus a coarser 25/50/75% grid.

**Coverage rule.** Group definitions come from source metadata: repository for SWE-bench, split-by-difficulty for AppWorld, domain for tau-bench. The primary proportional floor requires r_g(b) = max{1, min{n_g, ⌊k_b·n_g/N⌋}} tasks from every group g; a slack floor subtracts one task per group as a sensitivity check.

**Policy families.** Forced policies (uniform, group-stratified, cost-aware) must return a final conclusion; deferring bootstrap-tail policies may leave comparisons unresolved when bootstrap resamples disagree about which side of δ the partial effect lies on (main policy uses tail cutoff a=0.05). Comparators include coverage paired-normal tests, exact McNemar tests (0 pp only), Neyman coverage bootstrap (variance-aware allocation), and Serfling finite-population radius bounds.

**Sufficiency criteria.** A budget is sufficient only when FAR_cond(b) ≤ α, FRR_cond(b) ≤ α, coverage-failure(b) ≤ γ, and defer(b) ≤ η simultaneously hold (defaults α=γ=0.05, η=0.25). Error denominators are conditional on the full-benchmark decision class, not on whether the policy returned a conclusion, so deferrals still count against the denominator.

## Results & Highlights

- **Minimum sufficient task budget at 0 pp on the 5 pp grid (primary coverage-aware bootstrap):** AppWorld first meets all targets at 15%, tau-bench at 25%, SWE-bench Verified at 90%; SWE-bench Lite does not reach sufficiency by 95% — a six-fold range across benchmarks (15% vs 90%, plus one never-sufficient case).
- **SWE-bench Lite 0 pp failure mode:** the primary policy meets the error-and-coverage targets already at 25% task budget, but leaves 55.92% of comparisons unresolved; even at 95% budget, 27.25% remain unresolved, exceeding the 25% target — so the failure is not wrong decisions but excessive deferral.
- **SWE-bench Verified 0 pp:** meets error-and-coverage targets at 25% but leaves 93.64% unresolved; the unresolved-comparison target is only met at 90% budget, where 24.22% remain unresolved.
- **Threshold sensitivity for SWE-bench Lite:** at 5 pp the sufficient budget drops to 55%, at 10 pp to 35% (vs. never-sufficient at 0 pp); tau-bench moves from 25% (0 pp) to 35% (10 pp); AppWorld stays at 15% for all three thresholds; SWE-bench Verified stays at 90%.
- **Coverage rule sensitivity:** with a one-task slack floor on the 25/50/75% grid, the 0 pp SWE-bench Lite case becomes sufficient at 75% budget; a stricter repository-by-difficulty grouping instead leaves most Lite comparisons unresolved at the same budget.
- **tau-bench cheap-first cost check:** cost-aware forced evaluation at 25% task budget uses only 11.51% of tested-system cost on average but incurs 100% coverage failure and produces wrong pairwise conclusions including false rejections in 4 of 5 positive 5 pp cases and 2 of 2 positive 10 pp cases.
- **Coverage-failure magnitude for forced policies:** at 25% task budget, uniform forced evaluation has 99.96% coverage failure on both SWE-bench Lite and AppWorld, showing a bare task fraction cannot justify a decision.
- **Bootstrap cutoff sweep (AppWorld, 0 pp):** sufficient budget moves from 10% (a=0.10) to 15% (a=0.05) to 25% (a=0.02), confirming the cutoff is part of the policy specification.
- **Replay stability:** across bootstrap-sample-count sweeps (100/200/500), the largest unresolved-comparison range for a fixed source/threshold/budget is 1.69 pp and the largest conditional decision-error rate is 1.40% (still below the 5% target); SWE-bench Lite's unresolved rate at 75% budget is 29.7% with a 95% seed interval of 29.5–29.9%.
- **Allocation diagnostics:** Neyman allocation reduces some SWE-bench Lite budgets (75% at 0 pp, 50% at 5 pp, 25% at 10 pp on the coarse grid) but uses completed-record group variances; the Serfling finite-population policy is conservative and reaches sufficiency only for AppWorld (75%).
- **Classical paired tests:** the coverage paired-normal test matches the bootstrap conclusion for AppWorld, tau-bench, and SWE-bench Verified at all thresholds; on SWE-bench Lite it still finds no sufficient 0 pp budget by 95%, reaches 70% at 5 pp and 35% at 10 pp; exact McNemar moves SWE-bench Verified from 90% to 95%.
- **Pair-orientation dependence:** SWE-bench Lite's undirected 0 pp collapse yields 275 positive vs only 1 negative decision, leaving conditional false-accept estimates essentially unsupported — the directed (ordered) orientation is retained as the primary target.

## Authors & Credibility

Solo work by Wei-Jung Huang, an independent researcher (United States), with no disclosed institutional affiliation or funding. The work is unusually transparent methodologically: it relies entirely on public task-level records from SWE-bench, AppWorld, and tau-bench, uses the official AppWorld tooling to unpack all 36 leaderboard bundles, fixes reveal-order seeds, and ships a full reproducibility package (https://github.com/WilliamWJHuang/How-Many-Tasks-Are-Enough-for-Agent-Benchmark-Decisions) with code, schemas, manifests, and derived tables. The framing carefully distinguishes replay claims over completed records from population-level or online-stopping claims, which is an unusually honest scoping of what the evidence supports.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Genuine conceptual reframing. Prior work (Cer-Eval, FAQ, HELM, MT-Bench) targets score estimation or confidence intervals; this paper explicitly targets pairwise *decisions* and adds coverage plus unresolved-comparison dimensions that score intervals cannot capture. The "minimum sufficient task budget" is a new quantity, not a re-skin of existing sample-size formulas.
- **Rigor:** High. Error denominators are correctly conditioned on the full-benchmark decision class rather than on whether a policy returned a conclusion (a subtle but common mistake); bootstrap cutoff, replicate count, orientation, grouping, and coverage-floor sensitivity are all swept; classical paired tests and finite-population bounds are run as comparators rather than ignored.
- **Reproducibility:** Strong — public code, fixed seeds, derived tables, explicit per-source record counts (e.g., SWE-bench Lite 552 ordered pairs / 276 unordered, AppWorld 306/153, tau-bench 12/6). The "near-complete" filtering rule (≥95% of split) is stated.
- **Significance:** Practically important for anyone running or reading partial agent-benchmark reports; the SWE-bench Lite "never sufficient by 95%" result and the tau-bench cheap-first 100% coverage-failure result are directly actionable caveats.
- **Red flags / honest weaknesses:**
  - The tau-bench evidence rests on only **6 unordered pairs (12 ordered comparisons)** drawn from **4 leaderboard entries**, with banking-knowledge trajectories excluded due to retrieval-config mismatch — a severe small-n limitation the paper acknowledges but the headline still benefits from.
  - SWE-bench cost fields are absent, so all SWE-bench claims are task-count replays; the cost-aware narrative rests on a single tau-bench grid.
  - No coverage of OSWorld, WebArena, AndroidWorld, AgentBench, etc., because parsed multi-system task-level records are unavailable — generalization beyond software repair, app-use, and tool-agent-user interaction is untested.
  - Neyman and Serfling allocation policies use *completed-record* group variances, so their budget reductions are optimistic upper bounds; a live evaluator would need pilot/historical variance estimates the paper cannot supply.
  - The η=0.25 unresolved target and α=γ=0.05 are presented as a fixed comparison point but the paper itself notes deployment settings may need different values — readers must not treat the specific budget numbers as universal.
  - Single author, no peer review disclosed (arXiv preprint), so the breadth of comparator sweeps is impressive but unvetted.

## Strengths

- Reframes partial benchmarking as a decision problem with four simultaneous targets (FAR, FRR, coverage, deferral) instead of a single error number.
- Pinpoint diagnostic of *why* each benchmark fails (SWE-bench Lite: excessive deferral; SWE-bench Verified: late resolution; tau-bench: cheap-first coverage collapse) rather than a single aggregate verdict.
- Comprehensive sensitivity battery — bootstrap cutoff, replicate count, orientation, grouping, coverage floor, classical paired tests, allocation comparators — that largely rules out "the result is an artifact of one knob."
- Honest scoping: explicitly states the replay permutes a fixed completed record and does not estimate future-task performance.
- Open, seed-fixed reproducibility package with derived tables.

## Limitations

- Small tau-bench evidence base (12 ordered comparisons from 6 unordered pairs across 4 systems; one domain excluded).
- No cost fields for SWE-bench, so cost-aware claims are tau-bench-only.
- Coverage limited to SWE-bench, AppWorld, tau-bench; other major agent benchmarks (OSWorld, WebArena, AndroidWorld, AgentBench, MiniWoB++, Terminal-Bench) are not analyzed.
- Neyman/Serfling allocation results use completed-record variances and are not deployable as-is.
- All targets (α, γ, η) are illustrative comparison points, not universal recommendations; deployment-specific calibration is left to the practitioner.
- Single-author arXiv preprint with no disclosed peer review.

## Relevance

与 LLM agent 评估和基准可复现性方向高度相关：直接回应了"部分跑基准/省钱跑子集"这一常见工程实践是否可信的问题，为评估报告应披露的最小信息集合提供了可操作的检查清单，对从事 agent 评测、leaderboard 维护或采购评估的研究者具有直接参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How do the minimum sufficient budgets change under truly adaptive sequential stopping rules (e.g., confidence sequences, always-valid p-values) rather than fixed-grid replay?
- Would variance-aware Neyman allocation with *pilot-estimated* (not completed-record) group variances preserve the budget reductions, or collapse back to uniform performance?
- Do the SWE-bench Lite "never sufficient at 0 pp" and SWE-bench Verified "90% needed" findings replicate on OSWorld, WebArena, AndroidWorld, and AgentBench once parsed multi-system records become available?
- Is the 25% unresolved-comparison target (η=0.25) a reasonable default for leaderboard triage vs. procurement settings, and what does the cost–unresolved trade-off curve look like?
- Can learned semantic or failure-mode task groupings (instead of repository/difficulty/domain metadata) reduce sufficient budgets without inflating coverage failure?
- How sensitive are the false-rejection patterns (e.g., 4 of 5 positive 5 pp tau-bench cases) to the choice of bootstrap tail cutoff a vs. the replay-level α, given the two quantities play different roles?
