Can LLMs write reliable rubrics for paper reproduction?

首个针对「LLM 自动生成论文复现评分细则」的系统化元评估：在 PaperBench 上跨四种递进增强设置比较 Claude-Sonnet 与 GPT-5.4，最强「蒸馏技能」把外层 Pearson 从 0.62 拉到 0.78，逼近人类基线 0.83 —— 但生成的 rubric 仍普遍过细、偏代码、打分偏高。

Why this matters

Resolves whether LLMs can auto-generate reproduction rubrics — the annotation bottleneck (94–2,551 criteria per paper, >2,800 expert-hrs on similar benchmarks) that blocks scalable paper-to-code evaluation.

Directly relevant to HiRAS / PaperCoder agents and any rubric-based auto-judge.
Critical edge

Rubric representation

Each criterion r = (c, s), importance s ∈ {1..5}. Final reproduction score = importance-weighted share of passed criteria: Σ[Pass]·s / Σs. Checklist format matches HealthBench / ResearchRubrics / RubricBench.

Dual-axis meta-evaluation

Extrinsic — Spearman/Pearson between repo scores induced by generated vs ground-truth rubrics, over 5 real repos (PaperCoder, HiRAS) × 20 papers, evaluated by ChatGPT-4o-mini (top-K=10 retrieval).

Intrinsic — ROUGE-1/L + embedding-cosine matching P/R/F1 (Qwen3-Embedding-8B, τ=0.7) against human rubrics.

What the numbers say

  • Coverage scales up: criteria count 35.95 → 77.95 (Claude) and 50.15 → 87.15 (GPT-5.4), approaching human 83.20.
  • Intrinsic gains are smaller than extrinsic: cosine-F1 0.63→0.68 (~8%); augmentation mainly helps recall (coverage), precision barely moves.
  • Score distribution shifts from skewed-high toward bell-shaped under augmentation — but a mild high-score bias persists.

The honest finding

Generated criteria stay too long (~45 tokens vs human 29) and too code-centric. Across all categories Code Development eats >60% of score; Result Match <21% — vs humans who adaptively give RL papers 48.8% to Result Match.

These rubrics would systematically reward code completeness over empirical fidelity.

03

Strengths & Limitations

优势与局限并置

Strengths ↑

  • Principled dual-axis evaluation — avoids judging rubrics by surface text alone.
  • Honest, specific failure-mode diagnosis with a concrete DPO/toxicity case study (Table 3).
  • Cost-efficient distillation: one Claude-Opus run yields a reusable skill document.
  • Generalizes across two backbones with the same monotonic-improvement pattern.
  • Threshold sensitivity (τ ∈ {0.65,0.70,0.75}) confirms recall-driven findings.

Limitations ↓

  • Human baseline is non-independent — the authors' own re-annotation by research students; no inter-annotator agreement reported.
  • Only 20 ML papers; the 0.74-vs-0.78 gap is almost certainly within noise.
  • Missing ablations: agentic step count, contents of the skill doc, length-vs-quality trade-off.
  • Single evaluator (4o-mini) — its noise and retrieval failures propagate into correlations.
  • Intrinsic precision never beats human; model matches coverage, not quality. Only two generator backbones tested.
04

Open questions for follow-up

待深究
  1. Would an independent expert baseline shrink, erase, or widen the 0.78-vs-0.83 gap?
  2. What is the noise ceiling from inter-annotator agreement on the checklist re-annotations?
  3. Can the code-centric bias be fixed by reweighting or anti-bias instructions — more cheaply than stronger distillation?
  4. Does the distilled skill transfer to non-ML papers, or does it encode training-loop assumptions?
  5. How sensitive are correlations to the evaluator choice and top-K retrieval depth?
  6. Could a small held-out set learn a per-type reweighting toward human category-adaptive weighting?
