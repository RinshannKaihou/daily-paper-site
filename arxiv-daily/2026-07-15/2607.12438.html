Fisher Rank Inflation

A Spectral Signature of Memorization under Label Noise.

Satwik Bathula · Anand A. Joshi — University of Southern California, ECE

Key Finding

As a network memorizes corrupted labels, the Fisher effective rank of last-layer gradients transiently inflates, then collapses — peaking at the structure→memorization transition.

Key Metric · Peak Effective Rank
97.09 ±1.78

at 60% label noise vs 28.88 ±1.95 clean — a 3.4× rise, monotonic in corruption rate ρ (3 seeds).

Why This Matters

A computable, interpretable spectral lens to monitor memorization — directly inspiring noise-label detection, early stopping, and sample-screening design.

Critical Edge

No head-to-head comparison against established noisy-label detectors (AUM, Confident Learning, Co-teaching) on matched checkpoints — so its practical detector value (AUROC 0.60–0.81) remains unestablished.

01

Methodology

Spectral · Last-layer gradients

The analysis centers on the centered Fisher-gradient scatter matrix. For each example, the per-example gradient w.r.t. the final affine layer is gᵢ = (p̂ᵢ − e_yᵢ) ⊗ z̃ᵢ; centering yields S = Σᵢ ̃gᵢ̃gᵢ⊤, and the paper tracks its entropy effective rank er(S) = exp(−Σ pᵶ log pᵶ).

The theory is built as a sequence of checkpoint-level spectral statements. A direction is rank-increasing iff its eigen-mass sits below 1/er(S); corrupted labels inflate rank by either creating new eigendirections outside the clean span, or by dumping mass into existing low-energy directions.

A first-order leave-one-out (LOO) expansion gives each sample a quadratic score Aᵢ(S) = ̃gᵢ⊤ B_S ̃gᵢ; corrupted samples are enriched when their gradients carry more energy than clean ones in rank-increasing directions.

Thm 1 · Local inflation

Corrupted-gradient covariance raises er(S) via new directions (Case 1) or sufficient mass in rank-increasing directions (Case 2).

Thm 2 / Cor 1 · LOO enrichment

Corrupted samples dominate LOO rank contribution when tr(B_S(Σ_N − Σ_C)) dominates the perturbative remainder.

Prop 1 · NIR ≥ 1

Noisy-to-clean effective-rank ratio ≥ 1 whenever the noisy spectrum is more diffuse (majorization).

Thm 3 · Signal decay

Because effective rank is scale-invariant, LOO contributions vanish only once normalized spectra stabilize — explaining post-memorization weakening.

Strengths

what holds up
  • Clean, original spectral framing that genuinely complements loss / forgetting / influence analyses of memorization.
  • Coherent theory with named, testable conditions (rank-increasing directions; new-direction vs. in-span mechanisms; normalized spectral stability).
  • Consistent enrichment for corrupted examples across architectures, datasets, and real human noise (CIFAR-10N).
  • Well-designed clean-difficulty ablation credibly isolating label corruption from optimization difficulty.
  • Intellectual honesty: authors delineate the phenomenon as a signature, not an online stopping rule, and disclose the ViT/CIFAR-100 failures.

Limitations

where it strains
  • No comparison to standard detectors (small-loss, Co-teaching, AUM, Confident Learning) on matched checkpoints; AUROC 0.60–0.81 is hard to interpret without them.
  • First-order LOO degrades for ViT (negative sensitivity gap, Spearman 0.70) and CIFAR-100 (0.65) — higher-order effects uncaptured.
  • Only symmetric corruption swept; asymmetric / class- / instance-dependent noise untested despite theory predicting them.
  • Small scale: 20k training subsets, last-layer-only, vision-only; per-example gradient cost may not scale to ImageNet.
  • “Lead time precedes test degradation” is retrospective and threshold-dependent (esp. ViT), not a demonstrated online signal.
03

Questions for Further Exploration

How does Fisher Rank Inflation behave under asymmetric, class-dependent, or instance-dependent noise — and at ImageNet scale (Clothing1M, WebVision)?
Can rank-onset become a principled early-stopping / sample-reweighting rule, head-to-head vs AUM, Confident Learning, and small-loss on AUROC and final clean accuracy?
Why does first-order attribution break down for ViT / CIFAR-100 — what is the role of higher-order deletion effects, and can a second-order correction recover the signal?
Does the inflation–collapse trajectory appear in non-vision modalities (NLP, speech) and non-classification objectives (contrastive, detection)?
How does the phenomenon interact with explicit regularization (dropout, weight decay, label smoothing) and noise-robust losses (GCE, SCE) — do they suppress inflation by blocking memorization?
Is the centered last-layer scatter sufficient, or do earlier-layer rank dynamics tell a richer, layer-resolved memorization story?
