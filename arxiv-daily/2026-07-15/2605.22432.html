AMUSEv2

Anytime Muon with Stable Gradient Evaluation

Embeds Muon inside the Schedule-Free framework with a time-varying interpolation coefficient βt — stabilizing Muon's valley-wall oscillations so it trains anytime, with no learning-rate schedule.

Methodology

River-valley diagnosis

Following the river-valley decomposition (Song et al., 2025a), parameter space is split via the top-k Hessian eigenspace (k = #classes, Neural Collapse motivated): the dominant subspace Sk (steep valley walls, high curvature) and its flat orthogonal complement Sk⊥ (the river, bulk of parameters).

On a 5k MNIST + 3-layer MLP probe, Muon's update has a much smaller dominant ratio than SGD/AdamW — orthogonalization redirects the update into the bulk/river, explaining its fast progress. But the normalization is non-selective, so residual dominant components survive and get amplified into valley-wall oscillations (Appendix A: a 2D matrix-quadratic "bouncing" toy).

The AMUSE update

Muon is dropped into the Schedule-Free three-sequence formulation. A time-varying βt shifts gradient evaluation from the fast base sequence zt toward the averaged xt:

Yt = (1−βt)Zt + βtXt
Mt = µMt−1 + (1−µ)∇L(Yt)
Zt+1 = Zt − η·T(Mt)
Xt+1 = (1−ct+1)Xt + ct+1Zt+1
% after warmup T₀:
βt = 1 − (T₀/t)ρ·(1−βinit),   ρ ∈ [0,1]

Small early βt → fast adaptation near zt; growing βt → stable averaging near xt before orthogonalization. The schedule is horizon-independent, so it removes LR decay and is anytime-compatible. ρ=0 recovers fixed-β SF-Muon.

Why it works. Post-hoc EWA and LR-decay applied to AMUSE's xt trajectory yield almost no further loss reduction (Fig. 6) — AMUSE already tracks the river. Constant-LR Muon benefits substantially from both, confirming it oscillates. AMUSE also shows higher cosine similarity between consecutive Δxt updates, consistent with smoother river progress.

Results & Highlights

Across 6 image setups (5 seeds) and 3 LLM scales, AMUSE consistently beats (Schedule-Free) AdamW and Muon — and on 124M Llama also beats AdEMAMix, ADOPT, Signum, Prodigy, MARS, Lion, Sophia, and SOAP (all with tuned cosine schedules).

  • Image-domain sweep (5 seeds): highest test accuracy on CIFAR-10 (Wide-ResNet-16-8), CIFAR-100 (DenseNet), SVHN (ResNet-3-96), ImageNet-1k (ResNet-50), ISIC 2018 segmentation (U-Net), and MAE ViT-B/16 fine-tuning.

  • Ablation — drop Muon momentum (Fig. 26): removing the pre-orthogonalization momentum buffer causes a large drop for both AMUSE and SF-Muon; momentum is essential for smoothing stochastic noise.
  • Large-batch scaling: SF-AdamW struggles with the large batches typical of modern LLMs (Morwani et al., 2025); AMUSE scales effectively to 720M (batch 1984) and 1.3B (batch 2048).

Strengths & Limitations

+ Strengths

  • Clean theory-to-method story: the same river-valley lens diagnoses Muon's weakness and prescribes the SF averaging fix.
  • Horizon-independent closed form for βt elegantly resolves the early-adaptation vs. late-stability tradeoff that fixed-β SF-Muon cannot (Figs. 5, 23, 25).
  • Broad coverage: 6 image setups (5 seeds) + 3 LLM scales + a 10-optimizer comparison on 124M Llama.
  • Rescues fine-tuning of AdamW-pretrained checkpoints (MAE ViT) where vanilla Muon fails — a real deployment pain point.
  • Low marginal cost: one extra state copy, ~1.067× AdamW wall-clock, one extra hyperparameter.
  • Honest ablations (cap βt; drop momentum; EWA/WSD on Muon) that isolate why it works, not just that it does.

− Limitations

  • Scale. Largest LLM is 1.3B on ~26B tokens; behavior at 7B+ and multi-trillion-token regimes is unverified.
  • Evaluation depth. LLM results are perplexity-only — no downstream, zero-shot, or long-context benchmarks.
  • Diagnostic-vs-deployment gap. Mechanistic river-valley claims are shown at MLP/MNIST scale; at LLM scale they rest on indirect proxies (‖Δxt‖, EWA insensitivity, cosine similarity).
  • Extra memory (one full zt copy) vs vanilla Muon — non-trivial where optimizer state dominates.
  • Hyperparameter sensitivity studied only on 124M Llama over a small grid; aggressive ρ or long T0 not characterized.
  • No head-to-head with the concurrent schedule-free spectral optimizer of Apte et al. (2605.23061), targeting the same niche.
  • µ pinned at 0.95 without tuning — reported gaps may slightly flatter AMUSE.

Open Questions

Scaling

Does the perplexity advantage at 124M–1.3B persist at 7B–70B and multi-trillion-token budgets — and translate into downstream/zero-shot gains?

Memory

Can the extra zt copy be avoided via in-place momentum reuse or a low-rank averaged-sequence approximation?

Systems

How does AMUSE interact with gradient accumulation, ZeRO sharding, and tensor parallelism — does zt complicate sharding vs AdamW?

Adaptive β

Is the fixed (T₀/t)ρ schedule optimal, or could an online dominant-ratio / sharpness-driven βt beat it?

Concurrent work

A direct comparison with Apte et al. (2605.23061, schedule-free spectral) on identical compute — which stabilization mechanism generalizes better?

Beyond Transformers

Does the river-valley diagnosis carry to SSMs, MoE experts, or diffusion U-Nets where Hessian spectral structure differs?

Authors & Credibility

The same KAIST group authored the river-valley "Does SGD really happen in tiny subspaces?" line and the Schedule-Free river analysis (Song et al., 2025a,b) that AMUSE directly builds on — strong provenance on this exact problem. Coverage spans MLP, Wide-ResNet, DenseNet, ResNet, U-Net, ViT, and Llama-style Transformers at three scales, with baselines tuned from prior work rather than strawmen.
