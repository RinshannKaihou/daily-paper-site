> **一句话总结：** 本文重新审视 LLM agent 自动 harness 进化（harness evolution）的评测方法，提出在统一推理预算下与 test-time scaling 基线（并行采样、顺序精炼）公平对比；在 Terminal-Bench 2.1 上使用 Claude Opus 4.6 与 GPT-5.4 实验发现，自动 harness 进化并不能稳定胜过简单的 test-time scaling 基线，且在 held-out 任务上仅带来平均 +0.6 的微弱提升，存在严重过拟合，说明现有证据需要更谨慎的解读。

> **TL;DR:** This paper critiques the standard evaluation protocol for automatic harness evolution (e.g., Meta-Harness, AHE, AEVO), which uses unit-test feedback to search over harnesses and then reports accuracy on the same benchmark. Under a unified K=5 inference budget on Terminal-Bench 2.1 with Claude Opus 4.6 / GPT-5.4 / GPT-5.4 mini, harness evolution averages 67.4 pass@1 (no unit tests) and 75.8 pass@1 (with unit tests), underperforming parallel sampling (72.3 / 86.0) and sequential refinement (69.3 / 84.3); on a held-out test split of 34 tasks the evolved harness gains only +0.6 points on average over the initial harness (Claude +1.2, GPT-5.4 +0.0), indicating the reported gains stem largely from overfitting to the search set rather than generalizable harness design.

## 核心贡献

- 揭示现有自动 harness 进化评测的两个根本缺陷：（1）未在匹配的反馈与推理预算下与 test-time scaling 基线对比，无法区分"改进了 harness 设计"还是"只是多做了搜索"；（2）搜索与最终评测共用同一公开 benchmark，存在过拟合风险。
- 提出统一的预算视角（unified budget view），将并行采样（Parallel Sampling）、顺序精炼（Sequential Refinement）、harness 进化（Harness Evolution）以及新提出的 harness scaling（Harness Scaling）纳入同一框架，明确每种方法更新什么、观察何种反馈。
- 设计三类实验设置：无 unit test 反馈、有 unit test 反馈、以及搜索任务与评测任务不相交的泛化设置，系统性检验 harness 进化的有效性与迁移能力。
- 实证结论：在 Terminal-Bench 2.1 上，自动 harness 进化不能稳定胜过简单 test-time scaling 基线，且其增益几乎不能迁移到 held-out 任务，提示当前证据应被谨慎对待，并呼吁更公平的评测协议。

## Key Contributions

- Identifies two fundamental flaws in how automatic harness evolution is currently evaluated: (1) it is not compared against test-time scaling baselines under matched feedback/inference budgets, so apparent gains may come from extra search rather than better harness design; (2) the search set and the final evaluation set coincide (e.g., Terminal-Bench), inviting task-specific overfitting.
- Formalizes a unified budget framework covering four methods — Parallel Sampling, Sequential Refinement, Harness Evolution, and a newly introduced Harness Scaling (instance-guided, task-specific harness adaptation) — making explicit what each updates (trajectories vs. harness) and what feedback it observes.
- Runs controlled experiments on Terminal-Bench 2.1 across three frontier models (Claude Opus 4.6, GPT-5.4, GPT-5.4 mini) at a fixed K=5 budget in three settings: without unit tests, with unit tests, and a disjoint train/validation/test (45/10/34) generalization split.
- Provides qualitative analysis (Section 5, Appendix B case studies) showing that meta-agent edits are individually rational (adding behavioral rules, turn-budget trackers, verification gates, recovery hints) but mostly memorize task-specific fixes rather than distilling transferable strategies, leaving a stable core of hard failures unaffected.

## Methodology

The paper reframes the comparison of harness-evolution methods under a unified compute budget K (set to 5). All methods share common notation: a policy πθ, a task x ~ X, a harness h producing a trajectory y ~ πθ(·|x;h), an outcome R(y,g)∈{0,1} when a unit test g is available, and a summarization map Φ (the "Agent Debugger") that condenses an experience store into reusable observations.

Four methods are compared, each differing in what it updates and what feedback it sees:

1. **Parallel Sampling** (test-time scaling, width): draws K independent trajectories y₁..y_K with a *fixed* harness h. Without unit tests, a self-judge J picks argmax_k J(y_k); with unit tests, any accepted trajectory is returned.

2. **Sequential Refinement** (test-time scaling, depth): generates y₁ directly, then y_k ~ πθ(·|x, Φ(y_{k-1}[, R]);h) conditions each attempt on the previous trajectory (and its outcome if available). Returns the last trajectory or any accepted one.

3. **Harness Evolution** (dataset-guided): optimizes a *shared* harness across a batch of n tasks. At round k it draws m=1 rollout per task under h_k, stores evidence, and a meta-agent M proposes h_k = M(Φ(C_{k-1})). Instantiated with AHE [Lin et al. 2026] but with its explore agent disabled (so gains come from feedback-driven evolution, not retrieval of benchmark-tuned harnesses). The best harness ĥ is selected by aggregate outcome and used for final evaluation.

4. **Harness Scaling** (instance-guided, newly introduced): a harness-level analogue of test-time scaling that adapts the harness per single task: h_k = M(x, Φ(h_{k-1}, y_{k-1}[, R])), then resamples. This isolates whether gains come from reusable design vs. extra per-instance computation.

To ensure fairness, Parallel Sampling and Sequential Refinement use the same initial harness h₁ as the fixed harness; Harness Evolution and Harness Scaling are initialized from the same h₁. All rollouts run in freshly provisioned E2B sandboxes with 128k max generation, high reasoning effort, and results averaged over two runs. The generalization setting splits Terminal-Bench 2.1 into 45 train / 10 validation / 34 held-out test tasks and reports pass@1 on the test set.

## Results & Highlights

- **Without unit tests (Table 1, pass@1, average over 3 models):** Parallel Sampling achieves **72.3** vs. direct-sampling baseline **68.2** (+4.1), beating Harness Scaling (**71.8**), Sequential Refinement (**69.3**), and Harness Evolution (**67.4**, which actually *underperforms* direct sampling). On GPT-5.4 specifically, Harness Evolution *drops* accuracy from **75.3** to **69.7** (−5.6).
- **With unit tests (Table 2):** On pass@1, Parallel Sampling leads at **86.0** average vs. Harness Evolution **75.8** (a **+10.2** gap); on pass@5, Sequential Refinement leads at **91.8** vs. Harness Evolution **86.2**. Harness Evolution "barely improves over direct sampling" on pass@1, and its benefit only materializes when selecting among multiple trajectories.
- **Generalization (Table 3, disjoint 45/10/34 split, pass@1):** The evolved harness yields only **+0.6** average gain on held-out test tasks (Claude Opus 4.6 **+1.2**, GPT-5.4 **+0.0**), in sharp contrast to in-distribution gains — indicating the discovered revisions encode task-specific shortcuts rather than transferable design.
- **Qualitative finding (Section 5.1):** Meta-agent edits are individually rational (behavioral rules, turn-budget trackers, truncation of oversized tool outputs, finalization gates, recovery hints), but "most edits memorize fixes rather than distill strategies," and a stable core of hard failures (deep domain reasoning, constraints outside harness control) remains unaffected; growing persistent prompt text also causes context clob.

Hajishirzi and Tsvetkov are well-established senior researchers in NLP/agents, lending strong credibility. The framing is explicitly adversarial to a trendy subfield (automatic harness evolution: Meta-Harness, AHE, AEVO), and the authors disarm a likely rebuttal by disabling AHE's explore agent (which retrieves benchmark-specific harnesses) so that all reported gains come from feedback-driven evolution. Code is released at https://github.com/rethinking-harness-evolution. The use of Terminal-Bench 2.1 (a verified revision fixing 28/89 tasks for dependency drift and budget mismatches) and three frontier models strengthens methodological soundness.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** High conceptual value but moderate technical novelty. The core contribution is an evaluation critique + a controlled comparison protocol rather than a new algorithm; the only new method (Harness Scaling) is a straightforward per-instance analogue of test-time scaling. The value lies in asking the right question that the harness-evolution literature had not stress-tested.
- **Rigor:** The unified-budget framing is well-motivated and the disabling of AHE's explore agent is a genuinely fair design choice that preempts the "you handicapped the baseline" objection. However, only **K=5** and **m=1** are tested — a single budget point. There is no scaling-curve analysis showing whether harness evolution might win at larger K or larger task batches, which is exactly the regime its proponents would point to. Results are averaged over only **two runs**, and no variance/confidence intervals are reported despite acknowledged rollout stochasticity.
- **Reproducibility:** Reasonable. Configurations (Table 4), the initial minimal harness (single bash tool, no skills/middleware/memory), the summarization map (Agent Debugger), and the Terminal-Bench 2.1 split are documented; code is public. But the held-out split (45/10/34) is not released as a canonical artifact, and re-running requires E2B sandboxes plus frontier-model API access.
- **Significance:** Potentially high impact as a corrective to an active research direction; it could shift how harness-evolution papers are required to report results. But the scope is narrow: a single benchmark (Terminal-Bench 2.1) dominated by CLI/coding tasks where, as the authors themselves note, "a minimal setup consisting of a shell tool and a basic prompt already suffices," so Terminal-Bench may simply be *insensitive* to harness design — a confound that limits how far the negative conclusion generalizes.
- **Red flags / overclaiming:** The title "does not consistently outperform" is appropriately hedged, but the generalization claim rests on a single benchmark and a single split. The case-study evidence (Appendix B) is cherry-picked success stories (e.g., caffe-cifar-10, compile-compcert, mteb-retrieve all flip fail→pass), which somewhat cuts against the "marginal gains" narrative and is not quantified against the full task population. The omission of confidence intervals and budget sweeps is the most serious methodological gap.

## Strengths

- Asks a foundational, field-level question that prior harness-evolution work had not rigorously addressed, and provides concrete negative evidence rather than speculation.
- Unified budget framework cleanly separates "improved harness design" from "more test-time search," and the Harness Scaling ablation is an elegant way to isolate instance-level vs. dataset-level effects.
- Fair baseline handling: identical initial minimal harness across all four methods, explore agent disabled, matched K=5 budget, m=1 rollouts.
- Honest, well-argued discussion (Section 5.2) acknowledging that Terminal-Bench may be harness-insensitive and that the negative result is task-dependent, with concrete guidance on what benchmarks would be more diagnostic.

## Limitations

- Single benchmark (Terminal-Bench 2.1) — and one the authors concede may be insensitive to harness design — so the generality of the negative conclusion is unclear for tool/skill-heavy domains (e.g., web agents, retrieval-augmented agents).
- Single budget point (K=5, m=1) with no scaling curves; harness-evolution methods might behave differently at larger compute or larger task batches.
- Only two runs per configuration and no reported variance/confidence intervals, despite several reported deltas being small (e.g., +0.6, +1.2) and plausibly within noise.
- Generalization claim rests on one 45/10/34 split with no sensitivity analysis across alternative splits.
- Case studies highlight successes that contradict the aggregate "marginal gains" message without reconciling the two.

## Relevance

这项工作直击 LLM agent 自动 harness/工具链优化的评测方法论，对关注 agent 自动改进、test-time scaling 与 benchmark 可信度的研究者高度相关；其"区分真实 harness 改进与过拟合搜索"的视角，对设计自进化 agent 系统与评测协议都有直接借鉴价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Would harness evolution pull ahead at larger K (e.g., K=20–50) or with larger task batches, where shared design improvements could amortize better than independent sampling?
- Do the negative results replicate on harness-sensitive benchmarks (web agents, multi-tool retrieval, long-horizon planning) where a minimal shell harness is insufficient?
- How much of the pass@1 gap between Harness Evolution and Parallel Sampling is attributable to context clob from accumulating persistent prompt text, and does periodic pruning/reset recover performance?
- What is the variance of the held-out generalization result (+0.6 average) across alternative train/test splits of Terminal-Bench 2.1?
- Can a harness-evolution method that explicitly penalizes task-specific memorization (e.g., requiring edits to generalize across a held-out batch before acceptance) close the generalization gap?
- Does combining harness evolution (for reusable structure) with test-time scaling (for per-instance search) yield gains beyond either alone under a shared budget?
