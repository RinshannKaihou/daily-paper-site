Key finding

4-bit quantization damage is almost entirely first-order — a one-parameter-per-layer saturation model explains why, and beats production ModelOpt by 17–21% KL.

一句话总结 将混合精度量化损失视为布尔立方上的集合函数，证明 4-bit 下 85–93% 损失方差由一阶（逐层）效应解释，并由"饱和"机制驱动的 coverage 模型统一刻画。
The coverage model
f(S) = c · ( 1 − ∏i∈S (1 − ai) )
Each quantized layer consumes a fixed fraction ai (its break-rate) of the remaining headroom toward ceiling c. Saturation makes the error additive — and predictable from L+1 parameters.
▍ Key metric
85–93%
of quantization-damage variance is first-order (per-layer) across six LLMs at whole-layer FP4.
On exact 28 W4A4 lattices the share climbs to 96.9–99.7% (dense, ≤72B) and 90.7% on gpt-oss-120B.
▍ Why this matters

A provable, near-measurement-free allocator that beats production ModelOpt on KL — and keeps sub-4-bit LLMs still generating. Directly serves 4-bit deployment.

▍ Critical edge

The advantage is format-scoped: under coarse per-tensor activation scales the order-≥2 share hits 0.81 and nonparametric surrogates win — not a universal recipe.

01

Methodology — damage as a set function

Walsh · Möbius · Aumann–Shapley

The paper reframes mixed-precision allocation as a problem about a set function. For a block of L layers, the damage f(S) = ΔLCE is measured by actually quantizing layer-set S and evaluating the partially quantized model (stochastic rounding averaged over 16 draws), under the deployment distribution µp with p = 0.6. Two classical basis changes are then applied to f.

1Walsh / functional-ANOVA decomposition

Splits Var(f) by interaction order. By Parseval, the best degree-≤1 predictor's squared error exactly equals the order-≥2 energy (Theorem 1). Order-1 carries 85–93% of variance — 98–99% after noise correction.

2Möbius expansion + saturation

Coverage predicts nonzero coefficients at every order, alternating in sign — exactly the divergent overshoot observed (up to 32× true loss on a 10-layer Qwen3-8B lattice). Unifying cause: saturation.

f(S) = c · (1 − ∏(1 − aᵢ))

3A fit-free estimator

A single Aumann–Shapley path integral (loss gradient averaged over increasingly quantized contexts) recovers the optimal additive coefficient at a layer-specific effective density — with no sampled configurations, proven to preserve the exact layer ranking under coverage.

4A certificate on every result

The order-≥2 share is forecast in closed form from L+1 fitted parameters via the saturation-depth law (Prop. 5), telling the user in advance how well any additive model can do. Measured share tracks fitted depth τ at Spearman ρ = 0.958; exact prediction matches at Pearson r = 0.984.

03

Strengths & limitations

honest certificates, scoped gains

▲ Strengths

  • Unifies two empirical facts (concentrated first-order variance + near-perfect additive ranking) under one intuitive saturation mechanism with closed-form Möbius/Walsh predictions that are then confirmed.
  • Ships a certificate with every result — a principled bound on how much any additive model can improve. Rare and intellectually honest.
  • Aumann–Shapley estimator avoids the ~100L progressive evaluations CoopQ's SPQE needs — a real measurement-cost win.
  • Real deployment validation: matched-memory serving vs vendor NVFP4 on B200 (AIME25, GPQA, MMLU-Pro, HumanEval), not just calibration KL.
  • Broad scale coverage: 0.6B → 753B, dense + MoE, LLMs + a diffusion DiT.

▼ Limitations

  • Format-dependent. Saturation lives under per-block NVFP4; under coarse per-tensor activation scales the order-≥2 share explodes (up to 0.81) and surrogates win.
  • High-order interactions exist where scope ends: the diffusion DiT shows order-≥2 share 0.36 — above every LLM cell. The "90% first-order" story is an LLM-per-block-NVFP4 phenomenon.
  • Sub-4-bit results depend on an error-compensating base (GPTQ ladder); under round-to-nearest every allocator collapses.
  • End-task signal is noisy ≥4-bit: AIME-2025 shows no significant method ordering on Qwen3-30B — calibration KL is the load-bearing metric there.
  • Single-author, no public code; the measurement pipeline (lattice enumeration, nonlinear fits, path integral) is non-trivial to re-implement.
04

Open questions

for further exploration
Q1

Does the saturation law hold beyond NVFP4 — e.g. INT4 block-wise, FP6, MX formats — or is the 85–93% share specific to per-block-16 float?

Q2

How does the certificate behave under rotation pre-processing (QuaRot, SpinQuant, QuIP#)? Could composing lower the share enough to retire surrogates even on diffusion DiTs?

Q3

Can the Aumann–Shapley estimator be made calibration-data-aware, modeling the sampling variance the paper leaves unmodeled, to produce a data-aware certificate?

Q4

The EOS termination threshold (~0.45 nats) is discovered but not predicted — can coverage forecast it from break-rates, pricing "survival"?

Q5

At sub-4-bit, the derivation corpus sets the usable budget (58.3 vs 39.6 AIME at eff 3.0). Is there a principled corpus-selection rule under coverage?

Q6

At eff 2.75 the KL–accuracy ordering breaks (uniform lowest KL, scores 19.5 vs additive's 54.9). Can the framework warn when "lower KL = better" fails?
