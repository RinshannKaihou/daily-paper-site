> **一句话总结：** 这是 Oak Ridge 国家实验室主导的社区路线图（AISLE 一周年更新版），把去年提出的五个互联自主科学维度重新打分（M1–M14 大部分为 Partial/Open），并新增"信任与验证""安全与治理"两个一等维度（含 M15–M18），核心论断是：当前自主科学的瓶颈不再是生成候选发现，而是验证它们，因此能力-可靠性鸿沟而非模型能力本身才是关键约束。

> **TL;DR:** A 28-author community roadmap (ORNL/TM-2026/4663) that re-scores the one-year-old AISLE autonomous-science roadmap against evidence: most original milestones (M1–M14) are only Partially achieved or Open, two cross-cutting concerns (trust/verification, safety/governance) are elevated to first-class dimensions with four new milestones (M15–M18), and the central thesis is that the field's binding constraint is now the capability–reliability gap — benchmarks like CORE-Bench, ScienceAgentBench, and ASTA-Bench show agents that ace closed-ended questions still complete only a small fraction of open-ended research, and a published Nature correction [13] retracted novelty claims and a training-data leak, while no system has end-to-end self-validated a genuinely novel discovery.

## 核心贡献

- **对旧路线图做"可问责的修订"：** 不是再写一篇综述，而是用一年的证据对去年 AISLE 的 14 个里程碑逐条打分（achieved / partial / reframed / open），并公开记录（Table 1）。作者主张这种"承诺里程碑—再公开评估"的做法应成为社区惯例。
- **识别七项格局转变（Shift 1–7）：** 从孤立演示到验证发现、自驾实验室进入第二代（SDL 2.0）、接口层开始收敛（MCP+A2A）、推理/基础模型成为新底座、基准暴露能力-可靠性鸿沟、信任与治理走到台前、工业界成为主要玩家。
- **把两个"横切关注点"提升为一等维度：** 信任/验证/可复现（§3.6，M15–M16）与安全/安保/诚信/治理（§3.7，M17–M18），并明确论证：没有对等验证基础设施的互联生态只会放大而非抑制失败模式。
- **提出两个贯穿全文的分析透镜：** 自主性阶梯（tool → analyst → scientist）与能力-可靠性鸿沟，后者被认定为"比原始模型能力更关键的瓶颈"。
- **把草根网络 AISLE 定位为"互操作性织物"：** 论证自上而下的国家级动员（Genesis Mission）与自下而上的社区协调是互补而非冗余，AISLE 提供大型项目和商业厂商倾向于轻视的厂商中立接口与跨机构标准。
- **新增四个里程碑 M15–M18：** 覆盖全生命周期验证与确认、可复现性与效率基准、数字-物理接口处的筛查、跨机构 agent 身份与治理。

## Key Contributions

- **Accountable revision of a milestone-bearing roadmap.** Rather than surveying the field anew, the report scores the prior AISLE roadmap's 14 milestones (M1–M14) as achieved/partial/reframed/open against one year of evidence (Table 1), and argues this form of public rescoring should become a recurring community practice.
- **Seven landscape shifts.** Characterizes the past year along seven shifts: validated discoveries, SDL 2.0, consolidating interface layer (MCP/A2A), reasoning+foundation models as substrate, benchmarks exposing the capability–reliability gap, trust/governance moving to the foreground, and industry becoming a primary actor.
- **Two concerns elevated to first-class dimensions.** Trust, Verification & Reproducibility (§3.6) and Safety, Security, Integrity & Governance (§3.7), each with new milestones (M15–M18), justified by the claim that an interconnected ecosystem without commensurate verification would amplify failure modes.
- **Two interpretive lenses.** An autonomy ladder (tool/analyst/scientist) and a persistent capability–reliability gap, the latter identified as the binding constraint on autonomous science today rather than raw model capability.
- **A division-of-labor argument for AISLE.** Positions the grassroots network as the interoperability fabric that lets top-down national mobilization (Genesis Mission) and commercial platforms connect rather than re-silo, supplying vendor-neutral interfaces and cross-institutional standards that large programs underweight.
- **A scoped two-year plan.** Year 1 focuses on interfaces, protocol adoption, AI-driven metadata, and scaffolding verification; Year 2 targets federation, zero-trust coordination, cross-facility knowledge integration, and governance — deliberately short-horizon given the pace of change.

## Methodology

This is a community roadmap / position white paper (Technical Report ORNL/TM-2026/4663), not an experimental study, so "methodology" here means the report's own analytical procedure. The authors (a 28-person multi-institution consortium spanning ORNL, universities, and national labs) proceed in four steps, mirroring their four stated contributions:

1. **Landscape characterization via seven shifts.** They survey the year's developments and organize them into seven shifts (§2.1–§2.7), interpreted through two lenses the community converged on: a graded autonomy ladder (tool → analyst → scientist) and the capability–reliability gap (the gap between closed-ended question performance and open-ended research performance).

2. **Milestone scorecard.** They revisit the original five AISLE dimensions — (1) instrument/cyberinfrastructure integration, (2) agent-driven data management, (3) agent-driven orchestration, (4) interoperable agent interfaces, (5) education/workforce — and classify each prior milestone M1–M14 as achieved, partially achieved, reframed, or open (Table 1), citing specific evidence per dimension (§3.1–§3.5).

3. **Dimension elevation.** They promote two concerns that were cross-cutting afterthoughts in the original roadmap — trust/verification/reproducibility and safety/security/integrity/governance — to first-class dimensions (§3.6–§3.7), and propose four new milestones M15–M18 for them.

4. **Ecosystem positioning.** They situate the grassroots AISLE network within the new federal/international landscape (Genesis Mission, Trillion Parameter Consortium, European RAISE, Acceleration Consortium) and argue top-down mobilization and bottom-up coordination are complementary (§4).

The architecture is rendered two ways in Figure 1: (a) a closed discovery loop (hypothesize→execute→capture→analyze) coordinated by MCP (agent-to-tool) and A2A (agent-to-agent), enclosed by the two elevated dimensions, over an education foundation; and (b) a two-year trajectory climbing the autonomy ladder with representative milestones per phase (Tool 0–8 mo, Analyst 8–16 mo, Scientist 16–24 mo). Throughout, the report distinguishes *verification* (was the workflow built/executed correctly) from *validation* (does the result correspond to physical reality), arguing validation is the harder problem in the physical sciences where only measurement can settle a question.

## Results & Highlights

No quantitative results reported

This is a roadmap/white paper with no experiments of its own; its evidence consists of curated quantitative and qualitative facts about the state of autonomous science. The most load-bearing quantitative claims it marshals (all cited from prior work, not produced here):

- **Capability–reliability gap is sharp and measurable.** Agents that approach expert performance on closed-ended scientific questions "still complete only a small fraction of open-ended, end-to-end research tasks" on benchmarks such as ScienceAgentBench [15], ASTA-Bench [14], and Scicode (a research-coding benchmark curated by scientists that "remain[s] largely unsolved by frontier models") [16]. Reproducibility-focused benchmarks (CORE-Bench [26]) report agent accuracies that "in some settings, fall below random guessing."
- **A flagship autonomous-discovery result was publicly corrected.** The Szymanski et al. Nature autonomous materials-synthesis paper [13/76] was the subject of an Author Correction (Nature 650:8100, 2026) that re-characterized "novel" compounds as novel-to-a-platform, removed one compound that had leaked from the training data, and revealed the automated structural refinement was below human-expert quality. The report uses this as its central cautionary case.
- **Industry capital dwarfs academic budgets.** Lila Sciences assembled roughly half a billion dollars (~$235M Series A cited [30]) to build robotic "AI science factories"; Periodic Labs raised a $300M seed round for closed-loop materials discovery [31] — yet "several of the best-funded autonomous-discovery efforts have released little peer-reviewed validation as of this writing."
- **Production agents are kept deliberately bounded.** The first systematic study of agents in production [27] finds teams keep them human-supervised, running only a handful of steps before a human intervenes, with the majority still gated by human evaluation and reliability named the top obstacle.
- **No end-to-end self-validated novel discovery exists.** "At the time of writing, there is no verified instance of an agent autonomously making and experimentally self-validating a genuinely novel discovery end to end" — systems that reason most autonomously still rely on humans for physical experiments [5], the most autonomous synthesizers have had novelty claims contested [13], and full-loop systems operate only computationally without wet-lab validation [20].

The milestone scorecard (Table 1) itself is a key deliverable: of M1–M14, none are marked "achieved"; most are Partial (M1, M5, M7, M8, M12) or Open (M2, M3, M4, M6, M9, M11, M13, M14); only M10 (standardized cross-vendor agent interfaces) is "Reframed" around the MCP/A2A consolidation. The two dimensions closest to raw model capability (orchestration §3.3, agent interfaces §3.4) "moved the furthest"; data management, education, and federated instrument integration "moved the least."

## Authors & Credibility

This is a 28-author consortium technical report from Oak Ridge National Laboratory (ORNL/TM-2026/4663, July 2026), sponsored by the U.S. DOE Office of Science, Office of Advanced Scientific Computing Research (Contract DE-SCL0000175, "A Testbed for Multi-Agent Autonomous Science"). Lead author Rafael Ferreira da Silva (ORNL) also led the original 2025 AISLE roadmap [1] and the 2024 ORNL workshop report [35] this updates, giving the revision legitimate continuity. Co-authors include Ian Foster and Kyle Chard (Globus/federated-agent middleware [8,54]), Amanda Randles, Michela Taufer, Michael Bussmann, and others with direct HPC/scientific-workflow and SDL credentials. It is CC BY 4.0 licensed with a preferred BibTeX citation. The report is candidly scoped as a community position document rather than peer-reviewed research, and it explicitly invites future rescoring. Credibility is high for what it is — a stakeholder roadmap — though its claims about the field rest entirely on the cited literature rather than new measurements.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Genuine and somewhat rare in form. It is an *accountable revision* of a specific milestone-bearing roadmap rather than another survey, and the field "currently lacks" this practice per the authors' own claim (§2.8). Elevating trust/verification and safety/governance to first-class dimensions, with the "producing a candidate discovery is no longer the hard part; verifying it is" framing, is a substantive reframing rather than repackaging.
- **Rigor:** Methodologically honest for a roadmap: every milestone is given an evidence-linked status and a target year, and a Table 1 caveat explicitly notes statuses are "provisional and subject to confirmation against the cited evidence." The cautionary case study of the corrected Szymanski/DeepMind materials result [13] — including that errors stemmed from treating an automated pipeline's output as discovery *without independent verification*, not from robotics failure — is precisely analyzed and not over-generalized. The verification/validation distinction is drawn carefully.
- **Reproducibility:** N/A as an artifact (no code/data released; it is a document), but the report is itself a model of the provenance practice it preaches, with 92 numbered citations tying each claim to a source.
- **Significance:** High and timely. It lands amid the Genesis Mission (Executive Order 14363) mobilizing U.S. national labs, a flood of industrial capital ($235M Lila, $300M Periodic Labs), and concrete trust failures (corrected Nature result, fabricated citations at NeurIPS 2025 [17]). The interoperability-fabric argument for AISLE against re-siloing is well-founded.
- **Red flags / weaknesses worth naming:**
  - **Self-assessment of own consortium's agenda.** The roadmap both proposes milestones and scores them; the authors have institutional incentive to frame AISLE as essential ("the interoperability fabric") against federal/commercial programs. The claim that top-down and bottom-up are "complementary rather than redundant" is asserted more than demonstrated.
  - **Milestone statuses are qualitative and unbenchmarkable.** "Partially achieved" vs "Open" reflect author judgment, not measured criteria; no rubric is given for what evidence would flip a status. This undercuts the "accountability" rhetoric somewhat.
  - **No coverage of competing/failed interface stacks.** MCP and A2A are treated as the consolidating winners with little discussion of ACP/ANP beyond one survey citation [69], and the report under-weights that the "skills layer" [70] it celebrates is an Anthropic-developed construct.
  - **U.S.-centric framing.** The European digression (§4) honestly admits EuroHPC networking and proprietary-model access are unevenly available, but the milestones are still written largely against U.S. mobilization.
  - **Vagueness on the two-year horizon's accountability mechanism.** It commits to "convene the community" but does not specify who verifies milestone completion or how adoption is measured, leaving the accountability loop partially open.

## Strengths

- Central thesis — the capability–reliability gap, not raw model capability, is the binding constraint — is sharp, well-illustrated, and more useful than generic "AI for science" framings.
- The verification-vs-validation distinction and the argument that the strongest discovery claims come from math/computation (machine-checkable) rather than physics/chemistry/biology is genuinely insightful.
- Honest about field failures: names the corrected flagship result, fabricated citations, undisclosed AI writing, and dual-use risk at the digital-physical interface without defensiveness.
- Two complementary figures (closed-loop architecture + autonomy-ladder timeline over 0–8/8–16/16–24 months) make the roadmap concrete and visually scannable.
- The autonomy ladder (tool/analyst/scientist) gives a graded taxonomy that locates any specific system at a rung rather than asserting wholesale autonomy — a useful antidote to hype.
- The call to measure not only discovery rate/accuracy but systems-level costs (energy per validated experiment, compute per optimization cycle, end-to-end latency, verification overhead) is forward-looking and operationalizable.

## Limitations

- **No new experiments or measurements.** Every quantitative claim is inherited from cited literature; the report adds synthesis, not evidence.
- **Milestone scoring is judgmental, not metric-driven.** No objective thresholds define "achieved" vs "partial," so the scorecard is weaker as an accountability instrument than its framing implies.
- **Short on implementation mechanics.** It names priorities (federated data mesh, zero-trust coordination, disclosure-by-construction, hardware-rooted attestation) but gives little detail on who builds, funds, or adopts them, or on incentive structures for vendor participation.
- **Two-year horizon may be too short for governance.** Cross-institution agent identity, liability frameworks, and biosecurity screening (M17–M18) typically move on policy timescales longer than 24 months; the plan's realism here is questionable.
- **Industry evidence asymmetry acknowledged but unresolved.** The report notes best-funded efforts release little peer-reviewed validation, yet its own roadmap implicitly depends on their participation without a mechanism to compel open interfaces or provenance from them.
- **Limited treatment of cost/equity.** Dependence on "a small set of proprietary frontier models" is flagged as a reproducibility and sovereignty risk, but the equity implications for resource-constrained institutions are only briefly mentioned.
- **Autonomy-ladder placement of milestones is illustrative, not derived.** The mapping of M-numbers to rungs in Figure 1(b) is asserted rather than justified by capability evidence.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How would objective, metric-driven criteria (rather than author judgment) for "achieved/partial/open" milestone status be defined, and could the scorecard be made reproducible by an independent party?
- Given that no system has end-to-end self-validated a novel discovery, what minimal verified-discovery benchmark (analogous to CORE-Bench for reproduction) would meaningfully test the scientist rung of the autonomy ladder?
- The report flags dependence on a small set of proprietary frontier models as a reproducibility risk; how far can open-weight models with transparent training provenance substitute in multi-step scientific orchestration today, and where do they break?
- For the two elevated dimensions, what concrete provenance schema (extending W3C PROV over MCP, as in PROV-AGENT [73]) must a workflow emit so that an erroneous claim can be traced as it propagates agent-to-agent across edge/cloud/HPC?
- The dual-use screening proposal (know-your-customer at the digital-physical interface, modeled on gene-synthesis self-regulation [82]) is sketched briefly — what would a workable, cross-jurisdiction enforcement mechanism look like, and who is accountable when an agent in one institution drives an instrument in another?
