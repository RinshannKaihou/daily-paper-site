How Many Tasks Are Enough for Agent Benchmark Decisions?

A Replay Analysis of Public LLM Agent Benchmarks

Wei-Jung Huang · independent researcher (US) arXiv 2607.12338 Posted 2026-07-15 Code reproducibility package ↗
llm-agents agent-evaluation partial-evaluation benchmark-reproducibility statistical-decision-rules

Key finding

A task fraction is not a decision rule: minimum sufficient budgets span 15%–90% across agent benchmarks — and one never reaches sufficiency.

◎Key metric

90%

Minimum sufficient task budget for SWE-bench Verified at the strict 0 pp threshold (5 pp grid, primary coverage-aware bootstrap).

Six-fold range across benchmarks:
AppWorld 15% · tau-bench 25% · SWE-bench Verified 90%
SWE-bench Lite ✕ never sufficient by 95%

✦Why this matters

It decides whether cheap, partial benchmark runs can be trusted — and hands anyone running, maintaining, or reading agent evaluations a concrete reporting checklist.

▲Critical edge

The tau-bench evidence rests on only 6 unordered pairs — 12 ordered comparisons from 4 leaderboard entries — with one domain excluded.

01

Methodology

The paper treats a benchmark comparison as a decision problem, not a score-estimation problem. For two systems A (tested) and B (comparison) over a fixed completed task set, each task yields paired binary outcomes with paired task difference Dᵢ = Yᵢ,ₐ − Yᵢ,ʙ. The full benchmark decides positive for A when Δ_full > δ, with δ chosen before replay from {0, 5, 10} pp.

Replay procedure. Reveal orders are permuted over the fixed completed records. At each budget point, a policy observes the in-budget subset, computes the partial paired improvement, checks task-group coverage, and either accepts, rejects, or defers (leaves the comparison unresolved). SWE-bench / AppWorld use 500 reveal orders × 200 bootstrap samples per ordered pair; tau-bench uses 2,000 × 500. The main grid runs 5%–95% in 5 pp steps.

Sufficiency criteria. A budget is sufficient only when all four targets hold simultaneously: FARcond ≤ α, FRRcond ≤ α, coverage-failure ≤ γ, and defer ≤ η. Crucially, error denominators are conditioned on the full-benchmark decision class, not on whether the policy returned a conclusion — so deferrals still count against you.

Decision targets
FARcond, FRRcond, coverage, deferral — all must pass at once. Defaults α = γ = 0.05, η = 0.25.
Coverage rule
Proportional floor per task group: repo (SWE-bench), difficulty (AppWorld), domain (tau-bench). A slack floor drops one task per group as a sensitivity check.
Policy families
Forced (uniform, group-stratified, cost-aware) must conclude; deferring bootstrap-tail policies may stay unresolved (main cutoff a = 0.05).
Comparators
Neyman allocation, Serfling finite-population bounds, paired-normal and exact McNemar tests — to separate decision-rule effects from task-allocation effects.

SWE-bench Lite meets the error-and-coverage targets at 25% but leaves 55.92% of comparisons unresolved — even at 95% budget, 27.25% remain unresolved, breaching the 25% target. Its failure mode is excessive deferral, not wrong decisions.

Threshold sensitivityOn SWE-bench Lite the sufficient budget falls from never (0 pp) to 55% (5 pp) to 35% (10 pp). tau-bench rises from 25% to 35%; AppWorld and SWE-bench Verified stay fixed at 15% and 90%.
Cheap-first trap (tau-bench)Cost-aware forced evaluation at 25% budget uses just 11.51% of tested-system cost — but suffers 100% coverage failure and wrong pairwise conclusions: false rejections in 4 of 5 positive 5 pp cases and 2 of 2 positive 10 pp cases.
Bare fraction ≠ decisionAt 25% budget, uniform forced evaluation shows 99.96% coverage failure on both SWE-bench Lite and AppWorld — a task fraction alone cannot justify a decision.
Cutoff is policy, not noiseBootstrap-cutoff sweep on AppWorld (0 pp) moves the sufficient budget from 10% (a=0.10) to 15% (a=0.05) to 25% (a=0.02): the cutoff is part of the policy specification.
Replay stabilityAcross bootstrap-count sweeps (100/200/500), the largest unresolved-comparison swing is 1.69 pp and the largest conditional decision-error rate 1.40% — still under the 5% target.
Classical tests agree… mostlyCoverage paired-normal matches bootstrap on AppWorld, tau-bench and SWE-bench Verified at all thresholds; on SWE-bench Lite it still finds no sufficient 0 pp budget by 95%. Exact McNemar nudges SWE-bench Verified from 90% to 95%.

Strengths & limitations

＋ Strengths

  • Reframes partial benchmarking as a decision problem with four simultaneous targets (FAR, FRR, coverage, deferral) instead of one error number.
  • Pinpoint per-benchmark diagnosis — Lite: excessive deferral; Verified: late resolution; tau-bench: cheap-first coverage collapse — not a single aggregate verdict.
  • Comprehensive sensitivity battery (bootstrap cutoff, replicates, orientation, grouping, coverage floor, classical tests, allocation) largely rules out "artifact of one knob."
  • Honest scoping: replay permutes a fixed completed record; it does not estimate future-task or online-stopping performance.
  • Open, seed-fixed reproducibility package with derived tables and explicit per-source record counts.

－ Limitations

  • Small tau-bench base: 12 ordered comparisons from 6 unordered pairs across 4 systems; one domain excluded (retrieval-config mismatch).
  • No cost fields for SWE-bench, so all cost-aware claims are tau-bench-only — a single grid carries that narrative.
  • Coverage limited to SWE-bench, AppWorld, tau-bench; OSWorld, WebArena, AndroidWorld, AgentBench, MiniWoB++, Terminal-Bench not analyzed (records unavailable).
  • Neyman / Serfling allocation uses completed-record variances — optimistic upper bounds, not deployable as-is.
  • Targets (α, γ, η) are illustrative comparison points, not universal recommendations; deployment calibration is left to the practitioner.
  • Single-author arXiv preprint with no disclosed peer review.
Authors & credibility

Solo work by Wei-Jung Huang, independent researcher (US), no disclosed affiliation or funding. Unusually transparent: relies entirely on public task-level records, uses official AppWorld tooling to unpack all 36 leaderboard bundles, fixes reveal-order seeds, and ships code + schemas + manifests + derived tables. Carefully distinguishes replay claims over completed records from population-level or online-stopping claims. Reproducibility repo ↗

Questions for further exploration

How do minimum sufficient budgets change under truly adaptive sequential stopping (confidence sequences, always-valid p-values) rather than fixed-grid replay?
Would variance-aware Neyman allocation with pilot-estimated (not completed-record) group variances preserve the budget reductions — or collapse back to uniform?
Do "never sufficient at 0 pp" and "90% needed" replicate on OSWorld, WebArena, AndroidWorld, AgentBench once parsed multi-system records exist?
Is η = 0.25 a reasonable default for leaderboard triage vs. procurement, and what does the cost–unresolved trade-off curve look like?
Can learned semantic or failure-mode task groupings (vs. repo/difficulty/domain metadata) cut sufficient budgets without inflating coverage failure?
How sensitive are the false-rejection patterns (4 of 5 on tau-bench) to the bootstrap tail cutoff a vs. replay-level α, given the two play different roles?
