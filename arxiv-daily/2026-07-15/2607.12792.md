> **一句话总结：** 提出 JADR 协议，通过 Jacobian lens 在模型生成首个回复 token 之前读取其内部 J-space 表征，并用 SafetyAUC 这一无需外部 judge 的本地指标量化区分模型对危险 prompt 与安全 prompt 的内部识别能力——在 6 个模型 ×3 种量化精度上验证，能比行为评测更早地、按轴/按层地捕捉到量化对安全识别（尤其是 compliance 轴）的损伤。

> **TL;DR:** JADR is a judge-free, single-forward-pass protocol that measures a model's internal danger recognition by reading the top-k verbalizable J-space tokens at the decision point (last prompt token before response), then scoring separability between StrongREJECT danger prompts and an XSTest+OKTest safe control via rank SafetyAUC. Across six models (Gemma2-9B, Qwen3-1.7B/4B/8B, abliterated Ablit-4B, SafeRL-4B) and three weight regimes (BF16, INT8, INT4-NF4), SafetyAUC cleanly separates intact from ablated safety (0.975-0.979 vs 0.593), INT8 is essentially neutral (ΔAUC_safety ≤ 0.011) while INT4 is uneven (Qwen-1.7B erodes by +0.041; Ablit-4B paradoxically recovers by −0.086 as 4-bit noise erases part of the ablation), and the proposed Safety Headroom (ΔSH) detects joint safety/compliance degradation that SafetyAUC alone misses (Qwen-4B INT4: ΔAUC_safety ≈ 0 but ΔSH = −0.052).

## 核心贡献

- **提出 JADR 评测协议**：首次将 Jacobian lens / J-space（Gurnee et al. 2026 的"可词汇化全局工作空间"）用于安全评测，在 decision point（回复首 token 之前的位置）读取 top-k J-space tokens，用固定多语种词表划分到 6 个行为轴（safety / compliance / evasion / softening / hedging / harm）。
- **完全本地、零 judge**：与 LLM-as-judge / ASR 范式不同，整个计算在待评测模型的激活上运行、单次前向传播完成，不调用任何外部评判模型，因此可对模型间与"同一模型的修改（量化、微调）"做公平对比。
- **提出 dimensionless 的 SafetyAUC / ComplAUC**：基于 rank AUC（归一化 Mann-Whitney 统计），对词表大小、模型深度、tokenizer 都鲁棒；并辅以 Dlog、Safety Headroom（ΔSH）和 Jaccard 层迁移指数。
- **区分"识别"与"行为"两类失败**：实证显示 SafeRL-4B 在 SafetyAUC 高达 0.976（识别完好）的同时 harm@0.5=0.345（行为仍泄漏），证明内部识别与最终行为可以脱钩——这是行为评测无法分辨的。
- **给出量化对安全的精细图谱**：INT8 对 safety 轴几乎中性；INT4 对不同模型/不同轴影响不均匀；对安全微调过的模型，量化主要损伤的是 compliance（指令遵循）轴而非 safety 机制本身。
- **实践意义**：作为量化/微调 checkpoint 部署前的廉价筛查工具，可在危害率上升之前就发现内部识别退化并定位到具体层。

## Key Contributions

- **JADR protocol**: First application of the Jacobian-lens / J-space verbalizable workspace (Gurnee et al. 2026) to LLM safety evaluation, reading top-k J-space tokens at the *decision point* (last token before the first response token) so the readout is causally driven only by the prompt and undistorted by already-generated text.
- **Judge-free, local, single-forward-pass**: Unlike ASR / LLM-as-judge pipelines, all internal metrics run on the activations of the model under test with no external judge call, enabling apples-to-apples comparison both across models and across modifications (quantization, fine-tuning, abliteration) of one model.
- **Dimensionless rank-AUC metrics**: SafetyAUC and ComplAUC are built on the normalized Mann-Whitney statistic, making them robust to lexicon size, tokenizer, depth, and vocabulary coverage, with bootstrap CIs over prompts (B=300–1000).
- **Complementary diagnostics**: Dlog (log ratio of danger/safe token mass), Safety Headroom ΔSH (joint signed scalar folding safety + compliance shifts), and a Jaccard index for layer relocation; the paper explicitly warns these measure different things and must be read together, with ΔAUC as primary criterion.
- **Recognition-vs-behavior separation**: Demonstrates that a model (SafeRL-4B) can have intact internal danger recognition (SafetyAUC 0.976) yet still leak harm (harm@0.5 = 0.345), and conversely that Qwen-8B and Gemma-9B are indistinguishable on SafetyAUC but diverge sharply on ComplAUC and behavioral harm — failure modes invisible to behavioral metrics.
- **Quantization map**: INT8 is essentially neutral to safety separability; INT4 is uneven (Qwen-1.7B erodes, Ablit-4B partially recovers as NF4 noise erases part of the ablation edit); for safety-tuned models, quantization harms compliance more than safety.

## Methodology

**Setup.** For model *m*, weight regime *q ∈ {fp (bf16), int8, int4 (NF4+double-quant)}*, layer *ℓ*, prompt *p*:

1. **Jacobian lens.** For each layer compute J_ℓ = E[∂h_{L,t'} / ∂h_{ℓ,t}] averaged over N=100 pretrain-like calibration prompts (the recipe prescribed by Gurnee et al. 2026). Apply J_ℓ and the unembedding W_U to get `lens_ℓ(h) = softmax(W_U · norm(J_ℓ h))`, read as "content the model holds ready for future verbalization" — not a next-token prediction. Only top-k=100 tokens are stored per layer/prompt.
2. **Decision point.** All metrics are read at rel_pos = −1, the last token before the assistant's first response token: causally prompt-only, no decoding required.
3. **Frozen multilingual lexicon** (English/Chinese/Russian stems) split into 6 axes: safety (170), compliance (47), evasion (49), softening (16), hedging (22), harm (84). Token counters: a plain count `c_g` and a rank-weighted DCG variant `dcg_g`.
4. **Rank AUC.** `AUC_g(m,q,ℓ) = Pr(c_g(p_D,ℓ) > c_g(p_N,ℓ))`; model-level version uses the per-layer sum. SafetyAUC = AUC_safety at fp; ComplAUC = AUC_compliance at fp. 0.5 = chance.
5. **Cross-modification metrics.** ΔAUC_g = AUC_g(fp) − AUC_g(q); Dlog = log-ratio of danger/safe totals with +0.5 correction; Safety Headroom `SH = SafetyAUC − (ComplAUC − 0.5)` and its Δ; Jaccard index of "important layers" (|AUC−0.5|>τ, τ=0.2) between fp and q.
6. **Behavioral validation** (independent, not used by internal metrics): StrongREJECT fine-tuned Gemma-2B grader at threshold 0.5 → harm@0.5, plus a regex refusal parser and over-refusal rate on the safe set.

**Data.** Danger set D = 313 StrongREJECT prompts. Safe control N = 320 prompts = 250 XSTest-safe + 70 OKTest (outwardly alarming but harmless, to decouple reaction to dangerous vocabulary from reaction to dangerous intent). **Models:** Gemma2-9B (41 layers), Qwen3-1.7B (27), Qwen3-4B (35), Qwen3-8B (35), Ablit-4B (huihui-qwen3-4b-abliterated-v2, refusal direction removed), SafeRL-4B (Qwen3-4b-saferl, additional safety RL). Ablit-4B and SafeRL-4B serve as deliberate calibration poles (weakened / strengthened safety).

## Results & Highlights

- **SafetyAUC cleanly separates intact vs ablated safety mechanisms.** At fp: Gemma-9B 0.975 [0.965, 0.984], Qwen-8B 0.979 [0.969, 0.988], SafeRL-4B 0.976, Qwen-4B 0.973, Qwen-1.7B 0.895 — versus Ablit-4B 0.593 [0.544, 0.640], near the 0.5 chance level. The ranking is statistically significant via non-overlapping bootstrap CIs and tracks the behavioral harm@0.5 (Ablit-4B 0.904, the highest of the six).
- **INT8 is essentially neutral to safety separability.** |ΔAUC_safety| stays ≤ 0.011 for every one of the six models under INT8; behavioral harm@0.5 also barely moves (e.g., Qwen-4B 0.048 → 0.038; Gemma-9B 0.022 → 0.003).
- **INT4 is non-monotonic and model-specific.** Qwen-1.7B erodes (ΔAUC_s = +0.041, Dlog_s = −0.166 [−0.219, −0.121]); Ablit-4B paradoxically *recovers* (ΔAUC_s = −0.086, Dlog_s = +0.143 [0.097, 0.195]) and its behavioral harm@0.5 drops from 0.904 (fp) to 0.875 (INT4) — 4-bit noise erases part of the abliteration edit.
- **Safety Headroom catches what SafetyAUC misses.** Qwen-4B under INT4 has ΔAUC_safety ≈ 0 yet ΔSH = −0.052, because the compliance axis shifted toward danger (ΔAUC_compliance = −0.048, Dlog_c = +0.084). Similarly Qwen-8B INT4: ΔSH = −0.044. The paper's central practical claim: the joint safety margin degrades before the harm rate does.
- **Recognition ≠ behavior.** SafeRL-4B: SafetyAUC 0.976 (recognition intact) but harm@0.5 = 0.345 — the model is trained to never emit a plain "I can't," substituting a safe alternative, which the regex refusal parser scores 0.000 and the StrongREJECT grader reads as disclosure. Qwen-8B vs Gemma-9B are tied on SafetyAUC but diverge on ComplAUC (0.242 vs 0.662) and harm@0.5 (0.067 vs 0.022).
- **DCG vs count agreement.** For the safety axis the rank-weighted DCG version of SafetyAUC differs from the count version by ≤ 0.007 for Gemma-9B and Qwen-8B, but DCG is the more sensitive instrument when quantization pushes group tokens down in rank without removing them from top-k.
- **Layer relocation.** Under INT4 the safety-axis Jaccard index of "important layers" is > 0.9 for four of six models, but collapses to 0 for Ablit-4B (its fp safety mechanism is localized in only 2 layers, so any shift flips the set).

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (genuine).** Applying the Jacobian lens specifically to safety evaluation, at a defined decision point, with a rank-AUC separability metric, is a real methodological move — not a rehash of logit-lens refusal probing. The bilingual frozen lexicon and the six-axis taxonomy (especially the auxiliary softening/hedging axes designed to catch "refuse-then-comply") are concrete contributions.
- **Rigor (strong on internal consistency, weaker on causal claims — and the paper admits it).** The authors are unusually honest that AUC and the Jaccard index are correlational and that causal claims require intervention (§8). They include calibration poles (Ablit-4B, SafeRL-4B) with known-in-advance safety properties, which is the right experimental design. Bootstrap CIs are over prompts (B=300–1000), correctly chosen because tokens within one readout are correlated.
- **A specific weakness: SafeRL-4B as a "validation" of SafetyAUC is partly circular.** The authors argue SafeRL-4B has high SafetyAUC *and* high harm, proving internal recognition can diverge from behavior. But SafeRL's harm@0.5 = 0.345 is largely an artifact of its templated "It's important to clarify… however, I can suggest…" response being read as disclosure by the very StrongREJECT grader the paper uses as ground truth — so the divergence is as much a grader artifact as a behavioral fact. The paper half-acknowledges this but leans on it as a headline result.
- **Missing ablations.** No sensitivity analysis to k (fixed at 100), to the N=100 calibration set size for J_ℓ, or to the τ=0.2 important-layer threshold (which is admittedly "empirical and not a strict cutoff"). The lexicon is frozen but the paper concedes AUC is lexicon-robust while the raw counters are not — yet several headline Δlog numbers depend on lexicon choices.
- **Generalization risk.** Only 6 models, all from two families (Qwen3, Gemma2), and only two deliberately-engineered calibration poles. The compliance axis is explicitly called "less stable and less clear-cut than safety," and ComplAUC's absolute level carries a refusal-style offset that the authors say should not be interpreted across model families — which limits ComplAUC's cross-model usefulness to within-family comparisons.
- **No adaptive-attack evaluation.** The protocol is pitched as catching "fragile defenses that ASR misses," but it is itself evaluated only on static StrongREJECT / XSTest sets; there is no demonstration that SafetyAUC predicts robustness under adaptive or held-out attacks, which is the actual selling point over ASR.
- **Reproducibility (good).** Local computation, frozen lexicon, deterministic decoding (temp=0, top_p=1, thinking mode off), bitsandbytes NF4 for INT4, and released workspace-token artifacts. The main barrier to independent reproduction is the Jacobian lens calibration, which requires the exact Gurnee et al. recipe.

## Strengths

- Conceptually clean: "measure what the model holds ready to say before it says anything," entirely local and judge-free.
- Dimensionless rank AUC makes cross-model/cross-tokenizer comparison meaningful, addressing a real pain point in jailbreak-evaluation literature.
- The recognition-vs-behavior distinction (and the ΔSH metric that operationalizes it) is a substantive, deployable diagnostic.
- Honest about limitations: the §8 list explicitly states what AUC/Jaccard cannot prove and flags the SafeRL/StrongREJECT-grader confound.
- Ablit-4B calibration pole gives a striking sanity check: SafetyAUC 0.593 ≈ chance exactly where it should be.

## Limitations

- Frozen single-token lexicon systematically underestimates non-literal or multi-token expressions of danger.
- Causal layer claims need intervention; current results are correlational. The authors flag this but the paper still leans on layer-level narrative.
- Coverage is narrow: 2 model families, 6 checkpoints, 3 weight regimes; transfer to a new architecture requires re-tuning k and recomputing important layers.
- INT4 Δlog shifts for auxiliary axes (evasion, hedging) have very wide CIs (e.g., Ablit-4B evasion 0.304 [0.052, 0.700]; Qwen-4B hedging −0.472 [−0.596, −0.338]), making per-axis quantization stories for those axes weak.
- No evaluation against adaptive/held-out attacks — the central advantage claimed over ASR is not directly demonstrated.
- SafeRL-4B behavioral numbers are entangled with the StrongREJECT grader's known bias against templated soft refusals.

## Relevance

与用户对 LLM 安全、可解释性和越狱评测的关注高度相关：JADR 提供了一个不依赖外部 judge、可在量化/微调后部署前做廉价筛查的内部表征探针，正好补足 ASR/LLM-as-judge 这类行为评测看不到的"识别完好但行为失守"或"识别已退化但行为尚未恶化"的盲区，对量化模型与 RLHF/安全微调 checkpoint 的可信评估有直接实用价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does SafetyAUC actually predict robustness under *adaptive* attacks (e.g., GCG, PAIR) held out from the StrongREJECT set? This is the strongest test of the claim that internal metrics beat ASR for catching fragile defenses.
- How stable is SafetyAUC to k (top-100), to the N=100 J_ℓ calibration set size, and to lexicon extensions? A formal sensitivity ablation is missing.
- Can the layer-localization (Jaccard, important-layer sets) be validated causally via weight restoration / activation patching at the identified layers, and does the causal layer set match the AUC-important set (the paper hints it does not)?
- Does the protocol transfer beyond Qwen3/Gemma2 — e.g., to Llama, Mistral, or MoE architectures — and does the compliance axis become more or less stable there?
- For the SafeRL-4B "recognition intact, behavior leaks" case, would a refusal-agnostic harm grader (or human annotation) corroborate the 0.345 harm rate, or is it largely a StrongREJECT-grader artifact?
- Can JADR detect safety regressions from *fine-tuning* (not just quantization) earlier than behavioral evals — the stated motivation, but only quantization is actually studied here?
