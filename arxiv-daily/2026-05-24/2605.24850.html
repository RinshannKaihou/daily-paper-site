LLM Evaluation · Information Theory

Bigger Models Diverge Further from Natural Language

Rényi-entropy analysis of repeated subsequences reveals that GPT-3.5 → GPT-5 shows monotonic structural drift — scaling up does not close the gap with human text.

Kumiko Tanaka-Ishii Waseda University 2026-05-24
Key Metric
R² > 0.97
Log-power fit across 100 texts/condition
Welch t-test p ≈ 0 (GPT-5 vs NL)
Why This Matters

Scaling up LLMs makes them less, not more, like natural language in long-range structure — challenging the assumption that bigger models better capture linguistic patterns.

Critical Edge

Only GPT-family tested — no open-source or non-Transformer models. Analysis is descriptive: identifies gaps but proposes no mechanism or remedy.

Natural Language
Exponents (β, γ) stay stable across 35K–600K chars — "weak universality"
GPT Trend
β and γ increase monotonically from GPT-3.5 → GPT-4o-mini → GPT-5-mini → GPT-5
GPT vs NL
GPT text is more homogeneous (tighter boxplots) but systematically shifted higher
Baseline Gap
Maximal-repetition exponent η fails to distinguish GPT-5 from natural language; this method does

Methodology

  • Counts repeated subsequences Dm = Tm − Km for all block lengths m
  • Links Dm distribution to higher-order Rényi entropies Hα(m) for α = 2, 3, 4
  • Two-stage estimation: λm from Dm/Tm, then log₂Sm with Touchard-polynomial finite-size correction ∆α
  • Compares power-law (log₂Sm ∝ mβ) vs log-power (log₂Sm ∝ (log₂m)γ)
  • 100 texts per condition: GPT-3.5turbo (~35K chars), GPT-4o-mini (~111K), GPT-5-mini (~347K), GPT-5 (~601K) + length-matched Project Gutenberg

Results & Highlights

  • Log-power consistently outperforms power-law on natural language (R² > 0.97 vs 0.90–0.96)
  • β increases with α (frequent blocks); γ decreases with α (rare patterns) — heterogeneous entropy growth
  • Short NL texts favor power-law; longer texts shift toward log-power — structural reuse grows with length
  • GPT smaller models strongly prefer log-power; larger models show mixed model preference
  • Maximal-repetition η ≈ 2+ for individual texts, approaching NL — less discriminative than β, γ

Strengths

  • Information-theoretic grounding with explicit finite-size corrections — not just empirical fitting
  • Counterintuitive core finding: larger models diverge further from NL structure
  • Four GPT generations, length-matched controls, 100 texts/condition, Welch t-tests
  • Honest baseline comparison: proposed method outperforms established maximal-repetition diagnostic

Limitations

  • GPT-family only — no Llama, Mistral, DeepSeek, or non-Transformer architectures
  • Character-level analysis misses token/word-level structural patterns
  • Intra-text only — no cross-text population-level statistics beyond sample means
  • Descriptive: does not identify which model properties drive the exponent shifts
  • Exponents vs downstream quality not explored
  • Natural language universality tested on novels only; non-narrative genres unconfirmed

Relevance

A quantitative diagnostic tool from information theory for evaluating LLM long-range structure. The finding that model scaling does not bring generated text closer to natural-language entropy growth patterns is counterintuitive and has direct implications for LLM evaluation, text generation, and language modeling research.

Open Questions

  1. Do open-source models (Llama 3, Mistral, DeepSeek) show the same monotonic exponent increase with scale?
  2. Would training with repetition-aware losses or hierarchical generation shift exponents toward natural language?
  3. How do decoding strategies (temperature, top-p, top-k) affect β and γ — can strategic decoding close the gap?
  4. Does "weak universality" hold for non-narrative genres — scientific papers, legal documents, conversation?
  5. Can Rényi-order-dependent patterns (β↑ vs γ↓ with α) be linked to specific linguistic phenomena?
  6. Would subword- or word-level analysis reveal different structural patterns?
