> **一句话总结：** 本文将 Transformer 残差流建模为深度- token 连续场，把激活修补表述为局部源插入、修补效果用灵敏度场预测、下游传播用经验格林函数刻画、修补点选择作为伴随变分逆问题；在 GPT-2 small/medium 上验证了一阶响应预测在有界线性区内有效（非线性和叠加误差主要低于 20%），prompt 诱导的残差位移可在后段层迁移答案行为。

> **TL;DR:** Proposes a continuous-depth field-theoretic framework that treats the Transformer residual stream as a depth-token field R(t,x), formulating patching as localized source insertion J(t,x), patch effects as first-order sensitivity-field predictions δy≈∫a·J, downstream propagation as empirical Green-function response G=δR/δR, and patch-site selection as an adjoint variational inverse problem. Tested on GPT-2 small and GPT-2 medium with country-capital completion prompts (e.g., "The capital of Spain is" with " Madrid" vs " London" logit difference): sensitivity-field predictions match measured patch effects within a bounded linear regime where nonlinearity ηnl and superposition ηsup errors stay mostly below a 20% threshold; prompt-induced residual displacements across 20 country-capital pairs transfer answer behavior and move target-answer ranks upward, with the effect concentrated in late layers (ℓ∗=10 in GPT-2 small, ℓ∗=22 in GPT-2 medium).

## 核心贡献

- 将 Transformer 残差流视为深度-token 连续场 R(t,x)，把激活修补、因果追踪、路径修补和转向方向统一到场论语言下。
- 把修补建模为局部源插入 J(t,x)（瞬时 δ 函数或区间支撑源），下游传播由经验格林函数 G(t,x;t∗,x∗)=δR(t,x)/δR(t∗,x∗) 描述。
- 用伴随作用量 S[R,λ] 导出反向灵敏度方程（即伴随态/costate 方程），把修补点选择重构为受约束的变分逆问题，格林函数是其核。
- 在 GPT-2 small/medium 上验证前向线性响应：存在有界线性区，一阶灵敏度 a(ℓ,x)=∂y/∂Rℓ(x) 能预测有限修补效果；测得各向异性传播；高灵敏度位点集中；切片格林算子保留主要响应能量；prompt 诱导残差位移可迁移答案行为。
- 提出跨尺度 scaling 假设：若同族模型近似共享一个潜在响应几何，则小模型的约化响应指纹 + 大模型局部锚点可定义候选修补点映射，无需估计完整大模型格林算子。

## Key Contributions

- Formulates a continuous-depth field theory for the Transformer residual stream over a depth-token lattice, unifying activation patching, causal tracing, path patching, and steering directions under one mathematical language.
- Models patching as localized source insertion J(t,x); patch effects as sensitivity-field predictions (Eq. 1, δy≈∫a·J); downstream propagation as the empirical two-point Green function G(t,x;t′,x′)=δR(t,x)/δR(t′,x′); and patch-site selection as an adjoint variational inverse problem whose kernel is G.
- Derives the adjoint action principle S[R,λ] (Eq. 16) and the backward costate equation (Eq. 17), showing the sensitivity field a(t,x) equals the adjoint field λ, so forward (G) and backward (λ=a) response are two faces of the same linear-response structure.
- Empirically validates the forward linear-response objects in GPT-2 small and GPT-2 medium: identifies a bounded local linear regime, shows first-order autograd sensitivities predict finite patch effects, measures anisotropic residual propagation, constructs sliced Green operators that survive strong reduction, and demonstrates prompt-induced residual displacements transfer answer behavior.
- Proposes a scaling hypothesis: if related models approximate a shared latent response operator, reduced response fingerprints measured in a smaller model M plus local anchors in a larger model M′ can define candidate patch-site maps without estimating the full large-model Green operator.

## Methodology

The paper builds a field-theoretic formalism and tests its forward (measurement) side empirically. The residual stream is written as a discrete field Rℓ(x)∈ℝ^d_model over layer ℓ∈{0,…,L} and token position x, with a formal continuum limit R(t,x) as L→∞. The pre-norm Transformer block is written as a residual update R_{ℓ+1}(x)=R_ℓ(x)+Δt·F_ℓ[R_ℓ](x), Δt=T/L (Eq. 5), giving the depth evolution ∂_t R(t,x)=F_t[R](x) (Eq. 7). F_t is treated as a generic nonlocal (attention) residual operator; no explicit attention kernel is required.

Patching is formulated as adding a localized source to the depth evolution (Eq. 8): J(t,x)=δ(t−t∗)δ(x−x∗)·P(R^src(t∗,x∗)−R^0(t∗,x∗)), with a projector P onto the patched subspace (attention-head output, MLP direction, learned feature direction, or full residual vector with P=Id). Interval-supported patches over a layer window use the characteristic function χ_[t1,t2] (Eq. 10).

The observable is a scalar next-token logit difference y=logit(w_target)−logit(w_ref) (Eq. 27). The first-order response prediction is δy_pred=ε·a(ℓ,x)·J (Eq. 30/37), the residual-stream form of attribution patching. Linear response is obtained by expanding R=R^0+δR and keeping first-order terms, giving ∂_t δR(t,x)=∫dy L_t(x,y)δR(t,y)+J(t,x) (Eq. 13), where L_t is the Fréchet derivative of F_t along the clean trajectory. The Green function is the fundamental solution of this linearized equation (Eq. 14).

The inverse (patch-inference) side is encoded by an action S[R,λ]=∫λ(∂_t R−F_t[R])+S_readout (Eq. 16). Stationarity δS=0 recovers forward dynamics from δS/δλ and the backward adjoint equation −∂_t λ=∫dy L_t†(y,x)λ(t,y) with terminal condition λ(T,x)=δS_readout/δR(T,x) (Eq. 17). The adjoint equals the sensitivity field, λ(t,x)=δy/δR(t,x)=a(t,x) (Eq. 18). Patch inference seeks a source J minimizing a cost C[J] subject to δy[J]=Δy⋆ and the patched dynamics (Eq. 20); in the linear regime this becomes the constrained integral equation ΔR_target(t,x)≈∫G·J with J∈C (Eq. 21).

**Empirical setup.** Experiments use capital-completion prompts. The main fixed prompt is a newline-separated list of country-capital facts ending with "The capital of Spain is", with the observable the logit difference between single-token answers " Madrid" and " London". Residual-field sweeps compare this nonrepetitive list prompt with a repetitive capital-fact variant; the sensitivity-concentration experiment uses a prose version. Prompt-displacement experiments use 20 matched same-template country-capital pairs (e.g., "The capital of Spain is" / "The capital of France is", answers " Madrid"/" Paris"). A local intervention is R_{ℓ∗}(x∗)→R_{ℓ∗}(x∗)+εJ, J∈ℝ^d_model (Eq. 28); the measured effect is δy_meas(ε,J)=y(R+εJ)−y(R) (Eq. 29). Models: GPT-2 small (site ℓ∗=10 used for displacement tests) and GPT-2 medium (site ℓ∗=22). The full residual-to-residual Green operator has formal size (L·n·d)² (Eq. 45), too large to estimate exhaustively even at GPT-2 scale, motivating sliced Green operators G_(ℓ,x)←(ℓ′,x′)(i,i′)=∂R_ℓ(x,i)/∂R_{ℓ′}(x′,i′)∈ℝ^{d×d} (Eq. 46). Error metrics: nonlinearity η_nl=|δy(ε)−εδy′(0)|/|εδy′(0)| (Eq. 34), superposition error η_sup=|δy[ε(J1+J2)]−δy[εJ1]−δy[εJ2]|/max(|δy[εJ1]|+|δy[εJ2]|,ε0) (Eq. 36), and absolute/relative prediction errors E_abs, E_rel (Eq. 35).

## Results & Highlights

- Bounded local linear regime: at a fixed layer-token site, sweeping perturbation amplitude ε across two residual directions J1, J2 and their sum, the relative nonlinearity η_nl and superposition error η_sup "remain mostly below the 20% threshold across the displayed perturbative range" (Sec. VIII, Fig. 2). This band sets the operating range for all subsequent response measurements.
- Sensitivity-field prediction of scalar patch effects: pooling sites, directions, and amplitudes, the measured δy_meas aligns with the gradient prediction δy_pred=ε·a(ℓ,x)·J along the diagonal (Fig. 3); a single autograd pass at a site predicts δy for many directions J, replacing a direction-by-direction patching sweep. Representative sweeps (Fig. 4) show good linear prediction, mixed-response, low-signal denominator artifacts, and strong nonlinear deviation cases.
- Anisotropic residual propagation: localized perturbations produce a response field ⟨‖δR‖⟩(Δℓ,Δx) that "is strongest near the source site and spreads anisotropically across depth and token offset," with the repetitive prompt producing a periodic response pattern matching the prompt period (Sec. X, Fig. 5).
- Approximate depth composition: comparing directly measured downstream response with a re-injected hand-off field at an intermediate layer ℓ_mid, the field-level composition error η_comp^R is lowest "at intermediate amplitudes" and increases when ε is too small to measure robustly or too large for linear transport (Sec. X, Fig. 6) — an empirical signature of approximate dynamical transport G(ℓ2;ℓ0)≈G(ℓ2;ℓ1)G(ℓ1;ℓ0).
- Sensitivity concentration: the site score s(ℓ,x)=‖a(ℓ,x)‖ is "highly nonuniform, with most of the mass concentrated on a limited set of layer-token sites" (Sec. XI, Fig. 7), giving a site-selection rule that reduces the intervention space.
- Sliced Green operators survive strong reduction: component-level Green slices show dominant diagonal structure (componentwise persistence) plus off-diagonal mixing (Fig. 8); sorted-entry cumulative Frobenius energy shows same-token and nearby-token slices "are structured rather than diffuse" and preserve much of the response energy (Fig. 9) without constructing the full (Lnd)² tensor.
- Prompt-induced residual displacements as transformations: for 20 country-capital prompt pairs, the displacement J_A→B at (ℓ∗=10, x∗=−1) in GPT-2 small has its scalar effect predicted by the same local sensitivity (∆y_lin=ε·a_A·J_A→B), with "median relative error remains small over the tested range" (Figs. 10–11).
- Layer localization of displacement effect: sweeping the source layer with ε=1, the toward fraction is "weak in early layers and rises sharply in later layers" in both GPT-2 small and GPT-2 medium (Fig. 12) — the displacement is effective mainly late in the computation.
- Answer-rank improvement: injecting J_A→B (ε=1) moves the target answer token B upward in the full-vocabulary rank; "in some pairs ... the patched source prompt ranks the target token as well as or better than the clean target prompt" in GPT-2 small (Fig. 14), and in GPT-2 medium "for most prompt pairs, the patched source prompt moves the target answer token to nearly the same rank as in the clean target prompt, and often to rank one" (Fig. 15) — a larger-model replication of the answer-level effect.
- Alignment of displacement direction: J_A→B has positive alignment (angles < 90°) with both the country/capital embedding-difference reference directions and the local causal gradient a_A=∂y_AB/∂R, separating the residual-space transformation from any single embedding-space proxy (Fig. 13).

## Critical Assessment

The framework is conceptually coherent and the empirical forward-side tests are a reasonable first validation, but several paper-specific weaknesses stand out. First, the inverse (patch-inference) and scaling sides — the more ambitious claims — are formulated but not empirically solved: Sec. V defines the adjoint variational inverse problem and Eq. 21, and Sec. VI defines the shared-latent response hypothesis and the intertwiner condition (Eq. 24), yet the paper explicitly frames these as "theoretical targets that motivate the framework" and provides no experiment that actually infers a patch site from a desired behavioral shift or transfers a site map across model scales. The empirical contribution is therefore restricted to the forward response objects, which largely re-express attribution patching (the paper itself notes Eq. 37 "is the residual-stream form of attribution patching") and standard causal-tracing observations in field-theoretic notation. Second, the linear regime is narrow and fragile: the authors flag that "very small |ε| can produce denominator-driven artifacts" and that large relative error can reflect either genuine nonlinearity or a low-signal denominator artifact, so the predictive claims depend on a hand-selected perturbative band rather than a principled, automatic boundary. Third, all experiments use a single narrow task family (country-capital completion) on GPT-2 small/medium, so there is no evidence the response objects generalize to broader behaviors, longer contexts, or non-autoregressive architectures; generalization "beyond GPT-2 to larger or differently-architected models is not discussed" with data. Finally, the practical advantage over existing patching methods (causal tracing, path patching, attribution patching) is not quantified against any baseline method — no head-to-head comparison of site-selection quality, prediction error, or computational cost is reported.

## Strengths

- Unifies activation patching, causal tracing, path patching, and steering under a single, internally consistent mathematical language with explicit equations (Eqs. 1–21).
- The adjoint formulation correctly connects the sensitivity field to the costate equation from continuous-depth / neural-ODE literature, giving a principled (not ad hoc) backward sensitivity.
- Sliced Green operators and sensitivity site scores are a genuinely practical reduction: they sidestep the (Lnd)² full Green tensor and still expose diagonal persistence, off-diagonal mixing, and entrywise concentration.
- Prompt-displacement experiments (Figs. 10–15) connect the field formalism to the task-vector / function-vector / activation-addition literature and show a concrete, larger-model replication of the answer-rank effect.
- Empirical protocols (Algorithms 1–2) are stated concretely enough to reproduce on GPT-2.

## Limitations

- The inverse patch-inference problem and the cross-scale scaling hypothesis are formulated but not empirically validated; they remain future work.
- The forward results overlap substantially with attribution patching, which the paper itself acknowledges; the added predictive power over attribution patching is not quantified.
- The linear regime is bounded and sensitive to denominator artifacts at small ε and to nonlinear breakdown at large ε; no automatic, principled boundary-selection method is given.
- Evaluation is confined to a single task family (country-capital completion) on GPT-2 small/medium; no broader behaviors, longer contexts, or other architectures are tested.
- No baseline comparison (vs. causal tracing, path patching, attribution patching) on site-selection quality, prediction error, or compute is reported.
- No code release is mentioned, and authorship is a two-person academic + independent-researcher team with no stated venue review.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can the adjoint variational inverse problem (Eq. 21) be solved in practice to infer a patch site from a desired behavioral shift, and how does the inferred site compare to sites found by exhaustive or attribution patching?
- Does the shared-latent response / intertwiner hypothesis (Eq. 24) hold across real model scales (e.g., GPT-2 small → medium → large, or LLaMA-family), and how many large-model calibration patches are needed for identifiability?
- How wide is the bounded linear regime on harder or longer-context tasks, and can the perturbative boundary be selected automatically from the η_nl / η_sup diagnostics rather than by hand?
- Do sliced Green operators and sensitivity site scores recovered on country-capital completion generalize to indirect-object-identification or other established mechanistic-interpretability circuits?
- Can prompt-induced residual displacements be composed or chained to realize multi-step behavioral transfers, and do they remain predictable outside the late-layer regime where they are currently effective?
