Universal Boosts, Specific Suppressors: Sparse Autoencoder Steering of Medical Vision-Language Models

Farhad Nooralahzadeh, Benjamin Gundersen, Nicolas Deperrois, Hidetoshi Matsuom, Mizuho Nishio, Thomas Frauenfelder, Ahmed Allam, Christian Blüthgen, Michael Moor, Michael Krauthammer — ETH Zurich

Sparse Autoencoder steering at inference time cuts hallucinations in medical VLMs — quality-boosting features are universal across architectures, hallucination suppressors are model-specific.

Key Metric +17.0% Relative clinical composite gain
(CheXOne, MIMIC-CXR)
Why This Matters

No-retrain hallucination reduction for radiology AI — direct patient safety impact with plug-and-play deployment.

Critical Edge

Per-model suppress discovery adds overhead. SAE coverage gaps mean some hallucination modes persist uncaught.

Methodology

Top-K SAEs trained on late layers of medical VLMs to decompose activations into interpretable feature directions.
Causal intervention framework identifies which features boost clinical accuracy vs. induce hallucinations.
At decode time, a residual steering vector from identified directions is added per-token — no weight updates.
Cross-model alignment reveals: boost directions overlap strongly (universal), suppress directions are architecture-specific.

Results

Model Dataset Composite Gain Notes
RadVLM MIMIC-CXR +5.4% Statistically significant GREEN gain
LLaVA-Rad MIMIC-CXR +7.2% Consistent across metrics
CheXOne MIMIC-CXR +17.0% Largest gain — most room for improvement
Zero-shot transfer +7.7% GREEN improvement on IU-Xray, no retraining

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Purely inference-time — no weight updates, easy to layer onto existing pipelines
  • Validated across three distinct VLM architectures
  • Universal-boost / specific-suppress insight is actionable for future research
  • Zero-shot transfer confirmed on out-of-distribution data
  • Interactive feature dashboard released for community exploration

Limitations

  • Suppress directions must be re-discovered for each new model architecture
  • Evaluated only on chest X-ray report generation; other modalities untested
  • Steering quality is bounded by SAE decomposition fidelity
  • Inference-time latency overhead not discussed

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. What is the inference-time latency overhead of per-token SAE steering on clinical deployment pipelines?
  2. Can the universal boost directions serve as a diagnostic for VLM quality during training?
  3. How does the method perform on non-chest-X-ray modalities (CT, MRI, pathology)?
  4. Are hallucination-linked features consistent across languages in multilingual medical reports?
  5. Could suppress directions be gradually retired as model quality improves?
