Next Implicit Token Prediction for LLM Pre-training

Xiangdong Zhang, Debing Zhang, Shaofeng Zhang, Xiaohan Qin, Yu Cheng, Junchi Yan arXiv 2605.24956
NITP augments standard next-token prediction with a continuous cosine-similarity loss that trains the last hidden state to predict the shallow-layer representation of the next token — combating representation degeneration with ~2% training overhead and zero inference cost.
↗ arXiv ↗ Code
Key Finding
Standard NTP's one-hot supervision leaves hidden representations under-constrained — NITP fills this geometric blind spot with dense continuous targets from shallow layers.
Key Metric
+5.7%
MMLU-Pro on 9B MoE model, with only ~2% additional training FLOPs.
Why This Matters
A simple, near-free fix to the fundamental pre-training objective that yields consistent gains across all model scales with zero inference cost.
Critical Edge
Target layer at ~20% depth is a heuristic without principled selection. MSE loss causes catastrophic divergence. Untested beyond 45B and non-Transformer architectures.

Methodology

1

Shallow layers as semantic anchors. Representations from ~20% depth act as rich contextual encoders that resolve lexical ambiguity and preserve fine-grained semantics. These are extracted with stop-gradient as implicit targets zt+1.

2

Temporal-shifted prediction. The last hidden state ht is trained via a SwiGLU projection head to predict zt+1 using cosine similarity loss. Temporal shift is critical — same-position alignment collapses into trivial solutions.

3

Combined training. Total loss: LNTP + λ LNITP with λ ≈ 0.6–1.0. Hessian analysis shows NITP introduces positive curvature in NTP's semantic null space, lifting spectral degeneracy.

Cost Profile

  • Training overhead: ~2.3% additional FLOPs, ~1.8% wall-clock
  • Projection head discarded at inference → zero inference cost
  • Perplexity preserved: validation loss on Pile virtually identical (e.g., 2.006 vs 2.006 for 3B MoE)
  • MTEB frozen representations: 23/25 tasks improved

Strengths

  • Extremely practical: ~2% training overhead, zero inference cost, easy to implement
  • Strong theoretical grounding via Hessian spectral analysis connecting representation degeneration to NTP's rank-deficient Hessian
  • Comprehensive evaluation across 6 model scales, both dense and MoE
  • Frozen-representation MTEB evaluation directly demonstrates improved representation quality
  • Important negative result: same-position alignment achieves low loss but degrades performance

Limitations

  • Additional hyperparameters (target layer, loss weight λ) require tuning
  • Untested on models >45B and non-Transformer architectures
  • Target layer heuristic (~20% depth) lacks principled selection criterion
  • Does not explore interaction with other auxiliary objectives (e.g., multi-token prediction)
  • Only evaluated on Chinese and English benchmarks

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does NITP interact with multi-token prediction (MTP) objectives — could they be combined for even greater gains?
  • Does NITP's representation regularization benefit RLHF/post-training stages by providing better initialization?
  • Can the target layer be adaptively selected during training rather than fixed at a heuristic depth?
  • How does NITP affect representation quality in multilingual or code-generation settings?
  • Does NITP change the scaling laws — does it shift the compute-optimal frontier?
