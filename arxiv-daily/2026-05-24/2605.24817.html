RouteScan: Non-Intrusive MoE Safety Auditing via Expert Routing Telemetry

Bo Lv, Zhiheng Xu, KeDong Xiu, Ruyi Ding, Tianhang Zheng, Zhibo Wang, Kui Ren — Zhejiang Univ · Donghua Univ · Louisiana State Univ

arXiv 2605.24817 ↗ 2026-05-24
Key Finding
GPU thread-allocation patterns during MoE prefilling act as discriminative fingerprints for harmful inputs — detectable without reading the prompt.
Key Metric
0.93+
AUROC cross-domain
0.9979 peak (AdvBench)
~2.7% online overhead
Why This Matters
Detect malicious prompts without reading them — safety auditing that preserves user privacy.
Critical Edge
Only tested on 7B-class MoE. Privacy is empirical, not formal. Nsight trace overhead 17.8%.

Methodology

Stage 1

Telemetry Representation

Records active GPU thread counts per expert during prefilling. Constructs fine-grained per-layer normalized load vectors and coarse-grained structural statistics (coverage rate, Shannon entropy, coverage gap, concentration) into a unified representation R.

Stage 2

Hybrid Feature Scoring

Each telemetry dimension scored by reliability ρj = discriminative evidence × cross-source stability × conservative priors. Entropy-based adaptive support auto-selects how many dimensions to retain with soft reweighting.

Stage 3

Linear Detection + Calibration

Lightweight logistic regression on selected features. Source-adaptive regularization tunes inverse-L2 strength from validation margins. Platt scaling maps raw margins to calibrated harmful-risk scores.

Results

Cross-Domain Auditing

>0.93

AUROC across AdvBench, StrongREJECT, CatHarmfulQA, HarmBench-Std (leave-one-target-out). Peak 0.9979 on AdvBench. Fixed-threshold F1 0.85–0.95.

Jailbreak Transfer

>0.96

AUROC on unseen jailbreak wrappers (PAIR, CodeChameleon) when trained with mixed harmful + AutoDAN source. Near-perfect 1.0000 on seen wrapper.

Privacy Boundary

0.16

Token F1 for telemetry-to-text inversion. Zero sensitive-field recovery. 0/54 facts via LLM semantic audit. Aggregated prefill telemetry is structurally hard to invert.

Assessment

Strengths

  • Novel side-channel approach that avoids the privacy-safety tension in content-based auditing
  • Strong cross-domain generalization without target-domain data
  • Robust transfer to unseen jailbreak wrappers
  • Careful privacy boundary analysis via multiple attack pathways
  • Lightweight linear detector — only ~2.7% online overhead
  • Evaluated across multiple model architectures, GPU types, and adversarial settings
  • Paired benign sample construction isolates semantic intent from syntactic confounds

Limitations

  • Requires Nsight profiler access; not trivially deployable in cloud API or multi-tenant GPU environments
  • Evaluated only on 7B-class MoE; scalability to production-scale models untested
  • Only operates during prefilling; cannot leverage decoding-phase or cached-inference signals
  • Non-adaptive attacker assumption; gray-box adaptive attacks remain open
  • 17.8% Nsight trace-collection overhead is significant for production serving
  • Fundamentally tied to MoE sparse routing; does not transfer to dense LLMs
  • Privacy guarantee is empirical, not formal — stronger inversions may extract more

Open Questions

  1. How would adaptive gray-box attackers with surrogate MoE models craft prompts that evade RouteScan by mimicking benign telemetry distributions?
  2. Can RouteScan scale to production MoE models with hundreds of experts, and how does detection performance change with expert count?
  3. What is the minimum telemetry granularity needed — could coarser signals suffice while further reducing privacy risk?
  4. Could prefilling telemetry combine with decoding-phase signals for higher accuracy without reintroducing per-token privacy vulnerabilities?
  5. How would RouteScan perform under distribution shift from fine-tuning or model updates?
