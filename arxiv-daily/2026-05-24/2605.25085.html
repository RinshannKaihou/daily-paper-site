Polynomial Context-Truncation Sensitivity in Autoregressive Language Models: Sequential Wyner-Ziv Bounds for KV Cache Compression

Munsik Kim (independent) — KV cache compression reformulated as sequential Wyner-Ziv source coding, revealing polynomial—not geometric—decay of truncation sensitivity.

Key Finding
Truncation sensitivity decays as a power law w−α, not geometrically—fundamentally recasting how KV cache memory should scale with target distortion.
Key Metric
α ∈ [0.37, 0.58]
Polynomial exponent across Qwen2.5 & SmolLM2 (0.5–3B), yielding tight memory bound Θ(ε−1/α).
Why This Matters
If α ≈ 0.5, doubling the cache window only halves distortion—explaining why deployed schemes need far larger windows than geometric theory predicts.
Critical Edge
All evidence from models ≤ 3B. Transfer to frontier scale (70B+) is unknown, and suffix-only bounds exclude the most practical recurrent compression schemes.
Methodology

Theoretical Framework

KV cache modeled as online compression: at each step a causal encoder maps past tokens to a compressed code, a decoder reconstructs next-token distributions from the code plus query vectors. Introduces sequential Wyner–Ziv rate function via auxiliary processes; key assumption is polynomial truncation sensitivity TV ≤ C·w−α. Four core results follow: asymptotic lower bound via three martingales, polynomial sliding-window approximation replacing classical log(1/ε), conditional suffix-only lower bound via Bayes-risk, and explicit block-Markov achievability with window n1/(α+1).

Empirical Protocol

TV distance measured between full-context and window-truncated next-token distributions using a position-preserving protocol (retained tokens keep original absolute indices). Power-law vs. exponential fits compared by log-RMSE and AIC. Positional-encoding ablation confirms genuine content forgetting (agreement to three decimals).

Models & Domains

Four models (Qwen2.5-0.5B/1.5B/3B, SmolLM2-1.7B), two primary domains (Gutenberg literary text, Python source code), plus Wikipedia and JSON for robustness. 100 prefixes per domain, windows up to w = 8192.

Results & Highlights
  • Power-law is universal. Across all four models and domains, TV truncation sensitivity follows w−α with α ∈ [0.37, 0.58]. Polynomial beats exponential by 2–4× in log-RMSE.
  • α scales with model size. In Qwen2.5: α grows monotonically (0.44 → 0.51 → 0.58 from 0.5B to 3B) — larger models recover distributions from shorter windows.
  • Natural language decays faster than code. αnat > αcode across all models, consistent with code having more persistent long-range structure.
  • Sink-plus-recent dominates random by 30–85× in median KL distortion at k = 512, confirming positional structure as the operative mechanism.
  • Exponent-doubling validated. αKL/αTV ∈ [1.93, 2.38], bracketing the predicted factor of 2 from the local quadratic KL–TV relation.
  • Power law holds to w = 8192. With 16,500-token prefixes, decay is stable across five octaves without acceleration, well past the deployed 4k regime.
Assessment

Strengths

  • Clean integration of information theory with a practically important problem.
  • Polynomial vs. geometric distinction empirically convincing across multiple consistency checks (exponent recovery, position ablation, cross-model replication).
  • Tight theoretical characterization with matching upper and lower bounds within the suffix-only class.
  • Sink-plus-recent corollary directly connects theory to deployed systems (StreamingLLM, Mistral).
  • Transparent about limitations and open problems.

Limitations

  • Models ≤ 3B only; transfer to 7B–405B scale unknown.
  • Two-sided Bayes-risk condition assumed, not empirically validated.
  • Suffix-only class excludes recurrent summaries — the most practically interesting extension.
  • No mechanistic explanation for why decay is polynomial or what determines α.
  • Code corpus small (~0.6M characters); α estimates may shift.
  • Single-channel abstraction collapses multi-layer structure.
Open Questions
  1. Does polynomial truncation sensitivity hold at frontier scale (7B–405B), and does α continue to increase with model size?
  2. Can recurrent or propagating cache summaries beat the Θ(ε−1/α) suffix-only lower bound?
  3. What architectural and training-data properties mechanistically determine α — can it be predicted a priori from depth, width, head structure, or data statistics?
  4. How does a multi-layer recursive formulation change the bounds, given that queries depend on (possibly compressed) hidden states?
  5. Does the polynomial law extend to state-space models (Mamba, RWKV) where attention-specific structure used in the proofs does not apply?
