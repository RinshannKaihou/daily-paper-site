DNNs Under Class Imbalance: First Underfit, Then Overfit the Minority Class

Tracking per-class losses across 30 imbalanced datasets reveals a two-phase failure mode—the model first ignores minority samples entirely, then memorizes them without generalizing.

Mustapha, Hasan, Olatunji & Nabus 2026-05-24 arxiv.org/abs/2605.24908
Key Metric 110 : 1 Imbalance ratio with zero degradation
when classes are well-separated (SC = 0.91)
Why This Matters

Class overlap, not just imbalance ratio, governs DNN failure on minority classes—well-separated classes stay immune even at extreme imbalance.

Critical Edge

Purely empirical—no gradient or loss-landscape theory. Only fully-connected nets tested; ROS the sole remediation benchmarked. No new method proposed.

The Two-Phase Minority Failure
Phase 1 — Early Epochs

Underfitting: Minority Loss Rises

The model classifies everything as majority. Minority TPR drops to 0% while majority class is rapidly learned. The optimizer’s gradient is dominated by the majority class.

→
Phase 2 — Later Epochs

Overfitting: Memorized, Not Generalized

The overparameterized DNN eventually fits minority samples to reach 100% training TPR—but these representations fail at test time. Test TPR remains far below test TNR.

Methodology

  • 30 binary imbalanced datasets from UCI / KEEL, IR from 1.87 (pima) to 129.44 (abalone19), plus 3 balanced Iris-derived baselines
  • Silhouette coefficients quantify class separability per dataset
  • Architecture: FC-ReLU DNN, batch norm + 0.5 dropout, 50 neurons/layer, depth via 5-fold CV (2–6 layers)
  • Adam (lr=0.001), cross-entropy, min 250 epochs, per-class TPR/TNR tracked every epoch

Results & Highlights

  • Overall training loss mirrors majority class loss—the dominant class drives optimization
  • Balanced baselines: both classes converge at the same rate, 100% train accuracy within 20 epochs
  • More minority samples (e.g. protein_homo, 1,290) avoids early underfitting even at high IR
  • Random oversampling gives only marginal TPR improvement, always at some cost to TNR

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Systematic study across 30 datasets spanning wide IR & SC ranges
  • Per-class epoch-wise tracking reveals dynamics invisible in summary metrics
  • Balanced baselines + separability analysis add valuable context
  • Clear proof that IR and overlap jointly determine severity

Limitations

  • Binary classification + FC-only; no CNNs, transformers, or multi-class
  • No theory—entirely empirical training-curve observations
  • ROS only remediation tested; SMOTE, LDAM, cost-sensitive all absent
  • Tiny minority sets (ecoli: 7 samples) limit statistical robustness

Questions for Further Exploration

Q1Does the two-phase pattern hold for ResNets, ViTs, and multi-class long-tailed benchmarks (ImageNet-LT, iNaturalist)?
Q2Can epoch-wise minority loss trajectories trigger adaptive resampling or loss re-weighting mid-training?
Q3Is the initial minority loss increase driven by gradient dominance of the majority class, or decision boundary dynamics?
Q4How do LDAM, Procrustean training, or Debiased Contrastive Learning alter these per-class trajectories?
Q5Would early stopping on per-class metrics (not overall loss) mitigate overfitted minority representations?
