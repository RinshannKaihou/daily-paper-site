Mechanistic Interpretability

Representation Without Control: Testing the Realization Effect in Language Models

Ciarán Walsh & Emilio Barkett — Columbia University
A model can linearly decode a cognitive state at 93% accuracy — yet steering along that same direction produces zero behavioral change. Readability is not causality.
Key Metric
93% → 0 effect
Readout accuracy at layer 18 on held-out prompts; steering deltas median = 0 across all scales.
Why This Matters
Challenges the field's core inferential leap: finding a linearly decodable feature does not mean the model causally relies on it.
Critical Edge
Single model (Gemma 3 4B), single layer (18), single construct. Positive-control task had a duplicated-instruction bug. No bootstrap CIs on steering nulls.
Methodology
Behavioral Probe

54,450 rows across 25 models, 11 conditions. OLS regression with model/temperature/prompt fixed effects and HC3 robust SEs. Tested whether models reproduce the human realization effect in risk-taking.

Activation Readout

Extracted Gemma 3 4B residual-stream activations. Mean-difference direction (μrealized − μpaper) from 756 training pairs at layer 18. Validated on original held-out + DeepSeek-generated set (40 pairs).

Activation Steering

Applied the layer-18 direction via forward hooks at scales −50 to +150 on 648 behavior prompts. Compared steered vs. unsteered wager and risk-profile outcomes. Ran classification steering as positive control.

Results
Behavioral sensitivity present, direction wrong

Models respond to outcome framing but reverse the human pattern: realized losses increase wagers instead of decreasing them.

Readout succeeds cleanly

Layer-18 direction achieves 89–93% correct-direction classification on validation splits and 80.6% on behavior-evaluation prompts. Mean projection deltas consistently positive.

Projection–behavior link is negligible

Within matched pairs, projection deltas do not predict wager deltas (Pearson r = 0.072) or risk deltas (r = 0.001). Raw associations confounded by prompt structure.

Steering fails at every scale

Mean wager deltas small and positive; median deltas are zero at all scales. Negative scale (−50) produces no sign-symmetric reversal. Classification steering moves predictions only from 14.0% to 19.8% (near chance).

Assessment

Strengths

  • Clean experimental design grounded in a well-characterized behavioral economics phenomenon
  • Transparent, thorough reporting of null results — scientifically valuable
  • Train-only direction evaluated on independent held-out data is a strong generalization test
  • Within-pair analysis controlling for prompt structure is the right comparison
  • Clearly articulates correlational readout vs. causal control distinction

Limitations

  • All mechanistic results specific to Gemma 3 4B, layer 18, mean-difference direction — no layer sweep or multi-layer steering
  • Hosted models (Qwen, etc.) inaccessible for mechanistic analysis
  • Constrained numeric outputs may amplify format/parsing artifacts
  • DeepSeek held-out set is small (40 pairs) and single-source
  • No bootstrap or randomization intervals for steering deltas
  • Decoded direction may track broad semantic framing rather than the decision-relevant variable
Open Questions
  1. Would a systematic layer sweep (layers 14–22) or multi-layer steering schedule break the dissociation?
  2. Does the readout–control gap replicate across other architectures (Qwen, Llama) under local hook frameworks?
  3. Are numeric output tasks uniquely resistant to steering, or does the dissociation extend to free-text generation?
  4. Could contrastive directions (logistic probes, PCA) yield stronger causal control than mean-difference?
  5. What does the 90% readout vs. 0% control gap imply about representation geometry in transformer residual streams?
