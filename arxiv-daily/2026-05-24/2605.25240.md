> **一句话总结：** JudgmentBench 发布了 30 个真实法律任务、由执业律师配对标注的 1,539 条 rubric 评分与 1,530 条成对偏好判断，证明成对比较（comparative judgment）在恢复构造的质量排序上远优于 rubric 打分（Spearman ρ = 0.908 vs. 0.150，Δ = 0.758 [0.494, 1.021]），且标注时间约为 rubric 的 40%；该结论对人类标注者与 LLM 自动评分器均成立。

> **TL;DR:** Releases JudgmentBench, 30 real-world legal tasks from Harvey's BigLaw Bench with 1,539 rubric scores and 1,530 pairwise preference judgments elicited from the same practicing attorneys on the same items (~$242,000 billable-hour equivalent, 242 h). Using Claude Opus 4.6 outputs at three constructed quality levels (excellent/good/intermediate, N=50 variants per task-quality cell), comparative judgments recover the intended quality ordering substantially better than expert-developed rubrics for human annotators (mean Spearman ρ R̄_CJ = 0.908 vs. R̄_rub = 0.150, Δ = 0.758 [0.494, 1.021], B=2,000 hierarchical bootstrap) while requiring less than half the annotation time (median 1.92 min CJ vs. 4.74 min rubric). The pattern holds for LLM autograders: GPT-5.4 mean task-level Spearman vs. human orderings is 0.375 for CJ vs. 0.200 for rubrics, and GPT-5.4-mini is 0.611 vs. 0.242.

## 核心贡献

- 发布 JudgmentBench：首个在高专业度领域（法律）中，由同一批执业律师对同一组输出同时提供 rubric 评分与成对偏好判断的公开数据集，使两种评估方法的直接比较成为可能。
- 30 个来自 Harvey BigLaw Bench 的真实法律任务（16 个交易类、14 个诉讼类），配对 1,539 条 rubric 评分和 1,530 条成对偏好判断，标注成本约 242 小时、折合约 $242,000 计费工时。
- 直接实证比较：成对比较在恢复构造质量排序上显著优于 rubric（R̄_CJ=0.908 vs. R̄_rub=0.150，Δ=0.758 [0.494, 1.021]），且标注时间约为 rubric 的 40%。
- 结论对人类标注者与 LLM 自动评分器（GPT-5.4、GPT-5.4-mini）均成立，对执业经验水平、任务类别、标注时间等子集稳健。
- 配对数据结构支持更广泛的研究议程：如何在高专业度、无客观真值的领域 elicited、aggregate 并将专家判断用作监督信号。

## Key Contributions

- Releases JudgmentBench, the first publicly available dataset in a high-expertise domain in which both rubric scores and pairwise preference judgments are elicited from the same practicing attorneys on the same items — the precondition for any clean comparison between the two methodologies.
- 30 real-world legal tasks sampled from Harvey's BigLaw Bench (16 transactional, 14 litigation), annotated by experienced attorneys (including at major U.S. law firms) with 1,539 rubric scores and 1,530 pairwise preference judgments over 2,274 unique outputs; equivalent to ~$242,000 in billable attorney time (242 h 15 min at $1,000/h).
- A direct empirical comparison using LLM outputs (Claude Opus 4.6) at three constructed quality levels: comparative judgments recover the constructed quality ordering substantially better than rubrics (R̄_CJ = 0.908 vs. R̄_rub = 0.150, Δ = 0.758 [0.494, 1.021], B=2,000 hierarchical bootstrap 95% CI) while requiring less than half the annotation time (median 1.92 min CJ vs. 4.74 min rubric).
- The result holds for both human annotators and LLM autograders (GPT-5.4: 0.375 CJ vs. 0.200 rubric; GPT-5.4-mini: 0.611 CJ vs. 0.242 rubric, mean task-level Spearman vs. human orderings), and is robust across annotator experience, task category, and annotation-time subsets.
- The paired structure supports a broader research agenda on how expert judgment should be elicited, aggregated, and used as supervision (e.g., reward modeling, DPO) in domains without verifiable ground truth.

## Methodology

**Base dataset.** 30 tasks sampled from Harvey's BigLaw Bench (Harvey Team, 2024), a privately held dataset of 100 (prompt–documents–rubric) triplets developed by formerly practicing BigLaw attorneys to reflect hypothetical, representative litigation and transactional work products. Each triplet contains a prompt (legal task instruction), optional documents (attachments), and an expert-developed rubric with affirmative criteria (points for structure/style/substance) and negative criteria (subtractions for hallucinations/irrelevant material). The released 30-task sample (16 transactional, 14 litigation) was curated to balance perceived complexity, practice area, and task type.

**Constructed quality levels.** For each task, three work products were generated using Anthropic's Claude Opus 4.6 at three quality levels (excellent, good, intermediate) via a system prompt with 6 quality dimensions: analytical depth, precision, completeness, reasoning clarity, judgment, and nuance — each set to excellent/good/intermediate to induce variation. Reference documents were chunked and embedded with the Qwen-3 embedding model (Zhang et al., 2025); the top 10 chunks most similar to the task prompt were retrieved and included in context. A practicing attorney with 30+ years of transactional-law experience reviewed the approach over two iterations and confirmed prompt-induced variation realistically reflects associate work-product variation. To reduce sampling variation, N=50 independent output variants were generated per task–quality-level cell, randomized at the annotator level (two annotators may see different representations of an "excellent" output, but outputs are consistent within annotator). An LLM-as-a-Judge validation: OpenAI's GPT-5.4 preferred the excellent output to the good output 69/100 times and the good output to the intermediate output 67/100 times in a within-task pairwise setup.

**Annotation.** Two sources of expert lawyers: (1) 11 attorneys at two large U.S. law firms contributing through pro bono programs; (2) practicing attorneys recruited through Snorkel AI. Reported experience: 3–35 years, median 10; 62.7% with 8+ years (29.4% 8–11 y; 13.7% 12–15 y; 5.9% 16–19 y; 13.7% 20+ y); 37.3% senior associates, 11.8% partners, 9.8% counsel. Practice expertise concentrated in Litigation (70.6%), Transactions (62.7%), Regulatory (43.1%), Labor & Employment (33.3%) — multiple selections allowed. Each lawyer was assigned a pipeline of all 30 tasks in random order; for each task, one output variant per quality level was sampled, yielding three outputs assessed under two protocols: rubric scoring (score each of excellent/good/intermediate against the expert rubric) and comparative judgment (pairwise preference over the three unique pairs). The pipeline alternated methodologies across successive tasks (annotator did either rubric or CJ per task, not both), initialized randomly. Annotators could skip tasks outside their expertise but could not skip a methodology. They were encouraged to spend ≤20 min per task. Final dataset: 1,539 rubric scores and 1,530 pairwise preference judgments over 2,274 unique outputs; 242 h 15 min total (excluding 56 records with recorded times >30 min).

**Primary estimand.** D = R̄_CJ − R̄_rub, where R̄_CJ is the task- and annotator-averaged Spearman's rank correlation between (i) constructed rankings and (ii) rankings implied by fitting a Bradley-Terry model to annotators' comparative judgments, and R̄_rub is the task- and annotator-averaged Spearman's rank correlation between (i) constructed rankings and (ii) rankings derived from the average rubric score. Positive D favors CJ. Statistical significance assessed via B=2,000 hierarchical bootstrap iterations with 95% normal-theory CIs centered on the observed estimate.

**Autograders.** GPT-5.4 and GPT-5.4-mini performed rubric-based scoring and comparative judgments on the same set of completed task-method combinations from the human annotation process; prompts in Appendix B.3.

## Results & Highlights

- Human annotators — main recovery (Fig. 2): R̄_CJ = 0.908 vs. R̄_rub = 0.150, Δ = 0.758 [0.494, 1.021] (B=2,000 hierarchical bootstrap, 95% normal-theory CI). The advantage materializes despite CJ requiring much less time.
- Annotation time (Fig. 2): median 4.74 minutes per task for rubrics vs. 1.92 minutes for comparative judgments — CJ is roughly 40% of the rubric protocol's time cost, materially changing the cost-benefit calculus for expert-annotated benchmarks.
- Robustness: the performance difference persists across varying levels of annotator experience, task category, annotator experience, and annotation time (Appendix Table 4), and holds for pairs of adjacent quality levels only (Appendix Table 5). The advantage appears more consistent among highly experienced attorneys (Appendix Fig. 4), though the authors are cautious about over-interpreting this gradient.
- Rubric-score distribution check (Fig. 5): the relatively weak rubric performance is NOT explained by an inability to measure finer nuances (e.g., all outputs receiving generally high scores); positive rubric scores are widely distributed over outputs across the full range of achievable scores.
- LLM autograders (GPT-5.4): rankings following comparative judgments better recover constructed quality rankings both overall and within task-category subsets (Appendix Table 4). Mean task-level Spearman's rank correlation between human and GPT-5.4 autograder-implied quality orderings is 0.200 for rubrics vs. 0.375 for comparative judgments.
- Smaller autograder (GPT-5.4-mini): mean task-level Spearman's rank correlation between human and autograder-implied quality orderings is 0.242 for rubrics vs. 0.611 for comparative judgments — the CJ advantage is even larger for the smaller model.
- Constructed-quality validation: GPT-5.4 preferred the excellent output to the good output 69/100 times and the good output to the intermediate output 67/100 times in a within-task pairwise setup, confirming the outputs are qualitatively distinguishable.
- Annotation investment: 1,539 rubric scores + 1,530 pairwise preference judgments over 2,274 unique outputs for 30 tasks, totaling 242 h 15 min of attorney time (~$242,000 billable equivalent at $1,000/h).

## Critical Assessment

The paired within-expert, within-item design is methodologically strong and the dataset fills a genuine gap (no prior public dataset elicits both rubric and preference signals from the same experts on the same items in a high-expertise domain). However, several paper-specific weaknesses qualify the headline result. First, the "ground truth" is constructed: the three quality levels (excellent/good/intermediate) are induced by a Claude Opus 4.6 system prompt varying 6 quality dimensions, and the authors themselves acknowledge in Appendix A.1 that "prompt-induced variation could differ from naturally occurring variation in human work product in important respects." The entire comparison presupposes that the constructed ordering is the correct target — if the construction is flawed or artificially separable, the recovery metric measures sensitivity to the construction rather than to genuine legal quality. Second, the rubric performance (R̄_rub = 0.150) is strikingly low — near zero — which raises the question of whether the expert-developed BigLaw Bench rubrics are being fairly represented; the paper does not report inter-rater reliability for the rubric protocol separately, nor does it benchmark against alternative rubric designs or rubric-scoring aggregation schemes (e.g., weighted vs. unweighted). Third, the 30-task scope is modest and single-domain (legal); the authors explicitly note "generalization beyond chest radiography"-style concerns analogously — here, generalization to other high-expertise domains (medicine, finance, coding) with different quality structures is untested. Fourth, the comparative-judgment advantage is not free: CJ collapses to a single ordinal preference and cannot provide per-criterion diagnostic information, which the authors concede ("rubrics are weaker as a summary of overall quality ... but they remain valuable as an analytical instrument") — so the practical recommendation is nuanced, not a wholesale endorsement of CJ. Fifth, the autograder correlations with human orderings are low in absolute terms even for CJ (GPT-5.4: 0.375; GPT-5.4-mini: 0.611), suggesting LLM autograders remain weak proxies for expert legal judgment regardless of protocol. Sixth, the paper does not attempt the downstream training comparison (rubric vs. preference as supervision for post-training) that the dataset is structured to support — it explicitly defers this — so the practical implications for reward modeling are unvalidated.

## Strengths

- First public paired rubric + preference dataset from the same domain experts on the same items in a high-expertise domain — a genuinely novel resource.
- Clean within-expert, within-item design with constructed quality levels providing a methodology-independent target ordering for the construct-validity comparison.
- Large expert-annotation investment (~$242,000 billable equivalent) from practicing attorneys at major U.S. law firms, with reported experience demographics.
- Statistically rigorous primary analysis (B=2,000 hierarchical bootstrap, 95% CIs) with robustness checks across annotator experience, task category, annotation time, and adjacent quality levels.
- Cross-protocol consistency: the CJ advantage holds for both human annotators and two LLM autograders (GPT-5.4, GPT-5.4-mini).
- Public release of dataset (HuggingFace) and code (GitHub), enabling follow-up research on elicitation, aggregation, and supervision.
- Nuanced discussion: the authors do not overclaim — they explicitly note CJ cannot replace rubrics for per-criterion auditing and frame rubrics as still valuable as an analytical instrument.

## Limitations

- Constructed (prompt-induced) quality ordering is the evaluation target; the authors acknowledge it may differ from naturally occurring variation in human legal work product (Appendix A.1).
- Rubric performance near zero (R̄_rub = 0.150) is surprising and may reflect properties of the specific BigLaw Bench rubrics or their scoring/aggregation rather than rubrics in general; no alternative rubric designs or aggregation schemes are benchmarked.
- 30 tasks, single domain (legal); generalization to other high-expertise domains (medicine, finance, coding) or to continuous (non-discrete) quality distributions is untested.
- CJ collapses to ordinal preference and provides no per-criterion diagnostic information; the time/accuracy advantage must be weighed against the loss of auditable, decomposable signal.
- LLM autograder correlations with human orderings are low in absolute terms (GPT-5.4 CJ: 0.375; GPT-5.4-mini CJ: 0.611), so autograders remain weak proxies for expert legal judgment.
- The downstream training comparison (rubric vs. preference supervision for post-training) is explicitly deferred; implications for reward modeling/DPO are unvalidated.
- Inter-rater reliability for each protocol is not reported as a separate headline metric, though the bootstrap captures cross-annotator variance.

## Relevance

直接对应「LLM Inference Evaluation」方向：JudgmentBench 系统比较了 rubric 打分与成对偏好判断两种评估方法在高专业度法律任务上的表现，为 LLM 评估方法论（尤其是人类偏好信号采集、自动评分器校准、reward 信号构造）提供了实证依据；其专家标注配对数据结构也对「Self-Evolving Agents & Automated Research」中如何把专家判断用作无客观真值领域的监督信号有参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the CJ-over-rubric advantage replicate in domains with more objective ground truth (e.g., math, code), or with continuous rather than 3-level constructed quality variation?
- How sensitive is the near-zero rubric recovery (R̄_rub = 0.150) to rubric design and aggregation — would weighted, rubric-tree, or LLM-mediated rubric scoring close the gap?
- Can a hybrid protocol (rubric as scaffolding that informs comparative judgments, or rubrics derived retroactively from CJ) combine the diagnostic depth of rubrics with the recovery accuracy of CJ?
- How do the paired rubric and preference signals differ as supervision for post-training (RLHF/DPO vs. rubric-as-reward), especially in judgment-rich domains where reference answers are contested?
- How does annotation cost scale with the number of items under comparative judgment (O(n²) pairs), and can active-ranking or Bradley-Terry sampling reduce the cost while preserving recovery?
- What is the inter-rater reliability for each methodology separately, and how does it interact with the recovery advantage — are CJ's gains driven by higher agreement, lower cognitive load, or better capture of tacit holistic dimensions?
