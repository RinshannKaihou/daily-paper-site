Beyond the Target: From Imitation to Collaboration in Speculative Decoding

Jinze Li · Yixing Xu · Guanchen Li · Jinfeng Xu · Shuo Yang · Yang Zhang · Xuanwu Yin · Dong Li · Edith C.H. Ngai · Emad Barsoum AMD & HKU & UNT 2605.24793
CoSpec turns every draft–target mismatch into a collaboration opportunity: an RL-trained arbitrator learns when the smaller draft model actually knows better than the target, accepting its tokens selectively. The result — 2–4× faster inference that surpasses target-only accuracy across math, code, and QA.

Why This Matters

Draft models can be collaborators, not approximators — unlocking both faster inference and better quality from the same hardware.

Critical Edge
Not distribution-preserving • +32 GB memory overhead (174 vs 142 GB)
Speedup degrades to 1.2× at batch=64 • ~400 GPU-hrs training cost
Cannot repair double-failure cases where both draft & target fail

Methodology

CoSpec retains standard speculative decoding: a small draft model proposes K tokens; the target verifies in one teacher-forced pass. The innovation is at mismatch positions.

Arbitration Policy

  • Input: prompt + verified prefix + draft block + target tokens, separated by [SEP] tokens
  • Hybrid attention mask: bidirectional within draft–target region, causal for context
  • Backbone: draft model + LoRA adapters (rank 16) + binary classification head (accept/reject per position)

Two-Stage Training

  • Stage 1 — SFT warm-up: Run vanilla SPD on OpenR1-Math-220k, label mismatches with Qwen3-30B-A3B judge, train with soft BCE
  • Stage 2 — RL optimization: 12 rollouts per prompt. Shaped reward: correct rollouts earn proportional to accepted span length; incorrect rollouts penalized by target-surprisal + rejection cost. GRPO-style group-normalized advantages with sign rebalancing

Inference

  • Matched tokens: force-accepted
  • At mismatch: accept draft if πθ(accept) > λ (default 0.6)
  • Round stops at first rejected mismatch

Serving Throughput (vLLM)

  • 1.22–2.18× throughput improvement depending on request rate
  • +4.8% time-to-first-token overhead
  • 174 GB serving memory (vs 142 GB target-only)
  • Complementarity recovery: 50–65% of oracle union gap recaptured

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Elegant reframing from imitation to collaboration with formal complementarity gap proposition
  • Consistent gains across model families, temperatures, and benchmarks
  • Strong zero-shot transfer suggests domain-invariant interaction patterns
  • Comprehensive ablations isolate each design choice's contribution
  • Compute controls prove gains come from arbitration, not extra training

Limitations

  • Not distribution-preserving — unsuitable when exact target samples or log-likelihoods are needed
  • +32 GB memory overhead significant for constrained deployments
  • Cannot repair double failures where both draft and target fail identically
  • Speedup degrades to 1.2× under high-throughput batch=64 serving
  • ~400 GPU-hours training cost plus judge model dependency
  • Only tested on same-family draft/target pairs with shared tokenizers

Open Questions

  1. Can the arbitrator be shrunk to a lightweight MLP to cut the 32 GB overhead while preserving quality?
  2. How does CoSpec perform with cross-family draft/target pairs (e.g., Qwen draft + LLaMA target)?
  3. Does the complementarity gap survive with a much weaker draft (e.g., 1B draft for 70B target)?
  4. Can arbitration extend to multi-candidate settings where >2 models propose tokens?
  5. Are CoSpec gains orthogonal to quantization, pruning, or KV-cache compression?
