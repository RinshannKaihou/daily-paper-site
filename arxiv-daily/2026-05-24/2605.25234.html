On the Epistemic Uncertainty of Overparametrized Neural Networks

David Rügamer LMU Munich & MCML 2605.25234 2026-05-24
In overparametrized one-hidden-layer ReLU networks, parameter-space epistemic uncertainty persists even when the function is fully identified — arising from permutation symmetries and neuron splitting manifolds whose coefficients follow a symmetric Dirichlet distribution. 本文从参数不可辨识性视角系统分析过参数化ReLU网络的后验结构，证明即使底层函数被完全识别，参数空间的不确定性仍持续存在。
Key Finding
Single-chain MCMC almost surely explores only one of M! posterior modes — parameter uncertainty cannot vanish regardless of data volume.
Key Metric
0
Prediction RMSE → 0 with data, yet parameter covariance remains non-degenerate
Why This Matters
Challenges a foundational assumption — Bayesian DL's epistemic uncertainty does not vanish, theoretically justifying deep ensembles.
Critical Edge
Single-layer ReLU, no biases. No real-world data. Dirichlet result hinges on Gaussian/L₂ priors only.
Methodology

Setup

Single-hidden-layer ReLU networks f(x) = w₂ᵀ φ(W₁x) with M hidden units, L₂ regularization. Assumes a true function representable by M* < M neurons. Studies the gap between function-space and parameter-space identifiability.

Overparametrized Case (M > M*)

An assignment map partitions model neurons into groups assigned to each true neuron. Splitting coefficients (cₘ) lie on a simplex within each group. Tube-conditioning defines posteriors on zero-measure manifolds, yielding exact Dirichlet distributions Dir(α,…,α) with α = (p+1)/2.

MCMC Confinement

Under permutation-equivariant, Lipschitz-continuous updates (covering standard samplers), chains initialized in a permutation chamber's interior almost surely never cross the measure-zero boundaries. Each chain sees only one posterior mode.

Deep Ensemble Justification

Deep ensembles, initialized at different random points, land in different permutation chambers — accidentally recovering the correct permutation-mixture posterior in the correctly-specified, large-data limit. A feature, not a bug.

Results
Proposition 1: Posterior may concentrate on a single function equivalence class while parameter covariance remains non-degenerate or diverges.
Theorem 1: Overparametrized posterior moments satisfy 𝔼[ωₘ] = μ·ω* and individual neuron contributions shrink as M⁻¹ᐟ² while joint splitting-coefficient posterior stays non-degenerate.
Corollary 3: Single-chain MCMC almost surely confined to one mode — ensembles are theoretically necessary for full posterior coverage.
Empirical: RMSE → 0 with n, but tr(Cov(w|Dₙ))/d remains constant due to permutation invariance; restricting to one mode eliminates residual variance.
Dirichlet validated: Empirical splitting coefficients closely match theoretical Beta(α, (k−1)α) marginals across (n, M) combinations.
Continual learning: Non-identifiability distorts parameter importance estimates → overly restrictive updates; correction improves adaptation.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Complete theoretical characterization of both discrete and continuous non-identifiability
  • Assignment map + splitting coefficient framework gives an intuitive geometric picture
  • Surprising result that single-chain MCMC cannot explore all posterior modes
  • Provides theoretical justification for deep ensembles — a popular heuristic
  • Thorough empirical validation: convergence, Dirichlet marginals, moment matching, switching behavior

Limitations

  • Single-hidden-layer ReLU without biases; deeper architectures are future work
  • Dirichlet result depends on Gaussian prior / L₂ regularization; other priors unexplored
  • Limited practical guidance for MC dropout or variational inference
  • Function-space vs parameter-space distinction clear, but when does parameter uncertainty actually harm downstream tasks?
  • Synthetic experiments only; no real-world datasets or architectures
Open Questions
How do layer-wise non-identifiabilities compound in multi-layer networks — do Dirichlet-like splitting laws emerge?
Can the confinement result guide multi-chain or temperature-based sampling to efficiently cover all modes?
What happens under non-Gaussian priors (e.g., sparsity-inducing) — does the splitting manifold structure change fundamentally?
Can splitting coefficient characterization serve as a diagnostic for detecting overparametrization in trained models?
