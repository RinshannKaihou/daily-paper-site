CAML fixes the loss valley at the root of PINN failure

PINN gradient pathology stems from a loss valley — a connected manifold of global minimizers created by PDE operator non-uniqueness. CAML (Constraint-Aligned Loss with Manifold Lifting) reformulates zeroth-order terms as aligned constraints with a delay factor, collapsing the valley and aligning gradients.

Why This Matters
PINNs underpin scientific computing but are notoriously hard to train. CAML fixes the root cause of gradient pathology — not just the symptoms — making PDE solving reliably converge.
Critical Edge
Requires zeroth-order terms in the PDE. Pure derivative-based problems may not benefit. Only validated on 2D steady-state PDEs — scaling to 3D or time-dependent regimes is unproven.

Methodology

1. Loss Valley Theory

Theorem 4.1 proves that PDE operator non-uniqueness creates a connected manifold of zero-residual minimizers in function space. This maps to a highly distorted non-convex manifold in parameter space — the "loss valley" — with Hessian condition numbers exceeding 10²². The optimizer enters the valley (Phase I) but then struggles to navigate along it (Phase II) due to gradient conflict between PDE residual and boundary conditions.

2. Aligned Constraints

A learnable additive constant c is introduced into all zeroth-order terms of both the PDE residual and boundary conditions. This converts the problem into a controlled ill-posed formulation that enlarges the intersection between admissible solution sets. For linear zeroth-order terms, c has a closed-form solution; for nonlinear terms, Newton's method or gradient descent is used at each step.

3. Delay Factor

A piecewise-linear schedule λ(t) gates the PDE residual loss: zeroed initially, then ramped linearly to 1. This lets the optimizer first approach the boundary-admissible affine subspace before entering the loss valley, shortening the optimization path.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • First rigorous geometric characterization of PINN gradient pathology
  • Elegant — a single scalar c enlarges the admissible set without changing architecture
  • Consistent gains across 4 PDE types, 3 architectures, 5 baselines
  • Architecture-agnostic; composable with other PINN improvements
  • Open-sourced code; published at ICML 2026

Limitations

  • Requires zeroth-order terms — pure derivative PDEs may not benefit
  • Delay schedule introduces two tuning hyperparameters (td, tr)
  • Only 2D steady-state problems tested; no 3D or time-dependent evidence
  • Auxiliary c optimization overhead not benchmarked

Open Questions

  1. How does CAML scale to time-dependent PDEs where the loss valley structure may differ?
  2. Can the aligned constraint idea extend beyond additive constants to multiplicative or affine invariances?
  3. What is the per-step overhead of the auxiliary c optimization for nonlinear PDEs?
  4. How does CAML interact with operator-learning architectures (FNO, DeepONet)?
  5. Is there a theoretical bound on the condition-number improvement from aligned constraints?
