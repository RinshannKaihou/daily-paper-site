Learning, Fast and Slow: Towards LLMs That Adapt Continually

arXiv: 2605.12484

Date: 2026-05-12

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.12484

一句话总结： 本文提出 Fast-Slow Training (FST) 框架，将 LLM 的模型参数视为"慢"权重、优化后的文本上下文视为"快"权重，通过交替执行 RL 参数更新和 GEPA 提示进化，在推理任务上实现最高 3 倍的样本效率提升，同时将 KL 散度降低最多 70%，有效缓解灾难性遗忘并保持可塑性。
TL;DR: FST jointly optimizes LLM parameters ("slow" weights via CISPO/RL) and a population of evolved textual prompts ("fast" weights via GEPA), achieving up to 3x sample efficiency, higher performance asymptotes, 70% less KL divergence from the base model, preserved plasticity, and effective continual learning across sequential tasks.

核心贡献

  • 提出 Fast-Slow Training (FST) 框架，将 LLM 学习分解为"慢"权重（模型参数 RL 更新）和"快"权重（文本提示进化），两者交替协同优化
  • 利用 GEPA 的反思式进化提示优化，使"快"权重从文本反馈（包含思路、工具调用、错误等丰富信号）中快速吸收任务知识，突破 RLVR 的 1-bit-per-episode 信息瓶颈
  • 维护 Pareto 前沿的提示种群（而非单一最佳提示），在 RL 训练中均匀采样，使组相对优势同时混合提示变异和采样变异
  • 在 CodeIO、Math (Polaris)、HoVer-hard 三个任务上，FST 分别以 3.0x、1.4x、3.0x 更少的步数达到 RL 的峰值性能，且渐近性能更高
  • FST 训练的模型 KL 散度降低最多 70%，在塑性探测实验中 RL-init 在新任务上近乎崩溃（~0%），而 FST-init 接近 base-init 参考水平

Key Contributions

  • Introduces a dual-speed learning framework that treats model parameters as "slow" weights (updated via CISPO/RL) and textual context as "fast" weights (updated via GEPA reflective prompt evolution)
  • Fast weights absorb task-specific information from rich textual feedback (thoughts, tool calls, errors), overcoming the 1-bit-per-episode limit of scalar RL rewards
  • Maintains a Pareto-frontier population of K complementary prompts rather than a single best prompt, allowing group-relative advantages in RL to mix prompt-induced and sampling-induced variation
  • Achieves 1.4–3x sample efficiency improvement over RL across CodeIO, Math, and HoVer-hard tasks, with higher fitted performance asymptotes (+2.9 to +7.7 pp)
  • Reduces KL divergence from base model by up to 70%, preserving plasticity — FST-init models adapt effectively to new tasks while RL-init models collapse to near 0%
  • Demonstrates continual learning over 3 sequential tasks (HoVer → CodeIO → Physics): FST reaches near-peak in every stage while RL stalls on CodeIO at only +2.5pp gain vs FST's +19.6pp

Methodology

The framework models slow weights as parameters θ and fast weights as textual prompts ϕ ∈ Σ*. The joint objective maximizes expected reward over both:

$$\max_{\theta, \Phi} J(\theta, \Phi) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim D, \phi \sim U(\Phi), y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot|x,\phi)} [r(x, y)]$$

Slow weights are updated using CISPO (truncated importance-sampling REINFORCE) with group-relative advantages from verifiable rewards, following the ScaleRL recipe.

Fast weights are optimized using GEPA, a reflective evolutionary procedure that: (1) selects parent prompts from a Pareto frontier, (2) samples rollouts, (3) elicits textual critiques/mutations from a frozen reflection LM, and (4) evaluates and prunes candidates on an anchor set.

FST interleaving: Training proceeds in cycles of T=6 slow-weight updates. At each cycle start, GEPA runs on a lookahead batch with the current policy as rollout model, producing top-K candidate prompts. During the next T RL steps, each problem's rollout group uniformly samples from the K prompts, mixing prompt and sampling variation within the same advantage computation. All experiments use Qwen3-8B.

Results & Highlights

  • Data efficiency: FST reaches RL's running peak in 3.0x fewer steps on CodeIO, 1.4x on Math, 3.0x on HoVer-hard
  • Higher asymptote: Fitted sigmoid asymptotes: CodeIO 47.4% vs 43.0% (+4.4pp), Math 49.2% vs 46.4% (+2.9pp), HoVer-hard 25.0% vs 17.3% (+7.7pp)
  • Lower KL: At matched reward, FST sits significantly left of RL on the accuracy-vs-KL frontier across all four tested tasks
  • Plasticity preserved: Math → HoVer-hard probe: RL-init collapses to ~0%, FST-init reaches base-init reference level. Physics → HoVer-hard: FST-init 24.2% vs RL-init 19.9%
  • Continual learning: Sequential HoVer → CodeIO → Physics: FST gains +19.6pp on CodeIO (stage 2) vs RL's +2.5pp — an ~8x acquisition rate difference
  • Fast weights learn faster: On a synthetic star-graph task, FST escapes zero-reward by step ~50 (driven by GEPA), while RL takes ~300 steps
  • Both channels needed: GEPA alone lacks capacity for ceiling performance; FST-distill (fast-weight self-distillation) falls short of full FST; only jointly optimizing both fast and slow weights reaches the highest ceiling

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The specific instantiation — interleaving CISPO RL cycles with GEPA prompt evolution over a Pareto population — is novel and well-executed. The fast/slow conceptual framing draws on decades of neuroscience and ML history, which the authors properly credit. The key insight that fast weights can absorb rich textual feedback to reduce the burden on slow weights is non-trivial.
  • Rigor: Experiments are thorough across 3 training families + 1 synthetic task, with multiple evaluation axes (in-distribution, cross-domain, easy-to-hard). The sigmoid asymptote fitting for comparing methods is a principled choice over arbitrary checkpoint comparison. However, all experiments use a single base model (Qwen3-8B), limiting generalizability claims.
  • Reproducibility: Full hyperparameters promised in appendix. GEPA and CISPO are both from prior publications by overlapping author groups. The code link is mentioned in the paper header.
  • Significance: If these results hold at larger scales (70B+), this could meaningfully change how LLM post-training is done — the continual learning and plasticity preservation results are particularly compelling for deployed systems that need ongoing updates.
  • Red flags: (1) Only tested on Qwen3-8B — unclear if benefits transfer to larger models or different architectures. (2) GEPA's compute overhead is not analyzed; the method requires additional rollouts and a frozen reflection LM, which adds cost. (3) The cycle length T=6 and population size K∈{4,8} are not thoroughly ablated. (4) The continual learning experiment uses only 200 steps per task, which is quite short. (5) The RL baseline uses CISPO specifically — comparison with GRPO or other RL methods would strengthen claims of generality.

Strengths

  • Conceptually elegant framework with strong cognitive science motivation (System 1 vs System 2, complementary learning systems)
  • Comprehensive empirical evaluation across five distinct advantages with well-designed experiments
  • The plasticity probe (train on task X, then test learning on task Y) is a clean and convincing experimental design
  • Pareto-frontier prompt population is a smart design choice that preserves diversity and exposes richer gradient signal during RL
  • Directly addresses a practically important problem: how to keep updating LLMs without destroying their capabilities
  • The synthetic star-graph experiment elegantly isolates why fast weights help (rich feedback vs scalar reward)

Limitations

  • Only evaluated on Qwen3-8B; scaling behavior to larger models (70B+) is unknown
  • Computational overhead of GEPA cycles (additional rollouts + reflection LM calls) is not quantified
  • The framework relies on a frozen reflection LM (πref) for prompt mutation — quality of fast weights depends on this external model
  • Only 3–4 tasks tested; diversity of task types (especially long-horizon, open-ended tasks) is limited
  • Cycle hyperparameters (T=6, K∈{4,8}) not thoroughly ablated
  • Continual learning experiment uses short 200-step stages — unclear if benefits persist over longer training or more task switches
  • The FST-distill variant (transferring fast weights into parameters) underperforms full FST, suggesting the two channels cannot be easily collapsed post-hoc

Relevance

FST 框架直接解决 LLM 后训练中灾难性遗忘和可塑性丧失的核心难题，将上下文学习与 RL 参数更新结合、通过文本反馈突破标量奖励信息瓶颈的思路，对构建可持续适应的 LLM 训练系统具有重要参考价值。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does FST scale to frontier models (70B+ parameters)? Does the fast-weight channel remain effective when the base model already has strong in-context learning capabilities?
  2. What is the compute budget trade-off? How much additional compute do GEPA cycles add compared to pure RL training?
  3. Can fast weights be efficiently composed or transferred across related tasks in the continual learning setting?
  4. How does the choice of reflection LM (πref) affect FST performance? Is there a quality threshold below which fast-weight optimization degrades?
  5. Could FST be combined with parameter-efficient methods like LoRA for the slow weights, further reducing forgetting while maintaining the fast-weight channel?
