FibQuant: Universal Vector Quantization for Random-Access KV-Cache Compression

arXiv: 2605.11478

Date: 2026-05-12

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.11478

一句话总结： FibQuant 通过识别随机归一化-旋转接口产生的球面 Beta 分布规范源，将标量量化替换为径向-角度向量码本，在保持随机访问约束的同时实现了分数比特和亚比特 KV 缓存压缩，最高可达 34 倍压缩且注意力余弦相似度保持在 0.95 以上。
TL;DR: FibQuant is a universal fixed-rate vector quantizer for KV-cache compression that replaces scalar coordinate tables with a shared radial–angular codebook matched to the spherical-Beta source induced by the normalize–rotate interface, enabling fractional-bit and sub-one-bit compression (up to 34×) while preserving random-access decoding.

核心贡献

  • 识别规范源：证明了归一化+Haar 随机旋转后，任意输入分布的 k 维分块都具有相同的球面 Beta 密度 $f_{d,k}$，因此一个离线共享码本即可通用适配所有层、注意力头和模型。
  • 严格优于标量量化：在匹配比特率下，向量码严格优于标量乘积量化，增益可分解为胞腔成型因子（cell-shaping）和密度匹配因子（density-matching）。
  • 密集比特率轴：通过 $b = (\log_2 N)/k$ 实现分数比特和亚比特操作点，无需校准数据或变长编码。
  • 实际效果显著：在 GPT-2 上达到 34.1× 压缩（注意力余弦相似度 0.946），在 TinyLlama-1.1B 上 4× 压缩时困惑度仅差 fp16 约 0.1，8× 压缩时困惑度比标量 TurboQuant 低 3.6 倍。

Key Contributions

  • Canonical source identification (Theorem 1): Proves that after normalization and Haar rotation, every $k$-block marginal follows the same spherical-Beta density $f_{d,k}$ on the unit ball $B_k$, independent of the original KV distribution. This makes a single shared offline codebook universally correct across layers, heads, prompts, and models.
  • Strict vector-over-scalar dominance (Theorem 2): The optimal $k$-vector code strictly dominates the scalar product specialization at matched rate. At high rate, the gain factorizes into a cell-shaping term $\gamma_{\text{cell}}(k) \geq 1$ (geometric tessellation advantage) and a density-matching term $\gamma_{\text{dens}}(d,k) \geq 1$ (matching the true joint vs. marginal densities).
  • Dense rate axis: Since rate $b = (\log_2 N)/k$ is not restricted to integers, FibQuant provides fractional-bit (e.g., 1.5, 2.5 bits/coord) and sub-one-bit (as low as ~0.19 bits/coord) operating points while maintaining fixed-rate random-access addresses — something scalar codecs like TurboQuant cannot achieve.
  • Radial–angular codebook construction: Combines Beta-quantile radii (Bennett/Gersho companding), Fibonacci/Roberts–Kronecker quasi-uniform directions, and multi-restart Lloyd–Max refinement to build codebooks matched to $f_{d,k}$.
  • Empirical validation: On GPT-2 small KV caches, achieves 5×–34.1× compression with attention cosine similarity 0.99–0.946. On TinyLlama-1.1B end-to-end, within 0.10 perplexity of fp16 at 4× and 3.6× lower perplexity than TurboQuant at 8× compression.

Methodology

FibQuant preserves the same normalize–rotate–store serving interface as TurboQuant but replaces per-coordinate scalar Lloyd–Max tables with a $k$-dimensional vector codebook:

  1. Encode: For cached vector $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^d$, store fp16 norm $\nu = \|\mathbf{x}\|_2$ and compute rotated unit direction $\Pi\mathbf{x}/\nu$, where $\Pi$ is a shared Haar-random orthogonal matrix. Partition into $d/k$ blocks of dimension $k$.
  2. Quantize per block: Each block $\mathbf{y}^{(m)} \in B_k$ is encoded via nearest-codeword lookup against the shared offline codebook $\mathcal{C} = \{r_n \mathbf{u}_n\}_{n=1}^N$, where $r_n$ are Beta-quantile radii and $\mathbf{u}_n$ are quasi-uniform spherical directions.
  3. Decode: Table lookup $\hat{\mathbf{y}}^{(m)} = c_{i_m}$, then inverse rotation and norm rescaling $\hat{\mathbf{x}} = \nu \Pi^\top \hat{\mathbf{y}}$.

The key theoretical insight is Lemma 1 / Theorem 1: after normalization + Haar rotation, every $k$-block follows the spherical-Beta density $f_{d,k}(\mathbf{x}) = C_{d,k}(1 - \|\mathbf{x}\|^2)^{(d-k-2)/2}$, making the codebook source-agnostic. The codebook construction uses: (a) Beta-quantile radii via Bennett/Gersho companding, (b) Fibonacci spiral ($k=2$), Fibonacci sphere ($k=3$), or Roberts–Kronecker rank-one sequences ($k \geq 4$) for directions, and (c) multi-restart Lloyd–Max refinement. The rate per coordinate is $b = (\log_2 N)/k$, yielding a dense set of operating points.

For encoder efficiency with large $N$, a hierarchical radius-first + list-decoding scheme reduces complexity from $O(N)$ to $O(\sqrt{N})$ per block while preserving the fixed-rate bitstream and random-access decoder.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. The identification of the spherical-Beta canonical source induced by the normalize–rotate interface is a clean theoretical contribution. The insight that the k-block joint distribution is NOT a product of scalar marginals — and that exploiting this via vector quantization yields provable gains — is non-obvious and well-argued.
  • Rigor: Strong. Theorem 1 (universality) and Theorem 2 (strict dominance with factorized gain) are formally stated and proved. The high-rate gain decomposition into cell-shaping and density-matching factors provides interpretable structure.
  • Reproducibility: Good. The codebook construction is fully specified (Beta-quantile radii, Fibonacci/Roberts–Kronecker directions, Lloyd–Max with restarts). The paper provides exact hyperparameters ($M = 30N$ training samples, $R = 4$ restarts, $T_{LM} = 25$ iterations). Implementation would require moderate effort but is feasible from the description.
  • Significance: Potentially high impact. KV-cache compression is a critical bottleneck for long-context LLM serving. The sub-one-bit regime (0.19–1.0 bits/coord) is a genuinely new operating region for calibration-free random-access codecs. If results scale to larger models (Llama-3, etc.), this could be practically significant.
  • Red flags / concerns:
  1. Limited model scale: Only tested on GPT-2 small (117M) and TinyLlama-1.1B. Behavior at 7B–70B scale, and with Grouped Query Attention (where $d$ and $H_{kv}$ differ), is unknown.
  2. Encoder complexity: At aggressive operating points ($k=64, N=16384$), naive encoding costs $O(dN) \approx 10^6$ FLOPs per cached vector. The hierarchical scheme helps but adds implementation complexity.
  3. No wall-clock / latency measurements: The paper reports compression ratios and perplexity but not actual inference throughput or memory-bandwidth improvements, which are the practical metrics.
  4. Single shared rotation: Using one Haar matrix across all layers/heads is elegant but assumes universality holds well in finite-rate practice; residual distributional variation could be exploited by per-head codebooks.

Strengths

  • Clean theoretical contribution: identifies the canonical source and proves strict vector-over-scalar dominance with interpretable gain factorization.
  • Practical: preserves the random-access serving interface (fixed-rate, affine token addresses), making deployment straightforward.
  • Opens a new operating regime (sub-one-bit, fractional-bit) that no existing universal random-access codec covers.
  • Well-written with clear structure; appendices provide thorough supporting detail.
  • The radial–angular codebook design is elegant, combining classical ingredients (companding, Fibonacci lattices, Lloyd–Max) in a principled way matched to the identified source.

Limitations

  • Experiments limited to small models (GPT-2 117M, TinyLlama 1.1B); scaling to production-grade models (7B+) is unverified.
  • No actual serving latency or throughput benchmarks — compression ratios are reported but not the end-to-end speedup in a real inference engine.
  • The shared codebook assumption, while theoretically justified by Theorem 1, may leave performance on the table compared to per-layer or per-head calibrated methods at moderate compression ratios.
  • Lloyd–Max refinement is sensitive to initialization; the paper uses multiple restarts but does not characterize convergence guarantees.
  • The paper does not compare against recent hybrid methods that combine quantization with token eviction or low-rank projection.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does FibQuant scale to models with Grouped Query Attention (GQA) or Multi-Query Attention (MQA), where the KV cache has different dimensional structure?
  2. Can FibQuant be combined with token eviction methods (H2O, StreamingLLM) or low-rank projection (PALU) for further compression?
  3. What are the wall-clock latency and throughput improvements in a real serving framework (vLLM, TensorRT-LLM) with fused attention kernels?
  4. Does the universality claim (Theorem 1) hold well in finite-rate practice for very large models (70B+), or do layer/head-specific distributional features emerge that a shared codebook misses?
  5. How sensitive is the approach to the choice of Haar rotation matrix — would structured random matrices (e.g., subsampled Hadamard) provide similar universality with faster encoding?
