In-context learning to predict critical transitions in dynamical systems

arXiv: 2605.12308

Date: 2026-05-12

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.12308

一句话总结： 本文提出 TipPFN，一种基于先验数据拟合网络（PFN）的上下文学习框架，通过在合成分岔动力学系统上训练，能够从短且含噪的时间序列中推断系统距临界转变的相对距离（RDTC），在未见过的转变机制和真实世界系统上均实现了最先进的早期预警性能。
TL;DR: TipPFN is a transformer-based prior-data fitted network trained on synthetic bifurcation dynamics that predicts the relative distance to criticality (RDTC) from short, noisy time series via in-context learning, achieving state-of-the-art early warning of tipping points across 20+ datasets including real-world systems like AMOC, power grids, and neurological seizures.

核心贡献

  • 提出 TipPFN 模型：基于 Transformer 的先验数据拟合网络（PFN），能够从短且含噪的多元时间序列中联合预测系统距临界转变的距离（RDTC），无需针对具体系统重新训练。
  • 开发 TipBox 训练数据生成器：将经典的分岔系统（fold、Hopf、transcritical）嵌入高维随机非线性随机微分方程中，生成多样化的合成训练数据，包含约 120k 个系统样本和约 1M 个上下文-查询对。
  • 定义 RDTC（相对距临界距离） 作为连续监督目标，替代传统的二分类方法，使得模型能够提供时间分辨的临界性估计。
  • 卓越的泛化能力：仅在分岔诱导转变（b-tipping）上训练，但成功泛化至速率诱导（r-tipping）和噪声诱导（n-tipping）转变，以及真实世界系统，在零样本和上下文学习两种设置下均表现优异。
  • 支持灵活推理：支持 nowcasting（实时估计当前 RDTC）和 forecasting（预测未来 RDTC 轨迹），并能利用额外上下文轨迹提升预测精度。

Key Contributions

  • TipPFN model: A transformer-based prior-data fitted network that jointly predicts relative distance to criticality (RDTC) from short, noisy multivariate time series without per-system retraining.
  • TipBox data generator: A novel synthetic training framework embedding canonical bifurcation systems (fold, Hopf, transcritical) into high-dimensional randomized nonlinear SDEs, producing ~120k system samples and ~1M context-query pairs.
  • RDTC supervision target: Introduces a continuous Relative Distance to Criticality measure as a training target, replacing brittle binary classification and enabling time-resolved criticality estimation.
  • Cross-mechanism generalization: Trained exclusively on b-tipping systems yet generalizes robustly to r-tipping, n-tipping, and real-world observations across climate, biology, and engineering domains.
  • Flexible inference modes: Supports both nowcasting (real-time RDTC estimation) and forecasting (future RDTC trajectory prediction), with the ability to leverage optional context trajectories via ICL.

Methodology

TipPFN builds on the prior-data fitted network (PFN) framework, which performs amortized Bayesian inference via in-context learning. The core pipeline has three components:

  1. Synthetic data generation (TipBox): Canonical low-dimensional tipping systems (fold, Hopf, transcritical bifurcations) generate driving trajectories. These are coupled to auxiliary high-dimensional nonlinear SDE systems via random directed graphs with power-law degree distributions. The auxiliary system is stabilized to prevent spontaneous bifurcations that would confound the driving signal. Each system sample produces multiple episodes (different noise realizations and forcing schedules), yielding context-query pairs with variable observation dimensions (1–16 features) and window sizes.
  1. Architecture and training: TipPFN uses a TabPFN-style transformer with structured causal attention — context tokens attend bidirectionally, while query tokens only attend to past time steps (preventing future leakage). The model predicts quantile distributions for the masked RDTC target via pinball loss. A secondary auxiliary loss (scaled 0.2×) requires predicting a random subset of observed features from the remaining features, encouraging task-agnostic dynamical representations. RDTC is transformed via Λ\* = tanh(5Λ) to increase resolution near criticality. Training used ~1M context-query pairs from ~120k systems.
  1. Inference: Given a query time series and optional context episodes (from the same system), TipPFN outputs a posterior predictive distribution over RDTC in a single forward pass. Tipping-risk scores (e.g., 1 − median(Λ) or CDF(Λ < 0.05)) are derived from predicted distributions for early warning assessment. The model works with 0–3 context episodes, 1–16 observed features, and window sizes of 16–128 time steps.

Results & Highlights

  • Canonical systems: TipPFN achieves AUROC of 0.88–0.96 across fold, Hopf, and transcritical bifurcation systems, substantially outperforming classical EWS (0.54–0.64), Bury (0.70–0.84), and Huang (0.45–0.50) baselines.
  • Semi-real systems: On 9 reduced-order models including AMOC, SEIRx, and compost-bomb dynamics, TipPFN with context achieves AUROC of 0.62–0.97, consistently matching or exceeding all baselines including on r-tipping systems not seen during training.
  • Real-world transfer: On seizure prediction (SWEC-iEEG), TipPFN benefits from patient-specific context and outperforms all baselines. On Daphnia extinction with extremely sparse data (W=16), simulated context compensates for limited observations. On zero-shot real-world series — AMOC slowdown, power-grid blackouts, cyanobacteria collapse — TipPFN provides informative RDTC nowcasts that signal instability in advance of visible regime shifts.
  • Context utility: Adding even one context episode substantially improves performance; the gain comes from comparing query behavior against broader system behavior, not just from longer observation.
  • Lead time: Across 14 systems, TipPFN detects critical transitions earlier and more accurately than all baselines, as shown in Figure 1 of the paper.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. Applying PFNs/ICL to tipping-point prediction is a genuinely new direction. The RDTC target is a clean reformulation of the problem. The TipBox data generator design — embedding canonical systems into randomized SDEs — is clever and well-motivated. However, the core transformer architecture is directly inherited from TabPFN with adaptations only to task construction and masking, so the architectural novelty is modest.
  • Rigor: Strong. The evaluation covers 20+ datasets across synthetic, semi-real, sim-to-real, and real-world settings with multiple baselines. Ablation studies over context size, feature count, and window size are thorough. The fixed-configuration evaluation (one setting per method across all systems) is a fair and stringent protocol.
  • Reproducibility: Moderate. Training details (hyperparameters, compute) are provided in appendices. The synthetic data generator is well-described. However, no code release is mentioned, and TipBox is not publicly available as of writing. The real-world datasets are a mix of public and proprietary sources.
  • Significance: Potentially high impact. If the zero-shot real-world results hold up under broader evaluation, this could become a standard tool for early warning across climate science, ecology, and engineering. The ICL paradigm for scientific time series is still underexplored.
  • Red flags: (1) Real-world evaluation is limited to a handful of univariate series with surrogate RDTC labels — the zero-shot results are suggestive but not definitive. (2) Training exclusively on b-tipping yet claiming generalization to r-tipping is intriguing, but the mechanism for this transfer is not fully explained. (3) The comparison with Zhuge is somewhat unfair since Zhuge receives the forcing parameter as privileged input. (4) The paper does not discuss computational cost of inference, which matters for real-time deployment.

Strengths

  • Principled Bayesian formulation through PFN framework with theoretical grounding.
  • Flexible input handling: variable context, features, and window sizes without retraining.
  • Strong out-of-distribution generalization across tipping mechanisms.
  • Practical zero-shot capability for real-world systems where no context is available.
  • Well-designed synthetic data generator that encodes physical priors (bifurcation theory).
  • Multivariate input support and partial observability handling.

Limitations

  • Real-world evaluation is limited to few examples with surrogate labels; broader validation is needed.
  • The model is fixed after pretraining — no online adaptation as new observations arrive.
  • Spatial dependence of critical transitions (critical for climate/ecology) is not addressed.
  • Requires relatively clean, regularly-sampled time series; performance on irregularly-sampled or heavily gappy data is unclear.
  • Computational requirements for training (though not inference) are substantial.
  • The auxiliary SDE system is constrained to remain stable, which may limit the diversity of dynamical patterns the model encounters.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Can TipPFN be extended to spatially distributed systems where tipping propagates through networks of interacting components?
  • How does performance degrade with irregularly sampled or missing data, which is common in real-world observational records?
  • What is the minimum amount of context needed for reliable prediction in high-dimensional systems, and how does this scale with dimensionality?
  • Could the learned representations be fine-tuned via test-time training or low-rank adaptation for specific deployment scenarios?
  • How do the RDTC predictions compare with physics-based stability analysis (e.g., eigenvalue tracking) when the underlying dynamics are partially known?
