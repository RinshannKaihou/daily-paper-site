> **一句话总结：** 本文使用稀疏自编码器（SAE）分解六种LLM的内部激活，通过敏感度评分和三级联合证伪框架（F1因果效应、F2良构稳定性、F3违规偏好性）寻找编码语法违规的专用特征，但在13个BLiMP语言现象上整体结果偏负面——仅GPT-2有1个现象通过全部三条标准，说明当前LLM中不存在统一的语法违规检测器。

> **TL;DR:** This paper uses sparse autoencoders to decompose LLM activations across six models and 13 BLiMP phenomena, searching for dedicated "detector features" for grammatical violations via a sensitivity score and a three-criteria conjunctive falsification framework. The results are largely negative: F1 (causal effect) passes broadly, but F2 (stability on well-formed inputs) and F3 (preferential effect) are bottleneck criteria, and only one phenomenon in one model satisfies all three jointly — suggesting LLMs lack a unified set of grammatical violation detectors.

## 核心贡献

- 提出基于SAE的**敏感度评分Φ**，按特征在约束违规输入上的优先激活程度排序，实现无监督的违规特征候选集筛选。
- 设计**三级联合证伪框架**：F1（因果效应：消融是否提升违规句子似然）、F2（良构稳定性：消融是否不影响合法句子）、F3（违规偏好性：消融效应是否对违规句子更强），三者必须同时满足。
- 在**六种模型**（GPT-2 124M、Gemma-3 270M/1B/4B、Qwen3.5 2B/9B）和**13个BLiMP语言现象**上进行大规模实验。
- 核心发现：F1广泛通过，但F2和F3是主要瓶颈——违规相关特征往往编码了一般的流畅性信息而非违规专用信号。联合标准F1∩F2∩F3仅GPT-2上1个现象通过。
- 指出负结果的三个可能原因：敏感度评分未能发现满足F2/F3的特征、SAE的feature absorption效应、相关特征可能跨层分布式编码。

## Key Contributions

- Introduces a **sensitivity score Φ** that ranks SAE features by preferential activation on constraint-violating vs. well-formed inputs, enabling unsupervised identification of violation-specific feature candidates.
- Proposes a **conjunctive falsification framework** with three jointly evaluated criteria: F1 (causal effect on violations), F2 (stability on well-formed sentences), F3 (preferential effect on violations over grammatical ones).
- Conducts a **large-scale empirical study** across six Transformer models (GPT-2, Gemma-3 270M/1B/4B, Qwen3.5 2B/9B) and all 13 BLiMP linguistic phenomena using pre-trained SAEs from SAELens, Qwen Scope, and Gemma Scope 2.
- Key finding: F1 passes broadly across models, but F2 and F3 are bottleneck criteria — violation-related features often encode general fluency information rather than violation-specific signals. The joint criterion F1∩F2∩F3 is satisfied by only one phenomenon in GPT-2.
- Identifies three hypothesized reasons for negative results: sensitivity score limitations, SAE feature absorption effects, and cross-layer distributed encoding of violation features.

## Methodology

The study is grounded in the theoretical framework of Hu et al. (2026), where string probability is governed by two latent variables: message (m) and grammaticality (G). The goal is to find the mechanistic realization of G inside LLMs.

**Dataset.** BLiMP (Warstadt et al., 2020) minimal pairs covering 13 linguistic phenomena (subject-verb agreement, determiner-noun agreement, anaphor agreement, filler-gap dependency, island effects, NPI licensing, argument structure, s-selection, control/raising, binding, ellipsis, quantifiers, irregular forms). Split 80:20 train/test, stratified by phenomenon.

**Models and SAEs.** Six models across three families and scales: GPT-2 small (124M), Gemma-3 (270M, 1B, 4B), Qwen3.5 (2B, 9B). Pre-trained SAEs from SAELens, Qwen Scope, and Gemma Scope 2, applied to residual stream at four layers (25%, 50%, 65%, 85% of model depth). SAE architectures vary: Gemma-3 uses Matryoshka SAEs, Qwen3.5 and GPT-2 use Top-K SAEs.

**Sensitivity Score.** For each feature j at layer l, computes Φ_j as the mean difference in feature activation between bad and good sentences across all minimal pairs. Features with high positive Φ are candidates for violation indicators. Top-k features by descending Φ form the candidate set Â.

**Ablation Intervention.** Three forward passes per sentence: (1) baseline (SAE active, no modification), (2) targeted ablation (zero out Â features), (3) random control (zero out equal-sized random non-candidate features). Three preference margins capture effects: M_bad (ablation effect on ungrammatical), M_good (ablation effect on grammatical), M_pref (change in grammatical preference).

**Conjunctive Falsification Criteria:**
- F1: Targeted ablation significantly increases ungrammatical sentence likelihood vs. random control (one-sided Wilcoxon).
- F2: Targeted ablation does not significantly affect well-formed sentence likelihood (two-sided Wilcoxon, p ≥ α; margin threshold < 0.05).
- F3: Targeted preference change is significantly greater than random control (one-sided Wilcoxon).
- A phenomenon is confirmed only when all three hold jointly. Bonferroni-corrected α = 0.00025.

## Results & Highlights

- **Baseline accuracy**: Models prefer grammatical sentences 74–80% of the time (GPT-2: 80.1%, Gemma-3 270M: 75.2%, Qwen3.5 9B: 79.1%).
- **F1 passes broadly**: Across all models, most phenomena show that ablating high-Φ features increases ungrammatical sentence likelihood (e.g., GPT-2: 9/11 phenomena, Gemma-3 270M: 13/13, Qwen3.5 2B: 10/10).
- **F2 is the main bottleneck**: Targeted ablation almost always also affects well-formed sentences, indicating the identified features encode general fluency, not violation-specific signals. Gemma-3 270M has 0/4 phenomena passing F2; Qwen3.5 2B has 0/1.
- **F3 also fails frequently**: Even when effects exist, they are often comparable in magnitude for grammatical and ungrammatical inputs.
- **Joint criterion (F1∩F2∩F3)**: Only 1 phenomenon in GPT-2 satisfies all three. All other models: 0 phenomena pass jointly.
- **Per-phenomenon (GPT-2)**: Best-performing layers concentrate in the middle of the network (L7, L10). F1 and F3 often co-occur (driven by same sensitivity signal), while F2 is rarely satisfied even with top-1 feature setting.
- **No shared cross-category features**: No SAE features are consistently identified as violation detectors across all 13 linguistic phenomena.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: High. This is the first systematic study using SAE-based mechanistic interpretability to specifically probe for grammatical violation detectors in LLMs. The conjunctive falsification framework is a genuine methodological contribution that avoids the pitfalls of cherry-picked confirmatory analysis.
- **Rigor**: Very strong. The three-criteria conjunctive approach with Bonferroni correction is methodologically disciplined. The use of random control ablations provides a proper baseline. The per-phenomenon analysis and cross-model comparison add depth.
- **Reproducibility**: High. Uses publicly available benchmarks (BLiMP), pre-trained SAEs (Gemma Scope 2, Qwen Scope, SAELens), and standard open-source models. Methodology is well-specified.
- **Significance**: The negative result is scientifically valuable — it challenges the intuitive hypothesis that LLMs develop modular "grammar detectors" and suggests violation information is entangled with general fluency representations. This has implications for mechanistic interpretability, linguistic theory, and LLM reliability research.
- **Red flags**: The F2 threshold of 0.05 is acknowledged by the authors as lenient (they note 0.01 would be preferable). The top-k hyperparameter choice is not extensively discussed. The analysis is limited to models up to 9B parameters — scaling behavior remains unknown. The feature absorption issue in SAEs (Chanin et al., 2026) could be an artifact rather than a property of the model itself.

## Strengths

- Rigorous falsification-based methodology with three jointly evaluated criteria and proper statistical controls (Bonferroni correction, Wilcoxon tests, random control ablations).
- Broad empirical scope: six models, three architecture families, four SAE layers, 13 linguistic phenomena.
- Honest, transparent reporting of negative results with nuanced discussion of partial evidence and hypothesized explanations.
- The sensitivity score and conjunctive framework are reusable methodological contributions for future mechanistic interpretability work.
- Per-phenomenon analysis reveals that best-performing layers cluster in middle network layers, consistent with prior linguistic probing literature.

## Limitations

- Models tested are at most 9B parameters; findings may not generalize to frontier-scale models (70B+, 405B).
- The negative results could be an artifact of SAE limitations (feature absorption, insufficient sparsity) rather than evidence that LLMs lack violation detectors — the authors acknowledge this.
- The F2 threshold (0.05) is somewhat lenient; tighter thresholds might yield even more negative results.
- The study focuses on English syntactic phenomena (BLiMP); cross-linguistic generalization is untested.
- Alternative intervention methods (e.g., probing classifiers, causal tracing, distributed representations) might yield different conclusions about the encoding of grammaticality.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Would findings differ in larger models (70B+), where more abstract linguistic representations might emerge?
- Can probing classifiers or distributed representation methods recover violation-specific signals that SAEs miss?
- How does the feature absorption effect in SAEs specifically interact with grammaticality features — and would improved SAE architectures (e.g., meta-SAEs, cross-layer SAEs) change the results?
- Are the partial positive findings (F1 passing broadly) driven by general surprisal/anomaly detection rather than grammaticality per se?
- Could the framework be extended to semantic anomalies, pragmatics violations, or multilingual settings to test for domain-general vs. domain-specific violation detectors?
- What are the implications for LLM reliability: if models lack dedicated violation detectors, how do they achieve grammaticality judgments — and can this be improved?
