Mitigating Context-Memory Conflicts in LLMs through Dynamic Cognitive Reconciliation Decoding

arXiv: 2605.12185

Date: 2026-05-12

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.12185

一句话总结： 本文提出DCRD，一种两阶段动态解码框架，通过注意力图预测上下文-记忆冲突并动态路由到贪心解码或对比解码路径，同时构建了ConflictKG基准，在四个LLM和六个QA数据集上全面超越现有方法。
TL;DR: DCRD is a two-stage decoding framework that predicts context-memory conflicts via attention map analysis, then dynamically routes inputs to either greedy decoding (conflict-free) or fidelity-adaptive contrastive decoding (conflicting), achieving state-of-the-art across four LLMs and six QA benchmarks while introducing ConflictKG, a new knowledge conflict benchmark.

核心贡献

  • 提出两阶段动态解码框架DCRD：第一阶段通过分析注意力图中的上下文忠实度（contextual fidelity）来预测是否存在知识冲突；第二阶段根据预测结果动态路由到不同的解码路径。
  • 设计认知协调解码（Cognitive Reconciliation Decoding）：对每个token自适应调整对比强度参数α，基于上下文忠实度分数动态平衡参数化知识与上下文知识的权重，避免"过度干预"问题。
  • 构建ConflictKG基准数据集：基于Wikidata知识图谱生成冲突/非冲突QA对，通过多阶段质量控制和人工验证（99%合格率），模拟现实世界中频繁知识更新的场景。
  • 实验覆盖四个开源LLM（Llama2-7B/13B、Llama3-8B、Mistral-7B）和六个QA数据集，DCRD在冲突、非冲突和混合场景下均取得最优表现。

Key Contributions

  • Propose a two-stage dynamic decoding framework (DCRD) that first predicts context-memory conflicts by analyzing contextual fidelity from attention maps, then routes inputs to appropriate decoding paths.
  • Design Cognitive Reconciliation Decoding that adaptively adjusts contrastive intensity α per token based on fidelity scores, balancing parametric and contextual knowledge without over-intervention in conflict-free cases.
  • Construct ConflictKG, a knowledge conflict QA benchmark built from Wikidata with multi-stage quality control (DistilBERT filtering, GPT-4o verification, manual inspection), containing 4,466 conflict and non-conflict instances.
  • Demonstrate state-of-the-art performance across four LLMs and six QA datasets, improving over greedy decoding by up to 29.1% on NQ-Swap and maintaining robustness under varying conflict ratios and noisy contexts.

Methodology

DCRD consists of two core modules:

1. Conflict Prediction via Attention Maps: The method computes a contextual fidelity score S_{l,h} = α^l,h_o / (α^l,h_c + α^l,h_o) for each attention head h in each transformer layer l, where α_c and α_o are average attention weights to context and output tokens respectively. These scores are flattened and fed into a single-layer MLP classifier to predict whether a knowledge conflict exists. The key insight is that when the model's attention strays from context during generation, conflicts are more likely.

Results & Highlights

  • On NQ-Swap (high-conflict), DCRD outperforms greedy decoding by 17.7%–29.1% across four models, and outperforms CAD by 6.7%–11.6%.
  • On general QA (NQ, SQuAD, TriviaQA — low-conflict), DCRD consistently beats all baselines while CAD degrades accuracy by an average of 8.1% vs. greedy, validating the importance of dynamic routing.
  • On ConflictKG, DCRD exceeds baselines by 6.2%–14.2% on average, with only 10.1% performance drop when conflict ratio increases vs. 15.7% for COIECD and 15.1% for ADACAD.
  • Under noisy context (30% noise injection), DCRD drops only 0.1% while CAD drops 3.4% and COIECD drops 2.6%.
  • Inference efficiency: DCRD takes only 39% of COIECD's decoding time while achieving +7.3% higher average performance.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty (Moderate-High): The two-stage predict-then-route design is a natural but well-executed extension of prior contrastive decoding work. The attention-based fidelity score as a conflict predictor is a meaningful contribution, though the MLP classifier is deliberately simple.
  • Rigor (Moderate-High): Experiments are thorough — four models, six datasets, ablations on routing and classifier design, noise robustness, OOD transfer, and efficiency analysis. However, evaluation relies on GPT-4o rather than exact match, which introduces potential evaluator bias.
  • Reproducibility (Moderate): The paper provides implementation details (hyperparameters, prompts) and builds on open-source models. ConflictKG construction details are described, but the dataset release status is unclear. The classifier training data and procedure could be more explicit.
  • Significance (Moderate-High): The problem of context-memory conflicts is practically important for RAG systems. The routing mechanism elegantly addresses the key weakness of static contrastive decoding methods. ConflictKG fills a gap in evaluation benchmarks.
  • Red flags: Only tested on 7B/13B base models — not validated on larger models or instruction-tuned/chat variants. The GPT-4o-based evaluation metric lacks comparison to human judgment. The conflict predictor requires training data with known conflict labels, creating a bootstrapping dependency.

Strengths

  • Elegant two-stage design that cleanly separates conflict detection from conflict resolution
  • Dynamic routing avoids the critical failure mode of static methods (over-intervention in conflict-free cases)
  • Token-level adaptive α adjustment provides fine-grained control
  • Strong robustness to varying conflict ratios and noisy contexts
  • ConflictKG is a valuable contribution for the community with rigorous quality control
  • Competitive inference efficiency despite the added prediction stage

Limitations

  • Only evaluated on base models up to 13B parameters; scalability to larger models and chat-tuned models is unverified
  • Conflict predictor requires supervised training with labeled conflict data, which may not always be available
  • GPT-4o as evaluator introduces potential systematic bias; traditional EM results are not reported for comparison
  • The method assumes attention maps faithfully reflect model reliance on context, which may not hold for all architectures
  • No exploration of alternative classifiers beyond single-layer MLP
