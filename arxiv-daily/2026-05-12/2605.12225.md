> **一句话总结：** 本文首次将 TopK 稀疏自编码器应用于 Whisper 编码器的最后一层，发现其帧级嵌入中编码了从音素、词汇、形态到语义（甚至跨语言）的丰富层次化语言信息，并通过激活引导实验证实了这些特征的因果性。

> **TL;DR:** First application of a Sparse Autoencoder (TopK, K=45, 16K latents) to Whisper's final encoder layer. The SAE discovers monosemantic features spanning phonetic (phones, diphones), lexical (specific words like "his" at 99.3% precision), morphological ("-ly" adverbial suffix), semantic (numbers, profanity), positional, and language-specific representations. Crucially, the profanity feature (latent 3584) steers across English, Spanish, and French — including French, which was entirely absent from SAE training data — demonstrating language-independent semantic encoding in Whisper.

## 核心贡献

- 首次将 SAE 用于 ASR 模型（Whisper）的机制可解释性分析，填补了 SAE 研究从文本 LLM 到语音模型的空白。
- 从 Whisper 编码器最后一层的帧级嵌入中发现了从低层（音素、双音素）到高层（词汇、形态、语义）的完整层次化语言表征。
- 发现 Whisper 编码了位置信息——特定 latent 在固定时间戳激活（标准差低至 0.04 秒），可能帮助解码器确定词序。
- 通过激活引导（steering）实验建立了因果关系：关闭 profanity 特征可将脏话替换为发音相似的词，激活则可插入脏话。
- 发现跨语言语义特征：profanity 特征在英语、西班牙语和法语中均可引导，而法语完全不包含在训练数据中，证明该语义表征是语言无关的。

## Key Contributions

- First study applying Sparse Autoencoders to a Transformer-based ASR model (Whisper), extending mechanistic interpretability from text LLMs to audio models.
- Discovers a rich hierarchy of monosemantic features in Whisper's encoder: phonetic (phones, diphones like R-UW1 at 88.7% precision), lexical (word "his" at 99.3% precision), morphological ("-ly" suffix at 87.2% precision), semantic (numbers, profanity), positional (temporally precise activations with SD as low as 0.04s), and language-specific features (English vs. non-English classification at 74.7% precision / 91.2% recall).
- Demonstrates causal feature steering: deactivating the profanity latent (3584) replaces swear words with phonetically similar alternatives; activating it inserts profanity into clean text.
- Shows cross-lingual generalization of semantic features: the profanity latent steers in English, Spanish, and French — French was entirely absent from SAE training data, proving the abstraction originates in Whisper's encoder, not the SAE.
- A formal argument (Appendix A) establishes that SAE training data composition cannot alter the encoder's representational geometry M; it only affects sampling coverage.

## Methodology

**SAE Architecture:** A k-Sparse Autoencoder (based on OpenAI's implementation) is trained on frame-level embeddings from the final layer of Whisper base (512-dim). The SAE maps 512 → 16000 → 512 with a TopK activation (K=45), yielding 16.4M trainable parameters trained with MSE loss. Audio is padded/trimmed to 30 seconds; padded frames are removed during training.

**Training Data:** ~646K audio files from LJSpeech, LibriSpeech, VoxCeleb1, Mozilla Common Voice English, SLR39/61/67/71-75, and Musan, producing ~200M frame-level data points.

**Feature Analysis:** A dedicated analysis set of 28,414 files (LJSpeech + Common Voice English/Spanish + curated profanity subset) was constructed. For each hypothesized feature, precision and recall were computed. Automated labeling used GPT-OSS 120B on the 100 files closest to median activation per latent (76% accuracy on a 100-latent manual eval subset).

**Steering:** Causal analysis modifies latent values (set to large negative/positive) before passing through the SAE decoder to Whisper decoder, generating manipulated transcripts.

## Results & Highlights

- **Language:** Latent 5106 classifies non-English with 74.7% precision / 91.2% recall; many false positives are non-native English speakers.
- **Phonetic:** Features for phones, diphones (e.g., R-UW1: 88.7% precision / 64.9% recall), and potentially triphones are discovered, reflecting co-articulation effects.
- **Lexical:** Latent 28 represents the word "his" with 99.3% precision / 81.4% recall.
- **Morphological:** Latent 29 encodes the "-ly" adverbial suffix (87.2% precision / 17.8% recall); low recall attributed to feature splitting (multiple "-ly" latents exist, e.g., latent 9779).
- **Semantic — Numbers:** Latent 7710 detects numerical values (38.1% precision / 79.2% recall); semantic activations are not tightly temporally bounded.
- **Semantic — Profanity:** Latents 3584 + 104 jointly achieve 89.7% recall / 6.6% precision for profanity detection (profanity occurs in only 0.11% of the corpus; false positive rate is 1.45%). Steering convincingly replaces or inserts swear words with phonetically similar alternatives.
- **Cross-lingual:** Latent 3584 steers profanity in French (unseen during SAE training), demonstrating language-independent semantic encoding.
- **Non-linguistic:** Latent 15019 separates noise from speech at 97.7% precision. Positional latents activate at specific timestamps (e.g., latent 15039 at 10.09s ± 0.04s).

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** High. This is genuinely the first SAE application to ASR models. The cross-lingual steering finding — especially with French held out from training — is a strong and novel result.
- **Rigor:** Mixed. The paper provides precision/recall metrics for individual features and validates automated labels (76% accuracy). However: (1) Feature evaluation is heavily cherry-picked — only the most interpretable latents are presented. Out of 16,000 latents, how many are truly monosemantic vs. polysemantic or "diffuse"? No global statistics are reported. (2) The recall values for many features are quite low (e.g., "-ly" at 17.8%, profanity at 35.4% for the main latent), which the authors attribute to feature splitting but do not systematically quantify. (3) The analysis set is a subset of the training data, which the authors argue is acceptable (Appendix A), but this is a debatable choice — evaluating on held-out data would strengthen claims.
- **Reproducibility:** Good. Code is available. Whisper base is public. Training data is standard open-source corpora.
- **Significance:** Significant for the interpretability community. Demonstrates that SAEs transfer to audio modalities and that Whisper encodes far more linguistic information than strictly necessary for transcription. The noisy-channel interpretation (Discussion section) is thoughtful.
- **Red flags:** (1) Only Whisper *base* (the smallest variant) is studied — it remains unclear whether larger Whisper models or other ASR architectures show similar patterns. (2) The paper claims to demonstrate "cross-lingual feature steering" but only shows it for one semantic category (profanity) and only via qualitative examples (Tables 4-5). Quantitative cross-lingual steering metrics are absent. (3) The English-centric focus is acknowledged as a limitation but limits generalizability claims.

## Strengths

- Clean, well-motivated experimental design with clear causal evidence from steering experiments.
- The cross-lingual finding with French held out from training is compelling and elegantly demonstrates that the abstraction is in Whisper, not the SAE.
- The formal argument in Appendix A (domain space invariance) is a nice theoretical contribution.
- Broad coverage of feature types: phonetic, lexical, morphological, semantic, positional, and non-linguistic.
- Practical implications: understanding what Whisper encodes could inform privacy concerns (e.g., encoding profanity/semantics beyond what's needed for transcription).

## Limitations

- Only one model (Whisper base) and one SAE configuration (16K latents, K=45) are studied. No ablations over SAE hyperparameters.
- Feature analysis is inherently selective — the paper showcases the best examples without reporting global statistics on what fraction of 16,000 latents are interpretable.
- English-centric evaluation despite the cross-lingual claims.
- Low recall for many features (especially morphological and semantic) due to feature splitting, which is acknowledged but not systematically addressed.
- Steering results are qualitative (transcript examples) without quantitative evaluation (e.g., WER impact, success rate of steering).

## Relevance

高度相关。本文直接涉及稀疏自编码器（SAE）和机制可解释性，是用户"LLM Hidden States & Interpretability"方向的核心论文。将 SAE 从文本 LLM 扩展到语音 Transformer，为跨模态的表示几何和隐状态分析提供了新视角，尤其是跨语言语义特征的发现对理解模型内部信息编码具有重要意义。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- What fraction of the 16,000 latents are genuinely monosemantic vs. polysemantic or diffuse? A global census would clarify how much of Whisper's representational space is interpretable.
- Do the discovered features (especially semantic ones) survive in larger Whisper models (medium, large, turbo)?
- How do SAE features extracted from different encoder layers compare? The authors only study the final layer — earlier layers may encode different information hierarchies.
- Can cross-lingual steering be extended beyond profanity to other semantic categories (e.g., numbers, sentiment)?
- What are the implications for privacy? If Whisper encodes semantic information (profanity, etc.) that isn't needed for transcription, what other sensitive information might be recoverable from its representations?
