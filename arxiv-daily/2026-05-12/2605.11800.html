ROMER: Expert Replacement and Router Calibration for Robust MoE LLMs on Analog Compute-in-Memory Systems

arXiv: 2605.11800

Date: 2026-05-12

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.11800

一句话总结： ROMER是一个免训练的后校准框架，通过替换低激活专家为高频专家的副本并基于百分位数归一化重校准路由器logits，在模拟存算硬件噪声下将DeepSeek-MoE、Qwen-MoE和OLMoE的困惑度分别降低达58.6%、58.8%和59.8%。
TL;DR: ROMER is a training-free post-calibration framework that restores MoE LLM robustness on noisy analog compute-in-memory hardware by replacing underactivated experts with replicas of high-frequency experts and recalibrating router logits via percentile-based normalization, achieving up to ~59% perplexity reduction across three MoE architectures.

核心贡献

  • 首次系统性地揭示了模拟存算（CIM）硬件噪声对MoE LLM的两个独特失效模式：专家负载均衡被破坏（少数专家过载、多数休眠），以及干净训练的路由器决策在噪声下持续次优。
  • 提出ROMER框架，包含两个免训练组件：专家替换（将低激活专家的内存位覆写为高频专家权重副本，利用双路径计算将关键专家的噪声方差降低50%）和百分位数路由器校准（用IQR压缩logit分布，纠正噪声引起的路由过置信问题）。
  • 在四个MoE模型、多个基准上验证，ROMER在真实芯片噪声条件下一致达到最佳恢复效果，硬件开销仅~6-8%延迟和功耗增加。

Key Contributions

  • First systematic characterization of MoE-based LLM vulnerability on analog CIM hardware with noise models calibrated from real chip measurements, identifying expert load imbalance and suboptimal router logits as two primary failure modes.
  • Propose ROMER, a training-free robustness framework combining expert replacement (duplicating top-k experts into bottom-k memory slots for a 2x noise variance reduction) and percentile-based router calibration (compressing logit spread via IQR to stabilize routing under noise).
  • Extensive experiments across four MoE architectures (DeepSeek-MoE-16B, Qwen3-30B-A3B, OLMoE-1B-7B, Qwen1.5-MoE-A2.7B) demonstrate up to 58.6%, 58.8%, and 59.8% perplexity reduction under real-chip noise, with only ~6-8% hardware overhead.

Methodology

Noise Model: The authors calibrate a two-component noise model from real analog CIM chip measurements: (1) device noise modeled as multiplicative Gaussian perturbation on conductance values ($\tilde{w} = w + w \cdot \epsilon_{dev}$), and (2) ADC quantization noise modeled as additive uniform noise on the output. Parameters ($\sigma_{dev}$, $\sigma_{ADC}$, $\Delta$) are calibrated against chip measurements at temperatures 25°C and 80°C.

Expert Replacement: Based on cumulative activation magnitudes over a calibration corpus, experts are partitioned into top-k (most activated $T_l$), bottom-k (least activated $B_l$), and remaining. A bijection pairs each top-k expert with a bottom-k counterpart, whose memory is overwritten with the top-k expert's weights. During inference, both original and replicated copies contribute to the output, reducing noise variance by a factor of 2 for critical experts. Bottom-k logits are zeroed to discard them from routing.

Percentile-Based Router Calibration: Hardware noise inflates the spread of router logits (variance doubles on average, up to 3x in early layers). The method computes the IQR of logits, then compresses the distribution by lifting the lower half and suppressing the upper half by $\lambda \cdot \text{IQR}$, yielding an analytically exact spread reduction of $(1 - 2\lambda)$. The hyperparameter $\lambda \in (0, 0.5)$ controls calibration strength, with $\lambda = 0.4$ found optimal.

Results & Highlights

  • WikiText-103: ROMER achieves near-complete performance recovery, with only +0.05 to +0.34 perplexity degradation at 25°C and +0.31 to +0.95 at 80°C versus clean baseline, compared to vanilla degradation of +1.85 to +2.65 at 80°C.
  • Multi-benchmark (DeepSeek-MoE-16B): At 80°C, ROMER recovers ~62% of vanilla WikiText-2 degradation, improves ARC-Challenge accuracy by +6.4 points over vanilla, and outperforms the strongest baseline (weight-remapping) by 1.38 perplexity points on WikiText-2.
  • Expert activation heatmap analysis confirms ROMER substantially restores the balanced activation distribution seen in clean inference, eliminating overactivation hotspots.
  • Hardware overhead: Average +7.8% latency and +7.4% power across four models, in exchange for -56.7% average perplexity reduction. Qwen3-30B-A3B has higher overhead (+12.1% latency) due to its larger active parameter count.
  • Ablation: Both components are complementary — expert replacement with $n=4$–$6$ works best, and $\lambda=0.4$ is optimal for router calibration. Neither alone achieves full ROMER recovery.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty (High): This is the first work to systematically study MoE LLM vulnerability on analog CIM hardware and propose a tailored mitigation. The two failure modes (load imbalance + router suboptimality) are genuinely novel findings specific to the MoE-on-CIM intersection.
  • Rigor (Moderate-High): Experiments span four architectures, six baselines, and five benchmarks. The noise model is calibrated from real chips, lending practical validity. However, the percentile-based calibration is somewhat ad-hoc — the IQR-based heuristic lacks deeper theoretical justification for why it should be optimal.
  • Reproducibility (Moderate): All models and benchmarks are public, but the real chip noise calibration data is not described as released. Reproducing the exact noise parameters would require access to the same CIM chip.
  • Significance (Moderate-High): Highly relevant for the CIM + MoE deployment community, though this is a relatively niche intersection. The ~59% perplexity reduction is impressive, but the vanilla baselines (perplexity 27–35) make models "essentially unusable," so the bar for meaningful improvement is low.
  • Red flags: (1) The "up to 58.6–59.8% reduction" in the abstract is computed relative to the severely degraded vanilla, not the clean baseline — the actual gap to clean performance remains non-trivial. (2) The ablation shows router calibration alone at $\lambda=0.4$ with no expert replacement still achieves perplexity 20.80 at 85°C (Table 4), which is remarkably close to ROMER's combined result, raising questions about whether expert replacement adds much beyond router calibration at high temperatures. (3) No comparison against noise-aware training or quantization-aware methods for MoE models.

Strengths

  • First systematic study bridging MoE LLMs and analog CIM hardware — fills a genuine gap.
  • Training-free and requires no labeled data, making it practical for deployment.
  • Clear empirical analysis with well-designed ablations and heatmaps that build intuition.
  • Consistent improvement across four diverse MoE architectures and multiple noise levels.
  • Low hardware overhead (~6-8%) for substantial quality recovery.

Limitations

  • Performance gap to clean baseline remains non-trivial, especially at high temperatures (85°C) — ROMER does not fully close the gap.
  • The framework is specifically designed for analog CIM and does not address digital CIM or other hardware accelerators.
  • Expert replacement assumes the MoE model has enough "idle" experts with low activation, which may not hold for all architectures or workloads.
  • The calibration strength $\lambda$ is tuned per model; no adaptive or per-layer adjustment is explored.
  • Evaluation is limited to perplexity and commonsense reasoning benchmarks — no generation quality, long-form tasks, or downstream NLU evaluations.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Can ROMER's router calibration be adapted dynamically during inference as noise levels fluctuate (e.g., due to temperature changes)?
  • How does ROMER interact with quantization-aware training or mixed-precision methods for MoE models?
  • Is there a theoretical bound on the minimum achievable perplexity gap under CIM noise given a fixed number of expert replicas?
  • Would per-layer adaptive $\lambda$ (rather than a global value) yield further improvements, especially for early layers where noise-induced logit inflation is highest?
  • How does ROMER perform on generation tasks (e.g., summarization, translation) beyond perplexity and multiple-choice benchmarks?
