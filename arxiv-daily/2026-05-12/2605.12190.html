Key Contributions

  • Extends supersample-based conditional mutual information (CMI) generalization bounds from the batch i.i.d. setting to sequential decision-making problems where data is revealed adaptively along a causal trajectory.
  • Develops a sequential supersample framework that separates the learner filtration from a proof-side enlargement used for ghost-coordinate comparisons.
  • Shows that under a row-wise exchangeability assumption, the sequential generalization gap is controlled by sequential CMI — a sum of roundwise selector-loss information terms.
  • Establishes a Bernstein-type refinement that yields faster rates under suitable variance conditions.
  • Demonstrates the selector-SCMI proof strategy applies uniformly to online learning, streaming active learning with importance weighting, and stochastic multi-armed bandits.

Methodology

The paper develops a sequential supersample framework to extend information-theoretic generalization analysis to non-i.i.d. sequential settings. The key construction is as follows:

  1. Supersample construction: A 2n × T matrix is formed where each row contains a pair (real sample, ghost sample) for each round. A binary selector variable determines which entry is the real observation.
  1. Filtration separation: The learner's filtration (driven by actual observations) is separated from a proof-side enlarged filtration that includes ghost-coordinate information. This decoupling is essential for applying information-theoretic arguments without interfering with the learner's causal structure.
  1. Row-wise exchangeability: Under the assumption that each row of the supersample is exchangeable, the paper derives that the sequential generalization gap is bounded by the sequential CMI — defined as a sum of roundwise conditional mutual information terms between the selector variable and the loss.
  1. Bernstein-type refinement: By exploiting variance conditions on the per-round losses, the authors derive a tighter bound with faster O(1/T) rates rather than O(1/√T), analogous to Bernstein inequalities in concentration.

The framework is instantiated in three settings: (i) online learning with regret analysis, (ii) streaming active learning with importance-weighted losses, and (iii) stochastic multi-armed bandits with suboptimality gap analysis.

Results & Highlights

  • Provides the first systematic extension of CMI-based generalization bounds to sequential decision-making, filling a gap left by prior work limited to the i.i.d. batch setting.
  • The Bernstein-type refinement achieves faster convergence rates when per-round variance is controlled, improving on the standard √T rates.
  • The unified selector-SCMI proof strategy handles three distinct sequential problems (online learning, streaming active learning, bandits) under a single theoretical framework.
  • Demonstrates that information-theoretic bounds can capture the adaptive, causal nature of sequential data without requiring i.i.d. assumptions.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. Extending supersample CMI bounds to sequential settings is a natural but non-trivial step. The filtration separation idea is technically clean and resolves a fundamental obstacle that prevented prior CMI frameworks from handling adaptive data.
  • Rigor: Strong. The paper provides formal theorem statements with clear assumptions (row-wise exchangeability) and complete proofs. The Bernstein-type refinement is well-motivated.
  • Reproducibility: The theoretical nature of the work makes it largely self-contained. However, no code is mentioned for empirical validation of the bounds on practical algorithms.
  • Significance: Significant for the learning theory community. The framework opens a path toward algorithm-dependent generalization analysis in RL and bandits using information-theoretic tools. The practical tightness of the bounds compared to classical regret bounds remains an open question.
  • Red flags: The paper is purely theoretical with no empirical experiments. The row-wise exchangeability assumption, while standard, may be restrictive in some sequential settings (e.g., when the environment is adversarial). The practical utility of the bounds for guiding algorithm design is not demonstrated.

Strengths

  • Clean, unified theoretical framework that handles multiple sequential decision-making problems.
  • The filtration separation technique is an elegant solution to the causal trajectory problem.
  • Bernstein-type refinement provides tighter bounds under variance conditions.
  • Fills an important gap between batch i.i.d. CMI theory and sequential learning.

Limitations

  • Purely theoretical — no empirical validation on practical algorithms or benchmarks.
  • Row-wise exchangeability may not hold in adversarial or non-stationary environments.
  • The bounds may be loose compared to classical minimax regret bounds in well-studied settings.
  • Does not cover contextual bandits or reinforcement learning with function approximation.
