Information-Theoretic Generalization Bounds for Sequential Decision Making

arXiv: 2605.12190

Date: 2026-05-12

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.12190

一句话总结： 作者提出序列超样本（sequential supersample）框架，通过分离学习者滤过 Ht 与证明侧放大 σ-代数 Gt−1，把批量 i.i.d. 的 CMI 泛化界改造成因果的逐轮 selector–loss 互信息求和（SCMI），并在多臂老虎机情形下把 PAC-Bayes 的 O(K^{1/3}T^{2/3}) 界改进到 O(√(KT log K / Δmin))。
TL;DR: The paper extends supersample conditional mutual information (CMI) generalization bounds from the batch i.i.d. setting to sequential decision-making — online learning, streaming active learning with importance weighting, and stochastic K-armed bandits — by introducing a sequential supersample construction and roundwise SCMI quantity; in the bandit case the resulting static-regret bound improves the PAC-Bayes horizon order from O(K^{1/3}T^{2/3}) (Seldin et al. 2012; Haddouche & Guedj 2023) to O(√(KT log K / Δmin)).

核心贡献

  • 提出序列超样本构造：在第 t 轮同时抽取配对行 Ze_t=(Z_{t,0},Z_{t,1}) 与公平选择器 U_t∈{0,1}，但学习者只观察被选坐标 Z_t=Z_{t,U_t}；未被选坐标仅在证明中作为 ghost 用作训练-留出比较。
  • 关键的滤过分离：学习者滤过 H_t 仅含被选路径信息，而证明侧的局部放大 G_{t−1}=H_{t−1}∨σ(Ze_t) 临时暴露当前配对行用于 selected–ghost 比较，从而保持因果性。
  • 在行可交换性（row-wise exchangeability, Pt(·|H_{t−1})=τ_#Pt(·|H_{t−1})）和被选坐标更新律下，证明 train–holdout 差距等于逐轮 selector–loss 相关和（Lemma 1），并由 SCMI 控制（Theorem 2）：|Err^seq_n| ≤ √(2/n · Σ_t I(L^+_t; U_t | G_{t−1}))。
  • 给出 Bernstein 型加细（Theorem 3）：在二阶矩条件 E[(e_{t,0}(w))² | G_{t−1}] ≤ B·R^ho_n 下，泛化项以线性方式进入界 R^ho_n ≤ R^tr_n + (Bλ/(1−Bλ/(1−λb/3)))·(2/(λn))·Σ_t I(e_{t,0}(w);U_t|G_{t−1})。
  • 把框架特化到三个问题：在线学习（与 sequential Rademacher 复杂度 / Littlestone 维对应）、带重要性加权 1/p_t 的流式主动学习（Theorem 4 引入 1/p_min 因子）、带平滑 ε_t-探索的多臂老虎机（Theorem 5）。

Key Contributions

  • A sequential supersample construction that keeps the batch supersample's selected-vs-ghost comparison but replaces the single static selector with a causal sequence of roundwise selectors U_1,…,U_n, while a proof-side enlargement G_{t−1} exposes the current latent row only for analysis.
  • A clean separation between the learner filtration H_t (only selected-path variables) and the proof-side sigma-field G_{t−1}; this is what allows CMI arguments to respect causality in adaptive settings (Section 3.1).
  • A row-swap identity (Lemma 1) showing the sequential train–holdout gap is a sum of roundwise conditional selector–loss correlations, controlled by the new Sequential CMI (SCMI) quantity in Theorem 2.
  • A Bernstein-type fast-rate refinement (Theorem 3) and a Shifted-Rademacher variant (Appendix D.3) that turn the slow-rate √(info) dependence into linear-in-info bounds under a second-moment condition.
  • Three concrete instantiations: (i) online learning, where SCMI is linked to sequential Rademacher complexity and, under pathwise realizability, to the Littlestone dimension (Appendix C); (ii) streaming active learning with importance weighting, where the bound scales by 1/p_min (Theorem 4); (iii) stochastic bandits with smoothing, where the static regret bound is improved to O(√(KT log K / Δ_min)) (Theorem 5).

Methodology

The core construction is a per-round paired latent row Zet=(Z_{t,0},Z_{t,1}) drawn from a history-dependent kernel Law(Zet|H_{t−1})=Pt(·|H_{t−1}) — this is explicitly not an independence assumption across rounds. A fair selector U_t∈{0,1} with Pr(U_t=0|G_{t−1})=Pr(U_t=1|G_{t−1})=1/2 (Eq. 2) chooses Z_t=Z_{t,U_t}; the learner updates via Law(W_t|H_{t−1},Zet,U_t)=K_t(·|H_{t−1},Z_t) (Eq. 3), so the unselected coordinate never enters the algorithm. Predictable weights 0≤Q_t≤1, H_{t−1}-measurable (Eq. 4) support stopping rules and importance weighting.

The proof has three layers. (1) Row-swap symmetrization: under row-wise exchangeability Pt(·|H_{t−1})=τ_#Pt(·|H_{t−1}) (Eq. 6), the train–holdout gap reduces to one-coordinate signed correlations: 2·Err^seq_n = Σ_t E[E[ε_t L^+_t|G_{t−1}]] (Lemma 1, Eq. 7). (2) Variational bound: applying the conditional Donsker–Varadhan formula with test f(ℓ,u)=s_t·λ(2u−1)ℓ and bounding E[exp{s_t·λ·ε'_t·L^+_t}|G_{t−1}] ≤ exp(λ²/2) via cosh ≤ exp(·²/2) yields the slow-rate bound |Err^seq_n| ≤ √((2/n)·Σ_t I(L^+_t;U_t|G_{t−1})) ≤ √((2/n)·Σ_t I(W_t;U_t|G_{t−1})) (Theorem 2). (3) Bernstein refinement: under E[(e_{t,0}(w))²|G_{t−1}] ≤ B·R^ho_n (Eq. 9), for 0<λ<3/b with 1−Bλ/(1−λb/3)>0 the bound becomes R^ho_n ≤ R^tr_n + (Bλ/(1−Bλ/(1−λb/3)))·(2/(λn))·Σ_t I(e_{t,0}(w);U_t|G_{t−1}) (Theorem 3, Eq. 10).

For the bandit instantiation, the authors decompose the static regret of the smoothed exponential-weights policy ρ^{exp}_t into four terms (Eq. 13): deterministic smoothing cost K·ε_{t+1}, empirical optimization E[Δ(ρ^{exp}_t)−Δ̂_t(ρ^{exp}_t)], and two selector-SCMI transfer terms bounded via a proof-only virtual arm Ā_t∼ρ^{exp}_t and Lemma 12's selector-comparison correction (because the terminal posterior depends on the selected trajectory, Theorem 3 cannot be substituted directly). Setting ε_t=min{1/K, √(52 log K/(KtΔ_min))} and γ_t=2Kε_t yields the final √(KT log K/Δ_min) rate (Theorem 5).

Results & Highlights

  • General SCMI slow-rate bound (Theorem 2, Eq. 8): under row-wise exchangeability, |Err^seq_n| ≤ √((2/n)·Σ_{t=1}^n I(L^+_t;U_t|G_{t−1})) ≤ √((2/n)·Σ_{t=1}^n I(W_t;U_t|G_{t−1})) — a causal decomposition of the batch bound into roundwise increments.
  • Bernstein-type fast-rate bound (Theorem 3, Eq. 10): if E[(e_{t,0}(w))²|G_{t−1}] ≤ B·R^ho_n, then R^ho_n ≤ R^tr_n + (Bλ/(1−Bλ/(1−λb/3)))·(2/(λn))·Σ_t I(e_{t,0}(w);U_t|G_{t−1}) for 0<λ<3/b — the information penalty enters linearly rather than under a square root.
  • Streaming active-learning bound (Theorem 4, Eq. 12): E[R(W_n)] − L^{IW}_n ≤ (1/p_min)·√((2/n)·Σ_t I(L^{+}_{n,t};U_t|G^{act}_{t−1})) — the importance-weighted holdout identity gives L^{IW,ho}_n = E[R(W_n)] and the bound scales by 1/p_min where p_min>0 is the minimum query probability.
  • Bandit static regret bound (Theorem 5, Eq. 14): for K≥2 arms with unique optimal arm and π_s(a)≥ε_s, E[Δ(ρ^{exp}_t)] ≤ K·ε_{t+1} + (2 log K)/γ_t + (52/(t·ε_t·Δ_min))·Σ_{s=1}^t I(G_{s,0}(Ā_t);U_s|G^{bd}_{s−1}); with the prescribed ε_t, γ_t schedule this yields a universal constant C>0 such that E[Δ(ρ^{exp}_t)] ≤ C·√(KT log K/Δ_min).
  • Horizon-order improvement over PAC-Bayes baselines: existing PAC-Bayes bandit analyses (Seldin et al. 2012 [17]; Haddouche & Guedj 2023 [7]) give O(K^{1/3}T^{2/3})-type cumulative bounds, whereas the SCMI + gap-dependent variance evaluation achieves O(√(KT)) up to logarithmic and gap-dependent factors (Section 5).
  • Connection to classical complexity: in the online setting the SCMI sum is the data-dependent analogue of sequential Rademacher complexity, and under pathwise realizability it is controlled by the Littlestone dimension (Section 3.3, Appendix C) — mirroring the batch CMI↔VC dimension link of Harutyunyan et al. 2021 [9].

Critical Assessment

  • The framework is genuinely novel in separating the learner filtration H_t from a proof-only enlargement G_{t−1} — this is the technical move that lets batch supersample CMI respect causality, and the paper is clear that a terminal CMI term I(W_n;U^n|Ze) cannot localize where selector information enters the trajectory (Section 3.1).
  • A paper-specific weakness: all bounds are expectation bounds under bounded losses ℓ∈[0,1]; the authors themselves flag in Section 7 that high-probability guarantees, heavy-tailed losses, dynamic regret, contextual bandits, and reinforcement learning remain open. The Bernstein refinement therefore does not yet cover the partial-feedback settings where fast rates are most consequential.
  • The bandit result requires the standard positive-gap regime Δ_min>0 (Eq. before Theorem 5) and a smoothed ε_t-greedy play policy; the improvement to O(√(KT log K/Δ_min)) is gap-dependent and does not apply in the minimax/gap-free regime, so the headline improvement over PAC-Bayes baselines is narrower than it first appears.
  • Theorem 3 cannot be directly substituted in the bandit case because the terminal exponential-weights posterior ρ^{exp}_t depends on the whole selected trajectory; the authors fix this with an ad-hoc selector-comparison correction (Lemma 12) rather than a clean instantiation of the general theorem, which somewhat undercuts the "unified framework" framing.
  • No empirical validation is provided, so the practical tightness of SCMI bounds versus classical regret bounds (e.g., UCB, EXP3) is not demonstrated; the bounds may be loose because SCMI terms are hard to compute for non-trivial policies.

Strengths

  • Clean, technically rigorous extension of supersample CMI to sequential settings; Lemma 1's row-swap identity and the variational proof of Theorem 2 are self-contained and short.
  • Unified treatment of three sequential problems (online learning, streaming active learning, bandits) under one SCMI template with clearly itemized application-specific instances (Table 1).
  • Bernstein-type and Shifted-Rademacher fast-rate refinements (Theorem 3, Appendix D.3) give a genuine rate improvement when second moments are controlled.
  • Conceptually clarifies the relationship between SCMI, sequential Rademacher complexity, and Littlestone dimension (Section 3.3, Appendix C), paralleling the batch CMI↔VC dimension story.
  • Directed-information interpretation (Appendix B) connects the cumulative SCMI budget to classical causal information flow, giving the bound a satisfying information-theoretic reading.

Limitations

  • Expectation-only bounds; no high-probability versions are provided.
  • Bounded-loss assumption ℓ∈[0,1]; heavy-tailed losses are out of scope.
  • The bandit improvement is restricted to the positive-gap regime Δ_min>0 and requires a smoothed play policy with π_s(a)≥ε_s; minimax and gap-free rates are not improved.
  • No empirical experiments or code release; the practical tightness of SCMI for real algorithms is unverified.
  • Contextual bandits, reinforcement learning, and broader partial-monitoring problems are explicitly listed as open (Section 7).
  • The active-learning bound scales by 1/p_min, which can be large when the query probability is small, weakening the bound in label-efficient regimes.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Can the SCMI budget Σ_t I(W_t;U_t|G_{t−1}) be estimated online as a training-time observability signal for sequential/online LLM fine-tuning, analogous to how batch CMI has been used to monitor SGD overfitting?
  • How do the SCMI bounds compare numerically to classical minimax regret lower bounds (e.g., Ω(√(KT)) for bandits) when the policy class and reward distribution are specified concretely?
  • Can the framework be lifted to contextual bandits and finite-horizon MDPs while preserving the row-swap identity, or does partial feedback with state break the proof-side enlargement construction?
  • Does the Bernstein second-moment condition E[(e_{t,0}(w*))²|G_{t−1}] ≤ B·R^ho_n hold for standard bandit algorithms (UCB, Thompson sampling) or only for the smoothed exponential-weights policy analyzed here?
  • Can high-probability versions be obtained by combining the SCMI supermartingale with a confidence-weighted online confidence sequence, as suggested by the PAC-Bayes supermartingale literature [6, 7]?
