> **一句话总结：** 本文构建 RQ-Bench（1,434 个来自真实论文的"作者锚定研究问题"），通过独立评分、对比评分与领域专家评测三重对比，证明 LLM 评审系统性高估 AI 生成研究问题的新颖性（"新颖性幻觉"），而根本原因是 LLM 无法识别生成问题的**狭窄性与来源绑定性**。

> **TL;DR:** LLM judges produce a "novelty mirage" — they consistently rate model-generated research questions as more novel than author-anchored reference questions, while domain experts reach the opposite conclusion. The root cause is that LLM judges fail to penalize source-boundedness and narrowness in generated RQs, and prompting models to be broader actually degrades their LLM-judged novelty scores, revealing a fundamental misalignment between LLM and human notions of scientific novelty.

## 核心贡献

- 提出 **RQ-Bench**：基于约 20 万篇 2025–2026 年 arXiv CS 论文，筛选出 746 篇高质量来源论文，使用 Qwen3.5-27B-thinking（影响力引用识别）、Gemini-3.1-Pro（思路与贡献提取）、Gemini-3-Flash（gap 识别）和 Gemini-3.1-Pro（RQ 生成）四阶段流水线，构建含 **1,434 个作者锚定研究问题**的基准，覆盖 13 个 CS 子领域。
- 揭示**"新颖性幻觉"**：在独立评分中，所有测试模型（qwen3-30b、gpt-oss-20b、deepseek-v4-pro、gemma-4-31b、gemini-3.1-pro、gpt-5.5）的生成 RQ 均被 LLM 评审评为高于参考 RQ；对比评分更进一步放大这一偏差。
- 量化 **LLM 评审与人类专家的严重分歧**：在 50 个样本的人工对比评估（非显然性维度）中，专家偏好作者锚定 RQ 达 **78%（Expert-1）和 56%（Expert-2）**，而 LLM 评审（gemini-3.1-pro）偏好模型生成 RQ 达 **82%**；专家-专家一致率 60%，而专家-LLM 一致率低至 **22%**。
- 证明**狭窄性（narrowness）是 LLM 评审的盲点**：LLM 评审在来源绑定性维度上的判断与人类对非显然性的判断高度相关（一致率达 72–86%），但 LLM 的新颖性评分却与狭窄性成正相关，形成根本性错位。
- 发现**缓解狭窄性存在内在矛盾**：修改生成提示以减少来源绑定性后，LLM 评判的原创性（2.39→1.90）和非显然性（1.81→1.46）均显著下降，即 LLM 将"窄而表面化的新颖语言"等同于新颖性本身。

## Key Contributions

- Introduces **RQ-Bench**: a benchmark of 1,434 author-anchored research questions derived from 746 high-quality arXiv CS papers (top 10% by citation in 13 sub-fields, published 2025–2026), constructed via a four-stage LLM pipeline (influential citation identification, idea/contribution extraction, gap identification, RQ generation).
- Demonstrates the **novelty mirage**: under standalone LLM scoring, all evaluated models receive higher novelty scores than the author-anchored reference on all three metrics (non-obviousness, originality, gap-addressing); under comparative scoring, this preference sharpens further (e.g., gpt-5.5 non-obviousness win rate rises from 27.2% standalone to 49.1% comparative under the two-judge combined evaluation).
- Exposes a **stark expert–LLM disagreement**: over 50 sampled instances, human experts preferred author-anchored RQs for non-obviousness 78%/56% of the time (Expert-1/2), while gemini-3.1-pro preferred model-generated RQs 82% of the time; expert–expert agreement was 60%, but expert–LLM agreement dropped as low as 22%.
- Identifies **source-boundedness as the key missing dimension**: LLM-generated RQs score substantially worse than author-anchored RQs on source-boundedness (e.g., gpt-5.5 source-boundedness score 1.17 vs. 0.47 for GT under two-judge combined); human non-obviousness judgments align 72–86% with LLM source-boundedness judgments, confirming that human experts equate narrowness with low novelty.
- Shows a **fundamental trade-off in mitigation**: when generation prompts are modified to reduce narrowness, LLM-judged novelty scores (originality and non-obviousness) concurrently decline, suggesting LLM-judged novelty is partly conflated with the surface language of narrow gap-filling.
- Releases the **RQ-Bench dataset** publicly (declare-lab/rq-bench).

## Methodology

**Benchmark Construction (RQ-Bench)**

The pipeline starts from ~200K arXiv CS papers (2025-01-01 to 2026-04-10), filtered to the top 10% by citation count per sub-field, requiring LaTeX source and conference/journal indexing by Semantic Scholar. This yields 746 source papers across 13 CS sub-fields.

For each source paper, the pipeline proceeds in four stages:
1. **Influential citation identification** (`qwen3.5-27b-thinking`): classifies each citation as inspiration, competitor/baseline, or irrelevant, using title, citation frequency, section names, and local context (parent sentence ± 3 sentences).
2. **Idea and contribution extraction** (`gemini-3.1-pro`): extracts the paper's main ideas and contributions, used to anchor the subsequent gap and RQ extraction.
3. **Gap identification** (`gemini-3-flash`): for each influential citation, identifies gaps that are both grounded in the cited work and addressed by the target paper's contributions.
4. **RQ generation** (`gemini-3.1-pro`): synthesizes the gaps and contributions into author-anchored research questions. The final benchmark contains 1,434 RQs (mean 1.92 per paper), with 2,464 RQ–citation links and 3,151 distinct gap statements.

Verification of RQ quality: gemini-3.1-pro approved 98% of RQs against source paper text; human judges approved 100% of 50 randomly sampled RQs.

**Evaluation Framework**

Six frontier models are evaluated for RQ generation: `qwen3-30b-a3b-thinking-2507`, `deepseek-v4-pro`, `gemma-4-31b-it`, `gpt-oss-20b`, `gpt-5.5`, and `gemini-3.1-pro`. Each generates 5 candidate RQs per instance (Pass@5 comparison). Two judge models are used: `gemini-3.1-pro` and `deepseek-v4-pro`, both at maximum reasoning budget and greedy decoding.

Novelty is scored on three dimensions K = {Orig, Gap, NonObs} (originality, gap-addressing, non-obviousness), each 0–3. Narrowness is scored on two dimensions N = {SrcBound, DiagFrame} (source-boundedness, diagnostic framing), each 0–3 with lower being broader. Author-anchor overlap (Overlap@5) measures how closely the best generated RQ matches the author-pursued direction (0–4 scale). Results are reported under both **standalone** (independent scoring) and **comparative** (side-by-side) settings, using win/tie/lose rates.

Human expert evaluation covers 50 instances from cs.CL and cs.LG, comparing author-anchored RQs against best-of-5 gpt-5.5 outputs on non-obviousness, performed blind.

## Results & Highlights

**Author-Anchor Overlap:** All models score in the 1.0–1.33 range (same broad topic, different question), indicating they consistently generate alternatives rather than reproducing the author's direction. GPT-5.5 scores highest at 1.33.

**Standalone Novelty:** All model RQs receive higher Best@5 novelty scores than author-anchored references across all three metrics and both judges. However, strict win rates remain notably low: under the two-judge combined evaluation, even gpt-5.5 achieves only 27.2% strict win on non-obviousness and 38.4% on originality. High tie rates mask apparent performance gains.

**Comparative Novelty:** Shifting to side-by-side comparison sharpens results dramatically. GPT-5.5 non-obviousness win rate jumps from 27.2% (standalone) to 49.1% (comparative) under two-judge combined evaluation, confirming that the comparative format systematically amplifies preference for generated RQs.

**Human Expert vs. LLM Judge (Table 5):**
- Expert-1: 78% win for GT; Expert-2: 56% win for GT
- gemini-3.1-pro: 82% win for gpt-5.5; deepseek-v4-pro: 52% win for gpt-5.5
- Expert–expert agreement: 60%; Expert–LLM agreement: as low as 22%

**Narrowness (Tables 7–8):** Model-generated RQs are substantially more source-bound than author-anchored references. Author-anchored RQs score 0.47 on source-boundedness vs. 0.80–1.55 for models (gemini-3.1-pro best at 0.90 under deepseek judge). Loss rates against reference on source-boundedness range from 47.9% to 78.0% depending on model and judge. Diagnostic framing is negligible as a narrowness driver.

**Revised Prompt Experiment (Table 10):** Removing narrowness-constraining instructions from the generation prompt reduces gemini-3.1-pro's source-boundedness score from 1.00 to 0.47 (matching GT), but simultaneously degrades originality from 2.39 to 1.90 and non-obviousness from 1.81 to 1.46. Human experts still prefer GT RQs (62%/56% win rates) even with the broader prompts; LLM judges continue to favor model outputs 50%/40% of the time. Expert-judge agreement remains low (30–40%).

**Key Insight on Semantic Similarity:** GT RQs are counter-intuitively *more* semantically similar to background paper contributions than LLM-generated RQs (Table 6: GT scores 0.612 vs. models' 0.585–0.602 on Qwen3-Embedding-4B), so semantic similarity does not capture narrowness.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** High. The specific combination of (a) an author-anchored RQ benchmark from real papers, (b) a three-setting evaluation (standalone/comparative/human), and (c) the systematic identification of "narrowness" as the key missing dimension in LLM novelty evaluation is a meaningful contribution. Related work (IdeaBench, ResearchBench, DiscoveryBench) evaluates downstream artifacts, not upstream RQ formation.

**Rigor:** High overall. The multi-judge, multi-model setup with explicit win/tie/lose reporting (not just aggregate scores) is methodologically sound. The human expert study is small (50 samples, 2 experts) but the directional findings are clear and internally consistent. The revised-prompt ablation strengthens the causal argument. One caveat: inter-annotator agreement between experts (60%) is meaningful but not definitive — novelty is inherently subjective.

**Reproducibility:** Good. The dataset is publicly released (declare-lab/rq-bench), model names and configurations are specified, and the pipeline is described in sufficient detail to replicate.

**Significance:** High. As LLM-driven "AI scientist" pipelines mature, the reliability of LLM-as-judge for novelty is a first-order concern. This paper provides the first direct, large-scale empirical evidence that current LLM judges fail on this task in a specific and diagnosable way. The narrowness finding is actionable.

**Red Flags:**
- The "author-anchored RQ" as ground truth conflates "what was pursued" with "what is most novel." Authors sometimes pursue incremental directions; the GT RQ may not always be the globally most novel question. The paper acknowledges this but uses GT as a reference point, not a gold standard.
- The human expert study is from only two experts in cs.CL and cs.LG — generalization to other domains is unverified.
- The conclusion that "LLM-judged novelty is conflated with narrowness" is convincing but the causal mechanism is not fully explained: why do LLM judges prefer narrow, surface-level gap language? This deserves more analysis (e.g., attention patterns, prompt ablations on the judge side).
- GPT-5.5 overlap at 1.33 is higher than other models, but still only "same broad topic, different question" — the models may be generating genuinely different but valid research directions, which the framework cannot distinguish from failure.

## Strengths

- Addresses a timely and high-stakes problem with a principled experimental design.
- The four-stage benchmark construction pipeline is well-motivated and uses appropriate LLMs at each step.
- Three evaluation settings (standalone, comparative, human) triangulate the findings in a way that makes the "mirage" diagnosis robust.
- The narrowness dimension and the revised-prompt ablation make the paper's core claim concrete and falsifiable.
- Public dataset release with comprehensive statistics (Table 1) enables follow-up work.
- The paper is honest about what low author-anchor overlap means: models generate alternatives, not failures.

## Limitations

- **Domain coverage**: RQ-Bench is restricted to CS sub-fields on arXiv; conclusions about scientific novelty in biology, chemistry, or social science may not hold.
- **Expert scale**: Two domain experts over 50 samples is a thin human study; broader expert panels are needed to confirm the magnitude of disagreement.
- **LLM dependency in benchmark construction**: The author-anchored RQs themselves are generated by Gemini-3.1-Pro. If the benchmark-building LLM has systematic biases in what it considers a well-formed RQ, those biases propagate into the reference.
- **Causal ambiguity**: The paper shows that LLMs reward narrow, source-bound language, but does not explain the mechanism or whether prompt engineering on the judge side (explicit narrowness rubrics) can reliably fix this.
- **Static snapshot**: The benchmark covers papers through April 2026; models trained after this cutoff may already memorize some content.

## Relevance

本文直接揭示了 AI 辅助科研（AI-for-science）中 LLM 评审的核心可靠性问题，对设计研究问题生成、论文评审辅助、自动化思路筛选等系统具有重要的方法论警示意义；其"狭窄性作为新颖性代理"的发现对优化评估框架具体可操作。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Can adding an explicit narrowness rubric to the LLM judge prompt close the expert–LLM agreement gap, or is the misalignment too deep to fix via prompting alone?
2. Does the novelty mirage persist for full idea evaluation (method + feasibility + experiments), or is it specific to the research-question level?
3. Would a fine-tuned judge model — trained on human expert novelty annotations — generalize better than prompted frontier models?
4. How much of the expert preference for author-anchored RQs is hindsight bias (knowing the published outcome) versus genuine novelty judgment? Could a masked expert study help disentangle this?
5. Does the magnitude of the novelty mirage vary across scientific sub-fields, and does it correlate with how well the generating model performed on that sub-field's papers in training?
