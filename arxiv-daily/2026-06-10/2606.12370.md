> **一句话总结：** Bebop 框架通过理论证明 RL 训练中模型熵增是 MTP 接受率下降的主因，并提出端到端 TV loss + 概率拒绝采样的组合方案，在 Qwen3 系列模型上实现最高 1.8x 端到端 RL 训练加速。

> **TL;DR:** Bebop proves that RL-induced entropy growth is the dominant cause of MTP acceptance rate degradation (not distribution mismatch), then fixes it with probabilistic rejection sampling and a novel end-to-end Total Variation loss that directly optimizes multi-step acceptance, achieving up to 95% acceptance rates and 1.8x end-to-end RL training speedup on Qwen3.5/3.6/3.7 models.

## 核心贡献

- **理论诊断：** 证明 MTP 接受率被目标模型熵上界约束，呈清晰负线性关系（Proposition 1/2），并通过分解分析证明熵项是主因，distribution mismatch 贡献可忽略不计。
- **概率拒绝采样：** 相比贪心 target-only 采样，rejection sampling 的接受率等于 TV overlap，对熵变化更鲁棒；CE/KL 训练下接受率仍受熵影响，但 TV loss 训练后近乎不受熵影响。
- **端到端 TV Loss：** 直接最小化 TV 距离（L_TV = d_TV(p, q)），梯度有界（≤1），具有尾部抑制特性，避免 CE/KL 在高熵时均匀分配优化压力的问题；e2e 多步 TV loss 进一步建模多步验证的乘积结构。
- **Pre-RL 训练即足够：** TV loss + rejection sampling 使 draft 对 backbone 权重更新鲁棒，RL 训练全程无需在线更新 MTP head，节省显存和计算。
- **规模验证：** Qwen3.5-35A3B/3.6-Plus/3.7-Plus 多模型、Math/Code/SWE-bench/Agent 多任务，e2e TV loss 相比 CE baseline 提升接受率 3–8%（in-distribution），Agent 任务接受率最高达 97%，端到端 RL 加速最高 1.8x。

## Key Contributions

- **Theoretical root-cause diagnosis:** Establishes via Proposition 1 (target-only sampling) and Proposition 2 (rejection sampling with CE/KL training) that acceptance rate is linearly decreasing in target model entropy H(p), with the entropy-driven component dominating over distribution-mismatch in practice (Fig. 3 decomposition).
- **TV loss for MTP training:** Proposes minimizing the TV distance directly as the MTP training objective (Eq. 10), with a gradient that is bounded (|∂L/∂z_j| ≤ 1), probability-proportional, and tail-suppressing. This contrasts with KL's unbounded gradient and CE's uniform-mismatch behavior.
- **End-to-end (e2e) multi-step TV loss:** Extends the per-step TV loss to model the full product structure of multi-step acceptance length (Eq. 13), naturally weighting earlier MTP steps more heavily and adapting dynamically as training progresses.
- **Proposition 4 (entropy-invariant TV training):** Proves that TV-trained drafts satisfy probability-proportional mismatch, making the TV distance entropy-independent — empirically the entropy-acceptance slope is reduced by over 95% vs CE/KL training.
- **Pre-RL adaptation sufficiency:** Shows through decomposition (Eq. 16) that under rejection sampling, RL weight updates cause negligible draft-target TV mismatch, making pre-RL TV training sufficient for stable acceptance throughout RL.
- **Implementation released:** Code available via SGLang PR https://github.com/sgl-project/sglang/pull/26312.

## Methodology

### Problem Setting and Two Sources of Degradation
RL post-training uses a three-stage loop: rollout (generate trajectories), reward (evaluate), update (gradient step). MTP attaches lightweight draft heads to the backbone that predict γ future tokens in parallel; the target model then verifies them in one forward pass via speculative decoding. During RL, two factors degrade acceptance:
1. **Entropy increase:** The policy's output distribution spreads out during exploration, making any draft harder to match.
2. **Distribution mismatch:** Backbone weight updates leave frozen draft heads misaligned.

The paper's key empirical/theoretical finding (Fig. 3) is that **entropy is the dominant factor**, not mismatch. Under rejection sampling with CE loss, Δα_mismatch ≈ 0 — meaning the entire observed degradation is entropy-driven.

### Target Entropy Constraints on MTP Acceptance (§3)
- **Target-Only Sampling (Prop. 1):** α^TO = max_y p(y) ≈ a^TO − b^TO · H(p). The acceptance is the probability mass on the draft's greedy pick, which is monotonically decreasing in entropy, lower-bounded by exp(−H(p)).
- **Rejection Sampling (Prop. 2, CE/KL-trained):** α^RS = 1 − d_TV(p, q) ≈ a^RS − b^RS · H(p). CE/KL loss produces uniform per-token mismatch |q*−p| ≈ σ; the TV distance accumulates over the effective support |S_eff| ≈ exp(H(p)), yielding entropy-dependent acceptance.
- Under TV training (Prop. 4): Per-token mismatch is probability-proportional |q*−p| ≲ δ·p(v), so d_TV ≤ δ/2 regardless of entropy. The entropy-acceptance slope is empirically reduced >95% vs CE/KL.

### TV Loss and Gradient Analysis (§4)
**Per-step TV loss:** L_TV = d_TV(p, q) = 1 − Σ_v min(p(v), q(v)), with gradient:
∂L_TV/∂z_j = −q_j [1(q_j ≤ p_j) − S], where S = Σ_v 1(q_v ≤ p_v)·q_v.

Key properties:
- **Bounded gradient** (Prop. 3): |∂L/∂z_j| ≤ 1, unlike KL (q_j − p_j, unbounded when distributions diverge).
- **Tail suppression:** Gradient is proportional to q_j, so low-probability tokens receive near-zero gradient, focusing optimization on the tokens that matter for acceptance.
- **Selective behavior:** Tokens where q_j ≤ p_j get pushed up; tokens where q_j > p_j get pushed down; unlike CE which applies uniform pressure across the full vocabulary.

**Comparison with reverse KL:** Despite also being q_j-proportional, reverse KL's zero-forcing behavior allows the draft to drop modes of p, and its asymmetric penalty drives q ≤ p globally — both reducing TV overlap. TV loss avoids these failure modes.

**End-to-end multi-step TV loss (Eq. 13):**
L_e2e = 1 − (1/γ) Σ_{j=1}^{γ} Π_{i=1}^{j} α_i = 1 − (1/γ) Σ_{j=1}^{γ} Π_{i=1}^{j} (1 − d_TV(p_i, q_i))

This directly optimizes the normalized expected acceptance length, weighting earlier steps more (they appear in more product terms) and capturing compounding multi-step effects.

### MTP Adaptation Strategy for RL (§5)
Decompose acceptance change during RL as:
Δα_t = b·(H_t − H_0) [entropy-driven] + (Δα_t − b·(H_t − H_0)) [mismatch-driven]

Finding: Under rejection sampling + CE loss, Δα_mismatch ≈ 0. Under rejection sampling + TV loss, both components are near-zero. Therefore: **pre-RL MTP training with e2e TV loss is sufficient** — no online MTP updates needed during RL, saving optimizer state memory and MTP gradient computation throughout the entire RL run.

For cases where online co-training is desired, joint training with separate learning rates and gradient norm normalization is recommended to prevent MTP loss from interfering with the RL backbone.

### Experimental Setup
- Primary model: Qwen3.5-35A3B; extended to Qwen3.6-Plus, Qwen3.7-Plus.
- MTP training: lr = 3.5×10^−5, 1 epoch on mixed RFT data, γ = 3 (draft 3 tokens, target verifies 4 at once), batch 256, seq len 256K via Megatron.
- Comparison objectives: CE loss, KL loss, Reverse KL loss, per-step TV loss, e2e TV loss.
- Throughput measured via SGLang's MTP implementation with rejection sampling.

## Results & Highlights

**Acceptance Rate (Table 2, Qwen3.5-35A3B, γ=3, rejection sampling):**

| MTP Loss | Math | Code | SWE | Agent | MTBench (OOD) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| CE (baseline) | 75.0 | 71.3 | 75.1 | 90.3 | 65.3 |
| KL | +0.0 | +0.0 | +0.2 | +0.2 | +0.0 |
| Reverse KL | +1.3 | +1.0 | −0.2 | +1.0 | +0.5 |
| TV loss | +2.4 | +2.5 | +3.3 | +5.2 | +1.4 |
| **e2e TV (ours)** | **+3.0** | **+3.3** | **+8.0** | **+6.7** | **+2.3** |

- Agent tasks (CE baseline already 90.3%) pushed to 97.0% with e2e TV loss.
- At MTP Step 3, e2e TV outperforms CE by ~5%; advantage compounds for later steps.
- Under target-only sampling, all objectives give <0.3% difference (as expected theoretically).
- Throughput improvement scales roughly linearly with acceptance rate improvement.
- Qwen3.7-Plus with TV loss reaches up to 95% acceptance on agent tasks (near backbone convergence).
- Acceptance rates generalize: models trained without agent-specific data still achieve ~70% on agent tasks.
- **End-to-end RL speedup: up to 1.8x** in async RL training of Qwen3.5, 3.6, 3.7 models.
- TV loss maintains stable or slightly improving Step 1 acceptance during RL, while CE causes persistent decline.
- Pre-RL TV training + rejection sampling during rollout: consistent acceptance throughout entire RL, no online MTP updating needed.
- Online CE updating during RL actually degrades TV-trained checkpoints by making the draft distribution smoother.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** High. The core theoretical insight — that entropy is the dominant constraint on MTP acceptance and that CE/KL training's uniform mismatch structure is the mechanism by which this manifests — is new and non-obvious. Prior work (MiniMax 2026b, Chen et al. 2026b, Li et al. 2025) primarily attributed degradation to distribution mismatch. The TV loss derivation and Proposition 4 (entropy-invariant TV training) are technically sound and close the loop between theory and practice cleanly.

**Rigor:** Strong. The paper provides formal propositions with proof sketches, a clean decomposition framework (Eq. 16), gradient analysis, and ablations across 5 training objectives, 3 model families, and 5 task types. The decomposition in Fig. 3 is particularly convincing evidence for the entropy-dominance claim. The gradient comparison table (Table 1) clarifies the theoretical picture well.

**Reproducibility:** Moderate. The SGLang implementation is released. However, the async RL infrastructure, MTP module architecture details, and Qwen3 model weights (required for the full recipe) may not all be publicly accessible. The pre-RL MTP training procedure is straightforward enough to replicate on other model families.

**Significance:** High for the RL post-training community. Rollout is universally the bottleneck, and a 1.8x end-to-end speedup with a principled, low-overhead recipe (one pre-RL training epoch, no online overhead) is practically valuable at scale. The TV loss is likely to influence future MTP training beyond RL settings.

**Red flags:**
- The "up to 1.8x" end-to-end RL speedup is a peak figure; Table 2 acceptance improvements are 3–8%, which translates to modest per-token throughput gains. The path from ~8% acceptance improvement to 1.8x end-to-end needs careful reading in §6.2 (not included in the first 10 pages shown).
- All experiments use Qwen3 models tested by the Qwen team — independent replication on non-Qwen architectures (e.g., Llama-3, DeepSeek) remains future work.
- No discussion of whether TV loss or Bebop's recipe affects final policy reward quality or downstream task performance (only throughput/acceptance metrics are reported). This is a meaningful gap.
- Reverse KL underperforms TV despite also being q_j-proportional; the explanation (zero-forcing behavior) is theoretically motivated but merits more ablation.

## Strengths

- Theory and empirical findings are tightly coupled: each proposition has a direct empirical counterpart (Figs. 1, 3, 8).
- The entropy-invariance result (Prop. 4, >95% slope reduction) is strong and directly explains why TV training works throughout RL.
- Pre-RL sufficiency eliminates online training overhead entirely — a major practical benefit at scale (no MTP optimizer states, no MTP gradient passes during RL).
- e2e TV loss handles multi-step compounding correctly via the product structure, contrasting with prior work using fixed positional weights.
- SGLang implementation released, enabling adoption by the broader community.
- Results generalize out-of-distribution (MTBench OOD, agent tasks with no agent training data).

## Limitations

- All experiments on Qwen3 family; architecture generality is assumed but not demonstrated.
- The "up to 1.8x" end-to-end speedup headline may not reflect typical gains; average figures would be more informative.
- No task performance (reward) evaluation — it is assumed Bebop is training-neutral but this is not verified.
- Pre-RL MTP training adds one full training epoch; its cost vs. total RL budget is not quantified.
- γ = 3 is used throughout; sensitivity to draft length hyperparameter is not explored in the first 10 pages.
- The paper is from the model developer; external validation of the async RL infrastructure speedup claims is needed.

## Relevance

本文对研究 LLM 强化学习后训练效率和推测解码的研究者高度相关：TV loss 的理论推导提供了一种通用的、可迁移的 MTP 训练目标，而熵约束分析为理解 RL 训练动态提供了新视角。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Does Bebop's recipe affect final RL policy reward or downstream task quality, or is it truly neutral to training outcomes beyond throughput?
2. How does the entropy-acceptance relationship manifest on non-Qwen architectures (e.g., Llama-3.3, DeepSeek-V3) — is the linear relationship and the >95% slope reduction under TV training architecture-agnostic?
3. What is the cost of the pre-RL MTP training epoch relative to total RL training compute, and does this cost amortize across multiple RL runs?
4. Can the e2e TV loss be applied in standard inference serving (non-RL) to improve speculative decoding throughput on high-entropy generation tasks?
5. Why does reverse KL underperform TV despite both being q_j-proportional? Is the zero-forcing behavior explanation sufficient, or does the asymmetric penalty play a larger role in practice?
6. How does Bebop interact with different RL algorithms beyond GRPO (e.g., PPO, REINFORCE) — does entropy dynamics differ across algorithms in ways that affect the recipe?
7. The acceptance rate degrades more at later MTP steps (step 3 drops 3.5% vs step 1 drops 1.2% under CE). Does e2e TV loss equalize this across steps, or does it primarily recover later steps?
