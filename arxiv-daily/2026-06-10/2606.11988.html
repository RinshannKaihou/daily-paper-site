What Uncertainties Do We Need for Dynamical Systems? ↗
Yusuf Sale, Christopher Bülte, Felix Czaja, Joshua Stiller, Eyke Hüllermeier · 2026-06-10 · Perspectives / Position · Workshop paper

Dynamical systems carry five distinct uncertainty sources — and the aleatoric–epistemic split is not intrinsic to any of them.

一句话总结 本文系统梳理动力学系统中五类不确定性来源（初始条件、过程噪声、部分观测、参数不确定性、结构不确定性），明确其偶然性/认知性本质依赖于任务与建模假设，并指出将二阶分布和可信集迁移至轨迹空间是关键未来方向。

S1 initial condition · S2 process noise · S3 partial observation · S4 parameter · S5 structural — each with its own propagation law, default class, and horizon dominance.
So what

For neural ODE/SDE and physics-informed ML moving into safety-critical domains, this framework clarifies what uncertainty methods actually do — and what they fundamentally cannot.

Critical edge

No experimental validation; all claims about uncertainty behavior in practice — including horizon-dependent dominance — are argued qualitatively, never measured.

Methodology

A conceptual taxonomy, not a new algorithm

The paper starts from the deterministic ODE dx/dt = f(x) and progressively relaxes its assumptions to surface each uncertainty source. The running example is the nonlinear damped pendulum (state θ, ω; γ = 0.5, g/ℓ = 1.0) — a system rich enough to exhibit stable/unstable equilibria and separatrices, so it can illustrate all five sources concretely.

Strengths
  • Unified lens. Fills a genuine gap in the literature for ML practitioners building models of physical systems.
  • Task-dependence insight. The aleatoric/epistemic split is not intrinsic — practically important, often overlooked in applied work.
  • Strong literature ties. Bridges neural ODEs/SDEs, Bayesian filtering, SINDy, PINNs, credal ML — useful as a field entry point.
  • Concrete running example. The nonlinear pendulum (γ = 0.5, g/ℓ = 1.0) is fully specified and grounds every abstract claim.
  • Non-trivial open problems. Uncertainty over trajectory functionals and uncertainty near bifurcations are real, not restated cliché.
Limitations
  • No experimental validation. All claims about empirical behavior are asserted, not measured.
  • Opportunities section is brief. More vision than roadmap; no solutions or preliminary results.
  • Workshop scope. Six main pages; key insights would benefit from full journal treatment.
  • Computational tractability untouched. Second-order distributions and credal sets over trajectory spaces are flagged as important but scaling is not discussed.
  • Finite-dimensional only. Framework limited to ODEs/SDEs; PDEs and spatiotemporal systems are mentioned but not treated.
Questions for further exploration
  1. For neural SDEs trained end-to-end, which of the five sources does the learned diffusion σ(x) actually capture — pure S2, or does it silently absorb S4 / S5 as implicit misspecification?
  2. How do second-order distributions scale to trajectory spaces in practice — are variational or particle approximations tractable for high-dimensional SDEs?
  3. Near bifurcation boundaries where S4 becomes qualitative, can multimodal Bayesian posteriors over θ handle regime uncertainty, or are fundamentally new representations needed?
  4. If derived-quantity uncertainty (time to stabilization, region-entry probability) is more operationally useful, what ML architectures directly target functionals rather than pointwise state distributions?
  5. How does the framework extend to partially specified systems where even the state space X is uncertain — e.g., discovering hidden variables from data?
