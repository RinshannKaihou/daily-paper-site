> **一句话总结：** 在生产级 LLM 安全审核系统中，基于检索的选择性经验注入将准确率从 19.6% 提升至 71.5%，同时优于全局 prompt 注入，并且检索匹配质量比增大 Top-K 检索数量更重要——论文以"部署成本-质量权衡"框架系统量化了不同任务制度（短输出 vs. 重解码）下经验服务的实际部署价值。

> **TL;DR:** Production LLM systems accumulate reusable operational experience (policy rules, failure cases, edge-case traces), but injecting this experience has real serving costs. This paper frames *external experience serving* as a deployment-oriented quality-cost trade-off and evaluates four strategies — no experience, random experience, global injection, and selective retrieval — across a real production content moderation workload plus tool-use and GPQA contrast tasks. Selective retrieval dominates global injection once experience becomes case-dependent; matching quality matters more than retrieval depth (Top-K); and the cost-benefit profile differs substantially between short-output and decode-heavy tasks. External experience pays off selectively, not universally.

## 核心贡献

- 将"外部经验服务"重新定位为一个**部署工程决策**问题，而非单纯的模型能力提升问题，提出"服务接口盈亏平衡点"与"任务成本结构盈亏平衡点"两个分析维度。
- 在真实生产内容审核系统（每日处理约 12B tokens，具有 16 个一级、64 个二级、94 个三级安全分类）中完成了受控对比实验，准确率从无经验基线的 19.6% 提升至选择性检索的 71.5%。
- 发现 Top-K 检索深度的收益迅速饱和（在审核任务中 Top-10 达峰值 71.5%，Top-30 降至 68.1%），揭示了"检索质量 > 检索数量"的规律。
- 发现相同服务策略在短输出任务（审核、工具调用）和重解码任务（GPQA）中呈现截然不同的成本收益特征：在重解码任务中，检索反而能缩短生成长度并降低延迟。
- 提供了两个 oracle 上界估计（Oracle-Skip-Redundant 减少 14.7% 检索调用，Oracle-Optimal 减少 43.2%），量化了选择性服务的可优化空间。

## Key Contributions

- Proposes a deployment-oriented framing of external experience serving organized around two break-even decisions: **serving-interface break-even** (global injection vs. selective retrieval) and **task cost-structure break-even** (whether quality gains justify online cost under a given output regime).
- Real production evaluation on an LLM safety-guarding workload (Qwen3-8B, ~12B tokens/day, 105K-item experience repository) achieving +51.9 pp accuracy gain (Instruct) with selective retrieval vs. no-experience baseline.
- Demonstrates that global compact injection (compressed static prompt) is viable only when experience is small and broadly applicable; once experience becomes case-dependent, selective retrieval is the stronger operating point.
- Quantifies the **Top-K saturation effect**: on moderation, accuracy peaks at Top-10 (71.5%) and declines at Top-30 (68.1%), while prompt tokens continue growing — retrieval quality degrades by introducing weakly relevant items that dilute high-precision signals.
- Shows that **decode-heavy tasks exhibit a different cost profile**: on GPQA, retrieval reduces completion tokens (2.44K → 1.42K) and end-to-end latency (19.35s → 16.92s) despite larger prompts, because relevant experience shortens the downstream generation path.
- Includes Trigger-aware retrieval as a stronger deployable policy and an LLM selector as a headroom estimate, bounding the practical improvement space.

## Methodology

**Task setup.** Three tasks are evaluated (Table 1):

| Task | Setting | Model | Output regime |
|------|---------|-------|--------------|
| Moderation (primary) | Hierarchical safety classification (16/64/94-level taxonomy) | Qwen3-8B | Short-output |
| Tool-use | Tool invocation prediction | Qwen3-8B | Short-output |
| GPQA | Graduate-level multiple-choice QA | Qwen3-30B-A3B | Decode-heavy |

All tasks are evaluated with paired Instruct and Thinking inference regimes.

**Experience repository.** Each experience item is a sanitized, structured unit with fields: `[Trigger]` (when to apply), `[Common Failure]` (recurring model mistake), and `[Action]` (correction or decision rule). Repositories are constructed offline: moderation uses ~300K business-labeled examples via an ACE-style extraction pipeline, resulting in 105K items; tool-use has 465 items; GPQA has 559 items. The paper studies *serving* from an existing repository, not repository construction.

**Serving strategies compared** (Table 7):

1. **None**: no external experience injected (lower bound baseline).
2. **Random experience**: unmatched items injected — controls for whether gains come from relevance vs. mere context augmentation.
3. **Global compact**: all experience compressed into a shorter shared static prompt (cache-friendly; viable when experience is broadly applicable).
4. **Global full**: fixed number of full experience items injected unconditionally.
5. **Retrieval**: query-conditioned Top-K retrieval, the primary selective serving candidate.
6. **Trigger-aware**: stronger retrieval with trigger-aware query construction (deployable but adds selector-side cost).
7. **LLM selector**: LLM chooses the best experience slice — headroom estimate only, not a deployment baseline.

**Metrics.** Task quality (accuracy) plus serving cost: end-to-end latency (seconds), prompt tokens (K), and completion tokens (K). All experiments use prefix caching; results are averaged over three runs.

**Evaluation scope.** Explicitly scoped to serving-time decisions under a fixed repository. Does not compare memory architectures, repository construction methods, or universal serving architectures.

## Results & Highlights

**Serving-interface break-even (Table 2, Figure 1):**

- On moderation (Instruct), selective retrieval reaches **71.5% accuracy at 3.31K prompt tokens / 0.37s latency**, versus global compact at 46.4% (4.52K tokens, 0.28s) and no-experience at 19.6% (1.26K tokens, 0.25s).
- Random experience control reaches only 20.8%, confirming that gains require relevance, not just added context.
- Global full (50 items) drops to 17.2% at 18.1K tokens — prompt dilution degrades quality significantly.
- On tool-use, retrieval similarly improves accuracy from 67.9% to 89.0% (+21.1 pp, Instruct).

**Retrieval-side break-even: Top-K saturation (Figure 2):**

- Moderation accuracy rises from 54.9 at Top-1 to a peak of **71.5 at Top-10**, then declines to 68.1 at Top-30, while prompt tokens grow from 1.6K to 6.7K and latency from 0.30s to 0.61s.
- The degradation mechanism is signal dilution: larger retrieved sets introduce semantically adjacent but weakly matched items that lower the effective trigger threshold and compete with high-precision items.
- Trigger-aware retrieval improves GPQA accuracy from 64.7% (standard retrieval) to **66.7%** at comparable cost, while the LLM selector headroom estimate reaches 78.0% — indicating meaningful headroom but non-trivial deployment cost for the gap.

**Task cost-structure break-even (Table 4):**

| Task | Regime | Inference | None Acc. | Retrieval Acc. | ΔAcc. | Prompt (K) | Compl. (K) |
|------|--------|-----------|-----------|----------------|-------|------------|------------|
| Moderation | Short | Instruct | 19.6% | 71.5% | +51.9 | 3.31 | ~30.5 |
| Moderation | Short | Thinking | 32.8% | 75.2% | +42.4 | 3.31 | ~475 |
| Tool-use | Short | Instruct | 67.9% | 89.0% | +21.1 | 4.75 | ~2.6 |
| GPQA | Decode-heavy | Instruct | 61.9% | 64.7% | +2.8 | 1.85 | 1.42K |
| GPQA | Decode-heavy | Thinking | 68.3% | 70.0% | +1.7 | 1.91 | 5.12K |

- In short-output tasks, added cost is primarily prompt-side overhead; completion length changes little.
- In GPQA (decode-heavy), retrieval *reduces* completion tokens from 2.44K to 1.42K and latency from 19.35s to 16.92s despite larger prompts — retrieved experience appears to shorten downstream reasoning paths (missing-premise recovery and compact problem-solving heuristics).
- Thinking-mode retrieval improves quality but incurs large latency overhead, making it a fallback rather than a default path in short-output settings.

**Oracle estimates for selective serving (moderation, Instruct):**

- Oracle-Skip-Redundant (remove retrieval on cases where it is hindsight-redundant): reduces retrieval usage by **14.7%** while preserving accuracy.
- Oracle-Optimal (remove both redundant and retrieval-induced errors): raises accuracy to **0.764** while reducing retrieval usage by **43.2%**.
- These estimates are not deployable policies but confirm that selective serving is a meaningful optimization direction.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: Moderate-to-low on techniques (RAG and few-shot retrieval are well-established), but meaningful in *framing*: recharacterizing experience serving as a deployment cost-quality decision with explicit break-even analysis is a practically useful lens. The oracle selective-serving analysis and the output-regime contrast are the most original analytical contributions.
- **Rigor**: Reasonably strong given the constraints of proprietary data. The inclusion of a random experience control (critical for isolating relevance from prompt augmentation effects) and multiple baselines including global compact (a fair cache-friendly alternative) reflects methodological care. Paired Instruct/Thinking evaluation adds useful regime contrast.
- **Reproducibility**: Limited. The primary moderation task uses a proprietary production system, proprietary experience repository (105K items), and a risk-focused benchmark that is intentionally not traffic-distribution-matched. GPQA and tool-use are public tasks but secondary. Some implementation details are explicitly withheld for privacy/security reasons. Results cannot be reproduced externally.
- **Significance**: High practical significance for ML engineers deploying LLMs at scale, especially in safety-critical or policy-following settings where operational experience accumulates naturally. The cost-structure break-even analysis is directly actionable.
- **Red flags**: (1) The moderation baseline (19.6%) is unusually low — the paper explains this is because the benchmark is intentionally risk-focused and fine-grained (94 third-level labels), not distribution-matched, making the absolute numbers hard to interpret as live-traffic utility. (2) The paper does not evaluate the latency or cost of the retrieval system itself in full detail — only downstream end-to-end latency is reported. (3) The LLM selector is included as a "headroom estimate" but never as a deployable comparison, leaving the gap between current retrieval and best achievable quality partially unexplained. (4) The generalizability beyond these three tasks and the Qwen3 model family is unverified.

## Strengths

- Grounded in a real, large-scale production deployment (12B tokens/day, 105K-item repository) rather than synthetic benchmarks.
- Methodologically sound: random experience as a noise control, multiple serving strategy variants, paired inference regimes, prefix caching enabled for fair cost comparison.
- Clear analytical structure organized around two break-even decisions makes findings actionable.
- Identifies the Top-K saturation mechanism (signal dilution via weakly-matched items) with supporting case analysis.
- Decode-heavy vs. short-output regime contrast is a genuine and underappreciated deployment insight: retrieval can reduce total latency in long-generation tasks by shortening the generation path.
- Honest about scope limitations and explicitly warns against aggressive retrieval reduction in safety-critical settings.

## Limitations

- Proprietary primary task; core results cannot be reproduced externally.
- Moderation benchmark is risk-focused, not traffic-distribution-matched — absolute accuracy numbers are not live-traffic utility estimates.
- No full prefill/decode runtime breakdown; cost proxied by token counts and end-to-end latency.
- Retrieval system details (embedding model, index type, query formulation) are not fully disclosed.
- Selective-serving policy (always-on vs. gated) is not yet validated in a head-to-head deployment study; oracle results bound the space but do not demonstrate a practical gating policy.
- Study covers only three tasks and one model family (Qwen3); generalization is unverified.
- Experience repository construction methodology is out of scope — findings apply only to serving from a pre-built repository, not the full experience lifecycle.

## Relevance

该论文与生产级 LLM 系统的高效部署和检索增强生成（RAG）的成本优化直接相关，对研究 LLM 推理成本控制、在线服务策略设计或安全审核系统落地的研究者具有较高参考价值；其"检索质量优于检索数量"及"任务制度决定经验服务价值"两个结论对设计实际 RAG 系统有直接指导意义。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How is the retrieval system implemented — what embedding model, index structure, and query formulation are used? How does the retrieval latency itself factor into the end-to-end cost breakdown?
2. Can the oracle selective-serving estimates (14.7%–43.2% retrieval reduction) be approximated with a lightweight gating classifier? What features would such a classifier need?
3. The moderation experience repository contains 105K items extracted from ~300K labeled examples. What is the quality-coverage trade-off in repository construction — how many items are needed before retrieval quality saturates?
4. The Thinking regime shows large latency overhead with retrieval in short-output tasks. Is there a principled way to decide when to enable Thinking vs. Instruct for a given serving budget?
5. How does performance degrade over time as the policy and category taxonomy evolve — what is the "freshness" requirement for the experience repository?
6. Would the findings transfer to other safety-critical applications (e.g., medical triage, legal compliance) where experience also accumulates operationally?
