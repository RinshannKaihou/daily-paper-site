arxiv 2606.12234 · 2026-06-10
Key Finding

Activation steering trades fluency for control — and nearly fails on the models we actually deploy.

On The Effectiveness-Fluency Trade-Off In LLM Conditioning: A Systematic Study ↗
Macocco P. Rodríguez Blaas Zappella Baroni Suau
Key Metric
ρ=0.81

Type-Token Ratio vs. fluency — while perplexity achieves ρ=0. The field's go-to proxy is worthless; a simple lexical diversity score beats it decisively.

Why This Matters

Real deployed chatbots are instruction-tuned. Activation steering — the method the field relies on for LLM control — barely works on them.

Conflict of Interest

Several authors (Rodríguez, Suau, Blaas, Zappella) co-invented Linear AcT, one of the three steering methods benchmarked here. Read the steering comparisons with that in mind.

Methodology
Prompting 10 concept-injection templates; detoxify prompts from prior work.
LoRA SFT Rank r=2, 400 training sentences, 30 steps AdamW on attention modules.
ITI Layerwise bias additions as tangent vectors to a classifier hyperplane.
CAA Steering vectors from averaged source/target activation differences.
Linear AcT Optimal-transport linear map applied to all LayerNorm layers.
Models (base + instruction-tuned variants)
Qwen3-0.6B Qwen3-8B Qwen3-14B Smollm3-3B
Tasks: Concept Injection (60 concepts, 10 categories, 1,000 continuations each) and Toxicity Mitigation (Real Toxicity Prompts, 356 toxic source sentences).

Judge: OLMo2-32B-IT scores Concept Injection / Fluency / Instruction Following on 0–2 scale; validated against 1,200 human ratings.
Six Key Findings
F1
Steering buys control at fluency's cost. When activation steering succeeds, text degrades into repetitive concept loops — e.g., "If you are a carrot, then you are a carrot..."
F2
IT models resist steering entirely. On instruction-tuned variants, all three steering methods score barely above unconditioned baseline. The chatbots that need control can't be steered.
F3
Prompting and SFT win on injection, lose on removal. Activation steering is the only approach that can reliably suppress toxicity — the asymmetry is task-structural.
F4
Perplexity is a broken fluency proxy (ρ=0). Repetitive text has low perplexity. SFT produces complex-but-fluent text with high perplexity. Type-Token Ratio (ρ=0.81) is the replacement.
F5
Cheap metrics match expensive judges. Concept Similarity (SBERT) hits ρ=0.97; ToxClass hits ρ=0.96. No need to run a 32B judge for benchmarking.
F6
Larger base models are harder to steer. Steering effectiveness decreases with model scale — converging toward the IT-model near-zero seen in larger deployments.

Strengths & Limitations
Strengths
  • First benchmark jointly measuring effectiveness and fluency — addresses a real methodological gap the field ignored
  • IT model resistance finding is practically critical; was previously overlooked despite obvious deployment relevance
  • Human-validated LLM-as-judge with linear kappa reported per dimension (Concept Injection: 0.779)
  • Actionable metric guidance: researchers can drop expensive judges and switch to SBERT + TTR today
  • Code, datasets, and prompts released at github.com/apple/ml-act
Limitations
  • Concept removal tested only for toxicity — generalization to style, persona, or language removal is untested
  • Largest model is 14B; 70B+ behavior under these conditioning methods is unknown
  • LLM-as-judge shows ceiling effect: 72% of inputs received maximum Instruction Following score
  • SFT uses minimal configuration (r=2, 400 sentences, 30 steps) — may underrepresent fine-tuning's ceiling
  • Conflict of interest: authors co-invented Linear AcT, one of the evaluated methods
Open Questions
Q1 Does IT model resistance hold for RLHF-trained models (GPT-4, Claude) vs. SFT-only instruction-tuning? +
The paper tests SFT-based instruction-tuned models. Whether RLHF alignment specifically drives the resistance — or whether it's a general property of instruction tuning — is unknown. RLHF-trained models may prove even more resistant.
Q2 Can the effectiveness-fluency trade-off be directly optimized — e.g., via a steering objective that penalizes POS distribution shift? +
With TTR and POS KL as cheap fluency proxies, one could train or search for steering vectors that maximize concept injection while minimizing fluency degradation. This is an actionable research direction the paper opens but does not pursue.
Q3 Does prompting and SFT still fail at concept removal beyond toxicity (e.g., removing a language or persona)? +
The injection/removal asymmetry is tested only for toxicity. Whether prompting ("do not speak in French") reliably suppresses a language the way steering can is an open empirical question with direct deployment relevance.
Q4 Is the scale–steerability relationship monotonic, and does it converge to near-zero at 70B+? +
Finding 6 shows larger base models are harder to steer. If this scales monotonically, frontier base models may be as unsteerable as IT models today — a significant implication for the long-term viability of activation steering as a control method.
Q5 Could Type-Token Ratio serve as an auxiliary training objective to make steering methods fluency-preserving? +
TTR is differentiable with respect to token distribution via relaxation. Incorporating it as a regularizer when learning CAA-style steering vectors could produce methods that steer without degrading text quality — turning a diagnostic into a design constraint.
