> **一句话总结：** 系统比较ITI、CAA、Linear AcT三种激活引导方法与提示工程、LoRA微调在概念注入和毒性消除两类任务上的表现，发现激活引导以流畅性为代价换取效果，且对指令微调模型几乎失效；同时揭示困惑度是流畅性的劣质代理，Type-Token Ratio才是廉价有效的替代指标。

> **TL;DR:** A systematic comparison of five LLM conditioning methods (prompting, LoRA SFT, ITI, CAA, Linear AcT) across concept injection (60 concepts) and concept removal (toxicity mitigation) shows: activation steering trades fluency for effectiveness and nearly fails on instruction-tuned models; prompting and SFT dominate on injection but cannot reliably remove concepts; and cheap text metrics (Type-Token Ratio, Concept Similarity) closely match expensive LLM-as-judge scores while perplexity is uncorrelated with fluency.

## 核心贡献

- 首次在统一框架下同时评估LLM条件控制方法在**有效性**（概念注入/移除成功率）和**流畅性**（文本自然度）两个维度上的表现
- 揭示关键交互效应：激活引导方法（ITI、CAA、Linear AcT）对**指令微调模型几乎无效**，而对基础模型有效，这一发现对实际部署具有重大影响
- 证明**激活引导有效时必然损害流畅性**，通常表现为目标概念的重复循环；CAA在所有激活引导方法中权衡最佳
- 证明**困惑度（Perplexity）与流畅性无关**（Spearman ρ=0），而Type-Token Ratio（词汇多样性比率）与流畅性的相关性达到0.81
- 概念相似度（Spearman ρ=0.97）和ToxClass（ρ=0.96）可高效替代昂贵的LLM-as-judge评分，大幅降低评估成本

## Key Contributions

- First unified benchmark evaluating LLM conditioning methods jointly on effectiveness and fluency, across both injection and removal tasks
- Discovers that activation steering is far less effective on instruction-tuned (IT) models than base models — a critical finding given that IT models are the ones actually deployed as chatbots
- Shows that all three activation steering methods (ITI, CAA, Linear AcT) cause fluency degradation when they succeed, typically through repetitive concept-looping
- Demonstrates that perplexity has zero correlation with fluency (Spearman ρ=0), while Type-Token Ratio (ρ=0.81) and BERT-based Concept Similarity (ρ=0.97 for injection) are cheap, reliable alternatives to LLM-as-judge evaluation
- Provides a reproducible codebase at https://github.com/apple/ml-act covering all methods, datasets, and metrics

## Methodology

**Models tested:** Four LLMs in both base and instruction-tuned variants: Qwen3-0.6B, Qwen3-8B, Qwen3-14B, and Smollm3-3B.

**Conditioning methods (5 total):**
- *Prompting:* Ten concept-injection prompt templates (e.g., "You are a chatbot that specializes in \<concept\>."); detoxifying prompts from Suau et al. (2024) for removal
- *Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT):* LoRA adapters (rank r=2) trained on attention modules using AdamW for 30 steps; 400 training sentences per concept generated by Mistral-7B-instruct
- *ITI (Li et al., 2024):* Adds layerwise biases to activations as tangent vectors to a binary classifier hyperplane; applied to attention output layers only
- *CAA (Rimsky et al., 2023):* Subtracts last-token source/target activation differences, averaged into a steering vector added to all new tokens at attention output layers
- *Linear AcT (Rodriguez et al., 2025a):* Minimizes optimal transport loss to bring source activations toward target distribution via a linear map; applied to all LayerNorm layers

**Tasks and datasets:**
- *Concept Injection:* 60 concepts hierarchically organized into 10 categories (e.g., color → red, green, blue), 100 generic concept-free prompts, 1,000 continuations per concept+conditioning combination
- *Concept Removal (Toxicity Mitigation):* Real Toxicity Prompts (RTP) dataset; source sentences with toxicity > 0.875 (356 sentences), targets with toxicity < 0.0275 (379 sentences); evaluation subset with prompt toxicity > 0.875 and continuation toxicity < 0.05 (455 sentences)

**Evaluation metrics:**
- *LLM-as-judge (ground truth):* OLMo2-32B-IT rates each continuation 0-1-2 on three dimensions: Concept Injection/Removal (↑), Fluency (↑), Instruction Following (↑). Validated against 1,200 human ratings (linear kappa: Concept Injection 0.779, Fluency 0.620, Concept Removal 0.856)
- *Low-cost alternatives:* Concept Similarity (SBERT cosine sim with concept name, ρ=0.97), ToxClass (RoBERTa toxicity classifier, ρ=0.96), Type-Token Ratio (lexical diversity, ρ=0.81 with Fluency), Generation Similarity (pairwise SBERT sim across continuations), Instruction Similarity (prompt-continuation SBERT sim), Perplexity (OLMo2-13B), POS KL (KL divergence of POS tag distributions via SpaCy)

## Results & Highlights

**Finding 1 — Steering effectiveness costs fluency.** Across all models and concepts, when activation steering methods successfully inject a concept, fluency and instruction following scores drop significantly. The failure mode is repetitive output: e.g., Linear AcT on Smollm3-3B with target concept "carrot" produces "If you are a carrot, then you are a carrot. If you are a carrot, then you are a carrot...". CAA displays the best effectiveness-fluency trade-off among steering methods.

**Finding 2 — Instruction-tuned models resist steering.** On IT model variants (bottom row of Fig. 1), all three activation steering methods show Concept Injection scores barely above unconditioned baselines — steering essentially fails. Continuations from IT models after steering are largely indistinguishable from unconditioned IT model outputs. This is practically critical: IT models are the ones deployed as chatbots that most need controllability.

**Finding 3 — Prompting and SFT win on injection, lose on removal.** Simple prompting and LoRA SFT produce concept-relevant, fluent continuations that outperform activation steering methods on concept injection. However, for toxicity mitigation (concept removal), prompting and SFT score *below* all activation steering methods. The conclusion of Wu et al. (2025) that prompting beats steering is confirmed for injection but does not generalize to removal.

**Finding 4 — Perplexity is a bad proxy; Type-Token Ratio is better.** Perplexity has Spearman ρ=0 with LLM-as-judge Fluency scores. Low perplexity can result from highly repetitive, non-fluent text (predictable = low perplexity), while high perplexity can result from stylistically complex but perfectly fluent text (SFT-conditioned outputs). Type-Token Ratio (lexical diversity) achieves ρ=0.81 with Fluency and is a far better proxy.

**Finding 5 — Cheap metrics match expensive LLM-as-judge.** Concept Similarity (SBERT) achieves Spearman ρ=0.97 with LLM-judge Concept Injection scores. ToxClass achieves ρ=0.96 with Concept Removal. These cheap metrics can replace the computationally expensive LLM-as-judge for benchmarking purposes, while also providing interpretable insight into generation behavior.

**Finding 6 — Larger models are harder to steer.** Among activation steering methods, effectiveness generally decreases as model size increases.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** Moderate-to-good. The most novel contributions are: (1) the systematic joint evaluation of effectiveness and fluency, which the field has neglected; (2) the concrete demonstration that activation steering fails on instruction-tuned models; (3) the debunking of perplexity as a fluency proxy and promotion of Type-Token Ratio. The methods themselves are not new — this is a benchmark/analysis paper.

**Rigor:** Good. The human validation of LLM-as-judge (1,200 rated samples, linear kappa reported per dimension) is methodologically sound. The correlation analysis between cheap and expensive metrics is well-executed. The choice to evaluate on both base and IT model variants is crucial and shows good experimental design.

**Conflict of interest:** Several authors (Rodriguez, Suau, Blaas, Zappella) are co-inventors of Linear AcT (Rodriguez et al., 2025a), one of the methods being evaluated. While CAA is reported as the best-tradeoff steering method rather than Linear AcT, readers should be aware of this overlap.

**Reproducibility:** Good. Code is released on GitHub, datasets are publicly available (RTP, Qwen3, Smollm3), hyperparameters are reported, and prompts/concepts are detailed in the appendix.

**Scope limitations:** Concept removal is tested only for toxicity mitigation — a single concept type. The paper's removal conclusions rest on one task domain. Concept injection covers 60 concepts across 10 categories, which is broader but still limited to the relatively small Mistral-7B-instruct-generated synthetic corpus.

**Red flags:** The instruction-tuned models tested are relatively small (up to 14B). The finding that IT models resist steering may be partially architecture/training-data specific. The authors acknowledge they did not test "humongous" models and call for replication at larger scale.

## Strengths

- Directly addresses a real methodological gap: the field measures effectiveness while ignoring fluency, and this paper rigorously quantifies the cost
- The instruction-tuning + steering interaction is practically important and was previously overlooked; this is the paper's most impactful finding
- Rigorous LLM-as-judge validation against human annotations (linear kappa scores reported per dimension)
- The metric correlation analysis (cheap vs. expensive) provides actionable guidance: researchers can replace costly LLM judges with Concept Similarity and Type-Token Ratio
- Reproducibility: full code, datasets, and prompts are released
- Clear, honest conclusion section that identifies limitations and future work

## Limitations

- Concept removal tested only for toxicity — generalizability to other removal tasks (e.g., removing a language, a persona, a style) is untested
- Model scale: largest model tested is 14B; behavior of larger models (70B+) under these conditioning methods is unknown
- The LLM-as-judge itself (OLMo2-32B-IT) has known biases; 72% of inputs received maximum Instruction Following score, suggesting a ceiling effect
- Instruction Following metric shows lower human-LLM agreement (kappa 0.456 vs. 0.779 for Concept Injection), partly due to the judge's tendency to overrate certain continuation styles
- SFT uses a very small LoRA rank (r=2) with only 400 training sentences and 30 steps; results may not represent the full potential of fine-tuning approaches
- Activation steering is a broad category; results depend on the three specific algorithms tested and may not extend to all possible steering approaches

## Relevance

本文对大型语言模型的可控生成与安全部署具有直接意义，特别是关于激活引导在指令微调模型上失效的发现，以及廉价文本指标替代LLM-as-judge的方法，对研究LLM对齐、概念消除（如毒性缓解）和评估体系建设的研究者均有重要参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Do the instruction-tuned model resistance results hold for RLHF-trained models (e.g., GPT-4, Claude) vs. SFT-only instruction-tuned models? Is RLHF specifically responsible for the resistance?
2. Can the effectiveness-fluency trade-off be directly optimized — e.g., training a steering vector that maximizes Concept Injection while minimizing POS KL divergence?
3. For concept removal beyond toxicity (e.g., removing a language, removing a persona), do prompting and SFT still fail while activation steering succeeds?
4. The paper finds that larger base models are harder to steer — is this a monotonic relationship, and does it eventually converge to the same near-zero effectiveness seen in IT models?
5. Given that Type-Token Ratio is the best cheap fluency proxy, could it serve as an auxiliary training objective to help steering methods preserve fluency while maintaining effectiveness?
6. The code is available at apple/ml-act — are the exact concept lists and prompt sets released, enabling direct benchmark comparison for future methods?
