Key Finding

10 soft-prompt vectors + 80 gradient steps disentangle format skill from knowledge — enabling fair base-model benchmarking at near-zero cost.

Soft-Prompt Tuning for Fair and Efficient LLM Benchmark Evaluation ↗

Why It Matters

Standard benchmarks conflate formatting skill with knowledge — systematically penalizing base models. This protocol isolates true knowledge signal, enabling cheap pre-training recipe selection without full post-training.

Critical Edge

The "format-without-knowledge" guarantee is empirically motivated but not theoretically proven — the budget sufficiency must be re-validated for each new benchmark, and Math500 already shows it can fall short for complex LaTeX-formatted tasks.

01Methodology
S* = argmin_S Σ −log P_θ(y|S, E(x)) frozen θ · L=10 vectors · T=80 steps

Steps T80
Batch8

OptimizerAdamW
Init σ0.02

Key insight: the parameter budget is deliberately far below what is needed to encode benchmark-specific knowledge. The soft-prompt prepends Z = [S ; E(X)] to frozen token embeddings, optimized by NLL on a held-out train split. The flat knowledge-accuracy curves across all 80 steps empirically validate the "format-only" claim.

Two metrics: closed-ended datasets use log-likelihood for knowledge + exact-match generation for format; open-ended use LLM-as-a-judge (Qwen3-32B) with separate prompts — one ignores format, one ignores correctness.

H1 Format without Knowledge: soft-prompt saturates format compliance within the budget while knowledge accuracy stays flat.
H2 Reliable Rankings: once format is saturated, base-model rankings align with post-trained ground-truth rankings.
02Results
✓
H1 Confirmed — Format saturates, knowledge stays flat
Closed-ended: format hits ~100% by step 40; knowledge flat all 120 steps across all 7 models.
Open-ended: format reaches 80–100% by step 80. Step 80 = canonical checkpoint.
Even post-trained models show residual format gap at zero/few-shot; soft-prompt closes it to near-100%.
✓
H2 Confirmed — Rankings align with ground truth
Soft-prompted base model exact-match accuracy aligns closest to GT (soft-prompted post-trained) across all 7×7 model-dataset combos.
Bubble-sort swap distance lowest for soft-prompted base models across nearly all datasets.
Zero/few-shot fail due to score compression — all models score ~0, rankings collapse to alphabetical artifacts.
LoRA replication (Appendix A.10) confirms findings are not artifact of soft-prompt mechanics.
Exception — Math500: the 80-step / 10-vector budget appears insufficient for complex multi-step LaTeX formatting; few-shot shows marginal advantage here.
03Strengths & Limitations
Strengths
Falsifiable H1/H2 structure, systematically validated across 7 models × 7 datasets × 3 seeds.
Extreme efficiency: 640 samples, ~0.0006% parameters, single GPU.
Architecture-agnostic across four distinct model families (Llama-3, Qwen-3, OLMo-3, Mistral-3).
LoRA replication strengthens claim that method is not PEFT-specific.
"Score compression" diagnosis explains why zero/few-shot rankings fail — concrete and insightful.
Full judge prompt templates reproduced; high reproducibility from spec alone.
Limitations
Math500 exception suggests budget insufficient for complex LaTeX formatting tasks.
Only 7 benchmarks — no code generation, long-form, or safety-relevant datasets.
Heterogeneous post-training recipes across model pairs introduce ranking confounds.
H1 claim cleanest for closed-ended only; LLM-judge cannot fully disentangle format vs. knowledge for open-ended.
T=80 step choice empirically motivated but not fully ablated per benchmark.
No code released at preprint time.
04Open Questions
The paper shows 10 vectors / 80 steps suffice for factual QA and MCQ, but Math500's multi-step LaTeX format is an outlier. A principled budget-discovery method per benchmark (e.g., early-stopping on format accuracy) is identified as future work but not provided.
Currently each benchmark gets its own soft-prompt. If format structure generalizes across tasks (e.g., all short-answer factual QA), reuse could cut evaluation cost further — but this is not tested in the paper.
The claim is supported for standard SFT/RLHF on factual benchmarks. Capability-expanding methods like RLVR for math reasoning may genuinely create knowledge, challenging the hypothesis — not tested here.
Models tested range 1B–14B. The paper does not perform an explicit scale ablation — it's plausible format-following is comparably easy across scales, but 70B+ models are unexplored territory.
The paper makes a strong case for adoption in pre-training labs. Public leaderboards raise additional governance questions — who maintains the judge model, how to prevent gaming via extended soft-prompt tuning, and whether 640 samples is a meaningful overhead at scale.
