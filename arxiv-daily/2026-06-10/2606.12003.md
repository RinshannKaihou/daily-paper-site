> **一句话总结：** 提出基于嵌入空间聚类的无训练自一致性方法（EBA），在代码生成（HumanEval）和数学推理（MATH500）上分别超越随机选择最多4和17个百分点，并发现表示空间中几何中心位置与生成质量强相关。

> **TL;DR:** EBA (Embedding-Based Agreement) is a training-free inference-time selection method that clusters LLM samples in embedding space and picks the centroid of the dominant cluster, extending self-consistency from exact-match categorical tasks to open-ended generation (code, summarization). It consistently outperforms random selection and shows more stable scaling than LLM-judge (USC) or uncertainty-based (SCe) baselines, and works even with native hidden representations of the generating model.

## 核心贡献

- **问题扩展：** 将自一致性（Self-Consistency）从多选题/精确匹配场景推广至代码生成（HumanEval）、文本摘要（CNN/DM）和数学推理（MATH500）等开放生成任务。
- **EBA 方法：** 无需训练，对 N 个采样生成结果进行嵌入，通过凝聚聚类（余弦距离 + Silhouette 自动确定聚类数）找到主导聚类，选取最接近其质心的生成结果作为最终输出。
- **几何自一致性假设：** 将一致性重新定义为生成分布的几何属性——语义兼容的生成聚集在嵌入空间的稠密区域，不一致的生成出现在边缘。
- **与经典自一致性的统一：** 在精确答案场景（MATH500 提取答案）下，EBA 的缩放曲线与多数投票精确匹配，证明 EBA 是经典 SC 的连续域推广。
- **中心位置与质量相关性：** 图6显示，选取最接近全局质心的生成结果（Nearest to centroid）性能与 EBA 几乎相同，而选取最远离质心的生成结果（Farthest from centroid）在 n=256 时准确率下降近20个百分点——强有力地支持了几何假设。

## Key Contributions

- **Scope extension:** Lifts self-consistency from closed-form answer spaces (majority voting over categories) to open-ended generation (code, summarization) where lexically distinct outputs can be semantically equivalent.
- **EBA algorithm:** Agglomerative clustering (cosine distance, Silhouette-based automatic cluster count) over N sampled generation embeddings; selects the sample nearest to the centroid of the dominant (largest) cluster. Training-free and model-agnostic. Code: https://github.com/hitz-zentroa/EBA
- **Geometric hypothesis formalized:** Consistency modeled as a property of the geometric distribution Φ(x) = {φ(g₁), ..., φ(gₙ)} in ℝᵈ — semantically coherent outputs form concentrated modes while inconsistent ones are peripheral and sparse.
- **Stability findings:** Agreement signals hold across Llama 3.1-8B, Llama 3.1-70B, Qwen 3-8B, Qwen 3-32B; across Qwen-Embedding-8B, Gemma embeddings, and native last-token hidden representations.
- **Geometric-quality correlation:** Selecting nearest-to-global-centroid achieves nearly identical performance to EBA (demonstrating the geometry contains the signal); selecting farthest-from-centroid degrades accuracy by ~20pp at n=256.

## Methodology

**EBA Pipeline:**
1. Sample N generations {y₁, ..., yₙ} from the LLM at temperature T=0.7, top_p=1.0 (pool of 512; bootstrap 50 subsets per reported result).
2. Encode each generation using an embedding function φ(·) — either a pretrained external embedder (Qwen-Embedding-8B or Gemma) or the LLM's own final hidden representation (last sampled token).
3. Cluster embeddings using agglomerative hierarchical clustering (average linkage, cosine distance); number of clusters determined automatically via Silhouette analysis.
4. Identify the dominant cluster (largest count); select the generation closest to its centroid as the final output.

**Special case for HumanEval:** Clustering is applied over extracted final answers (short strings) rather than full generations, to avoid confounding effects of reasoning trace variation.

**Baselines:**
- **Random selection** — weakest, but meaningful lower bound.
- **Universal Self-Consistency (USC)** (Chen et al., 2023) — prompts the LLM itself to identify the most consistent response; constrained by context length; degrades with larger n.
- **Self-Certainty (SCe)** (Kang et al., 2026) — token-level uncertainty signal; generally competitive but less stable at large n.
- **Classical SC** — majority voting over extracted answers (MATH500 only).

**Datasets:** HumanEval (code, accuracy metric), MATH500 (math reasoning, accuracy), CNN/DailyMail (summarization, Rouge-1).

**Models:** Generation: Llama 3.1-8B, Llama 3.1-70B, Qwen 3-8B, Qwen 3-32B. Embedding: Qwen-Embedding-8B (primary), Gemma embeddings, native hidden reps. Generation via vLLM; native hidden reps via Hugging Face.

## Results & Highlights

- **Code generation (HumanEval):** EBA improves over random selection by up to **+4 pp** (Qwen 8B) and is consistently superior to USC and SCe, which plateau or degrade at high n. USC loses ~2pp at large n on Llama.
- **Math reasoning (MATH500):** EBA improves by up to **+17 pp** over random, shows strong near-monotonic scaling out to n=256. SCe peaks around n=16 then drops by ~4pp on Llama 8B (MATH500).
- **Summarization (CNN/DM):** Smaller but consistent gains (~+1 Rouge-1 point), consistent with the dense output space that makes clustering harder to differentiate.
- **Native hidden representations (Figure 4):** EBA with native embeddings is typically only 1–2 pp below external embedder performance, and still substantially above random — making it a practical cost-saving option.
- **Centroid proximity experiment (Figure 6):** Nearest-to-centroid ≈ EBA across all n; Farthest-from-centroid degrades ~20 pp at n=256 on MATH500. This dramatic asymmetry is the paper's sharpest analytical result.
- **EBA (Full Ans) vs. EBA (Extracted Ans) on MATH500 (Figure 5):** Full-generation agreement consistently underperforms extracted-answer agreement, showing embeddings are sensitive to reasoning trace variation even when final answers match — an important nuance.
- **Larger models:** Improvements are smaller in absolute terms (higher baseline), consistent with diminishing-returns selection; qualitative patterns persist.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** Moderate. The key conceptual move — reframing self-consistency as a geometric property of embedding space — is clean and provides a unifying lens. However, similarity-based voting in embedding space (e.g., Jiang et al., 2025 "semantic voting") and minimum Bayes risk decoding have overlapping ideas. The explicit connection to classical SC via the centroid-equivalence experiment and the "farthest vs. nearest" ablation are the genuinely novel empirical contributions. The claim of a unified geometric theory covering both closed-form and open-ended SC is intellectually interesting but not deeply formalized.

**Rigor:** Good. The bootstrapping strategy (50 iterations over a fixed pool of 512) is methodologically sound and noise-reducing. Experiments span two model families, multiple scales, three embedding spaces, and three tasks. The near-equivalence between EBA and nearest-to-centroid is a legitimate finding that, notably, also reveals EBA's own clustering step may be marginal. The paper is honest about this.

**Reproducibility:** High. Code is released, implementation details are thorough (Appendix A/B), hyperparameters are clearly stated, and all datasets are public benchmarks. The vLLM + HuggingFace stack is standard.

**Significance:** Practically meaningful. Inference-time selection is a real use case; replacing LLM-judge (USC) with a cheaper embedding-clustering approach with better scaling properties is useful. The finding that native hidden reps work nearly as well as external embedders could reduce deployment cost.

**Red flags:**
- The gap between EBA and the "nearest to global centroid" baseline is small. This suggests the clustering step is adding marginal value over simply picking the generation closest to the mean embedding — a simpler baseline. The paper acknowledges this but does not push further.
- Summarization gains are small (+1 Rouge-1); Rouge-1 is a weak metric for semantic quality, and the paper acknowledges that evaluation metrics may under-reward paraphrastic outputs.
- The paper does not compare against minimum Bayes risk (MBR) decoding, which is the most natural prior work for generation selection via pairwise utility.
- "Stable across model families" is tested on Llama and Qwen only — both transformer-based, instruction-tuned, similar scale. True robustness would require architecturally diverse or much larger models.
- The "full-generation EBA underperforms extracted-answer EBA" finding implies the method is sensitive to irrelevant variation in reasoning traces — a limitation for use cases where reasoning structure matters.

## Strengths

- Training-free, model-agnostic, and computationally cheaper than LLM-judge baselines (no additional LLM calls beyond sample generation).
- Native hidden representation variant provides a practical, embedder-free deployment path.
- Agglomerative clustering with Silhouette-based automatic cluster count requires no hyperparameter tuning.
- The geometric framing yields a testable, falsifiable hypothesis — and the centroid experiment is a strong empirical confirmation.
- Agreement signals strengthen monotonically with n (at least up to n=128–256), unlike USC and SCe which peak early — important for high-compute inference regimes.
- Three diverse task types (verifiable + non-verifiable) with appropriate metrics.
- Honest limitations section: acknowledges representation geometry / semantic alignment issues, clustering limitations, and computational overhead.

---

## Limitations

- **Representation geometry ≠ semantic equivalence:** Neural embedding spaces exhibit anisotropy and concentration in narrow high-dimensional cones (Liang et al., 2022), so proximity in embedding space does not always mean semantic equivalence. This can limit agreement estimation precision.
- **Clustering is nearly equivalent to centroid selection:** The small gap between EBA and "nearest to global centroid" suggests current agglomerative clustering may not capture fine-grained local structure of the generation distribution. More expressive geometric models may be needed.
- **Computational overhead:** EBA still requires N samples and N embedding extractions. For large N or long generations, embedding storage and computation can be non-trivial.
- **Scope of evaluation:** Only summarization, code, and math. Dialogue, long-form generation, and agentic reasoning are not tested — the paper explicitly flags this.
- **Reasoning trace sensitivity:** Agreement over full generations is consistently worse than over extracted answers, meaning the method is sensitive to chain-of-thought style variation even when answers agree.
- **No MBR comparison:** Minimum Bayes risk decoding (with an appropriate utility function) is the standard prior work for sample-based generation selection and is absent from the comparison.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How does EBA compare to minimum Bayes risk (MBR) decoding with a semantic similarity utility function — the most natural prior art omitted from the paper?
2. Since nearest-to-global-centroid nearly matches full EBA, is the clustering step necessary at all, or is the paper's real contribution simply "use the mean embedding as a quality proxy"?
3. Can the centrality signal be calibrated into a confidence score for downstream use (e.g., abstention or routing to a stronger model)?
4. Does the geometric-quality correlation persist in settings with genuinely multimodal answer distributions — e.g., multiple correct but structurally distinct code solutions for the same spec?
5. Would EBA benefit from a learned embedding space fine-tuned for semantic equivalence in a target domain (code, math), rather than a general-purpose text embedder?
6. How does EBA interact with chain-of-thought length and style diversity — could diversity-promoting sampling (e.g., different system prompts) improve cluster separability and boost quality further?
