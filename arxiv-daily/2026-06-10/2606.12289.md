> **一句话总结：** 本文提出标准可解释模型（SIM），以拉格朗日力学为基础，将"可解释性"形式化为三类对称性约束（概念语义共享、预测-概念依赖、有界推理），从而演绎推导出架构设计与训练目标，并证明 CLIP/Qwen 等大模型的链式思维解释并不满足可解释性对称条件。

> **TL;DR:** The Standard Interpretable Model (SIM) is a general, deductive theory of interpretable ML grounded in Lagrangian mechanics. It formalizes interpretability as three symmetries derived from user-relative premises, translates those symmetries into constraints and Lagrangians, and provides two concrete paths to satisfy them: parameter-update trajectories (soft) or architectural compilation (hard). Empirically it shows standard metrics like MAE are invalid proxies for interpretability, that chain-of-thought explanations in LLMs are largely independent of predictions, and that large concept-based models depend on only ~500 of 33,000 concepts.

## 核心贡献

- 提出首个从第一原理演绎推导可解释方法的通用理论框架（SIM），以用户为中心参数化可解释性定义。
- 将可解释性形式化为三类群不变对称性：**对称性 I** 概念语义在单调映射下不变；**对称性 II** 预测仅通过概念映射介导；**对称性 III** 概念组合复杂度受用户能力约束。
- 建立拉格朗日 $L = T - V$，其中动能项 $T$ 描述参数动力学，势能项 $V$ 包含可解释性约束，极小值对应最优可解释模型。
- 给出两条通向可解释性的路径：（1）Phase II——将约束加入损失函数，通过优化逐步满足；（2）Phase III——将约束直接编译进模型架构，结构上保证满足。
- 实验揭示：MAE 等标准指标是可解释性的无效代理；CoT 解释在 Qwen2.5 上违规率 > 0.8；Steerling-8B 的下一词预测实质上依赖约 500 个监督概念（1.5%）。
- 提出 PyTorch Concepts（PyC）软件库，将 SIM 的架构方案直接映射为可复用的 Python 模块。

## Key Contributions

- Introduces the Standard Interpretable Model (SIM), the first general deductive theory that systematically yields interpretable methods (architectures, losses, metrics) from user-defined premises.
- Formalizes three interpretability symmetries for a target entity with formal language and bounded computation:
  - **Symmetry I** (concept invariance under monotonic maps): the model's concept scores must induce the same preorder as the human's concept map, up to order-preserving reparametrisation.
  - **Symmetry II** (prediction-concept dependency): task predictions must lie in the image of the concept Jacobian, ensuring predictions depend exclusively on shared concepts.
  - **Symmetry III** (hypothesis invariance under composition transformation): the concept-to-prediction formula must lie in the kernel of a user-specified admissibility operator $K^{[h]}$, bounding reasoning complexity.
- Derives a single Lagrangian $L(\theta_{f,c,\phi}, \mathcal{D}, y, K^{[h]}) = T - V$ that encodes all three constraints and whose equations of motion yield gradient-based training rules directly.
- Derives a closed-form general architecture (Architecture IV, eq. 16) that structurally satisfies all three symmetries simultaneously, subsuming CBMs, SENNs, ProtoPNets, decision trees, and feature attribution as special cases.
- Applies SIM to diagnose and fix limitations in VLMs (CLIP, Moondream2, Qwen2): shows label-free concept maps frequently violate Symmetry I and can be repaired without fine-tuning using ordered prototypes.
- Shows CoT explanations in Qwen2.5 consistently score above 0.8 on the Symmetry II violation metric, providing formal quantitative support for prior qualitative findings of CoT unfaithfulness.
- Releases PyTorch Concepts (PyC), a software library with modular primitives (`ConceptBottleneckModel`, `ConceptEmbeddingModel`, `SteerlingModel`) that implement SIM architectures with built-in intervention support.

## Methodology

The SIM operates in a six-step pipeline (Figure 1):

**Step 1 — Interpretability premises.** The user specifies what interpretability means for a target entity $h$ (human expert, group of users, another AI). For the paper's main instantiation, $h$ is an entity with a fixed symbol vocabulary $W^{[h]}$, concept maps $c_w^{[h]}: Z \to \mathbb{R}$ assigning meaning to each symbol, and a bounded-reasoning operator $K^{[h]}: \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F}$ specifying admissible concept compositions. This makes interpretability explicitly user-relative — different premises yield internally consistent but distinct theories, analogous to how different Euclidean postulates yield different geometries.

**Step 2 — Interpretability symmetries.** Premises are formalised as group invariances. Each symmetry is a transformation group $\mathfrak{g} \in \mathfrak{G}$ acting on input space $Z$ such that the model output $f(z)$ is unchanged. The three derived symmetries are:
- *Symmetry I*: $\chi(c_w) = \chi(\mathfrak{g}_w \circ c_w^{[h]})$ for all strictly monotone $\mathfrak{g}_w$ — the total preorder induced by the model's concept map must match the human's.
- *Symmetry II*: $\exists \mathfrak{g}_c: \mathfrak{g}_c(\nabla_z f)^\top = (\nabla_z f)^\top$ where $\text{im}(\mathfrak{g}_c) \subseteq \text{col}(\nabla_z c)$ — prediction gradients must lie in the column space of the concept Jacobian.
- *Symmetry III*: $K^{[h]}(\phi(c(z; \theta_c); \theta_\phi), \ldots, \nabla_z^{(n)}\phi) = 0$ — the concept composition formula must lie in the kernel of $K^{[h]}$.

**Step 3 — Interpretability constraints.** Each symmetry is translated into an operational constraint $q(z) \leq 0$. For example, Constraint I is $\mathbb{1}_{\Delta c_w^{[h]} > 0} \cdot \gamma(-\Delta c_w) = 0$ (monotonicity violations penalised via hinge loss); Constraint II is $1 - \|Q_c^\top Q_f\|_F^2 / \text{rank}(\nabla_z f) = 0$ (gradient subspace alignment); Constraint III is $K^{[h]}(\phi, \ldots, \nabla_z^{(n)}\phi) = 0$.

**Step 4 — Lagrangian.** The full Lagrangian $L = T - V$ incorporates all three constraints into $V$, with Lagrange multipliers $\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3$ weighting each term alongside the prediction loss $\mathcal{L}$.

**Step 5 — Trajectories (Phase II).** Applying the principle of least action to $\iint L \, dz \, dt$ and discretising yields a momentum-based parameter update rule: $\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + (\theta_t - \theta_{t-1}) - \frac{(\Delta t)^2}{m} \nabla_\theta V(\theta_t)$. This is a gradient descent variant that penalises constraint violations during training but only locally encourages the symmetries.

**Step 6 — Interpretable architectures (Phase III).** Constraints are compiled directly into model structure. For instance, Constraint I is satisfied by construction using instance-based monotone concept maps; Constraint II by making $f = \phi(c_1, \ldots, c_l)$ so $f$ depends on $z$ only through $c$; Constraint III by restricting $\phi_\theta(c) = \sum_k \theta_k \psi_k(c)$ where $\phi_\theta \in \ker(K^{[h]})$. The general Architecture IV combines all three.

**Key distinction between Phase II and III:** Phase II (optimisation) enforces constraints only locally and approximately; the model may violate them elsewhere in input space. Phase III (architectural compilation) guarantees global satisfaction by construction, at the cost of reduced architectural flexibility.

## Results & Highlights

**Validation of Symmetry I (Figure 5):** DNN minimizing MAE achieves low absolute error but frequently reverses the human-induced preorder (highest Constraint I violation rate). DNN+L (loss-penalised) reduces violations. DNN+A (architectural) achieves the lowest violation rate. Key finding: **standard performance metrics such as MAE are inappropriate proxies for interpretability** — a model can fit scores precisely while reversing their semantic ordering.

**Validation of Symmetry II (Figures 6, 7):** DNN+L reduces Constraint II violations near the constrained training point after ~1000 epochs, but the alignment is local only. DNN+A enforces exact global alignment between $f$ and $c$ by construction. Level-set and gradient visualisations confirm that without architectural compilation, task predictions may not actually depend on concepts globally.

**Validation of Symmetry III (Figure 8):** Increasing $\lambda_3$ during DNN+L training progressively restricts the learned concept formula to simpler functions, converging to the architecturally-compiled hypothesis space as $\lambda_3 \to \infty$. The parameter $\lambda_3$ acts as a tunable complexity knob.

**Concept semantics in VLMs (Figure 9):** CLIP, Moondream2, and Qwen2 all produce inconsistent pairwise rankings for "red intensity" on MNIST images. Moondream2 and Qwen2 rankings are asymmetric (comparing A vs B gives different result than B vs A), violating a basic consistency requirement. This exposes a systematic risk in using pre-trained VLMs for concept annotation pipelines.

**Fixing broken semantics without training (Figure 10):** Moondream2's broken concept semantics for "red" can be perfectly repaired without fine-tuning by introducing ordered prototypes (sorted by red intensity) and assigning test images to nearest prototypes. The repaired ranking matches ground truth perfectly, showing the encoder contains the right information but the decoding is flawed.

**CoT explanations are not interpretable (Section 4.3.2):** Qwen2.5 prompted to produce predictions with CoT explanations scores consistently above 0.8 on the Symmetry II violation metric (0 = perfect, 1 = maximally violated). The prediction Jacobian $\nabla f$ is nearly orthogonal to the CoT explanation Jacobian $\nabla c$. This provides formal, quantitative evidence that CoT rationales are largely independent of the computation generating the prediction.

**Concept dependency in Steerling-8B (Figure 11):** Among ~33,000 supervised concepts, next-token predictions depend almost entirely on ~500 concepts (K≈500, ~1.5%) for Constraint II violation to drop below 0.1. Masking to top-16 concepts at test time reduces violation to the theoretical floor $\sqrt{1 - K/16}$, confirming structural concept-mediated prediction. Contrast with CoT: large concept-based models make predictions that genuinely depend on supervised concepts.

**Taxonomy of existing methods (Table 6):** CBMs fully satisfy Symmetry II by architecture but only partially address Symmetry I (via optimisation) and partially address Symmetry III. SAEs (sparse autoencoders) encourage Symmetry I disentanglement but do not constrain Symmetry II or III. Feature attribution methods assume identity concept maps (violating Symmetry I in practice) and do not constrain Symmetry III.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** Genuinely high. The use of Lagrangian mechanics as the unifying mathematical language for interpretability is original, not merely metaphorical — the paper actually derives equations of motion and architectural solutions from variational principles. The three-symmetry decomposition (semantic, structural, bounded-reasoning) is a concrete formal advance over prior conceptual frameworks (Doshi-Velez, Lipton) that identified desiderata without operationalising them.

**Rigor:** Strong in the theoretical sections. The derivations from premises to symmetries to constraints to architecture (Sections 3.1–3.6) are mathematically precise and internally consistent. The general architecture (eq. 16) and its Lagrangian (eq. 17) are explicit. The empirical validation is honest about scope: Section 4.2 explicitly notes the goal is theoretical validation, not SOTA benchmarking.

**Reproducibility:** PyTorch Concepts library is cited but no code repository URL appears in the main text (referenced as Guide Labs, 2026b for Steerling). The paper's controlled experiments (Sections 4.2, 4.3) use synthetic and well-known datasets (MNIST subsets for VLM tests) that should be reproducible. The theoretical framework is precisely enough specified that independent reimplementation is feasible.

**Significance:** Potentially field-defining. If adopted, the SIM would: (1) provide a shared vocabulary for comparing interpretability methods, (2) replace ad-hoc metric choice with principled constraint-derived metrics, (3) expose systematic flaws in widely used approaches (CoT, label-free VLM annotation). The software library lowers the barrier to adoption.

**Red flags:**
- The theory is instantiated for a specific class of target entities ("bounded and formal entities") in Section 3. How well SIM generalises to radically different user models (e.g., non-symbolic users, multi-stakeholder settings) is not fully explored.
- The three premises are reasonable but not derived from deeper axioms — they are still human choices. The paper acknowledges this ("different premises, different theories") but the claim that SIM is a *general* theory must be read with this in mind.
- The CoT experiment (Section 4.3.2) uses Qwen2.5 on an unspecified task type; generalisation across domains and model families is not shown.
- Computational cost of Constraint II (Jacobian computation) scales quadratically with representation dimension, potentially prohibitive for large models without approximation.
- The authors evaluate their own prior models (Steerling, Concept Embedding Models) using their own framework; independent replication of the comparative claims would strengthen the work.

## Strengths

- The Lagrangian formalism is substantive, not cosmetic: equations of motion, principle of least action, and variational optimisation are all genuine mathematical tools here.
- The three-symmetry taxonomy provides a clear, testable checklist for any proposed interpretable method.
- Falsifiability is explicitly addressed (Section 6.1): analytical, empirical, and epistemic falsification axes are defined — a rare and welcome commitment in a theory paper.
- The PyC library bridges theory and practice, making the framework actionable rather than purely conceptual.
- Empirical findings have immediate practical implications: the CoT result and VLM concept semantic failure are both actionable diagnostics for model developers.
- User-awareness (interpretability is relative to $h$) is formally built in, avoiding the common trap of defining interpretability as an absolute property.

## Limitations

- The current instantiation targets users with formal symbol vocabularies and bounded computation; extension to purely neural or sub-symbolic target entities is noted as future work.
- Constraint II (prediction-concept dependency) requires computing Jacobians, which is expensive for large models. The paper uses approximations (99.9%-energy effective rank) for Steerling but full tractability analysis is absent.
- The framework currently handles deterministic, time-invariant models; stochastic or temporally dynamic models are left for future work.
- Whether the three premises constitute an *exhaustive* characterisation of interpretability is openly acknowledged as unresolved (Section 6.3).
- Automation of the derivation pipeline (premises → constraints → architectures) is identified as a future challenge; currently each instantiation requires manual derivation.
- Large-scale architectural compilation (Phase III) for transformer-scale models is not demonstrated.

## Relevance

本文对关注可解释性统一理论、概念模型和大模型诊断的研究者直接相关。SIM 提供的对称性检验框架可直接用于评估现有方法（如链式思维、稀疏自编码器）的可解释性质量，而 PyTorch Concepts 库为构建新型概念架构提供了开箱即用的工具，对机理可解释性和概念瓶颈模型两条研究线均有实质性推进意义。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Can the derivation pipeline (premises → symmetries → constraints → architectures) be automated, e.g., via LLM-assisted symbolic derivation or program synthesis?
2. How does the SIM handle multi-stakeholder interpretability, where different target entities $h_1, h_2$ induce conflicting symmetry requirements on the same model?
3. For transformer-scale models, can Constraint II (Jacobian alignment) be approximated efficiently enough for use as a training signal, or only as a post-hoc diagnostic?
4. Does satisfying all three symmetries by architectural compilation (Phase III) incur a systematic capacity penalty relative to unconstrained models, and how does this trade off against interpretability gains?
5. The paper shows CoT explanations fail Symmetry II in Qwen2.5; do any existing LLM explanation methods (e.g., attribution-based, attention-based) satisfy Symmetry II, and at what cost?
