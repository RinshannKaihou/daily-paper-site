> **一句话总结：** 通过216小时跨六种部署配置的实验，发现GPU上的LLM推理服务（vLLM、NVIDIA Triton）普遍存在统计显著的宿主内存泄漏，泄漏率在不同部署配置间相差近两个数量级，且内存老化源于服务运行时层而非模型推理路径。

> **TL;DR:** A 216-hour empirical campaign across six co-located LLM serving deployments (vLLM standalone, Triton-wrapped vLLM, and a naive PyTorch/HuggingFace baseline) demonstrates that statistically significant process-private memory aging is universal in GPU-based LLM serving. Leak rates span nearly two orders of magnitude across configurations and originate in the runtime/orchestration layer, not the shared model inference path. Triton wrapping the legacy vLLM V0 engine is the worst offender (+157 KB/h), exhibiting a distinctive step-wise growth pattern, while vLLM V1 standalone is the cleanest (+1.8 KB/h). Critically, none of this aging is visible through client-facing latency or throughput metrics.

## 核心贡献

- **首次将软件老化（SAR）方法论应用于GPU上的LLM服务引擎**，在216小时实验（覆盖六种部署配置）中系统性地表征了内存老化现象。
- **发现内存老化的根源在于服务运行时层**，而非模型推理路径本身——三种部署共享相同的推理计算，但内存泄漏率相差近两个数量级。
- **2×2 因子实验**解耦了vLLM引擎代际（V0 vs. V1）与托管层（standalone vs. Triton）的影响，发现V1引擎比V0泄漏显著更少，Triton封装会进一步放大泄漏。
- **Triton+V0 组合表现出独特的阶梯式内存增长**（RSS与VMS同步离散跳升），与其他配置的平滑漂移截然不同，提示存在不同的底层机制。
- **提出了一套可复现的统计流水线**：Hamed-Rao修正的Mann-Kendall趋势检验 + Theil-Sen斜率估计 + Benjamini-Hochberg FDR控制，专门处理监控时序数据的强自相关性问题。

## Key Contributions

- **First application of software aging and rejuvenation (SAR) methodology to GPU-based LLM serving engines**, spanning six deployment configurations over 216 total hours of continuous operation under Poisson stress load.
- **Empirical proof that memory aging exists universally** in modern LLM serving: every deployment shows a small but statistically significant process-private memory (USS) leak after FDR correction.
- **Localization of the aging surface to the runtime/orchestration layer**, not the shared CUDA inference path. The two-orders-of-magnitude variation in leak rate across same-model deployments rules out the model or inference kernel as the source.
- **Factorial disentanglement of engine generation vs. hosting layer**: V1 engine leaks 7× less than V0 in standalone mode and 27× less under Triton; Triton amplifies leaks for both engine generations.
- **Discovery of a qualitatively distinct step-wise aging pattern** in Triton+V0 (E2), where RSS and VMS grow in correlated discrete jumps (largest increment ~1.8 MB, lag-0 correlation ρ ≈ 0.83), suggesting periodic anonymous memory mappings that are never released.
- **Demonstrates that client-side metrics (latency, throughput, drop rate) are insensitive to this aging** over 36-hour windows — a practical warning for production monitoring.

## Methodology

**Experimental Design.** Six 36-hour aging runs on three NVIDIA L40S GPUs (46 GB VRAM each), all serving Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct in bf16. Configurations:

| ID | Engine | Hosting | vLLM ver. | RPS target |
|-----|--------|---------|-----------|------------|
| E1 | vLLM V1 | standalone | 0.21.0 | 2.55 |
| E2 | vLLM V0 | Triton 25.09 | 0.10.1.1+nv | 2.17 |
| E3 | PyTorch+HF | FastAPI | n/a | 0.17 |
| E3b | PyTorch+HF | FastAPI | n/a | 0.05 (low load ablation) |
| A1 | vLLM V0 | standalone | 0.7.3 | 0.80 |
| A2 | vLLM V1 | Triton 25.09 | 0.10.1.1+nv | 1.75 |

The three primary engines run concurrently (one per GPU on the same host), reproducing a realistic multi-tenant deployment. A1 and A2 complete a 2×2 factorial crossing engine generation (V0/V1) × hosting layer (standalone/Triton).

**Workload.** Open-loop Poisson client with prompts from 3,000 arXiv abstracts; prompt lengths log-normal (median 1,500 tokens, up to 7,500), output lengths log-normal (median 200, max 1,500). Each engine is driven at 85% of its mechanical throughput ceiling to ensure sustained stress.

**Monitoring (34 indicators/run):**
- GPU: NVML at 1 Hz — VRAM, utilization, temperature, power, clocks, ECC
- Process: psutil at 5 s — USS, RSS, VMS, PSS, threads, FDs, CPU, I/O
- System: psutil at 5 s — host memory, swap, load, CPU
- Client: per-request timestamps, latency, TTFT, token counts, status

USS (Unique Set Size, process-private resident memory) is used as the primary leak indicator.

**Statistical Pipeline.** All traces are strongly autocorrelated (ρ ≈ 0.99). The pipeline uses:
1. Mann-Kendall trend test with Hamed-Rao autocorrelation correction (inflates test variance by effective sample size from the empirical ACF)
2. Theil-Sen slope estimator with exact confidence intervals from pairwise slopes (variance inflated by AR(1) factor (1+ρ)/(1−ρ))
3. Benjamini-Hochberg FDR control at q = 0.10 across ~200 tests
4. A trend is declared significant only when both the MK test rejects the null AND the 95% Theil-Sen CI excludes zero.

## Results & Highlights

**Memory Aging is Universal.** All six deployments show statistically significant USS growth after FDR correction. Autocorrelation near 1 (ρ ≈ 0.99) confirms slow, roughly linear growth over 36 hours.

**Leak Rates Across Primary Deployments (USS Theil-Sen slope/h, 95% CI):**
- E1 vLLM V1 standalone: +1.8 KB/h [0.7, 23.8 KB]
- E2 Triton + vLLM V0: +157 KB/h [33, 228 KB]
- E3 PyTorch + HF naive: +31 KB/h [21, 88 KB]

The ordering is counterintuitive: the most optimized engine (vLLM standalone) leaks least; the simplest (naive PyTorch) is in the middle; the most production-hardened orchestration stack (Triton+V0) leaks most.

**2×2 Factorial Results:**

|  | V0 engine | V1 engine |
|--|-----------|-----------|
| Standalone | A1: +13 KB/h | E1: +1.8 KB/h |
| Triton-wrapped | E2: +157 KB/h | A2: +5.9 KB/h |

V1 is 7× cleaner than V0 in standalone; 27× cleaner under Triton. Triton amplifies leaks for both generations.

**Load Independence.** E3b (PyTorch at 0.05 RPS, well below saturation) leaks *more* (+103 KB/h vs. +31 KB/h), confirming the leak is driven by elapsed time, not request volume or queue effects.

**Step-Wise vs. Smooth Growth.** E2 (Triton+V0) shows discrete memory jumps (top-1% increments ~1.8 MB, ρ_RSS,VMS ≈ 0.83), while all other configurations drift smoothly. This suggests periodic OS-level anonymous memory mappings that are never returned.

**GPU VRAM: No Trend.** Despite heterogeneous request sizes, VRAM occupancy is stationary in all deployments — the paged KV-cache management successfully contains device-side fragmentation over 36 hours.

**Client-Side: Invisible Aging.** Latency (p50/p95/p99), TTFT, throughput, and drop rate are stationary in virtually all runs. The ~5 ms/h p50 latency drift in E1 is statistically significant but operationally negligible (~2% over 36 hours).

**Practical Implication.** Extrapolating Theil-Sen rates to 30-day deployments: E1 accumulates ~1 MB, E3 ~22 MB, E2 ~110 MB of private memory per replica. At current rates none constitutes an immediate operational threat, but the step-wise E2 pattern could be unbounded.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty.** Genuinely novel contribution: the first SAR study targeting GPU-resident, framework-mediated LLM serving with multi-engine factorial comparison. Prior SAR work on LLM/AI systems was either CPU-only (Santos et al. 2026) or studied aging in LLM-generated code rather than serving engines. The framing and gap identification are clean.

**Rigor.** The statistical methodology is above average for the SAR field. The Hamed-Rao correction for autocorrelated Mann-Kendall and Benjamini-Hochberg FDR control are appropriate choices that most SAR papers omit. The 2×2 factorial design to disentangle confounds is thoughtful. However, there are real limitations:
- **Single-run design**: each configuration runs only once. The reported CIs capture within-run variance, not run-to-run variability. This is a significant caveat for slope estimates.
- **Version confound in the factorial**: the 2×2 spans three different vLLM versions (0.7.3, 0.10.1.1+nv, 0.21.0) because the legacy V0 engine cannot be selected in recent releases. Engine generation and version cannot be cleanly separated. The authors acknowledge this honestly.
- **Workload artificiality**: synthetic Poisson arrivals from arXiv abstracts do not capture real-world burstiness, prompt caching effects, or temporal locality. Some aging mechanisms may only emerge (or disappear) under production traffic.

**Reproducibility.** The paper describes a clear methodology with fixed indicator lists (de facto pre-registration), specific RPS targets, hardware specs, and software versions. The 36-hour/216-hour campaign is substantial. The authors explicitly commit to a reproducible framework. However, the actual code and monitoring scripts are not linked in the paper.

**Significance.** Practically significant for production LLM operators: the finding that standard client-side monitoring (latency/throughput) is blind to internal memory aging is an important operational insight. The Triton+V0 step-wise pattern is a concrete red flag for long-running deployments. The quantitative leak rates are useful baseline references.

**Red Flags.**
- The wide confidence intervals on some slopes (E1: [0.7, 23.8 KB/h]) suggest the point estimates are noisy — replication is essential before relying on specific numbers.
- No source-code attribution of the leaks (heap profiling is listed as future work). The paper characterizes but does not explain the aging mechanism.
- The paper is explicitly framed as "preliminary" — appropriate scientific humility, but limits actionability.
- 36 hours may be too short to observe late-onset aging; the authors acknowledge that effects building up over days or weeks may not yet be visible.

## Strengths

- Clean, honest scientific framing: the authors clearly scope what is and isn't shown.
- Methodological rigor substantially above the SAR field norm (autocorrelation correction, FDR control, pre-registered indicator list).
- The 2×2 factorial adds real inferential power that a simple comparison of E1 vs. E2 would lack.
- Counterintuitive result (optimized engine leaks least; orchestration layer dominates) is well-supported by the factorial evidence.
- The qualitative distinction between smooth-drift and step-wise growth in E2 is a novel empirical observation with practical relevance.
- Thorough threats-to-validity section; authors do not oversell findings.

## Limitations

- Single run per configuration: no run-to-run variability estimate; slope CIs are within-run only.
- 36-hour window may miss slow-onset or periodic aging effects.
- Version confound in the factorial prevents clean attribution of V0 vs. V1 engine generation effects.
- Synthetic workload: production traffic patterns (bursty arrivals, prompt repetition, cache hits) may change the aging signature.
- Single hardware platform (L40S) and single model (Qwen2.5-7B); generalization to A100/H100/B200 or larger models is unverified.
- No mechanism identification: heap profiling needed to explain *why* Triton+V0 produces step-wise growth.
- No rejuvenation strategies are proposed or evaluated — the paper characterizes the problem but does not solve it.

## 相关性

本文直接与大型语言模型推理服务的系统可靠性相关，对关注vLLM、NVIDIA Triton等推理引擎长期稳定性和资源管理的研究者具有实践参考价值；尤其是内存老化不可见于延迟/吞吐指标这一发现，对LLM生产部署的监控策略设计有重要启示。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. What is the specific source of the step-wise memory growth in Triton+V0? Heap profiling at the allocation level could identify whether this is a Python-side object accumulation, a C++ runtime issue, or OS-level anonymous mapping behavior in the Triton server.
2. Do these leak rates and patterns generalize to larger models (e.g., 70B, 405B parameters) and newer GPU architectures (H100, B200)? The KV-cache pressure and memory management behavior may differ substantially.
3. How do real production traffic patterns — bursty arrivals, shared prefix caching, high prompt repetition — affect the aging signature? Synthetic Poisson loads may suppress or amplify specific mechanisms.
4. What is the leak rate after n ≥ 3 independent replications per configuration? The single-run design leaves run-to-run variance unquantified; this is the most urgent empirical gap.
5. Does software rejuvenation (periodic restart or warm reload) effectively mitigate the observed aging? At what interval is rejuvenation cost-justified given the measured leak rates?
6. The paper notes that memory growth in PyTorch deployments is driven primarily by elapsed time rather than request volume. Is there a background thread or allocator behavior in the CUDA caching allocator that accumulates virtual address space reservations regardless of request load?
7. Given that the vLLM V1 engine is a nearly complete redesign of the scheduler and request lifecycle, can specific V1 changes (e.g., async task management, new resource acquisition/release paths) be credited with the 7–27× reduction in leak rate?
