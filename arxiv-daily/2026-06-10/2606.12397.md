> **一句话总结：** 提出流形幂迭代（MPI）路由器设计方法，通过单步幂迭代+L2范数收缩将路由器行向量对齐至对应专家矩阵的主奇异方向，在3B/11B规模预训练中实现~1.04x训练加速、25项下游基准平均约2分提升，且训练/推理开销几乎为零。

> **TL;DR:** MPI redesigns MoE router rows to converge toward the principal singular direction of their associated expert weight matrices via a one-step "Power-then-Retract" paradigm. Validated at 1B–11B scale on 350B tokens, it delivers ~1.04x faster convergence, improved downstream performance across 25 benchmarks, better load balancing, and near-zero overhead — all from a principled linear-algebraic router design that is plug-in compatible with existing MoE architectures.

## 核心贡献

- **识别根本缺陷**：指出现有MoE路由器行向量缺乏设计原则来编码专家信息，导致token-专家亲和度估计不准确，影响收敛和模型能力
- **理论动机**：将路由器行向量对齐至专家权重矩阵（W_g）的主奇异向量，等价于在Rayleigh商意义下最大化路由器对专家矩阵的投影，即最优信息压缩
- **流形幂迭代（MPI）**：提出"幂迭代-收缩"两步范式——先对路由权重执行一步幂迭代（R̂ = R·W_g·W_g^T），再通过L2范数收缩（R' = C·R̂/||R̂||）约束权重规模，C = C'/√N自适应于专家数N
- **理论等价性证明**：证明MPI更新等价于在最小更新原则下最大化路由器对专家权重投影的最速上升优化，确保路由器行向量自适应收敛至专家主奇异方向
- **大规模实验验证**：在FineWeb-Edu数据集上，以1B/3B/11B参数规模预训练350B tokens，跨AdamW/Muon/AdamH/MuonH四种优化器验证有效性

## Key Contributions

- Identifies the fundamental absence of design constraints on router rows and establishes the principled objective: each router row should align with the principal singular direction of its associated expert matrix
- Introduces **Manifold Power Iteration (MPI)**: a single power iteration step (`R_hat = R @ Wg @ Wg^T`) followed by L2 retraction (`R' = C * R_hat / ||R_hat||`), with `C := C'/sqrt(N)` to decouple scaling from expert count N
- Proves theoretically that MPI is equivalent to a steepest ascent optimizer maximizing the router's projection onto the expert weight manifold under a minimal-update principle, confirming adaptive convergence to principal singular directions
- Shows empirically that MPI achieves ~1.04x faster convergence, +2 average accuracy points across 25 downstream benchmarks, and improved load balancing at 3B and 11B scales — with only 0.2% training throughput overhead and zero inference overhead
- Demonstrates that MPI is optimizer-agnostic (validated across AdamW, Muon, AdamH, MuonH) and plug-in compatible with auxiliary load-balance losses and z-loss

## Methodology

**Core Motivation.** The MoE router is a weight matrix R ∈ R^{N×D}, where each row R_{[i]} acts as a proxy for the i-th expert. Token routing assigns to the top-K experts by highest inner products with R. Ideally, R_{[i]} should maximally encode the i-th expert's weight matrix W*_i. The Eckart-Young theorem implies the best single-vector representation of a matrix is its principal singular vector — the direction maximizing the squared projection (Rayleigh quotient). Conventional router designs impose no constraint to enforce this alignment, leaving a theoretical gap between router representations and expert identity.

**MPI Algorithm.** For each router row R_{[i]} and its associated expert gate weight W^i_g:

1. **Power Iteration step**: Compute R̂_{[i]} = R_{[i]} · W^i_g · (W^i_g)^T. This is a single matrix-vector product that progressively rotates R_{[i]} toward the dominant singular vector of W^i_g through the training process.

2. **Retraction step**: Normalize R'_{[i]} = C · R̂_{[i]} / ||R̂_{[i]}||_2, where C = C'/√N and C' is a scale-invariant hyperparameter (default C'=2 found via small-scale sweep). The division by √N decouples the routing logit scale from expert count N, preventing explosion as N grows.

The final gating weights are recomputed using the updated router: w' = Softmax(TopK(x · R'^T)).

**Implementation** (see Figure 1 in paper): The entire modification is a 2-line change in the MoE forward pass — one matrix multiplication for power iteration and one call to `F.normalize` for retraction. The `C_prime` hyperparameter scales the retraction. The pseudo-code uses standard PyTorch and is compatible with Megablocks.

**Theoretical Grounding.** Via first-order Taylor expansion and projection onto the spherical manifold tangent space, the MPI update Δr_M approximates the steepest ascent direction Δr_g = η·G·(I - R'^T_{[i]}R'_{[i]}) for the maximum projection objective. As training progresses, the update magnitude automatically decreases as R'_{[i]} aligns with the principal singular direction — a self-regulating convergence property.

**Why one iteration suffices.** The paper ablates increasing iterations to 10 for full convergence: this costs 5% throughput and actually *hurts* performance (pretraining loss +0.002–0.003, downstream accuracy −1.39 pp). The explanation is that aggressive alignment disrupts the stability of router optimization during training; single-step iteration provides a balanced, stable signal.

**Expert weight choice.** Three candidate matrices (W_g, W_p, W_o in GLU experts) are ablated; W_g is adopted as default with marginal advantages across setups.

## Results & Highlights

**Convergence (1B scale, Figure 2):** MoE with MPI achieves 0.013 lower pretraining loss than the MuonH baseline, consistent across all four optimizer variants. The loss advantage is maintained throughout training.

**Downstream benchmarks (Table 1, 25 tasks, 1B scale):** Average accuracy improvements across all optimizers:
- AdamW: 42.26 → 43.56 (+1.30 pp)
- AdamH: 42.59 → 43.93 (+1.34 pp)
- Muon: 43.01 → 43.55 (+0.54 pp)
- MuonH: 42.78 → 43.98 (+1.20 pp)

**Large-scale (3B and 11B, Table 3, 7 challenging benchmarks at 350B tokens):**
- 3B: MoE 36.37 → MoE w. MPI 38.70 AVG (+2.33 pp), with gains on ARC-C, MMLU, TriviaQA, NaturalQs, BBH, GSM8K, MBPP
- 11B: MoE 40.92 → MoE w. MPI 42.76 AVG (+1.84 pp)

**Perplexity (Table 2):** On validation, Math, and Code sets from OLMo 3:
- 3B: validation PPL 0.764→0.754, Math 1.688→1.581, Code 1.376→1.296
- 11B: validation PPL 0.728→0.723, Math 1.852→1.581, Code 1.263→1.259

**Convergence speed (Figure 3a):** MoE with MPI is approximately **1.04x faster** to reach the same loss at 11B scale.

**Load balancing (Table 4, Figure 4):** MPI reduces both MaxVio_Batch (1.133→1.024) and MaxVio_Global (0.964→0.711) at 3B scale, indicating more equitable expert utilization — attributed to the retraction design. This is an unexpected bonus beyond the stated goal.

**Router-expert alignment (Table 5):** The projection metric λ (Equation 11) increases dramatically with MPI across all 12 layers: from ~0.22–0.37 (vanilla MoE) to ~0.62–0.70 (MPI), confirming the theoretical claim of principal singular direction alignment.

**Efficiency:** Training overhead is 0.2% throughput reduction at 11B scale. Inference overhead is zero (router weights are pre-computed after training). No communication overhead is added.

**Compatibility:** MPI is shown compatible with router z-loss (coefficient 0.001), yielding an additional +0.68 pp downstream improvement. Compatible with Sigmoid activation (downstream improvement 41.64→42.05 vs. Softmax advantage narrowing).

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** The idea of aligning router rows with expert singular vectors is theoretically well-motivated and goes beyond purely heuristic router design. The connection to Rayleigh quotient maximization and the manifold perspective are genuine conceptual contributions. However, the individual components (power iteration, L2 normalization of weight rows) are each standard; the novelty is in their principled combination and theoretical unification as a router design principle. The paper is honest about this being a "redesign" rather than a new architecture.

**Rigor:** The theoretical analysis is solid — the Taylor expansion connecting MPI to steepest ascent on the spherical manifold is clean and well-presented. Convergence intuition is clearly supported. Empirical rigor is good: multiple optimizers at 1B, two model sizes at 3B/11B, multiple benchmark categories, ablations on both design choices (power iteration and retraction independently), and hyperparameter sensitivity analysis. The paper also reports a case where more iterations *hurts*, which is an honest negative result.

**Reproducibility:** The method is 2 lines of PyTorch code (shown in Figure 1), the only hyperparameter is C' (default 2, insensitive), and the training data (FineWeb-Edu) and evaluation suite (OLMo 3 benchmarks) are publicly available. Reproducibility is high. Architecture details are in Appendix B.

**Significance:** MoE routing is a bottleneck for efficient LLM scaling. A principle-driven, nearly-free improvement to router design that works across optimizers and scales is practically important. The ~1.04x convergence speedup and ~2 pp downstream gain are modest but consistent and real. The load balancing improvement is a meaningful bonus. The compatibility with z-loss and existing techniques makes adoption low-risk.

**Red flags / caveats:**
- The 1.04x speedup figure is visually impressive but numerically modest; it is also shown only at 11B. No token-efficiency curve showing "same loss at fewer tokens" is provided directly.
- The paper does not compare to other router improvement techniques (router z-loss alone, capacity factor tuning, expert choice routing, etc.) at large scale — only vanilla MoE baselines. Section 6 discusses orthogonality but does not run direct head-to-head comparisons.
- Scales tested (up to 11B, 350B tokens) are meaningful but not frontier-scale (100B+ parameters, trillions of tokens). Extrapolation claims should be treated cautiously.
- The improved load balancing is noted as "tentative" attribution to retraction design and deferred to future work — this loose end slightly weakens the load balance narrative.

## Strengths

- Theoretically principled: connects router design to classical linear algebra (Rayleigh quotient, power method, manifold optimization) with clear, verifiable mathematics
- Practically minimal: the entire method is ~2 lines of code, zero inference overhead, 0.2% training overhead
- Optimizer-agnostic: validated across AdamW, AdamH, Muon, MuonH — robust to the choice of optimizer
- Consistent gains: improvements hold across scales (1B→11B), token counts, and diverse benchmark categories (QA, math, code, reading comprehension, reasoning)
- Honest ablations: reports the failure mode of over-iterating (performance degrades), which builds trust in the analysis
- Plug-in compatible: works alongside z-loss, existing auxiliary losses, and standard router interfaces without architectural changes

## Limitations

- Scale validation stops at 11B / 350B tokens; frontier-scale (100B+) behavior is unknown
- No direct comparison to other router improvement baselines (e.g., expert choice routing, auxiliary loss variants) at matched compute
- The improved load balancing is unexplained mechanistically and deferred to future work
- Evaluated only on English, educational-domain pretraining data (FineWeb-Edu); multilingual or domain-specific MoE behavior is not studied
- The choice to use W_g (gate weight) over W_p or W_o for power iteration has only marginal empirical support and no theoretical justification for why the gate weight is the right proxy
- Sensitivity of method to expert granularity (number of experts N, expert size) beyond the tested configurations is not explored

## Relevance

本文直接关联大语言模型中稀疏激活混合专家（MoE）架构的高效预训练，对研究路由机制优化、专家专业化与负载均衡、以及在有限计算预算下提升MoE模型性能具有直接参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Does MPI improve expert specialization (do different experts develop more distinct singular directions over training), or does it primarily improve the routing accuracy of a fixed expert distribution?
2. How does the method scale to much larger expert counts (e.g., N=256 or N=1024 as in frontier MoEs), where the retraction constant C = C'/√N becomes very small?
3. Can the power iteration step be applied to combinations of expert weight matrices (e.g., W_g ⊗ W_o), and would this yield further alignment benefits?
4. Does MPI interact with expert merging or model pruning techniques where router rows are directly used to identify redundant experts?
5. At trillion-token training scales, does the convergence advantage compound or saturate — is the 1.04x speedup maintained or does the gap close/widen?
