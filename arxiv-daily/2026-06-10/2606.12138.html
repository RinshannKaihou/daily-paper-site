Unstable SAE features are not noise — they span reproducible low-rank subspaces, revealing basis ambiguity rather than training failure.

Unstable Features, Reproducible Subspaces: Understanding Seed Dependence in Sparse Autoencoders Gerasimov et al.  ·  T-Tech  ·  June 2026
sparse-autoencoders mechanistic-interpretability feature-stability LLM-interpretability representation-learning
Key Metric
4.5×
More perfect auto-interpretation scores for stable features vs. unstable features on SAEBench. Masking 4× as many unstable features still causes less EV damage than masking a single stable batch.
GPT-2 layer 7 · F = 214 · TopK k = 64 · 96 seeds
Why It Matters
Auto-interpretability scores can look high even for unreproducible features — stability is a complementary, essential quality signal any SAE analysis must check.
Dead-salmon control: random-model SAEs produce plausible explanations
Critical Edge
All primary experiments are on GPT-2 layer 7 only. Generalization to frontier-scale models is plausible but empirically untested, and the pooling construction is not demonstrated at very large dictionary sizes.
Threshold choices (θ = 0.7, ε = 0.05) affect exact stable/unstable fractions
Methodology
  1. 01 Per-feature stability probability p̂ᵢ — train N+1 SAEs with the same data but different seeds; for each anchor feature count cosine matches (≥ 0.7) across N runs. Stable: p̂ ≥ 0.95 · Unstable: p̂ ≤ 0.05.
  2. 02 Frequency-matched masking — mask N stable vs. 4N unstable features to equalise expected active mass, then measure EV and next-token CE degradation.
  3. 03 Geometric analysis — compute effective rank (ER/d) of decoder submatrices; measure cross-seed SVD subspace transfer to test whether unstable features' subspaces are reproducible.
  4. 04 Synthetic causal model — dictionary W = [full-rank; low-rank UV] in d=32, r=2 space. Low-rank features have p̂ ≈ 0.25; their subspace is still recoverable.
  5. 05 Cross-seed feature pooling — pool decoder vectors from multiple SAEs, deduplicate at θ = 0.7, initialise new F = 16 384-latent SAE from high-stability subset, briefly tune.

Strengths
  • + Scalable per-feature stability signal, no manual inspection, works with standard training infrastructure.
  • + 96 seeds × 5 SAE variants × multiple layers — strong statistical confidence in generality.
  • + Geometric analysis + synthetic model provide both correlational and causal evidence for basis-ambiguity mechanism.
  • + Dead-salmon control shows auto-interpretability alone is insufficient — stability is complementary.
  • + Cross-seed pooling is an immediately usable recipe for more stable SAEs without sacrificing EV.
Limitations
  • ! Primary results on GPT-2 layer 7; pooling construction not replicated across all models.
  • ! No stability–EV tradeoff holds for pooling construction only, not for single-run training objectives — easy to overgeneralise.
  • ! Exact stable/unstable set sizes are threshold-conditional (θ = 0.7, ε = 0.05); qualitative conclusions appear robust but quantitative claims are not.
  • ! Subspace reproducibility is consistent with basis ambiguity but does not rule out all alternative mechanisms.
  • ! Code and data release status not confirmed in available pages.
Questions for Further Exploration
Q1 Do any SAEBench variants condition on stability? Auto-interp scores can be high even for random-model SAEs — is the benchmark aware?
The dead-salmon control shows auto-interpretability metrics can appear high even when features are not reproducible. A stability-conditioned SAEBench variant would be more reliable as a quality gate for interpretability research.
Q2 Do the reproducible low-rank subspaces correspond to known geometric structures in transformers (anisotropy directions, collapse subspaces)?
The low-rank subspace containing unstable features is reproducible across seeds — connecting these subspaces to attention heads, dimensional collapse, or known residual stream geometry would be a strong mechanistic result.
Q3 Would cross-seed pooling hold for F = 2¹⁷ SAEs on frontier models?
The pooling construction improves stability without apparent EV penalty at F = 16 384. Computational scalability to very large dictionaries and frontier models is not demonstrated; wall-clock cost of pooling at scale is unknown.
Q4 Is there a principled way to choose TopK sparsity k on the stability–EV Pareto frontier?
Increasing k monotonically increases the unstable fraction while improving EV. An objective-guided selection procedure for k — rather than treating it as an ad-hoc hyperparameter — would benefit practitioners.
Q5 Can a training objective explicitly break basis ambiguity within reproducible low-rank subspaces?
Post-training shifts some features toward lower reappearance probabilities. A training objective that explicitly encourages recovery of individual features within reproducible subspaces (breaking the ambiguity during training) would be a principled solution to seed dependence.
