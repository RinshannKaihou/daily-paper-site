Key Metric
O(√LV) advantage
Softmax critical threshold: P* ~ L ln²(L)
vs. linear attention crossover: P* ~ L3/2 V1/2

Why This Matters
First-order transitions produce no precursor signal, providing a first-principles explanation for why emergent LLM capabilities appear without warning — with direct implications for AI safety monitoring.
Critical Edge
The model is a single-layer, single-head, supervised copy task with no causal masking — an extremely controlled abstraction far from real transformer behavior.
Transition Topology — Softmax vs. Linear Attention

Methodology
1
Setup. Single-layer softmax/linear attention on a supervised copy task: i.i.d. one-hot token sequences, targets y^a_i = x^(a,i−1). Fused weight matrix W^G combines W^Q and W^K.
2
Bayesian marginalization. Marginalizing over W^emb, positional encodings, and W^G yields a closed-form posterior p(G | {x_α, y_α}) ∝ exp(−S(G)), with action S decomposing into NNGP prior + data terms.
3
Order parameter reduction. Permutation equivariance reduces the high-dimensional G to two scalars: G = ĉ₁·1_L + ĉ_G·G*/√L — uniform pooling vs. copy behavior — without information loss.
4
Critical point derivation. P* determined analytically by S(0) = S(â*) — the condition where uniform and copy minima become degenerate, yielding closed-form Eq. 21.
5
Validation. Theory verified against both Adam optimizer training and full Bayesian SGLD sampling across multiple (P, L, V) sweeps — ruling out optimizer-specific artifacts.
Results
Softmax — Sharp Transition
â_G jumps discontinuously at P* ≈ 300 (experimental setup) with a sharp loss drop. Phase boundary P*(L,V) ~ L ln²(L) confirmed across full (P,L) and (P,V) planes. Gradient flow with large χ is equivalent to Adam training (Appendix K).
Linear — Three-Stage Learning
Stage (i) P < V²: no context. Stage (ii) P ~ V²: 2nd-order Z₂ symmetry break, uniform pooling begins. Stage (iii) P ~ L^(3/2)V^(1/2): smooth ĉ_G crossover to copy. Two distinct loss plateaus match predicted boundaries.
AI Safety Implication
First-order transitions give no observable precursor — capability monitoring of softmax transformers is intrinsically limited by first principles. Linear attention's 2nd-order transition is preceded by diverging fluctuations that could, in principle, be monitored.
Strengths
✓Closed-form analytical result — rare in mechanistic interpretability; derivation is systematic, not metaphorical.
✓Clean, testable prediction: P*(L,V) ~ L ln²(L) confirmed quantitatively across multiple parameter sweeps.
✓Two-dimensional order parameter reduction (ĉ₁, ĉ_G) is physically interpretable and links to observable unigram/bigram learning stages.
✓Independent MAP/Adam and full Bayesian SGLD validation rules out optimizer artifacts.
✓AI safety implication (first-order transitions preclude monitoring) is derived from first principles, not speculation.
Limitations
!Single-layer, single-head, no MLP, no causal masking — large gap to real transformer behavior.
!EK approximation (d_model → ∞) suppresses finite-size corrections; regime where these matter is uncharacterized.
!i.i.d. uniform token distribution — natural language's Zipf statistics may smear or shift phase boundaries.
!Multi-layer generalization (Appendix J) is outlined but not analyzed — first-order character may not survive layer coupling.
!Supervised setting used for tractability; real LLMs learn under unsupervised next-token prediction, a harder prior structure.
Open Questions
Q1
Does the first-order character persist in a full two-layer induction head, or does coupling between layers change the transition order?
Q2
Can the Bayesian framework be extended to causal (autoregressive) masking, and does masking shift P* significantly for real language model induction heads?
Q3
Under what (L, V) regimes does softmax actually require fewer samples than linear attention's crossover, and where does this advantage reverse?
Q4
How does a Zipfian (structured) token distribution modify the order parameter reduction and the shape of the phase boundary?
Q5
Even if classical fluctuation measures fail, could weight-space geometry or loss landscape sharpness serve as alternative predictors of the phase boundary approach?
