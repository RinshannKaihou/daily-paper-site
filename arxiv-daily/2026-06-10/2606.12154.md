> **一句话总结：** 提出"Horsehair"架构——将35B级大模型完全移出运行时路径，离线预构建经过验证的结构化知识库，运行时仅用BM25路由器+确定性渲染器+1B小模型（MiniCPM5-1B，4-bit）提供服务，在8GB显存消费级笔记本（RTX 4060）上实现端到端响应从4,465ms降至518ms、吞吐量从15.7升至131.4 tokens/s，同时意外发现小模型的保真度取决于答案的"封装格式"而非内容本身。

> **TL;DR:** The "Horsehair" system inverts the conventional LLM serving arrangement: a large 35B-class model works offline to harvest verified answers into a structured knowledge store, and the runtime path contains only a BM25 router, a deterministic renderer, and a 1B-class mouth model (MiniCPM5-1B, 4-bit). On an 8 GB consumer GPU laptop, this removes 8.4× latency and delivers 131 tokens/second end-to-end. Two unexpected findings: (1) keyword routing collapses from adequate to 1.5% top-1 on real documents while BM25 holds at 92.8%, and (2) verbatim fidelity of the small renderer is completely determined by answer packaging format — identical content in a JSON envelope yields 0/9 exact matches versus 9/9 under a marker-line format.

## 核心贡献

- **架构倒置（Horsehair系统）**：大模型（35B脑）仅在离线阶段工作，读取源文档并将经过验证的答案写入结构化知识库；运行时大模型完全沉默，仅有BM25路由+确定性渲染器+1B小模型（口）承担服务。
- **结构性瓶颈诊断**：三款不同大模型（Qwen3.6-35B-A3B、Gemma4-26B-A4B、GLM-4.7-Flash）在同一硬件上均显示4–7秒端到端延迟，证明延迟是"调用大模型"这一行为本身的代价，而非某个具体模型的问题。
- **端到端加速8.4×**：同一8GB笔记本，端到端吞吐量从15.7 tokens/s升至131.4 tokens/s，响应时间从4,465ms降至518ms；小模型流式解码速率226–237 tokens/s，TTFT 29–62ms；上下文窗口从4k扩展到128k时速度恒定不变。
- **路由实验**：在563条目/1,126查询的真实规模存储上，关键词/正则路由崩溃至1.5% top-1，BM25达92.8% top-1/99.4% top-3；置信门（margin < τ=0.05时上升14.8%查询）将有效top-1提升至98.0%。
- **格式依赖性发现**：MiniCPM5-1B对携带同一内容的不同封装格式表现截然不同：标记行格式9/9精确匹配，JSON字段格式0/9，引号字符串格式0/9，韩语字段引号格式3/9；重复性文本（236 token）无论格式均导致完全崩溃（0.00），而232 token非重复性答案完整存活。

## Key Contributions

- **The Horsehair architecture**: A named system (after the horsehair worm that steers a mantis's body) that strictly separates the "brain" (offline large model) from the "mouth" (runtime small model). The large model is not just deprioritized — it is absent from the runtime process entirely.
- **Structural bottleneck proof**: Three different 35B-class models all take 4–7 seconds end-to-end on the same hardware, all produce valid offline knowledge stores. This confirms the bottleneck is the act of invocation, not the model.
- **8.4× end-to-end throughput improvement**: From 15.7 to 131.4 tok/s (all overheads included). The gap between end-to-end (131) and decode rate (230) is explicitly attributed to HTTP overhead and one prompt evaluation — reported honestly rather than conflated.
- **Context-length is free**: Because the heavy work happened offline, expanding context from 4k to 128k tokens costs zero decode speed. VRAM grows ~22 MiB/1k context; 256k hits the 7.6 GB safety gate.
- **Envelope-fidelity law**: The marker-line structured format was originally chosen for speed and auditability. It turns out it was also, unknowingly, what kept the facts intact. This is a generalizable engineering finding for anyone building retrieval-then-relay systems.
- **Honest failure documentation**: Section 7 ("What Went Wrong Along the Way") documents three real failures: the original latency, the large model refusing to stay quiet (co-residency silently degrades into co-generation), and hardware freezes from VRAM pressure.

## Methodology

**System name:** Horsehair (연가시), named after the parasitic worm that controls a mantis's body. The large model is the body (knowledge, judgment, bulk); the small model is the mouth (delivery only).

**Hardware:** Single consumer laptop, RTX 4060 Laptop GPU with 8 GB VRAM, running ik_llama.cpp. Safety gates capped VRAM at 7,576 MiB and GPU temperature at 78°C throughout.

**Offline phase ("the brain works at night"):**
- The large model (any of Qwen3.6-35B-A3B, Gemma4-26B-A4B, or GLM-4.7-Flash) reads source documents — in these experiments, patent specifications and experiment logs totaling 17 documents.
- For each knowledge item, it produces an answer candidate with keys and aliases for retrieval.
- Each candidate passes a verification gate that checks it against its source. Only verified candidates enter the structured store; failed candidates are rejected.
- The structured format is machine-oriented, simultaneously human-readable and deterministically parseable. Internal format details are intentionally not disclosed in the paper.

**Runtime phase ("the brain goes quiet"):**
1. A BM25 router maps the incoming query to stored keys. Median routing latency: 2.4 ms over 563 entries (naive linear scan). A confidence gate (margin threshold τ=0.05) escalates low-confidence queries rather than answering them incorrectly.
2. A deterministic renderer extracts the factual answer line from the stored entry — zero model invocation, effectively zero time.
3. MiniCPM5-1B at 4-bit quantization streams the answer to the user. The small model is not asked to know or compose anything; the factual content is locked by the renderer.

**Routing experiment:** 563-entry store, 1,126 queries generated from document vocabulary. Three routers compared: keyword/regex (top-1: 1.5%, top-3: 4.6%), BM25 (top-1: 92.8%, top-3: 99.4%), Hybrid (same as BM25). Route p50 latency: ~2.4 ms for all three. Wrong-key rate for BM25: 7.2%.

**Fidelity experiment:** Envelope formats tested with byte-identical content across 9 repetitions each. Results: marker-line (9/9), Korean-field quoted (3/9), quoted string (0/9), JSON field (0/9). Length interaction: 64/149/232-token non-repetitive answers all score 1.00; 236-token repetitive answer collapses to 0.00 (model drifts into Chinese translation and paraphrase).

**Verification gate experiment:** 16 hand-labelled cases: 6 genuinely supported + 10 deliberately corrupted (fabrication, numerical error, logical reversal, contradiction, out-of-source claims). Gate performance: blocked 10/10 corrupted, admitted 6/6 supported — precision, recall, and F1 all 1.0 on this set.

## Results & Highlights

**Table 1: Offline brain comparison (same hardware, same task)**

| Model (offline brain) | Generation tok/s | Valid store entry |
|---|---|---|
| Qwen3.6-35B-A3B | 33.0 | Yes |
| Gemma4-26B-A4B | 20.8 | Yes |
| GLM-4.7-Flash | 17.6 | Yes |
| MiniCPM5-1B (runtime mouth) | 230 | — |

**Table 2: Runtime path comparison (N=5, all overheads included)**

| Configuration | Mean wall time | End-to-end tok/s | Decode tok/s |
|---|---|---|---|
| Qwen in runtime path | 4,464.6 ms | 15.7 | 33.0 |
| Gemma in runtime path | 6,957 ms | ~9.8 | 20.8 |
| Store + 1B-router | 756 ms | 90.1 | 226–237 |
| Store + keyword router | **518 ms** | **131.4** | 226–237 |

**Table 3: Routing accuracy (563 entries, 1,126 queries)**

| Router | Top-1 | Top-3 | Wrong-key | Route p50 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Keyword/regex | 0.015 | 0.046 | 0.493 | 2.45 ms |
| BM25 | **0.928** | **0.994** | 0.072 | 2.40 ms |
| Hybrid | 0.928 | 0.994 | 0.072 | 2.41 ms |

Correctly routed queries had a median score margin of ~0.30 over the runner-up; misrouted queries had a median margin near zero — making the confidence gate structurally sound.

**Context scaling:** Decode rate flat from 4k to 128k context (226–237 tok/s). VRAM grows ~22 MiB/1k. 256k fails the VRAM gate at 7.4 GB.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** The paper is honest here. The components individually — semantic caching (GPTCache), RAG (Lewis et al. NeurIPS 2020), knowledge distillation (Hinton et al.), speculative decoding — are all well-studied. The claimed novelty is the *specific combination*: offline harvesting by a large model into a verified, structured, deterministically renderable store, with the large model fully absent from the runtime path on consumer hardware, plus the envelope-fidelity finding which the author states does not appear in literature. This is a credible claim of practical novelty, not algorithmic novelty.

**Rigor:** Strong for what it measures. Five repeated runs per configuration (N=5), min/max reported alongside means, raw CSV files retained. The author carefully distinguishes end-to-end throughput, decode rate, and TTFT — and explicitly warns that conflating them "flatters the result." However, the evaluation scale is narrow: 563 entries from 17 documents, 1,126 queries generated from document vocabulary (acknowledged as an optimistic bound), and a 16-case verification gate test.

**Reproducibility:** Partial. The external behavior (latencies, routing accuracy, fidelity scores) is fully reported with hardware specifications and model names. The internal structured format and verification computation are withheld. The author explicitly invites others to reproduce with any "deterministically parseable, source-verifiable store format."

**Significance:** High for a narrow but real problem: enterprise/organizational LLM deployment on constrained, air-gapped, or budget hardware. The insight that "the four seconds were never the model's fault — they were the cost of asking" is genuinely useful framing. However, the system only handles queries answerable from a pre-built store; it refuses novel composition. This is a deliberate design choice, not an oversight.

**Red flags:**
- The 92.8% routing accuracy is measured on queries generated from document vocabulary — free conversational phrasing degrades it, and the author says so. This is the central operational risk.
- The verification gate test (16 cases) is too small to be a generalization claim. The author explicitly acknowledges this.
- The internal structured format is not disclosed, which means the envelope-fidelity finding (the most interesting result) cannot be directly reproduced.
- Single machine, single user: concurrency behavior is completely unmeasured.
- The offline harvesting cost for a large corpus (thousands of documents) is unmeasured.

## Strengths

- Exceptionally honest writing: limitations section is specific and detailed, failure cases are documented in Section 7, the author explicitly states what novelty the paper does and does not claim.
- The speed-fidelity-auditability trilemma framing (Figure 1) is a clean and useful conceptual contribution for practitioners.
- The envelope-fidelity finding is a genuinely unexpected and generalizable result: it suggests that for small-model relay systems, the answer representation format is at least as important as retrieval quality.
- Context-length-is-free result (Section 6.6) is practically valuable: a 128k context window fits on 8 GB without any speed penalty.
- The "co-residency silently degrades into co-generation" failure (Section 7) is an important practical warning for anyone building hybrid large/small model systems.
- Measurements are precise: three metrics (end-to-end throughput, decode rate, TTFT) reported separately with a clear explanation of why they differ.

## Limitations

- **Closed-world answers only**: The system answers what the store contains. It does not compose, infer, or handle novel questions. By design it refuses rather than improvises. This is appropriate for some organizational workloads but rules out open-ended QA.
- **Vocabulary-dependent routing**: BM25 at 92.8% top-1 is measured on queries generated from document vocabulary. Colloquial phrasing, synonyms, and spelling variants degrade routing, and the confidence gate cannot recover all of these — it escalates them rather than answers them, but a real fallback for out-of-vocabulary phrasing is noted as future work.
- **Small evaluation scale**: 17 documents, 563 entries, 16-case verification gate. Scaling to thousands of documents with millions of potential entries is unvalidated.
- **Internal format not disclosed**: The structured representation format and verification computation are intentionally withheld, limiting direct reproduction of the most interesting results.
- **Single machine, single user**: Concurrency behavior is completely unmeasured. Organizations with multiple simultaneous users would need to validate separately.
- **Offline harvesting cost unmeasured at scale**: Building the knowledge store from 17 documents is fast; the cost at thousands of documents is unknown.
- **Mouth model generalization**: The fidelity findings in Section 6.4 are specific to MiniCPM5-1B. Other small models may break differently.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. The envelope-fidelity finding is the most surprising result: why does a marker-line format produce 9/9 exact matches while JSON produces 0/9 for identical content? Is this a tokenization artifact (JSON special characters triggering summarization behavior), a training data effect, or something else? Does this generalize to other 1B-class models?
2. The 92.8% BM25 routing is measured on document-vocabulary queries. What is the degradation curve as query phrasing becomes more colloquial? At what distance from document vocabulary does the confidence gate stop being effective?
3. The author mentions that offline harvesting used patent specifications and experiment logs. How does the structured store approach generalize to more ambiguous source types (meeting transcripts, email threads, informal notes)?
4. Co-residency degrading into co-generation (Section 7) is flagged as a real pitfall. What is the mechanism — is the large model being invoked by the runtime framework, or is there some other coupling? How should practitioners detect this in their own deployments?
5. For the confidence gate: τ=0.05 is the disclosed threshold. How sensitive is the 98.0% effective top-1 figure to this threshold? What is the escalation rate vs. accuracy tradeoff curve?
6. How does this compare to a heavily quantized (2-4 bit GPTQ) 35B model at the same hardware budget in terms of per-query latency and coverage? The paper argues the bottleneck is structural, but a sufficiently quantized model might close the gap.
