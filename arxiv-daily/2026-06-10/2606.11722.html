Key Finding

ICALens extracts interpretable LLM directions using GPU-parallel FastICA with three stability recipes — no SAE training required.

Why This Matters

ICA matches SAE quality at a fraction of training cost, enabling rapid interpretability exploration on any new model — no saved checkpoints needed.

Critical Edge

ICA is fundamentally bounded: at most d components per layer, making it unable to produce the 16k–131k overcomplete latent dictionaries modern SAEs provide.

Methodology
1
Activation Corpus

1M post-block residual-stream activations from Pile-10k (context 1024 tokens), matching SAE checkpoint sites across GPT-2 Small, Gemma 2 2B, Qwen 3.5 2B Base.

2
Row Normalization

L2-normalize each activation vector before centering/whitening. Neutralizes massive-norm outlier tokens (attention sinks) that cause divergent FastICA.

3
p95-LIM Convergence

Accept a layer when 95% of components converge (not 100%). Prevents a few slow-converging components from rejecting an otherwise stable layer.

4
Adaptive Refitting

If a layer still fails, halve the target component count (down to min 16) until convergence. Returns the highest-resolution accepted fit per layer.

5
SAEBench Evaluation

Sparse Probing + Targeted Probe Perturbation (TPP) against Gemma Scope, Qwen-Scope, Matryoshka SAEs, ITDA, and PCA across three model families.

ERF — Effective Receptive Field

New per-component diagnostic: minimum left-context suffix needed to recover a component's signed activation. Spearman ρ(ERF, kurtosis) = −0.41 to −0.50 — high kurtosis predicts small ERF and easier human labeling.

Strengths
  • Three stability recipes produce a measurable, verifiable +400% layer acceptance rate — not a soft claim.
  • ERF is a novel, practically useful diagnostic: grounds the kurtosis–interpretability link mechanistically.
  • Human annotation is unusually rigorous: random sampling, contrastive prompt testing, independent second-expert audit.
  • Multi-model validation across GPT-2 Small, Gemma 2 2B, and Qwen 3.5 2B with public checkpoints released.
  • Zero training cost makes ICA attractive for rapid exploration of new model releases without infrastructure.
  • Full artifact release: code, ICA checkpoints (HuggingFace), ICA Explorer, and annotation tables.
Limitations
  • Compact basis only: at most d components per layer; no overcomplete dictionaries without architectural changes.
  • Residual stream states only: MLP outputs, attention outputs, and cross-layer bases left as future work.
  • Row normalization discards norm: removes token-level norm information that some methods treat as meaningful.
  • Non-Gaussianity is sufficient, not necessary: context-dependent Gaussian-like directions are invisible to ICA.
  • Ongoing paper: results may change before finalization; not yet peer-reviewed.
  • TPP sensitivity: authors themselves cite benchmark reliability concerns — results should not be over-generalized.
Questions for Further Exploration
Q1
Do ICA-exclusive directions (no close SAE counterpart) correspond to interpretable concepts that SAEs systematically miss?
Q2
Does the ERF peak in middle layers correlate with known circuits — induction heads, entity tracking — or attention pattern structure?
Q3
Row normalization discards norm. For steering/intervention experiments where norm magnitude matters, how much does this affect ICA-based interventions?
Q4
At what dictionary size would overcomplete ICA variants (JADE, Infomax) converge in performance with SAEs — and at what computational cost?
Q5
Would a shared ICA basis across layers (analogous to Gemma Scope's model-wide releases) reveal persistent cross-layer directions?
Q6
Do high-similarity cross-layer ICA component chains (linked by ICA Explorer) correspond to known circuits or residual-stream information flows?
