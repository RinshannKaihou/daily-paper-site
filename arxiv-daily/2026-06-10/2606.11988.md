> **一句话总结：** 本文系统梳理动力学系统中五类不确定性来源（初始条件、过程噪声、部分观测、参数不确定性、结构不确定性），明确其偶然性/认知性本质依赖于任务与建模假设，并指出将二阶分布和可信集（credal sets）迁移至轨迹空间是关键未来方向。

> **TL;DR:** A position/perspective paper (EIML@ICML 2026) that maps the aleatoric–epistemic uncertainty dichotomy onto dynamical systems, identifying five distinct sources of uncertainty, showing that their reducibility depends on task and modeling choices, and outlining open opportunities for ML in this domain.

## 核心贡献

- 识别并系统分析动力学系统中五类不确定性来源：初始条件不确定性（S1）、过程噪声（S2）、部分观测（S3）、参数不确定性（S4）、结构不确定性（S5），并逐一讨论其可约性
- 阐明偶然/认知不确定性的分类并非固有属性，而是依赖任务目标与建模假设——同一初始条件不确定性在不同假设下既可为认知性，也可为偶然性
- 指出动力学系统中不确定性的独特挑战：各类不确定性随时间累积、相互耦合，且其相对重要性随预测时域变化（短期由S1主导，中期由S2累积，长期由S4、S5决定）
- 论证标准概率分布（一阶分布）不足以表示认知不确定性，倡导将二阶分布与可信集方法从监督学习迁移到轨迹和泛函空间

## Key Contributions

- Provides a structured taxonomy of five uncertainty sources in dynamical systems (S1–S5), each with distinct temporal propagation characteristics and reducibility properties
- Demonstrates that the aleatoric/epistemic classification of any source is task- and assumption-relative, not intrinsic to the source itself — a conceptual clarification that has direct consequences for system design
- Identifies a key open problem: uncertainty in dynamical systems is naturally a functional quantity (a trajectory or a functional of one), whereas standard ML uncertainty methods target scalar predictions; new measures are needed
- Surveys the ML–dynamical systems interface bidirectionally: what ML contributes to dynamical systems modeling, and what dynamical systems concepts (filtering, control, reachability) contribute back to ML
- Published at the 2nd Workshop on Epistemic Intelligence in Machine Learning (EIML@ICML 2026)

## Methodology

This is a conceptual/perspectives paper with no new algorithm or experiment. The methodology is analytical and taxonomic, grounded in formal mathematical definitions.

**Formal setup.** The paper begins from the deterministic autonomous dynamical system dx/dt = f(x(t)), x(0) = x_0, and progressively relaxes its assumptions to introduce each uncertainty source. The running example throughout is the nonlinear damped pendulum (state: angle θ, angular velocity ω; damping γ=0.5, g/ℓ=1.0), which has a rich phase portrait with stable/unstable equilibria and separatrices — rich enough to illustrate all five sources concretely.

**Five sources of uncertainty:**
- **(S1) Initial condition uncertainty:** x_0 is unknown; represented as a distribution p_0 over the state space. Its propagation is governed by the Liouville equation (deterministic transport of probability). Epistemic in nature when x_0 is a fixed but unknown quantity; aleatoric when it reflects genuine natural variability in setup.
- **(S2) Process noise:** The ODE is replaced by an SDE dx = f(x)dt + σ(x)dW_t. Noise is injected continuously, making uncertainty a process rather than an initial condition. Governed by the Fokker-Planck equation. Prototypically aleatoric, though σ itself is usually unknown and must be estimated.
- **(S3) Partial observations:** The state is observed only through y_k = h(x(t_k)) + v_k. This leads to the Bayesian filtering problem (posterior p(x(t_k)|y_{1:k})). Uncertainty is reducible within the limits of observability; in unobservable directions it persists indefinitely.
- **(S4) Parameter uncertainty:** The evolution law is parametrized as f_θ; θ is unknown. Epistemic by nature — reducible by collecting more trajectory data. Notably, parameter errors compound over time through accumulated Jacobians (variational equation), potentially causing qualitative changes (e.g., bifurcations) near regime boundaries.
- **(S5) Structural uncertainty:** The model form itself is wrong — wrong functional class, missing state variables, wrong dynamics type, wrong observation model. Qualitatively distinct from S1–S4: "the truth" cannot be defined within any single model class, making structural uncertainty notoriously hard to represent or quantify. Related to misspecification in classical statistics.

**Horizon-dependent dominance.** S1 dominates at short horizons; S2 accumulates at medium horizons; S4 and S5 take over at long horizons as the attractor structure becomes the primary uncertainty object.

**Modeling uncertainty:** The paper surveys representations — first-order probability distributions (SDEs, filtering), second-order distributions (distributions over distributions, for epistemic uncertainty), sets and credal sets (differential inclusions, fuzzy differential equations, credal ML). It argues that for epistemic uncertainty, first-order distributions are principally insufficient (as argued formally in Sale et al., 2026), and that second-order or set-valued representations are more appropriate — but their extension to trajectory spaces is non-trivial.

## Results & Highlights

- No experimental results; this is a conceptual synthesis paper.
- Key conceptual results:
  - The aleatoric/epistemic classification of S1 (initial conditions) and S3 (partial observations) is genuinely task-dependent: the same source can be either, depending on whether x_0 is treated as a fixed unknown quantity or as a naturally varying random variable.
  - Structural uncertainty (S5) is qualitatively unique: it cannot be resolved "from inside any single model," echoing classical misspecification. This distinguishes it from all other sources.
  - Near bifurcation boundaries, parameter uncertainty (S4) becomes qualitative rather than quantitative — the posterior over θ may leave us uncertain about which dynamical regime the system is in (e.g., stable vs. chaotic), not just the magnitude of the state.
  - The paper identifies uncertainty in derived quantities (time to stabilization, probability of entering a region) as potentially more operationally meaningful than pointwise state uncertainty.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Low-to-moderate as a standalone technical contribution; moderate-to-high as a synthesis and reframing. The individual components (SDEs, Fokker-Planck, Bayesian filtering, second-order distributions) are all well-known. The value is in unifying them under a common lens and exposing their interactions. The task-dependence argument for aleatoric/epistemic classification is the sharpest intellectual contribution.
- **Rigor:** The mathematical formalism is solid and correctly deployed (Liouville, Fokker-Planck, Bayesian filtering, variational equations). The conceptual arguments are carefully qualified. No proofs or theorems are claimed beyond what the cited literature establishes.
- **Reproducibility:** Not applicable — no algorithms or experiments. The nonlinear pendulum example is fully specified (γ=0.5, g/ℓ=1.0) and reproducible.
- **Significance:** Potentially high as a reference paper for practitioners building ML models of physical systems, especially as neural ODEs/SDEs and physics-informed ML expand into safety-critical domains (weather, control, robotics). The framework helps clarify what uncertainty methods are actually doing and what they cannot do.
- **Red flags:** (1) The paper is quite short (6 main pages + references + 1 appendix figure) and workshop-level in scope — depth is limited. (2) The "opportunities" section (Section 7) is brief and points to open problems without providing solutions or preliminary results. (3) The reliance on Sale et al. 2026 (SSRN, not yet peer-reviewed) for a key theoretical claim is a minor concern. (4) No empirical grounding — all claims about uncertainty behavior (e.g., horizon-dependent dominance) are argued qualitatively but not demonstrated experimentally.

## Strengths

- Clear, well-organized conceptual framework that genuinely fills a gap in the literature
- The task-dependence insight (that aleatoric/epistemic is not intrinsic) is practically important and often overlooked in applied work
- Strong connection to existing ML literature (neural ODEs/SDEs, Bayesian filtering, SINDy, PINNs, credal ML) — makes the paper useful as an entry point to the field
- Concrete running example (nonlinear pendulum) grounds abstract concepts throughout
- Identifies non-trivial open problems (uncertainty over trajectories as functionals, uncertainty near bifurcations) that are not just restatements of known challenges

## Limitations

- No experimental validation; all claims about uncertainty behavior in practice are asserted, not measured
- The "opportunities" section is underdeveloped — more vision than roadmap
- Workshop venue limits the depth achievable; key insights deserve a full journal treatment
- Does not address computational tractability: representing second-order distributions or credal sets over trajectory spaces is mentioned as important but the computational challenges are not discussed
- The framework is limited to finite-dimensional systems (ODEs/SDEs); PDEs and spatiotemporal systems are mentioned but not treated

## Relevance

本文为将不确定性量化方法应用于时序预测、神经ODE/SDE、物理信息神经网络等场景的研究者提供了清晰的概念框架，尤其对区分不同不确定性来源、选择合适的表示方式（概率分布 vs. 可信集）具有直接指导意义。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. For neural SDE models trained end-to-end, which of the five sources (S1–S5) does the learned diffusion coefficient σ(x) actually capture — is it S2 alone, or does it absorb S4/S5 as a form of implicit misspecification?
2. How do second-order distributions (distributions over distributions) scale to trajectory spaces in practice — are variational or particle-based approximations tractable for high-dimensional SDEs?
3. Near bifurcation boundaries where parameter uncertainty (S4) becomes qualitative, can Bayesian approaches with multimodal posteriors over θ handle regime uncertainty, or are fundamentally different representations needed?
4. The paper argues uncertainty in derived quantities (time to stabilization, probability of entering a region) may be more operationally useful — what are concrete ML architectures that directly target these functionals rather than pointwise state distributions?
5. How does the framework apply to partially specified systems where even the state space X is uncertain (e.g., discovering hidden variables from data)?
