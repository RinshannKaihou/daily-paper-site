> **一句话总结：** ICALens 通过 GPU 并行 FastICA 加三项 LLM 专属稳定性配方（行归一化、p95-LIM 鲁棒收敛、自适应重拟合），在无需训练稀疏自编码器的情况下从 LLM 残差流中高效提取可解释方向；在 SAEBench 上稀疏探测与公开 SAE 相当，在小到中等预算的目标探测扰动任务中超越 SAE，同时引入有效感受野（ERF）指标揭示 ICA 方向从 token 局部到上下文依赖的连续谱。

> **TL;DR:** ICALens is a practical ICA-based workflow — combining GPU-parallel FastICA with three LLM-specific stability recipes and a new Effective Receptive Field (ERF) diagnostic — that recovers compact, human-interpretable directions from LLM activations without per-layer SAE training. On SAEBench, ICA matches public SAEs in sparse probing and outperforms them in targeted probe perturbation under small-to-medium feature budgets, positioning ICA as an efficient first lens that complements rather than replaces SAEs.

## 核心贡献

- **ICALens 工作流：** 首个针对现代 LLM 激活的稳定、高效、可审计 ICA 分析流程，解决了朴素 FastICA 在高维、高异方差 LLM 激活上收敛失败的问题。
- **三项 LLM 专属配方：** (1) 行归一化消除激活范数异常值影响；(2) p95-LIM 鲁棒收敛接受准则避免少数慢收敛分量拖垮整层；(3) 自适应重拟合在收敛困难层自动降低分量数。在 GPT-2 Small 上，接受层数从 2 层提升至 10 层（+400%），总迭代次数减少 21.5%。
- **有效感受野（ERF）：** 新诊断指标，度量重现某分量激活所需的最短左文本上下文长度，揭示浅层以 token 局部分量为主、深层以上下文依赖分量为主的层级规律，并解释高峰度与可解释性正相关的机制。
- **全面人类标注：** 对三个模型各随机抽样 50 个分量，150 个中 127 个获得高置信度工作标签；二次专家审计中 127 个标签 121 个被支持、6 个被部分支持、0 个被拒绝，112 个得分 ≥ 8/10。
- **SAEBench 评估：** 稀疏探测持续优于 PCA 和 ITDA，与公开 SAE 相当；在小到中等预算的目标探测扰动任务中超越公开 SAE，Matryoshka 紧凑 SAE 变体亦低于 ICA。
- **ICA–SAE 关系分析：** ICA 方向与 SAE 解码器方向呈中等余弦重叠（分布峰值约 0.4–0.5），非冗余亦非无关；ICA 分量倾向于追踪跨 token 的上下文因子，SAE 特征倾向于在单 token 或短 span 上尖峰激活。
- **完整开源：** 发布所有拟合 ICA 检查点、ICA Explorer 交互工具和人工标注数据。

## Key Contributions

- **ICALens workflow:** First practical, stable, and auditable ICA pipeline for LLM representations, overcoming the brittleness of standard FastICA on high-dimensional, anisotropic activation matrices.
- **Three LLM-specific stability recipes:** Row normalization (L2-normalize each activation vector before centering/whitening), p95-LIM robust convergence acceptance (accept a layer when 95% of components have converged rather than requiring all), and adaptive refitting (reduce target component count by halves until convergence). On GPT-2 Small, accepted layers increase from 2 to 10 of 12 residual-stream layers.
- **Effective Receptive Field (ERF):** A new per-component diagnostic measuring the minimum left-context suffix length needed to recover a component's signed activation response. ERF reveals a token-local to context-dependent spectrum across layers and establishes a mechanistic link between high kurtosis and interpretability: high-kurtosis components have small ERFs and are easier to label from top examples alone.
- **Rigorous human annotation:** Random component audit across 150 components (50 per model); 127 receive high-confidence working labels across word, phrase, form, sentence, long-range, global, and sophisticated interpretation types. A secondary contrastive expert audit validates 121/127 labels as supported (none rejected).
- **SAEBench benchmarking:** ICA is competitive with public high-capacity SAEs (Gemma Scope, Qwen-Scope) in sparse probing and outperforms them in Targeted Probe Perturbation (TPP) at small-to-medium intervention budgets. ICA consistently outperforms PCA and ITDA (a training-light sparse coding alternative).
- **ICA–SAE relationship characterization:** Moderate directional overlap (peak cosine ~0.4–0.5); ICA components tend to track broader contextual factors across spans while SAE features spike at localized tokens — complementary rather than redundant.
- **Full artifact release:** All ICA checkpoints (HuggingFace), interactive ICA Explorer, and human annotation tables.

## Methodology

**Motivation.** SAE training is expensive: Gemma Scope required 4–16B tokens per SAE, ~20% of GPT-3's training compute, and ~20 PiB of saved activations. The authors ask how much interpretable structure is already visible from activation geometry alone. Their key insight is that interpretable directions tend to be selective on tokens (activating in narrow contexts) and therefore statistically non-Gaussian — a signal that ICA can exploit directly.

**Core algorithm.** FastICA (Hyvarinen, 1999) is a fixed-point method. Given activation matrix X^ℓ ∈ ℝ^{n×d} from layer ℓ, it centers and whitens to obtain Z ∈ ℝ^{n×m}, then searches for rotation W ∈ ℝ^{m×m} maximizing non-Gaussianity via log-cosh contrast: max_{WW^T=I} (1/n)∑_i ∑_j G((ZW^T)_{ij}). The authors use the parallel FastICA variant in PyTorch (GPU-accelerated, building on FastICA_torch).

**Stability recipes (Section 3).**

1. *Row normalization:* Before centering and whitening, each activation vector x_i is L2-normalized: r(x_i) = x_i / max(‖x_i‖₂, ε). This neutralizes the massive-norm outlier tokens (attention sinks, high-norm positions) that dominate raw LLM activation spaces and cause slow or divergent FastICA convergence.

2. *Robust convergence acceptance (p95-LIM):* Standard FastICA uses worst-case stopping (max_j LIM_j < τ). In high-dimensional LLM fits, a few slow or oscillating components can exceed τ indefinitely, causing the entire layer to be rejected. The p95 fallback accepts a layer when p_{95}(LIM_j) ≤ τ, retaining all stable components while flagging the unstable tail for downstream audits.

3. *Adaptive refitting:* If a layer still fails both criteria, the target component count m is halved repeatedly (down to m_min = 16) until convergence is achieved, returning the highest-resolution accepted fit per layer.

**Activation corpus.** 1M post-block residual-stream activations sampled from the Pile-10k training split (context length 1024, uniform token sampling). Embedding-layer analysis uses the static embedding matrix (50,257 rows for GPT-2, 256,000 for Gemma 2 2B, 248,320 for Qwen 3.5 2B Base). This matches the activation sites used by the corresponding public SAE checkpoints.

**Effective Receptive Field (ERF, Section 4).** For each component j, a set of ~20 evidence examples is identified (those activating component j above half the maximum absolute score). For each evidence example (x, t), suffixes x_{t-k+1:t} of increasing length k = 1, …, K_max are fed to the model, and recovery is declared when component j remains in the top-15 components with the correct sign. ERF(j) is the mean shortest recovery length across evidence examples. K_max = 11 in experiments.

**Human annotation protocol (Section 5).** Expert annotators inspect components using the ICA Explorer interface, guided by ERF (low-ERF components inspectable from top examples; high-ERF components require context ablations). Labels receive a confidence level (High/Med/Low/Unclear) and an interpretation type (form, word, phrase, sentence, long-range, global, position, sophisticated). A secondary contrastive expert audit constructs paired activate/not-activate prompts to independently validate each high-confidence label.

**Benchmarking (Section 6).** SAEBench Sparse Probing ranks feature dimensions by class-contrast on training sets and trains top-k supervised probes; ICA and PCA scores are split into positive/negative sides (f_i^+ = max(s_i, 0), f_i^- = max(-s_i, 0)) before ranking. Targeted Probe Perturbation (TPP) zero-ablates the top-N features associated with a target class and measures the change in class probe scores (intended vs. unintended effects). Evaluation layers: GPT-2 Small layers 6 and 10; Gemma 2 2B layers 12 and 20; Qwen 3.5 2B Base layers 12 and 20.

## Results & Highlights

**Stability gains.** On GPT-2 Small with 1M activation rows, the three recipes together increase accepted layers from 2 (raw, max-LIM) to 10 of 12 (normalized, p95 fallback), reducing total FastICA iterations from 3,107 to 2,741.

**Non-Gaussianity.** ICA directions are substantially more non-Gaussian (excess kurtosis) than random projections and public SAE decoder directions across all three model families and all layers (Figure 3). This validates that ICALens successfully recovers the statistical objective on LLM activations.

**ERF spectrum.** Early layers are dominated by token-local components (ERF = 1); later layers shift toward medium- and long-context components, with a non-monotonic pattern where the largest-ERF components are most prevalent in middle layers. Spearman correlation between ERF and excess kurtosis ranges from -0.41 to -0.50 across models: higher kurtosis predicts smaller ERF and easier interpretability.

**Human annotation.** Of 150 randomly sampled components (50 per model), 127 receive high-confidence labels and 142 receive non-unclear labels. Labels span: word identity (After, Financial), phrase templates (Arriving/Leaving for a purpose), sentence-level structure (Scientific research / Citation), long-range patterns (Repetition of a prior section header), global topics (Gaming language), and sophisticated conditional patterns. Secondary audit: 121/127 supported, 112/127 scored ≥ 8/10 on a 0–10 support scale.

**Case studies.** (1) Polysemous word decomposition: four occurrences of "bank" in a paragraph are represented by distinct, contextually appropriate ICA component mixtures that separate financial from river-edge readings across layers. (2) Component traces across sentences: component C67 (Financial) remains near zero in a library sentence but activates broadly across "bank", "deposit", "check", "withdraw", "cash" — behaving as a contextual factor rather than a single-token detector. (3) Embedding-layer ICA on Qwen 3.5 2B recovers royalty (Royal), gender (Woman, Female), morphological, and surface-form components for king/queen/man/woman analogy pairs, revealing asymmetric associations hidden by single-vector-offset summaries.

**SAEBench sparse probing (Figure 10).** ICA is competitive with public high-capacity SAEs (Gemma Scope, Qwen-Scope) across all three model families and consistently outperforms PCA and ITDA. Matryoshka SAE variants with 128 and 512 dictionary sizes stay below ICA, indicating that simply reducing SAE dictionary size does not close the gap.

**SAEBench TPP (Figure 12).** At small-to-medium intervention budgets (N features ablated), ICA's intended effect rises quickly with low unintended collateral. Public SAEs become competitive and can surpass ICA only at large N, where their overcomplete dictionaries allow more class-relevant latents to accumulate. The authors appropriately caution that TPP can be sensitive to benchmark settings (citing Chanin, 2026) and interpret it jointly with sparse probing and ICA–SAE overlap analyses.

**ICA–SAE relationship.** Nearest-SAE cosine similarity distribution peaks around 0.4–0.5, with broad tails in both directions (Figure 13). The overlap is moderate and stable across depth (Figure 14). Token-wise activation comparison (Figure 15) shows SAE features spike at isolated tokens while ICA directions vary smoothly across related spans — complementary activation patterns, not redundant ones. A byproduct: ICA–SAE comparisons reveal that some public Neuronpedia labels are incomplete (e.g., GPT-2 L10/C142 labeled "conditional repetition" by ICA audit vs. "numerical values in code contexts" by Neuronpedia, with the actual feature firing on both).

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty.** Moderate-to-high within the interpretability niche. ICA is a classical method (1990s), and prior work has applied it to word embeddings, fMRI, and even transformer activations. The genuine contributions are: (1) engineering rigor that makes FastICA actually work on modern LLM activations (the naive approach fails badly); (2) the ERF diagnostic, which is a concrete, useful operationalization of context-dependence; (3) the systematic human annotation protocol with secondary expert audit; and (4) a well-conducted SAEBench evaluation that quantitatively repositions ICA among current tools. The conceptual framing — "ICA as a first lens, not a weak baseline" — is valuable for the community even if it is not a theorem.

**Rigor.** Strong relative to much interpretability work. The human annotation protocol is unusually systematic: random sampling, ERF-guided inspection, iterative hypothesis testing with contrastive prompts, and an independent second-expert audit. The SAEBench evaluation follows a standard protocol. The paper is appropriately cautious about TPP (citing a benchmark reliability concern). One weakness: the paper is ongoing and some results may change. The comparison fairness for TPP depends on SAE checkpoints not having been optimized for those specific intervention tasks.

**Reproducibility.** High: GPU-parallel FastICA code (GitHub), all ICA checkpoints (HuggingFace), ICA Explorer with interactive demo, and complete annotation tables in appendices. The activation corpus (Pile-10k) and evaluation layers are specified precisely.

**Significance.** Meaningful for practitioners. If SAE training costs are a bottleneck for rapid exploration of new models, ICA at a fraction of the cost with competitive (sometimes superior) benchmark performance is a directly useful alternative. The ERF diagnostic and the interactive explorer are independently useful tools. The finding that ICA and SAE directions are complementary (moderate overlap, distinct activation patterns) has implications for interpretability methodology.

**Red flags.**
- The paper is labeled "Ongoing" — results may not be final.
- The TPP advantage of ICA over public SAEs at small budgets may partly reflect that public SAEs were trained for reconstruction (not for TPP) and at larger dictionary sizes than the evaluation budgets favor. The comparison is fair by benchmark standards but should not be over-generalized.
- Two-author team with one independent researcher; no peer review confirmation yet.
- ICA returns at most d components per layer; it cannot be overcomplete without architectural changes. The authors acknowledge this as the primary limitation.

## Strengths

- Engineering contribution is concrete and verifiable: the three stability recipes produce a measurable, large improvement in accepted layer count.
- ERF is a novel and practically useful diagnostic that grounds the kurtosis–interpretability link mechanistically.
- Human annotation is more rigorous than typical interpretability papers: random sampling, ERF-guided scope estimation, contrastive prompt testing, and independent second-expert audit.
- Multi-model validation (GPT-2 Small, Gemma 2 2B, Qwen 3.5 2B Base) with publicly released checkpoints.
- No training cost makes ICA especially attractive for rapidly exploring new model releases.
- ICA–SAE comparison is analytically careful, revealing complementarity rather than making zero-sum claims.
- Full artifact release (code, checkpoints, explorer, annotations) supports reproducibility and community adoption.

---

## Limitations

- **Compact basis:** Standard FastICA returns at most d components in d-dimensional space. It cannot provide the large overcomplete dictionaries (e.g., 16k–131k latents) that modern SAEs use for high-resolution feature discovery. The authors acknowledge this and list overcomplete ICA variants (deflationary ICA, JADE, Infomax, extended Infomax, heavy-tail-aware objectives) as future directions.
- **Residual stream states only:** Experiments focus on post-block residual stream activations. Analysis of MLP outputs, attention outputs, residual updates, or cross-layer shared bases is left as future work.
- **Non-Gaussianity as selection criterion:** High kurtosis is sufficient but not necessary for interpretability. Context-dependent directions with broad Gaussian-like projections may be invisible to ICA.
- **Row normalization discards norm information:** The stability recipe that makes ICA tractable also removes the token-level norm information that some interpretability methods treat as meaningful.
- **Ongoing paper:** Described as "Ongoing" in the document header; results and analyses may change before a finalized version.
- **TPP sensitivity:** The authors themselves note (citing Chanin, 2026) that TPP can be sensitive to benchmark settings and caution against over-interpreting it in isolation.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. The paper shows ICA and SAE directions have moderate but non-trivial overlap (cosine ~0.4–0.5). Is there a principled way to identify which ICA components have no close SAE counterpart — and do those "ICA-exclusive" directions correspond to interpretable concepts that SAEs systematically miss?
2. ERF reveals that middle layers contain the most long-context-dependent components. Does this correlate with known circuit-level findings (e.g., induction heads, entity tracking) or with attention pattern structure?
3. Row normalization discards activation-norm information. For steering/intervention experiments where norm magnitude matters, how much does this loss affect the quality of ICA-based interventions compared to raw-activation decompositions?
4. The paper mentions overcomplete ICA variants (JADE, Infomax, deflationary ICA) as future directions. At what dictionary size would overcomplete ICA converge in performance with SAEs — and at what computational cost?
5. ICA is currently fit independently at each layer. Would fitting a shared ICA basis across layers (analogous to model-wide SAE releases like Gemma Scope) reveal cross-layer persistent directions, and would those be more or less interpretable?
6. The ICA Explorer links components across adjacent layers by cosine similarity. Do high-similarity cross-layer component chains correspond to known circuits or residual-stream information flows?
