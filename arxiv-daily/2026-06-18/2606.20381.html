Key Finding The dominant FP4 format E2M1 carries an inherent, geometrically-induced shrinkage bias from asymmetric rounding bins — and a uniform-grid recipe (UFP4) measurably closes the gap to BF16.

Rethinking Shrinkage Bias in LLM FP4 Pretraining: Geometric Origin, Systemic Impact, and UFP4 Recipe
Qian Zhao, Kunlong Chen, Changxin Tian, et al. (Ant Group, Ling Team) · 2026-06-18 · arxiv.org/abs/2606.20381

Key Metric

0.97%BF16 loss gap
UFP4 cuts BF16-relative LM loss error: Dense 1.5B 1.26%→0.97% · MoE 7.9B 2.36%→1.85% · MoE 124B 1.73%→1.39%.

Why This Matters

A clean geometric theory of FP4 instability plus a ready recipe for pushing pretraining from FP8 to FP4 — with a concrete hardware call to expose uniform 4-bit grids.

Critical Edge

No shipping accelerator exposes native E1M2/INT4 FP4 GEMM — results rely on simulated quantization in BF16 pipelines, so real-silicon speedup and accuracy are unverified.

Methodology — bin geometry × multiplicative depth

E[ ρG(t) − t | t ∈ Bi ] = (li − ri) / 2 E2M1 bins: l ≠ r ⇒ systematic negative bias (bin @ q=2 → −0.125) uniform grid (E1M2/INT4): l = r ⇒ bias = 0 GEMM signal attenuation: Q(A) = αA·A + RA ⇒ principal × αA·αB < 1 depth K: ∏(1−δk) ≈ exp(−Σδk) (multiplicative accumulation)
  • Shrinkage Bias: asymmetric E2M1 bins (ri > li) give a closed-form negative expected RTNE error; uniform grids satisfy l=r and eliminate it.
  • RHT inversion: the Random Hadamard Transform shifts tensors from dynamic-range-limited to local-resolution-limited — improving E1M2 (ΔSQNR>0) but degrading E2M1 (ΔSQNR<0). On outlier-heavy fwd_x, E1M2 overtakes E2M1 after rotation (Beff 0.56→0.97).
  • UFP4 recipe: E1M2/INT4 uniform grid, 1×16 blocks, FP32 single-level scale, RHT on all 3 GEMMs (fwd_y + bwd_dx + bwd_dw), stochastic rounding on dY only.
  • Controlled E2M1 baseline: one-factor ablations (RHT scope, block size, SR scope, 2D weight scale, max_fpx) on Dense 1.5B/200B tokens; lowest-loss config frozen before all comparisons.

Strengths

  • Elegant, original theory: shrinkage-bias geometry with a clean closed form.
  • Strong experimental scale (up to 124B MoE) with a properly tuned, non-strawman E2M1 baseline.
  • Honest about the residual BF16 gap and the hardware dependency of impact.
  • Practical fused-kernel analysis: full-RHT overhead is small when fused.
  • Narrow, actionable hardware recommendation (E1M2/INT4 as first-class grid).

Limitations

  • No native E1M2/INT4 GEMM silicon — results are quantization simulation, not real throughput.
  • Evaluation is loss-only — no downstream benchmark accuracy.
  • Single architecture family (Ling MoE/Dense); no cross-architecture test.
  • No head-to-head vs MixFP4 (adaptive) or HiFloat4 beyond discussion.
  • No end-to-end wall-clock / throughput / memory numbers; only micro-kernel latency.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Does the ~0.3–0.5pp loss reduction translate to MMLU/GSM8K/coding gains, or wash out at scale?
  • Could a hybrid (E2M1 for raw outlier tensors, E1M2 post-RHT) dominate both?
  • Once native E1M2 silicon (Ascend 960 / HiFloat4) ships, do simulated gains hold under real accumulation?
  • Does shrinkage bias matter for FP4 inference with QuaRot/SpinQuant/FlatQuant rotations?
