Randomized Sketching is Robust to Low-Precision Rounding on GPUs

arXiv: 2606.20195

Date: 2026-06-18

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20195

一句话总结： 作者在 GPU 上实现 SparseStack 稀疏子空间嵌入并扩展到 FP16 混合精度，实验发现确定性/随机/抖动三种舍入规则对嵌入质量几乎无差别，确定性舍入因开销最低而成为最佳性能–精度折衷。
TL;DR: This paper implements a SparseStack generalization of the GPU CountSketch kernel and evaluates FP16 mixed-precision accumulation with deterministic, stochastic, and dithered rounding. On an NVIDIA H100, across incoherent, coherent, and adversarial least-squares problems, the choice of FP16 rounding rule has essentially no effect on subspace distortion or sketch-and-solve accuracy — the sketch distribution (SparseStack vs. CountSketch) is the dominant accuracy factor. Deterministic round-to-nearest FP16 SparseStack thus gives the best performance-accuracy tradeoff.

核心贡献

  • GPU SparseStack 实现： 将 Higgins et al. 的 GPU CountSketch 内核扩展为 SparseStack 算子（每列 ζ 个非零），在相干输入上显著提升嵌入质量，代价是更多原子更新与更高内存流量。
  • 三种 FP16 舍入内核： 实现并对比 FP16 累加下的确定性舍入（round-to-nearest-even）、精确随机舍入（exact stochastic rounding）、抖动舍入（dithered rounding），并用 half2 打包 + 32 位原子操作向量化以避免地址冲突。
  • 舍入规则无关性的实证结论： 在所测问题范围内，三种 FP16 SparseStack 变体产生的子空间畸变 η(S,U) 与最小二乘残差几乎相同，说明稀疏随机 sketching 对低精度舍入规则鲁棒。
  • GPU 性能–精度折衷： 确定性 FP16 SparseStack 因无额外采样开销而成为 SparseStack 系列中性能最优者；FP16 CountSketch 最快但继承 CountSketch 在相干输入上的缺陷。
  • 开源高性能内核交付： 提供一组 FP32/FP16 稀疏 sketch 累加 GPU 内核（CountSketch、SparseStack 及多舍入模式），按基准测试构成当前 GPU 随机 sketching 的 state-of-the-art。

Key Contributions

  • A GPU SparseStack kernel extending the Higgins et al. (2025) CountSketch implementation to ζ nonzeros per input column, exposing the atomic-contention cost of higher embedding quality.
  • FP16 mixed-precision sparse sketching kernels with three rounding rules (deterministic RN, exact stochastic, dithered), vectorized via CUDA half2 packed atomics to avoid 32-bit address conflicts.
  • An empirical finding that, across incoherent Gaussian, coherent identity-like, and adversarial least-squares regimes on H100, FP16 SparseStack accuracy is insensitive to the rounding rule — the sketch distribution, not quantization, drives distortion.
  • Identification of deterministic round-to-nearest FP16 SparseStack as the best practical performance-accuracy tradeoff, recovering much of the throughput lost to SparseStack's extra updates.
  • A reusable set of high-performance GPU sketch kernels benchmarked as the current state of the art for GPU randomized sketching.

Methodology

Problem setup. For a tall matrix A ∈ R^{m×n} (m ≫ n), randomized sketching applies S ∈ R^{d×m} so that SA preserves the column-space geometry, enabling sketch-and-solve least squares min_x ‖SAx − Sb‖₂. The authors focus on oblivious subspace embeddings (OSEs), measuring quality via distortion η(S,U) = max{|σ_max(SU)−1|, |1−σ_min(SU)|} on an orthonormal basis U.

Sketching operators. CountSketch places one nonzero per column (one atomic update per row); SparseStack stacks ζ independent CountSketch blocks, putting ζ nonzeros per column (cost O(ζ·nnz(A))) and breaking the quadratic-in-n embedding dimension down to near-optimal d = O(n²/ε²) with ζ = O(log n/ε). In experiments ζ = 4.

Implementation. Built on Higgins et al.'s ELLPACK-style CountSketch kernel (one thread per (input row, feature col) issuing atomicAdd). SparseStack adds an unrolled inner loop over the ζ subdomains. The metadata row_idx for the j-th nonzero of a column is drawn uniformly from the j-th block [j·d/ζ, (j+1)·d/ζ − 1]; signs are Rademacher scaled by 1/√ζ.

Mixed-precision vectorization. A naive FP16 port causes threads to collide on the same 32-bit word (which packs two FP16). The fix restructures thread mapping so one thread reads two consecutive columns, computes FP32 products, quantizes, packs via __halves2half2, and commits with a single packed atomicAdd. Three quantize variants are compared:

  1. Deterministic round-to-nearest-even via native __float2half (lowest overhead).
  2. Exact stochastic rounding: fetch floor/ceil FP16 bounds, interpolate distances, pick upper bound with probability (x−x⁻)/(x⁺−x⁻), ensuring unbiasedness at high arithmetic latency.
  3. Non-subtractive dithering: inject zero-mean uniform noise scaled to ~1 ULP via a single FMA, then rely on hardware RN.

Experiments (NVIDIA H100). n = 1024 fixed; sketch ratios d/n ∈ {2,4,8,16,32,64,128,256}; tall matrices m ∈ {2¹⁹,2²⁰,2²¹}. Inputs span incoherent Gaussian, coherent identity-like, and a coherence sweep U(α) = orth((1−α)Q_rand + α·[I;0]). Least-squares tests include dense synthetic (singular values 100→1, easy/hard noise) and a coherent adversarial construction A = [I_n; τG/√(m−n)] with τ = 1e−3 and 0.5 noise, row-permuted. Compared kernels: FP32 CountSketch, FP16 CountSketch-MxP, FP32 SparseStack, three FP16 SparseStack variants, and FlashSketch.

Results & Highlights

  • Rounding-rule irrelevance (core finding). Across all distortion, coherence-sweep, and least-squares experiments, the three FP16 SparseStack rounding rules are visually indistinguishable in accuracy and cluster with FP32 SparseStack.
  • Distribution dominates over quantization. On incoherent Gaussian inputs, every method tracks the Gaussian-theory reference curve √(n/d). On coherent identity-like inputs, CountSketch and FlashSketch show much larger distortion while SparseStack variants stay grouped — the gap is driven by sketch structure, not FP16 rounding.
  • Coherence sweep (Fig. 2). As measured coherence μ(U) increases, CountSketch/FlashSketch degrade sharply at small d/n; SparseStack stays flat, with FP32 and all FP16 rounding curves nearly superimposed.
  • Throughput ordering (consistent across sizes): CountSketch-MxP > CountSketch > SparseStack-detm ≈ SparseStack-dither > SparseStack-stoch ≈ FP32 SparseStack > FlashSketch. Deterministic and dithered FP16 SparseStack recover most of the throughput lost to the ζ updates; exact stochastic rounding is slower due to inner-loop boundary checks.
  • Dense least squares (Fig. 3). All methods converge to similar residuals once d/n is large; mixed precision mainly reduces sketch-construction cost (the dominant phase in the d/n=2 runtime breakdown). FP16 SparseStack variants are indistinguishable in residual; deterministic is fastest.
  • Adversarial coherent least squares (Table 1). CountSketch/CountSketch-MxP are fastest but produce residuals 1–3 orders of magnitude larger (κ(SA) ≈ 1.5e3–4.9e3) than the normal-equations baseline. SparseStack (FP32 and all three FP16 variants) match the baseline residual ≈4.3e–4 with nearly identical κ(SA) ≈ 2–7. Deterministic FP16 SparseStack gives the best runtime among accurate variants.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: moderate. The engineering contribution (extending CountSketch to SparseStack + FP16 vectorized atomics with three rounding modes) is solid and useful but is an incremental, implementation-focused advance. The conceptual surprise — that rounding rule doesn't matter — is a negative result that is honestly reported but also somewhat anticipated by the authors' own intuition that sketch randomness already decorrelates errors.
  • Rigor: empirical only, no theory. The central claim ("rounding-agnostic accuracy") is supported by experiments but not by a finite-precision OSE analysis. The authors explicitly acknowledge this ("We do not prove that deterministic FP16 SparseStack satisfies an OSE guarantee"). Quantization-error independence is asserted via intuition, not proven. Carson–Daužickaitė is cited as analogous precedent, not as a proof for this setting.
  • Reproducibility: reasonably good. Clear kernel listings (Listings 1–4), specified hardware (H100), fixed n=1024, ζ=4, and explicit problem constructions (τ, noise levels, sketch ratios). However, no code/repository link is given in the PDF — the kernels are described as a planned integration into Ginkgo.
  • Baselines: appropriate but limited. Comparisons include CountSketch (FP32/FP16), SparseStack (FP32/FP16), and FlashSketch — the right peers. But no dense Gaussian sketch, no sparse-sign sketch (Cohen), and no multi-GPU solver (Chen et al. 2025) are benchmarked end-to-end.
  • Scope and potential overclaiming risk. The "rounding doesn't matter" conclusion is carefully hedged with "in the tested regimes" and "for the tested regimes," which is good. However, the regime is narrow: only FP16 (no BF16/INT8), only n=1024, only ζ=4, no very large accumulation counts where FP16 bias could compound, and only a handful of input families. Extrapolation to extreme coherence, very small d/n, or much taller matrices is not tested.
  • Statistical reporting. Results are mostly single-run point values or visually-overlaid curves; no variance/error bars are reported despite stochastic rounding and sketch randomness being in the loop. This is a real weakness for a paper whose headline is about stochastic behavior.
  • Red flags: None severe. No cherry-picking evident; the adversarial problem is genuinely hard for CountSketch and the SparseStack advantage is real. The throughput win for deterministic FP16 SparseStack is modest (≈15–20% over FP32 SparseStack), so the practical payoff, while real, should not be oversold.

Strengths

  • Clean, well-engineered GPU kernels with concrete code and a vectorization trick (half2 packed atomics) that is independently reusable.
  • Honest reporting of a negative result (rounding rule doesn't matter) with a sensible mechanistic explanation.
  • Strong baseline (FlashSketch) and a genuinely adversarial coherent least-squares benchmark where SparseStack's advantage is decisive.
  • Clear writing, well-defined metrics (distortion η, throughput in GB/s, relative residual, κ(SA)).
  • Credible author group with a path to production integration via Ginkgo.

Limitations

  • No theoretical finite-precision analysis; all conclusions are empirical.
  • Narrow precision scope (FP16 only); BF16, FP8, and integer formats untested.
  • Fixed n=1024 and ζ=4; sensitivity to these parameters is not systematically explored.
  • No error bars or variance estimates despite stochastic components.
  • No public code link in the preprint.
  • Throughput gains of FP16 SparseStack over FP32 SparseStack are modest; the biggest practical lever remains choosing SparseStack over CountSketch on coherent inputs.

Relevance

对 LLM 训练/推理的直接相关性有限——本文聚焦 RandNLA 的稀疏 sketching 内核与 GPU 混合精度实现，而非大模型本身。但其"低精度舍入规则在随机化算子下基本不影响精度"的结论，为高带宽受限、原子操作受限的 GPU 基础算子（可能用于大规模注意力近似、梯度投影、数据归因如 GraSS）采用 FP16/低精度提供了经验支撑，间接关系到推理效率与数值可靠性。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Does the rounding-rule irrelevance hold for BF16 and FP8 accumulation, and for much larger m (e.g., 2²⁴+) where FP16 bias could accumulate?
  • Can a finite-precision OSE bound be proved for quantized SparseStack, in the spirit of Carson–Daužickaitė's Nyström analysis?
  • How does the picture change with ζ ∈ {2,8,16} and with n ≫ 1024 (e.g., n = 10⁴–10⁵ typical in sketch-and-precondition solvers)?
  • Would error bars over many sketch seeds reveal any statistically significant rounding-rule effect that the single-run plots hide?
  • Can the FP16 SparseStack kernel accelerate end-to-end sketch-and-precondition iterative solvers (Chen et al. 2025) on multi-GPU systems, and does the throughput win compound over iterations?
  • Does the same rounding-agnostic behavior extend to sketching used inside attention approximation or gradient compression for LLM training?
