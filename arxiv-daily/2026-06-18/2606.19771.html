Beyond Entropy: Learning from Token-Level Distributional Deviations for LLM Reasoning

arXiv: 2606.19771

Date: 2026-06-18

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19771

一句话总结： 提出ICT框架，用Jensen-Shannon散度选出分布上最"独特"的前10% token进行稀疏梯度更新，理论上同时调控Shannon熵与二阶Rényi熵以避免熵塌缩/爆炸，在Qwen2.5(0.5B/1.5B/7B)七项基准上相对GRPO/20-Entropy/STAPO平均提升pass@4约4.58%，最高达14.9%。
TL;DR: The ICT (Independent Combinatorial Tokens) framework reframes RLVR token selection: instead of picking high-Shannon-entropy tokens, it selects the top 10% of tokens whose full logit distribution maximally deviates (Jensen-Shannon divergence) from the group-average distribution, and masks the other 90% from gradient updates. Backed by a Shannon + second-order Rényi entropy analysis showing these "unique" tokens sit at the critical bifurcation between entropy-collapse and entropy-explosion regimes, ICT yields ~4.58% average pass@4 gains (up to 14.9%) over GRPO/20-Entropy/STAPO on Qwen2.5-0.5B/1.5B/7B across 7 math/commonsense/Olympiad benchmarks — while using only 10% of the gradients.

核心贡献

  • 从标量熵转向分布特性： 首次用Jensen-Shannon (JS) 散度衡量每个token的logit分布相对组内平均分布的偏离，把"关键token"的定义从"高Shannon熵"改为"分布上独特"，规避了"相同熵值却代表截然不同结构"的歧义。
  • ICT稀疏策略梯度框架： 基于GRPO，仅对JS独特度排前10%的token施加梯度更新、其余90%掩码；引入warm-up阶段，并证明了"少即是多"——稀疏更新可媲美甚至超过全参数训练。
  • 双熵动力学理论： 用碰撞概率定义策略纯度β(π)，证明存在熵塌缩区(π(a)>β, ΔH2<0)与熵爆炸区(π(a)<β, ΔH2>0)，而位于临界分叉点附近的unique token能在同一方向上调控Shannon熵H1与二阶Rényi熵H2，从而隐式稳定探索。
  • 跨规模、跨域实证： 在Qwen2.5-0.5B/1.5B/7B与7个基准(Math500/GSM8K/MMLU-Stem/GPQA/AIME23/24/25)上，Total平均分别提升3.38/4.31/3.94个百分点，pass@4增益显著大于pass@1，说明产生了更多样的正确推理轨迹而非冗余路径。
  • 方法即插即用、开销极低： JS散度复用rollout阶段已有的logits批处理，不引入额外前向传播；稀疏反向传播反而节省算力。

Key Contributions

  • Distributional reframing of token importance: Replaces scalar Shannon-entropy selection with Jensen-Shannon divergence between each token's logit distribution and the group-averaged distribution, sidestepping the ambiguity that very different distributions can share the same Shannon entropy.
  • ICT sparse-GRPO framework: Selectively updates only the top-10% distributionally unique tokens and masks the rest; introduces a warm-up phase before activating the selector, and demonstrates "less-is-more" — sparse updates match or exceed full-parameter GRPO training.
  • Dual-entropy theoretical analysis: Defines strategy purity β(π) as collision probability and formalizes two opposing regimes — Entropy Collapse (π(a)>β ⇒ ΔH₂<0) and Entropy Explosion (π(a)<β ⇒ ΔH₂>0) — proving that critical branching tokens update both Shannon (H₁) and second-order Rényi (H₂) entropy coherently, preventing both pathologies without explicit entropy regularization.
  • Empirical scaling across model sizes and domains: On Qwen2.5-0.5B/1.5B/7B over 7 benchmarks (Math500, GSM8K, MMLU-Stem, GPQA, AIME23/24/25), ICT beats GRPO/20-Entropy/STAPO by +3.38/+4.31/+3.94 average Total points respectively; pass@4 gains consistently outpace pass@1, evidence of genuinely more diverse correct trajectories.
  • Practically free selector overhead: JS divergence reuses logits already computed during rollout scoring; the sparse backward pass actually reduces compute and memory pressure.

Methodology

ICT is built on Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO). The core idea is a distributional selector that picks which token positions contribute gradients.

  1. Group rollout and averaging. For each prompt q, sample G completions {oᵢ}. At decoding position t, form the group-averaged distribution P_avg(·|t) = mean over rollouts of softmax(L_{j,t}).
  2. Uniqueness score. For each token position (i,t), compute the Jensen-Shannon divergence u_{i,t} = D_JS(softmax(L_{i,t}) ∥ P_avg(·|t)), bounded in [0, log2] for numerical stability.
  3. Sparse mask. Keep the top-k% (default k=10) highest-uniqueness positions per response: M_{i,t} = 1{u_{i,t} ≥ Percentile(..., 100−k)}; mask the rest.
  4. Sparse-GRPO objective. Replace the standard GRPO per-token average with a mask-normalized sum: J_JS-GRPO(θ) = E[ Σ_i (1/Σ_t M_{i,t}) Σ_t M_{i,t}·Ψ_{i,t}(θ) ], where Ψ is the unchanged GRPO per-token objective (clipped ratio + KL penalty). Everything else (loss form, optimizer, reward) is identical to GRPO.
  5. Warm-up. For the first Θ steps, M_{i,t}=1 (dense updates) to stabilize the initial policy before sparsity activates.

Theoretical backbone. The authors use second-order Rényi entropy H₂ = −log Σ_a π(a)² and its "squared policy" p₂ ∝ π². They derive a first-order Taylor approximation for the entropy change induced by a small positive logit update Δθ to a single token a*:

ΔH₂(a) ≈ −2·Δθ_{s,a}·π(a)·( π(a)/β(π) − 1 )

so the sign depends solely on whether π(a*) exceeds β(π), yielding the two regimes. A homogeneity lemma shows that for dominant tokens (π(a)>e⁻¹), ∂H₁/∂π(a) and ∂H₂/∂π(a) are both negative, so reinforcing unique tokens co-regulates both entropies. Appendix A.3 then bridges high-JS tokens to the β(π) bifurcation boundary, arguing such tokens are maximally decision-sensitive and hence the right places to apply exploration-guided updates.

Results & Highlights

  • Main table (Table 1), Qwen2.5-7B Total: ICT 50.68 vs STAPO 48.54 vs GRPO 46.28 vs 20-Entropy 45.41 vs Base 46.52. On 1.5B: ICT 29.23 vs STAPO 25.71 vs GRPO 25.05; on 0.5B: ICT 16.35 vs GRPO 14.75.
  • Pass@4 vs Pass@1 differential: Gains in P@4 systematically exceed those in P@1 (e.g., 7B: +4.38 vs +3.50; 1.5B: +4.98 vs +3.64), indicating ICT produces a richer set of correct reasoning paths rather than redundant ones — direct evidence of better exploration.
  • Cross-domain generalization: Trained only on Math500/GSM8K but transfers to GPQA (commonsense/STEM) where 20-Entropy and GRPO show degradation. On 0.5B MMLU-Stem P@4, ICT reaches 40.62 vs GRPO 32.27.
  • Olympiad-level gains: AIME24 P@4 on 1.5B: ICT 13.64 vs STAPO 4.55 vs GRPO 4.56 — a ~3x improvement (the source of the 14.9% maximum relative gain claim, on small-sample AIME).
  • Sparsity ablation (Table 2, GSM8K 1.5B): 10%-unique 66.23/85.31 > 30%-unique 64.45/83.76 > 20%-unique 63.37/83.31 > 90%-frequent 60.62/85.08. Validates a sharp inflection in JS scores around the 10th percentile (Figure 5).
  • Selector comparison (Table 3, GSM8K): ICT-JS 66.23/85.31 > ICT-Wass 60.86/82.87 > ICT-KL 58.64/81.35. JS outperforms Wasserstein (geometric, ill-suited to categorical vocab) and KL (asymmetric, mode-seeking).
  • High/low-entropy ratio of selected tokens ≈ 1:1 (1.03 on GSM8K, 0.99 on MATH), empirically confirming the theoretical prediction that unique tokens balance both regimes.
  • Warm-up robustness (Figure 6): Activating sparsity at step 10/20/40 all converge to a comparable critic-score plateau (~0.6–0.8), so timing is not sensitive.
  • Token cloud (Figure 7): Unique tokens are content words ("remainder", "duck", "seventh"); frequent tokens are formatting scaffolding ("####", "the", "is") — consistent with the design intent.

  • Equal-contribution co-first authors (Zhengyang Li, Zeyu Liu).

  • The paper includes a substantial appendix with full gradient derivations, approximation-error bounds, an H₁/H₂ homogeneity proof, and detailed hyperparameters for both V100 and H200 clusters.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty (Moderate-High): Reframing critical-token selection from scalar entropy to full-distribution JS divergence is genuinely fresh and well-motivated; the H₂/strategy-purity lens is a clean way to argue about both collapse and explosion simultaneously. However, the broader idea of "sparse RLVR on a small critical token subset" overlaps substantially with recent prior work the authors themselves cite (20-Entropy / Beyond 80-20 rule, Critical Tokens, STAPO), so the conceptual delta is in the selection criterion, not the sparsity paradigm.
  • Rigor (Mixed — theory elegant, empirics under-reported): The information-theoretic derivation is clean and self-contained, but rests on a first-order Taylor approximation assuming single-token, small-step updates that the authors explicitly caveat (A.8) omits multi-token coupling, optimizer momentum (AdamW), and negative advantages. The H₁/H₂ homogeneity lemma only strictly holds for π(a)>e⁻¹≈0.37, which they concede is "indeed restrictive" in real LLM decoding (Appendix A.3.3 reports selected tokens have mean π=0.18±0.09) — so the Shannon-entropy co-control claim is more aspirational than proven for the actual selected tokens.
  • Reproducibility (Medium): Code is promised but not yet released ("will open-source in the future"). Hyperparameters are detailed for both V100 and H200 clusters, but A.7 openly admits standard deviations and paired significance tests are NOT yet reported — they are "will add" items. For small-sample benchmarks like AIME (30 problems) and GPQA, this is a real concern; the headline 14.9% max gain comes from AIME24 on 1.5B where P@4 went 4.56→13.64, i.e. ~3 extra problems out of 30.
  • Significance (Medium): The +3–4 average Total points are meaningful and consistent across three model scales, and the cross-domain (GPQA) transfer is a notable plus. But the gain is incremental over the STAPO baseline (which itself is close to GRPO) rather than transformative.
  • Red flags:
  1. The random-selection control is uncomfortably strong. Appendix A.5 reports that randomly choosing 10% of tokens yields GSM8K pass@1=63.65 / pass@4=84.99, only ~2.6 / 0.3 points below ICT-JS's 66.23 / 85.31. This suggests a large fraction of the benefit comes from any sparse update, not specifically from the JS uniqueness signal — undercutting the central claim that distributional deviation is the key driver. The paper does not discuss this gap.
  2. AIME25 = 0.00 across all methods on 0.5B (Table 1) — these cells contribute nothing and inflate the appearance of a 7-benchmark evaluation while being pure noise.
  3. Abstract numbers vs. body numbers: Abstract says "average pass@4 improvement of 4.58%" (the mean of 4.37/4.98/4.38 over the three baselines), which is fine, but the per-baseline framing in Section 1 ("4.37%, 4.98% and 4.38%") could mislead a skimmer into thinking these are three independent model-size gains rather than three baseline comparisons.
  4. Baselines are narrow: GRPO, 20-Entropy, and STAPO are the only comparisons. No DAPO, RLOO, GSPO, or other 2025-era RLVR improvements — so it is unclear how ICT stacks up against the strongest current public methods, only against token-selection siblings.
  5. No wall-clock/memory/throughput numbers despite the "computationally lightweight" selling point (promised "in the final version"); the FSDP offloading + 0.3 GPU-memory-util hints suggest the 7B runs were memory-constrained rather than efficient.
  6. Small-model V100 compute for 0.5B/1.5B limits claims about scaling; only the 7B run uses H200.

Strengths

  • Conceptually clean and well-motivated: the "same entropy, different structure" illustration (Figure 1) is an effective argument against scalar-entropy selection.
  • Theoretically grounded with a full derivation of H₂ gradient dynamics and the β(π) bifurcation, plus an honest appendix quantifying first-order-approximation error (Pearson >0.95, MAE <0.02 between predicted and measured ΔH₂).
  • Simple, drop-in modification to GRPO — only the gradient mask changes; everything else (reward, advantage, clipping, KL) is untouched, making adoption trivial.
  • Genuine cross-domain transfer (GPQA, MMLU-Stem) from math-only training, unlike the 20-Entropy baseline which degrades off-distribution.
  • Sparse backward pass is an honest computational win, even if not yet benchmarked.
  • Ablations (ratio, selector metric, warm-up timing, high/low-entropy composition) are thorough and directly support the theory.

Limitations

  • Theory assumes first-order, single-token, small-step updates; authors themselves flag (A.8) that momentum, coupling, and negative advantages are omitted.
  • The H₁/H₂ homogeneity condition (π(a)>0.37) rarely holds for actually-selected tokens (mean π≈0.18), so the Shannon-entropy co-control is only approximate and "left to future work" for large-scale empirical verification.
  • No statistical significance / standard errors reported yet (A.7); for AIME/GPQA small samples this materially weakens the headline gains.
  • Random 10% selection already captures most of the GSM8K benefit, so the marginal value of the JS criterion specifically is smaller than the framing implies.
  • Limited baselines (no DAPO/RLOO/GSPO); fixed 10% ratio is not adaptive.
  • Code not yet released; wall-clock/throughput claims unverified.
  • AIME25 is all zeros for the 0.5B model — effectively dead benchmark cells.

Relevance

这与LLM训练/推理优化的核心议题高度相关：ICT本质上是"哪些token才值得算梯度"的稀疏化RLVR训练方法，直接触及训练稳定性、探索-利用平衡与计算效率——既是训练方法创新，也可视为一种隐式的可解释性/重要性归因（被选中的unique token正是推理的关键分叉点），对关心RLVR可靠性、训练效率与token级可解释性的研究者都有借鉴价值。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Given that random 10% selection already reaches 63.65/84.99 on GSM8K vs ICT's 66.23/85.31, how much of the gain is from the JS uniqueness signal vs. simply from sparsity/regularization? A sparsity-matched random baseline at every model size is needed.
  • Does ICT compound with stronger RLVR methods (DAPO, GSPO, RLOO), or is it orthogonal to clip-ratio / advantage-estimator improvements?
  • The H₁/H₂ homogeneity breaks below π≈0.37, yet selected tokens average π≈0.18 — can the selector be biased toward tokens in the >0.37 regime, and would that change behavior?
  • Is the fixed 10% threshold a artifact of the JS-score elbow, or does the optimal ratio shift with model scale / dataset / rollout temperature?
  • Can the JS selector be made advantage-aware without the reported redundancy (Appendix A.3.4 suggests ũ = u·max(A,0)^λ with λ=0.1 matches pure JS — is that a sign the alignment is already saturated, or a missed opportunity)?
  • How does ICT interact with entropy-explosion-prone settings (very long rollouts, high temperature, tool-use agents) where collapse-vs-explosion dynamics may differ?
  • The authors promise wall-clock/memory/throughput numbers — without them, the "computationally lightweight" claim is unverified; what is the actual end-to-end training speedup vs. dense GRPO?
  • Could the distributional-deviation idea extend to process supervision / verifier-guided search, where per-step uniqueness could prune the search tree more intelligently than per-step entropy?
