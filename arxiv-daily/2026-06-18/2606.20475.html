Key Finding Accumulating signed marginal-advantage evidence across batches lets an offline agent separate stably-effective memory edits from accidental hits — winning 14/16 settings at ~75% less token cost.

Marginal Advantage Accumulation for Memory-Driven Agent Self-Evolution
Mingyu Yang, Keye Zheng, Congchao Cheng, et al. (Alibaba Intl.) · 2026-06-18 · arxiv.org/abs/2606.20475

Key Metric

~75%fewer tokens
Wins 14/16 settings (4 benchmarks × 4 models). ALFWorld/Qwen3.7-Max: 89.4% vs SkillOpt 88.1%, with 0 rollouts & ~5× faster wall-clock.

Why This Matters

A fully offline post-processing route to agent self-evolution — using weak sign-supervision to cut online rollout cost without losing quality on strong models.

Critical Edge

It loses precisely on weak-model × hardest-task cells, where judge sign-accuracy sits near 61.5% (α≈0.115) — barely above the floor that makes accumulation pointless.

Methodology — three standard parts, one structural design layer

The contribution is the framing — two structural conditions (alignability: track semantically equivalent ops across batches; comparability: cross-batch signals on a consistent scale) — not component novelty. MAA instantiates it with three off-the-shelf pieces:

1. Identity merge
Embedding merge (Qwen3-Embedding-4B, τ=0.85) → alignability. 15.7% of candidates merged.
2. Baseline diff
δ = u(Mi,B) − u(M,B) from LLM proxy → comparability. Shuffled, side-by-side to kill position bias.
3. Per-op EMA
β=0.9, bias-corrected → cancels alternating signs, amplifies stable direction. Safety floor: no-worse-than Reactive.
  • Directional-alignment assumption: P(signδ = signΔEval) ≥ ½ + α, α>0. Under it, EMA sign converges exponentially (sign-SGD).
  • Fully offline: zero environment rollouts during optimization; intervenes only on context-injected memory M = {mi=(idi,ci)}.
  • Engineering: candidate pool (≤20, mfloor=−50, max_age=10), top-k budget with linear decay, conflict resolution, best-checkpoint rollback on Dval.
  • Evaluation: 4 benchmarks (ScienceAgentBench, ALFWorld, SpreadsheetBench, HotpotQA) × 4 models (strong/weak × same/cross-family), 5 seeds, disjoint splits.

MAA vs Reactive gain scales with complexity: +7.1pp (SpreadsheetBench) > +4.2 (ALFWorld) > +2.7 (HotpotQA) > +2.3 (ScienceAgentBench) — cross-batch accumulation matters most where inter-batch distribution differences are large. Three EMA-trajectory patterns (stably-effective / spurious / scene-specific) are directly visualized in case studies.

Strengths

  • Clean, reusable conceptual framing (alignability vs comparability) generalizing beyond the method.
  • Honest mechanism diagnosis testing sign-alignment, not just end-to-end numbers.
  • Strong, concrete cost result: token / rollout / dollar / time breakdown.
  • Thorough ablation isolating differencing (dominant) vs amplitude (minor).
  • Illustrative case studies make the cancellation mechanism intuitive.

Limitations

  • Narrow baselines: only SkillOpt & Trace2Skill run; the reactive-memory family is discussed, not benchmarked.
  • Proprietary-model dependency (Qwen3.7-Max, GPT-5.4) for agent & judge — API-gated.
  • Loses on weak-model + hardest-task; value bounded by judge accuracy.
  • Marginal, possibly non-significant wins on strong models; no significance test, n=5.
  • LLM-as-judge length/content biases only partially mitigated; no code release mentioned.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How sensitive is α to the judge model — does a cross-family judge collapse MAA to the safety floor?
  • Do the 0.8–1.7pp strong-model margins survive a paired significance test across seeds?
  • How would the untested reactive/evolvable-memory family (A-MEM, MEMO, HyMEM, MEMGPT) actually compare under the same budget?
  • Does offline MAA evidence transfer usefully as a warm-start for online GRPO/RAGEN, or does proxy bias contaminate the policy gradient?
