Critical Percolation as a Synthetic Data Model for Interpretability

arXiv: 2606.20347

Date: 2026-06-18

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20347

一句话总结： 提出基于临界平均场渗流（critical mean-field percolation）的合成数据模型：在高维空间中嵌入幂律分布的稀疏分形簇，并用二叉树上的层级潜变量生成目标，从而得到一个同时具备稀疏性、自相似性、幂律统计与解析可解性、且已知临界指数无需调参的可解释性测试床；配套给出近线性时间的 cyclic coalescent 算法联合采样随机树与其层级分解，并通过线性探测（linear probe）实验证明 MLP 激活能线性解码真实层级潜变量。
TL;DR: The authors introduce a synthetic interpretability benchmark built on critical mean-field percolation clusters embedded in high-dimensional space, where sparse power-law fractal clusters are labeled by hierarchical latent variables on binary trees; they contribute a near-linear-time cyclic coalescent algorithm (building on Pitman's additive coalescent) to jointly sample the tree and its latent decomposition, and show via linear probes that a transformer-FFN-style residual MLP linearly represents the ground-truth hierarchical latents in its activations.

核心贡献

  • 提出渗流数据模型：首次将临界平均场渗流（Bethe 格子，d ≫ d_u=6，p=p_c）显式构造为层级合成数据集。输入是稀疏、低维（分形维 D=4）、幂律大小分布（指数 τ=5/2）的分形簇；目标由二叉潜变量森林上的层级函数生成。模型同时具备稀疏性、分类（taxonomic）层级、幂律、低本征维、自相似五项自然数据的关键结构性质。
  • 解析可解、无需调参：平均场+临界性使模型的关键性质由已知的临界指数（τ=5/2, σ=1/2, D=4, p_c=1/(2d−1)）固定，避免了合成基准常见的任意超参设定，赋予理论上的"原理性"。
  • cyclic coalescent 算法：基于渗流簇 ↔ 均匀随机标号树（Aldous）↔ additive coalescent（Pitman 1999）的映射，提出一种 cyclic coalescent 变体，利用 union-find（按秩合并+路径折半）在近线性 O(n·α(n)) 时间内联合采样一棵均匀随机树及其根化二叉合并（层级潜变量）分解。这是直接仿真高维渗流所做不到的高效层级构造。
  • 合成数据生成流程：用偏好附着法生成符合临界渗流的簇大小分布（推导出 p_01=1/3），对每个簇用 cyclic coalescent 生成潜树，再用分支随机游走嵌入到 d 维空间，给出可扩展到任意规模的端到端流程。
  • 探测实验证明线性可解码性：训练残差 MLP（模拟 transformer FFN 块，4×扩展+ReLU），用线性探测从激活回归真实潜变量 z_i，发现潜变量可被线性解码；并提出用 FOM≡s_latent/√s_cluster 这一指标比纯深度更能刻画潜变量的统计重要性，其 MSE 呈幂律趋势。

Key Contributions

  • A percolation data model: critical mean-field site percolation on a Bethe lattice (d ≫ 6, p = p_c) yields sparse, low-intrinsic-dimension (D=4) fractal clusters with a pure power-law size distribution (τ=5/2), labeled by hierarchical latent variables on a binary forest — simultaneously capturing sparsity, taxonomic hierarchy, power laws, low intrinsic dimension, and self-similarity, all fixed by known critical exponents without hyperparameter tuning.
  • A near-linear cyclic coalescent algorithm (O(n·α(n))) that jointly samples a uniform random labeled tree and its rooted binary merger hierarchy, exploiting the equivalence between critical mean-field clusters, uniform random labeled trees (Aldous), and the additive coalescent (Pitman 1999); implemented with union-find (union-by-rank + path halving).
  • An end-to-end synthetic dataset generation pipeline: preferential attachment for cluster sizes (p_01=1/3 derived from percolation), cyclic coalescent for latent trees, and branching random-walk embedding into d-dim space.
  • Probing evidence of linear representation: a residual MLP (transformer-FFN-style block) trained on the data linearly encodes the ground-truth hierarchical latents in its activations; a proposed figure-of-merit FOM = s_latent/√s_cluster explains per-latent probe MSE (power-law trend) better than latent depth.

Methodology

  • Data model (Sec. 2): Discretize unit cube [0,1)^d into hypercubic lattice Λ; occupy each site i.i.d. with p; at criticality p=p_c=1/(2d−1) (Bethe approximation z=2d). Targets y_x = f(Z_x) where Z_x is the top-down path of latents in forest F. Three assumptions make it tractable: mean-field (d≫6), criticality (p=p_c), and hierarchy (binary-tree latent construction). Activating bonds between adjacent sites sequentially, each merge introduces a fresh latent as parent of the two cluster roots.
  • Cyclic coalescent (Sec. 3): Variant of Pitman's coalescent. Two ideas: (1) one uniform draw simultaneously picks a tree size-proportionally AND a node uniformly; (2) trees arranged in a fixed random cyclic order, so the chosen tree merges only with its successor (preserving uniformity). Algorithm 1 runs n−1 union-find iterations, each amortized O(α(n)).
  • Synthetic data (Sec. 4): Preferential attachment (create new cluster w.p. p_01=1/3, else grow size-proportional cluster) for power-law cluster sizes; cyclic coalescent per cluster for latent trees; branching random walk embedding; targets y_x = (1/√(1+d_x)) Σ z_i^(x) (Eq. 4), a normalized linear sum of latent values, each z_i~N(0,1).
  • Experiments (Sec. 5): Two datasets — single cluster (2×10^5 points) and multi-cluster (2×10^6 points, clusters <500 dropped). Residual MLP: linear projection → L=3 MLP blocks (4× expand, ReLU, residual, Eq. 5) → linear output; d_model=256 (one-cluster) / 512 (multi-cluster); AdamW + cosine schedule. Baselines: Ridge, 1-NN. Linear probes (Alain & Bengio 2016) trained on raw input and each layer's residual stream / hidden activations to regress each z_i, stratified by latent depth and FOM.

Results & Highlights

  • Task is genuinely nonlinear: Ridge R² = 0.51 / 0.39 (one/multi), MSE 0.20 / 0.59; 1-NN (best practical estimate) R² = 0.94 / 0.86; MLP matches 1-NN at R² = 0.94 / 0.88, MSE 0.026 / 0.12 (Table 1).
  • Linear decodability confirmed (Fig. 4): Per-latent probe MSE on MLP activations is consistently lower than on raw input across all layers; hidden-layer activations probe better than residual stream. The MSE/raw-MSE ratio decreases with increasing FOM, i.e. coarse-grained (high-FOM) latents are more easily decoded from input geometry.
  • Power-law in FOM, not depth: Per-latent MSE shows a clean power-law trend against FOM (fits shown, slopes −0.64 / −0.20 for the last block). Plotting MSE vs latent depth alone (Fig. 5) shows no clear power law — FOM is the better importance predictor. Depth > 100 shows artificially low MSE due to selection bias (only the largest clusters reach deep latents).
  • No layerwise progression: Probe MSE is nearly identical across MLP blocks; no monotonic buildup of a hierarchical sum across layers. The authors flag this as a negative finding — the network does not obviously organize latents by depth across layers, and cumulative-sum probes (Fig. 7, App. F) confirm the absence of trend.
  • Artifact transparency: The low-FOM MSE drop in Fig. 4b is explicitly attributed to the min-cluster-size (500) filter, an honest disclosure rather than a hidden manipulation.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty (Moderate): The percolation-as-data-model idea is largely a re-packaging of Brill (2024/2025b) with a newly added explicit hierarchical latent construction. The genuinely new technical artifact is the cyclic coalescent algorithm and its O(n·α(n)) union-find implementation — a clean engineering/algorithmic contribution rather than a conceptual breakthrough. Combining sparsity+hierarchy+power-law+fractality in one principled, parameter-free model is a useful synthesis but each ingredient is well known.
  • Rigor (Mixed): Mathematical claims (critical exponents, Aldous/Pitman mappings) rest on established results; the cyclic coalescent correctness proof is deferred to App. E.2 (not visible in extracted text). Empirically, the experiments are correlational only — the authors themselves state "causal interventions are needed to validate specific interpretability hypotheses." Probe methodology is standard but defensible.
  • Reproducibility (Good): Hyperparameters in Tables 4–5 (App. B), two public code repos, deterministic seeds, clear dataset-construction recipe (p_01=1/3 derived). Main reproducibility risk is the heavy dependence on the cyclic coalescent implementation details, which are only sketched in Algorithm 1.
  • Significance (Narrow, forward-looking): As a workshop proof-of-concept, value is conditional. The paper does NOT yet demonstrate that percolation data reproduces SAE pathologies (feature splitting, absorption, composition) — it only shows linear decodability, which is a relatively weak bar (many toy tasks satisfy it). The real test — whether this benchmark discriminates among interpretability methods better than existing synthetic models (Chanin & Garriga-Alonso 2026, Bussmann et al. 2025) — is left to future work.
  • Red flags / concerns:
  • Self-citation density: The data model is the first author's own prior work; the "hierarchical construction" addition is the main delta, which is somewhat thin for a standalone claim of contribution.
  • No SAE experiments: Despite motivating the entire paper around SAE feature splitting/absorption, no SAE is ever run; the only interpretability tool used is a linear probe. This is a substantial gap between motivation and evidence.
  • Data realism: Authors concede the model assumes random population, lacks compositional feature relationships, and supports only vectorized regression — far from tokenized/classification LLM data. The bridge to "natural data" is asserted (fractals are ubiquitous) rather than empirically validated.
  • MLP-as-transformer-proxy: d_model ≤ 512, L=3, scalar regression — very far from real LLMs; claims about "LLM features" are speculative extrapolation.
  • Negative result under-reported: "no layerwise progression" is arguably the most interesting empirical finding and slightly undermines the interpretability narrative, but is presented briefly and somewhat deflated.

Strengths

  • Principled, parameter-free benchmark: critical exponents fix all key statistics, removing a major arbitrariness in synthetic interpretability data.
  • Unifies five desirable data properties (sparsity, taxonomic hierarchy, power laws, low intrinsic dimension, self-similarity) in a single, simple, textbook-grounded model.
  • The cyclic coalescent is an elegant, efficient, and genuinely useful algorithmic contribution; O(n·α(n)) enables arbitrary-scale generation.
  • Honest reporting: artifacts (min-cluster filter effect), negative results (no layerwise trend), and limitations are stated plainly.
  • Code and full hyperparameters released; good reproducibility posture.
  • Clean connection made between data-level taxonomic hierarchy (IS-A, set inclusion) and the latent forest, with an explicit comparison to compositional (HAS-A, part-whole) hierarchy (Table 2, Fig. 6).

Limitations

  • Only correlational probing; no causal interventions and, critically, no SAE experiments despite SAE pathologies being the core motivation.
  • Model supports only vectorized inputs and scalar regression; no tokenized data or classification — a large gap vs. real LLM interpretability needs.
  • Assumes random population of data and no compositional feature relationships; supercritical (cyclic-cluster) extension for overlapping concepts is future work.
  • MLP scale (d_model ≤ 512, depth 3) is tiny; extrapolation to LLM-scale features is unsupported.
  • Taxonomic-only hierarchy; the complementary compositional regime that dominates much PCFG-based LLM-data theory is out of scope.
  • Percolation → "natural data" link is argued by analogy (fractals are ubiquitous) rather than measured; no fit to a real dataset's intrinsic-dimension or cluster-size distribution is shown.

Relevance

  • Interpretability (direct, primary): The paper is explicitly a mechanistic-interpretability testbed. Its most actionable payoff for LLM interpretability is as a ground-truth benchmark for SAEs: because latents and their hierarchy are known by construction, one can quantitatively test whether SAEs recover the correct features and whether increasing dictionary size induces splitting/absorption analogous to real LLM SAEs. The negative "no layerwise progression" result is itself a caution for interpretability researchers who assume depth-aligned feature hierarchy.
  • LLM training / scaling laws (indirect): The percolation model's dual power-law structure (cluster sizes τ=5/2 and intrinsic dimension D=4) is positioned as a data-grounded explanation of neural scaling laws (Brill 2024), relevant to understanding why scaling works and to predicting data-limited regimes.
  • Representations / linear probing: Confirms and refines the linear-representation hypothesis for hierarchical latents, and introduces FOM as a data-geometry predictor of probe difficulty — useful for designing and evaluating probe studies on real models.
  • Agents / reliability (weak): No direct agent or reliability content; potential long-range relevance only if percolation structure proves to model the latent concept graph underlying tool-use or compositional generalization, which the paper does not address.
  • Data curation: The generation pipeline is a practical tool for constructing controlled benchmarks with known ground truth, valuable for the broader synthetic-data-for-evaluation subfield.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Do SAEs trained on percolation-trained MLP activations reproduce feature splitting, absorption, and composition in a way that quantitatively matches real LLM SAE pathologies? (The headline open question.)
  • Does the cyclic coalescent extend efficiently to supercritical percolation (cycles allowed) to model overlapping/compositional concepts, and does that better match LLM feature geometry?
  • Can the FOM power-law slope (−0.64 one-cluster vs −0.20 multi-cluster) be derived analytically from the percolation exponents, giving a theory-predicted probe-difficulty law?
  • Why is there no layerwise progression of hierarchical latents — is this a property of the residual-MLP architecture, of the normalized-sum target (Eq. 4), or genuine? Would a transformer with attention show depth-aligned hierarchy?
  • How sensitive are the linear-decoding results to embedding dimension d, cluster-size filtering threshold (500), and the random-walk embedding? Is the FOM trend an embedding artifact?
  • Can the model be extended to tokenized/classification data so it can serve as a benchmark for LLM interpretability rather than only MLP regression?
  • Does the percolation cluster-size distribution (τ=5/2) fit any real datasets' measured intrinsic-dimension/cluster statistics, validating the "natural data is fractal" claim empirically?
  • What causal interventions (activation patching, SAE feature ablation) would distinguish genuine hierarchical feature use from coincidental linear decodability?
