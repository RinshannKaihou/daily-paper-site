> **一句话总结：** 本文提出 Anchor Bias 这一几何度量，揭示多语大模型处理语码转换（CS）输入时会按语法骨架偏向源/目标语言锚点（目标框架 CS 偏移更大且 QA 退化更严重），并据此提出推理时干预方法 CANVAS——从输入本身抽取源语言 canvas，对目标语言 token 的上层隐藏状态做软插值对齐，在不更新参数的情况下稳定恢复 CS QA F1。

> **TL;DR:** The paper introduces Anchor Bias, a geometric metric showing that a code-switched hidden state is pulled toward whichever language supplies the grammatical frame; target-framed CS drifts target-ward and suffers larger QA drops. The authors propose CANVAS, an inference-time intervention that extracts an in-context source-language "canvas" and softly steers target-token hidden states in the top 30% of layers back toward the source anchor, recovering QA F1 across 13 MLLMs and four CS conditions without parameter updates.

## 核心贡献

- **Anchor Bias 度量（几何诊断）**：首次提出用 `AB = s̃(q_SRC, q_CS) − s̃(q_TGT, q_CS)` 量化 CS 隐藏态偏向源语言还是目标语言锚点；采用层级随机余弦归一化消除各向异性，并取上半层均值得到稳健分数。
- **语法骨架效应（Grammar-frame effect）**：跨 10 个 MLLM 一致发现——源框架 CS（GF-SRC）平均 AB=+0.246（源锚定），目标框架 CS（GF-TGT）平均 AB=−0.320（目标偏向），摆幅 ΔAB=+0.566；对应 QA 退化 GF-SRC 仅 −3.5 F1，GF-TGT 达 −6.2 F1，AB 与 ΔF1 正相关（ρ=+0.485, p<0.01）。
- **层级稳健性**：GF-SRC 在 75.9% 的层为源锚定，GF-TGT 仅 36.2%；最强目标偏向集中在最后 30% 层，为干预位点提供依据。
- **CANVAS 干预方法**：基于内部锚信号，设计 5 阶段推理时干预（token 划分 → 源 canvas 构造 → 对齐打分 → 自适应插值强度 α=clip(α₀−λγ) → 在线 canvas 软插值），仅在目标 token、上层、prefill 阶段作用，写入 KV cache 后贪心解码。
- **广泛验证**：13 个 MLLM、4 种 CS 条件下平均 F1 全面提升；最大增益出现在 GF-TGT；泛化到 PingPong 多轮对话（双语 +2.58、三语 +1.09 Acc）、RAG-QA、主题分类、西班牙语源锚点等。

## Key Contributions

- **Anchor Bias metric** — a geometric, anisotropy-normalized measure of whether a CS hidden state is source- or target-anchored, aggregated over the upper transformer layers.
- **Grammar-frame effect** — a consistent, architecture-spanning finding: source-framed CS stays source-anchored while target-framed CS shifts target-ward, and the latter correlates with larger QA F1 degradation.
- **Layer-wise diagnosis** — anchoring contrast concentrates in the final ~30% of layers, motivating a late-layer intervention window.
- **CANVAS** — a training-free, inference-time steering method that builds an in-context source "canvas" from the CS input itself and softly interpolates target-token hidden states toward it, with an adaptive controller α tied to the instance-level alignment score γ.
- **Generalization evidence** — gains extend across 13 MLLMs, four CS conditions, multi-turn dialog (PingPong), trilingual mixing, retrieval-augmented QA, and a non-English (Spanish) source anchor.

## Methodology

**Controlled diagnostic setup.** For each fact-seeking query (from SimpleQA Verified), the authors build a matched quadruplet `Q(x) = {q_SRC, q_TGT, q_GF-SRC, q_GF-TGT}`:
- `q_SRC` / `q_TGT`: pure source / target monolingual anchors (English / 9 target langs: Bengali, Spanish, French, Hindi, Korean, Marathi, Toba Batak, Urdu, Chinese).
- `q_GF-SRC`: English syntactic frame with target lexical inserts; `q_GF-TGT`: target frame with English inserts. Grammar-forced variants come from CodeMixQA; `q_TGT` produced by Qwen3-235B-A22B.
- 955 unique source queries × 2,880 matched comparisons.

**Anchor Bias (§3.1).** Mean-pool question-content token span per layer → `rℓ(q)`; raw `ABℓ = cos(rℓ(qs),rℓ(qc)) − cos(rℓ(qt),rℓ(qc))`. Normalize by layer-specific random-pair mean `µℓ` to combat anisotropy: `s̃ = (s−µℓ)/(1−µℓ)`. Final score averages normalized AB over the upper half of layers.

**CANVAS (§4).** Five stages:
1. Token partition via lightweight language tagging → `Csrc ∪ Ctgt ∪ Coth`.
2. Source canvas `csrcℓ` and target anchor `atgtℓ` from mean-pooling over respective token sets; canvas direction `uℓ = (csrcℓ − atgtℓ)/‖·‖₂`.
3. Canvas alignment score `γ = mean_{ℓ∈Lctrl} cos(mℓ, uℓ)` over control layers `Lctrl = {⌊0.7L⌋,…,L−1}`.
4. Adaptive intensity `α = clip[αmin,αmax](α₀ − λγ)` — stronger correction for more target-skewed inputs, single global schedule.
5. Online soft interpolation at each target-token position: `ĥℓ,t = (1−α)hℓ,t + α csrcℓ`, saved into KV cache; greedy decoding follows.

**Models.** Aya-Expanse-8B, Qwen3.5-{4B,9B,27B}, Llama-3.1/3.3-70B-Instruct, Mixtral-8x7B-Instruct, Phi-3.5-MoE-Instruct, Qwen3-30B-A3B, Qwen3.6-35B-A3B (plus Llama3.2-1B, Mistral-7B, Llama3.1-8B in the CANVAS table). Greedy decoding throughout; translations via Qwen3-235B-A22B through OpenRouter.

**Evaluation.** Normalized token-overlap F1 against SimpleQA reference; CS degradation = F1(q_SRC) − F1(q_CS). Internal signal = hidden-state comparison vs both monolingual anchors.

## Results & Highlights

**Anchor Bias (Table 1, 10 MLLMs):**
- Mean GF-SRC `AB = +0.246`, GF-TGT `AB = −0.320`, ΔAB `+0.566`. Largest swing: Qwen3.6-35B-A3B (ΔAB = +1.226, F1 drop −10.3 under GF-TGT).
- QA F1 mean drops: GF-SRC −3.5, GF-TGT −6.2 (vs SRC). Correlation `AB ↔ ΔF1`: ρ = +0.485, p < 0.01.
- Source-anchored layer share: GF-SRC 75.9% vs GF-TGT 36.2% (Δ +39.7).

**CANVAS (Table 2, 13 MLLMs):**
- Adapt beats BASE on average F1 for every model; largest gains on GF-TGT (e.g., Qwen3.6-35B-A3B +3.58, Qwen3-30B-A3B +2.48, Qwen3.5-27B +1.74, Llama3.3-70B +1.66, Aya-Expanse-8B +1.41).
- Statistically significant (BH-FDR ∗/∗∗) for 8/13 models; Rand (random direction) baseline much weaker, confirming directionality matters.
- Figure 3 (PCA onto SRC–TGT axis): centroids shift rightward (source-ward) post-intervention for both GF-SRC and GF-TGT.
- Figure 4: adaptive α scales with target-heaviness — GF-SRC (ρ=0.59, γ=−0.007, α=0.45) vs GF-TGT (ρ=0.40, γ=−0.139, α=0.61).

**Generalization (Table 3, PingPong multi-turn):** bilingual CS +2.58 Acc mean (Llama-3.1-8B +3.94, Aya-Expanse-8B +3.99); trilingual +1.09. Appendix also reports gains on RAG-QA and topic classification, and transfer to a Spanish source anchor.

**Safety:** on pure monolingual / source-heavy inputs (ρ_SRC ≥ 0.80), the adaptive controller drives α toward identity, preserving parametric performance.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (High).** The core move — using grammar-forced CS as a controlled probe to locate the CS hidden state on a SRC–TGT axis, then operationalizing that geometry as both a diagnostic (Anchor Bias) and an intervention target (CANVAS) — is genuinely fresh. Most prior CS work stays at the answer/behavior level; this is a representation-level account with a closed-loop mitigation. The "canvas from the input itself" trick (no external model call, no training) is a clean engineering contribution.
- **Rigor (Strong).** Anisotropy normalization with layer-specific random baselines, upper-half-layer aggregation, order-control experiments (App. G.5), random-direction (Rand) ablation, BH-FDR multiple-comparison correction, and a monolingual-safety check together form a careful methodology. Correlation AB↔ΔF1 (ρ=+0.485, p<0.01) is reported honestly rather than oversold.
- **Reproducibility (Good, with caveats).** Code is promised; all datasets/models are public. However: (i) hyperparameters α₀, λ, αmin, αmax are described as a "single global schedule across all evaluated models" but their exact values live in appendices (H.1/H.15) not seen here — readers must verify they were not per-model tuned despite the claim; (ii) translations depend on Qwen3-235B-A22B through OpenRouter, introducing a non-deterministic upstream dependency; (iii) "Qwen3.5" / "Qwen3.6" model identifiers are unusual and should be cross-checked against the release schedule.
- **Significance (Medium-High).** Practical upside is real but bounded: average BASE F1 is low (≈7–15 across models in Table 2, i.e., the task is hard), and absolute gains are ~1–3 F1 — meaningful as a proof-of-mechanism but not a deployment-grade CS fix. The conceptual contribution (frame determines anchor, anchor predicts degradation) is the more durable part.
- **Red flags.** (a) Several cited works and model names carry 2026 dates that cannot be independently verified in this environment; treat the bibliography as plausibly but not certainly accurate. (b) The source/target binarization is explicitly acknowledged as lossy, and trilingual gains are modest/mixed (one model negative), which limits the generality claim. (c) Token-level language tagging is described as "lightweight" with details deferred — segmentation errors on borrowed terms / named entities could silently distort both csrc and γ. (d) Table 1 reports only mean AB and F1; per-language breakdowns (9 targets) are pushed to appendices, so cross-language stability of the effect is not visible in the main text.

## Strengths

- Closed loop from diagnosis (Anchor Bias) to mechanism (frame → anchor → degradation) to mitigation (CANVAS), all using the same representational signal.
- Training-free, inference-time, writes only into KV cache — cheap and composable with other methods.
- Adaptive α tied to an instance-level geometric score (γ) gives principled, input-sensitive dosing rather than a fixed knob; near-identity on monolingual inputs is a desirable safety property.
- Broad model coverage (dense, MoE, 1B–70B) and four CS conditions including realistic Random/Selective and multi-turn dialog.
- Geometric verification via PCA projection (Figure 3) and α-vs-ρ sensitivity (Figure 4) actually show the intervention does what it claims.

## Limitations

- **Binary anchor compression.** Models CS as a single SRC–TGT axis; natural CS involves borrowed terms, named entities, local syntax, discourse context, and language-specific factual familiarity — explicitly called out by the authors.
- **Modest absolute gains on a hard task.** F1 values are low; the win is mostly relative and mechanism-confirming.
- **Trilingual/generalization fragility.** Gains shrink (sometimes negative) beyond bilingual settings, because forcing a binary partition is inherently lossy.
- **Upstream translation dependency.** q_TGT quality hinges on Qwen3-235B-A22B; translation noise could contaminate the "gold" anchor.
- **Token-tagging opacity.** Language-id granularity and error handling are deferred to an appendix not present in the excerpt; effect of mis-tags on γ and csrc is unquantified in the main text.
- **English-centric source by design.** Spanish-source generalization is shown only in appendix; the framework's symmetry under non-English sources is under-explored.
- **Greedy decoding only.** No sampling/temperature analysis; interaction with longer generations or tool-use/agent loops untested.

## Relevance

Highly relevant to several active LLM research threads:
- **Interpretability / representation geometry.** Anchor Bias is exactly the kind of geometric probe (cf. Marks & Tegmark 2024 "Geometry of Truth", Wendler et al. 2024 "Llama layers speak") that maps internal structure to behavior — directly useful for anyone studying multilingual circuits, language routing in MoEs, or where cross-lingual knowledge is stored.
- **Inference-time steering / activation editing.** CANVAS belongs to the same family as contrastive activation addition, ITI, and representation engineering, but is the first (to my knowledge) to target code-switched inputs and to derive the steering direction from the input itself (self-canonicalized), which is attractive for plug-and-play deployment without probe data.
- **Multilingual / cross-lingual consistency.** Gives a mechanistic gloss to known facts — multilingual models have English-centered processing pathways (Wendler, Zhao, Sharma) and language-dependent factual access (Qi 2023, Xu 2023). Concrete actionable signal for multilingual RAG (cf. Park & Lee 2025) and for fairness/hallucination-gap work.
- **Agents / reliability in mixed-language settings.** Real users (and increasingly multilingual agent traces) mix languages mid-utterance; a cheap inference-time stabilizer is directly useful for deployment reliability in non-English markets, including code-switched tool calls and RAG over mixed-language corpora.
- **Training data / curriculum implications.** The frame-dependent anchor suggests that *syntactic frame*, not just lexical language ID, drives internal routing — relevant to how multilingual pretraining and instruction-tuning data should be balanced.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Does Anchor Bias predict degradation on *generation* / long-form / agentic tasks, or only short-answer factoid QA where F1 is a clean signal?
2. Can the canvas direction be extracted *once* per model (offline prototype) rather than per-input, and how much γ/α adaptivity is actually necessary vs. a fixed mild α?
3. How does CANVAS interact with sampling, beam search, or existing steering vectors (e.g., ITI, CAA)? Additive or conflicting?
4. Is the grammar-frame effect stable under *intra-utterance* switching mid-sentence (true spontaneous CS), or only at the sentence-frame granularity tested?
5. Per-language breakdown: which of the 9 targets drive the +0.485 correlation, and are low-resource languages (Bengali, Marathi, Toba Batak, Urdu) more or less anchor-sensitive?
6. Does source-ward steering hurt culturally-bound facts whose correct answer lives in the target-language sub-space (risk of forcing English-centered recall)?
7. Interaction with MoE routing: do GF-SRC and GF-TGT activate different expert sets, and does CANVAS shift routing as well as residual hidden states?
8. Could the same anchor-bias framework diagnose and repair *cross-modal* "code-switching" (e.g., mixed text+image tokens) in VLMs?
9. How sensitive is CANVAS to errors in the lightweight language tagger — what is the breakdown threshold beyond which γ sign flips?
10. Does the effect survive instruction-tuning / RLHF intensity — i.e., is it a base-model property or reinforced by alignment?
