> **一句话总结：** 本文系统评测了三个可用 AI 评审系统（OpenAIReview、'coarse、Reviewer3）加零样本基线在 6 个 LLM 后端下的表现：在 ICLR/NeurIPS 论文上用评论数量是否能区分论文质量来度量（最佳 OpenAIReview+GPT-5.5 达 83% 成对准确率），并自建跨 8 个 arXiv 领域、4 类错误的扰动基准测量错误检出召回（最佳 71.6%，6 模型并集 83.3%），还分析了真实部署的 1,360 条用户反馈，发现主要短板是评论精度（误报与吹毛求疵）。

> **TL;DR:** The first head-to-head, system-level benchmark of real agentic AI reviewer systems (OpenAIReview, 'coarse, proprietary Reviewer3, plus zero-shot) across six frontier/efficient LLMs. On 197 ICLR/NeurIPS papers, comment volume tracks paper quality above chance (best OpenAIReview+GPT-5.5 at 83% pairwise accuracy). On a new perturbation benchmark across 8 arXiv domains and 4 error types, the best config catches 71.6% of injected errors, and the union of all 6 models reaches 83.3% — indicating models flag complementary errors. A 1,360-review public deployment shows net-positive user votes (1.44:1) but flags precision (false positives, nitpicks) as the key weakness.

## 核心贡献

- 首次以"端到端系统"而非"裸 LLM"为单位，对当前公开可用的 AI 评审系统（开源 OpenAIReview、'coarse 与闭源 Reviewer3，加上零样本基线）做横向基准测试，覆盖 2 个前沿模型（GPT-5.5、Claude Opus 4.7）与 4 个高效模型（DeepSeek-V4-Flash、Qwen3.6-35B-A3B、Gemini-3.1-Flash-Lite、Grok-4.1-Fast）。
- 提出基于"质量代理信号"的成对准确率评估：用评论数量是否在低/高质量论文对上正确排序来检验系统是否捕捉到真实质量信号，最佳组合 OpenAIReview + GPT-5.5 达 83%。
- 构建跨 8 个 arXiv 学科、4 类错误（Surface 局部数学编辑、Claim 错误断言、Logic 推理错误、Experimental 实验设计错误）的扰动基准，最佳配置召回 71.6%，并经 40 例人工审计（82.5% 有效）。
- 发现不同后端模型检测的错误高度互补——6 模型并集召回 83.3%，比单模型最佳高 11.7 个百分点，说明更好的 harness 设计还有提升空间。
- 分析真实部署 OpenAIReview（1,360 条评审、1,100 篇论文）：正面投票与负面投票比 1.44:1，1,348 条评论被标记为已解决；失败模式集中在误报（38.5%）、吹毛求疵（17.3%）与过分要求细节（15.2%）。

## Key Contributions

- First system-level (not raw-LLM-level) head-to-head benchmark of currently-deployable agentic reviewer systems: open-source OpenAIReview and 'coarse, proprietary Reviewer3, plus a zero-shot baseline, all evaluated as whole pipelines.
- A quality-proxy correlation study on real ICLR/NeurIPS papers using four proxies (community citations, conference awards, reviewer scores, composite), showing every system discriminates paper quality above chance without being trained to predict acceptance; best is OpenAIReview + GPT-5.5 at 0.83 pairwise (Mann–Whitney AUC) accuracy.
- A new perturbation benchmark: deterministic extraction + LLM generation/validation of errors across 8 arXiv subject classes and 4 error families, with a best-config recall of 71.6% and a stratified manual audit finding 82.5% of injected perturbations are valid errors.
- An empirical finding that backend models are complementary: the union of six models under OpenAIReview reaches 83.3% recall (95% CI [79.7, 86.9]), +11.7 points over the best single model, motivating ensemble/ harness design.
- A real-world deployment analysis of OpenAIReview (1,360 reviews, 1,100 papers): likes-to-dislikes 1.44:1, 1,348 comments marked resolved, with precision failures (false positives, nitpicks, over-asking) accounting for ~71% of downvotes.

## Methodology

**Systems under evaluation.** Four review systems, each takes a full paper and emits a list of (quoted passage, explanation) comments:
- *Zero-shot*: single prompt reviewing the entire paper.
- *OpenAIReview* (Chicago Human+AI Lab, 2025): passage-by-passage reviewing against a 5-preceding/2-following neighbor window plus a running summary of notation/definitions/equations/claims; consolidates and deduplicates comments; accepts PDF/Markdown/LaTeX/Word/arXiv HTML.
- *'coarse* (Van Dijcke, 2025): multi-agent (macro overview + per-section + adversarial proof-verification on math) with an editorial dedup pass.
- *Reviewer3* (proprietary): treated as a fixed black box (no internal model exposed), emits a small set of high-priority comments.

**Backbone models.** Front: GPT-5.5, Claude Opus 4.7. Efficient: DeepSeek-V4-Flash, Qwen3.6-35B-A3B, Gemini-3.1-Flash-Lite, Grok-4.1-Fast (all via OpenRouter, no reasoning mode). Reviewer3 is fixed.

**Evaluation 1 — quality-proxy correlation.** Papers drawn from SNOR (ICLR 2017–2025, NeurIPS 2021–2025 linked to Semantic Scholar). Four proxies, each with 30 high- and 30 low-quality papers: Community (top-cited vs. rejected-never-published), Conference (award vs. rejected), Reviewer (top vs. bottom mean score), Composite. Metric: pairwise accuracy = fraction of (low, high) pairs where the low-quality paper receives more comments (Mann–Whitney AUC, chance = 0.5). Frontier models run on a 74-paper subset ($204 API cost); efficient models on the full 240-paper set (197 unique). 95% CIs via 5,000-iteration cluster bootstrap over papers. Severity stratification (Major/Moderate/Minor).

**Evaluation 2 — perturbation benchmark.** 74 papers across 8 arXiv classes (Computational Complexity, ML, Econometrics, HEP, Mathematics, Atomic/Cluster Physics, Genomics, Applied Statistics). Five-stage pipeline: (1) deterministic LaTeX extraction of candidate spans; (2) Gemini-3-Flash-Preview generator proposes perturbations; (3) structural validator; (4) Claude-Sonnet-4.6 checklist verifier filters typos/non-errors; (5) inject to produce one corrupted paper. Four error families: Surface (local math edits), Claim (false statements), Logic (broken reasoning), Experimental (flawed design/analysis). Detection scored by fuzzy substring match (threshold τ) + Gemini judge (1–5 scale, ≥3 cutoff) on whether the reviewer's explanation identifies the same error. Recall = fraction of injected perturbations detected. Stratified audit of 40 perturbations: 82.5% valid.

**Evaluation 3 — real deployment.** OpenAIReview (Claude Opus 4.6 backend) deployed as a public web tool. 1,360 reviews of 1,100 distinct papers over 13 weeks. Users can like/dislike, mark resolved, or leave free-text feedback. 283 downvoted comments categorized by Gemini 3 Flash judge (validated on a 26-sample stratified subset).

**Complementarity analysis.** Paragraph-level Venn overlap (Jaccard) between systems, models, and human OpenReview reviewers; k-means (k=10, all-MiniLM-L6-v2 embeddings) clustering of comments into 5 groups.

## Results & Highlights

- **Quality-proxy correlation.** Every system discriminates quality above chance (0.5). Per-model under OpenAIReview: GPT-5.5 = 0.83 (c̄low − c̄high = +6.38, +40.4%), Claude Opus 4.7 = 0.74, Grok-4.1-Fast = 0.80 (efficient-model leader), DeepSeek = 0.60, Qwen = 0.62, Gemini = 0.61. Per-system best: OpenAIReview+GPT-5.5 = 0.83 overall, tied-best on every proxy (≥0.84); zero-shot+DeepSeek = 0.80; Reviewer3 = 0.80; 'coarse trails at 0.66. Signal concentrates in Major (0.59–0.78) and Moderate (0.61–0.91) tiers; Minor tier is near chance.

- **Perturbation recall (24-paper frontier subset).** OpenAIReview wins on every backend and every error category: GPT-5.5 = 71.6% (Experiment 85.2%, Claim 83.3%, Reasoning 70.0%, Surface 47.3%); Claude = 68.1%. Zero-shot+GPT-5.5 = 59.8%. 'coarse best = 20.7%; Reviewer3 = 26.5% — both below zero-shot, because they optimize for editorial prioritization (5–10 high-priority comments) rather than exhaustive enumeration. OpenAIReview's gains over zero-shot concentrate on prose-level errors (+10–18 points); Surface math recall barely moves (45.8%→47.3%), since running summary doesn't help locally-detectable errors. Across 8 domains, OpenAIReview leads everywhere except Math (where zero-shot edges it); cs.CC (Complexity) is hardest at 32.7–45.7%.

- **Complementarity.** Union of all 6 models under OpenAIReview = 83.3% recall (95% CI [79.7, 86.9]), +11.7 over best single model (gain robust at CI [8.1, 15.1]). Humans vs. AI union: humans take 80% of paper-level/novelty critiques, AI leads on surface (61%), claim (62%), experimental (68%), formal-math (65%) categories. Jaccard between humans and AI union = 0.230.

- **Real deployment.** 27,587 comments shown, 690 voted (2.5% engagement); 407 up vs. 283 down (1.44:1); 1,348 marked resolved (4.9%); resolve rate 24% on upvoted, 41% on downvoted (downvoted-resolve doubles as a dismissal mechanism). Downvote breakdown: 38.5% false positive, 17.3% trivial nitpick, 15.2% unreasonable detail ask (≈71% "unhelpful"), 6.4% parsing/OCR artifact, 22.3% correct-but-dismissed. One outlier paper drew 41 of 283 downvotes from a single user; removing it leaves the ranking unchanged (false positives still #1 at 33.9%).

- **Headline takeaways.** (1) Raw base-model strength matters more than harness design — zero-shot+GPT-5.5 already at 59.8%, OpenAIReview harness adds ~12 points, mostly by emitting more comments (19 vs. 3.7/paper). (2) Under the same harness, models are complementary, so ensembling/better harness design is a promising near-term direction. (3) Benchmarks stress recall; deployment surfaces precision as the more pressing limitation.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty.** Genuinely novel in scope: it is the first work to benchmark *independently-developed third-party reviewer systems head-to-head as whole pipelines* (rather than swapping LLMs in a single prompt), at a moment (mid-2026) when GPT-5.5-class models have substantially changed the landscape. The perturbation benchmark with type-stratified errors across 8 arXiv domains is a real methodological contribution over FLAWS/SPECS, which vary by review aspect rather than error type. However, the quality-proxy correlation metric (count whether low-quality papers get more comments) is a coarse proxy and the authors openly admit it.

**Rigor.** Generally strong: cluster bootstrap CIs over papers, severity stratification, manual audit of perturbation validity (82.5%), judge-validation of the downvote taxonomy (agreement on 23/26). Honest about limitations: no direct precision measurement, LLM-driven pipeline may share biases with LLM reviewers (a real circularity risk that could inflate recall), frontier subset limited to 24–74 papers by cost.

**Reproducibility.** Mixed. OpenAIReview, 'coarse, zero-shot, and the perturbation pipeline are open; the SNOR dataset is CC-BY-4.0; fixed seeds are used. But Reviewer3 is a black box (no model/prompts), the closed backbone models (GPT-5.5, Claude Opus 4.7, Gemini-3.1-Flash-Lite, Grok-4.1-Fast) are accessed via API and may drift, and the deployment data (vote/resolve signals) is not released. Cost figures ($204 for the frontier subset) are reported, helpful for replication budgeting.

**Significance.** High and timely. Directly addresses a live conference-policy question (should AI reviewers be deployed?) with both capability and user-acceptance evidence. The complementarity finding (union = 83.3%) is actionable for harness designers.

**Red flags / overclaiming check.**
- *Potential conflict of interest*: the authors are from the lab that maintains OpenAIReview, which "wins" most comparisons. The comparison is not obviously rigged (Reviewer3 ties zero-shot at 0.80 overall, and OpenAIReview's win on recall is plausible given its design), but the framing favors their system.
- *Perturbation benchmark self-assessment risk*: generator/judge and reviewed systems are all LLMs; injected errors may be biased toward LLM-salient mistakes. The authors flag this honestly and audit validity (not ecological representativeness).
- *Small frontier sample*: 24 papers for the headline 71.6% recall number — CIs are reported but the absolute recall on a 24-paper subset should be read with caution.
- *The 'coarse and Reviewer3 underperformance vs. zero-shot on recall is somewhat unfair*: those systems are designed for editorial prioritization (5–10 comments), so a recall metric structurally penalizes them. The authors acknowledge this, but the headline tables still list them as "worse."
- *Deployment signal is observational and thin*: 2.5% engagement rate, self-selected anonymous web users, one user contributing 41 of 283 downvotes — the precision-failure conclusion is directionally useful but not statistically clean.
- *Citation count is itself a noisy quality proxy*; the entire Evaluation 1 rests on this assumption.

## Strengths

- System-level comparison rather than raw-LLM swap — closer to how these tools are actually used.
- Two complementary evaluation axes (correlation with human quality signals + controlled perturbation recall) plus a real deployment, triangulating the conclusion.
- Honest, self-aware discussion of limitations, including the LLM-bias circularity in the perturbation pipeline and the precision blind spot.
- Type- and domain-stratified perturbation benchmark is reusable and fills a gap vs. FLAWS/SPECS.
- The complementarity finding (union 83.3%) is a concrete, actionable result for future harness design.

## Limitations

- Measures recall but not precision directly; the deployment gives only an indirect precision signal.
- Perturbation pipeline is LLM-driven, risking shared bias between generator/judge and the LLM reviewers under test (could inflate recall).
- Frontier-sample sizes are small (24 papers for the headline recall; 74 for correlation) due to API cost.
- 'coarse and Reviewer3 are structurally disadvantaged by a recall metric that rewards comment volume; their editorial-prioritization design is not fairly captured.
- Reviewer3 is a black box; closed backbone models can drift over time, threatening reproducibility.
- Quality proxies (citations, awards, scores) are noisy and the entire correlation study depends on them.
- Deployment data is observational, self-selected, with low engagement and a single-user outlier skewing downvotes.
- Recall is necessary but not sufficient for reviewer quality (organization, prioritization, calibration, engagement with contribution are not measured).

## Relevance

与 LLM 智能体（agents）可靠性评估高度相关：本文正是评估"多阶段 agentic 评审系统"的端到端行为、互补性与失败模式，可作为构建可解释、可信赖 LLM 智能体基准（包括错误检测、召回/精度权衡、模型集成）的直接参考；其扰动注入方法也可迁移到其他需要"已知 ground truth"的智能体能力评测。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How would a precision metric (e.g., expert-validated false-positive rate on real papers) change the system ranking, given that deployment signals point to precision as the dominant weakness?
- Can the LLM-bias circularity in the perturbation pipeline be broken by having domain experts (not LLMs) author a subset of errors, and does recall on expert-authored errors differ materially from LLM-authored ones?
- Since the union of 6 models reaches 83.3% recall, what minimal ensemble (in cost/latency) captures most of the gain, and does an explicit agreement/voting harness beat naive union?
- How stable are these results as GPT-5.5/Claude Opus 4.7 successors ship — is the "base strength matters more than harness" finding a transient artifact of the 2026 frontier, or structural?
- Does the quality-proxy correlation hold for non-ML fields (the perturbation benchmark spans 8 domains, but the correlation study is ICLR/NeurIPS only)?
- Can the recall-vs-prioritization tradeoff be measured jointly so 'coarse and Reviewer3 are not structurally penalized for emitting fewer comments?
- The deployment shows resolve-rate is higher on downvoted comments (41% vs. 24%) — is "resolve" being used as fix or as dismiss, and what does that imply for using engagement as a helpfulness proxy?
