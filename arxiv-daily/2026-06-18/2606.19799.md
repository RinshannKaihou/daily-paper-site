> **一句话总结：** 本文用控制实验（CIFAR-10/CNN，配对重复测量，Tesla T4，每组 10 次）量化了 TensorFlow/Keras 中两类资源泄漏代码坏味——Improper Model Reuse (IMR) 和 Unreleased Tensor References (UTR)——的能耗与碳排放影响，发现它们分别使单次训练能耗上升约 32% 和 46%（CO₂ 同比上升），且差异在统计上高度显著（p<0.001），表明资源管理应被视为一等软件质量属性。

> **TL;DR:** A controlled within-subject study on an NVIDIA Tesla T4 shows that two TensorFlow/Keras resource-leak smells — Improper Model Reuse (IMR) and Unreleased Tensor References (UTR) — raise per-run training energy by ~32% and ~46% respectively, with proportional CO₂ increases, all statistically significant (p<0.001), demonstrating that ML-specific code smells carry a measurable environmental cost.

## 核心贡献

- **首次量化 ML 专属资源泄漏坏味的能效/碳排影响**：在受控条件下测量 IMR 与 UTR 对 TensorFlow/Keras 训练能耗与 CO₂ 排放的实际影响，填补了「代码坏味 → 环境代价」的实证空白。
- **证明资源生命周期管理不善会带来可观可持续性开销，却不带来预测性能收益**：在保持架构、数据、超参与随机种子等价的前提下，坏味注入仅增加开销而无功能性收益。
- **方法学上采用配对重复测量（within-subject）设计**：逐 run 配对计算差值、95% CI、单侧配对 t 检验、Wilcoxon 稳健性检验、Cohen's d_z 效应量，强调效应大小与精度而非单纯 p 值。
- **提出坏味筛选准则（C1–C4）**：可注入性、可测量开销（≥5%）、与其他坏味相互独立、TF 2.x eager 兼容，为后续扩展建立可复用框架。
- **倡导将可持续性评估纳入 ML 软件工程流程**：把资源清理、坏味检测与能耗监控作为一等质量属性。

## Key Contributions

- First controlled empirical quantification of the energy and CO₂ impact of ML-specific resource-leak smells (IMR, UTR) in TensorFlow/Keras.
- Demonstrates under controlled conditions that resource-lifecycle mismanagement imposes substantial sustainability overhead without statistically significant predictive-performance gains.
- Adopts a rigorous paired repeated-measures methodology (per-run paired differences, 95% CIs, one-sided paired t-tests, Wilcoxon robustness checks, Cohen's d_z effect sizes).
- Defines explicit smell-inclusion criteria (C1–C4) to ensure injectability, measurable overhead, isolation, and TF 2.x eager compatibility.
- Argues for treating resource management and energy efficiency as first-class software quality attributes in ML development.

## Methodology

**Design.** Controlled within-subject (repeated-measures) experiment. For each run index i, a smell-injected configuration (IMR or UTR) is paired with its baseline under identical hardware, dataset, software stack, and fixed random seeds, computing paired difference d_i = X_i,smell − X_i,baseline. Ten independent OS processes per configuration; NumPy/TF/dataset-split seeds fixed for functional equivalence.

**Baseline.** TensorFlow 2.x/Keras CNN on CIFAR-10 (50k train / 10k test, 32×32, 10 classes), images normalized to [0,1], one-hot labels, on-the-fly augmentation (random flips + shifts). Architecture: three conv blocks (32/64/128 filters, two Conv2D+ReLU each, BatchNorm, MaxPool, Dropout 0.25) → Dense(128, ReLU) → Dropout 0.25 → Dense(10, softmax). Trained 50 epochs (split into 5 cycles × 10 epochs to support IMR injection), batch size 32, Adam, categorical cross-entropy.

**Smell injections.**
- *Improper Model Reuse (IMR):* the same `tf.keras` model instance is reused across the five 10-epoch training cycles without reinitializing weights, recompiling, or clearing the Keras session — mimicking improper lifecycle management.
- *Unreleased Tensor References (UTR):* a custom `tf.keras.callbacks.Callback` hooks `on_train_batch_end`; every 5 batches it runs a manual forward pass on the current mini-batch and appends the resulting prediction tensors to a persistent Python list, preventing TF from reclaiming the memory. It does not touch gradients, optimizer state, loss, or parameters.

**Environment.** Dedicated machine, NVIDIA Tesla T4 GPU, no concurrent loads. TF 2.x, Keras, NumPy, Pandas, Matplotlib. Each run isolated OS process; GPU memory cleared and environment reinitialized before each run.

**Measurement.** CodeCarbon (v2.4.1) via NVML samples to estimate electricity (kWh); CO₂ computed as a deterministic linear function of energy under a fixed regional carbon-intensity factor (so CO₂ inference is mathematically equivalent to energy inference).

**Statistics.** Mean paired difference d̄, SD of differences, SE, relative % change, 95% CI for d̄; one-sided paired-sample t-tests (df=n−1); Wilcoxon signed-rank for robustness; Cohen's d_z = d̄ / s_d for standardized effect size.

## Results & Highlights

- **Baseline:** 0.025646 kWh (25.65 Wh) and 0.011608 kg (11.61 g) CO₂ per run.
- **IMR:** 0.033797 kWh (33.80 Wh) and 0.015297 kg (15.30 g) CO₂ → **+31.78%** energy and emissions.
- **UTR:** 0.037393 kWh (37.39 Wh) and 0.016921 kg (16.92 g) CO₂ → **+45.80%** energy, **+45.77%** CO₂.
- **Statistical significance:** All paired t-tests significant at p<0.001. Mean diffs (SD): IMR energy +0.008150 (±0.000683), UTR energy +0.011746 (±0.000358); IMR CO₂ +0.003689, UTR CO₂ +0.005313. Large t-statistics (IMR t=37.83, UTR t≈103) reflect the very low variance of paired differences under tight control.
- **Effect sizes:** Cohen's d_z = 11.93 (IMR) and 32.80 (UTR) for energy — extremely large, reflecting consistency rather than per-run magnitude.
- **Robustness:** Wilcoxon signed-rank tests also p<0.001, consistent with parametric results.
- **Per-run data:** Appendix Table 3 provides all 10 runs × 3 configs for energy and CO₂; values are tightly clustered, confirming systematic overhead rather than stochastic noise.
- **Functional equivalence preserved:** smells alter only resource lifecycle/tensor retention, not the optimization trajectory.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty — moderate.** The idea that code smells affect energy is not new (test-smell energy work [5], general code-smell literature). The novel angle is *ML-specific resource-leak smells quantified for CO₂*, which is a genuine but incremental contribution. It builds directly on the authors' own prior cataloguing work [1].
- **Rigor — strong on design, weak on scope.** Paired repeated-measures design, fixed seeds, isolated processes, effect sizes, robustness checks, and threats-to-validity discussion are commendable. But the statistics are somewhat oversold: Cohen's d_z of 11–33 is an artifact of the near-zero paired-difference variance under perfect control, not a practically enormous per-run effect; and because CO₂ is a deterministic linear rescaling of energy, reporting it as a separate "finding" is largely cosmetic — the paper itself admits this.
- **Reproducibility — good.** Fixed seeds, public dataset (CIFAR-10), standard stack (TF 2.x/Keras), single GPU type, and a full per-run appendix table make the experiment largely reproducible. Missing: the exact CodeCarbon config (sampling interval), the exact carbon-intensity factor value/region, and source code release is not clearly stated.
- **Significance — limited as presented.** One CNN, one dataset (CIFAR-10), one GPU. The authors honestly acknowledge external validity is narrow. Extrapolating "+32–46%" to production-scale training (1,000–2,000 MWh) is suggestive but unvalidated.
- **Red flags / caveats.** (1) Measurement is *estimated* via CodeCarbon/NVML, not wall-plug power instrumentation — absolute kWh values are soft, only relative comparisons are robust. (2) The "+5% measurement noise" rebuttal is hand-wavy. (3) No accuracy/loss results are reported to back the "no predictive-performance gain" claim numerically — it's asserted, not shown. (4) The IMR design (5×10-epoch cycles on the same model) is somewhat artificial; real IMR misuse patterns may differ. (5) n=10 runs is modest.

## Strengths

- Clean, well-motivated controlled experiment with a textbook-grade threats-to-validity section.
- Translates a maintainability concern (code smell) into an environmental/metric one, opening a new evaluation axis for ML code quality.
- Per-run appendix data and explicit inclusion criteria (C1–C4) aid replication and extension.
- Honest about being preliminary/emerging-results; does not over-generalize beyond its setup.

## Limitations

- Single architecture (one CNN), single dataset (CIFAR-10), single GPU (Tesla T4) → narrow external validity.
- Energy/CO₂ are *estimates* (CodeCarbon), not direct measurements; carbon intensity factor is fixed and regional.
- No accuracy/loss/convergence numbers reported to substantiate "no functional benefit."
- CO₂ results are a linear rescaling of energy — analytically redundant, though reported for interpretability.
- Effect-size magnitudes (d_z ~ 11–33) are driven by controlled-design low variance and can mislead readers into overstating practical impact.
- Only 2 of many possible ML code smells examined.

## Relevance

This work sits at the intersection of **ML software engineering and green AI**, which is adjacent to (but not directly within) the LLM training/inference/interpretability/agents core. Its relevance to that cluster is indirect but real: (1) **Training efficiency** — resource-leak smells are exactly the kind of latent inefficiency that silently inflates large-model training cost and energy; at LLM scale (1,000–2,000 MWh cited), a 30–46% overhead would be catastrophic and is worth linting for in training scripts and data loaders. (2) **Inference / serving reliability** — tensor-reference leaks and model-reuse anti-patterns cause GPU memory creep, OOM crashes, and degraded throughput in deployed inference servers, tying directly to **reliability**. (3) **Agents and reproducibility** — agent workflows that repeatedly construct and discard models/tensors are prime candidates for these smells; controlled-seed methodology reinforces reproducibility discipline. The paper does not touch interpretability or alignment, and it studies CNNs rather than transformers/LLMs, so the connection to LLM specifics is by extrapolation.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Do IMR/UTR-style smells produce the same relative overhead on **transformer / LLM training workloads** (where tensor accumulation and gradient checkpointing dominate)? Does the percentage scale up or shrink with model size?
- How prevalent are these two smells (and the broader catalog from [1]) in **real public repositories and production ML codebases** — a large-scale mining study?
- What is the actual **accuracy/convergence impact** of the smells? The paper asserts none; is that rigorously true across seeds and longer training?
- Would **direct wall-plug power measurement** (e.g., RAPL/IPMI/physical meters) corroborate CodeCarbon's relative estimates, or does NVML-based inference systematically bias the comparison?
- Can **static analysis / linters** reliably detect UTR (callback-hidden tensor retention) and IMR, and could such tools be integrated into CI for ML projects to prevent energy regressions?
- How does the overhead interact with **mixed-precision, distributed training, and gradient accumulation** — typical LLM-training settings?
- Is the linear "energy ≈ CO₂" relationship still the right framing once **dynamic/grid carbon intensity (time-of-day)** is considered, which matters for scheduling green training jobs?
