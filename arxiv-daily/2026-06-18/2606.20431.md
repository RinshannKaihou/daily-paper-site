> **一句话总结：** 在一个可控的合成生成器-分类器 toy-world 中，作者用 SINDy 稀疏动力学辨识和 effective rank 分析表明：特征稀疏会促使更多 superposition（方向重叠），但遗忘并非重叠的必然结果——只有"表示强度低 + 重叠高"的特征才最脆弱；且稀疏任务在隐空间占用更多有效维度，细化和证伪了"重叠越多遗忘越多"的直觉。

> **TL;DR:** Using a synthetic generator–separator toy world with ground-truth latent features, the authors show that higher feature sparsity induces more superposition (directional overlap), but overlap alone does not cause forgetting—forgetting concentrates in the low representation-strength / high-superposition regime, while strong representations remain stable even when shared. SINDy-identified dynamics reveal boundary-specific dips (not steady drift), and task-level effective rank grows with sparsity, refining the common "more superposition ⇒ more forgetting" intuition.

## 核心贡献

- **可控的机制性实验框架**：提出 generator–separator 合成管线（冻结的生成器 Φ 和分类器 Ψ，加上可训练的 encoder/classifier/decoder），使 superposition、表示强度、遗忘等量可被精确定义与测量，排除真实数据中概念频率/共现/标签结构的混杂。
- **统一的可测量形式化**：在线性表示假设下，将"特征、表示强度、superposition 强度、特征保持(retention)、任务嵌入"统一定义为可计算量（pure-feature probe 得到方向 w_i(t)，表示强度为范数，superposition 为其他特征对该方向的投影平方和）。
- **SINDy 动力学辨识**：用稀疏非线性动力学辨识（多项式库 ≤10 阶，配合上三角掩码避免未训练特征泄漏）拟合 ∂R/∂t = g(R, S, t_since)，定量刻画遗忘如何依赖重叠、强度与曝光历史。
- **三条核心实证发现**：(1) superposition 随时间增长，但在任务边界出现瞬时 dip 后恢复（边界特异性干扰而非平稳漂移）；(2) 稀疏度更高→重叠更多，但只要表示强度足够，遗忘可被抑制；(3) 任务级 effective rank 随稀疏度增大，表明稀疏任务占用更广的隐空间方向。
- **可证伪的诊断工具**：把"干扰"这种模糊叙事转化为可测试机制（边界 dip、低 R/高 S 脆弱区），为 CL 算法设计与评估提供可复用的探针（pure-feature 嵌入、重叠指标、effective-rank 轨迹）。

## Key Contributions

- A controlled experimental framework that merges mechanistic interpretability with continual learning, enabling measurement of superposition and retention free of real-data confounds.
- Formal definitions—grounded in the linear representation hypothesis—of feature, representation strength, superposition strength, feature retention, and task embedding, all operationalized through pure-feature probes.
- A SINDy-based sparse dynamical identification that fits ∂R/∂t as a function of representation strength R, superposition S, and time-since-first-seen t_since, yielding a quantitative model of retention/forgetting.
- Three empirically robust findings: (1) sparser signals produce more overlap with structured boundary-dip-then-recovery dynamics; (2) forgetting is conditionally harmful—concentrated in low-R/high-S features—rather than a monotonic function of superposition; (3) effective rank of task embeddings grows with sparsity.
- A reusable diagnostic toolkit (pure-feature embeddings, overlap metrics, effective-rank traces) that surfaces falsifiable hypotheses and can be layered onto standard CL benchmarks.

## Methodology

The paper builds a two-part controlled setup to make superposition and forgetting observable.

**Data generation.** A frozen generator Φ: [0,1]^N → R^D maps sparse nonnegative feature vectors f (support size ≤ s, i.i.d. amplitudes) to observable signals X = Φ(f), and a frozen separator Ψ: [0,1]^N → {1,…,K} assigns labels via a fixed linear+softmax rule. Both are randomly initialized and kept frozen across all experiments, eliminating data-semantics confounds. Tasks are sequential distributions; task t activates a disjoint index set I_t ⊆ [N], and samples are drawn with support inside I_t.

**Modeling.** A trainable encoder E_θ: R^D → R^m and classifier C_θ form the predictor x ↦ C_θ(E_θ(x)). The latent dimension m is deliberately chosen smaller than |I_t| (per-task concept count) to force a bottleneck and representational pressure. Training uses standard experience replay (bounded buffer mixed with current-task minibatches), chosen for stability and to avoid confounds from stronger regularizers or architectural changes.

**Single-feature probes.** Grounded in the linear representation hypothesis, each ground-truth feature i is isolated via its canonical basis vector e_i, and its embedding at training time t is w_i(t) = E_θ(Φ(e_i)). From this:
- Representation strength R(i,t) = ‖w_i(t)‖₂.
- Superposition strength S(i,t) = Σ_{j≠i} (w_j(t)ᵀ v_i(t))², where v_i is the unit direction—captures directional overlap.
- Feature retention F(i,t) = ∂R/∂t, modeled as ∂R/∂t = g(R, S, t_since).

**SINDy identification.** A candidate library of polynomials (degree ≤ 10) is constructed over standardized (R, S, t_since); sparse coefficients are estimated with thresholded least squares, with an upper-triangular mask aligned to feature/task introduction times so derivatives are fit only where the feature has actually been observed.

**Task-level analysis.** Task embeddings are formed by stacking pure-feature or data-driven embeddings into a matrix T; their "volume" is measured by effective rank (Roy & Vetterli 2007): EffRank(T) = exp(−Σ p_i log p_i), p_i = σ_i / Σ σ_j over singular values—robust to small singular values and capturing spectral entropy.

**Research questions:** RQ1 (does sparser input yield more superposition?), RQ2 (are overlapped features more prone to forgetting?), RQ3 (how does sparsity affect effective dimension of task embeddings / capacity allocation?).

## Results & Highlights

- **RQ1 — Sparser features overlap more, with structured time dynamics.** Superposition strength S(i,t) rises systematically with sparsity (Fig. 2). Over time (Fig. 3), S shows a pronounced transient dip at each task boundary followed by gradual recovery and continued growth—evidence of boundary-specific interference and re-alignment, not steady drift. High-R regions coexist with intermediate-to-high S with reduced volatility, so strength and overlap are not strictly antagonistic.
- **RQ2 — Forgetting concentrates in low-R/high-S; strength is a moderator.** SINDy dynamics (Fig. 4) show the most negative retention (largest R drops) occurs for weakly represented, highly overlapped features. As R increases, the adverse dependence of F on S weakens and can even invert. The time-since-first-seen variable t_since acts as a consolidation variable: early after exposure F is more negative in high-S regions, later stabilizing toward zero. This directly refines "more superposition ⇒ more forgetting."
- **RQ3 — Sparser regimes occupy more latent volume.** Both aggregate and per-task effective rank increase with sparsity (Figs. 5–6), indicating broader allocation of directions to encode sparse tasks. Strikingly, at the moment a new task arrives, its embedding region's effective rank is comparable to previously seen tasks ("early uniformity"), suggesting a stable latent scaffold that new features map into before fine-grained adjustment. Boundary contractions mirror feature-level dynamics.
- **Robustness.** Appendix variants (Figs. 7–10) reproduce all qualitative conclusions. SINDy stability analyses (Figs. 11–12) show coefficient selection varies across runs, but the sign structure of dominant interactions (negative S-dependence at low R, moderating with R and t_since) is stable.
- **Headline refinement:** Representation strength and capacity allocation—not overlap alone—are the primary moderators of retention in continual learning.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: Moderate.** The contribution is a controlled diagnostic study, not a new algorithm or SOTA result. The value lies in cleanly operationalizing and partially refuting a common intuition ("overlap ⇒ forgetting"). The toy-world methodology and SINDy-fitted dynamics are a genuine methodological combination, but the high-level three takeaways are somewhat intuitive and echo prior CL representation-forgetting findings (e.g., Rypeśc et al. 2024 on rising rank).
- **Rigor: Mixed.** Strengths: well-defined quantities, a principled SINDy identification with masking to avoid leakage, and explicit stability analyses for the sign structure of learned dynamics. Weaknesses: results are almost entirely qualitative (heatmap reading, qualitative curve descriptions). There are essentially no numerical tables, no confidence intervals, no statistical significance tests, and the reported SINDy coefficients are not shown—only "sign structure is stable." This makes quantitative claims ("reduces forgetting," "more resilient") hard to verify from the text.
- **Reproducibility: Low-to-moderate.** Architectures and sampling are described at a high level and deferred to the Appendix ("Concrete architecture and sampling choices appear in the Appendix"), but no code release is mentioned in the main text. The frozen generator/separator is unspecified beyond "randomly initialized feed-forward/linear," so exact reconstruction would require substantial effort. The toy-world simplicity aids conceptual reproducibility but limits verifiability of exact numbers.
- **Significance: Modest but useful as a probe.** This is a mechanisms/diagnostics paper, not a benchmark-beating method. Its value is as falsifiable hypotheses and reusable probes for the CL community; impact depends on whether downstream work adopts them on real data.
- **Potential red flags / overclaiming risk:**
  - The retention metric (norm change) is **not** shown to correlate with actual classifier accuracy or standard CL forgetting metrics (acknowledged as L4). Strong claims about "forgetting" are therefore about a proxy, not performance—readers should not treat the findings as performance guarantees.
  - Disjoint task supports (L2) and uniform sparsity (L1) are strong simplifications; the "overlap ⇒ forgetting" refinement may not survive when tasks genuinely share features (arguably the more realistic and more interfering case).
  - SINDy with degree-≤10 polynomials risks misspecification (acknowledged L3); the "learned dynamical law" should be read as a descriptive fit, not a verified governing equation.
  - No comparison to any baseline CL method's representation dynamics, and no demonstration that the diagnostics predict downstream task accuracy better than chance.
  - The "early uniformity" of effective rank across seen/unseen tasks is intriguing but could also be an artifact of the frozen generator's geometry rather than an encoder property; this is not dissected.

## Strengths

- Cleanly isolates variables that are entangled in real data (feature frequency, sparsity, overlap, label structure), giving genuinely interpretable dynamics.
- Connects mechanistic-interpretability concepts (superposition, linear representation hypothesis) to continual-learning questions in a concrete, measurable way.
- The SINDy + effective-rank combination yields both single-feature and task-level views, and the boundary-dip finding is a non-obvious, testable phenomenon.
- Honest and thorough limitations section (L1–L4) that correctly bounds the claims.
- The three takeaways genuinely refine a popular intuition rather than merely confirming it; the "strength as moderator" framing is a useful conceptual contribution.

## Limitations

- **Toy world only:** Frozen generator/separator, disjoint task supports, uniform sparsity—far from realistic datasets or modern architectures (no transformers, no vision-language models, no real benchmarks).
- **Proxy, not performance:** Retention is measured as embedding-norm change; the link to actual class accuracy and standard CL forgetting metrics is explicitly left as future work (L4).
- **SINDy misspecification risk:** Degree-limited polynomial library may not capture the true dynamics; robustness rests on sign-structure stability rather than coefficient stability.
- **Largely qualitative evidence:** No numerical result tables, error bars, or significance testing in the main text; conclusions rest on reading heatmaps and curves.
- **No baselines or algorithmic payoff:** The framework diagnoses but does not propose or validate a mitigation that exploits the findings.
- **Generalization untested:** Whether boundary dips, low-R/high-S vulnerability, and sparsity-driven rank growth persist on real models/data is left entirely to future work.

## Relevance

This paper sits squarely at the intersection of interpretability and continual learning: it treats superposition as a geometric, measurable property and asks how representation strength and capacity allocation moderate forgetting. For work on LLM interpretability (superposition, feature geometry) and reliable sequential/training dynamics, it offers concrete diagnostic probes (pure-feature embeddings, overlap metrics, effective-rank traces) and falsifiable, mechanism-level hypotheses that complement accuracy-only CL evaluation—directly relevant to understanding representation retention in continually trained models.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Do the boundary-dip dynamics and low-R/high-S vulnerability replicate in real continual-learning settings (e.g., class-incremental image classification, continual fine-tuning of LLMs) where task supports overlap and feature frequencies are heavy-tailed?
- How tightly does the norm-based retention proxy correlate with standard CL forgetting metrics (e.g., BWT, average accuracy) and per-class accuracy—can effective rank or S(i,t) serve as early-warning signals that predict downstream forgetting?
- Would a mitigation that explicitly targets the low-R/high-S regime (e.g., consolidation/scheduling of weak overlapped features, or per-task latent-direction budgeting) actually improve retention in controlled and realistic CL benchmarks?
- How sensitive are the SINDy-identified dynamics to the function library (rational terms, kernels, neural bases) and to the latent bottleneck ratio m vs. |I_t|?
- Does the "early uniformity" of effective rank across seen/unseen tasks reflect a property of the encoder's scaffold, or is it an artifact of the frozen generator's spectral structure?
- Can the pure-feature probing methodology be adapted to large pre-trained models where ground-truth features are not directly accessible (e.g., using sparse autoencoder features as proxies)?
