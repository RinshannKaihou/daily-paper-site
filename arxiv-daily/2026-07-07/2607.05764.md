> **一句话总结：** 作者在交易类法律文档问答场景下，对比了全语料注入（inject）与两种结构化检索模式（navembed 嵌入检索、navindex LLM 导航结构化索引），在 20 题已验证基准上用抗位置偏置的成对裁判判定 navembed+rerank 在 18 题文档题中 16 题与 inject 打平且输入 token 减少 17.3×，navindex 在全部 18 题打平且回答上下文小约 56×、美元成本降 25%，并推导出缓存交叉点的闭式规则（语料需低于检索负载约 10 倍时缓存注入才更便宜）。

> **TL;DR:** The authors compare full-corpus injection (inject) against two structured retrieval modes — embedding retrieval (navembed) and LLM navigation over a compact structured index (navindex) — on a 20-question benchmark over six transactional legal documents judged by a position-bias-controlled, reference-anchored pairwise protocol. Semantic retrieval with reranking (RUN-012) tied inject on 16/18 document-bound questions while attending to 17.3× fewer input tokens (188K vs. 3.26M); navindex tied all 18 at a 1.61× smaller total token footprint, a ~56× smaller answering context, and 25% lower dollar cost ($1.21 vs. $1.61). They also derive a closed-form caching-crossover rule: cached inject is cheaper in dollars only while the corpus stays below roughly 10× the retrieval payload.

## 核心贡献

- **配对式法律 QA 评估**：在 20 题已验证答案的基准（18 题文档绑定 + 2 题域外控制题）上，用前向/反向双重判定、参考答案锚定的成对裁判协议，对比 inject 基线与两种结构化检索模式，全流程配以 Clopper–Pearson 95% 置信区间量化不确定性。
- **navindex 索引格式设计**：提出面向法律文书的导航描述符——七类布尔语义标志（isDefinition/hasMoney/hasDate/hasPercentage/hasLiabilityLimit/hasCondition/hasParty）、crossReferencedIds 跨引用图、定义项标志、硬性 10 节点上限；通过双文件架构（*.index.json 导航面 + *.full.json 条款库）让模型先按结构过滤再取文本，并将修正案优先级与定义项解析写入可发布的提示词。
- **闭式缓存交叉点规则**：将"token 足迹"与"美元成本"区分为两个独立维度，推导出 inject 比 navigate 更便宜的条件——语料规模 C < 10×R（检索负载）时缓存注入更划算；对 navindex 推得阈值约 128K tokens。
- **失败模式目录**：系统记录并摒弃了多个不宜上线的方案，包括 BM25 长度偏置（hybrid RUN-011 负载膨胀到 382K、token 减幅跌至 8.5×）、纯标题索引过度选择（R5 单题选中 87/72/38 节点）、以及无上限节点选择。
- **透明的不确定性边界**：给出裁判审计（2/15 判定可能错，95% CI [2%, 40%]）、强制决策探针（10–5 inject 偏好，p=0.30 与随机无异）、同体系裁判自偏好对称性论证，并将四阶段实验（Phase I–III 与 navindex Config 1–6）的运行历史与逐题账本全部公开。

## Key Contributions

- **Paired legal-QA evaluation**: On a 20-question benchmark with verified ground-truth answers (18 document-bound + 2 out-of-scope controls), compares the inject baseline against two structured retrieval modes using a forward/reverse, reference-anchored pairwise judge, with Clopper–Pearson 95% confidence intervals reported for every headline rate.
- **navindex format design**: A navigation descriptor for legal instruments encoding seven typed boolean semantic flags, an explicit crossReferencedIds graph, defined-term markers, and a hard ten-node selection cap; a dual-file architecture (*.index.json navigation surface + *.full.json provision store) lets the model filter on structure before fetching text, with amendment-precedence and defined-term resolution rules written into released prompts (Appendix D).
- **Closed-form caching-crossover rule**: Separates token footprint from dollar cost and derives that cached inject is cheaper per query exactly when C < 10·R (corpus below ~10× the retrieval payload); for navindex the threshold is ~128K tokens, against which the measured 163K six-document prefix exceeds it.
- **Catalogue of failure modes**: Documents configurations the authors would not ship — BM25 length bias (RUN-011 payload inflated to 382K, reduction falling to 8.5×), titles-only LLM scan over-selection (R5 selected 87/72/38 nodes on trial questions), and uncapped node selection — plus a descriptor-ladder design map (D1 heading-only through D5 full summary).
- **Honest uncertainty quantification**: Reports a judge audit (2/15 verdicts likely wrong, 95% CI [2%, 40%]), a forced-decision probe (10–5 inject lean, statistically indistinguishable from chance, p=0.30), a symmetric-self-preference argument for same-provider judge/answerer, and full per-question token/cost ledgers (Appendix B) reconciled to a stated price card.

## Methodology

The paper studies three retrieval modes over a held-constant input representation: a proprietary structure-aware chunking that splits documents into clause-aligned nodes rather than fixed-size spans (fixed chunking is not re-evaluated, as its fragmentation effect is already documented).

**Corpus and question set.** Fifteen transactional legal agreements (public SEC EDGAR / Kentucky pension FOIA / UK government filings) span three practice areas: six credit facility agreements (Fluor, Generac), three limited partnership agreements (Thomas Green Fund, Carlyle PE Partners), and six share/asset purchase agreements and disclosure schedules (Meta/Giphy, First Avenue Networks, PPG/Comex, Royal Wolf). The scored benchmark uses six of the fifteen documents. The scored set contains 20 questions with verified ground-truth answers — 18 document-bound (six per practice area, three per document) plus 2 out-of-scope controls that test correct declination rather than retrieval. Phases I–II used a 15-question DocNavBench subset; the 20-question scored set was written fresh at Phase III and shares no question with the tuning pool. Answering model is claude-sonnet-4-6; judge is claude-opus-4-7.

**Three modes.** (1) inject places the full corpus in the model's context on every query (cache-discounted prefix). (2) navembed pre-embeds document nodes, retrieves top-k by cosine similarity at query time, optionally reranks with a cross-encoder, and places only those nodes in the answering context. Five retrieval strategies were tested: R1 embedding top-k, R2 embedding + cross-reference expansion, R3 hybrid semantic + BM25 (reciprocal rank fusion), R4 semantic + cross-encoder rerank (retrieve 40, rerank, keep 10), R5 naive titles-only LLM index scan. (3) navindex replaces vector similarity with a three-step LLM navigation: S1 structured scan over a compact cached index (boolean pre-filters → relevance confirmation via titles/summaries → returns ≤10 node IDs), S2 deterministic fetch of verbatim clause text (following crossReferenceIds edges automatically), S3 grounded answer from only the fetched provisions. Twelve navembed configs (RUN-001–012) ran in three phases; six navindex descriptor configs were swept (Configs 1–6).

**Judging protocol.** Every question is answered twice in parallel (inject vs. navigate variant). The judge receives the question, the verified reference answer, and both model answers; each pair is judged in two directions (forward and reverse, positions swapped), and a win is awarded only when both directions agree. A hedged verdict in either direction is treated as a tie. A forced-decision variant (ties disallowed) was run on Config 2's Phase II pool. Judge error is bounded by a manual audit classifying 9/15 clearly correct, 4/15 defensible-but-debatable, 2/15 likely wrong.

**Cost model.** Token footprint (every attended input token, including cached context, governing long-context degradation and the window ceiling) is tracked separately from dollar cost (operational spend reshaped by cache reads at $0.30/M and one-time cache writes at $3.75/M, base input $3.00/M, output $15/M). The crossover rule is derived at warm steady state: c_inject = C·p_r vs. c_navigate = R·p_in, so inject is cheaper iff C < 10·R. navindex's two-call structure (cached S1 index ~98.6K + small S3 provision payload ~2.9K) yields its own ~128K threshold.

## Results & Highlights

- **navembed RUN-012 (semantic + rerank)** tied inject on 16/18 document-bound questions (inject preferred on 2, navembed on 0) and on both out-of-scope controls, while attending to 188,274 input tokens against inject's 3,263,796 — a 17.3× reduction — at $0.644 vs. inject's $1.614 (Phase III, 20-question scored set).
- **navembed RUN-009 (GTE embeddings)** achieved the largest token-footprint reduction of 29.9× (109,214 vs. 3,263,796 input tokens) but at a lower tie rate of 15/20 full set (13/18 document-bound), with inject preferred on 3 and navembed on 2.
- **navindex Config 6 (token-optimised JSON)** was judged tied with inject on all 18/18 document-bound questions and both controls (the highest single-run tie rate in either comparison; 95% CI [0.81, 1.00]), at a 1.61× smaller total token footprint (2.03M vs. 3.26M Nav/Inj Mtok), a ~56× smaller answering context (~2.9K vs. 163K tokens/query), and 25% lower recorded dollar cost ($1.2133 vs. $1.6140).
- **BM25 hybrid failure (RUN-011)** demonstrated lexical length bias: the hybrid payload inflated to 382,240 tokens, collapsing the reduction to 8.5× and pushing per-query payload (~19K) past the caching crossover so a warm-cached inject would beat it at steady state; inject wins rose to 4/18.
- **Caching-crossover rule**: across all twelve navembed configurations the token-footprint reduction held at 8.5–29.9×; the practical rule is that cached inject is cheaper in dollars only while the corpus stays below roughly 10× the retrieval payload. For the measured 163,147-token six-document prefix, both navigate modes stay cheaper at warm steady state (crossover for navembed ≈ 94K tokens, for navindex ≈ 128K tokens); for a single ~35K-token document, cached inject overtakes navembed after about seven repeated questions.
- **Node-cap leverage**: capping returned nodes at 10 (Config 2 vs. Config 1) cut navindex dollar cost by ~20% ($1.86 vs. $2.32) with navindex wins unchanged at 3 (ties dipped 11→9); raising the cap to 20 (Config 3) gave no benefit — additional nodes introduced noise rather than signal.
- **Judge audit**: 9/15 verdicts clearly correct, 4/15 defensible-but-debatable, 2/15 (13.3%) likely wrong (95% Clopper–Pearson CI [2%, 40%]); the forced-decision probe showed a 10/15 inject preference (95% CI [0.38, 0.88]) that is statistically indistinguishable from chance (exact binomial p=0.30).

## Authors & Credibility

All four authors (Mahmoud Hany, Mourad ElSheraey, Mahmoud Said, Peter Naoum) are at Syntheia Pty Ltd, the company whose proprietary structure-aware chunking and document-index API the work evaluates — this is an applied/industry paper rather than an independent academic benchmark. Author contributions are stated: Hany built and ran the evaluation program across all three phases and drafted the paper; ElSheraey revised the analysis, added uncertainty quantification and threats-to-validity, and prepared the final manuscript; Said implemented the cross-encoder reranking pipeline and cross-reference extraction; Naoum implemented the initial codebase and supervised experiments. The corpus is drawn entirely from public filings (SEC EDGAR, Kentucky pension FOIA, UK government sources), and the question set with verified reference answers plus all prompts (Appendix D) and per-question token/cost ledgers (Appendix B) are released as supplementary material. The reviewing lawyer (Horace Wu) authored the companion 52-question DocNavBench open-search set and verified the scored reference answers. The work is unusually transparent about its limits (Section 8, Threats to Validity), including the small n, single-annotator audit, same-provider judge/answerer, and absence of ground-truth node labels.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty.** Moderate and incremental rather than paradigm-shifting. The two retrieval modes (cosine retrieve-then-rerank; LLM navigation over a structured index) are individually well-trodden — navindex is explicitly positioned as closest in spirit to PageIndex (VectifyAI, 2025), and RAPTOR-style hierarchical indexing is cited. The genuine novelty is (a) the domain-specific encoding of legal structure (cross-reference edges, defined-term flags, amendment-precedence prompts) as traversable index fields, and (b) the closed-form caching-crossover rule that cleanly separates token footprint from dollar cost under prompt caching. The crossover derivation itself is a one-line algebraic rearrangement (C < R·p_in/p_r = 10R), so its contribution is conceptual framing rather than mathematical depth.

**Rigor.** Methodologically careful in ways that matter: position-bias control via forward/reverse judging with agreement-required wins, exact Clopper–Pearson intervals (correctly noting the Wald interval collapses at 0/1 boundaries), a forced-decision probe, a judge audit, and ledger-validated dollar accounting reconciled to a stated price card. However, several rigor gaps are material: (1) the headline navindex result rests on Config 6, which simultaneously changed the JSON serialisation, the question set, and the harness — so its tie count is confounded against Configs 1–5 (the authors flag this in the Table 3 note but it still underpins the headline "18/18 ties" claim); (2) only a single run per configuration, with n=18–20, yields wide CIs (e.g., RUN-012 ties [0.65, 0.99]); (3) the forced-decision probe was run only on the Phase II Config 2 pool with the non-reference preference judge, never on Config 6 under the final protocol — the authors themselves name this "the single most important follow-up"; (4) no ground-truth node labels exist, so retrieval recall/precision are unmeasurable and the diagnosis of RUN-012's two losses is conjectural.

**Reproducibility.** Strong on artifacts (released question set, reference answers, full prompts, per-question ledgers) but weak on the proprietary pipeline: structure-aware chunking and the Syntheia enrichment pass that populates boolean flags/summaries are not released, so navindex cannot be fully reproduced externally. The corpus is public, but two of the fifteen indexed documents (Netflix facility agreements) are in the open-search workspace and not bound to scored questions, and the scored six-document subset is a curated sample.

**Significance.** Practitioner-oriented and likely useful for legal-tech deployment decisions, but the external validity is narrow: one answering model (claude-sonnet-4-6), one judge (claude-opus-4-7), one 20-question set, six documents, single-run. The "tie" semantics are answer-equivalence relative to a reference, not correctness — a tie does not certify either answer is right, and the lawyer assessed only a handful of the 20 as genuinely hard, with most being precise retrieval-and-comprehension questions a modern LLM answers reliably once the governing text is in context. The evaluation therefore tests the retrieval condition cleanly but says little about legal reasoning.

**Red flags.** (1) The same provider (Anthropic) supplies both answerer and judge — the symmetric-self-preference argument is reasonable but unverified, and a cross-provider judge is explicitly deferred to future work; tie inflation through stylistic coherence is a real residual risk. (2) Reference answers were model-drafted before human verification, biasing the reference-anchored judge toward model-like phrasing. (3) Config 6 confounding (serialisation + question set + harness changed together) under the headline claim. (4) A noted corpus-selection artefact in Phase II where 11/15 questions were unanswerable because amendments were sampled without base documents — producing phantom ties; the authors argue the scored set is protected by construction, but the historical runs feeding the "8.5–29.9× across twelve configurations" claim are contaminated by this. (5) Questions are phrased answer-aware (the lawyer flagged this), potentially flattering retrieval relative to real-world issue-driven queries. No outright fabrication or overclaim beyond the stated caveats, but the headline "navindex tied on all 18" should be read against the [0.81, 1.00] CI and the un-run forced-decision probe on Config 6.

## Strengths

- Clean separation of token footprint (structural, governs long-context degradation) from dollar cost (cache-reshaped) as two independent axes, with the caching-crossover rule giving practitioners a concrete decision threshold.
- Rigorous judging protocol: forward/reverse with agreement-required wins, hedged-verdict-as-tie, reference-anchored criteria, and a forced-decision probe — directly addressing documented LLM-judge position bias.
- Honest, unusually detailed threats-to-validity section that names the missing ablations (descriptor-ladder rungs, forced-decision on Config 6), the artefacts (Phase II phantom ties), and the narrow scale — rather than hiding them.
- Failure-mode transparency: BM25 length bias, titles-only over-selection, and uncapped node selection are documented as configurations the authors would not ship, with dollar/token consequences quantified.
- Released artifacts (question set, verified reference answers, full system/judge prompts, per-question ledgers) enable partial external scrutiny.

## Limitations

- Single run per configuration over a 20-question, six-document benchmark — wide confidence intervals and no variance estimate across runs.
- Proprietary structure-aware chunking and Syntheia enrichment pass are not released, so navindex cannot be fully reproduced; only the prompts and the public corpus are open.
- No ground-truth node labels, so retrieval recall and precision are not directly measurable; the two RUN-012 inject wins are diagnosed conjecturally.
- Config 6 confounds serialisation, question set, and harness changes, complicating cross-configuration attribution of the headline 18/18 tie result.
- Judge and answerer share a provider (Anthropic); a cross-provider judge is deferred, leaving residual tie-inflation risk unmeasured.
- Reference answers were model-drafted before human verification, and questions are phrased answer-aware, which may flatter retrieval relative to real practitioner queries.
- No head-to-head judging between the two navigate modes (each was only judged against its own inject arm), so their relative quality rank is not established — only their token/cost profiles.
- Evaluation says nothing about legal reasoning: most questions are precise retrieval-and-comprehension items a modern LLM solves once the governing text is in context, so the comparison is a clean test of the retrieval condition, not of reasoning over cross-instrument dependencies (the cross-instrument reconciliation case is explicitly not isolated in the present question set).

## Relevance

This paper is directly relevant to RAG/retrieval-over-long-structured-documents research and to legal NLP: it operationalises structure-aware retrieval (cross-references, defined terms, amendment precedence) and gives a token-vs-dollar cost framework under prompt caching that transfers to any long-context retrieval setting. For work on long-context degradation, LLM-as-judge bias control, and structured-document QA, it offers both a concrete evaluation methodology and a deployable caching-crossover decision rule.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Would the navindex 18/18 tie result survive a forced-decision (ties-disallowed) judging run under the final reference-anchored protocol on the full 20-question scored set — i.e., are the ties genuine interchangeability or differences beneath the judge's resolution?
- How do the two navigate modes compare head-to-head (their answers judged directly against each other, not each against inject), and on which question types does structural navigation beat embedding retrieval?
- With ground-truth node labels, what is the retrieval recall/precision of each mode, and do RUN-012's two inject wins stem from relevant nodes ranked outside the top-10 after reranking (retrieval failure) or from reasoning failure?
- Does the caching-crossover rule (C < 10·R) hold across other providers' cache pricing (e.g., OpenAI, Google), and how does the 5-minute cache TTL interact with realistic asynchronous legal-review workflows where queries arrive days apart?
- How does the descriptor ladder (D1 heading-only → D5 full summary) affect answer quality when climbed one rung at a time as a controlled ablation, and is the boolean-flag pre-filter or the cross-reference graph the binding contributor to navindex's tie rate?
- Do the results transfer beyond Claude (e.g., to GPT-class or open answerers) and to corpora that exceed the context window, where inject ceases to be a runnable baseline at all?
