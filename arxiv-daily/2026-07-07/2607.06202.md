> **一句话总结：** UBEP 是面向华为 CM384 等生产级 superpod 的 MoE 通信库，通过 kernel 分解、层级化 token 调度和 Data-as-Flag 同步三项设计，将 All-to-All 延迟降低最高 52.4%、DeepSeek-R1 级模型端到端 TPOT 降低最高 11.1%。

> **TL;DR:** UBEP re-architects the Mixture-of-Experts Expert Parallelism Communication Library (EPCL) for modern multi-tier superpods (Huawei CM384, 256 NPU dies), replacing BSP-style global barriers with dependency-driven kernel decomposition, a topology-aware hierarchical token scheduler, and a Data-as-Flag synchronization scheme; on a production CM384 it cuts All-to-All latency by up to 52.4% versus the CANN EP baseline (e.g., 73 µs vs. 103 µs at 16-rank All-to-All) and improves end-to-end MoE inference TPOT by up to 11.1% on DeepSeek-R1-scale models.

## 核心贡献

- **刻画 superpod 上 BSP-EPCL 的三类根本瓶颈**：(1) 全局屏障导致的强制串行化；(2) 同步原语（flag/barrier/kernel launch）在高带宽 fabric 上占比过高，作者称之为"synchronization tax"（SyncAll 约占 15% 总执行时间）；(3) 多层 fabric 的拓扑无关调度带来严重 straggler——两层 hop 访问延迟可达本地访问的 11.5 倍。
- **UBEP 通信库**：在华为 CANN 栈上用约 10K 行 Ascend C 实现，原生面向多层 superpod，包含三项协同设计。
- **Kernel Decomposition**（§3.2）：把整体 All-to-All 拆成 token-sending / TokenCnt 计算等细粒度任务，分配给不同 AIV 组并行执行，用点对点异步信号替代 SyncAll，使地址计算与 token 发送重叠。
- **Hierarchical Token-level Scheduling**（§3.3）：将 token-to-core 映射建模为 NP-hard 优化问题，提出"延迟同质化"启发式 + 硬件加速 Quaternary Search（在 1µs 内完成），按 one-hop/two-hop 同时均衡负载与物理距离。
- **Data-as-Flag 同步**（§3.4）：利用 512B 原子写，把同步信号嵌进数据负载，给出 Token-Flag Fusion、Data-for-Checksum、Sentinel Polling 三种变体并分析其安全性（happens-before 论证 + 2^-256 碰撞概率上界）。
- **生产级规模验证**：在 256 NPU die 的 CM384 上对 Qwen3-30B / GLM-4.7 / DeepSeek-R1 / DeepSeek-V3.2（30B–685B 参数）评估，给出可移植性清单（Table 5）说明哪些设计依赖 CM384、哪些在其他 fabric 上可降级使用。

## Key Contributions

- **Characterizes three fundamental bottlenecks** of existing BSP-based EPCLs (DeepEP, UCCL-EP, CANN EP, Hybrid-EP) when deployed on modern multi-tier superpods: strict execution serialization from global barriers, a "synchronization tax" that dominates as link bandwidth scales, and topology-agnostic scheduling that ignores the 11.5× latency gap between intra-NPU and two-hop accesses.
- **UBEP**, a production-ready EPCL implemented in ~10K lines of Ascend C on the Huawei CANN stack, co-designing three mechanisms that are mutually synergistic.
- **Kernel Decomposition**: partitions AIVs into groups running independent tasks (token transmission vs. metadata/CalCumSum) in parallel, replacing global barriers with point-to-point data signals so the prefix-sum/address computation overlaps token dispatch.
- **Hierarchical Token-level Scheduler**: formally models token-to-core assignment as an NP-hard min-makespan problem (proved in Appendix B), then solves it heuristically via latency homogenization (per-hop capacity bound) plus a SIMD Quaternary Search mapper that converges in 4–5 steps within 1 µs.
- **Data-as-Flag synchronization**: three variants (Token-Flag Fusion, Data-for-Checksum, Sentinel Polling) that piggyback synchronization onto data via the UB fabric's 512B atomic writes, with a formal happens-before safety argument and a reserved sentinel encoding to eliminate SP's theoretical deadlock.
- **Production-scale evaluation** on a 256-die CM384 superpod across four models (30B–685B), with an explicit portability checklist (Table 5) separating ISA-independent ideas from CM384-specific assumptions.

## Methodology

UBEP targets the dispatch stage of MoE All-to-All on a two-tier UB fabric (CM384). The design is driven by three insights derived from measurement:

1. **BSP serialization is the binding constraint on fast fabrics.** On CM384, bandwidth jumps from ~50 GB/s (IB/RoCE) to 392 GB/s, so token dispatch saturates quickly and only half the AIVs are needed to fill the pipe (Figure 3b: latency efficiency drops from 100% at 8 cores to 31.1% at 48). The remaining cores sit idle at barriers. UBEP therefore *decomposes the monolithic kernel* so independent sub-tasks (Token-Sending, TokenCnt-Sending/CalCumSum) run concurrently on disjoint AIV groups, and the data dependency into Token-Reordering is resolved by point-to-point polling of address slots rather than SyncAll.

2. **Synchronization overhead is a tax.** SyncAll alone costs ~15% of execution time. UBEP replaces it with **Data-as-Flag**: the sender embeds a 32B flag/checksum into a 512B block that the UB fabric writes atomically, so the receiver infers completion by reading the data itself — no separate control plane. Three variants trade off bandwidth efficiency vs. pipelining granularity vs. deadlock risk, with Sentinel Polling giving ~100% effective bandwidth at the cost of a (mitigated) sentinel-collision risk.

3. **The unified address space is not flat.** Two-hop accesses are 11.5× slower than intra-NPU (Table 2: 218 ns vs. 2500 ns). UBEP models token assignment as minimizing max per-AIV completion time subject to load balance and assignment constraints, proves it NP-hard, and solves it heuristically by *latency homogenization*: (a) expert remapping via logical matrix transposition so each AIV gets a balanced one-hop/two-hop mix, and (b) equal-size token slices with a SIMD Quaternary Search (Algorithm 1) for prefix-sum lookup of expert IDs.

Implementation notes: global load/store is used for instruction-level point-to-point sync; receive address space is pre-partitioned so senders write tokens directly into destination HBM (no local aggregation); SIMD instructions (VectorCount, CompareScalar, FindFirstValue) replace scalar token loops. End-to-end runs use vLLM-Ascend v0.14.0.

## Results & Highlights

- **All-to-All latency reduced up to 52.4%** over CANN EP at the operator level (BS=128, EP=64 NPUs), with the breakdown study (Figure 8) showing gains from 34.7% to 52.4% across batch sizes 32–128 by overlapping CalCumSum with Token-Sending.
- **Head-to-head vs. CANN EP on identical CM384 hardware (Table 4):** at 16-rank All-to-All, UBEP achieves 73 µs vs. CANN EP's 103 µs (DeepEP on H800 is 118 µs as a protocol reference); UBEP improves effective bandwidth by **35.3%–40.8%** (e.g., 100 vs. 71 GB/s at 16 ranks; 66 vs. 48 GB/s at 256 ranks).
- **End-to-end TPOT improved up to 11.1%** (P99 latency reduction) on DeepSeek-R1-scale models (Figure 11), across Qwen3-30B, GLM-4.7, DeepSeek-R1, and DeepSeek-V3.2; the gain is smaller than the operator speedup because MoE All-to-All is only ~50% of per-token latency and ~20% of actual hardware execution time, the rest being FFN, attention, scheduling, and kernel-launch stalls.
- **Sensitivity sweeps (Figure 7):** average dispatch-operator improvement of ~46.4% across BS 8–64 (dropping to 35.9% at BS=128 as transmission dominates), ~43.9% average as ranks scale 64→256 (dropping to 35.1% at 256 ranks due to +13.2% volume and cores diverted to routing/checksum), and 38.6–41.9% speedups as expert count increases (Baseline spends ~70% of runtime on synchronization with many experts; UBEP keeps it near ~30%).
- **Hierarchical scheduler ablation (Figure 9):** on a 64-NPU / 256-expert / 8-hot-expert topology, max per-AIV token latency falls from **62.2 µs (CANN EP) → 48.1 µs (UBEP w/o mapping) → 43.5 µs (full UBEP)**, i.e., the topology-aware mapping alone removes a further ~4.6 µs beyond pure load balancing by avoiding cross-Tier-2 traffic.
- **Data-as-Flag ablation (Figure 10):** all three variants reduce latency by **31.0%–57.1%** vs. CANN EP's Stop-and-Wait across rank counts 16/32/64, with absolute gains of 3.7–4.6 µs at small data volumes; Sentinel Polling delivers the lowest latency across most settings while achieving theoretical 100% bandwidth utilization.

Three equal-contribution lead authors (Liu, Liu, Shen) and a second corresponding author from Huawei (Zhongzhe Hu).
- **Accepted to ACM SIGCOMM 2026** (top-tier networking/systems venue), a strong signal of peer-reviewed rigor; the paper includes a formal NP-hardness proof (Appendix B), a cost model derivation (Appendix A), and a safety/happens-before argument for Data-as-Flag.
- Evaluation is on **production hardware** (a real CM384 allocation of 256 NPU dies via 16 Ascend servers) rather than simulation, and uses the production **vLLM-Ascend v0.14.0** serving stack. ## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (genuine):** The combination is well-motivated and non-obvious. The "synchronization tax" framing and the measured 11.5× intra-NPU/two-hop latency gap (Table 2) are concrete new evidence for why BSP EPCLs break on sub-microsecond fabrics. Data-as-Flag with formal happens-before reasoning and the 2^-256 sentinel-collision bound is a real, defensible mechanism, not a re-skin of NVSHMEM/MSCCL++ fence-and-flag (which the related work correctly distinguishes).
- **Rigor (mostly strong, with caveats):** The NP-hard formulation and portability checklist (Table 5) are commendable, and the ablations cleanly isolate each component (scheduling, synchronization). However, the headline baselines are **incomplete**: DeepEP is shown only on H800 "as a protocol-capability reference," never on CM384, and Hybrid-EP / UCCL-EP / FUSCO are discussed in Table 1 and related work but **never benchmarked** — so the central comparison is essentially UBEP vs. CANN EP, the in-house predecessor. A reader cannot tell how much of the win comes from beating BSP in general vs. beating one particular CANN implementation.
- **Reproducibility (limited):** Code is not mentioned as released, and the entire stack (Ascend C, CANN, CM384 hardware, vLLM-Ascend) is Huawei-proprietary and not available to most academic groups. The portability checklist partially addresses this by stating which ideas survive on other fabrics, but the empirical claims are not independently reproducible without Ascend hardware access.
- **Significance (high for the superpod niche):** This is one of the first EPCLs co-designed for multi-tier superpods and directly informs next-gen fabrics (NVL576, UALink, SUE). The end-to-end 11.1% TPOT gain is modest next to the 52.4% operator gain, which the authors attribute honestly to dependency stalls rather than overclaiming.
- **Red flags / overclaiming:** Minor. The "up to 52.4%" headline is the best-case operator number; the user-visible 11.1% TPOT is reported but could be more prominent. The claim that UBEP "exhibits slightly longer raw communication latency than the Baseline" (§5.2) is an honest admission that kernel decomposition diverts cores, which strengthens credibility. The Sentinel Polling deadlock is admitted but dismissed via a probability bound that assumes uniform activation distributions — the paper itself notes activations occupy a "narrow subset," which actually *raises* collision risk in adversarial cases and deserves stress-testing.

## Strengths

- Co-designed for and evaluated on real production superpod hardware at 256-die scale, with four SOTA MoE models spanning 30B–685B parameters.
- Three mechanisms are individually well-motivated and *synergistic* as the paper explicitly argues (decomposition exposes fine-grained work; scheduler balances it; Data-as-Flag removes the barrier that would otherwise re-serialize it).
- Formal treatment: NP-hardness proof, cost model, happens-before safety argument, and an explicit portability checklist separating portable ideas from CM384-specific assumptions.
- Honest reporting of the trade-off (diverted cores increase raw transmission latency but net latency drops) and of why end-to-end gains trail operator gains.

## Limitations

- **Baseline breadth:** the only directly comparable same-hardware baseline is CANN EP; DeepEP, Hybrid-EP, UCCL-EP, and FUSCO are positioned qualitatively (Table 1) but not benchmarked, so cross-architecture claims rest on characterization rather than measurement.
- **Hardware lock-in:** requires UB fabric's 512B atomic writes and Ascend AIV-level concurrency; the paper's own Table 5 admits degradation paths, but the empirical 52.4%/11.1% numbers are CM384-specific and not reproducible on NVIDIA or AMD stacks without re-implementation.
- **Combine stage under-documented:** the paper focuses on dispatch ("without elaborating on trivial yet intricate details in combine"), leaving the reader to trust that combine benefits symmetrically.
- **Sentinel collision:** the 2^-256 bound assumes uniform distributions, while BF16/FP16 activations are highly non-uniform; adversarial weight/activation settings that approach the sentinel are not stress-tested empirically.
- **End-to-end ceiling:** only ~11% TPOT gain despite ~52% operator gain indicates large residual dependency/framework stalls outside EP communication; the paper flags this as future work but does not yet address it.

## Relevance

UBEP 直接命中大规模 MoE 推理与训练系统优化方向，对自研/部署 superpod（如华为 CM384、NVIDIA NVL576）上的 All-to-All 通信库设计有直接参考价值；其"同步税"、层级化拓扑感知调度和 Data-as-Flag 三项设计，对任何研究 MoE 专家并行通信瓶颈、想把端到端 TPOT 压到 sub-10ms 级的系统/网络研究者都是当前 SOTA 量级的可借鉴工作。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does UBEP perform against DeepEP/Hybrid-EP/UCCL-EP when they are *ported* to CM384 (or vice versa), isolating algorithmic wins from hardware-specific advantages? Is CANN EP a tuned or a strawman baseline?
- Can the Sentinel Polling deadlock risk be characterized empirically under adversarial or quantization-induced activation distributions (e.g., W4A8, heavy zero-padding) rather than the uniform-distribution bound?
- Does the combine stage see symmetric gains, and how does UBEP interact with Attention-FFN Disaggregation's bipartite M2N traffic in practice?
- How much of the residual end-to-end stall (operator 52% vs. TPOT 11%) can be recovered by extending the dependency-driven machinery to FFN/attention/scheduling, as the authors hint?
- How do the three mechanisms transfer to non-Ascend superpods — e.g., NVL576 with UALink, or SUE fabrics — where 512B atomicity and AIV-level concurrency assumptions differ?
