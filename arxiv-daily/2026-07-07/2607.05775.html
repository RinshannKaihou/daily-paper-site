§ 01

Methodology

An explicitly narrative synthesis — not a PRISMA-registered systematic review — of 27 papers / 19 benchmarks (2023–2026) across five overlapping areas: tool use, web navigation, multi-constraint planning, multi-agent coordination, and agent safety.

Inclusion required either a benchmark with quantitative error analysis, a failure taxonomy grounded in manual annotation with a reported inter-annotator agreement statistic, or a benchmark-validity study (contamination, leakage, test-suite adequacy). Leaderboard-only accuracy, product announcements, and out-of-scope modalities (e.g., embodied robotics) were excluded.

From 60+ named error categories (ToolScan's seven types, MAST's fourteen modes, TravelPlanner's constraint violations) the authors iteratively grouped them into six clusters aligned with stages of the reasoning-to-action pipeline, then corroborated boundaries via independent convergence across separately conducted annotation efforts (MAST κ = 0.88; ToolScan; REST-API). Table 2 consolidates representative figures as cited ranges with peer-review status flagged; the authors did not independently re-verify others' numbers.

§ 03

Strengths & limitations

Strengths

  • Genuinely balanced — pairs every failure cluster with an explicit "where agents have improved" section.
  • Transparent methodology: scope, inclusion criteria, and a documented iterative taxonomy-derivation procedure.
  • Table 2 is a high-value, categorized, traceable reference with peer-review status labeled at each citation.
  • Treats measurement validity (§4.6) as a first-class concern alongside capability failures.
  • Concrete practical implications: a diagnostic checklist mapping failure types to architectural interventions (§8.2).
  • Cross-cutting patterns framed as falsifiable candidate properties rather than dogma.

Limitations

  • Narrative, not pre-registered systematic review; cluster boundaries subjective and author-dependent.
  • Field moves fast — several cited figures were already superseded within months.
  • No independent reproduction; Table 2 compiles others' numbers without re-verification.
  • Heavily English-language, text- and web-based; robotics, embodied, and voice agents out of scope.
  • Majority of sources are arXiv pre-prints; only a minority fully peer-reviewed.
  • Multi-agent and safety clusters draw on only 2 papers each — the thinnest evidence base.
  • No quantitative meta-analysis or formal inter-taxonomy overlap measure.

  2. Does the test-time-scaling non-improvement finding generalize beyond general-purpose agents, or is it specific to saturated working-memory regimes? What is the accuracy–cost frontier shape?arXiv:2602.18998
  3. How much of apparent non-composability is itself an artifact of benchmark construction (binary success, no partial credit for near-miss) versus genuine model limits?§8.4 · Q3
  4. Can agent-safety benchmarks extend to explicitly measure bystander / third-party harm, not only instructing-user harm?§4.5
  5. What is the leave-one-out stability of the six-cluster taxonomy if the contested SWE-bench re-analyses or the single-industry WebArena 61.7% figure are removed?
  6. How do these patterns compare quantitatively against human-factors error-recovery literature in aviation and clinical medicine?§8.4 · Q4
