Why This Matters

Directly informs cross-lingual RAG and abstention systems: tells when a multilingual model should defer rather than answer.

Critical Edge

Headline AUROC comes from only two open-weights models — Claude Sonnet 4.5 was excluded from the open-vs-closed head-to-head.

01 / Method

Estimate uncertainty from the reasoning trace, not the answer label.

The central design choice: apply UE methods to the model's chain-of-thought (~150 words) while grounding correctness in MCQA exact match.

This sidesteps the LLM-as-judge / ROUGE / BERTScore correctness proxies that Santilli et al. (2025) showed misrank UE methods with language-dependent noise. Short MCQA labels alone are too thin to carry meaningful uncertainty; the reasoning trace is where the signal lives.

Two parallel, human-curated MCQA datasets — Global-MMLU (4 opts, 6 cats) and MMLU-ProX (10 opts, 14 cats) — yield 2,000 questions × 22 languages = 44,000 instances. Open-ended QA was deliberately rejected: its 1–2 word factoids would force the very proxies the authors want to avoid.

Nine UE methods span open-box (Token Entropy, Max Prob, Self Certainty), verbalized (Self Verbalized numeric probability), and sampling/consistency (Lexical Similarity, Semantic Entropy, EigValLaplacian, DegMat, Eccentricity), all via LM-Polygraph. Metric: AUROC.

03 / Balance

What holds up — and what doesn't.

Strengths

  • Scale and coverage (22 langs, 9 models, 9 methods, 44k instances) exceed all prior multilingual UE work.
  • Methodologically clean: anchors correctness to MCQA exact match, applies UE to the reasoning trace — avoids the proxies Santilli et al. showed inflate numbers.
  • Statistically disciplined: 95% CIs per cell; significance claims rest explicitly on non-overlapping intervals.
  • The English-reasoning finding is both scientifically interesting (comprehension vs generation asymmetry) and operationally useful.
  • Concrete, decision-relevant threshold table mapping calibration strategies to coverage/error trade-offs.

Limitations

  • Restricted to two MCQA datasets; open-ended generation with reliable correctness deferred to future work.
  • Only 9 inference-time methods — no training-based probes (e.g. Semantic Entropy Probes) evaluated.
  • Swahili, Yoruba, Telugu remain weakest even after English reasoning; closing the gap left open.
  • Scaling story confounded: smaller Qwen3 variants are distilled from the 235B teacher.
  • Self Verbalized depends on the model emitting a valid numeric probability; parse-failure fallback (u=1) frequency unreported.
  • RQ4 cross-lingual accuracy drop not decomposed by language family.
04 / Open

Questions this work leaves on the table.

  1. Does the English-reasoning benefit persist when reasoning is later post-edited back into the target language for end-user interpretability — or does translation reintroduce uncertainty noise?

  2. How do training-based UE methods (Semantic Entropy Probes) behave on this 22-language benchmark, and do they avoid the low-resource collapse of sampling-based methods?

  3. Is the small-model failure (<1B at 0.50 AUROC) a fundamental limit of UE signals in weak models, or would ensemble / few-shot prompting recover usable uncertainty?

  4. Can the comprehension-vs-generation asymmetry be confirmed with a mechanistic probe (hidden-state analysis) rather than a behavioral argument?

  5. How do the threshold strategies transfer to open-ended generation, where correctness labels are unavailable and the trade-off space differs?
