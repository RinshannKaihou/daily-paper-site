> **一句话总结：** 本文在三个跨模态的时间索引数据集（Yearbook 图像分类、Amazon Reviews 文本回归、arXiv 多标签文本分类）上，用统一的"时间漂移矩阵"协议系统比较了 MLP、CNN、ResNet、RNN、Transformer 以及冻结预训练编码器，发现归纳偏置越强、分布内准确率越高的模型往往漂移最快，而冻结的预训练编码器以牺牲少量分布内精度换取更平稳的长期表现。

> **TL;DR:** A unified "temporal drift matrix" evaluation across three long-range time-indexed datasets (Yearbook image classification, Amazon Reviews regression, arXiv multi-label classification) shows that architectures with stronger inductive biases (CNN/ResNet on Yearbook, recurrent nets on Amazon) reach the highest in-distribution accuracy but degrade fastest under temporal shift — on Yearbook CNN-M/CNN-L reach ~92.8% in-distribution accuracy yet decay 13.9 points to ~78.4–79.0% on future years, while the no-spatial-prior MLP-S decays only 7.1 points and the frozen DINOv3-S encoder is the steadiest backbone (7.9 decay); frozen pretrained encoders consistently trade in-distribution accuracy for steadier temporal behavior.

## 核心贡献

- 首次在三个异构、长时间跨度的时间索引领域（图像分类 Yearbook、文本回归 Amazon Reviews、多标签文本分类 arXiv）上，对从 MLP 到 CNN/ResNet、RNN、Transformer 再到冻结预训练编码器（DINOv2/v3、CLIP、SigLIP、BERT、RoBERTa、DeBERTa-v3 等）的广泛架构家族进行了统一的时序鲁棒性比较。
- 提出"时间漂移矩阵（Temporal Drift Matrix）"这一紧凑表示：模型在累积历史数据上训练，并在更早与更晚的时间区间上评估，从而一次性量化跨时序泛化；并定义了分布内分数 ID(M)、未来分数 Fut(M) 与衰减 Dec(M) 三个汇总统计量。
- 揭示出贯穿三个领域的核心规律：**使模型在分布内表现优异的强归纳偏置所提取的高度判别性、时代相关的特征，恰恰是最容易随时间漂移而失效的特征**；因此"分布内最优"与"时序最鲁棒"常常是相反的。
- 通过梯度显著性图（saliency maps）给出了机制性的定性解释：CNN/ResNet 随训练窗口变长会把注意力收紧到眼睛等强判别局部细节，而这些细节最绑定训练年代。
- 提供了实用的架构选型指引：部署时若重训练周期长、漂移压力大，应优先考虑冻结预训练编码器而非分布内最优模型。

## Key Contributions

- A unified cross-domain empirical comparison of temporal robustness spanning MLP, CNN, ResNet, RNN (GRU/LSTM), Transformer, and a large set of frozen pretrained encoders (DINOv2/v3, CLIP, SigLIP, ConvNeXt, ResNet50-IN, ViT-S16-IN21k, MAE, EVA02, BERT, RoBERTa, DeBERTa-v3, ELECTRA, MPNet, ModernBERT, DistilBERT, MiniLM-L6) across three long-range time-indexed datasets: Yearbook (image classification), Amazon Reviews (text regression), and arXiv (multi-label text classification).
- Introduction of the **temporal drift matrix** formalism: models are trained on cumulative historical data D≤k and evaluated on both earlier (in-distribution held-out) and later (forward) time slices, with three summary statistics — ID(M), Fut(M), and Dec(M) — that order every model from most to least temporally robust.
- A cross-domain finding that **stronger inductive biases yield higher in-distribution accuracy but faster temporal decay**, because the localized, period-specific features they exploit are precisely the ones that change most over time.
- A mechanism-level qualitative explanation via gradient saliency maps showing CNN/ResNet attention tightening to highly discriminative but era-specific facial details (eyes) as the training window widens, whereas the MLP keeps a diffuse, less era-bound attribution.
- Practical guidance: frozen pretrained encoders concede in-distribution accuracy in exchange for steadier behavior, making them preferable when retraining horizons are long.

## Methodology

The study fixes a single evaluation protocol across all three datasets and all model families so that differences come from architecture rather than from splitting or metric choices.

**Datasets (time-indexed):**
- **Yearbook** — 37,921 frontal portraits of US high-school seniors from 1905–2013, processed as 3-channel 32×32 tensors, binary sex labels, sliced into K=104 yearly intervals, scored by accuracy. Exhibits covariate + concept drift (hairstyles, clothing).
- **Amazon Reviews 2023** — sampled 300,000 reviews from 7 product categories, 2014–2023, sliced into K=20 half-year intervals, cast as 1–5 star rating regression scored by a per-rating balanced MSE (MSEbal, lower is better) that weights every rating level equally to counteract the heavy 5-star skew.
- **arXiv** — 2,866,787 title–abstract records from 2000–2025, K=26 yearly intervals, multi-label classification over 7 leaf subject categories (papers may carry several), scored by macro AUC (threshold-free, class-balanced). Loss is a class-weighted binary cross-entropy per category.

**Model families (chosen to span inductive-bias strength):**
- Image: MLP, CNN, ResNet, ViT at small/medium/large sizes (12 trained-from-scratch models), plus 9 frozen pretrained vision encoders (DINOv2-S, DINOv3-S, CLIP-B32, SigLIP-B, ConvNeXt-S, ResNet50-IN, ViT-S16-IN21k, MAE-B, EVA02-B) with a frozen backbone + linear head.
- Text: all four families (FFN, TextCNN, recurrent GRU/LSTM, Transformer encoder) operate on **cached frozen RoBERTa token embeddings** so that families differ only in inductive bias, not representation; plus 7–8 frozen language encoders (BERT, RoBERTa, DeBERTa-v3, ELECTRA, MPNet, ModernBERT, DistilBERT, MiniLM-L6).

**Temporal drift matrix M ∈ R^{K×K}:** M_ij = perf(f_i, D_j), where f_i is trained on cumulative data through period i. Diagonal = in-distribution held-out; upper-left = held-out on earlier periods; lower-right (j>i) = forward generalization to unseen future. Summary statistics: ID(M) = mean diagonal; Fut(M) = mean over j>i cells; Dec(M) = ID−Fut (accuracy/AUC) or Fut−ID (MSEbal), always oriented so larger = worse.

**Training protocol:** cumulative training per slice; one fixed split seed shared across models (70/30 stratified); Adam for image, AdamW for text (fixed per modality to avoid optimizer-as-confound); 5 seeds on Yearbook, 3 on text tasks; final-checkpoint model selection. ~165 4-GPU node-hours total (222 model-seed configs). Code/data public at github.com/learning-mechanisms/drift-happens and wandb.ai/drift-happens/drift-happens.

## Results & Highlights

- **Yearbook in-distribution vs. decay trade-off:** CNN-M and CNN-L reach the highest in-distribution accuracy (~92.8%) but decay the most, by **13.9 points (CNN-M) and 13.7 points (CNN-L)**, dropping future accuracy to ~78.4% and 79.0% respectively. By contrast the no-spatial-prior **MLP-S decays only 7.1 points** (from a lower 62.3% future floor), and the steadiest frozen encoder **DINOv3-S decays 7.9 points** (Table 5, Figure 12). MLP-L and ResNet-S/CNN-S decay 12.2–12.3 points — larger variants within a family decay more than smaller ones, leaving inductive bias and capacity partially confounded.
- **Yearbook saliency mechanism:** Extending training from 1950→1970 tightens CNN attention from a diffuse scatter over cheeks/mouth to a compact near-symmetric pair of bright spots at the eyes — the most discriminative but era-specific cue — while the MLP keeps attribution diffuse across the whole frame including background, explaining why the localized-feature models decay fastest.
- **Amazon Reviews (balanced MSE, lower better):** Recurrent/Transformer models fit best in-distribution (**BiGRU-S 0.680, TX-S 0.686**) vs. order-free FFN at ~0.761 and frozen encoders at 0.806–1.022, but the recurrent family decays most (**up to 0.128 balanced MSE**, climbing to ~0.824 on future reviews; recurrence worsens by 0.242 when trained on earliest reviews). The **FFN is the steadiest trained model** (only +0.075–0.089). The **frozen DeBERTa-v3 is the most stable model in the entire study at 0.043 decay**, again trading in-distribution accuracy for temporal steadiness.
- **arXiv (macro AUC):** A strong inductive bias yields **no in-distribution advantage** here — all four trained families land within ~97.2%–98.1% macro AUC (within a point of one another) because topic is already encoded in the shared frozen RoBERTa embeddings. Consequently no family decays faster than the rest: every family loses only **2.7–3.4 points** going forward. The exceptions are DeBERTa-v3 and ELECTRA frozen encoders, which fall further at 6.5 and 7.3 points. Where the bias gains nothing in-distribution it forms no period-specific features to lose.
- **Forgetting curves (Yearbook, Figure 4):** strongly biased networks start near 90% at zero gap and descend steepest; curves converge and cross as the gap widens, so the families that led in-distribution lose their edge on distant years and every model settles near two-class chance.
- **Compute footprint:** 222 model-seed configs; ~165.1 4-GPU node-hours total across the three datasets (Table 1).

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Genuine. Most prior robustness work varies either one architecture across datasets or one dataset across a narrow set of architectures; the cross-cutting (many architectures × multiple modalities × multiple drift mechanisms under one protocol) with the drift-matrix formalism is a real contribution, and the inductive-bias-vs-robustness inversion is a crisp, citable finding.
- **Rigor:** The shared protocol (fixed split seed, fixed optimizer per modality, cumulative training, ID/Fut/Dec decomposition, saliency-based mechanism check) is well controlled and the metric choices are sensible (balanced MSE for skewed ratings, macro AUC for imbalanced multi-label). Multiple seeds (5/3) are used. Compute is documented.
- **Reproducibility:** High — pinned environments, public code, public W&B artifacts, checksum-verified regeneration, license clarity. A rare strength.
- **Significance:** Practically actionable — it tells practitioners not to select by in-distribution accuracy alone when the retraining horizon is long. Conceptually it connects inductive bias to temporal decay in a way that generalizes across modalities.
- **Red flags / weaknesses (paper-specific, not generic):**
  - **Text models are constrained by a single shared frozen RoBERTa representation** (acknowledged limitation): because too few per-slice examples exist to train a text encoder from scratch, all text families differ only in a light head, so absolute text scores and family-level text conclusions must be read with that caveat — the paper itself says "their absolute scores should be read in that light."
  - **Inductive bias and capacity are partially confounded** within a family (small/medium/large variants), and the authors do not run capacity-matched pairs to disentangle them (explicitly flagged as future work). On Yearbook the smallest MLP/CNN/ResNet decay less than their larger siblings, so part of the "stronger bias decays more" claim could be scale.
  - **Narrow label spaces on text** — only a 5-point rating and 7 arXiv leaf categories, on a stratified subsample (300k reviews) rather than the full corpora — and the text time spans (~10 and ~25 years) are much shorter than the century-long Yearbook, so the text findings are weaker evidence than the image findings.
  - **No formal significance tests** — averages over a small fixed seed set only; the authors (correctly, honestly) emphasize family-level patterns over individual rankings, but per-model deltas of a few tenths (e.g., DINOv3-S 7.9 vs ViT-L 8.0 decay) are likely within seed noise.
  - **Descriptive, not mechanistic** by the authors' own admission: it measures how robustness varies with bias/pretraining without isolating the precise drifting cues, so "why" remains open.
  - **No comparison to temporal adaptation / continual-learning baselines** (e.g., ERM-vs-group-DRO-vs-continual methods as in Wild-Time) — by design, since the goal is inherent robustness, but readers should not mistake this for a methods benchmark.

## Strengths

- A genuinely unified, protocol-controlled comparison across architectures × modalities × drift types — the cross-cutting design is the study's main methodological value.
- The drift-matrix + ID/Fut/Dec formalism is compact, interpretable, and reusable; it could become a standard reporting artifact for temporal-robustness papers.
- The central, counter-intuitive finding (the features that make a model accurate in-distribution are the ones that erode fastest) is shown consistently in all three domains and explained qualitatively with saliency maps on Yearbook.
- Excellent reproducibility: pinned environments, public code, public W&B artifacts, checksum-verified regeneration, permissive licenses.
- Honest, self-critical Limitations section that flags the confounds (capacity, frozen RoBERTa, narrow text labels, descriptive-only) rather than hiding them.
- Practical, non-obvious takeaway for deployment: when retraining cycles are long, prefer frozen pretrained encoders and weigh the expected drift horizon, not in-distribution accuracy alone.

## Limitations

- Text tasks share one frozen RoBERTa embedding space, so text-family differences reflect only a light head and absolute text numbers are caveated.
- Inductive bias is confounded with capacity (no capacity-matched pairs); within-family smaller variants decay less, so scale is a partial alternative explanation on Yearbook.
- Narrow label spaces on text (5-point rating, 7 arXiv categories) and short text time spans (10 and 25 years) vs. a century for Yearbook; Amazon uses a 300k stratified subsample rather than the full 571M-review corpus.
- No formal statistical significance testing; small fixed seed sets (5/3), so fine-grained per-model rankings are noisy and the authors appropriately stress family-level patterns.
- Analysis is descriptive, not mechanistic — the precise drifting cues are not isolated.
- No adaptive/continual-learning baselines compared (by design, but limits practical method-level guidance).
- Many modern architectures remain untested; the authors call this "a first systematic pass rather than an exhaustive one."

## Relevance

对于关注分布偏移、模型鲁棒性评估与持续学习系统的研究方向高度相关：它提供了一个可复用的"时间漂移矩阵"评估协议和跨架构的实证基线，可直接用于评估持续学习/重训练策略的收益边界，也对真实部署中如何根据重训练周期选择架构给出明确指引。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Would the inductive-bias-vs-decay inversion survive **capacity-matched** architecture pairs (e.g., a wide MLP matched to a CNN's parameter count), or is part of the effect really about scale?
- How do the findings change when text models are allowed to **fine-tune or even pretrain their own encoder** per time slice, rather than sharing a frozen RoBERTa representation?
- Can the drift matrix be paired with **drift-triggered / cost-aware retraining** (Modyn-style) to measure not just decay but how cheaply decay can be undone under a fixed compute budget?
- Do the conclusions extend to **longer text time spans and richer label spaces** (full Amazon 1996–2023, full arXiv 176 categories) where language drift should be more pronounced and architecture gaps wider?
- Is there a **mechanistic** account linking specific inductive biases to the specific features that drift (e.g., predicting decay from the spectrum of learned features)?
- How do **temporal adaptation methods** (continual learning, group-DRO, test-time adaptation) reshape the ID/Fut/Dec ordering reported here under the same protocol?
