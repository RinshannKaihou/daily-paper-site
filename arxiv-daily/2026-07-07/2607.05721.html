SpanUQ: Span-Level Uncertainty Quantification for Large Language Model Generation ↗

Yimeng Zhang, Yingying Zhuang, Ziyi Wang, Yuxuan Lu, Pei Chen, Aman Gupta, Zhe Su, Ming Tan, Zhilin Zhang, Qun Liu, Manikandarajan Ramanathan, Rajashekar Maragoud, Edward Vul, Jing Huang, Dakuo Wang — Amazon & Northeastern University

uncertainty quantification LLM hallucination detection span-level hidden-state probe DETR span detection
Key finding

A ~25M-parameter probe distills multi-sample uncertainty into a single forward pass over frozen hidden states — locating LLM hallucinations at the span.

Key metric

0.939AUROC

vs SelfCheckGPT-NLI 0.808 (+13.1 pts) and MLP Probe 0.881 (+5.8) — at 10–20× faster. Holds 0.908–0.944 across five LLMs.

Why this matters

Span-level uncertainty in one forward pass directly serves trustworthy LLM deployment, self-refinement, and error localization in retrieval-augmented settings.

Critical edge

Span-level gains partly reflect a broadcast evaluation that structurally caps sequence- and claim-level rivals — at sequence level SpanUQ (ρ 0.851) only narrowly beats SelfCheckGPT-NLI (0.805).

Labels come from an automatic Wikipedia verifier (only 10% human-validated), risking circularity; it also cannot see aleatoric uncertainty and needs white-box hidden states.

01Methodology

SpanUQ is a ~25M-parameter probe (<0.2% of the LLM) attached to a frozen backbone, reading hidden states from a single forward pass — no sampling, no retrieval, no extra LLM calls at inference (<5% latency overhead). Five components:

  1. Multi-Layer Fusion. Hidden states from three layers in the peak-uncertainty zone (layers 22/24/26 for Qwen3-14B, chosen via a single-layer probing sweep following an inverted-U curve) are mean-fused into H̄.
  2. Token Encoder. H̄ is projected to dp=512 (10× compression) and contextualized by a 2-layer Transformer encoder with sinusoidal positions → shared feature pool Z.
  3. DETR-Style Span Decoder. Span detection as set prediction: N=32 learnable queries attend to Z through a 3-layer decoder; Hungarian matching assigns predictions; overlapping spans are handled naturally (unlike BIO). Boundary regression (L1 + Generalized IoU) gives differentiable continuous boundaries.
  4. Span Feature Enrichment. A differentiable soft boundary mask pool span content from Z via mask-weighted attention, fused with the query through a gated residual — giving the scorer access to what a span says, not just where.
  5. Prediction Heads. Boundary regression, validity classification, and a Mixture of Beta (MoB, J=3) uncertainty head. Beta (not Gaussian) is chosen because uncertainty is bounded in [0,1] and the mixture captures the empirically bimodal distribution.

Training: two-phase — span-detection warmup, then joint (MoB NLL + contrastive ranking + sequence-consistency + DETR auxiliary losses).   Inference: one LLM pass + probe; UCIR feeds Round-1 estimates back through the shared decoder (α=0.7, <15% overhead).   Labels: S=20 sampled responses (T=1.0) are decomposed into atomic claims and verified against Wikipedia; soft label u*k = fraction of samples in which span k is unsupported.

03Strengths

  • Principled SLUE formulation that cleanly fills the granularity gap with a concrete motivating example (Marie Curie / Nobel Prize year).
  • Genuinely lightweight: <0.2% of LLM params and <5% latency, yet 10–20× faster than sampling while more accurate on span metrics.
  • Informative ablations — every component earns its place (MoB is the biggest jump; UCIR lifts ranking; enrichment gate aids short spans).
  • Cross-model breadth: two families, three scales, dense + MoE, with a decomposability analysis showing span subsumes sequence.
  • Honest failure reporting (compositional hallucinations, nuanced/temporal claims, the 4B aggregation bias).
  • Useful negative result: the standard BIO tagger catastrophically fails (F1 0.079) on overlapping spans, motivating set prediction.

04Limitations

  • Epistemic-only. Reads hidden-state patterns; cannot capture uncertainty from the stochastic generation process itself — narrower than sampling-based semantic entropy.
  • White-box requirement. Needs internal hidden states, so it is inapplicable to closed API-only LLMs.
  • Heavy label construction. 20 samples/prompt + LLM-judge decomposition + Wikipedia verification — a one-time but compute-intensive cost tied to a specific judge model that may drift.
  • English factual QA only. Reasoning, math, code, and multilingual settings are untested and explicitly deferred.
  • Label circularity. Soft labels come from an automatic verifier; the probe may reproduce its biases rather than human ground truth; only 10% of test was human-validated.
  • Scale-dependent bias. The Qwen3-4B probe shows a +0.2 systematic overestimate in sequence-level MAE.

05Open Questions for Further Exploration

Q1Does SpanUQ transfer to non-factual uncertainty — multi-step reasoning, math/code, instruction-following — where “verifiable atomic claim” decomposition is far less clean?
Q2Could the probe train on cheaper pseudo-labels (e.g., self-consistency without Wikipedia verification) without losing AUROC, easing the circularity and compute burden?
Q3Does span-to-sequence decomposability (ρseq=0.839) survive distribution shift — a probe trained on Qwen3-14B applied to a new domain or post-finetune checkpoint?
Q4Can SpanUQ adapt to black-box API LLMs by distilling hidden-state probes from a small open surrogate trained to mimic the API?
Q5How does the Mixture-of-Beta head interact with the bimodal labels — would a more continuous labeling scheme expose calibration weaknesses the 0/1-clustered labels hide?
Q6Does feeding SpanUQ’s span scores back into self-refinement actually improve downstream task accuracy vs. sequence-level confidence gating?
