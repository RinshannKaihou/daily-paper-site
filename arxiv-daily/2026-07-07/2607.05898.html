Auditing of Unlearning Algorithms  · arXiv:2607.05898

Sahasrajit Sarmasarkar · Anastasia Koloskova (UZH) · Sanmi Koyejo (Stanford) · 07 Jul 2026

Key Finding

One membership-inference test cleanly separates certified from heuristic unlearning — and exposes methods whose certificates silently fail.

Why This Matters

A reusable falsification tool that quantitatively distinguishes genuinely safe unlearning from heuristic methods in nonconvex deep networks.

Critical Edge

Validated only for δ = 0; the δ > 0 extension is merely sketched, leaving most real (ε, δ) deployments unauditable.

01Methodology

The auditor has black-box access to the training algorithm A and unlearning algorithm U, plus — under a strengthened threat model — the initial weights xo and per-epoch shuffling order π. Across L independent runs it (1) samples a balanced sign vector S deciding which of m candidate batches enter the forget set, (2) trains then unlearns, (3) emits a guess Ŝ with balanced ±1 entries and abstentions, and (4) records the overlap score V counting correct non-zero guesses.

Lemma 4.1 reduces the subset-level guarantee to (2ε, 0)-local differential privacy via the triangle inequality; Theorem 4.2 converts the empirical mean (Chernoff) or median (binomial) of V into a valid, Type-I-controlled (ζ = 0.05) lower bound ε_LB. Two instantiations realize the guess step: a scalable batchwise LiRA-style per-batch Gaussian likelihood-ratio scorer, and a correlation-aware joint sign-vector predictor for enumerable Sm.

04Convex audit (sanity)

On a bounded cubic loss (d=1) with a linear-regression output-perturbation mechanism, ε_LB saturates near 10.19 at 2L = 200k test points and tracks the certified ε closely — confirming the large nonconvex gaps reflect genuine weak unlearning, not auditor slack.

05Strengths

  • Falsifiable hypothesis test yielding a quantity (ε_LB) directly comparable to a claimed (ε, δ) certificate — a real advance over per-example completeness scores.
  • LDP reduction + Theorem 4.2 give a principled, provably valid (Type-I-controlled) lower bound, not an ad-hoc MIA accuracy.
  • Two complementary instantiations (scalable batchwise vs. correlation-aware joint) span both ends of the m regime.
  • The Hessian case study (ε_LB = 142.5 where its certificate silently fails) is a compelling, concrete diagnostic.
  • Honest scope: restricts to δ = 0, states the δ > 0 reduction does not extend cleanly, flags stronger MIAs as future work.

06Limitations

  • δ = 0 only. The empirically validated audit covers pure (ε, 0); the δ > 0 version is stated but not run, leaving most real certificates unauditable.
  • Compute cost undisclosed. Each audit needs Γ + L train-and-unlearn cycles (up to Γ = 50, L = 500) with no wall-clock / GPU-hour figures — likely infeasible for large production models.
  • Small-scale only. TinyNet and a 2-layer LSTM; no ImageNet or modern-LLM evaluation.
  • No CIs on ε_LB; variance across runs used only internally. Weak baseline (pairwise L = 10) forces a zero bound under Clopper–Pearson.
  • Uniform-vs-adversarial gap under-ablated (Hessian 142.5 vs 10); single-scalar output cannot localize leakage to samples, batches, or layers.

07Open Questions

Does the certified-vs-heuristic separation hold at ImageNet / LLM scale, or does the per-batch leakage signal vanish as capacity grows?
Can the δ > 0 reduction (Appendix C) be tightened enough to audit real (ε, δ) deployments?
What is the real compute cost, and can calibration be amortized across algorithms or replaced by simulation-based inference?
Can the audit localize leakage per-layer / per-sample instead of returning a single scalar ε_LB?
Is the uniform-vs-adversarial gap driven by label overlap, feature support sharing, or memorization — and how does it scale with forget-set size?
Would stronger MIA scoring (e.g. RMIA-style) tighten the bounds where certified methods sit at ε_LB ≈ 0, leaving a wide undetermined range?
