Empirical Study / QCDS 2026 / Architecture Robustness

The features that make a model accurate in-distribution
are the ones that erode fastest.

Across three long-range time-indexed domains, stronger inductive biases win today but decay hardest under temporal shift; frozen pretrained encoders stay the steadiest.

Drift Happens: An Empirical Study of Neural Architecture Robustness to Temporal Distribution Shift · Robin Holzinger, Riccardo Colletti (equal contrib., UC Berkeley) · arXiv 2607.05908 · 2026-07-07
temporal-distribution-shift inductive-bias robustness frozen-encoders empirical-study

Key metric

13.9pts

Yearbook accuracy decay · CNN-M

CNN-M/CNN-L reach ~92.8% in-distribution but fall to ~78.4–79.0% on future years (−13.9 / −13.7) — while frozen DINOv3-S decays only 7.9 and the no-prior MLP-S just 7.1.

Why this matters

A reusable temporal-drift-matrix protocol and cross-architecture baseline for sizing continual-learning returns and picking deployment architectures by retraining horizon — not by in-distribution accuracy.

Critical edge

Inductive bias is confounded with capacity — no capacity-matched pairs were run, and smaller variants decay less, so scale could explain part of the effect.

01

Methodology — one protocol, three drift regimes

A single fixed protocol is applied across all three datasets so that any difference comes from architecture, not from splitting or metric choices. Each model becomes a temporal drift matrix M ∈ ℝK×K, where Mij is the performance of a model trained on cumulative data through period i and evaluated on period j. The diagonal is in-distribution; cells below it measure forward generalization to unseen futures.

Three summary statistics collapse each matrix: ID(M) mean in-distribution score, Fut(M) mean future score, and Dec(M) the decay (always oriented so larger = worse). Model families span the inductive-bias spectrum — from-scratch MLP, CNN, ResNet, RNN, Transformer at small/medium/large, plus a large zoo of frozen pretrained encoders (DINOv2/v3, CLIP, SigLIP, BERT, RoBERTa, DeBERTa-v3…) used as a frozen backbone with a linear head.

Controls: one shared split seed per dataset; optimizer fixed per modality (Adam / AdamW) to remove optimizer-as-confound; 5 seeds on Yearbook, 3 on text; final-checkpoint selection. Total: 222 model-seed configs, ~165 4-GPU node-hours.

03

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Unified cross-cut. One protocol across architectures × modalities × drift types — the study's main methodological value.
  • Reusable formalism. Drift-matrix + ID / Fut / Dec is compact, interpretable, and could become standard reporting.
  • Counter-intuitive, consistent. The ID-vs-decay inversion appears in all three domains, with a saliency-map mechanism on Yearbook.
  • Excellent reproducibility. Pinned envs, public code, public W&B artifacts, checksum-verified regeneration.
  • Honest limitations. Flags its own confounds (capacity, frozen RoBERTa, narrow text labels) rather than hiding them.
  • Actionable takeaway. Long retraining horizon → prefer frozen encoders; weigh drift horizon, not ID accuracy alone.

Limitations

  • Capacity confound. No capacity-matched pairs; smaller variants decay less, so scale could explain part of the effect on Yearbook.
  • Shared text representation. All text families differ only in a light head over one frozen RoBERTa embedding — absolute text numbers are caveated.
  • Narrow text labels. 5-point rating, 7 arXiv subjects; text spans (10 / 25 yrs) far shorter than a century of Yearbook; Amazon is a 300k subsample.
  • No significance tests. Small fixed seed sets (5 / 3); fine per-model rankings are noisy — family-level patterns are the honest unit.
  • Descriptive, not mechanistic. Measures how robustness varies without isolating the precise drifting cues; "why" stays open.
  • No adaptive baselines. Continual-learning / group-DRO / TTA not compared (by design) — not a methods benchmark.
04

Open questions for further work

Capacity-matched pairs. Does the inversion survive a wide MLP matched to a CNN's parameter count, or is part of the effect really scale?
Unfrozen text. How do findings change when text models fine-tune or pretrain their own encoder per slice, instead of sharing frozen RoBERTa?
Drift-triggered retraining. Can the matrix pair with cost-aware retraining (Modyn-style) to measure how cheaply decay can be undone under a fixed budget?
Longer spans, richer labels. Do conclusions extend to full Amazon 1996–2023 and full arXiv 176 categories, where language drift is more pronounced?
Mechanistic account. Can decay be predicted from the spectrum of learned features, linking specific inductive biases to the specific cues that drift?
Temporal adaptation. How do continual learning, group-DRO, and test-time adaptation reshape the ID / Fut / Dec ordering under this same protocol?
