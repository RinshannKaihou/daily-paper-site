01

Methodology

Danus wraps the Rethlas worker–verifier core with a main agent and a shared fact graph — a DAG whose nodes are statements with verifier-checked proofs and whose edges record logical dependencies. A mathematician states a problem in natural language; the main agent forms a plan, assigns directions to workers, and each worker tackles one claim at a time (lemma, counterexample, or toy example), iterating submit → verify → repair until the claim is admitted as a node citing its dependencies.

Three to nine workers run in parallel, split between "high" and "xhigh" reasoning effort to diversify the swarm across constructive and refutational routes. Because workers search the graph rather than carry the whole proof, per-worker context stays small even as the proof grows to thousands of facts. Revocation deletes a wrong fact together with all its transitive dependents (needed only once across all experiments).

A two-tier memory (per-worker local log + shared global memory of plans, dead ends, examples, and GPT-5.5-pro consultations) plus periodic main-agent summaries keep each agent's reading bounded while the system's effective horizon grows. The completed manuscript is itself re-submitted to the verifier as a whole, revised section by section until it passes — catching the "seam" errors created when verified facts are compressed into prose.

Strengths & Limitations

+ Strengths

  • Architecturally clean: fact graph as sole source of truth; strict role separation stops the planner from injecting unverified math.
  • Scales test-time compute on two axes — width (parallel workers) and depth (trusted accumulation + summarized memory).
  • Honest reporting of human input per case, including a PDF-misread bug and a flawed literature reference.
  • Writing pipeline that re-verifies the whole manuscript catches compression errors at the seams.
  • Tied to genuine new mathematics with companion arXiv papers; system open-sourced.

− Limitations

  • No systematic ablations on worker count, effort mix, main-agent model, or consultation frequency — justified only anecdotally.
  • Only apples-to-apples baseline is Rethlas on one problem; no comparison with Aletheia, QED, ProofCouncil, or AI co-mathematician.
  • Depends on closed proprietary models (GPT-5.5, Opus 4.8); exact reproduction and long-term stability not guaranteed.
  • Verifier trusts cited references — reference-borne errors propagate until human review (fact-graph reliability is conditional).
  • Human input remains decisive for the hardest steps; one shipped theorem had an incomplete lemma justification.

Open questions for further exploration

How do success rate and fact-graph size scale with worker count (3 → 20) and the "high"/"xhigh" effort mix — is there a diminishing-returns or interference regime?
What is the verifier's precision/recall on planted-error proofs, and how does error rate accumulate along dependency chains of depth 30–60?
Can the fact-graph + stateless-verifier design port to non-math domains — formal verification, code correctness, scientific argument construction?
How would Danus fare head-to-head against Aletheia, QED, ProofCouncil, or the AI co-mathematician on a shared benchmark of open problems?
What fraction of verified facts end up "wasted" (outside the supporting closure) as problems scale, and can re-assignment policy cut the overhead?
Is reliance on a separate frontier model (GPT-5.5-pro) for strategic hints a fundamental bottleneck, or can the main agent's memory substitute for it?
