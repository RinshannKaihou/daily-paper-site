> **一句话总结：** 作者综合 2023–2026 年 27 篇基准/审计论文（覆盖 19 个基准），提炼出 LLM Agent 的六大失败簇（工具调用、规划约束、长程退化、多智能体协调、安全、测量有效性），并指出失败随任务长度非线性累积、子能力不可组合，同时明确单轮工具调用、短程网页导航与窄域代码任务确有可信进步，部分排行榜分数则源于评测方法的修正而非纯粹能力提升。

> **TL;DR:** This Oxford synthesis of 27 agent-evaluation papers (19 benchmarks, 2023–2026) builds a unified six-cluster taxonomy of LLM agent failures — tool invocation, planning/constraint satisfaction, long-horizon degradation, multi-agent coordination, safety, and measurement validity — and shows that on TravelPlanner GPT-4 reaches only 0.6% final-plan success (rising to ~65% only with verifier+critic scaffolding), WebArena success climbs from ~14% to 61.7% (still below the ~78% human level), METR finds task-horizon doubling only every ~7 months, and post-audit SWE-bench scores for one configuration fall from ~12.5% to ~4% after leakage correction, so apparent leaderboard progress blends genuine gains with measurement-method tightening.

## 核心贡献

- 首次将工具使用、规划、长程推理、多智能体协调、安全与测量有效性整合为**统一六大失败簇**分类法（图 1），源自对 27 篇论文、19 个基准的迭代式归类，且与多个独立标注工作（MAST、ToolScan、REST-API 测试框架）相互印证。
- 提出**迭代推导的分类法构建流程**：从 60+ 原始错误类别归并到与"推理-行动管道"各阶段对应的六个簇，并以多个独立标注团队的趋同结果作为分类有效性的佐证。
- 在失败叙述之外**明确记录了真实进步**：单轮工具选择、短程网页导航（WebVoyager 85–90%、WebArena 14%→61.7%）、窄域代码任务（SWE-bench Verified 经校正后仍较 2024 基线高）、以及安全微调（InjecAgent 中安全微调显著降低注入脆弱性）。
- 汇编**分类定量对比表（表 2）**，按失败簇组织各基准代表性数值结果，同时标注同行评审/预印本/行业来源状态。
- 识别出跨簇结构性模式：**失败非线性累积、子能力不可组合、推理-动作接口脆弱、脚手架收益不均、能力与安全部分独立、测量基础设施成熟但滞后**。

## Key Contributions

- First synthesis to integrate tool use, planning, long-horizon reasoning, multi-agent coordination, safety, and measurement validity into a single, unified six-cluster taxonomy of LLM agent limitations, derived iteratively from 27 papers spanning 19 benchmarks (2023–2026).
- An explicit, documented taxonomy-derivation procedure that merges 60+ named error categories into clusters aligned with distinct stages of the agent's reasoning-to-action pipeline, and validates cluster boundaries via convergence across independently conducted annotation efforts (MAST's Cohen's Kappa 0.88; ToolScan's seven-type and REST-API's four-type tool-error taxonomies).
- A balanced account that explicitly documents where capability has genuinely improved (single-turn tool selection, short-horizon web navigation, narrowly scoped coding tasks, targeted safety fine-tuning) rather than presenting only a failure catalogue.
- A categorized comparative table (Table 2) consolidating representative quantitative findings across benchmarks, with peer-reviewed/pre-print/industry status flagged at each citation.
- Identification of recurring structural patterns: non-linear failure compounding with task length, non-composability of sub-skill competence, brittleness at the reasoning-action interface, uneven returns to additional scaffolding, partial independence of capability and safety, and a maturing-but-lagging measurement infrastructure.

## Methodology

The paper is explicitly a **narrative synthesis, not a PRISMA-registered systematic review**; the authors state this directly rather than overstating rigor. The procedure has three documented components:

1. **Scope and search.** They searched for benchmark, taxonomy, and audit papers (2023–2026) across five overlapping areas: (a) tool use/function calling, (b) web navigation/computer-use agents, (c) real-world multi-constraint planning, (d) multi-agent coordination and long-horizon execution, (e) agent safety/security under adversarial or underspecified conditions. 27 papers met inclusion criteria, covering 19 named benchmarks.

2. **Inclusion/exclusion criteria.** A paper was included if it (i) introduced a benchmark with quantitative error analysis (not just aggregate accuracy), (ii) proposed/validated a failure taxonomy grounded in manual trace annotation with a reported inter-annotator agreement statistic, or (iii) specifically investigated benchmark validity (contamination, leakage, test-suite adequacy). Papers were excluded if they reported only leaderboard accuracy without error analysis, were product announcements, or addressed out-of-scope modalities (e.g., embodied robotics).

3. **Taxonomy derivation.** Starting from every distinct error category named across all 27 papers (e.g., ToolScan's seven types, MAST's fourteen modes, TravelPlanner's constraint-violation categories — over 60 initial categories), the authors iteratively grouped conceptually overlapping categories into candidate themes aligned with stages of the reasoning-to-action pipeline, splitting/merging until clusters were conceptually distinct with minimal overlap. The resulting six clusters are corroborated by independent convergence: MAST's three top-level categories map onto clusters 4.4/4.6; ToolScan and the REST-API framework independently overlap cluster 4.1; long-horizon literature independently converges on context accumulation. Figures in Table 2 are reported ranges compiled from cited studies, not independently re-verified by the authors.

## Results & Highlights

The paper compiles (Table 2) rather than generates new experimental results; the following are concrete quantitative claims drawn from the synthesized sources:

- **TravelPlanner (planning/constraint satisfaction):** GPT-4 achieves only a **0.6% final-plan success rate** on multi-day travel itineraries despite far higher single-constraint satisfaction; subsequent reasoning models (o1-preview) raise this to ~**10%**, and reasoning + external formal verifiers/critics raise it to ~**65%** in the best reported configuration (arXiv:2404.11891) — still leaving a substantial unresolved share even with heavy scaffolding.
- **WebArena (end-to-end web tasks):** reported success rates rose from ~**14%** at benchmark introduction (Zhou et al., 2024) to **61.7%** for a specialized enterprise scaffold ~18 months later (Marreed et al., 2025), but the strongest current agents still trail human performance of ~**78%** — a ~16-point absolute gap remains.
- **METR time horizons (long-horizon task completion):** frontier models reach near-**100% success** on tasks taking skilled humans under ~4 minutes but **<10% success** on tasks taking humans more than ~4 hours; the 50%-reliability task length has been **doubling only ~every 7 months** since 2019 (Kwa et al., 2025).
- **SWE-bench measurement validity:** after independent leakage/test-suite correction, one representative SWE-agent configuration's reported success fell from ~**12.5% to ~4%** (Aleithan et al., 2024); adding stronger auto-generated unit tests changed leaderboard rankings in **~25–41%** of cases across SWE-bench Lite/Verified (Yu et al., 2025).
- **Berkeley Function-Calling Leaderboard (tool use):** in the multiple-function-call category, dedicated function-calling modes produced a substantially higher average number of incorrect function-call counts than prompting-based approaches (~**77.5 vs. 21 incorrect calls on average**), showing structured output trades one error profile for another rather than eliminating errors.
- **Long-horizon web degradation (arXiv:2512.04307):** success rates of **40–50%** on short-horizon task variants fall to **<10%** when the same task is embedded in a longer interaction history, even when relevant information is still within the context window.
- **MAST multi-agent failures (Cemri et al., 2025):** across 7 frameworks and 200+ tasks, 14 failure modes group into **~42% specification issues, ~37% inter-agent misalignment, ~21% verification/termination failures** (Cohen's Kappa 0.88); multi-agent gains over single-agent baselines are frequently minimal.
- **ToolEmu safety (Ruan et al., 2023):** even the **safest evaluated agent** exhibited potentially severe-outcome failures in **23.9%** of test cases; 68.8% of identified failures were confirmed valid by human annotators.
- **InjecAgent prompt injection (Zhan et al., 2024):** ReAct-prompted GPT-4 was vulnerable **~24%** of the time, rising to **~47%** with reinforced attack prompts; open-source models without safety fine-tuning exceeded **80%** vulnerability in some configurations.
- **Holistic Agent Leaderboard (Kapoor et al., 2025, accepted ICLR 2026):** evaluated 21,000+ rollouts across 9 models and 9 benchmarks at ~$40,000 cost; GAIA best accuracy ranged **64.8–93%** depending on scaffold/cost, and **higher reasoning effort reduced accuracy in the majority of runs**.
- **LoCoBench-Agent (arXiv:2511.13998):** non-linear efficiency loss of ~**3–5% per 50% length increase** as software-engineering conversation length grows.

Cross-cutting findings: failure compounds **non-linearly** with task length; sub-skill competence **does not reliably compose** into end-to-end success; additional scaffolding (more agents, tools, reasoning effort, or context) does **not uniformly improve** reliability and can reduce it.

## Authors & Credibility

All three authors — **Wael Albayaydh, Rui Zhao, and Ivan Flechais** — are at the University of Oxford (Department of Computer Science). Albayaydh and Flechais bring a peer-reviewed human-centered security background, including a **USENIX Security 2023 Distinguished Paper Award** (Albayaydh & Flechais, 2023) on smart-home bystander privacy, which they appropriately flag as adjacent rather than direct agent evidence. The synthesis itself relies predominantly on peer-reviewed conference papers (ICLR, ICML, ACL) and arXiv pre-prints, with explicit labeling of the two non-peer-reviewed/industry sources (Marreed et al. enterprise scaffold; Skyvern filtered WebVoyager benchmark) used only to corroborate patterns independently established elsewhere. Credibility is strengthened by the authors' explicit refusal to overclaim: they declare the work a narrative (not PRISMA-systematic) review, decline to re-verify others' numbers, and label the peer-review status of every cited figure.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** The contribution is genuinely integrative rather than empirical. The six-cluster taxonomy is novel as a *unified* frame, but each individual cluster restates findings already published by the source papers (e.g., ToolScan's seven error types, MAST's fourteen modes). The convergence argument — that independently developed taxonomies overlap — is the most original methodological move, though it is asserted rather than quantitatively tested (no formal overlap coefficient is reported across taxonomies).
- **Rigor:** Methodologically transparent and appropriately hedged, but the taxonomy boundaries are admittedly subjective ("a different research team... might draw cluster boundaries somewhat differently"). No inter-annotator agreement is reported for the authors' own grouping step — only for the underlying source papers. The headline 27-paper/19-benchmark scope is modest for a "synthesis," and category counts in Table 1 do not sum to 27 (acknowledged), making the corpus composition harder to audit.
- **Reproducibility:** As a narrative review it has no experiments to reproduce, but Table 2's figures are clearly cited to specific arXiv IDs and venues, enabling traceability. However, several "representative results" mix peer-reviewed and pre-print numbers without strict separation, and some pre-prints (e.g., arXiv:2602.18998 "General AgentBench test-time scaling," arXiv:2603.06870 LEAD) are very recent and unvalidated.
- **Significance:** High practical value as a diagnostic checklist and an honest counterweight to leaderboard optimism. The "non-composability of sub-skill competence" and "no-recovery bottleneck" framings are useful conceptual handles even if not new.
- **Red flags and overclaiming risks:**
  - **Weak-baseline confound in the WebArena 14%→61.7% claim:** the 61.7% figure comes from a single non-peer-reviewed industry pre-print (Marreed et al., arXiv:2503.01861) for a "specialized enterprise scaffold" — the authors flag this but still let the number anchor the headline progress narrative. Comparing a specialized enterprise system against the original benchmark's baseline GPT-4 agents conflates model progress with scaffold engineering.
  - **The 0.6%→65% TravelPlanner delta** spans three different system configurations (GPT-4, o1-preview, verifier-augmented), so the "improvement" reflects stacking additional components rather than a single system getting better; presented as a range it slightly overstates linear progress.
  - **Missing ablations:** no analysis of how synthesis conclusions change if any single source paper (especially the contested SWE-bench re-analyses) is excluded; the taxonomy's stability under leave-one-out is not examined.
  - **Data quirk:** the bystander-privacy paragraph (Section 4.5, drawing on the authors' own smart-home work) is a somewhat tangential insertion that does not change the taxonomy and could read as self-citation.
  - **Selective emphasis on SWE-bench correction (12.5%→4%):** this is the most dramatic measurement-correction figure cited and is from a single representative configuration (Aleithan et al.); generalizing it risks overstating how much leaderboard progress is "illusory."
  - The claim that the field is "maturing rather than stagnating" is reasonable but somewhat self-serving as a conclusion, and the paper does not quantify the share of apparent progress attributable to genuine capability vs. measurement tightening.

## Strengths

- Genuinely balanced: pairs every failure cluster with an explicit "where agents have improved" section, resisting both hype and dismissiveness.
- Transparent methodology with explicit scope, inclusion criteria, and a documented iterative taxonomy-derivation procedure.
- Table 2 is a high-value, categorized, traceable reference consolidating otherwise scattered benchmark numbers with peer-review status labeled.
- Honest treatment of measurement validity (Section 4.6) as a first-class concern alongside capability failures.
- Practical implications are concrete: a diagnostic checklist mapping observed failure types to recommended architectural interventions (Section 8.2).
- Cross-cutting patterns (non-linear compounding, non-composability, reasoning-action interface brittleness) are framed as falsifiable candidate properties rather than dogma.

## Limitations

- Narrative, not pre-registered systematic review; some relevant work inevitably omitted; cluster boundaries are subjective and author-dependent.
- Field moves fast — several cited figures had already superseded earlier ones within months and will likely be revised again.
- Authors did not independently reproduce any underlying experiment; Table 2 compiles others' reported numbers without re-verification.
- Coverage is heavily English-language, text- and web-based; failure patterns in other languages, robotics, embodied agents, and voice agents are out of scope.
- The great majority of sources are arXiv pre-prints; only a minority are fully peer-reviewed, though the two non-peer-reviewed industry sources are flagged at each citation.
- The multi-agent and safety clusters draw on only 2 papers each — the thinnest evidence base in the taxonomy.
- No quantitative meta-analysis or formal inter-taxonomy overlap measure; convergence is argued qualitatively.

## Relevance

该综述对关注 **LLM Agent 的工具使用、长程规划与评测有效性** 的研究者具有直接参考价值：它系统梳理了 TravelPlanner、WebArena、SWE-bench、BFCL、MAST、ToolEmu、InjecAgent 等主流基准的失败模式与定量结果，并提供了一个可作为"诊断清单"使用的六簇分类法，对设计更稳健的 Agent 架构、评测协议与安全机制均有指导意义。对于从事多智能体系统或长程任务的研究者，文中"子能力不可组合""no-recovery bottleneck"等结构性论断尤其值得纳入后续工作的问题定义。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Is the **non-composability of sub-skill competence** a fundamental property of autoregressive LLMs, or could explicit constraint-tracking modules / RL objectives defined over composed multi-step success close the gap? (Section 8.4, open question 1.)
- Does the **test-time-scaling non-improvement** finding (arXiv:2602.18998) generalize beyond the general-purpose agent setting, or is it specific to saturated working-memory regimes? What is the accuracy-cost frontier shape?
- How much of apparent non-composability is itself an artifact of **benchmark construction choices** (e.g., binary success criteria that give no partial credit for near-miss solutions) versus genuine model capability limits? (Section 8.4, open question 3.)
- Can agent-safety benchmarks be extended to explicitly measure **bystander / third-party harm** (per the smart-home privacy thread in Section 4.5) rather than only instructing-user harm?
- What is the **leave-one-out stability** of the six-cluster taxonomy — do cluster boundaries hold if the contested SWE-bench re-analyses or the single-industry WebArena 61.7% figure are removed?
- How do these failure patterns compare quantitatively against **human-factors error-recovery literature** in aviation and clinical medicine, where non-recoverable early errors and coordination breakdowns have well-studied analogues? (Section 8.4, open question 4.)
