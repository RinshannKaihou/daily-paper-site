Doomed from the Start: Early Abort of LLM Agent Episodes via a Recall-Controlled Probe Cascade
Ruan, Huang, Zhou, Wei, Wang, Sun

Agent failure is decodable from hidden states at round one — early enough to abort doomed episodes and halve the compute.

Why this matters

Replaces externally trained auditors with the agent's own activations at negligible cost — adding an episode-level recall guarantee no prior monitor provides.

Critical edge

Single environment. All evidence comes from TextCraft with two agents and 800 episodes each; generalization to web, tool-use, or embodied agents is unverified.

01 Methodology a cascade of calibrated gates

Each agent episode runs up to 20 rounds; a cascade of six abort gates sits at rounds 1–6. At every gate, a per-round logistic probe reads the residual-stream hidden state of the final action token (layer 14 for Llama-3.2-3B, layer 20 for Qwen-2.5-7B) and predicts 1−y. The gate threshold is set so its Clopper–Pearson exact-binomial lower confidence bound on per-round survival of successful calibration episodes meets a per-round recall budget tᵢ. The full budget vector (t₁…t₆) is jointly searched over 6⁶=46,656 candidates on a disjoint validation split to maximize savings subject to global recall ≥ ρ*. Probes come from task-grouped cross-fitting, so every episode is scored by a probe that never saw its task.

03 Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Deployable guarantee. Converts a probing observation into a risk-controlled policy with a clear, controllable episode-level recall floor — the quantity that matters for deployment.
  • Honestly non-oversold. Explicit abstention at targets the data cannot back; explicit gap between the heuristic margin and the formal certificate; explicit serving-stack engineering still owed.
  • Statistically careful. Clopper–Pearson calibration, task-grouped cross-fitting, disjoint task-level splits, 20-seed evaluation, and a direct check that the safety margin is correctly sized.
  • Practitioner-facing artifact. The rule-of-three sample-complexity bound tells you up front how much labeled data a given recall promise costs.
  • Amplifies weak scorers. Cascade structure lifts the surface scorer ~3.6× (vs ~1.9× for the probe), so the framework helps even where internal access is unavailable.

Limitations

  • Single environment. TextCraft only; generalization to web navigation, tool use, or embodied agents is unverified (exchangeability can break under shift).
  • Certificate ceiling. Formal guarantee caps near ρ*≈0.974 at this data scale; 0.98/0.99 require ~1.3×/2.6× more successful episodes.
  • Deployment gap. Activations come from an offline teacher-forced re-run; reading live activations from optimized serving stacks (fused attention, paged KV) is not implemented.
  • Tokens, not dollars. Savings are in generated tokens; wall-clock/dollar savings under batching and prefill–decode asymmetry are unmodeled.
  • Weak behavior baseline. The surface scorer is hand-crafted; stronger learned auditors are not compared inside the identical cascade.
04 Open questions for further exploration
  1. Does the early-decodable failure signal generalize to WebArena, ToolBench, ScienceWorld, AlfWorld — and where does the probe-vs-surface timing gap close?
  2. Can the heuristic δ-margin become a formal sequential multiple-testing procedure (fixed-sequence tests, e-values, confidence sequences) so the guarantee holds by theorem?
  3. How do savings translate to wall-clock / dollars under continuous batching and paged KV caches, and is freed capacity reusable for retries?
  4. Would a stronger learned behavior encoder inside the cascade close the probe-vs-surface gap, or do hidden states stay strictly more informative?
  5. Can the cascade extend to mid-trajectory recovery (e.g. hot-swap to a stronger model) rather than pure abort, while preserving the recall guarantee?
  6. How does it behave under distribution shift, and what is the minimum labeled recalibration sample that restores the guarantee?
