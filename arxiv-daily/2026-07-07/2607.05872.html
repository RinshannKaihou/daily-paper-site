Why this matters

Directly audits the untested geometric premise of the entire GaLore family — and converts the verdict into two cheap, usable things: a portable k* diagnostic you run before trusting any low-rank subspace, and a transport + lower-β2 recipe for memory-constrained LLM training. It also reconciles why random-projection variants match SVD GaLore.

Critical edge

The transport arm ships state transport and error feedback bundled together (the full LDAdam update), so the headline 18.73 vs 19.28 gain cannot be cleanly attributed to transport alone.

01

How the noise was isolated from drift

same-step split-batch control

The decisive instrument is a same-step split-batch control: at a single step on a pretrained checkpoint, one batch is split into two disjoint halves and the top-r subspace of each half's gradient is computed independently.

Because nothing changes between the two SVDs except the sample, any disagreement between them is pure estimator noise. Measured with the sign-invariant chordal distance ‖ΔU‖_F = √(2r − 2Σcosθᵢ), that same-step floor (0.725√(2r)) is essentially equal to the across-time rotation over a full refresh interval (0.742√(2r)) — so the per-refresh "rotation" is non-identifiability, not the motion of a stable object.

The cause is structural: the gradient spectrum has no gap at r (adjacent ratio ≈1.005) and a power-law tail (α=1.03–1.35) that shrinks only as N⁻¹ᐟ⁴, so no finite averaging budget can manufacture a gap. Generality is checked across 7 Pythia scales (70M–6.9B), GPT-2-large, Qwen2.5-3B, Llama-3-8B and a ViT-B on CIFAR-10, and reproduced on both NVIDIA L40S and AMD MI210.

03

What holds up, and what to query

+  Strengths

  • The same-step split-batch control is an elegant, decisive design that resolves a long-standing ambiguity in the GaLore literature.
  • Cross-validated by two independent estimators (direct count vs spectral inversion) and reproduced across scales, architectures, domains, and two GPU backends.
  • Exemplary honesty: a pre-registered prediction that fails is reported as failed; five explicit limitations are listed.
  • The energy-capture projector reframing explains why random-projection variants match SVD GaLore.
  • The k* probe is cheap, portable, and runnable before trusting any low-rank subspace.

!  Limitations

  • Transport is not separated from error feedback — both ship in the LDAdam update, blurring the clean attribution.
  • The canonical-GaLore β2 benefit is training-length dependent (40K only) and hardware-sensitive.
  • Basis-averaging sweep is one seed per cell; the closed-form N⁻¹ᐟ⁴ fit is reported only at 160M.
  • Perplexity is single-recipe per table, on WikiText/Pythia only; magnitudes shift between recipes.
  • Scope excludes LoRA and other low-rank weight parameterizations.
04

Open questions for follow-up

Q1

Would separating state transport from error feedback preserve the 18.73-vs-19.28 edge, or does error feedback carry most of the gain?

Q2

Does k* predict the optimal rank per layer — can adaptive rank (à la Q-GaLore) be grounded in measured k* instead of heuristics?

Q3

What generative model (power-law + spike covariance) reproduces both the spectrum and the overlap-vs-N curve the spiked prediction missed?

Q4

Does non-identifiability extend to fine-tuning, and to tasks with genuinely tiny subspaces (classification, per Gur-Ari et al.)?

Q5

Can a partial-rotation model (stable k*-dim core + Haar block) tighten the (r−k*)/2 bound into a quantitative predictor of the carry-vs-transport gap?

DIY

Before trusting any top-r subspace: split a batch, SVD each half, count agreeing directions. If r > k*, the excess is noise — transport the state, don't hunt a better tracker.
