> **一句话总结：** HARVEY 颠覆了以往"学习干净参考模型识别良性样本"的范式，转而迭代地学习一个强后门化的参考模型作为"毒样本预言机"，配合更优的 RCE 损失进行自适应数据集划分，在 CIFAR10/Tiny-ImageNet/GTSRB 上把攻击成功率压到 2% 以下，同时几乎不损失自然精度。

> **TL;DR:** HARVEY is a training-time backdoor defense that flips the conventional paradigm — instead of learning a clean reference model to spot benign samples (DBD, D-ST) it iteratively learns a strongly *backdoored* reference model as an oracle for *poisonous* samples, split via the RCE term of SCE loss. Across CIFAR10, GTSRB, and Tiny-ImageNet it cuts the attack success rate to under 2% in the worst case (e.g., Tiny-ImageNet ResNet34 average ASR 0.11% vs DBD's 100% failure and D-ST's 20.69% average ASR) while keeping natural accuracy essentially on par with the no-defense model.

## 核心贡献

- **范式反转——学习后门而非学习良性任务。** 作者指出"学一个强后门化的参考模型"远比"在良性数据上学参考模型"容易，因为后门本就比良性特征更易被模型习得。由此，用后门模型作为"毒样本预言机"能比以往任何良性参考模型更精准地分离毒样本与良性样本。
- **理论分析：RCE 损失才是划分的关键。** 论文证明对称交叉熵（SCE）中真正负责区分毒/良性样本的是其反向交叉熵（RCE）分量，CE 项反而会让划分变差。HARVEY 只用 RCE，且因其输出范围固定为 [0, C]，可用 C/2 作为无需调参的天然阈值，比 SCE 更稳定、更实用。
- **四阶段自适应划分流程。** ❶ 初始化（naive 训练后按 RCE 半分）→ ❷ 学习后门（用 LGA loss 学毒样本、用 unlearning 抑制良性样本，迭代收敛到强后门参考模型）→ ❸ Meta-splitting（用第一轮参考模型把后门目标类里的良性样本从毒样本集中抠出）→ ❹ 在清洗后的集合上常规监督训练。全程无需干净参考集。
- **跨攻击/架构/数据集的全面评估。** 在 BadNets、Trojan、Blend、CLB、IAB、WaNet 六种攻击，WRN16-1/ResNet18/ResNet34 以及 VGG11/MobileNetV2/DenseNet121 三种额外架构，CIFAR10/GTSRB/Tiny-ImageNet 三个数据集上系统对比 ABL、DBD、D-ST、CBD，HARVEY 是唯一在所有设置下都能稳定抑制后门且保持自然精度的方法。
- **鲁棒性论证。** 提供了自适应"复制良性样本"攻击（Section E）、跨目标标签（Section D.2）、跨中毒率（1%/5%/10%/20%，Section D.4）、干净数据退化测试（Section D.5）及详尽消融，证明方法不依赖超参魔法。

## Key Contributions

- **A paradigm shift on reference models.** Prior splitting defenses (DBD, ASD/Gao 2023) train a reference model on benign features to identify clean samples; HARVEY trains a reference model that deliberately overfits the *backdoor* and uses it as an oracle for *poisonous* samples. This is justified by the long-standing observation that a network learns the backdoor far more easily than the benign task, so the "poisonous side" is the easier oracle to learn.
- **A loss-theoretic finding: RCE suffices and SCE is suboptimal.** By rewriting SCE in KL terms, the authors show the RCE term alone yields a fixed-range loss in [0, C] where easy-to-learn (poisonous) samples collapse near 0 and hard samples sit near C. The CE term in SCE actually blurs this separation. HARVEY therefore splits with pure RCE at a parameter-free C/2 threshold, which they also show retrofits to DBD (Table 6) but cannot fix DBD's fundamental benign-feature-learning difficulty.
- **A four-stage adaptive splitting pipeline** (Initialization → Learn the Backdoor → Meta-splitting → Final training) that needs no clean reference set, no fixed split ratio, and no per-attack hyperparameter tuning (γ=0.01, λ=0.01, n=20 are fixed across all attacks).
- **Comprehensive, architecture- and dataset-wide evaluation** showing HARVEY is the only defense among ABL, DBD, D-ST, CBD that resists all six attacks on all settings, while preserving natural accuracy on average and in the worst case.

## Methodology

HARVEY is a training-time (anti-backdoor learning) defense that does **not** assume access to a clean reference dataset. The threat model is pure data poisoning: the adversary controls only the training data, the defender trains blindly.

**Foundational analysis (Section 2).** The authors decompose the Symmetric Cross-Entropy used by DBD/ASD into its CE and RCE (Reverse Cross-Entropy) components and rewrite RCE as `L_RCE = C·(1 − p(y|x))` with `C = −log ε`. Because the RCE loss has a fixed output range `[0, C]`, samples the model finds easy (poisonous samples, learned earlier per Li et al. 2021a) collapse to near 0 while hard benign samples sit near C. This yields a parameter-free split threshold of C/2 ≈ 4.605 (ε = 1e-5). Empirically (Fig. 1) they show that on a naive model the 50% lowest-RCE subset is already enriched in poison, that retraining on this subset further concentrates poison, and that additionally *unlearning* the high-RCE half pushes the poisoning rate of the low-loss subset even higher — motivating the learn+unlearn strategy.

**Stage ❶ Initialization.** Train θ* for T_init=20 epochs on the full poisoned set D̃, take the 50% lowest-RCE samples as the initial poisonous subset S⁽¹⁾ = D_bad⁽⁰⁾, the rest as D_bng⁽⁰⁾.

**Stage ❷ Learning the backdoor (the core).** Discard θ* and train a fresh reference model θ_ref over n=20 rounds. Each round: (a) *enhance* the backdoor by training θ_ref on S⁽ⁱ⁾ for T_poi=10 epochs with the Local Gradient Ascent (LGA) loss `sign(L_CE) − γ·L_CE` (γ=0.01) to lock onto poison without overfitting the few benign stragglers; (b) *unlearn* the benign set D_bng⁽ⁱ⁻¹⁾ for one epoch by maximizing CE only on samples still predicted correctly, stabilized by λ=0.01 and an indicator; (c) *split* D̃ with the updated θ_ref at threshold C/2 into D_bad⁽ⁱ⁾ (poisonous) and D_bng⁽ⁱ⁾ (benign); (d) form S⁽ⁱ⁺¹⁾ from the 50% lowest-RCE samples of D_bad⁽ⁱ⁾, re-adding low-RCE samples from D̃ so every class keeps ≥1% of its original count to prevent class bias. Over rounds, θ_ref becomes increasingly backdoor-specific (Fig. 3 shows recall stabilizing near 100% by round 10).

**Stage ❸ Meta-splitting.** Because the converged θ_ref⁽ⁿ⁾ is so backdoor-biased that benign samples of the *target* class also have low RCE loss (they co-trigger the backdoor), directly training on D_bng⁽ⁿ⁾ hurts natural accuracy (Fig. 4). HARVEY uses the *first-round* reference θ_ref⁽¹⁾ — which still has a notion of the benign task and yields two separable latent clusters — to further split D_bad⁽ⁿ⁾ into A (benign, high RCE) and B (poisonous, low RCE).

**Stage ❹ Final training.** Combine D_bng⁽ⁿ⁾ ∪ A as the final clean set D_bng and run standard supervised CE training (SGD, step-scheduled LR, full data augmentation) for T_cln=100 epochs to produce a backdoor-free model.

The full algorithm is given as Algorithm 1; all augmentation is disabled in Stages ❶–❸ to stabilize backdoor learning and re-enabled in Stage ❹.

## Results & Highlights

- **CIFAR10 / ResNet18 (Table 2):** HARVEY reaches average ASR **0.85%** and worst-case ASR **1.43%** (WaNet) at ACC **93.56%** (vs no-defense 93.73%). The next-best defender on ACC is D-ST at 91.62% but with 25.46% average ASR; ABL keeps ASR at 11.23% but drops ACC to 88.49%; CBD reaches 16.41% ASR at 90.09% ACC. DBD collapses to 73.04% average ASR (fails on Trojan 99.99%, Blend 99.91%, IAB 83.23%).
- **CIFAR10 / WRN16-1 (Table 2):** HARVEY average ASR **1.19%**, worst-case **1.83%** (Trojan), ACC **88.91%** — the highest ACC of any defender — while CBD catastrophically fails on Blend (ASR 85.12%) and D-ST fails on BadNets/Trojan/IAB/WaNet (all ASR > 98%).
- **Tiny-ImageNet / ResNet34 (Table 2):** HARVEY average ASR **0.11%**, worst-case **0.48%** (WaNet), ACC **57.08%** (on par with no-defense 57.02%). DBD fails completely (ASR 100% on *every* attack, average 100%), D-ST fails on Blend (ASR 97.63%), ABL fails on Blend (ASR 99.99%).
- **Dataset splitting quality (Table 3, Tiny-ImageNet):** HARVEY achieves average F1 **97.63%** and ρ_bng **0.02%** (poison left in the benign set), vs DBD's F1 **0.48%** / ρ_bng **16.59%** and D-ST's F1 **75.57%** / ρ_bng **1.93%**. On CIFAR10/WRN16-1 HARVEY's F1 of 79.82% is 49 percentage points above the best related work.
- **GTSRB (Table 8):** HARVEY average ASR **0.18%** (WRN16-1) and **0.00%** (ResNet18) while every baseline fails on at least one attack (e.g., CBD ASR 97.19% on Blend/WRN16-1, DBD ASR 100% on IAB/WRN16-1, ABL ASR 99.92% on WaNet/ResNet18).
- **Cross-architecture (Table 10):** On CIFAR10 with VGG11, MobileNetV2, DenseNet121, HARVEY keeps ASR ≤ 2.15% (worst case WaNet/VGG11) at near-no-defense accuracy.
- **Time cost (Table 5):** HARVEY takes 0.95h on CIFAR10 and 5.68h on Tiny-ImageNet vs DBD's 19.25h / 114.13h — roughly 20× faster than DBD while slightly slower than naive (0.52h / 3.19h).
- **Adaptive duplicating attack (Table 14):** Even when the adversary floods the dataset with duplicated benign samples to bait HARVEY into isolating benign data, ASR stays ≤ 2.57% (WaNet) across both target-label strategies, with F1 still in the 52–72% range.
- **Clean-data sanity check (Table 12):** On an unpoisoned dataset HARVEY retains accuracy within ~1% of naive training (e.g., CIFAR10/ResNet18 93.79% vs 93.87%), so deploying it prophylactically costs almost nothing.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty — genuine and well-motivated.** The "learn the backdoor, not the benign task, as the oracle" inversion is a real conceptual contribution, not an incremental tweak; the RCE-only analysis with the parameter-free C/2 threshold is a clean theoretical nugget that also explains *why* SCE-based defenses underperform. The meta-splitting trick for target-class benign samples is a non-obvious, paper-specific fix supported by t-SNE evidence (Fig. 6).
- **Rigor — strong.** Claims are backed by both KL-divergence derivations (Section 2.1) and large-scale empirical tables. The splitting-quality tables (Tables 3, 9) report precision/recall/F1/ρ_bng separately from the headline ACC/ASR, so one can audit *why* the defense works rather than just trusting the end-to-end number.
- **Baselines — mostly fair, with one caveat.** ABL, DBD, D-ST, CBD are reproduced with published hyperparameters; for sensitive ones (ABL's γ, CBD's phase-1 epochs) the authors grid-search and report the *best* baseline result, which favors the baselines rather than HARVEY. However, all five defenses are from 2021–2023; newer training-time defenses (e.g., 2024 anti-backdoor methods) are not discussed, leaving a possible "moving-target" gap. Also absent is comparison with model-based defenses like Februus or Fine-Pruning — defensible given the different threat model, but unstated.
- **Missing ablations.** The Stage ❷ iteration count n=20 is fixed everywhere but never ablated (Fig. 8 ablates γ, λ, T_poi, and the meta-splitting reference round, but not n). The class-replenishment "≥1%" heuristic and the 50% sub-sampling ratio in Stages ❶/❷ are also never varied. A sensitivity analysis on n would strengthen the claim that convergence is robust.
- **Data quirk worth flagging.** CLB uses very different poisoning rates (50% of the target class on small datasets, 100% on Tiny-ImageNet), so the CLB rows are not directly comparable to the 10%-poison rows for other attacks; the paper reports them side by side without normalizing, which somewhat flatters the CLB headline numbers for every defense.
- **Generalization scope.** Evaluation is image-classification only (CIFAR10, GTSRB, Tiny-ImageNet). No NLP, no real-world supply-chain-scale datasets, and all triggers are single-target, single-trigger. The adaptive attack considered (sample duplication) is a relatively weak adversary — a trigger-engineering or optimization-based adaptive attack is not tested.
- **No major red flags** on reproducibility (public code, fixed hyperparameters across attacks, hardware specified) or on statistical reporting, though no error bars / multiple seeds are reported — a systematic weakness shared with all baselines.

## Strengths

- Conceptually clean, well-motivated paradigm inversion backed by both theory and intuition ("the backdoor is the easier task, so learn it as the oracle").
- Parameter-free RCE-based splitting (fixed C/2 threshold) removes a major source of baseline fragility.
- Near-perfect defensive results *across the board* — it is the only method in the comparison that never fails on any attack/architecture/dataset combination, while keeping natural accuracy essentially at the no-defense level.
- Substantial speed-up over DBD (~20×) and competitive with naive training.
- Honest, thorough ablations including an adaptive attack, clean-data degradation, and cross-poisoning-rate robustness — the kind of evaluation reviewers ask for.

## Limitations

- Evaluated only on image classification and single-target/single-trigger backdoors; no NLP, no multi-trigger or all-to-all attacks.
- No statistical significance reporting (single-seed results); variance across target labels (Fig. 9) hints run-to-run noise exists but is not quantified for the main tables.
- Sensitive to the unlearning weight λ ≥ 0.015 (Fig. 8) and to the LGA weight γ > 0.015 for dynamic-trigger attacks (IAB, WaNet) — the operating window is narrow even if the chosen defaults work.
- At 20% poisoning rate (Table 11) natural accuracy degrades noticeably (e.g., Blend 89.64%), so the method is not immune to high poison fractions.
- The adaptive attack tested (benign-sample duplication) is relatively simple; stronger adaptive adversaries (e.g., trigger loss-matching, poison that mimics benign RCE signatures) are left to future work.
- Stage ❷ iteration count n=20 and the 50%/1% sub-sampling heuristics are not ablated.

## Relevance

高度相关于"可信机器学习/对抗性机器学习"研究方向：HARVEY 直击数据投毒引发的神经后门这一供应链安全核心问题，其"学习后门作为预言机"的范式反转与 RCE 划分分析对后门检测、数据清洗、鲁棒训练等子领域都有方法论启发，可作为 anti-backdoor learning 的代表性新基线。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does HARVEY extend to all-to-all or multi-trigger backdoors, where the "backdoor task" is no longer a single low-dimensional shortcut — does the oracle still converge cleanly?
- How does HARVEY behave under stronger adaptive attacks, e.g., an adversary who explicitly minimizes the RCE-loss gap between poisonous and benign samples during poisoning?
- Can the RCE-only, C/2-threshold insight be ported to *model-based* or *runtime* defenses (trigger inversion, STRIP-style filtering) that currently rely on CE/SCE?
- What is the minimum poisoning rate at which HARVEY's oracle fails to bootstrap (Stage ❶ needs the lowest-RCE half to be poison-enriched — is there a ρ below which this assumption breaks)?
- Does the paradigm transfer to NLP / tabular modalities where the "easy-to-learn backdoor" assumption may not hold?
