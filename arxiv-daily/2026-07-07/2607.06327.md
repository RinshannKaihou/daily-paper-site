> **一句话总结：** 首个跨22种语言的大规模不确定性估计（UE）评测表明：让模型用英语进行推理可显著提升低资源语言的UE质量，且UE方法的选择应随模型规模而变——小模型宜用基于概率的open-box方法，大模型则由自述置信度的Self Verbalized方法胜出。

> **TL;DR:** This paper presents the first large-scale multilingual evaluation of LLM uncertainty estimation (UE), comparing 9 methods across 22 languages on Global-MMLU and MMLU-ProX with 9 models (Gemma3, Qwen3, Claude Sonnet 4.5). The best method overall is Self Verbalized (avg AUROC 0.72), and switching reasoning to English closes the UE gap for low-resource languages (e.g., Yoruba AUROC 0.58→0.68, a +0.10 delta), while model scale matters: Token Entropy wins below ~30B but Self Verbalized takes over at 235B (0.65→0.77 AUROC).

## 核心贡献

- 提出标签锚定（label-grounded）的多语言UE评测框架：将UE应用于模型的CoT推理文本（平均150词），用MCQA选项的精确匹配作为正确性标签，避免LLM-as-judge、ROUGE/BERTScore等代理指标在多语言环境下引入噪声与偏置。
- 构建迄今最大规模的多语言UE基准：覆盖22种语言（高/中/低资源齐全，约占全球母语使用者80%）、9种UE方法（open-box与closed-box）、9个模型（Gemma3 0.27B–27B、Qwen3 4B–235B-A22B、Claude Sonnet 4.5），在Global-MMLU与MMLU-ProX上形成44,000个语言实例。
- 系统回答5个研究问题（方法鲁棒性、规模效应、推理语言、跨语言答案、阈值校准），并给出可部署的实操指南：何时用open-box概率法、何时用self-verbalized、如何选阈值。
- 揭示"生成语言比问题语言更重要"这一被既往工作忽视的结论，并指出采样一致性方法（Semantic Entropy、Eccentricity）在低资源语言上接近随机失效。

## Key Contributions

- A **label-grounded multilingual UE framework** that estimates uncertainty from the model's chain-of-thought reasoning (~150 words on average) while using exact-match MCQA labels as ground truth, sidestepping LLM-as-judge and embedding-based correctness proxies that Santilli et al. (2025) showed misrank UE methods.
- The **largest multilingual UE benchmark to date**: 9 UE methods, 9 models (5 Gemma3 from 0.27B–27B, 3 Qwen3 from 4B–235B-A22B including MoE, plus Claude Sonnet 4.5), 22 languages covering high/mid/low-resource settings (~80% of native speakers), yielding 44,000 language-specific instances from Global-MMLU and MMLU-ProX.
- A **comparative analysis organized around 5 research questions** (method robustness, model-scale effects, reasoning-language effects, cross-lingual answer settings, threshold calibration for selective prediction), producing actionable deployment guidance.
- The finding that **generation language matters more than question language**, an overlooked factor that reframes the reliability bottleneck as a generation (not comprehension) problem for low-resource languages.

## Methodology

The authors design an evaluation pipeline (Figure 1) whose central design choice is to apply UE methods to the model's *reasoning trace*, not the MCQA label itself, while keeping correctness grounded in MCQA exact match. This is motivated by the fact that (a) short MCQA labels are too short to derive meaningful uncertainty from, and (b) using LLM-as-judge / ROUGE / BERTScore to assess long-form correctness introduces language-dependent noise that misranks UE methods (Santilli et al., 2025). Prompts ask the model to produce a step-by-step explanation followed by a single-letter guess (Appendix D).

**Datasets:** Two human-curated, *parallel* MCQA datasets — Global-MMLU (Singh et al., 2025; 4 options, 6 categories) and MMLU-ProX (Xuan et al., 2025; 10 options, 14 categories). They sample 100 questions per category → 600 + 1,400 = 2,000 unique questions × 22 languages = 44,000 instances. The authors explicitly justify choosing MCQA over open-ended QA (TriviaQA/NQ/MKQA), since open-ended answers are 1–2 word factoids and require the very proxies they want to avoid (Appendix B).

**Models:** Claude Sonnet 4.5 (closed), Gemma3 (0.27B, 1B, 4B, 12B, 27B), Qwen3 (4B, 30B-A3B, 235B-A22B). Qwen3-235B-A22B was quantized to 4-bit for hardware reasons; a control study on Qwen3-30B found no statistically significant UE impact at 8-bit/4-bit (Appendix G).

**UE methods (9 total):**
- Open-box: Token Entropy, Max Prob (Maximum-Sequence-Probability), Self Certainty.
- Closed-box verbalized: Self Verbalized (numeric probability), qualitative verbalization.
- Closed-box consistency/sampling: Lexical Similarity (ROUGE-L), Semantic Entropy (Jaccard clustering), and graph-based dispersion (EigValLaplacian, DegMat, Eccentricity).
- Implementation via LM-Polygraph (Fadeeva et al., 2023).

**Metric:** AUROC, standard in the UE literature. Cross-lingual setting (RQ4) constructs per-instance answer options each drawn from a different language using the parallel structure of the datasets. Threshold strategies for selective prediction (RQ5): tEN (English-only calibration), tGLOBAL (pooled multilingual), tLANG (language-specific), with Oracle as the ceiling.

## Results & Highlights

- **RQ1 — Best method:** Across Gemma3-27B and Qwen3-235B-A22B over 22 languages, *Self Verbalized* is the best UE method with average AUROC 0.72, followed by Token Entropy at 0.71 and Self Certainty at 0.70; Max Prob averages 0.68 but collapses on low-resource languages (0.56 AUROC on Yoruba, 0.57 on Nepali).
- **RQ1 — Sampling methods fail on low-resource languages:** Eccentricity drops to 0.49 and Semantic Entropy to 0.50 AUROC on Yoruba (near-random 0.50 baseline). The cause is diagnosed via pairwise Jaccard diversity of reasoning: the correct-vs-incorrect diversity gap that powers these methods is 0.08–0.11 for high-resource languages (English 0.72 vs 0.63, German 0.73 vs 0.65, Spanish 0.74 vs 0.63, French 0.69 vs 0.64, p < 1e-6) but collapses to ≤0.03 for Yoruba (0.93 vs 0.93), Swahili (0.93 vs 0.92), Bengali (0.83 vs 0.80).
- **RQ2 — Scale matters for some methods:** Self Verbalized improves substantially with scale, 0.65 → 0.66 → 0.77 AUROC across Qwen3 4B/30B/235B, becoming the best method only at 235B (statistically significant vs 30B). Token Entropy is stable across scales and beats Self Verbalized below ~30B. Models below 1B parameters (Gemma3-0.27B, Gemma3-1B, Qwen3-0.6B) operate at the 0.50 random AUROC baseline — UE fails entirely on very small models.
- **RQ3 — English reasoning closes the gap:** Switching only the *reasoning* language to English (question stays in target language) improves average AUROC from 0.68 to 0.72. The gain is concentrated in low-resource languages: Yoruba 0.58 → 0.68, Swahili 0.58 → 0.64, Nepali 0.66 → 0.71 (all with non-overlapping 95% CIs), bringing them to the level of Germanic/Romance languages (0.71–0.73), which themselves do not change. Task accuracy also rises by +0.037 absolute (+5.4% relative) on average, with the largest gains in Yoruba (+17.6%), Nepali (+10.7%), Swahili (+10.5%).
- **RQ4 — Cross-lingual robustness:** When each answer option is in a different language from the question, task accuracy drops 4–6 percentage points but UE AUROC stays stable (0.68 vs 0.67 with overlapping CIs), showing UE does not rely on lexical match between reasoning and options.
- **RQ5 — Threshold selection for selective prediction (Self Verbalized, Claude Sonnet 4.5):** No filtering baseline = 86.5% accuracy; tEN = 91.2% accuracy with 43% relative error reduction (FP 7.7%, FN 6.6%); tGLOBAL = 92.4% with 56% error reduction; tLANG = 93.0% with 60% error reduction and 8.1% error detection, nearly matching Oracle's 62% reduction at 97.2% accuracy.

## Authors & Credibility

Authors are from INSAIT (Sofia) and Amazon: Andrea Alfarano (work done during an Amazon internship), Andrea Bacciu (equal contribution with Alfarano), Saab Mansour, Amin Mantrach, and Marcello Federico. Several authors are affiliated with the group that produced Monte Carlo Temperature (Cecere et al., 2025), a relevant UE sampling strategy, indicating genuine domain expertise in uncertainty quantification for LLMs. The evaluation builds directly on the well-cited LM-Polygraph framework (Fadeeva et al., 2023), uses the state-of-the-art closed model of the time (Claude Sonnet 4.5), and reports 95% confidence intervals throughout — a level of statistical hygiene (non-overlapping CIs are explicitly invoked to justify claims) that exceeds the norm for UE papers. Experiments ran on a documented Amazon EC2 g6e.48xlarge instance (8× NVIDIA L40S, 1.5 TiB RAM).

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Genuinely novel as a *multilingual* study. The only prior dedicated multilingual UE benchmark (Xue et al., 2025, MlingConf) covered 3 methods on 5 languages with machine-translated (non-post-edited) data and a 1-word median answer length; this work's jump to 22 languages, 9 methods, 9 models, human-curated parallel data, and ~150-word reasoning is a clear and substantial advance. The "label-grounded, apply-UE-to-reasoning" framing (following Podolak & Verma, 2025) is a sound methodological contribution.
- **Rigor:** Strong. Parallel human-curated datasets rule out translation-quality confounds; label-position analysis (Appendix J, Tables 3–4) explicitly excludes positional bias; 95% CIs are reported and used; a quantization control (Appendix G) and ECE analysis (Appendix K) are provided. Cross-lingual and English-reasoning conditions are carefully decoupled (reasoning language vs question language).
- **Weak baselines / coverage caveats:** The method set is restricted to inference-time methods — *no training-based* UE methods (e.g., Semantic Entropy Probes, Kossen et al., 2024, which the authors themselves cite) are evaluated, which is a real gap given that such methods are competitive on English. Claude Sonnet 4.5 is included but excluded from the open- vs closed-box head-to-head (RQ1) "to ensure a fair comparison" — reasonable, but it means the headline AUROC numbers come only from two open-weights models.
- **Missing ablations:** The explanation for *why* English reasoning helps is inferential (generation is the bottleneck, not comprehension), not mechanistically ablated — e.g., there is no test of whether intermediate reasoning *quality* (faithfulness, coherence) mediates the UE signal. The observation that small Qwen3 variants are distilled from the 235B teacher is noted as a confound for the scaling story but not controlled for.
- **Reproducibility:** Likely high. Datasets (Global-MMLU, MMLU-ProX) are public; the UE framework is LM-Polygraph (open source); prompts are listed in Appendix D; hardware is documented; the authors state they release their evaluation framework. The one opacity point is Claude Sonnet 4.5 as a closed API model.
- **Significance:** Practically significant. The English-reasoning prescription and the scale-aware method selection rule are immediately deployable, and the threshold-selection table (RQ5) gives concrete coverage/error trade-offs for abstention systems.
- **Red flags:** The cross-lingual accuracy drop in RQ4 (4–6 pp) is mentioned but not decomposed by language family — given the headline multilingual framing, this is a surprising omission. The "+17.6% on Yoruba" accuracy gain is an absolute-effect headline on a language where baselines are weak, and could read as slightly cherry-picked.

## Strengths

- Scale and coverage (22 languages, 9 models, 9 methods, 44,000 instances) substantially exceed all prior multilingual UE work.
- Methodologically clean: avoids the LLM-as-judge / n-gram-overlap correctness proxies that Santilli et al. (2025) showed inflate UE numbers, by anchoring correctness to MCQA exact match and applying UE to the reasoning trace instead.
- Statistically disciplined: 95% CIs reported per cell; claims of significance explicitly rest on non-overlapping intervals.
- The English-reasoning finding is both scientifically interesting (comprehension vs generation asymmetry) and operationally useful.
- Provides a concrete, decision-relevant threshold-selection table mapping calibration strategies to coverage/error trade-offs.

## Limitations

- Restricted to two MCQA datasets; open-ended generation with reliable correctness assessment is explicitly deferred to future work.
- Only 9 inference-time UE methods are evaluated — training-based probes are excluded.
- Three languages (Swahili, Yoruba, Telugu) remain the weakest even after English reasoning; fully closing this gap is left open.
- The scaling analysis is partially confounded by non-uniform training pipelines within a model family (smaller Qwen3 variants are distilled from the 235B teacher).
- Self Verbalized depends on the model emitting a valid numeric probability; the fallback (u = 1) on parse failure is a silent edge case whose frequency is not reported.
- Cross-lingual accuracy degradation (RQ4) is not decomposed by language family.

## Relevance

与多语言LLM的可信部署、不确定性/幻觉检测以及推理能力评测高度相关：其"UE应用于推理trace + 用英语推理缓解低资源语言可靠性瓶颈"的结论，直接可用于跨语言RAG与 abstention 系统的设计，也为评测多语言模型何时该"拒绝作答"提供了基准与方法选型依据。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the English-reasoning benefit persist when reasoning is later *post-edited* back into the target language for end-user interpretability, or does translation reintroduce uncertainty noise?
- How do training-based UE methods (e.g., Semantic Entropy Probes) behave on this 22-language benchmark, and do they avoid the low-resource collapse of sampling-based methods?
- Is the small-model failure (<1B at 0.50 AUROC) a fundamental limit of UE signals in weak models, or would ensemble/few-shot prompting recover usable uncertainty?
- Can the comprehension-vs-generation asymmetry be confirmed with a mechanistic probe (e.g., hidden-state analysis) rather than a behavioral argument?
- How do the threshold-selection strategies transfer to open-ended generation, where correctness labels are unavailable and the trade-off space (coverage, deferral cost) differs?
