01 / METHOD

A five-step interpretability pipeline

The authors target two open-loop, end-to-end driving models that score trajectory proposals with the Predictive Driver Model (PDM) score: GTRS (one head per subscore — progress, collision, drivable-area, direction, traffic-light, TTC) and iPAD (aggregate only). They insert a Sparse Autoencoder into the 256-dim per-trajectory latent sitting directly before the scoring head, then trace concepts to outputs.

  1. Inject. Insert the SAE into a dense, score-feeding latent — one 1-D vector per trajectory proposal.
  2. Train. L2 reconstruction + top-k sparsity + a reanimation term against dead neurons. TopK with 256 neurons, k=64 wins.
  3. Name concepts. Activation/attribution maximization, neuron clustering, input perturbation, and Concept Relevance Propagation (CRP).
  4. Link to heads. Attribution patching → activation patching → ACDC pruning → Gini specificity → a sparse neuron→PDM-head edge set.
  5. Edit. Zero-ablate the “malicious” neurons globally. No retraining required.

03 / VERDICT

Strengths & limitations, side by side

Strengths

  • First concept-level SAE interpretability framework built for trajectory-scoring models — per-proposal, not scene saliency.
  • Unusually careful SAE eval: two new driving-specific metrics (Ego Correlation, Ego Probing) with an explicit scenario-ID memorization penalty.
  • Mechanistically rich: SAEs + CRP + four-stage ACDC actually traces concepts to individual PDM heads.
  • Honest diagnostics — the iPAD backward-camera no-op finding and the penalty-head inversion discussion are exactly what justifies the field.
  • A clean, reproducible-in-principle test-time intervention beating even the no-SAE baseline.

Limitations

  • NAVSIM open-loop only — no closed-loop, pseudo-sim, or real-world validation of the very intervention it proposes.
  • Headline gain depends on hand-picking 3 neurons; no sensitivity over choice, SAE seed, or checkpoint.
  • Injecting the SAE itself costs EPDMS (0.496–0.497 vs 0.524); net gain over original is modest and shown only for GTRS.
  • NAVSIM’s scene reuse may inflate TopK concepts’ apparent monosemanticity, just as it sinks archetypal SAEs.
  • Penalty-head inversion (high score = no violation) undermines attribution for exactly the safety-critical heads.
  • No code release; key thresholds (λr, τ, q) and neuron-selection procedure under-specified.
04 / OPEN

Questions worth asking next

  • Does the +0.0963 gain survive re-training across seeds, latent sizes, and SAE families — or is it specific to this TopK checkpoint?

    The result rests on a single SAE. Without a sweep over Matryoshka/Archetypal families and multiple seeds, the clean three-neuron story could be a checkpoint artifact rather than a robust circuit.

  • Does “more conservative” open-loop behavior still avoid collisions in closed-loop, reactive simulation (CARLA / NuPlan)?

    The authors themselves cite the sim-to-real gap as foundational, yet leave their own intervention untested there. Slower trajectories can provoke rear-end or merging collisions that open-loop scoring cannot see.

  • Can the framework find distributed, multi-neuron failure modes — with an automated, threshold-free selection rule?

    Manually picking {177, 59, 71} does not scale. An automated, data-driven “malicious set” selector is the difference between a method and a case study.

  • Could re-architecting penalty heads (so high activation = violation, not its absence) make concept-to-head circuits trustworthy?

    The authors flag this inversion as the key obstacle for traffic-light / drivable-area heads. A redesign would directly improve the credibility of the safety-critical half of the circuit.

  • Do the learned concepts generalize to new cities and weather, or are they partly NAVSIM-specific memorizations?

    The archetypal-SAE collapse hints that NAVSIM’s repeated scenes leak into the dictionary. Out-of-distribution probing would separate genuine concepts from dataset fingerprints.
