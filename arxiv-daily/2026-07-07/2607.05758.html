Key finding

A coupled sample-plus-instrument digital-twin framework makes microscope operations predictable before execution — enabling open decision-making.

02 Why this matters

Fuses physics priors with learned residuals into interpretable predictive microscopy — directly informing self-driving-lab and materials-discovery workflows.

03 Critical edge

Evaluated on only two sample systems and one microscope, with no head-to-head comparison to established AFM simulators such as VEDA.

§ Methodology

The framework separates a sample twin (material state) from an instrument twin (signal formation + feedback), then couples them into a forward predictor. [1] The sample state is a restrictive descriptor vocabulary pulled from force–distance (FD) curves — free amplitude, characteristic crossings, phase extrema, the attractive–repulsive transition. [2] A PINN encoder (1D-conv + MLP, three heads) recovers these descriptors under an ODE-residual proxy and L1 sparsity. [3] A deterministic z-scanner couples the descriptor-informed amplitude/phase response with a calibrated PI feedback loop. [4] A sparse CNN-LSTM learns the residual correction in robust z-space with Huber loss. [5] An operating-point feature, 1/A'(d_op), localizes the dominant missing physics — noise amplification near light tapping — directly from the FD library with no extra parameters. Datasets: Multi-75 calibration grating and Tap-300 AlScN film; 75/25 FD splits, predefined held-out scan conditions, fixed seeds.

§ Strengths / Limitations

Strengths

  • Clean sample/instrument-twin split unifies calibration, discovery, and experiment design.
  • Every learned block anchored to a physically interpretable quantity — no black-box latent.
  • Strongest result is the missing-physics diagnosis: operating-point amplification & phase-channel gap.
  • Honest scoping — predictive layer delivered, autonomous planning explicitly deferred.
  • Transparent reporting of rejected options (RFF, uncertainty head, channel attention, augmentation).

Limitations

  • Only two sample systems, one microscope; cross-probe/sample/instrument generalization not shown.
  • No head-to-head vs established AFM simulators (e.g., VEDA).
  • Proprietary instrument internals (lock-in, detector noise, piezo nonlinearities) only indirectly observable.
  • Results via parity plots & qualitative phrasing, not full numeric tables with confidence intervals.
  • Deterministic scanner cannot reproduce the light-tapping instability regime.
  • Path to an autonomous planner (acquisition functions, MPC) outlined but not built.

§ Open questions

  • How does prediction accuracy degrade across probe geometry, cantilever, or microscope vendor?
  • Can the deterministic scanner be benchmarked head-to-head against VEDA on identical FD inputs?
  • What minimal acquisition-function / model-predictive-control loop turns this predictive layer into a working open-decision planner?
  • Does 1/A'(d_op) generalize as a failure predictor across tapping-mode AFM instruments, or is it controller-specific?
  • Could a foundation model trained on a community FD archive replace the hand-built descriptor vocabulary while keeping interpretability?
  • How sensitive are Q_quality / Q_safety to the heuristic thresholds (90th pct, half-weighted repulsive term, 90° phase)?
