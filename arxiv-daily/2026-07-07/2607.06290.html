Why this matters

Upgrades Yang's qualitative GP limit to an explicit, architecture-agnostic Wasserstein bound — the first at tensor-program generality, covering MLPs, RNNs, residual nets, and attention.

Critical edge

The proven O(Σ 1/√m) rate is weaker than the O((min n)−1/2) known for fully-connected nets — and the structural constant c is existential, never made explicit, limiting use as a finite-size approximation certificate.

Methodology

Proof strategy

This is a rigorous probability-theory paper; the “method” is the proof machinery for Theorems 1.2 and 2.4. The argument upgrades Yang's qualitative master theorem into a quantitative Wasserstein estimate by coupling the finite- and infinite-width executions line by line.

A Netsor program is a sequence of lines built from fixed H-var inputs and random A-var matrices W ~ N(0, 1/m), combined via MatMul (producing Gaussian-behaving G-vars) and NonLin h = ϕ(h₁,…,h_k). Two executions are defined: a finite-width forward pass, and an infinite-width one where each MatMul is replaced by a Gaussian with cross-covariance h̄*h̄' = m⁻¹E[h̄ᵀh̄'].

Theorem 1.2 — Main bound W_p( (h^(ℓ)/√n_ℓ)_ℓ , (h̄^(ℓ)/√n_ℓ)_ℓ )  ≤  c · Σ_{m∈n_hidd} 1/√m,
with c depending only on structural params (input dim, activation, order p) — independent of the hidden widths.

The proof proceeds by induction on program lines. NonLin lines use Lipschitz stability; MatMul lines exploit the fact that — conditioned on previous H-vars — both executions are Gaussian, with means/covariances that the structure lemmas (3.5/3.6) show are finite-dimensional functions of the parent Gram matrices. The two conditional Gaussians are coupled using the same standard Gaussian noise, leaving an error driven only by the difference of conditional means and covariance square-roots.

The companion kernel LLN (Corollary 1.3) is both consequence and ingredient: before each MatMul step the theorem is invoked only on the truncated program, and Rosenthal's inequality yields the kernel concentration needed to close the induction — without circularity. A non-degeneracy reduction (Lemma 3.9) replaces degenerate MatMul lines with equivalent NonLin lines so the Gram functions become differentiable. The NetsorK extension (scalar kernel variables + scalar-parametric nonlinearities with linear growth) reaches LayerNorm + softmax single-head attention.

Reading the numbers

All four slopes lie strictly below the −1/2 sampling floor and inside the (−1, −1/2) interval expected from the O(n⁻¹) + O(N⁻¹/²) noise mixture (Eq. 5.2) — cited as confirmation that the architectural signal is visible above the sampling floor.

Strengths & Limitations

Side-by-side

+ Strengths

  • First quantitative (finite-width, explicit-in-n) GP convergence at the generality of tensor programs — genuinely extends the quantitative-CLT literature beyond fully-connected nets.
  • Clean, reusable proof template: line-by-line induction + conditional-Gaussian coupling + structure lemmas + non-degeneracy reduction + Rosenthal. Kernel LLN is elegantly both consequence and ingredient.
  • Honest positioning against Yang's master theorem and the [BT22]/[Tre23]/[Fav+25] quantitative line, openly acknowledging the weaker rate.
  • One theorem, many architectures: the bound inherits to MLPs, weight-sharing RNNs, residual nets, and (via NetsorK) attention.
  • Upfront numerics: the section states plainly that non-FC results are corroborating evidence, not rate proofs, with a principled sampling-noise analysis.

! Limitations

  • Rate is not sharp: O(Σ 1/√m) is worse than the O((min n)−1/2) known for FC nets [Tre23; Fav+25]; closing the gap is left open.
  • Non-explicit constants: the prefactor c is existential — no dependence on Lipschitz constants, depth, or program length is quantified.
  • Stronger regularity than Yang: Lipschitz NonLin + local-Lipschitz-with-linear-growth (2.5) for NetsorK; LayerNorm constant depends on the ε floor.
  • Conditional attention result: Theorem 2.4 assumes G-var non-degeneracy, a property the paper does not verify for actual attention programs.
  • NTK regime only: forward pass / GP; feature-learning and trained-dynamics regimes [YH21] are out of scope.
  • Experiments are output-law only: no full-joint Wasserstein validation, no width-vs-noise ablation (N fixed at 5000), no head-to-head with [BT22]/[Tre23], and no released code.

Open Questions

For further exploration
1

Can the O(Σ 1/√m) rate be sharpened to O((min n_hidd)−1/2) — matching [Tre23] for FC — and what structural property of the program governs which rate is attainable?

2

Under what concrete, checkable conditions does the G-var non-degeneracy assumption of Theorem 2.4 hold for a realistic multi-head, multi-layer transformer with tied weights?

3

Can the existential constant c be made explicit as a function of Lipschitz constants, program length L, and depth — yielding a usable finite-width approximation certificate?

4

Does the inductive coupling extend to the fluctuation (1/√n) kernel scaling of Remark 2.3, giving a quantitative CLT (not just LLN) for the scalar kernel variables used in softmax attention?

5

How do these bounds behave for the backward pass / NTK, and ultimately for the trained network along positive training time — toward a tensor-program analogue of [MAT26]?
