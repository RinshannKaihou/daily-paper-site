01

Methodology

threat model · cyber layer · physical layer

Threat model. A legitimate but malicious cloud tenant (AWS / GCP / Azure / Alibaba / Huawei) drives GPU workloads through standard SDKs. Topology-aware schedulers (NVLink / InfiniBand placement) inadvertently co-locate attacker GPUs in one rack; PTP or provider clocks synchronize bursts. No root, firmware, or channel compromise is required — only tenant privileges.

Two user-space modulation methods. SWMA — a persistent CUDA kernel toggling between a tensor-core-saturating active mode and an idle passive mode via a cudaMallocManaged flag (up to 6,000 Hz on RTX 4090). LTMA — modulation embedded inside GPT-2 training primitives (GEMM, fused attention, backprop), so malicious signatures masquerade as normal training noise (up to 3,000 Hz).

Physical layer. The grid at the point of common coupling is three Norton/Thévenin equivalents: main grid, an inverter-based DER with frequency-shaped output impedance (PLL + outer voltage + inner current loops), and the GPU cluster as a near-constant-power load with negative incremental resistance Zl ≈ −Vl²/Pl. Stability is judged by the Middlebrook ratio |Zg/Zdl| < 1; a Hopf bifurcation of the Jacobian eigenvalues explains the damping collapse.

Strengths & limitations

side-by-side
Strengths
  • No compromise needed — only legitimate tenant privileges; no device, firmware, or channel access.
  • Two complementary attacks — precise/high-frequency SWMA vs. stealthy LTMA bracket the precision–concealment trade-off.
  • Principled analysis — CPL, NIR, Middlebrook ratio, Hopf bifurcation tie cyber workload to physical instability.
  • Multi-scale evidence — DAQ, 100-GPU cluster, inverter scans, 8 kW testbed, 118 / 9,241-bus sims.
  • Concrete Watt2Bit demos — IDMT trip times and EMI FSK decoding, not speculation.
  • Honest & ethical — explicit asynchronization treatment, layered countermeasures, ethics section.
Limitations
  • Perfect synchronization assumed for all grid-impact numbers; jitter study covers only one 2 kHz case.
  • Weak detector baseline (logistic regression) — inflates the stealth claim; no FFT/ML/operator detector tried.
  • Hardware scale is small — 8 kW / 6-GPU; data-center (>MW) effects only simulated, on idealized protection.
  • No real-tenant co-scheduling study — cloud placement policies and quotas could dilute aggregation.
  • No code or data released — Simulink model, 9,241-bus case, LTMA scripts, EMI decoder all closed.
  • Countermeasures qualitative only — none implemented or benchmarked; defensive cost/effectiveness open.

Questions for further exploration

open fronts
Under realistic cloud placement and scheduling jitter (στ > 100 µs, multi-rack dispersion), what is the expected THD and damping impact rather than the worst-case synchronized bound?
How much does detection improve with a simple FFT/STFT anomaly detector on existing 450 Hz NVML or 1 kHz BMC streams, or a lightweight trained classifier on CUPTI counters — can the ~25% TPR ceiling be materially broken?
What is the minimum DER penetration and attacker GPU fraction at which Bit2Watt becomes physically consequential on a measured (not simulated) >100 kW deployment?
Can the attack be neutralized at the cyber layer by capping user-controlled modulation bandwidth (scheduler-enforced ramp limits, GPU power-cap smoothing) without harming legitimate training throughput?
Does the Watt2Bit EMI exfiltration channel survive a noisy multi-tenant data center, and what is its achievable capacity under realistic EM environments?
How do cascade results change under realistic relay coordination, BESS buffering, and cloud-provider load-shedding policies?
