Separation Capacity of Scattering Networks on Low-Dimensional Datasets

Konstantin Häberle · Helmut Bölcskei
ETH Zurich — Signal & Information Theory
scattering-networks separation-capacity geometric-measure-theory filter-design learning-theory
Key Finding

Separation capacity of pooling-free scattering networks is governed by filter spectral sumsets and the RIP of one coupling matrix.

Key Metric
δs,d
Design criterion reduces to minimizing the RIP constant of a single filter–geometry coupling matrix (THΞ̂ AΛ,χ TΞ̂)½.
Sparse lower bound ratio = ((1−δ)/(1+δ))² · theory paper — no empirical benchmarks reported.
Why This Matters
Translates low-dimensional-data filter design into RIP minimization and matrix conditioning — bridging scattering theory to compressed sensing.
Critical Edge
Design criteria are existential — “make δ small / matrix well-conditioned” — with no constructive procedure, no example filter family, and no numerical illustration of the bounds.

Methodology

Stage 1 — General theory (§2). Data is modeled as a countably Hs-rectifiable set E ⊆ RM (submanifolds, unions of subspaces, hence sparse models). Cover's s-separation capacity SCs(Φ) is recast as a minimization over positive-measure subspaces (Lemma 2.2), then bounded by the rank of the differential on approximate tangent spaces (lower, Lemma 2.7) and by the rank of a second-moment Gram matrix (upper, Lemma 2.9). Real-analyticity of Φ∘ψj upgrades measure-support to an exact rank formula (Cor. 2.9.1).

Stage 2 — Scattering nets (§3). Wiatowski–Bölcskeki frames, fixed nonlinearity ρ(z)=zd, no pooling. A DFT rewrites each node as FM(ρ(f*gλ)) = Aλ vM,d(f̂) via the Veronese map; rank reads off as a d-fold sumset cardinality. Veronese/area-formula manipulation separates a geometry matrix from the filter matrix, and eigenvalue bounds yield the RIP (sparse) and eigenvalue-ratio (rectifiable) criteria.

Strengths

  • Fully proved theory with a clean message: capacity ≈ (sumset distinctness) + (RIP / conditioning of one matrix).
  • Unifies the sparse (union-of-subspaces) model as a special case of rectifiable sets; recovers the classical RIP constant as the figure of merit.
  • Tightness (Remark 2.8) and the exact-rank formula (Cor. 2.9.1) show the bounds are non-vacuous.
  • Honest about scope: pooling-free, fixed-monomial; criteria are stated as criteria, not overclaimed as algorithms.

Limitations

  • No empirical validation, no example filter family, no numerical illustration of the bounds.
  • Strong assumptions: pooling-free, ρ(z)=zd, real-analytic extensions, bi-Lipschitz polynomial charts.
  • The t−4s factor can be very pessimistic; tightness only shown for the flat (linear-subspace) case.
  • Heavy dependency on same-author 2026 companions [7], [9] for the underlying machinery.
  • Depth term grows like nj·dn−1; no analysis of whether deeper bounds stay informative.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can the criteria be made constructive? What is δs,d explicitly for canonical filters (DFT/DCT wavelets, Meyer, Wigner–Ville), and does minimizing it recover any known “good” first-layer filter?
  2. How do the bounds degrade when the pooling-free assumption is relaxed (averaging / modulus-max pooling) and ρ(z)=zd is replaced by a learned or ReLU/modulus nonlinearity?
  3. Can the t−4s factor be sharpened (e.g. t−2s or dimension-independent), and is the bound tight for any genuinely curved geometry?
  4. Does the depth-n degree growth imply an optimal finite depth, or does sumset-completeness saturate once each Hλ,S fills Z/MZ?
  5. Is there a quantitative link between this RIP criterion and the filter spectra learned by trained CNNs / scattering nets on real data?
  6. Can the exact-rank formula extend to merely Ck or Lipschitz feature maps, at the cost of an ε-slack?
