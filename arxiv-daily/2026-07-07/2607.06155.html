When Does Tool Use Increase the Expressive Power of Finite-Precision Recurrent Models? ↗
Nikola Zubić · Qian Li · Yuyi Wang · Davide Scaramuzza — University of Zurich · Shenzhen Research Institute of Big Data · CRRC Zhuzhou Institute
expressivity state-space-models tool-use turing-completeness finite-precision formal-languages oracle-machines
Key finding

Finite-state tools barely help finite-precision recurrent models — one minimal tape oracle makes a constant-state controller Turing complete.

Key metric · EQn separation
2n states→O(1)

Promise problem EQn needs ≥2n internal states without tools; a single constant-size controller with tape access solves it for every n.

Why this matters

Exact formal semantics for when tool use breaks recurrent models' regular-language ceiling — grounding Toolformer / ReAct's computational power.

Critical edge

Purely realizability theory: whether the exact one-hot {0,1} selective SSMs are learnable by gradient descent or stable under finite-precision training is unaddressed.

Finite-state tool

Bounded-interface oracle

Still regular. Essentially free.

Any finite-state bounded-interface oracle internalizes at a cost of only log₂|M|+O(1) bits — the augmented system remains finite-state and recognizes only regular languages (Proposition 4).

Tape oracle

Unbounded local read / write / move

Turing complete.

A controller with O(log|Q|+log|Γ|) bits of internal memory — constant in the input length — simulates any single-tape Turing machine (Theorem 7).

01

Methodology

Purely theoretical — exact constructions and unconditional lower bounds. No training, approximation, or probabilistic arguments.

Controller abstraction (§3). Any fixed finite-precision recurrent model with B bits of state — including finite-precision SSMs — is abstracted as a deterministic finite-state controller that reads input, then emits tokens, issues tool commands, receives observations, or halts. Tagged [TOOL]/[OBS] transcripts are shown to be a finite-state encoding convention with no extra power.

Finite-state tool case (§4). A product-state simulation: a no-tool controller tracking the pair (controller state, oracle memory) reproduces the exact scored output stream, proved by induction on generation steps.

Tape oracle & Turing completeness (§5–7). Theorem 7 builds a controller storing the simulated machine state, a mode flag, and at most one tape symbol, executing the read→write→move loop with the tape invariant preserved. Theorem 9 gives the EQn separation via a pigeonhole lower bound and a marking-and-comparing single-tape algorithm.

SSM realization & resource accounting (§8, App. A–B). Lemma 12 encodes each state by a standard basis vector and defines transition matrices columnwise so the SSM hidden state is always the one-hot encoding of the controller's state. The logarithmic universal simulation hard-codes the machine description in a fixed readout and transfers control to a fixed universal controller (Neary & Woods, 2009). The matching lower bound uses a cyclic-permutation-plus-absorbing-successor automaton whose trivial congruence structure forces dimension ≥ B−1.

03

Strengths & limitations

Strengths

  • Sharp, complete dichotomy at the right abstraction level: interface-driven, controller-agnostic, and unconditional.
  • All bounds are tight. The linear B−1 bound is matched by an explicit construction; the logarithmic O(log B) bound is complemented by the linear impossibility; constants are explicit (C₀=9).
  • Selectivity identified as the necessary and sufficient architectural ingredient for exact finite control in the interactive setting — a precise, reusable insight.
  • Two-sided resource accounting in the supplement prevents misreading the main results: the log and linear answers are both correct under precisely specified conventions.
  • Honest about assumptions — Remarks 5, 14, 20, 24 explicitly delineate what each theorem does and does not claim.

Limitations

  • Realizability only. No learning results, no approximation guarantees, no stochastic controllers. Learnability / finite-precision stability of the {0,1} constructions is unaddressed.
  • Architecturally narrow positive result. Theorem 13 requires selectivity and one-hot binary dynamics; it does not certify Turing completeness for vanilla non-selective Mamba/S4 (Remark 14).
  • Relevance to real LLMs is analogical. Toolformer/ReAct expose no clean finite command/observation interface; no empirical bridge is provided.
  • EQn lower bound is the standard FSM counting argument — the surprising part is the constant-size upper bound, not the 2n lower bound.
  • Theorem 19 depends on two strong conventions (free external tape; uncharged readout table). A reader could misread O(log B) as a property of the SSM itself.
  • No treatment of multiple concurrent tools, Transformers as controllers, or CoT-budget interaction — listed as future work.
04

Questions for further exploration

Q1

Can the exact one-hot {0,1} selective affine SSMs be learned by gradient descent on EQn or Leq, and are the transition matrices stable under finite-precision (e.g. bfloat16) training, or do they collapse to non-one-hot states?

Q2

How does the dichotomy change when the controller is a Transformer rather than a recurrent model — does the same finite-state-vs-tape distinction apply, and at what internal-memory cost?

Q3

With multiple concurrent tools (e.g. calculator plus tape), can a finite-state combination of finite-state tools ever exceed regular languages?

Q4

The O(log B) result relies on free tape and uncharged readout; what is the tight bound when the readout/parameter budget is charged, interpolating between O(log B) and B−1?

Q5

Does the chain-of-thought budget interact multiplicatively or additively with tool access — can bounded CoT plus a finite-state tool exceed regular languages, or is the tape oracle essential?

Q6

Is there a natural distribution over transition maps under which the average-case required dimension is substantially smaller than B−1?
