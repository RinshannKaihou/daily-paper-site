Causal Interventions on Continuous Variables: Verb Bias in Steering Vectors for In-Context Learning

Zhenghao Herbert Zhou, R. Thomas McCoy & Robert Frank — Yale University

Counterfactual editing of verb bias in steering vectors predictably flips or erases syntactic priming — yet additive steering cannot reproduce the error-driven inverse frequency effect, revealing a fundamental boundary: steering vectors encode transient activation, not the implicit learning dynamics of ICL.

Key Finding
Verb bias is causally encoded in steering vectors
Flipping the bias inverts priming direction; saturating it flattens cross-verb differences. Information is present and manipulable.
Key Metric
2,200 steering vectors
PCA (50 components) + beta regression on Llama-2-13B residual stream activations. Tested across 250 target pairs at 6 layers.
Why This Matters
Steering vectors capture transient activation, not implicit learning
A mechanistic mapping: activation steering ~ structural priming's transient activation; error-driven ICL likely requires low-rank MLP updates, not additive vectors.
Critical Edge
Single phenomenon, single model family
Only dative alternation tested. Llama-2-13B is the primary model. Negative IFE result leaves the needed alternative mechanism unspecified. Layer sampling sparse (6 of 40).

Experiment Pipeline

Exp 1
Steering Vectors Induce Priming
Extract PD/DO steering vectors from residual stream. Inject at target sentences. Measure RawPref shift. Priming emerges from layer 15 onward.
Exp 2
Counterfactual Editing of Verb Bias
PCA + beta regression localizes verb bias as 1D subspace. Minimal L2-norm edits: Unchanged, Flip (b→1−b), Fully Biased (0 or 1). Predictable causal effects.
Exp 3
Error Signal & IFE
Error signal = divergence between verb bias and prime structure. Present and manipulable in steering vectors. But additive injection does NOT produce inverse frequency effect.

Results & Highlights

Priming onset at layer 15
PD vectors increase PD preference; DO vectors decrease it. Layers 5–10 do not reliably produce structure-congruent priming.
Flip inverts, saturate flattens
Flip condition reverses the slope of priming vs. verb bias. Fully Biased produces flat slopes, eliminating all cross-verb variation.
Bias acts early, priming acts late
Verb bias intervention effects visible at layer 5, but priming only at layer 15+. Verb bias is an early lexical variable requiring downstream integration.
Error signal present but not causally sufficient
Causal edits produce gradient-like behavior. Untreated steering vectors do not reproduce IFE — additive injection captures only a component of ICL dynamics.
Consistent across Llama generations
No qualitative difference between Llama-2-13B, Llama-3-1B, and Llama-3-8B.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths
  • Clean three-experiment incremental design
  • Honest reporting of negative IFE results
  • Strong interdisciplinary bridge: psycholinguistics ↔ mechanistic interpretability
  • Counterfactual editing methodology is reusable
  • “Present vs. causally used” dissociation is a conceptual advance
Limitations
  • Single linguistic phenomenon (dative alternation)
  • Primary model is Llama-2-13B — becoming dated
  • Sparse layer sampling (6 of 40 layers)
  • Narrow error signal definition
  • No comparison with alternative ICL methods
  • Negative IFE result leaves needed mechanism unspecified

Questions for Further Exploration

Does the continuous intervention method generalize to non-linguistic graded features (sentiment intensity, formality, factual confidence)?
What architectural component beyond additive steering — e.g., low-rank MLP updates — is needed to produce error-driven inverse frequency effects?
Would findings replicate in encoder-decoder models or attention-only transformers?
Can PCA + regression be replaced with learned probes without changing causal intervention outcomes?
How do layerwise bias–prime integration dynamics relate to attention head circuits identified in other ICL work?
