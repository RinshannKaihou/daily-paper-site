Vocabulary Overlap Drives MoE Routing, Not Language Family — SSFT Updates <2% Params for Near-Full-FT Performance

Analyzing routing dynamics in OLMoE during multilingual continual pre-training reveals that token-level vocabulary overlap is the strongest predictor of expert selection patterns, leading to SSFT: a routing-analysis-driven expert selection method that matches full fine-tuning at a fraction of the cost.

Routing Dynamics

Language Specialization Emerges Only in Final Layers

OLMoE-Base (64 experts/layer, top-8 routing) was continually pre-trained on 35B tokens from CulturaX across 7 high-resource languages. The resulting model, OLMoE-M7, reveals a striking pattern:

Diffusion in early & middle layers Multilingual continual pre-training causes early and middle layers to diffuse expert usage toward language-agnostic distributions rather than developing language-specific routing.
Specialization in final 2 layers Cross-lingual Jensen-Shannon Divergence increases monotonically from early to final layers, with the largest separation emerging in layers 15–16 — the only place language-specific routing differentiates clearly.
English retains its own cluster Even after balanced multilingual training, English maintains distinct routing patterns with only partial convergence toward other languages.
Method

SSFT: Selective & Shared Expert Fine-Tuning

01 / ACTIVATION GAP
Identify specialists
Compute per-expert activation gap: difference in normalized activation frequency between top-2 languages. Threshold α=1%.
02 / SHARED EXPERTS
Add shared experts
Select k=5 experts per layer with highest mean activation across all languages for cross-lingual transfer.
03 / FINE-TUNE
Update selected only
Fine-tune only the selected experts and routers in the final 2 layers. ~75M (SEFT) to ~138M (SSFT) params.
Low-Resource Adaptation

For low-resource languages (300M tokens), SSFT uses a high-resource anchor language to identify specialized experts via shared vocabulary overlap — e.g., Marathi → Hindi, Estonian → Finnish, Catalan → Spanish.

Key Insight

Expert selection quality matters more than parameter count. SEFT-Top20 (30% experts, no shared) underperforms SSFT despite more parameters, proving shared experts provide genuine cross-lingual transfer.

Key Discovery

What Predicts Routing Similarity?

The paper's most striking finding: token-level vocabulary overlap between languages is by far the strongest predictor of how similarly MoE experts route those languages — not language family, syntax, or geography.

Critical Assessment

Limitations & Open Questions

Red Flags & Caveats
  • Only validated on OLMoE-Base (1B active / 7B total). Routing dynamics in larger MoE models (Mixtral, DeepSeek) remain unverified.
  • No comparison with established PEFT methods (LoRA, adapters, prompt tuning) — the community's most common baselines are absent.
  • Requires a high-resource anchor language with significant vocabulary overlap, limiting applicability to language isolates.
  • The 6 "low-resource" test languages still have 300M tokens available — not truly low-resource conditions.
  • English–Dutch pair explicitly excluded due to low vocab overlap (~9%) despite shared language family, exposing a boundary condition.
  • Concurrent work (Bandarkar et al., 2026) reports overlapping findings on language-specific routing in MoE layers.
Strengths

Why This Paper Matters

Analysis-Driven Design

Research program: analyze routing first, then design a practical method informed by findings. The vocabulary-overlap insight has implications beyond this paper for MoE adaptation strategies.

Reproducibility

Fully open stack: OLMoE model, CulturaX data, and code at github.com/aditi184/moe-routing-adaptation. Detailed hyperparameter appendices.

Strong Controls

SEFT-Top20 ablation cleanly disentangles parameter count from expert selection quality. Random-SEFT controls for selection bias.

Practical Impact

10× GPU-hour savings and catastrophic forgetting resistance make SSFT viable for resource-constrained multilingual adaptation scenarios.
