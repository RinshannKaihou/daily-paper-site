The Complexity of Verifying Quantised Feedforward Neural Networks

A complete complexity landscape for FNN verification under quantised arithmetic — proving that quantisation alone does not increase verification complexity. The NP-to-PSPACE jump comes from richer specification languages, not from finite-precision arithmetic.

Alsmann, Lange, Sälzer U. Kassel & RPTU Kaiserslautern-Landau 3 FNN classes · 2 spec types · 6 results
◆ Key Finding

Quantisation alone does not increase verification complexity. Both quantised and dynamically quantised FNNs remain NP-complete under LP specs. The jump to PSPACE is caused by richer BV specification languages.

◆ Key Metric
6 / 6
settled

Complete complexity landscape: 4 NP-complete, 1 PSPACE-complete, 1 NEXPTIME — no open cases for standard arithmetic.

◆ Why This Matters

Practitioners can build quantisation-aware verification tools without asymptotic overhead. The theoretical foundation for verifying INT8/INT4 networks is now solid.

◆ Critical Edge

Purely theoretical — no experimental validation. Restricted to feedforward ReLU networks only. The NEXPTIME upper bound for general float has a gap with PSPACE-hardness.

◆ Problem Taxonomy

FNN Classes

Rational (Q)
Exact rational weights and biases. Classical setting.
Quantised (F)
Weights/biases already in finite-width arithmetic (fixed-point or floating-point).
Dynamically Quantised (F, Q)
Rational FNN evaluated under a given finite-width arithmetic provided as input.

Specification Types

LP (Linear Programming)
Conjunctions of linear constraints defining convex sets. Polynomial-size solution certificates.
BV (Bit-Vector)
Fixed-width bit-vector logic. Enables non-convex, non-linear, and bit-level constraints. BV-SAT is NP-complete.

⚙ Proof Techniques

NP Membership
Guess an activation pattern or input witness of polynomial size, then verify the network output satisfies the specification in polynomial time.
NP Hardness
Reduction from 3SAT via FNN gadgets (Fig. 1) using only parameters {0, 1, 1/2} — representable in all non-trivial finite-width arithmetics.
PSPACE Upper Bound
Novel reduction to non-emptiness of succinctly represented NFAs. Construct NFAs for both the FNN behavior and BV formula, then check intersection in PSPACE.

Key Technical Lemma: Extracting individual bits from rationals in fixed-point and floating-point formats can be done in polynomial time via modular exponentiation (Lemmas 15, 16). This enables the polynomial-time TRANS and FINAL predicates for the succinct NFA construction.

✓ Key Contribution

  • Establishes the first systematic complexity landscape for quantised FNN verification (Table 1), covering all five decision problems.
  • Closes the PSPACE gap left by Henzinger et al. (2021) — they proved PSPACE-hardness; this paper provides the matching PSPACE upper bound (Thm 9).
  • Corrects a widespread misinterpretation: the NP-to-PSPACE jump is caused by richer BV specifications (non-convex, bit-level), not by quantisation itself.
  • The 3SAT gadget construction uses only {0, 1, 1/2} — proving hardness holds for any non-trivial finite-width arithmetic.

⚠ Critical Assessment

Novelty: High
Rigor: Very High
Reproducibility: Good
Significance: Significant
  • Purely theoretical — no experimental validation. Practical constant factors and solver performance remain unexplored.
  • Restricted to feedforward networks with (piecewise) linear activations. Does not cover CNNs, transformers, sigmoid, or GELU.
  • The NEXPTIME upper bound for general floating-point is loose — gap with PSPACE-hardness remains open.
  • No discussion of practical verification tools (Marabou, ERAN, nnenum) or whether existing solvers handle quantisation correctly.

✓ Strengths

  • Clean, systematic taxonomy covering all relevant FNN class × specification combinations.
  • Complete landscape with no remaining open questions for standard cases (Table 1).
  • The succinct NFA technique is elegant and potentially reusable for other verification problems.
  • Practical message is encouraging: quantisation does not make verification asymptotically harder under LP specs.
  • Well-motivated connection to IEEE 754 and post-training quantisation practice.

✗ Limitations

  • Does not cover recurrent networks, attention mechanisms, or non-linear activations (sigmoid, tanh, GELU).
  • No experimental evaluation — theoretical results may not directly translate to practical solver performance.
  • NEXPTIME vs PSPACE gap for general floating-point remains open.
  • Succinct NFA construction described at high level; implementation feasibility unclear.
  • Verification of fairness, privacy, and other non-safety properties not addressed.

? Open Questions

  1. Can the succinct NFA approach be implemented as a practical verification tool? How does it compare to Marabou or ERAN on real quantised networks?
  2. What is the true complexity of REACH(F, BV) for general floating-point — PSPACE-complete, NEXPTIME-complete, or in between?
  3. Do these results extend to CNNs, where weight-sharing structure might enable more efficient verification?
  4. How do results change for sigmoid, tanh, or GELU activations that are not piecewise linear?
  5. Can tractable subclasses of quantised FNNs (bounded depth, specific quantisation schemes) be identified to bridge theory and practice?
  6. How do rounding and overflow modes affect the practical verification landscape?
