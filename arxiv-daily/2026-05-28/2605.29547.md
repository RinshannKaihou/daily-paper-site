> **一句话总结：** 本文提出 S-Adam 优化器，通过基于随机方向导数方差的局部几何不稳定性（LGI）指标探测 Clarke 次微分直径，并利用指数阻尼机制 exp(-lambda*rho) 在奇异区域自适应减速更新。在量化感知训练（2/4/8-bit QAT）和极端小批量学习（batch size=2）场景下，S-Adam 相比 AdamW 在 CIFAR-100 上提升达 6%（QAT）/5%（小批量），Imagewoof2-160 上提升达 24.69%，且理论证明了 O(1/sqrt(T)) 最优收敛速率。

> **TL;DR:** S-Adam is a drop-in Adam replacement that probes local geometric instability via the variance of k randomized directional derivatives (LGI metric), then applies exponential damping exp(-lambda*rho) to shrink step sizes near singularities. Accepted at ICML 2026, it proves almost-sure convergence to Clarke stationary points at O(1/sqrt(T)) via differential inclusions. On 2-bit QAT (CIFAR-100) S-Adam reaches 18.67% vs AdamW's 15.94%; under batch-size-2 training on Imagewoof2-160 it achieves 75.87% vs AdamW's 51.18%. The k=2 probe setting adds only ~1.4% wall-clock overhead vs AdamW on ImageNet-scale QAT. The key insight: randomized smoothing is used as a diagnostic probe (estimating subdifferential diameter), not as a gradient surrogate.

## 核心贡献

- 提出局部几何不稳定性（LGI）指标：基于 k 个随机方向导数方差的高效估计器，度量 Clarke 次微分直径，不依赖 Hessian 计算，k=2 即可捕获大部分几何信号
- 设计 S-Adam 优化器：在标准 Adam 更新基础上引入自适应几何刹车 exp(-lambda*rho_t)，在高不稳定性区域（如量化边界、ReLU 折点）自动减速，在光滑区域恢复标准 Adam 动态
- 通过微分包含（differential inclusions）框架证明 S-Adam 几乎必然收敛到 (delta, epsilon)-Clarke 驻点，收敛速率为最优 O(1/sqrt(T))，且在光滑区域自动退化为标准 Adam
- 揭示 LGI 的双重解释：既度量 Clarke 次微分直径（非光滑视角），也近似刻画平滑化景观的相对曲率 kappa_delta（光滑视角）
- 证明 S-Adam 与近端方法（Prox-SGD）的等价关系：指数阻尼近似于动态正则化的近端算子

## Key Contributions

- Introduces the Local Geometric Instability (LGI) metric: a bounded [0,1) variance-ratio estimator of the Clarke subdifferential diameter, requiring only k forward passes per step (k=2 suffices in practice)
- Proposes S-Adam, which multiplies Adam's effective learning rate by exp(-lambda*rho_t) -- a non-degenerate geometric brake (lower-bounded by e^{-lambda} > 0) that preserves descent while suppressing chattering
- Proves almost-sure convergence to Clarke stationary points at O(1/sqrt(T)) using the Benaim differential inclusion framework, with a strict Lyapunov function argument showing d/dt f(x(t)) <= -C * dist(0, partial_C f(x(t)))^2
- Shows S-Adam is an implicit proximal method: exp(-lambda*rho_t) ~ 1/(1+lambda*rho_t) under first-order approximation, connecting geometric damping to dynamic regularization
- Demonstrates that LGI has a dual interpretation as measuring the relative curvature kappa_delta = delta*||Hessian||_F / ||grad|| of a delta-smoothed surrogate, unifying non-smooth and smooth perspectives

## Methodology

**LGI Metric Construction.** At each iteration t, S-Adam samples k random directions u_1,...,u_k uniformly from the unit sphere S^{d-1}, computes directional derivative estimates D_i = (f(w_t + delta*u_i) - f(w_t)) / delta, and forms the LGI score: rho_t = Var({D_i}) / (Mean({D_i^2}) + epsilon). By construction, 0 <= rho_t < 1, guaranteeing the brake exp(-lambda*rho_t) is in [e^{-lambda}, 1] -- updates never stall completely.

**S-Adam Update.** The algorithm follows standard Adam (moment estimates m_t, v_t with betas) but modulates the learning rate: eta_t_hat = eta_t * exp(-lambda * rho_t). The gradient direction is preserved unchanged; only step size is adapted. The probe count k controls a cost-fidelity tradeoff: k=2 is low-cost (1.4% overhead on ImageNet), k=8 gives higher-fidelity probing.

**Convergence Analysis.** The proof casts S-Adam as a stochastic approximation of the differential inclusion dx/dt in -alpha_bar(x) * H(x), where alpha_bar(x) >= e^{-lambda} > 0 is the mean brake coefficient and H(x) is the Adam-preconditioned Clarke subdifferential. Three steps: (1) the interpolated trajectory is an asymptotic pseudotrajectory of the DI; (2) f is a strict Lyapunov function with descent rate proportional to dist(0, partial_C f)^2; (3) the limit set lies in the Clarke stationary set by Benaim's invariance principle.

**Sample Complexity.** With k = O(L^6 / (epsilon^4 * Delta^2) * log(1/delta)) probes, the LGI estimate satisfies |rho_hat - rho| <= Delta with probability >= 1-delta (via Hoeffding + error propagation).

**Stability Analysis.** S-Adam has smaller uniform stability parameter beta than Adam: beta_{S-Adam} <= (max_t alpha_t) * beta_{Adam}, meaning it generalizes better due to smaller parameter sensitivity to data perturbations.

## Results & Highlights

**Experiment 1: Quantization-Aware Training (QAT).** Custom QATNet with 2/4/8-bit simulated quantization across CIFAR-100, TinyImageNet, Imagewoof2-160, and ImageNet:

| Optimizer | CIFAR-100 (2-bit) | TinyImageNet (4-bit) | Imagewoof (4-bit) | ImageNet (8-bit) |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|-------------------|------------------|
| Prox-SGD | 14.13% | 19.89% | 31.18% | 9.66% |
| AdamW | 15.94% | 18.91% | 33.24% | 8.63% |
| S-Adam (k=2) | 18.11% | 22.81% | 35.57% | 10.97% |
| S-Adam (k=8) | 18.67% | 23.18% | 35.19% | 11.24% |

- On CIFAR-100 (2-bit), Prox-SGD and AdamW collapse to <10% by epoch 10; S-Adam maintains a stable 18.6% trajectory
- Loss curves show baselines rebound/diverge after initial descent while S-Adam stabilizes near lower loss floors
- k=2 S-Adam is faster than AdamW on small-scale QAT benchmarks (14.33s vs 15.89s on CIFAR-100) and within 1.4% on ImageNet (8297s vs 8186s)

**Experiment 2: High-Noise Small-Batch Learning.** ResNet18 (ImageNet-pretrained) fine-tuned with batch sizes 2, 4, 16, 64:

| Optimizer | CIFAR-100 (BS=2) | CIFAR-10 (BS=2) | Imagewoof (BS=2) |
|-----------|------------------|-----------------|------------------|
| AdamW | 51.12% | 80.48% | 51.18% |
| S-Adam (k=8) | 56.11% | 86.13% | 75.87% |

- Largest gain: +24.69 points on Imagewoof2-160 at BS=2 over AdamW
- At BS=4: S-Adam reaches 81.22% on Imagewoof vs AdamW's 56.25% (+25 points)
- Advantage narrows as batch size increases (BS=64: CIFAR-10 94.18% vs 90.62%), consistent with geometric brake relaxing in smoother regimes
- Learning-rate sweep (1e-4, 5e-4, 1e-3): S-Adam wins at every LR

**Ablation Studies:**
- lambda=0 (removing damping): accuracy drops ~4.9% on TinyImageNet, confirming geometric damping is essential
- k=1 (disabling variance signal): degrades to AdamW-level performance, confirming LGI probe is the active ingredient
- delta=0.0001 (too-small perturbation): CIFAR-100 drops to 14.78%, showing probes need sufficient "reach" to sense quantization boundaries

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty: High.** The idea of repurposing randomized smoothing as a diagnostic probe (estimating Clarke subdifferential diameter) rather than a gradient surrogate is genuinely novel. The LGI metric is elegant: a bounded [0,1) variance ratio that naturally distinguishes smooth from non-smooth regions. The dual interpretation (subdifferential diameter vs. relative curvature) adds depth.

**Rigor: Strong.** The convergence proof via differential inclusions follows the established Benaim framework correctly. The strict Lyapunov function argument is clean. Sample complexity analysis for LGI estimation (Appendix C) uses proper concentration inequalities. The stability analysis (Appendix F) showing beta_{S-Adam} <= max(alpha_t) * beta_{Adam} is a nice theoretical result on generalization.

**Reproducibility: Good.** All hyperparameters are explicitly listed (lambda=2.0, delta=0.01, epsilon=1e-6, LGI cap=10.0, seed=42). Baseline parameters were "independently optimized for maximum convergence stability." Experiments run on NVIDIA A800.

**Significance: Moderate-to-High.** The paper targets a real and growing problem (non-smooth optimization in QAT, sparse models). The gains in extreme settings (2-bit QAT, BS=2) are substantial. However, the practical impact depends on whether these extreme regimes are representative of common use cases.

**Red Flags / Concerns:**
- **QAT baselines are suspiciously weak.** ImageNet 8-bit QAT accuracy of 8.63% (AdamW) and 9.66% (Prox-SGD) are extremely low -- well-trained 8-bit quantized models should achieve 55%+ on ImageNet. This suggests the experimental setup (QATNet architecture, training protocol) may not be standard or competitive, making the relative gains less informative. The paper uses a custom "QATNet" architecture rather than standard quantized ResNet/MobileNet, which raises questions about whether the results generalize.
- **Only 2 baselines compared.** No comparison with other recent non-smooth optimizers, gradient clipping strategies, or warmup schedules -- only AdamW and Prox-SGD.
- **Evaluation scope is narrow.** All experiments are image classification on small-to-mid datasets. No NLP, no transformers, no large-scale training (the "ImageNet" experiment uses 8-bit QAT with only ~11% accuracy).
- **The "Prox-SGD fails to converge" asterisk on CIFAR-100 QAT** suggests potential implementation issues rather than fundamental algorithmic limitations.
- **10-20 epoch training** is very short. It is unclear whether S-Adam's advantages persist in longer training regimes where AdamW might recover.

## Strengths

- Elegant and novel core idea: using randomized smoothing as a geometric probe, not a gradient surrogate
- Strong theoretical grounding: Clarke subdifferential framework, differential inclusions, Lyapunov analysis, sample complexity, stability bounds
- LGI is bounded by design [0,1), ensuring the brake never fully stalls updates
- S-Adam degrades gracefully to standard Adam in smooth regions (lambda=0 or rho->0)
- k=2 probes suffice for most of the benefit -- practical computational overhead is minimal (~1.4% on ImageNet)
- The connection to proximal methods (Appendix D) provides additional theoretical grounding
- Comprehensive ablations on lambda, k, delta, learning rate, and batch size

## Limitations

- **Weak baselines in QAT experiments.** The absolute accuracy numbers suggest non-standard or poorly tuned experimental setups. Without standard QAT benchmarks (e.g., quantized ResNet-18 on ImageNet achieving >55%), the practical significance of the improvements is unclear
- **No comparison with modern QAT methods** (e.g., LSQ, PACT, AdaRound) or other stabilization techniques (gradient clipping, LR warmup, SAM)
- **Evaluation limited to image classification** on relatively small datasets. No language models, diffusion models, or large-scale transformers
- **Short training schedules** (10-20 epochs) may not reflect practical training regimes
- **The perturbation scale delta=0.01 is fixed across all experiments.** Sensitivity to this hyperparameter is not thoroughly explored (only delta=0.0001 is tested as an ablation)
- **Theoretical assumptions** (path-differentiability, bounded iterates, moment estimator consistency) may not all hold in practice, and the gap between theory and practice is not discussed

## Relevance

本文针对非光滑深度学习优化（量化训练、小批量学习等）提出 S-Adam 优化器，对涉及量化模型部署、资源受限训练等场景的研究具有直接参考价值。其基于 Clarke 次微分的理论框架和 LGI 几何探针思路为理解梯度不稳定现象提供了新视角，ICML 2026 的接收也验证了其学术贡献。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does S-Adam perform on standard QAT benchmarks (e.g., quantized ResNet-50 on ImageNet with >55% accuracy) rather than the custom QATNet?
- Can the LGI metric be decoupled from the optimizer and used as a standalone training diagnostic or learning-rate scheduler?
- Does S-Adam provide benefits for large-scale transformer training (LLMs), where ReLU is replaced by GeLU/SwiGLU but quantization remains important?
- How does the perturbation scale delta interact with the quantization step size? Is there an optimal relationship?
- What happens when S-Adam is combined with other stabilization techniques (gradient clipping, warmup, SAM)?
- The sample complexity bound O(L^6 / (epsilon^4 * Delta^2)) seems loose -- can tighter bounds or adaptive k schedules improve efficiency?
