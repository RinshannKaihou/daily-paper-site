Why This Matters
MoE safety concentrates in a handful of experts, creating a single point of failure. As MoE architectures dominate LLM deployments, this architectural fragility demands immediate defensive attention.
Critical Edge
SSS may conflate safety-enforcement experts with harmful-topic-processing experts. Defensive implications — how to harden these experts — are acknowledged but unexplored.

Routing Probes — Why Routing Is Not the Safety Lever

I. Teacher-Forced Behavioral Contrast
Hold prompt fixed, compare routing under forced refusal vs. compliant continuations. Result: top-8 expert overlap ≈ 8/8 — routing is identical regardless of output behavior.
II. Refusal-Style Contrast
Prepend refusal prefix to benign prompts to flip mode; compare across topics. Within-topic JSD ≈ 0.01–0.03; cross-topic JSD ≈ 0.34 — topic dominates.
III. Matched Safety-Intent Contrast
Harmful/benign pairs preserving topic and syntax but removing unsafe intent. Matched JSD ≈ 0.10 vs. random cross-topic baseline ≈ 0.23 — safety intent shifts routing less than topic does.

RASET Framework

I
Identify Safety-Critical Experts Compute Safety Sensitivity Score: Sl,i = A(l,i; Dharm) − λ·A(l,i; Dnorm). Select top-K experts (λ=0.5, K=5–8 per model).
II
Constrained Expert-Level Adaptation Dual-objective loss: safety boundary violation (promote compliance, suppress refusal) + capability preservation (NLL on benign data + L2 regularization toward pre-trained weights). 250 harmful samples, 500 steps.
III
Verify Routing Stability Post-tuning: JS divergence < 0.04, top-8 expert overlap > 7.0/8. Routing remains nearly unchanged — the attack hides inside expert representations.

Strengths

  • Three complementary routing probes with proper controls rigorously isolate the routing-vs-representation question.
  • Evaluated on 5 diverse MoE architectures spanning 1B–42B parameters with heterogeneous routing designs.
  • Practically efficient: only 0.12%–0.95% of parameters modified with a clear, reproducible pipeline.
  • Routing preservation verification (JS divergence, expert overlap) demonstrates the attack works as theorized.
  • Well-structured paper with clear notation, formal SSS definition, and comprehensive appendices.

Limitations

  • SSS may conflate safety-enforcement experts with harmful-topic-processing experts — some experts may activate because harmful content involves specific topics, not because they enforce safety.
  • Defensive countermeasures (how to harden safety-critical experts) are mentioned but not developed.
  • Capability drops of 5–7.5 points are non-trivial; Pareto frontier between attack success and capability preservation is unexplored.
  • Only open-weight MoE models tested; proprietary MoE models (e.g., GPT-4) are not covered.
  • The matched safety-intent probe relies on manually constructed pairs, limiting scale and introducing potential topic-matching bias.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Can SSS be used proactively during alignment training to distribute safety enforcement more broadly across experts?
  • How stable is the safety-critical expert set across different data splits, random seeds, or fine-tuning rounds?
  • What is the overlap between RASET-identified safety-critical experts and those found via expert ablation methods?
  • Can a defense detect parameter-efficient modifications to safety-critical experts (e.g., via weight integrity checks)?
  • Are safety-critical experts “dedicated” or multi-functional — and does that distinction matter for hardening?
