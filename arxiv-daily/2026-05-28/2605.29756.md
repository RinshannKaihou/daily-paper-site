> **一句话总结：** 本文提出LFQ（Logit-aware Final-block Quantization），通过在最终Transformer块中使用交叉熵对齐logits（包含LM head），修复了block-wise PTQ在生成任务上的概率分布失准问题，在IFEval、MATH500、AIME等基准上持续提升低比特量化LLM的生成质量，同时保持语言建模和理解任务与全精度基线持平。

> **TL;DR:** LFQ is a simple, plug-in enhancement to block-wise PTQ that quantizes the final Transformer block by minimizing cross-entropy (instead of MSE) between FP and quantized model logits, including the LM head. Accepted at ICML 2026, it consistently boosts generative accuracy (e.g., +13 pp on AIME greedy for L1-Qwen-7B-Max at 4-bit, +2.4 pp on MATH500 for Qwen2.5-14B at 3-bit) across FlexRound/OmniQuant/Block-AP backends, dense and MoE models, while maintaining parity on WikiText2 and MMLU. The method only modifies the final block's optimization objective, adds negligible overhead, and is compatible with existing packing/unpacking kernels.

## 核心贡献

- 首次系统揭示了block-wise PTQ在生成任务上退化的两个根因：(i) 完全忽略LM head的量化优化；(ii) MSE目标无法保证token概率分布对齐——论文通过数学反例证明MSE更小但top-1 token翻转的情况
- 提出LFQ方法：仅对最终Transformer块（包含LM head）用交叉熵替代MSE作为优化目标，直接在logit级别对齐token概率分布
- 方法极其简洁——仅修改最终块的损失函数，不改变量化方案本身，因此完全兼容现有的packing/unpacking加速内核
- 在指令微调模型（Qwen2.5-7B/14B-Instruct）、推理模型（L1-Qwen-7B-Max、DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B）和MoE模型（Qwen3-30B-A3B）上全面验证，涵盖IFEval、GSM8K、MATH500、AIME等基准
- 消融实验充分：验证了LM head和交叉熵各自的必要性、仅需优化最终块的充分性、与LoRA补偿方法RILQ的正交互补性

## Key Contributions

- First systematic analysis showing that block-wise PTQ degrades generation quality due to: (i) complete omission of the LM head from optimization, and (ii) MSE objective's failure to preserve token probability ordering — demonstrated via concrete mathematical counterexample where lower MSE produces wrong top-1 token
- Proposes LFQ: replace MSE with cross-entropy loss for the final Transformer block only, directly aligning token-level distributions via KL divergence minimization
- Method is minimal and modular — only changes the final block's loss function, leaves quantization scheme unchanged, fully compatible with existing packing/unpacking kernels for hardware acceleration
- Comprehensive evaluation across instruction-tuned models (Qwen2.5-7B/14B-Instruct), reasoning models (L1-Qwen-7B-Max, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B), and MoE models (Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507)
- Thorough ablations: validates necessity of both LM head and cross-entropy, sufficiency of optimizing only the final block, and orthogonality with LoRA-based compensation (RILQ)

## Methodology

**Problem Setup:** Block-wise PTQ progressively quantizes each Transformer block by minimizing MSE between FP and quantized block outputs. The final block is distinctive because it feeds directly into the LM head (unembedding layer) that produces the token sampling distribution. Standard approaches ignore this structural difference.

**Key Insight (Section 2):** The authors provide a clean mathematical example showing that: (1) Even with minimal MSE at the block output level, the LM head can amplify perturbations to flip the top-1 token prediction. (2) Even when including the LM head, minimizing MSE on logits does not guarantee correct token predictions — a case with lower MSE can predict the wrong token while a case with higher MSE predicts correctly. By contrast, cross-entropy assigns a lower loss to the case that predicts the correct token, making it the proper objective for generation alignment.

**LFQ Objective (Eq. 3):** While blocks 1 through N-1 use standard MSE reconstruction, the final block is quantized by minimizing:
$$\min_{W_q} L_{CE}(X W_{FP} W_{Head}, X W_q W_{Head})$$
where the cross-entropy is computed over the softmax distributions of FP and quantized logits. Since minimizing CE is equivalent to minimizing KL divergence (which equals zero iff distributions are identical), this directly encourages the quantized model's token-level distribution to match the FP model. Cross-entropy also serves as a learning-to-rank objective, helping recover the FP model's top-k token ordering.

**Compatibility with existing PTQ methods:** LFQ specializes naturally to different block-wise PTQ backends — FlexRound (learns scaling factors s1, S2, s3), OmniQuant (learns clipping parameters gamma, beta), and Block-AP (learns scale s) — by simply changing the optimization objective for the final block's parameters from MSE to cross-entropy.

**Three key properties:** (i) Agnostic to the underlying block-wise PTQ method; (ii) Memory-efficient — only optimizes one block, runnable on a single GPU; (iii) Preserves quantization scheme compatibility with existing kernels.

## Results & Highlights

**Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (Table 1):**
- FlexRound+LFQ at W4: IFEval 71.35 vs 69.50 (+1.85), MATH500 73.4 vs 72.6 (+0.8), MMLU preserved at 72.48
- OmniQuant+LFQ at W4: IFEval 69.50 vs 68.21 (+1.29), MATH500 71.6 vs 69.8 (+1.8)
- Block-AP+LFQ at W3g128: IFEval 63.77 vs 61.00 (+2.77), MATH500 61.8 vs 60.0 (+1.8)
- Gains are consistent across all 6 model-bitwidth configurations

**Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct (Table 1):**
- FlexRound+LFQ at W3g128: IFEval 77.08 vs 75.05 (+2.03), MATH500 71.6 vs 69.6 (+2.0)
- OmniQuant+LFQ at W4: IFEval 75.23 vs 73.94 (+1.29), MATH500 75.2 vs 73.4 (+1.8)

**Reasoning Models (Table 2):**
- L1-Qwen-7B-Max at W4: AIME'24 greedy 43.33 vs 30.00 (+13.33 pp) — nearly closes gap to FP's 46.67; pass@8 55.09 vs 51.71 (within 0.21 pp of FP's 55.30)
- DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B at W4: AIME'04 greedy 26.67 vs 16.67 (+10.0 pp)
- At W3g128: L1-Qwen-7B-Max AIME'04 greedy 30.00 vs 23.33 (+6.67 pp)

**MoE Model (Table 3):**
- Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507 at W4g128: AIME'25 greedy 60.00 vs 53.33 (+6.67 pp), IFEval 82.99 vs 82.44

**Ablation Highlights (Tables 4, 9):**
- LM head alone helps, adding CE gives further gains — both components essential
- Applying LFQ to more blocks (k=2, k=3) shows negligible additional improvement; the final block alone is sufficient
- Removing LFQ from the final block while applying it to penultimate blocks causes generation quality to drop back to baseline levels, confirming the final block is critical

**LFQ vs RILQ (Table 5):**
- LFQ consistently outperforms RILQ on text generation (IFEval, GSM8K) across all backends
- RILQ is better on WikiText2/MMLU due to LoRA adapters adding capacity
- LFQ+RILQ combination (Appendix D) inherits strengths of both: generation quality near LFQ, language modeling near RILQ

**Robustness (Appendices F, G):**
- LFQ is insensitive to learning rate choice across different backends
- Works well with both C4 and WikiText2 as calibration data

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: Moderate-High.** The core insight — that MSE at intermediate activations does not preserve token prediction accuracy — is cleanly formulated and convincingly demonstrated via mathematical examples and empirical evidence. The solution (swap MSE for CE on the final block) is simple but non-obvious. The idea of including the LM head in the optimization is straightforward in hindsight but was universally overlooked.

- **Rigor: Strong.** Ablations are thorough: (i) LM head alone vs LM head + CE vs neither (Table 4, 7, 8); (ii) number of blocks with LFQ (Figure 2, Table 9); (iii) comparison with orthogonal LoRA-based method (Table 5, 10); (iv) learning rate sensitivity (Table 11); (v) calibration dataset sensitivity (Table 12). The mathematical analysis in Section 2 is elegant and convincing. The reasoning trajectory case study (Figure 1) provides nice qualitative evidence.

- **Reproducibility: Good.** Detailed hyperparameters provided (learning rates per method, calibration sample counts per model, sequence lengths). Uses publicly available C4 data in sequential order. Experimental settings follow prior work conventions. No code repository mentioned, but the method is straightforward to implement given the algorithm description.

- **Significance: High.** LFQ addresses a real and growing problem — quantization quality degradation especially hurts reasoning models that rely on long chain-of-thought generation. The method is embarrassingly simple to implement (change one loss function for one block) and can be combined with any existing block-wise PTQ method. The +13 pp improvement on AIME for L1-Qwen-7B-Max is substantial.

- **Potential concerns:**
  - Gains are most dramatic on reasoning models and AIME; on instruction-following (IFEval), improvements are more modest (typically 1-2 pp).
  - The method is only tested with weight-only quantization; it is unclear whether activation quantization would interact differently.
  - No results on models larger than 30B (MoE). Scalability to 70B+ dense models is not demonstrated.
  - At 3-bit group-wise quantization, the absolute performance gap to FP remains substantial (e.g., Block-AP+LFQ at W3g128 on MATH500: 61.8 vs FP 74.2), suggesting LFQ alone is insufficient at very low bit-widths.
  - The paper does not discuss wall-clock time or memory overhead during quantization (only claims it runs on a single GPU).
  - Calibration data is drawn sequentially from the start of C4, which is a somewhat arbitrary choice, though the appendix shows robustness to this.

## Strengths

- Elegant, well-motivated core idea with clean mathematical justification
- Extremely simple implementation — just change the loss function for one block
- Comprehensive evaluation: 7+ model architectures, 3 PTQ backends, 6+ benchmarks, 2 bit-widths
- Thorough ablation study covering all key design choices
- Demonstrates orthogonality and complementarity with LoRA-based compensation (RILQ)
- Strong results on reasoning models (AIME improvements are particularly compelling)
- Preserves backward compatibility with existing quantization kernels
- Accepted at ICML 2026 — peer-reviewed validation

## Limitations

- Only tested with weight-only quantization; interaction with activation quantization unexplored
- No experiments on models larger than 30B parameters (dense); 70B+ scalability unknown
- At very low bit-widths (3-bit group-wise), the remaining gap to FP is still large
- No discussion of quantization-time computational cost or comparison of wall-clock time
- Improvements on IFEval are modest (1-2 pp); the method is less impactful for short-response tasks
- The calibration data strategy (sequential C4) is simple but may not be optimal
- Limited to block-wise PTQ; applicability to layer-wise PTQ or QAT is not discussed

## Relevance

本文针对低比特量化LLM在生成任务（尤其是推理模型的长链思维）上的质量退化问题，提出了极简且有效的logit对齐方法。对于关注LLM高效部署、模型压缩、推理优化以及reasoning model量化的研究具有直接参考价值。LFQ可作为现有block-wise PTQ流程的标准增强步骤。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- What is the quantization-time computational overhead of LFQ compared to standard block-wise PTQ? Does the cross-entropy computation over the full vocabulary add significant cost?
- How does LFQ interact with joint weight-and-activation quantization schemes (e.g., W4A4)?
- Can the cross-entropy logit alignment idea be extended to layer-wise PTQ methods (GPTQ, AWQ) by adding a final logit-alignment fine-tuning step?
- Does LFQ provide meaningful gains at 2-bit quantization, or does the signal-to-noise ratio make it irrelevant?
- For models with tied embeddings (where the LM head shares weights with the embedding layer), does LFQ introduce any complications?
- How does LFQ affect code generation and multilingual generation tasks, which have different token distribution characteristics?
- Could temperature scaling or KL divergence with different temperature parameters provide even better alignment than standard cross-entropy?
