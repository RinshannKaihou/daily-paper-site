HPO: Hysteretic Policy Optimization for Stable Training under Sparse Rewards

Two minimal modifications to GRPO — hysteretic advantage down-weighting and batch-mean length normalization — that prevent negative-advantage dominance and length bias. The adaptive variant, A-HPO, requires zero manual tuning.

Methodology

Under sparse binary rewards, GRPO suffers from two coupled failure modes: sign imbalance (negative advantages vastly outnumber positive ones) and length bias (per-response normalization makes short correct outputs receive oversized gradients while long wrong outputs escape penalization).

Modification 1
Hysteretic Weighting
Positive-advantage samples keep w = 1. Negative-advantage samples get down-weighted by α ∈ [0,1]. Interpolates between positive-only and symmetric updates.
+
Modification 2
Mean-Length Norm
Replace per-response loss normalization (divide by |τi|) with batch-mean normalization (divide by |τ|). Decouples gradient magnitude from individual response length.

A-HPO (adaptive): Sets α = clip(p+/p−, αmin, 1) from batch-level sign frequencies. When negatives dominate (sparse regime), α is small; as balance improves during training, α drifts toward 1 and recovers symmetric updates. No manual tuning required.

On Countdown (Llama3.1-3B, Countdown-4), A-HPO improves 200-step reward by +0.14 over GRPO, with the largest gains when successful trajectories are rarest. At 32B scale on TeleLogs, the model reaches 95.86% pass@1, though it starts outside the strict sparse-reward regime.

Key insight: α=0 (positive-only) collapses to degenerate 16-token outputs. α=1.0 (fully symmetric) barely improves on GRPO. The sweet spot lies in between, and A-HPO tracks it automatically.

Diagnostic ablations confirm each component adds value: N-HPO (hysteresis only, GRPO normalization) improves over GRPO; A-HPO (both changes) further improves over N-HPO.

Strengths

  • Minimal intervention — exactly two targeted changes to GRPO, each clearly motivated by a diagnosed failure mode
  • A-HPO eliminates manual tuning of α, adapting automatically as the reward distribution shifts during training
  • Strong ablations isolate the contribution of each component (hysteresis vs. normalization) independently
  • Honest scope — explicitly targets sparse verifiable rewards, not dense/process-reward settings
  • Negligible overhead — a single sign-frequency computation and scalar clip per batch
  • Valuable negative result: α=0 (RAFT-like positive-only) collapses to degenerate short outputs, warning against discarding negative samples entirely

Limitations & Concerns

  • Only two tasks — TeleLogs (proprietary, Huawei 5G domain) and Countdown (arithmetic puzzle); no standard RLHF benchmarks (MATH, GSM8K, HumanEval)
  • No theoretical convergence analysis; the adaptive rule is a heuristic, not a principled estimator
  • Benefits narrow or vanish when tasks become easy or models are already strong (acknowledged by authors)
  • αmin=0.4 is itself a hyperparameter that may need adjustment for different setups
  • No comparison with process-reward-model or dense-reward approaches that tackle sparse rewards differently
  • Countdown results presented as training curves, not final-evaluation tables, making precise comparison harder

Open Questions

  1. How does A-HPO perform on standard RLHF benchmarks (MATH, GSM8K, HumanEval) where sparse binary rewards are common?
  2. Can the adaptive hysteretic rule extend to dense or process-reward settings, or does it specifically require binary sparse-reward structure?
  3. What happens when the sign-frequency approximation fails (e.g., positive and negative advantages have very different per-sample gradient magnitudes)?
  4. Is there a principled way to set αmin rather than treating it as a fixed hyperparameter?
  5. How does A-HPO interact with curriculum learning or reward shaping strategies that gradually reduce sparsity?
