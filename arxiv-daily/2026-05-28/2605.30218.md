> **一句话总结：** MarginGate 利用 top-1/top-2 logit margin 作为稀疏触发信号，仅在低 margin 步骤进行验证与修复，实现 LLM BF16 推理的完全确定性解码，将验证开销降低约 2 倍。

> **TL;DR:** Batch-level BF16 LLM inference can flip tokens compared to single-request decoding. MarginGate observes that such flips are sparse (<1.3% on most benchmarks) and strongly correlated with low logit margins. By verifying only low-margin steps and repairing mismatched K/V columns, MarginGate achieves 100% sequence-level determinism on Llama-3.1-8B and Qwen2.5-14B with ~16-19% trigger rates, cutting LLM-42's latency overhead by ~2x while guaranteeing exact reproducibility. The approach degrades to ~50% trigger rates on harder models (DSR1-Distill-Qwen-7B) where near-tie logits are more common.

## 核心贡献

- 发现 batch 推理导致的 token flip 具有高度稀疏性（在 MATH500、GSM8K、HumanEval 上仅 0.3-1.3%），且 flip 前的 K/V 扰动保持在 O(epsilon) 噪声水平，没有可测量的漂移
- 发现 top-1/top-2 logit margin 是翻转风险的有效预测信号，而 K/V 扰动幅度本身无法预测翻转
- 提出 MarginGate：基于 logit margin 的稀疏验证策略，保持高 margin 步骤在 BF16 快速路径上，仅低 margin 步骤触发确定性验证
- 通过替换当前 K/V 列（single-column repair）修复已确认的不匹配，实现 100% 序列级确定性解码
- 在 Llama-3.1-8B 和 Qwen2.5-14B 上分别仅需 18.56% 和 15.05% 的验证触发率，将 LLM-42 延迟增量降低 2.23x 和 1.99x
- 阈值在 MATH500 上标定后可直接迁移至 GSM8K、SharedGPT、HumanEval，无需重新标定

## Key Contributions

- Empirically demonstrates across 5 models and 3 flip-rate benchmarks that batch-induced token flips are sparse (0.3-1.3%) and step-local
- Shows that K/V perturbations remain flat at O(epsilon) before flips (no measurable drift), while low top-1/top-2 logit margins capture most flip risk
- Proposes MarginGate, a margin-triggered sparse verification policy: keep BF16 decoding on high-margin steps, verify only low-margin steps via deterministic recomputation, repair only the current K/V column on confirmed mismatch
- Achieves 100% sequence-level determinism on Llama-3.1-8B (18.56% trigger rate) and Qwen2.5-14B (15.05% trigger rate)
- Reduces LLM-42's latency increment by 2.23x / 1.99x relative to always-on verification on these models
- Demonstrates cross-dataset transfer: threshold calibrated on MATH500 transfers to GSM8K, SharedGPT, HumanEval without re-calibration
- Provides detailed K/V cache layer-wise diagnostics showing K-error peaks at mid-depth while V-error grows monotonically with depth

## Methodology

**Problem setup:** In BF16 LLM inference, the same request can emit different tokens when decoded alone vs. inside a larger batch. This is not sampling randomness but a numerical effect from different CUDA reduction orders changing partial sum accumulation. The paper defines "synchronous token divergence" (flip) as a token mismatch at the same position before any earlier divergence has occurred.

**Observation phase (Section 2):**
1. **Sparse flips (Section 2.2):** Across 5 models on MATH500, GSM8K, HumanEval, only 0.3-1.3% of synchronous decode steps flip. Llama-3.1-8B flips on 0.48% of steps on MATH500.
2. **K/V perturbation analysis (Section 2.3):** K/V traces show no measurable pre-flip drift. Both K and V errors remain at the O(epsilon) noise floor until the flip occurs (Delta=0), then jump 14-71x. This means K/V perturbation magnitude cannot predict flips.
3. **Logit margin signal (Section 2.4):** The top-1/top-2 logit margin (g_t^logit = logit[top-1] - logit[top-2]) provides a reference-free signal for near-tie flip risk. Low margins concentrate the flip probability.
4. **Single-column repair (Section 2.5):** Oracle repair traces confirm that replacing only the current K/V column at the flip position prevents trajectory drift, confirming the repair target is local.

**MarginGate policy (Section 3):**
- Each decode step runs the default BF16 batched path
- If g_t^logit < tau (calibrated threshold), invoke a deterministic verifier (LLM-42-style recomputation)
- If verifier disagrees with BF16 token, overwrite the current K/V column and emit the verifier token
- The trigger is step-local; repair uses verifier output as the correction target

**Threshold calibration (Appendix A):** The perturbation scale epsilon_pert is estimated on calibration prompts by comparing bs=N logits with deterministic reference logits. The threshold sweep range is set as 2*max(epsilon_pert), and the empirical tau_100 (smallest threshold achieving 100% sequence determinism) is selected from MATH500 frontier.

**Cost accounting:** Two key rates: verifier rate r_verify (fraction of steps triggering verification) and repair rate r_repair (subset where token actually disagrees). LLM-42 has r_verify=1; MarginGate targets r_verify << 1.

## Results & Highlights

**Main serving comparison (Table 6, A6000 GPU):**

| Model | BF16 (s) | Batch-Invariant | LayerCast | LLM-42 | MarginGate |
|-------|----------|----------------|-----------|--------|------------|
| Llama-3.2-1B | 2.22 | +118% | +133% | +69.9% | +9.3% |
| Llama-3.1-8B | 12.27 | +141% | +354% | +64.6% | +29.0% |
| Qwen2.5-7B | 12.79 | +129% | +347% | +58.7% | +49.8% |
| Qwen2.5-14B | 22.67 | +129% | +374% | +47.5% | +23.9% |
| DSR1-Dist-Qwen-7B | 23.06 | +134% | +345% | +72.0% | +84.5% |

**Key findings:**
- MarginGate overhead ranges from +9.3% (Llama-3.2-1B) to +84.5% (DSR1-Distill-Qwen-7B) vs. BF16 baseline
- Global interventions (Batch-Invariant Ops, LayerCast) pay 118-374% overhead on every step regardless of flip risk
- LLM-42 verifies every token at 47-72% overhead
- MarginGate is cheapest where low-margin steps are sparsest

**Batch-size scaling (Table 8):** Overhead grows with batch size (bs=8 to bs=64) for both LLM-42 and MarginGate, but MarginGate retains its advantage. For Llama-3.1-8B at bs=64: LLM-42 +106.5% vs. MarginGate +73.6%.

**Cross-dataset transfer (Table 9):** MATH500-calibrated threshold transfers to GSM8K, SharedGPT, HumanEval with consistent overhead. For Llama-3.1-8B: MATH500 +29.0%, GSM8K +34.4%, SharedGPT +39.4%, HumanEval +28.5% — all maintaining 100% sequence determinism.

**Threshold calibration (Table 10):** Empirical tau_100 values: Llama-3.2-1B = 1.0, Llama-3.1-8B = 4.0, Qwen2.5-7B = 8.0, Qwen2.5-14B = 4.0, DSR1-Distill-Qwen-7B = 16.0. DSR1 requires a much higher threshold due to broader near-tie regions.

**Layer-wise K/V analysis (Table 11, Appendix B):** K-error peaks at mid-depth and contracts toward output; V-error grows monotonically with depth (last/first ratio 2.6-16.4x, especially aggressive on Qwen family). Despite V growing 3-13x across depth, pre-flip error remains O(epsilon).

**Heterogeneous batches (Table 12):** Mixed-prompt batches yield similar results: flip rate 0.53% (vs. 0.48% homogeneous), recall at tau=0.25 is 95% (vs. 99%), at tau=0.5 is 100% for both.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: High.** The core insight — that logit margin (not K/V perturbation magnitude) predicts flips — is non-obvious and cleanly demonstrated. The sparse verification framework is a genuine conceptual advance over LLM-42's blanket per-token verification.
- **Rigor: Strong.** Five models, four datasets, threshold sweeps, cross-dataset transfer, batch-size scaling, layer-wise diagnostics, heterogeneous batch checks, and oracle repair traces. The ablation showing single-column repair is sufficient is well-executed. Threshold sensitivity is explored via Pareto frontiers (Table 7).
- **Reproducibility: Good.** The method is conceptually simple (margin threshold + conditional K/V replacement). Threshold calibration procedure is described in Appendix A with specific values. However, the implementation uses a "restart-from-rollback" prototype because public serving APIs do not expose mid-generation KV-column overwrite — a native integration would differ.
- **Significance: High for LLM serving.** Deterministic inference is critical for evaluation pipelines, regulated deployments, and reproducible research. The 2x latency reduction over LLM-42 makes deterministic inference much more practical.
- **Red flags / concerns:**
  1. **Prototype limitation:** The current implementation uses restart-from-rollback rather than native KV-column overwrite. The reported overhead may not reflect a production deployment; a native implementation could be faster or have different characteristics.
  2. **DSR1-Distill-Qwen-7B at 49.50%:** The paper honestly reports this boundary case but the approach is clearly less effective for models with broader near-tie regions. The authors frame this as "the same frontier, denser end" but it could indicate a fundamental limitation for distilled/reasoning models.
  3. **Conservative trigger mass:** The paper acknowledges that "much of the trigger mass is conservative coverage for high recall" — meaning many triggered verifications find no disagreement. This suggests room for better risk scores.
  4. **Scope limited to greedy BF16:** The paper explicitly excludes sampling, beam search, speculative decoding, and other precisions (FP8, INT8). These are important practical settings.
  5. **No comparison with simpler alternatives:** A random-sampling baseline (verify random k% of tokens) is not tested, which would help quantify how much the margin signal actually helps vs. any selective strategy.

## Strengths

- Elegant core insight: logit margin as a step-local flip predictor, reference-free and cheap to compute
- Thorough empirical evaluation with multiple models, datasets, and ablation axes
- Honest reporting of limitations (DSR1-Distill-Qwen-7B boundary case, prototype constraints)
- Clear cost-determinism frontier framing gives deployment guidance
- Cross-dataset transferability reduces operational burden
- Per-request policy design: can selectively enable determinism only for requests that need it
- Detailed K/V cache diagnostics (layer-wise structure, flip-aligned trajectories) provide valuable infrastructure insights

## Limitations

- Scope restricted to greedy BF16 decoding for dense open-weight models; excludes sampling, beam search, speculative decoding, quantized models
- Current prototype uses restart-from-rollback (conservative overhead measurement); native KV-column overwrite integration is future work
- Performance degrades significantly for models with broader near-tie regions (DSR1-Distill-Qwen-7B: 49.50% trigger rate, +84.5% overhead)
- No comparison against naive selective baselines (e.g., random verification at same trigger rate)
- Conservative trigger mass: many triggered verifications find no disagreement, suggesting the margin signal could be refined
- Single calibration dataset per model; sensitivity to calibration set composition is not fully explored
- Heterogeneous batch experiments are limited to MATH500 with left-padding; more diverse serving scenarios are untested

## Relevance

该工作直接解决 LLM 推理确定性问题，与模型部署和服务系统优化高度相关。Logit margin 作为稀疏信号的思路对理解 batch effects 有启发价值，适用于需要可复现推理的评估和部署场景。对于关注 LLM serving 系统（如 vLLM、SGLang）性能优化的研究者，该工作提供了实用的确定性推理方案。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does MarginGate interact with speculative decoding, where a local token change can cascade into different control flow?
- Can the margin signal be combined with other risk indicators (e.g., attention entropy, hidden state norms) to reduce the conservative trigger mass?
- What is the performance profile under FP8 or INT8 quantization, where numerical perturbations are larger and flips may be more frequent?
- How does the approach scale with very long sequences (e.g., 32K+ tokens) where cumulative K/V drift could compound?
- Would a learned threshold predictor (rather than a fixed margin threshold) further reduce verification calls while maintaining determinism?
- How does the approach perform with MoE architectures (e.g., Mixtral) where routing decisions add another source of numerical non-determinism?
