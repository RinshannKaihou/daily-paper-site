Latent Performance Profiling

Same benchmark score,
completely different brain.

LPP extracts 3 intrinsic metrics from hidden-layer activations of 8 LLMs (0.5B–14B) and reveals that models with near-identical MMLU-PRO/BBH/IFEval rankings use fundamentally different internal strategies — from compact low-dimensional subspaces to diffuse high-dimensional representations.

8 models, 3 families 28× parameter range 2 synthetic diagnostic tasks 27 pages · 2026-05-28
Key Finding
Benchmark rank reveals nothing about internal computation.

Models with matching MMLU-PRO/BBH scores diverge sharply in uncertainty calibration, representational dimensionality, and variance distribution. Spearman rho ≈ −0.75 to −0.32, p > 0.1.

Key Metric
3 scalars

Minimum entropy (uncertainty floor), Effective Rank (active dimensions), Participation Ratio (variance evenness) — computed from 100 forward passes on Alpaca prompts, single last-layer extraction.

Why This Matters
Model selection is flying blind.

If two models score identically on benchmarks but one has poorly calibrated uncertainty and the other uses efficient representations, your deployment outcome depends on the difference you can’t see.

Critical Edge
N=8 and circular task design.

All correlation analyses run on only 8 models. Synthetic tasks (AR, SPC) were designed post-hoc to align with LPP metrics — risking confirmation of what was built in. No comparison to existing probing methods.

The Three Latent Metrics

Minimum Entropy
H = −∑ p(v) log p(v)
Uncertainty floor across tokens. Higher floor = persistent uncertainty. Lower floor = well-calibrated confidence.
Effective Rank (ER)
ER = exp(−∑ σ̃ log σ̃)
Active dimensionality of hidden representations. Counts how many singular values of the covariance matrix are genuinely contributing.
Participation Ratio (PR)
PR = (∑λ)² / ∑λ²
Variance distribution evenness. Low PR = variance concentrated in few dimensions. High PR = diffuse spread.

Universal Hourglass Structure

Shallow and deep layers exhibit high entropy and high dimensionality. Middle layers form a compressed bottleneck — across all 8 models, all 3 families, independent of scale or training data. This “hourglass” is the most robust architectural finding in the paper.

Architecture family, not model size, drives clustering behavior: all LLaMA variants share similar entropy dynamics regardless of 3B vs 8B; Qwen and Mistral form separate groups.

Latent Profiles Diverge

Qwen-7B and Mistral-7B achieve comparable BBH accuracy but occupy opposite ends of the latent spectrum:

PropertyQwen-7BMistral-7B
PR (variance)Very lowHigh
ER (dimensionality)CompactDiffuse
SubspaceLow-dimHigh-dim
BBH accuracyComparable

Meanwhile, Qwen-3B outperforms Mistral-7B on IFEval (55% vs 45%) with less than half the parameters, and LLaMA-8B beats LLaMA-3B by only 0.2pp on IFEval despite being 2.6× larger.

Diagnostic Tasks Validate LPP

Two synthetic tasks, each with 100 samples, designed to isolate specific latent properties:

  • Ambiguous Reasoning (AR) — lexical ambiguity + disambiguating cue. Tests entropy calibration. AR accuracy strongly negatively correlates with minimum entropy.
  • Symbolic Pattern Completion (SPC) — synthetic sequences with hidden rules (alternating/mirrored). Tests representational compression. SPC accuracy negatively correlates with both PR and ER.
Key result: Mistral-7B achieves the highest SPC accuracy despite not topping any traditional benchmark. AR and SPC are largely uncorrelated with each other, confirming they probe orthogonal dimensions.

Strengths

  • Addresses a genuine problem: benchmarks are poor proxies for reliability.
  • Three metrics are formally defined, interpretable, lightweight (single forward pass, 100 prompts).
  • Hourglass structure is a genuine architectural insight consistent across families.
  • Architecture-family clustering (not size) is practically actionable.
  • Stability analyses across datasets, sample sizes, and context lengths are thorough.
  • Framework is complementary to benchmarks, not a replacement.

Limitations & Risks

  • N=8 for all correlations — statistical power is inherently limited.
  • Synthetic tasks designed post-hoc to align with LPP — circularity risk.
  • No comparison to existing probing literature (linear probing, representation engineering).
  • Only open-source models with accessible hidden states; API-only models restricted to entropy only.
  • No models above 14B parameters; no MoE architectures.
  • Link between latent profiles and real-world deployment outcomes is theoretical, not empirical.
  • English-only; cross-lingual generalization unknown.
The core tension: LPP claims to reveal what benchmarks miss, but validates itself against synthetic tasks designed to confirm its own metrics. The framework is compelling; the evidence base is thin.

Open Questions

  • Would LPP metrics survive quantization (4-bit/8-bit), fine-tuning, or RLHF? Could they detect model degradation?
  • Can LPP metrics predict real-world failures (hallucination rates, safety violations) prospectively?
  • How do profiles change during training — could entropy/ER/PR serve as training progress signals?
  • Would MoE models (Mixtral, DeepSeek-MoE) show qualitatively different profiles given conditional computation?
  • What is the minimum layer set for reliable LPP estimation?
  • Can the hourglass structure be linked to information-theoretic or optimization properties of transformer training?
