> **一句话总结：** 本文通过构建SVEB基准揭示了LLM强化学习中PPO critic退化为组平均基线的现象，提出两种无需额外训练的轻量方法——Numca（基于数值里程碑的领域启发式方法）和Hista（基于隐藏状态MinDistance的概率加权估计，有理论保证），在0.5B-14B模型和DAPO/GSPO/CSIPO等多种RL算法上实现一致提升，平均准确率提升0.5-1.7个百分点。

> **TL;DR:** This ICML 2026 paper shows that PPO critics in LLM RL collapse to group-average baselines (providing no fine-grained credit assignment), and proposes two lightweight, training-free solutions: Numca (domain-specific, using numerical milestones for math tasks) and Hista (general, using hidden-state similarity for probability-weighted reward averaging with theoretical guarantees). Hista consistently improves DAPO/CSIPO across 0.5B-14B Qwen2.5/3 models on math, science, general QA, and programming benchmarks (+0.5-1.7 avg pts), matching MCS@2 accuracy at GRPO-level cost. Code available at https://github.com/VOXXX1874/Hista.

## 核心贡献

- 提出SVEB（State Value Estimation Benchmark），首个专门评估LLM RL中状态价值估计质量的基准，涵盖数学、科学、通用QA和编程四个领域
- 揭示了PPO critic在LLM RL中的根本性失败：其价值估计退化为与GRPO相同的组平均基线，无法提供token级别的细粒度信用分配
- 提出Numca：将数学推理建模为稀疏奖励的目标到达问题，利用数值模式（整数、小数、分数）作为可验证的里程碑进行状态抽象和信用分配
- 提出Hista：通用框架，利用LLM隐藏状态定义MinDistance度量，通过概率加权平均不同rollout的回报估计状态价值，有理论保证（Theorem 5.2, 5.5）
- 在0.5B-14B的Qwen2.5/Qwen3/R1-Distill模型上，结合DAPO/GSPO/CSIPO算法，验证了方法的有效性和通用性

## Key Contributions

- Introduce SVEB, the first benchmark specifically designed to evaluate state value estimation quality in LLM RL, covering math, science, general QA, and programming domains
- Demonstrate that PPO critics fundamentally fail in LLM RL: their value estimates collapse to the same group-average baseline as GRPO, providing no token-level credit assignment (Figure 2 shows PPO estimates are nearly identical to group averages)
- Propose Numca (Numerical Milestone Credit Assignment): reframes math reasoning as a sparse-reward goal-reaching problem, using numerical patterns (integers, decimals, fractions) as verifiable milestones for state abstraction; implemented as a simple dictionary lookup with negligible overhead (Algorithm 1)
- Propose Hista (Hidden State Weighted Averaging): a general framework that uses LLM hidden states to define a MinDistance metric between generation states, then estimates state values via probability-weighted reward averaging across disjoint rollouts; provably more accurate than group-average baselines (Theorem 5.2 establishes correlation between hidden state similarity and reward agreement; Theorem 5.5 proves Hista yields better estimates than GRPO)
- Extensive evaluation across 0.5B-14B parameter models (Qwen2.5, Qwen3, R1-Distill) with DAPO/GSPO/CSIPO algorithms on math, science, general QA, and programming benchmarks

## Methodology

The paper identifies and addresses a critical gap in LLM reinforcement learning: the failure of state value estimation.

**Problem Identification (SVEB):** The authors construct SVEB by sampling ~1000 problems and ~5000 intermediate states per domain, using Monte Carlo Search with 20 rollouts (MCS@20) as ground truth. They show that PPO critics, whether initialized (PPO-1) or after training (PPO-N), produce state values nearly identical to the group-average reward (Figure 2), rendering the critic network useless for fine-grained credit assignment.

**Numca (Section 5.1):** Inspired by Hindsight Experience Replay, Numca treats mathematical reasoning as a goal-reaching task. Given a set of numerical patterns P (integers, decimals, fractions), it identifies "milestones" as subsequences matching these patterns. For each abstract state (the set of milestones achieved so far), it estimates V(s^M) by averaging the final rewards of all rollouts containing s^M. This creates a temporally abstracted decision process where credit is assigned based on milestone progress. Limitations: domain-specific to tasks with numerical patterns; fails on science/general QA (Table 2 shows Numca matches GRPO baseline on SVEB-SCIENCE).

**Hista (Section 5.2):** The general solution. Key insight: autoregressive LLM training is itself a powerful form of representation learning, so hidden states should serve as general-purpose state representations. Hista defines MinDistance (MD) between two state representations (sequences of hidden state vectors) and proves:
- **Theorem 5.2:** P(R_1 = R_2) is inversely correlated with MD(X_1, X_2) -- states with similar hidden states are more likely to receive the same reward
- **Theorem 5.5:** The hidden-state weighted estimator yields provably better state value estimates than the group-average (GRPO) baseline

In practice, Hista uses cosine similarity between hidden state representations to compute probability weights, then performs a weighted average of rewards from all rollouts in the training batch. It requires no additional training, no additional rollouts beyond what GRPO already generates, and adds negligible computational overhead.

## Results & Highlights

**SVEB Benchmark (Table 4):** Hista@40 achieves the lowest MAE across all SVEB domains (Number: 0.142, Math: 0.145, Science: 0.173, General: 0.157, Programming: 0.119), substantially outperforming GRPO@40 and PPO-N@40. Numca@40 is best on SVEB-Number (0.132) but degrades to GRPO-level on other domains. Hista matches the accuracy of the more expensive MCS@2.

**Base Model Training (Table 5, Simple Math Dataset):** Hista + DAPO yields consistent gains across Qwen2.5 models from 0.5B to 14B. Notable improvements: 14B DAPO +Hista improves AMC by +6.2 and AIME by -0.8 (mixed). Average improvement ranges from +0.5 (7B) to +1.6 (0.5B). GSPO + Hista shows no improvement on 1.5B due to fundamental conflict with GSPO's whole-response optimization.

**Instruct Model Training (Table 6, Hybrid Reasoning Dataset):** Hista improves both DAPO and CSIPO on Qwen2.5 instruct models across all domains (math, science, general, programming). CSIPO + Hista on 1.5B achieves the largest ALL-domain gain (+1.7). DAPO + Hista on 7B improves +2.5 in math, +1.5 in science, +1.7 in general, +1.4 in programming.

**Reasoning Model (Table 7):** On R1-Distill-1.5B trained on DAPO-17K (Hard Math), Hista improves average from 60.3 to 61.4 (+1.1), with AMC improving +5.0.

**Qwen3 Models (Table 8):** Hista improves DAPO across 0.6B-8B Qwen3 models, with 8B showing +1.5 average improvement (MATH +3.0, AIME +2.3). The 1.7B setting shows some regression attributed to RL stochasticity and OOD benchmarks.

**Training Dynamics (Figure 1, 5):** Hista shows faster convergence and more stable entropy during training compared to vanilla DAPO/GRPO.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: High.** The identification of PPO critic collapse in LLM RL is a clean, important empirical finding. Hista's use of hidden states for reward-weighted estimation is elegant and well-motivated. The theoretical grounding (Theorems 5.2 and 5.5) adds genuine intellectual contribution beyond mere engineering.

- **Rigor: Good with some gaps.** The SVEB benchmark is well-designed. Experimental coverage is extensive (3 model families, 4 size ranges, 3 RL algorithms, 8+ math benchmarks, science/general/programming evaluations). However, the GSPO incompatibility result (Table 5, 1.5B GSPO+Hista shows no gain) is acknowledged but not deeply analyzed. Some individual benchmark scores regress (e.g., MATH drops on 1.5B DAPO+Hista, CollegeMath drops on 1.5B CSIPO+Hista), though averages improve.

- **Reproducibility: Strong.** Code is publicly available. Experimental settings are well-detailed (datasets, model sizes, training hyperparameters in appendices). The methods are conceptually simple enough to reimplement.

- **Significance: High.** This addresses a central, underexplored problem in LLM post-training. The finding that PPO critics collapse is practically important -- it suggests the community's reliance on GRPO is not just simpler but may be no worse than critic-based approaches. Hista provides a lightweight path to better credit assignment without the costs of PRMs or tree search.

- **Red flags:**
  - The improvements, while consistent, are modest in absolute terms (typically 0.5-1.7 avg points). Some individual benchmarks regress, and the 1.7B Qwen3 result is weaker.
  - Numca is clearly a stepping stone to Hista; its main value is motivating the general approach rather than being practically useful on its own.
  - The theoretical analysis (Appendix A) relies on assumptions (A.1, A.2) that restrict to single-token answer parsing, which excludes open-ended generation tasks. The authors acknowledge this but the theory-practice gap is notable.
  - The MinDistance metric requires computing pairwise distances between all rollout states, which could become expensive for very long sequences or large batch sizes -- the scalability analysis beyond 14B is missing.

## Strengths

- Clean identification of a fundamental problem (critic collapse) with compelling empirical evidence
- Hista is simple, general, requires no additional training or rollouts, and adds negligible overhead
- Strong theoretical grounding with formal proofs
- Extensive experimental evaluation across multiple model sizes (0.5B-14B), architectures (Qwen2.5, Qwen3, R1-Distill), and domains (math, science, general QA, programming)
- SVEB provides a reusable diagnostic tool for the community
- Published at ICML 2026 with public code

## Limitations

- Improvements are modest in absolute magnitude (typically sub-2% average improvements)
- Theoretical assumptions (single-token answer parsing) limit formal guarantees to narrow task families
- Numca is limited to tasks with natural numerical patterns and serves mainly as motivation for Hista
- GSPO incompatibility not fully resolved -- the conflict between Hista's token-level MDP and GSPO's whole-response approach is a fundamental limitation
- No evaluation on models larger than 14B; scalability to 70B+ models is unknown
- Some individual benchmarks show regression even when averages improve, suggesting task-specific sensitivity
- The paper does not compare against PRM-based or tree-search-based credit assignment methods in RL training (only in SVEB evaluation)

## Relevance

本文针对LLM后训练中强化学习的信用分配和状态价值估计这一核心问题，提出了轻量且通用的Hista方法。对于关注LLM对齐、RLHF/RLAIF、数学推理强化学习及后训练优化的研究者具有直接参考价值。该工作揭示了PPO critic在LLM场景中的局限性，为设计更高效的RL训练算法提供了新思路。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does Hista interact with other advanced RL techniques like VAPO, or with curriculum learning strategies?
- What is the computational cost of MinDistance computation for very long reasoning chains (e.g., 10K+ tokens in agentic settings)?
- Can Hista be combined with PRM-based methods for even better credit assignment, as suggested in the discussion on Agentic RL?
- How does the choice of hidden layer for computing MinDistance affect performance? The paper uses the final layer but intermediate layers may capture different semantic information.
- Does Hista's advantage persist at 70B+ scale where hidden state representations may be less informative due to training dynamics?
- The assumption that hidden state similarity correlates with reward agreement (Theorem 5.2) -- how robust is this under distribution shift or adversarial prompt settings?
