LFQ — Logit-aware Final-block Quantization for Low-Bit LLMs

Block-wise PTQ silently destroys generation quality because MSE loss cannot preserve token prediction ordering. LFQ swaps one loss function on one block — and recovers near-full-precision reasoning accuracy.

Jung Hyun Lee, June Yong Yang, Jungwook Choi, Eunho Yang KAIST / LG AI Research / Hanyang University
Key Finding
MSE reconstruction at intermediate activations does not guarantee correct token predictions. The LM head amplifies perturbations enough to flip the top-1 token — a mathematical counterexample proves it.
Key Metric
+13.3pp
AIME'24 greedy accuracy gain for L1-Qwen-7B-Max at W4, recovering 43.33% vs FP's 46.67%.
Why This Matters
A one-block, one-loss-function change that plugs into any block-wise PTQ backend (FlexRound, OmniQuant, Block-AP) with negligible overhead and full kernel compatibility.
Critical Edge
Gains are dramatic on reasoning tasks but modest on short-response benchmarks (IFEval: +1–2 pp). At 3-bit group-wise, the gap to FP remains substantial. Not tested beyond 30B or with activation quantization.

Methodology

Core insight: Standard block-wise PTQ minimizes MSE between FP and quantized block outputs. But the final block feeds the LM head, which maps activations to a vocabulary-sized logit space. Small MSE errors get amplified into wrong token predictions. Cross-entropy, by contrast, directly penalizes distributional mismatch.

LFQ keeps MSE for blocks 1 through N-1. Only the final block (including the LM head) switches to cross-entropy:

min_{W_q} L_CE( X W_FP W_Head , X W_q W_Head )

Since minimizing CE is equivalent to minimizing KL divergence (which equals zero iff distributions match), this directly forces the quantized model's token-level distribution to replicate the FP model. CE also acts as a learning-to-rank objective, recovering the top-k token ordering.

The method is backend-agnostic: it works with FlexRound (learned scaling), OmniQuant (learned clipping), and Block-AP (learned scale) by simply changing the final block's objective.

Strengths

  • Elegant insight with clean mathematical justification
  • Embarrassingly simple — one loss function, one block
  • 7+ models, 3 PTQ backends, 6+ benchmarks
  • Thorough ablations on every design choice
  • Orthogonal and complementary with LoRA compensation (RILQ)
  • Full backward compatibility with packing/unpacking kernels

Limitations

  • Weight-only quantization; activation quantization untested
  • No experiments on models > 30B dense
  • 3-bit gap to FP still large (MATH500: 61.8 vs 74.2)
  • IFEval gains modest at 1–2 pp
  • No wall-clock quantization-time cost reported
  • Limited to block-wise PTQ; layer-wise / QAT not discussed

Open Questions

  1. Can logit alignment extend to GPTQ/AWQ via a final fine-tuning step?
  2. How does LFQ interact with W4A4 joint weight-activation quantization?
  3. Does LFQ help at 2-bit, or is SNR too degraded?
  4. What about code generation and multilingual tasks?
  5. Would temperature-scaled KL outperform plain cross-entropy?
