SafeDIG — Xue, Wang et al. 2026

Robust and Generalizable Safety Steering for Text-to-Image Diffusion Transformers

Position-aware sparse autoencoder interventions across DiT functional layers cut unsafe generation by ~16 percentage points on FLUX.1 Dev while preserving image quality — and reveal that where you steer matters as much as how.

Z. Xue Y. Wang Z. Bi L. Ma Z. Zheng Z. Yang B. Zhu L. Huang J. Xiao J. Lou
diffusion-transformer-safety sparse-autoencoder concept-erasure representation-engineering
Key Finding
Middle-trunk Double Stream Block interventions yield the best safety-transfer trade-off; late-trunk steering can catastrophically fail.
SAE safety features learned at cross-modal binding layers transfer most stably to unseen risk domains.
Key Metric
53.97% → 38.01%
Prompt-level ASR on FLUX.1 Dev. Line-level drops 24.74% → 11.51%. SD 3.5 Large: 77.79% → 55.36%.
Why This Matters
DiTs are replacing U-Nets; safety mechanisms must follow the architecture shift.
No retraining required. Decoder-only SAE transfer enables few-shot safety adaptation to new risk domains.
Critical Edge
38% unsafe rate remains. No code released. Routing weights undisclosed.
Baselines underperform base model on DiT. Compute: A100s, 2 TB, months of runtime. Residual risk is substantial.

Intervention Pipeline

01
Text Semantic
Harmful concepts weakly expressed in prompt embeddings. High interpretability, limited transferability.
02
Cross-Modal Binding
Double Stream Block: harmful semantics bind to visual directions. Best transfer-retention balance.
03
Rendering-Coupled
Single Stream Block: powerful but sensitive to domain shift. Can amplify harmful outputs.
Robustness-aware routing pre-ranks all (position, operator) pairs using semantic similarity, binding strength, stability, and cost scores before committing to SAE training.

Core Method

  • Safety-contrast activations from safe/harmful prompt pairs fed into per-position SAEs
  • Blend pulls activations toward transferred safety manifold; Repel pushes away from harmful sparse directions
  • Decoder-only transfer freezes source encoder as reusable sparse safety dictionary; only decoder fine-tuned for target domain
  • Stability regularization (Ωstable) constrains transfer geometry
  • Inference-time: no model retraining, lightweight activation steering

Strengths

  • Principled functional decomposition of DiT into text-semantic, cross-modal, and rendering stages
  • Comprehensive evaluation: two backbones, adversarial stress test, cross-domain ablation
  • Decoder-only transfer is clean, conservative, and needs minimal target-domain data
  • Eight appendices with pre-study, hyperparameters, evaluator configs, proofs, ablations

Limitations

  • Routing hyperparameters (α, ρ, η, ξ) fixed but values not reported
  • Prompt-pair construction is manual, not systematically evaluated for quality
  • Baselines (SAFREE, SAeUron, Erasing) increase ASR relative to base model on FLUX.1 Dev
  • Block-level granularity only; no attention-head or timestep-level analysis
  • Compute: A100s, 2 TB storage, months of runtime; no code release
Even with SafeDIG, overall ASR remains at 38% (FLUX.1 Dev) and 55% (SD 3.5 Large). Substantial unsafe generation persists.

Open Questions

  1. Can SafeDIG handle multiple harmful categories as simultaneous target domains?
  2. Could adaptive inference-time routing select intervention positions per-prompt rather than using a fixed ranking?
  3. How sensitive is the framework to the quality and quantity of safe-harmful prompt pairs?
  4. Would combining decoder-only transfer with LLM-style representation engineering improve cross-domain safety?
  5. Does the middle-trunk stability finding generalize to video generation DiTs?
  6. How does SafeDIG interact with external safety filters or RLHF-based alignment when applied jointly?
  7. Can the harmful-feature mask Mharm be learned online rather than estimated from feature overlap?
