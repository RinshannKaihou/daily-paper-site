Scaling Laws Multi-Task Learning Mechanistic

Why Larger Models Learn More:
Capacity, Interference, and Rare-Task Retention

Jing Huang, Daniel Wurgaft, Rachit Bansal, Laura Ruis, Naomi Saphra, David Alvarez-Melis, A. K. Lampinen, Christopher Potts, Ekdeep Singh Lubana — Stanford · Harvard Kempner · MIT · Anthropic
Headline claim: Small models allocate their limited neurons to high-frequency, low-complexity tasks, crowding out rare and complex tasks. Larger models escape this bottleneck through reduced gradient interference — once common tasks are well-learned, their gradients weaken, allowing slowly-accumulating rare-task features to stabilize.

So What
Some tasks are learnable only by scaling up, not by adding more data. Model size fundamentally changes learning dynamics — it is not just about expressivity.
Critical Edge
The synthetic framework assumes orthogonal task blocks — real language tasks share significant structure. Only two injected tasks tested in OLMo. The gap between linear theory and transformer dynamics remains substantial.
Core Mechanism
Features learned in order of utility = frequency × eigenvalue. Below a critical width, rare features are locally unstable and get overwritten each training step.

Methodology

  1. Phenomenological argument from Chinchilla-style power-law scaling: since the model exponent α exceeds the compute-optimal exponent γ, a model-scaling-learnable regime exists even with infinite data.
  2. Synthetic multi-task framework: K=32 linear regression tasks, power-law frequencies πk ~ k-β, power-law spectra λk,j ~ j-α. Linear-bottleneck student with shared encoder U ∈ Rd×N.
  3. OLMo pretraining: 4M–4B params on Dolma v1.7 (210B tokens) with injected comparison (TCMP) and modular addition (TADD) tasks at controlled frequencies down to 2.4×10-8.

Key Findings

  • Phase diagrams match theory: Per-task loss boundaries align with predicted utility-ordering staircase across frequency exponents (β = 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0).
  • Retention dynamics: Small models encode rare-task signal after injection but lose it before the next (exponential decay ~ e-2ηG). Larger models accumulate signal across injections.
  • Only 1B+ models learn tasks at frequency 2.4×10-7; smaller models plateau at random-chance accuracy.
  • Grokking connection: Only larger models at higher frequencies exhibit grokking on modular addition, suggesting grokking-like dynamics may be common for rare tasks in pretraining.
  • The bottleneck is fundamental: Extending training from 100K to 1M steps shows no improvement for above-capacity tasks.

C

Concerns & Limitations

  • ▲ Orthogonal task blocks in synthetic setting abstract away from overlapping, shared structure of real-world tasks.
  • ▲ Only two injected tasks in OLMo experiments; generalization to diverse task distributions is uncertain.
  • ▲ 4B parameter ceiling limits claims about frontier-scale models (70B+ might reveal different dynamics).
  • ▲ No exploration of data mixture optimization (e.g., DoReMi) to close the gap for smaller models.
  • ▲ No analysis of depth scaling vs. width scaling; all experiments scale width only.
  • ▲ Power-law scaling assumed asymptotically, which may not hold in practice.
