> **一句话总结：** 本文系统建立了量化算术下前馈神经网络验证的完整复杂度图谱——固定精度量化FNN在LP和BV规范下均为NP完全，动态量化FNN在BV规范下对定点和有限指数浮点算术为PSPACE完全，对一般浮点算术则为NEXPTIME。

> **TL;DR:** This paper provides a complete complexity landscape for verifying feedforward neural networks (FNNs) under quantised arithmetic. It introduces three FNN classes (rational, quantised, dynamically quantised) and two specification types (linear programming / LP and bit-vector / BV). The main results: (1) quantised FNN verification under both LP and BV specifications is NP-complete, matching the rational case; (2) dynamically quantised FNNs with BV specifications are PSPACE-complete for fixed-point and bounded-exponent floating-point arithmetic; (3) for general floating-point arithmetic, the problem is in NEXPTIME. The key insight is that quantisation alone does not increase complexity — the PSPACE jump comes from richer BV specifications, not from quantisation per se. This corrects a widespread misunderstanding of prior work (Henzinger et al., 2021).

## Key Contributions

- Establishes a complete complexity landscape for quantised FNN verification (Table 1), covering five decision problems across three FNN classes and two specification types.
- Proves NP-completeness for quantised FNN verification under LP specifications (Thm 5) and BV specifications (Thm 7), matching the rational case (Thm 4).
- Proves NP-completeness for dynamically quantised FNN verification under LP specifications (Thm 6), clarifying that quantisation per se does not increase complexity.
- Establishes PSPACE-completeness upper bounds for dynamically quantised FNNs with BV specifications under fixed-point and bounded-exponent floating-point arithmetic (Thm 9), closing the gap left by Henzinger et al. (2021) which only provided PSPACE-hardness.
- Shows that for general floating-point arithmetic, the dynamically quantised BV verification problem is in NEXPTIME (Prop 14), potentially harder than PSPACE due to alignment buffer requirements.
- Corrects a widespread misinterpretation of prior work: the NP-to-PSPACE complexity jump is caused by richer BV specification languages (non-convex, bit-level constraints), not by quantisation itself.

## Methodology

The paper adopts a formal computational complexity approach, classifying neural network verification problems into a taxonomy of FNN types crossed with specification formalisms.

**Three FNN classes:**
- **Rational FNNs** (Q): weights and biases are exact rationals, computations in Q.
- **Quantised FNNs** (F): weights and biases already represented in a finite-width arithmetic F (fixed-point or floating-point).
- **Dynamically quantised FNNs** (F, Q): rational FNN evaluated with respect to a given finite-width arithmetic F provided as input.

**Two specification types:**
- **LP specifications**: conjunctions of linear constraints defining convex sets. Solutions have polynomial-size representations (Theorem 1 / Korte and Vygen).
- **BV (bit-vector) specifications**: fixed-width bit-vector logic formulas enabling non-linear constraints and bit-level reasoning. BV satisfiability is NP-complete (Prop 2, Kovasznai et al.).

**Proof techniques:**
- NP-membership arguments use the certificate-based characterization of NP: guess an activation pattern or input witness of polynomial size, verify in polynomial time.
- NP-hardness reductions from 3SAT construct FNN gadgets (Fig. 1) using only low-precision parameters (0, 1, 1/2), which are representable in all non-trivial finite-width arithmetics.
- PSPACE upper bounds (Thm 9) use a novel reduction to the non-emptiness problem for succinctly represented non-deterministic finite automata (NFA). The authors construct succinct NFAs for both the FNN's input-output behavior and the BV specification formula, then check non-emptiness of their intersection in PSPACE (Prop 10, Galperin and Wigderson).
- Key technical lemmas (Lemmas 15, 16) establish that extracting individual bits from rational numbers in fixed-point and floating-point formats can be done in polynomial time using modular exponentiation, enabling polynomial-time TRANS and FINAL predicates for the succinct NFA construction.

## Results & Highlights

| Problem | Arithmetic | Specification | Complexity |
|---------|-----------|--------------|------------|
| REACH_Q(LP) | Rational | LP | NP-complete (Thm 4, prior) |
| REACH_F(LP) | Quantised | LP | NP-complete (Thm 5) |
| REACH(F,LP) | Dynamically quantised | LP | NP-complete (Thm 6) |
| REACH_F(BV) | Quantised | BV | NP-complete (Thm 7) |
| REACH(F,BV) | Dynamically quantised, fixed-point / bounded-exp float | BV | PSPACE-complete (Thm 9) |
| REACH(F,BV) | Dynamically quantised, general float | BV | NEXPTIME (Prop 14) |

- The NP-completeness results for quantised FNNs (Thms 5, 7) hold because the 3SAT reduction gadgets use only parameters 0, 1, and 1/2, which are exactly representable in any non-trivial finite-width arithmetic.
- The PSPACE-completeness for dynamically quantised FNNs with BV (Thm 9) matches the lower bound from Henzinger et al. (2021) and closes an open question in the literature.
- For general floating-point arithmetic, the alignment buffer needed for mantissa alignment prevents the PSPACE upper bound from applying, yielding only an NEXPTIME upper bound. Whether a matching NEXPTIME lower bound exists is left open.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** High. The paper provides the first systematic complexity landscape for quantised FNN verification, filling gaps left by prior work. The PSPACE upper bound (Thm 9) and the identification of the NEXPTIME case for general floating-point are genuinely new results. The clarification of Henzinger et al. (2021) is a valuable service to the community.
- **Rigor:** Very high. All results are formally stated as theorems with complete proofs. The reduction from 3SAT is clean and relies on well-chosen gadget constructions. The succinct NFA approach for PSPACE upper bounds is technically sophisticated and well-executed.
- **Reproducibility:** Good for a theory paper. Definitions are precise, proof sketches are detailed, and omitted proofs are provided in an appendix. The gadget constructions in Fig. 1 are explicit enough to reconstruct.
- **Significance:** Significant for both theory and practice. The key practical message — that quantised network verification under LP specs remains NP-complete — reassures practitioners that developing quantised-aware verification tools is feasible without asymptotic overhead. The theoretical contribution of closing the PSPACE gap for dynamically quantised BV verification is clean and definitive.
- **Red flags / Concerns:**
  - The paper is purely theoretical with no experimental validation. While this is appropriate for a complexity theory paper, the practical implications (e.g., constant factors, solver performance) remain unexplored.
  - The restriction to feedforward networks with ReLU activations is limiting. Extending to other architectures (CNNs, transformers) or activation functions would strengthen the contribution.
  - The NEXPTIME upper bound for general floating-point is somewhat loose — the gap between PSPACE-hardness and NEXPTIME membership remains open, and the paper does not provide strong evidence for either tightness.
  - The paper does not discuss how its results relate to practical verification tools (e.g., Marabou, ERAN, nnenum) or whether existing solvers already handle quantisation correctly.

## Strengths

- Clean, systematic taxonomy that covers all relevant combinations of FNN types and specification formalisms.
- Complete complexity landscape (Table 1) with no remaining open questions for the standard cases.
- Corrects an important misunderstanding in the literature about the source of the NP-to-PSPACE jump.
- The succinct NFA technique is elegant and potentially reusable for other verification problems.
- The practical message — quantisation does not make verification asymptotically harder under LP specs — is encouraging for tool developers.
- Well-written with clear motivation connecting theory to practice (e.g., IEEE 754, post-training quantisation).

## Limitations

- Restricted to feedforward networks with (piecewise) linear activation functions. Does not cover recurrent networks, attention mechanisms, or non-linear activations like sigmoid/tanh.
- No experimental evaluation or discussion of how these theoretical results translate to practical solver performance.
- The NEXPTIME upper bound for general floating-point arithmetic has a gap with the PSPACE lower bound — the true complexity of this case remains open.
- The succinct NFA construction is described at a high level; a more detailed treatment of its implementation feasibility would be valuable.
- Does not address verification of other properties (fairness, privacy) beyond reachability/safety.

## Relevance

本文从计算复杂性理论角度完整刻画了量化神经网络验证的复杂度图谱，对形式化验证和神经网络安全认证具有直接的理论指导意义，特别是证实了在LP规范下量化不增加验证复杂度，为开发量化网络验证工具提供了理论依据。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can the succinct NFA approach be implemented practically to create a verification tool that correctly handles quantised arithmetic? What are the performance characteristics compared to existing solvers like Marabou or ERAN?
- What is the true complexity of REACH(F, BV) for general floating-point arithmetic — is it PSPACE-complete, NEXPTIME-complete, or somewhere in between?
- Do these complexity results extend to convolutional neural networks, where the weight-sharing structure might enable more efficient verification?
- How do the results change for other activation functions (sigmoid, tanh, GELU) that are not piecewise linear?
- Can the gap between theory and practice be bridged by identifying tractable subclasses of quantised FNNs (e.g., bounded depth, specific quantisation schemes)?
- How do rounding and overflow modes affect the practical verification landscape — are some quantisation schemes provably easier to verify than others?
