> **一句话总结：** 本文系统研究了LLM训练中数据组织（排序）对模型性能的影响，提出了四个关键指导原则（边界锐化、循环调度、课程连续性、局部多样性）和两种排序方法STR与SAW，在几乎零额外计算开销下，跨多种模型规模（160M-1.7B）和任务（预训练与SFT）均获得一致性能提升。

> **TL;DR:** This paper provides the first systematic study of how data ordering (not selection) affects LLM training. By reusing pre-computed sample-level scores at near-zero additional cost, the authors identify four guiding principles -- Boundary Sharpening, Cyclic Scheduling, Curriculum Continuity, and Local Diversity -- and propose two practical ordering strategies (STR and SAW). Experiments across 160M-1.7B parameter models, 1B-50B tokens, and both pre-training and SFT stages show consistent improvements over random ordering and vanilla curriculum learning, with gains scaling up predictably per Chinchilla scaling law extrapolation.

## 核心贡献

- 首次系统性地研究LLM训练中数据组织（排序策略）对训练效果的影响，区分了数据选择与数据组织两个不同问题
- 提出四个数据组织指导原则：边界锐化（G1）、循环调度（G2）、课程连续性（G3）、局部多样性（G4），每个原则都有独立的算法实现和实验验证
- 设计了两种跨指导原则的组合排序策略STR（G1+G2）和SAW（G1+G2+G3），可选搭配JIT（G4），在预训练和SFT阶段均优于随机排序和传统课程学习
- 通过复用数据选择阶段已有的样本级评分，实现了几乎零额外计算开销的数据组织方案
- 通过Chinchilla Scaling Law外推，证明数据组织带来的性能增益在GPT-3（175B）到Llama 3.1（405B）规模下仍然保持

## Key Contributions

- First systematic exploration of data organization (ordering) for LLM training, distinguishing it from data selection
- Four formalized guiding principles: Boundary Sharpening (start easy, end hard), Cyclic Scheduling (periodic review of foundational data), Curriculum Continuity (smooth score transitions), Local Diversity (intra-batch heterogeneity)
- Two cross-guidance strategies: STR (Stair Ordering, combining G1+G2) and SAW (Saw Ordering, combining G1+G2+G3), both outperforming CL and DELT baselines, with optional JIT (G4) enhancement
- Reuse of pre-computed sample scores (originally for data selection) at near-zero extra compute cost
- Scaling law extrapolation showing consistent improvements from GPT-3 (175B) through Llama 3.1 (405B) scales

## Methodology

The paper formalizes the data pipeline in three stages:

1. **Data Scoring**: A scoring function g maps dataset D to a score vector gamma (quality, difficulty, learnability, etc.), typically already computed during data selection.

2. **Data Selection**: Standard subset selection using scores to reduce training cost.

3. **Data Organization** (the core contribution): A permutation function f_o reorders the dataset without changing its size, based on the same pre-computed scores.

The four guiding principles and their instantiations:

- **G1: Boundary Sharpening** -- Control data characteristics at training start (low-score for stability) and end (high-score for peak performance). Implemented via **Segment Ordering (SEG)**: partition sorted data into L segments by percentile, shuffle within segments, concatenate low-to-high.
- **G2: Cyclic Scheduling** -- Periodically revisit the full score spectrum to prevent catastrophic forgetting in one-pass training. Implemented via **Folding Ordering (FO)**: strided partitioning where sample i goes to fold (i mod L), creating L cycles each covering the full score range.
- **G3: Curriculum Continuity** -- Maintain smooth transitions in data attributes to avoid gradient shocks. Implemented via **Zig-zag Ordering (ZIG)**: reverse every odd-indexed fold from FO, creating a triangle-wave pattern for smoother boundaries.
- **G4: Local Diversity** -- Inject noise within local windows to prevent gradient collapse from homogeneous mini-batches. Implemented via **Jittering Ordering (JIT)**: partition sorted data into windows of size w, shuffle within each window.

**STR (Stair Ordering)**: Partitions sorted data into K sections at split points. Stable regions retain monotonic order; transition regions apply FO (folding) for knowledge review. Optionally applies JIT for local diversity.

**SAW (Saw Ordering)**: Same as STR but replaces FO with ZIG in transition regions, ensuring smoother attribute continuity (triangle-wave pattern). Optionally applies JIT for local diversity.

## Results & Highlights

**Guidance-level findings:**
- G1: Ending with high-score data is critical; starting with high-score data alone yields negligible benefit. Best SFT config is high-score at both boundaries.
- G2: FO-2 consistently outperforms vanilla CL, confirming periodic review helps. FO-3 achieves substantial SFT gains in math (DeepMath AIME24: 1.78 -> 2.96).
- G3: ZIG eliminates gradient norm spikes at cycle boundaries that FO exhibits, confirming smoother optimization.
- G4: JIT consistently improves all strictly-sorted methods (e.g., ZIG+JIT: 38.32 avg on FineWeb-Edu vs ZIG: 38.29). JIT-enhanced models find flatter minima (verified via weight perturbation analysis).

**Main results (Table 5, 160M model):**
- Pre-training (FineWeb-Edu avg): Random 37.09, CL 37.61, DELT 37.35, STR 38.65, SAW 38.78
- SFT Math (AIME24+AIME25 avg): Random 1.30, CL 1.78, DELT 2.42, STR 2.48, SAW 2.23
- SFT Code (HumanEval+MBPP avg): Random 55.37, CL 58.30, DELT 58.90, STR 60.83, SAW 60.48

**Scaling results (Table 6, 50B tokens):**
- 160M: Random 39.83/40.52, STR 41.27/43.13, SAW 41.51/43.10
- 1.7B: Random 47.28/47.72, STR 48.76/49.85, SAW 48.43/50.11

**Scaling law extrapolation (Table 7):** Gains persist from GPT-3 scale (2.876 -> 2.853 test loss) through Llama 3.1 405B (2.762 -> 2.738).

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty: High.** This is the first systematic treatment of data ordering for LLMs that goes beyond simple curriculum learning. The decomposition into four orthogonal guiding principles with clear algorithmic instantiations is a meaningful conceptual contribution. The insight to reuse pre-computed scores (already paid for during data selection) makes the approach practically deployable.

**Rigor: Good.** Each guiding principle is individually validated with ablation experiments. Gradient norm analysis and weight perturbation studies provide mechanistic evidence. The paper covers both pre-training (FineWeb-Edu, QuaRatedPajama) and SFT (math, code) settings. However, some concerns:

- The individual guidance experiments (Tables 1-4) are limited to a single model scale (160M), and it is unclear if the same guidance rankings hold at larger scales.
- The main comparison table (Table 5) shows DELT outperforming STR/SAW on some SFT code metrics (MBPP), which is underdiscussed.
- The scaling law extrapolation (Table 7) is theoretically motivated but remains unvalidated at actual GPT-3/Llama scales -- this is an extrapolation claim, not an empirical result.

**Reproducibility: Good.** Algorithms are specified in pseudocode. Code is open-sourced. Datasets (FineWeb-Edu, QuaRatedPajama, DeepMath-103K, OpenCodeInstruct) and model architectures (Mistral, Qwen3) are publicly available. Hyperparameters (L, w, rho) are discussed.

**Significance: High.** If the claims hold, this is essentially "free" performance improvement -- reorganize your training data using scores you already computed. The approach is orthogonal to model architecture, data selection, and training recipe improvements.

**Red flags:**
- STR and SAW show comparable performance in most settings, raising the question of whether G3 (continuity) adds meaningful value beyond G1+G2+G4.
- The paper acknowledges dependence on the quality of pre-computed scores; if the scoring function is poor, the organization strategy inherits those limitations.
- The AIME scores are very low across all methods (< 3%), making absolute comparisons on that benchmark noisy.

## Strengths

- Clean conceptual framework with four well-motivated guiding principles
- Near-zero additional compute cost by reusing existing scores
- Comprehensive experiments spanning pre-training and SFT, multiple domains (general, math, code)
- Scaling law extrapolation provides theoretical justification for applicability at frontier model scales
- Mechanistic evidence (gradient norms, loss landscape flatness) supports the intuitive motivations
- Open-source code release from Microsoft

## Limitations

- Heavily reliant on the quality of pre-computed sample-level scores; no discussion of how to handle cases where good scores are unavailable
- Scaling experiments only go up to 1.7B parameters; the Chinchilla extrapolation to 175B-405B is theoretical
- Individual guidance ablations are only at 160M scale
- STR and SAW show marginal differences in most settings, suggesting G3 (continuity) may be redundant in practice when G1+G2 already handle boundary effects
- The paper does not compare against recent data mixing or dynamic data selection methods that could also affect training order implicitly
- No discussion of wall-clock time overhead for the ordering step itself on very large datasets (e.g., billions of samples)

## Relevance

本文对LLM训练数据工程有直接参考价值。如果你关注预训练或SFT阶段如何提升模型性能而不增加计算预算，这篇论文提供了一个低成本的实用方案。四个指导原则和两种排序方法可以直接应用于现有训练流程中。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How sensitive are STR and SAW to the choice of scoring function? Would different scoring dimensions (e.g., domain diversity vs. quality) produce different optimal orderings?
- Can the guiding principles be extended to multi-modal training where data has multiple heterogeneous score dimensions?
- Does data organization interact with learning rate scheduling, batch size, or other hyperparameters in ways not captured by the current ablations?
- At truly massive scales (100B+ parameters, trillions of tokens), does the ordering overhead become non-trivial, and can the ordering be done online/streaming?
- How does data organization interact with modern techniques like data mixing strategies, DPO, or RLHF stages?
