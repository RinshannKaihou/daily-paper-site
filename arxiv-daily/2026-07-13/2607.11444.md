> **一句话总结：** 提出UMoE——一种预算保持的专家池重组方法：先剪掉与目标领域对齐度最低的一半专家，再用对称扰动从幸存者重生专家池至原始规模后做标准SFT；在Qwen3-30B-A3B/35B-A3B两种MoE架构、五个领域、12个基准上一致超越直接SFT（数学均分+3.40、SWE-bench Verified +6.0、GPQA-Diamond +3.10），且不增加任何推理开销。

> **TL;DR:** UMoE reorganizes a pretrained MoE's expert pool before domain SFT by pruning the half of experts with lowest domain-aligned REAP saliency, then regrowing to the original expert count via symmetric σ=0.05 perturbation of both parent and regrown experts — preserving architecture, parameter count, and inference cost. With one frozen recipe and no per-domain tuning, UMoE beats Direct SFT on Qwen3-30B-A3B and Qwen3.5-35B-A3B across math/code/science/tool-use/agentic coding and 12 benchmarks: +3.40 math-average (AIME24 +6.56), +6.0 pass@1 on SWE-bench Verified (16.2%→22.2%), +3.10 on GPQA-Diamond. Mechanism analysis shows Direct SFT spends ~42% of routed compute on experts removable with only −0.12 average loss, whereas pruning UMoE's pool by half costs 5.0 points.

## 核心贡献

- **提出UMoE（Unlocking MoE Capacity）**：首个"先剪后扩再SFT"的预算保持式领域专家池重组流水线。三步走——剪除目标领域显著性最低的一半专家、通过对称扰动从幸存专家重池至原始规模、再做标准全参数SFT——使重组后模型在专家数E、激活数K、参数量、推理成本上与原模型完全一致。
- **揭示直接SFT的专家池冗余**：机制分析表明，直接SFT把约42%的路由计算分配给可被事后剪除且几乎无损（−0.12）的低显著性专家子集；UMoE将这部分冗余容量转化为"事后剪除会损失5.0分"的有效领域容量，证据性地解释了增益来源。
- **跨架构、跨领域、跨数据规模的泛化性**：同一套冻结配方（REAP-50剪枝 + 路由/MLP双侧σ=0.05对称扰动）在两种MoE架构（Qwen3-30B-A3B 128专家、Qwen3.5-35B-A3B 256专家+共享专家）、五个领域、12个基准上全部正向；数据缩放实验15组配对对比全部倾向UMoE。
- **关键设计原则的消融落地**：证明"双侧对称扰动"（非单侧）+"同时扰动router行与MLP权重"是有效扩展的必要条件——单侧扰动反而比直接SFT差4.24分，并伴随路由份额向未扰动侧塌陷。
- **强基线下仍有效**：在高质量内部数学语料上，直接SFT已超过Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking（82.81 vs 81.06），UMoE仍进一步把均分提到84.17（+1.36），证明其不依赖弱基线。

## Key Contributions

- **Introduces UMoE, a budget-preserving expert-pool reorganization pipeline.** It prunes the half of routed experts with the lowest domain-aligned REAP saliency, regrows the pool to the original expert count E via symmetric perturbation-based expansion, then runs standard full-parameter SFT. The output model is architecturally identical to the input (same E, K, parameter count, inference cost), differing only in domain-aligned expert initialization.
- **Diagnoses and exploits expert-pool redundancy in Direct SFT.** Mechanism analysis (§5.2) shows Direct SFT routes ~42% of routed-expert compute to a low-saliency subset that is post-hoc prunable with only −0.12 average loss; UMoE converts this redundant capacity into consequential domain capacity (pruning it costs 5.0 points), giving a concrete causal account of where the gains come from.
- **Demonstrates generality with a single frozen recipe.** No per-domain hyperparameter tuning: the same REAP-50 pruning + symmetric router+MLP σ=0.05 perturbation recipe improves over Direct SFT on two MoE architectures, five domains (math, code, science, tool-use, agentic coding), and 12 benchmarks.
- **Establishes two load-bearing design principles through ablation.** Expansion must (i) perturb both the parent and regrown expert symmetrically (one-sided noise is destructive, −4.24 vs Direct SFT) and (ii) perturb both the router row and the MLP weights; routing dynamics (Fig. 3) show one-sided perturbation collapses routing share to the unperturbed half.
- **Validates robustness under a strong SFT regime.** On a high-quality in-house math corpus, Direct SFT already beats Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking (82.81 vs 81.06); UMoE still adds +1.36 to reach 84.17, ruling out the "weak baseline" explanation.

## Methodology

UMoE operates entirely as a pre-SFT reorganization of a pretrained sparse MoE; the downstream training objective is unchanged (standard next-token cross-entropy SFT).

**Step 1 — Domain-aligned pruning.** For each layer l and routed expert e, run the model on a small calibration subset C ⊂ D (1024 samples, max length 2048, seed 42) and score experts with REAP saliency:

S(l, e) = (1/|T_{l,e}|) Σ_{t∈T_{l,e}} g_{l,e}(h_t) ‖f_{l,e}(h_t)‖₂

where T_{l,e} is the set of calibration tokens that select expert e, g is the top-K-normalized gate, and f is the expert's MLP output. This couples activation strength with router gate, conditioned on selected tokens (separating per-activation saliency from routing frequency). Retain the top E/2 experts per layer (fixed 50% pruning ratio).

**Step 2 — Budget-preserving expansion.** Each freed slot is initialized round-robin from a retained survivor; under 50% pruning each survivor seeds exactly one new expert. For every parent–regrown pair initialized from tensor W, both sides are perturbed independently and symmetrically:

W'_parent = W + σ·s(W)·ε_p,   W'_regrown = W + σ·s(W)·ε_r,   ε_p, ε_r ~iid N(0, I)

with σ = 0.05 and s(W) the empirical per-parameter-group standard deviation (MLP projection, stored expert tensor, or full router matrix). Noise is applied to both router rows and MLP weights. "Symmetric" means parent and regrown are treated equally (not antithetic ±ε). Regrown experts inherit the parent's score-correction bias if present.

**Step 3 — Standard SFT.** Fine-tune all parameters of the reorganized model on D with one epoch, Adam (β1=0.9, β2=0.95, weight decay 0.1, grad clip 0.1), cosine LR peaking at 1e-4 (10% warmup) decaying to 1e-5, global batch 512, built on slime.

The complete procedure is given in Algorithm 1 (Appendix A). The same recipe is applied to all domains with no per-domain tuning.

## Results & Highlights

- **Math (Qwen3-30B-A3B, OpenMathReasoning 300k, avg@32 over 6 competitions):** UMoE lifts the six-benchmark average from 43.02 → 46.42 (+3.40) over Direct SFT, with the largest single-benchmark gain on AIME24 (+6.56: 58.13 → 64.69). On Qwen3.5-35B-A3B the gain is +1.67 (61.75 → 63.42), with HMMT-N25 +4.06.
- **Strong-baseline math (in-house corpus, ~150k samples, ~40k tokens/response):** Direct SFT already beats Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking (82.81 vs 81.06 six-benchmark avg), yet UMoE adds a further +1.36 to reach 84.17, with the peak gain +3.03 on HMMT Feb 2026.
- **Agentic coding (SWE-bench Verified, pass@1, 500 instances):** +6.0 absolute (16.2% → 22.2%), the largest single-domain gain in the paper.
- **Cross-domain (Qwen3-30B-A3B):** Code +2.73/+0.85 on LiveCodeBench v5/v6 (avg@8); Science +3.10 on GPQA-Diamond (avg@8); Tool-use +1.20/+1.40 on τ-bench/τ²-bench (airline+retail avg@8). Positive on every one of the 12 benchmarks.
- **Data scaling (15 paired comparisons):** All 15 favor UMoE. AIME 2026 +7.60 at 1B tokens and still +2.09 at 4B; LCB v5 +4.30 at 1.3B and +2.77 at 4.1B; GPQA-Diamond +2.14 at 0.5B and +3.10 at 1.9B.
- **Ablation (math, AVG6, vs Direct SFT 37.85):** Pruning criterion — Frequency +4.78, EAN-mean +2.54, REAP +5.16 (default). Expansion noise — one-sided regrown-only is destructive (−4.24); router-only +4.93, MLP-only +4.86, router+MLP +5.16 (default).
- **Mechanism — redundancy located (Table 5):** Pruning the trained Direct SFT model to its top-64 experts by REAP changes the average by only −0.12 (37.85 → 37.73), despite removing the subset that received ~42% of routed compute. Pruning trained UMoE to 64 experts costs 5.00 points (43.01 → 38.01), collapsing the UMoE-vs-DirectSFT gap from +5.16 to +0.28 — directly tying the gain to the reorganized extra capacity.
- **Optimization dynamics:** UMoE starts at higher loss (reorganization slightly degrades the model before training), then drops below Direct SFT within a few steps and ends lower (final train loss 0.396 vs 0.404).
- **Per-difficulty behavior:** Grouping problems by observed Direct SFT pass rate, the per-bin mean favors UMoE in every bin, with the largest gains (9–11 points) in the middle-difficulty bins.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty — moderate, well-positioned.** The individual ingredients are not new: REAP pruning (Lasby et al., 2026), sparse upcycling (Komatsuzaki et al., 2023), and Drop-Upcycling-style perturbation (Nakamura et al., 2025) all predate this work. The contribution is the specific recombination — prune-then-regrow-as-domain-specialization at fixed budget — plus the empirical demonstration that it generalizes with a frozen recipe. The framing as "turning generic capacity into domain capacity rather than adding capacity" is a useful conceptual reframing of upcycling.
- **Rigor — strong on the central claim.** The post-hoc pruning experiment (Table 5) is a genuinely clever mechanistic test: showing Direct SFT loses only −0.12 when half its pool is removed (vs −5.00 for UMoE) directly evidences the redundancy story rather than relying on loss curves. The data-scaling sweep (15/15 paired wins) and the ablation isolating one-sided perturbation as actively harmful (−4.24) are convincing.
- **Reproducibility — high.** All hyperparameters, calibration size, seed (42), and pruning ratio are stated; datasets are named (OpenMathReasoning, OpenCodeReasoning, Nemotron-SFT-Science-v2, Toucan, OpenSWE); training uses the open slime framework. The one gap is the in-house math corpus (~150k samples), which is not released and is the setting behind the headline "beats Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking" claim.
- **Significance — practical and timely.** The result that a near-zero-cost reorganization step (calibration on 1024 samples + weight copy/perturbation) yields consistent gains on a strong frontier base (Qwen3.5-35B-A3B) and on agentic SWE-bench (+6.0) is directly actionable for anyone doing domain post-training of MoE LLMs.
- **Red flags / caveats.** (1) The cross-domain Table 3 unweighted average conflates incomparable metrics (pass@1, avg@8) across columns; the "Unweighted Avg." column is illustrative only and should not be read as a single quality number. (2) Gains on Qwen3.5-35B-A3B (+1.67 math avg) are markedly smaller than on Qwen3-30B-A3B (+3.40), hinting that as base models improve and the inherited pool is already better-aligned, the headroom UMoE exploits may shrink — a trend the paper does not extrapolate. (3) The 50% pruning ratio is fixed throughout; adaptive ratios are explicitly deferred to future work, so it is unknown whether 50% is optimal or merely convenient. (4) Single-model-vendor evaluation (only Qwen-family MoEs with top-K=8) leaves open whether the recipe transfers to DeepSeek-style or shared-expert-heavy architectures with different routing.
- **Missing ablation.** No comparison against ESFT (expert-specialized fine-tuning, freezing non-relevant experts) or PERFT (routed PEFT) baselines as actual training runs — these are discussed in Related Work but never benchmarked head-to-head, so UMoE's advantage over cheaper adaptation methods is asserted, not measured.

## Strengths

- Preserves the exact deployment budget (E, K, params, inference cost) — a hard constraint many MoE-adaptation methods violate — making it drop-in compatible with existing serving stacks.
- Single frozen recipe across two architectures, five domains, 12 benchmarks, and three data scales; no per-domain tuning, which is unusual and credible for a method this simple.
- The mechanism analysis (post-hoc pruning, routing–saliency mismatch, optimization dynamics, per-difficulty pass rates) goes substantially beyond "it works" to explain why, with the post-hoc pruning result being particularly diagnostic.
- The strong-baseline experiment (in-house math, already above Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking) preempts the most likely criticism that gains come from a weak Direct SFT baseline.
- Honest about the one destructive configuration: one-sided perturbation is reported at −4.24 rather than buried, and the routing-collapse mechanism (Fig. 3) explains it.

## Limitations

- Only Qwen-family MoEs evaluated (Qwen3-30B-A3B, Qwen3.5-35B-A3B); generalization to DeepSeek-V4, Kimi-K2, or non-top-K routing schemes is unverified.
- Pruning ratio fixed at 50% throughout — no sensitivity analysis, and the authors flag adaptive ratios as future work.
- The headline in-house-math result depends on an unreleased corpus, limiting independent verification of the strong-baseline claim.
- Gains compress on the newer/bigger Qwen3.5-35B-A3B (+1.67 vs +3.40), suggesting the method's value may decrease as base pretraining improves expert alignment.
- No head-to-head training comparison against ESFT or PERFT, the most directly comparable adaptation baselines.
- The single perturbation scale σ=0.05 is presented without a σ sweep; it is unclear how sensitive the result is to this value.
- Evaluation is almost entirely avg@N with N=8/32 (high-variance, sampling-temperature-sensitive); only SWE-bench uses pass@1 over the full set.

## Relevance

与你的研究方向高度相关：本文处理的是前沿MoE大模型（Qwen3/3.5系列）在领域后训练中的专家容量重组问题，直接属于post-training / SFT / reasoning增强范畴，其"预算保持的剪枝-重生"范式对任何基于MoE的领域微调（尤其是math/code/agentic）都有可立即复用的工程价值，且其机制分析方法（REAP显著性、事后剪枝冗余诊断）可作为你评估其他MoE适配方法的参考工具。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the gain hold (or shrink further) on non-Qwen MoEs such as DeepSeek-V4 or Kimi-K2, whose expert specialization and routing statistics differ — especially architectures with a large shared expert?
- What is the sensitivity to pruning ratio? Is 50% optimal, or would a domain-adaptive ratio (e.g., based on the saliency distribution's knee) do better, particularly on already-well-aligned base models?
- How does UMoE interact with downstream RL (e.g., GRPO) on top of the reorganized SFT checkpoint — does the reorganized pool also help in the RL phase, or is the SFT-stage gain erased?
- Can the reorganization be made iterative or online (re-prune and re-expand mid-SFT as specialization shifts), rather than a one-shot pre-SFT step?
- Is the σ=0.05 perturbation scale robust, and is symmetric Gaussian perturbation optimal vs. structured perturbations (e.g., low-rank or dropout-style) that might seed more diverse expert functions?
- Does UMoE compose with PEFT methods (PERFT, LoRA-on-experts), or does full-parameter SFT remain necessary for the post-hoc-pruning-fragility (the −5.00 result) that underpins the gain?
- How do the gains behave on smaller calibration sets or noisier domain data — the paper uses 1024 clean in-distribution samples; is the REAP ranking stable under calibration drift?
