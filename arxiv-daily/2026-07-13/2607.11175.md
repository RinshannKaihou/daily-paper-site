> **一句话总结：** 本文是一篇综述，将医学智能体形式化为部分可观测下的序贯决策过程，提出"辅助—协作—全自主"三级自治分类与"框架/能力/环境"三轴 scaling spine，主张把临床环境扩展（工具+数据+clinical gym）与自我演化视为通往可信赖医学影像 agent 的最可行且最被忽视的杠杆，系统梳理了 300+ 文献（侧重 2025–2026 进展），涵盖 benchmark、训练范式（SFT/DPO/RLVR/self-play）、放射/病理/眼科等科室应用、部署互操作与监管风险。

> **TL;DR:** This is a large survey (>300 references) that reorganizes the medical-imaging agent literature around a deployment-first question: what tasks, contamination-resistant benchmarks, and interactive training environments must an agent master before clinical trust? It formalizes a medical agent as a six-module sequential decision process under partial observability, proposes a three-level autonomy taxonomy (assisted/cooperative/fully autonomous), and threads the whole field along a "scaling spine" of framework scaling (topology N_topo), capability scaling (loop depth D_loop), and environment scaling (|E|), foregrounding environment scaling and clinical self-evolution as the most actionable frontier. It catalogs 60+ benchmarks (e.g., MedAgentBench's 300 FHIR tasks, MedAgentGym's 72K tasks, HealthBench's 48,562 rubric criteria across 5,000 conversations) and 60+ systems, transferring concrete gains reported by surveyed works (e.g., Med-R1's +29.94% accuracy on Qwen2-VL-2B surpassing models 36× larger, ATPO's Qwen3-8B beating GPT-4o on diagnostic benchmarks, MMedAgent-RL's +23.6% on reasoning). It treats self-evolution as an aspirational frontier rather than a solved capability, and pairs the roadmap with a five-layer risk analysis (perception 3D gap, hallucination, cascade failures, deployment cost, governance).

## 核心贡献

- **部署优先的组织原则**：与同类医学 agent 综述"先能力后评估"不同，本文从真实临床场景、抗污染 benchmark 与可交互训练环境切入，把"现实就绪度"作为贯穿全文的主线（Section 3）。
- **形式化与三级自治分类**：将医学 agent 定义为六模块（感知 P、推理 R、规划 L、记忆 M、工具使用 T、动作反思 F）在部分可观测下的序贯决策系统 A=(P,R,L,M,T,F)，并提出 Assisted / Cooperative / Fully Autonomous 三级自治分类，统一了异质文献。
- **单一 scaling spine**：提出 `Readiness(A) ≈ f(N_topo, D_loop, |E|)`，把框架扩展（拓扑）、能力扩展（认知环路深度）与环境扩展（工具/数据/接口）作为正交可叠加的三条轴，明确"任何单轴扩展都会饱和"。
- **抬高 self-evolution 的地位**：把自我演化从被埋没的能力要点提升为 spine 的终点，系统迁移通用域 self-improving agent、agent gym、test-time compute scaling 的经验到医学影像。
- **临床环境扩展作为最可行杠杆**：论证 PACS/EHR/FHIR 生态下连接一个新专科模型/档案/接口，往往比再训一轮带来更高"就绪度/投入"比，并梳理 clinical gym（MedAgentGym、Healthcare AI GYM、MedAgentSim 等）。
- **完整 benchmark 与系统目录**：Table 1 给出按自治度分层的 60+ benchmark，Table 3 给出按科室分类的 60+ agent 系统，并附 GitHub 仓库（Awesome-Medical-Agents）持续维护。
- **五层风险与三时间窗路线图**：沿 perception→reasoning→coordination→deployment→governance 给出失败模式-缓解策略映射（Fig. 8），并按近/中/长期给出研究方向（Table 4、Fig. 9）。

## Key Contributions

- **Deployment-first organizing principle**: Unlike concurrent capability-centric surveys, the paper opens from the deployment destination — real clinical scenarios, contamination-resistant benchmarks, and interactive training environments — rather than treating evaluation as a closing appendix.
- **Formal definition + three-level autonomy taxonomy**: Medical agents are formalized as six interacting cognitive modules A=(P,R,L,M,T,F) acting under partial observability, with a three-level taxonomy (Assisted / Cooperative / Fully Autonomous) that unifies heterogeneous prior systems.
- **A single scaling spine**: `Readiness(A) ≈ f(N_topo, D_loop, |E|)` threads the whole survey, treating framework scaling (topology), capability scaling (loop depth), and environment scaling (reachable tool/data/interface ecosystem) as orthogonal, complementary, non-substitutable axes.
- **Elevation of self-evolution**: Lifts self-evolving systems from a buried bullet to the destination of the spine, transferring lessons from general-domain self-improving agents, agent gyms, and test-time compute scaling into medical imaging.
- **Foregrounding environment scaling**: Argues that, for agents already inhabiting PACS/EHR/FHIR ecosystems, connecting a new specialist model, archive, or FHIR endpoint often buys more readiness per unit effort than another round of fine-tuning.
- **Comprehensive benchmark and system catalogs**: Table 1 inventories 60+ benchmarks stratified by autonomy level; Table 3 catalogs 60+ representative agent systems by specialty, with a companion GitHub repository.
- **Five-layer risk map + three-horizon roadmap**: A pipeline-aligned failure-mode analysis (perception 3D gap, hallucination, cascade failures, deployment cost, governance) paired with near/medium/long-term research directions.

## Methodology

This is a **systematic literature survey**, not an empirical study, so "methodology" refers to its review protocol and organizational framework.

**Review protocol.** Over January 2022 – June 2026, the authors queried major literature databases and leading CV/ML/medical-informatics venues, conjoining three axes: an *agent* axis (agent, agentic, tool use, multi-agent, autonomous), a *medical-imaging* axis (radiology, pathology, CT, MRI, X-ray, report generation), and a *foundation-model* axis (LLM, foundation model, vision-language). The search returned 1,174 records (942 after de-duplication); three reviewers independently screened against a single inclusion criterion — *an autonomous loop that perceives, reasons, and acts on an imaging/clinical task* — with a fourth arbitrating disagreements, leaving 300+ studies supplemented by a snowball pass for the latest 2026 entries. Over 300 references are consolidated, with particular emphasis on 2025–2026 advances in self-evolution and agentic environments.

**Formal model.** A medical agent is defined as A=(P,R,L,M,T,F) — perception, reasoning, planning, memory, tool use, action/reflection — operating under *partial observability* (the agent never observes the patient's true state directly, only indirect evidence such as images, priors, and tool outputs). This reframes medical agency as acting under uncertainty rather than single-pass prediction.

**Three-level autonomy taxonomy** (Fig. 1):
- *Level 1 Assisted*: agent suggests under direct clinician control (e.g., interactive segmentation, report drafting).
- *Level 2 Cooperative*: agent decomposes tasks and invokes tools within a human-in-the-loop framework; clinician retains override authority (e.g., multi-agent differential-diagnosis pipelines).
- *Level 3 Fully Autonomous*: end-to-end workflow execution with exception-based human escalation. No current system fully achieves Level 3; emerging closed-loop radiology pipelines and self-evolving ecosystems are the closest approximations.

**The scaling spine** (Eq. 1, Fig. 2): `Readiness(A) ≈ f(N_topo, D_loop, |E|)`.
- *Framework scaling (N_topo)*: single tool-augmented agent → role-based multi-agent team → orchestration topologies (chain/tree/graph, learnable runtime graphs).
- *Capability scaling (D_loop)*: deepening the perceive-reason-plan-act-reflect harness around a fixed backbone; the central claim is that competence tracks loop engineering more than parameter count.
- *Environment scaling (|E|)*: enriching tools, data sources, and interfaces (MCP, FHIR, PACS), realized progressively through an adaptation ladder (prompting → SFT → DPO → RLVR → self-play → self-evolution).

**Benchmark stratification** (Fig. 3, Table 1): three levels of increasing autonomy/realism — Level 1 isolated single-capability (VQA-RAD, MedXpertQA, CheXbench, MedHallu); Level 2 tool-augmented interactive agency (MedAgentBench, MedCalc-Bench, AgentClinic, MedBrowseComp, ABRA's 21-tool OHIF/Orthanc setup); Level 3 system-level clinical operation (MedHELM, MedChain, MedAgentGym, PhysicianBench, EHR-Complex).

**Training-time adaptation ladder** (Fig. 6, Section 6): prompting/ICL → SFT → preference optimization (DPO/RLHF) → RLVR (GRPO and its 2025–2026 descendants DAPO, Dr. GRPO, GSPO, CISPO, and the agentic/multi-turn family MT-GRPO, ARPO, GTPO, Turn-PPO, AT2PO) → self-play/continual improvement (Agent Hospital, AMIE, EvoPatient, Healthcare AI GYM) → self-evolution across weights/memory/tools/architecture.

## Results & Highlights

Because this is a survey, "results" are quantitative findings consolidated from the cited literature rather than new experiments. The most concrete quantitative claims surfaced:

- **Med-R1 [122]**: GRPO-based training on Qwen2-VL-2B across eight imaging modalities yields a **+29.94% accuracy improvement over the base model, surpassing models 36× larger** — evidence that RLVR with verifiable rewards can substitute for parameter scaling in medical VLMs.
- **MMedAgent-RL [269]**: curriculum learning combined with multi-agent RL on Qwen2.5-VL (triage + attending physician agents) achieves a **+23.6% improvement on medical reasoning benchmarks**, learning when to defer between generalist and specialist rather than hard-coding routing.
- **CARE [50]**: the evidence-grounded agentic framework reports a **+10.9% VQA accuracy** gain attributable to grounding and accountability, on standard medical VQA.
- **ATPO [22]**: adaptive tree-structured policy optimization, formulating multi-turn medical dialogue as a Hierarchical MDP, enables a **Qwen3-8B model to surpass GPT-4o on diagnostic benchmarks** — a small-model-beats-frontier result via adaptive rollout budget allocation.
- **AMIE [241]**: LLM agents trained through autonomous physician-patient role-play **outperform primary care physicians in diagnostic accuracy** — the strongest self-play result transferred into the medical dialogue setting.
- **Kim et al. [117] (hallucination)**: general-purpose models surpass medically specialized ones on hallucination-free generation, **76.6% vs 51.3%**, suggesting narrow tuning can erode the factual grounding that broad pretraining confers.
- **Ghafoor et al. [73] (cascade mitigation)**: structured peer review among agents yields an **89% reduction in ethical violations** — evidence that the remedy for multi-agent pathology is often more, not less, multi-agent structure.
- **Ekram [54] (red-teaming)**: an "Educational Authority" framing bypasses guardrails **83.3% of the time**, arguing for context-aware rather than keyword-based safety in medical VLMs.
- **Snell et al. [228] (test-time compute)**: optimal test-time-compute allocation **outperforms 14× larger models** — the inference-time scaling result the survey transfers to medical agents.
- **HealthBench [8]**: establishes **48,562 physician-written rubric criteria across 5,000 clinical conversations** as the rubric standard now serving double duty as both eval and structured RL reward (Health-SCORE [283]).
- **Training gymms at scale**: MedAgentGym [274] exposes **72K biomedical tasks** as executable code with online RL; Healthcare AI GYM [100] spans **10 clinical domains with 3.6K+ tasks and 135 domain-specific tools**; HealthAdminBench [15] covers **135 healthcare administration tasks across 4 GUIs**; EHR-Complex [197] provides **52K interactive MIMIC-IV reasoning tasks**.
- **Survey corpus scale**: **1,174 records → 942 after de-duplication → 300+ retained studies**, consolidated into **over 300 references** with emphasis on 2025–2026 work — a concrete measure of the survey's coverage.

*No quantitative results are original to this paper; all numbers above are attributed to cited works. If the paper is read only for new empirical findings, it reports none.*

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: The paper's intellectual novelty is organizational rather than empirical. Its three real differentiators — (1) deployment-first ordering, (2) the unifying `f(N_topo, D_loop, |E|)` spine that subsumes prior per-tool/per-module taxonomies, (3) foregrounding environment scaling and self-evolution — are genuine contributions over concurrent medical-agent surveys (which it cites as capability-centric with evaluation as a downstream appendix). However, the scaling-spine idea is explicitly adapted from the general-domain environment-scaling literature [62, 97], so the originality is in the *transfer and synthesis*, not in the axes themselves.
- **Rigor**: The review protocol is disclosed and reasonably systematic (1,174 → 942 → 300+), with a single explicit inclusion criterion and arbitration. That said, inter-rater agreement statistics (e.g., Cohen's κ) are not reported, so reproducibility of the screening step cannot be verified quantitatively — a gap relative to a formal PRISMA-style systematic review.
- **Reproducibility**: High for a survey: the companion GitHub repo, full benchmark catalog with data/code links (Table 1), and system catalog (Table 3) give actionable entry points. The survey itself is descriptive and therefore fully reproducible; the cited systems vary widely in their own reproducibility.
- **Significance**: Likely to become a reference organizing framework for the medical-agent subfield, precisely because it imposes one consistent axis (the scaling spine) across architecture, cognition, training, application, deployment, and risk — most prior surveys keep these separate. The timing (July 2026, consolidating the 2025–2026 burst in RLVR/agentic-RL and clinical gyms) is well-judged.
- **Red flags / honest concerns**:
  - *Aspirational framing overstated*: the paper repeatedly positions self-evolution and Level-3 autonomy as "the destination," yet its own text concedes "no current system fully achieves Level 3" and self-evolution is "an aspirational frontier rather than a solved capability." The spine implies more progress than the evidence supports.
  - *Selective quantitative evidence*: the headline numbers (+29.94%, +23.6%, +10.9%, 8B-beats-GPT-4o) are drawn from cited works of widely varying rigor and auditability; none are re-evaluated here, and no ablation cross-cuts them. A reader could mistake survey-compiled numbers for a controlled comparison.
  - *Weak-baseline concern in cited claims*: claims such as "general models beat medical ones on hallucination, 76.6% vs 51.3%" (Kim et al.) depend entirely on which medical models were probed — the survey flags the counterintuitive nature but does not stress-test it.
  - *Missing ablations of its own framework*: the spine's three axes are asserted to be "complementary rather than substitutable," but no experiment (and the survey has none) demonstrates that scaling one axis in isolation saturates — this is a conceptual claim presented as established.
  - *Data quirk*: a few non-imaging agents (EHR operation, therapeutic reasoning) are included "only where they offer an architectural lesson," a scope decision that is defensible but somewhat ad hoc and could inflate the apparent imaging-agent corpus.
  - *Citation density vs. depth*: with 300+ references, many systems get one or two sentences; depth is uneven across specialties (radiology/pathology dense, ophthalmology sparse — a gap the authors themselves acknowledge).

## Strengths

- A genuinely unifying organizational axis (the scaling spine) that lets architecture, cognition, training, deployment, and risk be read through one lens — most prior surveys keep these separate.
- Deployment-first inversion is the right call for a clinical-AI survey: benchmarks and training environments belong up front, not as an appendix.
- Exhaustive, current catalogs: 60+ benchmarks stratified by autonomy level (Table 1), 60+ systems stratified by specialty (Table 3), with a maintained GitHub repo — unusually actionable for a survey.
- Honest about the gap between Level-1 competence and Level-3 readiness, and about self-evolution being aspirational.
- Strong treatment of the 2025–2026 agentic-RL algorithm burst (Table 2: DAPO, Dr. GRPO, GSPO, CISPO, MT-GRPO, ARPO, Turn-PPO, AT2PO) and of clinical gyms as the binding constraint — topics under-covered elsewhere.
- A pipeline-aligned five-layer risk map (Fig. 8) that pairs each failure mode with mitigation coverage (Strong/Moderate/Weak/None) is more useful than a flat risk list.
- Transfers concrete general-domain self-evolution mechanisms (SEAL, Darwin Godel Machine, ADAS, Gödel Agent) to medical imaging, naming exactly what each level (weights / memory+tools / architecture+code) would mean in a clinical setting.

## Limitations

- **No original experiments**: every quantitative claim is borrowed from cited works; the survey cannot verify or reconcile conflicting numbers across them.
- **Inter-rater statistics absent**: despite a disclosed screening protocol, no κ or agreement metric is reported, so the 1,174 → 300+ funnel is not auditable as a formal systematic review.
- **Self-evolution over-promised**: the spine's destination framing outpaces the evidence; the paper itself concedes Level 3 is unreached and self-evolution unsolved, creating a tension between rhetoric and substance.
- **Uneven depth across specialties**: radiology and pathology receive detailed treatment; ophthalmology, oncology, neurology are thinner, and dermatology/cardiology appear mainly in the application figure rather than the text.
- **Conceptual claims presented without verification**: "the three axes are complementary, not substitutable" and "competence tracks loop engineering more than parameter count" are asserted as findings but supported only by citation, not by any controlled comparison.
- **Scope boundary is fuzzy**: inclusion of selected non-imaging therapeutic/EHR agents is justified case-by-case, which is defensible but somewhat ad hoc and could be read as inflating the imaging corpus.
- **Limited critical engagement with negative results**: e.g., benchmarks cautioning that multi-agent gains are "uneven and sometimes illusory" once cost is accounted for [55, 67, 321] are cited but not deeply reconciled with the survey's multi-agent-positive framing.
- **Algorithm table coverage**: Table 2 lists recent policy-optimization algorithms but does not compare them empirically or recommend which suit which clinical task.

## Relevance

For a researcher tracking LLM agents, self-improving/self-evolving systems, and agentic RL, this survey is directly on-topic: it consolidates the 2025–2026 wave of GRPO-style RLVR, agent gyms, and self-evolving agent architectures, and explicitly maps general-domain self-improvement mechanisms (SEAL, ADAS, Gödel Agent, Darwin Godel Machine) onto a concrete vertical domain. It is especially useful as a structured entry point into medical-agent benchmarks and into environment scaling as a complement to parameter and test-time-compute scaling.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How would the three scaling axes (N_topo, D_loop, |E|) be measured quantitatively so that the "complementary, not substitutable" claim could be tested rather than asserted?
- Given Kim et al.'s result that general models beat medically specialized ones on hallucination-free generation (76.6% vs 51.3%), under what conditions does domain-specific fine-tuning actively erode factual grounding, and does this generalize beyond the studied subset?
- The survey concedes "no current system fully achieves Level 3" autonomy — what minimum combination of verifiable reward, audit trail, and exception-based escalation would make a Level-3 pilot ethically defensible in a single, well-scoped workflow (e.g., stroke CT triage)?
- Multi-agent cascade failures are identified as a topology-level pathology; can the 89% ethical-violation reduction from structured peer review [73] and the shared-pool vulnerability from MedSentry [31] be unified into a predictive topology-risk metric?
- The adaptation ladder ends in self-evolution across weights/memory/tools/architecture, but medicine is unusual in having intrinsic verifiable signals (measurement consistency, guideline conformance, follow-up outcomes) — which of these reward sources is most sample-efficient, and which carries the highest risk of reward hacking?
- How should the FDA Predetermined Change Control Plan and EU AI Act be extended so that a self-evolving agent whose *architecture or control code* changes post-deployment (not just weights) can still be certified?
- The benchmark catalog shows a persistent Level-1 → Level-3 performance gap widening with 2026 long-horizon benchmarks — is this gap primarily a perception (3D/video) problem, a memory/context problem, or a credit-assignment problem, and which axis of the spine most directly closes it?
