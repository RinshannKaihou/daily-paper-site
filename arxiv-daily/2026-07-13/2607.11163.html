Unified Gradient Projection: Language-Balanced Continual Learning for Multilingual Low-Resource ASR

Ziang Ren · Guodong Lin · Yuchen Ai · Kaize Tan · Wei-Qiang Zhang · Tsinghua University, Dept. of Electronic Engineering · arXiv v1, 13 Jul 2026 · NSFC 62276153

UGP projects gradients onto a language-balanced reference, pushing multilingual ASR forgetting to near zero without losing new-language plasticity.

01 Methodology

Sequential multilingual adaptation of Whisper: fine-tuning on a new low-resource target language catastrophically degrades previously learned languages.

Foundation preserved. Encoder frozen on Whisper-medium / large-v3; only decoder + embeddings update. Whisper-small uses full-parameter fine-tuning to avoid capacity under-utilization.

The core novelty. Replace A-GEM's random reference gradient with a language-balanced one — draw a fixed n=4 utterances uniformly from each prior language per step, so minor languages contribute equally to the reference.

B̃_i ~ UniformSample(B_i, n), i = 1..K g_ref = (1 / Kn) Σ_i Σ_{x∈B̃_i} ∇_θ L(x)

Conflict is detected via ⟨g_cur, g_ref⟩. If the angle is obtuse, project the current gradient onto the orthogonal complement of g_ref:

⟨g_cur, g_ref⟩ ≥ 0 → g_final = g_cur else g_final = g_cur − (⟨g_cur,g_ref⟩ / (‖g_ref‖²+ε))·g_ref

Two-level regulation. A mixed batch L_total = L_cur + λ·L_replay (λ=1) provides data-driven representation correction and joint multilingual transfer, while the projection governs the update trajectory inside that landscape.

Mechanism (Fig. 3): post-convergence pairwise gradient cosines under UGP cluster near 0 (0.08, −0.06, 0.03); full FT shows strong negatives (−0.50, −0.57). Evidence UGP reshapes the converged parameter geometry, not just single steps.

03 Strengths vs. limitations

A simple, effective recipe — but the empirical case rests on narrow baselines and unaudited variance.

+ Strengths

  • Drop-in A-GEM tweak with consistent gains across scales & data regimes, no extra memory overhead.
  • Clean ablation proves gradient-level projection and data-level replay are complementary, not redundant.
  • Interpretable mechanism — near-orthogonal cross-lingual gradients (cosines ≈ 0) vs FT's negatives (−0.50, −0.57).
  • Robust under scarcity down to 5 h/language — a genuinely useful finding for low-resource practitioners.

− Limitations

  • No sensitivity analysis — λ, n=4, buffer 2,000 all hardcoded to single values.
  • Missing baselines — no EWC or LoRA/adapter comparison despite discussing them.
  • No variance / significance; headline 0.04% may be seed-sensitive.
  • Easy rehearsal set (English, French at 6.65% baseline) inflates the stability gain.
  • Narrow scope — 5 target / 4 prior languages; large-K scalability untested.

04 Questions for further exploration

Q1 · SCALE

How does UGP behave when K grows to 20+ (CL-MASR scale)? Does per-language uniform sampling stay feasible, and does FWER stay bounded?

Q2 · HARD REHEARSAL

Near-zero forgetting was shown on easy languages (English/French). Does the claim survive when rehearsal languages are themselves hard for Whisper?

Q3 · ROBUSTNESS

How seed-sensitive is FWER 0.04%, and does the gap to ER (4.11%) hold over multiple runs with significance?

Q4 · ADAPTERS

Does combining UGP's balanced reference gradient with LoRA-based parameter isolation stack, or do they interfere?
