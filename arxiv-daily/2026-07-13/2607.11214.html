A Novel Method to Evaluate Models on Unreliable, Noisy and Inconsistent Labels: Adaptive Resolution Label Aggregation (ARLA) ↗
Natasha Randall · Gernot Heisenberg · TH Köln, Institute of Information Science · PREPRINT, v1 · 13 Jul 2026
segmentation-evaluation noisy-labels remote-sensing flood-prediction label-aggregation inference-time
Key Metric
0.52 → 0.80
+0.28 swing toward true performance on the Emilia-Romagna flood event (May 2023), at 1×1 subpatches, τ=2.5%. Recall stays 0.63 → flips the conclusion to under-prediction.
Why This Matters
A lightweight, retrain-free inference-time method for evaluating segmentation models under structurally noisy labels — directly relevant to remote sensing and disaster mapping.
Critical Edge
Parameter selection is circular. Choosing (s, τ) "to fit the size of the cloud-cover holes" presupposes you already know where the label errors are — the very information practitioners usually lack. Reported numbers are parameter-dependent and potentially arbitrary.

Methodology 3-step inference-time aggregation

01 · PARTITION

Re-grid into s×s

Split binary label L and prediction P (same spatial domain) each into an s×s grid of contiguous subpatches.

02 · AGGREGATE

Re-binarize at τ

Per subpatch, set all pixels to 1 if positive-class proportion ≥ sensitivity threshold τ%, else 0. Same (s, τ) applied to both.

03 · EVALUATE

Score on L′ vs P′

Compute standard segmentation metrics (precision, recall, F1) on the aggregated representations. Model is never retrained.

s=1 → image-level classification s=pixel res → original segmentation resolution ↔ expected error size sensitivity τ ↔ label noise level multi-class via majority / priority subpatch assignment

WHY BASELINES FAIL

Buffer ignores too much area (misleadingly high); soft labels both fail to remove error regions and erode accurate borders. Both break on large spatial label errors.

Strengths & Limitations side-by-side

Strengths

  • Tackles a real under-studied gap: evaluation (not training) under label noise.
  • Inference-time only — no retraining, no label cleaning, no extra annotators.
  • Two interpretable parameters with clear semantics (resolution ↔ error size, τ ↔ noise).
  • Honest, self-critical discussion; parameters must not be tuned to maximize metrics.
  • Open-source code and fully public datasets (Copernicus, Sentinel-2, Cityscapes).
  • Flood case study flips a qualitative conclusion (over- vs under-prediction) — compelling.

Limitations

  • Only two hand-picked patches + one flood event; no benchmark (acknowledged as future work).
  • No statistical uncertainty, CIs, or significance tests on any metric.
  • Optimal (s, τ) presupposes knowledge of the label-error size distribution — main practical weakness.
  • Could conceal genuine small-scale errors within an aggregation block (admitted).
  • Baselines (buffer, soft label) appear tuned less favourably than ARLA.
  • "Reliable label" reference is author-constructed, not independently audited.
  • Generalization beyond flood/RS (medical, urban) claimed but not demonstrated.

Open Questions for further exploration

Q1

How does ARLA behave on a large public benchmark (Cityscapes, ADE20K, medical) with controlled injected noise, vs buffer / soft-label / fuzzy-F1 / confident-learning — with CIs and a fixed parameter protocol?

Q2

Is there a principled, data-driven way to choose (s, τ) from label/prediction disagreement statistics alone, without annotator knowledge?

Q3

How does ARLA interact with other metrics (IoU, Boundary IoU, Hausdorff) — does aggregation systematically favour some over others?

Q4

Can the Fig. 6 power-law be formalized to predict the true-resolution asymptote and detect over-aggregation?

Q5

Does applying ARLA during training (loss-space coarse-graining) change conclusions — and risk making metric and objective circular?

Q6

What is the risk of an adversarial / lazy model exploiting the coarsening (gross over-prediction that still wins each subpatch majority)?
