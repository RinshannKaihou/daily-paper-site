Robustness Study · Post-Hoc Calibration

TEMP is directionally more stable than ISO — yet no statistically separable advantage survives Holm correction.

Condition-Stratified Robustness Analysis of Post-Hoc Calibration Methods for Probabilistic Classifiers ↗ Gurdeep Singh Virdee · Fergana State Technical University 2026-07-13

Key Metric
0.9895Holm-adjusted p — every H1 test
Identical across all four conditions. H1 (TEMP−ISO discrimination) carries no family-wise separable signal in either direction.
Why This Matters
A condition-stratified, pre-registered, Holm-corrected reporting template that exposes what aggregate calibration metrics hide — but its unnamed corpus limits benchmark value.
Critical Edge
The corpus, base classifier, and conditions C1–C4 are all unnamed — a reader cannot reproduce a single number. This is the paper's most serious red flag.

MMethodology

Post-hoc calibration is framed as a map g:[0,1]→[0,1] over a trained classifier's outputs, with the ideal condition P(Y=1 | g(f(x))=q) = q. Two complementary calibrator families are compared across four controlled in-dataset strata C1–C4.

  • TEMP — single-parameter monotonic logit rescaling g(p)=σ(logit(p)/T), fit on a held-out partition. Low variance, parametric.
  • ISO — stepwise non-decreasing non-parametric map fit to (p_i, y_i) pairs. Higher flexibility, greater variance under small calibration sets.
  • Platt considered but excluded — its two-parameter form confounds the single-parameter TEMP comparison.
  • Four pre-specified hypothesis groups: H1 discrimination deltas (+Holm step-down), H2 Brier differences, H3 calibration slope, H4 AUROC differences.
  • Deliberate asymmetry: only H1 receives inferential decoration; H2–H4 are directional only (registry lacks CIs / effect sizes).

RResults & Highlights

The four conditions tell divergent stories. Discrimination never separates (H1), but probability accuracy (H2) and proportional calibration (H3) favor TEMP decisively — directionally.

±Strengths & Limitations

Strengths
  • Pre-registered H1–H4 structure with explicit multiplicity control — a standard many calibration papers skip.
  • Honest disclosure of inferential gaps for H2–H4 instead of fabricated statistical authority.
  • Clean separation of discrimination, probability accuracy, and calibration slope.
  • Motivating example — pooled aggregation hiding ISO's C3 sign reversal — is concrete and persuasive.
  • Scope discipline: no external transportability claims are made.
Limitations
  • Corpus, base classifier, and the nature of C1–C4 are entirely undescribed.
  • No inferential statistics (CIs, effect sizes) for H2, H3, or H4 — directional only.
  • No external validation cohorts; all conclusions bounded to C1–C4.
  • C1–C4 are fixed strata with no sub-stratum heterogeneity audit.
  • H1's flat p=0.9895 provides no discriminating evidence either way.
  • No between-calibrator slope tests for H3 despite the large gap.
  • Single author; no described code or data release.

?Questions for Further Exploration

  1. What are conditions C1–C4 concretely — perturbation type, sample sizes, class balances?
  2. Would H2–H4 survive bootstrap CIs / paired tests / effect-size intervals, or are the directional TEMP advantages an artifact of the opaque corpus?
  3. Does the TEMP-vs-ISO slope gap (0.76–0.95 vs 0.14–0.27) persist on named benchmarks (CIFAR-10/100, ImageNet-C), and how does it interact with calibration-set size?
  4. How does the framework extend to multiclass (Dirichlet / matrix scaling), where ISO's variance disadvantage amplifies?
  5. Would adding Platt scaling and ensemble recalibration change the condition-level conclusions?
  6. Can the "registry-locked values" protocol be operationalized as a publicly hash-verifiable artifact?
