Implicit Neural Networks as Static Controllers: Certificates and Performance Separation

Giuseppe C. Calafiore · Laurent El Ghaoui ·2026-07-13

Key finding

Rewriting a neural controller as an implicit fixed-point exposes its algebraic loop — turning stability and quadratic performance into checkable LMI certificates.

A static neural feedback law becomes a trainable linear interconnection closed through a known activation map and solved as a fixed point. Well-posedness, exponential stability, and discounted infinite-horizon cost all reduce to LMIs/IQCs — and on a hard-constrained scalar unstable plant the two-ReLU controller provably beats every finite-order dynamic linear controller.

01

Methodology — representation, not layers

Instead of a stack of layers, the controller is written as a fixed point. W need not be block-triangular, so the inference graph may contain loops — the price is that well-posedness must be checked.

η = ϕ( Wη + U y + b ),    u = K y + Vη + d
% closed loop with stacked state s = [x ; η] → x⁺ = F s, u = G s

Well-posedness tests

Perron–Frobenius λpf(|W|)<1 and convex row-sum condition ‖W‖∞ ≤ κ<1 guarantee a unique fixed point before the law is ever used for feedback.

Analysis certificates

Three assumptions (nonexpansive activations, static & incremental sector bounds) turn the loop into a static IQC sᵗ MΛ s ≥ 0, yielding LMIs in (P,Λ) for stability (Thm 2–3) and performance E[Jβ] ≤ tr(P S₀) (Thm 4–5).

Train–certify–retrain gate

Nonconvex finite-horizon training with row-wise projection W ← Πκ(W); gradients via the adjoint fixed point. A candidate is accepted only if an independent post-training LMI is feasible (Algorithm 1).

Separation mechanism

On plant (43) the actuator limit |u₀|≤1 forces any linear gain |κ|≤1, an affine first-move cost every dynamic linear controller must pay. A saturated two-ReLU law u = −sat(1.5x) reaches the origin in ≤2 steps.

03

Strengths vs limitations

Strengths

  • Clean, reusable representation: feedforward ReLU, residual, LSTM, transformer special cases all analyzable with standard LMI/IQC tools — plus a separate, checkable well-posedness test.
  • Complete proof of a strict constrained-control separation against an entire controller class (Cdyn), not a numerical comparison — rare in certified neuro-control.
  • Honest framing: simulations labelled as such, heuristic training stated upfront, limitations enumerated.
  • General comparison template (Thm 9 + Llin) turns a future certified INC into a separation proof for general LTI plants.

Limitations

  • All separations are for a single scalar unstable plant; no multivariable or output-feedback separation is proven.
  • Joint controller/certificate optimization is nonconvex; training is heuristic, the gate only checks a fixed-controller LMI — no convergence or global-optimality guarantee.
  • Global sector LMIs are conservative for ReLU/saturation on unstable plants; regional certificates for W≠0 are flagged open and not demonstrated.
  • No disturbance/robustness margins, no observer-plus-readout, no dynamic implicit controllers — all listed as open.
04

Open questions

Q1

Can the scalar separation of Thm 6 lift to multivariable plants, or is the "drive-to-zero-in-two-steps" mechanism fundamentally scalar?

Q2

How tight are the global sector LMIs for unstable plants — do regional multipliers or activation-pattern enumeration shrink conservatism for W≠0?

Q3

Does the train–certify–retrain gate converge on harder benchmarks, or does the certificate-aware penalty frequently stall at infeasibility?

Q4

How does the saturated INC compare against explicit MPC and anti-windup saturated LQR — methods that can also implement piecewise-affine policies?

Q5

Can the representation extend to dynamic implicit controllers (hidden η carrying state across time) while preserving the LMI/IQC structure?

Q6

Does the certified two-state gap (2.5×) survive replacing the dense-search linear baseline with a provably optimal one?
