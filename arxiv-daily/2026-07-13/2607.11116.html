01

Methodology — a port-Hamiltonian anchored DEQ, audited

phase-space z=(q,p) · Anderson m=5, cap 300

The implicit state is phase-space structured z=(q,p), evolved by a damped, anchored, symplectic-Euler update conditioned on a context c:

qt+1 = qt + ε∇pK(pt,c) − ε r(c) pt + ε ra(q0−qt)
pt+1 = pt − ε∇qU(qt+1,c) − ε r(c) qt+1 + ε ra(p0−pt)

Fixed point solved by Anderson acceleration from the learned start point z₀; gradients flow via the implicit-function theorem through an adjoint solve.

The key methodological move — substitution test (T1): at evaluation, feed the solver's start point directly to the decoder, skipping the fixed-point solve. This isolates what the solver computes from the pathway flowing through it — unlike zeroing h∗, which deletes both at once.

03

Strengths

  • Rare intellectual honesty: designs experiments to kill its own preferred explanation (anchor causation) and reports the null.
  • The substitution test + four-test protocol are cheap, transferable to far larger models than those studied.
  • Per-seed reporting deliberately avoids the pooling trap (the blow-up seed would otherwise be invisible).
  • Full reproducibility: code, logs, dataset SHA-256, analysis scripts, free-GPU budget.
  • The confounded-ablation finding is a concrete methodological lesson likely to affect how implicit-module papers are reviewed.
04

Limitations

  • Narrow scope: one architecture family, two binary tasks, small phase dimensions (6–8); claim is design-specific.
  • Graph pipeline's custom VJP returns zero cotangents — fully-differentiated config validated on only one task.
  • Both tasks are solvable by a direct pathway — exactly where starvation bites; genuine-iteration tasks untested.
  • Blow-up regime observed only once (1/5 seeds) plus the gate-modified run; frequency uncharacterized.
  • Frozen / from-scratch encoders only; no end-to-end finetuning of a large encoder.
  • Single independent author, no peer review at posting (though CC-BY and fully open).

06

Open questions

where the negative result could break
  1. Does lazy identity collapse survive tasks where the direct context pathway provably cannot solve the problem — testing the conjecture that such tasks exert the missing gradient pressure?
  2. How do current looped-transformer and recurrent-depth LLMs fare under the substitution test — do any actually compute, or do some copy their loop-initial state?
  3. Is the substitution-vs-zeroing confound present in published ablation tables of implicit-layer papers, and would re-running them change conclusions?
  4. Can the four-test protocol extend to attention / CoT-style “reasoning,” where the module is a token-generation loop rather than a fixed-point solve?
  5. Is solver blow-up a continuous phase transition or a rare instability — as a function of anchor strength, spectral clamp, and gate expressivity?
  6. Does end-to-end encoder finetuning change the gradient landscape enough to make the implicit layer contribute, or does starvation persist?
