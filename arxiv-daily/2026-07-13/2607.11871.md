> **一句话总结：** 本文从表示层面（而非输入-输出层面）解释 LLM-as-Judge 评分偏差：在 7 个评判模型、7 类偏差、9 个基准上发现偏差沿低维类型特异性子空间分布并随层深锐化；沿该子空间做双向激活引导可在干净输入上复现偏差、在被污染输入上恢复公平评分（随机方向效果小一个数量级），且基于该几何特征的线性预测器在 3 个完全未见基准上预测评判失效达到 AUC 0.82（远超文本基线 0.63）。

> **TL;DR:** A representation-level reinterpretation of LLM-as-judge scoring bias: across 7 judges, 7 bias types and 9 benchmarks the authors show that biased activations are displaced from a tight baseline manifold along a low-dimensional, type-specific subspace that sharpens with depth and is recovered by three independent estimator families. Bidirectional activation steering along this direction reproduces biased scoring on clean inputs and restores fair scoring on biased ones (W₁ attack 1.62–3.43 vs ≤0.31 for matched-norm random directions), while a simple linear projection onto the same bias-direction features predicts judge failure on 3 fully held-out benchmarks (SocialMaze, BBQ, GPQA) at AUC 0.82 vs 0.63 for a zero-shot text-LLM detector, a +0.19 AUC delta.

## 核心贡献

- **首次给出 LLM-as-Judge 偏差的表示层统一解释**：将评判偏差从输入-输出层面的"表面线索 → 分数漂移"现象，重新刻画为隐藏状态中可刻画、可干预、可预测的低维几何结构，统一了几何结构、因果控制与跨域预测三个层面。
- **大规模实证刻画偏差图景**：在 7 个评判模型、7 类偏差（Prestige/Verbosity/Bandwagon/Authority/Sentiment/Refinement/Diversity）、9 个基准上构建 (𝒟_base, 𝒟_pos, 𝒟_neg) 受控三元组，揭示"负向惩罚强、正向激励弱"的强不对称性（负向 Cohen's d −0.35 到 −0.65，正向均值近零）。
- **几何刻画**：基准激活位于紧致流形 ℳ_base，偏差输入沿随层深锐化、有效维度仅 3–5 的类型特异子空间被位移；不同目标（Fisher 比 vs 边际损失）的估计器在深层收敛到同一轴。
- **双向因果控制**：注入 𝐯_bias 在干净输入上复现偏差（攻击），减去 𝐯_bias 在污染输入上恢复公平分（防御），且匹配范数的随机方向 W₁ 小 5.9–11.1 倍，bias-type 交换实验排除"通用读出方向"替代解释。
- **跨域操作化预测**：基于偏差方向投影的线性预测器在 3 个完全未参与训练的基准上达到 AUC 0.82，超过 zero-shot 文本 LLM 基线 0.63。

## Key Contributions

- First unified representation-level account of LLM-as-judge scoring bias, reframing it from an input-output catalog of surface-cue effects into a low-dimensional geometric structure inside the judge's hidden state that is simultaneously characterizable, causally intervenable, and operationally predictive.
- Large-scale empirical mapping across 7 judges (GPT-4.1, GPT-4o-Mini, Llama-3.1-8B, Llama-3.3-70B, Qwen3-14B, Gemma-3-12B, Deepseek-V3), 7 bias types and 9 benchmarks using tightly controlled (𝒟_base, 𝒟_pos, 𝒟_neg) triples that differ only in semantics-irrelevant surface framing; documents a strong evaluator-side negativity bias.
- Geometric characterization in three white-box judges: a tight baseline activation manifold ℳ_base with biased inputs displaced along a type-specific subspace of effective dimensionality 3–5 at layer 25, sharpening with depth, recovered by independent directional and discriminative estimators that converge in late layers.
- Bidirectional causal control (both attack and defense) via single-layer activation steering, with matched-norm random-direction and bias-type-swap controls that rule out the generic-perturbation and shared-readout-axis alternatives.
- A cross-domain outcome predictor that uses the same bias-direction features to anticipate judge degradation on three benchmarks entirely held out from training.

## Methodology

The paper builds a single pipeline covering analysis, intervention, and prediction.

**Task & controlled data.** A judge ℳ maps a templated input 𝑥 = 𝑃(𝑞, 𝑎) to a score 𝑠 ∈ [1,10]. Bias is operationalized as a semantics-preserving surface transformation 𝒯 (the standard convention in the LLM-as-judge bias literature). From 4,500 questions sampled evenly across 9 benchmarks and a 6-model answer pool, the authors build parallel 𝒟_base / 𝒟_neg / 𝒟_pos sets (31,500 + 31,500 perturbed samples) for 7 bias types (Table 1). Four template-insertion types leave the answer body bit-identical; three use LLM-assisted prose rewrites, validated by a TOST equivalence-test human evaluation.

**Activation analysis.** They record final-token hidden states at every layer, define a baseline manifold ℳ_base, and restrict to the "effective bias substrate" 𝒟_eff (paired instances whose score shift ≥ 𝛿_s integer points) and a "biased core" 𝒟_far (top Mahalanobis-decile from baseline) — a case-control framing in which null-shift samples are null observations, not negatives.

**Bias-direction estimation.** Two complementary families: directional-change estimators (arithmetic mean, geometric median, top PCA) that summarize per-sample activation shifts Δ𝐡, and discriminative-boundary estimators (LDA, regularized linear classifier, linear SVM) that take the separating hyperplane normal. All are unit-normalized so direction and intervention strength are decoupled. Within-family cosine agreement is ≥0.91; the two families converge cross-family to cos 0.55–0.65, so three representatives (Geometric Median, PCA, Classifier) are carried to causal experiments.

**Causal steering.** A single layer 𝑙 (selected as the one maximizing biased-vs-baseline separability, consistently mid-to-late, layers 15–31) is perturbed as 𝐡_𝑙′ = 𝐡_𝑙 + 𝛼·𝐯_bias. Attack (𝛼>0 on clean inputs) and defense (𝛼<0 on biased inputs) must both succeed. The strength 𝛼* maximizes the Wasserstein shift W₁ subject to output validity 𝑉(𝛼) ≥ 0.93 and Spearman rank-preservation 𝜌_𝑆(𝛼) ≥ 𝜌_𝑆^text (a text-perturbation anchor), localized by a two-stage exponential-then-binary plus low-temperature simulated-annealing search (~100 forward passes per triple).

**Outcome predictor.** Per-layer features concatenate bias-direction projections (onto 𝐯_LDA, 𝐯_CLS), manifold-deviation features (Mahalanobis distance, z-score vs ℳ_base) and semantic-context features (baseline-PCA projections). Two predictors are compared: a linear logistic regression on the bias-direction projections and a LightGBM pipeline with recursive feature elimination + Optuna. The operationally useful target predicts whether the judge's score degrades by ≥𝛿_o = 1 point (more permissive than the 𝛿_s = 2 used to fit 𝐯_bias).

## Results & Highlights

- **Strong evaluator-side negativity bias across all 7 judges (Table 2).** Negative perturbations produce large, highly significant score drops with Cohen's d between −0.35 (GPT-4.1, d=−0.346) and −0.65 (Qwen3-14B, d=−0.648), all p < 10⁻³, while the positive aggregate is near zero on every judge. On Llama-3.1-8B the mean negative shift Δ𝑠 = −0.853 (d=−0.580, p < 10⁻⁷) vs +0.243 for positives.
- **Bias types are heterogeneous and domain-interactive (Table 3).** On Llama-3.1-8B the per-type row-mean negative shift is Bandwagon −1.56, Diversity −0.94, Authority −0.79, Verbosity −0.60, Refinement −0.57, Prestige −0.38, Sentiment −0.07. Bandwagon− peaks on reasoning benchmarks, Diversity− on socially sensitive ones (SocialMaze, BBQ), Authority− on knowledge-intensive ones.
- **Low-dimensional type-specific bias geometry.** MDS/PCA show biased activations displaced off a tight baseline manifold; the effective dimensionality of the bias subspace is 3–5 directions per type at layer 25. L2-norms of biased vs baseline activations are indistinguishable (p>0.1) at every layer except the final readout head, so bias lives in the direction of displacement, not its magnitude. The LDA and Classifier estimators converge on a common axis in late layers despite different objectives.
- **Bidirectional causal control (Table 13).** On Llama-3.1-8B, the activation attack reaches W₁ 1.62 (Authority−), 3.43 (Bandwagon−), 1.13 (Refinement−), each exceeding the matched text-level baseline (0.59–1.66); defense recovers 52–62% of the pre-defense Wasserstein distance vs only 9–15% for text-rewrite defense, with output validity ≥0.93.
- **Random-direction control rules out generic perturbation (Table 14).** The ratio of bias-vector W₁ to matched-norm random-direction mean W₁ is 5.9× (Refinement+) to 11.1× (Bandwagon−); random directions leave Spearman correlation with the unperturbed baseline at ≥0.95, vs 0.58–0.81 for the calibrated bias direction (paired p < 10⁻⁹ across types).
- **Bias-type-swap control (Table 15).** Swapping in a different bias type's vector sits between random (within/swap 2.0–2.5×; swap/random 2.8–4.5×), ruling out both a shared readout axis and a fully type-orthogonal subspace.
- **Partial cross-architecture transfer (Table 12).** Layer-25 Classifier bias directions have cross-model cosine 0.47–0.62 between Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen3-14B and Gemma-3-12B (≥14 std above the ≈0 chance baseline), with the bias-type ordering Refinement+ > Bandwagon− > Authority− preserved across all three pairs.
- **Cross-domain outcome prediction.** The linear projection on bias-direction features attains AUC 0.82 on the three entirely unseen benchmarks (SocialMaze, BBQ, GPQA) vs 0.85 in-domain on Llama-3.1-8B. The richer LightGBM wins in-domain (dev AUC 0.93) but transfers worse (0.75). Both clear the zero-shot text-LLM detector baseline (0.63) by a wide margin; the two bias-direction projections alone carry >60% of the GBDT's Gini importance.
- **Defense robustness.** The 5-fold cross-validated defense retains ≥80% of in-sample W₁-reduction on held-out folds; the qualitative 4–6× advantage of activation defense over text-rewrite survives replacing the Spearman floor with an absolute 𝜌_𝑆 ≥ 0.85 constraint.

## Authors & Credibility

Authors are from AMAP/Alibaba Group, Mohamed bin Zayed University of Artificial Intelligence (MBZUAI), University of Southern California, and University of Michigan, Ann Arbor; the corresponding author Xiuying Chen and co-authors have a strong recent track record on LLM safety/representation (Gao et al. ACL 2025/2026; Chen et al. EMNLP 2024). The work is unusually well-scoped: every headline claim is paired with an appendix that bounds the residual confound (random-direction control J.1, bias-type-swap J.2, matched-budget text-attack J.3, absolute-floor defense K.2, held-out-trio predictor sensitivity M.7). Code/analysis pipelines are released; an attack-oriented release is deferred to responsible disclosure.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty.** Genuine and well-positioned. Two nearest neighbors exist — Adila et al. (2024) on unsupervised debiasing of QA answer-selection and Lai et al. (2025) on cross-layer logit aggregation for better-aligned scores — but neither targets LLM-as-judge scoring bias, neither provides bidirectional (attack + defense) causal control, and neither offers a cross-domain outcome predictor. The combination of geometric characterization + bidirectional steering + cross-domain prediction on the same object is the substantive novelty.
- **Rigor.** High. The controlled (𝒟_base, 𝒟_pos, 𝒟_neg) triples with bit-identical-body variants for four bias types, the matched-norm random-direction and bias-type-swap controls, the Spearman feasibility floor that blocks the "trivially corrupt the output" failure mode, and the nested train/dev/test split with three benchmarks fully held out from vector estimation are all designed to defeat the obvious alternative explanations. The claim is explicitly scoped to "interventional sufficiency" rather than unique causal pathway (a path-patching / causal-tracing mediation study would be needed for the stronger claim) — this honesty is a strength.
- **Reproducibility.** Strong for the three white-box judges: full estimator definitions, per-layer/per-method tables, algorithm pseudocode, and hyperparameters are in the appendices; analysis and outcome-prediction pipelines are released. A real gap: the headline AUC 0.82 rests on three specific held-out benchmarks (SocialMaze, BBQ, GPQA); the alternative-trio sensitivity analysis bounds it to ±0.025 across five other trio choices, which is reassuring but the absolute cross-domain AUC still depends on which domains are held out.
- **Significance.** Practically meaningful for the RLHF/evaluation community: judges sit inside alignment pipelines and silently propagate bias, so a judge-conditional activation detector that flags incoming (𝑞,𝑎) pairs as likely-to-be-mis-scored is operationally useful.
- **Red flags / weak spots.** (1) White-box access is required for both steering defense and the gray-box predictor; for proprietary API judges (GPT-4.1, GPT-4o-Mini) the entire representation-level machinery is inaccessible, and these are among the most-deployed judges — the paper acknowledges this but the mitigation ("prompt-level proxies") is hand-waved. (2) The seven bias types are operationalized as appended notes / prose rewrites following the standard cue convention; the bit-identical-body types are cleanest, but the three LLM-assisted rewrite types (Verbosity, Authority, Sentiment) conflate "bias" with whatever the rewrite LLM introduces, and Sentiment− is essentially flat (row mean −0.07), which weakens the universality of the negativity-bias story. (3) The calibrated steering strength 𝛼* spans an enormous range (2.19 for Refinement− at layer 7 to 75.75 for Bandwagon− at layer 31), which suggests the "unit-normalized direction" framing hides very different effective perturbation magnitudes per type and raises questions about comparability of the causal claims across types. (4) Cross-architecture cosine 0.47–0.62 is celebrated as "≥14 std above chance" but is far below the within-model ≥0.91, so cross-model defense transfer is "at degraded efficacy" rather than a drop-in — the practical defense story is mostly single-model. (5) The LightGBM predictor's cross-domain AUC (0.75) is actually below the linear projection's 0.82, an unusual inversion that the paper attributes to "richer features picking up domain-specific noise" — plausible but under-explored.

## Strengths

- Unified geometric + causal + operational framing tied to a single low-dimensional object.
- Exceptionally thorough controls (random-direction, bias-type-swap, matched-budget text-attack, absolute-floor defense, held-out-trio sensitivity) that address the most likely reviewer objections preemptively.
- Large and balanced evaluation: 7 judges × 7 bias types × 9 benchmarks, with strict/non-CoT defaults and confirmation that findings survive all four CoT×strict configurations.
- Operationally honest: explicitly anchors the cross-domain claim to the simpler linear predictor (which generalizes) rather than the higher-capacity GBDT (which overfits), and releases only analysis/prediction code.
- Clean separation of "stylistic discrimination" (sanity-check Target A, AUC ≈0.97) from the harder "judge-degradation prediction" (Target B), avoiding the trap of reporting the easy task.

## Limitations

- White-box access required for the core interventions; proprietary API judges — the most widely deployed — cannot benefit from activation steering or the gray-box predictor without prompt-level proxies that are not developed.
- Activation-level analysis covers only three mid-scale open-source judges (Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen3-14B, Gemma-3-12B); the behavioral asymmetry replicates across all seven judges but the geometric/causal claims rest on this trio.
- The three LLM-assisted rewrite bias types (Verbosity, Authority, Sentiment) potentially conflate the bias cue with incidental rewrite artifacts; Sentiment− is nearly flat, slightly undermining the universality of the negativity-bias finding.
- Enormous variation in calibrated 𝛼* (2.19–75.75) across bias types complicates cross-type comparison of steering efficacy.
- Cross-architecture transfer is partial (cos 0.47–0.62); cross-model defense is "degraded efficacy," not drop-in.
- The paper claims interventional sufficiency only, explicitly deferring the stronger unique-pathway claim (path-patching / causal tracing) to future work.
- Cross-domain AUC of 0.82, while well above the 0.63 text baseline, still leaves substantial residual error; the domain gap that hurts LightGBM suggests the geometry is not fully domain-invariant.

## Relevance

对研究 LLM-as-Judge、RLHF 评估管线与机制可解释性的方向高度相关：它直接把 representation engineering / activation steering 这套工具搬到了评估器一侧，给出了偏差的几何刻画、双向因果干预和跨域失效预测，与对齐、奖励建模可信度和评估基准有效性的研究兴趣紧密契合。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can path-patching or causal-tracing mediation upgrade the "interventional sufficiency" claim to a "unique natural pathway" claim, or does the bias subspace decompose into multiple redundant routes?
- Does the same bias-direction geometry exist in proprietary API judges, and can logit-only or output-distribution features approximate the activation predictor in a black-box regime?
- How does the bias subspace interact with judge training (instruction-tuning, RLHF, judge-specific fine-tuning such as Prometheus)? Does explicit judge training sharpen, erase, or rotate the subspace?
- Can domain-adversarial training on the predictor close the LightGBM cross-domain gap (0.75 → ≥0.82) while keeping its in-domain advantage?
- Is the evaluator-side negativity bias (large penalty for negative cues, near-zero reward for positive ones) a consequence of the same anchoring mechanism documented in human cognition, and does it transfer to pairwise/preference judges rather than scalar scoring judges?
- What happens when multiple bias types are stacked in a single input — does the geometry predict interference, additivity, or saturation?
