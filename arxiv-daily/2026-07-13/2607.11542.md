> **一句话总结：** 本文对温度缩放(TEMP)与保序回归(ISO)两种后校准方法在单一实验语料库的四个受控条件(C1–C4)下进行预注册的条件分层稳健性分析,发现 TEMP 在 Brier 分数与校准斜率上方向性更优(H1 经 Holm 校正后无显著差异,p=0.9895),但稳健性结论高度依赖于具体条件和所选指标,且未提供外部可迁移性证据。

> **TL;DR:** A pre-registered, condition-stratified robustness study compares temperature scaling (TEMP) against isotonic regression (ISO) across four in-dataset conditions (C1–C4) on an unnamed experimental corpus, evaluating four hypothesis groups (H1 discrimination deltas with Holm correction, H2 Brier differences, H3 calibration slope, H4 AUROC differences). TEMP shows directionally favorable behavior — Brier differences consistently negative (C1 −0.0002 to C4 −0.0074) versus ISO's sign reversals, and calibration slopes far closer to unity (0.7597–0.9493 vs ISO's 0.1364–0.2726) — but H1 yields no statistically separable advantage (all Holm-adjusted p = 0.9895) and H2–H4 lack confidence intervals or effect sizes, so the central claim ("robustness is condition-dependent and metric-specific") rests on directional rather than confirmed inferential evidence.

## 核心贡献

- 提出并执行了一个预注册(pre-registered)、条件分层的后校准稳健性分析框架,显式拒绝跨条件池化(pooled)报告,因为池化会掩盖 ISO Brier 差值在 C3 的符号反转。
- 在单一实验语料库的四个受控条件 C1–C4 下,系统比较参数化(TEMP,单参数 logit 缩放)与非参数化(ISO,阶梯单调映射)两类校准族,并通过四个假设组(H1 判别力差值 + Holm 校正、H2 Brier 差值、H3 校准斜率、H4 AUROC 差值)结构化评估。
- 引入"完整性优先"(integrity-first)的报告协议:所有数值来自一个"已验证注册表"(verified registry),手稿撰写期间不做任何事后重算;H2–H4 因缺少置信区间/效应量而被诚实降级为方向性结论,而非伪造统计权威。
- 明确划定范围边界:仅评估数据集内(in-dataset)的条件稳健性,不做任何外部可迁移性(transportability)声明,避免校准文献中常见的过度外推。

## Key Contributions

- A pre-registered, condition-stratified robustness analysis framework that explicitly rejects pooling across conditions, motivated by the authors' own observation that pooled aggregation hid a sign reversal in ISO Brier differences at C3 (negative −0.0037) while C1/C2/C4 were positive.
- A structured four-hypothesis evaluation (H1–H4) isolating distinct aspects of probabilistic prediction quality — discrimination (with Holm family-wise error control), probability accuracy (Brier), proportional calibration (slope), and ranking (AUROC) — rather than reporting a single aggregate headline metric.
- An integrity-first reporting protocol in which every numeric claim is tied to a registry-locked key, no derived statistics are introduced outside the registry, and inferential gaps (missing CIs/effect sizes for H2–H4) are disclosed in limitations rather than papered over.
- A deliberately scoped claim boundary: the study evaluates only in-dataset condition robustness (C1–C4) and makes no external transportability statement, positioning itself against over-extrapolation common in the calibration literature.

## Methodology

The study frames post-hoc calibration as a map g:[0,1]→[0,1] applied to a trained classifier's outputs f(x), with the ideal calibration condition P(Y=1 | g(f(x))=q)=q. Two calibration families are compared as complementary representatives:

- **Temperature scaling (TEMP):** a single-parameter monotonic logit rescaling g_TEMP(p) = σ(logit(p)/T), where T is fit on a held-out calibration partition. Low variance, parametric.
- **Isotonic regression (ISO):** a stepwise non-decreasing non-parametric map fit to calibration-set pairs (p_i, y_i). Higher flexibility but greater variance, especially with small calibration sets.

Platt scaling was considered as a third comparator but excluded because its two-parameter logistic form introduces confounds relative to TEMP's single parameter under limited calibration-set samples.

The evaluation domain is four controlled condition strata C={C1, C2, C3, C4} drawn from a single experimental corpus, each representing a distinct operating regime (a controlled perturbation of the data-generating process). The pipeline (Fig. 1) partitions raw classifier outputs into the four strata, applies TEMP and ISO independently within each, and feeds recalibrated scores into the metrics battery.

Four hypothesis groups, all pre-specified before analysis finalization following registered-report principles:

- **H1:** Condition-wise TEMP-minus-ISO discrimination deltas, with Holm step-down multiplicity control applied across the four condition-level tests.
- **H2:** Condition-wise Brier score differences (mean squared error between predicted probability and outcome) for each calibrator.
- **H3:** Condition-wise calibration slope (regression coefficient β from logit(ŷ)=α+β·logit(q); β=1 is perfect proportional calibration).
- **H4:** Condition-wise AUROC differences under each condition's best setup.

A deliberate asymmetry: only H1 receives inferential decoration (Holm-adjusted p-values); H2–H4 are reported as directional outcomes only because the registry lacks complete inferential tuples (CIs, effect sizes, adjusted significance). Three metrics are kept distinct because conflating discrimination, probability accuracy, and calibration obscures operationally meaningful differences.

## Results & Highlights

- **H1 (discrimination, with Holm correction):** TEMP-minus-ISO deltas are small across all conditions — C1: −0.0155, C2: −0.0111, C3: −0.0002, C4: 0.0139 — with identical Holm-adjusted p = 0.9895 everywhere, so no condition shows a family-wise statistically separable advantage between TEMP and ISO. H1 is non-supportive under multiplicity correction.
- **H2 (Brier score differences):** TEMP differences are consistently negative (improved/steady probability accuracy) — C1: −0.0002, C2: −0.0030, C3: −0.0052, C4: −0.0074 — whereas ISO shows sign reversals: positive (degradation) in C1/C2/C4 at 0.0267 / 0.0558 / 0.0405 and negative only in C3 at −0.0037. The starkest contrast is C4: TEMP −0.0074 vs ISO +0.0405, a five-fold asymmetry.
- **H3 (calibration slope):** TEMP slopes are far closer to unity (perfect proportional calibration) in every condition — C1: 0.7597, C2: 0.7902, C3: 0.9493, C4: 0.9000 — while ISO slopes never exceed 0.28 (C1: 0.1364, C2: 0.1720, C3: 0.2726, C4: 0.2162), indicating severe attenuation of the predicted-to-observed probability relationship under ISO.
- **H4 (AUROC differences):** Monotonically increasing from essentially zero in C1 (−0.0004) through C2 (0.0139) and C3 (0.0194) to C4 (0.0264), suggesting a condition-dependent discrimination advantage that grows with perturbation severity.
- **Calibration drift (Fig. 2):** ISO drift (delta adaptive ECE vs in-distribution baseline) exceeds 0.07 in C1, C2, and C4, while TEMP drift stays near zero and within the ±0.02 tolerance band.

## Authors & Credibility

Single author: Gurdeep Singh Virdee, affiliated with Fergana State Technical University, Uzbekistan. The paper adopts a registered-report-style framing (hypotheses H1–H4 pre-specified, Holm multiplicity control, registry-locked numeric values) and is unusually explicit about its inferential gaps. However, the single-author/single-institution provenance, the absence of a named dataset or model architecture, and the lack of a publicly described registry or code link weaken independent verifiability. The reference list is appropriate and covers the canonical calibration literature (Guo et al. 2017, Zadrozny & Elkan 2002, Ovadia et al. 2019, Nixon et al. 2019, Kumar et al. 2019).

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Modest. The core observation that aggregate calibration metrics mask condition-level structure, and that TEMP is more stable than ISO, is well-established in the calibration literature (Guo et al. 2017; Ovadia et al. 2019; Minderer et al. 2021). The genuine novelty is the procedural framing — pre-registered condition stratification with Holm correction and a registry-locked integrity protocol — rather than a new method or a new empirical finding.
- **Rigor:** Internally consistent and commendably honest about inferential gaps, but the rigor is undermined by missing inferential statistics for three of four hypothesis groups (H2–H4 have no confidence intervals, no effect sizes, no multiplicity-adjusted significance). The H1 Holm-adjusted p-values are all identical at 0.9895, which the authors attribute to small delta magnitudes and Holm's conservatism, but this also means H1 carries essentially no discriminating power — the headline "no significant separation" is unsurprising.
- **Reproducibility:** Weak. The experimental corpus is never named, the conditions C1–C4 are not described in terms of what they actually represent (no covariate-shift type, no perturbation mechanism, no sample sizes), the base classifier/model architecture is unnamed, and the "verified registry" is referenced but not publicly linked or described. A reader cannot reproduce any number. This is the most serious red flag.
- **Significance:** Limited. The claim "robustness is condition-dependent and metric-specific" is directionally reasonable but, without a named benchmark, named baseline classifier, or transportability analysis, the practical takeaway for practitioners is thin. The paper reads more as a methodological/reporting template than a benchmark study.
- **Red flags:** (1) Unnamed dataset and unnamed conditions C1–C4 — the most concrete entities in the paper are opaque labels. (2) No between-calibrator slope tests for H3, so the large TEMP-vs-ISO slope gap is directional, not confirmed. (3) Single author with no co-authors or acknowledged contributors at the named institution, and no code/data release mentioned. (4) The "registry-locked values" protocol is presented as an integrity safeguard but the registry itself is unauditable, so the safeguard is asserted rather than verifiable.

## Strengths

- Honest, pre-registered hypothesis structure (H1–H4) with explicit multiplicity control on H1 — a methodological standard many calibration papers skip.
- Transparent disclosure of inferential incompleteness for H2–H4 rather than fabricating statistical authority.
- Clear separation of discrimination (AUROC), probability accuracy (Brier), and proportional calibration (slope) metrics, avoiding the common conflation that hides operationally meaningful differences.
- The motivating example — pooled aggregation hiding ISO's C3 sign reversal — is a concrete, persuasive argument for condition-stratified reporting.
- Scope discipline: no external transportability claims are made, avoiding over-extrapolation.

## Limitations

- The experimental corpus, the base classifier, and the nature of conditions C1–C4 are not described; the entire empirical substrate is opaque.
- No inferential statistics (CIs, effect sizes, adjusted significance) for H2, H3, or H4 — three of four hypothesis groups are directional only.
- No external validation cohorts; all conclusions are bounded to C1–C4 within one unnamed corpus.
- Condition granularity may hide within-condition heterogeneity — C1–C4 are fixed strata with no sub-stratum audit.
- H1's Holm-adjusted p = 0.9895 uniformly provides no discriminating evidence either way.
- No between-calibrator slope tests for H3, despite the large TEMP-vs-ISO slope gap.
- Single-author study with no described code or data release.

## Relevance

对关注模型不确定性与校准稳健性的研究而言,本文提供了一个可借鉴的条件分层 + 预注册 + Holm 校正的报告框架,提醒评估后校准方法时不应仅看聚合指标而需检查条件级与指标级差异;但由于未命名数据集且缺乏外部验证,其直接的方法学或基准价值有限,更适合作为评估流程设计的参考而非新方法来源。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- What are conditions C1–C4 concretely — what perturbation does each represent, and what are their sample sizes and class balances?
- Would H2–H4 survive proper inferential testing (bootstrap CIs, paired tests, effect-size intervals), or are the directional TEMP advantages an artifact of the opaque corpus?
- Does the TEMP-vs-ISO slope gap (0.76–0.95 vs 0.14–0.27) persist across named public benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10/100 under standard corruption shifts, ImageNet-C), and how does it interact with calibration-set size?
- How does the framework extend to multiclass calibration (Dirichlet / matrix scaling) where ISO's variance disadvantage is amplified?
- Would adding Platt scaling and ensemble-based recalibration as comparators change the condition-level conclusions, given Platt was excluded only for confound reasons?
- Can the "registry-locked values" integrity protocol be operationalized as a publicly hash-verifiable artifact to make the safeguard auditable rather than asserted?
