> **一句话总结：** 本文系统研究了代码大模型递归自训练的崩溃现象，证明无评审崩溃最快、人工门控减缓但无法阻止、而AI自我评审会随模型退化陷入"橡皮图章"失效状态，从而得出稳定训练需要外生验证而非模型耦合自审的结论。

> **TL;DR:** This paper shows that recursive self-training of code LLMs collapses across all review regimes—no review fails fastest, human-style gates slow but cannot stop collapse, and AI self-gates degrade into a self-confirming "rubber-stamp" regime—concluding that stable recursive training requires exogenous verification, not model-coupled self-review.

## 核心贡献
- 首次系统性地实证研究了代码大模型（Code LLM）的递归自训练崩溃问题，在四种模型（SantaCoder、StarCoder2、Qwen2.5-Coder、CodeLlama）和五个评审策略上验证。
- 提出将代码评审形式化为"门控分布重加权"（gated distributional reweighting），统一刻画无评审、人工门控与AI自门控三种递归训练机制。
- 明确区分了外生（exogenous，Human gate）与内生（endogenous，AI self-gate）验证：关键区别在于验收规则是否与生成器耦合。
- 给出崩溃与门控失效的理论：定理2.3证明AI自门控在"自我确认验收"条件下退化为无门控自训练；命题2.1给出表征空间协方差谱集中的谱分析。
- 揭示"橡皮图章"失效模式：二分类自门控中验收分数上升而基准正确率下降，是最清晰的AI自评审失败案例。

## Key Contributions
- First systematic empirical study of recursive self-training collapse in code LLMs, validated across four model families (SantaCoder, StarCoder2, Qwen2.5-Coder, CodeLlama) and five filtering strategies over five recursive rounds.
- Formalizes code review as **gated distributional reweighting**, showing gated recursion differs from ungated recursion only through a sampling measure reweighted by an acceptance function r(x,c).
- Introduces the **exogeneity** distinction as the key axis separating review regimes: Human gates use fixed, model-independent criteria (compile checks, quality rules), while AI self-gates use scores coupled to the generator (perplexity, binary self-scoring).
- Provides theory of collapse and gate failure: Theorem 2.3 proves AI self-gating degenerates to ungated self-training under a self-confirming acceptance condition; Proposition 2.1 gives a spectral analysis showing representation-covariance concentration under recursive retraining.
- Identifies the **rubber-stamp failure mode**, where binary self-gating's acceptance scores rise while benchmark correctness falls—the clearest empirical demonstration of self-confirming AI review.

## Methodology
The paper combines recursive fine-tuning experiments with a theoretical framework. **Setup:** At each round t, model M_t generates synthetic code from a fixed prompt pool; a filtering gate selects 5,000 accepted samples; M_{t+1} is fine-tuned for 3,000 steps, repeated for 5 rounds (15k steps total). All methods share the same optimizer, learning-rate cosine schedule, and batch size—only the filtering policy differs.

**Three review regimes:** (1) *No review* (Vanilla) reuses all generated samples; (2) *Human gate* uses exogenous, model-independent filters—Compile (Python compile() syntax check) and Quality (fixed repetition-rate ≤0.3 and length ≥50 tokens rules), plus Compile+Quality combos; (3) *AI self-gate* uses the code LLM's own signals—Perplexity (retain lowest-25% by masked-completion perplexity) and Binary Classifier (retain top-25% by logit-difference self-score).

**Theory:** Recursive training is cast as gated sampling where accepted samples are reweighted by an acceptance function r(x,c). A linearized covariance recursion (Σ_{t+1} = AΣ_tA^T) yields a spectral-concentration result (Proposition 2.1). The self-confirming acceptance assumption (Assumption 2.2) leads to Theorem 2.3 (degeneracy to ungated recursion); Theorem 2.6 gives the non-collapse bound when AI review stays calibrated to human review with non-vanishing accepted mass.

**Evaluation:** HumanEval/MBPP (primary), LiveCodeBench (contamination-robust probe), plus HumanEval+/MBPP+ (augmented edge cases), via bigcode-evaluation-harness at temperature 0.8, top-p 0.95. Hardware: NVIDIA RTX 6000 Ada GPUs.

## Results & Highlights
- **Universal degradation:** All four models decline from pre-training baselines over 5 rounds under every strategy—collapse persists across model families and gate types.
- **Clear ordering:** No review (Vanilla) collapses fastest; Human gates slow but do not stop collapse; AI self-gates look strong early but lose filtering effect later.
- **Vanilla collapse magnitudes:** Qwen2.5-Coder falls HumanEval+ 0.372→0.043 (−88.4%) and MBPP+ 0.582→0.082 (−85.9%); SantaCoder MBPP+ drops 0.294→0.008 at R4 (−97.3%); CodeLlama-7B is most robust.
- **Rubber-stamp regime:** Binary classifier self-gating shows acceptance scores rising while benchmark correctness falls; Perplexity filter pass rate creeps up from ~0.167 (R1) to ~0.235 (R5), evidencing miscalibration per Theorem 2.3.
- **LiveCodeBench near-zero:** Harder/out-of-distribution tasks collapse most—Qwen2.5-Coder 0.238→0.000 by R5 regardless of strategy.
- **Collapse attractor:** Despite differing architectures, capacities, and starting baselines, all models converge to a common degradation floor (~0.04–0.14 HumanEval+ under Vanilla by R5).
- **No filter prevents collapse** across all model families; binary filtering achieves best R5 HumanEval+ for three of four models but with erratic MBPP+ behavior.

## Authors & Credibility
The authors are from Emory University (Xinyuan Song, Liang Zhao) and The University of Tokyo / LocationMind (Zekun Cai). Liang Zhao is an established researcher in machine learning. The work is a coherent integration of theory (gated reweighting framework, proven theorems with appendices) and substantial empirical experiments (4 models × 5 filters × 5 rounds × 4 benchmarks), with code released at https://github.com/Hik289/code-retraining.git, supporting reproducibility. The paper cites the relevant model-collapse literature (Shumailov, Dohmatob, Alemohammad) and modern AI-coding ecosystem reports (Copilot, Cursor, Devin).

## Critical Assessment
- **Novelty:** High. The paper makes a distinctive contribution by (a) porting the model-collapse problem into the code-LLM domain at repository scale, (b) introducing the exogenous-vs-endogenous gate taxonomy as the organizing principle, and (c) connecting it to the timely concern of AI-reviewed AI code. The "rubber-stamp" framing is intuitive and well-motivated.
- **Rigor:** Strong. Theoretical claims (Proposition 2.1, Theorem 2.3/2.6) are stated with explicit assumptions and full proofs in Appendix C. The shared-parameters assumption (ϕ_t = θ_t) is a clean, if idealized, abstraction of the real AI-self-gate setting; Theorem 2.3's degeneracy result follows logically. Empirics use standard benchmarks and harnesses with controlled optimizer/schedule.
- **Reproducibility:** Good. Code is released; hyperparameters (5k samples, 3k steps/round, temp 0.8/top-p 0.95, hardware) are reported; per-round tables are provided. Some details (exact prompt pool, full gating thresholds) are deferred to appendices.
- **Significance:** Timely and consequential for the AI-coding industry. The central warning—that automated AI code review coupled to the generator is not a substitute for exogenous verification—directly informs training-data pipelines and self-improving agent design.
- **Red flags / caveats:** Models are small-to-mid scale (1.1B–7B), so transfer to frontier models is unproven. Only 5 recursive rounds may not capture very long-horizon dynamics. The shared-parameters self-gate assumption is an extreme case; real AI reviewers (separate judge models) may not fully satisfy Assumption 2.2, weakening the direct applicability of Theorem 2.3 to practical judge-LLM pipelines. Human gates are deliberately weak proxies (compile + repetition/length), not full test suites, which may understate human-gate efficacy.

## Strengths
- Clean, unified theoretical framework (gated reweighting) that makes the three regimes directly comparable.
- Strong empirical breadth: 4 models × 5 filters × multiple benchmarks, all showing the same qualitative pattern.
- The exogenous/endogenous distinction is a genuinely useful and generalizable conceptual lens beyond code LLMs.
- Honest reporting: the paper openly notes AI self-gates can *appear* strong on HumanEval+ while failing on MBPP+ and LiveCodeBench, resisting cherry-picking.
- The rubber-stamp empirical signature (acceptance up, correctness down) is a memorable and checkable diagnostic.

## Limitations
- Model scale limited to 1.1B–7B parameters; frontier-model behavior is untested.
- Human gates are deliberately weak (syntax + shallow quality rules), not execution-test-based, so the "human gate cannot stop collapse" conclusion partly reflects gate weakness rather than a fundamental limit.
- The shared-parameter self-gate (ϕ_t = θ_t) is an idealization; separate judge models in practice are only loosely coupled, so Theorem 2.3 is a limiting-case result.
- Only 5 rounds; asymptotic long-horizon behavior is inferred rather than directly observed.
- Filter-pass-rate and acceptance-score dynamics are reported descriptively; deeper causal probing of *why* the self-signal miscalibrates is left to the theory's idealized model.

## Relevance
这项研究对训练稳定性、自演化智能体（self-evolving agents）与模型崩溃（model collapse）三大议题均高度相关：它将"模型崩溃"从图像/通用文本生成延伸到代码生成这一更可测试、更现实的仓库级闭环场景，并清晰指出"用模型自身作为评审/奖励信号"的自演化范式存在内在的自确认失效风险，提醒自改进智能体必须引入外生验证信号才能保持长期稳定。

## Questions for Further Exploration
- Does the collapse pattern (and the rubber-stamp regime) hold for frontier-scale code LLMs (e.g., 70B+), or does greater capacity and pretraining diversity delay/avoid it?
- How do *separate* judge LLMs (not identical to the generator) behave under recursion—do they satisfy Assumption 2.2, or do they provide genuine exogeneity only when sufficiently diverse?
- Would stronger Human gates based on actual execution test suites (not just compile + repetition) meaningfully prevent collapse, or merely delay it?
- Can mixing a fixed fraction of fresh human/real data into each round anchor the distribution and eliminate the collapse attractor?
- Is there a principled way to detect the onset of the self-confirming rubber-stamp regime online, to trigger re-anchoring?
- How do these results interact with reinforcement-learning-from-execution-feedback (RLCF) pipelines where the reward is genuinely exogenous?
