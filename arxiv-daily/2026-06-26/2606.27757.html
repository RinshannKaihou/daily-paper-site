Key Finding

Wrapping an LLM planner in a symbolic verifier plus a feedback–refine–guide loop lets it detect and self-correct plan errors — turning blind trial-and-error into targeted, verifiable repair.

How it works — the closed loop

The framework couples an LLM planner with symbolic components that read, check, and steer its output.

(1) NL prompting. The PDDL domain, initial state I and true goal Gtru are templated into a natural-language prompt prompt = Concat(template, Ξ, I, Gtru) — actions, predicates and effects become readable descriptions, so the LLM grasps constraint boundaries without parsing PDDL syntax.

(2) Feedback-driven self-refinement. The LLM emits an initial plan; a feedback-augmented prompt T' = Tinput ⊕ Tfeedback ⊕ Thistory fuses the base prompt, error feedback and history. The plan is regenerated and re-checked — looping until the verifier is clean or 5 iterations elapse.

(3) Multi-dimensional verifier. Three checks — syntactic (naming, parameters), semantic (via VAL), and goal-reachability (a landmark-based plan recognizer ranks candidate goals, comparing inferred Ginf with Gtru) — combine into Tfeedback = Tsyn ⊕ Tsem ⊕ Trecg for targeted correction. Two stopping strategies (stop-on-feasible vs stop-on-optimal) tune the trade-off.

The Feedback → Refine → Guide Cycle
①NL Prompt ConstructionMap PDDL → readable template; surface constraints & goals
②Initial Plan GenerationLLM produces a candidate action sequence
③Symbolic VerificationSyntactic · Semantic (VAL) · Goal-reachability checks
④Feedback InjectionMulti-dim errors → LLM-interpretable corrections
⑤Re-plan & Re-verifyRegenerate, loop back to ③
⟲ repeat until error-free — or max 5 iterations · temperature 0.1

Ablations & trade-offs

  • NL prompts alone: up to +15.2% feasibility, +6.5% optimality.
  • + augmented feedback: a further +14.5% feasibility, +9.0% optimality.
  • Plan length: feedback models pull ahead across lengths 2–14; gap widens as difficulty grows.
  • Token budget: 1024→2048 tokens brings large gains for feedback models, little for baselines.

Strengths vs. Limitations

✓ Strengths

  • Modular & ablatable. Clean NL-prompt + verifier + recognizer architecture, each component isolated.
  • Strong, consistent Blocksworld gains across three diverse LLMs — pushing DeepSeek-R1 to perfect coverage.
  • Interpretable feedback in human-readable form, aligned with how LLMs consume corrections.
  • Pragmatic knobs — stopping strategies and token-length analysis surface real trade-offs.
  • Trustworthy-AI framing — connects directly to reliable & secure AI agents.

! Limitations

  • Only two classical PDDL domains — no numeric, temporal, open or real-world environments (authors' own caveat).
  • Mystery absolute coverage stays low (best ~65.5%) — symbol-semantic confusion unresolved.
  • No code/data release, no variance, no significance testing reported.
  • Hyperparameters under-explored — sensitivity to the 5-iteration cap, temperature 0.1, and prompt templates.
  • Symbolic engineering persists: goals/domains remain PDDL-defined and human-authored.

Open questions for further exploration

Q1
How does it scale beyond classical PDDL to numeric, temporal, or open-world/dynamic domains?
Q2
Would learnable / self-generated feedback prompts beat the hand-templated NL mechanism?
Q3
What is the latency & token cost per task (verifier calls, iterations) vs. the coverage gains?
Q4
Can the recognizer's goal-reachability signal be made probabilistic to cut false goal-deviation flags?
Q5
Sensitivity to recognizer choice, the 5-iteration cap, and decoding temperature?
Q6
Do gains benefit weaker open-source LLMs more — and does feedback quality degrade on small models?
