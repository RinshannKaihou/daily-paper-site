> **一句话总结：** 本文提出 MultModLM，首个面向"RTL→硬件原理图"多模态生成的基准（99 个 RTL 模块 + 多阶段评估框架），并实证发现 LLM 作为评判者与人类评分者的一致性近乎为零（Cohen's κ≈0），揭示了 LLM-as-a-judge 在结构化精确领域的根本局限。

> **TL;DR:** MultModLM is the first multimodal benchmark for LLM-based RTL-to-hardware-schematic generation (99 modules, multi-stage evaluation), revealing that LLM-as-a-judge evaluators show near-zero agreement (Cohen's κ≈0) with human raters in structurally precise domains.

## 核心贡献
- 首次构建面向"RTL 描述→硬件原理图（图像）"多模态生成的基准数据集 MultModLM，包含 99 个覆盖算术单元、有限状态机、控制逻辑与数值属性检测器的 Verilog 模块。
- 提出多阶段评估框架，融合基于评分准则的打分、自评估、交叉模型评估、盲评与人工验证，各阶段互为补充以覆盖彼此的失败模式。
- 提供实证证据表明 LLM 评判者与人类评分者的一致性近乎为零（GPT vs Human κ=0.013，Gemini vs Human κ=0.000），暴露了 LLM-as-a-judge 在硬件原理图这类结构精确域的根本缺陷。
- 设计 8 维布尔型评分准则（数据通路、控制信号、时钟时序、复位、标签、整体功能正确性等），专门适应硬件电路"离散非渐变"的特性。
- 指出对视觉输出做形式等价校验是不可解难题，并呼吁开发领域感知的自动化结构验证工具作为可扩展的真值代理。

## Key Contributions
- Introduces MultModLM, the first multimodal benchmark for RTL-to-schematic generation, comprising 99 Verilog modules spanning arithmetic units, FSMs, control logic, and numeric property checkers.
- Proposes a multi-stage evaluation pipeline combining rubric-based scoring, self-evaluation, cross-model assessment, blind evaluation, and human validation, where each stage compensates for the failure modes of the others.
- Provides empirical evidence that LLM-based judges exhibit near-zero agreement with human raters (Cohen's κ≈0), exposing a fundamental limitation of LLM-as-a-judge in structurally precise domains.
- Designs an 8-criterion Boolean rubric (datapath, control signals, clocking/timing, reset, labeling, overall functional correctness, etc.) tailored to the inherently discrete, non-gradual nature of hardware schematics.
- Frames formal equivalence checking of visual outputs as an open challenge and motivates domain-aware, automated structural verification tools as scalable ground-truth proxies.

## Methodology
The authors frame schematic generation as fundamentally different from code generation: it requires capturing structural, functional, and visual aspects simultaneously, and there is no single ground-truth schematic (multiple structurally distinct schematics can be functionally equivalent). The dataset of 99 RTL modules was sourced from open-source repositories (e.g., OpenRISC OR1200) and human generation, with no ground-truth schematics provided. Correctness is defined along three axes: (1) functional consistency with the RTL, (2) structural fidelity of datapath/control organization, and (3) schematic validity/legibility including timing semantics. Since visual outputs cannot be simulated or formally verified, functional correctness is approximated through rubrics and grounded comparison with RTL code (not full formal equivalence, which is acknowledged as out of scope). The evaluation pipeline has five stages: prompt-driven zero-shot generation; self-evaluation (target model rates its own output via the rubric); grounded cross-evaluation (one model rates the other's schematic against the RTL); blind cross-evaluation (evaluator sees only the image, no RTL); and human evaluation (structured rubric-based verification). The 8-criterion rubric is scored as Boolean True/False rather than on graded scales, reflecting the discrete nature of hardware. Experiments were run on GPT 5.2 (Go version) and Gemini 3 Flash Pro.

## Results & Highlights
- LLM-as-judge reliability is near zero: Cohen's κ = 0.013 for GPT-vs-Human and 0.000 for Gemini-vs-Human, indicating automated judges are unreliable for hardware schematics.
- Cross-rated accuracy: Gemini 0.314 (rated by GPT) vs GPT 0.297 (rated by Gemini); both well below 1.0 (max 8 points), confirming models can produce visually interpretable schematics but functional correctness remains heavily constrained.
- Head-to-head wins on per-prompt totals: Gemini 37, GPT 33, Ties 29 — a marginal Gemini edge.
- Gemini outperforms on semantic/functional criteria C1–C6 (structural fidelity, datapath, control logic, timing); GPT leads only on visual-oriented criteria C7–C8, which the authors caution measure layout cleanliness rather than canonical-symbol correctness or hardware validity.
- The framework is presented as the first to explore multimodal feedback for hardware generation, and the authors argue the LLM-as-judge failure generalizes to other domains with non-executable visual outputs.

## Critical Assessment
- Novelty: High in framing — it is the first multimodal (RTL→schematic image) benchmark for LLMs in hardware, filling a genuine gap (prior benchmarks RTLLM, VerilogEval, HDLBits, AutoChip are all text-only NL/RTL→Verilog). The negative result on LLM-as-a-judge is timely and broadly relevant.
- Rigor: Moderate. The κ≈0 finding is striking and clearly reported, and the multi-stage design is thoughtful about compensating failure modes. However, the central correctness signal is itself approximated via rubrics rather than formally verified, and the human evaluation was restricted to "circuits of moderate complexity," so ground truth is itself partial. The distinction between "visual clarity" and "semantic/hardware correctness" is honestly flagged but leaves the leaderboard somewhat soft.
- Reproducibility: Partial. Prompts and the rubric JSON are described and example templates given, but the full dataset, rating JSONs, and human-annotation protocol are not clearly released via a public artifact link; the models referenced (GPT 5.2 Go, Gemini 3 Flash Pro) are proprietary and version-pinned, which limits exact replication.
- Significance: Conceptually important for the LLM-for-hardware and multimodal-evaluation communities — it both opens a new task and warns against trusting LLM judges in structural domains.
- Red flags: Small dataset (99 modules) dominated by numeric-property-checkers (76/99), so category balance is skewed; no formal equivalence checking means reported "accuracy" is a proxy; absolute accuracy numbers (~0.3) are low and the head-to-head margin (37 vs 33) is small enough to be within noise without confidence intervals.

## Strengths
- First multimodal benchmark at the LLM×hardware intersection, directly addressing a real gap.
- Honest, well-motivated negative result: the near-zero κ finding is a valuable, citable contribution beyond the benchmark itself.
- Multi-stage evaluation design is principled in its attempt to cover orthogonal failure modes.
- 8-criterion Boolean rubric is well matched to the discrete nature of hardware logic.

## Limitations
- No formal equivalence verification — correctness is approximated by rubrics and human judgment, so the leaderboard is inherently soft.
- Small and category-imbalanced dataset (76/99 are numeric property checkers), limiting breadth.
- Human evaluation limited to moderate-complexity circuits; scaling to hierarchical/multi-module designs is left open.
- Proprietary, version-pinned models and unclear artifact release hinder exact reproduction.

## Relevance
该工作处于 LLM 多模态评估与硬件设计自动化的交叉前沿：它不仅提出新任务（RTL→原理图），还给出 LLM-as-a-judge 在结构精确领域失效的实证，对构建可信的 LLM 硬件设计 Agent 与多模态评估基准具有直接参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration
- Can a partially-automated structural verifier (e.g., image→graph reconstruction → equivalence against an RTL-derived netlist) serve as a scalable ground-truth proxy to replace or constrain rubric judgments?
- How do the κ≈0 findings generalize beyond hardware to other non-executable visual-output domains (e.g., diagrams, schematics, architectural drawings)?
- Would scaling the dataset and rebalancing categories (fewer numeric checkers, more FSM/datapath/control) change the relative GPT-vs-Gemini ranking and the magnitude of the accuracy gap?
- Can a stricter visual rubric enforcing canonical gate symbols and standardized notation convert the currently inflated C7–C8 visual scores into a more meaningful correctness signal?
- Is the near-zero inter-rater agreement driven by ambiguous rubric criteria, model inability to interpret schematics, or both — and would chain-of-thought or tool-augmented judging improve agreement?
