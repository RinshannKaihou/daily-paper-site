> **一句话总结：** 本文提出两阶段 AC-IHT 算法，通过对系数与污染向量施加不同的硬阈值水平，在高维对抗污染回归中实现信号自适应的最小最大近最优估计、强 Oracle 性质与渐近正态性。

> **TL;DR:** The paper proposes a two-stage AC-IHT algorithm that applies separate hard-thresholding scales to the coefficient and contamination vectors, achieving signal-adaptive minimax near-optimal estimation, strong oracle property, and asymptotic normality in high-dimensional adversarially-contaminated regression.

## 核心贡献
- 针对高维对抗污染线性模型 $Y = X\beta^* + \sqrt{n}\theta^* + \xi$，提出两阶段 AC-IHT（对抗污染-迭代硬阈值）算法；关键创新在于对系数向量 $\beta$ 与污染向量 $\theta$ 在每次迭代中使用**不同的阈值水平**，从而精确刻画污染项 $\theta^*$ 对 $\beta^*$ 估计的影响。
- 证明估计量具有**信号自适应性（signal adaptivity）**：在恰当信号条件下，自适应达到比标准高维最小最大速率更锐利的估计精度与更准确的支持集恢复。
- 建立**强 Oracle 性质**：在信号条件下，估计量几何收敛到 Oracle 估计量，实现精确支持恢复；并进一步证明**渐近正态性**，为有效统计推断奠定基础。
- 通过匹配的最小最大下界（estimation 与 selection 两方面）证明算法的近最优性（仅相差对数因子）。
- 将算法推广到广义线性模型（GLM），并通过截断分解揭示对抗污染框架与重尾噪声回归的理论联系，证明 AC-IHT 在两种设定下均保持最小最大近最优。

## Key Contributions
- Proposes a two-stage Adversarial Contamination-resistant Iterative Hard Thresholding (AC-IHT) algorithm for high-dimensional linear regression with adversarial contamination $Y = X\beta^* + \sqrt{n}\theta^* + \xi$, where $\beta^*$ is $s$-sparse and $\theta^*$ is $\tilde{o}$-sparse. The core novelty is using **separate thresholding scales** for $\beta$ and $\theta$ per iteration, enabling a refined analysis of how the nuisance contamination $\theta^*$ impacts estimation of $\beta^*$.
- Establishes **signal adaptivity**: the first-stage estimator is minimax near-optimal for all $\ell_q$ ($q\in[1,2]$), while the second-stage estimator adaptively attains a sharper estimation rate (matching the "known-support" rate up to a $\log n$ factor) and tighter support recovery under proper signal conditions on $\beta^*$.
- Proves the **strong oracle property** — under signal conditions on both $\beta^*$ and $\theta^*$, the estimator converges geometrically to the oracle estimator $\beta^\dagger$, achieves exact support recovery $\text{supp}(\tilde\beta)=\text{supp}(\beta^*)$, and enjoys **asymptotic normality** enabling valid inference.
- Derives matching **minimax lower bounds** for both estimation (Theorem 4) and variable selection (Theorem 5), confirming near-optimality up to logarithmic factors.
- Extends the framework to **generalized linear models (GLMs)** and establishes a formal connection to **heavy-tailed regression** via a truncation-based decomposition, showing minimax near-optimality in both settings.

## Methodology
The paper considers the model $Y = X\beta^* + \sqrt{n}\theta^* + \xi$ with sub-Gaussian design $X$ (Assumption 1), where $\beta^*$ is $s$-sparse and $\theta^*$ is $\tilde{o}$-sparse. The algorithm minimizes the squared $\ell_2$ loss $\mathcal{L}(\beta,\theta) = \frac{1}{2n}\|Y - X\beta - \sqrt{n}\theta\|_2^2$ in two stages:

- **Stage 1 (Algorithm 1, dynamic thresholding):** Each iteration performs three steps — (1.1) gradient-descent updates $H_\beta^{t+1}, H_\theta^{t+1}$; (1.2) monotonic decay of separate threshold parameters $\lambda_{\beta,t+1}=(\kappa\lambda_{\beta,t})\vee\lambda_{\beta,\infty}$ (and similarly for $\theta$); (1.3) hard thresholding $\beta^{t+1} = \mathcal{T}_{\lambda_{\beta,t+1}}(H_\beta^{t+1})$, $\theta^{t+1} = \mathcal{T}_{\lambda_{\theta,t+1}}(H_\theta^{t+1})$. The decay runs until thresholds reach limiting statistical levels, providing an explicit stopping time. This yields a minimax near-optimal initial estimate.
- **Stage 2 (Algorithm 2, fixed thresholding):** Starting from the Stage-1 output, iterations continue with two **fixed** thresholds $\lambda_\beta=\lambda_{\beta,\infty}$ and $\lambda_\theta=\lambda_{\theta,\infty}$, performing gradient update + fixed hard thresholding for $O(\log n)$ steps. This acts as a "debiased" refinement step (no data splitting needed).

Key technical ingredients: Proposition 1 establishes restricted isometry of $X$ and restricted incoherence between columns of $X$ and the identity matrix $I_n$ (controlling how contamination can distort $\beta^*$ estimation). The thresholds $\lambda_{\beta,\infty}, \lambda_{\theta,\infty}$ have explicit forms depending on $\sigma$, $s$, $o$, and design constants. Signal conditions (Assumptions 3 & 4) on the minimum nonzero entries of $\beta^*$ and $\theta^*$ gate the oracle guarantees.

## Results & Highlights
- **Theorem 1 (Initial estimation):** $\hat\beta$ achieves $\|\hat\beta-\beta^*\|_2^2 \lesssim \sigma^2\big(\frac{s\log p}{n} + \frac{o^2\log^2 n}{n^2}\big)$, which is minimax near-optimal for all $\ell_q$, $q\in[1,2]$. The error has two regimes: an uncontaminated rate when $o$ is small, dominated by the squared contamination proportion as $o$ grows.
- **Theorem 2 (Signal-adaptive):** Under signal condition on $\beta^*$, $\|\tilde\beta-\beta^*\|_2^2 \lesssim \sigma^2\big(\frac{s+\log(1/\varrho)}{n} + \frac{(s\log p+o\log n)^2}{n^2}\big)$ — sharper than the minimax rate and matching the "known-support" rate up to $\log n$.
- **Theorem 3 (Oracle property):** Under signal conditions on both $\beta^*$ and $\theta^*$, geometric convergence to oracle estimator with rate $\sigma\sqrt{s/(n-o)}$ and exact support recovery.
- **Corollary 2 (Asymptotic normality):** $\sqrt{n}\gamma^\top(\tilde\beta_{S^*}-\beta^*_{S^*}) \xrightarrow{D} N(0, c_\xi\sigma^2\gamma^\top\Sigma^{-1}_{S^*,S^*}\gamma)$.
- **Simulations** ($p=1000, n=300, s=o=10$, 300 reps): AC-IHT achieves the lowest $\ell_2$, $\ell_\infty$, $\Sigma$-norm errors and best MCC (~0.99) / lowest symmetric difference across Gaussian, Rademacher, and Uniform noise, closely tracking the Oracle benchmark and substantially outperforming IHT-$\ell_1$, PIQ, Ada-Huber, AC-LASSO, and AC-SCAD. Figure 1 shows AC-IHT approaching the oracle estimator as $n$ grows from 200 to 800.

## Critical Assessment
- **Novelty:** High. While iterative hard thresholding (Ndaoud 2020) and adversarial contamination methods exist separately, this is (to the authors' knowledge) the first work to establish signal adaptivity and the strong oracle property (including asymptotic normality) under adversarial contamination. The use of separate thresholding levels for $\beta$ and $\theta$ is a technically motivated and effective innovation.
- **Rigor:** Strong. Full minimax upper and lower bounds for both estimation and selection; explicit constants; explicit algorithmic parameter choices; geometric convergence proofs. Assumptions are standard for the sub-Gaussian iterative-algorithm literature.
- **Reproducibility:** Moderate-to-good. Algorithm details, tuning parameters ($\kappa=0.9$, $\eta=0.75$), simulation settings, and a Massart-type information criterion for tuning are described. No code link is provided in the main text, but the algorithm specification is sufficiently detailed for reimplementation.
- **Significance:** Meaningful for robust high-dimensional inference — the asymptotic normality result distinguishes it from existing convex (Lasso-type) contamination methods and enables downstream statistical inference.
- **Red flags:** The adaptive tuning used in experiments lacks theoretical backing (acknowledged as a limitation). The sub-Gaussian design restriction limits applicability to heavy-tailed covariates (a gap relative to some recent robust-statistics work). The persistent logarithmic gap between upper and lower bounds remains open.

## Strengths
- Clean two-stage design with a clear division of labor: Stage 1 provides efficient initial estimation; Stage 2 performs debiased refinement.
- Separating thresholding scales for $\beta$ and $\theta$ is both intuitive and theoretically powerful, yielding tighter $\ell_2$ bounds for $\beta^*$ than joint thresholding.
- Provides a complete statistical picture: minimax near-optimality, signal adaptivity, selection consistency, oracle property, and asymptotic normality — rare in the contamination literature.
- Broad generalizability demonstrated through GLM extension and the heavy-tailed-regression connection.
- Empirical results are compelling, with AC-IHT closely approaching the Oracle benchmark and robust across noise distributions.

## Limitations
- The Massart-type adaptive tuning criterion used in simulations lacks theoretical guarantees.
- A logarithmic gap remains between the established upper and lower bounds.
- Theory is restricted to sub-Gaussian designs; extension to heavy-tailed covariates is left open (Remark 2).
- Asymptotic normality requires the fairly strong sample-size condition $n \succ \max(s^2\log^2 p, s^3)$.
- No released code; reproducibility depends on careful reimplementation.
- Experiments are limited to a single (moderate-dimensional) regime; behavior under near-$n\approx p$ settings or higher contamination fractions is not explored.

## Questions for Further Exploration
- Can the adaptive (Massart-type) tuning criterion be endowed with theoretical guarantees, e.g., via an oracle-inequality analysis?
- How does AC-IHT behave empirically in the near-$p \gg n$ or near-$o \approx \sqrt{ns\log p/\log n}$ regimes where theory predicts a phase transition?
- Can the separate-thresholding idea be extended to heavy-tailed designs using truncation-based tools, closing the gap noted in Remark 2?
- Is the $\log n$ gap between the upper bound (Theorem 2) and the lower bound (Theorem 4) fundamental, or can it be removed with a sharper analysis?
- Does the asymptotic normality result extend to debiased estimators for general linear functionals of $\beta^*$ beyond coordinate projections?
- How does AC-IHT compare against the (non-computable) minimax-optimal multivariate-depth estimator of Gao (2020) in finite samples?
