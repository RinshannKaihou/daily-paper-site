⟶ Key finding

Commit your analysis to the next LLM — a model that can exist yet cannot exist yet cannot be hacked against, and hacks rarely transfer.

Why this matters

As LLMs become judges and labelers, “tune the prompt until significant” quietly inflates findings — preregistering for an unreleased model restores out-of-sample credibility to LLM-driven science.

↳ stakes rise as autonomous AI scientists p-hack unsupervised
⚠ CAVEAT

Critical edge

It does not eliminate p-hacking — some hacked configurations survive to the next model, and a determined adversary could file many private preregistrations and report selectively.

Scope is also narrow: only two binary tasks, four providers, 100 items each, and a threshold-based (not p-value) hacking criterion.

01

Methodology

temporal lock + non-transfer

The protocol fuses two facts into one commitment device: a confirmatory LLM released after preregistration could not exist at commitment time, and a configuration that hacks one model frequently fails to transfer to the next.

In four steps a researcher finalizes the procedure on today's models, stamps the analysis plan, preregisters a broad eligible-model set (e.g. Claude/GPT/Gemini/Grok with concrete inclusion thresholds such as price-per-token, benchmark score, or output-violation rate), then runs the confirmatory analysis on the first eligible model released afterward.

Evaluation uses a two-stage design: a retrospective pilot on 14 pre-preregistration models, then a prospective confirmatory run on the first eligible post-t₀ release plus five follow-ups. Two tasks are null by construction: review classification (ICLR 2020 reviews predate public LLMs, so any non-trivial “AI” rate is a false positive) and dietary comparison (true “Day 2 lower” base rate = 50%). Eleven configurations sweep prompt structure, decoding params and batching. The headline number is the mitigation rate — among cells where a hack succeeds on a model, the fraction where it fails to carry over to the next released model.

Why it works · two ingredients

① Non-existence at t₀

The confirmatory model hasn't shipped yet, so there is literally nothing to tune against — the usual p-hacking loop is starved of a target.

② Cross-model non-transfer

Even given a target, prompt/decoding hacks are model-specific; a config that games one LLM routinely misfires on the next.

③ What an LLM preregistration adds

Beyond a classic study: a “no-data” commitment to an unreleased model, the eligible-model set + selection rule, prompting/decoding as conditions, output-validity & parsing rules, and model-level abandonment rules (AsPredicted-compatible).

✓ Strengths
  • Elegant, practical core idea venues and researchers can adopt now — ships a ready AsPredicted template.
  • Self-applied preregistration gives the central claim a genuine out-of-sample confirmation, not just retrospective analysis.
  • Comprehensive robustness checks (threshold sweep, adversarial selection, per-provider, detection power) make the headline numbers hard to dismiss.
  • Clean null-by-construction design removes ambiguity about what even counts as a hack.
  • Targets a concrete, growing problem — LLM-as-a-judge and autonomous AI scientists — with concrete policy recommendations.
! Limitations
  • Does not eliminate p-hacking — some hacked configurations still propagate to the next model.
  • Only two binary tasks, four model families, 100-item samples; multi-class, free-form, regression, other corpora untested.
  • Hack is a fixed threshold on the target-label rate, not a p-value; multiple-testing corrections deferred to future work.
  • Eligible-model-set spec is non-trivial (families added/deprecated), and registry/embargo could be gamed via multiple private preregistrations.
  • Imposes a real-if-modest delay: median ~10 days for the next release, with seasonal gaps.
04

Open questions

for further exploration
1

Does mitigation hold for non-binary outputs — multi-class labeling, free-form generation, regression-style continuous outcomes?

2

Can the criterion become a p-value with multiple-testing correction, including for boundary-null tasks?

3

How does mitigation scale with the number of released models and provider diversity over a longer horizon?

4

Could the eligible-model set be gamed by predicting provider release schedules, and how predictable are those?

5

How should the protocol adapt for autonomous AI-scientist pipelines running end-to-end without human intervention?

6

Does non-transfer of hacks generalize to agentic / multi-step workflows, or only single-shot annotation?
