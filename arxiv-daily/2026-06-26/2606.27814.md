> **一句话总结：** ATOD 通过退火的 OPD-RL 调度（早期教师蒸馏主导、后期奖励驱动加强）与回合级分歧-不确定性加权（T-DUR），让小模型多轮智能体先模仿教师再超越教师，在 ALFWorld/WebShop/Search-QA 上稳定优于纯 OPD、GRPO 及现有蒸馏基线。

> **TL;DR:** ATOD is a hybrid on-policy distillation algorithm that anneals between teacher distillation (early) and reward-driven GRPO (late), plus a turn-level disagreement-uncertainty reweighting (T-DUR), enabling small multi-turn agents to first imitate then surpass their teachers on ALFWorld, WebShop, and Search-QA.

## 核心贡献
- 提出退火的 OPD-RL 联合训练目标：早期让 OPD（教师密集 token 级监督）主导以解决稀疏奖励 RL 的冷启动低效问题，随后逐步加强 GRPO 奖励驱动信号，使学生在模仿之后仍能超越教师上限。
- 提出 T-DUR（Turn-level Disagreement-Uncertainty Reweighting）：从采样 token 的对数概率中估计回合级师生分歧与学生不确定性两类信号，经逐轨迹归一化与 Soft-OR 融合，把蒸馏权重集中到高价值的决策回合上，避免对例行回合的无效监督。
- 将 OPD 与 GRPO 统一编码到单个 token 级混合优势 A_t = κ(s)·A^OPD + ρ(s)·A^GRPO 中，并用标准 GRPO 截断代理优化；T-DUR 只重加权蒸馏项，不削弱奖励信号。
- 在 ALFWorld、WebShop、Search-QA 上跨越三种学生规模（Qwen3-0.6B/1.7B/4B）验证：平均成功率比 OPD 高 3.03、比 GRPO 高 23.62，并比对应教师模型高 2.16 个百分点。
- 通过充分的消融与训练动力学诊断（OPD/RL 信号幅度、回合权重、师生差距曲线）解释了"退火调度"与"回合级加权"各自的作用机制。

## Key Contributions
- An annealed OPD–RL training objective that lets teacher distillation dominate early (fast bootstrap) and gradually hands control to reward-driven GRPO, avoiding both RL cold-start inefficiency and the OPD teacher ceiling.
- T-DUR (Turn-level Disagreement-Uncertainty Reweighting), a soft distillation gating mechanism that estimates turn-level utility from student–teacher disagreement and student uncertainty computed only from sampled-token log-probs, then fuses them via per-trajectory normalization and a Soft-OR (probabilistic t-conorm) into a continuous turn weight.
- A unified hybrid advantage A_t = κ(s)·A^OPD + ρ(s)·A^GRPO optimized with the standard GRPO clipped surrogate, where T-DUR reweights only the distillation term and the annealing schedules κ(s)↓, ρ(s)↑ are Lipschitz-smooth.
- Empirical validation across ALFWorld, WebShop, and Search-QA and three student sizes (Qwen3-0.6B/1.7B/4B): ATOD improves average success rate by 3.03 over OPD and 23.62 over GRPO, and surpasses the corresponding teacher models by 2.16 points.
- Diagnostic analyses (OPD/RL signal magnitude, mean turn weight, teacher–student gap) and ablations isolating annealing vs. T-DUR vs. token-level reweighting to justify each design choice.

## Methodology
ATOD post-trains a small student policy πθ on its own on-policy rollouts inside multi-turn environments. For each prompt it samples a group of G trajectories, computes environment rewards, and forms group-relative GRPO advantages. The key idea is a single token-level hybrid advantage:

A_t = κ(s)·A^OPD + ρ(s)·A^GRPO,  with  A^OPD = (log πT(a_t|s_t) − log πθ(a_t|s_t))·w_{k(t)}.

**Dynamic OPD/RL annealing.** A linear progress variable p(s)=min(s/T,1) drives κ(s) from κ_init down to a floor κ_min>0 (a weak teacher anchor against reward hacking) and ρ(s) from ρ_init up to ρ_max over T annealing steps. The actor is updated with the standard GRPO clipped surrogate using A_t; no explicit KL penalty is added because the teacher effect is already inside A^OPD.

**T-DUR (turn-level reweighting).** For each turn k, two sampled-token statistics are computed: a disagreement proxy d_k (mean teacher–student log-prob gap) and an uncertainty proxy h_k (mean student negative log-prob, an unbiased martingale-difference estimator of average token entropy). Both are per-trajectory min–max normalized and fused with Soft-OR, w_k = 1 − (1−d̃_k)(1−h̃_k), so a turn is upweighted when either signal is high. Crucially, T-DUR lifts token-importance ideas to the turn level (the natural decision unit) and only gates the distillation component.

## Results & Highlights
- Across the three student sizes, ATOD beats the second-best baseline by 3.89% (0.6B), 5.07% (1.7B), and 2.13% (4B) in average success rate, and surpasses its teacher on ALFWorld and WebShop for all sizes.
- For the 0.6B student starting near-zero on ALFWorld (0.78%) and WebShop (4.69%), ATOD reaches 82.81% and 86.72%—a >100× relative gain on ALFWorld vs. the vanilla model; GRPO only reaches 30.47% on ALFWorld, underscoring OPD's fast bootstrap.
- Concrete 1.7B average SR: ATOD 71.58 vs OPD 64.91 vs SOD 65.59 vs TCOD 66.05 vs GRPO 40.30 vs SDAR 41.94 vs Vanilla 15.59; teacher (4B GRPO) = 68.93.
- Training dynamics (Fig. 5–6): OPD/SOD warm-start fast but plateau, GRPO grows slowly, ATOD keeps OPD's fast start and keeps improving late; OPD signal magnitude decays while RL signal stays strong; ATOD also learns shorter, more efficient trajectories than GRPO.
- Ablations (Fig. 4): removing annealing causes the largest drop (0.6B: 82.8%→75.8%); turn-level T-DUR beats both token-level reweighting and uniform weighting (gap most notable on 1.7B).

## Authors & Credibility
From Tencent Inc. (Zefang Zong, Yang Li, Peng Chen) and Tsinghua University (Qitai Tan). The work targets a problem Tencent cares about—deploying small agentic models in latency/privacy/resource-sensitive settings—and uses Qwen3 students with Tencent-relevant agent benchmarks. The method section is unusually precise: closed-form objectives, a martingale-difference proof for the entropy proxy, Lipschitz analysis of the annealing schedule, a full algorithm box (Algorithm 1), and detailed hyperparameter/baseline tables in the appendix (8 GPUs, 150 steps, actor LR 1e-6, group size 8, annealing steps T=80, κ: 1.0→0.1, ρ: 1.0→2.0).

## Critical Assessment
- **Novelty:** The core idea—combine OPD with GRPO via an annealed schedule—is conceptually simple rather than revolutionary, and the components (annealing, disagreement/uncertainty weighting, reverse-KL OPD) are individually well established. The genuine novelty is (a) lifting token-importance reweighting to the turn level for agents, and (b) the clean unified-advantage formulation that gates only the distillation term.
- **Rigor:** Strong. Closed-form equations, a small theoretical justification (Claim 1 + Lipschitz properties), consistent wins across 3 benchmarks × 3 student sizes, ablations isolating each component, and informative diagnostic plots.
- **Reproducibility:** Good on paper—explicit hyperparameters, teacher/student checkpoints, baseline definitions, and prompt templates in the appendix. Caveat: requires the specific GRPO-trained teacher checkpoints (e.g., Qwen3-4B GRPO, Qwen3-30B-A3B GRPO), which may not be released.
- **Significance:** Practically useful for the small-agent regime; the >100× lift on 0.6B ALFWorld is striking and the "first imitate then surpass teacher" framing is well-motivated.
- **Red flags:** The reported gains over the teacher (2.16 avg) are modest and Search-QA stays within 1% of the teacher, so "surpassing the teacher" is benchmark-dependent. Only 150 training steps are reported for all sizes, and the Search-QA suite aggregates 7 QA datasets into one number, masking per-dataset behavior. Cost/compute comparison vs. baselines is not reported.

## Strengths
- Clean, implementation-friendly formulation: OPD+GRPO folded into one GRPO-style clipped surrogate; no extra value model; T-DUR needs only sampled-token log-probs (no full-vocabulary teacher sampling).
- Motivated by a clear, empirically demonstrated complementarity (OPD fast-but-plateaus, GRPO slow-but-higher-ceiling).
- Consistent improvements across three diverse agent domains (embodied, web, tool-augmented QA) and three model scales.
- Strong, mechanistic ablations and diagnostics that actually explain why each part helps.

## Limitations
- Gains over the teacher are small and uneven (essentially tied on Search-QA); "beyond teacher" claim is benchmark-specific.
- Single short training window (150 steps) for all sizes; no scaling-law or longer-horizon study.
- Search-QA collapses 7 datasets into one success-rate figure; per-dataset and multi-hop vs. single-hop breakdowns are absent.
- No compute/cost or wall-clock comparison against OPD/GRPO baselines; T-DUR adds teacher forward passes that aren't accounted for.
- Evaluated only on Qwen3 students; generalization to other families untested.
- Benchmarks are relatively short-horizon (ALFWorld/WebShop/Search-QA); behavior on much longer multi-turn trajectories is not shown.

## Relevance
This work sits squarely in autonomous-agent post-training: it specifically addresses how to transfer multi-turn agentic competence from a strong teacher to a small deployable student while still allowing reward-driven improvement beyond imitation. The "first imitate, then explore" recipe and turn-level credit assignment are directly relevant to building efficient, on-policy-distilled tool/web/embodied agents.

## Questions for Further Exploration
- How does ATOD behave on much longer-horizon tasks (e.g., 50+ turn web/marathon agent benchmarks) where turn error compounding is more severe?
- Is the linear κ/ρ annealing schedule optimal, or would adaptive (loss- or reward-gap-driven) scheduling do better?
- Can T-DUR be extended to also gate the RL advantage, or to use environment-derived turn criticality (e.g., option-level value) rather than only log-prob signals?
- How sensitive are results to the teacher choice and teacher–student capacity gap (here 4B/30B teachers)?
- Does the modest Search-QA gain indicate a ceiling of distillation for open-domain knowledge tasks vs. structured interaction tasks?
- What is the marginal compute cost of ATOD's teacher forward passes relative to the reported success-rate gains over GRPO?
