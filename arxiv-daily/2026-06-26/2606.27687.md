> **一句话总结：** 该论文提出一种预注册协议——在确定实验流程和候选模型集后，于预注册之后发布的第一个符合条件的 LLM 上运行验证分析，从而利用"可 p-hack 一个模型的配置通常无法迁移到下一个模型"这一特性来缓解基于 LLM 的研究中的 p-hacking 问题。

> **TL;DR:** The authors propose preregistering an LLM experiment's procedure and eligible-model set, then running the confirmatory analysis on the first LLM released after preregistration—since that model cannot exist at commitment time, it cannot be hacked against, and configurations that hack one model frequently fail to transfer to the next.

## 核心贡献
- 提出"为下一个 LLM 预注册"协议：研究者在现有模型上确定实验流程，预注册分析方案与候选模型集，随后在预注册后发布的第一个符合条件的模型上执行验证分析。
- 利用两个机制：(1) 确认模型在承诺时点尚不存在，因而无法被针对性攻击；(2) 能 p-hack 某模型的配置往往无法迁移到下一个模型。
- 在两个真实值为零（按构造为无效效应）的任务上系统评估：评论分类（AI 生成评论占比真实值为 0%）与饮食对比（后一天卡路里更低的真实比例为 50%），覆盖 20 个模型（4 家厂商）与 11 种配置。
- 缓解率分别达到 73.9%（评论任务）和 72.7%（饮食任务），并在多种压力测试下保持稳健；作者本人按协议进行了预注册（2026-03-25），预注册实验复现了协议的有效性。
- 提供了面向 AsPredicted/OSF 平台的 LLM 实验最小预注册模板（表 1）。

## Key Contributions
- Proposes a four-step protocol: (1) finalize the experimental procedure on current models, (2) preregister the procedure, (3) preregister a broad set of eligible future models, (4) run the confirmatory analysis on the first eligible model released afterward.
- Exploits two ingredients together: confirmatory models do not exist at commitment time (so cannot be hacked against) and p-hacking configurations frequently fail to transfer across models.
- Empirically evaluates on two tasks with true null effects by construction—review classification (true AI-prevalence 0%) and dietary comparison (true "Day 2 lower" rate 50%)—across 20 models from four providers and 11 LLM-analysis configurations.
- Reports mitigation rates of 73.9% (review) and 72.7% (dietary), with robustness to strategic configuration selection (≥60%), single-provider restriction (≥50%), and retained detection power comparable to re-measuring with the same model.
- The authors followed their own protocol, preregistering on March 25, 2026 (Grok 4.20 as confirmatory model); the preregistered run reproduced the effectiveness (6 of 7 hacking configurations failed to carry over, ~85.7%).

## Methodology
The protocol combines temporal commitment with cross-model non-transfer of hacks. In a four-step workflow, a researcher finalizes procedures on currently available models, preregisters the analysis plan plus a broad eligible-model set (e.g., naming eligible families like Claude/GPT/Gemini/Grok with concrete inclusion thresholds such as price-per-token, benchmark scores, or output-violation rates), and then executes on the first eligible model released after the preregistration timestamp. The authors provide an AsPredicted-compatible template specifying what an LLM preregistration must add beyond a traditional study (the "no data" commitment to an unreleased confirmatory model, the eligible-model set and selection rule, prompting/decoding parameters as conditions, output-validity and parsing rules, and model-level abandonment rules).

Evaluation uses a two-stage design: a retrospective "pilot" on 14 models released before preregistration, then a prospective confirmatory analysis on the first eligible post-preregistration model plus five follow-up releases. Two tasks are constructed so the target effect is null by construction: (1) review classification—LLMs annotate whether ICLR 2020 reviews (predating public LLMs) are AI-generated, so any non-trivial "AI" rate is a false positive; (2) dietary comparison—LLMs judge which of two consecutive diet-logging days is lower in calories, where the true base rate is 50%. Eleven configurations vary prompt structure (zero-shot, counterfactual, instructional, directional, few-shot, framed+imputation), decoding parameters (low top-p, high temperature), and batching. The primary metric is the mitigation rate: among model+configuration pairs where a hack succeeds on a model, the fraction where the hack fails to carry over to the next released model. Position bias in the dietary task is controlled by randomizing display order and inverting labels accordingly.

## Results & Highlights
- **Main mitigation rates:** The protocol would have blocked successful transfer of the p-hack in 17 of 23 hackable cells on the review task (73.9%, Wilson 95% CI [53.5%, 87.5%]) and 32 of 44 on the dietary task (72.7%, CI [58.2%, 83.7%]).
- **Robustness to hacking thresholds:** Mitigation remains high across a sweep of thresholds (Figure 1), unless thresholds are very small.
- **Adversarial configuration selection:** Even if the researcher strategically preregisters the configuration with the highest target-label rate or the one most often hacked historically, mitigation stays ≥60% (Figure 2).
- **Single-provider restriction:** Mitigation is ≥50% for each individual provider, though single-provider subsets have small denominators and heterogeneous results (Figure 3).
- **Detection power preserved:** On the diet task with injected real effects of varying strength, detection rate under the protocol overlaps (95% CIs) with re-measuring using the same model—the protocol costs no detectable power (Figure 4).
- **Preregistered prospective run:** The authors' own preregistration (confirmatory model Grok 4.20) reproduced effectiveness—of 7 configurations that hacked the prior model, hacking failed to carry over in 6 (~85.7% overall).
- **Cost in time:** Mean waiting time for the next eligible release is ~14.5 days (median ~10.3 days) over the study window; the largest gap occurred during the December 2025–January 2026 winter break.

## Authors & Credibility
Maria Thomas and Kristina Gligorić (Johns Hopkins University) and Nihar B. Shah (Carnegie Mellon University, jointly supervising). Shah has a strong track record on peer review, LLM-as-a-judge, and statistical inference for LLM annotations (e.g., prediction-powered inference, detecting LLM-generated reviews); Gligorić co-authored active-learning work on unconfident LLM annotations. The work is NSF/ONR-supported. The strongest credibility signal is methodological self-application: the authors preregistered their own confirmatory experiment on AsPredicted before any post-preregistration model existed, and the confirmatory result was decided by a model they could not pre-test. Code/configurations and the preregistration are publicly linked.

## Critical Assessment
- **Novelty:** High and original. The key insight—committing to a model that does not yet exist, combined with the empirical non-transfer of hacks across models—is a simple but previously unarticulated mechanism that adapts classical preregistration to the LLM era. It complements rather than competes with debiasing (PPI, DSL, doubly-robust) and robustness/multiverse approaches, which the authors correctly note do not themselves stop p-hacking.
- **Rigor:** The two-stage retrospective + preregistered-prospective design, threshold sweeps, adversarial-selection stress tests, single-provider analyses, and a dedicated detection-power analysis together form a thorough empirical case. Using tasks with ground-truth nulls is a clean way to know p-hacking when it occurs.
- **Reproducibility:** Strong. Datasets are public (ICLR reviews, MyFitnessPal), all 11 configurations and decoding parameters are documented, evaluations go through a unified OpenRouter API, and the authors' own preregistration and code are linked.
- **Significance:** Timely and policy-relevant, especially given autonomous AI scientists found to p-hack without human direction. The proposed venue-level adoption is concrete and low-cost to authors.
- **Red flags / caveats:** The threat model assumes a single confirmatory run on one model; a determined adversary could privately file many preregistrations and report selectively (acknowledged; mitigated by registry duplicate-detection/embargo). Operationalizing a "hack" via a fixed threshold rather than a p-value (necessary for the boundary null in the review task) is a simplification; a full p-value/multiple-testing instantiation is left to future work. Empirical scope is limited to four providers, two binary-classification tasks, 100 items each, and threshold-based analyses—generalization to multi-class, free-form generation, regression outputs, or other corpora is unverified. Single-provider and some strategic-selection sub-analyses have small denominators and should be read cautiously.

## Strengths
- Elegant, practical core idea that is easy for venues and researchers to adopt; provides a ready-to-use AsPredicted template.
- Self-applied preregistration gives the central claim a genuine out-of-sample confirmation rather than only retrospective analysis.
- Comprehensive robustness checks (threshold sweep, adversarial config selection, per-provider, detection power) make the headline numbers hard to dismiss.
- Clean controlled design using tasks where the true effect is zero by construction, removing ambiguity about what counts as a hack.
- Directly addresses a growing, concrete problem (LLM-as-a-judge and autonomous AI scientists), with clear policy recommendations.

## Limitations
- Does not eliminate p-hacking—some hacked configurations do propagate to the next model.
- Evaluated only on two binary tasks, four model families, and 100-item samples; transfer to other task types, corpora, and statistical analyses is untested.
- Hack is operationalized as a fixed threshold on the target-label rate, not a p-value; multiple-testing corrections and conventional significance-level reporting are future work.
- Eligible-model-set specification is non-trivial (families are added/deprecated over time) and a determined adversary could exploit registry/embargo weaknesses via multiple private preregistrations.
- The protocol imposes a real (if modest) delay waiting for the next eligible release; median ~10 days but with seasonal gaps.

## Relevance
该工作直接关系到 LLM 作为数据生成器、标注器或裁判（LLM-as-a-judge）时下游假设检验的可重复性与可信度：它把传统的预注册思想扩展到 LLM 时代，为缓解"通过调参/改 prompt 直到显著"的 p-hacking 提供了一种可落地、可验证的机制，对依赖 LLM 输出的实证研究（以及日益增多的自主 AI 科学家系统）具有重要方法论意义。

## Questions for Further Exploration
- How does mitigation hold up for non-binary outputs (multi-class labeling, free-form generation, regression-style continuous outcomes)?
- Can the protocol be extended to use a p-value with multiple-testing correction as the hacking criterion, including for tasks with boundary nulls?
- How does mitigation rate scale with the number of released models and provider diversity over a longer time horizon?
- Could the eligible-model-set selection be gamed by predicting provider release schedules, and how predictable are those schedules?
- How should the protocol be adapted for autonomous AI scientist pipelines that run end-to-end without human intervention?
- Does the non-transfer of hacks generalize to agentic/multi-step LLM workflows, or only to single-shot annotation/classification?
