Methodology

A well-posed dual-constraint problem solved by alternating projections — not an ad-hoc fix.

Aurora targets the tall up/gate projection matrices in SwiGLU MLPs. The authors first recast Muon's update as the solution to a spectral-norm trust-region problem, then augment it with an equal-row-norm constraint.

min ⟨∇W, ΔW⟩ s.t. ‖ΔW‖2 ≤ η // Muon + ‖ΔW‖row ≈ uniform // Aurora's extra constraint

Under uniform row norms the spectral relaxation collapses to left semi-orthogonality (Stiefel), so the problem becomes steepest descent on the intersection of the Stiefel manifold and the equal-row-leverage manifold. The practical solver (Alg. 3) alternates two projections, proven locally linearly convergent (Thm 4.1). A costlier Riemannian reference (Alg. 2) validates it but is O(m²) and used only for checks.

Aurora is roughly K× costlier than Muon's polar step — but overlaps with gradient communication, so distributed wall-clock penalty stays low.

Aurora — inner loop

i.Damped row normalization with momentum scalar β → equalize row leverage
ii.Newton–Schulz polar-factor approximation → restore semi-orthogonality
↻Repeat for K inner steps
Constraint target: semi-orthogonal ∧ equal-row-norm
Mid-step variant: U-NorMuon (stateless, tall-only norm) bridges Muon → Aurora.

Strengths & Limitations

Principled theory and broad empirical wins — but modest scale and post-hoc forensics.

Strengths ▲

  • Clean mechanistic story: row leverage scores → neuron death, validated internally and on external open-weight models.
  • Principled, not ad-hoc — Aurora solves a well-posed dual-constraint problem with a local linear-convergence theorem.
  • Consistent wins across two scales, eight benchmarks, and the modded-nanoGPT speedrun.
  • Thorough ablation isolating tall-only normalization, statelessness, and polar scaling.
  • Practical: low distributed overhead, released code, clear optimizer–architecture co-design message.

Limitations ▼

  • Scale (≤1.1B, 100B tokens) is far below frontier; generalization to 100B+ params is unproven.
  • MLP-width scaling conflates width with parameter count — not a controlled width sweep.
  • Open-weight neuron-death detection is post-hoc, threshold-sensitive; authors call it "preliminary."
  • K× polar-computation cost; benefit depends on hardware-specific communication overlap.
  • Aurora↔Riemannian agreement shown only on a single early-step momentum buffer.

Open Questions

Where Aurora's claims are weakest — and where they could go next.
Q1
Does the mitigation and loss advantage persist at frontier scale (≥10–100B params, trillion-token training), or vanish as other instabilities dominate?
Q2
Could an adaptive schedule switch Aurora → plain Muon once leverage converges uniform, saving compute?
Q3
Is the width-scaling result robust under a controlled sweep (fixed compute/param count, varying only expansion factor)?
Q4
Does Aurora change learned representations beyond loss/benchmark gains — feature diversity, interpretability?
Q5
How does Aurora compose with other spectral innovations (Contra-Muon, soft SV maps, Riemannian Muon) beyond the one combo tested?
