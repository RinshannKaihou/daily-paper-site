01

Methodology

A generalization of classical feature-attribution sufficiency to arbitrary free-text explanations — with the input distribution made a first-class, measured object.

Classical sufficiency asks whether a selected feature subset alone predicts the label as well as the full input. The authors lift this to generic explanation methods g(e|x) mapping inputs to distributions over explanations. An explanation e is sufficient when the model's output given the explanation equals its output marginalized over alternative inputs sharing that explanation.

Because that conditional depends on an input distribution qx(x) — implicit in supervised learning but absent for LLMs — they prove (Theorem 3.2) that sufficiency can flip between sufficient and insufficient across distributions whenever the output isn't constant given the explanation.

To make this actionable, the same LLM is prompted to generate alternative inputs x′ conditioned on its self-generated explanation, encoding the model's own beliefs about plausible inputs. SCSUFF is the normalized expected KL divergence between the model's output on the true input and on those alternatives, computed purely from log-likelihoods.

DEF 2.2 · SUFFICIENCY
q(y|x) = q(y|e), marginalizing output over inputs sharing explanation e.
DEF 4.1 · SELF-CONSISTENT
F(y|x) = E[F(y|x′)] under the model's own alternative-input generation.
SCSUFF ∈ (0,1]
Normalized expected KL between the two — 1 = self-consistently sufficient.
SCSUFF — computation flow
The model's own explanation and beliefs are turned into a measurable consistency gap — no external judge, no predefined shortcut.

03

Strengths & Limitations

+

Strengths

  • Principled, general, dataset- and model-agnostic framework — unifying CoT, post-hoc rationales, counterfactuals and prototypes where prior methods were feature-specific.
  • Cleanly isolates and proves the often-overlooked role of the input distribution in defining sufficiency (Theorem 3.2).
  • SCSUFF is computable directly from log-likelihoods — no external judge, no predefined biases or shortcuts.
  • Honest, actionable negative result: explanations are broadly insufficient and disconnected from scale/accuracy.
  • Connects the metric to internal representations, opening a path toward detecting and optimizing sufficient explanations.
!

Limitations

  • Self-consistency ≠ faithfulness to humans: it evaluates agreement among model-generated artifacts, not human-decodability.
  • Uninformative when an explanation fully determines the input (identical alternatives) — a degenerate case acknowledged but not guaranteed away.
  • Depends on the quality/variety of LLM-generated alternatives; only ~70% judged constraint-satisfying, and the lenient prompt sees the original input.
  • Hidden-state predictability analysis is limited to 8B models on two datasets — generalization unclear.
  • Using the model itself as the hint-mention verifier is cost-driven but methodologically imperfect.
04

Open Questions

Where does this go next?
The metric measures sufficiency but does not yet produce it. The most promising directions turn SCSUFF from a probe into an objective.
Q1
Can SCSUFF be optimized as a training objective (RL, or a representation-level probe) to produce genuinely sufficient explanations — not just measure them?
Q2
How does the model-decodable vs. human-decodable information gap scale with size and instruction tuning? When does self-consistency start to imply human-faithfulness?
Q3
Does localizing SCSUFF-relevant info beyond the final token — across layers or attention heads — reveal which circuits drive the explanation–behavior mismatch?
Q4
How robust is SCSUFF when alternatives come from a stronger external model (e.g. GPT-5) rather than the LLM under evaluation — do the insufficiency conclusions hold?
Q5
Could the input-distribution relativity of sufficiency be exploited adversarially to make any explanation appear sufficient — and what does that mean for safety auditing?
