> **一句话总结：** Mosaic 是一个开源、可扩展的可微 PDE 求解器基准测试框架，将 14 个求解器封装在统一的前向/梯度容器接口下，系统性地揭示了在梯度精度、计算成本、Jacobian 条件数与设置兼容性方面的实践性差异。

> **TL;DR:** Mosaic is an open-source, extensible benchmark suite that wraps 14 differentiable PDE solvers behind a uniform containerized forward/gradient interface and surfaces practically relevant differences in gradient accuracy, computational cost, Jacobian conditioning, and setup compatibility.

## 核心贡献
- 提出统一、容器化的求解器接口（基于 Tesseract），每个求解器暴露 `apply()` 与 `vector_jacobian_product()` 两个入口，屏蔽语言与自动微分（AD）策略差异，可仅通过修改配置字符串即切换 14 个后端。
- 定义了标准化的评估协议，覆盖设置兼容性、梯度精度（以中心差分为参考）、计算成本、Jacobian 条件数与端到端优化收敛性五个维度，且协议与求解器内部实现无关。
- 跨四个物理域（传热 H、结构力学 S、2D/3D Navier–Stokes F2/F3）构建了渐进难度的基准任务，其中 H 域作为"阴性对照"用于隔离 AD Bug 与混沌物理。
- 通过 14 个求解器的实证评估，揭示了具有实践指导意义的发现：梯度质量通常"够用"，真正的瓶颈在于内存限制、数值稳定性与设置兼容性，而非梯度精度本身。
- 全部求解器接口、评估代码与模板开源，并通过 CI 持续重新评估以保持结果随求解器改进而更新。

## Key Contributions
- A uniform containerized solver interface (built on Tesseract) exposing `apply()` and `vector_jacobian_product()` regardless of language or AD strategy, so all 14 backends are swappable via a single config string.
- A standardized, solver-agnostic evaluation protocol measuring setup compatibility, gradient accuracy (vs. central finite differences), computational cost, Jacobian conditioning, and end-to-end optimization convergence.
- A four-domain benchmark with a deliberate difficulty progression (heat transfer, structural mechanics, 2D cylinder inflow optimization, 3D Navier–Stokes IC recovery), using the heat domain as a negative control to isolate AD bugs from chaotic dynamics.
- Empirical findings across 14 solvers that validate the benchmark by surfacing order-of-magnitude variation in cost and conditioning plus structural incompatibilities that eliminate solvers from realistic tasks.
- An open-source release with continuous re-evaluation designed to keep published results current as solvers improve.

## Methodology
Mosaic organizes evaluation around three concepts: a physical setup (a concrete geometry, boundary conditions, and operating regime within a domain), a benchmark task (a fixed optimization problem with a control variable, objective, and reference solution), and a solver backend (the software implementing the forward map and optionally its gradient). Each backend is packaged as a Tesseract container exposing `apply(inputs) → outputs` and `vector_jacobian_product(inputs, cotangents) → vjp`; forward-only solvers (OpenFOAM, deal.II) receive automatic finite-difference gradients serving as reference baselines. Because solver APIs differ too widely for a single generic wrapper, each task defines a task-specific interface schema that multiple solvers implement, making the protocol independent of solver internals. Gradient accuracy is validated against central finite differences along 6–20 random perturbation directions (cosine similarity and relative ℓ2 error), with full Jacobian SVD spectra computed for the Navier–Stokes tasks. Performance metrics (primal/VJP wall-clock, their ratio, peak (V)RAM) are measured across 3–4 problem sizes on 4× V100 GPUs. Optimization convergence is run with shared hyperparameters and a fixed iteration budget (500 for F2/F3, 2000 for H, 2500 for S), with success defined as reaching within 1% of the best solver's objective. The four domains form a difficulty progression, and the heat domain serves as a negative control because its smooth, well-conditioned physics means any gradient failure is attributable to an AD bug rather than ill-conditioned dynamics.

## Results & Highlights
- All differentiable solvers achieve cosine similarity > 0.999 between AD and finite-difference gradients at their optimal perturbation size, confirming AD correctness — yet the optimal perturbation ε shifts by orders of magnitude across solvers, rollout length, and viscosity.
- Computational cost varies by orders of magnitude at the same resolution; the fastest backends (Exponax, JAX-CFD) support only periodic boundaries, while solvers handling the full F2 drag task (XLB, PICT) are up to ~200× slower.
- Jacobian conditioning varies dramatically across solvers at the same resolution, driven by numerical method rather than AD strategy: projection-based solvers develop steep spectral drop-offs (large κ) with rollout length, while LBM/spectral methods (Exponax, XLB) maintain flatter spectra.
- Memory becomes the binding constraint before gradient accuracy at larger resolutions and longer rollouts — several solvers exceed the 16 GB GPU limit and fail entirely; source-transformation AD (JAX-based) keeps a near-constant VJP/forward ratio, while tape-based adjoints (FEniCS, Firedrake) scale more steeply.
- On the negative-control domains (H, S) all solvers converge to the same optimum; on F3 IC recovery, Adam stagnates above 40% error while L-BFGS drops below 6%, and projecting the gradient onto the divergence-free subspace (L-BFGS+proj) reaches below 0.5%.
- On the hardest F2 drag task, structural incompatibilities (periodic-only BCs, penalization) thin the field to 3 of 7 backends; XLB and PICT achieve ~60% drag reduction, PhiFlow partially converges (~35%). This attrition is itself a key finding: setup compatibility constrains solver choice before gradient quality matters.
- Integration effort varies hugely: PhiFlow/JAX-CFD need ~12 shared lines, while Warp-NS needs >800 bespoke lines for its IC VJP alone; INS.jl uses Zygote AD yet still requires >470 gradient-specific lines.

The work is grounded in deep familiarity with the differentiable-physics ecosystem (citing JAX-CFD, PhiFlow, FEniCS, OpenFOAM, NeuralFluid, APEBench, PDEBench), and the authors openly acknowledge their own tooling dependency on Tesseract. The paper is paired with an open-source release (Apache 2.0), pinned lockfile, tagged reproduction tag (v0.1+paper-repro), and CI-driven continuous re-evaluation, lending strong reproducibility credibility.

## Critical Assessment
- **Novelty:** High. No prior benchmark systematically measures gradient properties across independent solver backends; existing efforts (APEBench, PDEBench) focus on forward accuracy or learned surrogates, and NeuralFluid reports gradient runtime without assessing accuracy, conditioning, or compatibility. The OpenAI-Gym-inspired "standardize access, ship no ML" design philosophy is a genuinely useful framing.
- **Rigor:** Strong. The protocol is solver-agnostic, finite-difference references are used carefully (with solver-specific optimal ε and U-shaped perturbation sweeps), a negative-control domain isolates AD bugs from chaotic physics, and the dual-purpose experiments validate the benchmark rather than just report numbers. Hardware, timing protocol, and per-domain specs are documented in detail.
- **Reproducibility:** Excellent. Pinned lockfile, tagged release, containerized backends, two reproduction paths (regenerate figures from archive or re-run from scratch), and CI that re-runs the suite on every change.
- **Significance:** Practitioner-facing and infrastructure-focused rather than a single SOTA result; the lasting contribution is the reusable evaluation infrastructure and the (mildly provocative but well-supported) message that "gradient quality is usually good enough."
- **Red flags:** The three stated assumptions (finite-difference reference reliability, out-of-the-box config representativeness, standalone tasks as proxy for pipelines) are acknowledged but untested. Results are pinned to specific solver versions and V100 hardware, so rankings may shift. The inclusion of the authors' own Tesseract runtime is a (declared) dependency that could bias toward Tesseract-friendly architectures. Benchmark tasks are intentionally simpler than SOTA challenges, so the "gradients are good enough" conclusion may not extrapolate to harder problems.

## Strengths
- Genuinely uniform interface across 14 heterogeneous solvers spanning 4 languages and 5+ AD/adjoint strategies, lowering the barrier to cross-solver comparison.
- Clean experimental design with a negative control that disentangles AD bugs from chaotic/ill-conditioned physics.
- Highly practitioner-relevant, actionable findings (rollout length is the critical scaling parameter; conditioning predicts stability better than pointwise accuracy; reverse-mode AD offers the best effort-to-capability tradeoff).
- Strong reproducibility posture: Apache 2.0, pinned lockfile, CI-driven re-evaluation, two reproduction paths.
- Honest about scope and limitations, including solver-version pinning and the standalone-task proxy assumption.

## Limitations
- Benchmark tasks are deliberately simpler than state-of-the-art challenges, so conclusions (especially "gradient quality is good enough") may not transfer to harder, ill-posed, or high-dimensional problems.
- Relies on central finite differences as ground truth, which is itself subject to perturbation-size sensitivity (acknowledged but inherent).
- Results are hardware-specific (4× V100) and pinned to specific solver versions; relative rankings may differ on CPU or newer GPUs.
- No learned/hybrid components are tested, so failure modes of solver-in-the-loop ML pipelines are not captured.
- Only four domains; multiphysics, compressible flow, and Enzyme/LLVM-level AD backends are left to future work.
- The bespoke-LOC metric for integration effort is admittedly coarse and excludes glue code.

## Relevance
本论文直接对应数值求解器可靠性与基准测试这一主题：它首次系统性地、与求解器内部无关地测量了可微 PDE 求解器的梯度属性（精度、成本、条件数、兼容性），并通过阴性对照与统一接口为"评估数值软件梯度是否可信"提供了可复现的方法论，对任何依赖可微物理的训练、最优控制或反问题流程都具有选型与质量保障价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration
- Do the "gradient quality is good enough" and "conditioning predicts stability" conclusions hold on harder, higher-dimensional, or chaotic (e.g., turbulence) problems beyond the tested setups?
- How do rankings shift on modern GPUs (e.g., H100) and on CPU-heavy backends, given that all timings were V100-only?
- What failure modes appear when these solver gradients are embedded in solver-in-the-loop ML training versus the isolated optimization tasks tested here?
- Can the finite-difference reference be replaced or augmented by an analytical or higher-precision reference for ill-conditioned problems where ε tuning is unstable?
- How does Enzyme/LLVM-IR-level AD compare to framework-level (JAX/PyTorch) and hand-derived adjoints when added as backends?
