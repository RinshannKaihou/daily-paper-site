> **一句话总结：** KernelSight-LM 是一个内核级 LLM 推理模拟器，通过 roofline×efficiency 模型与离散事件调度器，在无需逐设备 profiling 的前提下实现跨代 GPU 性能预测，并同时输出内核级延迟分解以支撑服务策略分析。

> **TL;DR:** KernelSight-LM is a kernel-level LLM inference simulator that predicts per-kernel latency on unseen GPUs from datasheets alone (or with a one-shot microbenchmark) and composes them through a vLLM-faithful discrete-event scheduler to yield accurate end-to-end TTFT/TPOT/throughput with kernel-level bottleneck breakdowns.

## 核心贡献
- 提出两层内核延迟预测架构：Tier A 仅用 datasheet 零样本预测未见 GPU（12.1% 内核级误差），Tier B 用一次模型无关的微基准扫描将误差降至 3.8%。
- 将 transformer 执行分解为基本内核（GEMM、attention prefill/decode、elementwise/memory），用 roofline 模型承担硬件缩放、用学习到的无量纲效率因子 η 承担设备无关残差，从而实现跨代迁移。
- 逐行重实现了 vLLM v1 调度器（含连续批处理、chunked prefill、两级 GPU/disk 前缀缓存），并显式建模 host 端开销（CPU/GPU overlap、launch、TP 同步），使模拟批次组成与真实引擎逐步对齐（Spearman 相关 +0.985）。
- 端到端在 6 个模型族（0.5B–70B）上达到 Tier A 的 15.4%/12.8%/3.0% 和 Tier B 的 14.3%/6.2%/2.7% 的 TTFT/TPOT/吞吐中位误差，所需目标设备数据远少于已有系统。
- 输出内核级瓶颈分解（如 prefill 阶段 GEMM 占 ~77%、decode 阶段 attention 随 KV 增长占主导），支持软硬件协同设计与容量规划。

## Key Contributions
- Two-tier kernel latency prediction: zero-shot cross-generation (Tier A, datasheet-only, 12.1% per-kernel MAPE) and target-measured refinement (Tier B, one-shot model-agnostic microbenchmark sweep, 3.8% MAPE).
- A roofline×efficiency decomposition where the device roofline absorbs all hardware scaling and a learned dimensionless efficiency factor η is the only quantity that must be transferred across GPU generations, enabling extrapolation to unbenchmarked hardware (e.g., Blackwell GB200).
- Faithful re-implementation of vLLM v1's scheduler step-for-step (continuous batching, chunked prefill, two-tier prefix cache) plus an explicit host-overhead channel, validated to track the real engine's per-step batch composition.
- End-to-end accuracy across six model families (0.5B–70B) of 15.4%/12.8%/3.0% (Tier A) and 14.3%/6.2%/2.7% (Tier B) for TTFT/TPOT/throughput at the median, matching dedicated profiling tools while collecting far less target-device data.
- Kernel-level latency breakdowns that reveal shifting bottleneck composition (GEMM-dominated prefill vs. attention-dominated long-context decode), supporting what-if analysis and hardware/software co-design.

## Methodology
KernelSight-LM combines a kernel-level latency predictor with Vidur's discrete-event serving simulator. Each kernel's latency is modeled as `t = max(t_roof·η, t_0)`, where the roofline bound `t_roof` is computed entirely from datasheet peaks (peak compute, peak bandwidth, SM count) plus a wave-quantization correction `u`, `t_0` is a portable launch floor, and `η` is a learned efficiency factor trained on dimensionless features (arithmetic intensity, occupancy, wave count, L2 residency) with per-family heads selected from a low-capacity pool (regularized linear, GBDT/bagged trees, small MLP ensemble, and a bounded analytical form adapted from NeuSight). Communication (tensor-parallel all-reduce) follows a bandwidth-bound ring-all-reduce formula. Per-kernel predictions are aggregated up the transformer hierarchy (layer → step body) and combined with a host-overhead model `t_step = max(t_gpu + n·c_disp, ℓ) + max(0, c_ovl − t_gpu) + c_tail` to capture CPU/GPU overlap. The simulator faithfully replays vLLM v1's token-budget scheduling loop, prices mixed prefill-decode steps kernel-by-kernel, and models hierarchical prefix caching; Tier B additionally interpolates a measured efficiency grid (density-routed, falling back to the learned model outside the convex hull) and calibrates host constants from a short serving sweep.

## Results & Highlights
- **Cross-generation per-kernel (Tier A, GB200 held out):** mean MAPE 12.1% vs NeuSight's 22.0% (1.8× improvement), with the largest gains on memory-bound/attention kernels (attention decode 9.3 vs 26.3, 2.8×; RoPE 18.0 vs 36.7, 2.0×); GEMM is a near-tie (~12.8 vs 13.4) as both sit near the irreducible cross-gen floor.
- **In-distribution per-kernel (Tier B, GB200):** mean MAPE 3.8% vs AIConfigurator's 27.7% (7.3× improvement); elementwise/memory kernels improve ~10× (e.g., Embedding 0.9 vs 64.1, KV-cache write 3.3 vs 43.1) because AIConfigurator never measures them and uses a fixed `bytes/(0.8·BW)+3µs` shortcut.
- **End-to-end (GB200, production conversation trace):** Tier B TPOT median 6.2% (p99 27%), throughput p50 2.7% (≤1% at light load, ≤8% at saturation); TTFT median 14.3% but long-tail (p99 81%) dominated by long-context (19–23k token) prefill where attention is least transferable.
- **Baselines fail structurally:** AIConfigurator's TTFT median is 59% (no host-overhead model) and Vidur collapses to 436% median error (its profiles use a different mode than actual vLLM graphed runs), making KernelSight-LM ~4× more accurate on TTFT than either.
- **Bottleneck breakdown:** Figure 8 shows prefill is GEMM-dominated (~77%) while decode attention grows with KV length to dominate at long context — illustrating why a fixed kernel decomposition is inaccurate.

## Authors & Credibility
The paper lists ten authors spanning Amazon Web Services (Santa Clara, CA) and Boston University (the input metadata captures the first six; Ashish Khetan, Xiang Song, George Karypis, and Martin Herbordt are additional authors). The team combines AWS production ML-systems expertise with academic HPC credentials (George Karypis is a noted parallel-algorithms researcher; Martin Herbordt leads GPU computing at BU). The work targets real production serving (vLLM v1, NVLink, NCCL) on a broad GPU ladder (Ampere, Hopper, Ada, Blackwell GB200 plus edge GDDR7/LPDDR5X parts) and is benchmarked against strong prior systems (NeuSight, AIConfigurator, Vidur) on commercial EC2 instances and BUSCC clusters, lending substantial empirical credibility.

## Critical Assessment
- **Novelty:** Genuinely bridges a real gap — no prior system jointly offered cross-generation extrapolation *and* full serving-dynamics simulation *and* kernel granularity. The roofline×efficiency factorization (datasheet carries scaling, η is the only learned/transferred quantity) is a clean, well-motivated decomposition, and the per-family low-capacity head selection is a sound inductive-bias choice for a low-data extrapolation regime.
- **Rigor:** Strong. Authors isolate variables carefully (same datasheet roofline, same held-out ladder when comparing to NeuSight), report full error distributions (p50/p90/p99/max), validate the simulator line-by-line against vLLM v1 source (Spearman +0.985), and confirm aggregation fidelity (≈2% residual vs real vLLM). The honest §B limitation analysis (η portability experiment) is a highlight.
- **Reproducibility:** Mixed. Software stack (NGC PyTorch 26.04, vLLM v0.19.0, CUDA 13) and GPU/model tables are fully specified, but code release is not mentioned, the kernel-microbenchmark database and learned head weights are not clearly released, and the production "conversation trace" is proprietary — independent reproduction would be difficult.
- **Significance:** High for capacity planning, procurement, and co-design on not-yet-owned hardware; the kernel-level breakdown is uniquely useful for diagnosis.
- **Red flags:** None severe. The headline 1.8×/7.3× per-kernel gains are concentrated in kernels that matter less at end-to-end level (GEMM dominates runtime and is only a tie), and the authors openly acknowledge this. GB200 is the sole held-out target generation (no second-gen cross-validation).

## Strengths
- Unified two-tier system spanning profiling-free extrapolation to high-fidelity in-distribution prediction within one roofline×efficiency formula.
- Explicit, faithful modeling of vLLM v1 serving dynamics (continuous batching, chunked prefill, prefix cache) plus a host-overhead channel that prior simulators/forecasters omit.
- Honest, well-designed limitation analysis quantifying exactly which kernel families transfer across generations and which don't.
- Uses production fused kernels (FlashAttention) rather than isolated synthetic operators, capturing real engine dispatch.
- End-to-end accuracy validated on a realistic production trace across a wide model/hardware matrix with distributional (not just mean) error reporting.

## Limitations
- The cross-generation transfer assumption fails most for attention: η is large (~3–3.5) and varies up to 3.8× across devices, so closing the gap requires at least one serving-matched measurement per new architecture rather than pure extrapolation.
- Per-kernel gains don't translate one-to-one to end-to-end (GEMM dominates runtime and is only a tie), so realized improvements are smaller than per-kernel means suggest.
- TTFT accuracy degrades sharply near saturation: error rises from ~15% at ρ≲0.9 to 74% at ρ≈1.2 (queue buildup amplifies per-step error).
- Only GB200 is used as the held-out target generation; broader cross-generation validation (e.g., future architectures) is untested.
- No mention of public code/data release; the evaluation trace is proprietary, limiting independent reproduction.

## Relevance
该工作与推理可观测性 / 内核级模拟高度相关：它把端到端 LLM 服务性能拆解到 GPU 内核粒度并输出延迟分解，正好是当前推理基础设施中缺乏细粒度瓶颈归因能力的关键缺口。其零样本跨代预测能力对在尚未采购的硬件上做容量规划、采购决策与软硬件协同设计尤为实用，可作为推理性能基准与容量规划的内核级仿真底座。

## Questions for Further Exploration
- Can attention efficiency η be made transferable via richer dimensionless features (e.g., achieved-bandwidth ceilings, cache-residency distributions) or does it fundamentally require per-architecture calibration?
- How does the predictor degrade on emerging architectures with qualitatively different memory hierarchies (e.g., GDDR7/LPDDR5X edge parts, 3D-stacked logic-in-memory)?
- What is the minimum source-GPU "ladder" size/diversity needed to keep cross-generation error below a target threshold?
- Could the host-overhead model be extended to accurately cover the saturated regime (ρ≳1) where TTFT error explodes to 74%?
- Does kernel-level breakdown composition change meaningfully under newer attention variants (Mamba/SSM, grouped-query, sliding-window) or speculative decoding?
- Can the simulator be coupled with an optimizer to do closed-loop auto-tuning of TP, batching, and prefix-cache policies, and how does that compare to Bayesian-optimization config search?
