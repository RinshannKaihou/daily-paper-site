01

The method, at a glance

A two-stage iterative thresholding scheme that decouples coefficient recovery from nuisance contamination.

The core novelty: different thresholding levels for β and θ in every iteration — a refinement that lets the analysis isolate exactly how the contamination nuisance θ* distorts recovery of β*.

The model treats the response as corrupted by a sparse adversarial vector:

Y = Xβ* + √n · θ* + ξ ,   β* is s-sparse , θ* is o-sparse , X sub-Gaussian

Both stages minimize the squared ℓ₂ loss 𝓛(β,θ) = ½n ‖Y − Xβ − √n·θ‖², alternating a gradient-descent update with hard thresholding. Stage 1 coarsely locates the signal with a decaying threshold; Stage 2 fixes the threshold at its statistical floor and debiases — no data splitting required. Proposition 1 supplies the restricted-isometry & incoherence geometry; explicit thresholds λβ,∞, λθ,∞ depend only on σ, s, o, and design constants.

Stage 1 · Algorithm 1

Dynamic thresholding — initialization

Per iteration: gradient step → monotonic decay of separate thresholds λβ,t, λθ,t → hard-threshold both vectors. Decay runs until thresholds hit their statistical floor, giving an explicit stopping time and a minimax near-optimal initial estimate.

λ_{β,t+1} = (κ·λ_{β,t}) ∨ λ_{β,∞}  ·  κ = 0.9, η = 0.75
Stage 2 · Algorithm 2

Fixed thresholding — debiased refinement

From the Stage-1 output, hold both thresholds fixed at λβ,∞, λθ,∞ and run O(log n) gradient + fixed-threshold steps. A debiasing pass that yields the sharper, signal-adaptive, asymptotically-normal estimator.

→ geometric convergence to oracle β† · exact support recovery

03

Strengths & limitations

Where the contribution lands hard — and where it still bends.

What lands hard

Strengths
  • Clean two-stage division of labor: Stage 1 efficient initialization, Stage 2 debiased refinement.
  • Separate thresholding scales for β and θ are intuitive and theoretically powerful — tighter ℓ₂ bounds than joint thresholding.
  • A complete statistical picture: minimax near-optimality, signal adaptivity, selection consistency, oracle property, asymptotic normality — rare in the contamination literature.
  • Broad reach via the GLM extension and an explicit heavy-tailed-regression connection.
  • Empirically compelling: closely approaches the Oracle benchmark, robust across noise distributions.

Where it bends

Limitations
  • The Massart-type adaptive tuning used in simulations lacks theoretical guarantees.
  • A persistent logarithmic gap sits between the upper and lower bounds.
  • Theory is restricted to sub-Gaussian designs; heavy-tailed covariates are left open (Remark 2).
  • Asymptotic normality needs a strong sample condition: n ≻ max(s²·log²p, s³).
  • No released code — reproducibility depends on careful reimplementation.
  • Experiments cover only one moderate-dimensional regime; near n≈p or high-contamination behavior unexplored.
04

Open questions

Threads worth pulling for anyone extending this line.
Q1 · THEORY

Can the adaptive Massart-type tuning be given guarantees — e.g. via an oracle inequality?

Q2 · REGIME

How does AC-IHT behave near p ≫ n or o ≈ √(ns·log p / log n), where theory predicts a phase transition?

Q3 · HEAVY TAILS

Can separate-thresholding extend to heavy-tailed designs via truncation tools, closing the gap in Remark 2?

Q4 · TIGHTNESS

Is the log n gap between the upper bound (Thm 2) and lower bound (Thm 4) fundamental, or removable?

Q5 · INFERENCE

Does asymptotic normality extend to debiased estimators for general linear functionals of β*?

Q6 · BENCHMARK

How does AC-IHT compare in finite samples to Gao (2020)'s non-computable minimax-optimal depth estimator?
