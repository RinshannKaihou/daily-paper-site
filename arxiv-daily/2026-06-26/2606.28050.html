In controlled in-context QA, LLMs do not judge better than they generate — the evaluator de-attends to context 3–5× and barely reads the answer it scores.

The evaluator spends almost none of its attention on the answer it scores — 0.3–0.5% across five datasets — while de-attending to the supporting context by 3–5×. Generation beats self-evaluation on 3 of 4 benchmarks, overturning "verification is easier."

Why this matters

Self-evaluation fails at the attention-routing level, not the training level — a direct warning for LLM-as-a-Judge, self-reflection, and RLHF reward pipelines.

Critical edge
⚠

Only two models tested; attention analysis rests on one open model and one decision token, and the mechanism cannot explain the MuSiQue inversion.

Methodology

shared context · same model · judge your own answer

Each instance is a triple (c, q, a*) — context passage, question, gold answer synthesized from c. Two sequential tasks share the same instances and the same model L:

Tgen — the model generates a free-text answer a = L(c, q). Teval — the same L judges whether that same a is correct given (c, q, a). An oracle L* (GPT-4o, validated against GPT-5.4 at 92% agreement, 99.5% intra-consistency) scores a vs a*, defining generation accuracy GA and ground-truth eval labels; eval accuracy EA is how often L's self-judgment matches the oracle. The asymmetry measure is ∆ = EA − GA.

Three studies extend the pipeline: (1) task asymmetry — ∆ across datasets, stratified by hop count; (2) mechanistic interpretability — last-token attention (layers 24–31) over 184 jointly-verified samples, decomposed into context / question / candidate spans, plus causal ablations; (3) transferability — rank-16 LoRA on q/k/v/o in Gen / Eval / Both configs, testing whether the two abilities share parametric structure. All prompts zero-shot; n=500 per dataset (±4.4 pp half-width at p=0.5).

Strengths

what holds up
  • Clean experimental control isolates task difficulty from differential memory retrieval — a genuine advance over confounded open-domain prior work.
  • Striking, robust mechanism: 3–5× context de-attention and 0.3–0.5% candidate attention, causally corroborated by C-MASK / C-SWAP across five datasets.
  • Honest reporting — the acquiescence artifact is exposed rather than dressed up as a transfer success; limitations and unexplained phenomena are openly flagged.
  • Pre-registered hypotheses and a statistical-sufficiency analysis (n=500, ±4.4 pp) lend the conclusions credibility.
  • Translates mechanism into an actionable deployment warning: acquiescence risk whenever generation SFT is in the recipe.

Limitations

where it frays
  • Narrow generalization: two models, one of which (GPT-4o-mini) cannot be internally probed; attention rests on a single open model and a single decision token.
  • Scope restricted to short-answer QA with unambiguous binary correctness — abstractive, opinion-based, and long-form evaluation are out of scope.
  • The MuSiQue inversion lacks a mechanistic explanation; attributed to a generation-accuracy floor, not an eval-side attention advantage.
  • All prompts fixed and zero-shot; prompt-sensitivity and few-shot effects are untested.
  • English-only; cross-lingual generalization not guaranteed. LoRA budgets modest; best calibration still can't recover base GA.

Questions for Further Exploration

open threads the paper hands off
1Does the 3–5× de-attention signature hold for stronger/larger models (70B+, GPT-4-class) and for long-form generation beyond the first token?
2Can a logit-lens / tuned-lens analysis pin the exact layer where the candidate fuses into the residual stream — where the decision is "baked in"?
3Would few-shot or evaluator-specific prompting (forcing re-reading of context and candidate) close the ∆ gap — and at what cost?
4Is the acquiescence bias from generation SFT a function of LoRA rank, set size, or dataset heterogeneity — mitigable with calibration-aware objectives?
5Can the generation-floor vs. attention-advantage dichotomy be disentangled with controlled hop-count and distractor-density variations?
6Do these findings extend to non-QA regimes (summarization, code, reasoning) where correctness is less binary and more subjective?
