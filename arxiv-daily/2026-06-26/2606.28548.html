Why This Matters

As context windows explode, per-token SAE features scale linearly; turn-averaging is a zero-code-change fix that makes long-context and multi-turn attribution tractable.

Critical Edge

The entire evaluation — feature descriptions, summaries, pairwise judgments — is LLM-generated, with autointerp prompts differing across architectures (a confound the authors flag), and only validated at 7B / 32K-width scale.

01

Methodology

A mean across the token dimension acts as a low-pass filter — no SAE code changes required.
Low-pass over tokens
x̄ = (1/|T|) Σ x_t preserves dimensionality → a standard SAE trains on it unchanged. The nested split forces the inner half to reconstruct the turn mean, the full set to reconstruct tokens.
min ‖ x̄ − dec(enc(x̄)) ‖²   // BatchTopK, k=128, layer 19  +  α · ‖ x_t − dec(enc(x_t)) ‖²

The core idea is minimal: for a turn T with residual-stream activations x_t, compute the turn average x̄ = (1/|T|) Σ x_t and train a standard SAE on x̄ with ordinary reconstruction loss. Because the mean preserves dimensionality, no SAE architecture or training-code change is required — the active feature count becomes fixed, no longer growing linearly with context length.

Averaging acts as a low-pass filter: broad concepts (topic, style, behavioral traits) reflected across many positions survive, while token-level noise (single-token detectors, syntactic patterns) is smoothed away. Two hybrid variants are then explored.

Training is on Qwen-2.5-7B-Instruct — single-layer SAEs at layer 19 (~70% depth) and multi-layer at 6/11/16/21 — using LMSYS-Chat-1M. Turn-averaged attribution graphs are built with the circuit-tracer framework, with edge weights normalized by source/target turn lengths to be length-agnostic.

Turn-averaged SAE

Standard SAE trained on x̄. Fixed-size turn representation; best coverage, worst discrimination.

Per-token (max-pooled) SAE

Baseline on x_t. Strong discrimination (95% 10-way), but feature count scales with length.

Decoupled SAE

Separate SAEs on x̄ and on (x_t − x̄). Reconstructs, but the two sets are causally disconnected → blocks attribution.

Nested SAE · best of both

Single encoder split into inner [0:16384] + full [0:32768], Matryoshka-style joint loss. Highest attribution sufficiency; recovers turn-level and per-token detail in one model.

All three architectures show strong edge-weight ↔ effect agreement; the nested model notably beats post-hoc aggregated per-token features on completeness / replacement sufficiency. A 14-turn jailbreak→degeneration case yields an interpretable chain (AI-safety disclaimers → nuclear/domination features → adversarial-prompt detection → degenerate output) that per-token features cannot reproduce (they instead attribute to "chemical compound names" / "JSON").

Best for coverage

Turn-averaged. High-level, length-agnostic concepts across long docs. Weaker at fine discrimination.

Best for discrimination

Per-token max-pooled. Sharp token-level matching, but scales with context and fails off-distribution on mean inputs.

Best overall · attribution

Nested SAE. Recovers both granularities in one model and posts the highest sufficiency scores.

03

Strengths & Limitations

A clean, low-friction idea — with an LLM-dependent evaluation pipeline.

＋ Strengths

  • Minimal-change method: averaging preserves input dimensionality, so existing SAE architectures and training code work unmodified.
  • Clean low-pass-filter intuition that explains exactly why high-level features emerge from the mean.
  • Nested SAE elegantly recovers both turn-level and per-token detail in one model and achieves the best attribution sufficiency.
  • Large-scale validation: 36,000 interventions, dual LLM-judge and deterministic embedding (Qwen3-Embedding-8B) metrics.
  • Cross-length generalization: chat turns → 30K-token documents, plus compelling attribution case studies.

− Limitations

  • Small scale: only a 7B model with SAE width 32,768; frontier-scale and much wider SAEs untested.
  • LLM-dependent evaluation: descriptions / summaries / judgments are generated; turn-averaged and per-token autointerp prompts differ (non-identical conditions) and no ground-truth benchmark exists.
  • Qualitative results are drawn from a small number of manually selected, illustrative cases.
  • Averaging assumes uniform information across positions — prior work suggests it concentrates (period / last token), which uniform averaging ignores.
  • Decoupled model is causally disconnected, limiting its analytical utility.
04

Questions for Further Exploration

Open threads the paper explicitly leaves on the table.
Q01 · SCALING

Do turn-averaged SAEs behave the same at frontier scale with much wider dictionaries — does the coverage advantage persist or saturate?

Q02 · WEIGHTING

Information concentrates at specific tokens (period, last token of entities). Would position-weighted averaging beat uniform averaging on feature quality?

Q03 · BENCHMARK

Can a ground-truth, non-LLM benchmark for "feature coverage" be constructed to remove the autointerp confound entirely?

Q04 · SAFETY STEERING

How do turn-averaged features interact with behavioral / safety steering — can they monitor or control persona and jailbreak traits end-to-end?

Q05 · PARTITION RATIO

Does the nested inner/outer ratio generalize, or is the 16K/16K split task-specific?

Q06 · TRANSFER

Could the same averaging / reshaping principle extend to other activation-based analyses (e.g. natural-language autoencoders) with measurable gains?
