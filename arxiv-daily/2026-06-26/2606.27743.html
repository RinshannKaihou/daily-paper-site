01Methodology — Learning to Allocate

L2A turns a frozen LLM fθ into a dynamic, input-dependent compute graph. A scalar budget b ∈ [0,1] (larger = more compute) is embedded and fed to three lightweight gating MLPs that each condition on both the hidden state and the budget — so the policy reacts to real system signals (deadline, spot time-to-interruption, KV-cache headroom, queue length), not just input difficulty.

LoRA adapters (rank 16) let the backbone adapt to sparse execution paths without catastrophic forgetting. Training jointly samples data and a budget b ~ D, minimizing a unified objective L = L_ce + γ·L_kd + λ·(C−B) + λT·R_tok — balancing task accuracy, KL distillation (logical consistency), budget matching, and reasoning-length cost. Gate temperature anneals toward near-binary decisions; at deployment the conservative aggregate b = min{b_time, b_mem, b_queue} protects against the tightest constraint.

03Assessment — strengths × limitations

Strengths RIGOR · HIGH

  • Genuine runtime adaptivity — one trained model spans the full budget range; no per-budget models.
  • Principled unified objective with interpretable roles per term, validated cleanly by ablations.
  • Strong OOD robustness — policy generalizes across math, code, and symbolic reasoning.
  • Practical budget calibration mapping real signals (deadline, memory, queue) to a normalized budget.

Limitations REPRO · MODERATE

  • Head-pruning speedups implementation-dependent and not consistently realized in wall-clock time.
  • Budget calibration assumes accurate online latency/memory estimators; errors bias the policy.
  • Structured <think>/<answer> format requires consistent prompting; may not suit all settings.
  • Single hardware/serving-stack validation; no code release; D, annealing schedule, estimators under-specified.
