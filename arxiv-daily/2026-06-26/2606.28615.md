> **一句话总结：** 本文将经典特征归因的"充分性"概念推广到任意自由文本解释，并提出基于模型自身信念的"自一致充分性"度量 SCSUFF，实验表明当前 LLM 解释普遍不充分，且与模型规模、准确率几乎无关。

> **TL;DR:** The paper generalizes classical feature-attribution sufficiency to arbitrary free-text LLM explanations, proves sufficiency is relative to the input distribution, and introduces SCSUFF — a model-self-conditioned metric showing LLM explanations are generally insufficient and only weakly tied to scale or accuracy.

## 核心贡献
- 将经典的"充分性"(sufficiency)概念从结构化特征归因推广到任意自由文本解释方法（如思维链、事后理由），给出统一的充分性定义（Definition 2.2）。
- 从理论上证明并刻画了解释充分性依赖于输入分布：当模型输出在给定解释下不恒定时，充分性会随输入分布变化（Theorem 3.2）。
- 提出用 LLM 自身生成"给定解释下的替代输入"来逼近输入分布，从而形式化"自一致充分性"(self-consistent sufficiency)，即模型输出、自解释与替代输入信念三者之间的一致性（Definition 4.1）。
- 引入信息论度量 SCSUFF：通过归一化的 KL 散度衡量自由文本解释保留了多少模型输出信息，无需依赖预定义的偏见或捷径。
- 实验覆盖 9 个 LLM、4 个数据集，发现 LLM 解释普遍不充分、与模型规模/准确率/输出熵相关性极弱，但 SCSUFF 高低可从最终 token 的隐藏状态中预测，可用于检测与优化解释。

## Key Contributions
- Generalizes classical sufficiency (from feature attribution) to arbitrary free-text explanation methods g(e|x), yielding a unified Definition 2.2 applicable to CoT, post-hoc rationales, counterfactuals, and prototypes.
- Proves explanation sufficiency is *relative to the input distribution* (Theorem 3.2): it can flip between sufficient and insufficient across distributions unless the model output is constant given the explanation.
- Proposes using the LLM itself to generate alternative inputs conditioned on an explanation, encoding the model's own beliefs about plausible inputs, and formalizes *self-consistent sufficiency* (Definition 4.1) aligning model output, self-explanation, and input-belief distributions.
- Introduces SCSUFF, a dataset- and model-agnostic information-theoretic metric (normalized expected KL divergence between F(y|x) and the mean of F(y|x′) over generated alternatives) that evaluates sufficiency without predefining biases/shortcuts.
- Empirically shows across 9 LLMs and 4 datasets that LLM explanations are generally insufficient, weakly correlated with model size/accuracy/output entropy, and that top/bottom SCSUFF scores are predictable from final-token hidden states.

## Methodology
The authors start from the classical notion of sufficient feature attributions (where the selected feature subset alone predicts the label as well as the full input) and lift it to generic explanation methods g(e|x) that map an input to a distribution over explanations. An explanation e is *sufficient* (Definition 2.2) when q(y|x) = q(y|e), where q(y|e) marginalizes the model output over the conditional distribution of alternative inputs sharing that explanation. Because this conditional depends on an input distribution q_x(x) — implicitly defined in classical supervised learning but absent for LLMs — they prove (Theorem 3.2) that sufficiency can change with the input distribution whenever the model output is not constant given the explanation.

To make the definition actionable for LLMs, they prompt the same LLM to generate alternative inputs x′ conditioned on its self-generated explanation e (preserving the information stated in the explanation), defining a model-induced conditional F(x′|e). *Self-consistent sufficiency* (Definition 4.1) then requires F(y|x) to equal the expectation of F(y|x′) under the explanation-and-alternative generation process. The metric SCSUFF is the normalized expected KL divergence between these two distributions, computed purely from LLM log-likelihoods and rescaled to (0,1) so that 1 denotes a self-consistently sufficient explanation. The framework requires only three components (a predictive model, an explanation, and an alternative-input distribution), so it extends beyond pure self-consistency (e.g., predictive model with/without CoT, or a different LLM for alternatives). Experiments span Qwen3 (0.6B–14B), Llama3.2/3.1, and Ministral 8B over MMLU, MMLU+authority, MMLU+reorder, IMDB, and BBQ.

## Results & Highlights
- SCSUFF generally agrees with targeted perturbation tests where applicable: Mann-Whitney U tests give p<0.05 in 5 of 6 model-dataset pairings (the lone exception being Qwen-MMLU+reorder), and the false-positive rate of labeling an unfaithful explanation as sufficient stays below 0.02 at a 0.97 threshold.
- Counterfactual self-explanation (CSE) does *not* consistently agree with SCSUFF — models ranking high on CSE (e.g., Llama on BBQ, MMLU+authority) score lowest on SCSUFF — indicating accurate counterfactuals don't imply sufficient general explanations.
- Sufficiency is demonstrably relative to the input distribution: on IMDB, adding "contradiction" few-shot examples to the alteration prompt drops SCSUFF near zero for Qwen and Llama despite identical explanations, confirming the theoretical prediction.
- LLM explanations are broadly insufficient (SCSUFF < 0.5 in most settings), and SCSUFF shows no correlation with task accuracy (ρ=0.07) and only weak correlation with model size (ρ=−0.23) and output entropy (ρ=0.24) — scaling or improving accuracy does not yield more sufficient explanations.
- Final-token last-layer hidden states separate high- and low-SCSUFF samples in 5 of 6 model-dataset pairs, with logistic-regression accuracies above 0.84 and ridge R² of 0.23–0.82, suggesting sufficiency information is localized in the final token's representation.

## Authors & Credibility
All three authors — Nhi Nguyen, Shauli Ravfogel, and Rajesh Ranganath — are at New York University, with Ranganath being a well-established researcher in probabilistic ML, interpretability, and causal inference. The work builds directly on the group's prior line on explanation sufficiency and encoding (Puli et al., NeurIPS 2024). ## Critical Assessment
- **Novelty:** High. Bridging classical feature-attribution sufficiency to arbitrary free-text LLM explanations — and explicitly making the input-distribution dependence a first-class object — is a clean conceptual generalization that prior LLM-explanation evaluations (targeted perturbations, SFA, CSE, CCF) do not provide. The "model's own beliefs" framing for the input distribution is distinctive.
- **Rigor:** Strong. The core claim (sufficiency is relative to input distribution) is supported by a formal theorem with a full proof, and the SCSUFF estimator is derived cleanly from KL divergence. Comparisons use proper statistical tests (Mann-Whitney U, Spearman ρ) with confidence intervals.
- **Reproducibility:** Good. Public code, deterministic generation (temperature 0), specified sampling budgets (5 alternatives for dataset-level, 70 for sample-level), and modest compute (2× A100). Some prompts are delegated to the appendix.
- **Significance:** The negative result — that LLM explanations are generally insufficient and do not improve with scale — is a meaningful, actionable finding for the interpretability/mechanistic-interpretability community, and the hidden-state predictability hints at a detection/optimization loop.
- **Red flags / caveats:** SCSUFF is a self-consistency measure, not a faithfulness-to-humans measure; the authors are explicit that human-decodable information is an open problem. The metric becomes uninformative if an explanation fully fixes the input (acknowledged but not fully resolved). SCSUFF is "lenient" because the alteration prompt sees the original input, and results depend on the quality of LLM-generated alternatives (only ~70% judged constraint-satisfying). The cross-model consistency of the hidden-state predictability is limited to 8B models on two datasets.

## Strengths
- Provides a principled, general, dataset- and model-agnostic framework where prior methods were either feature-specific or comprehensiveness-oriented.
- Cleanly isolates and proves the often-overlooked role of the input distribution in defining explanation sufficiency.
- The information-theoretic SCSUFF is computable directly from log-likelihoods, requiring no external judge or predefined biases.
- Honest, useful negative results about the state of LLM explanations and their disconnection from scale/accuracy.
- Connects the metric to internal representations, opening a path toward detecting and optimizing sufficient explanations.

## Limitations
- Self-consistency ≠ faithfulness to a human reader: the metric evaluates internal consistency among model-generated artifacts, not whether a human could decode the explanation.
- Metric is uninformative when an explanation fully determines the input (identical alternatives), a degenerate case that is acknowledged but not guaranteed away.
- Depends on the quality and variety of LLM-generated alternative inputs; the lenient setting (alteration prompt sees the original input) may overestimate sufficiency.
- Hidden-state predictability analysis is limited to 8B models and two datasets; generalization is unclear.
- Using the model itself as the hint-mention verifier (for targeted-metric comparison) introduces cost-driven but imperfect methodology.

## Relevance
这篇论文直击可解释性研究的核心问题——自由文本解释（如思维链）是否真正反映了模型生成输出的内部过程。它将经典"充分性"概念推广并与隐藏状态分析结合，对理解"解释 vs. 真实内部计算"（explanations vs. hidden states）的鸿沟非常关键，为用内部表征检测、引导甚至优化 LLM 解释提供了可操作的度量与理论依据。

## Questions for Further Exploration
- Can SCSUFF be optimized as a training objective (e.g., via reinforcement learning or a representation-level probe) to produce genuinely sufficient explanations, rather than just measuring them?
- How does the gap between model-decodable and human-decodable information scale with model size and instruction tuning — i.e., when does self-consistency start to imply human-faithfulness?
- Does localizing SCSUFF-relevant information beyond the final token (e.g., across layers or attention heads) reveal which circuits actually drive the explanation–behavior mismatch?
- How robust is SCSUFF when alternative inputs are generated by a stronger external model (e.g., GPT-5) rather than the LLM under evaluation, and does this change the insufficiency conclusions?
- Could the input-distribution relativity of sufficiency be exploited adversarially to make any explanation appear sufficient or insufficient, and what does that imply for deploying such metrics in safety auditing?
