When AI Reviews Its Own Code: Recursive Self-Training Collapse in Code LLMs ↗
Xinyuan Song · Zekun Cai · Liang Zhao  —  Emory University · University of Tokyo / LocationMind
Key finding

Recursive self-training of code LLMs collapses under every review regime—and AI self-review quietly degrades into a self-confirming rubber-stamp.

Why this matters

Self-evolving agents that grade their own code carry a built-in self-confirming failure.

The central warning for the AI-coding industry: a generator-coupled AI reviewer is not a substitute for exogenous verification in training-data and self-improvement pipelines.

Critical edge

Tested only at 1.1B–7B scale—frontier-model behavior is unproven. The shared-parameter self-gate (ϕ=θ) is an idealized limit case, and human gates are deliberately weak proxies (compile + repetition/length), not real test suites.

01 Methodology

gated distributional reweighting

Each round, a model generates synthetic code from a fixed prompt pool; a filtering gate keeps 5,000 accepted samples; the next model is fine-tuned for 3,000 steps. This repeats for 5 rounds (15k steps total). Crucially, all strategies share the same optimizer, cosine schedule, and batch size—only the filtering policy differs, isolating the gate as the variable.

The authors formalize code review as gated distributional reweighting: accepted samples are reweighted by an acceptance function r(x,c). The key conceptual axis is exogeneity—whether the gate's criteria are model-independent (Human) or coupled to the generator (AI self-gate). A linearized covariance recursion (Σ↦AΣAᵀ) gives a spectral-concentration result (Prop. 2.1); under a self-confirming acceptance assumption, Theorem 2.3 proves AI self-gating degenerates to ungated training.

02 Results

universal degradation · 4 models × 5 filters

Despite differing architectures, capacities, and starting baselines, all models converge to a common degradation floor (~0.04–0.14 HumanEval+ under Vanilla by R5)—a collapse attractor. Harder, out-of-distribution tasks (LiveCodeBench) collapse most, hitting 0.000 by R5 regardless of strategy.

03 Strengths

what lands well
  • Clean unified framework—gated reweighting makes the three regimes directly comparable.
  • Empirical breadth: 4 models × 5 filters × 4 benchmarks, same qualitative pattern everywhere.
  • The exogenous / endogenous distinction is a generalizable lens beyond code LLMs.
  • Honest reporting—flags that AI self-gates can look strong on HumanEval+ while failing MBPP+/LiveCodeBench.
  • The rubber-stamp signature (acceptance ↑ while correctness ↓) is a memorable, checkable diagnostic.

04 Limitations

where it bends
  • Model scale capped at 1.1B–7B; frontier-model behavior untested.
  • Human gates are deliberately weak (syntax + shallow quality), not execution-test suites—so "human gate can't stop collapse" partly reflects gate weakness.
  • The shared-parameter self-gate (ϕ=θ) is an idealization; real separate-judge pipelines are only loosely coupled.
  • Only 5 rounds; asymptotic long-horizon dynamics are inferred, not observed.
  • Self-signal miscalibration is described empirically; deeper causal "why" is left to the idealized theory.

05 Open questions

where the next paper starts
  1. Does the collapse—and the rubber-stamp regime—hold for frontier-scale code LLMs (70B+), or does capacity and pretraining diversity delay or avoid it?
  2. How do separate judge LLMs (not identical to the generator) behave under recursion—do they satisfy the self-confirming assumption, or offer genuine exogeneity only when diverse enough?
  3. Would real execution test suites as Human gates actually prevent collapse, or merely delay it?
  4. Can mixing a fixed fraction of fresh real data into each round anchor the distribution and kill the collapse attractor?
  5. Is there a principled online detector for the onset of the rubber-stamp regime—to trigger re-anchoring before damage compounds?
  6. How do these findings interact with RLCF pipelines where the reward is genuinely exogenous execution feedback?
