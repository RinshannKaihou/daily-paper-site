Robust Harmful Features Under Jailbreak Attacks: Mechanistic Evidence from Attention Head Specialization in Large Language Models

Yanchen Yin  ·  Dongqi Han (equal contrib.)  ·  Linghui Li  —  Beijing University of Posts and Telecommunications
一句话总结 越狱攻击并非整体抹除模型的安全表征，而是选择性抑制早期层的「对抗受损头」(ACHs)，而中层「安全对齐头」(SAHs) 仍保持鲁棒激活（即「鲁棒有害特征」）。这一机制支撑了一种无需训练、直接读取内部激活的有害内容检测方法。
Key Finding

Jailbreaks don't erase safety —
they silence just 8 early attention heads,
while mid-layer safety features keep firing underneath.

Methodology — three mechanistic steps

Step 1 — Back-tracing the refusal direction. Following Arditi et al. (2024), a single refusal direction r(L*) is extracted by difference-in-means at the end-of-instruction token in mid layers (Llama-3 layer 12, Llama-2 layer 14). Because attention heads act linearly through their OV circuits, the direction is back-propagated layer-by-layer through transposed W_O/W_V matrices to a per-head adjoint r_v that scores each head's contribution to the refusal signal.

Step 2 — Activation & overlap classification. Head activations are collected across benign / harmful-refused / attack-succeeded datasets, modeled with KDE, and separated by the Overlap Coefficient (OVL). A two-stage filter (τ=0.5) isolates discriminative heads, then splits attack-shifted heads by direction of change into ACHs (suppressed) and SAHs (strengthened).

Step 3 — Causal & token-level validation. ACHs are ablated via negative-feature injection (α=0.8) with 10-seed random-head controls; SAHs are ablated under attack inputs. Per-token contributions decompose harmful-request vs attack-template tokens. The training-free detector aggregates standardized activations in a single forward pass across Llama / Qwen / Gemma.

Results & evidence

Where the heads live — attention-head specialization (schematic, Llama-3)

Attack-template tokens suppress early-layer ACHs; mid-layer SAHs stay robustly active.

Under a successful jailbreak, attack-template tokens drive ACH activations down (median ≈ −1.5) while pushing SAH activations up (median ≈ +1.5) — a gap of more than 3 standard deviations. On ordinary harmful tokens, the two head types respond nearly identically (within ±0.15).

The split is strikingly stable across families: ACHs concentrate in layers 0–3 (>60% of cases) and SAHs in layers 5–12, in both Llama-3 and Llama-2.

Assessment — strengths & limitations

＋ Strengths

  • Clean causal chain — statistical head classification → ablation → token attribution, each stage corroborating the others.
  • Honest framing — explicitly avoids overclaiming ACHs as the sole attack pathway.
  • Strong practical payoff — a genuinely training-free, single-forward-pass detector competitive with trained safety models and far more robust on adversarial data.
  • Cross-family consistency — Llama-2/3 mechanistic agreement; Llama/Qwen/Gemma detection gains.
  • Transparent — sensitivity analyses on KDE bandwidth & thresholds, plus public code release.

! Limitations

  • Attention-only — MLP layers and richer refusal subspaces / cones are not modeled.
  • Scale-limited — mechanistic tests on 7–8B strongly-aligned models; only preliminary 70B results (Appendix E).
  • First-order approximation — single refusal direction may miss multi-dimensional safety structure.
  • Strict paired-sample filter — only ~378 (Llama-2) / ~176 (Llama-3) successful attack pairs survive from ~10k, limiting statistical power & attack diversity.
  • White-box only — precludes black-box deployment of the detector.
  • Modest, uneven gains over dedicated safety models (e.g., Llama-2-7B only 0.752).
04 /

Questions for further exploration

1
Do ACHs/SAHs generalize to 70B+ or weaker-aligned models, and does the early/mid-layer split hold under scaling?
2
What role do MLP layers play in sustaining Robust Harmful Features — the residual safety signal SAH ablation fails to remove?
3
Would a richer refusal subspace/cone (multiple directions) reveal head subtypes beyond the ACH/SAH binary?
4
Is ACH suppression the pathway actually exploited by optimization-based attacks (e.g., GCG), or merely one sufficient route?
5
Could protecting ACHs from suppression — via fine-tuning or runtime monitoring — yield more robust alignment?
6
How transferable are the ACH/SAH sets across attack paradigms (GCG vs AutoDAN vs many-shot)?
