Modulating Cross-Modal Convergence with Single-Stimulus, Intra-Modal Dispersion

arXiv: 2604.21836v1

Date: 2026-04-24

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.21836v1

一句话总结： 本文基于广义Procrustes分析提出单刺激层面的模内离散度指标，发现视觉模型间一致性高的刺激能将视觉-语言跨模态对齐提升近两倍，为"柏拉图表征假设"提供了更精细的逐刺激验证视角。
TL;DR: The authors introduce a stimulus-level measure of representational dispersion across vision models using Generalized Procrustes Analysis (GPA). They show that images on which diverse ViT models agree (low intra-modal dispersion) yield up to 2x higher cross-modal alignment with language models compared to high-dispersion stimuli. This effect is robust across two selection strategies (mean ranking and PCA on residuals) and generalizes across multiple vision-language model pairings, offering a fine-grained tool for probing the Platonic Representation Hypothesis.

  • 发现视觉模型间低离散度的刺激能够显著增强视觉-语言模型间的跨模态对齐（最高可达两倍提升），验证了模内收敛与跨模态收敛之间存在强耦合关系。
  • 该效应在两种刺激选择策略（均值排序和PCA残差空间排序）下均保持稳健，并在多种视觉-语言模型组合中泛化。
  • 补充实验表明：去掉高离散度刺激会提升跨模态对齐，去掉低离散度刺激则会降低对齐，进一步验证了结论的可靠性。

Key Contributions

  • Introduces a Generalized Procrustes Analysis (GPA)-based methodology for measuring per-stimulus representational dispersion across vision models, enabling fine-grained analysis beyond global averages.
  • Demonstrates that low intra-modal dispersion (high agreement among vision models) strongly predicts high cross-modal alignment with language models, with up to 2x improvement in CKNNA scores.
  • Shows robustness of the finding across two distinct stimulus selection strategies (mean-dispersion ranking and PCA on the residual matrix) and across multiple model pairings.
  • Provides supplementary evidence that excluding high-dispersion stimuli systematically increases vision-language alignment, while excluding low-dispersion stimuli decreases it.
  • Validates through leave-one-out analysis that stimulus-level dispersion captures a shared cross-model property, with held-out model deviations predictable from partial-dispersion scores (Pearson r = 0.21--0.43, all p < 0.001).

Methodology

The paper builds on Generalized Procrustes Analysis (GPA), a classical shape-analysis technique, to align representations from multiple vision models into a shared consensus space. The key steps are:

  1. Representation extraction: CLS-token representations from the penultimate transformer block of four ViT models trained with different objectives -- MAE, DINOv2, CLIP, and CLIP+finetuned on ImageNet-12K (all ViT-Huge except DINOv2-Giant).
  1. GPA alignment: Each model's representation matrix is centered, Frobenius-normalized, zero-padded to a common dimensionality (1536, matching DINOv2), and rotated via orthogonal transformations to minimize the sum of squared Frobenius distances to a joint consensus representation.
  1. Per-stimulus dispersion quantification: For each stimulus, the Procrustes residual is computed as the Euclidean distance between each model's projected representation and the joint representation. This yields a residual matrix R of shape (number of stimuli x number of models).
  1. Stimulus selection strategies: Two methods are used to summarize dispersion -- (a) mean dispersion across models, and (b) the first principal component (PC1) score of the residual matrix. Stimuli are ranked and partitioned into low-dispersion, high-dispersion, and random sets.
  1. Cross-modal alignment measurement: Centered Kernel k-Nearest Neighbors Alignment (CKNNA, k=10) is used to measure local similarity between vision model representations and language model representations (BLOOM, OpenLLaMA, LLaMA families) on the Wikipedia Image Text (WIT) dataset (1024 image-caption pairs).

Results & Highlights

  • Low-dispersion stimuli consistently produce the highest vision-language alignment across all tested model pairings. The effect is largest for DINOv2 and CLIP+FT models paired with language models.
  • The gap between low- and high-dispersion conditions is substantial -- up to a factor of two in CKNNA scores for some pairings.
  • The pattern holds under both stimulus selection strategies (mean ranking and PCA-based), confirming it is not an artifact of a particular ranking method.
  • Supplementary analysis (Fig. S1) shows that dropping the top 25% high-dispersion stimuli raises CKNNA above the full-set baseline across all 48 vision-language pairings, while dropping the bottom 25% lowers it.
  • Leave-one-out analysis (Fig. S2) shows that dispersion computed from three vision models predicts a held-out fourth model's per-stimulus deviation from consensus (Pearson r = 0.21--0.43, Spearman correlations similarly significant).

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The application of GPA residuals for per-stimulus dispersion is a natural but meaningful extension of existing Procrustes-based shape analysis (Williams et al., 2021). The core insight -- that intra-modal agreement predicts cross-modal alignment -- is intuitive and important, though perhaps not surprising given the Platonic Representation Hypothesis.
  • Rigor: The paper is well-controlled: two independent selection strategies, four vision models, twelve language models, and supplementary leave-one-out analyses. However, the WIT dataset is small (1024 stimuli), and the low/high dispersion sets are selected from the same data used to compute alignment, raising a potential circularity concern (mitigated but not fully eliminated by the supplementary drop-analysis).
  • Reproducibility: The methodology relies on publicly available models and a standard dataset (WIT). The GPA procedure is well-specified. No code repository is mentioned, which is a minor limitation.
  • Significance: The per-stimulus lens is genuinely useful for the field of representational alignment. It opens the door to understanding why some stimuli converge and others diverge, which has practical implications for dataset curation and brain-model comparison. However, the paper does not characterize the visual or semantic properties that distinguish low- from high-dispersion stimuli, leaving the most interesting question unanswered.
  • Red flags: The paper is relatively short (4 main sections, workshop format) and does not include error bars or statistical significance tests on the main CKNNA comparisons (only the supplementary leave-one-out analysis reports p-values). The WIT dataset size limits generalizability. The effect is demonstrated on ViT-family models only; whether it extends to CNNs or other architectures is unknown.

Strengths

  • Clean, principled methodology grounded in classical shape analysis (GPA), with clear mathematical formulation.
  • Strong experimental design: multiple selection strategies, multiple model families, supplementary analyses.
  • The per-stimulus perspective is a genuine methodological contribution that addresses a gap in the representational alignment literature.
  • Results are visually clear and the paper is well-written and concise.
  • The leave-one-out analysis strengthens the claim that dispersion captures a genuine shared stimulus property, not just an artifact of the specific model set.

Limitations

  • The WIT dataset is small (1024 stimuli) and from a single domain (Wikipedia). Generalizability to larger, more diverse datasets is untested.
  • No analysis of what visual or semantic features distinguish low- from high-dispersion stimuli -- the paper explicitly flags this as future work, but it limits the practical takeaway.
  • No error bars or formal statistical tests on the primary CKNNA comparisons in the main figures.
  • Only ViT-family vision models are tested; whether results extend to CNNs (ResNet, ConvNeXt) or hybrid architectures is unknown.
  • Workshop paper length limits depth of discussion and related work coverage.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • What visual or semantic features characterize low- vs. high-dispersion stimuli? Are low-dispersion images simply more "typical" or "canonical," or do they share specific compositional or semantic properties?
  • Does the relationship between intra-modal dispersion and cross-modal alignment hold for larger and more diverse datasets (e.g., LAION-5B subsets, COCO, or ImageNet)?
  • Can the GPA-based dispersion measure be applied to brain recordings (fMRI/MEG) to predict which stimuli will show high vs. low cross-subject or cross-species alignment?
  • How does the dispersion measure behave when applied to models with fundamentally different architectures (CNNs vs. ViTs vs. diffusion models)?
  • Could low-dispersion stimulus selection be used as a practical data curation strategy for improving vision-language model training efficiency?
