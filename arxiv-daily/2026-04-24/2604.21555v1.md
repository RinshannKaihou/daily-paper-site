> **一句话总结：** 本文提出概念分离曲线（CSCs），一种不依赖分类器的句嵌入评估方法，通过对比语义否定（插入否定词）与表面扰动（插入冠词）对嵌入向量的影响差异，跨语言、跨模型地量化评估嵌入模型捕获句子概念的能力，发现部分SOTA模型因位置编码敏感性反而表现不如简单基线。

> **TL;DR:** The paper introduces Concept Separation Curves (CSCs), a classifier-free, annotation-free evaluation method for sentence embeddings. By comparing cosine similarity distributions between original sentences and two types of single-token insertions -- negation particles ("not"/"niet") for semantic change and articles ("a"/"the", "de"/"het") for surface-level noise -- CSCs visualize and quantify (via overlap score) how well an embedding model distinguishes meaning changes from form changes. Tested across 6 models (TF-IDF, FastText, GroNLP, MPNET, RobBERTa, LaBSE), 3 corpora (CompetentNL, ESS, ParaCrawl), and 2 languages (Dutch, English), the method reveals that some SOTA models (sBERT-MPNET, sBERT-RobBERTa) exhibit position-sensitivity artifacts resembling hashing rather than concept encoding, while domain-matched models (GroNLP for Dutch, LaBSE for English) achieve best separation. Sentence length is the strongest limiting factor for the method's discriminative power.

## 核心贡献

- 提出概念分离曲线（CSCs），一种无需分类器、无需标注的句嵌入质量评估方法，通过对比语义扰动与非语义扰动的嵌入距离分布来评估模型概念编码能力。
- 设计了基于单token插入的自动化扰动框架：插入冠词作为表面层模糊（Fuzzing），插入否定词作为语义否定（Negation），两者修改相同数量的token以控制变量。
- 定义几何重叠度量（overlap score），通过归一化高斯核密度估计曲线的交面积量化语义与非语义效应的分离程度。
- 揭示了多个SOTA嵌入模型（如sBERT-MPNET、sBERT-RobBERTa）存在严重的token位置敏感性，其行为更像位置哈希而非概念编码。
- 跨语言（荷兰语/英语）、跨领域、跨句子长度进行系统性实验分析，为嵌入模型评估提供了可解释、可复现的基准框架。

## Key Contributions

- Introduce Concept Separation Curves (CSCs), a classifier-independent, annotation-free evaluation method for sentence embeddings that contrasts semantic and surface-level perturbation effects.
- Propose an automated perturbation framework based on controlled single-token insertion: articles for non-semantic fuzzing, negation particles for semantic change, with equal token modification count to control for confounds.
- Define a geometric overlap measure (normalized area under min of two KDE curves) to quantify separation between semantic and non-semantic effects in embedding space.
- Identify failure modes in SOTA models where sBERT-MPNET and sBERT-RobBERTa exhibit extreme position-sensitivity, behaving more like positional hashing algorithms than concept encoders.
- Conduct systematic analysis across 2 languages (Dutch, English), 3 diverse corpora (short-skill descriptions, survey questions, web-crawled text), and 6 embedding models, demonstrating cross-model interpretability.

## Methodology

The CSC framework evaluates sentence embeddings through a three-stage pipeline:

**1. Perturbation Generation.** Each sentence undergoes two types of single-token insertion:
- *Fuzzing* (surface-level): Insert articles ("a"/"the" in English, "de"/"het" in Dutch) at randomly selected positions. This preserves meaning while changing form.
- *Negation* (semantic): Insert negative particles ("not"/"niet") at randomly selected positions. This reverses propositional meaning with minimal structural change.

Both operations insert exactly one token, controlling for the confound of different modification magnitudes. Up to 3 perturbations per type are generated per sentence, with random position selection to avoid positional bias.

**2. Embedding and Similarity Computation.** Each original sentence and its perturbed variants are embedded using the target model. Cosine similarity is computed between the original embedding and each fuzzed/negated embedding, yielding two distributions of similarity scores.

**3. Visualization and Quantification.** Gaussian Kernel Density Estimation (KDE) is applied to both similarity distributions. Curves are surface-normalized (area = 1) to handle unequal output volumes. The overlap score is computed as the normalized intersection area of the two density curves (range 0--1, where 0 = perfect separation, 1 = complete overlap).

**Models tested:** TF-IDF (no stopwords removed), FastText, GroNLP (Dutch BERT), sBERT-MPNET, sBERT-RobBERTa, LaBSE (cross-lingual).

**Data:** CompetentNL (Dutch short skill descriptions, <10 tokens), ESS Questionnaire (English questions, variable length), ParaCrawl (Dutch/English web text, long sentences), plus a filtered ParaCrawl subset matched to CNL sentence lengths.

## Results & Highlights

- **GroNLP achieves best Dutch separation** (overlap 0.0221 on CNL), demonstrating clear concept separation where fuzzed sentences cluster near cosine similarity 1.0 and negated sentences shift leftward.
- **LaBSE achieves best English separation** (overlap 0.1174 on ESS), consistent with its cross-lingual training design.
- **FastText fails to encode concepts**: on CNL data, negated sentences are *more similar* to originals than fuzzed ones (overlap 0.6652), indicating the model does not capture propositional meaning.
- **sBERT-MPNET and sBERT-RobBERTa show position-sensitivity artifacts**: a secondary peak appears near cosine similarity -1.0 for both fuzzing and negation, suggesting these models react to token position shifts rather than semantic content. Overlap scores reach 0.9810 (ESS-MPNET), indicating near-total failure to distinguish concept changes from surface noise.
- **Sentence length is the strongest limiting factor**: filtered (short) ParaCrawl sentences show clearer separation than unfiltered (long) ones for the same model and language (e.g., LaBSE Dutch: 0.4632 filtered vs. 0.5168 unfiltered). The single-token perturbation constitutes a smaller relative change in longer sentences.
- **Language effect is present but minor**: Dutch embeddings show greater spread in overlap scores (mean 0.6668, sigma 0.2658) compared to English (mean 0.7992, sigma 0.1622), partially explained by more English-only models in the evaluation.
- **TF-IDF outperforms expectations** on short Dutch sentences, likely because the bag-of-words representation naturally handles article insertions well.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty (Moderate):** The idea of using perturbation-based evaluation is not new (Adi et al. 2017, Wang et al. 2022), but the specific framing as "Concept Separation Curves" with the overlap metric and dual-perturbation design (fuzzing vs. negation) is a useful contribution. The method is elegantly simple, which is both a strength and a limitation.

**Rigor (Moderate):** The experimental design controls for token count differences between perturbation types, which is commendable. However, the perturbation strategy is extremely narrow -- inserting a single article or negation particle -- which may not generalize to broader notions of semantic change. The cosine similarity metric collapses high-dimensional changes to a single scalar, potentially missing nuanced effects in individual dimensions. The authors acknowledge this limitation.

**Reproducibility (High):** The method is straightforward to implement, uses publicly available models and corpora, and the code is open-sourced. The clear algorithmic description and use of standard tools (scipy KDE) make replication easy.

**Significance (Moderate):** The finding that some SOTA models exhibit position-sensitivity artifacts is valuable and raises genuine concerns about what these embeddings actually encode. However, the practical impact is limited by the narrow perturbation scope and the fact that the method does not directly predict downstream task performance (as the authors honestly note).

**Red flags:**
- The perturbation approach is limited to languages where negation can be expressed via simple word insertion; many languages require morphological changes instead.
- The overlap metric can be gamed: a model where fuzzing pushes embeddings away and negation pulls them closer would score 0 overlap but would not represent good concept encoding.
- No comparison with established embedding evaluation benchmarks (e.g., SentEval, MTEB), making it hard to contextualize the overlap scores.
- The sample sizes and statistical significance testing are not discussed.

## Strengths

- Classifier-free and annotation-free evaluation approach eliminates confounds from downstream task design.
- Clean experimental design with controlled perturbation magnitudes (equal token count).
- Honest discussion of limitations, including the sentence length constraint and lack of direct downstream task correlation.
- Cross-lingual validation (Dutch and English) strengthens generality claims.
- Reveals important failure modes (position-sensitivity in MPNET/RobBERTa) that are not apparent from standard benchmarks.
- Open-source code and interactive demo enhance accessibility.

## Limitations

- Single-token insertion is an extremely narrow perturbation strategy; real semantic change involves more complex linguistic operations.
- The method's discriminative power degrades significantly with longer sentences, limiting applicability to paragraph or document-level embeddings.
- Negation via particle insertion is language-specific and does not extend to languages with morphological negation or different syntactic structures.
- The cosine similarity metric may not capture local, dimension-specific changes in high-dimensional embeddings.
- No correlation with downstream task performance is established, making practical utility unclear.
- Synonym/antonym-based perturbations were deemed infeasible due to sparse coverage, which would have been a more semantically meaningful test.

## Relevance

本文提出的CSCs方法为句嵌入模型的概念编码能力提供了一种新颖且无需标注的评估视角。该方法揭示了部分主流嵌入模型存在位置敏感性缺陷，对理解嵌入空间结构和设计更鲁棒的表示学习方法具有参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How do CSC overlap scores correlate with performance on standard embedding benchmarks (MTEB, SentEval, STS tasks)?
- Can the perturbation framework be extended to use paraphrasing, synonym substitution, or LLM-generated semantic variants for more ecologically valid semantic change?
- Does the position-sensitivity artifact observed in MPNET/RobBERTa persist with recent embedding models (e.g., E5, BGE, GTE)?
- How would the method perform on morphologically rich languages (e.g., Turkish, Finnish) where negation is not expressed via free particles?
- Can a dimension-wise analysis of embedding changes (rather than cosine similarity) reveal more fine-grained information about which embedding dimensions encode conceptual vs. positional information?
- Would reducing sentences to shorter formats (e.g., via summarization or core proposition extraction) before applying CSCs improve discriminative power on long texts?
