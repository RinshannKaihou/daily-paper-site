> **一句话总结：** 本文提出两阶段AI风险认证框架，借鉴航空业认证范式，将可接受失败率delta的规范性设定与基于RoMA/gRoMA统计验证工具的技术评估分离，为高风险AI系统（如自动驾驶紧急制动）提供无需模型内部访问、可审计且可复现的部署前安全证书，填补了当前AI法规（如EU AI Act、NIST AI RMF）中量化风险验证方法的空白。

> **TL;DR:** This paper proposes a two-stage pre-deployment certification framework for high-risk AI systems, inspired by aviation certification (DO-178/ARP 4754). Stage One requires a regulatory authority to formally set an acceptable failure probability delta and perturbation domain epsilon; Stage Two uses the RoMA and gRoMA black-box statistical verification tools to compute an auditable upper bound on the system's true failure rate. The framework requires no access to model internals and scales to arbitrary architectures. A case study on an Autonomous Emergency Braking (AEB) vision system demonstrates practical viability. The paper maps the certification procedure onto existing regulatory obligations (EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF, Council of Europe Convention), arguing it fills the critical gap where these frameworks demand risk assessment but provide no quantitative verification method. Key limitations include reliance on normality assumptions for confidence score distributions, conservatism of Hoeffding bounds, and inability to address out-of-distribution inputs or explainability/fairness concerns.

## 核心贡献

- 提出两阶段认证架构，将可接受失败概率delta的规范性决策与技术验证过程正式分离，消除了当前合规评估中价值判断与工程评估的混淆。
- 设计了阶段间的接口规范，使证书具有明确的通过/失败语义——RoMA测试仅在公开声明的delta下有意义，使用相同参数重测可确认或推翻原证书。
- 在高分辨率自动驾驶紧急制动（AEB）系统上展示结构化概念验证，证明该框架在工业相关、安全关键的架构上可实际部署，且无需访问模型内部。
- 将两阶段结构映射到EU AI Act、NIST AI RMF和欧洲委员会公约等现有合规义务上，为工程设计与监管要求之间建立明确的对应关系。

## Key Contributions

- A formal two-stage certification architecture that decouples the normative determination of acceptable failure probability (delta) from its technical verification, eliminating the conflation of value judgment and engineering assessment in current conformity assessment practice.
- An interface specification between stages that gives the certificate determinate pass/fail semantics -- the RoMA test is meaningful only relative to a publicly stated delta, and a re-test against the same delta and epsilon either confirms or defeats the original certificate.
- A structured proof-of-concept on a high-resolution Autonomous Emergency Braking (AEB) vision system, demonstrating that the framework produces a well-defined, auditable certification output for an architecture of practical relevance without access to model internals.
- A mapping of the two-stage structure onto existing compliance obligations under the EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF, and Council of Europe Framework Convention, grounding the engineering design in the regulatory requirements it is intended to satisfy.

## Methodology

The framework draws an analogy to aviation certification standards (DO-178, ARP 4754) and adapts them to neural network systems. The methodology consists of two stages:

**Stage One (Normative Determination):** A competent regulatory authority formally fixes two parameters: (1) an acceptable failure probability delta (analogous to aviation's 10^-9 catastrophic failure rate per flight hour), and (2) a perturbation domain epsilon defining the input neighborhood within which stable behavior is required. These parameters cannot be derived from engineering alone -- delta is a normative, societal decision, and epsilon reflects deployment context.

**Stage Two (Statistical Verification):** The RoMA (Robustness Measurement and Assessment) and gRoMA (global RoMA) tools compute a statistical upper bound on the system's true failure rate. The procedure works as follows:
- Sampling: Generate randomly perturbed inputs within the epsilon-neighborhood around test points.
- Data Extraction: Pass each sample through the network and extract runner-up confidence scores.
- Distribution Normalization: Verify normality via Anderson-Darling test; apply Box-Cox transformation if needed.
- Probabilistic Assessment: Use Z-score calculations and Gaussian CDF to compute the probability of misclassification under perturbation.
- For global certification, gRoMA aggregates per-category scores using Hoeffding's inequality to bound the error margin.

The system is certified if and only if the computed upper bound P <= delta. The case study on AEB further introduces a Risk Budgeting approach, decomposing delta across failure modes (transient interference, optical degradation, stochastic noise) with exposure-weighted summation.

**Fallback pathways:** When Anderson-Darling rejects normality, the framework offers domain narrowing (redefining epsilon to find sub-domains with Gaussian properties) or brute-force exact counting (computationally prohibitive but assumption-free).

## Results & Highlights

- The paper is primarily a framework/design paper rather than an empirical evaluation. It does not present large-scale benchmark comparisons.
- Prior empirical validation of RoMA (cited from Levy et al., 2025) showed sub-1% deviation from formal verification ground truth on ACAS Xu networks (300 neurons), with computation time reduced from hours to under 16 minutes.
- Even when normality assumptions were violated (character-level perturbations in LLMs), empirical accuracy remained high (0.17% deviation from ground truth: 94.44% estimated vs. 94.61% true).
- The AEB case study demonstrates the complete certification pipeline from normative parameterization through statistical verification to certification outcome, but the authors explicitly note it is a "structured proof-of-concept" rather than a full empirical validation on a deployed production system.
- The framework's legal analysis maps onto specific articles of the EU AI Act (Articles 9, 15, 33) and NIST AI RMF functions (Govern, Measure).

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** The paper's novelty lies not in new algorithms (RoMA and gRoMA are prior work by the same authors) but in the regulatory application framework. The two-stage architecture -- separating normative delta-setting from technical verification -- is a genuinely useful conceptual contribution. However, the delta-setting stage is essentially a call for regulatory action rather than a technical solution, which limits the paper's direct implementability. The paper is more of a position/policy paper with a technical backbone than a purely technical contribution.

**Rigor:** The paper is commendably honest about its limitations (Section VI). It acknowledges the normality assumption vulnerability, Hoeffding bound conservatism, OOD blindness, and the immaturity of RoMA/gRoMA as industry tools. The engagement with legal scholarship is serious rather than superficial. However, the AEB case study is a conceptual demonstration, not a full empirical evaluation -- no actual AEB model is tested, no real delta threshold is established by a regulator, and no certification outcome is reported against ground truth. The "case study" is essentially a walk-through of how the framework would apply, not evidence that it works in practice.

**Reproducibility:** The RoMA and gRoMA algorithms are described in prior publications. The regulatory mapping is straightforward to reproduce. The case study, being conceptual, is inherently reproducible as a thought exercise. However, no code or data is provided for the AEB demonstration.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a genuinely important gap. The observation that major AI regulations demand safety verification without specifying what "safe" means or how to measure it is correct and consequential. The aviation analogy is apt and well-developed. If adopted, this framework could meaningfully improve AI governance. The risk, however, is that the paper functions more as a sophisticated advocacy piece for RoMA/gRoMA adoption than as a rigorous evaluation of the framework's practical viability.

**Red flags:**
- The AEB "case study" is a structured walkthrough, not an empirical study. No actual model is certified. This is a significant gap for a paper that claims to demonstrate industrial deployability.
- The delta-setting stage is entirely deferred to regulators -- the paper provides no guidance, methodology, or case study for how this normative process should work, despite calling it "the most immediate priority."
- The framework's applicability to LLMs (arguably the most important AI systems to regulate) is explicitly limited by normality assumption failures, with no clear path to resolution beyond "future work."
- Hoeffding bounds are acknowledged as "notoriously conservative," which could make certification impractically stringent, but this is not quantified or empirically investigated.

## Strengths

- The paper identifies a real and consequential gap in AI governance with precision: regulations exist but verification instruments do not.
- The aviation certification analogy (DO-178, ARP 4754) is well-chosen and deeply developed, providing a concrete historical precedent for how quantitative safety thresholds can be established and enforced.
- The two-stage separation of normative and technical tasks is a clean architectural choice that prevents the common conflation of "what should we accept?" with "can we verify it?"
- Exceptional intellectual honesty about limitations. The threats-to-validity section is comprehensive and does not shy away from identifying fundamental constraints.
- Strong interdisciplinary engagement with both legal scholarship and engineering standards, giving the paper credibility across audiences.
- The framework's black-box nature is a genuine practical advantage -- it can be applied to proprietary commercial models without requiring white-box access.

## Limitations

- The AEB case study is conceptual rather than empirical. No actual certification is performed on a real system, which undermines the claim of "demonstrating industrial deployability."
- The normality assumption for runner-up confidence scores is a foundational dependency that fails for certain input modalities (notably LLMs under character-level perturbation), limiting the framework's generality.
- The delta-setting stage is entirely normative and the paper provides no mechanism, methodology, or case study for how regulators should determine appropriate failure thresholds. The paper calls this "the most immediate regulatory priority" but offers no concrete guidance.
- Hoeffding's inequality produces conservative bounds that may render certification impractically stringent for real systems, but the degree of conservatism is not quantified empirically.
- The framework does not address out-of-distribution behavior, data drift, fairness, explainability, or adversarial attacks -- all of which are critical for comprehensive AI safety.
- RoMA and gRoMA are academic tools that have not been stress-tested at industrial scale or over the decades that aviation certification standards have undergone.
- The paper does not engage with the practical question of who bears the cost of certification sampling (which could be enormous for delta = 10^-9) and whether this is economically feasible.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How would the framework handle systems with continuous output spaces (e.g., regression models in financial forecasting) rather than classification? Would the runner-up confidence score approach generalize?
- What is the practical sample size required to certify delta = 10^-9 at 99% confidence using Hoeffding's inequality, and is this computationally and economically feasible for high-resolution vision systems?
- How sensitive is the certification outcome to the choice of perturbation domain epsilon? Is there a principled method for selecting epsilon that avoids both over-narrow and over-broad certification scopes?
- Could conformal prediction or distribution-free concentration inequalities replace the normality assumption, extending the framework's applicability to LLMs and other modalities where normality fails?
- What institutional mechanisms would be needed for the delta-setting stage, and how might regulatory capture or industry lobbying influence the choice of delta in practice?
