Probabilistic Verification of Neural Networks via Efficient Probabilistic Hull Generation

arXiv: 2604.21556v1

Date: 2026-04-23

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.21556v1

一句话总结： 本文提出了一种基于回归树引导的概率包络生成框架，通过边界感知采样和迭代细分策略，高效地为高斯噪声输入下的神经网络安全概率提供有保证的上下界，在ACAS Xu和火箭着陆器基准测试上显著优于ProbStar和BaB方法。
TL;DR: This paper proposes a regression-tree-guided probabilistic verification framework for neural networks under Gaussian input noise. The method computes guaranteed upper and lower bounds for the safe probability by efficiently finding safe/unsafe probabilistic hulls via boundary-aware sampling, regression-tree subdivision, and iterative probabilistic prioritization. It outperforms ProbStar and branch-and-bound baselines on ACAS Xu (5D) and Rocket Lander (9D) benchmarks, achieving tighter bounds with up to 10x speedup and handling non-ReLU activation functions as a blackbox.

核心贡献

  • 提出了基于回归树引导的输入空间细分策略，能够沿安全边界高效地生成纯安全或纯不安全的概率包络（probabilistic hulls），避免了在已确定为安全或不安全区域的不必要细分。
  • 设计了边界感知采样方法，通过拒绝采样机制过滤远离安全边界的样本点，使回归树更精确地沿边界分割输入空间。
  • 开发了基于概率优先级的迭代精化机制，每次选择未知区域中概率质量最大的包络进行细分，确保计算资源集中在最关键的区域。
  • 方法不依赖于特定的激活函数类型，可将神经网络视为黑盒，相比仅支持ReLU的ProbStar具有更强的通用性。

Key Contributions

  • A regression-tree-guided input space subdivision strategy that efficiently produces purely safe or purely unsafe probabilistic hulls along the safety boundary, avoiding unnecessary subdivision in confirmed regions.
  • A boundary-aware sampling method that uses rejection sampling to filter samples far from the safety boundary, enabling regression trees to split more precisely along the boundary in input space.
  • An iterative refinement mechanism with probabilistic prioritization that always subdivides the unknown hull with the highest probability mass, concentrating computational effort on the most critical regions.
  • The framework treats the neural network as a blackbox and is compatible with any activation function, offering greater generality than ProbStar which is restricted to ReLU networks.
  • Demonstrated significant improvements over ProbStar and branch-and-bound (BaB) on ACAS Xu (5D, 45 networks) and Rocket Lander (9D) benchmarks, with tighter bounds (Us-Ls) and up to 10x speedup.

Methodology

The paper addresses the problem of estimating the probability P(Gamma(y) | x ~ N(mu, Sigma)) that a neural network f satisfies safety constraints Gamma(y) = Cy >= a when the input x follows a multivariate Gaussian distribution. Since exact computation is intractable, the method computes conservative upper and lower bounds by constructing safe and unsafe probabilistic hulls.

Probabilistic Hulls. A probabilistic hull is a box [a1,b1] x ... x [ad,bd] in the input space whose probability under the Gaussian distribution can be computed exactly via the error function. A hull is safe if all outputs in f(H) satisfy Gamma, and unsafe if all violate it.

Main Algorithm (Algorithm 2). The framework maintains three sets: R_safe, R_unsafe, and R_unknown. Initially, R_unknown contains a single large region covering the input space (with probability >= 99.999%). The main loop iteratively: (1) selects the unknown hull with the highest probability, (2) subdivides it using boundary-aware regression trees, (3) classifies the resulting sub-hulls as safe, unsafe, or unknown via CROWN verification, and (4) terminates when the total unknown probability falls below epsilon.

Boundary-Aware Subdivision (Algorithm 1). The key innovation is using regression trees to guide subdivision. First, samples are drawn from a combined distributional + uniform strategy. If both safe and unsafe samples exist, rejection sampling eliminates samples far from the boundary using a rank-based elimination probability. The remaining boundary-aware samples are used to build a regression tree, where each leaf becomes a probabilistic hull. The impurity measure for tree splits is MSE(S_left, S_right) / L^alpha, where L is the variation in the split dimension and alpha controls geometric penalty.

Safety Verification. CROWN (a linear bound propagation tool) is used to verify whether each hull's output set satisfies or violates the safety property. Hulls verified as safe go to R_safe, unsafe to R_unsafe, and undetermined to R_unknown.

Termination Guarantee. The algorithm is guaranteed to terminate with Us - Ls < epsilon, as the volume of unknown regions converges to zero with repeated subdivision (adapted from standard branch-and-bound termination proofs).

Results & Highlights

  • ACAS Xu (Property P2, 45 networks): BaB times out on all benchmarks. The proposed method uniformly outperforms ProbStar on the unknown probability bound (Us - Ls), while maintaining comparable runtime. GPU parallelization yields an average 4.8x speedup over the non-parallel version.
  • Rocket Lander (9D, 4 configurations): The method consistently produces significantly tighter bounds than ProbStar. For example, on Net 1 & Prop 2, the unknown probability drops from 0.357 (ProbStar) to 0.079 (proposed), with runtime reduced from 319s to 23s (non-parallel).
  • Tanh Networks (distilled from ACAS Xu): On non-ReLU networks, the method always terminates before the 1800s timeout, while BaB struggles. The bounds are also much tighter, demonstrating the method's generality across activation functions.
  • Ablation Studies: The impurity scheduler is the critical component for accuracy. Disabling it causes Us-Ls to degrade significantly (e.g., from 0.247 to 0.919 on Rocket Lander). Mixed sampling provides marginal but measurable gains. The optimal beta threshold is benchmark-dependent: beta=0 (immediate soft impurity) for high-dimensional spaces, beta=0.75 (late activation) for lower-dimensional ones.

Critical Assessment

Novelty (Moderate-High): The combination of regression trees with probabilistic hull generation for neural network verification is novel. The boundary-aware sampling via rejection is a pragmatic contribution. However, the individual components (regression trees, CROWN, branch-and-bound) are well-established, so the novelty lies primarily in their composition.

Rigor (Moderate-High): Theorem 4.1 (correctness of bounds) and Theorem 4.2 (termination guarantee) provide formal backing. The proofs are sound though adapted from standard branch-and-bound theory. The ablation studies and grid search add empirical rigor.

Reproducibility (Moderate): The paper provides hyperparameter details and uses publicly available benchmarks and tools (auto_LiRPA, CROWN). However, the full implementation is not linked (no GitHub/code repository mentioned), which hinders direct reproduction. The grid search is only done on one network per benchmark and then applied to all others, raising questions about hyperparameter sensitivity.

Significance (Moderate-High): Probabilistic verification under stochastic inputs is practically important for safety-critical systems (autonomous vehicles, aerospace). The ability to handle non-ReLU activations is a meaningful advantage. The empirical improvements over ProbStar are substantial.

Red Flags:

  • No code repository is provided.
  • The method is still fundamentally limited by CROWN's over-approximation error, which the authors acknowledge.
  • The curse of dimensionality remains an issue in the worst case, despite mitigation from boundary-aware subdivision.
  • Only feedforward networks are considered; no extension to recurrent or transformer architectures.
  • The termination condition used in practice (max probability of unknown hulls <= epsilon) is a relaxation of the theoretical condition (sum of unknown probabilities <= epsilon), which could affect the tightness of the final bounds.

Strengths

  • Strong empirical results with clear improvements over state-of-the-art baselines on standard benchmarks.
  • The framework is activation-function-agnostic, treating the network as a blackbox for sampling.
  • The boundary-aware subdivision is a principled way to focus computational effort on the critical safety boundary.
  • Well-structured paper with clear algorithmic descriptions, formal correctness/termination guarantees, and thorough ablation studies.
  • The iterative probabilistic prioritization is intuitive and effective for concentrating effort on high-probability unknown regions.

Limitations

  • Scalability remains constrained by CROWN's performance and the inherent curse of dimensionality; the highest dimensionality tested is 9D (Rocket Lander), which is still relatively modest.
  • Only feedforward networks with independent Gaussian inputs are supported; correlated inputs require diagonal covariance preprocessing.
  • No open-source code release mentioned at the time of publication.
  • Hyperparameters (sampling weights, tree depth, alpha, beta) require benchmark-specific tuning via grid search.
  • Regions with small boundary intersections may still require many subdivisions, as acknowledged by the authors.
  • The paper does not compare against sampling-based approaches like Monte Carlo estimation for probability bounds.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does the method scale to networks with hundreds of inputs (e.g., image classifiers) where regression tree subdivision may become ineffective?
  • Can the boundary-aware sampling strategy be combined with more advanced output range tools (e.g., alpha-beta-CROWN) to further improve verification tightness?
  • What is the performance gap compared to pure Monte Carlo estimation with the same computational budget, and can the two approaches be fruitfully combined?
  • Could symbolic representations like Taylor models replace box-based probabilistic hulls to reduce the number of required subdivisions?
  • How sensitive are the results to the choice of regression tree depth and the elimination probability formula?
  • Is there a principled way to set the hyperparameters (alpha, beta, sampling weights) without grid search for a new benchmark?
