To See the Unseen: on the Generalization Ability of Transformers in Symbolic Reasoning

arXiv: 2604.21632v1

Date: 2026-04-24

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.21632v1

一句话总结： 本文揭示了decoder-only transformer在符号推理中无法泛化到未见token的根本原因是(un)embedding坍缩（unseen token的表示趋同），并提出copy attention、冻结/重置embedding和增加数据多样性等组合方法来缓解这一问题，同时在Gemma 3系列模型中验证了未使用token确实存在embedding坍缩现象。
TL;DR: This paper identifies and explains a "representational collapse" phenomenon where the embeddings/unembeddings of unseen tokens converge to nearly identical vectors during training, preventing decoder-only transformers from distinguishing multiple novel symbols in symbolic reasoning tasks. The authors provide a theoretical proof (Lemma 4.1) showing that l2-regularized GD/SGD naturally contracts unseen-label weights, and propose a combination of interventions -- copy attention heads, freezing/resetting embeddings, and data diversity -- that achieves controlled generalization to unseen tokens. They also show that 99 deliberately unused tokens in Gemma 3 models exhibit embedding collapse (mean cosine similarity 0.78 in the 1B model), making them a poor initialization for finetuning compared to seen tokens.

核心贡献

  • 发现并理论证明了embedding坍缩现象： 在l2正则化的softmax分类器中，未见类别的权重会在梯度下降过程中自然收缩趋同（Lemma 4.1），即使特征表示在训练中不断变化，只要layernorm保证了特征的有限范数，该结论依然成立。
  • 提出了copy attention机制： 一种轻量级架构修改，通过独立的注意力头直接从输入embedding中复制token，绕过多层MLP的传播瓶颈，使模型能够精确复制seen和unseen token。
  • 系统性实验验证了多种干预策略的组合效果： 包括冻结embedding、主动遗忘（active forgetting）和临时主动遗忘，结合copy attention在不同数据多样性（|P|=240, 960, 9600）下的表现。
  • 在Gemma 3真实模型中发现实证证据： Gemma 3系列模型中99个预留未使用token的embedding存在明显坍缩（1B模型中平均余弦相似度0.78 vs. 使用token的0.09），且用这些token做微调比用已见token慢约10倍才达到同等精度。
  • 解释了已有启发式方法有效性的机制： 主动遗忘（active forgetting）等方法的有效性本质上是通过重置坍缩的embedding来起作用，为这些经验性方法提供了理论解释。

Key Contributions

  • Identifies embedding collapse as a root cause of generalization failure: The paper proves theoretically (Lemma 4.1) that l2-regularized gradient descent on softmax classifiers causes the last-layer weights of unseen labels to contract and collapse, providing a mechanistic explanation for why transformers cannot distinguish multiple novel tokens.
  • Proposes copy attention heads: A minimal architectural modification using separate attention heads with dedicated query/key parameters that attend directly to input embeddings (bypassing MLP layers), enabling reliable copying of both seen and unseen tokens.
  • Comprehensive intervention study: Systematically evaluates combinations of copy attention, embedding freezing, active forgetting, and temporary active forgetting under varying data diversity conditions (|P| = 240, 960, 9600), demonstrating that copy attention plus sufficient diversity or embedding freezing achieves generalization to unseen tokens.
  • Real-world evidence in Gemma 3 models: Demonstrates that 99 deliberately reserved unused tokens across the Gemma 3 family (1B, 4B, 12B, 27B) exhibit embedding collapse, with mean cosine similarities of 0.78/0.33/0.23/0.24 for unused vs. 0.09/0.03/0.02/0.01 for used tokens. Finetuning with unused tokens requires ~10x more steps to reach 90% accuracy compared to using seen tokens like adjectives or letters.
  • Provides mechanistic explanations for prior heuristic methods: Explains why active forgetting and temporary active forgetting work -- they counteract embedding collapse by periodically reinitializing (un)embeddings to their approximately orthogonal initial values.

Methodology

The paper combines theoretical analysis with controlled empirical studies:

Theoretical Analysis (Section 4): The authors prove Lemma 4.1 for a softmax classifier p(y|x,W,theta) = exp(phi^T W_y) / sum_k exp(phi^T W_k), trained with l2-regularized GD on the negative log-likelihood. The key result states: if labels i and j are absent from the data, then at each step t, ||W_i^{(t+1)} - W_j^{(t+1)}|| <= (1 - lambdaeta_t + eta_t^2 p_t) * ||W_i^{(t)} - W_j^{(t)}||, where p_t is the maximum probability assigned to classes i or j over all training examples. This contraction holds regardless of whether the feature parameters theta are fixed or changing, provided phi(.) has bounded output (guaranteed by layernorm). When the model fits training data well (p_t approaches zero), the weights collapse. Section 4.2 confirms this empirically under AdamW: Figure 2 shows that unseen tokens' unembeddings have much higher cosine similarity than seen tokens, especially with layernorm present, even without explicit regularization (lambda=0).

Copy Attention Mechanism (Section 3): The authors introduce copy attention heads that replace the standard value and output projections (YW_VW_O) with direct input embeddings (X*W_E). This creates a shortcut from input tokens to the output distribution. When embeddings are approximately orthonormal and the copy head attends sharply to a token, that token's output probability increases. Unlike Boix-Adsera et al. (2024), the copy attention uses separate query/key parameters, preserving MLPs in standard attention blocks.

Propositional Logic Experiments (Section 5): Synthetic Horn clause reasoning problems generated via forward-chaining (modus ponens), with up to 20 predicates and 40 rules per question. A 10-layer decoder-only transformer (60-75M parameters) is trained from scratch. Experiments vary: (1) data diversity (|P| = 240, 960, 9600 predicate names), (2) architecture (vanilla vs. copy attention), (3) embedding management (trainable, frozen, reset every 100 steps, temporary reset).

Gemma 3 Analysis (Section 6): The authors examine the 99 unused tokens in Gemma 3 models (1B through 27B) by computing pairwise cosine similarities and singular value decomposition of (un)embedding matrices. They also finetune Gemma 3 1B IT on logic problems using unused tokens, English adjectives, and ASCII letters as predicates, comparing convergence speed.

Results & Highlights

  • Vanilla transformers fail to generalize to unseen tokens even with high data diversity (|P| = 9600), achieving near-zero accuracy when all symbolic variables are replaced by unseen tokens. The model either substitutes unseen tokens with seen ones or ignores them entirely in chain-of-thought reasoning.
  • Copy attention alone is insufficient without diversity -- it fails when all variables are unseen (regardless of |P|), because embedding collapse makes different unseen tokens indistinguishable even through the copy mechanism.
  • Freezing embeddings to their initial (approximately orthogonal) values enables generalization across all diversity levels, with copy attention providing additional gains. However, freezing embeddings leads to significantly worse language modeling loss on C4 (shown in Appendix E), making it impractical for general-purpose models.
  • Active forgetting (reinitializing embeddings every 100 steps) combined with copy attention and sufficient diversity achieves strong generalization, supporting the hypothesis that the main bottleneck is embedding collapse.
  • Temporary active forgetting works only with copy attention: Without copy attention, performance collapses once reinitialization stops. With copy attention, the model retains its inductive bias even after reinitialization ends.
  • In Gemma 3, unused token embeddings are highly correlated: Mean cosine similarity 0.78 (1B) to 0.24 (27B) for unused tokens vs. 0.09 to 0.01 for used tokens. The collapse effect is stronger in smaller models with lower embedding dimensions.
  • Finetuning with collapsed unused tokens is ~10x slower: Reaching 90% accuracy on logic problems takes ~5000 steps with unused tokens vs. ~500 steps with adjectives or letters, suggesting that embedding collapse is a practical concern for downstream applications relying on reserved tokens.
  • Instruction tuning amplifies collapse: Singular value analysis shows that instruction-tuned Gemma 3 models concentrate more mass on top singular values compared to pretrained versions, with the 1B model showing the strongest effect.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The embedding collapse phenomenon for unseen tokens is a genuine and non-obvious finding. While the idea that models struggle with unseen tokens is not new, the formal proof that l2-regularized GD contracts unseen-label weights (even with changing features) is a clean theoretical contribution. The copy attention mechanism is a straightforward but effective architectural modification, closely related to prior pointer network and copying mechanism work. The Gemma 3 analysis provides valuable real-world evidence but is largely observational.

Rigor: The theoretical analysis (Lemma 4.1) is rigorous and clearly stated with appropriate assumptions. However, the result applies to the softmax output layer specifically, and extending it to the full transformer training dynamics requires additional assumptions about feature boundedness. The experimental methodology is thorough, with controlled ablations across multiple dimensions (diversity, architecture, embedding management). However, the main experiments use only 60-75M parameter models, leaving questions about scalability.

Reproducibility: The propositional logic task is synthetic and clearly described. The NanoDO library is referenced. However, the Gemma 3 analysis depends on proprietary model weights (though these are open-weight). The paper provides sufficient hyperparameter details for reproduction.

Significance: The practical implications are substantial -- if reserved/unused tokens in production LLMs have collapsed embeddings, this has direct consequences for anyone fine-tuning models using special tokens (e.g., new control tokens, domain-specific tokens). The theoretical result also provides a principled explanation for why "active forgetting" works empirically.

Red flags:

  • The proposed solutions (freezing embeddings, resetting embeddings) are acknowledged by the authors themselves as "somewhat unsatisfactory" for general-purpose models, since freezing embeddings hurts language modeling performance.
  • The gap between the theoretical setting (softmax classifier with fixed features) and the full transformer training dynamics is significant; the lemma captures a necessary but perhaps not sufficient condition for the observed failure.
  • The copy attention mechanism adds architectural complexity that may not scale trivially to very large models.
  • All main experiments are on propositional logic only; the paper does not demonstrate that these findings transfer to more complex reasoning domains (mathematical reasoning, code, etc.).
  • The multi-token symbols study (Appendix F) is acknowledged as preliminary and reveals additional failure modes (truncation, hallucination) that the proposed methods do not address.

Strengths

  • Clean theoretical result (Lemma 4.1) that precisely characterizes the collapse mechanism under minimal assumptions.
  • The connection between the theoretical analysis and practical observations in Gemma 3 models is compelling and strengthens the paper's impact.
  • Systematic ablation study covering multiple interventions and their combinations under controlled diversity conditions.
  • Honest discussion of limitations, including the trade-off between embedding freezing and language modeling performance.
  • The copy attention mechanism is simple, well-motivated, and empirically effective in the controlled setting.
  • Explains prior heuristic methods (active forgetting) mechanistically rather than treating them as black-box tricks.

Limitations

  • Scalability concerns: Main experiments are limited to 60-75M parameter models on synthetic propositional logic. It remains unclear whether the findings and proposed solutions transfer to billion-parameter models on real-world tasks.
  • Impractical solutions: Freezing embeddings hurts general language modeling; resetting embeddings is a workaround rather than a principled fix. The paper does not propose a solution that simultaneously preserves language modeling capability and enables unseen-token generalization.
  • Single-task evaluation: Only propositional logic (Horn clauses) is studied systematically. The extent to which embedding collapse affects other symbolic reasoning tasks (arithmetic, algebraic manipulation, code execution) is not addressed.
  • Tied embedding assumption: The theoretical analysis and some empirical results assume tied embedding/unembedding matrices. Many popular models (e.g., GPT series) use untied embeddings, where the collapse dynamics may differ.
  • Multi-token symbols are unresolved: The preliminary study (Appendix F) shows that even when all constituent tokens are seen, multi-token variable reasoning introduces new failure modes (truncation, prefix confusion, hallucination) that the proposed methods do not address.
  • No comparison with alternative tokenization strategies: The paper does not explore whether using different tokenizer designs (e.g., character-level, BPE with reserved symbol slots) could sidestep the collapse problem entirely.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does embedding collapse affect untied embedding/unembedding architectures (e.g., GPT-4, Claude) in the same way, or does separating the two matrices provide natural resistance to collapse?
  2. Can regularization techniques specifically designed to maintain embedding diversity (e.g., orthogonal regularization, contrastive losses on embeddings) prevent collapse without sacrificing language modeling quality?
  3. How does embedding collapse interact with other known failure modes of LLM reasoning, such as length generalization, numerical reasoning errors, and sensitivity to irrelevant context?
  4. Would pre-training with a small fraction of "symbolic reasoning" data involving diverse random tokens (analogous to the high-|P| regime) be a practical way to build in resistance to collapse at scale?
  5. Is the collapse of unused tokens in Gemma 3 a cause or a consequence of their being unused -- i.e., would proactively training on these tokens during pre-training prevent collapse and make them viable for downstream use?
