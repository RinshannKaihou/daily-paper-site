Robustness Analysis of POMDP Policies to Observation Perturbations

arXiv: 2604.21256v1

Date: 2026-04-24

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.21256

一句话总结

本文提出了 Robust Interval Search (RIS) 算法，通过双层次优化和二分搜索来量化有限状态控制器（FSC）表示的 POMDP 策略在观测扰动下的鲁棒性，区分了粘性（sticky）和非粘性（non-sticky）两种对抗扰动模型，并分别给出了指数级和多项式级复杂度的求解方法。

TL;DR

POMDP 策略在部署时往往面临观测模型不精确的问题，但现有方法很少量化策略对此的鲁棒性。本文定义了"策略观测鲁棒性问题"：给定一个由有限状态控制器（FSC）表示的 POMDP 策略，找出最大的观测偏差阈值 δ，使得在最坏情况下的观测扰动下，策略仍能保证达到预期的性能目标。核心方法 RIS 利用最坏情况值函数关于 δ 的单调性，将问题转化为双层次优化框架，外层用二分搜索缩放 δ，内层通过参数化/区间 Markov 链验证性能。作者区分了两种对抗模型：(1) sticky variant——一旦观测被扰动，后续所有观测持续被扰动，复杂度指数于 FSC 大小；(2) non-sticky variant——每步独立决定是否扰动，复杂度多项式于 FSC 大小。理论上证明了单调性引理、算法可靠性与收敛性命题，以及 sticky 变体与参数化 MC 的等价定理。实验在多个基准 POMDP 问题上验证了方法的有效性。该工作对安全关键场景中的策略部署具有重要意义。

  1. 形式化策略观测鲁棒性问题：首次严格定义了 POMDP 策略在观测扰动下的鲁棒性量化问题，区分了 sticky（持续扰动）和 non-sticky（每步独立扰动）两种对抗模型，给出了基于观测偏差邻域的扰动语义。
  1. 提出 Robust Interval Search (RIS) 算法：利用最坏情况值函数关于扰动幅度 δ 的单调性，设计双层次优化框架——外层 Modified Bisection Search (MBS) 搜索最大鲁棒 δ，内层通过参数化 Markov 链（sticky）或区间 Markov 链（non-sticky）验证给定 δ 下的最坏情况性能。
  1. 复杂度分析：证明 non-sticky 变体可归约为多项式规模的参数化/区间 MC 验证问题（复杂度关于 FSC 节点数多项式），而 sticky 变体需要指数级规模的参数化 MC（关于 FSC 大小指数），并给出了相应的归约定理（Theorem 1）。

Key Contributions

  1. Formalization of the Policy Observation Robustness Problem: Rigorously defines the problem of quantifying POMDP policy robustness to observation perturbations, distinguishing between sticky (persistent perturbation) and non-sticky (per-step independent perturbation) adversarial models, with perturbation semantics based on observation deviation neighborhoods.
  1. Robust Interval Search (RIS) Algorithm: Exploits the monotonicity of worst-case value with respect to perturbation magnitude delta to design a bi-level optimization framework — outer-level Modified Bisection Search (MBS) finds the maximum robust delta, while the inner level verifies worst-case performance via parametric Markov Chains (sticky) or interval Markov Chains (non-sticky).
  1. Complexity Analysis: Shows that the non-sticky variant reduces to polynomial-size pMC/IMC verification (polynomial in FSC node count), while the sticky variant requires exponential-size pMC construction (exponential in FSC size), with a formal reduction theorem (Theorem 1).

Methodology

Problem Formulation

Given a POMDP with a finite-state controller (FSC) policy, the authors define the Policy Observation Robustness Problem: find the maximum observation deviation delta such that the worst-case expected return (under adversarial observation perturbation within delta-neighborhoods) remains above a threshold.

Two adversarial models are considered:

  • Sticky (RIS-S): Once the adversary perturbs an observation at some time step, it must continue perturbing all subsequent observations. This models sensor degradation or persistent bias.
  • Non-sticky (RIS-NS): At each time step, the adversary independently decides whether to perturb the observation. This models transient noise or intermittent interference.

Core Algorithm: Robust Interval Search (RIS)

The RIS algorithm operates as a bi-level optimization:

Outer level — Modified Bisection Search (MBS):

  • Exploits the key property that worst-case value is monotonically non-increasing in delta.
  • Binary searches over delta in [0, delta_max] to find the largest delta where worst-case value meets the threshold.
  • MBS extends standard bisection to handle the case where inner verification may return approximate results.

Inner level — Worst-case value computation:

  • For the non-sticky variant: constructs an interval Markov Chain (IMC) from the product of POMDP and FSC, where transition probabilities depend on which observations could be perturbed. The IMC has size polynomial in the FSC. Parameter lifting is used as a subroutine for solving the induced optimization.
  • For the sticky variant: constructs a parametric Markov Chain (pMC) that tracks whether perturbation has been "activated" (sticky state). This requires duplicating FSC states into perturbed/unperturbed copies, yielding size exponential in the FSC. Theorem 1 establishes formal equivalence between the pMC solution and the original robustness problem.

Key Theoretical Results

  • Monotonicity (Lemmas 1 & 2): Increasing delta can only decrease the worst-case value, enabling bisection search.
  • Soundness (Proposition 1): If RIS returns a delta, it is a valid robustness guarantee.
  • Convergence (Proposition 2): RIS converges to the true maximum robust delta under appropriate precision settings.
  • Theorem 1: For the sticky variant, the constructed pMC exactly captures the worst-case value of the original POMDP-FSC product under sticky perturbation.

Results & Highlights

  • The authors evaluate RIS on several benchmark POMDP problems (including standard domains from the POMDP literature).
  • Non-sticky variant (RIS-NS) is practically efficient: the polynomial-size IMC construction makes it scalable to moderate-sized FSCs. Parameter lifting provides effective approximate solutions for the inner optimization.
  • Sticky variant (RIS-S) is more expensive due to the exponential pMC construction, but remains feasible for small-to-moderate FSC sizes.
  • The experiments demonstrate that the robustness measure provides meaningful information: policies that appear equally good under nominal conditions can have dramatically different robustness profiles.
  • The bisection search converges quickly in practice due to the monotonicity property, requiring relatively few inner verification calls.

Critical Assessment

The paper addresses a genuinely important and underexplored problem: quantifying how much observation noise a deployed POMDP policy can tolerate before performance degrades. The formalization is clean, and the distinction between sticky and non-sticky perturbation models is both practically motivated and theoretically interesting.

Strengths:

  • The monotonicity insight is elegant and directly enables the practical bisection search algorithm.
  • The formal reduction from the robustness problem to pMC/IMC verification is rigorous and connects to a mature set of tools.
  • The complexity gap between sticky and non-sticky variants is well-characterized and provides clear guidance on when each formulation is tractable.
  • The paper is well-written with clear problem definitions and proof sketches.

Limitations:

  • The exponential complexity of the sticky variant is a serious practical limitation. For FSCs of even moderate size (say, 20+ nodes), the pMC construction becomes intractable. The paper does not explore approximation or relaxation strategies for this case.
  • The approach is limited to FSC-represented policies. While FSCs are a natural policy representation for POMDPs, many modern POMDP solvers produce policies in other forms (e.g., policy trees, neural network policies). Extending the robustness analysis to these representations is non-trivial and not discussed.
  • The inner verification relies on parameter lifting, which itself can be expensive or approximate for large parametric models. The paper does not deeply analyze the trade-offs between approximation quality and computational cost in the inner loop.
  • The experimental evaluation, while present, could be more extensive. It would be valuable to see results on larger, more realistic POMDP domains and comparisons with alternative robustness assessment approaches (e.g., simulation-based stress testing).
  • The paper assumes a specific form of observation perturbation (L-infinity deviation within neighborhoods). Other perturbation models (e.g., distributional shifts, structured perturbations) are not considered.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can the sticky variant be made more tractable through approximate pMC construction, abstraction, or sampling-based methods?
  2. How does the robustness profile change when the FSC itself is optimized for robustness during policy computation (i.e., joint policy-robustness optimization)?
  3. Can the framework be extended to continuous observation spaces or neural network policies?
  4. What is the relationship between the robustness measure proposed here and other formal guarantees such as shielded reinforcement learning or constrained POMDPs?
  5. How sensitive is the computed robustness delta to the choice of observation distance metric and neighborhood definition?

Relevance

This work is relevant to anyone working on:

  • Safe deployment of POMDP policies in domains where sensor reliability is uncertain (robotics, autonomous driving, aerospace).
  • Formal verification of stochastic systems, particularly the intersection of POMDPs and parametric/interval Markov chains.
  • Robust decision making under uncertainty, where the observation model may not be perfectly known at deployment time.
  • Policy analysis and certification, as a complement to policy synthesis — verifying that a computed policy is robust before deployment.
