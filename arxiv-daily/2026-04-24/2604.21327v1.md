> **一句话总结：** 本文揭示了测试时强化学习(TTRL)中中等频率采样样本是伪标签噪声的主要来源，且GRPO的组归一化优势估计会放大这些虚假信号，提出了DDRL框架（频率感知采样+去偏优势估计+共识离策略精炼），在多个数学推理基准上相对ETMR平均提升1.7%-6.7%。

> **TL;DR:** This paper identifies that medium-frequency sampled responses create an "ambiguity region" producing most spurious reward signals in test-time RL (TTRL), and shows that GRPO's group-relative advantage normalization systematically amplifies these bad signals. The proposed DDRL framework uses frequency-based confidence-aware sampling to filter ambiguous samples, replaces GRPO advantages with fixed +1/-1 assignments, and adds a lightweight off-policy SFT refinement stage. Evaluated on Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B, Qwen2.5-3B, and LLaMA-3.1-8B across AIME 2024, AMC, and MATH-500, DDRL consistently outperforms TTRL and ETMR baselines with relative gains up to +19.0% on AIME 2024.

## 核心贡献

- 揭示了TTRL中虚假奖励信号的来源：中等采样频率的回答构成"模糊区域"，其正确性高度不稳定，是伪标签噪声的主要来源；通过贝叶斯分析给出了理论解释。
- 发现GRPO的组归一化优势估计会在低共识场景下系统性地放大虚假信号——当正样本稀少时（恰好是伪标签最不可靠的情形），GRPO会赋予最大的优势值。
- 提出DDRL统一框架，包含三个互补组件：（1）平衡置信度感知采样——排除中等频率的模糊样本；（2）去偏优势估计——用固定的+1/-1替代GRPO归一化优势；（3）基于共识的离策略精炼——通过拒绝采样+SFT巩固RL阶段的高共识行为。
- 在三个不同规模的LLM和多个数学推理基准上验证了DDRL的优越性，在Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B上相对ETMR平均提升6.7%。

## Key Contributions

- Identifies the origin of spurious reward signals in TTRL: medium-frequency sampled responses form an "ambiguity region" with highly unstable correctness, constituting the dominant source of pseudo-label noise, supported by Bayesian analysis.
- Reveals that GRPO's group-relative advantage normalization systematically amplifies spurious signals in low-consensus regimes -- precisely when pseudo-labels are least reliable, GRPO assigns the largest advantage magnitudes to positive samples.
- Proposes DDRL, a unified framework with three complementary components: (1) balanced confidence-aware sampling that excludes ambiguous medium-frequency responses, (2) debiased advantage estimation replacing GRPO normalization with fixed +1/-1 assignments, and (3) consensus-based off-policy refinement that consolidates high-consensus behaviors via rejection sampling and SFT.
- Demonstrates consistent improvements over TTRL and ETMR across three LLMs (Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B, Qwen2.5-3B, LLaMA-3.1-8B) on AIME 2024, AMC, and MATH-500, with up to +19.0% relative gain on AIME 2024.

## Methodology

DDRL operates in three stages during test-time adaptation:

**Stage 1: Balanced Confidence-Aware Sampling (BCS).** For each test query, N=64 rollouts are sampled. A pseudo-label is derived via majority voting, and response frequencies are computed. Rather than using all rollouts, BCS selects K samples per query: up to K/2 positive samples (matching the pseudo-label) and K minus positives as negative samples (chosen from the lowest-frequency responses). This deliberately excludes medium-frequency responses that form the ambiguous region. Low-frequency responses are treated as negatives because rare outliers are statistically more likely to be incorrect hallucinations, while high-frequency alternatives may be valid reasoning paths.

**Stage 2: Debiased Advantage Estimation (DAE).** Instead of GRPO's group-normalized advantages (which scale inversely with the number of positive samples), DAE assigns fixed advantages: +1 for positive samples, -1 for negative samples. This eliminates the amplification effect where GRPO assigns disproportionately large advantages to samples in low-consensus (unreliable) scenarios.

**Stage 3: Consensus-Based Off-Policy Refinement (COR).** After the RL phase, M=128 responses are sampled from the updated policy. For each query, rejection sampling retains only responses agreeing with the majority-vote consensus. The model is then fine-tuned on this curated dataset for 5 SFT epochs. This stage distills high-consensus behaviors at a fraction of the cost of additional RL training (3-5 minutes vs. up to 72 minutes for extended RL).

## Results & Highlights

- **Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B**: DDRL achieves +19.0% on AIME 2024, +4.1% on AMC, +4.9% on MATH-500 over ETMR, averaging +6.7% improvement. This corresponds to a 15.3% relative improvement over TTRL.
- **LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct**: DDRL achieves +9.0% on AMC, +13.6% on MATH-500 over ETMR, with best average performance among all methods, a 12.7% relative improvement over TTRL.
- **Qwen2.5-3B (base)**: More moderate but consistent improvements of +1.7% average gain over ETMR, suggesting DDRL's effectiveness depends on base model capacity.
- **Ablation**: DAE alone already yields consistent improvements across all models; BCS helps most when the base model is strong; COR provides stable additional gains across all settings.
- **Efficiency**: The off-policy refinement stage (5 SFT epochs, 3-5 minutes) outperforms extending on-policy RL training (up to 72 minutes), demonstrating that consolidation is more efficient than prolonged exploration.
- **Advantage dynamics analysis** reveals DDRL exhibits adaptive behavior: strongly negative mean advantage early in training (conservative learning) that gradually increases as pseudo-label quality improves, a pattern absent in standard TTRL.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty (Moderate-High):** The analysis of why GRPO fails in the unsupervised TTRL setting is the most novel contribution. The insight that group-relative normalization amplifies spurious signals specifically in low-consensus regimes is non-obvious and well-supported. The individual components (frequency-based filtering, fixed advantages, rejection sampling SFT) are individually simple, but the diagnosis and unified treatment are well-motivated.

**Rigor (Moderate-High):** The empirical analysis of answer frequency vs. correctness is thorough, with Bayesian justification in the appendix. The ablation study properly isolates each component's contribution. However, the fixed +1/-1 advantage is a deliberately conservative choice, and the paper acknowledges this without exploring more expressive alternatives. The theoretical analysis is limited to the Bayesian explanation of the frequency-correctness relationship rather than formal convergence guarantees.

**Reproducibility (Moderate):** Implementation details are provided (learning rates, number of rollouts, temperatures), and the code is promised. However, the paper relies on the verl framework, and several hyperparameters (K for sampling, number of training episodes per dataset) require tuning. The sensitivity analysis for refinement size is provided but limited to one hyperparameter.

**Significance (Moderate-High):** As TTRL is a rapidly growing research direction, identifying and mitigating a fundamental failure mode (spurious signal amplification) has clear value. The findings are likely to influence future TTRL method design. The limitation to mathematical reasoning with well-defined correctness criteria is acknowledged.

**Red flags:** The code is not yet available ("will soon be released"). DDRL slightly underperforms ETMR on AIME 2024 with LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct. The improvements on Qwen2.5-3B are modest, suggesting the method may not benefit weaker base models as much. The paper does not compare against approaches that use external verifiers or self-consistency without RL, which would provide useful context.

## Strengths

- Clear, well-structured diagnosis of the spurious signal problem in TTRL, moving beyond symptom treatment to root-cause analysis.
- The observation that GRPO's inductive bias (rare positive samples are valuable) is fundamentally mismatched with TTRL's semantics (rare positive samples indicate unreliable pseudo-labels) is a key insight.
- Simple and practical solutions: the fixed advantage scheme is trivial to implement on top of existing TTRL pipelines.
- Comprehensive evaluation across three different model types (base, instruction-tuned, math-specialized) and three benchmarks of varying difficulty.
- The off-policy refinement stage is shown to be significantly more efficient than extended RL training, with strong empirical support.

## Limitations

- Evaluated only on mathematical reasoning benchmarks with deterministic answers; unclear whether frequency-based confidence signals generalize to open-ended generation tasks where correctness is more subjective.
- The fixed +1/-1 advantage is a conservative choice that discards potentially useful information about relative sample quality; more expressive advantage formulations remain unexplored.
- The method assumes majority voting can produce reasonable pseudo-labels, which may fail on extremely hard problems where the model's initial accuracy is very low.
- Marginal improvements on weaker base models (Qwen2.5-3B) suggest the approach is less effective when the model lacks sufficient initial competence.
- The off-policy refinement stage adds additional sampling cost (M=128 rollouts per query), which increases inference-time compute.

## 相关性

本文对测试时强化学习的虚假信号进行了深入分析并提出有效的缓解方案，对理解LLM在无标签场景下的推理能力提升具有重要参考价值，特别是在数学推理任务的自我改进和测试时适应方面。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can the frequency-based confidence analysis be extended to open-ended generation tasks (e.g., code generation, scientific QA) where answer correctness is less binary?
- Would uncertainty-aware or adaptive advantage scaling (rather than fixed +1/-1) yield further improvements, especially for intermediate-difficulty problems?
- How does DDRL interact with other test-time scaling strategies such as chain-of-thought prompting or self-reflection?
- Is there a theoretical connection between the Bayesian analysis of answer frequency and the convergence properties of the resulting policy gradient updates?
- Can the off-policy refinement stage be replaced or augmented with preference optimization (e.g., DPO) for additional gains?
