## 一句话总结

本文通过理论与实验证明，不同的微调方式（冻结不同层、全量微调等）在持续学习中定义了本质上不同的问题——方法在不同微调机制下的性能排名差异显著（Kendall's τ 低至 0.44），打破了"一种方法通吃所有场景"的假设。

## TL;DR

This paper shows that the choice of fine-tuning regime (which layers to train) fundamentally changes the continual learning problem itself, not just the difficulty. Using a projected optimization framework, the authors prove theoretically that gradient magnitude, forgetting, and their coupling vary across trainable-depth regimes. Empirically, across five datasets and four CL methods on ResNet-18, they find low inter-regime rank correlations (mean Kendall's τ ranging 0.44–0.72), meaning the best method in one regime is often not the best in another. This challenges the community's implicit assumption that CL method comparisons are regime-independent and calls for regime-aware benchmarking.

## 核心贡献

- **揭示了微调机制对持续学习问题的本质影响**：首次系统性地证明不同微调深度（从仅训练最后一层到全量微调）不仅是难度的变化，而是定义了不同的持续学习问题
- **建立了投影优化理论框架**：将微调机制建模为对可训练参数子空间的正交投影约束，推导了梯度幅度、遗忘率和二者耦合关系在不同机制下的变化规律
- **提供了排名不稳定性的实证证据**：在5个数据集上，不同微调机制下持续学习方法的性能排名相关性低（Kendall's τ 最低仅0.44），表明方法选择高度依赖微调机制
- **提出了进度上界定理**：在L-平滑假设下，给出了投影更新下的优化进度理论界限，形式化解释了为何更宽松的微调机制更难控制遗忘

## Key Contributions

- **Demonstrates that fine-tuning regimes define fundamentally different CL problems**, not merely different difficulty levels — the ranking of CL methods changes substantially across regimes
- **Introduces a projected optimization framework** that models fine-tuning regimes as constrained optimization over trainable parameter subspaces, with the orthogonal projector P_S as the central mathematical object
- **Derives theoretical progress bounds** under projected updates using L-smoothness assumptions, showing how gradient magnitude, forgetting, and their coupling scale with trainable depth
- **Provides comprehensive empirical evidence** across five datasets (CIFAR-100, Fashion MNIST, QMNIST, MNIST, KMNIST), four CL methods (online EWC, LwF, SI, GEM), and five depth regimes on ResNet-18, revealing low Kendall's τ rank correlations (0.44–0.72) between regimes
- **Challenges regime-agnostic CL benchmarking** by showing that no single method dominates across all fine-tuning configurations

## Methodology

The paper frames fine-tuning regimes as constrained optimization problems. Given a parameter space θ, a regime S defines the trainable subset, and P_S is the orthogonal projector onto the coordinates in S. Updates are performed as θ ← θ − η·P_S∇L, meaning only trainable coordinates receive gradient updates.

**Theoretical framework:**
- Defines gradient magnitude G(S, t) = ∥P_S∇L_t∥ and forgetting F(S, t) as the loss increase on previous tasks after learning task t
- Under L-smoothness of the loss, derives a progress bound showing that the per-step improvement depends on the interaction between the projector P_S and the gradient structure
- Shows analytically that as S grows (more parameters trainable), both G and F increase, and their coupling strengthens

**Experimental setup:**
- Architecture: ResNet-18
- Five depth regimes: last block, last two blocks, last three blocks, last four blocks, full fine-tuning (all layers)
- Four CL methods spanning three families: online EWC (regularization), LwF (knowledge distillation), SI (regularization), GEM (gradient projection)
- Five benchmark datasets: CIFAR-100 (20 tasks), Fashion MNIST (5 tasks), QMNIST (5 tasks), MNIST (5 tasks), KMNIST (5 tasks)
- Task-incremental setting with known task boundaries
- Evaluation metric: Average accuracy and forgetting across all learned tasks
- Ranking stability measured via Kendall's τ rank correlation across regime pairs

## Results & Highlights

- **Rank instability is pervasive**: Mean Kendall's τ (excluding diagonal) ranges from 0.44 (QMNIST) to 0.72 (KMNIST), with Fashion MNIST and CIFAR-100 at 0.49. Even the highest value (0.72) indicates substantial ranking disagreement.
- **Gradient magnitude and forgetting scale with trainable depth**: As more layers become trainable, both metrics increase, but their coupling (correlation) also strengthens, making forgetting harder to control in permissive regimes.
- **No universal winner**: Different CL methods dominate in different regimes. A method that excels with full fine-tuning may underperform when only the last block is trainable.
- **GEM shows regime-dependent behavior**: The gradient projection method benefits from the implicit constraint of fewer trainable parameters in shallow regimes but struggles with the increased gradient coupling in deep regimes.
- **Regularization methods (EWC, SI) are more stable** across regimes in terms of rank position but not necessarily top-performing in any single regime.
- **The theoretical framework correctly predicts** the empirical trends: gradient magnitude increases with S, forgetting increases with S, and their coupling strengthens.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: 6/10** — The observation that fine-tuning configuration affects CL performance is not entirely new, but the systematic framing as "distinct problems" with both theoretical and empirical support adds meaningful structure to an underappreciated issue. The projected optimization formalism is clean but not deeply exploited theoretically beyond the basic progress bound.
- **Rigor: 6/10** — The theoretical framework is sound but relies on strong assumptions (L-smoothness, fixed learning rate). The empirical design covers reasonable breadth (5 datasets, 4 methods, 5 regimes) but is limited to one architecture and one axis of variation. Crucially, no per-regime hyperparameter tuning was performed, which means the rankings could partially reflect suboptimal hyperparameters for specific regimes rather than intrinsic method properties.
- **Reproducibility: 7/10** — The experimental setup is described in reasonable detail (architecture, datasets, number of tasks, regimes). The notation table in the appendix helps. However, code is not mentioned as available, and some hyperparameters are not fully specified.
- **Significance: 7/10** — The core message — that CL benchmarks should account for fine-tuning regimes — is important and well-argued. If the community takes this seriously, it could improve the rigor of CL method comparisons. The finding that Kendall's τ can be as low as 0.44 is striking and worth highlighting.
- **Red flags**: (1) No per-regime hyperparameter search could significantly bias the rankings — a method that is well-tuned for one regime may look artificially bad in another. (2) Only ResNet-18 is tested; modern architectures (Transformers, ViTs) may behave differently. (3) The task-incremental setting with known boundaries is the easiest CL scenario; the findings may not transfer to class-incremental or boundary-agnostic settings. (4) The theoretical contribution is relatively thin — the progress bound is straightforward from standard L-smoothness results applied to projected gradients.

## Strengths

- Clean and intuitive formalization of fine-tuning regimes as projected optimization
- Comprehensive empirical sweep across datasets, methods, and depth configurations
- The Kendall's τ analysis is a principled way to measure ranking stability
- Honest and thorough limitations section
- The paper raises an important meta-scientific point about benchmarking practices in CL

## Limitations

- **No per-regime hyperparameter tuning**: This is the biggest confound. The rankings may reflect default hyperparameters favoring certain regimes rather than intrinsic method-regime interactions.
- **Single axis of regime variation**: Only trainable depth is explored. Other axes (learning rate schedules, partial unfreezing strategies, adapter-based regimes) are not considered.
- **Single architecture**: ResNet-18 is a small, well-studied model. Findings may not generalize to larger or architecturally different models.
- **Task-incremental setting only**: The easiest CL scenario. Generalization to class-incremental or task-free CL is unknown.
- **Limited theoretical depth**: The progress bound is a direct application of existing optimization tools and does not yield surprising or highly informative predictions beyond "more trainable parameters → harder to control."
- **Small-scale experiments**: 5-task splits on relatively simple datasets (MNIST variants) limit the practical impact of the findings.

## Relevance

本文对持续学习社区具有重要参考价值——它提醒研究者在比较方法时必须考虑微调机制的差异，而非假设排名在不同配置下保持一致。对于实际应用中选择微调策略的研究者，本文提供了"方法选择应与微调机制匹配"的实证依据。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How do the rankings change when each regime gets its own tuned hyperparameters? Would the low Kendall's τ persist, or are the observed differences largely artifacts of shared hyperparameters?
2. Do these findings transfer to modern architectures like Vision Transformers or large language models, where fine-tuning regimes (LoRA, adapters, prefix tuning) are even more diverse?
3. How does the interaction between regime and method change in class-incremental learning, where the task identity is not available at test time?
4. Can the projected optimization framework be extended to derive regime-optimal CL methods — methods designed specifically for a given parameter subspace?
5. What happens when the axis of variation includes not just trainable depth but also learning rate scaling, gradient clipping, or mixed-precision training?
