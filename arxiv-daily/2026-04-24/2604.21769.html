Who Defines \"Best\"? Towards Interactive, User-Defined Evaluation of LLM Leaderboards

arXiv: 2604.21769v1

Date: 2026-04-23

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.21769v1

一句话总结： 本文通过深入分析LMArena数据集发现其主题分布严重偏向编程和AI领域，不同提示类别的模型排名差异巨大（如Claude在数据处理类从全局20名外跃升至前5），并设计了一个交互式可视化工具让用户自定义选择和加权提示类别来生成符合自身需求的排名，定性研究(N=10)表明该工具能有效提升排行榜评估的透明度和上下文适配性。
TL;DR: This FAccT 2026 paper reveals that LMArena's dataset is heavily skewed toward developer/AI topics (~30%), and model rankings change dramatically across prompt categories (e.g., Claude models jump from outside the global top-20 to top-5 in "Data Processing & Analysis"). The authors build an interactive visualization tool that lets users select and weight prompt slices to produce context-specific rankings, and a qualitative study (N=10) shows this approach improves transparency and supports more deliberate model selection decisions.

核心贡献

  • 重新定义LLM排行榜评估范式： 将排行榜评估从被动接受固定聚合分数转变为一个交互式的、用户驱动的数据切片探索与加权过程。
  • 深入分析LMArena数据集： 构建了三层主题层次结构（8个顶层、53个中层、400个细粒度类别），揭示数据集严重偏向编程/AI主题（约30%），且包含大量低质量提示（如1000+条纯问候语、重复的"strawberry"问题）。
  • 揭示排名不稳定性： 发现不同提示类别间排名差异显著——"数据处理与分析"类别与全局排名的Spearman相关系数仅0.60，数学类别的miniMax-m1模型从全局第19跃升至该类别第1。
  • 分析偏好判断的局限性： 在数学类中，正确答案不等于获胜；解释风格（简洁性50%、详细阐述48%、结构丰富性39%）显著影响用户偏好。在价值敏感主题（如历史与地缘政治）中，多元视角和立场鲜明的回答的偏好不稳定。
  • 设计交互式可视化工具作为设计探针： 支持用户选择、加权、检查提示切片，实时观察排名变化，并通过定性用户研究验证其有效性。

Key Contributions

  • Reframing leaderboard evaluation as interactive sensemaking: Proposes treating LLM leaderboard evaluation as an interactive process of exploring and reweighting data slices rather than accepting fixed aggregate scores determined by benchmark designers.
  • Empirical analysis of LMArena dataset composition: Constructs a three-level topic hierarchy (8 top-level, 53 mid-level, 400 fine-grained categories) revealing heavy skew toward programming/AI topics (~30%), over 1,000 low-quality greeting prompts, and highly repetitive prompts that distort rankings.
  • Demonstration of ranking instability across slices: Shows that model rankings vary substantially across categories -- Spearman correlation between "Data Processing & Analysis" category rankings and overall rankings is only 0.60; Claude models outside the global top-20 jump to top-5 in that category.
  • Investigation of preference judgment limitations: Demonstrates that in math, correctness alone does not determine preference -- explanation styles (conciseness 50%, elaboration 48%, structure richness 39%) significantly influence human judgments. For value-dependent topics, pluralistic vs. position-taking responses receive inconsistent preferences.
  • Interactive visualization interface as a design probe: A web-based tool with coordinated category selection and ranking views, supporting hierarchical slice selection, weight adjustment, and example inspection, validated through a qualitative user study (N=10).

Methodology

The paper follows a three-part methodology:

1. Dataset Analysis of LMArena (Human Preference 140K):

  • Analyzes 135,634 preference judgments across 53 LLMs collected over April-July 2025.
  • Constructs a semantic topic hierarchy via a two-stage pipeline: (a) generating topic descriptions with GPT-5 mini, embedding with text-embedding-3-small, and k-means clustering (k=400); (b) organizing clusters into higher-level categories using GPT-5.2 with manual refinement.
  • Computes per-category win rates with Bayesian smoothing (Beta-Binomial model) and measures ranking divergence via Spearman correlation.
  • Investigates explanation style effects on math preferences using GPT-5.2 to identify six style characteristics across response pairs.
  • Analyzes value-dependent judgments in History & Geopolitics using three LLM labelers (GPT-5-mini, Gemini 3 Flash, Claude Opus 4.5) with majority voting.

2. Interactive Visualization Interface:

  • A web application (React frontend, Flask backend) with two coordinated views: (a) category selection panel with hierarchical tree, weight adjustment, and example inspection; (b) model ranking panel that dynamically recomputes rankings based on user-selected slices and weights.
  • Design goals: shift evaluation control to stakeholders, support incremental sensemaking, and provide access to concrete prompt examples.

3. Qualitative User Study (N=10):

  • 45-minute one-on-one sessions with practitioners (AI engineers, data analysts, lecturers, etc.; average 5.5 years experience).
  • Participants used the interface to select the best model for a scenario from their own professional context.
  • Think-aloud protocol with thematic analysis of transcribed sessions.

Results & Highlights

  • Dataset skew: Programming and software development prompts account for ~30% of the LMArena dataset, reflecting overrepresentation of developer/AI practitioner preferences.
  • Ranking variability: Category-level Spearman correlations with overall rankings range from 0.60 (Data Processing & Analysis) to 0.93+ (Applied Engineering). Claude models, which rank outside the global top-20, achieve top-5 in Data Processing & Analysis. miniMax-m1 jumps from 19th globally to 1st in Math.
  • Style over substance in math: When both models give correct math answers, Jaccard similarity between winning response styles is only 0.3 (lower than random pairs at 0.5), indicating preferences are driven by distinct stylistic factors rather than similar reasoning quality. Conciseness (50%), elaboration (48%), and structure richness (39%) are the most frequent winning characteristics.
  • Unstable value-dependent judgments: In History & Geopolitics, whether pluralistic or position-taking responses are preferred is inconsistent across evaluators, making aggregate rankings unreliable for such topics.
  • User study findings: Five themes emerged -- (1) participants challenged/confirmed prior model perceptions; (2) fine-grained category selection improved contextual relevance; (3) example inspection grounded decisions; (4) participants calibrated how category-level results should count based on sample size and task importance; (5) participants reframed leaderboards as decision aids rather than definitive answers.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty (Moderate): The idea that aggregate rankings obscure category-level variation is not new (prior work on disaggregated evaluation exists). The main novelty lies in the interactive visualization approach and the detailed empirical analysis of LMArena's topic distribution and preference patterns. The explanation style analysis in math and the value-dependent judgment analysis add genuine insight.
  • Rigor (Moderate-High): The dataset analysis is thorough -- three-level hierarchy construction with robustness checks (Cohen's kappa=0.71), Bayesian smoothing for small samples, multi-LLM labeling with majority voting. However, the user study is small (N=10), qualitative, and conducted by the same group that built the tool, introducing potential confirmation bias. The choice of k=400 is described as an "empirical middle ground" without a principled selection criterion.
  • Reproducibility (Moderate): The dataset is public (LMArena Human Preference 140K on HuggingFace). However, the topic hierarchy construction relies on proprietary models (GPT-5 mini, GPT-5.2) with non-deterministic outputs, and 10-13% of the hierarchy involved manual refinement. Full reproducibility of the topic labels is unlikely. The interface code is not mentioned as publicly available.
  • Significance (Moderate-High): The paper makes a compelling argument that is practically relevant -- many organizations rely on leaderboard rankings for model selection without understanding the underlying data composition. The interactive slicing approach is a meaningful contribution to the responsible AI evaluation toolkit.
  • Red flags: The user study lacks quantitative metrics or a baseline comparison. The tool only uses win rates as a performance metric, which the authors themselves identify as a limitation. The reliance on future GPT models (GPT-5, GPT-5.2) for labeling raises questions about label quality that are not independently validated.

Strengths

  • Provides concrete, quantitative evidence of dataset skew and ranking instability in a widely-used leaderboard (LMArena).
  • The explanation style analysis in math (showing that stylistic factors, not correctness, drive preferences) is a particularly insightful finding.
  • The interactive visualization design is well-grounded in HCI and information visualization principles, with tight coordination between category selection and ranking views.
  • Honest about limitations -- the paper explicitly discusses risks of user-defined evaluation (narrow objectives, organizational biases) and does not overclaim.
  • Well-structured presentation with clear connections between the dataset analysis findings and the design of the visualization interface.

Limitations

  • Small qualitative user study (N=10) limits generalizability; no quantitative evaluation of the tool's effectiveness compared to baseline approaches.
  • Only uses win rates as the performance metric; cannot capture correctness, safety, or other important dimensions.
  • Topic hierarchy relies on proprietary LLMs (GPT-5 mini, GPT-5.2) and manual refinement, limiting reproducibility.
  • The analysis is limited to LMArena; generalizability to other leaderboards (HELM, Open LLM Leaderboard, etc.) is not demonstrated.
  • No longitudinal evaluation of whether interactive evaluation actually leads to better model deployment decisions in practice.
  • The tool does not support intersections across dimensions (e.g., language + category combinations) or fully user-defined slices.

Relevance

本文对LLM排行榜评估的批判性分析与交互式可视化方案，对于关注LLM评估方法论、模型选型和AI系统负责任部署的研究者具有重要参考价值。其揭示的LMArena数据集偏差和排名不稳定性问题，直接影响我们在使用排行榜指导模型选择时应持有的审慎态度。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How would the interactive slicing approach perform with a larger, more diverse user base (e.g., non-technical stakeholders, domain experts without AI background)?
  2. Can the topic hierarchy construction be made fully reproducible using open-source models, and how sensitive are the findings to the choice of clustering parameters?
  3. What metrics beyond win rates could be integrated into the interactive framework, and how would users navigate the complexity of choosing different metrics for different slices?
  4. How do the findings about stylistic preference bias in math generalize to other deterministic domains (e.g., code generation, factual QA)?
  5. Could the interactive evaluation paradigm be extended to support collaborative or multi-stakeholder decision-making processes within organizations?
