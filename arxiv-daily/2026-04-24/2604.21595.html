A Kernel Nonconformity Score for Multivariate Conformal Prediction

TL;DR: The authors propose the Multivariate Kernel Score (MKS), a kernel-based nonconformity score for multivariate conformal prediction that computes a regularized Mahalanobis distance in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space. The score equals the Gaussian process posterior variance and decomposes into an MMD density term plus a KPCA directional correction, producing prediction regions that are neither ellipsoids nor density level sets but kernel-adapted regions. MKS strictly generalizes the Mahalanobis approach (linear kernel recovers it exactly), achieves finite-sample coverage guarantees with convergence rates depending on the effective rank of the RKHS covariance operator rather than ambient dimension, and reduces prediction region volumes by 5-86% relative to ellipsoidal baselines across synthetic and real datasets, with the largest gains at higher output dimensions and tighter coverage levels.

  • Introduces the Multivariate Kernel Score (MKS), which generalizes the regularized Mahalanobis distance from input space to RKHS feature space, enabling nonlinear prediction regions without parametric shape assumptions

Methodology

The paper proposes the Multivariate Kernel Score (MKS) for multivariate conformal prediction within the split conformal framework. The core idea is to compute a nonconformity score as the Mahalanobis distance in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) rather than in the original output space.

Score definition. Given calibration residuals {epsilon_i} in R^d, a positive definite kernel k with feature map phi, and centered feature vectors tilde{phi}(epsilon) = phi(epsilon) - mu_hat_phi, the MKS is defined as the regularized quadratic form:

where C_hat_phi is the sample covariance operator in the RKHS. Using the kernel trick and Woodbury identity, this reduces to a computable expression involving only the centered Gram matrix tilde{K} and centered kernel vectors tilde{k}_*(epsilon).

Gaussian process connection. The score equals exactly the GP posterior variance at the test residual location, with regularization parameter gamma playing the role of observation noise variance. This provides geometric richness (density, directional covariance, manifold structure) while the conformal calibration ensures validity regardless of whether the GP model is correctly specified.

MMD-KPCA decomposition. The score decomposes as MKS = MMD^2(delta_epsilon, Q_T) - sum of alpha_j^2 / (lambda_j + gamma). The MMD term measures the discrepancy between a test residual and the calibration distribution (acting as a kernel density score). The KPCA correction reduces the score for residuals aligned with high-variance directions in feature space. The regularization gamma controls the balance: large gamma recovers density-only scoring, small gamma uses full anisotropic structure.

Special cases. With the linear kernel, MKS recovers the regularized Mahalanobis distance exactly. For calibration points, the score equals gamma times the kernel ridge regression leverage score.

Coverage guarantees. Under i.i.d. exchangeability, the conditional coverage deviation converges at a rate depending on the effective rank r(Sigma) = tr(Sigma)/||Sigma||_op of the RKHS covariance operator, which can be much smaller than both d and T for smooth kernels. The analysis is also extended to alpha-mixing dependent sequences under a known-covariance simplification.

Results & Highlights

  • Synthetic data (d=2): On a bivariate regression task with non-Gaussian heteroscedastic noise, MKS achieves the smallest prediction region volume at every coverage level (alpha = 0.01 to 0.10) under both linear and MLP models, with volumes 9-31% smaller than Mahalanobis and 21-38% smaller than density-only. The advantage grows at tighter coverage levels.
  • Real data (d=2,3,4): On three regression datasets (House, Bio, Blog), MKS produces smaller prediction regions than Mahalanobis across all configurations, with volume ratios (MKS/Mahalanobis) ranging from 0.14 to 0.95. The largest gains occur at higher dimensions (d=4) and tighter coverage levels (alpha=0.01), with volume reductions up to 86%.
  • Coverage maintained: Empirical coverage tracks the nominal 1-alpha level within sampling variability across all datasets, dimensions, and coverage levels, consistent with the distribution-free guarantee.
  • Density ablation: MKS outperforms the density-only score at every tested lengthscale value, confirming that the improvement comes from the KPCA correction term rather than kernel density estimation alone.
  • Worst-slab coverage: Under the misspecified linear model, MKS achieves the best worst-slab coverage at every alpha, indicating the volume reduction reflects genuinely tighter fit rather than sacrificed conditional coverage.

Critical Assessment

Novelty (High): The key insight of computing the Mahalanobis distance in RKHS feature space and connecting it to GP posterior variance is conceptually clean and well-motivated. The MMD-KPCA decomposition provides interpretable structure. The unification of Bayesian geometry with frequentist coverage guarantees is a meaningful conceptual contribution. However, the individual components (RKHS Mahalanobis, GP posterior variance, MMD, KPCA) are all well-established; the novelty lies in their synthesis and application to conformal prediction.

Rigor (High): The theoretical analysis is thorough, with complete proofs of finite-sample coverage guarantees under both i.i.d. and alpha-mixing settings. The convergence rate analysis correctly identifies the effective rank as the key quantity. The paper honestly acknowledges that the alpha-mixing analysis assumes known covariance (Assumption B.15) and that extending to estimated covariance under mixing is left for future work.

Reproducibility (Moderate): The algorithm is clearly specified (Algorithm 1) and the experiments use standard datasets and models (Ridge regression, MLP via scikit-learn). However, no code is provided. The kernel lengthscale and regularization are set via heuristics rather than principled selection. The volume estimation uses Monte Carlo sampling, which introduces estimation noise.

Significance (Moderate-High): The paper addresses a genuine gap in multivariate conformal prediction: existing methods are either restricted to ellipsoidal regions (Mahalanobis) or lose directional information (density-based). MKS combines both. The practical gains are substantial at higher dimensions, though the experiments are limited to d=2,3,4.

Red flags:

  • The hyperparameter selection (lengthscale ell and regularization gamma) uses simple heuristics rather than principled methods. The paper acknowledges this but defers it to future work, which is a notable gap.
  • The experiments only go up to d=4, leaving open how the method scales to truly high-dimensional outputs where the GP interpretation might suggest computational challenges (Gram matrix of size T x T).
  • The computational cost of computing the score for each test point involves a T-dimensional matrix inversion, which could be prohibitive for large calibration sets.
  • Under the alpha-mixing analysis, the assumption of known covariance (Assumption B.15) significantly limits practical applicability.

Strengths

  • Elegant theoretical framework that unifies GP posterior variance, MMD, and Mahalanobis distance through the RKHS lens
  • Clean decomposition into interpretable density and directional covariance components
  • Strict generalization of existing ellipsoidal methods (linear kernel recovery)
  • Dimension-free convergence rates via effective rank of the RKHS covariance operator
  • Consistent empirical improvements across multiple datasets, models, and coverage levels
  • Honest discussion of limitations and clear acknowledgment of assumptions

Limitations

  • Kernel and hyperparameter selection (lengthscale, regularization) is not addressed rigorously; only heuristics are used
  • Experiments limited to low-dimensional outputs (d=2,3,4); scalability to higher dimensions is unclear
  • Computational cost is O(T^3) for the initial Gram matrix inversion and O(T^2) per test point, which may be expensive for large calibration sets
  • The alpha-mixing analysis requires known population covariance, which is unrealistic in practice
  • No comparison with more recent methods like conformalized normalizing flows or optimal transport approaches, though these are mentioned in the literature review
  • The method inherits the standard limitations of kernel methods: sensitivity to kernel choice and difficulty scaling to very large datasets

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How should the kernel lengthscale and regularization parameter be selected in a principled manner, e.g., via marginal likelihood optimization implied by the GP connection or cross-validation on conformal p-values?
  • Can the computational cost be reduced through Nyström approximations, random Fourier features, or sparse GP methods while preserving coverage guarantees?
  • How does MKS perform on truly high-dimensional outputs (d >= 10) where ellipsoidal methods break down but kernel methods also face challenges?
  • Can the coverage analysis under alpha-mixing be extended to the case of estimated covariance operators, removing Assumption B.15?
  • What is the relationship between MKS and conditional conformal prediction methods (e.g., conformalized quantile regression) for achieving input-dependent prediction regions?
  • Could the KPCA correction term be adapted to incorporate input-dependent covariance structures, producing locally adaptive kernel prediction regions?
