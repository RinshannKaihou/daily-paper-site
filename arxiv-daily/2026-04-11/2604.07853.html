QaRL: Rollout-Aligned Quantization-Aware RL for Fast and Stable Training under Training–Inference Mismatch

Hao Gu, Hao Wang, Jiacheng Liu, Lujun Li, Qiyuan Zhu, Bei Liu, Binxing Xu, LEI WANG, Xintong Yang, Sida Lin, Sirui Han, Yike Guo

QaRL: Rollout-Aligned Quantization-Aware RL for Fast and Stable Training under Training–Inference Mismatch

一句话总结： QaRL 通过将训练侧前向与量化 rollout 对齐（低比特 GEMM + STE 反向）来消除训练-推理精度失配，并提出 TBPO（序列级双裁剪）抑制量化 rollout 中长文本产生的"错误 token"，在 Qwen3-30B-A3B MoE 上数学平均分达 51.2，接近 BF16 的 52.1，并保留 1.3× 训练加速。
TL;DR: QaRL tackles the training–inference precision mismatch that arises when RL rollouts run quantized but learner updates run BF16. It aligns the learner's forward pass with the quantized rollout via on-the-fly low-bit GEMM (W4A16/W8A8) with STE backward, and introduces TBPO (Trust-Band Policy Optimization) — a sequence-level objective with dual clipping on negative samples — to suppress repetitive/garbled "error tokens" that destabilize PPO. On Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base MoE over AIME2024/2025, AMC, MATH-500, Minerva, OlympiadBench, QaRL reaches an average math score of 51.2 vs quantized-rollout GSPO's 45.7 (+5.5) and BF16's 52.1, while delivering a 1.3× training speedup over BF16.

核心贡献

  • 提出 QaRL：在解耦的混合 RL 系统中，训练侧前向直接执行低比特 GEMM（W4A16/W8A8）并与量化 rollout 对齐，用 STE 反向更新 BF16 主权重，从根源上消除"量化 rollout + 全精度训练"导致的训练-推理失配。
  • 识别出量化 rollout 的关键失败模式：长响应中误差累积产生重复、乱码的"错误 token"，这些 token 在负样本中使 PPO 的策略比率 rprox 爆炸（低概率下 pold=0.02→pθ=0.05 使 rprox=2.5），突破信任域。
  • 提出 TBPO（Trust-Band Policy Optimization）：对负样本采用双裁剪 [1−δl, 1+δh]，并将策略比率与失配权重提升到序列级（token 概率的几何平均），整条响应超出信任带则丢弃，从而正确区分错误 token 与低概率探索 token。
  • 在 Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B/7B、Qwen3-8B-Base、Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base（MoE）上验证：QaRL TBPO 在数学基准上接近 BF16 性能，显著超过量化 rollout 训练，同时保留低比特吞吐收益。

Key Contributions

  • QaRL pipeline for decoupled hybrid RL: low-bit rollout inference (W4A16 or W8A8, with W4A16 preferred for large/MoE models due to memory bounds), rollout-aligned quantization-aware training that executes low-bit GEMM directly on quantized master weights with STE backward (between fake-quant QAT and end-to-end FQT, deliberately avoiding 4-bit gradients), and weight synchronization that publishes the materialized low-bit tensors to the rollout engine each step.
  • Identification of the "error token" failure mode: under-trained quantized policies produce repetitive/garbled tokens in long responses; these accumulate via autoregressive error amplification, concentrate in negative trajectories, and drive the PPO ratio to extreme values that token-level clipping cannot control.
  • TBPO (Trust-Band Policy Optimization): dual clipping for negative-advantage samples (both lower bound 1−δl and upper bound 1+δh) plus sequence-level ratios and clipping (geometric mean of token probabilities), dropping entire responses whose sequence-level ratio exceeds the trust band. This correctly clips garbled-token responses while preserving low-probability exploration tokens.
  • Empirical validation across four model scales (1.5B–30B-A3B MoE) showing QaRL TBPO recovers most of the degradation caused by quantized-rollout training and approaches BF16 performance, with throughput speedups.

Methodology

Setting. Modern LLM RL pipelines are hybrid: rollouts run in an inference engine (vLLM/SGLang) while policy optimization runs in a training backend (FSDP/Megatron). Even with the same θold, kernel differences induce a learner–sampler mismatch π_learner ≠ π_sampler. Quantizing only the rollout engine ("Quantized Rollout Training") makes π_sampler a quantized approximation π_quant-sampler while π_learner stays BF16, so the importance weight w_mismatch = π_learner/π_quant-sampler drifts far from 1 and grows with response length.

QaRL alignment (Section 3.1). Three components:

  1. Low-bit rollout inference in the rollout engine (W4A16 or W8A8; W4A16 gives higher throughput than W8A8 on large/MoE models due to memory constraints).
  2. Rollout-aligned quantization-aware training: master weights θ_BF16 are kept; on-the-fly Ŵ_low-bit = Quant(θ_BF16) is computed and low-bit GEMM is executed directly (not fake-quant simulation) to mirror the rollout's arithmetic. Backward uses STE on master weights. This sits between fake-quant QAT (high-precision arithmetic) and FQT (low-bit backward); FQT is avoided because 4-bit gradients introduce too much error.
  3. Weight sync: after each step, the already-materialized Ŵ_low-bit is published to the inference engine, avoiding redundant re-quantization and ensuring sampler weights match the learner's forward format.

The authors explicitly do NOT enforce bitwise-identical kernels (which costs ~2× slowdown per He and Lab 2025; Zhang et al. 2025); instead they tolerate π_quant-sampler ≠ π_quant-learner and compensate via w_mismatch. They also empirically reject θ_FP16 master weights (Qi et al. 2025) because dynamic loss scaling causes gradient NaN underflow, which does not occur with θ_BF16.

TBPO (Section 3.2). For A < 0, standard PPO only enforces a lower bound, so high-rprox tail tokens dominate the gradient. TBPO applies dual clipping: positive samples use clip(r, 0, 1+εh); negative samples use clip(r, 1−δl, 1+δh). The mismatch weight is two-sided capped w̃ = clip(w, [−log c, log c]). Crucially, ratios and clipping are sequence-level: r_seq-prox and w_seq-mismatch use the geometric mean of token-level log-probabilities (exponential of average log-difference) for numerical stability across long sequences. Entire responses exceeding the sequence-level trust band are masked out. The surrogate is J(θ) = E[w̃(θ) · r̃(θ) · Â] shared by all tokens in o.

Training setup. Framework: Verl + vLLM; 8× NVIDIA H800 GPUs. Dataset: OpenR1-Math-46K (46,000 math problems). Models: Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B/7B, Qwen3-8B-Base, Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base. Results & Highlights

  • On Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base MoE (GSPO), QaRL TBPO reaches an average in-distribution math score of 51.2 vs quantized-rollout GSPO's 45.7 (+5.5) and BF16 GSPO's 52.1 — recovering most of the 6.4-point gap caused by quantized rollout, while delivering a 1.3× training speedup over BF16.
  • On Qwen3-8B-Base, average in-distribution math performance drops from 51.8% (BF16) to 43.9% with quantized-rollout GRPO, while QaRL TBPO maintains 48.9% (only 2.9% below BF16); on MATH-500 specifically, BF16 reaches 88.1, quantized rollout 82.2, QaRL TBPO 83.6.
  • On Qwen2.5-7B-Math, BF16 GRPO averages 43.3% in-distribution; quantized-rollout GRPO drops to 40.9%; QaRL TBPO reaches 43.5%, slightly exceeding BF16, with AIME24/25 improving from 19.1/9.6 (quantized rollout) to 19.5/13.3.
  • Speed (Fig. 9b): for the 30B-A3B MoE, quantized-rollout training delivers 1.4× speedup over BF16, while QaRL delivers 1.3× (modest overhead from in-training quantization). Across 7B/8B/30B both quantized approaches beat BF16.
  • Ablation (Fig. 8): GSPO is unstable (error tokens in negatives cause KL drift and collapse); MIS GSPO loses sample efficiency; positive-only GRPO loses exploration; on-policy GRPO reduces error amplification but hurts efficiency; dual-clip GRPO bounds ratios but tokens after error tokens remain contaminated. Only TBPO achieves stable learning, high reward, and tight KL control.
  • Quantization-scheme ablation (Fig. 9a): FP8 W8A8, W4A16, and W4A8 all exhibit similar reward curves and nearly identical final rewards once stabilized by TBPO — RL convergence is largely insensitive to the specific low-bit format. W4A16 is adopted for most experiments due to superior speed on larger models.
  • TBPO vs GRPO under quantized rollout (Table 3): TBPO delivers an average margin of +2.9 points over GRPO under w4a16 quantized rollout training.

Critical Assessment

The paper's framing of error tokens as the key instability driver is well-motivated and the sequence-level dual-clipping fix is principled, but the empirical wins are uneven: on Qwen3-30B-A3B MoE the headline +5.5 gain over quantized-rollout GSPO still leaves QaRL 0.9 points below BF16 (51.2 vs 52.1), and on Qwen3-8B QaRL TBPO's in-distribution average (48.9) remains 2.9 points below BF16 (51.8) — so the method narrows but does not close the quantization gap, which the authors attribute to residual router shift in MoE forward passes without fully resolving it. A second weakness is the throughput story: QaRL's 1.3× speedup on the 30B-A3B MoE is actually slightly worse than plain quantized-rollout training's 1.4×, because the in-training quantization adds overhead; the paper does not quantify this overhead's breakdown or whether it could be reduced. Third, the sequence-level objective discards entire responses when r_seq-prox exceeds the band, which the authors note hurts data efficiency (Appendix D.4 shows MIS-style rejection sampling "limits reward ceiling") — TBPO trades sample usage for stability, and the paper flags replacing sequence-level optimization with token-level approximations as future work, implicitly conceding the current approach is costly. Fourth, the method is evaluated only on math/code/logic reasoning with verifiable rewards (RLVR); it is unclear whether TBPO's dual-clipping design transfers to RLHF with learned reward models where advantage distributions differ. Finally, the paper does not release code and the hyperparameters (e.g., δl=0.0003, δh=0.0007, εh=0.0004) are extremely small, raising questions about how tightly tuned they are to the Qwen family and whether they generalize.

Strengths

  • Clean problem decomposition: separates the system-level mismatch (fixed by QaRL alignment) from the algorithmic instability (fixed by TBPO), making each contribution independently motivated.
  • The error-token diagnosis is concrete and illustrated (Table 6, Fig. 6): garbled tokens like "RL RL RL RL" and "ĠÐ±ÐµÐ" are shown to escape token-level clipping while exploration tokens get wrongly clipped — sequence-level clipping fixes exactly this.
  • Pragmatic engineering stance: explicitly rejects bitwise-consistent kernels (2× slowdown) and FP16 master weights (NaN underflow), with empirical justifications.
  • Broad model coverage including the difficult MoE case (Qwen3-30B-A3B), where router shift compounds the problem.
  • Quantization-scheme ablation showing TBPO makes the choice between W8A8/W4A16/W4A8 largely irrelevant to final reward is a useful practical finding.
  • Honest about limitations: the conclusion explicitly names fully quantized RL training and token-level approximations as future work.

Limitations

  • Does not close the gap to BF16 (Qwen3-8B: 48.9 vs 51.8; Qwen3-30B-A3B: 51.2 vs 52.1).
  • QaRL's 1.3× speedup is slightly worse than plain quantized-rollout's 1.4× on the MoE — the alignment overhead is not free.
  • Sequence-level clipping discards whole responses, hurting sample efficiency; the paper acknowledges this and flags token-level approximations as future work.
  • MoE router shift under quantization is identified but not resolved (TBPO stabilizes but does not eliminate it).
  • Evaluation limited to RLVR (verifiable rewards); no RLHF/learned-reward-model experiments.
  • Hyperparameters (εh=0.0004, δl=0.0003, δh=0.0007) are very small and may be tightly tuned to Qwen; generalization is unproven.
  • No public code release stated in the PDF.
  • KV-cache quantization is explicitly skipped ("fails to provide meaningful throughput benefits in vLLM"), leaving a potential acceleration source unexplored.

Relevance

直接对应"LLM 训练稳定性与可观测性（LLM Training Stability & Observability）"——核心是量化 rollout 引起的训练-推理失配、错误 token 导致的 PPO 信任域破裂与 KL 漂移；同时涉及"计算可靠性与精度误差（Computation Reliability & Precision Errors）"，因为 QaRL 通过低比特 GEMM + STE 在训练侧对齐量化推理的算术行为，本质上是把精度失配作为可靠性问题来工程化解决。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Could the sequence-level trust band be made adaptive (e.g., scaled by response length or quantization bit-width) rather than fixed at very small εh/δl/δh?
  • Does TBPO's dual-clipping design transfer to RLHF with learned reward models, where advantage distributions are less cleanly separated into "error-token negatives" and "exploration positives"?
  • The MoE router shift under quantization is identified but unresolved — could a router-aware quantization scheme (keeping router weights at higher precision) eliminate the residual 0.9–2.9 point gap to BF16?
  • Is the W4A16-vs-W8A8 throughput crossover (W4A16 faster on large/MoE due to memory bounds) stable across GPU architectures, or does it shift on H20/B200 where INT8 Tensor Cores differ?
  • Could the error-token detection be made explicit (e.g., via perplexity or repetition-rate thresholds) so that contaminated responses are filtered before entering the replay buffer, reducing the load on sequence-level clipping?
