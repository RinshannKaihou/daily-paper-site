Distributed Multi-Layer Editing for Rule-Level Knowledge in Large Language Models

Yating Wang, Wenting Zhao, Yaqi Zhao, Yongshun Gong, Yilong Yin, Haoliang Sun

arXiv: 2604.08284

Date: 2026-04-11

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.08284

Distributed Multi-Layer Editing for Rule-Level Knowledge in Large Language Models

一句话总结： 本文通过细粒度因果追踪发现规则知识在 Transformer 中呈"形式特异"分布（公式/描述集中在早期层，实例集中在中层），据此提出 DMLE——对公式与描述共享早期层更新、对实例单独做中层更新，在 GPT-J-6B/Qwen2.5-7B/Qwen2-7B/LLaMA-3-8B 上实例可移植性平均提升 13.91pp、规则理解提升 50.19pp。
TL;DR: This paper studies rule-level knowledge editing in LLMs, where a single rule must stay consistent across three forms (formula, description, instance). Fine-grained causal tracing on GPT-J-6B, Qwen2-7B, Qwen2.5-7B, and LLaMA-3-8B reveals a form-specific layer organization: formulas and descriptions peak in early layers (e.g., layers 2–4 on GPT-J-6B) while instances peak in middle layers (e.g., 13–15). The proposed DMLE applies a shared early-layer update to formula+description and a separate middle-layer update to instance. On RuleEdit-200, DMLE improves instance portability by 13.91pp and rule understanding by 50.19pp on average over the strongest baseline, with the largest RU gain being 84.92 vs MEMIT's 11.25 on GPT-J-6B.

核心贡献

  • 将 RuleEdit 基准从 80 条几何规则扩展到 200 条人工核验的数学与物理规则（共 600 对齐样本），每条规则包含公式、描述、实例三种对齐形式。
  • 通过细粒度因果追踪揭示规则知识的"形式特异"层组织：公式与描述在早期层（GPT-J-6B 第 2–4 层）峰值，实例在中层（第 13–15 层）峰值；该规律在 Qwen2-7B、Qwen2.5-7B、LLaMA-3-8B 上一致成立。
  • 提出 DMLE（Distributed Multi-Layer Editing）：基于 MEMIT，对公式与描述共享早期层最小二乘更新，对实例单独做中层更新，使编辑策略与因果追踪揭示的层组织对齐。
  • 在四个基座模型上验证：DMLE 在规则级指标（实例可移植性 IP、规则理解 RU）上大幅超越 ROME/MEMIT/GRACE/PROMPT，平均提升 IP 13.91pp、RU 50.19pp。

Key Contributions

  • Extends the RuleEdit benchmark from 80 to 200 manually verified rules spanning mathematics (number theory, geometry, algebra, calculus, discrete math, logic, probability, decision theory) and physics (classical mechanics, E&M, thermodynamics, waves, optics), each with aligned formula/description/instance forms (600 samples total).
  • Fine-grained causal tracing on GPT-J-6B, Qwen2-7B, Qwen2.5-7B, and LLaMA-3-8B reveals a form-specific layer-wise organization: formulas and descriptions concentrate in early layers (e.g., GPT-J-6B layers 2–4; LLaMA-3-8B layers 7–9) while instances peak in middle layers (e.g., GPT-J-6B layers 13–15; LLaMA-3-8B layers 15–16), consistent across all four model families.
  • DMLE (Distributed Multi-Layer Editing), a MEMIT-based framework that applies a shared early-layer least-squares update to formula+description and a separate middle-layer update to instance, aligning edits with the observed layer-wise storage patterns rather than a single contiguous block.
  • Empirical wins on rule-level metrics: average improvements of +13.91pp on instance portability (IP) and +50.19pp on rule understanding (RU) over the strongest baseline across the four base models, while remaining competitive on standard reliability/generalization/locality.

Methodology

RuleEdit-200 construction. 200 rules sourced from Wikipedia Mathematics and OpenStax College Physics, each constructed into three aligned forms via a template-guided pipeline: Gemini generates samples following a hand-crafted prototype template that includes the rule name and a manually specified counterfactual target (e.g., changing the geometric mean from √(a×b) to √(a+b)). All samples manually reviewed by an annotator (bachelor's degree, CET-6 English, task familiarity) for grammatical correctness, cross-form consistency, and numerical-instance derivability from the edited rule. Final dataset: 200 rules, 600 aligned samples.

Causal tracing (Section 4). Adapts Meng et al. (2022a)'s causal intervention framework to multi-form rule knowledge. For each prompt–target pair (x, y): run clean to get p(y|x); construct corrupted input x̃ by adding Gaussian noise ε ~ N(0, ν) to subject-token input embeddings (ν=0.025 for GPT-J-6B/Qwen2-7B/Qwen2.5-7B, ν=0.027 for LLaMA-3-8B); restore hidden state at layer l to its clean counterpart and measure recovery r_l = p(y|x̃; h_l ← h_clean_l) − p(y|x̃). Tracing set: 1,000 samples per form (200 rules replicated 5× and shuffled), with form-specific targets (final symbolic expression for formula, verb-led phrase for description, final numerical answer for instance). Focus on MLP states (motivated by Geva et al. 2021's key-value memory view). Models: GPT-J-6B, Qwen2-7B, Qwen2.5-7B, LLaMA-3-8B.

DMLE editing (Section 5). Built on MEMIT. For a rule with prompts {x^(f), x^(d), x^(i)} and targets {y^(f), y^(d), y^(i)}, the goal is F*(x^(k)) ≈ y^(k) for k ∈ {f, d, i} while preserving unrelated behavior. Let D_f, D_d, D_i denote key–value pairs for each form. The shared formula+description update solves: ΔW_fd := argmin_Ŵ [Σ_{(k_f,v_f)∈D_f} ‖Ŵk_f − v_f‖² + Σ_{(k_d,v_d)∈D_d} ‖Ŵk_d − v_d‖²]. The instance update solves: ΔW_i := argmin_Ŵ Σ_{(k_i,v_i)∈D_i} ‖Ŵk_i − v_i‖². Both admit closed-form solutions (linear associative memory view, Kohonen 2009; Anderson 1972). ΔW_fd is applied to the early-layer group, ΔW_i to the middle-layer group, with layer ranges determined by the causal-tracing peaks for each model.

Evaluation. Open-ended generation with DeepSeek-V3.2 as automatic evaluator. Baselines: ROME, MEMIT (parameter-updating); GRACE, PROMPT (non-parametric). Baselines edit each form independently and report averages. Five metrics: reliability (Rel), generalization (Gen), locality (Loc) — standard editing metrics; plus rule understanding (RU, cross-form consistency) and instance portability (IP, applying the edited rule to numerical instances).

Results & Highlights

  • On GPT-J-6B, DMLE achieves RU 84.92% vs MEMIT's 11.25% (+73.67pp) and ROME's 9.13%; IP reaches 59.19% vs MEMIT's 23.08% (+36.11pp) and ROME's 20.50%, while reliability stays competitive (95.00% vs MEMIT's 88.88%).
  • On Qwen2.5-7B, DMLE reaches RU 85.50% vs MEMIT's 20.25% (+65.25pp); IP 63.51% vs MEMIT's 36.50% (+27.01pp); reliability 96.50% (competitive with MEMIT's 97.16%).
  • On Qwen2-7B, DMLE reaches RU 87.63% vs MEMIT's 19.63% (+68.00pp); IP 64.58% vs MEMIT's 39.92% (+24.66pp); reliability 98.17% (highest among all methods).
  • On LLaMA-3-8B (the challenging case), DMLE still achieves the best IP 41.21% (vs MEMIT's 26.58%, +14.63pp) and RU 45.88% (vs MEMIT's 12.38%, +33.50pp), though reliability drops to 54.16% (below GRACE's 92.00% and PROMPT's 95.17%) — the authors attribute this to MEMIT's comparatively weaker performance on LLaMA-3-8B.
  • Averaged across all four models, DMLE improves IP by +13.91pp and RU by +50.19pp over the strongest baseline.
  • Against parameter-updating methods (ROME, MEMIT) specifically, DMLE outperforms MEMIT by an average of 29.26pp on IP and 68.97pp on RU across GPT-J-6B, Qwen2.5-7B, and Qwen2-7B.
  • Ablation (Table 2, Qwen2-7B and LLaMA-3-8B): FLSU (fixed-layer separate updates) → FLJU (fixed-layer joint) gives only limited benefit; SFSU (sequential form-specific, separate updates at correct layers) improves over both fixed-layer variants; DMLE (shared formula+description update + separate instance update) further beats SFSU — e.g., on Qwen2-7B RU: FLSU 82.50 → FLJU 84.25 → SFSU 84.88 → DMLE 87.63. This confirms both form-specific layer allocation AND shared formula/description updates matter.

Authors & Credibility

The work builds on the RuleEdit benchmark of Zhang et al. (2026) and the MEMIT/ROME lineage (Meng et al. 2022a,b). Code is publicly available at https://github.com/Pepper66/DMLE. Evaluation uses DeepSeek-V3.2 as an automatic evaluator for open-ended generation. Causal tracing follows the established Meng et al. (2022a) framework. The dataset construction is documented in detail (Appendix A) including rule sourcing, template-guided counterfactual generation via Gemini, and manual quality control by a qualified annotator.

Critical Assessment

The paper's central mechanistic finding — that rule knowledge is form-specific across layers (formula/description in early layers, instance in middle layers) — is well-supported by causal tracing across four model families and directly motivates the DMLE design. However, several weaknesses limit the strength of the conclusions. First, the locality metric is omitted entirely for non-parametric baselines (GRACE, PROMPT) with the note "Locality is not directly compared for non-parametric methods," but DMLE's own locality scores are weak on LLaMA-3-8B (27.17 for ROME, 23.00 for MEMIT, with DMLE's locality not clearly reported in the main table for LLaMA-3-8B) — the paper does not adequately address whether the large multi-layer edits harm locality, which is a core editing concern. Second, the reliability regression on LLaMA-3-8B is severe: DMLE scores 54.16% reliability vs GRACE's 92.00% and PROMPT's 95.17%, meaning the edit often fails to take effect at all on this model; the authors attribute this to "the comparatively weaker performance of MEMIT on the same model" but do not investigate whether the distributed two-layer-group strategy itself is ill-suited to LLaMA-3-8B's architecture. Third, the causal tracing uses only 200 unique rules replicated 5× to reach 1,000 samples per form — replication does not increase statistical diversity, so the layer-organization finding rests on effectively 200 rules, and no confidence intervals or significance tests are reported. Fourth, the evaluation relies on DeepSeek-V3.2 as an automatic judge for open-ended generation, but no human verification of the judge's accuracy is reported, leaving open whether the large RU gains (e.g., 84.92 vs 11.25) reflect real rule understanding or judge artifacts. Fifth, the DMLE layer ranges are determined per-model from causal-tracing peaks, but the paper does not report how sensitive the results are to the exact layer boundaries — whether a ±2 layer shift preserves the gains is untested. Finally, the comparison is limited to ROME/MEMIT/GRACE/PROMPT and does not include newer editors like AlphaEdit (cited but not benchmarked) or MELO, so the head-to-head against the state of the art is incomplete.

Strengths

  • Clean mechanistic motivation: the causal-tracing finding (form-specific layer organization, consistent across 4 model families) is a genuine insight that directly informs the editing design rather than being a post-hoc rationalization.
  • The RuleEdit-200 benchmark is a useful contribution: 200 rules across math and physics with three aligned forms and manual verification is more structured than typical fact-editing benchmarks.
  • The ablation (Table 2) cleanly separates two design axes — form-specific layer allocation vs. shared vs. separate updates — showing both contribute (SFSU beats fixed-layer variants; DMLE beats SFSU).
  • Large, consistent rule-level gains (IP +13.91pp, RU +50.19pp average) across four model families, with code released.
  • The framing — "effective rule-level editing depends not only on where to edit, but also on how multi-form knowledge is distributed and coordinated" — is a thoughtful generalization of locate-then-edit.

Limitations

  • Severe reliability regression on LLaMA-3-8B (54.16% vs GRACE's 92.00%), attributed but not resolved.
  • Locality under-reported for DMLE on LLaMA-3-8B and omitted for non-parametric baselines; the locality cost of distributed multi-layer edits is not fully characterized.
  • Causal tracing uses 200 rules replicated 5× (no new diversity); no confidence intervals or significance tests on the layer-organization finding.
  • Automatic evaluation via DeepSeek-V3.2 with no reported human verification of judge accuracy.
  • No sensitivity analysis on layer-range boundaries (±2 layers).
  • No comparison against AlphaEdit (cited), MELO, or other recent editors.
  • Only math/physics rules with single-line formulas; generalization to chemistry, biology, or multi-line rules is untested.
  • "Preprint. Under review." — not yet peer-reviewed.
  • The counterfactual targets are generated by Gemini and may introduce LLM-specific stylistic artifacts that favor or disfavor certain editors.

Relevance

直接对应"LLM 隐藏状态与可解释性（LLM Hidden States & Interpretability）"方向——核心是通过因果追踪定位规则知识在 Transformer 各层的因果贡献，揭示形式特异的层组织；同时涉及"LLM 推理评估（LLM Inference Evaluation）"，因为它构建了 RuleEdit-200 基准并以实例可移植性、规则理解等指标评估编辑后模型的跨形式推理一致性。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Is the form-specific layer organization (early for formula/description, middle for instance) specific to math/physics rules, or does it generalize to procedural knowledge in other domains (chemistry, code, commonsense)?
  • How sensitive are the DMLE gains to the exact layer-range boundaries — does a ±2 layer shift preserve the +50pp RU improvement, or is it sharply peaked?
  • Could the severe LLaMA-3-8B reliability regression be fixed by a model-specific layer-range re-derivation or by combining DMLE with AlphaEdit's null-space constraints?
  • Does the automatic DeepSeek-V3.2 judge agree with human raters on rule-understanding correctness, especially for the large RU gaps (84.92 vs 11.25)?
  • Would extending the tracing set beyond 200 unique rules (rather than replicating) strengthen or weaken the form-specific layer organization claim?
  • Can DMLE handle sequential/lifelong rule edits without catastrophic forgetting, given that it modifies two separated layer groups rather than one?
