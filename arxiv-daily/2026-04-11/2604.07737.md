# SepSeq: A Training-Free Framework for Long Numerical Sequence Processing in LLMs

> **一句话总结：** 提出 SepSeq 训练无关框架，在长数值序列中每隔 k 个 token 插入分隔符作为注意力汇，将分散的 Softmax 注意力重新局部化；在 9 个 LLM 与 10 个任务上平均相对准确率提升 35.6%，同时推理 token 消耗下降 16.4%。

> **TL;DR:** Across 9 LLMs (Qwen3-0.6B to 30B, QwQ-32B, DeepSeek-V3/R1, Claude-3.7-Sonnet, Gemini-2.5-Pro, GPT-4.1, GPT-4o) and 10 tasks (6 synthetic: counting, indexing, max/min-int/float; 4 real: number-string, number-list, stock, weather, 200 samples each), SepSeq versus the strongest non-SepSeq baseline (Vanilla/CoT/ICL) raises average accuracy from 51.6% to 69.9% (+35.6% relative) — e.g., indexing +123.0%, stock +100.0%, weather +60.9% — while reducing average response length from 3,375 to 2,823 tokens (−16.4%); on QwQ-32B the gain is +69.0% (34.2%→57.8%) and on GPT-4.1 +35.6% (61.0%→82.7%).

## 核心贡献

- 识别注意力分散为长数值序列处理的瓶颈：标准 Softmax 的归一化分母在长且数值相似的 token 上累积，稀释了对真正相关值的注意力，导致精度退化；并用注意力可视化（Figure 1B/C）与定理形式化分隔符的跨段注意力抑制效应。
- 提出 SepSeq 训练无关、即插即用框架：将分隔符 τsep（如 '\n'）每 k 个 token 替换标准分隔符 τsp，把长序列切为段；关键设计是替换而非新增 token，保持与 vanilla 基线相同的 token 长度，无序列长度开销。
- 机械化分析：分隔符作为注意力汇吸引后续注意力，膨胀 Softmax 分母从而渐近抑制跨段注意力（Theorem 2，公式 9），使注意力局部化；Assumption 1（分隔符注意力）经 10 次独立试验在层与头级别经验验证（Figure 2A/B）。
- 9 LLM × 10 任务全面验证：平均相对准确率 +35.6%、推理 token 消耗 −16.4%，并证明 CoT/ICL 反而有害（51.6%→49.0%/48.9%），说明失败源于输入解析而非推理能力不足。

## Key Contributions

- Identifies attention dispersion as the bottleneck for long numerical sequence processing: the Softmax normalization denominator accumulates contributions from many irrelevant/similar tokens in long numerical inputs, diluting attention to truly relevant values; formalized via Assumption 1 (separator attention) and Theorem 2 (cross-segment attention suppression) with attention visualizations (Figure 1B/C).
- Proposes SepSeq, a training-free, plug-and-play input-formatting framework: replaces the standard delimiter τsp with a specific separator τsep (e.g., '\n') every k-th token to partition a long numerical sequence into segments; crucially this is a substitution (not insertion) that maintains identical token length to the vanilla baseline, avoiding any sequence-length overhead.
- Mechanistic analysis showing separators act as attention sinks that inflate the Softmax denominator (Z^SepSeq > Z^Vanilla), asymptotically attenuating cross-segment attention (Equation 9); Assumption 1 empirically verified across 10 independent trials at both layer and head levels (Figure 2A/B) on Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking.
- Comprehensive validation on 9 LLMs and 10 tasks: +35.6% average relative accuracy gain and −16.4% inference token consumption, plus the finding that CoT and ICL are actively harmful (51.6%→49.0%/48.9%), indicating failures stem from input parsing/management rather than reasoning ability.

## Methodology

Problem definition: given a numerical sequence S=[x1,...,xn] and a natural-language query q, generate y=f_θ(q,S); tasks are sequence-complete, position-sensitive, and precision-critical. The bottleneck analysis attributes failure to attention dispersion in standard Softmax attention Attention(Q,K,V)=softmax(QK^T/√dk)V: as N grows, the denominator Σexp(s_j) accumulates many weakly relevant contributions from locally similar numerical tokens, diluting attention to the relevant values. A preliminary transcription experiment (Appendix A) on Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507 shows 100% verbatim reproduction accuracy for sequences ≤256 numbers but collapse to 14% at 257–512 and 0% at 513–1024 (Table 4).

SepSeq inserts a separator token τsep (e.g., '\n') every k-th interval, formalized as SepSeq(S,k) = [x1⊕τsp⊕...⊕xk⊕τsep] ⊕ [xk+1⊕τsp⊕...⊕x2k⊕τsep] ⊕ ... (Equation 4). The substitution strategy ensures identical token length to vanilla. Mechanistically, for a token xi after τsep, Qi·K^T_τsep is substantial whereas the baseline Qi·K^T_τsp is negligible, inflating the Softmax denominator so cross-segment attention ASepSeq[xi,xj]/AVanilla[xi,xj] < 1 (Equation 9). Baselines: Vanilla (standard delimiter, Barbero et al. 2024), Chain-of-Thought (Wei et al. 2023), In-Context Learning (single exemplar). Models: 9 LLMs — open-source Qwen3 family (0.6B–30B, dense and MoE, instruct and reasoning modes) + QwQ-32B + DeepSeek-R1/V3; proprietary Claude-3.7-Sonnet, Gemini-2.5-Pro, GPT-4.1, GPT-4o. Datasets: synthetic Dsyn (1,200 samples, 6 task types × 200, four length intervals S/M/L/XL = 2–32/33–128/129–256/257–512) and real Dreal (800 samples, 4 categories × 200: number-string, number-list, stock, weather with human-generated questions on real financial/meteorological time series). Metrics: Accuracy and Answer Rate (valid non-null responses / total). Main experiments use k=16; results are mean±std over 10 independent runs. Program-of-Thought comparison in a 2×2 design (Vanilla vs PoT × Standard vs SepSeq), 200 samples/task across 10 tasks.

## Results & Highlights

- Effectiveness (Table 1, 10-task average over 9 models, 10 runs): SepSeq raises average accuracy from 51.6% (Vanilla) to 69.9% (+35.6% relative) while CoT (49.0%) and ICL (48.9%) actively hurt. Per-task relative gains: indexing +123.0% (38.8%→86.6%), counting +79.6% (42.7%→76.7%), stock +100.0% (13.2%→27.4%), weather +60.9% (26.7%→44.6%), max-float +26.8% (63.9%→81.0%), max-int +22.7% (75.3%→92.4%); number-string and number-list show ~0% gain (already near ceiling or format-dependent). Answer rate improves +2.6% average (90.5%→92.8%).
- Across models (Table 2): consistent gains irrespective of family/architecture — QwQ-32B +69.0% (34.2%→57.8%), Qwen3-8B +53.0% (45.5%→69.6%), Qwen3-30B-A3B +48.6% (54.3%→80.7%), Claude-3.7-Sonnet +37.3% (57.3%→79.1%), GPT-4.1 +35.6% (61.0%→82.7%), Gemini-2.5-Pro +34.3% (58.9%→79.1%), DeepSeek-R1 +15.4% (61.1%→70.5%), DeepSeek-V3 +11.4% (45.7%→50.9%); both dense (Qwen3-8B +53.0%) and MoE (Qwen3-30B-A3B +48.6%) architectures benefit.
- Robustness — separator interval (Figure 3A): performance saturates for k≥8 with negligible variance for k∈{16,32}; the Thinking model holds ~90% accuracy across k∈{8,16,32} with <0.4% deviation, showing hyperparameter robustness; k=16 used in main experiments.
- Robustness — separator symbol (Figure 3B): on Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507, for basic Dsyn tasks CR and Backslash are best (82.8% each) but for complex Dreal tasks LF is best (49.9% vs Backslash 45.2%), indicating a complexity-dependent processing-strategy shift.
- Scale dependence (Figure 3C): SepSeq underperforms vanilla at 0.6B (insufficient capacity), inflection at 4B where gains begin, and >80% accuracy on 14B — the method requires a minimum emergent capability threshold.
- Reasoning vs Instruct (Figure 3A): Thinking model's vanilla baseline (71.4%) matches Instruct's saturated peak (72.1%), but with SepSeq the Thinking model leaps to 90.2%, breaking the Instruct ceiling — SepSeq acts as a structural multiplier for reasoning models.
- PoT complementarity (Table 3, 10 tasks × 200 samples): SepSeq+Standard 70.35% vs Vanilla+Standard 53.25%; PoT+SepSeq 84.45% vs PoT 78.90% — SepSeq is complementary to (not redundant with) tool-assisted reasoning, improving upstream input processing while PoT strengthens downstream computation.
- Efficiency (Figure 4): average response length drops from 3,375 tokens (Vanilla) to 2,823 (SepSeq) = −16.4%; per-task reductions up to −42.9% (indexing) and −31.1% (weather), because SepSeq mitigates the repetition loops and hallucinated gibberish vanilla LLMs produce when losing track of long sequences.
- Transcription bottleneck (Appendix A, Table 4): Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507 achieves 100% verbatim reproduction for ≤256 numbers but collapses to 14% at 257–512 and 0% at 513–1024; the Thinking variant is worse overall (40.80% vs 62.80%), suggesting reasoning overhead acts as noise for rote mechanical tasks.

## Critical Assessment

The headline +35.6% relative accuracy gain is an average that obscures a highly bimodal distribution: the gains are concentrated in the synthetic precision tasks (indexing +123.0%, counting +79.6%) and two real tasks (stock +100.0%, weather +60.9%), while number-string shows +0.0% and number-list only +0.9% — meaning SepSeq provides essentially no benefit on tasks where the failure mode is logical reasoning over structure rather than raw sequence attendance, yet these are averaged into the headline figure. The "−16.4% token consumption" efficiency claim is a side-effect of reduced repetition/hallucination rather than a genuine algorithmic compression, so it conflates accuracy improvement with cost reduction and would not hold on tasks where the model already produces concise outputs. The mechanistic theory (Assumption 1, Theorem 2) rests on the unproven premise that Qi·K^T_τsep is "substantial" for separator tokens — this is empirically shown for one model (Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking) on 20-integer sequences over 10 trials, but the paper does not establish that the attention-sink property generalizes across all 9 evaluated models, architectures (dense vs MoE), or training paradigms (instruct vs reasoning), leaving the universality of the mechanism an open question. The scale-dependence result (SepSeq underperforms vanilla at 0.6B, inflection at 4B) is acknowledged as a limitation but its practical implication — that SepSeq can degrade small-model deployments — is understated given the prevalence of small on-device models. The separator-symbol finding (LF best for complex tasks, CR/Backslash best for basic) is explained via "cognitive load theory" borrowed from human psychology (Sweller 1988), which is a speculative analogy for transformer attention rather than a validated mechanism. Finally, the absence of any comparison against attention-modification methods (e.g., Scalable-Softmax, StreamingLLM attention sinks) or KV-cache compression baselines means SepSeq's advantage over structural alternatives is not established.

## Strengths

- Clean, actionable idea: a training-free, plug-and-play input formatting change (replace delimiter with separator every k tokens, identical token length) that yields large gains on precision-critical numerical tasks with zero training cost.
- Strong empirical breadth: 9 diverse LLMs (open and proprietary, dense and MoE, instruct and reasoning) × 10 tasks (6 synthetic + 4 real with human-generated questions on real financial/meteorological data), reported as mean±std over 10 independent runs.
- The finding that CoT and ICL are actively harmful (51.6%→49.0%/48.9%) is a valuable diagnostic: it localizes the failure to input parsing/management rather than reasoning, justifying an input-formatting fix over a prompting fix.
- PoT complementarity result (PoT+SepSeq 84.45% > PoT 78.90% > SepSeq 70.35% > Vanilla 53.25%) cleanly demonstrates SepSeq improves upstream input processing orthogonal to downstream tool computation.
- Hyperparameter robustness (k≥8 plateau, <0.4% deviation) lowers deployment friction; the structural-multiplier effect on reasoning models (Thinking 71.4%→90.2%) is a striking, well-illustrated finding.

## Limitations

- Headline +35.6% average obscures bimodality: number-string +0.0%, number-list +0.9% — SepSeq gives near-zero benefit on tasks whose failure mode is logical reasoning over structure rather than raw sequence attendance.
- The −16.4% token reduction is a by-product of reduced repetition/hallucination, not algorithmic compression; it would not hold on tasks where the model already produces concise outputs.
- The mechanistic theory's core premise (separators as universal attention sinks) is empirically verified on only one model (Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking) on short 20-integer sequences; generalization across all 9 models/architectures/training paradigms is not established.
- Scale dependence: SepSeq degrades performance at 0.6B and requires ≥4B parameters, an understated practical risk for small on-device deployments.
- The separator-symbol finding is explained via human cognitive load theory (Sweller), a speculative analogy for transformer attention rather than a validated mechanism.
- No comparison against structural alternatives (Scalable-Softmax, StreamingLLM attention sinks, KV-cache compression), so SepSeq's advantage over attention-modification methods is unknown.
- No code/dataset release URL in the PDF, limiting independent reproduction.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the separator-as-attention-sink property (Assumption 1) hold across all 9 evaluated models, or is it specific to Qwen-family architectures — and if not universal, which model families benefit from a different sink token?
- On tasks where SepSeq gives ~0% gain (number-string, number-list), what is the actual failure mode, and would a different segmentation strategy (variable-length, semantic-boundary) recover gains?
- How does SepSeq interact with KV-cache compression and attention-sink methods (StreamingLLM, Scalable-Softmax) — are the effects additive or redundant?
- For sub-4B models where SepSeq degrades performance, is there a minimal intervention (e.g., learned separator embedding, fine-tuned separator token) that lowers the capacity threshold?
- Does the −16.4% token-reduction benefit persist when the baseline already uses concise-output prompting, or is it entirely a hallucination-suppression side-effect?
- Can the cross-segment attention suppression theorem (Equation 9) be tightened into a quantitative bound on accuracy recovery as a function of k, rather than the asymptotic inequality shown?
