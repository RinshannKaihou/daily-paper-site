TEMPER: Testing Emotional Perturbation in Quantitative Reasoning

Atahan Dokme, Benjamin Reichman, Larry Heck

arXiv: 2604.07801

Date: 2026-04-11

TEMPER: Testing Emotional Perturbation in Quantitative Reasoning

一句话总结： 提出教师-学生情感翻译框架构建 TEMPER-5400 基准（900 题×6 情感），在 18 个模型（1B 到前沿）上证明：保持数值不变仅改写情感基调即可使推理准确率下降 2–10 个百分点，而中和化可恢复约 70% 的性能损失，隐藏态分析显示情感文本使表征偏移 3–4×。
TL;DR: On TEMPER-5400 (5,400 emotion-neutral pairs from 900 problems × 6 Ekman emotions across GSM8K, MultiArith, ARC-Challenge), evaluated on 18 instruction-tuned models (1B–frontier) under base and zero-shot CoT prompting, emotional framing of mathematically identical problems reduces accuracy by 2–10 pp (e.g., GSM8K mean drop 3.9% CoT / 6.1% base vs. original; disgust 5.9% mean, 10.0% peak on ARC) versus neutral originals, while length-matched non-emotional paraphrases show ~1% no-direction change; neutralization recovers ~70% of lost performance and shifts final-layer representations 3–4× less than emotional variants on four architectures (Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen-2.5-7B, Gemma-2-9B, Mistral-7B).

核心贡献

  • 教师-学生情感翻译框架：学生 Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct + LoRA，冻结 DistilRoBERTa 情感分类器（GoEmotions 7 类，加 768→100→7 bottleneck 头在 72K 情感数学样本上训练，99.6% 验证准确率）提供辅助监督；组合损失 L = L_CE + λ·L_aux，λ 调节情感强度-保真度权衡，配合 EMO_100（100 维潜空间 MSE）与 EMO_7（7 类 KL 散度）两种辅助损失变体。
  • TEMPER-5400 基准：5,400 对情感-中性对（900 题×6 情感，共 10,800 条翻译），来自 GSM8K（400 题）、MultiArith（300 题）、ARC-Challenge（200 题）；用 5 个翻译器变体集成生成 + 多翻译器语义过滤 + 非情感改写控制构造，三阶段过滤后情感化仅 0.8–1.4% 改变数学内容。
  • 18 模型评估证据：跨 1B–前沿规模（Llama-3.2-1B 到 GPT-5.4、o3）证明情感框架使准确率下降 2–10%，非情感改写无系统性变化，建立情感内容（而非表面改写）作为独立的脆弱性维度。
  • 中和化恢复与表征证据：中和化恢复约 70% 性能损失，且隐藏态分析显示情感文本使最终层表征偏移 3–4×（cosine distance），线性探针 4 类 F1≈1.0 完美分离情感文本。

Key Contributions

  • A teacher-student emotion translation framework: student Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct + LoRA (rank 16, α=32, Q/V projections) trained against a frozen DistilRoBERTa emotion classifier (GoEmotions 7-class, with a 768→100→7 bottleneck head trained on 72K emotional math samples, 99.6% validation accuracy); combined loss L = L_CE + λ·L_aux, with EMO_100 (MSE on 100-d latent) and EMO_7 (temperature-scaled KL on 7-class) auxiliary variants producing five translators (CE-Only, Emo7-L/H, Emo100-L/H).
  • TEMPER-5400: a benchmark of 5,400 semantically verified emotion-neutral pairs (900 problems × 6 Ekman emotions = 10,800 translations) across GSM8K (400 problems), MultiArith (300), and ARC-Challenge (200), built via a five-variant ensemble with BERTScore-based selection and three-stage filtering (MVP automated check + Haiku LLM judge + human review).
  • Behavioral evidence across 18 instruction-tuned models (1B to frontier: Llama-3.2-1B ... GPT-5.4, o3) that emotional framing reduces reasoning accuracy by 2–10 pp while length-matched non-emotional paraphrases produce no systematic change, establishing emotion as a distinct fragility axis.
  • Neutralization recovery (≈70% of lost performance recovered) plus representational evidence: emotional text shifts final-layer representations 3–4× further than neutralized/paraphrase versions across four architectures, with a 4-class linear probe achieving F1≈1.0 for emotional states.

Methodology

The emotion translator is a teacher-student system. The student is Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct adapted with LoRA (rank 16, α=32, targeting Q/V projections); final-layer (layer L=32) hidden states (4096-d) are mean-pooled and linearly projected into the teacher's representation space. The teacher is a DistilRoBERTa classifier pretrained on GoEmotions for 7-class emotion classification (anger, disgust, fear, joy, neutral, sadness, surprise), with a two-layer bottleneck head (Linear 768→100, ReLU, Linear 100→7) trained on 72K emotional math samples (99.6% validation accuracy) providing two supervision signals: the 7-class probability distribution and the 100-d intermediate representation. Training uses combined loss L = L_CE + λ·L_aux; for EMO_7, L_aux is temperature-scaled KL (T=2) on the 7-class distribution; for EMO_100, L_aux is MSE on the 100-d latent vector. Five translator variants are created by crossing supervision type and weight: CE-Only (λ=0), Emo7-L (λ=0.05), Emo7-H (λ=0.5), Emo100-L (λ=0.02), Emo100-H (λ=0.2). Training corpus: 10,000 problems sampled from AQuA-RAT, MathQA, ASDiv; DeepSeek-V3 generates emotional variants in six Ekman emotions producing 126K training pairs (60K forward, 60K backward, 6K identity). Translators trained 2 epochs on a single A100-40GB (batch 12, lr 2e-4, cosine decay), ~8 hours/epoch; forward translation temperature 0.7, back-translation 0.3; quality-control pipeline with up to 3 retries (temperature reduced 30% per retry).

TEMPER-5400 construction: ensemble of five variants generates candidates; semantically altered candidates filtered (candidates cluster within 1–3% BERTScore); lowest-BERTScore (most emotional, content-preserving) translation selected and paired with its neutralization from the same variant. Reasoning evaluation uses greedy decoding (temperature 0) under base prompting and zero-shot CoT; prompt templates in Appendix F. Non-emotional paraphrase control generated with DeepSeek-V3 under 10 hard constraints (length/lexicality matched, no emotional content). Representational analysis extracts mean-pooled final-layer representations from four models (Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen-2.5-7B, Gemma-2-9B, Mistral-7B) for all 900 problems in original/emotional/neutralized/paraphrase variants (12,600 states/model); cosine distance from original computed per variant; a 4-class linear probe trained on hidden states.

Results & Highlights

  • Accuracy degradation (Table 3): emotional framing reduces accuracy by 2–10 pp across all 18 models and 3 datasets under both prompting strategies. Mean drops scale with difficulty: MultiArith 2.0% (ceiling effects), GSM8K 3.9% (CoT) / 6.1% (base, more realistic), ARC-Challenge 6.0% (emotional framing of answer choices interferes with option discrimination). Open-src avg O/E/N on GSM8K Base: 78.6/73.5/77.4; Frontier avg: 96.5/94.2/95.0. Even 70B+ and frontier APIs lose 2–4%.
  • Per-emotion (Table 4, base prompting, 18-model mean): disgust is most disruptive (5.9% mean, 10.0% peak on ARC), fear second (4.3%), joy least (1.9%), surprise (2.3%). Emotional samples causing failures have higher classifier confidence (intensity proxy) than non-failures: 0.726 vs 0.691, consistent across all 18 models.
  • Neutralization recovery: neutralization recovers 70% of emotion-broken problems overall (MultiArith 81%, ARC 70%, GSM8K 66%); recovery is partial because neutralized text stems from the emotional version (~2% of neutralizations introduce semantic changes). Since the neutralizer never sees the original, recovery confirms degradation is tied to emotional style not content corruption.
  • Non-emotional paraphrase control: length-matched non-emotional paraphrases produce ~1% mean absolute difference with no consistent direction across all 18 models on 900 samples under base and CoT, confirming emotional content (not rewording/length/complexity) drives degradation.
  • Failure patterns (Table 5, Sonnet 4.6 classifying 1,866 emotional failures across 4 models): Constraint degradation 49.0%, Attention competition 30.0% (6.7× more frequent than in originals: 30.0% vs 4.5%), Premature completion 8.6%; originals are largely unstructured (51.0% other). Disgust triggers attention competition disproportionately (40.1% of its failures vs 22–32% for others).
  • Representational evidence (Figure 2): emotional variants shift final-layer representations 3–4× further from original than neutralized/paraphrase versions, consistent across all four architectures; disgust and fear produce the largest shifts (matching their accuracy impact); 4-class linear probe F1≈1.0 identifies emotional variants nearly perfectly, with neutralized and original the most confused pair.
  • Translation quality (Table 2): five Llama translators achieve emotion accuracy 0.980–0.996, BERTScore 0.906–0.913, Roundtrip BERTScore 0.941–0.948, MVP 0.981–0.990; human eval (322 HITs, majority vote) emotion detection 86.9% (anger lowest 55% due to anger-disgust confusion), math preservation 100% majority / 98.3% individual; annotator-LLM agreement strong (86.9% vs 89.3% emotion detection, identical translator richness ranking).
  • Multi-architecture validation: Qwen-2.5-7B and Mistral-7B translators replicate the degradation (3.4–4.6% mean O→E drop vs 4.2–5.1% with Llama translations), confirming the effect is not a Llama-translator artifact.

Critical Assessment

The emotional translators themselves depend on LLMs throughout the pipeline (DeepSeek-V3 generates the 126K training pairs and the non-emotional paraphrase controls; Claude Haiku 4.5 is the LLM judge for semantic fidelity), so the benchmark's content-preservation guarantee is mediated by LLM judgment rather than formal verification — and the paper acknowledges ~0.8–1.4% of emotionalizations and ~2.0–3.0% of neutralizations alter mathematical content, with ~2% of neutralizations introducing semantic changes (e.g., dropping a quantifier), which means the "mathematically identical" claim is approximate and the reported degradation includes a small contamination from content drift rather than pure emotional effect. The evaluation uses greedy decoding (temperature 0) on exactly 900 problems (400 GSM8K + 300 MultiArith + 200 ARC), so per-cell accuracies rest on small sample sizes (e.g., ARC has only 200 problems × 6 emotions), and bootstrap CIs are deferred to an appendix not shown in the extracted text. The headline "2–10%" range conflates dataset means with per-model-per-dataset extremes, and at frontier scale (GPT-5.4) the emotional drop "approaches the noise floor where recovery provides diminishing returns" per the authors' own limitation discussion, weakening the claim that emotion is a distinct fragility axis for the largest models. The hidden-state analysis is restricted to four small/mid open-source models (7B–9B); it does not cover the frontier APIs whose internal representations are inaccessible, so the representational-interference mechanism is only indirectly inferred for them.

Strengths

  • Clean experimental isolation of emotion as a variable: a teacher-student translator preserves all numerical content (verifiable via MVP automated check) while changing only emotional tone, with a non-emotional paraphrase control that matches length/lexicality — a methodologically strong design that the prior EmotionPrompt/NegativePrompt work (which changed tone and content simultaneously) lacked.
  • The intensity-controllable λ trade-off (Table 1) with a smooth gradient from subtle coloring (λ=0.02) to word salad (λ=400), validated by three independent intensity measures (classifier confidence, human 1–10, LLM 1–10), gives a principled way to select conservative operating points.
  • Triangulated evaluation: automated metrics at scale (600/λ), LLM-as-judge (Haiku 4.5), and human AMT annotators (322 HITs, 3 annotators, majority vote, Fleiss κ=0.613) form a complementary hierarchy, and the paper reports where they disagree (e.g., the fine-tuned classifier matches target emotion in 14/15 annotator disagreements).
  • Multi-architecture generalization: translators trained on Qwen-2.5-7B and Mistral-7B replicate the degradation, ruling out a Llama-translator artifact.
  • Failure-pattern analysis (constraint degradation, attention competition, premature completion) provides mechanistic insight beyond aggregate accuracy, and attention competition's 6.7× enrichment under emotional framing is a concrete, testable signature.

Limitations

  • Content-preservation is approximate (0.8–1.4% of emotionalizations alter math content; ~2% of neutralizations introduce semantic changes), so reported degradation includes a small content-drift contaminant rather than being pure emotional effect.
  • Small per-cell sample sizes (ARC only 200 problems × 6 emotions); bootstrap CIs deferred to appendix; the "2–10%" range conflates means with extremes.
  • Frontier-scale effect is small (1–3% for GPT-5.4/o3) and approaches the noise floor per the authors' own discussion, weakening the distinct-fragility-axis claim for the largest models.
  • Hidden-state/linear-probe analysis restricted to four 7B–9B open models; frontier APIs' internal representations are inaccessible, so representational interference is only inferred for them.
  • Six Ekman basic emotions only (acknowledged); the math corpus is short word problems (whether emotional interference scales with problem length is open); evaluation covers three reasoning benchmarks and does not extend to domains like medical/legal text where emotion is more naturally interleaved with technical content.
  • Dataset/code release gated on acceptance (no public URL), limiting independent verification at time of writing.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How much of the 2–10% degradation is attributable to the ~0.8–1.4% content drift versus pure emotional tone, and would a formally-verified (symbolic) content-preservation filter change the headline numbers?
  • Does the neutralization-as-defense result generalize to agentic/multi-turn settings where emotional framing accumulates across turns, or does residual perturbation compound?
  • Can the attention-competition failure signature (6.7× enrichment) be localized to specific attention heads/layers, enabling a targeted intervention beyond whole-input neutralization?
  • For frontier models where the drop approaches the noise floor (GPT-5.4 1–3%), what sample size is needed to establish statistical significance, and does the effect re-emerge under harder/distribution-shifted reasoning?
  • Does the teacher-student translation framework transfer to non-emotional stylistic attributes (formality, sarcasm) with the same intensity-control and content-preservation properties?
