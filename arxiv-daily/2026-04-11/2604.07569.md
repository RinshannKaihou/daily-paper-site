# Learning is Forgetting: LLM Training As Lossy Compression

> **一句话总结：** 将 LLM 预训练建模为有损压缩过程，用软熵估计器在 OLMo2 7B 预训练 checkpoints 上验证信息瓶颈（IB）两阶段预测（先扩展表示后压缩），发现模型逐步逼近最优压缩界；压缩最优性与偏好信息均能预测 47 个开源模型在 6 个基准上的下游性能（最优性 r=0.52，偏好信息 r=0.76，p<0.001）。

> **TL;DR:** Using a soft-entropy estimator on 10,000 C4 samples, the authors show OLMo2 7B pre-training follows the Information Bottleneck two-phase prediction (expansion then compression, approaching the I(X;Z)=I(Z;Y) bound) across OLMo2 1B/7B/32B checkpoints; across 47 open-weight models from 6 families evaluated on 6 benchmarks (MMLU Pro, BBH, Math LVL5, IFEval, GPQA, MuSR), compression optimality (expressivity/complexity) predicts performance (r=0.52, p<0.001) vs. expressivity alone (r=0.08, p=0.575), and preference information predicts performance (r=0.76, p=0.001); the 1B model fails to compress, oscillating off the bound.

## 核心贡献

- 用可扩展的软熵估计器（Conklin 2025）首次将率失真理论/信息瓶颈框架应用于 LLM 规模，计算模型表示与输入/输出的互信息
- 在 OLMo2 7B 预训练 checkpoints 上验证 IB 两阶段预测：先增加与输出的互信息（拟合阶段），后压缩与输入的互信息（压缩阶段），逐步逼近最优压缩界；该相变发生在 next-token 预测损失饱和时
- 发现规模效应：7B 与 32B 模型完成两阶段并逼近界，但 1B 模型完成扩展阶段后无法压缩，在界外振荡
- 证明压缩最优性（expressivity/complexity 比值）显著预测 47 个模型在 6 个基准上的下游性能（r=0.52, p<0.001），而单独 expressivity 不预测（r=0.08, p=0.575）；偏好信息预测性能（r=0.76, p=0.001）

## Key Contributions

- First application of the soft-entropy estimator (Conklin 2025) to LLM scale, enabling computation of mutual information I(X;Z) and I(Y;Z) between model representations and input/output labels without the memory bottlenecks of prior binning approaches
- Validates the Information Bottleneck two-phase prediction (Tishby & Zaslavsky 2015) on OLMo2 7B pre-training: a fitting phase (increasing I(Y;Z)) followed by a compression phase (decreasing I(X;Z)), approaching the optimal compression bound I(X;Z)=I(Z;Y); the transition coincides with next-token-prediction loss saturation
- Identifies a scale effect: 7B and 32B models complete both phases and approach the bound, but the 1B model fails to compress — it completes expansion but oscillates off the bound, suggesting a parameter threshold for optimal compression
- Shows compression optimality (expressivity/complexity) significantly predicts downstream performance across 47 models / 6 benchmarks (r=0.52, p<0.001), while expressivity alone does not (r=0.08, p=0.575); preference information (I(Z;preferred)) predicts performance (r=0.76, p=0.001)
- Demonstrates that across 5+ open-weight model families, all models converge near the same point on the IB bound at end of training, suggesting compression-as-learning is a general property of deep-learning models, not a single model's artifact

## Methodology

**Soft-entropy estimator:** For a batch of B tokenized samples (sequence length S) from corpus T, model θ with L layers and representation dimension h produces representations Z∈R^{L×B×S×h}. The estimator (from Conklin 2025, not original to this paper but first applied to LLMs here): (1) normalize each representation z to the unit sphere S^h; (2) sample n anchor points {w_i} uniformly from S^h; (3) softly assign each normalized representation to anchors via softmax of cosine similarities divided by temperature ε; (4) aggregate across batch/sequence to get a per-layer probability vector ẑ_l of length n; (5) compute Shannon entropy H(ẑ_l). Model entropy H(Z) = (1/L)Σ_l H(ẑ_l)/log(n), bounded 0–1 (efficiency). Temperature ε is calibrated to enable comparison across different representation dimensionalities (Appendix E.1.1).

**Mutual information:** I(X;Z) = H(Z) − Σ_x P(X=x)H(Z|X=x), where X are feature labels (token IDs, or preference labels, or language IDs). For output information I(Y;Z), Y is trailing context. Conditional estimates P(Z|X) approximated via n-gram back-off (Katz 1987): token, bigram, trigram, quadgram levels (quadgram becomes sparse; token-level used by default).

**Optimality metric:** Optimality = I(Y;Z)/I(X;Z) = expressivity/complexity, approaching 1.0 as a representation system approaches the IB bound I(X;Z)=I(Z;Y).

**Data and sampling:** 10,000 samples from C4 (Raffel et al. 2020), max context length 512, for entropy/MI estimates. Preference estimates from 10,000 samples of Tulu (Lambert et al. 2024). Estimates are relative (held constant across models), not claims of true latent entropy.

**Models:** Pre-training analysis on OLMo2 family (OLMo et al. 2025) — 1B, 7B, 32B variants with intermediate checkpoints (7B is primary). Additional pre-training analyses on SmolLM2 (Allal et al. 2025) and Pythia (Biderman et al. 2023) in Appendix D. Cross-model comparison: 47 open-weight models from 6 families.

**Downstream evaluation:** 6 benchmarks — MMLU Pro, BBH, Math LVL5, IFEval, GPQA, MuSR — aggregated from Fourrier et al. 2024 (Open LLM Leaderboard v2). Spearman correlations and partial Spearman (controlling for parameter count) reported.

## Results & Highlights

- **Pre-training approaches optimal compression (§4.1, Figure 1):** OLMo2 7B follows the IB two-phase prediction — first increasing I(Y;Z) (expressivity), then compressing I(X;Z) (complexity) while approaching the bound I(X;Z)=I(Z;Y). The compression transition coincides with next-token-prediction loss saturation on C4. The model's proximity to the bound tracks loss reduction.
- **Scale effect (§4.1, Figure 5):** 7B and 32B models both complete expansion and compression, approaching the bound. The 1B model completes the expansion phase but fails to compress — it oscillates while slowly moving away from the bound, suggesting a parameter threshold is necessary for optimal compression at a given data complexity (consistent with scaling laws, Kaplan et al. 2020).
- **Compression optimality predicts performance (§4.2, Figure 6, 47 models):** Token-level complexity significantly correlates with aggregate performance (r=−0.38, p=0.006 — lower complexity = better). Expressivity alone does not (r=0.08, p=0.575). Compression optimality (expressivity/complexity) significantly predicts performance (r=0.52, p<0.001). Preference information I(Z;preferred) strongly predicts performance (r=0.76, p=0.001).
- **Contextual information matters (§4.2, Figure 7):** Less token-level information and more bigram/trigram/quadgram information correlates positively with performance. Optimality at each back-off level is consistently positively related to performance (Spearman correlations above each facet). This holds despite estimates being computed on general C4 data, not the evaluation distributions.
- **Convergence across families (§4.1, Figure 5 top-left):** Across diverse open-weight models (6 families, different hyperparameters and training methodologies), representations converge and cluster near the same point on the IB bound at end of training, suggesting compression-as-learning is a general property.
- **Post-training edits information (Appendix B):** Post-training increases preference information across models in two families while minimally changing complexity — suggesting pre-training is responsible for broad compression, while post-training edits what information survives.
- **Per-benchmark breakdown (Appendix C):** Optimal compression of C4 significantly predicts performance on math, reasoning, and factual knowledge benchmarks, but NOT instruction following. Instruction following is predicted by preference information instead.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** High — applying the Information Bottleneck framework at LLM scale (7B–32B, 47 open-weight models) with a scalable soft-entropy estimator is genuinely new, and the finding that compression optimality predicts downstream performance (while expressivity alone does not) is a substantive empirical result. The two-phase IB prediction had only been confirmed on small feed-forward networks (MNIST, Shwartz-Ziv & Tishby 2017); this extends it to Transformers on complex language data.

**Paper-specific weaknesses:**
1. The IB bound used is the simplified line I(X;Z)=I(Z;Y) (Footnote 1 admits this "follows from the data processing inequality and is generally looser than the true information bottleneck frontier"). The authors note the true frontier may require I(X;Z)>I(Z;Y), so "approaching the bound" may overstate optimality — models could be far from the true IB frontier while near this looser line.
2. Entropy estimation is a known hard problem; the authors acknowledge "most approaches underestimating the true entropy" (Paninski 2003) and explicitly disclaim that their estimates are relative (sample-dependent), not true latent entropy. The soft-entropy estimator's convergence properties are asserted via citation to Conklin 2025 (a thesis) rather than independently validated here, and the choice of n anchor points and temperature ε is calibrated but not sensitivity-analyzed in the main text.
3. The n-gram back-off (token/bigram/trigram/quadgram) is a crude approximation of P(Z|X) — the authors note quadgrams become "increasingly sparse" and that the residual information encodes the full 512-token context window, which is intractable to compute. This means a substantial portion of contextual information is unmeasured, and the "majority of information encodes local context" finding may partly reflect the measurement ceiling.
4. The 1B "failure to compress" result is striking but the paper does not rule out that it is an artifact of the estimator at small representation dimensions (h is smaller for 1B), nor does it test whether longer training would eventually produce compression. The scale threshold claim is correlational.
5. Causal claims are weak: "pre-training is responsible for broad compression, post-training edits information" is inferred from two model families (Appendix B) without controlled intervention. The link from compression optimality to performance is correlational (r=0.52) across models that differ in many ways (data, scale, architecture), though partial Spearman controlling for parameter count is reported.

## Strengths

- Scales information-theoretic analysis to LLM scale (7B–32B, 47 models) for the first time
- Validated two-phase IB prediction on Transformers/language (previously only MNIST feed-forward nets), addressing a known generalization gap
- Strong predictive result: optimality r=0.52 and preference information r=0.76 across 47 models/6 benchmarks
- Honest about estimation limitations (relative estimates, not true entropy)
- Practical implication: optimality as a cheap stopping-criterion/model-selection criterion (single forward pass vs. full benchmark suite)
- ICLR 2026 peer-reviewed; code and additional visuals released

## Limitations

- Uses the looser IB bound I(X;Z)=I(Z;Y), not the true information bottleneck frontier
- Soft-entropy estimator convergence validated only in cited thesis; anchor count/temperature sensitivity not in main text
- n-gram back-off is a crude P(Z|X) approximation; substantial long-range context unmeasured
- 1B "failure to compress" may be estimator/scale artifact; not ruled out
- Correlational performance links across heterogeneous models; causal claims about pre/post-training weak (2 families)
- Estimates computed on C4; generalization to other distributions untested

## Relevance

直接映射到「LLM 隐藏状态与可解释性」与「LLM 训练稳定性与可观测性」两个关注领域：论文用信息瓶颈理论分析 LLM 表示空间的互信息结构（复杂度 vs 表达力），揭示预训练的两阶段压缩动力学，并将压缩最优性作为可观测指标预测下游性能，为训练可观测性和整体模型可解释性提供信息论框架。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the two-phase IB trajectory hold under the true information bottleneck frontier (not the looser I(X;Z)=I(Z;Y) line), and how much does the bound looseness affect the "optimal compression" claim?
- Can compression optimality serve as a real-time pre-training stopping criterion or checkpoint selector in practice, and how does it compare to loss-based heuristics?
- Why does the 1B model fail to compress — is it a fundamental capacity threshold, an estimator artifact at small h, or a function of training duration?
- Does the preference-information-predicts-performance result (r=0.76) hold for safety-relevant preferences (not just helpfulness), and can it guide alignment training?
