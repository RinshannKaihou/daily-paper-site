Relevance
本文通过系统的频谱分析揭示了优化器几何对Transformer FFN表示缩放的根本影响，与理解大模型缩放规律、设计新型优化器（如Muon族）、以及优化器-架构协同设计的研究方向高度相关。发现"匹配损失不等于匹配表示"对模型评估方法学也有重要启发。
Methodology
Architecture & Training: GPT-style decoder-only Transformers with Pre-RMSNorm, RoPE, squared-ReLU FFNs, QK-normalization (modded-nanoGPT configuration). Primary experiments at 160M (12 layers, 12 heads, d_model=768) with FFN width sweep D=m*d_model for m in {1,...,8}. Replication at 350M (24 layers, 32 heads, d_model=1024) with m in {1,2,3,4}. Trained on FineWeb-Edu (3.15B tokens at 160M, 4.19B at 350M), sequence length 512, batch size 1024. Optimizers compared: AdamW (lr=3e-3), Muon (lr=2e-2, Newton-Schulz orthogonalization), NorMuon (Muon + per-neuron normalization), Dion at rank fractions r in {1/2, 1/4, 1/8, 1/16}. - TAIL hard-rank scaling: Muon beta_hard=1.02 (R^2=0.81), AdamW beta_hard=0.44 (R^2=0.66). NorMuon reaches 1.04. Key Contributions

  1. Optimizer-induced spectral scaling laws: The same Transformer architecture produces markedly different FFN spectral-capacity scaling laws depending on optimizer. Hard-soft rank asymmetry is optimizer-dependent, not an architectural invariant.
  2. Matched loss != matched geometry: AdamW trained for 12K steps matches Dion(1/16) perplexity but exhibits near-zero hard-rank scaling (beta_hard=0.03) vs. Dion's 0.50, demonstrating that optimizer-induced representation geometry is not a transient convergence artifact.
  3. Frequency-dependent capacity allocation: MID tokens show the largest AdamW-to-Muon hard-rank gain (+0.69), followed by TAIL (+0.58) and HEAD (+0.33). Optimizer effects are most diagnostic in MID and TAIL regimes.
  4. Update rank as a capacity control knob: Dion rank sweep shows orthonormalization alone is insufficient -- low update rank (r=1/16) produces AdamW-like scaling, while high rank (r=1/2) approaches Muon/NorMuon.
  5. Optimizer effects exceed architectural interventions: In TAIL hard-rank, AdamW-to-Muon shift (|Delta beta|=0.74) is 2.0x the attention-rank effect and 2.5-5.2x RoPE/NoPE effects. Attention-rank intervention changes which optimizer is best matched to the architecture.
  6. Expanded trainable architecture space: Muon-family optimizers train partial PostLN configurations where AdamW diverges, showing optimizer geometry affects both capacity utilization and the feasible architecture space.

Critical Assessment

**Novelty
High.* This is the first work to systematically show that optimizer choice changes spectral scaling exponents* (not just efficiency factors) in LLM representations. The "matched loss != matched geometry" finding is a genuinely new insight with practical implications.
**Rigor
Strong.** The experimental design is careful: fixed architecture/data, controlled optimizer sweeps, FFN width sweeps (8 points at 160M, 4 at 350M), learning rate ablations, extended training controls, layer-wise robustness checks, and architectural intervention comparisons. The Dion rank sweep is a clever controlled experiment isolating update rank from orthonormalization.
**Reproducibility
Good.** Full hyperparameters provided in appendix (Tables 9-11). Modded-nanoGPT is public. Compute requirements are modest. Single-seed limitation is acknowledged.
**Significance
Potentially high, with caveats.** The finding that optimizer geometry reshapes representation scaling more than architectural interventions is impactful. However, the link to downstream task performance is not established -- spectral capacity may not directly translate to better benchmarks. The work raises an important hypothesis but does not close the loop on practical impact.
**Red flags / concerns
**
**Single-seed experiments
** All scaling fits use single seeds per (optimizer, width) configuration. The authors acknowledge this but do not quantify seed variance. Some HEAD/MID hard-rank fits have low R^2 (e.g., AdamW MID hard-rank R^2=0.36), which is acknowledged but somewhat undermines precision of those exponents.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths
  1. Elegant framing: the optimizer as a representation scaling axis, not just a convergence tool.
  2. Clean controlled experiments: Dion rank sweep is particularly clever for isolating causal mechanisms.
  3. "Matched loss != matched geometry" is a powerful conceptual contribution with practical implications for model evaluation.
  4. Comprehensive appendix (30 tables, layer-wise analyses, Renyi-family sweeps, learning rate ablations, PostLN trainability, positional processing analysis).
  5. Honest about limitations (scale, single-seed, FFN-only, no downstream tasks).
  6. The finding that architectural interventions have optimizer-dependent effects is important for the architecture search community.
Limitations
  1. Scale: 160M/350M models are far from frontier scale. The compute required for scaling-law measurements (training 8 width variants per optimizer) makes 1B+ experiments expensive, but the gap remains.
  2. FFN-only scope: The analysis does not extend to attention layers, which also contain significant parameters and may exhibit different optimizer-dependent behavior.
  3. No downstream task evaluation: Perplexity and spectral metrics are proxies; the practical impact of better spectral scaling on downstream performance is unverified.
  4. Single-seed uncertainty: No error bars from multiple seeds; some fits have low R^2 values that warrant caution in interpreting exact exponent values.
  5. Correlation vs. causation: While Dion rank sweeps provide some causal evidence, the broader claim that optimizer geometry causes better representations (rather than correlating with factors that cause better performance) is not fully established. The extended training experiment is the strongest causal evidence, but it only shows that AdamW's hard-rank scaling degrades, not that Muon's better scaling is the mechanism for its lower perplexity.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does better TAIL hard-rank spectral scaling translate to improved performance on rare-knowledge benchmarks (e.g., long-tail factual QA, low-resource language tasks)?
  2. Do these optimizer-dependent spectral scaling laws persist at 1B+ parameter scale, or does the gap narrow as models get larger?
  3. Can spectral diagnostics (hard-soft asymmetry, Renyi-family profiles) be used as real-time training monitors to guide optimizer switching or hyperparameter scheduling?
  4. How does the optimizer interact with data mixture, multilinguality, and data quality -- are spectral effects amplified or dampened under heterogeneous training data?
  5. Is there a causal pathway from Muon's superior spectral scaling to its lower perplexity, or are these parallel phenomena driven by a common underlying mechanism?
  6. Could hybrid optimizers (e.g., AdamW for attention layers, Muon for FFN layers) achieve the best of both worlds, as the paper's co-design framing suggests?
  7. What happens to spectral scaling under mixed-precision training, distributed training, or different normalization schemes (LayerNorm vs. RMSNorm)?
