## 一句话总结

本文提出 IAdaPID-ADG 优化器，将 AMSGrad 的非递增有效学习率与 DiffGrad 的梯度差调制因子融入自适应 PID 框架，同时解决深度学习优化中的收敛性和训练稳定性问题。

## TL;DR

Momentum-based SGD 存在两个经典缺陷：梯度噪声大且各参数分布不均（Adam 通过自适应学习率解决），以及动量累积导致的参数过冲振荡（PID optimizer 通过控制论中的微分项解决）。AdaPID 将两者统一，但继承了 Adam 的收敛性保证不足和训练不稳定问题。本文从 AMSGrad 借用 running-maximum 操作保证有效学习率单调非递增，从 DiffGrad 借用 sigmoid 调制因子根据梯度变化量自动缩放步长，两者结合构成 IAdaPID-ADG。在 MNIST、CIFAR-10 和两个宫颈癌医学影像数据集上，IAdaPID-ADG 的训练损失比所有对比优化器低数个数量级，训练/测试精度显著更高且曲线更稳定。消融实验验证了每个组件的独立贡献。

## 核心贡献

1. **收敛性修复**：首次将 AMSGrad 的 running-maximum 机制引入 AdaPID，对二阶矩估计 $v_t$ 和梯度差二阶矩估计 $d_t$ 均施加 $\max$ 操作（公式 17-20），确保有效学习率单调非递增，从理论上避免了 AdaPID 的收敛性问题。

2. **稳定性修复**：首次将 DiffGrad 的调制因子 $\mu_t = 1/(1 + e^{-|\Delta g_t|})$ 引入 AdaPID，当梯度变化剧烈时放大步长以加速收敛，当梯度趋于稳定时缩小步长以避免振荡。

3. **统一优化器 IAdaPID-ADG**：将上述两种修复整合进 AdaPID 框架（Algorithm 5），在不改变 AdaPID 核心积分-微分结构的前提下，同时改善收敛性和稳定性。

4. **跨域验证**：在标准视觉基准（MNIST、CIFAR-10）和真实医学影像数据集（IARC、AnnoCerv 宫颈癌数据）上均验证了有效性。

## Key Contributions

1. **Convergence fix**: Introduces AMSGrad-style running-maximum operations on both the squared gradient EMA ($v_t$) and the squared gradient-difference EMA ($d_t$) within AdaPID, ensuring a non-increasing effective learning rate and guaranteeing convergence.

2. **Stability fix**: Incorporates a DiffGrad-inspired sigmoid modulation factor $\mu_t = \sigma(|\Delta g_t|)$ that scales step sizes according to gradient variability -- amplifying steps when gradients change rapidly and damping them near convergence.

3. **Unified IAdaPID-ADG optimizer**: Combines both fixes into a single coherent algorithm (Algorithm 5) that retains AdaPID's integral-derivative control structure while addressing its two inherited Adam deficiencies.

4. **Cross-domain evaluation**: Validates on benchmark vision datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10) and real-world cervical cancer medical imaging datasets (IARC, AnnoCerv), demonstrating consistent improvements across domains.

## Methodology

### Background

The paper builds on three optimizer families:

- **Adam** (Algorithm 2): Computes exponential moving averages of gradients ($m_t$) and squared gradients ($v_t$), uses bias-corrected estimates to produce per-parameter adaptive learning rates. Addresses noisy/varying gradients but has convergence issues.

- **PID Optimizer** (Algorithm 3): Uses integral term $I_t = \gamma I_{t-1} - \eta g_t$ and derivative term $D_t = \gamma D_{t-1} + (1-\gamma)(g_t - g_{t-1})$ to regulate updates via control theory. Addresses overshoot but uses a fixed global learning rate.

- **AdaPID** (Algorithm 4): Combines Adam's adaptiveness with PID's control structure. Update rule: $w_t = w_{t-1} - \eta \left( \frac{K_i I_t}{\sqrt{\hat{v}_t} + \sigma} + \frac{K_d D_t}{\sqrt{\hat{d}_t} + \sigma} \right)$, where $v_t$ tracks squared gradients and $d_t$ tracks squared gradient differences.

### Proposed IAdaPID-ADG (Algorithm 5)

Three modifications over standard AdaPID:

1. **DiffGrad modulation factor** (Step 4): $\mu_t = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-|\Delta g_t|}}$, a sigmoid of the absolute gradient difference. Multiplied into the learning rate in the final update.

2. **Running-maximum second moments** (Step 9): $\hat{v}_t^{\max} = \max(\hat{v}_{t-1}^{\max}, v_t)$ and $\hat{d}_t^{\max} = \max(\hat{d}_{t-1}^{\max}, d_t)$, applied symmetrically to both squared gradient and squared gradient-difference terms.

3. **Final update** (Step 11): $w_t = w_{t-1} - \eta \mu_t \left( \frac{K_i I_t}{\sqrt{\hat{v}_t^{\max}} + \sigma} + \frac{K_d D_t}{\sqrt{\hat{d}_t^{\max}} + \sigma} \right)$

Key hyperparameters: $\gamma = 0.9$, $\beta = 0.99$, $\eta = 0.001$, $K_i = 0.5$, $K_d = 1$.

## Results & Highlights

- **Training loss**: IAdaPID-ADG achieves training losses "multiple orders of magnitude lower" than all competing optimizers (AMSGrad, DiffGrad, AdaPID) across all four datasets.
- **Training accuracy**: Significantly higher than all baselines on all datasets.
- **Test accuracy**: Consistently outperforms all baselines, with more stable accuracy curves.
- **Ablation study** (MNIST): Each added component (AMSGrad max operation, DiffGrad modulation factor) provides incremental improvement, confirming both modifications are independently beneficial.
- **Datasets**: MNIST (handwritten digits), CIFAR-10 (natural images), IARC (cervical cancer screening), AnnoCerv (cervical cell annotation).

## Critical Assessment

### Strengths

- **Principled design**: Each component directly addresses a known limitation (convergence via AMSGrad's max, stability via DiffGrad's modulation), with clear theoretical motivation.
- **Symmetric treatment**: The max operation is applied to both $v_t$ (squared gradients) and $d_t$ (squared gradient differences), which is a natural and elegant extension given AdaPID's symmetric use of both terms.
- **Comprehensive evaluation**: Covers both standard benchmarks and real-world medical imaging datasets, lending practical credibility.
- **Ablation study**: Demonstrates that each component contributes independently, ruling out the possibility that improvement comes from just one modification.

### Limitations

- **Incremental novelty**: The paper is primarily an engineering combination of three existing ideas (AdaPID + AMSGrad + DiffGrad). Each individual modification is a direct transplant from prior work, with no new theoretical analysis (e.g., convergence rate bounds) beyond what AMSGrad already provides.
- **No large-scale or modern architecture experiments**: Evaluation is limited to relatively small-scale datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10) and medical imaging tasks. No experiments on modern large-scale architectures (Transformers, large ResNets) or NLP tasks, which limits generalizability claims.
- **Missing comparison with recent optimizers**: The paper compares against AMSGrad, DiffGrad, and AdaPID but does not include comparisons with more recent and widely-used optimizers such as AdamW, LAMB, Lion, or Sophia.
- **Hyperparameter sensitivity not analyzed**: The paper presents "best-performing models obtained across multiple runs using different hyperparameter configurations" but does not analyze how sensitive IAdaPID-ADG is to its six hyperparameters ($\gamma, \beta, \eta, K_i, K_d, \sigma$).
- **No theoretical convergence proof for the combined optimizer**: While AMSGrad provides convergence guarantees for Adam, the paper does not extend or prove convergence guarantees for the full IAdaPID-ADG (which includes the additional DiffGrad modulation factor and PID structure).

## Relevance

This paper is relevant to researchers working on:
- **Optimization algorithms** for deep learning, particularly those interested in the intersection of control theory and adaptive methods.
- **Medical image analysis** practitioners who may benefit from more stable and faster-converging optimizers on domain-specific datasets.
- **Optimizer design** methodology, as a case study in how to systematically combine existing techniques to address specific failure modes.

The work is less relevant to practitioners using large-scale Transformer training, as the evaluation does not extend to those settings.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How does IAdaPID-ADG perform on large-scale Transformer pretraining (e.g., GPT-style or BERT-style models) where AdamW is the current standard?
2. What is the theoretical convergence rate of the full IAdaPID-ADG optimizer (including the sigmoid modulation factor), and does the modulation factor preserve AMSGrad's convergence guarantee?
3. How sensitive is IAdaPID-ADG to the choice of $K_i$ and $K_d$ gains? Is there an optimal scheduling strategy for these coefficients?
4. Could the DiffGrad modulation factor be replaced by a more principled adaptive mechanism (e.g., one derived from line search or trust-region methods)?
5. Does the running-maximum operation on $d_t$ (squared gradient differences) provide meaningful benefit beyond the running-maximum on $v_t$ alone, given that $d_t$ already captures change information?
