Key Contributions

  1. Five-stage methodology formalizing the complementarity of existing interpretability techniques (activation patching, SAE decomposition, causal ablation, robustness testing, deployment evaluation) into a disciplined pipeline
  2. Intermediate causal regime concept: features that look causally important by correlational standards are only partially causal. This is argued to be the typical case for real language models with redundant solutions
  3. Selectivity-causality anticorrelation (r = -0.56): the most strikingly selective SAE features (e.g., Sid at 321:1 selectivity) have the smallest causal effects, while moderately selective features (Jane, Tom, Mary at 9:1 to 19:1) have the largest causal effects
  4. Detection vs. causal robustness gap: under prompt frame reformulation ("When...went to" -> "After...arrived at...it was...who handed"), feature firing patterns survive (activation drops only 10-20%) but ablation effects drop by ~50% (mean 0.89 -> 0.42 logits)
  5. Fidelity stratification (NLA-inspired): the 15 name-selective features explain only 30.7% of activation variance at the critical layer/position, vs. the SAE's full reconstruction ceiling of 99.7%
  6. Three causal regimes hypothesized: (1) clean causal (features selective and ablation produces large effects, e.g., refusal directions), (2) selective-but-inert (correlational only), (3) intermediate (the IOI case)
  7. Cost-based deployment evaluation: under stated assumptions ($50/FN, $0.42/FP, 2% error rate), SAE-only at threshold 15 is optimal, with composition strategy sensitive to cost ratio and error base rate

Critical Assessment

**Novelty
Moderate.** Each individual technique (activation patching, SAE training, ablation, robustness testing) is well-established. The primary novelty is the systematic five-stage pipeline and the empirical findings about the intermediate causal regime. The selectivity-causality anticorrelation and the detection-vs-causal robustness gap are genuine contributions, though they may not surprise researchers who have worked extensively with SAEs.
**Rigor
Strong.** The pre-registration of seven hypotheses before experimentation is commendable and rare in interpretability work. The NLA-inspired fidelity and reliability stratifications provide quantitative grounding. The robustness testing across three distinct distribution shifts is thorough. The honest discussion of what the deployment evaluation is and is not (Section 12.4 "What this is and is not") is exemplary.
**Reproducibility
Excellent.** Public model (GPT-2 small), standard library (TransformerLens), CPU-only computation (~20 minutes total). A motivated researcher could replicate the entire paper in an afternoon. No code repository is mentioned in the abstract, but the implementation details are sufficiently specified.
**Significance
Moderate to High.** The detection-vs-causal robustness gap is an important cautionary finding for the field. The intermediate causal regime framing provides a useful conceptual tool. However, the generalizability question looms large — GPT-2 small on IOI is a well-studied toy setting, and whether these findings transfer to frontier models on complex tasks remains entirely open.
**Red Flags
**
Point 6
Single-author paper claiming a broad methodological framework based on one model and one task. The five stages are reasonable but not validated beyond this setting.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths
  1. Pre-registered hypotheses (H1-H7) provide a strong methodological template that increases trust in the empirical findings
  2. The selectivity-causality anticorrelation (r = -0.56) is a concrete, surprising finding with clear implications for SAE-based interpretability practice
  3. The detection-vs-causal robustness gap is empirically clean and operationally important: a feature that reliably fires may not reliably cause
  4. The fidelity stratification (31% vs 99.7% variance explained) quantifies a gap that is often discussed qualitatively but rarely measured
  5. Exceptional honesty about limitations — the author explicitly states what the deployment numbers are and are not, and identifies the small model/task/SAE as limitations
  6. The three-regime framework (clean causal, selective-but-inert, intermediate) provides a useful conceptual vocabulary
  7. All experiments reproducible on CPU in 20 minutes
Limitations
  1. Demonstrated only on GPT-2 small (124M params) performing IOI — whether the intermediate causal regime generalizes to larger models (billions of parameters) and more complex tasks (open-ended generation, multi-hop reasoning) is entirely unknown
  2. The SAE is deliberately small (1024 features, 3000 steps) and below the scale where monosemanticity peaks — some findings may reflect SAE limitations rather than model properties
  3. The NLA-inspired evaluations are reframings of existing SAE data, not actual NLA experiments — no NLA was trained or compared
  4. The deployment evaluation uses entirely synthetic cost parameters — the 99.1% savings figure should not be quoted out of context
  5. The held-out name experiment reveals that multi-token names fundamentally break the single-position SAE methodology, a limitation that applies to most real-world text
  6. The paper does not compare against other systematic interpretability pipelines (e.g., Automated Circuit Discovery, Sparse Feature Circuits) to validate that the five-stage approach produces strictly better findings

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does the selectivity-causality anticorrelation (r = -0.56) replicate with larger SAEs (e.g., 64k+ features as in Templeton et al. 2024) or with gated SAEs (Rajamanoharan et al. 2024)?
  2. At what model scale does the "intermediate causal regime" give way to either the clean causal or selective-but-inert regime, if at all?
  3. Can the detection-vs-causal robustness gap be bridged through better feature extraction, or is it a fundamental property of distributed representations?
  4. How do the findings change for tasks without a known circuit ground truth — i.e., where Stage 1 (probe design) cannot be validated against prior literature?
  5. Does the optimal deployment monitoring configuration change qualitatively when the monitored behavior is safety-relevant (e.g., deception, harmful content) rather than a simple name-identification error?
  6. What is the relationship between feature redundancy (many features each contributing small causal effects) and the model's generalization ability?
