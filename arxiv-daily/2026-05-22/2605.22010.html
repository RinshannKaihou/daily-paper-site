Methodology
### Problem Setting The paper studies one-hidden-layer neural networks parameterized by a distribution $\rho \in \mathcal{P}(\mathcal{S})$ where $\mathcal{S} = \mathbb{R}^d$ or $\mathbb{S}^{d-1}$: $$f_\rho(x) = \mathbb{E}_{w \sim \rho} \sigma(x; w)$$ trained via (projected) gradient descent on the excess MSE loss $\mathcal{L}(\rho) = \|f_\rho - f^*\|^2$. Two dynamics are compared: - Mean-field dynamics $\rho_t^{\text{MF}}$: infinite-width, infinite-data gradient flow (Wasserstein gradient flow) - Finite dynamics $\hat{\rho}_t^m$: $m$ neurons, $n$ samples, step size $\eta$ ### Key Assumption The core assumption is on the convergence rate of the mean-field dynamics: $\int_0^\infty \sqrt{\mathcal{L}_t} \, dt = O(\log d)$, meaning the loss must decay fast enough. Results & Highlights
- The paper provides the first non-asymptotic, uniform-in-time propagation of chaos result for deterministic (noiseless) gradient flow on shallow neural networks. - Previous uniform-in-time PoC results all required stochastic (Langevin) dynamics with noise enabling contraction via Log-Sobolev inequalities; this work achieves it in the deterministic setting. - The result applies to high-dimensional settings with a burn-in time of $O(\log d)$, relevant for single-index and multi-index models. - Empirical verification (Section 5): The authors verify on synthetic examples that the convergence rate assumption is met in practice when the target measure is sufficiently smooth. - The polynomial dependence on dimension $d$ is explicit, avoiding exponential dependencies that plague some
