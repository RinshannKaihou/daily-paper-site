Methodology
### Problem Formulation The paper frames RLIF as training LLMs with GRPO where rewards come entirely from the model's own signals rather than external ground truth. The key insight is that single-reward RLIF exhibits Goodhart's Law: optimizing a single proxy leads to reward hacking rather than genuine reasoning improvement. ### Two Complementary Internal Rewards - Cluster Voting (Answer-Level): For each prompt, sample G completions, extract final answers, cluster by equivalence, and reward completions whose answer belongs to the largest cluster. Critical Assessment

**Principled and well-motivated design**
The complementarity argument is compelling — cluster voting constrains answer correctness while self-certainty constrains reasoning structure, and each plugs the other's degeneracy.
**Thorough ablation study**
The single-reward decomposition (Figure 3) cleanly demonstrates the distinct failure modes of each reward and how multi-reward delays but does not fully prevent collapse, motivating KL-Cov.
**Strong quantitative results**
Near-supervised performance on math benchmarks and surpassing supervised methods on some code benchmarks is impressive for a fully unsupervised approach.
**KL-Cov is well-targeted**
Rather than a blanket KL penalty, targeting only the top 2% covariance tokens is elegant and empirically shown to be robust to coefficient choice across a 200x range.
**Honest about limitations**
The authors acknowledge that multi-reward alone still eventually collapses, and that KL-Cov is necessary rather than optional.
**Alpha/beta weighting not systematically studied**
The authors use alpha=beta=0.5 throughout without exploring asymmetric weighting, which could yield further gains.
