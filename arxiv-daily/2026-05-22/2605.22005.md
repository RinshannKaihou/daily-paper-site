# Check Your LLM's Secret Dictionary! Five Lines of Code Reveal What Your LLM Learned (Including What It Shouldn't Have)

## 一句话总结

仅用5行PyTorch对LLM的lm_head权重矩阵做SVD分解，无需模型推理即可揭示模型的训练数据构成、安全隐患和故障token，发现RLHF后训练对齐无法消除预训练中已形成的伦理风险词汇子空间。

## TL;DR

The authors show that singular value decomposition (SVD) of a transformer LLM's `lm_head` weight matrix -- requiring only five lines of PyTorch and no model inference -- reveals interpretable semantic subspaces. Each left singular vector identifies vocabulary tokens most readily selected along that direction. Analyzing GPT-OSS-120B, Gemma-2-2B, and Qwen2.5-1.5B, they find systematic differences: GPT exhibits a graduated hierarchy of functionally differentiated subspaces; Gemma is dominated by pre-19th-century English orthography; Qwen shows broad multilingual coverage with ethically concerning subspaces. Crucially, these problematic subspaces originate in pretraining and survive RLHF alignment. They introduce the Vocabulary Cluster Score (VCS) to quantify subspace coherence and the Weighted Projection Score (WPS) as a static glitch token detector, recovering the well-known glitch token "shokubutsu-hyakka-tsu" (ID 137606) in GPT-OSS-120B without inference.

## 核心贡献

1. **lm_head SVD 方法论**: 提出仅5行PyTorch代码对`lm_head`权重矩阵进行SVD分解的方法，无需模型推理或输入数据，即可静态揭示模型的词汇选择偏好和训练数据构成。

2. **Vocabulary Cluster Score (VCS)**: 引入词汇聚类得分来量化子空间一致性，可用于指导tokenizer优化——保留高VCS token、剪枝低VCS token可产生更小、几何一致性更强的词表。

3. **Weighted Projection Score (WPS)**: 定义 $\text{WPS}(v) = \sum_k S[k] \cdot |U[v, k]|$，用于无推理检测故障token（glitch token）。在GPT-OSS-120B上成功恢复了CJK社区已知的glitch token "物哲科通"（ID 137606）。

4. **Base-Instruct对比揭示对齐局限**: 直接追踪了base模型和instruction-tuned变体之间`lm_head`奇异向量的token级变化，首次提供直接证据表明伦理风险子空间源于预训练，且RLHF无法消除。

5. **安全审计建议**: 提出问题词汇内容的根本原因分类法（taxonomy），呼吁将`lm_head` SVD分析作为模型发布前的标准安全审计步骤。

## Key Contributions

1. **lm_head SVD Methodology**: Demonstrates that SVD of the `lm_head` weight matrix (5 lines of PyTorch, no inference) statically reveals a model's vocabulary selection preferences and training data composition.

2. **Vocabulary Cluster Score (VCS)**: A quantitative measure of subspace coherence that can guide tokenizer optimization -- iteratively retaining high-VCS tokens and pruning low-VCS ones yields smaller, more geometrically coherent vocabularies.

3. **Weighted Projection Score (WPS)**: A static, inference-free glitch token detector. Applied to GPT-OSS-120B, it recovers the well-known glitch token in the CJK community without any model forward pass.

4. **Base-Instruct Comparison**: First direct token-level evidence tracking `lm_head` singular vector changes between base and instruction-tuned variants, showing ethically concerning subspaces originate in pretraining and persist through RLHF.

5. **Safety Auditing Framework**: Proposes a taxonomy of root causes for problematic vocabulary content and calls for `lm_head` SVD analysis as a standard pre-release safety auditing step.

## Methodology

**Core approach**: Given `lm_head` weight matrix $W \in \mathbb{R}^{V \times d}$, compute the economy SVD $W = U S V^\top$, where $U \in \mathbb{R}^{V \times d}$ contains left singular vectors, $S \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ holds singular values, and $V^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ contains right singular vectors.

**Key insight**: Because RMSNorm normalizes the hidden state onto the unit sphere before the `lm_head` projection, only directional structure matters. The singular directions are thus the fundamental axes of vocabulary selection. Each left singular vector $U[:, i]$ identifies tokens most readily selected when the hidden state aligns with the $i$-th singular direction.

**Models analyzed**:
- **GPT-OSS-120B**: Graduated hierarchy of functionally differentiated subspaces (syntactic structure, attribute taxonomy, formal specification vocabulary, software engineering terminology) with gentle singular value decay.
- **Gemma-2-2B**: Dominated by pre-19th-century English orthography (long-s typography), forming a stepwise clustering structure in the singular value spectrum that may contribute to high output controllability.
- **Qwen2.5-1.5B**: Script-level clustering with geometrically dispersed subspaces spanning unrelated languages and domains, including ethically concerning subspaces.

**Analytical tools**:
- Singular value spectrum analysis reveals training data composition and curation philosophy.
- Leiden community detection applied to left singular vector top-k token sets to identify vocabulary clusters.
- VCS quantifies how coherent each singular vector's top-k vocabulary cluster is.
- WPS identifies geometrically marginalized tokens as glitch token candidates.

**Base-Instruct comparison**: Compares singular vector structure between base and instruction-tuned variants of Qwen2.5-1.5B and Gemma-2-2B. Gemma's `lm_head` structure is virtually unchanged by RLHF; Qwen undergoes substantial remapping of symbol clusters to multilingual lexical clusters. Ethically concerning subspaces persist in both variants.

## Results & Highlights

- **Model fingerprints differ systematically**: GPT-OSS-120B shows a graduated hierarchy; Gemma-2-2B is dominated by archaic English orthography; Qwen2.5-1.5B shows broad multilingual coverage with concerning subspaces.

- **RLHF alignment does not fix pretraining problems**: Ethically concerning vocabulary subspaces present in base models persist through instruction tuning with comparable structure. This is theoretically supported by Davis-Kahan bound from matrix perturbation theory -- RL updates only 5-30% of parameters (sparse subnetworks), so a small-norm perturbation is unlikely to rotate dominant singular vectors.

- **Glitch token detection without inference**: WPS successfully identifies the glitch token "物哲科通" (ID 137606) in GPT-OSS-120B, a well-known glitch token in the CJK language community, without any model forward pass.

- **Gemma's stepwise spectrum**: The stepwise clustering structure in Gemma's singular value spectrum may contribute to its high output controllability -- an intriguing structural observation.

- **Problematic subspaces arise from over-representation, not under-representation**: Unlike glitch tokens (under-represented in pretraining), ethically concerning vocabulary clusters appear in upper eigenvectors with substantial singular values, implying over-representation.

## Critical Assessment

### Strengths

- **Elegant simplicity**: The method genuinely requires only 5 lines of PyTorch and no inference -- an unusually low barrier to adoption for a safety auditing tool.
- **Directly actionable**: Unlike many interpretability papers that are interesting but hard to operationalize, this provides a concrete auditing procedure with quantifiable metrics (VCS, WPS).
- **Compelling empirical evidence**: The base-instruct comparison showing RLHF's failure to remove problematic subspaces is the first direct token-level evidence of this phenomenon.
- **Practical glitch token detection**: Recovering a known glitch token statically is a convincing proof-of-concept.
- **Well-connected to theory**: The Davis-Kahan bound provides theoretical grounding for why RLHF doesn't alter singular vector structure.
- **Responsible disclosure**: The authors chose not to directly publish ethically inappropriate vocabulary found in Qwen, demonstrating ethical awareness.

### Limitations

- **Single-author work**: The breadth of claims across multiple models and domains could benefit from additional validation and peer review from a larger team.
- **Limited model coverage**: Only three models analyzed (GPT-OSS-120B, Gemma-2-2B, Qwen2.5-1.5B). Generalization to other architectures (e.g., Llama, Mistral, Claude) is unverified.
- **No downstream task evaluation**: The paper shows SVD reveals interesting structure but does not directly demonstrate that fixing identified issues improves downstream safety metrics.
- **Threshold ambiguity**: VCS and WPS require thresholds for practical use, but clear guidelines for setting these thresholds are not established.
- **Ethically concerning content not published**: While responsible, this also makes independent verification of the safety claims difficult.
- **GPT-OSS-120B is not a mainstream model**: The model analyzed is relatively obscure, which may limit the generalizability of glitch token findings.

## Relevance

This paper is highly relevant to:
- **LLM safety and alignment research**: Provides a new tool for pre-release safety auditing that complements adversarial red-teaming.
- **Model interpretability**: Offers a static, inference-free window into what models have learned, connecting to Logit Lens and related mechanistic interpretability work.
- **Tokenizer design**: VCS-guided tokenizer optimization could lead to more efficient and safer vocabularies.
- **AI governance and regulation**: The call for `lm_head` SVD analysis as a standard auditing step has direct policy implications for model release requirements.
- **Training data auditing**: The method reveals training data composition at the vocabulary level, relevant to data provenance and copyright concerns.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How well does WPS generalize to other models beyond GPT-OSS-120B for glitch token detection? What is the false positive/negative rate compared to inference-based methods?

2. Can VCS-guided tokenizer pruning actually improve downstream task performance or safety metrics in a controlled experiment?

3. What happens when we apply SVD analysis to `lm_head` matrices of models trained with explicit safety training (e.g., Constitutional AI)? Do they show systematically different singular vector structures?

4. The paper notes that RLHF updates only 5-30% of parameters. Would targeted fine-tuning specifically on the `lm_head` layer be more effective at removing problematic subspaces?

5. How does the method scale to models with vocabulary sizes beyond 200K tokens (e.g., some multilingual models)? Does the computational cost remain practical?

6. Can the SVD analysis be extended to intermediate layer weight matrices to reveal deeper structural properties of the model's knowledge representation?
