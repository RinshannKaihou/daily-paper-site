# Two is better than one: A Collapse-free Multi-Reward RLIF Training Framework

## 一句话总结

本文提出了一种多奖励 RLIF 框架，通过结合聚类投票的答案级奖励和基于自确定性的完成级奖励，并引入 KL-Cov 正则化，解决了单奖励 RLIF 方法中的奖励黑客、熵崩塌和推理结构退化问题，在无需外部监督的情况下逼近有监督 RLVR 的性能。

## TL;DR

Existing single-reward RLIF (Reinforcement Learning from Internal Feedback) methods suffer from reward hacking and entropy collapse. This paper proposes a multi-reward RLIF framework combining cluster-voting answer-level rewards with token-wise self-certainty completion-level rewards, normalized via GDPO and regularized with novel KL-Cov, achieving performance close to supervised RLVR without external ground truth.

## 核心贡献

1. **多奖励 RLIF 框架**：将训练信号分解为两个互补组件——基于聚类投票的答案级奖励（评估最终答案的一致性）和基于 token 级自确定性的完成级奖励（评估推理过程的置信度）。
2. **GDPO 归一化**：对每个奖励通道独立进行 z-score 归一化，避免因尺度差异导致某一信号主导优化。实验表明简单的合并后归一化会让自确定性通道的梯度占据主导（约一个数量级），有效屏蔽聚类投票通道。
3. **KL-Cov 正则化**：计算 token 对数概率与优势值的协方差，识别对熵崩塌贡献最大的前 2% token，仅对这些 token 施加有针对性的 KL 惩罚，其余 ~98% token 使用标准 GRPO 裁剪更新，从而精准抑制崩塌同时保留探索能力。
4. **无监督逼近监督方法**：在 5 个数学推理和 2 个代码生成基准上，展示了接近有监督 RLVR 方法的性能，且训练稳定性大幅优于现有无监督方法。

## Key Contributions

1. **Multi-Reward RLIF Framework**: Decomposes training signals into two complementary components — cluster-voting-based answer-level reward (evaluating final answer consistency) and token-wise self-certainty-based completion-level reward (evaluating reasoning confidence).
2. **GDPO-based Per-Channel Normalization**: Independently z-scores each reward channel to prevent scale-induced dominance. Empirical gradient diagnostics show naive combine-then-normalize lets self-certainty dominate gradients by ~10x, silencing the cluster channel.
3. **KL-Cov Regularization**: Computes covariance between token log-probabilities and advantages to identify the top 2% of tokens most responsible for entropy collapse, applying a targeted KL penalty only to those tokens while leaving ~98% under standard clipped GRPO updates. This precisely suppresses collapse without hampering exploration.
4. **Closing the Gap with Supervised Methods**: Achieves near-supervised RLVR performance across 5 math reasoning and 2 code generation benchmarks, with substantially better training stability than existing unsupervised methods.

## Methodology

### Problem Formulation

The paper frames RLIF as training LLMs with GRPO where rewards come entirely from the model's own signals rather than external ground truth. The key insight is that single-reward RLIF exhibits Goodhart's Law: optimizing a single proxy leads to reward hacking rather than genuine reasoning improvement.

### Two Complementary Internal Rewards

- **Cluster Voting (Answer-Level)**: For each prompt, sample G completions, extract final answers, cluster by equivalence, and reward completions whose answer belongs to the largest cluster. This measures answer consensus but is invariant to the reasoning path — vulnerable to answer-space drift.
- **Self-Certainty (Completion-Level)**: Measures the model's token-level confidence via average KL divergence from a uniform distribution. This rewards confident reasoning but is invariant to answer correctness — vulnerable to template-mode collapse.

### Reward Combination via GDPO

Each reward channel k is normalized independently within the group of G completions:

```
R_k_normalized(g) = (R_k(g) - mean_k) / (std_k + epsilon)
```

The combined advantage is: `A_g = alpha * R_cluster_normalized + beta * R_cert_normalized`, with alpha = beta = 0.5.

### KL-Cov Regularization

1. Compute covariance between token log-probabilities and rollout advantages for each token.
2. Select the top k=0.02 (2%) highest-covariance tokens.
3. For high-covariance tokens: replace GRPO clipping with a targeted KL penalty against the reference policy (absolute log-importance-ratio).
4. For remaining ~98% tokens: use standard clipped GRPO surrogate.

### Training Setup

- Base models: Qwen2.5-1.5B-Base and Qwen2.5-3B-Base
- Training data: MATH training split (unlabeled prompts only)
- Optimizer: GRPO with lr=3e-6, cosine schedule
- 8 rollouts per prompt, max completion length 3072 tokens
- GRPO KL penalty gamma=0.005
- Hardware: 4x NVIDIA GH200 120GB GPUs
- 40 epochs (340 steps), batch size 842 prompts/step

## Results & Highlights

### Main Results (Table 1, Step 160 / Epoch 18)

**Qwen2.5-1.5B:**
| Method | GSM8K | MATH500 | MMLU-Pro | LCB v6 | CRUXEval-O |
|--------|-------|---------|----------|--------|------------|
| GRPO-GT (supervised) | 74.9 | 55.2 | 31.6 | 1.7 | 24.9 |
| INTUITOR | 22.4 | 22.6 | 24.5 | 0.8 | 12.6 |
| Multi-reward | **69.5** | **46.0** | **27.7** | **3.3** | **21.8** |
| Multi-reward + KL-Cov | 68.3 | 51.6 | 29.9 | 2.6 | 20.5 |

**Qwen2.5-3B:**
| Method | GSM8K | MATH500 | MMLU-Pro | LCB v6 | CRUXEval-O |
|--------|-------|---------|----------|--------|------------|
| GRPO-GT (supervised) | 84.9 | 64.4 | 40.3 | 6.4 | 41.0 |
| INTUITOR | 73.3 | 55.0 | 33.1 | 6.6 | 36.1 |
| Multi-reward | 82.8 | 60.0 | 39.3 | 4.4 | 41.0 |
| Multi-reward + KL-Cov | 80.7 | 62.4 | 39.0 | **7.7** | 38.3 |

### Key Findings

- **Multi-reward + KL-Cov vs. supervised**: On 1.5B, trails GRPO-GT by only 6.6 pp on GSM8K, 3.6 pp on MATH500, and 1.7 pp on MMLU-Pro. On 3B, the gap narrows further.
- **Training stability**: Multi-reward + KL-Cov retains 89-93% of peak performance on 1.5B and 95-99% on 3B at step 340, while INTUITOR collapses to near-zero accuracy by step 60-100.
- **Complementary failure modes**: Cluster-only collapses via answer-space drift (rising entropy, lengthening completions). Self-certainty collapses via token-mode collapse (entropy drops to 0.03-0.17 nats, length shrinks to ~100 tokens).
- **KL-Cov robustness**: Beta_cov sweep over {0.0005, 0.05, 0.1} (200x range) shows all settings maintain stable accuracy and completion length through step 280.
- **OOD generalization**: Math-trained reasoning transfers to code benchmarks without any code-domain training. On 3B LCB v6, multi-reward + KL-Cov achieves 7.7%, surpassing even supervised GRPO-GT (6.4%).

## Critical Assessment

### Strengths

- **Principled and well-motivated design**: The complementarity argument is compelling — cluster voting constrains answer correctness while self-certainty constrains reasoning structure, and each plugs the other's degeneracy.
- **Thorough ablation study**: The single-reward decomposition (Figure 3) cleanly demonstrates the distinct failure modes of each reward and how multi-reward delays but does not fully prevent collapse, motivating KL-Cov.
- **Strong quantitative results**: Near-supervised performance on math benchmarks and surpassing supervised methods on some code benchmarks is impressive for a fully unsupervised approach.
- **KL-Cov is well-targeted**: Rather than a blanket KL penalty, targeting only the top 2% covariance tokens is elegant and empirically shown to be robust to coefficient choice across a 200x range.
- **Honest about limitations**: The authors acknowledge that multi-reward alone still eventually collapses, and that KL-Cov is necessary rather than optional.

### Limitations

- **Alpha/beta weighting not systematically studied**: The authors use alpha=beta=0.5 throughout without exploring asymmetric weighting, which could yield further gains.
- **Dependent on capable base models**: The framework requires a base model with sufficient initial reasoning ability. Results on 1.5B are noticeably weaker than 3B, and the paper does not study at what point base model capability becomes insufficient.
- **Code benchmarks near floor on 1.5B**: LCB v6 scores are 0.8-3.3% for all methods on 1.5B, making comparisons there unreliable.
- **Single training domain**: All training uses MATH prompts only; the framework is not tested with diverse training domains, which could affect cluster voting quality.
- **No code released yet**: Reproducibility cannot be independently verified.
- **Computational cost of cluster voting**: Generating multiple completions per prompt and clustering them adds overhead that is not quantified.

## Relevance

- **Highly relevant** for researchers working on LLM reasoning, RLHF/RLAIF, and unsupervised/self-supervised training of language models.
- Directly addresses the practical problem of training collapse in RL for reasoning, which is a widespread issue in the post-DeepSeek-R1 era.
- The KL-Cov technique is potentially applicable beyond RLIF to any RL training setup where entropy collapse is a concern.
- Relevant to anyone seeking to eliminate the dependency on costly human annotations or verified solutions for RLVR.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How does the framework perform with asymmetric alpha/beta weighting (e.g., emphasizing answer quality vs. reasoning confidence differently per domain)?
2. Can additional internal rewards (beyond two) further improve stability, or does the marginal benefit diminish quickly?
3. How well does the approach transfer when training on diverse multi-domain prompts rather than MATH-only?
4. What is the minimum base model capability required for the framework to show gains? Is there a scaling law relationship?
5. How does KL-Cov compare computationally to simpler alternatives like adaptive entropy bonuses or curriculum-based KL annealing?
6. Can the cluster voting reward be improved with semantic similarity rather than exact answer matching for open-ended generation tasks?
