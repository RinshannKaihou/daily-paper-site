Methodology
The I-SAFE framework operates through the following pipeline: 1. Input decomposition and structural prior. Inputs are decomposed into identifiable components (e.g., protein sequence residues). An external structural prior P(t) (KLIFS binding-pocket annotations) identifies domain-relevant components independently of the model. 2. Matched perturbation pairs. For each input, two interventions are constructed: - Mechanistic perturbation: applies the perturbation operator to prior-selected components (KLIFS pocket residues). - Spurious perturbation: applies the same operator with identical cardinality to components outside the prior (non-pocket residues). - Operators include masking (replacing with mask token) and physicochemically constrained substitution (replacing

- Absolute metrics (Table 1): Under mechanistic perturbations, TAPB shows the strongest coherence with lowest QBM (0.515) and WCM (0.443). DeepDTA shows the least coherent response with highest QBM (0.842) and WCM (0.768). DeepConvDTI lies in between (QBM: 0.737, WCM: 0.512). Key Contributions

  1. Propose I-SAFE, a model-agnostic post-hoc distributional auditing framework that evaluates how raw model output distributions reorganize under structurally guided perturbations, rather than just measuring scalar sensitivity.
  2. Define three complementary metrics grounded in optimal transport theory: QBM (quantile-level location coherence), WCM (ordinal coherence via optimal pairing), and TI-WCM (translation-invariant shape coherence removing mean shifts).
  3. Introduce matched perturbation pair design (mechanistic vs. spurious) where both interventions share the same operator and cardinality, differing only in alignment with the structural prior.
  4. Demonstrate that among three DTI models with comparable AUROC (~0.876-0.907), only TAPB shows positive prior-relative contrasts on both QBM (+0.093) and WCM (+0.063), with the signal localized to location and ranking rather than shape preservation.
  5. Provide formal proofs that all metrics are normalized to [0,1], WCM is scale-invariant, and TI-WCM upper-bounds WCM.

Critical Assessment

**Novelty
** The shift from scalar sensitivity auditing to full distributional auditing via optimal transport is a genuine methodological advance. The three-metric decomposition (location, ordinal, shape) is well-motivated and provides richer diagnostic information than prior work. The connection to Wasserstein distance theory is natural and well-exploited.
**Rigor
** The formal definitions, propositions, and proofs are clean and rigorous. All metrics are normalized to [0,1] with clear interpretive meaning. The matched perturbation design controls for operator and cardinality. Confidence intervals are computed across 5 seeds with non-parametric bootstrap. However, the paper does not establish formal statistical tests (e.g., hypothesis testing framework) for the contrasts.
**Reproducibility
** Strong. The supplemental archive includes per-seed audit outputs, and the reproduction pipeline runs in ~17 minutes without GPU. All models are audited from frozen checkpoints with publicly available training protocols. KLIFS annotations are a public database.
**Significance
** The core finding -- that comparable AUROC masks fundamentally different structural behaviors -- is important for the scientific ML community. However, the current evidence is limited to one task domain and one structural prior. Whether the framework generalizes broadly remains to be demonstrated.
**Red flags
** (1) The framework requires a structural prior, which creates a dependency on domain-specific knowledge that may be unavailable or incomplete. (2) The paper audits only target-side interventions with drug held fixed; joint or drug-side interventions are left to future work. (3) The auditing set has significant class imbalance (5.1% positive pairs), which may affect metric behavior but is not discussed. (4) The prior-relative interpretation does not establish causal validity, which the authors acknowledge but could be more prominently flagged.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths
  1. Principled theoretical grounding in optimal transport with clean formalization and proofs.
  2. Three complementary metrics provide multi-axis diagnostic information unavailable from scalar summaries.
  3. The matched perturbation design rigorously isolates the role of prior alignment.
  4. Model-agnostic and requires only black-box access -- no internal representations needed.
  5. Computationally efficient post-hoc audit (17 minutes on laptop, no GPU required).
  6. Clear separation between predictive performance and distributional coherence as distinct evaluation dimensions.
  7. The finding that TAPB's signal is localized to location/ranking (not shape) demonstrates the diagnostic value of the multi-metric decomposition.
Limitations
  1. Requires external structural priors, limiting applicability in domains without curated domain knowledge.
  2. Demonstrated on only one task domain (DTI on Davis benchmark) with one structural prior (KLIFS).
  3. Only target-side perturbations are audited; drug-side and joint interventions are not explored.
  4. The auditing set has severe class imbalance (5.1% positive, 94.9% negative), which may bias the output distributions being compared.
  5. No formal hypothesis testing framework for the prior-relative contrasts; interpretation relies on confidence interval overlap.
  6. The perturbation operators (masking, constrained substitution) are relatively simple; more sophisticated domain-specific perturbations could yield different results.
  7. QBM sensitivity to quantile grid choice is acknowledged but only briefly explored in appendix.
