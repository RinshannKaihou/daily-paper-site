Represented Is Not Computed: A Causal Test of Candidate Algorithmic Intermediates in a Transformer

Methodology

Training shuffles N/B/D field order across all 6 permutations, forcing reliance on field tags rather than position. Architecture. Key Contributions

  1. Introduces base-digit extraction as a clean mechanistic interpretability testbed with explicit closed-form intermediates: $\lfloor N/B^D \rfloor \bmod B$
  2. Trains 10-layer decoder-only Transformers (3 seeds) to 99.83% exact-answer accuracy on held-out number-base intersections (N in {0,...,999}, B in {2,...,30})
  3. Shows linear probes decode closed-form intermediates with high R^2: B^D from D_ones at layer 0 (R^2=1.00), quotient-like quantities at layer 2 (R^2=0.96), final answer from output stream at layer 9 (R^2=0.94)
  4. Demonstrates via cumulative attention ablation that output behavior is most sensitive to early D_ones communication (layers 0-1), not the later layers where quotient-like quantities peak
  5. Uses key/value patching to show the D_ones-to-output route carries D-selective information exclusively: N or B donor changes have zero behavioral effect, while D donor changes flip behavior almost perfectly
  6. Sparse circuit search (greedy right-to-left) finds mostly factorized N, B, D routes (94.44% IoU across seeds) that converge only at output streams, retaining 28.33% of relations and 20.25% of layer-edges while preserving 93.40% accuracy
  7. Initialization probes reveal substantial pre-training decodability of intermediates, showing high R^2 need not mark a learned causal intermediate

Critical Assessment

**Novelty
High.** The paper makes a clean, memorable contribution: "represented is not computed." While the general warning about probes vs. causality exists in the literature (Belinkov 2022, Elazar et al. 2021), this paper demonstrates it with unusual clarity in a setting where the algorithmic ground truth is known exactly. The dissociation is quantitative and striking -- probes show R^2=0.96 for quotient-like quantities that the causal route demonstrably ignores.
**Rigor
Very strong.** Three independent seeds, held-out intersections (not just examples), all six input permutations during training, cumulative ablation sweeps in both directions, patching with matched source sets across conditions, initialization baselines, threshold sensitivity analysis, and a smaller companion model to test scale dependence. The 95% bootstrap CIs (over 3 seeds) are reported throughout. The task is precisely defined and the closed-form solution eliminates ambiguity about what the "right" algorithm is.
**Reproducibility
Excellent.** Task defined by closed-form formula, small model (10-layer, width 384), code publicly available. The arithmetic setting eliminates the ambiguity that plagues interpretability work on language.
**Significance
High for mechanistic interpretability.** This is a cautionary methodological paper that directly challenges a common interpretability workflow (probe first, conclude algorithm). The clean dissociation makes the argument hard to dismiss.
**Potential concerns
**
**Scale limitation
** 10-layer, width-384 model. Whether the same probe-causality divergence occurs in larger models (e.g., 7B-parameter LLMs) remains unknown, though the 5-layer companion showing the same pattern is encouraging.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths
  1. Exceptionally clean experimental design: closed-form ground truth, held-out intersections, permutation-invariant training
  2. Multi-method triangulation: probes, ablations, patching, and circuit search all converge on the same conclusion
  3. Initialization probes are a clever control that strengthens the argument substantially -- showing some intermediates are decodable before training undermines the "training created this representation" narrative
  4. The D-selectivity patching result is particularly convincing: 0% source-exact / 99.84% donor-exact for D-change vs. 100% source-exact for N or B changes
  5. Honest about what is and is not claimed: "the claim is deliberately narrower than saying that the model never implements the closed-form computation"
  6. Draws a clear analogy to cognitive neuroscience (decoding vs. perturbation)
Limitations
  1. Small model scale (10 layers, 384 width) -- generalization to practical-scale models is uncertain
  2. Single task type (arithmetic) -- the divergence pattern may not hold for tasks where the algorithmic structure is less clean
  3. Output-side computation remains a black box; the paper shows what arrives at O[0] and O[1] but not how they compute the answer
  4. Only 3 seeds -- the bootstrap CIs are descriptive, not inferential
  5. The "represented is not computed" framing, while memorable, applies specifically to the D_ones-to-output route and the closed-form intermediates tested; it does not rule out that other routes or nonlinear intermediates play a causal role

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does the probe-causality divergence persist in LLMs (e.g., 7B+ models) performing arithmetic, or is it specific to small toy models?
  2. What is the output-side computation? How do O[0] and O[1] combine the separate N, B, D inputs to produce the correct two-digit answer?
  3. Can the initialization-probe finding (high decodability before training) be predicted theoretically from the architecture, tokenization scheme, and data distribution?
  4. Would training with explicit intermediate supervision (e.g., forcing the model to output B^D as an auxiliary target) make the staged route causally effective?
  5. How does this relate to Nikankin et al. (2024), which found LLMs use "a bag of heuristics" rather than algorithms for arithmetic? Are these two faces of the same phenomenon?
  6. Is the factorized N/B/D routing pattern a general feature of multi-input integration in Transformers, or specific to this task structure?
