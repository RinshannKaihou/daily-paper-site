Results & Highlights
- State-of-the-art attack success across 15 models, including instruction-tuned (LLaMA-2, Qwen, Mistral, Gemma), multimodal (LLaVA), and reasoning models. - Outperforms all baselines: Surpasses DiM ablation, Concept Cones, Multiple Directions, and specialized per-prompt jailbreak attacks (GCG, SAA). - CLE-A > CLE-P: The additive variant consistently outperforms the projective variant, demonstrating that continuous per-token reprojection is suboptimal -- a single prompt-dependent perturbation is sufficient. - Ablation insights: Performance is driven by the combination of accurate probes, layer selection, and margin optimization. Critical Assessment

**Principled theoretical contribution**
The reframing from "refusal direction ablation" to "latent-space evasion attack" is elegant and provides genuine explanatory power. Showing the exact correspondence between DiM ablation and DeepFool's minimum-confidence attack is a clean, impactful insight.
**Strong empirical coverage**
Evaluation across 15 models spanning instruction-tuned, multimodal, and reasoning architectures demonstrates broad applicability.
**Actionable insight for safety**
By clearly characterizing the vulnerability (linear separability of refusal), the work points toward concrete defenses -- alignment procedures that discourage linear separability.
**Surprising finding that CLE-A outperforms CLE-P**
The discovery that a single fixed perturbation suffices over per-token reprojection is counterintuitive and practically significant (lower computational cost, stronger attack).
**Well-connected to prior art**
The recasting of multiple prior methods (DiM, Concept Cones, MD) within a unified evasion framework is thorough and illuminating.
**Linear probe assumption**
The theoretical framework relies on the linear separability of refused vs. answered representations. While this held across all tested models, it may not hold for future models with different alignment procedures. The authors acknowledge this but do not test it empirically.
