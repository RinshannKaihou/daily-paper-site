Relevance
This paper is relevant to researchers working on: - Optimization algorithms for deep learning, particularly those interested in the intersection of control theory and adaptive methods. - Medical image analysis practitioners who may benefit from more stable and faster-converging optimizers on domain-specific datasets. - Optimizer design
Methodology
### Background The paper builds on three optimizer families: - Adam (Algorithm 2): Computes exponential moving averages of gradients ($m_t$) and squared gradients ($v_t$), uses bias-corrected estimates to produce per-parameter adaptive learning rates. Addresses noisy/varying gradients but has convergence issues. - PID Optimizer (Algorithm 3): Uses integral term $I_t = \gamma I_{t-1} - \eta g_t$ and derivative term $D_t = \gamma D_{t-1} + (1-\gamma)(g_t - g_{t-1})$ to regulate updates via control theory. Addresses overshoot but uses a fixed global learning rate. - AdaPID (Algorithm 4): Combines Adam's adaptiveness with PID's control structure. Update rule: $w_t = w_{t-1} - \eta \left( \frac{K_i I_t}{\sqrt{\hat{v}_t} + \sigma} + \frac{K_d D_t}{\sqrt{\hat{d}_t} + \sigma} \righ
Results & Highlights
- Training loss: IAdaPID-ADG achieves training losses "multiple orders of magnitude lower" than all competing optimizers (AMSGrad, DiffGrad, AdaPID) across all four datasets. - Training accuracy: Significantly higher than all baselines on all datasets. - Test accuracy: Consistently outperforms all baselines, with more stable accuracy curves. - Ablation study (MNIST): Each added component (AMSGrad max operation, DiffGrad modulation factor) provides incremental improvement, confirming both modifications are independently beneficial. - Datasets: MNIST (handwritten digits), CIFAR-10 (natural images), IARC (cervical cancer screening), AnnoCerv (cervical cell annotation).

Critical Assessment

**Principled design**
Each component directly addresses a known limitation (convergence via AMSGrad's max, stability via DiffGrad's modulation), with clear theoretical motivation.
**Symmetric treatment**
The max operation is applied to both $v_t$ (squared gradients) and $d_t$ (squared gradient differences), which is a natural and elegant extension given AdaPID's symmetric use of both terms.
**Comprehensive evaluation**
Covers both standard benchmarks and real-world medical imaging datasets, lending practical credibility.
**Ablation study**
Demonstrates that each component contributes independently, ruling out the possibility that improvement comes from just one modification.
**Incremental novelty**
The paper is primarily an engineering combination of three existing ideas (AdaPID + AMSGrad + DiffGrad). Each individual modification is a direct transplant from prior work, with no new theoretical analysis (e.g., convergence rate bounds) beyond what AMSGrad already provides.
**No large-scale or modern architecture experiments**
Evaluation is limited to relatively small-scale datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10) and medical imaging tasks. No experiments on modern large-scale architectures (Transformers, large ResNets) or NLP tasks, which limits generalizability claims.
