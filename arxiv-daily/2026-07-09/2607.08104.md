> **一句话总结：** 本文放弃经典的 L-平滑+有界方差框架，改用 Hölder 连续梯度(ν∈(0,1])与"仅 p 阶矩有界"(p∈(1,2])的重尾噪声假设，首次证明不含任何梯度裁剪/归一化的 vanilla 带动量 SGD 在强凸、凸、非凸三类目标上均依期望收敛；关键约束为 ν+1≤p，非凸已知 p 时的速率为 O(T^{-(p-1)/2p})，对比带动量 normalized SGD 的最优 O(T^{-(p-1)/(3p-2)}) 慢一档，从而把"最朴素"的动量优化器刻画为未来更复杂算法必须超越的基线。

> **TL;DR:** This theory paper proves, for the first time, that vanilla SGD with momentum — with neither gradient clipping nor normalization — converges in expectation under heavy-tailed noise (stochastic gradient with only a bounded p-th moment, p∈(1,2]) for strongly convex, convex, and nonconvex objectives. The trick is to abandon classical L-smoothness in favor of Hölder-continuous gradients (parameter ν∈(0,1]); convergence holds whenever ν+1≤p. For nonconvex functions the rate is O(T^{-(p-1)/2p}) (known p) or O(T^{-(p-1)/4}) (unknown p), strictly slower than the optimal O(T^{-(p-1)/(3p-2)}) of normalized SGD with momentum [Liu & Zhou, 2025], but it holds without the bounded-gradient assumption that Fatkhullin et al. [2025] need. Heatmaps over 100 (ν,α) pairs on synthetic Pareto-noise functions and a WikiText-2 / Transformer-XL run confirm the ν+1≤p boundary and that normalized SGD with momentum reaches lower loss faster (best lr 0.005 vs vanilla's 0.1).

## 核心贡献

- **首次刻画带重尾噪声的 vanilla 动量 SGD 收敛性：** 在不使用任何梯度裁剪或归一化的前提下，给出强凸(Theorem 3.2)、凸(Theorem 3.4)、非凸(Theorem 3.6)三种目标下带动量 SGD 的依期望收敛证明；此前该设定被普遍认为在 L-平滑下不可处理。
- **用 Hölder 平滑替代 L-平滑以规避方差爆炸：** 指出 L-平滑(ν=1)与严格重尾(p<2)在数学上不相容——此时 E[‖∇f_S(x)‖²] 无法被界定，标准下降引理失效；改用 ν∈(0,1] 的 Hölder 连续梯度把需要界定的量降为 ‖·‖^{ν+1}，并以 ν+1≤p 作为整套分析的核心约束。
- **给出更弱假设下的 vanilla SGD 速率(Theorems 3.1/3.3/3.5)：** 在不需要"有界梯度"假设的前提下(对比 Fatkhullin et al. 2025)，得到与现有工作相当或更细化的 vanilla SGD(无动量)速率；所有结果在 ν=1、p=2 时退化为经典 L-平滑+有界方差结论。
- **诚实定位 vanilla 方法的固有局限：** 明确证明所得速率劣于 normalized/clip 类变体的最优速率(非凸: O(T^{-(p-1)/2p}) vs 最优 O(T^{-(p-1)/(3p-2)}))，将 vanilla 动量 SGD 确立为后续更复杂优化器"必须超越"的基线。
- **实验验证 ν+1≤p 的近必要性：** 在 100 组 (ν,α) 组合(每组 20 次独立试验)的合成函数热图上，高性能点集中在 ν+1≤α 的下三角区域；并在 WikiText-2 上用 Transformer-XL 证实 vanilla 动量 SGD 不裁剪也能收敛。

## Key Contributions

- First convergence-in-expectation guarantee for **vanilla SGD with momentum** (no clipping, no normalization) under heavy-tailed noise across all three objective classes (strongly convex, convex, nonconvex). Prior consensus held this regime intractable under L-smoothness.
- Diagnosis of the **incompatibility between L-smoothness (ν=1) and strictly heavy-tailed noise (p<2)**: the squared-norm term E[‖∇f_S(x)‖²] is unbounded, so the classical descent lemma collapses. The fix is to analyze Hölder-continuous gradients (ν∈(0,1]) and bound the (ν+1)-norm instead, yielding the central constraint **ν+1≤p** that ties function smoothness to the noise tail index.
- Sharper vanilla-SGD (no-momentum) rates (Theorems 3.1, 3.3, 3.5) that **remove the bounded-gradient assumption** required by the closest prior work (Fatkhullin et al. 2025), and that exactly recover the textbook O(1/T), O(1/√(ηT))+η, and O(log T / T^{ν+1}) bounds when ν=1, p=2.
- Honest characterization of the **sub-optimality gap**: for nonconvex objectives vanilla momentum SGD achieves O(T^{-(p-1)/2p}) (known p) vs. the optimal O(T^{-(p-1)/(3p-2)}) of normalized momentum SGD, positioning vanilla momentum SGD as the fundamental baseline to beat.
- Empirical validation that the **ν+1≤p condition is near-necessary**: over a 10×10 grid of (ν,α) on synthetic Pareto-noise test functions (20 trials each), the top-performing (ν,α) pairs cluster inside the ν+1≤α region, and a real WikiText-2 / Transformer-XL (26.9M params) run confirms vanilla momentum SGD converges without any gradient control.

## Methodology

The paper is a theoretical optimization study. The setting is the finite-sum empirical risk minimization problem min f(x) = (1/n)Σf_i(x), analyzed for strongly convex, convex, and nonconvex f. Two assumptions replace the standard bounded-variance + L-smooth pair:

- **Assumption 2.1 (Hölder-continuous gradient):** ‖∇f(x)−∇f(y)‖ ≤ L‖x−y‖^ν for ν∈(0,1]. Setting ν=1 recovers standard L-smoothness; ν<1 weakens it.
- **Assumption 2.2 (bounded p-th moment noise):** the stochastic gradient is unbiased, E[G_ξ(x)] = ∇f(x), and E[‖G_ξ(x)−∇f(x)‖^p] ≤ σ^p for p∈(1,2]. When p=2 this is bounded variance; when p<2 the variance is unbounded (heavy tails).

The analyzed algorithm is **Algorithm 1 — Vanilla SGD with Momentum**: m_t := βm_{t-1} + (1−β)∇f_{S_t}(x_t); x_{t+1} := x_t − η_t m_t, with β=0 giving plain vanilla SGD and no clipping/normalization anywhere.

The technical core is the observation that under L-smoothness + heavy tails the term E[‖d_t‖²] that every SGD analysis needs is unbounded, breaking the descent lemma. Switching to the Hölder descent lemma (Lemma 2.1, which bounds ‖d_t‖^{ν+1} instead of ‖d_t‖²) lets the analysis go through. Four auxiliary lemmas then do the work: a generalized q-norm expansion (Lemma 2.2), a von Bahr–Esseen bound on mini-batch noise (Lemma 2.3), and the Lyapunov/monotonicity step E[‖X‖^{ν+1}] ≤ (E[‖X‖^p])^{(ν+1)/p} (Lemma 2.4) — this last step is exactly where the constraint **ν+1≤p** is forced. The key Lemma 3.1 bounds E[‖∇f_S(x)‖^{ν+1}] and recovers the standard bound when ν=1, p=2.

Six theorems give rates for the six (objective × momentum) combinations. For momentum (the genuinely new results), constant step sizes η are used and the rate is expressed in η and T, recovering the classical O(1/(ηT)+η) momentum-SGD rates at ν=1, p=2. The convex/nonconvex theorems additionally need a bounded-iterate assumption (Assumption 3.1, standard in the convex/nonconvex upper-bound literature). Experiments use three synthetic Hölder functions (strongly-convex / convex / nonconvex variants in Eq. 2), inject two-sided Pareto noise (ω_t)_i = s_i·u_i^{−1/α} with tail index α∈(1,2) and scale λ, sweep 100 (ν,α) pairs with 20 trials each over 4,000 steps, and add a WikiText-2 / Transformer-XL (6-layer, 8-head, ~26.9M params, 200 epochs, batch 20) run comparing vanilla vs. normalized momentum SGD.

## Results & Highlights

- **Nonconvex, known p (Theorem 3.6):** vanilla SGD with momentum achieves min_{t} E[‖∇f(x_t)‖] = O(T^{-(p-1)/2p}), versus the **optimal** O(T^{-(p-1)/(3p-2)}) of normalized SGD with momentum [Liu & Zhou 2025]. Since (p-1)/(3p-2) ≥ (p-1)/2p for p∈(1,2], the vanilla rate is strictly slower — the paper explicitly states the O(T^{-(p-1)/(3p-2)}) rate "is optimal [Zhang et al., 2020]," quantifying the price of dropping gradient control.
- **Nonconvex, unknown p (Theorem 3.6):** the rate degrades further to O(T^{-(p-1)/4}) (with η=T^{-1/2}), strictly worse than the known-p case.
- **Strongly convex (Theorem 3.2):** vanilla SGD with momentum gives E[f(x_T)−f*] = O((1−ηE_2/4)^T + η^{(ν+1)/...}); in particular when ν+1=p the non-momentum case (Theorem 3.1) reaches the refined rate O(1/T^{p-1}), matching Fatkhullin et al. 2025 **without** their bounded-gradient assumption and recovering O(1/T) at ν=1, p=2.
- **Convex (Theorem 3.3/3.4):** vanilla SGD (no momentum) achieves min_t E[f(x_t)−f*] = O(log T / T^{(p-1)}) when ν+1=p; the momentum version gives O(1/(ηT) + η^{p-1}), recovering the standard O(1/(ηT)+η) of Sebbouh et al. 2021 at ν=1, p=2.
- **Empirical necessity of ν+1≤α (Section 4, Figure 1):** across 100 (ν,α) pairs × 20 trials on synthetic functions, the top-35 performing combinations (red markers) concentrate inside the ν+1≤α region of each heatmap, empirically validating the theoretical constraint; outside it, convergence is poor or unstable.
- **WikiText-2 / Transformer-XL (Figure 3):** vanilla SGD with momentum (best lr 0.1) converges without any clipping/normalization, while normalized SGD with momentum (best lr 0.005) reaches a lower training loss faster, matching the predicted sub-optimality of the vanilla variant on a ~26.9M-parameter language model.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty — solid but incremental and correctly scoped.** The genuinely new content is the three momentum theorems (3.2, 3.4, 3.6); the non-momentum theorems (3.1, 3.3, 3.5) are presented as refinements of Fatkhullin et al. 2025. The key technical device — switching from ‖·‖² to ‖·‖^{ν+1} via the Hölder descent lemma to evade the unbounded-variance collapse — is clean and well-motivated, and the ν+1≤p constraint emerges naturally from Lemma 2.4 rather than being imposed. The honest framing ("inferior to optimal," "fundamental baseline to surpass") is commendable and prevents overclaiming.
- **Rigor — high.** Every theorem is stated with explicit step-size conditions, all rates recover known classical bounds as special cases (a strong sanity check), and proofs are relegated to appendices. The convex/nonconvex momentum results additionally rely on Assumption 3.1 (bounded iterates ‖x_t−x*‖≤D_1, ‖z_t−x*‖≤D_2); the authors flag this as "relatively strong" but standard — though a reader should note that for ν<1, strong convexity + Hölder smoothness already forces a bounded-diameter domain (they prove diam ≤ (L/μ)^{1/(1−ν)}), so the boundedness is partly baked into the assumptions rather than free.
- **Reproducibility — moderate.** Synthetic experiments are fully specified (exact functions in Eq. 2, Pareto noise formula, λ=0.01/0.1, 4,000 steps, 20 seeds, 10×10 grid), and the Transformer-XL config (6 layers, 8 heads, ~26.9M params, dim 256/32/1024, batch 20, 200 epochs, lr grid {0.001…0.1}, β=0.9) is given. No code release is mentioned, but the setup is reconstructable.
- **Significance — useful theoretical baseline, narrow practical impact.** The main value is closing the "does vanilla momentum even converge under heavy tails?" question; it does not propose a better optimizer (the authors are explicit that normalized/clipped variants are faster). Practitioners training under genuinely heavy-tailed gradients gain little actionable speedup.
- **Red flags / weaknesses to probe.** (1) **Weak experimental baselines:** the only real-data experiment is a single small LM on WikiText-2; there is no comparison to clipped SGD (Zhang et al. 2020) or gradient-noise-PowerPak estimation, only to normalized momentum SGD, and no test-set/perplexity numbers — only training loss. (2) **The ν+1≤p condition is hard to check in practice:** the authors admit ν "cannot be reliably estimated in practice," which is why ν+1≤p was only validated on synthetic functions where both are controllable; the condition's practical necessity on real workloads is therefore asserted, not demonstrated. (3) **Cherry-picking risk in the heatmaps:** only the "top 35" pairs are highlighted, and performance is averaged over 20 seeds but no confidence intervals or statistical tests are given for the boundary claim. (4) **Constant step size for momentum theorems:** Theorems 3.2/3.4/3.6 use constant η and express the rate as O((1−ηc)^T + η^...); translating these into the T-dependent rates quoted for comparison with Liu & Zhou requires a specific η(T) schedule whose optimality is asserted rather than derived. (5) The convex/nonconvex results inherit the bounded-iterate Assumption 3.1, which somewhat undercuts the "no gradient control / weaker assumptions" selling point.

## Strengths

- First principled proof that the most widely used optimizer (SGD+momentum) converges in expectation under heavy-tailed noise without any gradient surgery — a genuinely useful negative-result-adjacent theorem.
- Clean, unified Hölder-smoothness framework that simultaneously handles all three convexity regimes and recovers the entire classical rate ladder as the special case ν=1, p=2.
- Transparent, non-overclaimed positioning: the paper repeatedly stresses that its rates are sub-optimal and explicitly serves them as a baseline, which is the scientifically honest way to present a "vanilla still works" result.
- The Table 1 comparison matrix is a high-quality piece of scholarship that makes the contribution's exact place in the literature verifiable at a glance.

## Limitations

- The momentum theorems use constant step sizes and require tuning η against T; the practically appealing decreasing-step-size form is not the focus, and the optimal η(T) schedule is not rigorously derived.
- All real-data evidence is a single small language-model training-loss curve; there are no downstream-task numbers, no comparison to clipped SGD, and no larger-scale validation.
- The pivotal ν+1≤p condition is only controllable/verifiable on synthetic functions — ν is not estimable in practice — so the theory's practical reach is uncertain.
- Bounded-iterate assumptions (Assumption 3.1) are still needed for the convex and nonconvex momentum results, partially re-introducing the kind of boundedness the paper sets out to avoid.
- Experiments report averages over seeds with shaded min/max bands but no formal statistical tests, so the "near-necessary" claim for ν+1≤p is qualitative.

## Relevance

对关注优化理论与训练动力学的研究者，本文提供了重尾噪声下"最朴素动量 SGD 仍收敛"这一基础性定理与 ν+1≤p 这一可解释的平滑度-尾指数约束，是理解深度/强化学习中梯度裁剪、归一化等技巧为何有效的理论对照基线；但其结论偏理论，未给出更快的优化器或大规模实验，对纯应用导向的工作直接启发有限。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can the constant-step-size momentum theorems be re-proven with an explicit optimal η(T)=Θ(T^{-1/p}) schedule whose rate is derived rather than asserted, closing the gap to the quoted O(T^{-(p-1)/2p})?
- Is the bounded-iterate Assumption 3.1 removable for the convex/nonconvex momentum cases, e.g. via a coercivity argument, or is it inherent to the heavy-tailed + momentum combination?
- How do the rates change under coordinate-wise or layer-wise heterogeneity in p (different noise tail indices for different parameters), which is the realistic case in deep networks?
- Does the ν+1≤p boundary remain predictive on modern large-scale training (e.g. billion-parameter LMs) where ν is not controlled, if ν is estimated online from gradient-moment statistics?
- A direct empirical comparison against clipped SGD [Zhang et al. 2020] and AdaBelief-style adaptive methods under controlled heavy-tailed noise would clarify how much of the normalization advantage is tail mitigation versus step-size adaptation.
