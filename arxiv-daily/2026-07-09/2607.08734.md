> **一句话总结：** 本文提出"正确性一致度"（correctness agreement）这一决策级评估指标，发现即使在中等量化精度下 LLM 的准确率与困惑度看似保持稳定，量化模型与基座模型在具体样本上的正确预测重合度已显著下降，并通过统计与散度分析证明 Query/Key 投影层比 Value/Output 层对量化更敏感。

> **TL;DR:** The authors show that accuracy and perplexity hide quantization-induced behavioral drift in LLMs: across Llama-3.2-3B, Vicuna-7B, Mistral-7B, and Llama-3.1-8B quantized from 8-bit to 2-bit (legacy and K-quant via llama.cpp), a new decision-level metric "correctness agreement" falls well below accuracy even at moderate precision (e.g., Llama-3.2-3B drops from 55.5% base accuracy to 41.4% CA at Q8_0 on HellaSwag/Winogrande/ARC), and statistical/divergence analysis of attention weights reveals Q3_K–Q2_K as non-linear breakdown points with query/key projections the most distorted components.

## 核心贡献

- **提出"正确性一致度"（Correctness Agreement, CA）指标**：在样本层面衡量基座模型与量化模型"同时预测正确"的重合度，独立于绝对准确率，能够暴露准确率/困惑度所掩盖的行为漂移。
- **首次系统性地将量化建模为注意力权重的结构算子**：用均值、标准差、偏度、峰度等统计量与 KS、KL、余弦相似度、欧氏距离等散度指标，刻画 8-bit 到 2-bit 全精度区间的权重分布漂移。
- **完成 8-bit→2-bit 的受控扫描实验**：覆盖 legacy（Q8_0/Q5_0/Q4_0）与 K-quant（Q6_K–Q2_K）两大族系，在四个模型上同时追踪块级注意力投影漂移并将其与决策行为变化相联系。
- **识别出非线性行为断点与层级敏感性**：指出 Q4_K 是安全量化上限、Q3_K 是退化起点、Q2_K 是崩溃区；Query 与 Key 投影恒定比 Value/Output 更脆弱，为结构感知的混合精度策略提供依据。
- **量化评估维度的系统性对比（Table 1）**：相对以往工作（Jin 2024、Dutta 2024、Kurtic 2025 等），本文是首个同时覆盖统计、散度、多比特、层级敏感性、行为评估与传统性能指标的研究。

## Key Contributions

- Introduces **correctness agreement (CA)**, a decision-level metric quantifying the overlap of correct predictions between base and quantized models, mathematically bounded by `0 ≤ CA ≤ min(Acc_base, Acc_quant) ≤ 1`, exposing drift that accuracy and perplexity miss.
- Formalizes **post-training quantization as an operator** `Tc(θ) = Dc(Qc(θ))` on attention projection weights, and provides a statistical + distributional toolbox (mean/std/skewness/kurtosis; KS/KL/cosine/Euclidean) to measure structural deviation block-by-block.
- Conducts the **first controlled 8-bit→2-bit sweep** across legacy and K-quantization schemes, tracking block-level attention-projection drift and empirically linking it to decision-behavior changes on four LLMs.
- Identifies **non-linear behavioral breakpoints** and **layer-wise sensitivity**: Q4_K as safe upper bound, Q3_K as degradation onset, Q2_K as breakdown regime; query and key projections consistently more distorted than value/output projections.
- Provides a comparative table (Table 1) showing prior quantization-evaluation work covers at most a subset of statistical analysis, divergence, multi-bit evaluation, layer-wise sensitivity, behavioral evaluation, and conventional performance — this work covers all six.

## Methodology

The paper studies post-training quantization (PTQ) as a joint structural-and-behavioral transformation. The framework is built on a decoder-only transformer with N blocks and hidden width d, with four self-attention projection matrices per block: query (Q), key (K), value (V), output (O).

**PTQ as an operator.** A finite set of quantization configurations C encodes a bit-width b ∈ {2,3,4,5,6,8} and a scheme (legacy or K-quant). For each c, the quantizer Qc and dequantizer Dc define `Tc(θ) = Dc(Qc(θ))`; the quantized model is p_{Tc(θ)}. All quantization is performed with llama.cpp, the de facto C/C++ PTQ framework, covering legacy block-wise uniform linear quantization (Q8_0, Q5_0, Q4_0, k=256) and statistically-informed K-quantization (Q6_K, Q5_K, Q4_K, Q3_K, Q2_K).

**Structural deviation in weight space.** Two families of functionals are applied to the dequantized weights:
1. Statistical functionals s(·) — kurtosis, skewness, mean, standard deviation — aggregated across the four projections per block and across all N blocks to yield model-level statistics; drift is `Δs(c) = s(c) − s(θ)`.
2. Divergence functionals d(·,·) — Kolmogorov–Smirnov statistic, Euclidean distance, cosine similarity, KL divergence over empirical histograms — computed between base and quantized-dequantized weight matrices per block and per projection.

**Correctness agreement (Definition 1).** For a benchmark with deterministic scoring score(·) ∈ {0,1}, base label z_m and quantized label z_m^(c) are defined; accuracy is the mean of z_m. Correctness agreement is `CA(c;θ,D) = (1/M) Σ 1[z_m=1 ∧ z_m^(c)=1]`, measuring the fraction of examples both models get right. The paper proves `0 ≤ CA ≤ min(Acc_base, Acc_quant) ≤ 1`, so CA's scale is tied to both accuracies and it deliberately ignores jointly-incorrect cases.

**Models, data, hardware.** Four models: Llama-3.2-3B, Vicuna-7B-v1.5, Mistral-7B-v0.1, Llama-3.1-8B. Perplexity evaluated on WikiText-2 and C4; zero-shot benchmarks HellaSwag, Winogrande, ARC. Hardware: 8× NVIDIA Tesla V100-SXM2 (32GB HBM2). The analysis proceeds in two stages — first weight-space statistics/divergence across bit levels, then relating these to perplexity, accuracy, and correctness agreement.

## Results & Highlights

- **Correctness agreement stays far below accuracy even at 8-bit.** On the macro-average of HellaSwag/Winogrande/ARC (Table 4), Llama-3.2-3B has 55.5% base accuracy but only **41.4% CA at Q8_0** — a ~14-point gap that conventional accuracy (53.4%) hides. The same pattern holds for Vicuna-7B (55.6% acc vs 46.7% CA at Q8_0), Mistral-7B (56.9% vs 48.2%), and Llama-3.1-8B (55.0% vs 45.6%).
- **Low-bit breakdown is severe and consistent.** At Q2_K, Llama-3.2-3B accuracy drops to 48.7% (from 55.5% base) and CA to **38.5%**; Llama-3.1-8B falls to 52.4% accuracy / **44.1% CA**. The authors identify **Q4_K as the safe upper bound, Q3_K as the degradation onset, and Q2_K as the breakdown regime**.
- **Query and Key projections are consistently the most distorted.** Per-layer KL divergence and kurtosis analyses (Figs. 3, 5) show Q and K exhibit the largest spikes in skewness, greatest mean shifts, and steepest kurtosis collapse under Q3_K/Q2_K, while V and O remain comparatively stable — a pattern that recurs across all four models.
- **Perplexity is a misleading proxy.** On WikiText-2 (Table 2), Q3_K actually yields *lower* perplexity than base for some models (Llama-3.2-3B: 1.967 vs base 2.300; Llama-3.1-8B: 2.033 vs base 2.167), yet correctness agreement still degrades — demonstrating a disconnect between language-modeling loss and decision-level consistency.
- **Structural perturbations are non-linear.** Statistics and divergences stay stable from Q8_0 through Q5_K (near-zero skewness, stable kurtosis), then spike sharply at Q3_K/Q2_K: cosine similarity drops and KL divergence rises sharply, confirming non-linear breakpoints rather than smooth degradation.
- **Smaller models are more fragile.** Llama-3.2-3B exhibits larger statistical deviations than the 7B/8B models at the same bit-width, consistent with its larger accuracy/CA drops.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Genuine and well-scoped. While the idea that "accuracy ≠ behavior" echoes Dutta et al. (NeurIPS 2024, "Accuracy is not all you need"), the specific contributions — formalizing PTQ as a structural operator on attention projections, the bounded correctness-agreement metric, and the joint statistical/divergence/behavioral sweep from 8-bit to 2-bit — are a fresh combination. Table 1 effectively argues this is the first study covering all six evaluation dimensions simultaneously.
- **Rigor:** The metric definition includes a proven bound (`0 ≤ CA ≤ min(Acc_base, Acc_quant)`), which is a nice touch and clarifies why CA is structurally below accuracy. However, the bound also means CA's absolute value is partly an artifact of accuracy ceilings — a CA of 41% at 53% accuracy is not directly comparable across models with different ceilings, and the paper does not normalize for this (e.g., a conditional agreement rate given correctness). Variance is reported (±std), suggesting multiple seeds/subsamples, but the exact resampling protocol is not spelled out in the main text.
- **Baselines / comparators:** The study treats accuracy and perplexity as the "weak baselines" to debunk, which is fair, but it does not compare CA against other behavioral-consistency metrics from the literature (e.g., Dutta et al.'s own metrics, token-level agreement, logit divergence). This makes the "first behavioral metric" framing slightly stronger than warranted — it is more precisely "a new decision-level behavioral metric."
- **Missing ablations:** There is no isolation of which benchmark drives the CA drop (results are macro-averaged across HellaSwag/Winogrande/ARC with no per-task breakdown in the main text), no analysis of whether the jointly-incorrect-but-different examples matter, and no link from layer-wise sensitivity to a remediation (e.g., does protecting Q/K at low bits recover CA?). The "structure-aware precision allocation" is offered as future work rather than validated.
- **Data quirk:** Zero-shot multiple-choice scoring via summed token log-probabilities is deterministic but sensitive to tokenizer and answer formatting; the paper acknowledges determinism but does not discuss sensitivity to prompt templates, which can shift which examples are "correct" and thus CA.
- **Overclaiming risk:** Low. The conclusions (Q4_K safe, Q3_K degradation, Q2_K breakdown, Q/K most sensitive) are well-supported by the tables and figures. The "illusion of equivalency" title is appropriately provocative and backed by the CA-vs-accuracy gap.

## Strengths

- Clean, well-motivated central insight: accuracy and perplexity systematically under-report quantization harm, demonstrated with a concrete, bounded metric.
- Comprehensive empirical scope: 4 models × 9 quantization configs × 2 quantization families × 5 datasets/benchmarks, plus layer-wise statistical and divergence analysis.
- Actionable structural findings (Q/K sensitivity, Q4_K/Q3_K/Q2_K regimes) that directly motivate mixed-precision quantization strategies.
- Honest about perplexity's unreliability — explicitly shows Q3_K can lower perplexity while degrading behavioral agreement.
- Table 1 provides a crisp positioning against seven prior quantization-evaluation works.

## Limitations

- Correctness agreement's absolute value is constrained by both models' accuracy ceilings; no ceiling-normalized variant (e.g., agreement conditioned on base correctness) is provided, complicating cross-model comparison.
- Main-text results aggregate three benchmarks into a macro-average with no per-task breakdown, hiding whether the effect is concentrated in, say, ARC vs HellaSwag.
- No comparison against alternative behavioral-consistency metrics (token agreement, logit KL, Dutta et al.'s metrics), so the marginal value of CA over existing behavioral measures is unmeasured.
- Findings are not translated into a remediation: the Q/K-sensitivity insight motivates structure-aware quantization, but no experiment validates that protecting Q/K recovers CA.
- Model scope is limited to 3B–8B decoder-only LMs; instruction-tuned/chat models, larger 70B+ models, and reasoning-heavy evaluation (e.g., math reasoning as in Li et al. 2025) are not covered.
- Prompt-template and scoring-protocol sensitivity for multiple-choice CA is not analyzed.

## Relevance

对关注 LLM 高效推理与量化部署的研究者，本文直接揭示了"准确率/困惑度正常 ≠ 行为一致"这一评估盲区，其正确性一致度指标和层级敏感性结论可用于设计更可靠的量化评估流程与混合精度策略，与量化模型评测、注意力机制分析、模型压缩等方向高度相关。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Would a ceiling-normalized correctness agreement (e.g., P(quant correct | base correct)) change the relative ranking of models or bit-widths, and does it reveal a cleaner non-linear breakpoint?
- How much of the CA gap can be recovered by a structure-aware scheme that keeps Q/K at higher precision while compressing V/O — and does this recover decisions or merely accuracy?
- Does the Q/K-sensitivity pattern persist in instruction-tuned and 70B+ models, or in reasoning tasks (math/code) where Li et al. (2025) observed distinct quantization degradation?
- Which of HellaSwag, Winogrande, or ARC drives most of the CA drop, and is the behavioral drift concentrated in specific reasoning categories?
- How does correctness agreement compare to token-level agreement and logit-divergence metrics — are they redundant, and which best predicts downstream task reliability?
- How sensitive is CA to prompt template and multiple-choice scoring protocol, given that scoring is deterministic but template-dependent?
