Methodology

analytical + confirmatory

A three-part argument linking the hidden-layer spectrum to output-weight stability, then testing which solver survives that spectrum.

(1) Spectral stability bound

For the ELM hidden matrix H ∈ ℝN×L with SVD H = UrΣrVrᵀ, the pseudoinverse is H⁺ = VrΣr⁻¹Urᵀ, so ‖H⁺‖₂ = 1/σmin(H). Output weights β = H⁺T then satisfy ‖Δβ‖₂ ≤ ‖ΔT‖₂ / σmin(H) — isolating the smallest singular value, not κ itself, as the quantity that governs amplification. κ(H)=σmax/σmin measures how close a direction is to being collapsed.

(2) Width-dependent conditioning

Invoking Rudelson–Vershynin bounds for sub-Gaussian random-feature matrices: σmin(H) ≥ c(√N − √L) w.p. ≥ 1−2exp(−C(N−L)). Pushing hidden width L toward N collapses σmin and worsens conditioning — predicting the monotone degradation later seen in Figure 1.

(3) Controlled solver comparison

Backward-stable SVD pseudoinversion is benchmarked against Newton–Schulz / hyperpower iterations (Xk+1=Xk(2I−HXk)) across five prescribed spectral regimes, plus ELM runs on MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, ISOLET.

Strengths

what holds up
  • Clean, self-contained derivation: σmin(H) → κ(H) → pseudoinverse sensitivity → output-weight stability. A solid teaching-grade reference.
  • Width-dependent random-matrix rule σmin ≥ c(√N−√L) gives an intuitive, citable thumb-rule for choosing hidden width L vs. sample size N.
  • The five-regime synthetic comparison isolates conditioning cleanly; the 36/36-vs-0/36 contrast is a memorable, defensible headline.
  • Honest framing — positions itself as a "compact spectral interpretation" and lists regularized ELM, randomized SVD, GPU scaling as future work.

Limitations

where it falls short
  • Only 3 pages; promised ELM accuracy on MNIST/Fashion-MNIST/ISOLET is mentioned but never numerically reported.
  • No comparison with regularization-based remedies (ridge/Tikhonov, truncated SVD as a deployed method) — the most common practical fix.
  • Random-matrix bound assumes iid sub-Gaussian entries; real ELM matrices violate this, yet the gap to the c(√N−√L) prediction is never quantified.
  • No code, seeds, hyperparameters, dataset splits, or synthetic-spectrum construction details — reproducibility unsupported.
  • Findings are largely confirmatory of known numerical-linear-algebra folklore rather than surprising.

Questions for Further Exploration

open threads
  1. How does ridge/Tikhonov-regularized ELM (β=(HᵀH+λI)⁻¹HᵀT) change the σmin-governed bound, and what λ schedule keeps κeffective bounded on the three benchmarks?
  2. Does truncated SVD — discarding singular directions below a threshold — match full SVD's reliability at lower cost? Where is the optimal truncation relative to σmin(H)?
  3. For real activations (sigmoid, ReLU) and correlated inputs, how large is the gap between the σmin(H) ≥ c(√N−√L) bound and the empirical σmin(H)?
  4. Can Newton–Schulz be stabilized (preconditioning, spectral scaling, relaxation) to recover convergence where it now fails 0/36?
  5. What are the actual classification-accuracy differences between SVD and iterative solvers on the benchmarks, and does solver choice correlate with κ(H) at a given (N, L)?
