> **一句话总结：** 本文将"换裁判导致分数变化"重新定义为测量效度问题，通过 Qwen3 参数缩放(1.7B→32B)与 MiniMax 发布版本(M2→M2.7)两条升级路径在四个评测数据集上的对比，发现只有 Qwen3 1.7B→4B 带来稳健的相邻增益，更强的裁判减少但不消除位置/冗长偏差，且当投票误差高度相关时多裁判投票收效甚微，结构化辩论虽能大幅改变判决却因缺乏解析日志而无法归因。

> **TL;DR:** This paper reframes evaluator-replacement ambiguity in LLM-as-judge as a measurement-validity problem, comparing a Qwen3 parameter-scaling axis (1.7B→32B) against a MiniMax release path (M2→M2.7) across four datasets (LLMBar, PandaLM, Chatbot Arena, Judge's Verdict); only Qwen3 1.7B→4B yields a robust adjacent gain (LLMBar 0.463→0.617) while no MiniMax adjacent step is significant, stronger judges reduce but do not eliminate position/verbosity bias (MiniMax-M2.7 still flips 14.7% under A/B reversal), and jury voting is bounded by high error correlation (ρ=0.944–0.972 for Qwen3) so it barely moves accuracy.

## 核心贡献

- **提出可审计的 LLM-as-judge 测量框架**：将裁判可靠性分解为四个可观测维度——判决效度（accuracy/Cohen's κ/Spearman）、偏差鲁棒性（位置翻转/冗长偏差/粒度敏感性）、聚合独立性（误差相关系数 ρ 与陪审团增益）、协议可审计性（解析状态/回退行为/中间判决），并把每个构念映射到具体实验。
- **双轴评估设计**：首次系统对比"参数缩放"（Qwen3 1.7B→4B→14B→32B）与"发布版本升级"（MiniMax M2→M2.1→M2.5→M2.7）两条实际可用的升级路径，明确避免对 MiniMax 内部机制做因果断言。
- **核心反直觉发现——升级不可互换**：在 18 组相邻 McNemar 检验经 Holm 校正后，仅 Qwen3 1.7B→4B（LLMBar 与 Arena）保持显著；9 组 MiniMax 相邻比较无一达到 p<0.05，最大相邻差仅 0.022。
- **量化陪审团误差相关性**：直接估计同质陪审团误差相关系数 ρ，证明 Qwen3 同质陪审团 ρ 高达 0.944–0.972，使 K=1→3→5 在 LLMBar 上仅从 0.463 微动到 0.475→0.482；ρ 校正的 beta-binomial 预测将中位绝对误差从 0.078 降至 0.008。
- **辩论可审计性警示**：跨能力配对辩论产生最大判决偏移（Qwen3-1.7B+GLM-5.1 偏移 +0.317），但因实现缺失原始响应与解析日志，这些偏移无法归因于"审议"，只能作为协议报告要求而非审议效应证据。
- **凝练最小审计报告标准**：给出 LLM-as-judge 报告应包含数据集切片（有效 N/可解析 N/平局处理）、解析成功率与回退规则、A/B 反转与 padding 探针、ρ 与 K（而非仅 K）、以及原始输出/解析状态/各轮判决。

## Key Contributions

- **Auditable measurement framework for LLM-as-judge**: Reliability is decomposed into four observable components — judgment validity (accuracy/Cohen's κ/Spearman), bias robustness (position flip/verbosity/granularity sensitivity), aggregation independence (error correlation ρ and jury gain), and protocol auditability (parse status/fallback/intermediate verdicts) — each mapped to a concrete experiment.
- **Two-axis evaluator study**: A controlled comparison of a parameter-scaling axis (Qwen3 dense 1.7B→4B→14B→32B) against an observed released-model axis (MiniMax M2→M2.1→M2.5→M2.7), deliberately avoiding causal claims about MiniMax internals (the M2 report documents the series but the API sequence is not a controlled ablation).
- **Core counter-intuitive finding — upgrades are not interchangeable**: After Holm correction over 18 adjacent McNemar tests, only Qwen3 1.7B→4B remains significant (on LLMBar and Arena); none of the nine MiniMax adjacent tests reaches p<0.05, with adjacent differences at most 0.022 accuracy.
- **Quantification of jury error dependence**: Direct estimation of homogeneous-jury ρ shows Qwen3 ρ=0.944–0.972 on LLMBar, so increasing jury size K=1,3,5 barely moves accuracy (0.463→0.475→0.482); the ρ-corrected beta-binomial prediction reduces median absolute error from 0.078 to 0.008 (LLMBar) versus the independence assumption.
- **Debate auditability warning**: Cross-capability debate pairs produce the largest shifts (Qwen3-1.7B+GLM-5.1 shifts final-vs-round-1 accuracy by +0.317), but because the implementation records round verdicts without raw responses or parse-success flags, these shifts cannot be attributed to deliberation and are presented as a reporting requirement rather than evidence of a deliberation effect.
- **Minimal audit-trail reporting standard**: A distilled checklist requiring benchmark slices (valid N, parseable N, tie handling), parser success rate and fallback rule, A/B reversal and padding probes, ρ alongside K (not K alone), and raw outputs/parser status/round verdicts for debate protocols.

## Methodology

The paper treats LLM-as-judge reliability as a multi-dimensional measurement property rather than a single accuracy score, operationalized through four constructs (Table 1): judgment validity, bias robustness, aggregation independence, and protocol auditability.

**Evaluator panel.** Eight main judges split across two axes: a Qwen3 parameter axis (1.7B, 4B, 14B, 32B) approximating a controlled parameter sweep, and a MiniMax release-generation axis (M2, M2.1, M2.5, M2.7) treated as an observed release sequence (not a controlled ablation). GLM-5.1 and mimo-v2-pro serve as cross-family reference judges in selected experiments but are not members of either axis.

**Datasets.** Four complementary judgment settings: LLMBar (419 adversarial pairwise examples), PandaLM testset-v1 (894 valid pairwise examples after removing tie-majority cases), Chatbot Arena (1,997 valid pairwise examples from a 2,000-example seed-42 sample), and Judge's Verdict (200 TechQA-derived examples with three-level pointwise labels supporting exact-match and rank-based evaluation).

**Metrics and protocols.** Pairwise experiments report accuracy and Cohen's κ on parseable verdicts; Judge's Verdict additionally reports Spearman rank correlation. Main single-judge runs use near-greedy decoding (T=0.1) to reduce sampling noise. Homogeneous juries sample one judge multiple times at T=0.7 (to expose dependence structure); heterogeneous juries combine different judges at T=0.1 to isolate model composition. Adjacent model comparisons use exact two-sided McNemar tests on parse-shared examples, with Holm correction controlling the family of 18 adjacent tests so significance reflects paired judgment changes rather than parser-coverage differences.

**Jury error-correlation estimation.** The intra-class error correlation ρ is estimated from the vote matrix; three quantities are compared — an independence prediction, a ρ-corrected beta-binomial prediction (with α=ps, β=(1−p)s, s=1/ρ−1), and observed jury accuracy.

**Debate.** A structured-debate protocol pairs two judges for up to three rebuttal rounds on LLMBar; the study records round verdicts and final verdicts but, critically, not raw responses or parse-success flags (a self-disclosed limitation).

Four robustness checks cover human-ceiling calibration, corrected adjacent significance, limited prompt sensitivity, and Arena seed stability.

## Results & Highlights

- **Single-judge non-interchangeability (RQ1).** On LLMBar, Qwen3 accuracy climbs sharply from 1.7B to 4B: **0.463 → 0.617** (Wilson intervals [0.416,0.511] → [0.632,0.721] appear in Table 3, with the 1.7B→4B step p<.001). In contrast, the nine MiniMax adjacent comparisons produce differences of **at most 0.022 accuracy**, and **none reaches uncorrected p<0.05**; after Holm correction across all 18 adjacent tests, only the Qwen3 1.7B→4B steps on LLMBar and Arena remain significant. No single judge wins all datasets: GLM-5.1 leads LLMBar (0.900), MiniMax-M2.7 leads PandaLM (0.857), mimo-v2-pro leads the sampled Arena slice (0.742).
- **Bias persists in stronger judges (RQ2).** Position-flip rate on LLMBar falls from **0.320 (Qwen3-1.7B)** to **0.117–0.147 (MiniMax releases)**, and verbosity bias under a fixed padding string falls from **0.547 (Qwen3-1.7B)** to roughly **0.13 (MiniMax)**. The accuracy–flip correlation is strong (Pearson r = −0.957), yet **MiniMax-M2.7 still changes 14.7% of verdicts under A/B reversal**, so high accuracy does not eliminate measurement artifacts.
- **Jury gains are bounded by error correlation (RQ3).** Independence predictions have median absolute error **0.078 (LLMBar)** and **0.093 (PandaLM)**, reduced to **0.008 and 0.004** (max error <0.02) by the ρ-corrected beta-binomial model. Estimated correlations are very high: Qwen3 homogeneous juries **ρ = 0.944–0.972** on LLMBar, MiniMax **ρ = 0.664–0.706**. Consequently, for Qwen3-1.7B on LLMBar, K = 1, 3, 5 yields only **0.463, 0.475, 0.482** — barely moving accuracy.
- **Debate produces the largest protocol-level shifts but is unauditable (RQ4).** Cross-capability pairs dominate: Qwen3-1.7B paired with GLM-5.1, MiniMax-M2.7, or mimo-v2-pro shifts final-vs-round-1 accuracy by **+0.317, +0.305, and +0.289**; Qwen3-4B + GLM-5.1 shifts by **+0.243** reaching 0.897. Same-family and top-vs-top shifts are small (**+0.005 to +0.050**). Because round-1 parse failures silently fall back to "A" and later failures retain the previous verdict, these shifts cannot be attributed to deliberation.
- **Robustness checks.** PandaLM retains headroom (best-judge leave-one-annotator-out κ = 0.753 vs human ceiling 0.920); Judge's Verdict sits near its noisy ceiling (best κ = 0.620 vs human κ = 0.562); Arena seed perturbations produce a 0.004–0.032 accuracy spread, smaller than the robust Qwen3 1.7B→4B Arena gap.

The methodology exhibits strong measurement discipline: paired McNemar tests on parse-shared examples, Holm correction over 18 tests, explicit Wilson intervals, and self-disclosed threats to validity covering construct, internal, statistical-conclusion, and external validity. The paper's intellectual honesty is notable — it repeatedly narrows its own claims (e.g., explicitly stating the MiniMax API sequence is not a controlled ablation and the debate result is "suggestive rather than causal").

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty.** The framing of evaluator-replacement ambiguity as a measurement-validity problem (rather than a model-selection problem) is a genuine reframing. The four-construct decomposition and the minimal audit-trail checklist are a useful organizing contribution, even though individual ingredients (position bias, Condorcet/correlated-voter theory, multi-agent debate) are well-established — the novelty is in integrating them into one auditable protocol.
- **Rigor.** Strong by design: parse-shared McNemar tests with Holm correction, direct ρ estimation with a beta-binomial correction that demonstrably tracks observed jury accuracy (0.078→0.008 median error), and explicit confidence intervals. The ρ-correction is the methodological highlight and is empirically necessary.
- **Reproducibility.** Mixed. Datasets are public (LLMBar, PandaLM, Chatbot Arena, Judge's Verdict/TechQA) and judges are accessible (Qwen3 open weights, MiniMax/GLM/mimo APIs). However, the paper relies on MiniMax released APIs whose behavior is not version-pinned, and the debate experiment is explicitly not reproducible for its key claim because raw responses and parse-success flags were not logged — a self-acknowledged gap.
- **Significance.** Practically high for the evaluation-methodology community: the result that no MiniMax adjacent release step is significant (max 0.022) and that jury ρ reaches 0.944–0.972 directly challenges common practices of "scale the judge" and "add more jurors." The reporting-standard recommendations are actionable.
- **Red flags / weaknesses.** (1) The MiniMax axis is an observed release sequence, not a controlled ablation, so the "upgrades not interchangeable" conclusion is conditioned on two specific, non-randomized upgrade paths — the paper is careful about this but readers may overgeneralize. (2) The headline debate shifts (+0.317 etc.) rest on an implementation with unauditable fallback behavior (round-1 parse failures silently default to "A"), so the most dramatic numbers are the weakest evidence. (3) Panel is small: two families, two cross-family references; the strongest robust adjacent effect is a single low-end Qwen3 step (1.7B→4B), which is a narrow base for broad methodological claims. (4) The McNemar tests are adjacent-pair only; there is no formal between-axis test, so "the two axes differ" is shown descriptively rather than inferentially.

## Strengths

- Intellectually honest throughout: the paper repeatedly narrows its claims and foregrounds its own limitations (e.g., "best read as a suggestive protocol pattern and a reporting requirement, not as a clean estimate of deliberation").
- The ρ-corrected beta-binomial jury analysis is a clean, quantitative demonstration of why majority voting fails for LLM judges and is the paper's strongest technical contribution.
- A genuinely useful, operational reporting checklist (Table 4) that ties each reliability risk to a concrete required report item and the failure mode it avoids.
- Balanced dataset choice (adversarial LLMBar, broad PandaLM, human-preference Arena, pointwise Judge's Verdict) prevents single-slice overfitting of conclusions.

## Limitations

- The MiniMax release path is an observed sequence, not a controlled intervention, so adjacent non-significance cannot isolate which training stage (if any) drove capability.
- The debate experiment — which produces the largest numerical shifts in the paper — lacks raw responses and parse-success logs, making it non-reproducible for its headline claim.
- Narrow panel: only two primary model families plus two cross-family references; generalization to other families, languages, and instruction distributions is unverified.
- Adjacent McNemar tests do not constitute a formal between-axis statistical test, so the central "two axes differ" claim is descriptive.
- Construct coverage excludes calibration, long-form rationale quality, and domain-specific consistency; parser coverage and sample sizes differ across datasets.

## Relevance

对于关注大模型自动评测与可信赖评估的研究而言，本文直接关系到"LLM-as-judge 是否可信"这一核心问题：它系统揭示了换裁判、陪审团聚合、辩论协议等常见做法中容易被忽视的测量偏差与相关性陷阱，并提供了一套可操作的审计报告清单，对评测基准构建、奖励模型评估和模型对比实验的设计都有直接参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Would the "upgrades not interchangeable" finding hold under a fully controlled within-family ablation (e.g., same training data, differing only in parameter count), rather than comparing a parameter sweep to an opaque release sequence?
- How much of the jury error correlation ρ=0.944–0.972 is driven by the shared prompt versus shared model? A prompt-factorial × jury experiment could disentangle prompt-induced from model-induced correlation.
- Can the debate protocol be re-implemented with full parse-success logging to determine whether the +0.317 cross-capability shifts are genuine deliberation effects or parser-fallback artifacts?
- Do the bias-reduction trends (position-flip 0.320→0.117, verbosity 0.547→0.13) persist on longer-form, multi-turn, or non-English tasks where the Judge's Verdict human ceiling is already near the noisy judge (κ=0.562)?
- Is there a principled way to report a single "reliability score" that combines accuracy, bias probes, ρ, and auditability, or does multidimensional reporting inherently resist compression to one number?
