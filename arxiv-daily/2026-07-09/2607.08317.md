> **一句话总结：** 作者构建了一个含 235 个人类觉得简单但前沿 AI 仍会失败的问题的 blind-spots-bench，配合三阶段分类法和自动评分流水线，评测 38 个 LLM/VLM/图像生成模型，发现闭源前沿模型在文本任务上领先开源模型约 10%，但没有任何模型在所有子任务上占优，且视觉感知类任务（如计数）对所有模型仍是瓶颈。

> **TL;DR:** The authors introduce blind-spots-bench, a 235-question diagnostic benchmark of human-easy but AI-hard tasks (string manipulation, counting, drawing a 5-legged dog) with a structured taxonomy and an automated grading pipeline; evaluating 38 LLM/VLM/image-gen models, they find closed-source frontier models (Gemini-3.1-Pro 83.3% text / 66.9% multimodal) lead open-weight leaders (GLM-5.2 ~74% text) by ~10%, while perceptual counting stays below 60% for every model and scaling/tool-use do not consistently help.

## 核心贡献

- **盲点基准数据集 blind-spots-bench**：从研究生 AI 课程的 287 个原始问题中清洗、标注得到 235 个对人类简单但前沿模型失败的开放式问题，并配有人工撰写的结构化参考解（含正确条件、常见失败模式），数据集在 Hugging Face 上公开发布。
- **细粒度任务分类法**：提出三大类（Object-centric / Abstract reasoning / Language and knowledge）共 12 个子任务标签（如 spatial reasoning、perceptual counting、character-level manipulation），并支持对多标签问题的失败模式归因分析。
- **端到端自动评测流水线**：基于 Inspect AI 框架，用 solver 模型作答 + gemini-3-flash 作为 grader（带代码执行能力）输出二值评分，并经过人工校验（文本任务人机一致率 96.6%、图像任务 90.9%）。
- **38 个模型的系统评测**：覆盖 Qwen3/3.5、GLM 系列、Kimi K2.5/2.6、DeepSeek-V4、Gemma-4、GPT-OSS、Gemini-2.5/3/3.1、GPT-5/5.x 以及 GPT-Image / Gemini-Image 等，按 mean@4 和 pass@4 分别报告。

## Key Contributions

- **blind-spots-bench dataset**: 235 human-trivial, frontier-model-failing open-ended questions, curated from a graduate AI course (collected October 2025), each with a structured reference solution specifying the expected answer, correctness conditions, and common failure modes; released on Hugging Face.
- **Fine-grained task taxonomy**: three high-level categories (Object-centric, Abstract reasoning, Language and knowledge) decomposed into 12 sub-tasks, enabling failure-mode attribution even for multi-label questions (e.g., a generated square image failing on attribute binding vs. on geometric reasoning).
- **Reproducible automated grading pipeline** built on Inspect AI: a solver model answers without in-context examples or CoT, then a gemini-3-flash grader (with code execution) produces binary grades, validated against human judgment at 96.6% (text) / 90.9% (image-gen) agreement, with a pro-Google bias check finding no substantial bias.
- **Broad frontier-model evaluation**: 38 models spanning open-weight LLMs/VLMs (Qwen3/3.5, GLM-4.7~5.2, Kimi K2.5/2.6, DeepSeek-V4, Gemma-4, GPT-OSS), closed-source frontier (Gemini-2.5/3/3.1, GPT-5/5.x), and specialized image generators (GPT-Image, Gemini-Image), reported by mean@4 and pass@4.

## Methodology

**Data collection.** Students in a graduate-level EPFL AI course (MATH-700, taught by Emmanuel Abbé) were each asked to propose five questions that "appeared easy to humans but were failed by frontier models available around October 2025." This yielded ~287 raw questions, reduced to 235 through a three-stage review: (1) manual well-posedness check, (2) difficulty thresholding removing both model-easy and human-hard items, and (3) linguistic refinement of ground-truth annotations.

**Question formats and taxonomy.** Three formats are labeled: text-only (46.2% of samples), image-gen (35.6%), and multi-to-text (18.2%). The taxonomy has three categories broken into 12 sub-tasks — Object-centric (attribute/pattern recognition, spatial reasoning, perceptual counting, generative counting, attribute binding), Abstract reasoning (arithmetic, logical, geometric/graph, constraint reasoning), and Language and knowledge (character-level manipulation, irrelevant-context robustness, knowledge QA). Spatial reasoning is the most frequent sub-task (53 occurrences).

**Grading pipeline.** Built on the Inspect AI framework. The solver is prompted with the question only (no ICL, no CoT scaffolding); the grader (gemini-3-flash, with code execution enabled to verify hard constraints like counting or substring checks) receives the question, the solver's attempt, and the structured reference solution, and outputs a binary C/I grade with no partial credit. Full grader prompts for text and image tasks are provided in Appendix B. Text tasks are repeated k=4; image-gen tasks k=1 due to cost.

**Grader validation and bias check.** The authors sample >100 responses balanced across solver models and across correct/incorrect labels, manually annotating correctness. Human-grader agreement: 96.6% on textual outputs, 90.9% on image-gen. A separate same-provider bias check (Table 6) finds no substantial pro-Google bias — if anything, the false-positive rate is higher for non-Google models.

**Models and configuration.** All models run with thinking enabled at medium effort, max 32,768 output tokens, no tools unless stated. Open-weight models served via EPFL RCP AIaaS (vLLM backbone). A subset of models is additionally evaluated with a Python execution environment (up to 5 tool calls) to study tool-use impact.

## Results & Highlights

- **Closed-source frontier leads open-weight by ~10 points.** On text-only problems, Gemini-3.1-Pro reaches **83.3±3.2%** (pass@4 90.7%) and GPT-5.5 reaches **84±3.2%**, versus the strongest open-weight GLM-5.2 at **73.8±3.6%** and Qwen3.5-397B at **71.1±3.9%** — roughly a 10-point gap that the authors note persists even between models with comparable Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index (AAII) scores.
- **Multimodal gap is wider.** On multi-to-text questions, Gemini-3.1-Pro scores **66.9±6.4%** and Gemini-3-flash **64.5±6.2%**, while the best open-weight VLM Qwen3.5-122B only reaches **48.8±6.9%** and Kimi-K2.5 42.4% — an 18-point open-vs-closed gap.
- **Image generation stays barely above half.** Gemini-3-Pro-Image tops Table 2 at **54.8±5.5%**, with GPT-Image-2 close behind at **51.2±5.5%** at roughly 4× lower cost ($5.26 vs $19.23 per 100 samples); older Gemini-2.5-flash-image only reaches 22.6%.
- **Perceptual counting is a universal bottleneck.** On the 21-question perceptual-counting sub-task, even the strongest model only reaches **57.14%**, with many models at 30–40%; attribute/pattern recognition (6 questions) peaks at just **41.67%**.
- **Complementary model strengths, no single dominator.** GPT-5.5 scores 88.6% on arithmetic reasoning; DeepSeek-V4 reaches 75% on character-level manipulation; Kimi scores 83% on arithmetic reasoning but trails by ~10 points on character-level manipulation.
- **Cost-performance is highly non-linear.** Going from 60% to 70% text-only accuracy requires roughly a **10× cost increase**; open models (DeepSeek-V4, Qwen3.5, GLM-5.2) sit on a better cost-accuracy frontier than comparably-priced frontier models like Gemini-3.1-Flash-Lite and GPT-5.4-mini.
- **Tool use is not uniformly beneficial (Table 3).** Code execution helps Gemini-3.1-Flash-Lite (+9.03%) and GLM-5.1 (+7.64%), but hurts GPT-5.4 (−5.32%) and Qwen3.5-397B (−2.08%); qualitative inspection shows models generate correct code but mishandle inputs (e.g., mis-copying an input string).
- **Scaling is inconsistent within families.** In Qwen3.5, the 122B model drops 10–14% versus the 35B model on logical, geometric, and arithmetic reasoning, and the 397B model does not fully recover; in Gemma-4, the 26B model is ~8% worse than E2B on irrelevant-context robustness.
- **Positive correlation with AAII.** blind-spots-bench text-only accuracy correlates positively with the Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index (Fig. 1a), supporting that the benchmark measures general reasoning rather than unrelated adversarial prompts, though open models underperform relative to closed models at equal AAII.

## Authors & Credibility

The work comes from EPFL (École Polytechnique Fédérale de Lausanne). Senior advisors are **Emmanuel Abbé** (recognized ML theorist) and **Etienne Bamas**. The project originated in the EPFL graduate course "Reasoning in Artificial Intelligence" (MATH-700) taught by Abbé, with students each contributing five failure-inducing questions — a legitimate and reproducible crowdsourcing design. Open-weight models were served through the EPFL Research Computing Platform (RCP AIaaS) using vLLM, giving credibility to the deployment configuration. Dataset and evaluation code are publicly released (Hugging Face `matsant01/blind-spots-bench`; GitHub `matteosantelmo/reasoning-blind-spots`), which materially aids reproducibility. The authors transparently disclose the grader (gemini-3-flash) and run an explicit same-provider bias check.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: Moderate but real. The individual failure modes (character-level manipulation, perceptual counting, attribute binding) are each well-studied in prior work the paper itself cites (Xiong et al., Wiedemer et al., TallyQA). The genuine contribution is the unified taxonomy and the cross-family, cross-modality head-to-head on naturally-occurring frontier failures rather than perturbed versions of existing benchmarks (contrast with GSM-Symbolic, VarBench). The novelty is curation/taxonomy, not a new capability.
- **Rigor**: Strong on methodology design (binary grading with code execution, balanced human validation set, explicit pro-Google bias check), but the headline "≈10% gap" rests on a 235-sample, subtask-imbalanced set where the standard errors on sub-task scores are large (e.g., ±7% on multi-to-text). A sub-task with only 6 questions (attribute/pattern recognition) yielding "41.67%" is essentially 2.5/6 — a number reported to two decimals that overstates precision.
- **Reproducibility**: Good in principle (public dataset, public pipeline, named framework, named grader model, fixed k). Caveat: the grader is itself a fast-moving frontier model (gemini-3-flash); results will drift as the grader is updated, so the benchmark is only reproducible against a pinned grader version the paper does not explicitly pin.
- **Significance**: Useful as a diagnostic stress test, but the closed-vs-open ~10% gap is by now a familiar pattern across many benchmarks, so the headline finding is not surprising. The more novel claims — tool use hurting several frontier models, intra-family 122B underperforming 35B — deserve targeted follow-up because they contradict common scaling intuitions.
- **Red flags**: (1) **Sample-source bias** the authors themselves flag — questions were authored to break two specific frontier models circa Oct 2025, so the benchmark may systematically over-sample those models' particular weaknesses and under-sample others. (2) **No human baseline** despite the entire framing resting on "easy for humans" — a human accuracy measurement would have directly quantified the gap the paper is built on. (3) **Small per-subtask n** (6–21 questions) makes per-subtask rankings like "DeepSeek-V4 best on character-level manipulation" statistically fragile. (4) **No open-weight image-generation models** are evaluated (cited as a "technical constraint"), leaving the image-gen leaderboard as a two-vendor (Google, OpenAI) comparison.

## Strengths

- Genuinely diagnostic rather than agglomerative: the taxonomy + per-question failure heatmaps let a reader see exactly *where* a model fails, not just an aggregate score.
- Rare breadth: 38 models including both frontier closed and open-weight LLMs/VLMs plus image generators in one comparable harness.
- Methodologically careful about the biggest threat to an AI-graded benchmark — same-provider bias — and reports the bias check transparently even though it slightly inflates non-Google scores.
- Cost-performance and AAII-alignment analyses turn raw scores into actionable model-selection guidance (e.g., GLM-5.2 / Qwen3.5-397B / DeepSeek-V4 on the cost-efficient frontier).
- Public dataset, public grading pipeline, and full grader prompts in the appendix make the work auditable.

## Limitations

- Small (235) and subtask-imbalanced dataset; several subtasks have only 6–21 samples, giving wide error bars that undercut fine-grained ranking claims.
- Sampling bias: questions were crowdsourced specifically to defeat two Oct-2025 frontier models, so the benchmark may be tilted toward those models' idiosyncratic weaknesses and miss others.
- No human baseline, which is a notable gap given the central "easy for humans" framing.
- Image-generation evaluation is restricted to closed-source models (GPT-Image, Gemini-Image); no open-weight image generators are included.
- The AI grader (gemini-3-flash) is a moving target; reproducibility depends on a grader version the paper does not explicitly pin.
- Only single-attempt (k=1) for image generation due to cost, so image-gen mean@1 numbers are noisier than the text mean@4 numbers they are compared against.

## Relevance

This paper is highly relevant to multimodal model evaluation and benchmark construction interests: it provides a concrete diagnostic suite for probing where current LLMs/VLMs/image-generators still fail on human-trivial tasks, and its taxonomy + grading pipeline are directly reusable for evaluating future frontier models. The finding that open-weight leaders (GLM-5.2, Qwen3.5, DeepSeek-V4) approach closed-source accuracy on text reasoning at lower cost is also useful for cost-aware model selection.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How would adding a measured human baseline (e.g., 50 non-author annotators) change the framing — are some questions actually non-trivial for humans under timed conditions?
- Would the ~10% open-vs-closed gap shrink or disappear if open-weight models were given the same effective thinking-token budget as the closed models, given that Qwen3.5's high token counts hint at longer reasoning?
- Is the intra-family scaling inversion (Qwen3.5-122B < 35B on abstract reasoning) robust to seed variation, or is it within noise given the ±4% standard errors?
- Can the grader be replaced by a deterministic/verifier-based checker (e.g., constraint solvers for graph/counting tasks) to remove dependence on a frontier grader model entirely?
- How does the pro-Google bias check generalize as the grader model is upgraded — is gemini-3-flash's apparent neutrality stable across grader versions?
- Do the frontier-model-specific sampling biases (questions authored against two Oct-2025 models) mean the benchmark will systematically flatter whichever models were *not* targeted during collection?
