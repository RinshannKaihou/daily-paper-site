Methodology M·01

Group Sequential Testing (Pocock) · gsDesign3-in-Python · 6 composable stopping rules

The benchmark is modelled as n datasets with an overall mean-of-means score (Eq. 1); pairwise comparison uses the observed difference δ̂ = S_A − S_B (Eq. 2). The central enemy is peeking: naively re-checking p-values as data streams in inflates Type I error.

Z_k = (S_A − S_B) / √(σ̂² / N_k) ↔ stage-adjusted α_k (Pocock, constant c)

The fix is group sequential testing: evaluation splits into t stages of batch b; at each stage a Z-statistic is compared against a Pocock-adjusted threshold so the overall Type I error equals nominal α — valid even under repeated peeking. Six stopping rules layer on top:

efficacy · significance
equivalence · within ±margin
precision · MDES / CI width
threshold · cross cutoff
futility · unlikely diff
diminishing · marginal gain

Defaults (untuned): init batch 600, batch 100, β = 0.9, Pocock spending. Scoring via LLM-as-a-Judge (Llama 3.1 8B) on the leaderboard's released predictions.

The hidden-failure argument

The common "1K or fewer examples" heuristic yields CIs exceeding ±5.5 points — too wide for real decisions, yet the loss is invisible to the user. The adaptive framework exposes the CI directly: reliability loss becomes transparent, not buried.

86 of 100 candidate models were filtered out early in the model-selection case study; without the threshold filter, 50% of budget was used instead of 30%.

Strengths S·04

  • Principled, assumption-explicit alternative to arbitrary sub-sampling; the ±5.5-point CI result for 1K heuristics is a memorable, citable finding.
  • Stopping rules are modular and map cleanly to real engineering decisions — deployment gate, candidate filtering, equivalence testing.
  • Genuinely fair baselines: the fixed baseline gets a stronger per-sample bootstrap test and the adaptive method still wins on reliability and cost.
  • Honest about i.i.d. / normality / reproducibility limits and about which comparisons are simply "futile."

Limitations L·05

  • Validated on a single benchmark family (Open VLM Leaderboard) with LLM-as-a-Judge scoring; text-only LLMs and natively-scored tasks untested.
  • Relies on i.i.d. and CLT-normality; clustered or adversarial example ordering could violate assumptions.
  • Pairwise comparisons scale poorly; multiple-testing correction for full ranking is acknowledged but left informal.
  • Users must specify equivalence margins / MDES / thresholds — statistical expertise the paper does not fully lower.
  • No head-to-head against tinybenchmarks, Anchor Points, or active testing on the same data.

Questions for further exploration Q·06

Open threads the paper leaves — and where the framework could break.

  1. Behavior on text-only LLM benchmarks or natively-scored tasks (exact-match, pass@k) where LLM-as-a-Judge variance disappears?
  2. What is the right multiple-testing correction for full K-model ranking, and how much efficiency is lost when properly applied?
  3. Can the diminishing-returns rule adapt to per-dataset variance — so small high-variance sets (HallusionBench) are sampled more, not capping the overall CI?
  4. Does i.i.d. hold for ordered benchmarks (difficulty-sorted, curriculum-ordered), and how robust is the Pocock threshold to such violations?
  5. A same-data head-to-head vs tinybenchmarks / Anchor Points / active testing on cost and reliability is missing — where does sequential testing win or lose?
