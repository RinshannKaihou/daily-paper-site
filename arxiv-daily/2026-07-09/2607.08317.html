Key finding · arXiv 2607.08317

On 235 human-trivial tasks, no frontier model dominates — and perceptual counting stays below 60% for every model tested.

Blind-Spots-Bench: Evaluating Blind Spots in Multimodal Models

Santelmo, Wei, Fakih, Bauer, Garcia Giraldo, Li, Bamas, Abbé — EPFL

2026 · 07 · 09
multimodal-eval benchmark failure-analysis vlm llm-reasoning

Key metric · universal ceiling

57.14%

The best any model scores on perceptual counting (21-question sub-task). Many sit at 30–40%. Even the strongest image generator tops out at 54.8% overall.

Why this matters

A reusable diagnostic suite exposing where LLMs, VLMs and image generators still fail on human-trivial tasks — with cost-aware open-vs-closed guidance.

Critical edge

No human baseline is measured — despite the entire “easy for humans” framing resting on that gap.

Methodology

How the bench was built

Data. Students in an EPFL graduate AI course (MATH-700, taught by Emmanuel Abbé) each proposed five questions “easy for humans but failed by frontier models c. Oct 2025.” ~287 raw questions were reduced to 235 via a three-stage review: well-posedness, difficulty thresholding, and annotation refinement.

Formats. Text-only (46.2%), image-gen (35.6%), multi-to-text (18.2%). A three-tier taxonomy spans Object-centric, Abstract reasoning, and Language & knowledge — 12 sub-tasks total; spatial reasoning is the most frequent (53 occurrences).

Validation. Human–grader agreement of 96.6% (text) / 90.9% (image); an explicit same-provider check found no substantial pro-Google bias.

Strengths & Limitations

What holds up · what doesn't

Strengths

  • Diagnostic, not agglomerative. Taxonomy + per-question heatmaps show exactly where a model fails, not just a score.
  • Rare breadth. 38 models — frontier closed, open-weight LLMs/VLMs, and image generators in one comparable harness.
  • Honest about its main threat. Explicit same-provider bias check; grader and prompts fully disclosed.
  • Actionable selection guidance. Cost-performance + AAII analyses place GLM-5.2 / Qwen3.5-397B / DeepSeek-V4 on the efficient frontier.
  • Auditable. Public dataset, public pipeline, full grader prompts in appendix.

Limitations

  • Small, imbalanced (n=235). Several subtasks have only 6–21 samples; per-subtask rankings are statistically fragile.
  • Sampling bias. Questions were authored to defeat two Oct-2025 frontier models — may flatter models not targeted.
  • No human baseline. The “easy for humans” gap the bench is built on is never directly measured.
  • Image-gen: closed only. No open-weight image generators evaluated (cited as a technical constraint).
  • Moving grader. gemini-3-flash is itself a fast-changing frontier model; no pinned version, so results drift.

Open Questions

For further exploration
  1. Would a measured human baseline (e.g. 50 non-author annotators) change the framing — are some questions non-trivial under timed conditions?
  2. Does the ~10% open-vs-closed gap shrink if open models get the same effective thinking-token budget as closed ones?
  3. Is the intra-family scaling inversion (Qwen3.5-122B < 35B) robust to seed variation, or within ±4% noise?
  4. Can the grader be replaced by a deterministic verifier (constraint solvers for graph/counting) to remove frontier-grader dependence?
  5. Does the pro-Google neutrality finding hold as the grader model is upgraded across versions?
  6. Do the collection biases systematically flatter whichever models were not targeted during question authoring?
