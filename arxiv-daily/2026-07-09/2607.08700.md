> **一句话总结：** 针对 deep-research 引文质量评估中的"LLM 评判模型（judge）"设计问题，作者构建了一个包含 1,248 条人工审核标注的对抗性长文基准（25 个领域、624 个 attribution-citation 对），系统评测了来自 3 个家族的 8 个开箱即用评判模型；结论是 cheaper judge（如 GPT-5-mini）在 source relevance 上最强（F1=0.908），而在 factual support 上所有模型置信区间重叠、无法统计区分，且标量 F1 会掩盖对下游 RLVR 训练至关重要的方向性偏差（pass-rate drift / FPR / FNR），因此校准 judge 是使用引文 rubric 作为 reward 的前提，但并不需要最贵的模型。

> **TL;DR:** On a new adversarial long-form **Deep-Research Citation Benchmark** (624 attribution-citation pairs, 1,248 human-reviewed rubric decisions, 378 hard cases), the authors benchmark 8 off-the-shelf LLM judges from 3 families (Anthropic/Google/OpenAI) on the two LLM-judged citation dimensions, source relevance and factual support. Cheaper judges are competitive: **GPT-5-mini** leads source-relevance pass-class F1 at **0.908** vs. the weakest baseline **Claude Sonnet 4.6 at 0.700**, while on factual support **every 95% CI overlaps** (GPT-OSS-120B 0.649 [.56,.72] vs. Claude Opus 4.6 0.750 [.68,.82]) so no model is statistically distinguishable. Cost spans a **49× range** and does not predict accuracy; scalar F1 hides substantial directional bias (pass-rate drift, FPR, FNR) that would shape any downstream RLVR reward loop.

## 核心贡献

- **构建了 Deep-Research Citation Benchmark**：一个跨越 25 个领域的对抗性长文基准，覆盖 624 个 attribution-citation 对，对全部 1,248 个 LLM-judged 决策（source relevance + factual support 各 624）都做了人工审核，其中 378 个（263 个 source relevance、115 个 factual support）是 council 不一致时由人工裁决的 hard cases。
- **首个针对引文质量 rubric 任务的多 judge 横向评测**：在不做任务特定微调的前提下，比较 3 个模型家族（Anthropic / Google / OpenAI）共 8 个通用 LLM judge，回答"哪类 judge 适合做引文质量评分、成本多大、偏差如何"。
- **揭示标量 F1 掩盖方向性偏差**：即使 F1 接近，judge 在 pass-rate drift、FPR、FNR 上差异巨大，而这些正是下游 RLVR 训练循环会强化的方向；论文据此把"judge 校准"立为把引文 rubric 当作 reward signal 的前提。
- **给出成本-质量脱钩的实证**：judge 单次决策成本跨 49× 区间，但成本并不预测精度，最便宜的几个模型（GPT-OSS-120B、Gemini 3.1 Flash Lite、GPT-5-mini）在关键维度上反而最有竞争力。

## Key Contributions

- Introduce the **Deep-Research Citation Benchmark**: a single adversarial long-form report spanning 25 topic domains, yielding 624 attribution-citation pairs and 1,248 human-reviewed rubric decisions, including 378 hard cases (263 source relevance, 115 factual support) adjudicated from judge disagreements.
- First multi-judge, multi-family head-to-head on a **citation-specific rubric task**: 8 off-the-shelf general-purpose LLMs (no task-specific fine-tuning) from Anthropic, Google, and OpenAI, measured on both accuracy (pass-class F1, Cohen's κ) and reward-signal bias (pass-rate drift, FPR, FNR).
- Demonstrate that **scalar F1 hides directional bias** that matters for RLVR: judges with similar F1 can differ sharply in whether they over-reward bad citations or under-reward good ones — the asymmetry a downstream training loop would actually reinforce.
- Establish the **cost-quality decoupling**: per-decision cost spans a 49× range yet does not track accuracy, and the cheapest tiers contain the most competitive judges on key dimensions.

## Methodology

The paper builds on the two-stage source-attribution pipeline of Onweller et al. (2026): an AST-based parser extracts attribution-citation pairs from markdown-formatted long-form LLM output, then a three-dimension evaluator scores each pair on **link accessibility** (deterministic HTTP 200 check, excluded from judge comparison), **source relevance** (LLM-judged), and **factual support** (LLM-judged).

**Benchmark construction.** A clean long-form report with attributed sources was generated across 25 deliberately diverse topic domains (Amtrak, Pokémon, calculus, black holes, artisan sourdough baking, quantum computing, Bhutanese music, etc.). About **60% of attributed claims were adversarially edited** using **19 strategies** (wrong date/number/entity, negation, fabricated detail, source mismatch, semantic drift, reversed causation, partial truth, minimization, etc.), with some claims receiving multiple edits; the rest were left clean. Parsing yields 624 attribution-citation pairs.

**Gold labels.** A **council of 6 LLM judges** (GPT-5-mini, GPT-5.2, Claude Opus 4.5, Claude Opus 4.6, Gemini 2.5 Pro, Gemini 3 Pro) independently ran the evaluation pipeline. A human reviewer examined every decision, corrected errors, and adjudicated the final label for every non-unanimous case. Gold pass rates: link accessibility 98.4%, source relevance 79.3%, factual support **18.4%** (kept intentionally low to stress-test discrimination on the hardest dimension).

**Judges under test (Table 1).** 8 off-the-shelf models from 3 families: Claude Haiku 4.5, Claude Sonnet 4.6, Claude Opus 4.6 (Vertex); Gemini 3.1 Flash Lite, Gemini 3.1 Pro (Vertex); GPT-5-mini, GPT-5.4-mini (OpenAI); GPT-OSS-120B (Bedrock).

**Metrics.** Primary accuracy: pass-class F1 and Cohen's κ (binary threshold 0.5). Reward-signal bias: **pass-rate drift** Δ = r_judge − r_gold, **FPR** = FP/(FP+TN), **FNR** = FN/(FN+TP). F1/κ reported with 95% bootstrap CIs over 2,000 resamples. Cost modeled as one LLM call per judged dimension per pair from each provider's June 2026 API pricing, expressed as a base-10 log index relative to the cheapest model.

## Results & Highlights

- **Source relevance (Table 2):** pass-class F1 spans **0.700 [0.66, 0.74] (Claude Sonnet 4.6) to 0.908 [0.89, 0.93] (GPT-5-mini)**, a 0.208 spread; GPT-5-mini also leads κ at 0.636. GPT-5-mini is the third-cheapest model.
- **Factual support (Table 2):** every 95% CI overlaps, from **GPT-OSS-120B 0.649 [0.56, 0.72]** to **Claude Opus 4.6 0.750 [0.68, 0.82]** — the authors explicitly conclude **no model is statistically distinguishable** on this dimension and the apparent Opus lead is within noise.
- **No single model dominates:** GPT-5-mini leads source relevance (F1=0.908) but ranks 4th on factual support (F1=0.710); Claude Opus 4.6 leads factual support (F1=0.750) but ranks 3rd on source relevance (F1=0.866).
- **Cost-quality decoupling:** per-decision cost spans a **49× range** (GPT-OSS-120B index 0.00 → Claude Opus 4.6 index 1.69). **Gemini 3.1 Flash Lite (2nd cheapest)** reaches class-balanced average F1 of **0.771**, competitive with models up to **8× more expensive**. Cost does not predict accuracy (Figure 3).
- **Systematic strictness on source relevance:** all 8 judges under-predict the gold pass rate of 79.3%, producing predicted pass rates of only **42.9%–72.0%** — a uniform tendency to under-reward citations, which in an RLVR loop would cause signal sparsity and over-hedging.
- **Factual-support FNR varies widely at similar F1:** from **0.183 (GPT-5.4-mini)** to **0.470 (GPT-OSS-120B)**, i.e. most judges reject a large fraction of genuinely supported citations.
- **Hard cases (Section 4.3):** on the 378 adjudicated disagreements, GPT-5-mini's source-relevance F1 drops 0.076 (0.908 → 0.832); Claude Sonnet 4.6 collapses from 0.700 to 0.420; rankings shift substantially, so full-set F1 is an **incomplete proxy** for reliability on ambiguous citations.
- **Adversarial detection (Section 5.2):** judge rejection of edited citations ranges from ~86% (subtlest edits: wrong attribution, minimization, partial truth) to near 100% (negations, semantic drift); the low factual-support F1 is therefore driven by **over-rejection of correct citations**, not acceptance of fabricated ones.
- **Inter-judge agreement:** pairwise agreement among the 8 evaluated judges ranges from 76.9% (GPT-5.4-mini vs Claude Sonnet 4.6, κ=0.535) to 93.6% (GPT-OSS-120B vs Gemini 3.1 Pro, κ=0.860); 21 of 28 pairs sit between 82% and 88%.

## Authors & Credibility

Authors are from the **Commercial Technology and Innovation Office, PricewaterhouseCoopers (PwC) U.S.** — an industry/applied research setting rather than an academic lab, which is consistent with the practical "how cheap can the judge be for production deep-research agents" framing. Several authors (Lumer, Huber, Feld, Subbiah) are concurrently publishing the underlying source-attribution pipeline paper (Onweller et al., 2026) and adjacent agent/prompt-caching work (Lumer et al., 2026a/b; Sen et al., 2026), so the benchmark and methodology sit on top of a coherent, growing body of work. The experimental protocol is transparent: exact API identifiers, cost assumptions, judge prompts, and the full list of 19 adversarial edit strategies are all reported (Tables 1–2, Appendices A–B).

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Genuine and well-scoped. The gap is real — prior citation RL systems (WebGPT, GopherCite, Self-RAG) assume a single large/dedicated judge, prior LLM-as-judge work (MT-Bench, Prometheus) does not study citation-specific rubrics, and prior RAG eval frameworks (RAGAS, ARES, ALCE, FactScore) do not compare candidate judge models or characterize their directional bias. The cost-vs-bias framing tied to RLVR reward-signal quality is the paper's sharpest angle.
- **Rigor (weak spot):** On the paper's headline dimension — factual support — **every 95% CI overlaps**, so the benchmark literally cannot separate the 8 judges. The "no single model dominates" conclusion is therefore partly an artifact of the task being too hard/noisy to discriminate, not proof of judge equivalence; the authors acknowledge this honestly but the marketing of the result undersells it. The factual-support gold pass rate of only 18.4% also makes F1 a noisy, base-rate-sensitive metric.
- **Circularity in gold labels:** Gold labels are produced by a **council of 6 LLMs** and only human-*reviewed*/adjudicated, not human-generated from scratch. Two of the evaluated judges (GPT-5-mini, Claude Opus 4.6) were in that council — a direct train/test contamination that the authors flag and partially defuse via the human-adjudicated subset, but which still means "agreement with gold" partly measures "agreement with the council." This is the benchmark's most important caveat.
- **Missing ablations:** No sensitivity analysis to prompt phrasing, to the 0.5 binarization threshold, or to batching (the authors list these as open questions). The relevance rubric explicitly overlaps with factual support (Appendix A admits "provide supporting information" conflates the two), and this overlap is the single largest source of disagreement — yet no tightened-rubric ablation is run to quantify the fixable portion.
- **The RLVR framing is hypothetical:** the paper motivates everything around "what a downstream RLVR loop would reinforce" but **never actually trains a model** to show that the measured FPR/FNR asymmetries produce the predicted over-hedging or reward hacking downstream. The link to RLVR is asserted, not demonstrated.
- **Generalization:** all 1,248 decisions come from a **single long-form document** (25 topics, but one report). The authors explicitly note this as the main limitation. Single-document benchmarks are vulnerable to document-specific artifacts.
- **Reproducibility:** good on the judge side (exact API IDs, prompts, edit strategies, CI method) but the benchmark data itself — the adversarial document and the per-pair gold labels — release status is not clearly stated; without it the core numbers cannot be independently reproduced.

## Strengths

- Clean, focused research question ("do you need a frontier model as a citation verifier?") with a directly actionable answer for practitioners building deep-research agents.
- Reports the metrics that actually matter for reward design — pass-rate drift, FPR, FNR — not just scalar F1, and visualizes them per judge (Figures 3 and 5).
- Honest about its own null results: explicitly states the factual-support CIs overlap and that no model dominates, rather than overclaiming a winner.
- Cost-quality decoupling result is concrete and useful: the cheapest tier is competitive, contradicting the default "use the biggest model as judge" heuristic.
- The 378-case adjudicated subset is a genuinely informative ablation, showing that full-set F1 is a poor proxy for hard-case reliability and that rankings reshuffle.

## Limitations

- All results derive from a **single adversarial document**; generalization across document genres, lengths, and citation densities is untested.
- **Gold labels come from an LLM council**, only human-reviewed/adjudicated, with **2 of 8 evaluated judges sitting inside the council** — a structural contamination risk.
- The **factual-support dimension is under-powered**: 18.4% gold pass rate plus overlapping CIs means the benchmark cannot statistically distinguish judges on the hardest, most important dimension.
- **No downstream RLVR experiment** — the reward-hacking/signal-sparsity consequences are argued, not measured.
- No ablations on prompt design, the 0.5 binarization threshold, or batching/caching (which the authors note could further change cost conclusions).
- The source-relevance rubric **conflates** with factual support (Appendix A), and this conflation is the dominant source of disagreement — a self-identified but un-remediated design flaw.
- Benchmark and gold-label data release not clearly specified, limiting independent reproduction.

## Relevance

直接相关于 LLM-as-judge、citation/grounding 评估与 rubric-based RLVR reward signal 的研究兴趣：它系统量化了"judge 选型"这一在 deep-research agent 与 RAG 训练中常被默认为"用最强模型"的工程决策，提供了 cheap judge 可用、但方向性偏差必须单独校准的实证依据，对设计带引用 reward 的训练流水线和 judge 校准方法都有直接参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Would tightening the source-relevance rubric to explicitly exclude the factual-support overlap (acknowledged in Appendix A) materially raise inter-judge agreement and source-relevance F1?
- How do the FPR/FNR rankings change if the 0.5 binarization threshold is swept, or if judges output calibrated probabilities instead of hard binaries?
- Does the predicted reward-hacking (high-FPR judge) and over-hedging (high-FNR judge) actually materialize when a policy is RL-trained against each judge on the same citation rubric?
- Do the cost-quality and bias findings transfer to multi-document benchmarks and to non-English / non-web source corpora?
- Can ensembling or pairwise-agreement-based filtering of judges (Section 5.4) recover discriminability on the factual-support dimension where single-model CIs overlap?
