> **一句话总结：** 针对基于 ReRAM crossbar 的存内处理（IMP）加速器面临的三大部署难题——浮点缩放因子需额外 FP 处理器、现有剪枝方法需 Sparsity Table 做数据对齐、器件非理想性（写入波动 WV、固定故障 SAF）导致精度退化——本文提出 CRIMP 综合学习框架：用 bit-shift 单元近似 2 的幂次缩放因子实现纯整数量化，用 kernel-group + crossbar 两级粗-中粒度剪枝消除对齐硬件，并构建感知 crossbar 高度/ADC 取整的真实 runtime 模拟器做非理想性适配训练，三者在一个训练过程中联合优化；在 Cifar-10 上得到完全整数化、剪枝且可靠的 VGG-16 与 ResNet-56，精度仅下降 2.19% 和 1.26%，计算功耗与计算面积平均降低 122.38× 和 19.51×，且不引入任何额外硬件开销。

> **TL;DR:** CRIMP is a single-training-process learning framework that makes DNNs compact and reliable for ReRAM crossbar-based In-Memory Processing (IMP) accelerators while removing all extra hardware: it forces quantization scaling factors to powers of two so existing bit-shift (S+A) units replace FP multipliers; it combines kernel-group pruning with whole-crossbar pruning to eliminate the Sparsity-Table alignment logic that prior fine-grained IMP pruners (XBA, SPRC, PIM-Prune) need; and it builds a runtime simulator that mirrors the actual crossbar inference flow (2-bit cells, crossbar height H, ADC rounding) so non-ideality adaptation (write variation, stuck-at-faults) is injected at training time. On Cifar-10, CRIMP yields fully integer-only, pruned, reliable VGG-16 and ResNet-56 models with only 2.19% and 1.26% accuracy drops respectively, versus the strongest IMP-aware pruner PIM-Prune which on ImageNet ResNet-18 reaches 32.41% sparsity at 1.09% drop while CRIMP reaches 50.50% sparsity at 0.99% drop — all with zero hardware overhead and 122.38× / 19.51× average reductions in computing power / computing area over the original model.

## 核心贡献

- **多粒度 crossbar 对齐剪枝**：首次提出 kernel-group 剪枝（使每层剩余 kernel 数为 crossbar 宽度的整数倍）与 crossbar 整块剪枝相结合的"粗+中"粒度方案，在保持数据对齐的同时免去 Sparsity Table（ST）对齐硬件——而 ST 面积约为 crossbar 阵列的 6 倍，去掉它直接提升集成密度。
- **纯整数 bit-shift 量化**：将输入、权重、偏置、下一层 scaling factor 全部强制为 2 的幂次（S=2^n），从而用 crossbar 内已有的 Shift-and-Add（S+A）单元近似完成 scaling factor 乘法，彻底消除 FP multiplier（每个 multiplier 约引入 7% 功耗、9% 面积开销）。
- **运行时感知非理想性适配**：推导出 WV 误差的数学期望仅与 crossbar 高度 H 和波动程度 ε 相关（E(Error) 与 H 的关系，当 H·0.75·(e^(ε²/2)−1)<0.5 时 ADC 取整可完全消除误差），据此构建真实 runtime 模拟器，将非理想性注入训练以学习抗噪模型，且不引入任何硬件或运行时开销。
- **单一训练过程联合优化框架**：将上述三技术融入一个"零-恢复"（zero-recovery）两阶段训练过程（先 kernel-group 剪枝、再 crossbar 剪枝，每阶段穿插量化与非理想性适配），利用稀疏训练学习重要性因子 δ 并配合恢复 epoch 动态调整模型结构，扩大搜索空间以获得更高精度。
- **强于 SOTA 的硬件收益**：相比原始模型，平均计算功耗降低 122.38×、计算面积降低 19.51×；相比需要 ST 与 FP multiplier 的现有 IMP 剪枝方法，在更高稀疏率下保持相近或更好精度且无任何额外硬件。

## Key Contributions

- **Multi-grained, crossbar-aligned pruning that removes alignment hardware.** A new kernel-group pruning step (rounding the remaining kernel count per layer to an integer multiple of crossbar width X_Bw) is combined with whole-crossbar pruning; because both operate at kernel/crossbar granularity the pruned model stays data-aligned and needs no Sparsity Table, whose area the authors measure at ~6× that of a crossbar array.
- **Integer-only quantization reusing in-crossbar bit-shift units.** All scaling factors (input, weight, bias, next-layer) are forced to powers of two (S=2^n) so multiplications collapse to shifts executable on the existing Shift-and-Add (S+A) units, eliminating FP multipliers (each ~7% power, ~9% area overhead per Tile, multiplier area up to 11× a crossbar).
- **Runtime-aware non-ideality adaptation with a derived crossbar-height bound.** The authors derive E(Error) as a function of crossbar height H and write-variation ε, showing the ADC's round function zeroes the error when H < ⌊2/(3·(e^(ε²/2)−1))⌋ (e.g., H≤133 for ε=0.1, H≤33 for ε=0.2), and build a realistic runtime simulator that mirrors the 2-bit-cell/8-bit-weight mapping, per-crossbar ADC rounding, and S+A recombination to inject WV/SAF during training with zero hardware/runtime overhead.
- **A single co-optimization learning framework with zero-recovery.** Algorithm 1 fuses pruning, quantization, and non-ideality adaptation into one two-phase (kernel-group then crossbar) training process with alternating zero-epochs (temporarily mask unimportant δ) and recovery-epochs (update via momentum so zerorized importance factors can revive), enlarging the architecture/weight search space for higher accuracy while reducing total training overhead versus training each technique separately.
- **Large hardware savings.** Versus the original model, CRIMP achieves average computing-power reduction of 122.38× and computing-area reduction of 19.51×, while still producing fully integer-only, pruned, reliable models that outperform state-of-the-art IMP-aware pruners on sparsity/accuracy at zero hardware overhead.

## Methodology

CRIMP targets DNN deployment on ReRAM crossbar IMP accelerators (evaluated on the ISAAC architecture, 128×128 crossbars, 2-bit cells, 8-bit weights mapped to 4 cells, S+A recombination, simulated via NeuroSim and CACTI at 32nm). It co-optimizes three "large-accuracy-loss-but-hardware-free" techniques inside one training process:

1. **Crossbar-aligned pruning (multi-grained).** Built on the fully-folded weight-mapping scheme. *Kernel-group pruning* removes whole kernels per layer and rounds the remaining kernel count to an integer multiple of X_Bw, which both saves crossbars directly and (for square crossbars) lets downstream-layer kernels fully occupy crossbar rows. Because coarse kernel pruning alone loses accuracy, it is followed by *crossbar pruning*: a differentiable mask layer after each conv/FC layer assigns one learnable mask value per crossbar; zerorized masks remove whole crossbar blocks. Kernel-group pruning is run first to shrink the number of mask values, making the mask-layer training easier to converge. Importance is read from BN γ (kernels) or mask values (crossbars), driven by sparse training.

2. **Integer-only quantization.** Inputs use symmetric quantization (voltage can be ±); weights use asymmetric quantization (conductance is positive only). All scaling factors S_X, S_W, S_B, S_X+1 are forced to 2^n so the crossbar can compute Q_X^{L+1} = 2^(a1+a2−b1)·Q_X^L⊛(Q_W^L−Z_W^L)+2^(a3−b1)·(Q_B^L−Z_B^L) purely with S+A shifts, no multiplier. Forcing a=1 in a·2^b sacrifices some accuracy, recovered via co-training.

3. **Runtime-aware non-ideality adaptation.** The authors derive the expected per-VVM error from WV as E(Error) = (D/H)·Round(H·0.75·(e^(ε²/2)−1)), proving it depends only on crossbar height H and ε, and that ADC rounding nullifies the error below a derived H upper bound — a guideline that also informs device design. They build a runtime simulator that quantizes weights to 8-bit, splits each into four 2-bit cell values, perturbs each by (e^θ−1), re-splits by crossbar height H, accumulates, rounds, and adds to the ideal result — efficiently computing all non-ideality errors in parallel *separately* from the ideal forward pass so random noise does not harm convergence. SAF is modeled by fixing 9.04% of cells to 0 and 1.75% to 3.

4. **Comprehensive learning framework (Algorithm 1).** A two-phase training loop. Each phase runs initial epochs (train without perturbation so δ values become informative), then alternating zero-epochs (apply quantization + non-ideality-adapted forward + zero out δ below a per-layer threshold derived from the global importance rank and target pruning ratio) and recovery-epochs (standard forward + sparse-training update, where momentum lets previously-zerorized δ recover). Crossbar-aligned pruning, quantization, and adaptation are all tied to the loss and co-optimized; only the first phase uses BN γ as δ, the second adds mask-layer values.

## Results & Highlights

- **Quantization (Table 2):** CRIMP-Quan matches the FP-scaling IAO baseline in accuracy. On ImageNet ResNet-18, CRIMP-Quan reaches 69.48% vs baseline 69.79% (drop 0.31%) vs IAO's 69.54% (drop 0.25%); on Cifar-10 VGG-16 it actually *improves* to 93.39% vs baseline 93.28% (−0.11% drop) and on Cifar-10 ResNet-56 to 93.37% vs 93.34% (−0.03% drop) — i.e., negligible-to-no degradation despite forcing all scaling factors to powers of two and removing FP multipliers.
- **Compact models without non-ideality (Table 3):** CRIMP-PQ beats SOTA IMP-aware pruners on sparsity and/or accuracy without the ST/FP overhead they require. On ImageNet ResNet-18 it reaches 50.50% sparsity at 0.99% accuracy drop (final acc 68.80%) versus PIM-Prune 32.41% sparsity / 1.09% drop (68.67%), SPRC 26.41% / 1.94% (67.82%), and XBA 24.89% / 3.24% (66.07%). On Cifar-10 VGG-16 it reaches 88.25% sparsity while *gaining* accuracy (−0.43% drop, 93.71%) versus CSAO's 34.90% sparsity / 0.20% drop. On Cifar-10 ResNet-56 it reaches 51.36% sparsity at 0.16% drop (93.18%) versus CSAO's 23.50% / 0.40%.
- **Hardware efficiency (Fig. 7):** Compared to the original (uncompressed) model, CRIMP achieves an average total power reduction of 4.44×, a **computing-power reduction of 122.38×**, a total area reduction of 3.16×, and a **computing-area reduction of 19.51×** — the largest gains among LSRR, CSAO, SPRC, XBA, PIM-Prune — precisely because it sheds both the FP multipliers and the ST alignment hardware that the crossbar-column/row pruners must keep.
- **End-to-end compact + reliable models (Table 4):** On Cifar-10 VGG-16 with SAF and ε=0.1 and 88.25% pruning, CRIMP loses only 2.19% (93.28%→91.09%) with zero hardware/runtime overhead — competitive with DRD's 0.52% drop (which ignores runtime crossbar characteristics) and far better than VACTSF's 6.46% drop; on Cifar-10 ResNet-56 (51.36% pruned) it loses 1.26% (93.34%→92.08%). Notably CRIMP's non-ideality adaptation is effective: VGG-16 without SAF and at ε=0.1 drops only 0.08% (93.28%→93.20%), and ResNet-56 only 0.16%.
- **Crossbar-height / pruning / NIA ablation (Fig. 8):** On LeNet-5/Mnist, NIA training under pruning recovers accuracy from 90.54% (no NIA, ε=0.8) to 96.54% (with NIA, same ε) — a ~6-point gain — and larger crossbar height H strictly lowers accuracy at fixed ε, empirically confirming the derived E(Error) bound. Below the derived H bound, ADC rounding alone can fully cancel WV error (Fig. 5: error [1 1 −1] at H=10 collapses to [0 0 0] at H=2).

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (genuine).** The single most original piece is the derived closed-form E(Error) bound tying WV-induced error to crossbar height H and the resulting claim that ADC rounding can null the error below H = ⌊2/(3·(e^(ε²/2)−1))⌋ (e.g., H≤133 at ε=0.1). This is a real modeling contribution that prior software-only correctors (CorrectNet, DRD) miss because they multiply parameters by e^θ without modeling the per-crossbar ADC round and H split. Combining this with power-of-two scaling factors (to reuse S+A) and kernel-group + whole-crossbar pruning (to drop the ST) is a coherent, non-trivial integration.
- **Rigor — mixed.** The pruning and hardware comparisons are fairly done: Table 3 normalizes against the *same* baselines and Fig. 7 measures power/area with NeuroSim+CACTI at 32nm, accounting for the ST and FP multiplier the competitors carry. *However*, several baselines are handicapped: LSRR and SPRC are modeled at 4-bit quantization (the authors' footnote 2) while CRIMP uses 8-bit, which inflates CRIMP's area/power advantage in Fig. 7 in a way that is not fully isolated. SPRC's baseline accuracy for ResNet-56 on Cifar-10 is reported as "-" (footnote 1), making that one comparison non-quantitative. The end-to-end Table 4 omits ImageNet entirely, so the strongest reliability claims rest on Cifar-10/Mnist.
- **Reproducibility — moderate.** Algorithm 1 and the equations are specified in enough detail to reimplement, the non-ideality parameters are standard (9.04% SF1, 1.75% SF0; ε∈{0.1,0.5}), and training epoch counts are given. But no code/model checkpoints are stated to be released, the runtime simulator is custom, and the per-layer pruning-ratio schedule and initial-epoch count s are under-specified, so an independent reimplementation would need reverse-engineering.
- **Significance.** The contribution is real and useful for the IMP-accelerator community: demonstrating that you can throw out both the FP multiplier *and* the ST while *also* gaining non-ideality robustness is a clean Pareto improvement. The derived H-bound is independently citable as a device-design guideline.
- **Red flags / overclaims.** (1) The 122.38× and 19.51× headline numbers are *computing* power/area only; total power/area reductions are a much more modest 4.44× and 3.16× — the abstract leads with the larger numbers, which could mislead a casual reader. (2) CRIMP's best reliability numbers in Table 4 are often the *unpruned* or *no-SAF* variants (e.g., VGG-16 ε=0.1 no-SAF = 0.08% drop), while the headline "2.19% / 1.26%" are the pruned + SAF + ε=0.1 configurations — a reasonable but favorable framing. (3) No ablation isolating the contribution of the zero-recovery procedure vs plain sparse-training pruning, and no ImageNet end-to-end reliability result, so the scalability claim to "complex models" is under-evidenced. (4) On Mnist LeNet-5 (Table 3) CRIMP-PQ actually loses more accuracy than LSRR (0.31% vs 0.08%) at higher sparsity — honestly reported but worth noting that the advantage is dataset/model dependent.

## Strengths

- Cleanly removes two whole classes of hardware overhead (FP multipliers and Sparsity Tables) simultaneously while *adding* non-ideality robustness — a genuine triple win rather than a single-axis improvement.
- The derived analytical bound on WV error as a function of crossbar height H is a reusable, device-design-relevant result, not just an algorithmic trick.
- The runtime simulator mirrors the actual inference flow (2-bit cell mapping, per-crossbar ADC round, S+A recombination) instead of the cheaper but inaccurate "just multiply weights by e^θ" approximation used by DRD/CorrectNet — and Fig. 5 gives an intuitive worked example of why crossbar height matters.
- Strong empirical results against a credible set of IMP-aware baselines (XBA, SPRC, PIM-Prune, CSAO, LSRR) and non-ideality correctors (R-V-W, AIIR, KD, CorrectNet, CTSF/VACTSF, DRD), evaluated with a standard NeuroSim+CACTI 32nm flow.
- Co-optimization in one training process (with the zero-recovery trick using momentum to revive zerorized importance factors) is a practical way to avoid the compounding accuracy loss of applying pruning, quantization, and noise adaptation sequentially.

## Limitations

- End-to-end reliability results (Table 4) cover only Mnist and Cifar-10; ImageNet appears only in the no-non-ideality pruning table, so robustness on large-scale models is not demonstrated.
- Headline efficiency numbers (122.38× / 19.51×) are computing-only; total (the number a system designer actually pays) is 4.44× / 3.16× — the gap is large enough that the framing is misleading.
- Several competitors in Fig. 7 are modeled at 4-bit quantization while CRIMP uses 8-bit, confounding the power/area comparison; a bit-matched comparison would strengthen the claim.
- No released code or checkpoints stated; the custom runtime simulator and under-specified pruning schedule / initial-epoch hyperparameter reduce independent reproducibility.
- No ablation isolating the zero-recovery procedure's contribution, and the kernel-group pruning first hurts on small Mnist/LeNet-5 (0.31% vs LSRR's 0.08%), showing the method's advantage is model/dataset dependent.
- All non-ideality modeling (WV lognormal, SAF rates) assumes fixed distributions; drift over device lifetime or temperature-dependent variation is not considered.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does CRIMP's runtime-aware non-ideality adaptation and the derived H-bound transfer to Transformer / large-language-model workloads, or does the accuracy cost of integer-only power-of-two scaling become prohibitive at scale?
- How does the framework behave when the SAF rate and WV ε are not stationary — e.g., under device aging or temperature drift over months of inference — given the adaptation is trained once at deployment?
- What is the bit-matched (all-8-bit) power/area comparison against LSRR/SPRC, and does CRIMP's computing-area advantage survive when ST and multiplier overhead are removed from all baselines?
- Can the zero-recovery momentum trick be formally analyzed (does it provably escape the ReLU-zero deadlock, or are there failure modes where δ stays permanently dead)?
- How does CRIMP interact with finer crossbar sizes (e.g., 64×64 or 256×256) and with emerging multi-level-cell (>2-bit) ReRAM, given the central role of H in the error bound?
- Is the framework compatible with on-device fine-tuning / continual learning, or does the co-optimized training need to be redone whenever the model or device population changes?
