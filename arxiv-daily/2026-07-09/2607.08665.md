> **一句话总结：** 本文将"对已选模型重复采样"与"切换到其他模型"视为同一份单查询预算下的竞争性用途，提出在线边际增益贪婪策略 RoR；在 GSM8K/MATH-500/GPQA-Diamond/HumanEval+ 四个基准上相对 budget-aware best-of-K、cascade、单提交路由等基线取得更优的成本-质量 Pareto 前沿，其中在最异构的 GPQA 上匹配成本下准确率领先 +10.7 个点，且增益随验证器质量下降而缩减，与配套理论的 recoverability asymmetry 一致。

> **TL;DR:** The paper casts resampling the committed LLM versus rerouting to an alternative model as competing uses of one per-query cost budget under an imperfect verifier, and proposes an online marginal-gain-greedy policy (RoR). Replayed on 11-model open-weight pools over GSM8K, MATH-500, GPQA-Diamond, and HumanEval+, RoR attains a favorable cost–quality Pareto front, beating the strongest baseline budget-aware best-of-K by up to +10.7 points on GPQA-Diamond at matched cost while cutting cost ~3.5x versus single-routing the best model on GSM8K; gains are verifier-gated, shrinking as verifier quality degrades from q=1 to q=0.6.

## 核心贡献

- 首次将"对已 commit 的模型重复采样 (resample)"与"切换到其他模型 (reroute)"形式化为同一份单查询成本预算下、在不完美验证器约束下最大化期望正确率的预算化测试时模型选择问题（Section III）。
- 提出在线 Resample-or-Reroute (RoR) 分配策略：用带伪计数先验的后验均值维护每个候选模型的实时成功率估计，每一步把下一单位预算投入到"估计的边际正确率/单位成本"最高的动作（RESAMPLE 或 REROUTE），同时给出一个不可部署的 oracle 分配作为参考上界，并附带一个 UCB 探索变体（Section IV）。
- 将策略行为与配套论文 [arXiv:2607.03436] 的"选择 vs 采样的可恢复性不对称 (recoverability asymmetry)"联系起来，解释何时 resample 优于 reroute（Section V）。
- 在四个重新生成的多抽取正确率张量（11 个开源模型、8 个预训练谱系、每格 k=30 次种子对齐抽取、T=0.2）上进行系统级 replay 评测，给出相对 single-route、单提交 router、budget-aware best-of-K、FrugalGPT 式 cascade、random allocation 的成本-质量 Pareto 前沿，并做了验证器质量消融、真实价格 replay、agreement/部分测试套件验证器等多重鲁棒性检查（Sections VI–VIII）。

## Key Contributions

- First formulation that treats resampling the committed model and rerouting to an alternative as competing uses of a single explicit per-query cost budget under an imperfect verifier, cast as maximizing expected correctness subject to per-query cost ≤ B.
- An online resample-or-reroute (RoR) policy with a marginal-gain-greedy allocation rule a* = argmax Δcorr(a)/cost(a), a posterior-mean belief update p̂ = (s·p̄ + w)/(s + n) with pseudo-count s, a UCB exploration variant, plus a non-deployable oracle allocation as a reference ceiling.
- A theoretical grounding linking the policy's greedy preference to the recoverability asymmetry of the companion paper: when a committed model's reproducible success probability is high, resampling has larger marginal correctness-per-cost than rerouting, and the reachable correctness interpolates as O_repro ≤ O_agg ≤ O_exp.
- A reproducible CPU-only replay evaluation over four regenerated multi-draw pools, releasing the tensors and code, accompanied by a parametric verifier-quality ablation, a real OpenRouter-price replay, a label-free self-consistency (agreement) verifier, and a partial-test-suite code verifier — mapping out where the conclusions carry over and where they weaken.

## Methodology

The problem is set up as budgeted correctness maximization. For query i, a pool of models M with per-draw costs c_1...c_M and a per-query budget B, drawing model m yields a correct answer with unknown probability p_im. An (imperfect) verifier v scores candidates and is used both for final answer selection and for guiding allocation; v is never assumed to reveal ground-truth correctness. A policy observes the history of drawn candidates and verifier scores and, at each step, picks an action a ∈ {RESAMPLE(m), REROUTE(m')} until spent cost reaches B, then returns a final answer; the objective is max_π E[1{â correct}] s.t. cost ≤ B.

**Belief update.** Let p̄_m be model m's offline accuracy calibrated once on the train split. During query i, after n_im draws of which w_im were verified correct, the policy scores m by the posterior mean p̂_im = (s·p̄_m + w_im)/(s + n_im), where the pseudo-count s controls how strongly the offline prior anchors the estimate. A small s means one or two failed draws pull p̂ below a fresh model's p̄ and thereby trigger a reroute; a run of successes keeps the policy resampling the committed model. For an untried model n_im=0 so p̂_im = p̄_m and rerouting competes on the offline prior alone.

**Allocation rule.** At each step the policy takes the action with the highest estimated marginal correctness per unit cost, a* = argmax_a Δcorr(a)/cost(a). Under early stopping Δcorr for resampling reduces to p̂_im (the probability the next draw of m succeeds), so the rule reduces to a* = argmax_m(affordable) p̂_im/c_m. Both actions are scored on the same scale, which is what makes them genuinely competing uses of one budget. Two variants are evaluated: a greedy variant and a UCB variant adding an exploration bonus 2·ln(t+1)/(n_im+1)/c_m; an oracle allocation with access to true p_im provides a non-deployable upper bound.

**Computational cost.** Each step scores at most M actions with O(1) arithmetic; a query takes at most ⌈B/c_min⌉ steps, giving per-query overhead O(M·B/c_min) time and O(M) memory — negligible relative to one LLM call. The policy is stateless across queries except for the M offline priors p̄_m.

**Evaluation protocol.** Policies are replayed offline on pre-computed draws: a "sample from model m" consumes one of its k=30 pre-computed draws without replacement, order randomized per trial. Queries split 50/50; priors calibrated on train half; test-half accuracy averaged over 20 random draw orderings, sweeping per-query budget B. The replay runs in Python/NumPy on CPU only, so every reported number reproduces from the released tensors with no model inference. Cost is proxied by parameter count in billions (a monotone stand-in for $/token), with a separate provider-calibrated price-vector replay (Table VII).

## Results & Highlights

- **GPQA-Diamond (most heterogeneous pool), verifier q=1, matched mid budget:** RoR reaches 0.968 accuracy at cost 25.9 versus budget-aware best-of-K 0.861 at cost 27.0 (+10.7 points at matched cost), +4.1 over the FrugalGPT-style cascade (0.926 at 26.2), and roughly +40 points over either single-commit policy (router 0.566 at cost 8.0, single-route best model 0.551 at cost 8.0) — rerouting matters most where the pool genuinely specializes.
- **GSM8K (saturated), verifier q=1:** RoR reaches 0.993 at mean cost 9.2, i.e. 24–34% cheaper than the cascade (12.1, acc 0.992) and budget-aware best-of-K (13.9, acc 0.983), and 3.5× cheaper than single-routing the best model (9.2 vs 32.0) while also being 2.7 points more accurate (0.993 vs 0.966). The oracle ceiling is 1.000 at cost 7.0.
- **MATH-500 (intermediate), verifier q=1:** RoR 0.887 at cost 26.4 is +2.1 points over budget-aware best-of-K (0.867 at 25.5) at matched cost, and +10.3 points over the one-commit router (0.784) at 18% lower cost.
- **HumanEval+ (code), verifier q=1:** RoR 0.952 at cost 20.5 vs budget-aware best-of-K 0.852 (+10.0 points) and single-route 0.858 at cost 32.0; on this near-saturated benchmark with a near-perfect execution verifier, the undirected baselines catch up at high budget (cascade ties at 0.952, random allocation 0.959), so RoR's advantage concentrates at low budget.
- **Verifier-gated gains (Fig. 2, q degraded 1.0 → 0.8 → 0.6 at matched mid budget):** GPQA falls 0.968 → 0.710 → 0.675; MATH-500 0.887 → 0.811 → 0.794; GSM8K 0.993 → 0.979 → 0.970. At q=0.6 on MATH-500 RoR's margin inverts slightly against budget-aware best-of-K (0.794 vs 0.803), confirming that rerouting pays only when the verifier can be trusted.
- **Real-price replay (Table VII, OpenRouter $/M output tokens, July 2026 snapshot):** RoR leads MATH-500 0.903 at $0.42 against best-of-K 0.800 and cascade 0.870, leads GPQA 0.970 at $0.31 vs 0.919/0.929, and ties the cascade on GSM8K (0.992 at $0.06); conclusions carry over, and budget-aware best-of-K weakens because price ordering no longer tracks ability.
- **Deployable code verifier (Table IX, HumanEval+, in-prompt base tests gate early stopping, EvalPlus suite is truth):** measured base-verifier false-accept rate only 1.0%; RoR under this real verifier nearly matches its perfect-verifier ceiling (0.897 vs 0.897 at B=26; 0.918 vs 0.921 at B=58) and stays ahead of base-verified best-of-K (0.870/0.838).
- **Label-free agreement verifier (Table VIII, consensus A=2):** on open-ended GSM8K agreement is informative (RoR 0.958 vs agreement-gated best-of-K 0.942); on multiple-choice GPQA agreement is nearly uninformative and the ordering reverses (0.552 vs 0.572), yielding a concrete deployment rule.
- **Sensitivity (Table VI, B=26):** pseudo-count s changes accuracy by at most 1.7 points over an order of magnitude (s=0.5→8); std over 20 draw orderings is 0.003/0.009/0.023 on GSM8K/MATH-500/GPQA, small relative to reported margins.
- **Latency (Table X):** RoR is sequential (2.5 rounds on HumanEval+, 3.3 on GPQA, 3.6 on MATH-500) — higher latency than the one-round parallel best-of-K/single-route, but uses fewer rounds than the cascade (2.9–3.8) while reaching at least its accuracy, and is Pareto-efficient on the accuracy–latency trade-off.

## Authors & Credibility

Single-authored by Teng-Ruei Chen, affiliated with the Institute of Bioinformatics and Systems Biology, National Yang Ming Chiao Tung University (Hsinchu, Taiwan) and Krixvon (Taipei). The paper is explicitly a method-oriented follow-up to the author's own companion theoretical study [arXiv:2607.03436], which decomposes the router-to-oracle gap into reproducible specialist advantage and single-draw label noise and proves the recoverability asymmetry that this paper operationalizes. The experimental setup is unusually reproducible: multi-draw correctness tensors with k=30 seed-aligned draws per (query, model) cell, a fully CPU-only NumPy replay (no model inference needed to reproduce any number), a 20-permutation draw-ordering average with reported standard deviations, an audited generation environment probe (Table IV) including exact NVIDIA/CUDA/vLLM/PyTorch versions, and a stated intent to release tensors and code. The framing honestly separates what is recoverable from what requires an oracle, and reports negative as well as positive results (UCB variant loses on GPQA at low budget; agreement verifier reverses on multiple-choice). It is a preprint (July 2026), not peer-reviewed.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Moderate. The marginal-gain-greedy rule and the best-arm-identification borrowing are individually standard; the genuine novelty is the *framing* — treating resampling and rerouting as competing uses of one explicit per-query budget under an imperfect verifier. As the paper's own Table I concedes, no prior family combines all four of {chooses among models, repeated draws, per-query budget, imperfect verifier}. However, the intellectual heavy lifting (the recoverability asymmetry that justifies the greedy preference) sits in the companion paper [8], so this contribution is primarily an engineering/empirical instantiation rather than a new theoretical result.
- **Rigor:** Strong on reproducibility and honest reporting. Multi-draw tensors with k=30 draws, 20 random draw orderings, reported standard deviations, a CPU-only replay, an audited environment probe, and a stated code/tensor release set a high bar. The verifier-quality ablation, the real-price replay, and the two "real verifier" instantiations (agreement, partial test suite) directly probe the paper's central assumption rather than hiding it.
- **Weak baselines / cherry-picking concerns:** The single-commit router (0.551–0.566 on GPQA) and single-route best-model baselines are deliberately weak/budget-independent points, so the headline "+40 points on GPQA" overstates the practical contribution — the *strongest* deployable baseline is budget-aware best-of-K, against which the GPQA margin is +10.7 points (still solid, but not +40). The "+10 points over the one-commit router at 18% lower cost" on MATH-500 similarly leans on a weak baseline. The honest comparisons are the +2.1 (MATH-500), +10.7 (GPQA), +10.0 (HumanEval+) over budget-aware best-of-K.
- **Missing ablations:** The cost model is parameter count, a monotone proxy the paper admits does not preserve real $-ratios; although the OpenRouter price replay (Table VII) is a single July-2026 snapshot from one provider, there is no sensitivity analysis over multiple price vectors or latency-constrained/batched serving — a real deployment concern the paper defers to future work. There is no closed-source/proprietary model in the 11-model pool, so the gains are shown only on open-weight models of comparable capability ranges.
- **Verifier assumption is load-bearing:** The main table (q=1) assumes a verifier that stops as soon as a drawn answer is verified correct — the idealized setting where the recoverability asymmetry is fully available. The paper is commendably explicit that the gains are verifier-gated (Fig. 2) and even invert on MATH-500 at q=0.6 against budget-aware best-of-K (0.794 vs 0.803), and the agreement-verifier result reverses on GPQA. The practical value therefore depends on the deployer having a verifier stronger than consensus; for multiple-choice tasks the paper itself concludes its mechanism can degrade.
- **Saturation caveat:** On the two near-saturated benchmarks (GSM8K, HumanEval+) the undirected baselines (cascade, random allocation) catch up at high budget, so RoR's advantage there is concentrated at low budget / is mostly a cost saving rather than an accuracy ceiling improvement — the paper states this clearly, but a casual reader of the headline numbers could miss it.
- **Reproducibility:** High. Deterministic CPU replay, released tensors, audited environment, reported variance. This is the paper's strongest pillar.
- **Significance:** Real but narrow. The framing is a useful conceptual contribution to the routing/test-time-compute literature, but the empirical demonstration is confined to four benchmarks, one 11-model open-weight pool, a parameter-count cost proxy, and an offline replay rather than an online serving system.

## Strengths

- Clean, novel problem formulation that unifies two previously separate literatures (model routing and test-time sampling) under one explicit per-query budget with an imperfect verifier, clearly positioned against related families in Table I.
- Highly reproducible methodology: CPU-only NumPy replay, k=30 seed-aligned multi-draw tensors, 20 draw orderings with reported standard deviations, audited environment probe, and a stated tensor/code release.
- Intellectually honest reporting of negative and boundary results — UCB variant loses on GPQA at low budget, agreement verifier reverses on multiple-choice GPQA, undirected baselines catch up on saturated benchmarks, gains are verifier-gated and invert at q=0.6 on MATH-500.
- Practically grounded robustness checks: provider-calibrated real-price replay (OpenRouter, July 2026), a deployable code verifier (partial test suites, measured 1.0% false-accept rate), and a label-free self-consistency verifier — yielding concrete deployment rules (agreement suffices for open-ended answer spaces; multiple-choice needs a stronger verifier).
- Genuine cost savings demonstrated, not just accuracy gains: 3.5× cheaper than single-routing the best model on GSM8K while more accurate; RoR is Pareto-efficient on the accuracy–latency trade-off versus the adaptive cascade.
- Clear theoretical grounding via the companion paper's recoverability asymmetry, which explains *when* resampling beats rerouting rather than treating the policy as a black box.

## Limitations

- The cost model is parameter count, which preserves the pool's cost ordering but not real serving economics; the real-price replay is a single provider snapshot (OpenRouter, July 2026) with no sensitivity over multiple price/latency vectors.
- The main results assume a reliable verifier (q=1) with early stopping — the idealized regime where the recoverability asymmetry is fully available; gains shrink materially as the verifier degrades and can invert against budget-aware best-of-K on MATH-500 at q=0.6.
- Evaluation is confined to four benchmarks and a single 11-model open-weight pool across eight pretraining lineages; no proprietary/closed-source models are included, and only exact-match / execution-scored correctness is considered.
- RoR is adaptive and sequential, incurring a round-trip per draw (2.5–3.6 rounds) — higher latency than one-round parallel best-of-K/single-route; batched and latency-constrained serving, where draws can be parallel, is left to future work.
- The strongest-deployable-baseline margins (+2.1 on MATH-500, +10.7 on GPQA, +10.0 on HumanEval+) are smaller than the headline numbers suggest, because the single-commit router and single-route baselines are deliberately weak budget-independent points.
- The theoretical justification (recoverability asymmetry) lives in the companion paper [arXiv:2607.03436]; this work's contribution is the policy and its empirical evaluation rather than new theory.
- The replay does not capture prompt-adaptive behaviors (e.g. revising the prompt between draws); only static single-turn question answering and code generation are studied.

## Relevance

直接相关于模型路由 (model routing) 与测试时计算 (test-time compute) 的交叉研究：把 best-of-K 采样和模型切换纳入同一预算决策的框架，对低成本高质量 LLM 推理/服务系统的设计有直接参考价值，尤其是验证器质量如何门控增益这一结论对实际部署很有指导意义。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does RoR behave when the verifier is a learned process-reward model or a stronger execution verifier whose q is neither perfect nor random — does the parametric q-axis accurately predict real-verifier performance beyond the two instantiations tested?
- Can the greedy rule be extended to batched / latency-constrained serving where multiple draws of one query run in parallel, recovering the latency advantage of one-round best-of-K while keeping the allocation gains?
- Does the recoverability-asymmetry rationale still hold over proprietary/closed-source model pools where capability and cost gaps are larger, or over agent-style multi-turn tasks rather than single-turn QA?
- The oracle allocation reaches 1.0/0.944/1.0 at cost ~7 — how much of the remaining gap to the ceiling is closed by a better router signal versus more sampling, and can a lightweight learned router be plugged in to sharpen the offline priors p̄_m?
- Since budget-aware best-of-K is competitive on homogeneous/saturated pools while RoR wins on heterogeneous ones, can an online detector decide per-query whether to invoke the full RoR machinery or fall back to cheaper best-of-K?
