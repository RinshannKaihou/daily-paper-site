Key Finding

Resample the committed model or reroute to another — one budget, two competing uses, solved by an online greedy policy.

Resample or Reroute? Budget-Aware Test-Time Model Selection for Large Language Models
Teng-Ruei Chen · NYCU & Krixvon, Taiwan · 2026-07-09 · arXiv:2607.08665 · preprint (not peer-reviewed)

Key Metric

+10.7pts

Accuracy lead of RoR over budget-aware best-of-K on GPQA-Diamond at matched cost — the strongest deployable baseline (not the deliberately-weak single-commit router).

Why This Matters

Unifies best-of-K sampling and model switching under one per-query budget — directly informing low-cost, high-quality LLM serving, with gains gated by verifier quality.

Critical Edge

Gains are verifier-gated: they shrink as the verifier degrades, and even invert against best-of-K on MATH-500 at q=0.6 (0.794 vs 0.803).

Methodology

Resampling the committed model and rerouting to an alternative are cast as competing uses of one per-query budget B under an imperfect verifier. For each query, a pool of models M has per-draw costs c_m; the policy observes draws and verifier scores, then at each step picks RESAMPLE(m) or REROUTE(m') until cost hits B, then returns a final answer — maximizing expected correctness.

Belief update. A posterior mean blends an offline-calibrated prior p̄_m with online successes, with pseudo-count s controlling how strongly the prior anchors the estimate:

p̂_im = ( s · p̄_m + w_im ) / ( s + n_im )

Allocation rule. The greedy action maximizes estimated marginal correctness per unit cost — so resample and reroute are scored on a common scale:

a* = argmax_a Δcorr(a) / cost(a)

Per-step overhead is O(M · B / c_min) time and O(M) memory — negligible beside a single LLM call. Evaluated via deterministic CPU-only NumPy replay over k=30 seed-aligned multi-draw tensors, averaged over 20 draw orderings, sweeping budget B; cost is proxied by parameter count, with a separate OpenRouter-price replay.

Strengths

  • Novel unifying framing. First to treat resample and reroute as competing uses of one explicit per-query budget under an imperfect verifier.
  • High reproducibility. CPU-only NumPy replay, k=30 seed-aligned tensors, 20 draw orderings with reported std, audited environment, stated code release.
  • Honest reporting. Publishes negative results — UCB loses on GPQA at low budget; agreement verifier reverses on multiple-choice.
  • Real-world robustness checks. OpenRouter-price replay, deployable code verifier (1.0% false-accept), label-free agreement verifier — yielding concrete deployment rules.
  • Genuine cost savings, not just accuracy: Pareto-efficient on the accuracy–latency trade-off versus the adaptive cascade.

Limitations

  • Cost model is parameter count — preserves ordering but not real $-ratios; real-price replay is a single July-2026 provider snapshot.
  • Verifier-gated gains. Main table assumes q=1; margins shrink materially as the verifier degrades.
  • Narrow evaluation. Four benchmarks, one 11-model open-weight pool, no proprietary/closed-source models.
  • Sequential latency. 2.5–3.6 rounds per query; batched / latency-constrained serving deferred to future work.
  • Headline numbers overstate the practical margin: single-commit baselines are deliberately weak — the real deployable margin is +2.1 / +10.7 / +10.0.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does RoR behave under a learned process-reward model or execution verifier whose q is neither perfect nor random — does the parametric q-axis predict real performance beyond the two cases tested?
  2. Can the greedy rule extend to batched / latency-constrained serving where draws run in parallel, recovering one-round best-of-K's latency while keeping the allocation gains?
  3. Does the recoverability-asymmetry rationale hold over proprietary pools with larger capability/cost gaps, or over agent-style multi-turn tasks rather than single-turn QA?
  4. The oracle reaches 1.0 / 0.944 / 1.0 near cost 7 — how much of the remaining gap closes via a better router signal versus more sampling?
  5. Since best-of-K is competitive on homogeneous pools while RoR wins on heterogeneous ones, can an online per-query detector decide whether to invoke full RoR or fall back to cheaper best-of-K?
