Do You Need a Frontier Model as a Citation Verifier? Benchmarking Rubric LLMs for Deep-Research Source Attribution
Ethan Leung, Elias Lumer, Corey Feld, Austin Huber, Vamse Kumar Subbiak, Kevin Paul  ·  PwC U.S.
LLM-as-judge citation verification rubric RL / RLVR deep-research agents benchmark cost-quality decoupling
01 Key metric
49×

Per-decision cost spread across the 8 judges — yet cost does not predict accuracy. The cheapest tier contains the most competitive models on the dimensions that matter.

02 Why this matters

The default “use the biggest model as judge” in deep-research and RAG pipelines is wrong. Cheap judges work — but their directional bias needs separate calibration before they feed reward signals.

Directly relevant to LLM-as-judge selection, grounding/citation eval, and rubric-based RLVR reward design.

03 Critical edge

Gold labels come from an LLM council — and 2 of the 8 tested judges sit inside it. So “agreement with gold” partly measures “agreement with the council.” The benchmark's most important caveat.

Methodology

Built on the two-stage source-attribution pipeline of Onweller et al. (2026): an AST-based parser extracts attribution–citation pairs from markdown long-form output, then a three-dimension evaluator scores each pair on link accessibility (deterministic HTTP 200, excluded), source relevance (LLM-judged), and factual support (LLM-judged).

A clean long-form report was generated across 25 domains (Amtrak, Pokémon, black holes, artisan sourdough, Bhutanese music…). ~60% of claims were adversarially edited using 19 strategies (wrong date/entity, negation, fabrication, source mismatch, semantic drift, reversed causation, minimization…), yielding 624 pairs. Gold labels came from a council of 6 judges, human-reviewed and adjudicated on every disagreement.

Strengths & limitations

Strengths
+Clean, focused question — “do you need a frontier model as a citation verifier?” — with a directly actionable answer for practitioners.
+Reports the metrics that actually matter for reward design (pass-rate drift, FPR, FNR), not just scalar F1.
+Honest about null results — explicitly states the factual-support CIs overlap; no winner overclaimed.
+Concrete cost-quality decoupling — cheapest tier is competitive, contradicting the “biggest model” heuristic.
+The 378-case adjudicated subset shows full-set F1 is a poor proxy for hard-case reliability.
Limitations
−All results derive from a single adversarial document; cross-genre/length/density generalization untested.
−LLM-council gold labels with 2 of 8 tested judges inside — structural contamination risk.
−Factual support under-powered: 18.4% gold pass + overlapping CIs cannot statistically separate judges.
−No downstream RLVR experiment — reward-hacking/over-hedging argued, not measured.
−Source-relevance rubric conflates with factual support (Appendix A) — dominant disagreement source, un-remediated.
−Benchmark/gold-label data release status unclear; core numbers not independently reproducible.
Q.

Open questions for further exploration

  1. Would tightening the source-relevance rubric to exclude the factual-support overlap (acknowledged in Appendix A) materially raise inter-judge agreement and source-relevance F1?
  2. How do FPR/FNR rankings change if the 0.5 binarization threshold is swept, or if judges output calibrated probabilities instead of hard binaries?
  3. Does the predicted reward-hacking (high-FPR) and over-hedging (high-FNR) actually materialize when a policy is RL-trained against each judge on the same rubric?
  4. Do the cost-quality and bias findings transfer to multi-document benchmarks and to non-English / non-web source corpora?
  5. Can ensembling or pairwise-agreement filtering of judges (§5.4) recover discriminability on factual support where single-model CIs overlap?
