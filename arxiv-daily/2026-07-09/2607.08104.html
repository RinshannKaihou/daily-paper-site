Critical Edge

01 Methodology

A finite-sum risk min f(x) = (1/n)Σfᵢ(x) is analyzed for strongly-convex, convex, and nonconvex f. Two assumptions replace the textbook bounded-variance + L-smooth pair:

(A2.1) Hölder gradient  ‖∇f(x)−∇f(y)‖ ≤ L‖x−y‖ν, ν∈(0,1]. ν=1 recovers L-smoothness; ν<1 weakens it.
(A2.2) Bounded p-th moment  E‖G−∇f‖p ≤ σp, p∈(1,2]. p=2 is bounded variance; p<2 means heavy tails.

Algorithm 1 — Vanilla SGD with Momentum
m_t := β·m_{t-1} + (1−β)·∇f_{S_t}(x_t)
x_{t+1} := x_t − η_t · m_t
β=0 → plain vanilla SGD. No clipping. No normalization. Anywhere.

The technical crux: under L-smoothness + heavy tails the term E‖d_t‖² every analysis needs is unbounded — the descent lemma collapses. Switching to a Hölder descent lemma (bounds ‖d_t‖ν+1 instead) rescues it; the Lyapunov step E‖X‖ν+1 ≤ (E‖X‖p)(ν+1)/p is exactly where the constraint ν+1≤p is forced.

Six theorems cover all six (objective × momentum) cells. The three momentum theorems (3.2 / 3.4 / 3.6) are the genuinely new results; convex/nonconvex ones add a standard bounded-iterate assumption. All rates recover the classical ladder at ν=1, p=2.

03 Strengths

  • First principled proof that the most widely used optimizer (SGD+momentum) converges in expectation under heavy-tailed noise with zero gradient surgery — a genuinely useful negative-result-adjacent theorem.
  • Clean, unified Hölder-smoothness framework handles all three convexity regimes and recovers the entire classical rate ladder at ν=1, p=2.
  • Transparent, non-overclaimed positioning: repeatedly stresses its rates are sub-optimal and serves them as a baseline — the honest way to present a "vanilla still works" result.
  • Table 1 comparison matrix lays out ~14 prior results by assumptions (smoothness, clipping, normalization, momentum) and rate — making the novelty verifiable at a glance.

04 Limitations

  • Momentum theorems use constant step sizes tuned against T; the practically appealing decreasing-step form isn't the focus, and the optimal η(T) schedule is asserted, not derived.
  • All real-data evidence is a single small LM training-loss curve — no downstream metrics, no clipped-SGD comparison, no larger-scale validation.
  • The pivotal ν+1≤p condition is only controllable on synthetic functions; ν is not estimable in practice, so practical reach is uncertain.
  • Bounded-iterate Assumption 3.1 is still needed for convex/nonconvex momentum results — partially re-introducing the boundedness the paper sets out to avoid.
  • Heatmaps report seed averages with shaded min/max bands but no formal statistical tests, so the "near-necessary" claim for ν+1≤p is qualitative.

05 Questions for Further Exploration

Q1 · SCHEDULECan constant-step momentum theorems be re-proven with an explicit optimal η(T)=Θ(T−1/p) schedule whose rate is derived, closing the gap to O(T−(p−1)/2p)?
Q2 · ASSUMPTIONSIs bounded-iterate Assumption 3.1 removable for convex/nonconvex momentum via a coercivity argument, or inherent to the heavy-tailed + momentum combination?
Q3 · HETEROGENEITYHow do rates change under coordinate-wise or layer-wise heterogeneity in p (realistic in deep networks, where different parameters see different tail indices)?
Q4 · SCALEDoes ν+1≤p stay predictive on billion-parameter training if ν is estimated online from gradient-moment statistics — where ν is not controlled?
Q5 · BASELINESA direct comparison vs clipped SGD (Zhang '20) and AdaBelief-style adaptive methods under controlled heavy-tailed noise would isolate how much of normalization's advantage is tail mitigation vs step-size adaptation.
Q6 · PRACTICEGiven ν is not estimable, can the theory yield a usable runtime diagnostic (e.g. an online p-estimate guardrail) rather than an unverifiable feasibility condition?
