> **一句话总结：** 本文通过对 GPQA Diamond 和 AIME 上 53 个运行者、265,000 条采样的跨运行者审计发现，自一致性（模型自身多次采样的一致性）只是正确性的"弱且依赖场景"的代理指标：在前沿模型 gpt-4.1 上甚至出现高一致性（C=0.89）却校准最差（ECE=0.41）、48% 的高置信答案是错的，且这些高置信错误跨不同运行者、提示词乃至跨厂商（GPT 与 Claude）反复出现——因此不应把"一致即正确"作为路由/级联/拒答的可信阈值。

> **TL;DR:** A large-scale cross-runner audit (53 runners, K=50, 265,000 samples on GPQA Diamond and AIME) shows that LLM self-consistency agreement is only a positive-but-weak predictor of correctness (Spearman ρ 0.20–0.59, all significant under item-clustered resampling), and it breaks down most on the most consistent frontier model: gpt-4.1 reaches C=0.89 on GPQA yet has the lowest ρ(C,M)=0.20 and worst ECE=0.41, with 48% of its high-agreement (C≥0.8) answers wrong — so confidence-routed cascades toward it are dominated by simply always using the mid-tier model, and confident errors recur across runners, prompts, and even providers (GPT vs Claude agree on 48–63% of shared wrong items).

## 核心贡献

- **大规模跨运行者审计框架**：在 GPQA Diamond 与 AIME 两个基准上，53 个运行者各采集 K=50 样本，共 265,000 条样本，使用分层（运行者聚类）自助法与 Holm 多重比较校正，首次系统审计"自一致性作为置信度/拒答/路由信号"的真实可靠性。
- **揭示前沿模型"过度自信"悖论**：在 gpt-4.1 家族内，前沿模型 gpt-4.1 一致性最高（C=0.89），但 ρ(C,M) 最低（0.20）、ECE 最差（0.41），且在 77% 的 GPQA 案例上 C≥0.8，其中 48% 是错的——与已知的"后训练校准退化"一致。
- **复发 vs. 噪声的分离 + 选项打乱对照**：证明 gpt-4.1 上 28% 的案例被每个运行者都答错（50% 至少被一个答错），这些错误是跨运行者/提示词复发的；选项打乱对照揭示 gpt-4.1-mini 准确率从 0.50 掉到 0.29，且模型系统性回避"D"（约 16% vs 25% 均匀基线），即部分"一致性"是位置先验而非语义。
- **诚实的零/边际结果**：链式思维（CoT）确实提升准确率（ΔA=+0.067 GPQA），但对一致性-正确性信号只是边际改善；基于一致性的级联路由虽然胜过随机路由（峰值 0.36 vs 0.34），却被"始终用中端模型"（0.40）压制。
- **跨家族探测共享偏差**：探索性复现在 Claude（haiku/sonnet/opus）三层上，opus 同样最自信（C=0.94）、最不准、校准最差；且 GPT 与 Claude 在共同答错的题目上 48–63% 选了同一错误选项（haiku p=.003、sonnet p=.005），说明"跨模型一致"本身也可能是共享偏差而非独立佐证。
- **发布去标识化数据与流水线**：开源不含 GPQA 题面、含每运行者-每案例答案分布与 C/A/M 的 schema 校验数据集，支持复现。

## Key Contributions

- **A large-scale (265,000-sample) cross-runner audit** of self-consistency as a confidence/routing/abstention signal, spanning GPQA Diamond and AIME, with 53 runners each drawing K=50 samples and a hierarchical runner-clustered bootstrap with Holm multiplicity control — the first controlled, cross-replicated test of whether the agreement signal that downstream systems already rely on actually tracks correctness.
- **Quantification of the "frontier over-confidence" paradox**: within the gpt-4.1 family the frontier model is the most self-consistent (C=0.89 on GPQA, C≥0.8 on 77% of cases) yet has the lowest agreement-correctness correlation (ρ(C,M)=0.20) and worst calibration (ECE=0.41 vs 0.22 for CoT-mini), with 48% of its high-agreement answers wrong.
- **Separation of recurring from stochastic confident-wrongness**, plus an option-shuffle control showing mini accuracy drops from 0.50 to 0.29 under permutation and "D" is systematically under-selected (~16% vs 25% uniform), so part of GPQA "confidence" is positional rather than semantic.
- **Honest null/marginal findings**: CoT robustly improves accuracy (ΔA=+0.067 on GPQA, +0.069 on AIME) but only marginally improves the agreement-correctness signal, and a confidence-routed cascade (peak 0.36) is dominated by always using the mid-tier model (0.40).
- **An exploratory cross-family check** on three Claude tiers reproduces the frontier over-confidence (opus C=0.94, C≥0.8 on 88%, ECE=0.35, accuracy 0.58 trailing sonnet's 0.63), and shows GPT and Claude pick the same wrong answer on 48–63% of shared-wrong items (significant for haiku p=.003 and sonnet p=.005), implying cross-model agreement can encode shared bias, not independent confirmation.
- **A released de-identified, schema-validated dataset and analysis pipeline** (per-runner/per-case rows with answer distributions and C/A/M, no GPQA question text) sufficient to reproduce the clustered analysis.

## Methodology

The study is a secondary meta-analysis of model-output data produced by 53 graduate-course runners. Each runner was assigned one of three controlled two-condition comparisons on the gpt-4.1 family at temperature=1.0, K=50:

- **Axis A — model tier**: nano vs. mini (zero-shot).
- **Axis B — prompt strategy**: mini zero-shot vs. mini chain-of-thought.
- **Axis C — mid vs. frontier**: mini vs. gpt-4.1 (zero-shot).

Runners wrote their own prompts (prompt wording is treated as part of the measurement variance) but each runner ran both conditions of its axis on the same overlapping cases, yielding 5,300 case-result rows over 394 unique cases with a mean of 2.5 runners per (condition, case) cell. Benchmarks were chosen to bracket opposite ends of an answer-space spectrum: GPQA Diamond (graduate-level four-option multiple choice, {A,B,C,D}, where high C is the discriminating regime) and AIME (integer-answer competition math, {0,…,999}, where C is floored for any non-reasoning model).

Per (case, condition) the paper computes self-consistency C = n_maj/K, sample accuracy A = n_correct/K, and majority-correct indicator M = 𝟙[majority answer = ground truth]; because deployed majority-vote systems return the majority answer, M is the primary outcome and C is treated as the deployed confidence score under audit. The primary statistic is Spearman ρ. Because rows are not i.i.d. (each runner contributes many rows; each case is seen by many runners), all confidence intervals use a hierarchical runner-clustered bootstrap (resample runners with replacement, then cases within runner; B=2000), with a case-clustered bootstrap and a leave-one-runner check as robustness. ECE and Brier are reported as operational diagnostics of deployment use. Two post-hoc focal contrasts (Axis-B CoT effect on M, Axis-C frontier degradation on ρ(C,M)) are reported with a Bonferroni-for-two check; Holm correction is applied only to the family of twelve per-cell ρ(C,M) tests. Additional studies include: a confidence-baseline comparison (verbalized confidence, P(True), and a rank-average combo on gpt-4.1-mini, 50 cases each), an option-shuffle control (K=50 under original order and two random permutations on 48 GPQA cases), a stop-when-consensus adaptive-sampling simulation, a cost-aware C-routed cascade simulation, a controlled logged-snapshot re-run (gpt-4.1-2025-04-14 vs gpt-4.1-mini-2025-04-14, K=20, 48 GPQA cases), and an exploratory agent-mediated cross-family check on Claude haiku/sonnet/opus (K=10, 48 GPQA / 24 AIME cases).

## Results & Highlights

- **Agreement is a positive but weak predictor everywhere**: all twelve per-cell ρ(C,M) values are positive (range 0.20–0.59) and survive Holm correction (adj. p ≤ 0.0024) and remain positive/excluding-zero under a case-clustered bootstrap (Table 4); no cell exceeds ρ(C,M)=0.6, so most correctness variance is unexplained by agreement (Table 1).
- **Frontier over-confidence is the headline failure**: on GPQA, gpt-4.1 has the highest mean C (0.89) yet the lowest ρ(C,M) (0.20) and worst ECE (0.41) vs 0.22 for CoT-mini; a high-agreement (C≥0.8) gpt-4.1 answer on GPQA is wrong 48% of the time (95% CI [.40,.56]) (Tables 1, 5; Figure 1). The frontier degradation reproduces on AIME (Δρ(C,M)=−0.26 [−0.40,−0.12], p=6×10⁻⁴) and under a logged-snapshot re-run (gpt-4.1 mean C 0.89 vs mini 0.82 at identical majority accuracy 0.48, ECE 0.41 vs 0.34).
- **CoT improves accuracy but only marginally the signal**: paired ΔA=+0.067 on GPQA (Wilcoxon p=2.1×10⁻⁵) and +0.069 on AIME (p=2.9×10⁻⁴), but the effect on the primary ρ(C,M) is only borderline (+0.123 [−0.030,0.265], p=0.025 on GPQA; null on AIME).
- **Confidence-routed cascade is dominated by always-mini**: a C-routed cascade peaks at 0.36 vs 0.34 for random routing, but always-mini reaches 0.40, always-gpt-4.1 only 0.33, and an oracle router 0.44 (Table 9).
- **Confident errors recur and are partly shared across providers**: on gpt-4.1/GPQA, 28% of unique cases are confidently wrong for every runner and 50% for at least one; GPT and Claude pick the same wrong option on 48–63% of shared-wrong items (haiku p=.003, sonnet p=.005), and six items are answered wrongly by all four models (Table 8).
- **Position bias inflates agreement**: option-shuffling drops gpt-4.1-mini point-estimate accuracy from 0.50 [0.35,0.65] to 0.29 [0.19,0.41], and "D" is under-selected at ~16% (mini) / ~15% (gpt-4.1) vs a 25% uniform baseline (Figure 4).
- **Adaptive sampling works even where abstention does not**: a stop-when-consensus rule uses 60% fewer samples on average (19.8 vs 50) at equal majority error (0.603), and 82% savings on CoT-mini.
- **Cross-family frontier over-confidence recurs**: Claude opus is most self-consistent (C=0.94, C≥0.8 on 88%) yet trails sonnet in accuracy (0.58 vs 0.63) and has the worst ECE (0.35) and a lower ρ(C,M) (0.44 vs sonnet's 0.74) (Table 7).

## Authors & Credibility

- Sole author **Kaihua Ding**, University of Pennsylvania (Wharton AI & Analytics Initiative, AI Education Innovation Fund; NAIRR Pilot / Jetstream2 allocation NAIRR250223). The author's self-cited prior work spans output-based error estimation (PhD thesis, Michigan, 2018) and AI-system evaluation/assessment design (preprints 2025), directly relevant to the clustered-estimation and measurement framing used here.
- The dataset comes from 53 graduate-course runners, each independently submitting model-output rows; the author performs a secondary de-identified meta-analysis and is explicit that runners "shared a course context and may have exchanged code or discussion, so runs are independently submitted but not guaranteed statistically independent" — a dependency the hierarchical runner-clustered bootstrap is designed to correct.
- Data and code are publicly released at the author's GitHub (de-identified per-runner/per-case rows with answer distributions and C/A/M), with GPQA question text withheld per its access terms.
- The work is unusually self-critical: it openly reports borderline CIs (e.g., E1's ΔM CI crosses zero), declares endpoints as post-hoc/not-preregistered, and explicitly states no inferential claim is preregistered.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: The core conceptual point — "agreement is not accuracy, and self-consistency is a self-referential analogue of self-preference bias" — is not new (it echoes Panickssery et al. 2024, Kadavath et al. 2022, and the post-RLHF calibration regression in OpenAI 2023). The genuine novelty is the *quantification under a controlled cross-replicated design*: separating recurring confident errors from sampling noise via overlapping runner assignments, and the cross-family shared-error test with a label-permutation null. These are real contributions over prior consistency-as-hallucination-signal work (SelfCheckGPT, semantic entropy).
- **Rigor**: Strong on inference hygiene — hierarchical runner-clustered bootstrap with B=2000, Holm correction over the twelve ρ(C,M) tests, case-clustered robustness, leave-one-runner check, and a controlled logged-snapshot re-run. However, the central limitations are serious: (1) **model snapshots and run timestamps were never logged and are unrecoverable**, so the replication is across runners/prompts, not across time, and the paper cannot verify the course runs occurred under an identical backend snapshot; the controlled re-run is underpowered (K=20, 48 cases) and its individual ρ/ECE gaps are not significant (Δρ=−0.06 [−0.37,0.26]). (2) Runners are a graduate-course cohort, not independent labs — a clustering artifact the bootstrap mitigates but cannot fully remove. (3) A minority of GPQA items are flagged as ambiguous or mislabeled (e.g., one B-keyed item every model answers D), which inflates apparent confident-wrongness.
- **Reproducibility**: Good in principle (released de-identified data + code, documented bootstrap protocol), but materially weakened by the unlogged snapshots — a third party could reproduce the *analysis* but not the *raw runs* on a verified identical model.
- **Significance**: Practically important for anyone building confidence cascades, adaptive-sampling, or multi-judge panels: the result that routing toward the most consistent frontier model is counterproductive (always-mini 0.40 beats C-routed cascade 0.36) is a concrete, deployable caution. The cross-family shared-error finding (GPT and Claude agree on the same wrong answers above a marginal-preserving null) is the most novel and consequential result, though it is explicitly exploratory and agent-mediated with K=10 and no temperature control.
- **Red flags / overclaiming**: The paper is commendably restrained — it does not claim self-consistency is useless (it explicitly endorses adaptive sampling) and labels its cross-family check as exploratory. The main overclaim risk is scope: the entire primary audit is a single provider family (gpt-4.1) on two benchmarks (four-option MCQ and integer math), with no open-ended generation, no code, and no reasoning-trained models. Generalization to free-form generation and to non-MCQ settings is untested. The weakest specific weakness: the verbalized-confidence baseline parsed on only 39/50 GPQA cases and 3/50 AIME cases, making the "no signal dominates" comparison between self-consistency and verbalized confidence materially underpowered and the AURC comparison not directly comparable (the paper flags this in a footnote but it undercuts Table 6).

## Strengths

- Exceptional statistical hygiene: hierarchical cluster bootstrap, Holm correction, permutation tests for correlation differences, case-clustered robustness, and honest reporting of borderline CIs.
- The recurrence-vs-noise separation is a genuinely useful methodological move that prior consistency work lacked.
- The option-shuffle control is a clean, convincing demonstration that a measurable fraction of MCQ "confidence" is positional bias, not semantic certainty.
- The cross-family shared-error permutation test directly addresses the most important practical question ("if two different providers agree, is that safer?") and gives a sobering negative-leaning answer.
- Honest about its own weaknesses: the limitations and ethics sections are unusually candid about snapshot logging, ambiguous items, and the exploratory status of the Claude check.

## Limitations

- **Single-provider primary audit** (gpt-4.1 family only); the Claude check is agent-mediated, K=10, no temperature control, internal reasoning cannot be disabled, and absolute values are not comparable across families.
- **No logged model snapshots or timestamps** — the most damaging limitation; the replication is across runners and prompts, not across time or backend version.
- **Only two benchmarks**, both constrained-answer (MCQ and integer math); no open-ended generation, code, or longer-form reasoning tasks.
- **K=50 leaves substantial measurement noise**; the confidence-baseline and shuffle controls use only 50 and 48 cases respectively, so their CIs are wide.
- **Runner-implemented prompts** add variance, and runners are a non-independent course cohort.
- **A minority of GPQA items appear ambiguous or mislabeled**, which inflates apparent confident-wrongness and is not fully controlled for.
- Does not study reasoning-trained (o1-style) models, whose internal reasoning may already supply the calibration lift measured for prompted CoT.

## Relevance

这项工作与「LLM 置信度估计 / 自一致性 / 校准」以及「LLM-as-judge / 多评判系统」方向高度相关——它直接审计了路由、级联、自适应采样和拒答系统中被默认采用的"一致性即正确"假设，给出了"何时可用、何时失效"的量化地图，对构建可信 LLM 评估与推理时计算调度具有直接的工程与安全性指导价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the frontier over-confidence paradox hold for reasoning-trained models (e.g., o1/R1-style systems) whose internal chain-of-thought may already calibrate the output distribution, and does explicit CoT become redundant there?
- How does the agreement-correctness relationship change on free-form generation and code tasks where the answer space is unbounded (where C cannot be trivially computed as n_maj/K without a semantic-equivalence step)?
- Can a corrected confidence signal — e.g., self-consistency *deflated* by an estimated option-position-prior term, or combined with a cross-family disagreement penalty — recover the discriminative power that raw C loses on frontier models?
- Are the shared cross-family confident errors concentrated in specific knowledge domains (memorized facts, common misconceptions) that could be detected and excluded a priori?
- What is the effect of K (sample budget) on the frontier over-confidence gap — does it shrink, persist, or worsen as K grows, and is there a K at which C becomes trustworthy again on gpt-4.1-class models?
