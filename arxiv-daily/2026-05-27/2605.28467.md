> **一句话总结：** 提出激活一致性训练（ACT），通过在推理模型思维链生成之前的激活层施加一致性约束来防御越狱和提示注入攻击，在自适应攻击下显著优于输出级一致性训练（BCT），并揭示防御机制可被编码为激活空间中的一个近似线性方向。

> **TL;DR:** Activation Consistency Training (ACT) defends reasoning LLMs against jailbreaks and prompt injection by enforcing hidden-state consistency at the assistant-turn boundary—before chain-of-thought generation. ACT outperforms output-level consistency training (BCT) under adaptive attacks across five reasoning models, and its defense mechanism is interpretable as a single linear "refusal direction" in activation space.

## 核心贡献

- 将激活一致性训练（ACT）形式化为推理模型的提示注入防御方法，仅需自监督的干净-包裹提示对，无需固定标签
- 在五个推理模型（Qwen3-1.7B, Qwen3-8B, Gemma-4-E4B-it, GPT-OSS-20B, Phi-4-reasoning）上系统评估了ACT和BCT在静态和自适应攻击下的表现
- 提供了机制性证据：ACT的防御编码为助手轮次边界处的近似线性激活偏移，可恢复出单一转向方向双向控制推理模型的拒绝行为
- 证明ACT在思维链被替换为顺从性推理轨迹时仍能保持拒绝能力，而BCT则倾向于跟随预填充的顺从推理

## Key Contributions

- **ACT as prompt-injection defense:** Formulates Activation Consistency Training as a defense against indirect prompt injection, requiring only self-supervised pairs of clean and adversarially-wrapped prompts (no fixed labels needed).
- **Generalization to reasoning models:** Evaluates both ACT and BCT across five reasoning model families on both jailbreak and prompt-injection settings, with adaptive GRPO-trained attackers for realistic threat modeling.
- **Mechanistic interpretability:** Demonstrates that ACT's defense is encoded as a roughly linear shift in activation space at the assistant-turn boundary. A single recoverable steering direction bidirectionally controls refusal behavior with minimal effect on benign inputs at moderate strengths.
- **Robustness to CoT manipulation:** Shows ACT remains robust even when the model's chain-of-thought is replaced with a compliant trace from the undefended base model, pivoting to refuse prefilled jailbreaks—while BCT follows the compliant reasoning trajectory.

## Methodology

**Activation Consistency Training (ACT):** For each training pair (p_clean, p_wrapped), ACT minimizes the squared L2 distance between hidden states of the wrapped prompt and the cached clean-prompt activations at shared suffix positions, across all transformer layers. The shared suffix typically comprises ~6 tokens (end-of-user markers, assistant-turn opener, and first thinking-channel tokens), identified by walking backward from both chat-templated sequences.

**Training setup:** ACT uses LoRA fine-tuning. Clean-prompt activations are pre-computed and cached, requiring only a single forward-backward pass per step on the wrapped prompt. A benign regularization term (lambda-weighted loss on clean-clean pairs from Alpaca) mitigates utility degradation.

**Prompt-injection pairs:** 500 naive + forged-completion injections from Cleaned-Alpaca (90/10 mix), plus 500 benign Alpaca prompts. Evaluated on OPI (680 examples, 5 attack formats), AlpacaFarm (208 examples, 4 attack types), and SEP (held-out benchmark).

**Jailbreak pairs:** ~100 (clean harmful request, jailbreak rewrite) pairs per model using PAIR-generated attacks, plus 100 benign Alpaca prompts. Scored with StrongReject (ASR threshold 0.5).

**Adaptive attacks:** Per-target GRPO-trained reinforcement learning attackers optimized directly against each defended model, representing a realistic deployment threat model.

**Mechanistic analysis:** ACT direction defined as d_ACT[l] = normalize(E_i[h_ACT[l, t_i] - h_base[l, t_i]]). Inference-time steering with +/-alpha * d_ACT tests causal control of refusal. Compliant-CoT prefill intervention tests whether defense is encoded upstream of reasoning.

## Results & Highlights

- **Static prompt injection:** ACT and BCT both drive ASR near zero on Qwen3-1.7B, Qwen3-8B, and Gemma-4-E4B-it. BCT slightly outperforms ACT on GPT-OSS-20B (OPI ASR 4.4% vs 29.6%).
- **Adaptive prompt injection:** ACT consistently outperforms BCT on every model except Phi-4-reasoning. On GPT-OSS-20B, BCT leads on static evals but ACT overtakes under adaptive attack (57.4% vs 37.0% ASR).
- **Static jailbreaks:** ACT reaches <=4% ASR on 4/5 models. Both defenses substantially reduce ASR from baseline.
- **Adaptive jailbreaks:** ACT drops adaptive attacker ASR to near zero on 3/5 models. BCT reduces attack effectiveness but much less reliably.
- **Utility:** Both methods roughly preserve MMLU and OPI clean accuracy, within a few points of undefended baselines. ACT lags slightly behind on MMLU for jailbreak checkpoints.
- **Compliant-CoT prefill:** ACT pivots to refusal within the first sentence even when given a compliant chain-of-thought from the base model. BCT follows the prefilled compliant reasoning and produces harmful responses.
- **Linear refusal direction:** On Qwen3-8B, adding d_ACT to the base model at alpha=10 induces near-complete refusal on jailbreaks; subtracting it from the ACT model restores compliance. False refusals on benign prompts stay near zero through alpha=5.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** ACT itself was introduced by Irpan et al. (2025) for non-reasoning models. This paper's novelty lies in extending it to reasoning models, formulating it as a prompt-injection defense, and providing mechanistic interpretability. The mechanistic findings (linear refusal direction, CoT-prefill robustness) are genuinely insightful.
- **Rigor:** Strong experimental design with five diverse reasoning models, three PI benchmarks, both static and adaptive attacks, and utility measurements. The adaptive GRPO attacker is a significant strength, addressing the "attacker moves second" critique. However, Phi-4-reasoning and Gemma-4-E4B-it remain weak spots that are not fully resolved.
- **Reproducibility:** Training data is from public sources (Alpaca, PAIR, HarmBench). LoRA training with cached activations is computationally accessible. The shared suffix extraction procedure is clearly described. Likely reproducible.
- **Significance:** Addresses a critical gap—reasoning models' extended CoT as a new attack surface. The finding that ACT encodes refusal *before* CoT generation (not within it) has important implications for the broader safety training paradigm. The recoverable linear refusal direction opens paths for training-free defenses.
- **Red flags:** Training sets are small (~100 pairs per model for jailbreaks), raising questions about scaling to production. Neither defense achieves 100% security. Agentic prompt injection is explicitly left as future work, limiting immediate practical impact for tool-using deployments.

## Strengths

- Comprehensive evaluation across five reasoning model families with both static and adaptive (GRPO) attackers
- Mechanistic interpretability analysis reveals a clean, interpretable linear refusal direction—rare for training-based defenses
- Compliant-CoT prefill experiment elegantly demonstrates ACT's robustness to reasoning manipulation
- Shared suffix extraction is model-agnostic and requires no assumptions about adversarial content distribution
- Both methods are self-supervised, requiring no human-labeled refusal data

## Limitations

- Small training sets (~100 jailbreak pairs per model) may not capture the full diversity of adversarial strategies
- Neither ACT nor BCT achieves robust defense on all models (Phi-4-reasoning and Gemma-4-E4B-it remain vulnerable to adaptive attacks)
- Evaluation limited to non-agentic, single-turn prompt injection; agentic/tool-use settings untested
- White-box attacks with direct gradient access not evaluated
- Unclear how ACT-defended models behave under further downstream fine-tuning
- ACT loss applied uniformly across all layers—no investigation of layer-specific or head-specific supervision (noted as future work)

## Relevance

本文直接回应了推理模型（如o1/o3、DeepSeek-R1等）在长思维链下面临的新型安全威胁。ACT提供了一种计算高效且可解释的防御范式，对关注LLM安全对齐、提示注入防御和模型可解释性的研究者具有重要参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does ACT perform when scaled to larger training sets (1000+ pairs) and applied to frontier-scale reasoning models?
- Can ACT be composed with SecAlign-style preference optimization for additive robustness?
- Which specific layers, attention heads, or token positions are most load-bearing for ACT's defense?
- Does the recovered linear refusal direction generalize across model families, or is it model-specific?
- How does ACT interact with agentic prompt injection, where untrusted documents are retrieved mid-trajectory?
- Can inference-time steering with d_ACT approximate ACT's effect without any fine-tuning, and at what robustness cost?
