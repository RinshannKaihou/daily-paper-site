Single-Rollout Hidden-State Dynamics for Training-Free RLVR Data Selection

[Jianghao Wu, Jianfei Cai, Weiqiang Wang, Jin Ye, Daniel F. Schmidt, Yasmeen George]

arXiv: 2605.28631

Date: 2026-05-27

Tags: [RLVR, data-selection, reasoning, hidden-states, LLM]

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.28631

一句话总结： 提出SHIFT，通过单次推理的隐藏状态变化（RIRS）在无需训练和标签的条件下，从大规模无标注池中筛选少量高效RLVR训练样本，在超低预算下显著优于已有方法。
TL;DR: SHIFT selects high-impact RLVR training examples from an unlabeled pool using a single inference rollout per candidate. It computes the Reasoning-Induced Representation Shift (RIRS) as a training-free utility proxy and uses quality-weighted farthest-first CoreSet in an RIRS-augmented feature space to balance informativeness and diversity—achieving strong in-domain and OOD gains with as little as 0.1–2% of the training pool.

核心贡献

  • 形式化了pre-RL数据选择问题： 在大规模无标注池上、无任何训练信号和标签的条件下，选出少量样本后再标注并进行RLVR训练。
  • 提出RIRS（推理诱导表示位移）： 仅通过一次确定性推理的前后隐藏状态差作为样本效用的轻量代理指标，无需ground-truth或reward评估。
  • 设计质量加权最远优先CoreSet算法： 在RIRS增强特征空间中同时优化信息量与多样性，支持大规模候选池。
  • 超低预算下全面领先： 在数学推理（MATH-500, AMC）和医学QA（MedQA, MedXpertQA）等多个benchmark上，仅用0.1%–4%数据即超越多种无训练baseline，部分场景甚至超越全量RLVR。

Key Contributions

  • Formalizes RLVR data selection in a pre-RL regime where no training-time signals or labels are available on the full candidate pool.
  • Introduces RIRS (Reasoning-Induced Representation Shift): the start-to-end hidden-state delta from a single deterministic rollout as a training-free proxy for instance utility.
  • Proposes a quality-weighted farthest-first CoreSet algorithm in an RIRS-augmented feature space to jointly optimize informativeness and coverage.
  • Demonstrates consistent gains across mathematical reasoning and medical QA at ultra-low budgets (0.1%–4%), sometimes surpassing full-data RLVR on OOD benchmarks.

Methodology

SHIFT operates in three stages:

  1. Single-Rollout Inference: For each candidate instance $x$, the base LLM performs one deterministic (greedy, T=0) reasoning trace. Hidden states are extracted at the start ($s(x)$) and end ($e(x)$) of the trace, averaged across all transformer layers.
  2. RIRS Computation: The reasoning-induced representation shift is $\Delta(x) = e(x) - s(x)$. The utility proxy is $q(x) = \|\Delta(x)\|_2$, log-compressed to $\tilde{q}(x) = \log(1 + q(x))$. The feature vector for coverage is $\phi(x) = [s(x); \Delta(x)]$, L2-normalized.
  3. Quality-Weighted Farthest-First Selection: Initialize with the highest-$\tilde{q}$ instance. Iteratively add instances maximizing $\tilde{q}(x) \cdot d(x, S)$ where $d(x,S)$ is the min distance to already-selected instances in $\phi$-space.

Only the selected subset receives verifiable reward annotation; RLVR (GRPO) is then trained on this small set.

  • At 2% budget on MATH-500, SHIFT exceeds full-data RLVR on AMC (38.55 vs 33.73), demonstrating OOD generalization from few impactful examples.
  • At 0.1% budget on MedQA, SHIFT nearly matches full-data RLVR on MedXpertQA-U (12.22 vs 12.39).
  • Ablation confirms both RIRS-based coverage ($\phi$-space CoreSet) and quality-weighting contribute complementary gains.
  • RIRS ranking strongly correlates with actual per-instance RLVR gain (Spearman ρ = 0.818, p = 0.0038).

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. The idea of using inference-time hidden-state dynamics as a training-free data selection signal for RLVR is new and well-motivated. The RIRS formulation is simple yet effective.
  • Rigor: Strong. Systematic comparison against 6+ baselines across 2 domains, multiple budgets, and 7+ evaluation benchmarks including OOD transfer. Ablations isolate contributions of each component.
  • Reproducibility: Good. Code is released, experimental details (GRPO hyperparameters, GPU setup, evaluation protocol) are provided. Experiments use publicly available models (Qwen3-1.7B, Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B).
  • Significance: High for the low-budget RLVR setting. The ability to identify impactful examples without any training or labels is practically important for specialized domains.
  • Red flags: Experiments are limited to relatively small models (1.5–1.7B parameters). Scaling behavior on larger models is not demonstrated. The RIRS computation still requires one full inference pass per candidate, which may be non-trivial for very large pools despite being cheaper than training.

Strengths

  • Elegant formulation: RIRS is simple to compute yet captures meaningful reasoning dynamics.
  • Truly training-free and label-free at selection time—addresses a real gap in existing pipelines.
  • Strong empirical results at ultra-low budgets, with convincing OOD generalization.
  • Direct validation of RIRS–RLVR gain correlation (Spearman ρ = 0.818).
  • Clean algorithmic design (Algorithm 1) that is easy to implement.

Limitations

  • Only tested on small models (1.5–1.7B); unclear if RIRS scales to 7B+ models where hidden-state dynamics may differ.
  • One rollout per candidate still costs $O(N)$ inference passes; wall-clock comparison with training-time baselines is not provided.
  • Limited to two task families (math and medical QA); generalization to code generation, scientific reasoning, or open-ended tasks is unexplored.
  • The quality-weighted farthest-first greedy heuristic has no formal approximation guarantee.
  • RIRS uses layer-averaged hidden states; layer-wise analysis is absent.

Relevance

本文提出的SHIFT方法对于低资源场景下的RLVR训练具有直接实用价值，特别是在标注昂贵的专业领域（如医学、法律）中，能以极低成本筛选出高效训练样本。其基于推理时隐藏状态动态的思路也为理解LLM内部推理过程提供了新视角。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does RIRS remain effective as a utility proxy for larger models (7B, 70B) where layer-wise hidden-state dynamics may be qualitatively different?
  2. Can RIRS be combined with training-time signals in a two-stage pipeline (SHIFT for initial filtering, then training-time refinement)?
  3. How does RIRS behave for open-ended reasoning tasks (code generation, creative writing) where answer verification is non-trivial?
  4. Is there a principled way to choose the budget $B$ based on the distribution of RIRS magnitudes across the pool?
  5. Which transformer layers contribute most to the RIRS signal—early, middle, or late layers?
