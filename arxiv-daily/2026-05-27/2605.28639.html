The Attentional White Bear Effect in Transformer Language Models

arXiv: 2605.28639

Date: 2026-05-27

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.28639

一句话总结： 当Transformer语言模型被指令抑制某个概念时，该概念的内部表征反而保持高度可恢复，类似于人类心理学中的"白熊效应"——行为对齐与表征对齐之间存在根本性差距。
TL;DR: When transformers are instructed to suppress a concept, the forbidden concept remains highly decodable from hidden representations, continues to influence attention routing, and measurably leaks into downstream generations — even when the model successfully avoids producing the word itself. This "attentional white bear" effect persists across pooling strategies, indirect semantic controls, and multiple model families (Llama-3.1-8B, Mistral-7B, Gemma-7B-IT), exposing a fundamental gap between behavioral alignment and representational suppression.

核心贡献

  • 揭示行为对齐与表征对齐的根本差距： 证明抑制指令并未减少被禁概念的内部表征，反而使其在隐状态中保持高度可解码性，即使模型在输出层面成功避免了该概念的词汇表达。
  • 提出"注意力白熊效应"的计算类比： 借鉴认知心理学中Wegner的"白熊"思想抑制悖论，系统性地验证了Transformer模型在概念抑制下呈现类似的表征持续现象。
  • 多维度实验框架： 通过四项互补实验（表征探测、注意力分析、行为语义泄露、跨模型泛化）全面评估抑制效果，涵盖17个概念类别、986条结构化条目和136个提示模板。
  • 间接语义抑制对照设计： 证明即使指令中从未直接提及被禁概念（仅使用语义描述），表征持续效应依然存在，排除了词汇重叠作为唯一解释的可能性。
  • 跨架构泛化验证： 在Llama-3.1-8B、Mistral-7B和Gemma-7B-IT三种模型上复现了一致的结果，表明该现象是对齐Transformer的普遍属性。

Key Contributions

  • Fundamental gap between behavioral and representational alignment: Demonstrates that suppression instructions do not reduce internal representation of prohibited concepts — they remain highly decodable from hidden states even when lexical output is successfully constrained.
  • Computational analogue of the "white bear" effect: Draws a systematic parallel to Wegner's ironic process theory in cognitive psychology, showing that transformers exhibit a similar representational persistence under suppression.
  • Multi-pronged experimental framework: Four complementary experiments (representational probing, attention analysis, behavioral semantic leakage, cross-model generalization) across 17 concept categories with 986 structured entries and 136 prompt templates.
  • Indirect semantic suppression controls: Shows the effect persists even when the prohibited concept is never lexically named in the instruction (only described semantically), ruling out lexical overlap as the sole explanation.
  • Cross-architecture replication: Consistent results across Llama-3.1-8B, Mistral-7B, and Gemma-7B-IT, suggesting the phenomenon is a general property of aligned transformer models.

Methodology

The paper employs four experiments with a carefully controlled prompting framework:

  1. Concept Library: 17 concept categories (weapons, violence, self-harm, politics, religion, privacy, fraud, etc.) with multiple views per concept — direct aliases, indirect semantic descriptions, contextual prompts, positive examples, matched negatives, and hard negatives (986 total entries across 136 templates).
  1. Experiment 1 — Latent Recoverability: Layer-wise linear probes trained on residual-stream activations at each transformer layer. Probes classify whether the target concept is recoverable from hidden states under suppression vs. concept-absent baselines. Evaluated under three pooling strategies (last-token, mean non-padding, target-token) with 65/35 train-test splits across 3 seeds. Suppression salience is quantified as ∆ = f_c(h_sup) - f_c(h_abs).
  1. Experiment 2 — Attention Routing: Measures attention mass allocated to concept-associated token regions under suppression vs. direct mention and unrelated suppression controls. Analyzes both aggregate and head-level attention patterns.
  1. Experiment 3 — Behavioral Semantic Leakage: Evaluates whether suppressed concepts influence downstream generations semantically despite lexical avoidance. Uses semantic similarity between generated outputs and prohibited concepts, comparing suppression against absent baselines.
  1. Experiment 4 — Cross-Model Analysis: Replicates the probing analysis in Mistral-7B and Gemma-7B-IT using indirect suppression prompts with mean pooling.

Models: Llama-3.1-8B (primary), Mistral-7B, Gemma-7B-IT. Greedy decoding throughout.

Results & Highlights

  • Probing accuracy: Linear probes recovered target concepts with high reliability (mean AUC = 0.86–0.92, accuracy = 0.78–0.83 across pooling strategies).
  • Suppression salience: Under last-token pooling, peak suppression effect at layer 3 (∆ = 0.278, d = 0.722, p < .001). Under mean pooling, peak at layer 1 (∆ = 0.632, d = 2.11, p < .001) — suppression-related recoverability remained elevated across nearly the entire stack.
  • Non-monotonic dynamics: Last-token pooling revealed sharp early-layer peaks (layers 1–4) that diminished in deeper layers, while mean pooling showed sustained recoverability throughout.
  • Indirect suppression: Even without naming the target concept, indirect suppression produced elevated recoverability (peak ∆ = 0.529 at layer 32, d = 1.745, p < .001).
  • Attention routing: Aggregate attention to concept regions was slightly lower under suppression than direct mention (∆ = −0.002), but specific middle-layer heads (e.g., layer 14 head 4) showed large suppression-specific increases (∆ = 0.068, d = 1.73, p < .001), suggesting localized monitoring circuitry.
  • Semantic leakage: Despite zero explicit alias leakage under suppression, outputs remained measurably closer to prohibited concepts than absent baselines (∆ = 0.012, d = 0.23, p = .008). Indirect suppression showed comparable leakage (∆ = 0.012, d = 0.19, p = .027).
  • Cross-model: All three architectures exhibited consistent non-monotonic suppression profiles with peak ∆ = 0.481–0.526 (all p < .001, d = 1.25–1.53). The ordering mentioned > suppressed ≫ absent was statistically reliable across all models.
  • Graded (not ironic) pattern: The dominant result was f_c(h_mentioned) > f_c(h_suppressed) ≫ f_c(h_absent), not the stronger ironic form. Localized attention heads occasionally showed stronger white-bear-like dynamics.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The "white bear" analogy for transformer suppression is conceptually compelling and well-motivated. The systematic multi-experiment, multi-model validation adds significant rigor beyond prior anecdotal observations. However, the core insight — that suppressed concepts remain decodable — is somewhat expected given prior work on probing and representation persistence (Belinkov 2022, Burns et al. 2022).
  • Rigor: Strong experimental design with careful controls (matched negatives, hard negatives, indirect suppression, unrelated suppression controls). Statistical reporting is thorough. The concept library is well-structured. However, linear probes are a relatively simple tool — stronger claims about internal representations could benefit from non-linear probes or causal interventions.
  • Reproducibility: Code and data are publicly available. The concept library and prompt templates are well-documented. Models are publicly accessible. Results should be reproducible.
  • Significance: The distinction between behavioral and representational alignment is an important conceptual contribution to AI safety. The finding that output-level benchmarks may miss substantial internal processing has direct implications for alignment evaluation methodology.
  • Red flags: The paper explicitly acknowledges it does not demonstrate the full ironic process theory analogue (suppression > mention). The behavioral leakage effect sizes (d = 0.19–0.23) are small. The causal role of persistent representations during generation remains unestablished — the paper only shows correlational evidence. The 8B-parameter models are relatively small; generalization to frontier models is uncertain.

Strengths

  • Rigorous multi-experiment design with extensive statistical controls and multiple pooling strategies
  • Indirect semantic suppression controls effectively rule out the simplest lexical overlap confound
  • Cross-model replication across three architectures strengthens generalizability claims
  • Clear distinction between behavioral and representational alignment with actionable implications for safety evaluation
  • Well-designed concept library with 17 categories spanning diverse safety-relevant domains
  • Honest acknowledgment of limitations (correlational, not causal; graded, not ironic pattern; small model scale)
  • Open-source code and data

Limitations

  • Linear probes only show statistical recoverability, not that concepts are stored in discrete or symbolic form
  • Causal role of persistent representations during generation is untested — activations may merely accompany, not drive, constraint following
  • Behavioral leakage effect sizes are small (d ≈ 0.2), raising questions about practical significance
  • Limited to 7–8B parameter instruction-tuned models; unclear whether effects scale to larger frontier models
  • Attention allocation is an incomplete proxy for causal information flow in transformer circuits
  • Suppression prompts may introduce subtle distributional biases beyond pure suppression intent
  • Does not test sustained suppression over long-horizon generation or multi-turn interactions
  • Non-autoregressive architectures and other generation paradigms are not evaluated

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does the white bear effect scale to larger frontier models (GPT-4 class, 70B+), or do larger models develop more effective representational suppression mechanisms?
  2. Can causal interventions (activation steering, circuit ablation) determine whether persistent representations actively contribute to successful constraint following or merely accompany it?
  3. How does the effect change under sustained suppression over long-horizon, multi-turn interactions where the model must maintain constraints across many generations?
  4. Can representational alignment metrics be incorporated into alignment training objectives (e.g., penalizing latent concept recoverability during RLHF)?
  5. Do different alignment techniques (RLHF, DPO, constitutional AI, concept erasure) produce different representational suppression profiles?
  6. Can the localized suppression-sensitive attention heads identified in Experiment 2 be leveraged for mechanistic monitoring of constraint compliance in deployed systems?
