> **一句话总结：** 本文通过单样本动力学和时序稀疏自编码器（T-SAE）深入剖析了样本难度对 RLVR 训练的非单调影响，发现中等难度样本效果最佳，过难样本会诱发退化行为并损害模型已有能力，并提出了基于反向推理改写和 T-SAE 特征引导的自适应训练策略。

> **TL;DR:** This paper provides a mechanistic study of how sample difficulty shapes Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Reward (RLVR) for LLMs. Through controlled subset training, one-sample amplification, and T-SAE-based feature dynamics analysis, the authors reveal that sample difficulty has a non-monotonic effect: medium-difficulty samples yield the strongest gains, easy samples provide stable but smaller improvements, and hard samples are heterogeneous—often amplifying shortcut reasoning and suppressing existing reasoning features. Two interventions (backward-reasoning reformulation and RFGO) are proposed to better utilize hard samples.

## 核心贡献

- **首次系统性地从机制层面研究样本难度对 RLVR 的影响：** 不同于以往仅基于结果统计（通过率、奖励方差等）选择或过滤样本的工作，本文深入到模型内部计算机制层面，揭示不同难度样本如何改变模型的优化动力学和推理表征。
- **发现难度的非单调效应与单样本异质性：** 中等难度样本提供最稳定、最强的学习信号；过难样本在单样本层面表现高度异质——部分样本能提升性能，但许多样本会放大"捷径推理"（如直接输出答案跳过推理过程），导致性能严重退化。
- **引入 T-SAE 追踪推理特征动态：** 使用 Temporal Sparse Autoencoder 作为冻结探针，跨训练步骤追踪稀疏推理特征的激活变化。发现简单样本强化直接回答特征、抑制深度推理特征；中等样本平衡强化计算和多步推理特征；困难样本引入大量新推理特征（35个 vs 简单样本5个），但同时广泛抑制已有推理特征。
- **提出两种难度自适应干预策略：** (1) 将前向难题改写为反向推理变体，提高可采样到的有效轨迹比例；(2) RFGO（Reasoning Feature-Guided Optimization），利用 T-SAE 特征动态进行 token 级加权和零方差组的代理奖励。

## Key Contributions

- **First mechanistic study of sample difficulty in RLVR:** Unlike prior work that filters or reweights samples based on outcome-level statistics, this paper investigates how difficulty reshapes internal optimization dynamics and latent reasoning representations.
- **Non-monotonic difficulty effect with per-sample heterogeneity:** Medium-difficulty samples provide the strongest and most stable gains. Hard samples are highly heterogeneous at the individual level—some improve performance, but many amplify spurious shortcuts (e.g., answer-only outputs without reasoning) and degrade existing capabilities.
- **T-SAE-based reasoning feature dynamics tracking:** Using a frozen Temporal Sparse Autoencoder probe, the authors track sparse reasoning feature activation changes across training steps. Easy samples reinforce direct-answer features while suppressing deliberation; medium samples balance computation and multi-step reasoning; hard samples recruit many new features (35 vs 5 for easy) but broadly suppress existing reasoning features.
- **Two difficulty-adaptive interventions:** (1) Backward-reasoning reformulation converts hard forward-solving tasks into tractable inverse problems; (2) RFGO leverages T-SAE feature dynamics for token-level weighting and proxy rewards for zero-variance groups.

## Methodology

The paper employs a multi-level experimental approach:

1. **Difficulty-regime training (aggregate level):** Training Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B, Qwen2.5-Math-7B, and DeepSeek-Math-Instruct-7B on easy@8, medium@8, and hard@8 subsets of MATH, using GRPO with group size 8. Success rate over k rollouts serves as the difficulty proxy.

2. **Difficulty curriculum:** Constructing finer-grained splits by varying rollout budgets (k=2,4,8) to progressively include harder-but-solvable or more persistently unsolved samples.

3. **One-sample amplification:** Training on individual samples in isolation to study per-sample dynamics, tracking reward evolution, KL divergence, and downstream accuracy. This reveals that hard samples produce larger KL growth and unpredictable downstream effects.

4. **T-SAE feature dynamics:** Training a frozen T-SAE on the final full-data RL checkpoint's layer-16 residual stream, then applying it across all training steps to track reasoning-feature emergence, suppression, and reinforcement using a ReasonScore metric.

5. **Intervention experiments:** (a) Backward-reasoning rewrites—replacing numerical values with unknowns to create inverse reasoning variants of hard samples; (b) RFGO—reweighting token-level updates by future sentence-level T-SAE feature movement and providing feature-based proxy rewards for zero-variance rollout groups.

## Results & Highlights

- **Non-monotonic difficulty effect:** "Easy+Medium" training consistently outperforms the full dataset across benchmarks, confirming that hard samples dilute useful learning signals.
- **Hard sample instability:** Hard@8 samples achieve lower average MATH-500 scores (32.61) with much larger variance compared to easy/medium samples. Many hard samples cause performance to drop below the base model.
- **Spurious reward problem:** Harmful hard samples often produce correct answers via shortcuts (answer-only output, goal misunderstanding, question-answer mismatch) that outcome-only verification still rewards.
- **Feature emergence imbalance:** Hard@8 induces 35 unique emerging reasoning features vs 5 (easy) and 4 (medium), but these co-occur with broad suppression of existing reasoning features.
- **Backward-reasoning reformulation:** AugHard@8 surpasses full-data training on AMC (43.37 vs 40.96), AIME-2024 (13.33 vs 10.00), and MinervaMath (26.47 vs 25.37).
- **RFGO:** Achieves the best overall average performance (38.40 vs 36.67 for full data), with highest AMC (44.56) and AIME-2024 (16.67) scores without data rewriting.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** High. While sample difficulty in RLVR has been studied empirically, this paper is among the first to provide a mechanistic interpretation using sparse autoencoder feature dynamics, going beyond outcome-level statistics.
- **Rigor:** Strong experimental design with multiple base models (1.5B and 7B), multiple benchmarks, controlled subset training, and one-sample amplification. The T-SAE analysis is well-motivated and carefully constructed with frozen probes and ReasonScore metrics.
- **Reproducibility:** Good. The paper uses open-source models (Qwen2.5-Math, DeepSeek-Math), standard datasets (MATH), and clearly describes the methodology. The backward-reasoning rewrite procedure and RFGO formulation are detailed.
- **Significance:** Moderate-to-high. The findings about hard sample heterogeneity and the non-monotonic difficulty effect have direct practical implications for RLVR training pipelines. The proposed interventions are lightweight but not presented as SOTA solutions.
- **Red flags:** The interventions are explicitly positioned as proofs-of-concept rather than comprehensive solutions. The backward-reasoning reformulation is limited to problems with numerical quantities. The T-SAE analysis is correlational—causal claims about feature suppression are not fully established.

## Strengths

- Comprehensive multi-level analysis from aggregate curriculum down to single-sample dynamics to feature-level mechanisms
- Novel use of T-SAE for tracking reasoning-feature evolution during RL training
- Clear empirical evidence for the non-monotonic difficulty effect with practical implications
- Detailed qualitative analysis of failure modes in harmful hard samples (7 categories of spurious reasoning documented)
- Two well-motivated interventions that validate the mechanistic findings

## Limitations

- Backward-reasoning reformulation only applies to problems with numerical quantities, limiting generalizability to other domains (e.g., programming, open-ended reasoning)
- T-SAE probe is trained on the final full-data checkpoint, potentially biasing the feature basis toward full-data patterns
- Experiments focus on mathematical reasoning (MATH dataset); generalization to other RLVR domains (code, science) is not demonstrated
- RFGO adds computational overhead from T-SAE forward passes during training
- The causal relationship between feature dynamics and performance changes remains correlational
- Limited to GRPO-based RLVR; unclear if findings transfer to PPO-style or other RL algorithms

## Relevance

本文对理解和改进 LLM 的 RLVR 训练具有重要参考价值。核心发现——中等难度样本效果最佳、过难样本的异质性和潜在危害——为设计更高效的数据选择策略提供了机制层面的理论支撑。对于正在研究 RLVR 训练优化、课程学习或模型可解释性的研究者尤其值得关注。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How do these difficulty-dependent dynamics transfer to non-mathematical domains such as code generation or scientific reasoning?
2. Can an online difficulty estimator dynamically filter samples during training to maintain the "medium-difficulty sweet spot" as the model's capability evolves?
3. Would the T-SAE feature dynamics differ if the probe were trained on a base model checkpoint rather than the final RL checkpoint?
4. How does the non-monotonic difficulty effect interact with other RLVR design choices such as reward shaping, KL coefficient tuning, or rollout group size?
5. Can causal intervention experiments (e.g., ablating specific T-SAE features during training) establish a causal link between feature dynamics and performance changes?
