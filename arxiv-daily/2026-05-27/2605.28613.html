Implicit Regularization in Perturbed Deep Matrix Factorization: Spectral Conditions and Stability

[Jingzhe Wang, Hung-Hsu Chou]

arXiv: 2605.28613

Date: 2026-05-27

Tags: [implicit-regularization, matrix-factorization, optimization, low-rank, spectral-stability]

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.28613

一句话总结： 本文严格量化了加性噪声对深度矩阵分解中梯度下降隐式正则化的影响，证明了低秩阶段在扰动下仍然持续存在，并给出了扰动大小的显式误差界。
TL;DR: This paper proves that the low-rank implicit regularization observed in gradient descent for deep matrix factorization is stable under additive noise perturbations to the target matrix, providing explicit spectral conditions for the noiseless case and quantitative bounds on how perturbation shifts the low-rank interval and approximation error.

核心贡献

  • 无噪声低秩区间的显式谱条件： 推导了目标矩阵谱、初始化尺度和步长共同决定低秩区间（$T_0 < T_1$）是否非空的充分条件，补充了先前工作 [4] 中缺失的中间特征值依赖性分析。
  • 扰动梯度下降的收敛保证： 在目标矩阵被高斯噪声 $E$ 腐蚀的设定下，证明了扰动梯度下降的收敛性，并量化了扰动对迭代复杂度和特征值恢复的影响。
  • 隐式正则化的稳定性： 证明低秩阶段在扰动下持续存在（Theorem 5.1-5.3），给出了扰动区间 $[\tilde{T}_0, \tilde{T}_1]$ 的偏移量和低秩近似误差的显式界。
  • 数值验证： 通过实验验证了谱条件的正确性，并展示了不同噪声水平下隐式正则化的鲁棒性。

Key Contributions

  • Explicit spectral conditions for noiseless low-rank intervals: Derived sufficient conditions showing how the target spectrum, initialization scale, and step size jointly determine whether a nonempty low-rank interval exists, filling a gap in prior work [4] regarding the dependence on intermediate eigenvalues.
  • Convergence guarantees under perturbation: Proved convergence of gradient descent for the perturbed deep matrix factorization problem, quantifying how additive Gaussian noise $E$ affects iteration complexity and eigenvalue recovery.
  • Stability of implicit regularization: Showed the low-rank phase persists under perturbation (Theorems 5.1–5.3), with explicit bounds on the shifted interval $[\tilde{T}_0, \tilde{T}_1]$ and the low-rank approximation error, all scaling with $\|E\|$.
  • Numerical validation: Experiments confirm spectral conditions and demonstrate robustness of implicit regularization across noise levels.

Methodology

  • Framework: Deep matrix factorization $\min_{W_1,...,W_N} \|W_N \cdots W_1 - \hat{W}\|_F^2$ with identical positive initialization $W_j(0) = \alpha I$ and gradient descent.
  • Noise model: Additive symmetric Gaussian perturbation $\tilde{W} = \hat{W} + E$, where $E_{ij} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ i.i.d., with $\|E\|$ bounded via sub-Gaussian concentration.
  • Spectral analysis (noiseless): Decomposed the low-rank interval start time $T_0 = A(\lambda) + B(\lambda) + C(\lambda)$ and proved monotonicity conditions ensuring $T_0 < T_1$ via Lemmas 3.1–3.5, leading to Theorem 3.6.
  • Perturbation analysis: Reduced perturbed dynamics to eigenvalue-level scalar recursions via simultaneous diagonalization (Lemma 4.1), then applied Weyl's inequality and Davis-Kahan theorem to bound eigenvector/eigenvalue perturbations.
  • Stability proofs: Combined noiseless spectral conditions with matrix perturbation theory to bound interval shifts (Theorem 5.2) and approximation error (Theorem 5.3).

Results & Highlights

  • Spectral gap requirement (Theorem 3.6): Eigenvalue gaps must satisfy $\lambda_\ell - \lambda_{\ell+1} \geq \frac{1}{\kappa(\lambda_\ell, \lambda_{\ell+1})} (\lceil\sqrt{\lambda_\ell/3}/\alpha\rceil - \lceil\sqrt{\lambda_{\ell+1}/3}/\alpha\rceil)$ for nonempty low-rank intervals; smaller step sizes always favor observability.
  • Perturbed low-rank bound (Theorem 5.1): Effective rank error scales as $r(\hat{W}_L) - r(W(k)) \leq 2L\frac{\|E\|}{\lambda_1} + 3\epsilon\, r(\hat{W}_L^+)$ with optimized $\epsilon'$ and $\alpha$.
  • Interval shift (Theorem 5.2): Both $\tilde{T}_1 - T_1$ and $\tilde{T}_0 - T_0$ are bounded by terms proportional to $\|E\| / (\lambda_{L+1} - \|E\|)$, confirming graceful degradation.
  • Approximation error (Theorem 5.3): $\|W(k)_L - \hat{W}_L\|_F < \frac{4\sqrt{2L}}{\delta_s}\lambda_1 + \sqrt{L}\|E\| + \frac{\epsilon\sqrt{L}(\lambda_1 + \|E\|)}{4}$, where $\delta_s$ is the minimum spectral gap.
  • Experiments: With $n=20$, $N=2$, eigenvalues $(10, 5, 1)$: rank-1/2/3 plateaus clearly visible at $\eta=0.005$, disappear at $\eta=0.1$; noise levels $c=0.5, 1.0$ shift plateau endpoints but preserve low-rank structure.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: Moderate-to-high. The noiseless result refinement (Theorem 3.6) fills a genuine gap. The perturbation stability analysis is the main novelty, extending implicit regularization theory to noisy settings relevant to differential privacy.
  • Rigor: High. All claims are accompanied by complete proofs (with appendix). Uses standard tools (Weyl, Davis-Kahan, sub-Gaussian concentration) correctly.
  • Reproducibility: Good. Experimental setup is fully specified ($n=20, N=2, \alpha=0.01$, specific eigenvalue choices). No deep learning framework needed — pure matrix operations.
  • Significance: Moderate. Deep matrix factorization is a theoretical proxy for deep linear networks; practical implications for nonlinear deep learning remain indirect. The connection to differentially private matrix approximation is noted but not deeply explored.
  • Red flags: None major. The restriction to symmetric PSD ground-truth and symmetric perturbation is clearly stated. The $N=2$ simplification in several results is acknowledged.

Strengths

  • Rigorous, complete proofs with clear theorem structure
  • Explicit, interpretable bounds (not just asymptotic or order-of-magnitude)
  • Natural problem motivation (noisy measurements, differential privacy)
  • Builds on and clarifies prior work [4] by making intermediate eigenvalue dependence transparent
  • Clean numerical experiments that directly validate theoretical conditions

Limitations

  • Restricted to symmetric PSD ground-truth and symmetric noise; non-symmetric or indefinite perturbations left as future work
  • Several results simplified to $N=2$ layers; general $N$ analysis is substantially more complex
  • Only deep linear networks studied; extension to nonlinear architectures is open
  • Gaussian noise model assumed for $\|E\|$ concentration; deterministic analysis conditional on $\|E\|$ bound
  • Small-scale experiments ($n=20$); no large-scale or real-data validation
