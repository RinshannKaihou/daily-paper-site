Semantic Optimal Transport for Sparse Autoencoder Feature Matching and Circuit Compression

arXiv: 2605.28567

Date: 2026-05-27

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.28567

一句话总结： 本文提出将稀疏自编码器（SAE）特征表示为激活加权分布而非单一解码向量，通过最优传输在共享参考空间中计算跨层Wasserstein距离，统一解决了跨层特征匹配和电路压缩两大问题，并在GPT2和Gemma-2-2B上显著优于现有基线。
TL;DR: A distributional framework that represents SAE features as activation-weighted distributions over hidden states and uses Wasserstein distance in a shared reference space to enable accurate cross-layer feature matching and automatic circuit compression into interpretable supernodes, with theoretical guarantees on invariance, stability, and margin-based recovery.

核心贡献

  • 统一框架： 将跨层特征匹配和电路压缩统一建模为"跨流形语义距离估计"问题，揭示两者本质上是同一挑战。
  • 分布式特征表示： 放弃传统的单一解码向量范式，将每个SAE特征表示为激活加权的经验分布，保留了更丰富的语义上下文信息。
  • 跨流形最优传输： 通过将各层特征分布投影到共享参考空间并用Wasserstein距离比较，实现了跨多层语义距离的可计算近似。
  • 理论保证： 证明了激活缩放不变性（Proposition 1）、Wasserstein距离的扰动稳定性（Theorem 1）、以及有限样本下的匹配恢复条件（Theorem 2-3）。
  • 自动电路压缩： 基于语义距离矩阵自动将特征电路聚类为可解释的超级节点，无需人工标注。
  • 细粒度语义区分： 在"数字加法"特征实验中，能正确识别功能相似但语义微妙的特征，而其他方法均失败。

Key Contributions

  • Unified formulation: Frames cross-layer feature matching and circuit compression as instances of the same cross-manifold semantic distance estimation problem.
  • Distributional feature representation: Replaces the standard single decoder-vector paradigm with activation-weighted distributions over hidden states, preserving richer semantic context.
  • Cross-manifold optimal transport: Projects layer-specific distributions into a shared reference space and computes Wasserstein distances for semantically meaningful cross-layer comparison.
  • Theoretical guarantees: Proves activation-rescaling invariance, stability under measure perturbations, and explicit margin conditions for perfect recovery of true feature matches and semantic groups under finite samples.
  • Automatic circuit compression: Clusters feature circuits into interpretable supernodes without human labeling, scalable to circuits without open-source manual compressions.
  • Subtle meaning discrimination: Correctly identifies functionally similar digit-addition features that differ in subtle ways (e.g., "first number is 5" vs. "second number is 5"), where decoder-vector and LLM-based baselines fail.

Methodology

  1. Activation-weighted distribution: For each SAE feature $i$ at layer $\ell$, the method selects the top-$K$ activating token positions and constructs an empirical distribution $\hat{\mu}_{i,T}^{(\ell)} = \sum_{t \in I_i^{(\ell),K}} \hat{w}_{i,t}^{(\ell)} \delta_{x_t^{(\ell)}}$, where weights are normalized activations.
  1. Shared reference space projection: Each token position's layer-specific hidden state is replaced with a shared-space representation $z_t^{(R)}$. For circuit compression, $z_t^{(R)} = \text{Concat}(x_t^{(1)}, \ldots, x_t^{(L)})$ across all layers to preserve cross-layer semantics.
  1. Wasserstein distance: Semantic distance between features is computed as the Wasserstein distance $W_c(\hat{\mu}_i^{(A \to R)}, \hat{\nu}_j^{(B \to R)})$ in the shared reference space, using coordinate-wise Lipschitz ground costs.
  1. Feature matching: Given a target feature at layer A, find the nearest neighbor at layer B by minimizing Wasserstein distance.
  1. Circuit compression: Given a feature circuit graph, partition nodes into supernodes by minimizing within-supernode average Wasserstein distance, solved via standard clustering on the pairwise distance matrix.
  1. Evaluation: Tested on JumpReLU SAEs (Gemma-2-2B) and TopK SAEs (GPT2), with baselines including SAE Match, Feature Flow, Attribution Patching, and LLM Selection (gpt-oss-120b).

Results & Highlights

  • Feature matching (consecutive layers): On GPT2 Layer 5→6, achieves 68.2% accuracy vs. 66.2% (FeatFlow) and 55.6% (Match). On Gemma-2-2B Layer 12→13, achieves 58.6% vs. 20.4% (FeatFlow).
  • Feature matching (cross-layer): Dramatic improvements — GPT2 Layer 5→11: 23.2% accuracy vs. 7.8–22.2% for baselines. Gemma-2-2B Layer 0→25: 34.8% vs. 4.4–11.6% for baselines. The advantage grows with layer distance.
  • Circuit compression: Achieves 61.5% (GPT2) and 68.1% (Gemma-2-2B) supernode classification accuracy, outperforming FeatFlow (53.5%/66.6%), Match (55.6%/55.6%), LLM Selection (60.2%/65.7%), and Attribution (51.3%/54.1%).
  • Digit addition experiment: Human evaluators consistently rank the proposed method's matches higher than all baselines for identifying subtle functional distinctions (e.g., matching "first digit is 3" to "first digit is 5" rather than to "second digit is 3").
  • Qualitative circuit compression: Automatically identifies supernodes similar to open-source manual compressions for the "Dallas" circuit, and discovers meaningful structure in "rhyming" and "incrementing sequences" circuits without human input.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. The key insight — that both feature matching and circuit compression are instances of cross-manifold semantic distance estimation — is genuinely unifying and non-obvious. The distributional representation of SAE features (vs. single decoder vectors) is a meaningful conceptual shift.
  • Rigor: Strong theoretical component with three main results (invariance, stability, recovery) and full proofs in the appendix. The margin-based recovery conditions are particularly useful as they provide practical confidence estimates. However, the theoretical analysis assumes deterministic TopK selection, which may not hold for all SAE variants.
  • Reproducibility: Moderate concern. The authors state code will be released upon publication but it is not yet available. The NeurIPS checklist confirms this. Experimental details are in the appendix and appear sufficient.
  • Significance: High for the mechanistic interpretability community. Automatic circuit compression addresses a real scalability bottleneck. Cross-layer feature matching improvements are substantial, especially for distant layers where existing methods break down.
  • Red flags:
  • Evaluation relies heavily on LLM-as-judge (gpt-oss-120b) which introduces its own biases, though this is standard in the field.
  • Only tested on GPT2-small and Gemma-2-2B — scalability to larger models is unverified.
  • The shared reference space for circuit compression uses concatenation of all layers' hidden states, which could be memory-intensive for large models.
  • Sample size of 100 feature pairs per matching setup is modest.

Strengths

  • Elegant unification of two previously separate problems under a single mathematical framework.
  • Strong theoretical guarantees that are practically interpretable (margin conditions directly translate to confidence scores).
  • Consistent outperformance across multiple evaluation metrics (LLM eval, accuracy, CE, VE) and two different model architectures.
  • The digit addition experiment provides compelling evidence that the method captures true functional similarity, not just surface-level token overlap.
  • Practical utility: automatic circuit compression directly addresses the manual bottleneck in interpretability research.

Limitations

  • Tested only on small models (GPT2-small with 12 layers, Gemma-2-2B with 26 layers); scalability to 70B+ parameter models is unclear.
  • Code not yet publicly available at time of writing.
  • The shared reference space construction (concatenation of all layer hidden states) may not scale well in memory or computation for very deep models.
  • Evaluation depends on LLM-based judges which may have systematic biases.
  • The method requires access to SAE activations over a corpus, adding computational overhead compared to decoder-vector-only methods.
  • Only considers nonnegative SAE activations; extension to signed activations is left open.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does the method scale to models with 70B+ parameters and 80+ layers, where the shared reference space concatenation becomes very high-dimensional?
  • Can the framework be extended to compare features across different models (not just different layers of the same model)?
  • How sensitive are the results to the choice of $K$ in top-K activation selection?
  • Could alternative ground costs (e.g., learned cost functions) improve matching quality beyond Euclidean/cosine in the shared space?
  • What is the computational cost comparison versus LLM Selection for large-scale circuit compression on circuits with thousands of nodes?
