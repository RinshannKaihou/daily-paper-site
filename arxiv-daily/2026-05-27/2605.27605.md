> **一句话总结：** Poolside 提出 Laguna M.1（225.8B 总参数 / 23.4B 激活）和 XS.2（33.4B / 3B）两个面向长程智能体编码的 MoE 基础模型，依托工业化"模型工厂"流程，XS.2 从启动到发布仅用五周，在 SWE-bench Verified 上分别达 74.6% 和 69.9%，在同规模开源模型中具有竞争力。

> **TL;DR:** Poolside presents Laguna M.1 (225.8B total / 23.4B active params) and XS.2 (33.4B / 3B active), two MoE foundation models for long-horizon agentic coding. Built using an industrial "Model Factory" approach, XS.2 went from inception to release in just 5 weeks. On SWE-bench Verified, M.1 scores 74.6% and XS.2 scores 69.9%, competitive with state-of-the-art open models in their weight classes. XS.2 is released under Apache 2.0.

## 核心贡献

- 提出两个专为长程多步智能体编码设计的 MoE 基础模型：M.1（225.8B 总参数，23.4B 激活参数）和 XS.2（33.4B 总参数，3B 激活参数）
- 构建"模型工厂"（Model Factory）系统，将模型开发流程工业化——以 Dagster 作为实验资产 DAG 控制平面，Titan 作为统一训练库，Atlas 作为推理引擎，Hive 作为合成数据框架
- XS.2 从启动训练到发布仅用五周，充分验证了模型工厂的快速迭代能力
- 开发 AutoMixer 自动化数据混合优化框架，通过代理模型群训练学习数据配比到下游能力的映射
- 在 SWE-bench Verified 上 M.1 达 74.6%、XS.2 达 69.9%，在同规模开源模型中具有竞争力
- XS.2 权重以 Apache 2.0 开源发布

## Key Contributions

- Two MoE foundation models specifically designed for long-horizon, multi-step agentic coding: M.1 (225.8B total / 23.4B active) and XS.2 (33.4B total / 3B active)
- An industrial "Model Factory" system with composable components: Dagster (asset DAG control plane), Titan (PyTorch-based training library with 2200+ customizations), Atlas (vLLM-based multi-accelerator inference), Hive (synthetic data generation framework), and a custom batch scheduler using FoundationDB for sub-minute placement
- XS.2 developed from inception to release in just 5 weeks, demonstrating the Model Factory's rapid iteration capability
- AutoMixer: automated data mixture optimization framework that trains ~60 proxy models per ablation to learn a surrogate mapping from mixture proportions to downstream capabilities
- Comprehensive end-to-end pipeline: pre-training on 30T+ tokens with Muon optimizer and WSD learning rate schedule; post-training with mid-training (60B tokens), SFT (3 epochs × 40B), and agentic RL using CISPO
- Quantization recipes for FP8, INT4, and NVFP4 with mixed-precision strategies to enable low-VRAM deployment of XS.2
- Competitive results on agentic benchmarks: M.1 achieves 74.6% on SWE-bench Verified; XS.2 achieves 69.9%, both competitive with SOTA open models in their weight classes
- Open-source release of XS.2 weights under Apache 2.0 license

## Methodology

**Architecture:**
- MoE Transformer with pre-norm, RMSNorm, and token-choice routing
- M.1: 225.8B total, 23.4B active params per token; global attention on every layer
- XS.2: 33.4B total, 3B active; 8 of 256 experts per token plus one shared expert; interleaved sliding window attention (SWA, window=512) and global attention at 3:1 ratio; GQA with 8 KV heads; RoPE positional encodings
- XS.2 improvements over M.1: WSD learning rate schedule, routed expert modulation, reduced dense layers from 3 to 1, more efficient attention mechanism

**Pre-training:**
- 30T+ tokens from web, code, and synthetic sources (~27T unique token pool)
- M.1 trained on 6,144 H200 GPUs; XS.2 on 2,048 H200 GPUs
- Muon optimizer (Moonlight variant) throughout all training stages including SFT and RL
- WSD schedule for XS.2: peak LR 5×10⁻⁴ determined by a fitted scaling law: lr*(N,D) = 10^4.488 · N^(-0.4639) · D^(-0.2661)
- Long-context extension: 4K → 32K → 128K via YaRN, final inference at 256K via RoPE scale doubling
- Data mix (XS.2): 30.6% raw code, 25.2% web, 25.4% synthetic/code-text, 9.0% math, 6.6% knowledge, 1.4% instruction-like, 1.1% academic papers, 0.7% books

**Post-training (3 stages):**
1. **Mid-training** (~60B tokens): broad mix of chat (30%), reasoning (40%), and coding/agent data (30%); batch size 128, max seq 131K, cosine LR with Muon
2. **SFT** (3 epochs × ~40B tokens): ~85% agentic coding trajectories, ~15% other domains; includes multi-harness training with OpenHands, OpenCode, and Mini-SWE-Agent trajectories
3. **Agentic RL** with CISPO: token-level REINFORCE with importance-sampling-ratio clipping; length-weighted leave-one-out advantage; binary task verifiers (unit tests, shell assertions, exact numeric match); small shaping penalties for parsing errors and degenerate trajectories

**Quantization (XS.2):**
- FP8: W8A8 with SpinQuant R1 rotation + dynamic activation scaling; no quality degradation
- INT4: W4A16 with AWQ; mixed-precision (first 30 layers INT4, last 10 layers INT8) to handle outlier activations in deeper layers
- NVFP4: quantization-aware distillation (QAD) to recover quality; 1×16 microscaling groups
- FP8 KV cache: per-tensor amax-based scaling with 128 long-context agentic trajectories for calibration

**Distributed Training:**
- Full sharding stack: DDP + FSDP + TP + Expert Parallel (EGP × ETP) + Pipeline Parallel
- Compute-communication overlap for MoE: fused dispatch/combine into CUTLASS grouped GEMM kernels with NVLink communication overlapped at fine granularity
- Custom batch scheduler with per-job eviction, FoundationDB-backed topology, and sticky pod respawn for sub-minute placement
- Cross-replica hash checks for silent data corruption detection

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:**
- The MoE architecture for agentic coding is not novel per se, but the comprehensive Model Factory approach — treating model development as an industrial process with full lineage tracking, composable components, and automated data mixture optimization — is a distinctive process-level contribution
- AutoMixer is a practical and well-validated approach to data mixture optimization
- The combination of WSD scaling law, distributed Muon, and compute-communication overlap for MoE represents solid systems engineering
- Special token initialization via subtoken averaging and the TITO API for RL-training/deployment alignment are practical innovations

**Rigor:**
- Comprehensive technical report covering every stage from architecture to quantization
- Results reported as mean of 4 runs to address benchmark noise — a good practice
- Detailed ablations for architecture choices (Appendix A.2), data mixtures (AutoMixer), and quantization strategies
- External baseline scores taken only from official sources to avoid evaluation setup bias
- Proactive detection and patching of benchmark hacking vulnerabilities

**Reproducibility:**
- XS.2 weights released under Apache 2.0 significantly enhances reproducibility
- Detailed methodology descriptions including exact hyperparameters, data mix proportions, and training schedules
- However, the Model Factory infrastructure (custom scheduler, Dagster integration, code execution platform spanning ~1M repos) is not released, limiting full replication
- Training required 2,048-6,144 H200 GPUs, making reproduction expensive

**Significance:**
- Demonstrates that MoE models with very few active parameters (3B) can be highly competitive on agentic coding tasks
- The Model Factory approach may be as impactful as the models themselves for the field's understanding of how to scale model development
- XS.2 under Apache 2.0 provides a strong open-source baseline for agentic coding research
- Contributions to vLLM (reasoning and tool parsers) benefit the broader community

**Red Flags:**
- None significant; the paper is transparent about limitations, instability issues encountered during M.1 training, and benchmark vulnerabilities
- The competitive gap with larger models (e.g., DeepSeek-V4 Flash, Claude Sonnet 4.6) is honestly reported
- Commonsense benchmark regressions from AutoMixer optimization are acknowledged

## Strengths

- **Efficiency:** XS.2 achieves strong agentic coding performance with only 3B active parameters, making it deployable on consumer hardware
- **Model Factory methodology:** The industrial process approach enables rapid iteration (5 weeks from M.1 to XS.2) and is well-documented
- **Comprehensive coverage:** Rare depth of detail spanning architecture, data curation, training stability, post-training, quantization, and evaluation
- **Open-source commitment:** XS.2 under Apache 2.0 is a valuable contribution to the community
- **Training stability insights:** Detailed documentation of expert collapse, LM head gradient precision issues, and padding token routing problems with concrete solutions
- **Practical RL design:** Careful reward shaping with binary verifier + small penalties, TITO API for train/deploy alignment, and FP8 KV cache for efficient RL rollouts
- **Quantization rigor:** Mixed-precision strategies (INT4/INT8, NVFP4 with QAD) with empirical validation on agentic benchmarks

## Limitations

- **Model Factory not open-sourced:** The infrastructure (Titan, Atlas, Hive, custom scheduler, code execution platform) that enables the rapid development cycle is proprietary
- **M.1 weights not released:** Only XS.2 is available under Apache 2.0; M.1 remains closed
- **Computational requirements:** Training on 2,048-6,144 H200 GPUs limits reproducibility to well-resourced organizations
- **Commonsense regressions:** AutoMixer optimization for coding/math causes modest drops on ARC-C (-6.8%), WinoGrande (-1.4%), PIQA (-0.9%), HellaSwag (-1.3%)
- **Benchmark hacking vulnerabilities:** While patched, the discovery that all four agentic benchmarks are vulnerable to cheating raises concerns about the reliability of reported scores across the field
- **Evaluation noise:** Despite 4-run averaging, agentic evaluations remain inherently noisy due to infrastructure limitations
- **No human evaluation:** All results are benchmark-based; no human preference or real-world deployment studies
- **M.1 training instabilities:** Expert collapse, logit drift, and padding routing issues required significant debugging; XS.2 benefited from these lessons but the report doesn't fully quantify the cost of these iterations

## Relevance

本文对智能体编码和长程任务规划领域具有重要参考价值。模型工厂方法论展示了如何将模型开发从手工作坊式转变为工业化流程，AutoMixer 提供了自动化的数据混合优化方案，XS.2 的开源发布为社区提供了一个高效的智能体编码基础模型。特别是 MoE 架构在极少激活参数（3B）下实现强大智能体能力的经验，对高效模型设计具有启发意义。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does the Model Factory handle version control and data lineage when multiple research streams modify shared components simultaneously?
- What is the detailed architecture of the custom batch scheduler's per-job eviction policy, and how does it compare to gang scheduling alternatives?
- How sensitive is AutoMixer's surrogate model to the choice of proxy model size (~0.5B MoE)? Would larger proxies yield significantly better mixtures?
- What specific architectural changes would be needed to extend the MoE routing to support dynamic expert counts per token?
- How does the WSD scaling law generalize to dense models or non-MoE architectures?
- What is the long-term impact of FP8 KV cache quantization on very long agentic trajectories (>100K tokens)?
- Could the TITO API approach be extended to handle multimodal inputs in agentic settings?
- How does the instruction-following judge correlate with human preference at scale, and what are its failure modes?
