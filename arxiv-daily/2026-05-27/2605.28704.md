> **一句话总结：** 本文提出"可区分性"框架，证明浮点神经网络在任意归约顺序和有界ulp误差的非精确激活函数实现下，Sigmoid、tanh、ReLU、ELU、SeLU、GeLU、Swish、Mish、sin等主流激活函数仍能作为通用表示器（四层网络即可），同时揭示cos、cosh等函数无法实现通用表示。

> **TL;DR:** A general distinguishability framework showing that floating-point neural networks with arbitrary reduction orders and inexact activation implementations (bounded ulp errors) remain universal representators for Sigmoid, tanh, ReLU, ELU, SeLU, GELU, Swish, Mish, and sin with four-layer architectures, while cos, cosh, and related activations provably fail.

## 核心贡献

- **可区分性（Distinguishability）框架：** 提出统一理论框架，形式化刻画浮点神经网络在第一层区分不同输入的能力，并证明该能力是通用表示的充要条件
- **必要性条件：** 证明若激活函数在原点附近导数过小（如 |ρ'(x)| ≤ 2^{-p}），则对应浮点网络无法表示所有浮点函数；将此前仅针对cos的反例推广至 cos、1+x²、cosh、e^{-x²/2} 等更广泛类别
- **充分性条件：** 在温和条件下证明可区分性也是充分条件，仅需激活函数满足"条件1"（三个参考点的存在性），即可保证四层网络实现通用表示
- **实用性推广：** 在任意归约顺序（而非固定左到右）和非精确激活实现（最多K=10 ulp误差）下，为9种主流激活函数建立了通用表示性结果
- **深度泛化：** 将先前仅限于ReLU/Step等分段线性激活函数的理论结果，拓展至光滑、非线性的实际常用激活函数

## Key Contributions

- **Distinguishability framework:** Introduces a general framework formalizing when floating-point neural networks can distinguish every pair of distinct inputs in the first layer, proving this is both necessary and (under mild conditions) sufficient for universal representability.
- **Necessary conditions for non-universality:** Shows that if an activation function has bounded derivative near zero (|ρ'(x)| ≤ 2^{-p}), its floating-point implementation cannot be a universal representator. Extends prior isolated counterexamples to broad classes including cos, 1+x², cosh, and e^{-x²/2}.
- **Sufficient conditions for universality:** Proves that a simple condition (Condition 1: existence of three reference points C₀, C₁, C₂) guarantees four-layer universal representability under arbitrary reduction orders and bounded ulp errors.
- **Practical activation coverage:** Establishes universal representability for 9 widely-used activation functions (Sigmoid, tanh, ReLU, ELU, SeLU, GELU, Swish, Mish, sin) under significantly more realistic floating-point execution models than previously known.
- **Generalization beyond piecewise-linearity:** Overcomes the limitations of prior work that relied on piecewise-linear activations, fixed left-to-right reduction, and correctly rounded implementations.

## Methodology

The paper develops a purely theoretical framework based on the IEEE 754 floating-point standard. Key methodological components:

1. **Reduction order formalization:** Defines floating-point summation via binary trees (reduction orders) rather than assuming fixed left-to-right evaluation, capturing GPU-like parallel reduction behavior.

2. **Floating-point neural network definition:** Networks are defined as compositions of floating-point affine transformations (with arbitrary reduction orders) and coordinate-wise activation functions, where activations may have bounded ulp errors: σ(x) = ⌈ρ̂⌋(x) + ε(x) with |ε(x)| ≤ K·ulp(ρ̂(x)).

3. **Distinguishability analysis:** 
   - *Necessary condition:* If ∃ x ≠ x' such that σ(φ(x)) = σ(φ(x')) for all floating-point affine φ, the network cannot represent functions with f(x) ≠ f(x').
   - *Sufficient condition:* Under Condition 1, distinguishability with range [-2^{emax}, 2^{emax}] implies four-layer universal representability.

4. **Separating points:** For correctly rounded activations, the analysis uses "separating points" η where σ(η⁻) ∉ {σ(η), σ(η⁺)} or vice versa. If such points exist across a range of exponents, the domain is distinguishable.

5. **Transferability lemmas:** Technical lemmas (Lemmas 19-26) establish that under Condition 1, floating-point values can be "transferred" to distinguish arbitrary input pairs via sequences of affine transformations.

| **ReLU** | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes | **Universal** |
| **ELU** | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes | **Universal** |
| **SeLU** | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes | **Universal** |
| **GELU** | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes | **Universal** |
| **Swish** | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes | **Universal** |
| **Mish** | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes | **Universal** |
| **sin** | ✅ Yes (p≥2) | ✅ Yes (p≥7) | **Universal** |
| **cos** | ❌ No | ❌ No | **Not universal** |
| **cosh** | ❌ No | ❌ No | **Not universal** |

- Four-layer networks suffice for universal representability across all 9 practical activation functions.
- For Sigmoid and tanh under correct rounding: universal over F^d (p≥9) or (-2^{emax}, 2^{emax})^d (2≤p≤8).
- For non-correctly-rounded implementations (≤10 ulp): universal over F^d (p≥15) or (-2^{emax}, 2^{emax})^d (7≤p≤14) for Sigmoid and tanh.
- The correctly rounded cos activation cannot represent functions distinguishing inputs 0 and ω (the smallest positive float), extending to broad classes with similar local behavior near zero.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** High. The paper is the first to establish universal representability of floating-point neural networks with non-piecewise-linear activations under arbitrary reduction orders and inexact implementations. The distinguishability framework is an elegant and natural formulation.
- **Rigor:** Very high. The paper contains 36 theorems/lemmas with complete formal proofs (many in the appendix). The floating-point arithmetic analysis is meticulous, carefully handling edge cases (subnormals, overflow, NaN).
- **Reproducibility:** N/A — this is a purely theoretical contribution with no experimental components. All results are formally proven and verifiable.
- **Significance:** High for the theory community. Bridges a critical gap between the idealized real-arithmetic assumptions of universal approximation theorems and the reality of finite-precision computation. Practically relevant as low-precision formats (float16, bfloat16, float8) become increasingly common.
- **Red flags:** None significant. The main limitation is acknowledged: the sufficient condition requires a "Condition 1" that, while mild, still excludes some pathological activation implementations. The technical gap between necessity (range F∪{-∞,∞}) and sufficiency (range [-2^{emax}, 2^{emax}]) is noted but seems inherent to overflow avoidance.

## Strengths

1. Comprehensive theoretical framework covering 9+ practical activation functions under one unified approach
2. Handles realistic floating-point execution semantics (arbitrary reduction orders, bounded ulp errors) rather than idealized assumptions
3. Clean necessary and sufficient condition characterization with intuitive distinguishability interpretation
4. Extends prior counterexamples from isolated cases (cos) to entire classes of functions
5. Rigorous proofs covering all floating-point edge cases (subnormals, overflow, NaN, ±∞)
6. Clear presentation with well-organized tables summarizing results for each activation function

## Limitations

1. **Purely theoretical:** No empirical validation or experimental demonstration of the theoretical claims. While proofs are rigorous, practical implications are not quantified.
2. **Constructive but impractical network sizes:** The four-layer universal networks may require exponentially many neurons (in the number of floating-point values) — the constructions are existence proofs, not practical architectures.
3. **Domain restrictions:** Universal representability is proven over bounded floating-point domains (-2^{emax-2}, 2^{emax-2}), not the full floating-point range, due to overflow considerations.
4. **Condition 1 assumption:** The sufficient condition requires the existence of specific reference points (C₀, C₁, C₂), which, while mild, may not hold for all conceivable activation implementations.
5. **Single-input distinguishability:** The framework primarily analyzes first-layer distinguishability; deeper interactions between layers are not fully explored beyond the four-layer construction.

## Relevance

本文对关注神经网络表达能力理论和低精度计算的研究者有重要价值。随着float16/bfloat16/float8在实际部署中日益普及，理解有限精度对网络表达能力的影响对模型量化、混合精度训练和数值稳定性分析具有直接指导意义。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Can the four-layer construction be improved to three or even two layers for specific activation functions under floating-point arithmetic?
2. How do the results extend to modern low-precision formats (bfloat16 with p=8, float8 with p=3-4) where the number of representable values is severely limited?
3. What is the minimum network width required for universal representability under these floating-point execution models?
4. Can the distinguishability framework be extended to analyze approximate universal approximation (within ε tolerance) rather than exact representability?
5. How do these results interact with quantization-aware training and post-training quantization — can the theory predict which quantization schemes preserve expressivity?
