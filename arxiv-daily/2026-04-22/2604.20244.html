Hybrid Policy Distillation for LLMs

arXiv: 2604.20244v1

Date: 2026-04-22

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.20244v1

一句话总结： 本文提出 Hybrid Policy Distillation (HPD)，通过在 token 级别把 forward/reverse KL 融合为一组带符号的重加权权重，并在离线教师数据上轻量地注入学生采样的单 token，以兼顾覆盖性与 mode-seeking，在数学推理、对话和代码任务上以更低计算成本匹配甚至超越 SFT+OPD 两阶段管线。
TL;DR: HPD unifies SFT, FKLD and RKLD as a single reweighted token-level log-likelihood objective, then hybridizes forward- and reverse-KL via a masked negative K1 estimator plus one student-sampled non-expert token per step. The hybrid avoids full on-policy rollouts, stabilizes entropy, and yields +41% on Qwen2.5-3B math reasoning and +77.9% on LLaMA3-3B math over SFT, while also serving as a better initialization for subsequent OPD/DPO.

核心贡献

  • 提出统一的 "reweighted log-likelihood" 视角，把 SFT、FKLD、RKLD 解释为同一损失的不同 token 级权重选择，便于跨散度方向、优化策略和数据机制系统地设计新方法。
  • 提出 HPD：对每个离线 expert token 用 negative K1 估计 reverse-KL 间隙，正值触发 forward-KL 学习、非正值被 mask，从而以 token 级掩码的方式混合 FKL/RKL。
  • 引入"轻量 on-policy 采样"：每步仅额外采一个学生 token 用于惩罚过估计的非 expert 动作，并把其概率质量重定向回 expert，既避免全序列 rollout，又修正 train/inference 行为不一致。
  • 在 Qwen2.5 和 LLaMA3 两族、1B-8B 规模、数学推理（长 CoT）、UltraFeedback 对话、HumanEval/MBPP 代码三类任务上做了系统实验，并给出消融（去 sampling / 去 reinforce）与 OPD、DPO 接续训练的组合验证。

Key Contributions

  • A unified reweighted log-likelihood formulation that expresses SFT, SeqKD, FKLD, RKLD and JSD/GKD as different choices of a token-level weight w(a_t|s_t), making the design space (divergence direction, loss vs reward, on vs off policy) explicit.
  • HPD algorithm: uses a negative K1 estimator on the expert token to decide per-token whether to apply forward-KL reinforcement or to mask it, giving a masking-based (not fixed-coefficient) hybrid of FKL and RKL.
  • A "lightweight on-policy" twist: one student-sampled non-expert token per step is added only to suppress overestimated actions (via reinforce when k'1 < 0), avoiding full autoregressive rollouts while closing the train-inference gap.
  • Empirical demonstration across math reasoning (AIME24/25, AMC, MATH, OlympiadBench, GPQA), personalization (AlpacaEval2, Arena-Hard, MT-Bench) and coding (HumanEval, MBPP), plus DPO and iterative self-distillation extensions showing HPD serves as a stronger SFT replacement.

Methodology

HPD starts from reframing KD as L(θ) = -E[w(a_t|s_t) log q_θ(a_t|s_t)]. Under this lens, SFT uses an indicator weight, FKLD uses p(a_t|s_t), and RKLD uses log p - log q. The Monte-Carlo K1 estimator of KL (Schulman, 2020) is then used as a reward-style signal:

  1. For each offline expert token a_t, compute k1 = q_θ(a_t|s_t)[log p(a_t|s_t) - log q_θ(a_t|s_t)]. The expert weight becomes w_t = p(a_t|s_t)+k1 when k1>0 (student under-estimates, so apply FKL-style pull), and k1 when k1≤0 (mask FKL to avoid conflicting gradients).
  2. Sample one student token a_t ~ q_θ(·|s_t) with a_t ≠ a_t*. Compute its k'1. If k'1 < 0 (over-estimation), apply a reinforce-style negative weight w_t = k'1 to suppress and redistribute mass, and double the expert FKL weight (2p+k1) to redirect the freed mass to the expert action.
  3. The final loss (Eq. 15) combines both: L_HPD = -E[w_t log q_θ(a_t|s_t) + w_t log q_θ(a_t|s_t)].

Training is run with cosine-decay LRs, batch 256, 2k-10k steps. Teachers (e.g. Qwen2.5-7B, LLaMA3.1-8B) are first SFT'd on OpenR1-Math-8192 and optionally RL-finetuned with DAPO (clip-higher 0.28). Appendices B-D provide gradient derivations for K1, JSD, and the combined HPD loss.

Results & Highlights

  • Off-policy math reasoning (Table 2): Qwen2.5-3B student jumps from SFT 28.25 avg to HPD 39.83 (+41%); LLaMA3-3B 19.43 → 34.56 (+77.9%). HPD beats SeqKD, RKLD, JSD on every benchmark, with t-test p<0.01 on most cells.
  • Training dynamics (Figure 1): SFT collapses entropy and stalls KL reduction; HPD preserves entropy, continues to shrink KL gap, and keeps improving downstream AIME24-25 score through 2500 steps.
  • Dialogue (Table 3): on Qwen2.5-7B→1.5B, HPD gets AlpacaEval2 AE-LC 13.75, AE-WR 14.25, Arena-WR 21.80, MT-1T 7.23 - highest among all distillation baselines and close to teacher MT-1T 9.00.
  • Code (Table 4): HPD avg 66.35 (DS-Coder 6.7B→1.3B) and 77.35 (Qwen-Coder 7B→1.5B), beating KD/RKLD/JSD; KD wins MBPP in one setting so HPD is not monotonically best per-benchmark but wins on average with lower variance.
  • On-policy (Table 5): HPD+OPD reaches 33.41 avg on Qwen2.5-1.5B vs SFT+OPD 29.56, indicating HPD is a better initialization than SFT for subsequent OPD.
  • DPO follow-on (Table 6): HPD→DPO reaches Arena-WR 25.10 (Δ=+11.92 vs SFT M_T), and two rounds of iterative self-distillation with DPO continue to improve (Table 7).

Critical Assessment

Novelty. Moderate. Reframing SFT/FKLD/RKLD as a weighted log-likelihood is clean but similar unified views appear in Wang et al. (ABKD, 2025) and Wu et al. (2025). The actual new ingredient is the masking rule on k1 plus a single-token student sample with reinforce weight - incremental rather than paradigm-shifting, but well-executed.

Rigor. Good on gradient derivations (Appendices B-D) and on reporting statistical significance (t-test p<0.01 flagged per cell). However:

  • Baselines omit several recent strong OPD variants (e.g. DistiLLM-2's contrastive objective is mentioned in related work but not run on the same setup as HPD in the main tables).
  • The "lightweight on-policy" claim is defended against full rollouts but no wall-clock or FLOPs numbers are given to quantify the efficiency advantage. The reader cannot verify the "less cost" claim quantitatively.
  • Entropy-collapse comparison in Figure 1 is only against SFT, not against RKLD/JSD, which are more natural competitors for entropy dynamics.

Reproducibility. Helped by released code and detailed hyperparameters (Tables 8-10). Teacher models are public (Qwen2.5, LLaMA3). Concern: DAPO RL finetuning of teachers adds a non-trivial reproduction step, and OpenR1-Math-8192 / DAPO-MATH-17k pipelines are heavy.

Significance. Useful practical result: if HPD's +41%-78% math gains reproduce on independent runs, it's a meaningful drop-in improvement over SFT for small-model distillation. The DPO / iterative angle (Sec 7) is the most interesting extension but feels rushed - only one model (Qwen2.5-3B) and two iterations.

Red flags / overclaiming.

  • "Matches teacher model performance" (Sec 7) is based on Arena-Hard WR (25.10 vs teacher 26.80) and AE-LC (17.68 vs 18.46), which are narrow judge-model-based metrics. Calling this "comparable to teacher" overstates; AIME/MATH gaps remain.
  • Strong-to-weak distillation claims rest on LLM-as-a-judge with Qwen3-30B-A3-Instruct as judge - this is the same family as the teacher model for some runs, raising judge-bias concerns not addressed.
  • No ablation isolating the "double expert weight when k'1<0" design choice (Eq 14); the authors ablate sampling and reinforce but not the specific coefficient doubling.
  • GPQA (OOD) gains are smaller (+2-4 pts), but the paper emphasizes in-domain math gains in headline numbers.

Strengths

  • Clean unifying formulation makes the design space legible and is a good teaching framework regardless of HPD's ultimate adoption.
  • Breadth of experiments: two model families, three task domains, both off-policy and on-policy settings, plus DPO follow-up.
  • Explicit handling of entropy collapse with test-time entropy curves (Figure 1d, 2d) - a training-observability angle often skipped.
  • Released code and clear algorithm box (Algorithm 1) make the method implementable.
  • The K1-as-reward framing ties distillation neatly to the PPO/GRPO RL literature, which is conceptually useful.

Limitations

  • Requires shared tokenizer between teacher and student (stated in Appendix B), so cross-family distillation (e.g. LLaMA teacher → Qwen student) is out of scope.
  • Only one additional student token is sampled per step; the paper does not explore whether N>1 helps or saturates.
  • Evaluation on long-CoT tasks uses avg@8/32 which is stochastic; the exact seeds and sampling temperatures are reported (temp 0.7, top-p 0.95) but variance bars are not shown on most bar plots.
  • The DPO/iterative self-distillation section is mostly suggestive (Tables 6-7) and done on one model size.
  • No discussion of how HPD behaves under quantization or with mixed-precision training, which is where distillation is most deployed in practice.

Relevance

与用户的"LLM 训练稳定性与可观察性"方向高度相关：论文显式追踪 entropy、KL gap、mean advantage 等训练/推理期指标，并展示 SFT 下的 entropy collapse 问题——这与你关注的"hidden states、训练动力学和 train/inference 行为不一致"话题直接吻合。但与量化误差、system card、silent data corruption 等方向关联较弱。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does HPD compare to DistiLLM-2 and ABKD (α-β divergence) under the same teacher/student/data triple? The paper surveys these but does not reproduce them on its own math benchmarks.
  • Does the "one student token per step" trick generalize to N>1 or to adaptive N that grows as the student improves?
  • What is the wall-clock / FLOPs overhead of HPD vs vanilla FKLD and vs a short-rollout OPD (e.g. 16-token rollouts)? Would clarify the "lightweight" claim.
  • Can HPD's masking rule be applied to the KL term inside PPO/GRPO objectives to stabilize RL post-training, given the K1-as-reward framing?
  • How does HPD behave when the teacher itself is noisy or miscalibrated (e.g. quantized teacher)? The mask on k1 might amplify teacher errors.
  • Is the LLM-as-a-judge result (Qwen3-30B judging Qwen2.5 student vs Qwen2.5 teacher) biased by shared pretraining data? A human-eval or cross-family judge would strengthen the dialogue claims.
