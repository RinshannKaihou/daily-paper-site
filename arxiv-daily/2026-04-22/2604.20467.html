Mechanistic Interpretability Tool for AI Weather Models

arXiv: 2604.20467v1

Date: 2026-04-22

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.20467v1

一句话总结： 作者开发了一个基于 Streamlit 的开源交互式可视化工具，将 GraphCast 等 AI 天气模型 processor 阶段的潜在表示按地理区域组织起来，并结合 cosine similarity 与 PCA 初步分析潜在通道方向与中纬度天气波、比湿等气象特征的对应关系。
TL;DR: The authors build an open-source Streamlit-based visualization tool that organizes latent feature vectors from the processor stage of AI weather models (demonstrated on GraphCast) and, via cosine similarity and PCA, surfaces latent-space directions that qualitatively correspond to meteorological features such as mid-latitude synoptic-scale waves and specific humidity.

核心贡献

  • 提出首个专门面向 AI 天气模型（GraphCast 小型版本，1° 分辨率，16 个 processor steps，512 维潜在通道，共 83,902,464 个潜在数据点）的机制可解释性可视化工具，填补 NLP/视觉领域之外的机制可解释性工具空白。
  • 提供交互式工作流：选择模型配置、预报时刻、地理圆形区域、是否使用跨层 latent translator、以及最活跃通道数 T，自动加载所有 processor step 与 mesh node 的潜在特征。
  • 集成两种定量分析：cosine similarity（全局与 top-T 通道子空间两种变体）和 PCA（用户指定主成分数 C）。
  • 通过两个案例研究（中纬度天气槽/偶极结构、非洲萨赫勒比湿梯度）展示工具发现潜在通道与可解释气象特征之间的线性组合对应关系。
  • 开源仓库（GitHub: ktempestuous/latent_space_visualiser_weather_models），可复用于其他模型配置并供社区扩展。

Key Contributions

  • First dedicated mechanistic-interpretability visualization tool for AI weather models, built on Streamlit with an eye toward both meteorologists and ML researchers.
  • A structured pipeline to extract, organize and display latent feature vectors across mesh nodes and all 16 processor steps of GraphCast (small, 1° resolution, 10,242 mesh nodes at finest resolution, channel dim 512).
  • Integrated cosine-similarity analysis (computed over either the top-T most-activated channels or the full 512-dim latent vector) and PCA (user-specified C components) over user-selected geographic circles.
  • Optional "latent translator" (affine transform following Beylich & Craig) to align bases across processor steps.
  • Two exploratory case studies showing qualitative alignment between PCA directions / maximally-activated channels and (i) mid-latitude synoptic-scale dipole patterns around troughs and (ii) specific-humidity gradients in the Sahel.
  • Open-source release with reproducible instructions, supporting ERA5 inputs and GraphCast latent extraction.

Methodology

  • Model under study. GraphCast (small, 1° resolution), run a single 6-hour step; latent vectors extracted at the output of every processor step (16 total). Each mesh node carries a 512-dim feature vector, yielding ~83.9M latent data points per forecast.
  • Region selection. User specifies a circular geographic region of interest (centred at a chosen lat/lon with a chosen radius) where a meteorologically meaningful feature is known to exist.
  • Top-channel extraction. For each mesh node in the region, the T most-activated latent channels are identified; the union/ranking is used to produce global activation maps for those channels.
  • Cosine similarity. A reference node is drawn from the region; cosine similarity is computed between its latent vector and all other mesh nodes' vectors globally. Two variants: restricted to the top-T channels vs. the full 512-dim vector.
  • PCA. Fit on the selected regional data, then transform the global latent field into this basis; track variance per component and which channels dominate each component.
  • Cross-step alignment. When comparing intermediate processor steps, an affine "translator" maps all steps into the basis of the final (16th) step.
  • Case study parameters. Primary analysis uses processor step P = 16, T = 15 top channels, C = 8 PCA components. Two meteorological targets:
  1. A 500 hPa geopotential trough at 40°N, 100°W (radius 20°) on 2016-03-09 18:00 UTC, plus 24 h later and a contrasting 2017-06-01 case at 46°N, 30°W (radius 12.59°).
  2. 1000 hPa specific humidity in the Sahel, circle at 15°N, 15°E (radius 20°), for 2017-06-02 06:00 UTC (summer) and 2016-12-02 18:00 UTC (winter).
  • Implementation. Streamlit app (Python) reading ERA5 inputs from ECMWF and GraphCast latent dumps.

Results & Highlights

  • Synoptic-scale waves (Case 1). The first PCA component over the North American trough region shows a pronounced alternating dipole across Northern mid-latitudes (negative west / positive east of troughs), extending weakly into the Southern Hemisphere. The pattern qualitatively tracks the eastward shift associated with a subsequent Atlantic blocking event 24 h later and also appears (with opposite tilt) at a different synoptic setup in June 2017.
  • Channel consistency. Across the three synoptic forecasts, channels 464, 360, 33, 269, 30 and 126 recur among the top-6 PCA contributors (Table 1); bolded channels appear in at least two of the three forecasts.
  • Processor depth. Channel activations at P = 4 are already visually similar to those at P = 16, suggesting large-scale spatial structure is established early in the processor stack.
  • Cosine similarity. Over the North American trough, cosine similarity in either top-15 or full-512 space does not produce a clean interpretable pattern; however, shifting the reference region to 50°N, 48°W (radius 5.81°) yields a visible synoptic wave-like similarity map – notably without the original trough lighting up, hinting that magnitude (not just direction) of the latent vector matters.
  • Specific humidity (Case 2). The first PCA component correlates visibly with ERA5 specific humidity (Sahel extent, Australian humidity, Atlantic swirl, Eastern Pacific prong-like structures). Individual top channels (426, 33, 172) show more diffuse spatial patterns than in Case 1. Several channels (e.g. 464, 360, 426) appear in both case studies' top lists, interpreted either as a genuine physical relationship between moisture and synoptic waves or as polysemantic neurons.
  • Hemispheric asymmetry. Dipole structures are systematically weaker in the Southern Hemisphere, which the authors speculate reflects land–sea distribution.

Key caveat: all findings are qualitative visual associations, not quantitative causal or correlational tests.

Critical Assessment

  • Framing overclaims "mechanistic" interpretability. The tool performs standard representation-analysis (PCA + cosine similarity on hidden states) over geographic regions. This is closer to probing/representation engineering than to circuits-style mechanistic interpretability (no feature circuits, no causal interventions, no SAE training here – SAEs are only cited via MacMillan & Ouellette 2025 as related prior work). Calling the paper a "mechanistic interpretability tool" somewhat stretches the term.
  • Purely qualitative evaluation. There are no quantitative metrics: no correlation coefficients between PCA directions and meteorological fields, no probe accuracy, no causal ablation of channels, no statistical significance tests. Claims like "channel X corresponds to feature Y" rest on eyeballing maps.
  • Cherry-picked case studies. Only two meteorological phenomena, handpicked regions, 2–3 forecast dates each. No adversarial or null-region controls (e.g. running the same pipeline on a region with no distinctive feature, or on a randomly-initialized GraphCast).
  • Polysemanticity acknowledged but not disentangled. The observation that channels 360/464 appear in both synoptic-wave and humidity case studies is offered as either physical coupling or polysemantic neurons – the tool cannot distinguish these.
  • Model scope. Only the small (1°) GraphCast is analyzed; transferability to Aurora, AIFS-CRPS, Pangu-Weather, or larger GraphCast configurations is claimed but not demonstrated.
  • "Translator" is a black box. Cross-layer alignment relies on an affine map from an unpublished companion paper – users cannot fully evaluate what it does.
  • Missing ablations. No study of sensitivity to T (top-15), C (8 PCA components), circle radius, or reference-node choice for cosine similarity (the paper hints the reference choice is critical but doesn't systematize it).

Strengths

  • Fills a concrete gap: AI weather models are increasingly deployed and genuinely need interpretability tooling beyond post-hoc attribution.
  • Fully open-source and framework-agnostic in principle (Streamlit + Python), with clear reproduction instructions and public data.
  • Thoughtful UI choices: circular ROI selection, top-T channel extraction, optional cross-step translator, flexible PCA.
  • Case studies are domain-meaningful and the dipole-around-trough and Sahel-humidity findings are plausible enough to motivate deeper follow-up.
  • Honest about limitations in the Discussion (acknowledges polysemantic neurons, unexplained activations, qualitative nature).

Limitations

  • Demonstrated only on GraphCast-small; transformer-based models listed as future work.
  • No quantitative or causal evidence that identified directions are the features driving predictions (interpretation ≠ mechanism).
  • Cosine similarity shown to be sensitive to reference-region choice in ways the paper does not rigorously characterize.
  • Single forecast lead time (6 h); no exploration of how representations evolve over forecast horizons.
  • Terminological mismatch with the established mechanistic-interpretability literature (circuits, SAEs, activation patching) may confuse readers expecting those techniques.
  • No user study or comparison against simpler baselines (e.g. raw channel activations vs. PCA vs. SAE features).

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Can the identified PCA directions be causally validated via activation patching or channel ablation, so that zeroing/perturbing channel 464 demonstrably degrades GraphCast's synoptic-wave forecast skill?
  • How does the tool's output compare against sparse-autoencoder features trained on the same GraphCast latents (as in MacMillan & Ouellette 2025)? Do SAE features yield cleaner, more monosemantic correspondences than raw PCA directions?
  • Why does cosine similarity depend so strongly on the reference-region choice? Is this a vector-magnitude artifact, and would cosine on centred/whitened latents behave more stably?
  • Do the same top channels (360, 464) survive as important in larger GraphCast configurations or in unrelated AI weather models (Aurora, AIFS-CRPS, Pangu)? If not, the "universal feature" interpretation collapses.
  • How stable are the identified directions across retrained / randomly-seeded GraphCast models? Without seed variation, it's unclear whether these channels are incidental or structural.
  • Could null-region and shuffled-latent control experiments be added to quantify how much of the observed "feature correspondence" is just spatial autocorrelation in the input data?
  • For the hemispheric-asymmetry claim: is it robust after controlling for land–sea fraction and data density in ERA5, or is it an artifact of training-data imbalance?
