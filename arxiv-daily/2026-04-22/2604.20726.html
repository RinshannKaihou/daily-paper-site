Exploiting LLM-as-a-Judge Disposition on Free Text Legal QA via Prompt Optimization

arXiv: 2604.20726v1

Date: 2026-04-22

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.20726v1

一句话总结： 在 LEXam 法律自由文本问答基准上使用 ProTeGi 自动优化任务提示时，宽松评判模型（Qwen3-32B）给出的反馈比严格评判模型（DeepSeek-V3）产生的提示更通用、跨评判模型迁移效果更好，表明评判模型的"性格倾向"会系统性塑造被优化提示的泛化能力。
TL;DR: The paper shows that when automatically optimizing task prompts for legal free-text QA on the LEXam benchmark via ProTeGi, feedback from a lenient judge (Qwen3-32B) yields more transferable and structurally robust prompts than feedback from a strict judge (DeepSeek-V3), producing an asymmetric lenient to strict transfer advantage and suggesting practitioners should optimize against permissive judges.

核心贡献

  1. 首个系统性的法律领域任务提示自动优化研究： 首次将基于反馈的提示优化方法 ProTeGi 应用于 LLM-as-a-Judge 评估的法律问答任务，并证明自动优化在所有任务模型和评判组合上始终优于 LEXam 原有的人工设计的学生风格基线提示（绝对提升 2.79% 到 6.74%）。
  2. 揭示评判模型"倾向性"对提示优化的影响： 系统对比宽松（Qwen3-32B）与严格（DeepSeek-V3）两类评判模型的反馈风格，发现严格评判产生限制性、针对特定案例的过拟合提示，而宽松评判产生开放性、结构化、可复用的提示。
  3. 发现非对称跨评判迁移现象： 用宽松评判优化的提示迁移到严格评判时收益明显大于反方向迁移（例如 Qwen3-235B 上 +12.06 vs +5.77），甚至有时超越直接为严格评判优化的提示；据此建议实务中应优先使用宽松评判做提示优化。
  4. 对 ProTeGi 的适配改造： 将原本用于分类任务（二元正误）的 ProTeGi 改造为面向连续 0-1 评分的"低于基线即为失败样本"的梯度生成策略，并采用贪心束宽 1 的搜索以控制法律长文本评估成本。

Key Contributions

  1. First systematic study of task-prompt automatic optimization for an LLM-as-a-Judge legal QA benchmark (LEXam), showing ProTeGi-based optimization consistently beats the human-designed student-style baseline across four task models (Qwen3-32B, Qwen3-235B, gpt-oss-20B, gpt-oss-120B) and two judges, with gains of +2.79 to +6.74 points.
  2. Empirical characterization of judge "disposition" as an optimization signal. Strict judges emit corrective, omission-averse feedback that produces restrictive, case-specific, overfitted prompts; lenient judges emit permissive feedback yielding structurally reusable prompts (e.g. IRAC-style scaffolds in German legal method).
  3. Asymmetric cross-judge transfer finding: lenient to strict transfer produces substantially higher deltas (+3.50 to +12.06) than strict to lenient (+1.00 to +5.77), and lenient-optimized prompts can outperform strict-optimized ones even when evaluated by the strict judge.
  4. Methodological adaptation of ProTeGi to continuous-score (0-1) settings by defining "errors" as samples below the baseline score, plus a greedy beam-width-1 trajectory to make legal LLM-as-a-Judge optimization tractable.
  5. Practical recommendation: practitioners should optimize against permissive judges for generalizability; benchmark designers should document evaluator disposition, since evaluator choice materially affects prompt rankings.

Methodology

  • Dataset: LEXam free-text subset (excludes MCQ). 2,841 Swiss/EU/international-law questions (English and German). 60-question optimization set (one per course, for gradient generation), 240-question validation set (for prompt selection), 2,541-question held-out test set.
  • Task models: Qwen3-32B, Qwen3-235B, gpt-oss-20B, gpt-oss-120B (two families, two sizes each) via DeepInfra. Temperature 0.6 for Qwen, 1.0 for gpt-oss; max 8192 tokens.
  • Judges: Qwen3-32B (labeled lenient) and DeepSeek-V3 (labeled strict), temperature 0. Judge prompt reused from LEXam. Scores on 0-1 scale.
  • Optimizer: ProTeGi adapted for continuous scores — a sample is treated as an "error" when its score under a candidate prompt is below its baseline score. Optimizer model equals the task model being optimized (to isolate judge-disposition effects from optimizer/task mismatch).
  • Search: Greedy, beam width 1, 6 rounds. 4 sampled responses per question per candidate. Baseline-threshold acceptance criterion. Fixed seed 42.
  • Cross-judge transfer: Each prompt optimized under judge A is then scored against judge B to test transfer. Ensemble-optimized prompt (minimum-score aggregation of Qwen3-32B + DeepSeek-V3 + GPT-4o as in LEXam) also evaluated.
  • Metric: Mean judge score as percentage with bootstrapped SE on the 2,541-question test set; delta over baseline reported.

Results & Highlights

  • Matched optimization (same judge for opt and eval): Gains +2.79 to +6.74 across all cells. Lenient judge feedback consistently yields larger and more consistent gains (range 4.20-6.74) than strict feedback (range 2.79-5.97).
  • Cross-judge transfer (Table 2): lenient to strict delta vs strict to lenient delta:
  • gpt-oss-20b: +7.17 vs +2.31
  • Qwen3-32B: +3.50 vs +1.00
  • gpt-oss-120b: +3.92 vs +3.21
  • Qwen3-235B: +12.06 vs +5.77
  • Ensemble optimization using min-score aggregation underwhelms: only +0.79 matched gain and transfer of +0.99 / -0.78, consistent with conflicting-gradient hypothesis.
  • Partition analysis (Appendix B): Improvements are balanced across legal areas (criminal, private, public, interdisciplinary), jurisdictions (Swiss, international, generic), and languages (English, German). Some trajectories even flipped the prompt from English to German without hurting cross-lingual performance, which is surprising given the legacy LEXam expectation of a single prompt.
  • Qualitative analysis: Strict-judge prompts accumulate fictional legalese ("Ist-Zustand, Zieldefinition, Normkonzept"), over-specific references (e.g., "Kollektivgesellschaft", "Art. 303 StGB"), symptomatic of memorization. Lenient-judge prompts produce generic legal-reasoning scaffolds (Kurzüberblick -> Kernfragen -> Rechtsgrundlagen -> Analyse -> Ergebnis) and adaptive question-typing logic.
  • Quality sanity check: The authors manually inspect 10 top-delta questions and argue optimized answers show genuine methodical legal reasoning rather than formatting-only gains. Optimized answers are longer, but judges reportedly did not penalize verbosity (which cuts both ways — see Limitations).

Critical Assessment

  • The paper's framing — that "disposition" of a judge is the load-bearing variable — is supported by a narrow empirical design: only two judges (Qwen3-32B, DeepSeek-V3). Labeling Qwen3-32B as "lenient" rests on LEXam's prior observation and on score magnitudes here; a counterfactual where a strict but well-calibrated judge rewards the same structural scaffolds is not ruled out.
  • The title's word "exploiting" is somewhat adversarial but the paper is not really about adversarial reward hacking — it's a study of optimization regularization. The core asymmetry could alternatively be framed as "strict judges are higher-variance signal providers; greedy APO overfits to them." This interpretation is not really disentangled from "disposition."
  • Higher-quality answers or judge-gaming? The manual review of 10 high-delta questions is a good-faith check, but 10/2541 is not a rigorous quality audit and is performed by authors with a stake in the result. A blinded expert eval (law students/practitioners) would be required to claim real legal-reasoning gains rather than judge-gaming. The authors acknowledge answers got longer and judges "didn't penalize" verbosity — but LLM judges are well-documented to be verbosity-biased, so this cuts against the "real quality" claim more than the authors lean into.
  • Optimizer = task model is defended as isolating judge effects, but it also couples capability and interpretation of feedback; with a weaker optimizer model, strict feedback might behave differently. No ablation on this.
  • Beam width 1, seed 42, single trajectory per condition: The paper openly calls this out as a limitation (uncertainty quantification in APO). Given how close some deltas are (e.g. +1.00 for DeepSeek to Qwen on Qwen3-32B), per-trajectory stability is a real concern.
  • Ensemble baseline as comparison is only run for one task model (Qwen3-32B). The claim that ensemble "underperforms" is thus based on a single cell.
  • Judge prompt is held constant to LEXam's — so any bias in the judge prompt itself propagates into the "disposition" signal the authors attribute to the model. In principle, disposition is a model+prompt property, not model-only.
  • Benchmark scope: Swiss law via LEXam is genuinely under-served, but findings may not transfer to common-law or to non-legal domains. The paper acknowledges this.

Strengths

  • Clean, focused research question with three interlocking hypotheses, each testable.
  • Adapts a well-known APO method (ProTeGi) to continuous-score evaluation in a sensible way (below-baseline as "error").
  • Evaluates on a large held-out test set (2,541 questions) with bootstrap SE — good statistical hygiene compared to typical APO papers.
  • Multi-model coverage (4 task models across 2 families and 2 size tiers) makes the asymmetric-transfer finding less cherry-picked.
  • Public code and prompts; transparent about AI tool use and funding.
  • Qualitative prompt analysis (Appendix A) concretely shows the memorization vs. scaffold distinction and makes the "disposition shapes generalizability" claim tangible rather than speculative.

Limitations

  • Only two judges, and disposition is a single axis the authors impose on them; generalization to other judge pairs (GPT-4o, Claude-4-Sonnet, Gemini-2.5-Pro, Prometheus-style specialized judges) is untested.
  • No human evaluation. "Higher judge scores reflect improved legal quality" is argued qualitatively over 10 items. Verbosity bias is a real confound.
  • Single optimization run per condition (seed 42, greedy, beam 1). Uncertainty of the optimization procedure itself is not quantified — the authors flag this explicitly in the conclusion.
  • Optimizer model tied to task model prevents distinguishing judge-disposition effects from optimizer-capability effects.
  • Ensemble condition only run for Qwen3-32B; its "conflicting gradients" story is under-evidenced.
  • No analysis of judge self-preference bias (Qwen3-32B judge evaluating Qwen3-32B task model outputs — which appears across several cells).
  • No ablation on number of ProTeGi rounds or on gradient-sample size.
  • Results may be specific to Swiss exam-style law; no external legal domain is tested.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does the lenient-to-strict asymmetry hold with more than two judges and specifically with judges whose leniency comes from different mechanisms (e.g., a lenient but high-variance judge vs. a lenient low-variance judge)?
  2. Is the asymmetry a verbosity/length-bias artifact? If you add a length penalty to the judge prompt, do lenient-optimized prompts still dominate?
  3. How would results change if the optimizer model were fixed (e.g., always a strong third model like Claude or GPT-4) and only the judge disposition varied? This would cleanly isolate the "judge feedback" effect.
  4. Does beam search with wider beam or multiple seeds change the strict-feedback prompts? Maybe strict feedback is just noisier rather than pathologically overfitting.
  5. Would a human expert blind evaluation on the gain-deltas confirm that "lenient-optimized" prompts produce genuinely better legal reasoning, or would experts prefer the more-constrained strict prompts?
  6. Can the same "disposition asymmetry" be shown for non-legal domains (e.g., medical QA, long-form factuality)? If yes, this generalizes to a broader APO recommendation.
  7. What happens under iterative co-optimization where both the task prompt and the judge prompt are tuned jointly — does this break the asymmetry or amplify it?
  8. Is there a way to detect "judge-gaming" prompts automatically (e.g., via entropy of cited statutes, or divergence between judge score and an independent quality classifier) so practitioners can flag overfitting during APO?
  9. How sensitive are the findings to judge temperature 0 decoding? Judges at T=0 can have strong tie-breaking biases — would stochastic judge sampling change the gradient signal?
  10. How does this interact with quantization of judges (since judge dispositions may shift under low-precision inference, which ties into your ML reliability interests)?
