The parameter-matched head baseline is particularly informative: on ARC and OpenBookQA it receives a *larger* parameter budget than the graph network yet still loses by substantial margins, confirming the gains arise from multi-node communication rather than classifier capacity.

## Critical Assessment

**Strengths:**
- **Novel and well-motivated architecture**: The idea of composing frozen LLMs as graph nodes communicating through a shared latent space is creative and well-grounded in prior work on latent space compatibility and the Platonic Representation Hypothesis.
- **Strong empirical gains on ARC-Challenge and OpenBookQA**: The 11.4pp improvement on ARC-Challenge is particularly impressive, demonstrating clear value from multi-model composition.
- **Rigorous ablations**: The parameter-matched learned head baseline is a crucial control that isolates whether gains come from communication or classifier capacity. The gradient flow validation experiment (13% signal retention through one frozen boundary, permutation control confirming genuine geometric alignment) is well-designed.
- **Emergent routing**: The unsupervised development of asymmetric attention toward Phi-3-mini is an intriguing finding, suggesting the architecture discovers useful structural properties autonomously.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Thin MMLU margin**: The +1.2pp improvement on MMLU over the best single model is modest and based on a single training run. The authors acknowledge this limitation honestly, but it tempers claims of broad applicability. The 6.7pp gap over the parameter-matched head is more meaningful but partly reflects the learned head's surprisingly poor performance on MMLU (60.5% vs. 66.0% greedy), suggesting the head baseline may be flawed for instruction-tuned models.
- **Non-specializing layer-1 projections**: Despite using distinct framing prefixes, $W_1$-$W_3$ converge to nearly identical gradient norms. The averaging operation reduces pressure for differentiation, meaning representational diversity derives entirely from model heterogeneity rather than learned specialization. This limits the architecture's ability to leverage the framing prefixes meaningfully.
- **Limited task scope**: Evaluation is restricted to four-choice multiple-choice benchmarks. The architecture's applicability to open-ended generation, longer contexts, or non-classification tasks is untested and non-trivial -- the output node's design (cross-attention over two vectors producing a four-class softmax) is task-specific.
- **Compute and hardware requirements**: Although only 17.6M parameters are trained, the full graph requires loading five LLMs (~12B parameters total) on a single 80GB A100, limiting accessibility and scalability.
- **Single-seed results**: All results use one run per benchmark. Given the modest MMLU margin and known scheduler instability (cosine annealing causing oscillating validation accuracy), multi-seed confidence intervals would strengthen the claims.
- **Depth limitations unexplored**: Gradient flow is validated at two-layer depth only. The 13% attenuation per frozen boundary would compound quickly -- a 5-layer graph might retain only $0.13^4 \approx 0.03\%$ of the gradient signal, making deeper architectures potentially infeasible without architectural modifications.
