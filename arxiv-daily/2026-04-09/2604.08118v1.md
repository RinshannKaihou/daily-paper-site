## Methodology

The paper operates within the AQLM (Additive Quantization for Language Models) framework, where each group of g weights is encoded as a sum of M codewords from learned codebooks with K=256 entries each.

**Problem identification.** The authors introduce the *representational ratio* ρ = N/KM, where N is the number of weight groups per layer, K is codebook size, and M is the number of codebooks. At 3bpp (M=3), ρ ~ 0.07 (overcomplete), but at 2bpp (M=2), ρ ~ 18 (undercomplete) -- a 256x reduction in error-correction capacity. In the undercomplete regime, greedy sequential k-means initialisation commits errors in the first codebook that subsequent codebooks cannot absorb.

**Proposition 1 (Greedy Suboptimality Bound).** The suboptimality gap of greedy sequential assignment decomposes into three terms: direct cost (squared displacement from optimal first-codebook entry), coupling (how well C2 can compensate), and residual mismatch (always non-negative). When ρ >> 1, limited capacity in remaining codebooks makes the coupling term unable to compensate for first-codebook errors.

**OA-EM algorithm.** Starting from k-means-initialised centroids, OA-EM alternates for R=3 rounds:
- **M-step**: Optimise centroids via S=100 Adam steps minimising Hessian-weighted reconstruction error: L_EM = (1/N) * Σ e_i^T H_i e_i, where H_i = X^T X + λI is the damped block-diagonal Hessian.
- **E-step**: Hard reassignment of each weight group to its nearest centroid under Mahalanobis distance: b_i = argmin_k (w_i - c_k)^T H_i (w_i - c_k).

This directly reduces the direct cost term from Proposition 1 for output-sensitive groups, as the Hessian weighting concentrates centroids on groups that matter most for reconstruction loss.

## Critical Assessment

**Strengths.**
- The paper addresses a genuinely important and overlooked bottleneck. The insight that "more search cannot fix bad initialisation" is simple yet powerful, and the empirical evidence is thorough.
- The representational ratio ρ provides an elegant theoretical lens for understanding when additive quantization becomes brittle, cleanly explaining the qualitative difference between 2bpp and 3bpp regimes.
- The basin persistence finding -- that PV-tuning improves models *within* their existing basin rather than moving them between basins -- is a notable contribution with implications beyond this specific method.
- OA-EM is a drop-in replacement that preserves the O(1) LUT dequantization pathway, making it immediately practical for edge deployment.

**Weaknesses.**
- The method is evaluated on only three models (Llama 3.2 3B, Llama 3.1 8B, Qwen 2.5 3B) from two architecture families. The Qwen results show OA-EM slightly losing on average downstream accuracy (0.606 vs. 0.603), suggesting the benefit may not be universal and likely depends on weight distribution smoothness.
- No comparison against structured methods (QuIP#, QTIP, GLVQ) in absolute terms. While the authors argue orthogonality, practitioners need to know whether improved free-form quantization can match or approach structured alternatives in the quality-speed trade-off, especially since GLVQ reports state-of-the-art 2-bit perplexity.
- The OA-EM hyperparameters (R=3 rounds, S=100 steps, η=10^-4) are fixed across all experiments without ablation. The sensitivity to these choices is unclear.
- The theoretical contribution (Proposition 1) is a decomposition of the suboptimality gap rather than a bound that quantifies *how much* OA-EM improves upon greedy initialisation. The connection is qualitative rather than quantitative.
- The paper focuses on the 3B-8B parameter range. Whether these findings transfer to larger models (70B+) or smaller models (<1B) remains an open question.
