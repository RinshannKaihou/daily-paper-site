## Critical Assessment

**Strengths:**
- The core idea of leveraging mechanistic interpretability for practical model evaluation is novel and well-motivated. The two failure modes of existing proxies (underspecification and overconfidence) are clearly articulated.
- The paper provides thorough empirical validation across multiple datasets, shift types (style, institutional, geographic, temporal, adversarial), and model zoos with diverse pretraining strategies.
- The discovery of the Universal Generalization Motif and the distinction between pre- and post-deployment circuit behaviors (layer topology vs. rewiring dynamics) provides genuine mechanistic insight.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Computational cost.** Circuit discovery (EAP-IG) requires gradient-based estimation of every edge's contribution, costing roughly 13x more than a forward pass. The authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest using EAP without integrated gradients for a 5x speedup, but this degrades faithfulness.
- **Hyperparameter sensitivity.** DDB depends on the partition ratio $\tau$, and the optimal value ($\tau=0.3$) is determined empirically. It is unclear whether this choice transfers to architectures beyond ViT-B/16 or to models with different depth-width ratios.
- **Limited architectural scope.** All experiments use ViT-B/16 variants. Whether the Universal Generalization Motif and the proposed metrics generalize to larger ViTs, CNNs, or hybrid architectures remains untested.
- **Threshold calibration dependency.** The CSS alarm system requires surrogate corrupted domains for calibration. The quality of this calibration depends on how well the synthetic corruptions match the real distribution shifts encountered at deployment -- a assumption that may not always hold.
- **The "Generalization Motif" analysis is qualitative.** While the CCA-derived motifs motivate DDB, the actual DDB metric does not directly use the motif. The gap between the qualitative observation and the quantitative metric could be bridged more rigorously.
