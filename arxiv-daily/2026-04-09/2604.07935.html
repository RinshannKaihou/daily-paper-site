Critical Assessment

Strengths:

  • The paper identifies a genuinely important and underappreciated tension in SSM research: the divergence between cloud and edge optimization objectives. The concept of the "Hyperscale Lottery" is a clean, memorable framing.
  • The quantitative analysis is concrete and well-structured. The escalation of edge penalty from 28% to 48% as model size shrinks is a compelling and actionable finding.
  • The use of multiple evaluation methodologies (GPU measurement, analytical modeling, roofline analysis) provides robustness.

Weaknesses:

  • At only 4 pages, the paper is more of a position statement than a thorough empirical study. The analysis is limited to latency/throughput and does not examine accuracy tradeoffs -- do the architectural changes that hurt edge efficiency also improve model quality?
  • The edge ASIC model is purely analytical (no real silicon validation), and the Stream framework results for SSD formulations are marked as N/A, leaving gaps in the comparison.
  • The paper focuses exclusively on the Mamba family. While Mamba is the most prominent SSM, the argument would be stronger with evidence from other architectures (e.g., RWKV, Griffin, Jamba).
  • The recommendation to treat optimizations as "deployment-time transforms" is appealing in principle but underspecified in practice -- the paper does not demonstrate how such a decoupling would work architecturally.
  • The comparison between sequential and SSD formulations on the A100 shows SSD achieving dramatically higher throughput (e.g., 2260 vs 735 tok/s for Mamba-2), which somewhat undermines the argument that these changes are net-negative; the issue is specifically about the B=1 edge regime, not a blanket indictment.
