## Methodology

The authors evaluate five node embedding methods -- **ASNE, DGI, GraphSAGE, node2vec, and VERSE** -- across five empirical datasets (Cora, PubMed, Facebook, Wikipedia, BlogCatalog) spanning 2,405 to 22,407 nodes. For each method-dataset pair, they compute **30 embeddings** at each dimension from D = 4 to D = 4096 (powers of 2). Stability is assessed from two perspectives:

- **Representational stability**: Measured via aligned cosine similarity (global), distance correlation (global), k-NN Jaccard similarity (local), and second-order cosine similarity (local).
- **Functional stability**: Measured via stable core, Jensen-Shannon divergence, disagreement, and min-max-normalized disagreement on downstream node classification outputs.

Downstream performance is evaluated using both logistic regression and MLP classifiers.

## Critical Assessment

**Strengths:**
- The study is well-scoped and addresses a genuinely underexplored question at the intersection of embedding stability and dimensionality selection.
- The evaluation is thorough: five methods, five datasets, four representational measures, four functional measures, and 30 repetitions per setting.
- The paper is transparent about methodological choices and provides code for reproducibility.
- The distinction between local and global representational stability, and between hard and soft prediction stability, adds nuance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is primarily **empirical and observational**. It offers no theoretical explanation for why certain methods become more stable at higher dimensions while others do not. Understanding the mechanism -- whether it relates to the loss landscape, optimization dynamics, or model capacity -- would significantly strengthen the contribution.
- The analysis is **limited to small/medium graphs** (under 25K nodes). The authors acknowledge this limitation due to computational cost, but it raises questions about whether findings generalize to the large-scale graphs common in industrial settings.
- **Dimension is varied exponentially** (powers of 2), which means sharp transitions between adjacent dimensions (e.g., D=2048 to D=4096) may be missed. Linear interpolation would provide a finer-grained picture but is admittedly costly.
- The study does not propose a **practical guideline or method** for selecting dimension with stability in mind. Practitioners are left to run their own grid search.
- The use of different codebases for different methods (PyTorch Geometric, grape, karateclub, reference VERSE implementation) introduces a confound -- observed instability differences may partly reflect implementation quality rather than inherent algorithmic properties.
