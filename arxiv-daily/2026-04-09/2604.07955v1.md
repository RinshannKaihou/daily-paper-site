## Critical Assessment

**Strengths**:
- The identification of the compensation-aware error is a clean, principled insight that arises from careful mathematical analysis of the optimization objective. The fix is natural and well-motivated.
- The method is remarkably practical: minimal code changes, negligible computational overhead, and seamless integration with existing frameworks.
- Comprehensive evaluation across diverse model families (Llama 2/3/3.1, Qwen3, DeiT), quantization settings (W2A16, W3A16, W2A4KV4), and calibration configurations.
- The paper is well-organized, with clear derivations and thorough ablation studies.

**Limitations**:
- The gains are sometimes modest, particularly on larger models (e.g., Llama3-70B shows only marginal improvements in some settings), suggesting the compensation-aware error may be less critical when the model has sufficient capacity to absorb quantization noise.
- The method inherits the limitations of the underlying frameworks (GPTQ/GPTAQ): it remains a layer-wise, Hessian-based approach that does not capture cross-layer dependencies beyond the asymmetric calibration mechanism.
- The memory overhead for storing W^(0) and P2, while manageable, doubles the calibration memory compared to GPTAQ for the largest layers, which could be a concern for extremely large models on memory-constrained hardware.
- The catastrophic failure of SpinQuant-based methods at 2-bit quantization on Llama3-70B is noted but not fully analyzed or resolved.
- The paper does not compare against more recent quantization methods beyond AWQ, GPTQ, and GPTAQ (e.g., methods like QuIP# or AQLM that use different algorithmic paradigms).
