> **一句话总结：** 稳定性边界（Edge of Stability）不是一个全局优化现象，而是一种选择性机制——它根据训练数据子集的梯度方向对齐程度和梯度幅值持续性，不均匀地重新分配学习资源，放大某些数据子集的学习进度，同时抑制其他子集。

> **TL;DR:** The edge of stability (EoS) is not a uniform optimization bottleneck but a selective inductive bias: the stability constraint redistributes optimization effort across the training distribution based on which data subsets' gradients align with the top Hessian eigenvector and maintain non-vanishing gradient magnitude, amplifying progress on favored subsets while suppressing others.

---

## 核心贡献

现有的稳定性边界（EoS）研究将其视为全局优化属性——顶层 Hessian 特征值自稳定在阈值 $\lambda_1 \approx 2/\eta$ 附近，从而约束整个损失景观。本文提出并论证了一个不同的视角：**EoS 是选择性的**，它对训练分布中不同数据子集的学习进度施加不对称影响。

核心贡献如下：

1. **因果证明 EoS 的选择性**：通过分叉干预实验（branching intervention），从完全相同的训练轨迹出发，一个分支保持 EoS 状态（固定学习率），另一个退出 EoS（学习率减半）。两分支在子集损失上的分歧提供了 EoS 因果效应的直接证据。

2. **识别受益的两个必要条件**：梯度方向对齐（gradient directional alignment）× 梯度幅值持续性（gradient persistence）。仅满足其一不足以获得 EoS 优势。

3. **几何决定受益者**：数据分布的几何结构（而非模型状态）决定哪个子集主导顶层 Hessian 特征方向，从而预测 EoS 的受益方。

4. **连接两条研究线**：将参数空间的隐式正则化与数据分布上的归纳偏置联系起来，提供了对"平坦最小值是否改善泛化"这一争议的新解释。

---

## Key Contributions

Prior EoS analysis treated the phenomenon as a global optimization property—the top Hessian eigenvalue self-stabilizes near the threshold $\lambda_1 \approx 2/\eta$, constraining the loss landscape uniformly. This paper reframes EoS as a **selective mechanism** with asymmetric effects across data subgroups.

1. **Causal demonstration of EoS selectivity**: A branching intervention design starts two branches from an identical training state at EoS onset $t^*$. The baseline branch stays at the original learning rate (remaining at EoS); the exit branch halves the learning rate ($\eta \to \eta/2$), raising the stability threshold to $4/\eta$ and exiting EoS. Divergence in per-subset losses causally implicates the stability constraint.

2. **Two jointly necessary conditions for a subset to benefit**: (i) directional alignment—the subset's aggregate gradient $\nabla \ell_k$ must align with the top Hessian eigenvector $\mathbf{v}_1$ (measured by $\cos^2\theta_k$); (ii) gradient persistence—the subset must sustain non-vanishing gradient magnitude throughout training. Either condition alone is insufficient.

3. **Data geometry predicts the beneficiary**: The geometric composition of the input distribution at the time of EoS onset determines which subset dominates $\mathbf{v}_1$ and, hence, which subset benefits. This is a purely data-distributional, model-independent prediction.

4. **Bridging implicit regularization and inductive bias**: The paper takes a step toward connecting the literature on flat minima / parameter-space implicit regularization with the literature on which examples drive learning, arguing these are two sides of the same coin at EoS.

## Methodology

### Prototype Taxonomy

The authors partition the training distribution into four geometry-defined groups based on the joint distribution $P_{X,Y}$, independent of any trained model:

- **Inliers**: High-density points near class centroid $\mu_c$ with correct labels.
- **Boundary points**: In-distribution examples near the inter-class boundary, identified by high label ambiguity in their local $k$-NN neighborhood.
- **Input-outliers**: Geometrically atypical inputs, far from $\mu_c$ in input space, with correct labels. Synthetically constructed by displacing inlier seeds along the inter-centroid direction $v_\text{diff} = \mu_1 - \mu_0$ by $\alpha \cdot v_\text{diff}$ ($\alpha = 3$ or $10$).
- **Output-outliers**: High-density inputs assigned incorrect labels (flipped from inliers).

Experiments use a binary CIFAR-10 task (automobile vs. truck, $n = 10{,}000$) with $n = 200$ prototype-labeled points (50 per group, 25 per class) tracked throughout training alongside the full dataset.

### Branching Intervention Design

- **Baseline branch**: Full-batch GD at $\eta = 0.01$ (MSE or CE loss), continues at EoS indefinitely.
- **Exit branch**: Follows the baseline trajectory up to $t^*$ (first step where $\lambda_1 \geq 2/\eta$), then drops $\eta \to \eta/2$, pushing the stability threshold to $4/\eta$ and exiting EoS.
- Both branches share identical architecture (2-hidden-layer MLP, width 512, ReLU), initialization, data, and step budget. The only difference is the learning rate applied after $t^*$.
- Intervention effect $\Delta\ell_k = \ell_k^\text{exit} - \ell_k^\text{EoS}$: positive values mean EoS achieves lower loss (benefits that group).
- All results reported as medians across 5 seeds.

### Key Metrics

- **Per-group loss $\ell_k$**: Average loss over examples in group $k$.
- **Directional alignment $\cos^2\theta_k = (\nabla\ell_k \cdot \mathbf{v}_1)^2 / \|\nabla\ell_k\|^2$**: Measures how strongly a subset's gradient couples to the EoS-constrained oscillation mode.
- **Curvature influence $(\nabla\ell_k \cdot \mathbf{v}_1)^2 = \|\nabla\ell_k\|^2 \cdot \cos^2\theta_k$**: Combines direction and magnitude; groups are ranked by their contribution to the dominant Hessian direction.

### Theoretical Grounding

The analysis builds on the self-stabilization framework of Damian et al. (2023). At EoS, the optimizer oscillates along $\mathbf{v}_1$; the EoS drift for subset $k$ contributes $-(\alpha/\beta)\langle\nabla\ell_k, \nabla\lambda_1\rangle$. The selector $\langle\nabla\ell_k, \nabla\lambda_1\rangle$ decomposes into alignment (direction) and persistence (magnitude) components, motivating the two experimental interventions. A per-subset loss decomposition (Appendix F) shows that $\cos^2\theta_k$ serves as an empirical proxy for the theoretical EoS selector.

### Counterfactual Experiments for Mechanism Isolation

**Alignment isolation**: A random-direction displacement counterfactual creates input-outliers displaced by the same distance $\alpha$ from the centroid but in a random orthogonal direction instead of $v_\text{diff}$. This preserves all geometric statistics (centroid distance, group size, labels) but destroys directional coherence. Result: alignment collapses by an order of magnitude and the selective EoS advantage dissolves.

**Persistence isolation**: MSE vs. cross-entropy (CE) training on identical data isolates gradient persistence. Under MSE, per-example gradients scale with the residual and persist even for confidently classified points. Under CE, gradient saturation eliminates the gradient signal for confidently classified groups. Result: under CE, input-outliers' curvature influence collapses despite maintained directional alignment; output-outliers (with persistent gradients from the wrong label) become the sole beneficiary.

## Results & Highlights

### Main Result: EoS Creates Selective Trade-offs (Section 3)

Under MSE training with the branching intervention, post-branch prototype losses diverge in a group-specific pattern:
- **EoS helps**: input-outliers and output-outliers achieve lower loss under EoS than under the exit branch ($\Delta\ell_k > 0$).
- **EoS hurts**: inliers and boundary points make faster progress under the exit branch ($\Delta\ell_k < 0$).

This selective trade-off replicates qualitatively across CNN, ResNet-14, SGD (batch size 128), and GD with momentum ($\beta = 0.9$), confirming it is not an artifact of the MLP or full-batch setting.

### Alignment Dynamics (Section 3.3, Figure 5)

During progressive sharpening (before $t^*$), input-outlier alignment $\cos^2\theta_k$ rises rapidly toward 1, dominating $\mathbf{v}_1$ at EoS onset. Under the EoS baseline, self-stabilization resolves this: input-outlier curvature decreases as their loss falls, $\mathbf{v}_1$ rotates toward boundary points, and the alignment transfer produces the observed trade-off. Under the exit branch, this resolution does not occur—input-outlier alignment remains elevated through $t^{**}$, confirming the resolution is specifically driven by EoS dynamics.

### Two Necessary Conditions Verified (Section 4)

**Alignment experiment (Figure 6)**: Coherent displacement ($v_\text{diff}$) yields high alignment and curvature influence, capturing the EoS advantage. Random orthogonal displacement eliminates this—the EoS trade-off largely disappears even though centroid distance is matched.

**Persistence experiment (Figure 7)**: Under MSE, input-outliers maintain high alignment and curvature influence, capturing EoS. Under CE, gradient saturation collapses input-outlier curvature influence by orders of magnitude; output-outliers (whose gradients persist due to wrong labels) become the sole beneficiary.

### Alignment From Natural Distribution (Section 4.3, Figure 8)

On the full natural CIFAR-10 distribution (10,000 training examples), centroid distance $\|x_i - \mu_c\|$ and per-example alignment $\cos^2(\nabla\ell_i, \mathbf{v}_1)$ are uncorrelated during progressive sharpening (Spearman $\rho = -0.11$). At EoS onset, a positive correlation emerges ($\rho = 0.39$), showing that data geometry predicts which natural examples dominate $\mathbf{v}_1$—not as a synthetic artifact, but as an emergent property of EoS dynamics.

### Geometry Shifts the Beneficiary (Section 5, Figures 15–17)

On a harder class pair (cat vs. dog), setting $\alpha = 3$ makes boundary points (not input-outliers) the most geometrically atypical subset. Correspondingly, boundary points dominate $\mathbf{v}_1$ and become the primary EoS beneficiary. Increasing $\alpha = 10$ restores input-outlier atypicality and transfers the EoS advantage back to them. This confirms the geometry-governs-beneficiary principle.

### Generalization and Robustness Consequences (Section 5.2)

**Adversarial robustness (Figure 9)**: PGD adversarial accuracy ($\varepsilon = 0.03$, evaluated on boundary test points) is higher for the EoS branch when boundary points dominate $\mathbf{v}_1$ ($\alpha = 3$), and lower when input-outliers dominate ($\alpha = 10$). EoS implicitly sharpens the decision boundary precisely where the dominant subset sits.

**OOD generalization (Figure 10)**: When input-outliers dominate $\mathbf{v}_1$ ($\alpha = 10$), the EoS branch achieves better OOD generalization on input-outliers at large $\alpha_\text{test}$, indicating that EoS-time optimization on a subset transfers to OOD generalization along that subset's displacement direction.

## Critical Assessment

This paper does what it says it does: it causally demonstrates that EoS benefits are unevenly distributed across data subgroups and mechanistically isolates two necessary conditions. The experimental design is careful—the branching intervention is a genuine causal intervention, not a correlation observation, and the counterfactuals (random direction vs. aligned direction; MSE vs. CE) are well-controlled.

The key conceptual contribution—reframing EoS as a data-distributional inductive bias rather than a global stability boundary—is genuinely novel and has explanatory power for conflicting findings in the flatness literature. The mechanistic story is internally consistent and the derivation grounding it (self-stabilization framework, Appendix F) adds theoretical weight.

The main limitation the authors themselves acknowledge is scope: experiments are on small-scale CIFAR-10 binary tasks with full-batch GD on an MLP. Whether these effects persist at large model and dataset scale, with mini-batch optimizers, or with modern architectures is explicitly left open. The robustness and OOD results are described as "preliminary" and evaluated on single seeds in some cases.

---

## Strengths

- **Causal rather than correlational**: The branching intervention provides direct causal evidence—not just observation that some groups learn faster—by holding everything fixed except EoS membership.
- **Mechanism isolation is principled**: The two counterfactuals (random-direction displacement for alignment; CE vs. MSE for persistence) are minimal, clean modifications that independently ablate each hypothesized necessary condition.
- **Prediction and verification**: The geometry-governs-beneficiary principle makes a concrete prediction (changing $\alpha$ changes the dominant group), which is verified across class pairs and architectures.
- **Connects disparate literatures**: The paper explicitly bridges the EoS/implicit-regularization line and the sample-centric learning line (influence functions, data cartography, memorization), a connection that has not been made before in this mechanistic form.
- **Fully reproducible**: Open-source codebase, detailed experimental setup in Appendix A, and modest compute requirements.

---

## Limitations

- **Scale**: All primary results are on binary CIFAR-10 with a 2-hidden-layer MLP and full-batch gradient descent. The EoS framework is most cleanly defined in this setting; extension to mini-batch, adaptive optimizers (Adam), or large-scale training requires the self-stabilization argument to generalize—which is flagged as an open theoretical question.
- **Prototype taxonomy is pixel-space**: Input-outliers are defined by centroid distance in pixel space. For convolutional architectures, learned features reshape the input-to-gradient mapping, so the dominant group may not be the one with largest pixel-space centroid distance (as shown in the CNN/ResNet appendix results, where the predictive quantity $(\nabla\ell_k \cdot \mathbf{v}_1)^2$ remains informative but the identity of the dominant group shifts).
- **Generalization results are preliminary**: Adversarial robustness (single seed) and OOD generalization findings are suggestive but not systematically evaluated across architectures, seeds, and broader distribution shifts.
- **Synthetic outlier construction**: Input-outliers and output-outliers are synthetically constructed, not naturally occurring. The natural-distribution experiment in Section 4.3 provides partial reassurance, but the prototype framing does not directly apply to the full 10-class CIFAR-10 or more complex distributions.
- **Focus on full-batch GD**: The EoS self-stabilization mechanism is established for full-batch GD. The SGD and momentum experiments in Appendix C are "preliminary" (3 seeds, qualitative), and the analysis of mini-batch EoS lacks the same theoretical foundation.

## Relevance

This paper is highly relevant to anyone working on:

- **Optimization theory and training dynamics**: Provides a new, data-centric lens on EoS phenomena—a topic at the frontier of understanding why overparameterized networks converge to useful solutions.
- **Understanding which examples drive learning**: Directly connects to the data cartography / influence function / memorization literature by showing that a structural property of the optimizer (EoS) systematically privileges certain data subgroups.
- **Implicit regularization and flatness**: Offers a possible resolution to the conflicting findings in the flatness literature: whether flat minima help depends on which subset dominates the top curvature direction during training, not on scalar sharpness alone.
- **Robustness and generalization**: The preliminary results suggest that understanding EoS selectivity could inform data selection and hyperparameter choices when specific generalization targets (adversarial robustness vs. OOD generalization) are prioritized.
- **Fairness and subgroup learning**: Though not framed in fairness terms, the mechanism—EoS systematically suppresses progress on some data subgroups—has potential implications for understanding disparate impact of standard training procedures.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. **Does EoS selectivity persist under mini-batch SGD at scale?** The self-stabilization theory grounding the mechanism applies most cleanly to full-batch GD. Does a stochastic analog exist, and does the dominant subset still track data geometry rather than batch composition?

2. **Can the beneficiary be engineered?** If data geometry determines the EoS beneficiary, can one deliberately construct or re-weight a dataset to make a desired subset dominant at $t^*$, achieving targeted generalization improvements without changing the optimizer?

3. **What happens in multi-class settings?** The prototype taxonomy is binary. With many classes and a complex inter-class geometry, do multiple subsets compete for dominance of $\mathbf{v}_1$ over training, and do they cycle as different groups saturate?

4. **Is the curvature-influence score $(\nabla\ell_k \cdot \mathbf{v}_1)^2$ useful as a diagnostic in practice?** Could it serve as a training-time signal to identify which data subsets are being (or will be) systematically disadvantaged by the EoS regime, before the training run completes?

5. **How does this interact with data augmentation?** Strong augmentation changes the effective input distribution seen by the optimizer. Does it shift the dominant group, and does this explain some of augmentation's generalization benefits beyond the conventional variance-reduction account?

6. **Does the loss function choice (CE vs. MSE) have systematic downstream effects on what models learn beyond training loss?** The paper shows CE gradient saturation shifts the EoS beneficiary to output-outliers—does this translate to measurable differences in the learned representations or downstream task performance beyond the training distribution?
