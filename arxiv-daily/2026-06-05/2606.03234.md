> **一句话总结：** 正确推理路径在到达答案之前会在隐层表示空间中自然收敛，利用这一现象设计辅助损失函数 Hidden-Align，在零额外推理开销下显著提升 RL 数学推理性能。

> **TL;DR:** Correct rollouts in RLVR training naturally cluster in hidden-state space just before the answer token — Hidden-Align exploits this by adding a pairwise cosine-similarity loss that consolidates the shared "correct-decision" representation, yielding +3.8 to +6.2 pp average accuracy gains over DAPO across three model scales with zero inference overhead.

---

## 核心贡献

本文基于一个几何观察提出了 **Hidden-Align**：在强化学习（RLVR）训练的语言模型中，所有正确 rollout 在**锚点 token**（答案标记 `\boxed{` 紧前的 token）处的最后一层隐层状态已经自然聚类（余弦相似度约 0.84），但仍保留来自各自推理路径的残差方差。对该位置施加辅助损失，主动拉近正确 rollout 的隐层表示，可以帮助模型在决策点提炼出更鲁棒的"正确决策表示"，从而在不改变推理策略多样性的情况下提高 pass@1 和 pass@k 指标。

1. **几何规律的发现与量化**：首次系统分析了 RLVR 训练模型中不同 token 位置、不同层深度的正确 rollout 隐层状态相似度分布，确认锚点 token 处的最后一层具有独特的"甜点"性质。
2. **Hidden-Align 损失**：一个极简的辅助损失，公式为 $\mathcal{L}_{\cos} = 1 - \frac{1}{|P|}\sum_{(i,j)\in P}\cos(\mathbf{h}_i, \mathbf{h}_j)$，与主 RL 损失以 $\lambda=0.001$ 的权重联合训练。
3. **两阶段梯度方案**：针对微批次训练（micro-batch training）导致同组 rollout 被分割的工程挑战，设计了 Gather-Backward 两阶段反传方案，实现精确梯度计算且不增加显存峰值。
4. **跨尺度验证**：在 Qwen3-1.7B、4B、14B 三个规模上验证有效，零推理额外开销。

---

## Key Contributions

1. **Geometric discovery:** The last-layer hidden states of correct rollouts converge naturally at the anchor token (the token immediately before `\boxed{`) with cosine similarity ~0.84, forming an incomplete cluster that is distinct from both the reasoning chain (~0.4) and the answer tokens (>0.9). This pattern holds consistently across model scales (1.7B, 4B, 14B).

2. **Hidden-Align auxiliary loss:** A single pairwise cosine-alignment loss applied only to correct rollouts at the anchor token, combined with DAPO at weight λ = 0.001. The loss is orthogonal to all existing RLVR variants operating at the token-probability level and can be dropped in on top of any group-relative RL method.

3. **Two-phase backward pass:** An engineering contribution that solves the micro-batch group-splitting problem: Phase 1 gathers L2-normalized anchor embeddings for all correct rollouts (no gradients stored); Phase 2 re-forwards each micro-batch and computes the loss against detached references, yielding exact gradients identical to the full-batch formula with bounded memory.

4. **Group-preserving batch reordering:** Ensures rollouts from the same prompt are co-located on a single GPU during data-parallel training so the alignment loss always operates on complete groups.

## Methodology

### Anchor Token

For each response $y_i$, the anchor token is defined as the token immediately preceding the `\boxed{` marker. This position occupies a geometric sweet spot:
- Too early (reasoning chain): similarity ~0.4, too dispersed for alignment to be meaningful.
- At the anchor: similarity ~0.84, compact but not fully converged — the ideal target for auxiliary gradients.
- At/after the answer: similarity >0.9, saturated and gradient-free.

Only the **last transformer layer** (e.g., L36 for Qwen3-4B) exhibits strong position-dependent variation. Shallower layers show weaker structure and are not useful.

### Alignment Loss

$$\mathcal{L}_{\cos} = 1 - \frac{1}{|P|} \sum_{(i,j)\in P} \cos(\mathbf{h}_i, \mathbf{h}_j)$$

where $P$ is the set of all correct-answer pairs within a prompt group, and $\cos(\mathbf{h}_i, \mathbf{h}_j) = \hat{\mathbf{h}}_i^\top \hat{\mathbf{h}}_j$ with L2-normalized hidden states. The loss is skipped when fewer than two correct rollouts exist in a group.

Total objective: $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{DAPO}} + \lambda \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\cos}$, with $\lambda = 0.001$.

### Gradient Dynamics

The gradient of $\mathcal{L}_{\cos}$ with respect to hidden state $\mathbf{h}_i$ is:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_{\cos}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_i} = -\frac{1}{|P|} \sum_{\substack{j \in \mathcal{C}_g \\ j \neq i}} \frac{1}{\|\mathbf{h}_i\|} \left(\hat{\mathbf{h}}_j - s_{ij}\hat{\mathbf{h}}_i\right)$$

This pushes each correct hidden state toward the other correct states while the projection term $s_{ij}\hat{\mathbf{h}}_i$ prevents trivial collapse to a single point.

### Why Not Other Loss Types?

The paper ablates five candidate loss types at the anchor token:
- **Alignment** (pull correct rollouts together): the only one that consistently improves.
- **Separation** (push correct away from incorrect): redundant with the DAPO advantage signal.
- **Diversity** (push all apart): opposes natural convergence.
- **Negative** (push incorrect rollouts apart): no clear benefit.
- **Cluster** (pull all rollouts together): conflates correct and incorrect.

Combinations of alignment with other losses dilute or negate the gain.

## Results & Highlights

**Main results (average pass@1 across 8 benchmarks):**

| Model | Base | + DAPO | + DAPO + Hidden-Align | Gain vs DAPO |
|-------|------|--------|-----------------------|--------------|
| Qwen3-1.7B | 23.82 | 37.37 | **41.20** | +3.83 pp |
| Qwen3-4B | 37.53 | 51.97 | **58.16** | +6.19 pp |
| Qwen3-14B | 40.76 | 60.26 | **65.68** | +5.42 pp |

**Per-benchmark highlights (Qwen3-4B):**

| Benchmark | DAPO | Hidden-Align | Delta |
|-----------|------|--------------|-------|
| AIME 2024 | 48.12 | 59.17 | +11.05 |
| AIME 2025 | 43.44 | 51.46 | +8.02 |
| AIME 2026 | 40.31 | 50.31 | +10.00 |
| AMC 2023 | 88.28 | 91.41 | +3.13 |
| AMC 2024 | 58.75 | 62.15 | +3.40 |
| HMMT Feb 2025 | 19.79 | 27.08 | +7.29 |
| Minerva Math | 56.25 | 56.62 | +0.37 |
| OlympiadBench | 54.15 | 67.06 | +12.91 |

**Pass@k analysis:** Hidden-Align outperforms DAPO across all k values (1 to 32) on all six competition benchmarks. The gap is especially pronounced on harder benchmarks (AIME 2025/2026, HMMT), indicating that the method deepens the model's understanding of correct solution structure, not just greedy performance.

**Training dynamics:** The alignment loss decreases steadily throughout training for Hidden-Align, whereas the DAPO baseline shows a partial downward trend that spikes sharply around steps 400–500, suggesting that RL training alone induces fragile partial alignment that degrades in later stages. Hidden-Align maintains stable alignment while consistently achieving higher accuracy reward during steps 200–300 in particular.

**Ablation summary:**
- Anchor position: only `pre_boxed` (the canonical anchor) yields substantial improvement; `pre_eos`, `pre_boxed_open`, and `answer position` are all inferior.
- Layer depth: only the last layer (L36) is effective; intermediate layers (L16–L32) show marginal or no improvement.
- Loss weight: λ = 0.001 is optimal; λ = 0.01 causes training divergence.

## Critical Assessment

Hidden-Align is a clean, well-motivated idea. The key insight — that correct rollouts already partially align at a specific position, and that amplifying this trend helps — is empirically grounded and theoretically coherent. The method is genuinely lightweight: one loss term, one hyperparameter, and a non-trivial but tractable engineering solution for micro-batch training.

However, several questions deserve scrutiny:

1. **Generalization beyond math with `\boxed{}`:** The method requires identifying a task-specific anchor token. The authors acknowledge this but do not demonstrate a solution. For code generation, dialogue, or open-ended tasks, there may be no clean equivalent of the pre-`\boxed{` position.

2. **Fixed λ = 0.001:** The sensitivity analysis shows that λ = 0.01 causes divergence, meaning the useful window is narrow. Adaptive scheduling could improve stability and potentially unlock larger gains, but this is left as future work.

3. **Scale ceiling not tested:** Results are on 1.7B–14B. Whether the phenomenon and the benefit persist at 70B+ is unknown. The geometric convergence pattern is empirically consistent across tested scales, but extrapolation is uncertain.

4. **DAPO-specific baseline:** All experiments use DAPO as the base RL algorithm. The claim that Hidden-Align is orthogonal to all RLVR variants is plausible but not empirically verified on GRPO, Dr. GRPO, VAPO, etc.

5. **The +0.37 pp gain on Minerva Math** is essentially noise, suggesting the method's advantage is concentrated on competition-level benchmarks where pass@1 has more room to improve over DAPO. On easier benchmarks (AMC), both methods approach saturation.

---

## Strengths

- **Zero inference overhead:** Hidden-Align modifies only the training loss; the deployed model is unchanged.
- **Plug-and-play orthogonality:** The loss operates in representation space and is orthogonal to all existing methods operating on token probabilities (advantage shaping, sampling strategies, KL penalties).
- **Principled design:** The two-phase backward pass achieves exact gradients rather than approximating group statistics from incomplete micro-batches.
- **Consistent pass@k improvement:** Gains on both pass@1 (greedy) and pass@k (diverse) confirm that the method improves both the quality of the best decision and the model's underlying understanding of correct solutions.
- **Strong competition-level results:** AIME 2024/2025/2026 and OlympiadBench gains are substantial and practically meaningful.

---

## Limitations

- **Format dependency:** The current formulation is specific to `\boxed{}` answer format. Generalizing to other formats requires identifying a suitable anchor position per task, which may not always exist or may require heuristic choices.
- **Fixed loss weight:** λ is tuned to 0.001 and held constant. The narrow useful range (0.0005–0.005) and divergence at 0.01 suggest the method is somewhat sensitive; adaptive scheduling is unexplored.
- **Unverified at 70B+ scale:** Validation is limited to 1.7B, 4B, and 14B. The geometric phenomenon may change at larger scales or with different training regimes.
- **Single base RL algorithm:** All experiments use DAPO. While the method is theoretically agnostic to the RL algorithm, combining with GRPO, Dr. GRPO, or VAPO is not experimentally confirmed.
- **Saturation on easy benchmarks:** On AMC 2023/2024, DAPO already performs well, and Hidden-Align provides marginal gains. The method's utility is most pronounced in the gap between model capability and benchmark ceiling.

## Relevance

This paper is directly relevant to anyone working on:
- **RLVR training for mathematical reasoning** — it is a drop-in improvement on top of DAPO with no architectural changes.
- **Representation learning as a training signal** — the method extends the REPA/alignment paradigm (successful in diffusion models and speech) to LLM RL training.
- **Understanding LLM internal geometry during RL** — the analysis of hidden-state cosine similarity across token positions and layers is a novel diagnostic contribution independent of the method itself.

For researchers focused on LLM reasoning, Hidden-Align occupies a unique niche: it is neither a reward design change nor a sampling strategy change, but a representation-level regularizer that exploits a previously unobserved geometric structure in correct rollouts.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. **Does the anchor-token convergence phenomenon generalize to non-mathematical domains?** For code generation tasks using special delimiter tokens (e.g., `<answer>` or end-of-code markers), does the same geometric sweet spot appear? If so, Hidden-Align could be applied broadly.

2. **What is the mechanistic explanation for why the last layer shows strong position-dependent variation while shallower layers do not?** This likely relates to how the LM head projects to vocabulary — the last layer encodes the "commitment to answer" that shallower layers have not yet resolved.

3. **Can adaptive λ scheduling improve the method?** The current scheme uses a fixed weight. A curriculum that starts small and increases as training progresses (or that is conditioned on the current alignment loss value) could potentially improve both stability and final performance.

4. **How does Hidden-Align interact with process reward models (PRMs)?** PRMs provide step-level correctness signals; Hidden-Align aligns representations at a single critical token. Could combining them further improve reasoning quality?

5. **Is there a "negative" version of Hidden-Align — pushing incorrect rollouts apart at the anchor token — that could complement the alignment loss?** The paper finds separation alone is not useful, but perhaps incorrect-rollout divergence at a different position (e.g., the first diverging token between correct and incorrect paths) could be informative.
