> **一句话总结：** Muon 在大语言模型预训练中比 Adam 快约 2 倍的根本原因在于曲率——Muon 的谱归一化更新方向具有更低的归一化方向锐度（NDS），从而产生更小的二阶曲率代价，使每步损失下降更大。

> **TL;DR:** Muon's ~2x training efficiency advantage over Adam in LLM pretraining stems from curvature geometry: Muon's spectrally normalized update direction has lower Normalized Directional Sharpness (NDS), incurring a smaller second-order curvature penalty per step, not a larger first-order gradient gain. Data imbalance amplifies this gap, and the advantage increasingly concentrates in within-layer Hessian structure as training proceeds. A formal quadratic model under Kronecker-structured Hessians proves Muon achieves smaller average NDS and lower loss than GD when curvature heterogeneity is sufficiently strong.

## 核心贡献

1. **曲率视角的新解释**：首次从优化景观的局部几何出发，通过二阶泰勒展开将每步损失下降分解为一阶增益与二阶曲率代价之差，实验证明 Muon 与 Adam 的一阶增益相当，但 Muon 的曲率代价系统性地更小。

2. **NDS 是核心变量**：将曲率代价进一步分解为更新范数与归一化方向锐度（NDS，$\mathcal{S}_F(W;Z) = \langle Z, \mathcal{H}[Z]\rangle / \|Z\|_F^2$）之积，发现 Muon 和 Adam 的更新范数相当，因此 Muon 的优势完全来自更低的 NDS（Adam/Muon NDS 比均值约为 1.76）。

3. **数据不平衡放大 NDS 差距**：利用可控 Zipf-PCFG 合成数据实验，证明随着数据越来越不平衡（Zipf 指数 s 从 0 到 1），Muon 相对 Adam 的 NDS 差距从 0.63 扩大到 1.13（增幅约 1.8 倍）。

4. **Within-/Cross-layer 分解**：将 NDS 分解为层内和跨层贡献，发现 Muon 的跨层 NDS 在训练中迅速下降，使得中后期 Muon 的 NDS 优势主要由层内曲率决定（within-layer fraction 从 ~14% 上升到 ~44%）。

5. **理论证明**：在满足四个关于 Hessian 低 Kronecker 秩、同时对角化、曲率异质性和梯度对齐假设的二次模型上，**Theorem 5.5** 严格证明：对任意有限 horizon T，Muon 的平均 NDS 小于 GD；当曲率异质性足够强时，Muon 的累积损失也小于 GD。

## Key Contributions

1. **Curvature-centric explanation**: First work to decompose the per-step loss decrease of Muon vs. Adam via a second-order Taylor expansion, isolating that Muon's advantage is in the curvature penalty term, not the first-order gradient alignment term.

2. **NDS as the decisive quantity**: By factoring the curvature penalty as $I_D^{(2)}(W,Z) = \frac{1}{2}\|Z\|_F^2 \cdot \mathcal{S}_F(W;Z)$, the paper shows that Muon's smaller curvature cost is entirely explained by its lower Normalized Directional Sharpness (NDS), since update norms are comparable across the two optimizers.

3. **Data imbalance amplifies the gap**: Controlled Zipf-PCFG experiments demonstrate that heavier data imbalance widens the NDS gap between Muon and Adam by up to 1.8x, consistent with prior work attributing Muon's edge to tail-heavy distributions.

4. **Within- vs. cross-layer decomposition**: NDS is decomposed into within-layer ($\mathcal{S}_F^{\text{within}}$) and cross-layer ($\mathcal{S}_F^{\text{cross}}$) components; Muon's cross-layer NDS drops rapidly during training, so its late-training advantage concentrates in within-layer Hessian blocks. The within-layer NDS gap is not uniformly distributed across layers — it is concentrated at the first and deepest layers.

5. **Formal theoretical guarantee**: Under four empirically validated assumptions (low Kronecker rank Hessian, simultaneous diagonalizability, curvature heterogeneity, gradient alignment), Theorem 5.5 proves Muon attains smaller average NDS and achieves lower loss than GD for all finite horizons when curvature heterogeneity is sufficiently large.

## Methodology

**Experimental setup.** A 124M-parameter NanoGPT is trained on FineWeb. All curvature quantities ($\Delta_D$, $I_D^{(1)}$, $I_D^{(2)}$, NDS) are computed at matched validation-loss checkpoints (not matched training steps), ensuring fair comparison since Muon reaches lower loss faster. Hessian-vector products are computed exactly at this scale (Hessian computation scales quadratically with parameter dimension, making 124M a practical ceiling for such analysis).

**Second-order Taylor decomposition.** The one-step loss decrease along update direction $Z$ is approximated as:
$$\Delta_D(W,Z) \approx \langle G, Z\rangle - \frac{1}{2}\langle Z, \mathcal{H}[Z]\rangle = I_D^{(1)}(W,Z) - I_D^{(2)}(W,Z)$$
This separates first-order gain (gradient alignment) from second-order curvature cost.

**NDS decomposition.** The curvature penalty factors as:
$$I_D^{(2)}(W,Z) = \frac{1}{2}\|Z\|_F^2 \cdot \mathcal{S}_F(W;Z), \quad \mathcal{S}_F(W;Z) = \frac{\langle Z,\mathcal{H}[Z]\rangle}{\|Z\|_F^2}$$
This allows independent measurement of update scale vs. update direction curvature.

**Within-/cross-layer NDS decomposition.** For a model with $L$ layers, the Hessian decomposes into layer-wise blocks $\mathcal{H}_{\ell\ell'}$:
$$\mathcal{S}_F^{\text{within}}(W_t;Z_t) = \sum_\ell \langle Z_{t,\ell}, \mathcal{H}_{\ell\ell}[Z_{t,\ell}]\rangle / \|Z_t\|_F^2$$
$$\mathcal{S}_F^{\text{cross}}(W_t;Z_t) = \sum_{\ell \neq \ell'} \langle Z_{t,\ell}, \mathcal{H}_{\ell\ell'}[Z_{t,\ell'}]\rangle / \|Z_t\|_F^2$$

**Synthetic data control.** Zipf-PCFG with Zipf exponent $s \in \{0, 0.5, 1\}$ is used to generate training corpora with controlled imbalance. A 9M NanoGPT (4 layers, dim 256) is trained for 10k steps under each condition.

**Theoretical model.** The quadratic model $\mathcal{Q}(Y) = \mathcal{L}(W_0) - \langle G, Y\rangle + \frac{1}{2}\langle Y, \mathcal{H}[Y]\rangle$ is analyzed under four assumptions:
- **Assumption 5.1** (Low Kronecker Rank): $\text{mat}(\mathcal{H}) \approx \sum_{k=1}^r B_k^\top \otimes A_k$ with $r \ll \min\{d_1^2, d_2^2\}$; empirically verified via explained Frobenius energy ratio $\xi(r)$, which reaches 0.8 at $r \approx 3$–5 for attention matrices.
- **Assumption 5.2** (Simultaneous Diagonalization): The Kronecker factors $\{A_k\}$ and $\{B_k\}$ share common orthogonal eigenbases $U$ and $V$; simultaneous-diagonalization scores of 0.892 ($A_k$) and 0.845 ($B_k$) confirm this empirically.
- **Assumption 5.3** (Curvature Heterogeneity): Positive paired curvatures $w_i$ have a two-level structure $w_H \gg w_L$; the ratio $w_1/w_{88} \approx 2.59 \times 10^6$ in practice.
- **Assumption 5.4** (Gradient Alignment): The gradient lies largely in the top-curvature subspace; the cumulative gradient energy ratio $\zeta(q) = 0.871$ confirms this.

**Key mechanism (proof sketch).** In the diagonalized basis, Muon assigns equal amplitude to every active mode ($Z^{\text{Muon}} = \sum_i \text{sgn}(r_{i,t}^{\text{Muon}}) M_i$), fixing its NDS at $\mathcal{S}_F(Z^{\text{Muon}}) = \alpha w_H + (1-\alpha)w_L$. GD's NDS is residual-energy-weighted and oscillates above this average because gradient bias toward high-curvature modes forces GD to over-invest energy there. Muon's loss contraction factor is $\Gamma^2$ while GD's is $R = C(P_0^{\text{GD}})C(1-P_0^{\text{GD}}) > \Gamma^2$ when heterogeneity is sufficiently strong.

## Results & Highlights

| Finding | Quantitative evidence |
|---|---|
| Muon achieves larger realized loss decrease than Adam | Confirmed at all matched validation-loss levels on 124M NanoGPT / FineWeb |
| First-order gains comparable | Both optimizers' $I_D^{(1)}$ curves nearly overlap across all training stages |
| Adam's curvature penalty consistently higher | Adam/Muon curvature penalty ratio tracks NDS ratio with average **1.76** |
| Update norms comparable | Adam/Muon $\|Z\|_F^2$ ratio stays close to 1.0 throughout training |
| Data imbalance amplifies NDS gap | NDS gap $\Delta(s)$ grows from **0.63 → 1.13** (1.8x) as Zipf $s$ goes 0 → 1 |
| Within-layer fraction rises for Muon | Muon: **14% → 44%**; Adam: 27% → 34% over 15k steps |
| Quadratic model: Muon NDS ratio vs GD | Muon achieves **0.09x** of GD's NDS; Adam at **0.50x** (Figure 7a) |
| Quadratic model: loss decrease ratio | Muon achieves **5.60x** of GD's loss decrease; Adam at **1.22x** (Figure 7b) |

The theoretical result (Theorem 5.5) identifies the exact condition for Muon to dominate GD in total loss: $\rho + 1 > 1/\alpha > 1 + \sigma_H/\sigma_L$, where $\rho = w_H/w_L$ is the curvature ratio, $\alpha = m/q$ is the proportion of high-curvature modes, and $\sigma_H, \sigma_L$ are gradient coefficients. This condition requires outlier curvature values to be much larger than the rest, consistent with the six-orders-of-magnitude heterogeneity observed empirically.

## Critical Assessment

This paper succeeds in providing the most mechanistically precise explanation of Muon's advantage to date. Prior explanations relied on associative memory or long-tail data narratives; this work grounds the gap in a concrete, measurable quantity (NDS) that can be computed and decomposed. The theoretical analysis is careful: all four assumptions are verified empirically with quantitative scores rather than hand-waving.

The key limitation is **scale**: Hessian computation scales as $O((mn)^2)$ in parameter dimension, confining the main analysis to 124M parameters. It is not demonstrated that the same curvature decomposition holds at 7B or 70B scale. The paper acknowledges this, but the implicit assumption that the findings generalize to frontier-scale training is unverified. Additionally, the theoretical comparison is between Muon and GD (not Adam directly) because Adam's coordinate-wise normalization does not admit closed-form analysis in the Kronecker-mode basis — a gap between theory and practice.

The within-/cross-layer gap being concentrated in "the first and deepest layers" (Appendix C finding) is mentioned but not elaborated in the main text, leaving an interesting open question about layer-wise optimizer design.

---

## Strengths

- Clean decomposition: realized loss decrease = first-order gain - curvature penalty; all terms are directly measurable.
- NDS as a single explanatory variable that unifies data imbalance, layer structure, and theoretical results.
- All theoretical assumptions empirically validated in-text with quantitative scores (e.g., $\eta_{sd} = 0.892/0.845$, $\xi(r=4) \approx 0.8$, $\zeta(88) = 0.871$).
- Provides principled support for Muon variants that target flat Hessian directions (Zhu et al. 2026), predicting their NDS reduction mechanism.
- The within-/cross-layer decomposition is a useful diagnostic for future optimizer design.

---

## Limitations

- Hessian-based analysis restricted to 124M-parameter models; scalability to production LLM sizes (10B+) is untested.
- Theoretical results compare Muon vs. GD, not Muon vs. Adam, because Adam resists closed-form analysis in the Kronecker eigenmode basis.
- Focus on causal LLMs only; no verification on diffusion models, vision transformers, or other architectures.
- Assumes stationarity of Hessian structure within a step — valid locally but may not capture rapid spectral changes in early training.
- The within-layer gap being layer-dependent (concentrated at first/deepest layers) is relegated to an appendix and not theoretically explained.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Does the NDS gap between Muon and Adam hold at 7B+ parameter scale, or does the Kronecker structure break down and erode the advantage? Can proxy quantities (e.g., trace of Hessian blocks) stand in for exact NDS at frontier scale?

2. The within-layer NDS gap is concentrated at the first and deepest layers (Appendix C). What is the layer-role explanation — is this related to residual stream dynamics, embedding/unembedding matrix conditioning, or attention pattern formation?

3. Theorem 5.5 requires $\rho + 1 > 1/\alpha > 1 + \sigma_H/\sigma_L$. How tight is this condition in practice? Are there training phases (e.g., early warmup) where it fails, which could explain Muon's slower convergence in some early-training comparisons?

4. If the curvature perspective is correct, can we design a hybrid optimizer that applies Muon's spectral normalization selectively to high-NDS layers and Adam to low-NDS layers, capturing the best of both?

5. The paper suggests that recent Muon variants enhancing flat-direction dynamics (Zhu et al. 2026) benefit from further NDS reduction. Can NDS be monitored cheaply online (e.g., via randomized trace estimators) to guide adaptive direction updates?
