> **一句话总结：** 异步强化学习中，丢弃行为策略信息会导致正负优势样本在当前策略对数概率尺度上失衡，ASymPO 通过按每个响应自身的当前尺度归一化损失，在不依赖行为策略的情况下恢复响应级零和平衡，稳定训练。

> **TL;DR:** In asynchronous LLM post-training, dropping behavior-policy probabilities causes positive and negative advantage responses to enter the loss at mismatched current-policy log-probability scales, breaking the zero-sum balance that group-relative RL relies on. ASymPO fixes this by normalizing each response's token loss by its own current average token negative log-probability — no behavior-policy information required — and empirically matches or approaches GRPO performance across three model families on mathematical reasoning benchmarks.

---

## 核心贡献

1. **识别尺度失衡故障模式**：在当前策略独立（current-policy-only）异步组相对 RL 中，正负优势响应在当前策略下的对数概率尺度可能显著不同。即使原始优势之和为零，较大尺度的负样本也会使损失的负侧主导训练，导致奖励崩溃。

2. **提出 SPO（Scaled Policy Optimization）**：一种简单基线，对负优势响应的损失贡献乘以固定系数 α∈(0,1)，削弱其影响，改善训练稳定性，但依赖手动调参。

3. **提出 ASymPO（Asymmetric-Scale Policy Optimization）**：自适应方案——将每个响应的 token 损失除以该响应在当前策略下的平均 token 负对数概率（停止梯度），从而自动将正负响应的损失贡献归一化至可比尺度，理论上证明可精确实现响应级零和平衡。

4. **简化 Rollout–Learner 接口**：ASymPO 无需传输 per-token 行为策略对数概率、策略版本标签或推理精度 logit 重计算，接口仅需采样 token 和标量奖励，显著降低系统复杂度。

## Key Contributions

1. **Scale-imbalance failure mode identification**: In current-policy-only asynchronous group-relative RL, stale negative-advantage responses can have far larger current negative log-probability scales than positive responses. Even with zero-sum raw advantages, the negative side of the loss dominates, destabilizing training.

2. **SPO (Scaled Policy Optimization)**: A simple baseline that multiplies negative-advantage loss contributions by a fixed coefficient α∈(0,1), reducing their influence. Effective but requires a manually chosen hyperparameter.

3. **ASymPO (Asymmetric-Scale Policy Optimization)**: An adaptive method that divides each response's token loss by that response's own current average token negative log-probability (stop-gradient). Theorem 2 proves this achieves exact response-level zero-sum balance: the normalized positive and negative loss contributions sum identically to the raw positive and negative advantage sums.

4. **Compact rollout–learner interface**: ASymPO removes the per-token behavior log-probability channel, policy-version tags, and inference-precision logit recomputation from the rollout–learner interface — requiring only sampled tokens and scalar rewards.

## Methodology

**Problem setup.** Asynchronous RL decouples rollout workers (sampling from recent policy snapshots) from the learner (training on a newer policy). This creates distribution drift between the behavior policy π_b (which generated responses) and the current policy π_θ.

**Standard correction (GRPO/PPO-style).** Uses importance ratios ρ_{g,i} = π_θ(a_{g,i}|x, a_{g,<i}) / π_b(a_{g,i}|x, a_{g,<i}) with clipping. This bounds per-token drift and preserves response-level loss balance (Theorem 1), but requires transporting π_b log-probs through the rollout–learner interface.

**The naive current-policy loss** (dropping π_b entirely):

L_naive(θ) = (1/G) Σ_g A_g · S_{θ,g}

where S_{θ,g} = -(1/m_g) Σ_i log π_θ(a_{g,i}|x, a_{g,<i}) is the average token negative log-probability. Without the behavior-policy reference scale, if stale negative samples have inflated S_{θ,g}, the negative side dominates.

**SPO** introduces a fixed asymmetric coefficient:
- C_g^SPO = 1 for A_g ≥ 0; C_g^SPO = α for A_g < 0

**ASymPO** sets C_g^AsymPO = 1 / sg(S_{θ,g}), giving:

L_ASymPO(θ) = (1/G) Σ_g A_g · S_{θ,g} / sg(S_{θ,g})

The gradient is ∇_θ L_ASymPO(θ) = (1/G) Σ_g [A_g / sg(S_{θ,g})] · ∇_θ S_{θ,g}, so each response's update is normalized by its own current scale. The stop-gradient ensures normalization does not interfere with the gradient direction.

**Theorem 2 (Exact balance).** Under zero-sum advantages and S_{θ,g} > 0 for all g, ASymPO satisfies:
- Σ_{g∈P} A_g · S_{θ,g}/sg(S_{θ,g}) = Σ_{g∈P} A_g (positive contributions equal raw positive advantage sum)
- Σ_{g∈N} |A_g| · S_{θ,g}/sg(S_{θ,g}) = Σ_{g∈N} |A_g| (symmetric for negative side)

## Results & Highlights

**Setup.** Three model families — Qwen3-1.7B-Base, Qwen3-4B-Base, LLaMA-3.2-3B-Instruct — trained on 4k MATH problems with 8 rollouts per prompt. Baselines: Naive Loss, GPG, GRPO (behavior-corrected), SPO, ASymPO. Evaluated on AIME24, AIME25, MATH500, AMC23, GSM8K, Minerva-Math using mean@8 and pass@8.

**Stability (RQ1 & RQ3):** Naive Loss and GPG collapse during training on all three model families, confirming that removing importance sampling alone (GPG) is insufficient — the zero-sum positive-negative balance is the critical property. GRPO, SPO, and ASymPO all maintain stable reward trajectories.

**Benchmark accuracy (mean@8):**

| Method     | Qwen3-1.7B Avg | LLaMA-3.2 Avg | Qwen3-4B Avg |
|------------|---------------|---------------|--------------|
| GRPO       | **37.45**     | 30.84         | **45.52**    |
| SPO        | 36.41         | 29.61         | 45.45        |
| ASymPO     | 36.98         | **31.70**     | 45.07        |
| Naive Loss | collapsed     | collapsed     | collapsed    |
| GPG        | collapsed     | collapsed     | collapsed    |

- ASymPO surpasses GRPO on LLaMA-3.2-3B-Instruct (31.70 vs 30.84 mean@8; 49.19 vs 46.09 pass@8).
- ASymPO beats GRPO on AIME24 and MATH500 for Qwen3-1.7B, and on GSM8K and Minerva-Math for Qwen3-4B.
- Neither SPO nor ASymPO uniformly dominates GRPO, but current-policy-only methods are competitive and require far simpler infrastructure.

**RQ2 (ASymPO vs SPO).** ASymPO generally improves over SPO and avoids SPO's sensitivity to the manually chosen α. The advantage is clearest on LLaMA-3.2-3B-Instruct; results are mixed on Qwen3-4B-Base.

## Critical Assessment

The paper makes a clear, well-scoped contribution: it identifies a specific failure mode (scale imbalance in current-policy-only async RL), provides a theoretical explanation, and proposes an elegant fix (ASymPO) that requires no new hyperparameters. The framing is honest — the authors explicitly note that ASymPO is not a complete trust-region method and does not bound the policy ratio.

The practical motivation is sound: eliminating per-token behavior log-probability transport simplifies distributed training infrastructure in non-trivial ways (token-aligned versioning, numerical precision consistency, logit recomputation at inference precision). This engineering argument complements the algorithmic contribution.

The empirical results are credible but modest in scope: three small-to-medium models, one task domain (mathematical reasoning with verifiable rewards), and results that show rough parity with GRPO rather than clear superiority. The claim is carefully stated as "competitive," not "better."

---

## Strengths

- **Theoretically principled.** Theorem 2 provides an exact guarantee (not an approximation bound) that ASymPO achieves response-level zero-sum balance — a clean and verifiable property.
- **Infrastructure simplification is real.** Removing the behavior log-prob channel eliminates three concrete engineering costs (Table 1): transport overhead, version bookkeeping, and inference-precision drift.
- **No new hyperparameters.** Unlike SPO, ASymPO's coefficient is fully determined by the current policy's own statistics, removing sensitivity to α.
- **Honest experimental design.** The authors include GPG as a control to isolate whether "avoiding importance sampling" alone explains stability — it does not.
- **Clear failure-mode analysis.** Section 3 gives a concrete algebraic account of why naive current-policy loss breaks, grounded in the decomposition of Σ A_g S_{θ,g} into behavior-scale and drift terms.

---

## Limitations

- **Scope limited to math reasoning with verifiable rewards.** The paper does not evaluate RLHF settings with learned reward models, multi-turn interaction, or long-horizon agent tasks.
- **ASymPO does not bound the policy ratio.** Unlike PPO-style clipping, the method provides no guarantee about how far π_θ drifts from π_b; in heavily asynchronous regimes with very stale responses, current-policy probabilities alone may not characterize the off-policy mismatch.
- **Normalization is a heuristic, not a trust-region method.** ASymPO's behavior depends on how advantages are normalized, response length variation, and extreme current-policy probabilities.
- **Performance gap with GRPO is small but present.** On Qwen3-1.7B-Base and Qwen3-4B-Base, GRPO achieves the best average score. The infrastructure savings must be weighed against this potential accuracy cost in production settings.
- **SPO requires α tuning.** While ASymPO solves this, SPO's α = 0.2 is borrowed from RIFT (Liu et al., 2026), and its generality across tasks, models, and staleness regimes is not evaluated.

---

## Relevance

This paper is directly relevant to practitioners building asynchronous LLM post-training pipelines (e.g., large-scale GRPO/PPO deployments). The infrastructure simplification argument is particularly valuable for systems where storing and transporting per-token behavior log-probs is a bottleneck. It is also relevant to researchers studying the theory of group-relative RL objectives, off-policy correction, and the interaction between training staleness and optimization stability.

Less relevant for: researchers focused on RLHF with learned reward models, preference optimization (DPO/IPO), or online synchronous RL settings where distribution drift is negligible.

---

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. **How does ASymPO behave under extreme staleness?** The paper uses a staleness threshold of 0.5 and mini-batch size 32. Would ASymPO degrade under higher staleness (e.g., threshold 1.0 or unbounded replay buffers)?

2. **Does response-level normalization interact with response length?** Longer responses have more tokens and potentially different S_{θ,g} distributions. Does ASymPO inadvertently upweight or downweight long responses?

3. **Can ASymPO be combined with lightweight drift diagnostics?** The paper acknowledges it does not bound the policy ratio. A hybrid that adds a soft KL penalty or staleness filter on top of ASymPO might recover some of the trust-region guarantees.

4. **Generalization beyond math reasoning.** The scale-imbalance diagnosis should apply to any group-relative RL setting. Do the same dynamics emerge with learned reward models or binary preference labels?

5. **Theoretical connection to importance weighting.** Is there a regime (e.g., small drift, specific advantage distributions) where ASymPO's normalization provably approximates the first-order effect of importance-ratio correction?
