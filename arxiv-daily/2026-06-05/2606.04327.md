> **一句话总结：** 本文通过引入"内 Hessian 矩阵"这一新工具，完整刻画了两层神经网络中神经元分裂（neuron splitting）所产生的稳态平台（stationary plateau）上每个点的局部几何性质——究竟是局部极小值还是鞍点。

> **TL;DR:** This paper gives a complete classification of every stationary point on the "neuron-splitting plateau" of a two-layer neural network: a new per-neuron curvature object called the inner Hessian determines, based on its definiteness and the splitting coefficients, whether each embedded point is a local minimum or a saddle. Splitting a saddle always gives saddles; splitting a local minimum generically introduces saddles.

---

## 核心贡献

1. **完整分类定理（Theorem 1）：** 当窄网络的驻点是局部极小值，且父神经元的内 Hessian 矩阵为正定（PD）、负定（ND）或不定（ID）时，本文给出平台上每个嵌入点是极小值或鞍点的充要条件，以分裂系数 λ 的区域划分为依据。

2. **确定鞍点区域（Theorem 2）：** 在内 Hessian 不为零矩阵的条件下，参数空间 Λ 中存在一个正测度子集（sure-saddle region）Λ_saddle，落在该区域的任意分裂系数都保证产生鞍点。

3. **局部有效/局部常数神经元（Theorem 3）：** 在更弱的条件下——父神经元"局部有效"时必然存在鞍点；父神经元"局部常数"时平台全为局部极小。两者之间存在一个小的理论缺口。

4. **鞍点传染性（Theorem 4）：** 若窄网络的驻点本身是鞍点，则无论分裂系数如何选择，宽网络上的所有嵌入点均为鞍点（"saddles beget saddles"）。

## Key Contributions

1. **Complete classification theorem (Theorem 1):** When the original narrow-net stationary point is a local minimum, the definiteness of the parent neuron's inner Hessian (PD / ND / ID) together with the splitting coefficient vector λ fully determines whether each embedded wide-net point is a local min or saddle.

2. **Sure-saddle region (Theorem 2):** Under the mild assumption that the inner Hessian is non-zero, a concrete positive-measure region Λ_saddle ⊂ Λ is identified where any splitting coefficient choice yields a saddle — a computationally tractable escape direction.

3. **Locally effective / locally constant neurons (Theorem 3):** Under the weakest conditions, splitting a locally effective neuron always introduces saddle points; splitting a locally constant neuron preserves local minimality. A small gap between these two regimes remains open.

4. **Saddle propagation (Theorem 4):** Splitting a saddle point of the narrow network always produces an all-saddle plateau in the wide network, regardless of coefficients.

## Methodology

**Setup.** The paper studies the quadratic empirical loss of a single-hidden-layer network f(x; θ) = v⊤σ(Wx) with smooth (twice continuously differentiable) activation σ. The narrow network has r hidden neurons; splitting the r-th neuron into (m − r + 1) daughter neurons with the same input weights but redistributed output weights according to coefficients λ = [λ₀, …, λ_{m−r}] summing to 1 produces the wide network.

**Inner Hessian.** The key analytical object is the inner Hessian matrix H^{in}_i ∈ ℝ^{d×d} for the i-th hidden neuron:

    (H^{in}_i)_{j,j'} = v_i ⟨ŷ − y, z''_i ∘ x_j ∘ x_{j'}⟩

where z''_i = σ''(w_i⊤X) is the element-wise second derivative of the i-th neuron's pre-activation output vector. This is exactly the second term in the Hessian decomposition of L with respect to w_i — the curvature contribution from the residual (not from the Jacobian part).

**Proof strategy.** Two complementary directions of perturbation analysis are used:
- To prove local minimality: for any perturbed wide-net point θ', construct an "auxiliary pre-split" narrow-net perturbation θ' that lies in the neighborhood of the narrow-net local min. Since the narrow-net local min assumption bounds L(θ') − L(θ) ≥ 0, and the post-split splitting gap L(θ') − L(θ) ≥ L(θ') − L(θ) ≥ 0, the embedded point is also a local min.
- To prove saddle status: construct explicit descent directions using the inner Hessian's eigenvectors, showing L(θ') − L(θ) < 0 to second order.

**Three plateau types.** Because neural networks have no non-trivial local maxima (Lemma 1 — any local maximum must be a locally constant point), every stationary plateau is one of: all-localmin, localmin-saddle (mixed), or all-saddle.

## Results & Highlights

| Theorem | Condition on inner Hessian | Conclusion |
|---|---|---|
| Theorem 1 | H^{in} is PD, ND, or ID | Full characterization of every plateau point |
| Theorem 2 | H^{in} ≠ 0 | Positive-measure sure-saddle region identified |
| Theorem 3.1 | H^{in} may be 0; parent is locally effective | Saddle exists (existence, not full characterization) |
| Theorem 3.2 | H^{in} = 0; parent is locally constant | No saddle — all-localmin plateau |
| Theorem 4 | Original point is a saddle | All-saddle plateau (saddles beget saddles) |

**Key asymmetry.** Splitting a local minimum *generically* destroys the all-localmin property and introduces saddles; the only escape is the degenerate case of a locally constant neuron. Splitting a saddle is completely stable — it always yields saddles. This means increasing network width converts isolated bad regions into more escapable structures with saddle escape directions.

**Plateau-type evolution law.** Under width expansion by neuron splitting:
- Saddle → all-saddle plateau (universally)
- Local minimum + PD inner Hessian → mixed (localmin for λ ∈ Λ_PD; saddle otherwise)
- Local minimum + ND inner Hessian → mixed (localmin for λ ∈ Λ_ND; saddle otherwise)
- Local minimum + ID inner Hessian → all-saddle plateau

**Connection to training dynamics.** The local-min–saddle line structure on the plateau is a geometric analogue of the "river-valley landscape" (Wen et al. 2024, arXiv:2410.05192), where gradient trajectories move along low-curvature directions while oscillating transversely. The sure-saddle region also provides theoretical backing for splitting-based architecture growth algorithms such as "split-and-descent."

## Critical Assessment

**Strengths:**
- The inner Hessian is a natural and well-motivated object: it arises directly from the second-order Taylor expansion of the neuron output, and the connection to the Hessian decomposition (Eq. 12 / Appendix B) is rigorously established.
- The results are genuinely stronger than prior work: earlier analyses (Fukumizu & Amari 2000; Fukumizu et al. NeurIPS 2019; Zhang et al. JMLR 2022) proved only sufficient conditions for saddles to appear; this paper gives necessary and sufficient conditions under the PD/ND/ID cases.
- Theorem 4 (saddles beget saddles) is clean, elegant, and practically useful for understanding width expansion.
- The perturbation analysis is careful and the proof structure (pre-split + post-split decomposition) is well-explained.

**Limitations:**
- **Two-layer networks only.** The framework handles one-hidden-layer networks. Extension to deeper architectures — where splitting at an intermediate layer propagates effects through subsequent layers — is not addressed and would require substantial new ideas.
- **Smooth activations required.** The inner Hessian involves σ'', so ReLU networks are excluded. The ReLU case is separately treated in [19] (Wu, Simsek, Ged 2024), which provides a complete classification for shallow ReLU-like networks. The two approaches are complementary but the smooth-activation results may not directly inform the piecewise-linear setting.
- **Single neuron split.** The analysis considers splitting one neuron at a time. Simultaneous splitting of multiple neurons would produce higher-dimensional plateaus whose geometry could be qualitatively different.
- **Empirical validation absent.** The paper is purely theoretical. It is not verified numerically whether the sure-saddle region identified by Theorem 2 is easily reachable during training, or whether the locally-constant neuron degenerate case actually occurs in practice.
- **The gap in Theorem 3.** A small theoretical gap remains between "locally effective" (saddle exists) and "locally constant" (no saddle). Whether all neurons in the gap produce saddles or not is left open.

---

## Strengths

- Provides the first *complete* characterization (necessary and sufficient) of every plateau point for smooth two-layer networks, unifying and extending 20+ years of partial results.
- Introduces the inner Hessian as a clean per-neuron diagnostic — interpretable as the second-order sensitivity of the loss to perturbing the neuron's input weights — with an explicit connection to the full Hessian decomposition.
- The "sure-saddle region" (Theorem 2) is a concrete, positive-measure, computationally tractable region in splitting-coefficient space, making the theory actionable for practitioners designing network growth algorithms.
- Cleanly explains the empirical observation that wider networks are easier to train: width expansion converts local minima into saddle-containing plateaus with escape directions.
- The proof strategy (pre-split auxiliary perturbation + Cauchy-Schwarz bounds) is modular and could potentially be adapted to related settings.

---

## Limitations

- Restricted to two-layer networks and smooth activations; practical deep learning uses both deeper architectures and ReLU-like activations.
- No numerical experiments to validate the practical relevance of the identified geometric regions.
- Single-neuron-split analysis only; multi-neuron simultaneous splitting remains uncharacterized.
- The theoretical gap between locally effective and locally constant neurons (Theorem 3) leaves a regime where the plateau type is undetermined.
- Stationarity is with respect to the empirical (training) loss; generalization implications are not discussed.

---

## Relevance

**For optimization theory researchers:** A high-relevance, technically rigorous contribution that closes a long-standing open question about the geometry of neuron-splitting plateaus. The inner Hessian formalism is likely to be adopted in follow-up work.

**For practitioners of neural architecture growth / network expansion:** The sure-saddle region result (Theorem 2) provides principled guidance: unless you choose splitting coefficients very carefully (Λ_PD or Λ_ND), you will land on a saddle with an escape direction — which is actually *good* for optimization.

**For loss landscape / NTK researchers:** Connects to and extends several active threads (mode connectivity, disappearance of bad basins with width, river-valley dynamics), offering a local geometric perspective.

**For those working on ReLU networks:** This paper does *not* apply directly; see Wu, Simsek, Ged (arXiv:2402.05626) for the ReLU analog.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Can the inner Hessian framework be extended to deeper networks (more than one hidden layer)? The difficulty is that splitting a layer-1 neuron affects the Hessian structure at layer 2 as well — what is the right multi-layer generalization?

2. What is the practical distribution of inner Hessian definiteness in trained networks? Is PD, ND, or ID the typical case at a local minimum found by SGD?

3. The paper shows that the sure-saddle region has positive Lebesgue measure. But does a gradient-based optimizer starting from a uniform random split coefficient land in this region with high probability? Is there an optimization-dynamics perspective?

4. How does the result interact with the NTK regime? In the infinite-width limit, the loss landscape linearizes — does the inner Hessian vanish (locally constant regime) for all neurons, making every splitting plateau all-localmin?

5. Can the locally constant neuron degenerate case (all-localmin plateau) be characterized by a simple closed-form condition on the network parameters and training data, analogous to the inner Hessian definiteness condition?

6. The paper connects to the "split-and-descent" algorithm (Wu, Wang, Liu, NeurIPS 2019). Does the sure-saddle region translate into a concrete recommendation for how to choose splitting coefficients to guarantee descent?
