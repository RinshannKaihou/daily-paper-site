> **一句话总结：** Muon 优化器在训练过程中动量矩阵的奇异值分布遵循关于模型规模的幂律，且各层指数差异显著（从 $M^{-0.25}$ 到 $M^{-0.96}$），这意味着统一的 Newton-Schulz 迭代配置在大规模训练时对深层网络不够用——本文给出第一套定量的、层感知的 NS 迭代选取准则。

> **TL;DR:** The singular value quantiles of Muon's momentum buffers stabilize quickly during training and then follow clean power laws in model size, but with markedly different exponents per layer. Mid-to-early layers scale gently (~$M^{-0.25}$) and the standard 5-step Newton-Schulz (NS) configuration remains adequate at frontier scale; final layers (especially MLP projections and attention O) scale aggressively (up to $M^{-0.96}$) and will fall into NS failure territory at ~300B parameters unless more NS iterations or better-tuned coefficients are used. The fitted laws give practitioners a principled, layer-aware recipe for choosing the minimal NS cost that preserves update quality.

## 核心贡献

1. **首个关于 Muon 动量谱的系统研究。** 跨越 77M 到 2.8B 参数的 GPT-2 风格模型，在 4 个相对深度检查点 (mid-early / mid / mid-late / final) 追踪 Q、K、V、O 四个注意力矩阵和两个 MLP 投影矩阵的奇异值分位数，共 24 个动量缓冲区。

2. **稳定化 + 幂律发现。** 在短暂的预热之后，所有被追踪的分位数都稳定在一个由层类型和模型规模共同决定的固定值，该稳定值在 log-log 坐标下与模型规模呈极干净的线性关系（即幂律），且不同层的指数截然不同。

3. **层感知的 NS 配置准则（实用工具）。** 利用上述幂律，从 2.8B 外推到 300B 乃至更大规模，给出每个层类型所需的最少 NS 步数，避免过度计算同时不牺牲更新质量。

## Key Contributions

1. **First systematic study of Muon's momentum spectrum.** Trains GPT-2-style models (77M–2.8B) and tracks singular value quantiles ($q \in \{0.1, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 0.9\}$) for all six momentum matrices (Q, K, V, O attention projections + MLP up/down projections) at four relative depth checkpoints (mid-early, mid, mid-late, final), yielding 24 momentum buffers per model across 7 model sizes.

2. **Stabilization phenomenon + spectral power laws.** After a short burn-in (a few hundred steps), every tracked quantile stabilizes at a value determined solely by layer type and model size. These stabilization values fit remarkably clean power laws in model size $M$ on log-log scales, with layer-dependent exponents that range from approximately $-0.25$ (mild, mid-to-early layers) to $-0.96$ (aggressive, final MLP/O layers).

3. **Layer-aware NS configuration recipe.** The fitted laws allow practitioners to predict, for any target scale, the fraction of singular directions a given NS configuration will orthonormalize, and to choose the cheapest NS configuration (number of iterations / polynomial coefficients) that still covers the directions that matter.

## Methodology

### Background: Muon and Newton-Schulz

Muon replaces raw gradient updates with orthonormalized updates. At each step it maintains a momentum buffer $M_t \leftarrow \mu \cdot M_t + G_t$ and applies NS to produce the update $O_{t+1} = \text{NS}(M_{t+1})$, then steps $\Theta_{t+1} \leftarrow \Theta_t - \eta \cdot O_{t+1}$.

NS normalizes the matrix by its Frobenius norm to map all singular values into $[0,1]$, then iterates a composition of degree-5 odd polynomials. The key limitation: since NS maps through odd polynomials satisfying $f(0)=0$, it cannot orthonormalize directions whose singular values fall below a threshold (~0.003 for the canonical 5-step NanoGPT polynomial). Any direction below that threshold is effectively suppressed by $10\times$ or more.

The **NanoGPT 5-step** NS configuration (from Cesista et al., 2025) uses optimized polynomial coefficients (five distinct degree-5 polynomials composed). **DeepSeek-V4** uses a 10-step composition ($f = a^{\circ 2} \circ c^{\circ 8}$), which is more accurate but 2× more expensive.

### Experimental Setup

- **Models:** GPT-2-style, 77M / 160M / 354M / 600M / 1.2B / 1.6B / 2.8B parameters (model dims 512–2560, 8–32 layers, seq len 512–1024).
- **Training:** Chinchilla-optimal token budget ($20\times$ model parameters), FineWeb dataset, L40 or H200 GPUs.
- **Tracking:** Frobenius-normalized momentum matrix $\tilde{M}^{(t)} = M^{(t)} / \|M^{(t)}\|_F$ at every training step for quantiles $q \in \{0.1, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 0.9\}$. The 50th quantile $\sigma_{0.5}$ is the median singular value; $\sigma_{0.1}$ captures the small (bulk) singular values most at risk.
- **Stabilization values:** Estimated by averaging the quantile trajectories over steps 1300–1500 (post-burn-in), then fitting a power law $\sigma_q(M) = c \cdot M^\alpha$ on log-log scale.

### How Much Orthonormalization is Needed?

A controlled experiment with **rank-$p$ orthonormal updates** (using only the top-$p$ fraction of singular vectors) shows:
- $p = 0.9$: essentially indistinguishable from full Muon.
- $p = 0.5$: only a minor gap; recovers full Muon performance.
- $p = 0.25$: ~10–20% less token-efficient than full Muon.
- $p = 0.1$: ~50% less token-efficient, clearly impractical.

This establishes that the **50th quantile** is the critical threshold: NS needs to orthonormalize at least the top half of singular directions to preserve Muon's benefits. The scaling laws derived are thus self-consistent — they predict whether the 50th-quantile singular value sits above or below the NS failure threshold at a given scale.

### Spectral Dynamics

Two behaviors observed during training:
- **Q and K matrices:** exhibit a sharp decrease followed by a recovery before stabilizing.
- **V, O, MLP matrices:** quantiles increase monotonically from the start before stabilizing.

In all cases, stabilized values decrease monotonically with model size — larger models have smaller bulk singular values, making NS failure increasingly likely with fixed configurations.

The full singular value spectrum (shown for the 2.8B model at step 1450) is dominated by a single large outlier, with the remaining values concentrated near zero and decaying roughly exponentially. This heavy concentration near zero is precisely what puts late layers at NS failure risk.

## Results & Highlights

### Power Law Exponents by Layer

| Layer position / type | Scaling exponent $\alpha$ |
|---|---|
| Mid-early through mid-late (most layers) | ~$-0.25$ |
| Mid-late Q projection | ~$-0.27$ |
| Mid-late V projection | ~$-0.26$ |
| Mid O projection | ~$-0.23$ |
| Mid K projection | ~$-0.25$ |
| Final O projection | ~$-0.66$ |
| Final MLP Up projection | ~$-0.96$ |

A factor-of-32 increase in model size reduces mid-layer stabilization values by only ~$2\times$ (since $32^{0.25} \approx 2$), while the final MLP Up projection's value decreases by nearly $32\times$ (since $32^{0.96} \approx 28$).

### Case Study: Extrapolating to 300B Scale

Starting from the fitted laws at 2.8B:

**Mid-late Q layer:** $\sigma_{0.5}(2.8\text{B}) \approx 5 \times 10^{-3}$, exponent $-0.27$.  
At 300B: $5 \times 10^{-3} \cdot 100^{-0.27} \approx 1.4 \times 10^{-3}$.  
This sits **above** the NanoGPT 5-step failure threshold, so the standard configuration suffices.

**Final O layer:** $\sigma_{0.5}(2.8\text{B}) \approx 10^{-3}$, exponent $-0.66$.  
At 300B: $10^{-3} \cdot 100^{-0.66} \approx 5 \times 10^{-5}$.  
This falls **inside** the NanoGPT 5-step failure regime — a more accurate configuration (e.g., DeepSeek-V4's 10-step composition) is required for this layer.

### Practical Implication

A uniform NS configuration across all layers is unavoidably suboptimal at scale: the layers that need the most accurate orthonormalization (late layers) are precisely those with the fastest-shrinking singular values. A layer-aware strategy can use the cheap 5-step NS for the majority of layers and invest in more NS iterations only for the handful of late layers that require it.

## Critical Assessment

**What the paper does well:**
The core empirical observation — that singular value quantiles stabilize and then follow power laws in model size — is clearly demonstrated with extensive plots across all 24 tracked buffers and 7 model sizes. The self-consistency argument (rank-$p$ experiments + spectral scaling laws) is thoughtfully constructed. The case study extrapolation to 300B is concrete and actionable.

**What to scrutinize:**
- The power laws are fit over a 40× range (77M to 2.8B). Extrapolating 100× further to 300B is a significant leap that assumes the power law continues cleanly — a standard assumption in scaling law papers but not guaranteed.
- The experiments use GPT-2-style (decoder-only, dense attention) models trained on FineWeb. The authors explicitly note the laws may not transfer to MoE architectures, different attention variants, or other optimizers.
- The rank-$p$ ablation uses no error feedback (unlike Dion which does), so the performance gaps observed may be conservative estimates of how much orthonormalization quality actually matters in practice.
- The paper does not propose or validate new NS coefficient designs tuned to the empirical singular value distributions — this is left as future work, which is the most practically impactful follow-on.
- All models trained to Chinchilla-optimal budgets only; behavior at overtraining ratios (common for inference-efficient models) is unstudied.

---

## Strengths

1. **Fills a real knowledge gap.** Despite Muon's adoption in Kimi-K2, GLM-5, and DeepSeek-V4, essentially nothing was known empirically about how the NS approximation quality evolves during training or with scale. This paper is the first to ask and answer the question rigorously.

2. **Remarkably clean empirical signal.** The power laws are tight across all layer types (Figure 16 shows all 24 buffers × 5 quantiles fitting cleanly), which is non-obvious — messy empirics would have been equally plausible.

3. **Directly actionable output.** The fitted exponents and the NanoGPT/DeepSeek-V4 failure thresholds (from Figures 8 and 9) combine to give a layer-by-layer decision boundary that practitioners can apply without re-running experiments.

4. **Honest about the rank-$p$ self-consistency loop.** The authors carefully note that the scaling laws may underestimate NS misses for configurations that only orthonormalize ≤25% of directions (since rank-$p$ quantile trajectories deviate from full Muon's). This caveat matters for aggressive cost reduction.

5. **Strong institutional backing and clean writing.** MIT theory group, well-motivated background section, explicit algorithm box, tight 9-page main body.

---

## Limitations

1. **Architecture specificity.** GPT-2-style dense transformers only. MoE, GQA, linear attention, SSM, and hybrid architectures are all unaddressed. Given that frontier models are overwhelmingly MoE, this is a material gap.

2. **Scale of extrapolation.** The 100× extrapolation from 2.8B to 300B is well-motivated but unverified; compute constraints prevented validation at intermediate scales (e.g., 10B or 30B).

3. **No new NS coefficient designs.** The paper diagnoses the problem precisely but stops short of prescribing new polynomial coefficients optimized for the observed singular value distributions. The practical payoff (compute savings at frontier scale) is deferred entirely to future work.

4. **Chinchilla training only.** Modern practice increasingly involves over-training relative to Chinchilla (e.g., 10× or more tokens per parameter for smaller models). Whether the spectral dynamics differ in over-training regimes is unknown.

5. **Small model floor at 77M.** No exploration of whether the power laws break down below ~100M parameters, where training dynamics often differ.

---

## Relevance

**Directly relevant to:** Anyone training or planning to train large language models with Muon or Muon-derived optimizers (Dion, NorMuon, MuonBP). The results are immediately applicable for choosing NS iteration count by layer, and serve as a warning for teams training at >10B scale with the standard 5-step NanoGPT configuration.

**Indirectly relevant to:** Researchers studying Shampoo, SOAP, or other preconditioned/orthonormalized optimizers that rely on iterative matrix function approximations, since the core question — how does the approximation quality degrade with scale? — is identical in structure.

**Connection to broader scaling law literature:** This paper extends the Kaplan/Chinchilla tradition of scaling law empirics from loss-vs-compute to optimizer internals — a methodologically natural but previously unexplored direction.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. **Do these power laws transfer to MoE architectures?** Kimi-K2 and DeepSeek-V4 are MoE — do expert layers have the same spectral dynamics as dense layers? The expert routing creates very different gradient statistics.

2. **What happens at over-training ratios?** If a 1B model is trained on 100B tokens (100× Chinchilla), do the singular value quantiles stabilize at the same values as at Chinchilla-optimal training? The momentum buffer will have seen far more gradient signal.

3. **Can NS coefficients be designed per-layer using these distributions?** The authors suggest this as future work. Given the tight empirical distributions (exponential bulk decay with one outlier), it seems plausible to derive near-optimal polynomials analytically or via cheap optimization.

4. **How do these laws interact with learning rate schedules?** The stabilization values are measured under constant learning rate followed by linear decay. Do cosine schedules, warmup, or cooldown phases alter the spectral trajectory?

5. **Is the spectral stabilization a cause or an effect of Muon's training dynamics?** The paper treats it as a characterization, but the question of whether the stabilization is a convergence criterion or a coincidental artifact of Chinchilla training warrants further investigation.

6. **What is the relationship between layer depth and scaling exponent?** The paper reports exponents at four relative depth positions but does not give a functional form for how the exponent varies continuously with depth. A theory grounding this (even heuristic) would be valuable.
