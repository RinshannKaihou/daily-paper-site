LLMs can leak training data — but under natural prompts they almost never want to, with prefix attacks eliciting a 36× stronger memorization signal.

LLMs Can Leak Training Data But Do They Want To? A Propensity-Aware Evaluation of Memorization in LLMs ↗ Barmina · Schneider-Kamp · Galke Poech  •  Univ. of Southern Denmark Jun 2026
memorization privacy LLM evaluation training-data-leakage propensity-vs-capability PropMe SimpleTrace

Key Metric

36×

stronger memorization under prefix attacks vs. natural prompts

NVR: 0.036 (prefix) vs. 0.001 (generic/specific)
DFM Decoder on Dynaword

Why It Matters

Current capability-only memorization audits systematically overestimate real deployment privacy risk — organizations are auditing a threat that rarely materializes.

Critical Edge

Only one model family tested; no instruction-tuned models evaluated. The reassuring "low propensity" finding may not transfer to actual deployed systems where RLHF-fine-tuning substantially alters both capability and propensity profiles.

Methodology

1

PropMe Framework — Three Settings

Generic & Specific (propensity): natural prompts generated by GPT-5.5, no direct training-data overlap. Prefix (capability): adversarial 50-token prefixes extracted directly from training examples, model evaluated against the full doc.

2

Propensity Metric Transformation

A single formula maps base metric f_b into [0,1] by comparing propensity vs. capability scores — isolating whether a model reproduces data spontaneously or only when attacked.

PM formula

PM(M,x) = ½(1 + (f_b^p − f_b^c) / (f_b^p + f_b^c))

3

SimpleTrace — Deterministic Attribution

4-step suffix-array pipeline: maximal span extraction → unigram rarity filtering (top 5% rarest spans) → document retrieval → span merging. Runs 100 queries in ~1 min on Common Pile (460B tokens).

4

Models & Data

Comma v0.1 (English base, Common Pile 463.6B tokens) & DFM Decoder (continual pre-training of Comma + Danish Dynaword). All experiments at temperature 0, 100 samples per setting.

Results

Memorization Gap

Prefix attack NVR = 0.036; generic/specific NVR = 0.001 — a consistent 36x gap across all model-corpus pairs. Propensity scores remain well below the neutral 0.5 in all non-adversarial settings.

Propensity Scores (Comma / Common Pile)

PMNVR = 0.040 (generic), 0.153 (specific). PMFMR = 0.0 (generic), 0.50 (specific). All below or at neutral — model is not spontaneously leaking.

Continual Pre-training Effect

DFM Decoder shows reduced Common Pile memorization capability (NVR 0.032 → 0.024; ALS 50.35 → 40.83 tokens). Training mixture composition — not duration — governs what is memorized.

Training-stage Stability

Memorization metrics nearly unchanged across Stage 1 through Stage 3 of DFM Decoder training. Dynaword ALS: 15.68 / 17.37 / 24.75 tokens — all identical across stages.

SimpleTrace Validation

99% document retrieval on Common Pile (460B tokens), 100% on Dynaword. One missed retrieval: document-ID ambiguity in near-duplicate code documents, not a text-span failure.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • First principled capability/propensity separation for memorization — fills a real conceptual gap in privacy auditing.
  • SimpleTrace is deterministic, open-source, and faster than probabilistic alternatives; OLMoTrace's codebase was never public.
  • Bilingual evaluation (English + Danish) is unusual; adds evidence that findings may not be language-specific.
  • Honest anti-greenwashing caveat in Section 9: low propensity should not excuse copyright violations.
  • Continual pre-training experiment provides novel causal evidence that data mixture, not training duration, governs memorization.
  • High reproducibility: temperature 0, public datasets, documented prompt generation, 30+ metric definitions in appendix.

Limitations

  • Single model family from one institution — conclusions need replication on Llama, Mistral, Falcon, or GPT-class models.
  • No instruction-tuned or RLHF-fine-tuned models — these are the actual deployment artifacts; propensity profiles may differ substantially.
  • 100 samples per setting yields wide confidence intervals for rare events (FMR ≈ 0.02 = 2 out of 100); statistical uncertainty is not quantified.
  • Propensity metric formula has a discontinuity at f_b^p = 0 (defined by convention, not the formula) — may suppress real signals in low-memorization regimes.
  • Requires access to the full training corpus — inapplicable to closed models (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini).
  • GPT-5.5 prompt generation introduces non-determinism; exact prompt regeneration is not guaranteed even with documented instructions.

Open Questions

  1. How do propensity scores change for instruction-tuned or RLHF-fine-tuned versions of the same base models? Does alignment training reduce memorization propensity, capability, or both?
  2. Is the 36x capability-propensity gap consistent across model scales? Does it narrow or widen as models grow larger?
  3. Can the PropMe metric detect differences between models trained on public-domain data vs. data containing PII or copyrighted material?
  4. How sensitive are propensity scores to prompt generator quality and style? Do different LLM prompt generators systematically shift measured propensity?
  5. How does SimpleTrace performance degrade on FineWeb-scale corpora (trillions of tokens)? Is suffix-array indexing still computationally feasible at that scale?
