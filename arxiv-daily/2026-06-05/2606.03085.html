Key Finding
Exponential combinatorial search over LLM components collapses to a continuous gradient problem — unlocking multi-component causal tracing at scale with 229× speedup and order-of-magnitude stronger signal.
Multi-component Causal Tracing in Large Language Models
Zirui Yan · Dennis Wei · Dmitriy A. Katz · Prasanna Sattigeri · Ali Tajer — RPI / IBM Research, 2026
Key Metric
229×
faster than greedy search
MLP metric gain: 6.8×10⁷ vs 125 (top-k on GPT2-medium / Professions). CounterFact: 8376 vs 108.
Why This Matters
Single-component causal tracing systematically misses non-linear interactions that drive LLM bias and factual storage.
Critical Edge
"Causal tracing" is interventional sensitivity analysis — selected components are metric-relative, not proven to store information. A different metric yields a different subset. No downstream editing validation confirms the components actually drive behavior change.
1 Methodology
P
Problem: Exponential Subset Selection

Given N model components (attention heads, MLP neurons), find the ≤S-subset that maximally boosts a metric. Feasible set size: O(NS) — exhaustive search impossible even for DistilGPT2 (N≈4K).

1
Continuous Relaxation

Lift binary mask m ∈ {0,1}^N to [0,1]^N. Initialize at 0.5 for symmetric exploration. Mixture pass: h̄ᵢ = (1−mᵢ)·fᵢ + mᵢ·h'ᵢ

2
Inverse Reward Transformation

Replace raw metric with L = 1/(1 + ℓ(D,m)), keeping gradients in a stable (0,1) range across arbitrary metric scales — solving DCM's instability failure mode.

3
Scheduled Binary Penalty

reg(m) = λ₁‖m‖₁ + λ₂mᵀ(1−m). L1 drives sparsity; binary-violation term (max at 0.5, zero at integers) pushes mask to clean {0,1}. Both λ scheduled up over epochs.

4
Adam + Discretization

LR 0.1 (attention) / 0.5 (MLP). After each epoch: threshold at τ=0.5 → discrete H. Stop when |H|≤S. No convergence guarantee; empirically stable across GPT2 family and LLaMA-13B.

3 Strengths & Limitations
Strengths
  • Principled formulation. Combinatorial→continuous relaxation with a proved complexity theorem (Theorem 1).
  • Strong efficiency. 229× speedup well-supported; tradeoff curve (Fig. 3) matches greedy quality.
  • Broad model coverage. DistilGPT2 through LLaMA-13B, Qwen3, Llama-3.2 — more comprehensive than typical interpretability work.
  • Non-linearity evidence. Figure 1 gives direct, quantified proof that multi-component interactions are non-linear in real models.
  • Ablation rigor. Appendix D: penalty ablations, binary violation analysis, single-component verification, DCM failure mode.
  • Open-source code. github.com/ZiruiYan/multi-component-causal-tracing
Limitations
  • Metric dependency. Component selection is entirely relative to the chosen metric; generalizability is unclear.
  • No convergence guarantee. Hyperparameter sensitivity (λ₁, λ₂, LR, epochs) requires grid search for new settings.
  • Small datasets. WinoGender=44 samples; no variance estimates across seeds or splits reported.
  • English/GPT-centric. Multilingual or MoE architectures untested.
  • No editing validation. Identified components not tested for whether editing them changes behavior (gap vs. ROME/MEMIT).
  • Causality label. Method is interventional sensitivity mapping, not causal proof — a distinction largely elided.
4 Open Questions
Q1
Can PGB-CT be extended to multi-metric Pareto tracing — finding components that improve fairness without degrading accuracy?
Q2
Do components identified for gender bias overlap with those for factual knowledge? Jaccard overlap would reveal whether LLM internals are organized by task or information type.
Q3
Is counterfactual direction asymmetric? Would reversing female→male vs. male→female select the same heads?
Q4
From identification to editing: do zero-ablation or activation-steering of top-S components actually change downstream behavior?
Q5
With WinoGender at 44 samples, how stable are selected subsets across random seeds and dataset splits? Variance unreported.
