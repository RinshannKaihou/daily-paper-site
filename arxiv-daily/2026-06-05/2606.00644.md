> **一句话总结：** ForeSci 提出一个时间受控基准，通过四类研究决策任务（方向预测、瓶颈发现、战略规划、投稿定位）测试 LLM Agent 能否仅凭截止日期前的历史证据做出前瞻性 AI 研究判断，并揭示了"证据-决策解耦"这一核心失效模式。

> **TL;DR:** ForeSci is a temporally controlled benchmark with 500 tasks across four AI domains and four decision families, evaluating whether LLM agents can make forward-looking research judgments from pre-cutoff evidence alone. Results show agentic methods improve evidence traceability but suffer from evidence-decision decoupling — citing correct evidence while forecasting the wrong research object, causal role, or time horizon.

## 核心贡献

- 提出 **ForeSci** 基准：500 个任务，覆盖四个快速演进 AI 领域（LLM Agents、LLM 微调与后训练、RAG 与检索、视觉生成模型）× 四类研究决策任务族，每个任务配有截止日期对齐的离线知识库
- 设计**严格时间控制机制**：后截止日期论文在生成阶段完全隐藏，LLM 骨干网络训练时间早于截止时间，禁用网络搜索，防止数据泄漏和"后见之明"
- 提出**四维互补评估协议**：预测事实性（Fact）、未来目标对齐（FTA）、证据可追溯性（Trace）、审稿人说服力（Pers.），各维度捕获不同失效通道
- 发现并命名**证据-决策解耦**（Evidence-Decision Decoupling）失效模式：Agent 能引用正确的前置证据，却在决策对象、因果角色、干预方式或时间跨度上出现系统性偏移
- 展示 **前瞻性预测模式**：同一流水线可配合 2026-05-15 截止日期的新文献，生成对 2026-05-16 至 2026-08-15 窗口的真正前瞻预测，支持持续更新评估

## Key Contributions

- **ForeSci benchmark**: 500 tasks across four fast-moving AI domains × four decision families, each paired with a cutoff-aligned offline knowledge base and hidden post-cutoff validation targets
- **Strict temporal integrity**: post-cutoff papers hidden during generation, all LLM backbones trained before the cutoff, web search disabled — enforcing genuine foresight rather than hindsight
- **Four complementary metrics**: Prediction Factuality (Fact, atomic-fact F1 against hidden claim bank), Future-Target Alignment (FTA, bge-m3 semantic similarity or ranking alignment), Evidence Traceability (Trace, rubric-based LLM-as-judge), and Reviewer Persuasiveness (Pers., task-family rubric)
- **Evidence-decision decoupling identified** as a recurring, previously unnamed failure mode across all tested systems
- **Prospective forecasting mode**: the same pipeline can be refreshed with new literature to generate real forward-looking research judgments rather than only retrospective evaluation

## Methodology

**Task construction pipeline.** ForeSci harvests candidate papers from arXiv via domain-specific queries, enriches metadata with Semantic Scholar, deduplicates, and applies two filtering stages: (1) domain-relevance screening, (2) benchmark-core screening for papers with central domain contributions and future-facing signals. The corpus is chronologically truncated at cutoff time *t* to form the public knowledge base K_{≤t}(q).

**Taxonomy induction.** ForeSci builds an evolving temporal taxonomy T_{d,t} = (V_{d,t}, E_{d,t}) for each domain *d* and cutoff *t* using TaxoAdapt, where nodes represent research subdirections and edges represent method-evolution relations. From these nodes, five high-order signal types are extracted: candidate directions, method-development signals, bottleneck signals, feasibility/dependency/risk notes, and venue-community metadata — all LLM-extracted then human-expert validated.

**Four task families** (125 tasks each):
1. **Direction Forecasting** — pick the single research direction most likely to gain momentum in the post-cutoff window; grounded in node evidence records and method-development signals
2. **Bottleneck–Opportunity Discovery** — identify one root bottleneck in a subdirection and explain what one-hop opportunity it would unlock; grounded in bottleneck signals and full-text evidence
3. **Strategic Research Planning** — rank a fixed set of candidate research directions for a hypothetical lab making a near-term plan; grounded in candidate directions, feasibility, and risk notes
4. **Venue-Conditioned Positioning** — given a project description, rank or recommend venue families with framing rationale; grounded in full-text evidence and venue-community metadata

**Temporal cutoffs** vary across instances: three-month (Dec 31, 2025), six-month (Sep 30, 2025), and venue-specific deadline settings after Sep 30, 2025.

**Evaluated systems** (5 methods × 4 LLM backbones):
- Native LLM (no retrieval)
- Hybrid RAG (sparse+dense retrieval)
- CoI-style, ResearchAgent-style, ARIS-style (three offline-adapted agentic systems constrained to the offline knowledge base)

**LLM backbones**: Qwen3-235B, GPT-5.2, GLM-4.6, Gemini-3 — all trained before the relevant cutoff dates.

**Evaluation**: Automatic scoring uses DeepSeek-V4 as judge; rubric-style metrics (Trace, Pers.) are run multiple times and reported as mean ± variance. Human validation of the automatic metrics is provided in Appendix C.

**Error analysis.** Four evidence-to-decision drift types are identified via LLM-based classification with human expert verification:
1. *Scope/granularity drift* — discusses the right research direction at wrong level of specificity
2. *Causal-role drift* — assigns wrong causal role (e.g., treating an enabled opportunity as the root bottleneck)
3. *Intervention-mode drift* — targets the right issue but recommends the wrong type of intervention
4. *Temporal-horizon drift* — targets the wrong maturity stage (near-term vs. long-term)

## Results & Highlights

**Overall performance (Table 2 highlights):**
- Agent-style methods generally improve Fact and Trace over Native LLM and Hybrid RAG, but no single method is uniformly best across all backbones, task families, and metrics
- ResearchAgent-style achieves the best FTA on GPT-5.2 (0.635) and Trace on Qwen3-235B (0.563); ARIS-style achieves the best overall Pers. on GPT-5.2 (0.861)
- Reviewer Persuasiveness does not consistently benefit from agentic methods — structured retrieval artifacts sometimes add noise to final justifications

**Task-family variance**: Strategic Planning has the highest low-score rates on Fact and FTA, reflecting the difficulty of matching both a ranked decision and its supporting facts. Failures are strongly family-dependent, validating the need for multiple evaluation signals.

**Drift impact (Figure 3b)**: Severe drift substantially reduces content-facing metrics — causal-role drift lowers Fact by 1.13 SD; scope/granularity and intervention-mode drift lower FTA by 1.22 and 1.12 SD respectively. Trace is more weakly coupled to content drifts and its direction depends on drift type.

**High-Trace / low-FTA paradox**: Answers with high evidence traceability but low FTA show substantially higher drift severity across all drift types — a model can be confidently grounded in local evidence while systematically choosing the wrong research object or horizon.

**Prospective forecasting**: Applying the pipeline to a 2026-05-15 cutoff generates 12 forward-looking prediction tasks for the 2026-05-16 to 2026-08-15 window, demonstrating the benchmark can support genuine prospective evaluation as new literature emerges.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** High. ForeSci occupies a clear gap: existing LLM benchmarks for research evaluate retrospective QA, literature synthesis, or workflow execution — none systematically test forward-looking strategic decision-making under strict temporal control. The four-family decomposition and multi-signal evaluation are principled and well-motivated.

**Rigor:** Solid, with some caveats. Temporal integrity is carefully enforced (offline KBs, pre-cutoff backbones, no web search). The four-metric design avoids conflating qualitatively different failure modes. Human expert validation of taxonomy structures and benchmark instances adds credibility. However, Reviewer Persuasiveness and Trace are rubric-based LLM-as-judge scores — subject to evaluator model biases. The authors acknowledge this and report variance across repeated runs.

**Reproducibility:** Moderate. The construction pipeline is described in sufficient detail. Promised code/artifact release will be critical; without it, replication is very difficult given the complex taxonomy induction and human-LLM collaborative task construction. Hidden validation targets are intentionally withheld.

**Significance:** High for the research-agent community. The evidence-decision decoupling finding is an important diagnostic insight: agentic systems that appear well-grounded (high Trace) can still be systematically wrong at the decision level (low FTA). This reframes how we should evaluate AI research assistants.

**Red flags:**
- Scope is limited to four AI sub-domains — generalizability to other scientific fields (biology, chemistry, etc.) is not demonstrated
- Benchmark-core human expert validation quality is not quantitatively reported (inter-annotator agreement, etc.)
- Strategic Planning and Venue Positioning involve inherently preference-sensitive decisions where "correct" answers may be legitimately contested; the paper acknowledges this but the rubric scores remain approximations
- The prospective forecasting demonstration (12 tasks) is a proof of concept only — not a rigorous evaluation

## Strengths

- Fills a genuine gap: prospective, evidence-grounded research decision-making as an evaluation target
- Rigorous temporal integrity design with multiple enforcement mechanisms
- Multi-signal evaluation separates orthogonal failure modes that a single aggregate score would conflate
- Evidence-decision decoupling is a novel, actionable diagnostic finding with practical implications for research-agent design
- The benchmark is designed to be refreshable as new literature emerges, giving it ongoing utility
- Human–LLM collaborative task construction with expert validation adds credibility over fully automated pipelines

## Limitations

- Limited to four AI sub-domains; does not test generalization to other scientific fields
- Evaluation depends on hidden post-cutoff targets and LLM-as-judge metrics — rubric-based scores are approximations of scientific value rather than direct measurements
- Strategic Planning and Venue Positioning are preference-sensitive; the benchmark is better suited to comparing failure modes than certifying a single best method
- Cannot capture tacit community knowledge, unpublished work, private reviewer expectations, or downstream adoption signals
- The prospective forecasting mode demonstration is preliminary (12 tasks, not scored)

## Relevance

ForeSci 直接针对 LLM Agent 在科研辅助决策中的能力边界，揭示了"可溯源但决策错误"这一系统性缺陷，对研究评估方法设计、AI 辅助科研工具开发以及自动化研究规划系统的研究者具有直接参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Does evidence-decision decoupling correlate with model scale — do larger models show less drift despite similar traceability scores?
2. How does the benchmark perform when models are given explicit chain-of-thought prompting that separates evidence retrieval from decision synthesis?
3. Can the four drift types (scope, causal-role, intervention-mode, temporal-horizon) be detected and corrected automatically, e.g., via a verifier model?
4. Would a domain outside AI (e.g., drug discovery, materials science) show similar failure patterns, or is decoupling specific to fast-moving AI research where future evidence is harder to infer?
5. How does ForeSci performance correlate with human expert judgment — do human researchers show the same evidence-decision decoupling pattern, and at what rate?
