> **一句话总结：** PropMe框架通过对比前缀攻击（能力）与非对抗性提示（倾向）两类设置，并配合SimpleTrace确定性归因工具，发现大模型前缀攻击下的记忆信号比自然使用场景强出约36倍，现有能力导向评估严重高估了实际部署中的训练数据泄露风险。

> **TL;DR:** PropMe is a framework that separates LLM memorization into two distinct quantities: *capability* (can the model reproduce training data under adversarial prefix attacks?) and *propensity* (does the model tend to reproduce it during ordinary use?). Using SimpleTrace — an open-source, deterministic suffix-array-based tracing pipeline — evaluated on two open models (Comma and DFM Decoder) over two datasets (Common Pile and Danish Dynaword), the paper finds that prefix attacks elicit 36x stronger memorization signals than generic prompts, while propensity scores remain uniformly low across all settings. Practically, models *can* leak training data but rarely *want to*. If you care about realistic privacy risk assessment for deployed LLMs, or need a reproducible training-data attribution pipeline, this paper is worth reading.

## 核心贡献

- 提出**PropMe**评估框架，将记忆行为明确拆解为"能力"（adversarial能诱发）与"倾向"（日常使用自然出现），揭示现有仅关注能力的评估方法系统性高估实际泄露风险
- 引入**倾向转换指标**（propensity metric, $PM_{f_b}$）：将倾向得分（propensity setting）与能力得分（capability setting）通过公式结合，输出 $[0,1]^2$ 空间内可比较的单一分数，高倾向+低能力表明自发倾向强，高能力+低倾向表明模型受控但不主动泄露
- 发布**SimpleTrace**开源工具：基于infini-gram后缀数组，对大规模训练语料进行确定性、并行化的归因追溯，在Common Pile（460B tokens）上100条查询约1分钟，几乎实现完美检索（pass rate 1.00）
- 通过双语（英语/丹麦语）、双模型（基础模型/持续预训练模型）对比实验，发现持续预训练新语料可降低原语料的记忆能力，且记忆档案在训练阶段间基本稳定

## Key Contributions

- **PropMe framework**: the first systematic propensity-aware memorization evaluation, spanning a full spectrum from propensity-focused (generic, non-adversarial prompts) to capability-focused (prefix attacks) settings
- **Propensity metric transformation**: a principled formula $PM_{f_b}(M, x) = \frac{1}{2}(1 + \frac{f_b^p - f_b^c}{f_b^p + f_b^c})$ that produces a score in $[0,1]^2$ encoding whether a model has high capability but low propensity (= low risk in deployment) or high propensity despite low capability (= unexpected spontaneous leakage risk)
- **SimpleTrace**: open-source, deterministic training-data attribution pipeline using suffix-array infini-gram indexing; adds multi-worker parallelization, rarity-based span filtering, and an adaptive nv-recall variant over prior tools; validated at near-perfect retrieval on two large corpora
- **Empirical finding**: a consistent ~36x gap between capability and propensity across all model-corpus pairs; propensity scores remain well below the neutral value of 0.5 in all non-adversarial settings, arguing that audits should report both capability and propensity, not one in isolation
- **Continual pre-training insight**: DFM Decoder (continual pre-training of Comma on Dynaword + Common Pile) shows reduced Common Pile memorization capability, confirming that training on partially different data can attenuate memorization of earlier corpora

## Methodology

### PropMe Framework Overview

PropMe evaluates memorization under three prompt settings per dataset, each comprising 100 samples:
- **Generic**: plausible, naturally-phrased prompts generated by GPT-5.5 with low expected training-data overlap (domain-level only, no dataset URL)
- **Specific**: similar to Generic but GPT-5.5 is additionally given the dataset URL, producing prompts inspired by but not extracted from training data
- **Prefix**: standard adversarial prefix-attack setting (Karamolegkou et al., 2023) — prompts are prefixes of 50 tokens extracted directly from training examples; model is evaluated against the full training document

Generic and Specific operationalize the **propensity setting**; Prefix operationalizes the **capability setting**.

### Propensity Metric Transformation

Given a base metric $f_b \in [0,1]$ (e.g. near-verbatim recall, full-match ratio), the propensity metric is:

$$PM_{f_b}(M, x) = \frac{1}{2}\left(1 + \frac{f_b^p(M,x) - f_b^c(M,x)}{f_b^p(M,x) + f_b^c(M,x)}\right)$$

with $PM_{f_b} = 0$ when $f_b^p = 0$. Interpretations:
- **High capability, low propensity** ($f_b^c$ high, $f_b^p$ low): $PM$ is low — model can reproduce data under elicitation but rarely does under ordinary prompting
- **Low capability, high propensity** ($f_b^c$ low, $f_b^p$ high): $PM$ is high — model spontaneously reproduces training data even without targeted elicitation
- **Equal values**: $PM = 0.5$ — neutral, consistent behavior across settings

The transformation is applied to two base metrics: average near-verbatim recall (NVR) and generations full matches ratio (FMR), yielding $PM_{NVR}$ and $PM_{FMR}$.

### SimpleTrace Pipeline

SimpleTrace is a 4-step deterministic attribution pipeline built on infini-gram (suffix-array-based):
1. **Maximal span extraction**: for each token in a generation of length $L$, query the suffix array for the longest verbatim prefix appearing in the training corpus; candidates filtered to well-formed, word-boundary-respecting, maximal spans (with code/math mixed-mode)
2. **Unigram rarity filtering**: each maximal span is scored by its tokens' joint unigram probability; retain the $K = \lceil 0.05 \cdot L \rceil$ rarest spans to eliminate boilerplate
3. **Document retrieval**: secondary index lookup for each retained span to retrieve matching training documents; matches classified as full, normalized, or partial
4. **Span merging and aggregation**: adjacent/overlapping retained spans collapsed by sequential greedy merge; metrics computed over 30+ summary statistics

Validated at 99% source-document retrieval / exact-match on Common Pile (100 queries, 460B tokens), 100% on Dynaword (100 queries, 6.83B tokens). Running 100 queries takes ~1 minute on Common Pile (4 CPU cores) and ~10 seconds on Dynaword (10 CPU cores).

### Models and Datasets

- **Comma v0.1**: monolingual English model pre-trained on Common Pile v0.1 (521 GB, 463.6B tokens)
- **DFM Decoder Open v0**: continual pre-training of Comma over 30B additional tokens (2/3 Dynaword + 1/3 Common Pile) in 3 stages; released checkpoint is Stage 3
- **Common Pile v0.1**: 521 GB open, permissibly licensed English text
- **Danish Dynaword**: 5.66M samples (6.83B Llama 3 tokens) of Danish text

All experiments run at temperature 0 (greedy decoding). Prompt sets generated by GPT-5.5 for Generic and Specific settings.

## Results & Highlights

### Key Quantitative Findings

**Memorization is substantially stronger under prefix attacks than non-adversarial prompts:**
- For DFM Decoder on Dynaword: NVR = 0.036 under prefix vs. 0.001 for generic/specific — a **36x difference**
- FMR rises to 0.07 under prefix vs. 0.00 under both non-adversarial settings
- Average longest span (ALS): 50.35 tokens (prefix) vs. 27.95 (generic) for Comma on Common Pile

**Propensity scores are uniformly low:**
- $PM_{NVR}$ = 0.040 (generic) and 0.153 (specific) for Comma on Common Pile — both well below neutral 0.5
- $PM_{FMR}$ = 0.0 (generic) and 0.50 (specific) for Comma on Common Pile — the specific 0.5 reflects equal full-match rates in specific and prefix settings, both at 0.02
- DFM Decoder propensity scores are substantially below 0.5 across all training sets and non-adversarial settings

**Continual pre-training reduces Common Pile memorization:**
- Comma vs. DFM Decoder on Common Pile: NVR drops from 0.032 (prefix) to 0.024; ALS drops from 50.35 to 40.83 tokens
- DFM Decoder is the only model with FMR = 0 throughout all settings on Common Pile; Comma has FMR = 0.02 under specific/prefix
- A strong drop in verbatim propensity (−0.5) corresponded with a weaker increase in near-verbatim propensity (+0.1279) — memorization shifts from stronger to weaker type

**Memorization profiles are stable across training stages:**
- DFM Decoder memorization metrics nearly unchanged from Stage 1 through final checkpoint for both Dynaword and Common Pile
- Dynaword ALS: 15.68, 17.37, 24.75 tokens (generic/specific/prefix) — identical across all three stages
- Suggests memorization is fixed early in training and that the training mixture, not its duration, governs what is memorized

**Two distinct memorization profiles:**
- Common Pile: longer localized verbatim fragments (ALS dominates; FMR near 0)
- Dynaword: shorter fragments but occasional complete generation-level reproductions (FMR 0.07 under prefix attacks)

|---|---|---|---|---|
| All queries | 0.99 | 0.99 | 1.00 | 1.00 |
| Full document | 1.00 | 1.00 | 1.00 | 1.00 |

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** The capability vs. propensity distinction is a genuine conceptual contribution, and the paper is the first to operationalize it explicitly for memorization with a principled metric transformation. The distinction is motivated by the growing literature on propensity vs. capability in agentic safety settings — adapting this lens to memorization is timely and well-motivated. SimpleTrace adds real engineering value as a deterministic, open-source alternative to probabilistic attribution methods.

**Rigor:** The experimental setup is clean and reproducible: temperature 0, greedy decoding, three well-defined prompt settings, 100 samples per setting, two corpora, two models. The propensity metric formula is formally defined. SimpleTrace validation is thorough with unit tests and end-to-end retrieval experiments. The paper is honest about what propensity scores mean and do not mean (Section 6: "low propensity does not imply absence of memorization").

**Reproducibility:** High. SimpleTrace is open-source, both datasets (Common Pile, Dynaword) are publicly licensed, and the prompt generation procedure (GPT-5.5 with full prompting instructions in Appendix E) is documented. The one reproducibility concern is GPT-5.5 prompt generation, which introduces non-determinism in the Generic/Specific prompts.

**Significance:** Moderate-to-high for the memorization evaluation community. The finding that prefix attacks produce 36x stronger memorization signals than natural prompts — and that propensity scores remain well below neutral — has direct implications for how organizations should scope privacy audits. The GDPR framing gives this practical relevance beyond academic benchmarking.

**Red flags:**
- The evaluation covers a **single model family** (Comma + its DFM Decoder continual pre-training derivatives) — generalizability to architecturally diverse models (decoder-only vs. encoder-decoder, different scales, instruction-tuned models) is explicitly acknowledged as a limitation but significantly constrains the conclusions
- **100 samples per prompt setting** is small for estimating rare events (full-generation memorization with FMR ≈ 0.02 means 2 out of 100 generations — confidence intervals would be wide)
- The **propensity metric formula** has a known issue: when $f_b^p = 0$, the metric is defined as 0 by convention rather than by the formula, which creates discontinuity and could suppress real propensity signals in extremely low-memorization regimes
- The paper's main finding ("models rarely want to leak data") is broadly reassuring but may be specific to the *open, permissively-licensed* training data used — the conclusion might not transfer to models trained on copyrighted or personally identifiable data
- **No instruction-tuned models** are evaluated — deployed models are almost always instruction-tuned, and RLHF/RLHF-like fine-tuning may substantially change both capability and propensity profiles in ways not captured here

## Strengths

- Clean conceptual framing that directly addresses a gap in how the field thinks about memorization risk
- Deterministic, open-source attribution tool (SimpleTrace) that fills a practical need — OLMoTrace's codebase was not publicly available
- Bilingual/multilingual evaluation (English + Danish) is unusual and valuable for understanding whether memorization properties are language-independent
- The continual pre-training experiment provides novel evidence that training mixture composition, not duration, primarily determines what is memorized
- Honest, measured discussion of limitations and the anti-greenwashing warning (Section 9: "lower memorization propensities should not be used to green-wash potential copyright infringement problems")
- Thorough appendices: full validation tables, span length distributions across all conditions, prompt generation instructions, 30+ metric definitions

## Limitations

- Single model family from a single institution limits generalizability — conclusions about capability/propensity ratios need replication on models like Llama, Mistral, Falcon, or GPT-class models
- Requires access to the full training corpus — not applicable to closed models (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini) where training data is not publicly available
- 100 samples per prompt setting produces wide confidence intervals for rare events (FMR ≈ 0.02 estimates); statistical uncertainty is not quantified
- Propensity metric formula has a defined-by-convention corner case at $f_b^p = 0$ that creates discontinuity
- Evaluation restricted to base models — instruction-tuned and RLHF-fine-tuned models, which are the actual deployment artifacts, are not studied
- How data mixture composition (same-language vs. cross-language mixing) affects memorization propensity remains unresolved — the English/Danish mixture studied may not generalize to other language pairs
- GPT-5.5 used for prompt generation introduces non-determinism and a potential confounder; the prompting instructions are documented but regenerating identical prompt sets is not guaranteed

## Relevance

该论文与大模型安全评估和隐私保护方向高度相关，PropMe框架为LLM训练数据泄露风险的现实场景评估提供了新基准，SimpleTrace工具对需要追溯模型输出来源的研究者（如数据归因、版权合规、幻觉检测）具有直接实用价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How do propensity scores change for instruction-tuned or RLHF-fine-tuned versions of the same base models? Does alignment training reduce memorization propensity, capability, or both?
2. Is the 36x gap between prefix-attack capability and natural propensity consistent across model scales? Does it narrow or widen as models grow larger?
3. Can the PropMe propensity metric detect differences in memorization risk between models trained on public-domain data vs. models trained on data containing PII or copyrighted material?
4. How sensitive are the propensity metric values to the quality and style of Generic/Specific prompts? Since these are GPT-5.5-generated, do different prompt generators systematically shift the measured propensity?
5. SimpleTrace achieves near-perfect retrieval on Common Pile and Dynaword — how does its performance degrade on much larger corpora (e.g., FineWeb-scale, trillions of tokens), and is the suffix-array indexing still computationally feasible?
