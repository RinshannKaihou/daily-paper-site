Adam's Normalized Directional Sharpness is 1.76× Muon's on average. Data imbalance (Zipf s: 0→1) widens the NDS gap from 0.63 to 1.13 — a 1.8× amplification.
Why This Matters
First curvature-grounded theory for Muon's 2× pretraining efficiency
Prior explanations relied on associative memory or long-tail data intuitions. NDS is a concrete, measurable quantity that unifies data imbalance, layer structure, and Theorem 5.5 in one framework.
Critical Edge
Hessian analysis capped at 124M; theory vs. Adam is actually vs. GD
Hessian computation scales as O((mn)²) in parameters — impossible at 7B+. Theorem 5.5 formally compares Muon vs. GD (not Adam) because Adam resists closed-form Kronecker-mode analysis.
Methodology

NDS decomposition. Curvature penalty factors as: I_D^(2) = ½‖Z‖² · S_F(W;Z) where S_F(W;Z) = ⟨Z, H[Z]⟩ / ‖Z‖² (NDS). Update norms are comparable for Muon and Adam — so the difference is entirely in the direction-sharpness term.

Within- vs. cross-layer NDS. NDS decomposes into layer-wise blocks. Muon's cross-layer NDS drops rapidly during training; the late-training advantage concentrates in within-layer Hessian structure. Muon's within-layer fraction: 14% → 44% over 15K steps (Adam: 27% → 34%).

Data imbalance control. Zipf-PCFG with s ∈ {0, 0.5, 1} generates controlled imbalance. NDS gap Δ(s): 0.63 → 1.13 as s: 0 → 1 — heavier tail amplifies curvature heterogeneity.

Theoretical framework. Quadratic model under four empirically validated assumptions:

Assumption 5.1: Low Kronecker Rank
Hessian ≈ Σ B_k^T ⊗ A_k; reaches 80% explained energy at r≈3–5 for attention matrices
Assumption 5.2: Simultaneous Diagonalizability
Kronecker factors share eigenbases; empirical score 0.892 (A_k) / 0.845 (B_k)
Assumption 5.3: Curvature Heterogeneity
Paired curvature ratio w₁/w₈₈ ≈ 2.59×10⁶ — six orders of magnitude
Assumption 5.4: Gradient Alignment
Gradient energy in top-curvature subspace: ζ(88) = 0.871

Key mechanism. In the Kronecker eigenmode basis, Muon assigns equal amplitude to all active modes (spectral normalization = flat allocation). GD's gradient-weighted allocation over-invests energy in high-curvature modes, amplifying the curvature penalty. Muon's loss contraction factor Γ² < GD's R = C(P₀)C(1−P₀) when curvature heterogeneity is sufficiently large.

Theorem 5.5 condition: ρ+1 > 1/α > 1 + σ_H/σ_L (ρ = curvature ratio, α = high-curvature mode proportion). The six-orders-of-magnitude heterogeneity observed empirically easily satisfies this.

Strengths / Limitations
Strengths
  • Clean three-way decomposition (Δ_D = first-order − curvature cost) with all terms directly measurable
  • NDS as a single explanatory variable that unifies data imbalance, layer structure, and theoretical result
  • All four theoretical assumptions validated in-text with quantitative scores — no hand-waving
  • Within-/cross-layer NDS decomposition is a practical diagnostic for future optimizer design
  • Predicts and explains why Muon variants targeting flat Hessian directions (Zhu et al. 2026) benefit mechanistically
Limitations
  • Hessian analysis restricted to 124M parameters — scalability to 7B+ is untested and practically difficult
  • Theorem 5.5 compares Muon vs. GD, not Adam — Adam's coordinate-wise normalization resists Kronecker-mode analysis
  • Causal LLMs only — no diffusion models, vision transformers, or other architectures
  • Within-layer NDS gap concentrated in first and deepest layers (Appendix C) is not theoretically explained in main text
  • Assumes within-step Hessian stationarity — may not capture spectral changes in early training
Open Questions
  • Does the NDS gap persist at 7B+ scale, or does the Kronecker structure break down — can proxy quantities (trace estimates) substitute for exact NDS?
  • Why is the within-layer NDS gap concentrated at the first and deepest layers — is this residual stream dynamics, embedding conditioning, or attention pattern formation?
  • Can a hybrid optimizer apply Muon's spectral normalization selectively to high-NDS layers and Adam elsewhere — capturing the best of both?
  • Can NDS be monitored cheaply online (randomized trace estimators) to guide adaptive direction updates?
