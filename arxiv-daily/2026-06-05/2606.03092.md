> **一句话总结：** 将LLM推理预算分配建模为约束优化问题，用拉格朗日乘子的经济学"影子价格"推导出基于Lambert W函数的最优闭式分配策略（CLEAR），在资源紧缺场景下相比均匀分配最高提升24个百分点准确率。

> **TL;DR:** CLEAR (Constrained Latent-utility Equilibrium Allocation for Reasoning) formalizes LLM batch inference as a constrained optimization problem where each query's reasoning utility follows a three-phase Strict-Surge-Ample S-curve. Using Lagrangian duality, the optimal per-query token allocation has a closed-form solution via the Lambert W function, governed by a single global "shadow price" λ* found via bisection search. Compared to uniform allocation, CLEAR gains up to +24 accuracy points on mathematical reasoning under tight budgets (256 tokens/query), with no backbone retraining required. The framework elegantly handles rational abandonment — zero-budgeting queries whose expected utility cannot justify their token cost.

## 核心贡献

- **影子价格理论**：将批量推理预算分配问题的拉格朗日对偶变量定义为"推理的影子价格"λ*，证明最优分配当且仅当每个活跃查询的边际效用等于λ*（Shadow Price Parity条件）。
- **Lambert W 闭式解**：在shifted-surge效用函数假设下，推导出每个查询的最优token分配 t*_i 的解析表达式（公式5），无需数值迭代即可求解单个查询的分配量。
- **理性放弃（Rational Abandonment）**：当查询的最大净剩余为负时，系统自动分配零预算，将节省的算力重新分配给有正期望回报的查询——这是对均匀分配和比例分配策略的关键改进。
- **CLEAR系统**：将上述理论实例化为三步推理控制算法：(1) 用DeBERTa-v3-base预测每个查询的涌现阈值 τ_i；(2) 用二分搜索找到市场出清的λ*；(3) 按Lambert W策略分配token并截断生成。
- **跨领域验证**：在数学推理（MATH-500, AMC-23, AIME-24/25, OlympiadBench, Minerva）和代码生成（HumanEval+, MBPP+, BigCodeBench）上均优于均匀分配，且无需重新训练主干模型。

## Key Contributions

- **Economic framing of inference budgeting:** Treats batch-level token allocation as a global constrained optimization problem (maximizing aggregate reasoning utility subject to a fixed total token budget), revealing that the Lagrange multiplier λ is the "shadow price of computation" — the marginal gain in total potential from relaxing the budget by one token.
- **Shifted-surge utility model:** Models per-query reasoning utility φ_i(t) as zero below an emergence threshold τ_i, then growing as α_i(t−τ_i)·exp(−β_i(t−τ_i)) above it — capturing the empirically observed Strict→Surge→Ample three-phase behavior.
- **Closed-form Lambert W allocation policy (Theorem 4.2):** Given the shadow price λ and per-query parameters, the optimal token allocation is t*_i = τ_i + (1/β_i)[1 − W_0(λe/α_i)], where W_0 is the principal branch of the Lambert W function. This is exact, not approximate.
- **Market-clearing bisection:** The global optimal λ* is found by bisection over the monotone aggregate demand function, taking O(40 iterations) with negligible overhead versus autoregressive generation.
- **Plug-and-play, no retraining:** CLEAR wraps any existing reasoning LLM as an inference-time controller; only the lightweight DeBERTa threshold predictor (86M params) is fine-tuned on GSM8K/MATH.

## Methodology

**Utility Modeling.** The paper starts from an empirical observation: when you sample many reasoning trajectories at different lengths for a fixed query, the pass rate (proxy for utility) follows an S-shaped curve — near-zero below a minimum threshold, sharply rising through a "Surge" phase, then plateauing with diminishing returns in the "Ample" phase. This motivates the shifted-surge parameterization φ_i(t) in Eq. (1).

**Optimization Setup.** Given N queries with fixed decoding strategy, the system allocates a token vector t = [t_1,...,t_N] to maximize Σφ_i(t_i) subject to Σt_i ≤ B_total (Eq. 2). The objective is non-concave (due to the Strict phase), but the Lagrangian reveals a clean economic principle: at optimality, every active query's marginal utility must equal the global shadow price λ* (Eq. 3).

**Lambert W Closed Form.** Solving ∂φ_i/∂t = λ for the surge utility yields a transcendental equation whose exact solution is the Lambert W function (Section 4.2, Theorem 4.2). This avoids per-query numerical optimization — given λ, each query's allocation is computed in O(1) via a standard library call to W_0.

**Global Equilibrium via Bisection.** Since t*_i(λ) is strictly monotone decreasing in λ, the aggregate demand C(λ) = Σt*_i(λ) is also invertible. CLEAR runs bisection over [λ_min=0, λ_max=α] for 40 iterations to find the unique market-clearing λ* satisfying C(λ*)=B_total (Section 5.2).

**Threshold Predictor.** The key input τ_i (emergence threshold) is predicted by a DeBERTa-v3-base encoder fine-tuned to regress log-solution-length on the query text (MSE loss, 10 epochs, AdamW). Crucially, the Lambert W allocation depends only on the ratio λ/α, so the global shadow price self-adjusts to absorb threshold prediction noise — the system is rank-order preserving, not absolute-magnitude-dependent.

**Adaptive β.** Rather than fitting per-query decay rates (high variance), β is set globally as 1/max(ε, B̄−τ̄), where B̄ is the designated budget and τ̄ is the mean predicted threshold — a model-intrinsic hyperparameter that adapts to the aggregate budget surplus.

**Rational Abandonment.** Queries where net surplus J_i(t*_i) = φ_i(t*_i) − λ·t*_i ≤ 0 receive t_i=0 and are not generated at all; their budget is redistributed to solvable queries. This is a hard, principled cutoff — not a soft heuristic.

**Backbone Models.** Qwen2.5-Math-7B-Instruct (primary) and Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct (scaled validation). Evaluation uses four synthetic traffic streams (Balanced, Mostly-Easy, Mostly-Hard, U-Shaped) drawn from MATH-500, AMC-23, AIME-24/25, OlympiadBench, Minerva; N=500 queries per stream.

## Results & Highlights

**Mathematical Reasoning (Table 1, Qwen2.5-Math-7B):**
- **Balanced stream:** CLEAR(Lambert) +11.6 pts over Uniform at budget=256; gap closes to +1.2 at budget=2048.
- **Mostly-Easy stream:** Largest gain, +24.0 pts at budget=256; uniform allocation wastes tokens on unsolvable hard queries.
- **Mostly-Hard stream:** +5.2 pts at budget=256; abandonment prevents budget dilution.
- **U-Shaped stream:** +14.2 pts at budget=256.
- All gains shrink as budget grows (converging to uniform in the abundant-budget regime), confirming the theory's prediction about regime transitions.

**Code Generation (Table 2):**
- HumanEval+: 36.8 → 43.1 (+6.3 pts)
- MBPP+: 39.4 → 45.9 (+6.5 pts)
- BigCodeBench: 16.2 → 19.8 (+3.6 pts)
- Evaluated with best-of-4 protocol; threshold predictor retrained on HumanEval/MBPP completion lengths.

**Scaled Model (Table 4, Qwen3-30B-A3B):** Gains are smaller in absolute terms (+0.4 to +2.4 pts at budget=1024–4096) but CLEAR still consistently beats Uniform and matches or exceeds TALE-EP and Heuristic variants.

**Structural Utility Variants (Table 3):** Replacing shifted-surge with Triangular or Quadratic utility shapes yields similar performance (19.4/18.8/19.4 vs. 5.4 for Uniform at budget=256, Balanced), confirming that the benefit comes from the global market-clearing + abandonment mechanism, not from the exact utility parameterization.

**Robustness to Predictor Noise (Figure 11):** Performance degrades gracefully under log-normal noise injected into τ predictions; CLEAR maintains advantage over Uniform across all noise levels tested (σ up to 1.0).

**Scale Invariance of α (Figure 8):** Accuracy is insensitive to the choice of α; only the market-clearing λ* shifts proportionally (log λ* ∝ log α), confirming the self-correcting property of the Lambert W allocation.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** Genuinely novel framing. The application of Lagrangian shadow pricing and the Lambert W closed form to LLM token budgeting is not present in prior work. The closest prior art (TALE-EP, Self-Budgeter, DSC) focuses on per-query intra-generation control or teacher-guided prompting, not batch-level market-clearing allocation with rational abandonment. The economic analogy is more than cosmetic — it yields a tractable, theoretically grounded algorithm.

**Rigor:** Strong theoretical core. The proofs in Section 4 are clean and the Lambert W derivation is exact, not an approximation. The three-regime analysis (Abundant/Scarce/Abandonment) maps cleanly to the empirical S-curves. However, the assumption that α and β are global (query-agnostic) hyperparameters is a significant simplification — the paper acknowledges that per-query β estimation is intractable, but this means the utility shape is only partially individualized (via τ_i alone).

**Reproducibility:** Good. Algorithm 1 gives full pseudocode, Tables 5-7 specify all hyperparameters and dataset splits, the threshold predictor architecture (DeBERTa-v3-base) and training details are fully reported, and code is available. The evaluation datasets (MATH-500, AIME-24/25, etc.) are standard.

**Significance:** High practical relevance for API providers and edge-device deployments. The +24 pt gain at 256 tokens/query on a Mostly-Easy stream is striking — though this is a cherry-picked favorable scenario where many queries are easily solvable but uniform allocation starves them. In real production, the traffic distribution is unknown and may not be as favorable. The gains at larger budgets (1024+) are modest (1-2 pts), which matters for practitioners who can afford more compute.

**Red Flags / Caveats:**
1. **Batch-level only:** The method requires knowing the full batch of queries before allocating — it is not applicable to streaming or per-query online settings. This is acknowledged but is a real deployment limitation.
2. **Predictor training domain:** The threshold predictor is trained on GSM8K/MATH (easy-to-moderate math). The paper designs evaluation to be OOD, but the predictor's ranking quality on truly novel domains (e.g., code, science) requires validation.
3. **Greedy decoding only:** Experiments use temperature=0 (greedy) for the main evaluation and best-of-K for code. The framework assumes fixed decoding strategy; how shadow price theory extends to stochastic sampling or beam search is unaddressed.
4. **Oracle gap remains large:** CLEAR closes roughly 50-60% of the gap to Oracle in tight-budget regimes, with room left primarily attributed to predictor noise. Improving the predictor is identified as future work but not solved.
5. **Qwen3-30B gains are small:** At larger budgets on the 30B model, gains shrink to +0.4 pts, suggesting the method's value is concentrated in the small-model, tight-budget regime.

## Strengths

- Principled theoretical foundation with provably optimal allocation policy under the shifted-surge utility model.
- Lambert W closed form makes the per-query computation O(1) — negligible overhead versus LLM generation.
- Rational abandonment is a qualitatively important feature: concentrating tokens on solvable queries rather than diluting uniformly is a correct insight.
- Robustness to predictor noise (relies on ranking, not absolute values) is a practically important property.
- Generalization to code generation without modification to the allocation algorithm is encouraging.
- Full pseudocode + hyperparameter tables + public code enable reproduction.
- ICML acceptance provides peer-review validation.

## Limitations

- Batch-level allocation requires all queries in a batch to be known upfront; incompatible with streaming inference.
- Global α and β hyperparameters mean the utility shape is the same for all queries (only the threshold τ_i varies per query); this is a coarse approximation of true per-query utility heterogeneity.
- Performance gains diminish rapidly as budget increases, limiting applicability to tight-resource regimes.
- Threshold predictor trained on math data; generalization to diverse task types (science, law, coding) requires domain-specific retraining.
- The framework handles only token-count budgets; latency, memory, or monetary cost budgets would require different utility parameterizations.
- Experiments limited to Qwen2.5/Qwen3 families; results may not transfer to other model families with different reasoning dynamics.

## Relevance

对推理时计算扩展和LLM效率感兴趣的读者，此文提供了将最优化理论与实际推理预算管理相结合的严谨范式，尤其对API成本敏感型部署或边缘设备场景具有直接参考价值，Lambert W策略的理论优雅性与工程简洁性并重，值得精读。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Can the shadow price framework be extended to online/streaming settings, where queries arrive sequentially and the batch composition is unknown? (e.g., via a rolling-window or stochastic approximation of λ*)
2. How does CLEAR interact with speculative decoding or early-exit mechanisms that also attempt to reduce per-query compute?
3. The shifted-surge utility model is fit empirically — is there a principled way to learn utility curve parameters (α, β, τ) jointly from query-level feedback signals (e.g., reward model scores) rather than treating α/β as fixed globals?
4. Table 3 shows Triangular utility performs nearly identically to shifted-surge — does this mean the utility shape doesn't matter much, and the key mechanism is purely rational abandonment + market clearing? If so, is a simpler heuristic (e.g., predict τ, abandon queries with τ > B̄, split budget proportionally among survivors) competitive?
5. How would CLEAR behave under a multi-objective budget (e.g., jointly minimizing token count and maximizing accuracy across queries of different importance weights, rather than treating all queries equally)?
