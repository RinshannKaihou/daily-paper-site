> **一句话总结：** EvalStop 是一个可插拔调度原语，以连续 k 次 eval 分数下降为信号自动终止奖励过优化的 RLHF 训练任务，在 80% RLHF 工作负载、64 GPU 场景下实现精度 98.3% / 召回 99.3% / FPR 1.5%，同时将 JCT 提升 9.4%、计算浪费减少 21.8%，且可与任意基础调度器组合使用。

> **TL;DR:** EvalStop is a composable scheduling wrapper for multi-tenant RLHF fine-tuning platforms that monitors downstream eval scores (world feedback) and terminates training jobs after k=2 consecutive eval-score declines. It reframes scheduler-level early stopping as a detection problem, achieving 98.3% precision / 99.3% recall / 1.5% FPR on identifying reward-hacking jobs while delivering +9.4% JCT and -21.8% wasted compute over SRTF-Est. Crucially, it dominates all baselines — fixed-progress stopping incurs 64.5% FPR on healthy RLHF; loss-plateau detection misses 62% of hacking cases — and its gains compose across every base scheduler tested (9–25% JCT improvement).

## 核心贡献

- **检测问题重构（C1）**：将 RLHF 调度层面的早停建模为检测问题，引入"信息边界"（`SchedulerJobView`）使真实 hacking 标签对调度器不可见，确保检测质量可被诚实测量。
- **可组合检测原语（C2）**：EvalStop 跟踪每个任务的连续 eval 分数下降次数，在满足阈值（RLHF：k=2，DPO：k=3）后立即终止任务、释放 GPU、保存最优检查点；与仅降优先级的 EvalSched 不同，彻底移除任务。
- **跨调度器可组合增益（C3）**：EvalStop 作为包装层，将处理后的集群状态交还任意基础调度器（FIFO、SJF-Est、SRTF-Est、LossAware），在所有测试调度器上均实现 9–25% JCT 提升，检测质量保持稳定。
- **多轴鲁棒性验证**：在 eval 噪声（σ=0.01–0.12）和 hacking 基础率（20%–80%）双维度下验证稳健性，精度分别保持 ≥81% 和 ≥89%，远超基线。
- **关键洞察**：系统性指标（JCT/TTFUC）具有误导性——固定进度停止在 JCT 上优于 EvalStop，但其对健康任务的 FPR 高达 64.5%，这一质量损失在纯系统指标中完全不可见。

## Key Contributions

- **Detection framing (C1):** Reframes scheduler-level RLHF early stopping as a binary detection problem. Introduces the `SchedulerJobView` information boundary that hides ground-truth hacking labels from schedulers, enabling honest measurement of detection quality — a framing absent from all prior ML scheduling work.
- **Composable early-stop primitive (C2):** EvalStop tracks per-job consecutive eval-score declines and, on k consecutive drops (k=2 for RLHF, k=3 for DPO), terminates the job, releases GPUs immediately, and preserves the best checkpoint (saved k eval steps ago). Termination beats deprioritization: EvalSched (deprioritize only) has 2.2x worse JCT than SRTF-Est because deprioritized jobs still occupy the cluster.
- **Composable systems gains (C3):** EvalStop wraps any base scheduler (Algorithm 1). It processes early-stop decisions first, then delegates updated cluster state to the base policy. JCT gains of 9–25% over the unwrapped base scheduler hold across FIFO, SJF-Est, SRTF-Est, and LossAware.
- **Robustness analysis:** Two additional sweeps (E7 eval-noise, E8 hacking-fraction) demonstrate that EvalStop's detection quality is driven by the eval signal, not by simulator artifacts or base-rate luck, unlike StopAt-0.65 whose "57% precision" in the main experiment is purely a base-rate artifact.
- **Critical insight on metric deception:** Systems metrics alone are deceptive — StopAt-0.5 achieves the best JCT in Table 1, but simultaneously destroys 65% of healthy RLHF jobs. Quality-preserving detection requires a detection axis evaluated independently of JCT.

## Methodology

### Problem Setup: Three Signals of Decreasing Proxiness

RLHF training gives the scheduler access to three signals:

1. **Training loss (proxy²):** Monotonically decreasing throughout training, including during reward hacking. A SLAQ-style scheduler interprets this as "job making progress, keep running."
2. **Reward model score (proxy):** Also monotonically increasing — the policy directly optimizes this objective, so it is always rising regardless of true quality.
3. **Downstream eval score (world feedback):** Rises to a peak at ~55% training progress, then degrades as reward hacking takes over.

Only the eval score reveals the overoptimization phenomenon. This is the signal EvalStop uses.

### Job Model and Workload

The simulator models three job types with distinct structural behaviors:
- **LoRA jobs:** 10–60 min, 1–2 GPUs, eval every 10% progress. Monotonically improving eval (exponential convergence).
- **DPO jobs:** 30–120 min, 2–4 GPUs, eval every 20% progress. Diminishing returns pattern.
- **RLHF jobs:** 60–360 min, 4–8 GPUs, eval every 15% progress. Mixed: 60% exhibit reward hacking (eval peaks at U(0.55, 0.75) progress then drops U(0.10, 0.30)), 40% are structurally healthy (monotone concave + noise). Training loss in both regimes decays to a plateau at U(0.50, 0.70) progress — indistinguishable from the training-loss perspective.

### EvalStop Algorithm (Algorithm 1)

```
Require: Base scheduler S, decline thresholds k_RLHF=2, k_DPO=3
State: per-job consecutive decline count d[j]

on eval_result(j, score):
    if score < prev_score[j]:
        d[j] += 1
    else:
        d[j] = 0
    if d[j] >= k_type(j):
        mark j for early stopping

schedule(cluster, waiting, running):
    for each j in running marked for early stopping:
        Terminate j; release GPUs; save best checkpoint
    return S.schedule(cluster, waiting, running')
```

The mechanism is intentionally simple — a k-consecutive-decline rule equivalent to a simplified change-point detector. The authors explicitly choose this over CUSUM or Bayesian change-point detection for interpretability: k has a direct operational meaning ("how many bad evals before we stop") that platform operators can reason about.

### Information Boundary

EvalStop respects the non-clairvoyant boundary (Motwani et al., 1994): it observes only job type, eval scores at scheduled checkpoints, and the count of consecutive declines. It never accesses true job duration or ground-truth hacking labels.

### Experimental Design

- **Simulator:** Discrete-event, heap-based event dispatch, slot-based GPU allocation with 2-minute preemption overhead, Poisson arrivals (λ=1.0 jobs/min).
- **Scale:** 200 jobs, 5 tenants, 5 seeds (42, 123, 456, 789, 1024). Statistical significance via Welch's t-test.
- **Six experiments:** E1 (mixed workload, 32 GPUs), E2 (RLHF-heavy, 64 GPUs), E3 (workload sensitivity), E4 (composability across 4 base schedulers), E5 (threshold sensitivity, k=1..5), E6 (eval frequency sensitivity, 5–30% intervals), E7 (eval-noise sweep), E8 (hacking-fraction sweep).
- **Baselines:** FIFO, SJF-Est, SRTF-Est, LossAware (SLAQ-style), EvalSched (deprioritize only), StopAt0.5+SRTF, StopAt0.65+SRTF, LossPlateau+SRTF.
- **Metrics:** JCT, TTFUC (Time-to-First-Useful-Checkpoint), Wasted Compute Fraction, Saved Compute Fraction, Precision, Recall, FPR.

## Results & Highlights

### Main Result: RLHF-Heavy Workload (E2, 80% RLHF, 64 GPUs)

| Scheduler | JCT | TTFUC | Wasted | Saved | Precision | Recall | FPR |
|-----------|-----|-------|--------|-------|-----------|--------|-----|
| SRTF-Est (no early stop) | 1124±59 | 971±56 | 34.5% | 0% | — | — | — |
| StopAt0.5+SRTF | **622±74** | 551±72 | **14.9%** | **46.5%** | 57.1% | 100% | 64.5% |
| StopAt0.65+SRTF | 790±31 | 693±31 | 19.5% | 31.7% | 57.1% | 100% | 64.5% |
| LossPlateau+SRTF | 1093±60 | 947±58 | 32.4% | 3.2% | 57.0% | 38.3% | 24.7% |
| **EvalStop+SRTF** | **1018±53** | **883±50** | 26.9% | 9.9% | **98.3%** | **99.3%** | **1.5%** |

Key observations from the authors:
- **Systems metrics alone are deceptive:** StopAt-0.5 appears best on JCT/Wasted, but its 64.5% FPR means it destroys two-thirds of healthy RLHF jobs. This quality cost is invisible to JCT/TTFUC metrics.
- **Loss-only detection is insufficient:** LossPlateau+SRTF misses 62% of true hacking cases (recall 38.3%) while still incurring 24.7% FPR, because training loss plateaus at similar progress in both hacking and healthy RLHF regimes.
- **EvalStop** is the only method achieving high precision AND low FPR simultaneously with meaningful systems improvement (+9.4% JCT, -21.8% wasted compute, p=0.03 and p<0.001 respectively).

### Composability (E4)

| Base Scheduler | ΔJCT | ΔTTFUC | ΔWasted | Precision | FPR |
|----------------|------|--------|---------|-----------|-----|
| FIFO | +8.9% | +8.5% | +21.7% | 98.6% | 1.4% |
| SJF-Est | +6.5% | +5.9% | +21.5% | 97.9% | 1.9% |
| SRTF-Est | +9.4% | +9.1% | +21.8% | 98.3% | 1.5% |
| LossAware | +25.2% | +4.8% | +20.3% | 97.3% | 2.4% |

LossAware shows the largest JCT gain because LossAware alone has pathological priorities (over-prioritizes RLHF jobs with falling loss); EvalStop partially compensates by terminating the overoptimizing ones.

### Workload Sensitivity (E3)

| Workload | RLHF% | ΔJCT | ΔWasted | Stopped | Precision | FPR |
|----------|--------|------|---------|---------|-----------|-----|
| LoRA-heavy | 10% | +2.1% | +13.3% | 8 | 100% | 0.0% |
| Mixed | 30% | +5.6% | +17.7% | 31 | 100% | 0.0% |
| RLHF-heavy | 80% | +9.4% | +21.8% | 93 | 98.3% | 1.5% |

Benefit scales cleanly with RLHF fraction. EvalStop does not fire spuriously on LoRA, DPO, or healthy RLHF.

### Threshold Sensitivity (E5)

| k | JCT | Wasted | Stopped |
|---|-----|--------|---------|
| 1 | 874±50 | 19.4% | 127 |
| **2** | **1018±53** | **26.9%** | **93** |
| 3 | 1093±56 | 32.8% | 57 |
| 4 | 1123±61 | 34.6% | 2 |
| 5 | 1123±59 | 34.5% | 0 |

k=2 balances early detection against false positives. k≥4 essentially never triggers (converges to SRTF baseline).

### Eval Frequency Sensitivity (E6)

| Eval freq | JCT | Saved | ΔJCT vs SRTF |
|-----------|-----|-------|---------------|
| 5% | 867±75 | 24.1% | +22.8% |
| 10% | 993±34 | 14.8% | +11.6% |
| **15%** | **1018±53** | **9.9%** | **+9.4%** |
| 20% | 1111±96 | 4.8% | +1.1% |
| 30% | 1123±63 | 0.7% | +0.1% |

More frequent evaluation enables earlier detection and larger compute savings — at 5% intervals, EvalStop saves 24% of planned compute. Platform operators can invest in more frequent evaluation and more than recover the cost through earlier overoptimization detection.

### Detector Robustness

**E7 (eval-noise sweep, σ = 0.01 to 0.12):**

| σ | EvalStop Precision | FPR |
|---|--------------------|-----|
| 0.01 | 100% | 0.0% |
| 0.03 | 96.6% | 2.9% |
| 0.05 | 91.3% | 7.2% |
| 0.08 | 85.3% | 11.6% |
| 0.12 | 81.1% | 14.0% |

At realistic LLM benchmark noise (σ≤0.05, matching typical 2–5% standard errors), precision stays ≥91%. EvalStop retains at least a 24 percentage-point precision gap over both competitors at every noise level.

**E8 (hacking-fraction sweep, 20%–80%):**

| Hacking fraction | EvalStop Precision | FPR | StopAt0.65 Precision |
|------------------|--------------------|-----|----------------------|
| 20% | 89.1% | 2.4% | 20.0% (=base rate) |
| 40% | 95.3% | 2.1% | 38.0% (=base rate) |
| 60% | 98.3% | 1.5% | 57.1% (=base rate) |
| 80% | 99.4% | 1.1% | 78.1% (=base rate) |

StopAt-0.65's precision follows the base-rate identity line exactly — confirming it is a constant classifier, not a detector. EvalStop's precision is driven by detection quality, not prevalence.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** Moderate-to-good. The core insight — use downstream eval rather than training loss as the stopping signal — is intuitive but the systematic formalization, information-boundary framing, and evaluation methodology are genuine contributions. The detection-problem framing is the paper's strongest intellectual move: it surfaces a quality axis that pure JCT-centric evaluation would miss entirely, and this alone is a meaningful contribution to the ML scheduling literature.

**Rigor:** The experimental design is notably careful — 5 seeds, Welch's t-test, an information boundary that prevents schedulers from using hidden labels, and multiple robustness sweeps. The threshold sensitivity (E5) and eval-frequency sensitivity (E6) analyses are proactive and useful. The inclusion of Table 7 (E1 mixed workload) in the appendix is appreciated.

**Reproducibility:** Moderate. The simulator code is described in enough detail to re-implement, but there is no mention of code release. Without the simulator code, full reproduction requires reimplementation effort. The hyperparameter table (Table 6) is complete and appreciated.

**Significance:** High practical relevance. Reward overoptimization is a known production problem, and the composable-primitive design means EvalStop can be added to existing schedulers (including OpenRLHF) with minimal integration effort. The "deploy at the scheduler level, not the trainer" philosophy is practically attractive because it requires no changes to training code.

**Red flags:**

1. **Simulator-only evaluation:** The paper's most significant limitation. Real RLHF training curves may differ from the parametric models used (hacking peak at U(0.55, 0.75), healthy monotone concave + noise). The authors acknowledge this and call real-trace replay (on TRL, OpenRLHF, HuggingFace W&B logs) the "primary missing validation."

2. **Eval cost not fully modeled:** Eval duration is fixed per model size (1/3/5 min for small/med/large), but eval scheduling itself is not a decision variable. More frequent evaluation (E6 shows 5% interval is best) consumes GPU time — the net compute savings must account for eval overhead, which is not fully captured.

3. **Noise model is Gaussian and i.i.d.:** Real LLM benchmark variance is likely heavy-tailed and auto-correlated across checkpoints (bootstrap variance over evaluation items). At σ>0.15, the k=2 threshold is likely inadequate. The authors acknowledge this and note adaptive thresholds (Bayesian change-point detection) as future work.

4. **Homogeneous hardware only:** The simulator uses homogeneous GPUs without network I/O, gradient accumulation, or elastic scaling — a significant simplification relative to production heterogeneous clusters.

5. **k is a hyperparameter with type-level differentiation:** The paper uses k_RLHF=2 and k_DPO=3 without explaining how these values are set in deployment (per-job-type tuning? cross-validation on historical traces?). The sensitivity analysis (E5) shows the operating range is narrow (k≥4 barely triggers), which is informative but raises questions about robustness to workload drift.

## Strengths

- **Clean architectural decomposition:** The five-module factoring (World Feedback, Decline Detector, Stop and Save, Information Boundary, Base Scheduler) maps cleanly to contributions and makes the system easy to reason about.
- **Detection framing is the right abstraction:** Systems metrics (JCT) alone are genuinely misleading for this problem. The paper demonstrates this crisply with Table 1 and the StopAt baseline analysis — a conceptual contribution with practical implications.
- **Composability is real:** The algorithm (Algorithm 1, ~15 lines) genuinely wraps any scheduler with no modification to the base scheduler code. Integration with OpenRLHF is mentioned as a direct path.
- **Robustness sweeps are proactive and honest:** E7/E8 were not forced by reviewers — the authors anticipate the "maybe it's an artifact" critique and address it directly. The E8 result (StopAt-0.65 precision = base rate exactly) is a particularly clean demonstration.
- **The eval frequency finding has direct operational value:** More frequent eval can more than pay for itself through earlier detection (5% intervals: 22.8% JCT improvement vs. 9.4% at 15%). This is an actionable recommendation for platform operators.
- **Fairness is verified:** Jain's fairness index is within noise of SRTF-Est, an important property for multi-tenant platforms.

---

## Limitations

- **Simulator-only evaluation:** No deployment on real RLHF training runs (TRL, OpenRLHF, actual W&B traces). Real overoptimization dynamics may differ from the calibrated parametric model.
- **Eval cost not fully integrated:** The compute savings calculation does not fully account for the cost of running evaluations more frequently. The 5%-interval finding (24.1% compute saved) may overstate net gains.
- **i.i.d. Gaussian eval noise:** Real LLM benchmark noise is auto-correlated and heavy-tailed. Adaptive or confidence-aware thresholds would extend the operating range beyond σ=0.05.
- **Homogeneous hardware:** Heterogeneous GPUs, network I/O, gradient accumulation, and elastic scaling are absent — all common in real clusters.
- **Single-plateau loss model:** Both hacking and healthy RLHF training loss plateau at U(0.50, 0.70) by construction, which is what makes loss-plateau detection fail. Real workloads may show more varied loss dynamics that could partially help loss-based detectors.
- **k selection in deployment:** The paper does not specify how k would be set in deployment for new workload types or when the hacking fraction is unknown.
- **Scope:** EvalStop detects and terminates; it does not adapt the training algorithm, update the reward model, or recover the job. Integration with training-level mitigations (constrained RL, reward ensembles) is noted as complementary but not empirically demonstrated.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How do real RLHF training curves (from TRL runs, OpenRLHF logs, or public W&B traces) compare to the parametric model used in the simulator? Specifically, how often does eval peak-then-degrade vs. plateau vs. collapse suddenly?
2. What is the full net compute impact when eval overhead is counted? The E6 results show JCT improvement at 5% eval intervals, but eval itself consumes GPU time — does the saving hold after accounting for eval cost?
3. Could a learned classifier (trained on historical eval trajectories) outperform the k-consecutive-decline heuristic, and would the interpretability trade-off be worth it for production operators?
4. How does EvalStop interact with iterative RLHF (where the reward model is periodically retrained on new human labels)? Would retraining reset the eval trajectory in ways that confuse the decline detector?
5. Can the generalization claimed in Section 5 (DPO overtraining, small-data overfitting, mode collapse) be empirically validated? The underlying principle is sound, but the structural differences between these regimes and RLHF hacking may matter.
6. What is the right eval metric to use as world feedback in practice? The paper abstracts this to a scalar eval score, but in real deployments the choice of downstream benchmark and aggregation strategy significantly affects the signal quality.
7. How would EvalStop behave on multi-objective RLHF (e.g., simultaneous optimization of helpfulness and harmlessness), where different eval metrics may peak at different training stages?
