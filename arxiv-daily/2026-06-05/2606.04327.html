Key Finding
A new per-neuron inner Hessian completely classifies every point on a neuron-splitting plateau — and splitting a local min almost always creates escapable saddles.
A Geometric Characterization of the Stationary Plateau for Two-Layer Neural Networks
Ding · Li · Sun  ·  CUHK-Shenzhen / U. Minnesota / UIUC  ·  2026-06-05
Key Metric
+meas
Sure-saddle region has positive Lebesgue measure — saddles are generic, not exceptional, after splitting
Why This Matters
Explains why wider networks train easier: width expansion converts isolated local minima into plateaus full of saddle escape directions.
Critical Edge
Two-layer smooth networks only. ReLU excluded. No empirical validation. Deep architectures require entirely new ideas.
Methodology

Setup: Neuron Splitting

Two-layer network f(x;θ) = v⊤σ(Wx) with r hidden neurons and smooth activation σ. Splitting the r-th neuron into (m−r+1) daughters with same input weights but redistributed output weights according to λ = [λ₀,…,λ_{m−r}] summing to 1 creates a stationary plateau — every λ gives a stationary point of the wide network at the same loss value.

The Key Object: Inner Hessian

(H^{in}_i)_{j,j'} = v_i ⟨ŷ − y, σ''(w_i⊤X) ∘ x_j ∘ x_{j'}⟩ The second-order sensitivity of the loss to perturbing neuron i's input weights — curvature from the residual, not from the Jacobian part.

Plateau Type Law

Saddle point → All-saddle plateau
Loc. min + ID H → All-saddle plateau
Loc. min + PD/ND H → Mixed (min + saddle)
Loc. const neuron → All-localmin plateau

ID = indefinite inner Hessian; PD = positive definite; ND = negative definite. The all-localmin case (locally constant neuron) is the degenerate exception.

Results

Theorem Classification Table

TheoremConditionConclusion
Thm 1 H^{in} is PD, ND, or ID Full N&S characterization of every plateau point
Thm 2 H^{in} ≠ 0 Sure-saddle region Λ_saddle has positive measure
Thm 3.1 Locally effective neuron Saddle exists (existence, not full characterization)
Thm 3.2 Locally constant neuron All-localmin plateau — no saddles
Thm 4 Original is a saddle All-saddle plateau — saddles beget saddles
Key asymmetry: Splitting a saddle always yields saddles (universally, regardless of λ). Splitting a local minimum generically introduces saddles — the only escape is the degenerate locally-constant case.

Practical Implication

Unless you choose splitting coefficients in Λ_PD or Λ_ND (a restricted subset), you will land on a saddle with a descent direction. This is good for optimization — saddles are escapable, isolated local minima are not. Width expansion converts bad isolated regions into traversable plateau structures.

Strengths
  • First complete classification: Necessary and sufficient conditions for every plateau point under smooth activations — unifying 20+ years of partial sufficient-condition results (Fukumizu & Amari 2000, NeurIPS 2019, JMLR 2022).
  • Clean analytic object: Inner Hessian is interpretable as second-order sensitivity to input weight perturbation; explicitly connected to the full Hessian decomposition.
  • Actionable sure-saddle region: Theorem 2 identifies a concrete positive-measure region in λ-space — directly usable for split-and-descent algorithm design.
  • Elegant Theorem 4: "Saddles beget saddles" is a clean, strong, practically useful statement about width expansion behavior.
  • Modular proof strategy: Pre-split auxiliary perturbation + post-split decomposition could potentially extend to related settings.
  • Credible team: Sustained track record on loss landscape analysis; builds on prior work in Mathematics of Operations Research (2022).
Limitations
  • Two-layer only: Extension to deeper architectures requires handling how a layer-1 split propagates through subsequent layers — a substantially new problem.
  • Smooth activations required: Inner Hessian involves σ''; ReLU networks are excluded. See Wu, Simsek, Ged (2024) for the ReLU analog.
  • Single neuron split: Simultaneous multi-neuron splitting creates higher-dimensional plateaus with potentially different geometry — not analyzed.
  • No empirical validation: The sure-saddle region is geometrically identified but not verified to be easily reachable during actual training.
  • Theorem 3 gap: The regime between "locally effective" and "locally constant" neurons remains theoretically uncharacterized.
Gap: locally-effective → ∃ saddle (Thm 3.1) locally-constant → no saddle (Thm 3.2) Between these two: OPEN QUESTION
Questions for Exploration
Can the inner Hessian framework extend to deeper networks? Splitting a layer-1 neuron affects the Hessian structure at layer 2 — what is the multi-layer generalization?
What is the empirical distribution of inner Hessian definiteness (PD/ND/ID) at local minima found by SGD in practice? Is any one regime typical?
The sure-saddle region has positive measure. Does a gradient-based optimizer starting from a random split coefficient land in this region with high probability?
In the NTK / infinite-width limit, does the inner Hessian vanish (locally-constant regime) for all neurons, making every splitting plateau all-localmin?
Can the locally-constant degenerate case be characterized by a simple closed-form condition on network parameters and training data?
Does the sure-saddle region from Theorem 2 translate directly into a concrete recommendation for how split-and-descent algorithms should choose λ?
