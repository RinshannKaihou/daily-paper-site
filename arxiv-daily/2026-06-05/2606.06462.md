> **一句话总结：** 提出 Benchmark Agent——首个全自动基准测试构建系统，通过"规划器+执行器"双组件架构，以人工标注约 1/20 的时间成本生成跨模态、用户定制化的高质量评测基准，人工验收率达 96–98%。

> **TL;DR:** Benchmark Agent is the first fully autonomous agentic system for customizable benchmark construction, using a dual-component Planner–Executor architecture driven by GPT-5.1 to generate high-quality, user-specified evaluation benchmarks across text, image, audio, and multimodal domains at roughly 20x lower cost than human annotation.

## 核心贡献

- 提出 **Benchmark Agent**：首个端到端自主基准生成框架，支持用户以自然语言描述评测需求，系统自动完成子任务分解、数据集检索、转换计划生成、样本实现与质量控制全流程
- 双组件架构：**Benchmark Planner**（设计Agent + Grounding Agent + Allocation Agent）负责高层规划与可行性验证；**Benchmark Executor** 负责样本级实现与配额控制
- 实现**持续刷新**能力：基准可随模型迭代动态更新，彻底摆脱"一次性构建"范式
- 以约 **0.2–0.3 分钟/样本**的速度生成基准（对比人工 5–6 分钟/样本），验证了成本效率优势
- 通过15个跨领域代表性基准的大规模实验，验证了系统生成基准的质量、判别力与一致性

## Key Contributions

- **Benchmark Agent**: the first fully autonomous system for end-to-end benchmark construction, taking natural-language user requirements as input and producing runnable evaluation benchmarks as output
- **Dual-component architecture**: Benchmark Planner handles high-level planning (subtask design, dataset grounding, feasibility allocation); Benchmark Executor handles sample-level realization and quality/quota control
- **Continual and fast refreshability**: benchmarks can be updated on-demand as models evolve, replacing static one-time builds
- **Cost efficiency**: ~0.2–0.3 min/sample vs. 5–6 min/sample for human annotation (roughly 20–25x speedup)
- **Validated quality**: human acceptance rates of 96–98%; LLM-as-Judge UIA scores of 68–82 across five benchmarks; consistent model-rank ordering on Qwen3.5 series confirming discriminative power

## Methodology

**Problem framing.** Existing benchmarks are labor-intensive to build, hard to reuse, and quickly become saturated as models improve. The paper demonstrates saturation empirically — Qwen model scores on MMLU, GSM8K, and similar benchmarks exceed 80% within months of release. Benchmark Agent reframes benchmark construction as an agentic planning-and-execution task.

**Benchmark Planner.** Given a user requirement R, the Planner decomposes it into a subtask set S = {s_i}. Three agents collaborate:
- *Design Agent* (Proposer/Revising/Discarding tools): iteratively generates, refines, and prunes candidate subtasks until the set is well-posed, diverse, and coverage-aligned.
- *Grounding Agent* (Preference/Searching/Transformability tools): for each subtask, retrieves candidate datasets from General-Bench and validates whether feasible transformation plans exist. It scores candidate (subtask, dataset, plan) triples on three dimensions: alignment, transformation robustness, and evaluation signal preservation. A subtask without any valid grounding triggers redesign.
- *Allocation Agent* (Allocation/Diagnose/Adjustment tools): given all grounded instantiations, determines feasible sample quotas under global constraints via a closed-loop mechanism, diagnosing and adjusting infeasible allocations.

The final benchmark specification is B = {(s_i, G_i)}^N_{i=1}, where each G_i is a set of validated (dataset, transformation plan, quota) triples.

**Benchmark Executor.** Converts the specification into concrete evaluation items through:
- *Sample-level Realization*: an LLM orchestrates per-sample transformation actions at each step, guided by the dataset-level plan t_{i,j} but adapting to per-sample state. Non-LLM tools used include content synthesis tools (TTS, image resizing, audio mixing, web search, noise injection) and script-based processing tools (file conversion, metadata editing, content decomposition).
- *Verification & Quality Control*: every generated sample is checked for semantic validity and format compliance. Invalid samples are discarded or re-generated within the constrained pipeline. Quota replenishment continues until quotas are met or raw data is exhausted.

**Backbone model:** GPT-5.1 for all agent reasoning. Dataset pool: General-Bench (ICML 2025). Covers text-only (T), audio-text (A), image-text (I), and audio-text-image (O) modalities.

## Results & Highlights

**Benchmark quality (Table 1):**
- Human acceptance rates: 96.09%–98.65% across five representative benchmarks
- LLM-as-Judge overall scores: 67.98–77.79; UIA (User Intention Alignment) 68.54–81.48
- FSQ (Format & Schema Quality) and QAC (Question-Answer Coherence) consistently high (87–99), indicating well-formed and coherent items
- CQC (Context-Question Correspondence) and TSD/SSC (target-signal dependency / skill-specific challenge) are lower and more variable — grounding fidelity and difficulty control identified as key open challenges
- Qwen3.5 model series shows consistent scaling trends (71.06→87.23 on Multi-Perspective; 45.26→54.49 on Math-Reasoning) confirming discriminative power

**Comparison with direct LLM generation (Table 2):**
- Direct prompting of GPT-5.4, Claude Sonnet 4-6, Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview, and Qwen3.5-397B-A17B achieves much lower UIA (20–43) and SSC (11–35) scores despite adequate FSQ/QAC
- Demonstrates that an agentic workflow is essential to preserve user intent and signal quality — surface-level formatting can be achieved without agents, but intent alignment cannot

**Backbone model ablation (Table 3):**
- Results are stable (±5–8 overall) across Qwen3.5-397B-A17B, GPT-5.4, Claude Sonnet 4-6, and Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview as agent backbone
- Closed-source models score slightly higher on intent-heavy dimensions; the framework itself is model-agnostic

**Component ablation (Table 4):**
- Removing Design Agent most hurts UIA (-3 to -14 pts), confirming its role in intent alignment
- Removing TC+Scoring causes the largest drops in CQC and TSD for multimodal benchmarks
- Removing SLP and Verification each degrade overall quality, particularly for Omni-Understanding

**Cost (Table 5):**
- Human: 5–6 min/sample; Benchmark Agent: 0.2–0.3 min/sample

**New findings from continuous evaluation (Table 6):**
- Qwen2.5-VL-32B outperforms smaller Qwen2.5 variants but lags behind Qwen3-VL-32B across most domains
- Models show unbalanced performance: strong in geography/math, weaker in fine-grained art and animal understanding
- LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 series (fully open) lags far behind commercial and semi-open models (avg ~38 vs. ~80 for Gemini-3-Pro)

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** Genuine and well-positioned. While automated benchmark generation is a known direction (BenchAgents arXiv:2410.22584, AutoBench), this is the first system to claim full autonomy across the entire pipeline — from user intent to quality-controlled, runnable benchmarks — with multi-agent Planner/Executor separation and transformability grounding. The brain-cerebellum inspiration for the dual-component design is conceptually neat if somewhat decorative.

**Rigor:** Generally strong. Three evaluation axes (human, LLM-as-judge, consistency checks) are well-motivated. The LLM-as-judge protocol is detailed with six sub-metrics. Component ablations are thorough. The cost analysis is direct but simplified (minutes per sample without accounting for API costs).

**Reproducibility:** GPT-5.1 is used as backbone — a proprietary and rapidly-evolving model. Results may shift as GPT-5.1 is updated or deprecated. The dataset pool (General-Bench) is public and the paper promises open code and a demo page. Reproducibility is contingent on those releases.

**Significance:** High. Benchmark saturation is a documented and pressing problem. A system that enables on-demand, customized benchmark refresh at low cost would materially change how the community evaluates models. If the system generalizes beyond the tested domains and the quality claims hold under independent scrutiny, this is a meaningful infrastructure contribution.

**Red flags:**
- Backbone is GPT-5.1 (closed-source, potentially expensive at scale); real-world deployment cost not reported in dollar terms
- CQC and SSC remain noticeably lower than FSQ/QAC, suggesting generated benchmarks may be easier or less precisely targeted than advertised
- "Continual evaluation" finding (Section 4.4) is preliminary and observational; no causal claims about what drives model failures
- The abstract in the search index was extremely sparse (one sentence), suggesting the metadata pipeline did not extract the full abstract — not a paper flaw, but worth noting

## Strengths

- First end-to-end autonomous benchmark construction system with a clearly articulated and verified pipeline
- Strong empirical validation: 15 benchmarks, three evaluation axes, multiple backbone ablations
- Cost efficiency demonstrated concretely and quantitatively
- Benchmark Planner's grounding validation loop is a principled mechanism to prevent hallucinated or infeasible benchmark designs
- Reveals genuinely interesting findings about MLLM imbalance across domains via the generated benchmarks

## Limitations

- Quality dimensions most central to benchmark utility (CQC, TSD, SSC) remain the hardest to optimize and score lowest
- Relies on a proprietary, non-reproducible backbone (GPT-5.1) for the main results; open-backbone results exist but are secondary
- Dataset pool constrained to General-Bench; coverage of highly specialized domains is untested
- No analysis of benchmark contamination risk — if generation draws on data similar to model pre-training, saturation may recur quickly
- Human evaluation done at the item level; no end-to-end user study of whether practitioners find these benchmarks useful for actual model selection decisions

## Relevance

该工作直接解决了大语言模型和多模态模型评测基础设施的可扩展性瓶颈，对于需要快速定制评估场景（如新模型发布、特定领域能力探测）的研究者极具参考价值；其"规划器+执行器"双代理范式和基于可行性验证的Grounding机制，也对构建可靠的自动化数据生成流水线有较强的方法论借鉴意义。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How does benchmark quality degrade for highly specialized domains not well-represented in General-Bench (e.g., clinical radiology, legal reasoning, low-resource languages)?
2. What is the full dollar cost per benchmark at scale using GPT-5.1, and does the system remain practical with open-source backbones (the ablation suggests modest degradation — what is the quality-cost tradeoff curve)?
3. Can the Grounding Agent's transformability scoring be externally validated — i.e., do high-scoring (dataset, plan) pairs actually produce better benchmarks than low-scoring ones in a controlled study?
4. How quickly do the generated benchmarks themselves saturate as models are trained on the broader corpus of open benchmarks? Is there evidence of "meta-saturation"?
5. Would a closed-loop system that detects benchmark saturation automatically and triggers regeneration be feasible, and what would the compute overhead look like in practice?
