> **一句话总结：** 与其事后手术式地过滤LLM输出中的幻觉，不如直接从一个经校准的、偏向高可信度区域的后验分布中采样，从而在保持相同统计保证的同时显著提升输出的实用性。

> **TL;DR:** Instead of post-hoc filtering LLM outputs to remove hallucinated claims, this paper calibrates a posterior sampling distribution conditioned on outputs falling in a high-factuality region, obtaining the same conformal risk-control guarantees as prior work while producing substantially more complete and useful responses.

---

## 核心贡献

本文针对现有基于保形预测（conformal prediction）的幻觉控制方法的根本缺陷提出了系统性解决方案。现有方法（尤其是 Mohri & Hashimoto 2024）在生成完成后做事后过滤，将低置信度声明删除，导致输出残缺、不连贯，甚至完全无用（例如所有声明都被删除时只剩空响应）。

**核心思路转变**：将校准目标从"事后过滤器"转移到"采样分布"本身。具体地，将 LLM 的输出分布重新表述为一个后验分布，条件事件为"输出落在高分区域"，即：

$$\mathbb{E}_{D_\text{cal}, x_\text{test}, y_\text{test} \sim Q(\cdot | x_\text{test}, D_\text{cal})}[\ell(x_\text{test}, y_\text{test})] \leq \alpha$$

**三项技术创新**：
1. **后验族定义**：用一维阈值参数 τ 参数化势函数族 Φ^τ，该族将完成按声明级置信度乘积得分进行阈值化——即只保留联合置信分数超过 τ 的输出的概率质量。
2. **离线校准程序**：仅使用基础模型样本（无需从候选后验中额外采样），通过重要性重加权解决归一化常数 Z^τ(x) 未知的问题。引入混合先验（将显式弃权响应混入，质量为 β）确保 Z^τ 有下界，使后验始终定义良好。
3. **单调化保证**：校准目标函数关于 τ 不一定单调，论文通过显式取单调上包络 H̄_i(τ) 解决此问题，并借助 Glivenko-Cantelli 论证将有限样本下的经验后验保证渐近延伸到总体后验。

## Key Contributions

This paper identifies a fundamental tension in conformal hallucination control: post-hoc filtering removes hallucinated claims but degrades response utility by producing incomplete, incoherent, or trivially empty outputs. The authors reframe the problem as **posterior sampling** from a calibrated family of distributions.

**Key technical contributions:**

1. **Posterior family via potential functions**: The LLM prior p_LLM is augmented with a potential Φ^τ(y|x) = 1{∏_i s(c_i | x, c_{<i}) ≥ τ}, thresholding completions by the product of per-claim factuality scores. This defines a family of posteriors indexed by a scalar τ ∈ [0,1].

2. **Off-policy calibration**: Since sampling from each candidate posterior is expensive, the paper develops an off-policy procedure that estimates the conditional normalization constant Z^τ(x) from M base-model samples ("particles") per calibration prompt. An explicit abstention atom (mixture weight β) ensures Z^τ is bounded away from zero, making the empirical posterior ρ̃^τ well-defined for all τ.

3. **Monotonization + conformal guarantee**: The per-prompt risk estimate Ĥ_i(τ) may be non-monotone due to discrete score steps; the paper enforces monotonicity via a running supremum H̄_i(τ) = sup_{τ'≥τ} Ĥ_i(τ'), then applies standard conformal risk control (Theorem 3.2). A Glivenko-Cantelli lemma (Lemma 3.3) shows uniform convergence of empirical-posterior risk to population-posterior risk as M → ∞.

## Methodology

**Setup**: Given a prompt x and an LLM prior p_LLM, define the posterior:

$$p^\tau_\text{LLM}(y|x) \propto p_\text{LLM}(y|x) \cdot \Phi^\tau(y|x), \quad \Phi^\tau(y|x) = \mathbf{1}\!\left\{\prod_{i=1}^K s(c_i \mid x, \mathbf{c}_{<i}) \geq \tau\right\}$$

where s(·) is a per-claim factuality scorer (GPT-4o eliciting 0-1 confidence scores), and K is the random number of claims in y.

**Mixture prior**: To handle pathological cases where p_LLM places no mass on score-passing completions, the prior is mixed with an abstention atom:

$$p_\beta(y|x) = (1-\beta)\, p_\text{LLM}(y|x) + \beta\, \delta_\text{abstain}(y)$$

**Off-policy calibration (Algorithm 1)**:
- For each calibration prompt x_i, sample M particles y_{ij} ~ p_LLM(·|x_i)
- Score each particle to get Φ^τ(y_{ij}|x_i) and annotate loss ℓ(x_i, y_{ij})
- Form the empirical normalization: Z̃^τ(x_i, T_i) = β + (1-β)/M · Σ_j Φ^τ(y_{ij}|x_i)
- Compute per-prompt empirical risk Ĥ_i(τ) via importance reweighting; monotonize to H̄_i(τ)
- Return τ̂ = inf{τ | 1/(n+1) Σ_i H̄_i(τ) + 1/(n+1) ≤ α}

**Scoring**: GPT-4o asked to rate confidence for each claim on [0,1]; a small uniform jitter γ=0.01 is added to ensure continuous scores for calibration at arbitrary α levels.

**Experiments**:
- Model: Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct (4-bit quantized, on single NVIDIA RTX A6000 48GB)
- Datasets: FActScore (682 biography prompts), MATH (500 problem instances)
- Scorer: GPT-4o; labels: GPT-5.4 (biography factuality via Wikipedia)
- Baseline: Mohri & Hashimoto (2024), re-implemented to encourage sentence-split outputs for fair comparison
- 10 random splits; 50 instances as test, remainder as calibration; M=20 particles per prompt; β=0.1

## Results & Highlights

**Validity (Figure 1)**: Both the proposed method and the MH baseline achieve valid coverage (empirical risk ≤ target) at all factuality levels. Monotonization does not introduce excessive slack. Below the model's base capacity (~0.4 for FActScore, ~0.5 for MATH), both methods reduce to sampling from the prior and produce identical behavior.

**Utility (Figure 2)**:
- *FActScore (Likert, non-abstaining)*: The proposed method's non-abstaining generations degrade less sharply than MH as target factuality increases — driven primarily by better preservation of completeness and helpfulness.
- *MATH (completeness, non-abstaining)*: The proposed method not only degrades less — in instances where it does not abstain, completeness *increases* with target factuality (since higher τ concentrates mass on fully correct proofs). MH increasingly produces incomplete answers at high targets (≥0.6).
- *Unconditional (including abstentions)*: MH performs better here because an LLM judge prefers a polished refusal over a partial-but-risky answer; this reflects a limitation of LLM-based evaluation rather than user utility.

**Claim-level proxy metrics (Figure 3)**:
- At intermediate targets, both methods emit similar numbers of claims.
- Conditioned on non-abstention, the proposed method emits substantially more claims — MH concentrates filtering on hard prompts and leaves easy ones untouched, while the posterior method pushes mass toward high-quality regions uniformly.
- Claim precision is similar across methods.
- Hallucination-free rate is slightly lower for the proposed method conditioned on non-abstention, since the posterior can only shift mass toward safer regions if such regions exist under p_LLM.

## Critical Assessment

**Strengths:**

1. **Conceptually clean reframing**: The insight that post-hoc filtering is fundamentally limited because it cannot shift probability mass is correct and well-articulated. Posterior sampling is the right abstraction.

2. **Non-trivial theory**: The calibration guarantee (Theorem 3.2) is finite-sample and assumption-lean (exchangeability only). The Glivenko-Cantelli convergence to the population posterior is a meaningful asymptotic result. Monotonization is a clever practical fix backed by theoretical justification.

3. **Off-policy calibration is a real contribution**: Avoiding on-policy samples from each candidate posterior (which would require running the model under each τ separately) is computationally essential; the importance reweighting derivation makes this tractable.

4. **Honest empirical comparison**: The authors are transparent about where their method underperforms (unconditional FActScore Likert when LLM judges prefer clean refusals over partial answers) and explain the mechanism behind the gap.

**Limitations:**

1. **Particle efficiency**: With M=20 particles per prompt, the empirical posterior is a coarse approximation. The method may become unreliable when high-scoring completions are rare — the abstention atom provides a fallback, but at the cost of utility. More efficient samplers (SMC/particle filtering) are noted as future work but not demonstrated.

2. **Scorer dependence**: GPT-4o is used as a factuality scorer. While the theory is scorer-agnostic for validity, downstream utility is heavily scorer-quality-dependent. The paper includes a GPT-4o-mini ablation (Appendix D.3) but the dependence on a paid, closed-source oracle is a deployment concern.

3. **Claim decomposition granularity**: The paper encourages the generator to split outputs into sentences rather than formally decomposing into atomic claims. This is a simplification that may hurt the quality of the factuality score product, particularly for claims with strong inter-dependencies.

4. **β sensitivity**: β governs the utility-abstention tradeoff but the paper only reports β=0.1. The sensitivity analysis is deferred to Appendix D.4 and is not fully explored in the main text.

5. **Computation cost**: At inference time, 20 particles + GPT-4o scoring per prompt is expensive. The paper runs on a single A6000 but cost at scale is not characterized.

---

## Strengths

- Theoretically grounded reframing of conformal hallucination control as posterior inference
- Off-policy calibration with only base-model samples (no expensive on-policy collection)
- Finite-sample guarantees (Theorem 3.2) + asymptotic consistency (Corollary 3.4)
- Empirically demonstrates improved utility on both open-ended generation (FActScore) and structured reasoning (MATH)
- Honest about the utility-abstention tradeoff and the role of the LLM judge's preferences

---

## Limitations

- Particle-based posterior approximation becomes inefficient when the model rarely produces high-scoring outputs; no SMC or adaptive sampling demonstrated
- Reliance on GPT-4o as scorer introduces cost and closed-source dependency
- Claim-level scoring via sentence-splitting is a coarser proxy than true atomic claim decomposition
- β hyperparameter sensitivity underexplored in the main paper
- Computational cost at scale (20 particles × GPT-4o scoring per prompt) not characterized

---

## Relevance

**High relevance** to anyone working on:
- LLM factuality and hallucination reduction (especially conformal / statistical approaches)
- Test-time compute and inference-time scaling (posterior sampling as an alternative to beam search / repeated sampling)
- Trustworthy / calibrated generation for high-stakes applications (medical, legal)
- Connections between conformal prediction, Bayesian inference, and language generation

This paper sits at the intersection of conformal prediction theory and LLM deployment practice. It advances the theoretical foundations of the Mohri & Hashimoto (2024) / Quach et al. (2024) / Rubin-Toles et al. (2025) line of work by moving calibration upstream into the generative process itself.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. **SMC integration**: Can sequential Monte Carlo steering (Lew et al. 2023; Loula et al. 2025) be used as the posterior sampler, and does it recover the finite-sample guarantee of Theorem 3.2 in that setting?

2. **Scorer-free extension**: Can the scoring function be replaced by a learned reward model or a self-consistency score, reducing reliance on GPT-4o?

3. **Adaptive β**: Could β be calibrated jointly with τ to explicitly optimize the utility-abstention tradeoff for a specific deployment context?

4. **Multi-hop claims**: For tasks where claims are strongly interdependent (e.g., multi-step math proofs), does the product-of-scores potential function Φ^τ remain a good proxy for overall correctness, or does claim-level factorization break down?

5. **Human evaluation**: The utility measurement relies on an LLM judge (GPT-5.4 / GPT-4o). Do human annotators agree with the judge's preference for fluent refusals over partial-but-informative answers?
