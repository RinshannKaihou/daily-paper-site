> **一句话总结：** Muon 优化器中动量的作用是一个谱滤波器——它先滤除梯度矩阵中的噪声扰动、放大信号-噪声之间的奇异值谱隙，使得随后的正交化步骤作用在一个"信号已分离"的干净矩阵上，从而在理论上和实验上均优于先正交化后加动量（Post-polar）或去掉动量（Polar-only）的方案。

> **TL;DR:** This paper provides the first theoretical explanation for why Muon must apply momentum *before* orthogonalization. Momentum acts as a spectral low-pass filter on the gradient stream: it suppresses noisy perturbations, widens the spectral gap between signal and noise singular values, and aligns the momentum buffer's leading singular subspaces with the true gradient signal. The polar (orthogonalization) step then operates on this denoised matrix rather than on the raw, noise-contaminated gradient — achieving provably stronger signal alignment than reversing the order or omitting momentum entirely. Theory is validated on NanoGPT and LLaMA 350M pretraining.

## 核心贡献

1. **谱隙理论（Theorem 1 + Corollary 1）**  
   在"信号加扰动"梯度模型下，动量缓冲区 $M_t$ 的第 $r$ 和第 $r+1$ 个奇异值之间的谱隙随有效窗口 $T = 1/(1-\beta)$ 单调增大，扰动底部以 $O((2T-1)^{-1/4})$ 衰减。奇异子空间对齐误差也以 $O(T^{-1/4})$ 收缩，说明大动量系数（大 $T$）使动量缓冲区的前 $r$ 个奇异子空间越来越准确地对准信号方向。

2. **Pre-polar 严格优越性（Theorems 2 & 3）**  
   对任意固定梯度信号矩阵和绝对连续扰动分布，Pre-polar 流水线在期望信号对齐上严格优于 Post-polar 和 Polar-only。在秩-1 加噪高斯模型的低 SNR 区，Polar-only 的信号对齐随矩阵规模 $m \to \infty$ 趋于零，而 Pre-polar 凭借足够大的窗口 $T$ 仍能恢复信号。

3. **多任务实验验证**  
   合成加噪模型、CIFAR-10、NanoGPT 静止探针与轨迹探针，以及端到端 NanoGPT / LLaMA 350M 训练，全面支持上述理论预测。

## Key Contributions

1. **Spectral gap theorem (Theorem 1 + Corollary 1).**  
   Under a signal-plus-perturbation gradient model with bounded-variance mean-zero orthogonal (BVMZOS) noise, the momentum buffer $M_t$ develops a spectral gap between its $r$-th and $(r+1)$-th singular values that grows with the effective window size $T = 1/(1-\beta)$. Concretely, signal singular values are bounded below by $c_\text{sig}\lambda_k - O((2T-1)^{-1/4})$ while noise singular values are bounded above by $O((2T-1)^{-1/4})$, and the top-$r$ singular subspace alignment error decays as $O(T^{-1/4})$. Larger momentum (larger $\beta$, larger $T$) widens this gap — the opposite of what classical convergence theory predicts.

2. **Pre-polar provably dominates alternatives (Theorems 2 & 3).**  
   For any absolutely continuous perturbation distribution, the Pre-polar pipeline (momentum then polar) strictly dominates in expected signal alignment over both Post-polar (polar per-step, then momentum) and Polar-only (no momentum). Under a rank-1 spiked Gaussian low-SNR model, Polar-only alignment vanishes as $m \to \infty$, while Pre-polar recovers the signal whenever $T$ is sufficiently large.

3. **Empirical validation across scales and settings.**  
   Stationary probes (fixed checkpoint, streaming gradients) and trajectory probes (sliding buffer during live training) on NanoGPT and LLaMA 350M confirm every theoretical prediction qualitatively and quantitatively.

## Methodology

### Problem setup and the three pipelines

The paper defines and compares three weight-update pipelines at each training step $t$, all using momentum buffer $M_t = \beta M_{t-1} + (1-\beta)G_t$ and polar factor $\mathcal{O}(X) = UV^\top$ (thin SVD):

| Pipeline | Update rule | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Pre-polar** | $\Delta W_t \propto \mathcal{O}(M_t)$ | Standard Muon: polar after momentum |
| **Post-polar** | $\tilde{M}_t = \beta\tilde{M}_{t-1} + (1-\beta)\mathcal{O}(G_t)$, $\Delta W_t \propto \tilde{M}_t$ | Orthogonal-SGDM: momentum after polar |
| **Polar-only** | $\Delta W_t \propto \mathcal{O}(G_t)$ | No momentum baseline |

Figure 1 shows that Pre-polar dominates throughout NanoGPT and LLaMA 350M training, with the gap emerging clearly in validation loss.

### Gradient model

Gradients are modeled as $G_t = G_t^\text{sig} + \Xi_t$, where:
- $G_t^\text{sig} = \sum_{k=1}^r \lambda_k(t) u_k v_k^\top$ is the coherent signal in a time-invariant low-rank subspace;
- $\Xi_t$ is BVMZOS noise (bounded variance, mean-zero, pairwise orthogonal in expectation).

The effective window size is $T := 1/(1-\beta)$, summarizing how many past gradients the momentum kernel meaningfully integrates.

### Key theoretical insight: noncommutativity

Momentum and orthogonalization do not commute. The polar factor $\mathcal{O}(\cdot)$ replaces all singular values with 1, erasing the amplitude gap between signal and noise. If applied first (Post-polar), the resulting per-step polar gradient has no amplitude structure for momentum to exploit. If momentum acts first (Pre-polar), it suppresses noise amplitudes before $\mathcal{O}(\cdot)$ discards them entirely, so the polar factor sees a matrix whose singular subspaces already align with the signal. The guiding principle: *denoise first, then orthogonalize*.

### Proof tools

- Theorem 1 uses Weyl's inequality on singular values and a martingale argument for the BVMZOS noise.
- Corollary 1 uses Davis–Kahan / Wedin's $\sin\Theta$ theorem to convert the spectral gap into subspace alignment bounds.
- Theorem 2 uses an absolute-continuity argument to show that the polar factor of a non-denoised signal matrix cannot consistently align with the signal direction.
- Theorem 3 specializes to the rank-1 spiked Gaussian model under low SNR to give explicit asymptotics.

### Empirical probes

Two complementary probes are used to validate theory on real training runs:

- **Stationary probe**: $K$ mini-batch gradients collected at a fixed checkpoint (weights frozen), removing non-stationarity. Used for clean comparison of momentum pipelines.
- **Trajectory probe**: A sliding $K$-step gradient buffer maintained during live training, capturing the effect of time-drifting weights. Validates that Pre-polar dominance survives the non-stationarity of actual LLM training.

Signal alignment is measured via:
- **Rank-$r$ alignment** $\text{Align}_r(A; \bar{G}) = \|U_r(\bar{G})^\top A V_r(\bar{G})\|_F / \sqrt{r} \in [0,1]$
- **Full-rank alignment** $\text{Align}_\text{full}(A; \bar{G}) = \langle A, \mathcal{O}(\bar{G})\rangle_F / \min(m,n)$

## Results & Highlights

### Spectral gap: theory matches experiment (Figures 2, 3)

- On NanoGPT's `h.0.attn.c_proj` layer, filtered singular value spectra show the momentum buffer pulling signal singular values toward the mean-gradient spectrum while suppressing tail singular values — exactly as Theorem 1 predicts.
- Per-step filtering ratios ($\text{Filt}_k(\beta) = \sigma_k(M_K^{(\beta)})/\sigma_k(G_K)$) stay near 1 for $k \le r$ and drop for $k > r$, widening with $\beta$.
- Noise suppression ratio $R(T)$ lies above the $(2T-1)^{1/4}$ theoretical floor across all tested layers and checkpoints.
- Subspace alignment errors ($\sin\Theta_U$, $\sin\Theta_V$) decay as $c_r(2T-1)^{-1/4}$ with fitted constants closely matching the theory (e.g., 0.32 and 1.02 at ranks 1 and 5).

### Pre-polar dominance: signal alignment (Figures 4, 6)

- On stationary probes: Pre-polar full-rank signal alignment rises monotonically with $\beta$ from ~0.2 ($\beta=0.5$) to ~0.9 ($\beta \to 1$), while Post-polar and Polar-only remain flat near 0.1 across all $\beta$.
- On trajectory probes during NanoGPT training: Pre-polar full-rank alignment stays ~0.6 throughout training; Post-polar and Polar-only remain near 0.1.
- The Pre-polar advantage is especially pronounced during the warmdown phase (steps 3600–5100), consistent with a higher effective SNR regime.

### End-to-end LLM training (Figure 1)

- NanoGPT (5100 steps): Pre-polar reaches ~3.4 validation loss vs. ~3.6 for Post-polar and ~4.0+ for Polar-only at step 5000.
- LLaMA 350M (3000 steps): Pre-polar reaches ~3.0 vs. ~3.4 for Post-polar and ~4.5 for Polar-only at step 3000.

## Critical Assessment

This paper addresses a real and frustrating gap: Muon's empirical superiority over momentum-free variants was observable but not explained, and prior theory actively contradicted practice (predicting that heavy momentum hurts). The spectral filtering framing is intellectually clean and the noncommutativity argument is the kind of insight that, once stated, seems obvious in retrospect.

The theoretical model (BVMZOS noise, coherent signal) is a reasonable proxy for mini-batch SGD gradient streams but is not a consequence of SGD dynamics — it is an assumption. The rank-1 Gaussian model used in Theorem 3 is a further simplification. The paper is upfront about this and provides both stationary and trajectory probes to show robustness beyond the exact model.

One practical gap: Muon implementations commonly use Nesterov momentum (applying polar to a linear combination of $M_t$ and $G_t$), which the paper explicitly notes is *not* covered by the analysis. This is a meaningful caveat for practitioners comparing against production Muon code.

The $O(T^{-1/4})$ convergence rate for subspace alignment is slow (e.g., $T=100$ gives only 3× improvement over $T=1$), which might suggest limited practical benefit for moderate $\beta$ — though the empirical results show large signal alignment differences in practice, suggesting the bound is loose.

---

## Strengths

- **Resolves a theory-practice contradiction**: Prior theory said heavy momentum hurts in Muon; this paper proves it helps, giving the correct mechanism.
- **Clean conceptual contribution**: The "denoise first, then orthogonalize" principle is a memorable and actionable design rule for matrix-based optimizers beyond Muon.
- **Rigorous proofs with matching experiments**: Theorems 1-3 have direct empirical counterparts validated through carefully designed probes. The stationary/trajectory probe methodology is a useful contribution in itself.
- **Quantitative predictions verified**: The $(2T-1)^{-1/4}$ floor appears in real gradient spectra from NanoGPT.
- **Scale of validation**: LLaMA 350M end-to-end training is non-trivial and shows consistent improvement throughout, not just at initialization.

---

## Limitations

- **Model assumptions**: The BVMZOS noise model and time-invariant coherent signal are idealized. Real gradients during LLM training drift significantly (the trajectory probe partially addresses this but does not fully resolve it).
- **Nesterov momentum excluded**: Most practical Muon code uses Nesterov momentum, which falls outside the paper's analysis. This limits direct applicability to practitioners using out-of-the-box Muon.
- **Theorem 3 restricted to rank-1 spiked Gaussian**: Extending the quantitative asymptotic separation result to general rank-$r$ and non-Gaussian perturbations is left as future work.
- **Time-varying $\beta$ not analyzed**: Practical schedules ramp $\beta$ during warmup (enlarging $T$ dynamically), which requires a time-varying filter analysis the paper defers.
- **No wall-clock comparison**: The paper does not report whether Pre-polar yields better loss *per compute unit* compared to simply training longer with Polar-only — though the end-to-end curves suggest yes at matched step counts.

---

## Relevance

This paper is relevant to:
- **Optimizer design for LLM pretraining**: Directly explains and justifies Muon over AdamW and Orthogonal-SGDM variants.
- **Matrix-based optimizers broadly**: The spectral filtering principle applies to any optimizer that ends in a nonlinear spectral step (Shampoo, SOAP, Scion).
- **Theoretical understanding of momentum**: The frequency-domain / filtering view of momentum (building on prior work framing momentum as a low-pass filter) is extended to the matrix setting with new, provable consequences.
- **Gradient geometry / low-rank structure**: The signal-persistence and low-effective-rank structure of neural network gradients, which this paper exploits, connects to broader empirical observations about gradient spectra in large models.

**Bottom line**: A solid theory paper that fills an important gap in understanding why Muon works as designed. The core insight (noncommutativity + spectral filtering = denoise first) is broadly applicable and well-executed. Main caveat is that the Nesterov momentum variant used in practice falls outside the formal analysis.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. **Nesterov extension**: Can the spectral filtering analysis be extended to the Nesterov momentum variant $\nu M_t + \kappa G_t$ used in practical Muon? Does the same Pre-polar dominance hold?

2. **Adaptive $T$ scheduling**: The paper notes that $\beta$ is often ramped during training. Can a time-varying version of Theorem 1 justify or guide $\beta$ scheduling strategies for Muon?

3. **Shampoo and SOAP**: The conclusion suggests the principle extends to other matrix-based optimizers that end in a spectral step. Does Pre-denoising (applying EMA before the Shampoo preconditioner) give provable gains analogous to those shown here for Muon?

4. **Optimal momentum coefficient**: Given the spectral gap scales as $(2T-1)^{-1/4}$, is there a principled way to choose $\beta$ based on the SNR of the gradient stream for a given layer and training stage?

5. **Connection to implicit bias**: The paper focuses on signal alignment at each step. What is the cumulative effect on the implicit bias of the trained model — does Pre-polar produce models with different generalization properties than Post-polar at matched training loss?
