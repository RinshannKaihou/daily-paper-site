EEG Foundation Models

Beyond Accuracy: Robustness, Interpretability and Expressiveness of EEG Foundation Models

Urban Širca, Maryam Alimardani, Stefanos Zafeiriou, Konstantinos Barmpas

2026-05-17 arxiv.org/abs/2605.17562

No single EEG foundation model dominates across all failure modes — and the real bottleneck for head-only performance isn’t representation quality, it’s the pooling strategy.

Key Metric
NeuroRVQ leads at 69.6% balanced accuracy with 10× fewer parameters; flatten pooling closes gap by +21%.
Why This Matters
Overturns the prevailing narrative that poor head-only performance reflects bad pre-trained features — it was the readout all along.
Critical Edge
An author co-created NeuroRVQ (which leads clean accuracy). Synthetic perturbations may not reflect real-world EEG artifacts.
Robustness Paradox
BrainOmni: best at noise (−5.4% at −5 dB) yet worst at channel dropout (−13.3%). Robustness doesn’t transfer.

MMethodology

Six EEG-FMs (BIOT, CBraMod, LaBraM, NeuroRVQ, BrainOmni, REVE) benchmarked against EEGNet across eight datasets (motor imagery, motor execution, ERP, working memory, sleep staging, eyes-open/closed).

Robustness: Four test-time perturbations on clean-trained models — additive white/pink noise (10 to −15 dB SNR), random channel dropout (0.1–0.5), region-based dropout, region-specific noise injection.

Interpretability: AttnLRP (first application to EEG-FMs) + Gradient×Input fallback. Attribution maps compared across clean vs. perturbed, pre-trained vs. fine-tuned checkpoints.

Expressiveness: Block-wise linear probing across transformer depth; pooling ablation (mean vs. flatten). Subject-independent cross-validation throughout.

RResults & Highlights

  • Top six FMs cluster within 4.4% average balanced accuracy (65.2–69.6%)
  • Two noise regimes: model-limited at moderate noise, signal-limited at extreme (≤ −3 dB)
  • Zero-padding penalizes variable-channel models; removing channels recovers performance (BrainOmni: −13.3% → −2.4%)
  • All models attend to neurophysiologically correct regions (central for motor, occipital for ERP)
  • Attribution maps stay spatially stable under noise while predictions degrade — models “look right but read wrong”
  • Flatten pooling: NeuroRVQ +21%, LaBraM +14%; REVE drops 19% when flipped to mean

SStrengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • First three-axis evaluation beyond clean accuracy
  • Persuasive pooling ablation overturns prior interpretation
  • Well-motivated AttnLRP application
  • Zero-padding vs. channel-removal ablation reveals architectural sensitivity

Limitations

  • Clean-data-only training; no noise-augmented comparison
  • Synthetic perturbations oversimplify real artifacts
  • Cannot disentangle BrainOmni’s three robustness factors
  • Attribution = sensitivity, not reliance

?Questions for Further Exploration

Would noise-augmented or adversarial pre-training close the observed robustness gaps?
Can the pooling finding be explained theoretically in terms of information loss in token aggregation?
How do attribution maps look on misclassified trials — wrong regions or failed decoding?
Does the “late block repurposing” pattern hold across pre-training objectives (contrastive vs. masked vs. reconstruction)?
How transferable are these findings to other biosignal foundation models (ECG, EMG, MEG)?
What is the interaction between perturbation type and fine-tuning data size?
