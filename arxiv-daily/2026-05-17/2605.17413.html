Ablating Safety: Mechanisms for Removing Alignment in Language Models for Security Applications

Isaac David & Arthur Gervais · University College London

Task-only LoRA — training on authorized security data without explicit refusal suppression — achieves the best utility-risk tradeoff (security 0.87, spillover 0.13), while single-vector refusal projection barely helps security (0.46→0.50) but makes models 4.7× more unsafe.

Key Finding 5× spillover Refusal projection barely improves security scores but explodes unsafe out-of-scope compliance from 0.10 to 0.47.
Key Metric 0.87 Security score under task-only LoRA — an 89% lift from the 0.46 aligned baseline.
Why This Matters Pareto, not switch Alignment removal is a utility-risk frontier — "decensoring" is the wrong frame for security evaluation.
Critical Edge 0.5B–3B only Compact models only; 60-prompt benchmark is small. No weight-space abliteration or SAE steering tested.

Methodology

The paper evaluates four families of alignment-removal interventions on Qwen2.5 and SmolLM2 models using a 60-prompt security benchmark (SECURITY-AR):

  • Projection pilot — 4 models × 4 conditions × 26 tasks (416 completions). Refusal directions extracted as mean activation difference between refusal/non-refusal prompts; hook projects out component: h′ = h − α(hᵀr̂)r̂.
  • Extended LoRA evaluation — 3 Qwen2.5 models × 11 conditions × 60 prompts (1,980 completions). Four LoRA variants tested with rank 16, α 32, AdamW lr 5e⁻⁵ on 240 security + 480 benign examples.
  • Scoring — refusal/attempt classifiers, sandbox validators, rubric review, rule-based scorers. Bootstrap CIs over prompts. Controls include random-direction, harmless-direction, and topic-matched projections.

Strengths

  • Rigorous multi-metric framework separating refusal reduction from genuine capability recovery
  • Comprehensive control conditions preventing overclaiming from contrast-set artifacts
  • Responsible release design with clear boundaries between public and withheld artifacts
  • The task-only LoRA finding has important practical implications for red-teaming
  • Well-scoped research questions with clear operationalization

Limitations

  • Small prompt suite (60 prompts) — may not capture full diversity of security tasks
  • Only compact open-weight models (0.5B–3B); results may not transfer to frontier-scale
  • Rule-based scoring may miss subtle forms of unsafe compliance
  • Does not cover full space of de-alignment methods (weight orthogonalization, SAE steering, circuit-level edits, preference tuning)
  • LoRA training data is synthetic — may not reflect real-world distributions

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How do the utility-risk frontiers change when scaling to 7B–70B models? Does the model-dependence finding suggest a scaling benefit for safety retention?
  2. Can sparse autoencoder-identified refusal features achieve more targeted ablation with less spillover?
  3. What happens with adversarially chosen out-of-scope probes rather than generic non-operational requests?
  4. Does the task-only LoRA finding generalize across different PEFT methods (prefix tuning, IA3, prompt tuning) and non-security domains?
  5. How stable are the extracted refusal directions across training checkpoints — could direction drift serve as a training observability signal?
