> **一句话总结：** 本文系统评估了六个EEG基础模型在鲁棒性、可解释性和表达能力三个维度上的表现，发现没有任何单一模型在所有失效模式下占优，归因图显示模型关注正确的脑区但无法解码被扰动的信号，且此前被认为表征质量差的问题实际上主要由池化策略导致。

> **TL;DR:** First systematic benchmark of six EEG foundation models across robustness (test-time perturbations), interpretability (AttnLRP attributions), and expressiveness (block-wise probing). No single model dominates all failure modes; models attend to neurophysiologically correct regions but fail to decode corrupted content; poor head-only performance is largely a pooling artifact, not a representation quality issue.

## 核心贡献

- 首次对六个EEG基础模型（BIOT、CBraMod、LaBraM、NeuroRVQ、BrainOmni、REVE）在八个数据集上进行鲁棒性、可解释性和表达能力的系统评估
- 揭示了鲁棒性排序高度依赖于扰动类型：噪声鲁棒性最强的模型（BrainOmni，−5 dB仅损失5.4%）在通道dropout下最为脆弱（−13.3%），表明不同鲁棒性维度之间无法迁移
- 首次将AttnLRP方法应用于EEG-FM，证明模型的空间归因与神经生理学预期一致，但归因图在扰动下保持空间稳定而预测退化，说明模型"看对了地方但读错了信号"
- 通过块级探针发现微调主要重塑后期transformer块，早期块已包含任务相关信息
- 证明此前被归因于"低质量预训练表征"的head-only性能低下主要由mean pooling导致，替换为token flatten后NeuroRVQ和LaBraM分别提升21%和14%

## Key Contributions

- First systematic three-axis evaluation (robustness, interpretability, expressiveness) of six EEG foundation models across eight datasets and five perturbation types
- Demonstrates that robustness rankings are perturbation-dependent: BrainOmni leads on additive noise (−5.4% at −5 dB) but ties for worst on channel dropout (−13.3%), while REVE leads on dropout (−5.6%)
- First application of AttnLRP to EEG-FMs, showing all models concentrate relevance on task-appropriate brain regions (central for motor, occipital for ERP/eyes)
- Block-wise probing reveals fine-tuning repurposes late transformer blocks while early blocks retain pre-trained task information
- Identifies pooling strategy (mean vs. flatten) as the primary cause of poor head-only performance, not representation quality — flipping pooling closes the gap with supervised baselines

## Methodology

The authors benchmark six EEG-FMs (BIOT, CBraMod, LaBraM, NeuroRVQ, BrainOmni, REVE) against EEGNet as a supervised baseline across eight datasets spanning motor imagery, motor execution, ERP, working memory, sleep staging, and eyes-open/closed tasks.

**Robustness** is assessed via four test-time perturbations applied to clean-trained models: (i) additive white and pink noise at SNRs from 10 to −15 dB, (ii) random channel dropout with probabilities 0.1–0.5 (zero-padded), (iii) region-based channel dropout (primary vs. control electrode regions), and (iv) region-specific noise injection.

**Interpretability** uses AttnLRP (Attention-Aware Layer-Wise Relevance Propagation) as the primary attribution method — the first application to EEG-FMs — supplemented by Gradient×Input for models where AttnLRP is numerically unstable. Attribution maps are compared between clean and perturbed conditions and between pre-trained and fine-tuned checkpoints.

**Expressiveness** employs block-wise linear probing across transformer depth to track where task information emerges, and ablates pooling strategies (mean vs. flatten) to isolate representation quality from readout design.

All models follow the benchmarking protocol of Lee et al. (2025) with subject-independent cross-validation.

## Results & Highlights

- **Clean accuracy**: Top six FM models cluster within 4.4% average balanced accuracy (0.652–0.696), confirming prior findings of modest gains over baselines. NeuroRVQ leads on average despite having 10× fewer parameters than the largest model.
- **Noise robustness**: Two regimes emerge — a model-limited regime at moderate noise (5–10 dB) where architecture matters, and a signal-limited regime at extreme noise (≤ −3 dB) where all models converge to a task-specific floor.
- **Channel dropout**: Zero-padding penalizes variable-channel models severely; removing channels entirely (rather than zeroing) recovers performance (BrainOmni: −13.3% → −2.4%), indicating fragility stems from handling zero inputs, not information loss.
- **Attribution alignment**: All models concentrate relevance on neurophysiologically expected regions — central electrodes for motor tasks, occipital for ERP/eyes — partially addressing trustworthiness concerns.
- **Perturbation attribution**: Attribution maps remain spatially stable under noise while predictions degrade, suggesting models attend to correct regions but decode corrupted content.
- **Pooling ablation**: Replacing mean pooling with flatten pooling boosts head-only performance by 21% (NeuroRVQ) and 14% (LaBraM); conversely, switching REVE from flatten to mean pooling drops it 19%.

Stefanos Zafeiriou is a well-established researcher in computer vision and signal processing. Konstantinos Barmpas is also an author of NeuroRVQ (one of the benchmarked models), which constitutes a potential conflict of interest, though the paper evaluates it alongside competitors fairly. The paper is a preprint (arXiv, May 2026) and has not yet been peer-reviewed.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: High. This is the first study to systematically evaluate EEG-FMs beyond clean accuracy along three complementary axes. The AttnLRP application and pooling ablation are particularly insightful contributions.
- **Rigor**: Strong experimental design with eight datasets, six models, multiple perturbation types, and subject-independent cross-validation. The zero-padding vs. channel-removal comparison is a well-controlled ablation. However, all models are trained on clean data only, so robustness conclusions may not transfer to models trained with augmentation.
- **Reproducibility**: Good. All six benchmarked models have public checkpoints. The evaluation protocol follows a published benchmark. Perturbation parameters are specified in appendices. However, full code release is not mentioned.
- **Significance**: High impact for the EEG-FM community. The pooling finding alone challenges a prevailing narrative in prior work and has immediate practical implications for how future models should be evaluated.
- **Red flags**: (1) An author of this paper created one of the benchmarked models (NeuroRVQ), which happens to lead on clean accuracy. (2) BIOT fails on all benchmarks and is excluded from analysis — while arguably fair, it reduces the model set. (3) Synthetic perturbations may not capture real-world EEG artifacts (muscle, eye movements, electrode shift). (4) Attribution analyses are limited to correctly classified trials only.

## Strengths

- First comprehensive three-axis evaluation framework for EEG-FMs beyond accuracy
- Compelling pooling ablation that overturns prior interpretation of poor head-only performance
- Well-motivated use of AttnLRP for transformer interpretability in EEG
- Careful distinction between zero-padding and channel-removal dropout reveals architectural sensitivities
- Clear practical recommendations: use flatten pooling for head-only evaluation, consider architectural choices for specific deployment noise profiles

## Limitations

- All models trained on clean data only; does not assess whether noise-augmented training closes robustness gaps
- Synthetic perturbations are simplifications — real EEG artifacts (muscle, eye movement, electrode displacement) are more complex
- Cannot disentangle the three factors behind BrainOmni's noise robustness (multimodal pre-training, VQ tokenizer, channel compression) without matched ablations
- Attribution maps show sensitivity, not reliance — only region dropout tests reliance directly
- Only correctly classified trials are analyzed for interpretability, leaving error analysis unexplored
- Fixed-channel models (EEGNet, CBraMod, BIOT) cannot be evaluated under channel removal, creating an asymmetry in the dropout protocol

## Relevance

本文对EEG基础模型的系统性评估框架与鲁棒性分析方法对BCI领域具有重要意义，其发现——特别是池化策略对表征质量评估的干扰——对后续EEG预训练模型的设计和评估有直接指导价值。AttnLRP归因方法的应用也为EEG模型的可信度提供了新的验证途径。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Would noise-augmented or adversarial pre-training close the observed robustness gaps, particularly for channel dropout?
- Can the pooling finding (mean vs. flatten) be explained theoretically in terms of information loss in token aggregation?
- How do attribution maps look on misclassified trials — do models attend to wrong regions or fail to decode correct regions?
- Does the "late block repurposing" pattern hold across pre-training objectives (contrastive vs. masked prediction vs. reconstruction)?
- How transferable are these findings to other biosignal foundation models (ECG, EMG, MEG)?
- What is the interaction between perturbation type and fine-tuning data size — do FMs become more robust with more downstream data?
