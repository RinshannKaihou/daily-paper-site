> **一句话总结：** 本文在均值场极限下将无限深、无限宽的 Transformer 训练建模为神经 PDE 的最优控制问题，通过伴随灵敏度分析推导条件 Wasserstein 梯度的显式公式，证明了梯度流在 COT 度量空间中的存在唯一性，并在 NTK 可注入（等价于 log-sum-exp 函数族线性无关）条件下证明了梯度流以线性速率收敛到全局最小值。

> **TL;DR:** This paper extends mean-field training theory from ResNets (neural ODEs) to transformers (neural PDEs) in the limit of infinite depth and width, deriving the conditional Wasserstein gradient via adjoint sensitivity analysis, proving well-posedness of gradient flow, and establishing local linear convergence to global minima when the attention NTK is injective — a condition equivalent to linear independence of cumulant generating functions modulo affine functions.

## 核心贡献

- 将无限深（连续深度变量 $s\in[0,1]$）和无限宽（参数的概率测度表示）的 Transformer 训练建模为双重均值场问题：token 分布沿层演化 + 注意力参数的测度表示，最终形成控制神经 PDE 的最优控制问题。
- 建立前向传播的适定性（Proposition 1）：token 分布通过连续性方程 $\partial_s \mu(s) + \text{div}(\mu(s)\Phi_{\rho(\cdot|s)}[\mu(s)]) = 0$ 演化，等价于 Banach 空间 $C^0(S,\mathbb{R}^d)$ 上的 ODE，流映射存在唯一。
- 利用伴随灵敏度分析（Theorem 1）：推导出训练风险的条件 Wasserstein 梯度显式公式，涉及满足反向 ODE 的伴随变量 $m_\rho^{(j)}(s)$，建立上梯度 $|\nabla L|(\rho)$ 的表达式。
- 证明梯度流方程的适定性（Theorem 2-3）：在条件最优传输（COT）度量空间中，梯度流等价于最大下降曲线，且当初始参数的 $V$-边际紧支撑时，梯度流曲线存在且唯一。
- 给出 NTK 可注入性的充要条件（Proposition 8）：NTK 正定性等价于累积生成函数（log-sum-exp）族 $\{g_{\mu^{(j)}}\}$ 模仿射函数的线性无关性（弱独立性），并在强独立性条件下同样成立。
- 在 NTK 可注入假设下证明局部线性收敛（Theorem 4）：当初始损失 $L(\rho_0) < CN^{-3}\lambda_0^3$ 时，梯度流以速率 $O(e^{-CN^{-1}\lambda_0 t})$ 收敛到全局最小值。

## Key Contributions

- **Dual mean-field formulation:** Represents transformers as both infinitely deep (continuous depth parameter $s \in [0,1]$) and infinitely wide (probability measure over attention parameters $\theta = (Q, q, V)$), yielding a neural PDE — not a neural ODE as for ResNets — because attention couples multiple token distributions simultaneously.
- **Well-posedness of the forward pass (Proposition 1):** Token distributions evolve via the continuity equation $\partial_s \mu(s) + \text{div}(\mu(s)\Phi_{\rho(\cdot|s)}[\mu(s)]) = 0$, equivalently characterized by flow maps satisfying an ODE on the Banach space $C^0(S, \mathbb{R}^d)$, with existence and uniqueness following from Carathéodory theory.
- **Adjoint sensitivity analysis (Theorem 1):** Derives an explicit formula for the first-order perturbation of the training risk $L(\rho)$, involving adjoint variables $m_\rho^{(j)}(s) \in M_d(S^j)$ satisfying backward ODEs (Definition 1), and establishes $|\nabla L|(\rho)$ as a strong upper-gradient.
- **Gradient flow well-posedness (Theorems 2–3):** Proves equivalence between the gradient flow continuity equation and the curve of maximal slope characterization in the COT metric. Existence is obtained via Minimizing Movements (De Giorgi scheme) and uniqueness via Grönwall-type arguments, assuming compactly supported $V$-marginal.
- **NTK injectivity characterization (Proposition 8):** Shows that the injectivity of the attention NTK map is equivalent to the weak independence property — linear independence of cumulant generating functions $\{g_{\mu^{(j)}}(q) = \log \int e^{\langle q,y\rangle} d\mu^{(j)}(y)\}$ modulo affine functions. Provides necessary and sufficient conditions.
- **Local linear convergence (Theorem 4):** Under NTK injectivity at initialization ($\lambda_0 = \int_0^1 \lambda_{\min}(K_1[\rho_0](s))\,ds > 0$) and sufficiently small initial loss ($L(\rho_0) < CN^{-3}\lambda_0^3$), gradient flow converges at rate $L(\rho_t) \leq L(\rho_0)\exp(-CN^{-1}\lambda_0 t)$.

## Methodology

The paper extends the mean-field training framework of [Cetraneo et al. 2024] from ResNets to transformers. The key methodological pipeline is:

### 1. Mean-Field Attention and Transformer Model (Section 2.1–2.2)

- **Attention parameterization:** Uses $\theta = (Q, q, V)$ with $K = I_d$ fixed (a change of variables for regularity) and a supplementary bias $q$ that breaks symmetry and is crucial for NTK injectivity (Section 3).
- **Mean-field multi-head attention:** For $\rho \in \mathcal{P}(\Theta)$, defines $\Phi_\rho[\mu](x) = \int_\Theta \varphi_\theta[\mu](x)\,d\rho(\theta)$, recovering finite-head attention via empirical measures.
- **Infinite depth:** Depth is a continuum $s \in [0,1]$; the parameterization $\rho \in \mathcal{P}^{\text{Leb}}_2([0,1] \times \Theta)$ has uniform marginal on $[0,1]$ with disintegrations $\rho(\cdot|s)$.
- **Neural PDE:** Token evolution satisfies the continuity equation (4), which for discrete tokens reduces to coupled ODEs (3). The flow map $\Lambda_\rho$ satisfies the Banach-space ODE $d\Lambda/ds = A_{\rho(\cdot|s)}(\Lambda)$ in $C^0(S, \mathbb{R}^d)$ (8), with well-posedness via Carathéodory theory (Theorem 9, Appendix C).

### 2. Gradient Flow via Adjoint Sensitivity (Section 2.3)

- **Training risk:** $L(\rho) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=1}^N \ell^j(x^j(1))$ over dataset $\{(\mu^{(j)}, x_j)\}$.
- **Conditional Optimal Transport:** The COT metric $W^{\text{COT}}_2$ on $\mathcal{P}^{\text{Leb}}_2([0,1] \times \Theta)$ enforces layer-wise structure (parameters at different depths do not mix during training).
- **Adjoint variables (Definition 1):** Measure-valued functions $m_\rho^{(j)} \in C^0([0,1], M_d(S^j))$ satisfying backward ODEs (17), acting as the continuous-depth limit of backpropagation.
- **First-order perturbation (Theorem 1):** $\frac{d}{dt}L(\rho_t) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_j \int_0^1 \int_\Theta \langle m_{\rho_t}^{(j)}(s), D_\theta A^j_\theta(\Lambda_{\rho_t}^j(s)) \cdot v_t(s)\rangle\,d\rho_t(\theta,s)$.
- **Gradient flow equation (Definition 2):** $\partial_t \rho_t - \text{div}_\theta(\rho_t \nabla L[\rho_t]) = 0$ with $\nabla L[\rho](s,\theta) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_j D_\theta A^j_\theta(\Lambda^j_\rho(s))^* m_\rho^{(j)}(s)$.
- **Existence (Theorem 5):** Via Minimizing Movements scheme, showing $\tau$-lower-semicontinuity of $L$ and $|\nabla L|$ on $W^{\text{COT}}_2$-bounded sets using Arzelà–Ascoli compactness.
- **Uniqueness (Theorem 6):** Via local Lipschitz regularity of adjoint variables (Lemma 9) and the gradient field (Lemma 10), combined with Grönwall.
- **Extension to unbounded $V$ (Theorem 7):** By controlling support growth of the $V$-marginal over finite time horizons (Lemma 11).

### 3. Convergence via NTK Analysis (Section 2.4–3)

- **Neural Tangent Kernel (Definition 4):** $K[\rho](s)(m,m) = \int_\Theta \|\sum_j D_\theta A^j_\theta(\Lambda^j_\rho(s))^* m^{(j)}\|^2 d\rho(\theta|s)$, with smallest eigenvalue $\lambda_{\min}$.
- **$V$-partial NTK (Definition 5):** $K_1[\rho](s)$ captures only the $V$-derivative component, providing a lower bound $K \geq K_1$.
- **P-L inequality (Proposition 4):** $\|\nabla L[\rho]\|_{L^2(\rho)}^2 \geq CN^{-1}(\int_0^1 \lambda_{\min}(K[\rho](s))\,ds)\,L(\rho)$.
- **Local convergence (Theorem 4):** If $L(\rho_0)$ is sufficiently small relative to $\lambda_0 = \int_0^1 \lambda_{\min}(K_1[\rho_0](s))\,ds$, then $L(\rho_t) \leq L(\rho_0)e^{-CN^{-1}\lambda_0 t}$.
- **NTK injectivity ↔ independence of cumulant generating functions (Proposition 8):** Under FixUp initialization ($V=0$), injectivity of the $V$-NTK map is equivalent to the weak independence property: $\{g_{\mu^{(j)}}(q) = \log \int e^{\langle q,y\rangle} d\mu^{(j)}(y)\}$ are linearly independent modulo affine functions.
- **Strong independence (Definition 6):** A stronger condition on directional cumulant generating functions $g_{\mu,e}$ that implies weak independence and is verified for:
  - Discrete distributions satisfying distinctness condition (eq. 35), verified almost surely for i.i.d. samples (Proposition 10).
  - Uniform distributions on cubes with distinct radii.
  - Symmetric multivariate Laplace distributions with distinct covariance matrices (Proposition 11).
  - Two-component Gaussian mixtures with distinct separation parameters.
  - All distributions stabilized under Gaussian convolution (Proposition 12).

## Results & Highlights

- **Proposition 1 (Forward well-posedness):** The forward pass through infinitely deep transformers admits a unique solution, with token evolution characterized by flow maps satisfying ODEs in $C^0(S,\mathbb{R}^d)$.
- **Theorem 1 (Gradient formula):** The conditional Wasserstein gradient of the training risk has an explicit form via adjoint variables satisfying backward ODEs — the continuous-depth analogue of backpropagation.
- **Theorems 2–3 (Gradient flow existence & uniqueness):** Gradient flow curves exist and are unique in the COT metric space. Equivalence is established between the continuity equation formulation and the variational (curve of maximal slope) characterization.
- **Proposition 8 (NTK injectivity characterization):** The injectivity of the attention NTK is equivalent to linear independence of log-sum-exp (cumulant generating) functions modulo affine functions. This is the central technical result, providing both necessary and sufficient conditions.
- **Theorem 4 (Local linear convergence):** Under NTK injectivity at initialization and small initial loss, gradient flow converges at an explicit linear rate $O(e^{-CN^{-1}\lambda_0 t})$ to a global minimizer, with no spurious local minima. The rate depends explicitly on the NTK conditioning $\lambda_0$ and sample size $N$.
- **Practical token distributions:** The NTK injectivity condition is verified for discrete distributions (a.s. for i.i.d. samples from absolutely continuous measures), uniform distributions on cubes, Laplace distributions, and Gaussian mixtures — covering practically relevant initialization scenarios.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: High.** The dual mean-field formulation (over both tokens and parameters) is new. The observation that transformer training = neural PDE control (vs. ResNet = neural ODE control) is conceptually important and non-trivial. The NTK injectivity characterization (Proposition 8) is a clean algebraic result with no direct prior analogue.
- **Rigor: Very high.** Every major claim is a formal theorem with detailed proof (50-page paper, ~25 pages of appendices). The well-posedness proofs use careful Grönwall estimates, Arzelà–Ascoli compactness, and Bochner integration theory. The NTK analysis connects to classical results on moment generating functions.
- **Reproducibility: Moderate (as a theory paper).** The results are mathematical proofs that can be verified independently. However, no experimental validation is provided — the gap between the mean-field limit and finite architectures remains unquantified empirically.
- **Significance: High for theory, moderate for practice.** The results provide the most rigorous training theory for deep transformers to date. However, the FixUp initialization ($V=0$), infinite depth/width limits, and small-initial-loss condition are far from standard practice (e.g., Adam optimizer, LayerNorm, large initial loss).
- **Red flags:** (1) The $K=I_d$ simplification removes the key matrix from attention, which may affect expressivity claims. (2) The supplementary bias $q$ is crucial for NTK injectivity but non-standard. (3) The Polyak–Łojasiewicz analysis requires checking NTK positivity only at initialization, but the small-loss threshold $L(\rho_0) < CN^{-3}\lambda_0^3$ may be impractically restrictive. (4) No discussion of how the mean-field limit connects to finite-width/depth architectures via quantitative approximation bounds.

## Strengths

- First rigorous mean-field training framework specifically designed for transformers, with the key insight that attention coupling transforms the ODE structure (ResNets) into a PDE structure (transformers).
- The flow-map perspective (viewing transformers as ODEs on $C^0(S,\mathbb{R}^d)$ rather than PDEs on measure spaces) provides superior regularity: quadratic Lipschitz dependence vs. exponential for the direct PDE formulation.
- Clean and complete NTK injectivity theory: necessary and sufficient conditions (Proposition 8), verified for diverse practical token distributions, with stability under Gaussian convolution.
- The results are independent of the number of tokens — a significant advantage over prior work [Song et al. 2024, Wu et al. 2023] whose convergence guarantees depend on token count.
- The COT (conditional optimal transport) framework naturally enforces the layer structure during training, preventing parameter mixing across depths.

## Limitations

- **Infinite limits:** The mean-field regime (infinite depth and width) is an idealization. No quantitative bounds on how large depth/width must be for the theory to apply.
- **Small initial loss:** The convergence result requires $L(\rho_0) < CN^{-3}\lambda_0^3$, which may be impractically small. Standard random initialization produces large initial loss.
- **FixUp initialization:** The NTK analysis assumes $V=0$ at initialization, which is non-standard and may affect training efficiency in practice.
- **No experimental validation:** The paper is purely theoretical with no experiments on synthetic or real data to validate the predictions.
- **Simplified attention:** Fixing $K=I_d$ removes the key matrix and adding bias $q$ is non-standard. The impact on practical expressivity is unclear.
- **Accessibility:** The mathematical machinery (Wasserstein gradient flows, Bochner integrals, Carathéodory ODEs in Banach spaces, adjoint sensitivity analysis) is highly specialized, limiting the audience.

## Relevance

本文将最优传输和 PDE 理论应用于 Transformer 训练动力学的严格分析，建立了首个同时处理无限深度和无限宽度的训练理论框架。NTK 可注入性的代数刻画为理解注意力机制的优化景观提供了新视角，对研究大规模 Transformer 的训练稳定性和收敛性有重要理论参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can quantitative finite-approximation bounds be established, i.e., how many layers and heads suffice for the mean-field predictions to be accurate within $\varepsilon$?
- Does the NTK injectivity condition hold for real-world token distributions (e.g., embeddings from pretrained language models) that are high-dimensional, heavy-tailed, and exhibit clustering structure?
- Can the PDE perspective inspire new practical training algorithms — e.g., PDE-aware learning rate schedules, or architectures that respect the neural PDE structure more faithfully?
- What is the optimization landscape under large-initial-loss regimes? Does gradient descent still converge in practice even when the PL condition is not satisfied?
- Can the framework be extended to decoder-only (causal) transformers with masked attention, or to architectures with normalization layers (LayerNorm, RMSNorm)?
- The supplementary bias $q$ is crucial for NTK injectivity — does this suggest a practical benefit of adding query biases to attention layers?
