Key Finding

The Euclidean assumption in activation steering is fundamentally wrong.

The true local geometry of transformer activations is the pullback Fisher metric — deviating from Euclidean by >97 % in relative spectral norm, with an effective dimensionality of only 2–17 % of the ambient space. FishBack derives the correct closed-form steering direction from this geometry.

Methodology

The paper develops a differential-geometric framework for activation steering. The natural geometry of the activation space is induced by the model's own output distribution, not assumed Euclidean.

1. Pullback Fisher Metric

The Fisher information metric of the softmax output layer is pulled back through the Jacobian of subsequent transformer layers, defining a Riemannian metric on the intermediate activation space.

2. Closed-Form Steering Direction

From this metric, the authors derive the minimum-distortion steering direction — the direction that maximally changes the output probability of a target concept while minimizing changes to the overall output distribution. This yields an analytically tractable, iterative procedure.

3. Recursive Decomposition

The pullback metric admits a layer-wise recursive structure, enabling efficient computation and revealing how information geometry transforms across layers.

4. Unification of Existing Methods

CAA, ActAdd, ITI, and others each implicitly approximate the true Fisher metric in different ways. Their performance differences are predicted by how well their implicit metric matches the true pullback Fisher metric.

Results & Highlights
  • Metric deviation: The pullback Fisher metric deviates from Euclidean by >97 % in relative spectral norm on GPT-2, with effective dimensionality of only 2–17 % of the ambient space — confirming that Euclidean steering operates largely in irrelevant directions.
  • Off-target KL reduction: Iterative pullback steering achieves 1.3×–2.5× reduction in off-target KL divergence compared to Euclidean gradient ascent, and 1.5× reduction compared to CAA at matched concept probability.
  • Consistent improvement: Results hold across three verb-morphology concepts and four different layers in GPT-2.
  • Spectral diagnostic: A single spectral ratio (implicit metric cost / Fisher-optimal cost) quantitatively predicts the performance gaps between existing methods.
Critical Assessment
Novelty: High Rigor: High Significance: High Reproducibility: Mod–High

The application of pullback Fisher information geometry to activation steering is novel and provides a principled theoretical foundation that was previously missing. The unification of existing methods under a single geometric framework is a significant conceptual contribution. Closed-form derivations, spectral analysis, and rigorous evaluation on GPT-2 support the claims.

Strengths vs. Limitations
Strengths
  • Principled theoretical foundation replacing ad hoc Euclidean assumptions with correct information geometry.
  • Unifies multiple existing methods under a single framework with clear explanatory power.
  • Closed-form solution avoids data-driven geometry estimation or manifold fitting.
  • Spectral diagnostic offers a practical tool for understanding and predicting method performance.
Limitations
  • Evaluation limited to GPT-2 and verb-morphology concepts — generalization to larger models and diverse steering tasks is unclear.
  • Computational overhead of pullback Fisher metrics (Jacobian computation, spectral decomposition) at each layer is not analyzed.
  • The 2–17 % effective dimensionality finding's implications for scaling and efficiency are unexplored.
  • Abstract-only review: full details of recursive decomposition and iterative procedure could not be verified.
Questions for Further Exploration
  1. How does the computational cost of computing pullback Fisher metrics scale with model size (e.g., LLaMA-7B, GPT-3 scale)?
  2. Does the 2–17 % effective dimensionality finding generalize to larger models, or does it change qualitatively?
  3. Can FishBack be applied to more complex steering tasks such as safety alignment, factual editing, or style transfer?
  4. What is the relationship between the pullback Fisher metric and other geometric analyses of transformer representations (e.g., anisotropy, intrinsic dimensionality estimates)?
  5. Could the spectral diagnostic be used as an automated tool to select the best layer and method for a given steering task?
