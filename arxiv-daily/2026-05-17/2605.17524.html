Coordinate Heterogeneity Governs Binary Quantization

From InfoNCE to Recall — a single-author theory paper that reveals the hidden structure behind why binary quantization works on contrastive-learning embeddings, unifying two opposing system designs under one framework.

arxiv.org/abs/2605.17524 ↗ Wenxuan Xiao · Changsha University
binary-quantization contrastive-learning approximate-nearest-neighbor vector-search scaling-law
Key Finding
Coordinate heterogeneity — non-uniform per-coordinate variances — is the single quantity governing BQ quality. Off-diagonal covariance contributes 30–50% of ranking signal.
Key Metric
Scaling law predicts fidelity with LOO-R² = 0.889. Full-Σ predictions match empirical F within ±0.02 (explanation ratio 97–105%).
Why This Matters
First principled guide for choosing between rotation-based (RaBitQ) and coordinate-preserving (QuIVer) BQ strategies — no more guesswork in vector search design.
Critical Edge
Requires coordinate Gaussianity from InfoNCE training. Fails for supervised or hand-crafted features. Single author — theoretical claims need broader verification.

Methodology

A theory chain resting on Assumption 1 (coordinate Gaussianity), justified by Betser et al. (2026)'s proof that InfoNCE induces asymptotically Gaussian coordinates.

1. Fidelity / G Decomposition

Search recall decomposes into ranking fidelity F (quantization noise) and semantic margin G (data-dependent signal). Theory analyzes F; treats G as given.

2. Stein's Lemma Analysis

Under Gaussian prior, Cov(sign(Z), inner products) admits closed form. The full covariance matrix Σ — not just diagonal variances — determines fidelity.

3. Rotation Analysis

Haar-random rotation uniformizes coordinate variances (max deviation ≈ ∥Σ∥op · log D / D). Helps linear correctors, destroys implicit importance weighting.

4. Magnitude Bit & Scaling

2-bit BQ (sign + magnitude threshold) strictly superior to 1-bit. Fidelity predicted from three covariance statistics yielding a two-parameter model.

Results & Highlights

  • Full-Σ predictions match empirical F within ±0.02; diagonal-only underestimates by 0.20–0.36
  • 2-bit BQ: fidelity +0.049 to +0.132, recall +0.110 to +0.210 across all datasets
  • Rotation is context-dependent: +307% recall on GIST vs. −0.5pp on Cohere
  • Local fidelity in graph neighborhoods exceeds global by 1.2–4.1×
  • GIST-960 produces F = 1.037, correctly flagging Gaussian prior violation
  • All contrastive models: QQ-plot R² ≥ 0.9959, norm CV ≤ 0.09

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Elegant unification of QuIVer vs. RaBitQ under one framework
  • Surprising finding: off-diagonal covariance = 30–50% of signal
  • Practical design guide: rotate for linear-corrected BQ, preserve axes for Hamming BQ
  • Scaling law enables model selection without search experiments
  • Thorough validation with proper negative controls

Limitations

  • Fails for supervised or hand-crafted features
  • Covariance matrix access may be expensive for very large corpora
  • Sharper O(D) graph navigation bound empirically unverified
  • Single-author — extensive theoretical claims need community verification
  • Limited exploration of non-contrastive training objectives

Questions for Further Exploration

Can the framework extend to non-InfoNCE objectives (masked LM, autoregressive losses) by characterizing their induced coordinate distributions?
Does the heterogeneity framework apply to higher-bit quantization (4-bit, 8-bit) or is it specific to the extreme 1–2 bit regime?
Could the scaling law be integrated as a loss term during embedding training to explicitly optimize for BQ-friendly representations?
What happens for multi-modal embeddings (e.g., CLIP) where two encoders may induce different covariance structures?
Can local fidelity amplification in graph search be exploited for adaptive bit-width schemes — 2-bit near nodes, 1-bit elsewhere?
