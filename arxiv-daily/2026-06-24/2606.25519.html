Quantization Inflates Reasoning: Token Inflation as a Hidden Cost of Low-Bit Reasoning Models

arXiv: 2606.25519

Date: 2026-06-24

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25519

一句话总结： 本文发现低比特(INT4/INT3)后训练量化会让推理模型即使保持最终答案正确率，也会生成更长的思维链(CoT)，作者提出"CoT Token Inflation Ratio(CTIR)"度量这一隐藏的测试时计算开销，并证明该膨胀(小模型INT3下平均CTIR高达276%)会侵蚀量化的每token加速、甚至恶化端到端延迟，而推理时控制(重复惩罚/简洁提示)只是用精度换长度，只有量化感知训练(QAT)才能实现精度-长度的帕累托改进。
TL;DR: Standard quantization evals report accuracy and per-token latency, but those metrics hide a real cost for reasoning models: INT4/INT3 post-training quantization makes models (Qwen3-4B, Phi-4-Reasoning-Plus, Qwen3-30B-A3B) generate substantially longer chains-of-thought even when accuracy is preserved. The authors introduce the CoT Token Inflation Ratio (CTIR) and show inflation reaches +276% (Qwen3-4B AWQ-INT3), produces more repetitive reasoning steps, and erases quantization's serving speedup (a 1.1x CTIR adds 33% p90 time-to-first-token). Inference-time knobs (repetition penalty, conciseness prompts) only trade accuracy for length; only quantization-aware training (DiscQuant) gives a Pareto improvement. Worth reading if you deploy, evaluate, or benchmark quantized reasoning LLMs.

核心贡献

  • 揭示量化对推理模型的"隐藏成本"： 在数学推理、代码生成、科学问答、智能体工具调用四类任务上，INT4/INT3 后训练量化(PTQ)常能保持最终答案准确率，却让模型生成更长的思维链，从而抵消甚至逆转每token的加速收益（如 Qwen3-4B 在 BBH 上 BF16 285s vs INT4 292s，尽管 W4A16 Marlin 内核在 batch 1-16 下有 1.2-1.4x 的 tokens/s 提速）。
  • 提出量化指标 CoT Token Inflation Ratio (CTIR)： 定义并形式化了量化模型相对全精度参考模型的平均CoT长度相对增量(见公式)，暴露出传统量化评估(只报精度+每token延迟)的盲区——推理token用量应与精度一并上报。
  • 刻画 token 膨胀的行为成因与真实服务代价： 膨胀表现为更多中间推理步与更高的步级语义重复(用 Qwen3-Embedding-4B 计算余弦相似度的重复率指标量化)，并直接抬高用户感知的 time-to-first-token、拉长KV缓存驻留、压低连续批处理吞吐(1.1x CTIR 使 p90 TTFT 从 1050s 升至 1400s，吞吐从 0.055 降至 0.047 req/s)。
  • 系统评估缓解策略并给出工程结论： 推理时控制(重复惩罚、简洁提示)只是精度-长度的权衡且不稳定(no restate 让 INT4 CoT 减30%但精度90%→81%)；校准数据选择(自生成的 Math-CoT 优于 Wikitext/C4)是低成本手段；唯有量化感知训练(DiscQuant)在精度与长度两轴同时占优(68%精度@9.5k token，逼近 BF16 的 73%@8.5k)，是唯一帕累托改进方向。

Key Contributions

  • Identifies "reasoning-token inflation" as a hidden test-time-compute cost of low-bit PTQ on SOTA reasoning models (4B–30B, dense and MoE): quantized models frequently preserve final-answer accuracy while emitting longer CoTs, offsetting expected per-token speedups.
  • Introduces the CoT Token Inflation Ratio (CTIR), a metric comparing average reasoning length between a quantized model and its BF16 reference, averaged across benchmarks. CTIR exposes an evaluation gap missed by accuracy+per-token-latency protocols.
  • Analyzes the behavioral anatomy of inflation and its serving impact: inflation appears as more intermediate steps and greater step-level semantic repetition; it raises user-facing time-to-first-token, extends KV-cache residency, and cuts continuous-batching throughput (measured on vLLM, H100, batch 64).
  • Evaluates mitigation strategies across three cost tiers: inference-time controls (repetition penalty, conciseness prompts) trade accuracy for brevity inconsistently; reasoning-domain calibration data (Math-CoT) is a cheap win; quantization-aware training (DiscQuant) is the only Pareto-dominant option, improving both accuracy and token usage at the cost of one-time retraining.

Methodology

  • Models (3 architectures, dense + MoE): Qwen3-4B-Thinking-2507, Phi-4-Reasoning-Plus (14B), Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking (MoE).
  • Benchmarks: Math — AIME25 (full 30 q), GSM8K; Code — LiveCodeBench (LCB), MBPP; Science/broad — GPQA-Diamond, BBH; general baselines — HellaSwag, MMLU-Pro, PIQA; agentic — BFCLv4-Multiturn. Each benchmark sampled at 100 questions (seed 42).
  • Inference: All outputs generated with vLLM at temperature 0 to remove sampling variance and isolate quantization effects on generation trajectory.
  • Quantization (weight-only PTQ): RTN (zero-cost baseline), GPTQ (second-order reconstruction), AWQ (activation-aware), HQQ (calibration-free), ParoQuant (pairwise Givens-rotation). Applied at INT4 and INT3 with group size 128; BF16 as full-precision reference throughout.
  • CTIR definition: Given CoT length ℓ_cot(M,x)=|tok(y_cot)|, dataset average ℓ̄(M;D), then CTIR(M_q;D) = (ℓ̄(M_q;D) − ℓ̄(M_org;D)) / ℓ̄(M_org;D) × 100%.
  • Step-level repetition analysis: Each CoT step embedded with Qwen3-Embedding-4B; a step is "repeated" if any pairwise cosine similarity ≥ τ; repetitiveness = fraction of repeated steps.
  • Serving measurements: Qwen3-30B-A3B on H100, 1k-token prompt, batch 64, controlling CoT budget to isolate length effects on TTFT and throughput.
  • Mitigation study: (i) repetition_penalty r ∈ {1.1…1.4}; (ii) conciseness prompts ("do not think", "few tokens", "no restate"); (iii) calibration corpora {ultrachat_200k, wikitext, c4, OpenR1-Math-220k (Math-CoT), codeforces-cots (Code-CoT), the latter two self-generated by BF16}; (iv) QAT via DiscQuant.

Results & Highlights

  • INT4 PTQ broadly preserves accuracy; reasoning tasks are far more sensitive than general tasks. Most (model, quantizer, benchmark) INT4 cells show negligible (<1pt) or small (1–2pt) drops; reasoning-heavy tasks (esp. AIME25) and smaller models degrade more. INT3 widens the reasoning/general gap.
  • Token inflation is widespread and scales with quantization noise. INT4 gives mild but measurable CTIR; INT3 is dramatic:
  • Qwen3-4B AWQ-INT3 avg CTIR +276.1%, HQQ-INT3 +292.1% (up to +1051% on MBPP under HQQ-INT3).
  • Qwen3-30B INT3: AWQ +32.8%, HQQ +60.1% — substantial but smaller, showing larger models are more resilient.
  • GPTQ (reconstruction-based) consistently yields smaller CTIR than AWQ/HQQ, better preserving the BF16 trajectory.
  • Accuracy preservation does not imply end-to-end speedup. Qwen3-4B on BBH: 285s (BF16) vs 292s (INT4) despite the Marlin W4A16 kernel delivering 1.2–1.4× tokens/s.
  • Agentic workloads inflate tokens without changing tool-use. On BFCLv4-Multiturn, GPTQ-INT4 raises Qwen3-4B CoT tokens +13.3% while tool calls move only 7.56→7.85; GPTQ-INT3 raises Qwen3-30B CoT +23.5% with tool calls essentially flat (7.44→7.46). End-to-end latency increase stems from extra reasoning tokens, not more tool calls.
  • Serving penalty: a 1.1× CTIR raises p90 TTFT from 1050s→1400s (+350s, +33%) and drops throughput 0.055→0.047 req/s.
  • Mitigation outcomes:
  • Repetition penalty reduces CoT length but with clear accuracy cost (delicate, can suppress useful reasoning).
  • Conciseness prompts are unstable: "no restate" cuts INT4 MBPP CoT −30% but accuracy 90%→81%; INT3 prompts cause 5–11pt accuracy drops.
  • Calibration data matters a lot: Math-CoT vs Wikitext raises accuracy 28%→65% and cuts CoT 30k→12k tokens; reasoning-domain corpora beat generic text on both axes.
  • QAT (DiscQuant) is the only Pareto improvement: 68% accuracy @ 9.5k avg CoT tokens, outperforming every PTQ config on both axes (vs BF16 73% @ 8.5k).

  • The mix of ML-systems researchers and industry serving experts lends strong credibility to the serving-cost analysis (vLLM, TTFT, throughput, continuous batching).
  • Rigorous experimental protocol: fixed temperature-0 decoding to remove sampling variance, consistent model/quantizer grid, full AIME25 set, explicit BF16 reference throughout.
  • Results align with and extend recent quantization-for-reasoning work (Li et al. 2025; Liu et al. 2025) but focus on a deployment-critical metric (token usage) those papers under-examine.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty — High (concept) / Medium (technique). The framing of "quantization inflates reasoning length" and the CTIR metric are genuinely fresh and practically important; it is a measurement/analysis paper, not a new algorithm. The QAT conclusion piggybacks on DiscQuant rather than proposing a new QAT method.
  • Rigor — Strong. Controlled temp-0 generation, multi-model (dense+MoE) multi-quantizer grid, serving measurements on real hardware (H100, vLLM, batch 64), and a quantitative repetition metric tied to an embedding model. Confidence is high that the core phenomenon is real.
  • Reproducibility — Good. Models are open (Qwen3, Phi-4), quantizers are standard (GPTQ/AWQ/HQQ/ParoQuant), inference is vLLM; calibration corpora are public or self-generated. CTIR and repetition formulas are explicit. Minor gaps: the repetition threshold τ value and exact prompt wording are in appendices (not seen here); not all appendix hyperparameters are in the main text.
  • Significance — High for deployment/benchmarking practice. Directly argues (with evidence) that quantized-reasoning-model evals should report token usage — a likely-to-be-adopted recommendation. Practitioners who deploy reasoning models should read it before trusting INT4 "free speedup" claims.
  • Red flags / caveats:
  • Sample sizes are small (100 questions per benchmark, 30 for AIME25), so per-benchmark CTIR numbers are noisy; the headline 276%/292% averages rest partly on a few benchmarks (e.g., MBPP shows >1000% under HQQ-INT3, dominating means).
  • Some extreme CTIR values coincide with models already in failure mode (e.g., Qwen3-4B AWQ-INT3 accuracy is 20% on AIME25), so "inflation at preserved accuracy" and "inflation under collapse" are sometimes conflated; the paper mitigates this with same-correctness instance analysis but headline averages don't fully separate the two regimes.
  • Weight-only PTQ only — no weight+activation or KV-cache quantization in the main study, so the effect size for full W8A8/W4A4 pipelines is unaddressed.
  • QAT (DiscQuant) result is shown on Qwen3-4B only; whether QAT Pareto-improvement holds at 30B/MoE is not demonstrated.
  • The vLLM TTFT numbers (1050s–1400s) are unusually large in absolute terms, suggesting a specific long-output/high-load regime; relative conclusions are sound but absolute latency magnitudes may not generalize to typical deployments.

Strengths

  • Frames a previously under-measured, deployment-critical phenomenon with a clean, adoptable metric (CTIR).
  • Connects a low-level effect (quantization noise) all the way up to serving KPIs (TTFT, throughput, KV-cache residency) — end-to-end story is compelling.
  • Honest about mitigation trade-offs: explicitly states that prompting/sampling are "inconsistent" and that only QAT is Pareto-dominant.
  • Covers dense and MoE models across 4B–30B, multiple quantizers, and both INT4 and INT3.
  • Actionable recommendation: report reasoning-token usage alongside accuracy — easy for the community to adopt.

Limitations

  • Small per-benchmark sample sizes (100, or 30 for AIME25) inflate variance; extreme per-task CTIRs can dominate averages.
  • Headline inflation numbers sometimes mix "accuracy-preserved" and "accuracy-collapsed" regimes.
  • Scope is weight-only PTQ; activation/KV-cache quantization and W8A8/W4A4 untested in main results.
  • QAT Pareto claim validated on one small model only; no QAT result at 30B/MoE.
  • Serving numbers are from one hardware/stack/regime (H100, vLLM, batch 64, long outputs); absolute latencies may not generalize.
  • Does not propose a new quantizer or QAT method — strictly diagnostic.

Relevance

对关注大模型推理成本控制与高效部署的读者高度相关：它指出在评估/部署量化推理模型时必须把"思维链token用量"纳入指标，否则会高估量化的实际加速，这正是高效推理与test-time-compute研究的直接交集。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Does token inflation persist (or shrink) under weight+activation / KV-cache quantization (W4A4, W8A8), or under higher batch sizes / shorter-output regimes?
  • Can a QAT method be designed that specifically penalizes trajectory-length drift (not just task loss) to suppress inflation more aggressively than DiscQuant?
  • Is CTIR correlated with a measurable shift in attention/calibration statistics that could be detected without running full benchmarks — enabling a cheap inflation predictor?
  • Does inflation behave the same in instruction-tuned (non-reasoning) models, or is it specific to RL/CoT-trained reasoning models?
  • How do the small sample-size variances affect the 276%/292% headlines; would a 500–1000-question eval change the ranking of quantizers by CTIR?
  • For agentic workloads, does multi-turn inflation compound over rounds in a way that further penalizes end-to-end task completion?
