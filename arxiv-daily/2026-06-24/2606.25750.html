RAS: Measuring LLM Safety Through Refusal Alignment

arXiv: 2606.25750

Date: 2026-06-24

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25750

一句话总结： 本文提出 SafeVec 白盒评测流程，从安全对齐参考模型中逐层提取"拒绝方向"，通过测量目标模型隐藏状态与该方向的余弦对齐程度，并经家族特异性校准映射为 0–100 的 RAS 分数；在 Llama/Gemma/Qwen 上能区分对齐、未审查与消融模型、与攻击成功率(ASR)趋势一致，且平均比判官式评测快约 210 倍，但仅适用于可获取隐藏状态的开权重模型。
TL;DR: A white-box safety metric (RAS) that measures LLM safety from internal hidden states rather than generated outputs. SafeVec extracts layer-wise "refusal directions" from a safety-aligned reference model, selects a stable layer window, and scores a target model by how strongly its hidden states align with that refusal direction under unsafe/jailbreak prompts, mapping raw cosine similarities to a calibrated 0–100 score. Across Llama-3.1-8B, Gemma-3-4B, and Qwen2.5-7B, RAS cleanly separates aligned vs. uncensored/abliterated variants, tracks output-level attack success rate, and runs ~210× faster than judge-based evaluation — but only works for open-weight models with hidden-state access and is calibrated per family, not as a strict per-model ASR clone.

核心贡献

  • 提出 SafeVec：一种基于表征层的白盒 LLM 安全评测流程，从安全对齐参考模型逐层提取"拒绝方向"(refusal direction = μ_unsafe − μ_safe 归一化)，不再依赖输出/判官。
  • 提出 RAS (Refusal Alignment Score)：通过家族特异性校准(坏锚点尺度 ba、严重度斜率 α、架构乘子 γa、sigmoid 映射)把原始余弦相似度映射为可比较的 0–100 安全分数。
  • 设计稳定层窗口选择准则(基于 unsafe 余弦均值、safe 抑制分数与 separation gap)，并为 Llama(22–30)、Gemma(27–29)、Qwen(22–26) 分别选定窗口。
  • 在三大模型族上验证：RAS 能区分对齐/未审查/abliterated 模型，与 ASR 趋势一致，跨族可泛化，且平均快约 210 倍(单次目标评分约 14 秒 vs 判官式约 2969 秒)。
  • 明确将 RAS 定位为行为评测的"补充"而非替代：衡量的是模型"内部是否进入拒绝状态"，而非是否最终拒绝。

Key Contributions

  • SafeVec, a white-box, representation-level safety evaluation procedure that extracts per-layer refusal directions from a safety-aligned reference model and scores targets by hidden-state alignment instead of generated text.
  • RAS (Refusal Alignment Score), a calibrated 0–100 metric that maps architecture-specific raw cosine similarities into a comparable safety scale using a bad-anchor scale, severity slope, architecture multiplier, and sigmoid transform.
  • A principled layer-window selection criterion (based on unsafe cosine mean, safe-suppression score, and separation gap), yielding family-specific windows (Llama 22–30, Gemma 27–29, Qwen 22–26).
  • Empirical validation across Llama-3.1-8B, Gemma-3-4B, and Qwen2.5-7B showing RAS separates aligned/uncensored/abliterated variants, tracks ASR, generalizes across families with calibration, and is ~210× faster on average.
  • A clear scoping of RAS as a complementary white-box auditing/regression-testing signal rather than a replacement for behavioral evaluation.

Methodology

SafeVec is a five-stage white-box procedure. The evaluator needs hidden-state (residual-stream) access to the target model, plus three prompt sets (safe S, unsafe U, jailbreak J) and a per-family safety-aligned reference model M_ref,a with a small calibration set C_a of aligned/uncensored/abliterated variants.

  1. Refusal direction extraction. For each decoder layer l, feed safe/unsafe prompts into the reference model, take last-token residual-stream activations, compute mean safe/unsafe activations, and define the refusal direction r_l = μ_unsafe_l − μ_safe_l, normalized to r̂_l. Intuition: it captures how the representation shifts when processing unsafe vs. safe requests.
  1. Layer window selection. Not all layers are informative (early layers encode lexical info, late layers affected by readout). Per layer compute the unsafe cosine mean, safe-suppression score q_l = −m_safe_l, and separation gap g_l = m_unsafe_l − m_safe_l. Pick a consecutive stable window W where the gap is large and stable, validated against calibration models (safer models should align more).
  1. Calibration model scoring. For each calibration model compute average cosine alignment on unsafe and jailbreak prompts:

UnsafeScore(M) = mean_{l∈W,u∈U} cos(h_l^M(u), r̂_l) and analogously JailbreakScore(M).

  1. RAS calibration. Combined score s(M) = w_u·UnsafeScore + w_j·JailbreakScore with w_u = w_j = 0.5. Refusal drop d(M) = s_ref − s(M). A bad-anchor scale b_a is estimated (median of high-risk subset with d > 0 and ASR ≥ τ=0.8, or 0.9-quantile fallback). Normalized drop d̃(M) = d(M)/b_a. A per-architecture severity slope α_a and a shrink-controlled multiplier γ_a (λ=0.5) translate normalized drops to ASR-equivalent severity, giving aligned drop d_aligned(M) = γ_a·d̃(M). Finally a sigmoid maps to 0–100: RAS(M) = 100 / (1 + exp(β(d_aligned(M) − c))) with c = 0.75, β = 5.0.
  1. Target model scoring. Apply the same cosine-similarity computation to the target model and pass through the fixed calibration to get RAS.

Key hyperparameters: τ=0.8, q=0.9, w_u=w_j=0.5, c=0.75, β=5.0, λ=0.5. Reference models: meta-llama/Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, google/gemma-3-4b-it, Qwen/Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct. Output-level ASR computed separately on HEx-PHI (330 prompts, 11 categories) using greedy decoding (512 max tokens) and a SORRY-Bench fine-tuned Mistral-7B judge. Hardware: 1× NVIDIA H100 PCIe 80GB, 2× AMD EPYC 7313, 256 GB RAM.

Results & Highlights

  • RQ1 (representation-level separation): Safe and unsafe models separate clearly along the refusal direction in middle-to-late layers; green (safe) curves sit consistently above red (unsafe) curves in the selected windows.
  • RQ2 (correlation with output safety): Raw SafeVec scores already track ASR before calibration; after calibration RAS preserves the trend (e.g., Llama: aligned Instruct UnsafeScore 0.283 / ASR 0.106 vs. abliterated −0.155 / 0.933; Lexi-Uncensored 0.064 / 0.939). Authors note RAS tracks ASR trends at the family level, not a strict per-model ranking.
  • RQ3 (cross-family generalization): Same procedure works across Llama/Gemma/Qwen; calibrated RAS ranks aligned above unsafe variants in all three, though family-specific calibration is required.
  • RQ4 (efficiency): RAS is far faster than judge-based scoring.

  • Documented exception: In Gemma, Viesar/gemma-3-4b-opus-reasoning-distill has lower refusal-alignment than the reference yet a lower ASR (0.076 vs 0.230) — evidence some models achieve output safety via mechanisms not captured by a single reference refusal direction. This is an honest limitation the authors disclose.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: Moderate. The core idea — that refusal is encoded as a direction (Arditi et al. 2024) — is not new; the contribution is repurposing it as an evaluation metric rather than a steering/intervention mechanism. This is a sensible and useful reframing, but it is an incremental application of an existing interpretability result.
  • Rigor: Fair. The pipeline is well-specified with concrete hyperparameters, and the authors provide raw numbers (Tables 1/3/4) alongside ASR. However, the central "strong correlation" claim (RQ2) is supported visually and by rank-ordering, not by a reported correlation coefficient (Pearson/Spearman/τ), confidence intervals, or significance tests. Given small calibration sets (6–8 models per family), this is a notable gap.
  • Reproducibility: Moderate-to-Good. Method is described in enough detail to reimplement; reference models, layer windows, and hyperparameters are all listed. Weaknesses: no code repository linked in the paper, the safe/unsafe/jailbreak prompt sets are described only qualitatively (sizes, provenance, and exact contents not fully given), and several magic constants (τ=0.8, q=0.9, c=0.75, β=5.0, λ=0.5) are asserted without sensitivity analysis.
  • Significance: Moderate. A ~200× faster white-box safety signal is genuinely useful for model auditing and post-training regression testing, but it is explicitly scoped to open-weight models of the same family, so it cannot replace behavioral evals for closed APIs or for high-stakes deployment.
  • Red flags / caveats:
  • Authors themselves state RAS "should be interpreted as a refusal-alignment metric that tracks ASR trends at the family level, rather than as a strict per-model ranking identical to ASR." Read accordingly.
  • A single reference refusal direction is assumed; the documented Gemma exception shows some models use alternative safety mechanisms, so RAS can be misled (false negatives for genuinely-safe-but-differently-aligned models).
  • The abstract's "tracks output-level attack success rate" and "substantially faster" are accurate in spirit, but note a minor inconsistency: text says average "216.88× faster" while Table 2 reports 210.13×.
  • Only mid-size models (4B–8B) are tested; behavior at larger scale and for different alignment recipes (e.g., DPO vs RLHF, reasoning models) is unverified.

Strengths

  • Conceptually clean and well-motivated: reframes safety evaluation from "does it refuse?" to "does it internally enter a refusal state?".
  • Genuinely efficient: single forward pass, no generation, no judge model; ~210× speedup.
  • Honest about scope and exceptions (Gemma counter-example is disclosed rather than hidden).
  • Architecture-aware calibration is a thoughtful design to make raw cosines comparable across families.
  • Practical positioning as a complement/regression-test signal for open-weight auditing is realistic and useful.

Limitations

  • Requires white-box access to residual-stream activations — inapplicable to closed/proprietary APIs.
  • Calibrated within a model family; cross-family comparison quality depends entirely on the calibration set.
  • Measures alignment with a single reference refusal direction; models with different safety mechanisms (alternative directions, multi-vector refusal) can be mis-scored.
  • Small evaluation scale (only 4B–8B models; 6–8 calibration models per family); no larger-scale or reasoning-model validation.
  • No quantitative correlation statistics, sensitivity analysis on hyperparameters, or released code/prompt sets.
  • RAS is a trend metric, not a strict ASR substitute — risks of false confidence in high-stakes deployment.

Relevance

对关注 LLM 安全对齐与可解释性的研究者，本文把"拒绝方向"从干预手段转化为高效的白盒安全度量，提供了可在对齐训练/微调后做快速安全回归测试的实用工具；但其仅适用于同族开权重模型、且与 ASR 仅为趋势级一致，故更适合作为行为评测的加速补充而非权威基准。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • What is the actual Pearson/Spearman correlation between RAS and ASR per family, with confidence intervals, and how does it degrade as model size or alignment recipe (DPO/RLHF/reasoning) changes?
  • Can RAS be extended to multi-vector / multi-category refusal directions (per HEx-PHI category) instead of a single global direction, to reduce the Gemma-style false negatives?
  • How robust is RAS to distribution shift in the unsafe/jailbreak prompt set, and how stale is it relative to new adversarial attack styles?
  • Could a closed-API approximation be built (e.g., via logit/prob proxies or token-level refusal-token probabilities) to recover some of the speed benefit without hidden-state access?
  • How does RAS behave under fine-tuning safety regression — can it detect subtle safety degradation that output-level ASR misses due to benchmark staleness?
