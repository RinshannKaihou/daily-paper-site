> **一句话总结：** SafeGen 是一个结合大语言模型（GPT-5）与形式化验证的框架，利用文档级超知识图谱（HyperKG）从设计与安全文档中抽取可验证规格并自动生成功能安全断言（FSA），通过门级到 RTL 的故障映射支持 stuck-at 和 bridging 故障，实现语义级、可追溯的故障危害度分级，在 FOC 电机控制系统中验证了优于仿真法和已有 LLM 断言生成方案的效果。

> **TL;DR:** SafeGen is an LLM-driven, formal-verification-assisted framework that uses a document-level Hyper Knowledge Graph (HyperKG) enriched with FMEDA guidelines and RTL control-data-flow information to auto-generate Functional Safety Assertions (FSAs), then grades gate-level fault criticality (stuck-at + bridging) via Formal Property Verification, outperforming both traditional simulation and prior LLM-based assertion generators on an FOC motor-drive case study.

## 核心贡献

- **首次用 LLM 生成功能安全断言（FSA）**：构建功能安全感知的 HyperKG，结合 FMEDA 指南与 RTL CDFG，使 LLM 能捕获全局安全/功能语义并生成与规格逐条关联、可追溯的断言。
- **语义级故障危害度评估**：将 LLM 语义推理与形式化验证（FPV）结合，依据"被违反断言所对应规格的重要性"对门级故障进行细粒度、可解释的危害度打分，而非传统的二元 safe/unsafe 分类。
- **规格抽取与重要性赋值**：提出基于多次独立推理（N=15 抽取、M=30 打分）+ 聚类与中位数聚合的统计化流水线，缓解 GPT-5 等推理模型温度不可调带来的不确定性，得到 70 条规格、标准差 < 1 的稳定重要性分数。
- **扩展门级到 RTL 故障映射**：在已有 Boolean-difference MUX 建模基础上新增对 bridging 故障（wired-AND/OR、dominant）的支持，将门级故障效应准确映射至 RTL 信号。
- **数字-物理联合仿真验证平台**：为开源 FOC（磁场定向控制）PMSM 电机驱动系统搭建闭环数字-物理协仿真环境，并自编设计与功能安全文档，作为真实安全关键场景的基准。

## Key Contributions

- **First LLM-based generation of Functional Safety Assertions (FSAs)**: A functional-safety-aware HyperKG (built with HyperGraphRAG and FMEDA guidelines, then merged with the RTL control-data-flow graph) lets the LLM capture both local RTL structure and global safety intent, producing assertions each linked to its specifications for full traceability.
- **Semantic-level fault criticality grading**: Integrates LLM semantic reasoning with Formal Property Verification (FPV) to score each gate-level fault by the cumulative importance of the specifications whose linked assertions are violated — moving beyond the pessimistic binary safe/unsafe classification of structural simulation.
- **Robust specification extraction and importance assignment**: A statistical pipeline of independent multi-round inference (N=15 for extraction with agglomerative clustering, M=30 for scoring with median aggregation) counteracts LLM nondeterminism, yielding 70 specifications with importance-score standard deviations below 1.
- **Extended gate-to-RTL fault mapping**: Augments prior Boolean-difference/MUX fault-injection mapping to handle bridging faults (wired-AND, wired-OR, dominant), in addition to stuck-at faults, at the RTL abstraction.
- **Digital-physical co-simulation validation**: Builds a closed-loop FOC PMSM motor-drive testbench with custom design and functional-safety documents, validating the framework in a realistic ISO 26262-style safety scenario.

## Methodology

**Problem.** Conventional simulation-based fault analysis treats fault criticality as binary (propagates to an output → unsafe), which is overly conservative at the module level: a fault in an auxiliary counter may be flagged unsafe yet have negligible impact on the main control loop, triggering redundant hardening. Functional input stimuli rarely map to circuit functions, so multi-level semantic grading is hard.

**SafeGen pipeline (5 stages):**

1. **Document-level HyperKG construction.** HyperGraphRAG-style hyperedges (capturing n-ary relations among ports, FSMs, safe states, safety levels) are extracted from FMEDA guidelines, safety, and design documents; entities/hyperedges are embedded for vector retrieval and annotated with textual descriptions.

2. **Specification extraction & importance assignment.** RAG over the HyperKG extracts all verifiable functional/performance/interface/safety specs. The LLM runs N=15 times; candidate spec sets are clustered by cosine similarity (threshold 0.95), the dominant cluster's highest-confidence sample is chosen (→ 70 specs). Importance is scored 0–4 over M=30 independent rounds, taking the median; all std devs < 1.

3. **FSA generation.** The document HyperKG is fused with the RTL CDFG (extracted via PyVerilog, aligned by fuzzy string matching) into an enhanced HyperKG. For each signal, BFS context retrieval (hop limit h=5) plus multi-LLM document/signal summarizers feed the LLM, which generates per-signal SystemVerilog Assertions. An LLM then maps assertions → specifications (one-to-many) for semantic tracing.

4. **Gate-to-RTL fault effect mapping.** Boolean differences between fault-free and faulty netlist fan-in cones (ΔOⱼ = O^ff ⊕ O^ft) are inserted as MUXes at RTL outputs/registers. Bridging faults are first converted to logically equivalent gate structures (AND/OR gate for wired-AND/OR; aggressor drives fan-out for dominant) before differencing.

5. **FPV-based criticality grading.** Cadence JasperGold runs FPV on the fault-injected RTL; each fault's violated assertion set maps to an affected specification set, and criticality C_f = Σ Ĩⱼ over affected specs.

**Experimental setup.** Open-source FOC design driving a PMSM (7 pole pairs, 24 V bus, 0.5 Ω, 0.4 mH, ±10% torque-deviation safety threshold). Tools: Synopsys IC Compiler / StarRC / TestMAX for fault lists; Cadence JasperGold (FPV) and Xcelium (simulation). GPT-5 for all LLM tasks. 32-core AMD EPYC 7313 workstation.

## Results & Highlights

- **Assertion quality (Table 1):** SafeGen produced 976 SVAs, 745 syntactically correct, 250 proven — vs AssertLLM (424/313/83) and AssertionForge (667/460/105). Proven-assertion COI coverage reached 83.7% Statement / 83.7% Branch / 83.8% Functional / 84.5% Toggle, beating both baselines (~65–67%).
- **Ablation:** Removing the HyperKG drops SafeGen to 625/459/170 and ~64–67% coverage — confirming the HyperKG's central role.
- **Fault-evaluation assertions:** For internal signals, SafeGen generated 8,541 SVAs (7,243 syntactically correct, 1,748 proven) with 100% Statement / 99.9% Functional / 99.6% Toggle coverage.
- **Fault detection (Table 2):** SafeGen recovers 80.6% of stuck-at and 82.6% of bridging faults flagged by closed-loop functional simulation (the de-facto ground truth), while structural simulation was shown to be overly pessimistic — 3,894 bridging and 34,351 stuck-at faults it labeled unsafe had no system-level impact.
- **Criticality insights:** Highly critical faults cluster in reset logic, PI-controller numeric saturation, and control arithmetic — components essential to closed-loop stability; low-criticality faults affect only timing/observability. Each fault's score is traceable to violated specifications.
- **Runtime (Table 3):** HyperKG build 2 min; spec extraction/scoring 20 min; assertion generation 12.5 h; gate-to-RTL mapping 6.7 h; FPV 3.1 days → total ~3.7 days vs 5.5 days for structural fault simulation (excluding manual testbench prep), with parallelizable LLM/mapping stages.

## Critical Assessment

The core claim — that semantic, specification-linked FPV yields more meaningful (less pessimistic) fault grading than structural simulation — is well supported and intuitively sound: tying faults to violated safety specifications is more actionable than raw output observability. The 80–83% agreement with functional simulation, plus the demonstrated over-pessimism of structural simulation (tens of thousands of false "unsafe" verdicts), is a convincing proof of concept. The HyperKG ablation cleanly shows its value.

However, several caveats temper the conclusions:

- **Single case study.** Validation is limited to one open-source FOC design. Generalization to large industrial SoCs (where COI blow-up is the real bottleneck) is unproven; the FOC design is comparatively small.
- **LLM-dependence and cost.** All results hinge on GPT-5 with 15–30 redundant inference rounds for statistical stability — expensive, non-reproducible across model versions, and effectively an admission that single-shot LLM outputs are unreliable here. No cost/latency breakdown of the LLM API calls is given.
- **Ground-truth circularity.** The "ground truth" functional safety documents are themselves derived from RTL fault simulation (±10% torque threshold), and SafeGen's specs are extracted from those same documents — the evaluation is partially self-referential.
- **FPV convergence.** The paper motivates RTL-level analysis to avoid COI blow-up but does not report FPV runtimes per fault or convergence-failure rates beyond asserting "difficult to converge" assertions were removed (introducing potential selection bias).
- **Bridging-fault extension is incremental.** The stuck-at→bridging generalization follows naturally from the MUX/Boolean-difference machinery of prior work [34].

## Strengths

- Novel and well-motivated: first to use LLMs specifically for *functional-safety* assertion generation, with a clean spec-assertion-fault traceability chain.
- The HyperKG (n-ary relations) is a genuinely better fit than binary KGs for safety documents where behavior depends on joint FSM/protocol/datapath interactions.
- Strong, fair comparison against AssertLLM and AssertionForge with multiple JasperGold-derived metrics and a clean ablation.
- Meaningful efficiency gain (3.7 vs 5.5 days) with parallelizable LLM stages.
- Engineering realism: real EDA flow, digital-physical co-simulation, ISO 26262 framing.

## Limitations

- Validated on a single, relatively small FOC design; scalability to industrial-scale netlists untested.
- Heavy reliance on GPT-5 with many redundant runs raises reproducibility, cost, and model-drift concerns; reasoning-model temperature cannot be tuned, forcing the workaround.
- Functional safety documents (and their ±10% threshold) are author-defined, and specs are extracted from them — partial circularity.
- FPV convergence details and any selection bias from dropping "difficult" assertions are under-reported.
- Open problem: fault coverage is admittedly low (functional, not test-pattern stimuli), so "undetected" faults are assumed unimportant without independent verification.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How does SafeGen scale to a multi-million-gate industrial SoC where FPV COI convergence is the dominant bottleneck — what fraction of faults fail to converge, and is there a principled (non-hand-removal) way to handle them?
2. How sensitive are the 70 extracted specifications and their importance scores to (a) the choice of base LLM (GPT-5 vs open-source models) and (b) HyperKG extraction quality — is there a principled metric for HyperKG completeness?
3. Could the spec-extraction/importance pipeline be cross-validated against an independent FMEDA from a certification body, removing the current self-referential ground-truth concern?
4. What is the dollar/API-cost and token budget of the N=15 + M=30 inference rounds, and could a cheaper consensus scheme (e.g., self-consistency with fewer rounds, or a distilled model) preserve stability?
5. The ±10% torque threshold defines "unsafe"; how robust is the criticality ranking to threshold choice, and can the framework output continuous risk rather than depending on this cutoff?
6. Can the gate-to-RTL bridging-fault mapping be extended to more complex defect models (e.g., open faults, delay faults, or transistor-level shorts) needed for advanced-node safety analysis?
