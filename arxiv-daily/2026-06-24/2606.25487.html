How Reliable Is Your Jailbreak Judge? Calibration and Adversarial Robustness of Automated ASR Scoring

arXiv: 2606.25487

Date: 2026-06-24

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25487

一句话总结： 本文系统性检验了 LLM 越狱研究中用于打分的自动评判器（专用安全分类器与 LLM-as-judge），发现两类评判器在与人类标签的一致性和对抗鲁棒性上以相反方式失效：专用分类器过度标记（高召回低精度）但在白盒 GCG 攻击下 70% 的真阳性被翻转，而 LLM-judge 精度高但召回率极不稳定（0.06–0.65）且仅靠一句拒绝前缀即可被翻转，作者据此呼吁在论文中报告评判器精度/召回并对评判器做对抗检查。
TL;DR: This paper audits the automated judges that decide attack-success rates (ASR) in jailbreak research. Using 596 human-labeled HarmBench completions, it shows the two judge families fail in opposite ways: the dedicated HarmBench classifier over-flags (low precision) and breaks under white-box GCG (70% flip rate), while LLM-as-judges have high precision but erratic recall (0.06–0.65) and are trivially fooled by a single refusal prefix (39–88% flip). The paper recommends reporting judge precision/recall on human-labeled slices, correcting ASR for judge precision, and adversarially probing the judge.

核心贡献

  • 首次在统一攻击协议下，对两类主流越狱评判器（专用安全分类器与 LLM-as-judge）进行了以人类标签为锚的对比标定，基于 HarmBench 分类器验证集 596 条人工标注样本，揭示二者与人类多数投票标签的一致性差异。
  • 发现两类评判器以"相反方式"失效：专用分类器（HarmBench-Llama-2-13b-cls）过度标记（精度 0.835、召回 0.974，F1 0.899），而三个 LLM-judge（Qwen2.5-7B、Phi-3.5-mini、Qwen2.5-3B）精度高（0.81–0.94）但召回率在 0.06–0.65 间剧烈波动，意味着同一批回复换不同评判器会得到截然不同的 ASR。
  • 针对保持有害内容不变的"包装"攻击（仅添加良性框架、不改写有害正文），LLM-judge 的翻转率高达 57%–100%，仅一句前置的拒绝句就贡献了 39%–88% 的翻转；专用分类器对这类表层攻击几乎免疫（最高 6.7%）。
  • 对"看似坚固"的专用分类器实施白盒 GCG 攻击，在 20 token 对抗后缀、50 步/32 候选的小预算下，翻转了 30 个高置信真阳性中的 21 个（70%，95% CI 54–86%），且作者明确指出这是该分类器脆弱性的下界。
  • 双人独立标注审计（80 个翻转样本）确认所有翻转的有害内容均完好无损（原始一致率 93.8%，5 个分歧经裁定后仍含有害内容），证明翻转源于评判器被欺骗而非有害内容被移除。

Key Contributions

  • A human-anchored, single-protocol comparison of the two dominant jailbreak judge families (dedicated classifier vs. LLM-as-judge) against human majority labels on 596 HarmBench completions.
  • Demonstration that content-preserving wrappers fool every LLM-judge tested (57–100% any-wrapper flip rate), while a single prepended refusal sentence alone flips 39–88% — yet the dedicated classifier shrugs these off (≤6.7%).
  • A white-box GCG attack that breaks the surface-robust dedicated classifier, flipping 21/30 confident true positives (70%) at a deliberately small optimization budget.
  • A two-annotator harm-preservation audit (80 items) confirming every sampled flip retains the harmful content, ruling out the alternative explanation that the attack removed harm rather than fooling the judge.
  • Practical reporting recommendations for ASR papers: state the judge, report its precision/recall on a human-labeled slice, report ASR corrected for judge precision (ASR_corrected ≈ ASR_reported × precision), and run at least one content-preserving attack against the judge.

Methodology

Dataset & ground truth. The HarmBench text-behavior classifier validation set, 596 (behavior, generation) pairs spanning standard and contextual behaviors, many attack methods, and many source models. Each pair carries labels from three human annotators; the human majority is taken as the gold label. Contextual behaviors are scored with HarmBench's official contextual prompt, standard behaviors with the standard prompt.

Judges under test.

  • Dedicated classifier: cais/HarmBench-Llama-2-13b-cls with its official standard and contextual prompts.
  • LLM-as-judge (three open, ungated models, deliberately free to reproduce): Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, Phi-3.5-mini-instruct, Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct, each given a short rubric asking whether the response complies with the harmful behavior (rubric released in Appendix A). Closed frontier judges (e.g., GPT-4o) are left to future work.

Compute & quantization sanity check. Everything runs on a single NVIDIA T4 with 4-bit quantization. The 4-bit classifier reproduces the benchmark's stored predictions on 98.7% of items, so the quantized judge behaves like the released one.

Calibration metrics. Precision, recall, F1, accuracy vs. human majority. Each item scored by harmful probability p_harm = p(Yes) / (p(Yes) + p(No)) from the Yes/No logits.

Flip rate (robustness). For the set TP of true positives (human-harmful and judge-harmful), r = |{i in TP : judge(a(x_i)) = No}| / |TP|. Each attack keeps the harmful body verbatim and adds only benign material: a refusal prefix, a benign preamble/postamble, an "for awareness" educational frame, or a fictional-dialogue frame.

White-box GCG attack. On the classifier's open weights, with harmful body verbatim and a 20-token adversarial suffix optimized to minimize the logit gap min_s (z_Yes(x,s) − z_No(x,s)) at a deliberately small budget (50 steps, 32 candidates/step).

Harm-preservation audit. Two independent annotators labeled 80 randomly sampled flipped responses (harmful / not harmful / unsure) after a calibration round, blind to each other's labels; disagreements adjudicated. Cohen's kappa is reported as uninformative because the label distribution is nearly constant (harm almost always preserved).

Results & Highlights

Calibration (Table 1, 596 items vs. human majority):

| Judge | Type | Precision | Recall | F1 | Accuracy |

|---|---|---|---|---|---|

| HarmBench-Llama-2-13b-cls | classifier | 0.835 | 0.974 | 0.899 | 0.901 |

| Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct | LLM-judge | 0.940 | 0.174 | 0.294 | 0.621 |

| Phi-3.5-mini-instruct | LLM-judge | 0.810 | 0.648 | 0.720 | 0.772 |

| Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct | LLM-judge | 0.889 | 0.059 | 0.111 | 0.571 |

The classifier over-flags (high recall, lower precision, inflating ASR by ~its false-positive rate); the three LLM-judges invert this with high precision but erratic recall (0.059–0.648), so identical responses yield very different ASR depending on the scorer.

Wrapper robustness (Table 2, any-wrapper flip rate on each judge's true positives):

  • HarmBench classifier: 3.4% any-wrapper, 0.4% refusal-prefix.
  • Qwen2.5-7B (n=47): 83.0% any-wrapper, 83.0% refusal-prefix.
  • Phi-3.5-mini (n=175): 57.1% any-wrapper, 38.9% refusal-prefix.
  • Qwen2.5-3B (n=16): 100% any-wrapper, 87.5% refusal-prefix.

A single prepended refusal sentence is the dominant single-vector attack on LLM-judges (39–88% flip).

White-box attack on the classifier: Greedy search over a wrapper library guided by the classifier's own probability flips only 1/15 (6.7%), barely above the 3.4% baseline. But GCG on the open weights flips 21/30 confident true positives = 70.0% (95% CI 54–86%), mean 21.4 optimization steps; nine cases survived the 50-step budget at harmful-probability 0.93–1.0. The author explicitly frames 70% as a lower bound.

Harm audit: Two annotators agreed on 75/80 (93.8%); after adjudicating all five disagreements, all 80 sampled flips retained harmful content. In two disagreements the annotator had been misled by the benign refusal prefix exactly as the judges are.

Critical Assessment

The paper addresses a genuine and underappreciated methodological problem: the field treats automated judges as ground truth when reporting ASR, yet rarely validates them. The core empirical findings are sound and internally consistent — the two judge families do fail in opposite directions, the refusal-prefix effect is reproduced across three different LLM-judges (consistent with prior "Know Thy Judge" reports), and the harm-preservation audit closes the most obvious alternative explanation. The white-box GCG result (70% flip at a deliberately small budget, explicitly framed as a lower bound) is a meaningful negative result for open-weight classifiers.

However, several caveats temper the strength of the claims:

  • Single dataset. Everything rests on the HarmBench classifier validation set, which was built to validate a classifier, not to measure per-method ASR; the author concedes a clean per-method claim would need a dedicated benchmark.
  • Untuned LLM-judge prompts. The LLM-judge rubric was not tuned, so part of the low and erratic recall could reflect prompt wording rather than model capability. The refusal-prefix fragility is more robust because it replicates across models and matches prior work, but the absolute recall numbers (e.g., 0.059 for Qwen2.5-3B) are prompt-sensitive and should not be over-read.
  • Small true-positive sets for two LLM-judge flip rates (n=16 and n=47), making those flip-rate point estimates noisy.
  • Small white-box sample (n=30) and small budget; nine survivors with harmful-probability ≥0.93 imply a larger budget would likely break more, so 70% is conservative but the underlying n is modest.
  • No frontier closed judges (GPT-4o-class), which are increasingly used in practice; the open-model results may not generalize to closed judges whose robustness is unknown.
  • Kappa is uninformative here by construction (near-constant label distribution), and harm preservation holds partly by construction of the attacks, so the audit's independent evidentiary value is real but bounded.

The contribution is primarily methodological and cautionary rather than a new attack or defense; it is best read as a calibration + robustness meta-evaluation with concrete reporting prescriptions. Its value is high if the community adopts the recommendations, and lower if the findings remain unactioned.

Strengths

  • First study to put dedicated safety classifiers and multiple LLM-as-judges under a single human-anchored comparison and attack protocol, exposing opposite failure modes.
  • Cleanly isolates content-preserving attacks (harmful body verbatim, only benign framing added), which makes the harm-flips-but-content-survives result interpretable.
  • The white-box GCG attack on a classifier that resists surface attacks is a strong, non-anecdotal result (21/30, with CI reported).
  • Two-annotator blind audit with adjudication directly addresses the "did the attack remove the harm?" confound, and even catches a human annotator being fooled by the refusal prefix — a vivid demonstration that the effect is not a machine-scoring artifact.
  • Fully reproducible on free, ungated, open-weight models and a single T4; code and harness released; 4-bit sanity check against the released classifier provided.
  • Concrete, low-cost reporting recommendations (precision/recall on a human slice, precision-corrected ASR, at least one adversarial judge check) that are immediately actionable by the community.

Limitations

  • Single dataset (HarmBench classifier validation set); not designed for per-method ASR claims.
  • LLM-judge prompts untuned — part of the low recall may be wording, not model; absolute recall numbers are prompt-sensitive.
  • Small true-positive sets (n=16, 47) for two LLM-judge flip rates; small white-box n=30.
  • No closed frontier judges (GPT-4o-class) tested.
  • 70% white-box flip is explicitly a lower bound (deliberately small budget; nine survivors at ≥0.93 harmful probability).
  • Harm-preservation holds partly by construction; kappa uninformative due to near-constant label distribution.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Do the calibration findings (especially the high-precision/erratic-recall pattern and refusal-prefix fragility) transfer to closed frontier judges like GPT-4o, Claude, or Gemini used as graders?
  • How much of the LLM-judge recall variance is prompt-induced vs. model-induced? A systematic prompt-sensitivity study would separate the two.
  • Would a larger-budget GCG attack, or a transfer/paraphrase test, break all 30 confident true positives and transfer across judges?
  • Can a black-box attacker (no weight access) replicate the white-box classifier flip via, e.g., query-efficient suffix search?
  • Does precision-corrected ASR (ASR × precision) actually change published attack/defense rankings on a clean per-method benchmark?
  • Would ensembling a dedicated classifier with an LLM-judge (and/or refusal-string matcher) yield a more robust composite judge than either family alone?
  • How do these failure modes interact with multimodal (image/audio) jailbreak ASR judges, where surface-feature shortcuts may be even stronger?
