Key Finding

Despite arbitrary adaptive rounds and local sample reuse, the complete public transcript's Fisher information decomposes clientwise — as if each client acted independently. This transcript Fisher contraction (Lemma 4.2) is the key innovation that unlocks a unified federated van Trees lower bound.

Why This Matters

Prior lower bounds for DP federated learning were either problem-specific or restricted to non-interactive, single-pass protocols. This work provides the first unified framework covering central DP, local DP, and heterogeneous federated DP as special cases — under the most general interactive model with no restrictions on rounds, adaptivity, or sample reuse. The posterior-independence insight may find applications well beyond this paper.

Critical Edge

  • Restricted to squared ℓ2 loss — classification and testing not covered
  • Focused on zCDP rather than standard (ε,δ)-DP; conversion may lose tightness
  • No matching upper bounds constructed — tightness not proven for all settings
  • Assumes i.i.d. local samples from a common parametric family; heterogeneous distributions not considered
  • Does not address computational constraints or communication costs
  • Public-transcript assumption may be restrictive; extension to private channels not discussed

Methodology

The proof follows a clean three-step architecture under a federated protocol with m clients, T adaptive rounds of public communication, and clientwise sample-level zCDP guarantees:

  1. Likelihood factorization & posterior independence — Transcript likelihood factorizes across clients (Eq. 4), so local datasets are conditionally independent given the transcript (Eq. 6). Unique to public-transcript protocols.
  2. Fisher information decomposition — Score projection gives Tr IG(θ) = Σl Eθ ‖Eθ[Sl | G]‖², with zero cross-client covariance (Eq. 7).
  3. Per-client Fisher contraction — Each term is bounded two ways: ordinary L² contraction (≤ nl Tr Ix) and zCDP contraction (≤ (e2ρl−1) nl² ‖Ix‖op). Minimum of both yields the final bound.

The van Trees inequality then converts the Fisher bound into a minimax risk lower bound. Concrete rates follow by plugging in model-specific Fisher information.

Results

  • Gaussian mean
    ∼ σ² [Σl(d/nl + d²/(ρlnl²))−1]−1
  • Linear regression
    Same rate structure; design covariance enters through constants only
  • Nonparametric (Sobolev α)
    Two-component tradeoff: non-private rate (σ²/(mn))2α/(2α+d) and privacy-driven rate (σ²/(ρmn²))α/(α+d)
  • Special cases
    m=1 → central DP van Trees; nl=1 → local DP scaling; pure ε-DP via ρ = ε²/2

Strengths

  • Handles the most general interactive federated model — no restrictions on rounds, adaptivity, or sample reuse
  • Clean, modular proof separating information-theoretic and privacy arguments
  • Elegant posterior-independence insight with potential applications beyond this paper
  • Broad applicability demonstrated through three canonical problems
  • Unifies central DP, local DP, and federated DP lower bounds in a single framework
  • Clientwise information expression provides clear heterogeneity intuition

Limitations

  • Squared ℓ2 loss only
  • zCDP focus
  • No upper bounds
  • I.I.D. assumption
  • No compute/comm. costs
  • Public transcript only

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can the transcript Fisher contraction extend to approximate (ε,δ)-DP directly, without zCDP conversion, for tighter bounds?
  2. Do existing federated algorithms achieve these rates for linear and nonparametric regression in the general interactive setting?
  3. How do the bounds change under user-level DP (protecting all samples per user) rather than sample-level DP?
  4. Does the posterior independence property (Eq. 6) inform the construction of optimal federated estimators?
  5. Can the framework accommodate heterogeneous local distributions or corrupted clients?
