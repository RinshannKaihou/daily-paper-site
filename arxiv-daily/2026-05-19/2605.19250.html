Causal Evidence for Attention Head Imbalance in Modality Conflict Hallucination

Jinrui Jiang, Zhangtai Wu, Zhen Wu, Xinyu Dai — Nanjing University

2026-05-19 5 models tested 7B–8B params
Key Finding
Hallucination-driving heads are broadly distributed while resisting heads are concentrated — an asymmetric imbalance that causes MLLMs to favor erroneous text premises over visual evidence.
Key Metric
−22.20 pp hallucination reduction on InternVL3 (69.03% → 46.83%) via MACI. Conflict detection AUROC 0.89–0.95.
Why This Matters
First head-level causal evidence explaining why visual evidence fails to prevail in multimodal conflicts — actionable for inference-time hallucination mitigation.
Critical Edge
Driving-head ablation ↓ hallucination. Resisting-head ablation ↑ hallucination. Random-head ablation: no effect. Clean causal proof across five architectures.

Methodology
Driving Heads
Distributed across layers, aggregate weight 1.51× greater. Top-5 contribute 14.0% of importance.
→ Push toward hallucination
Resisting Heads
Concentrated in few high-importance heads. Top-5 contribute 27.2% of importance.
→ Suppress hallucination

Path patching replaces individual attention-head activations with clean-run counterparts to measure each head's causal contribution to the hallucination advantage (log-likelihood gap between hallucinated and factual tokens). Positive importance scores mark driving heads; negative scores mark resisting heads. Evaluated over 256 prototype samples, disjoint from validation/test.

MACI trains a Lasso logistic probe on resisting-head activations to detect conflict. When detected, driving heads are zero-ablated during generation — conditional, not indiscriminate.

Significance High
  • First head-level path patching applied to modality-conflict hallucination — moves beyond correlation to causal attribution.
  • Random-head controls, split-half stability (79.6%/64.7% top-30 overlap), dual-judge cross-validation (κ: 0.784–0.863).
  • Code promised but not yet available; evaluation limited to two benchmarks.
  • Non-conflict accuracy drops on InternVL3 (−4.66pp) and LLaVA (−6.49pp) suggest driving heads are not purely conflict-specific.

Strengths
  • Clean causal methodology — path patching provides stronger evidence than correlational approaches.
  • Consistent findings across five diverse MLLM architectures (dynamic-resolution tiling, MLP projection, cross-attention).
  • Conditional MACI intervention is well-motivated: resisting heads detect conflict, driving heads suppressed only when needed.
  • Strong transfer: object-identified driving heads generalize to attribute/relation conflicts and SCI benchmark.
Questions for Further Exploration
  • Can driving/resisting heads be leveraged during fine-tuning via regularization losses to permanently reduce imbalance?
  • How does the head distribution shift as model scale increases beyond 8B, or in Mamba-based VLMs?
  • Could resisting-head activations serve as a general-purpose visual-fidelity signal for other hallucination types?
  • Is the twofold asymmetry a consequence of training data composition, architectural inductive biases, or both?
  • Can MACI be combined with decoding-level methods (VCD, OPERA) for complementary reduction?
Limitations
  • Component identification relies on object-conflict data and the prefill stage only.
  • Conflict-detection probe requires labeled samples; zero-shot transfer may not hold for more diverse distributions.
  • Evaluated on only two benchmarks (MMMC, SCI-SemanticConflict); broader evaluation (HallusionBench, POPE) not provided.
  • Does not address settings where visual input itself is misleading.
  • k+/k− require per-model validation tuning, though sensitivity analysis shows reasonable stability.
