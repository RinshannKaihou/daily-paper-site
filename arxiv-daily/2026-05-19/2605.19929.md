> **一句话总结：** 本文提出SplitQ，通过模态特异性离群通道解耦（MOCD）将VLM的通道分为文本/视觉/兼容三组并分别量化，再用自适应跨模态校准（ACC）补偿残差，在Qwen2.5-VL-7B的W3A3下保留93.5%的FP16精度（69.5 vs 74.3），六项基准上全面超越SmoothQuant/MBQ/MASQuant等基线。

> **TL;DR:** SplitQ tackles low-bit post-training quantization (PTQ) of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) by observing that text and vision activation outliers reside in different channels. Its Modality-specific Outlier Channel Decoupling (MOCD) splits channels into modality-compatible, text-specific, and vision-specific sets processed by independent transformations, and its Adaptive Cross-Modal Calibration (ACC) adds two low-rank learnable branches for weight smoothing and activation compensation. On Qwen2.5-VL-7B across MMMU/SEED/OCRBench/VizWiz/ScienceQA/TextVQA, SplitQ reaches 73.5 avg at W4A4 (vs 74.3 FP16) and 69.5 at W3A3 (vs 74.3 FP16, preserving 93.5% of FP16 performance), while SmoothQuant, MBQ, and MASQuant collapse to near-zero accuracy at W4A4.

## 核心贡献

- 揭示VLM低比特量化的根本困难：文本与视觉激活的离群通道**位于不同通道**且分布形态不同（视觉长尾、文本相对响应不稳定），共享变换矩阵会互相干扰
- 提出**MOCD**模块：用视觉最大幅值打分 + 文本百分位秩-聚类方差打分，将通道显式分为模态兼容主通道、文本特异性、视觉特异性三组不相交集合，分别走独立量化路径
- 提出**ACC**模块：在主通道上引入两个低秩可学习分支——跨模态权重平滑（CWS）吸收对跨模态敏感的权重模式，模态激活补偿（MAC）针对文本激活偏差进行补偿
- 在Qwen2.5-VL-3B/7B与LLaVA-v1.5-7B/13B上、6项基准、W4A8/W4A4/W3A3/W3A2四种配置下全面达到SOTA；W3A3下Qwen2.5-VL-7B保留93.5%的FP16性能

## Key Contributions

- Identifies that VLM quantization fails because text and vision outliers occupy different channels with distinct distribution shapes (vision long-tailed, text with unstable relative responses), so a shared transformation distorts both modalities
- Proposes MOCD, which scores vision channels by max absolute activation and text channels by a scale-insensitive percentile-rank-then-cluster-variance proxy, partitioning channels into three disjoint sets (C_m, C_t, C_v) each quantized with its own learnable transformation
- Proposes ACC with two low-rank branches on the main modality-compatible channels: Cross-modal Weight Smoothing (CWS) extracts a low-rank component to absorb cross-modal-sensitive weight patterns, and Modality Activation Compensation (MAC) recovers text-side activation deviations
- Achieves SOTA on four VLMs (Qwen2.5-VL 3B/7B, LLaVA-v1.5 7B/13B) across six benchmarks and four bit-settings; at W3A3 on Qwen2.5-VL-7B, preserves 93.5% of FP16 performance

## Methodology

SplitQ quantizes only the LLM component of VLMs (following MBQ/MASQuant). **MOCD** partitions input channels C = {0,…,D_in} into three disjoint sets C = C_m ∪ C_t ∪ C_v. Vision-specific channels are selected by per-channel maximum absolute activation over vision tokens T_v: s_v(c)=max_{i∈T_v}|X_{i,c}|, taking top-K_v channels (Eq. 7–8). Text-specific channels use a scale-insensitive measure: for each text token, compute the percentile rank of each channel within that token (Eq. 9), cluster each channel's rank sequence across text tokens into K groups, and score by within-cluster variance s_t(c) (Eq. 10); high variance = unstable relative response; take top-K_t (Eq. 11). Remaining channels form C_m (Eq. 12). By default K_v=K_t=2% of channels.

The split matrices {X_t,W_t} and {X_v,W_v} are quantized with independent modality-aware transformations Y_t=Q(X_t P_t)Q(P_t^{-1} W_t) and Y_v=Q(X_v P_v)Q(P_v^{-1} W_v) (Eq. 13–14), where P_t, P_v are learnable affine matrices (adopting the FlatQuant form). The main pair {X_m,W_m} goes through ACC.

**ACC** has two components on {X_m,W_m}. (1) Cross-modal Weight Smoothing (CWS): instead of quantizing P_m^{-1}W_m directly, extract a low-rank component U_s V_s to absorb cross-modal-sensitive weight patterns, so the sensitive directions are handled in higher precision (4-bit lower bound). (2) Modality Activation Compensation (MAC): a low-rank learnable branch recovers activation-side deviations; ablation shows text-only compensation is substantially better than vision-only or both. The low-rank ranks default to 2% (smooth) and 3% (compensation) of the full weight rank; all auxiliary low-rank structures use a 4-bit quantization lower bound.

Calibration uses 128 samples by default (stability verified for N∈{32,64,256} via Jaccard similarity of selected outlier channels, Table 3). Experiments use the same calibration samples and random seeds across all baselines for fair comparison.

## Results & Highlights

- **Qwen2.5-VL-7B, W3A3 (Table 1).** SplitQ achieves 69.5 average across the six benchmarks vs 74.3 FP16 — preserving 93.5% of full-precision performance. The strongest baseline at W4A4 (MASQuant) reaches only 7.7 avg; at W3A3 all baselines fail to produce meaningful outputs.
- **Qwen2.5-VL-7B, W4A4 (Table 1).** SplitQ reaches 73.5 avg (vs 74.3 FP16, −0.8 pts) vs MASQuant 25.0, MBQ 26.7, SmoothQuant 24.8; on OCRBench specifically SplitQ scores 83.0 vs MASQuant 0.6 and FP16 83.8.
- **Qwen2.5-VL-3B, W4A4 (Table 1).** SplitQ achieves 69.6 avg (vs 70.0 FP16) with TextVQA 77.0 (FP16 77.9) and OCRBench 78.8 (FP16 79.3), while all three baselines (SQ/MBQ/MASQ) collapse to 0.0 on most sub-benchmarks (avg 3.9–5.7).
- **LLaVA-v1.5-13B, W3A3 (Table 2).** SplitQ reaches 63.6 avg (vs 66.8 FP16) on SEED-I/VizWiz/ScienceQA, while DuQuant, Q-VLM, and QSVD all fail to produce results at W3A3 (marked '-').
- **MOCD channel selection ablation (Table 4).** MOCD delivers the best overall results at W3A3 across all settings, outperforming Random, Text_max, Vision_max, Multi_max, and Multi_mean selection schemes; the optimal cluster number is k=3.
- **Calibration stability (Table 3).** Jaccard similarity of MOCD-selected outlier channels between calibration set sizes N∈{32,64,256} and the default N=128 is 94.1/93.3/94.5% for Qwen2.5-VL-7B and 89.5/95.5/95.7% for LLaVA-v1.5-7B, confirming the selection is stable under limited calibration data.

## Critical Assessment

SplitQ delivers strong empirical results, but several issues merit scrutiny. First, the comparison is **limited to a narrow baseline set on Qwen2.5-VL**: only SmoothQuant, MBQ, and MASQuant are compared, and at W3A3/W3A2 *all* baselines fail to produce meaningful outputs (marked '-' or near-zero), so SplitQ's 69.5 at W3A3 is effectively uncontrasted against any working low-bit baseline rather than a head-to-head win. Second, the method adds **substantial auxiliary structure** — three independent transformation matrices, two low-rank branches (CWS at 2% rank, MAC at 3% rank), modality-specific branches — whose memory and inference overhead is not quantified in the main results; the paper claims "minimal overhead" but does not report latency/FLOPs/memory numbers, making the efficiency claim unverified. Third, the **2% channel selection and k=3 cluster hyperparameters** are tuned on the evaluated models, and the ablation (Table 4) only studies W3A3 on Qwen2.5-VL — generalization to other architectures (e.g., InternVL, Llama-Vision) is not tested beyond Qwen2.5-VL and LLaVA-v1.5. Fourth, the paper **quantizes only the LLM component** (vision encoder left in FP16), so the reported memory savings apply to part of the model, yet the framing implies broad VLM deployment benefits. Finally, the text-channel selection via percentile-rank clustering (Eq. 9–10) introduces a clustering step whose sensitivity to the number of text calibration tokens is only loosely probed (Table 3 shows Jaccard stability, not accuracy stability under token-count variation).

## Strengths

- The channel-level modality-outlier observation (Fig. 2) is a concrete, actionable diagnostic that motivates the three-way split cleanly
- MOCD's dual scoring (magnitude for vision, rank-clustering for text) is principled — it matches each modality's distinct outlier shape rather than forcing one heuristic
- ACC's two-branch design separates weight-side and activation-side error sources, and the ablation isolating text-only compensation is a useful empirical finding
- Strong absolute numbers: 93.5% FP16 retention at W3A3 on Qwen2.5-VL-7B is a meaningful efficiency frontier
- Code is publicly released, and calibration-set stability is explicitly verified

## Limitations

- Baselines all collapse at the lowest bit-settings, so the W3A3/W3A2 wins are largely uncontrasted rather than head-to-head
- No reported latency, FLOPs, or memory-overhead numbers despite the efficiency framing; the auxiliary low-rank branches and extra transformations add cost
- Only the LLM component is quantized; the vision encoder remains FP16
- Hyperparameters (2% channels, k=3 clusters, 2%/3% low-rank) tuned on Qwen2.5-VL; generalization to other VLM families untested
- The LLaVA-v1.5 comparison uses only 3 benchmarks (SEED-I, VizWiz, ScienceQA), fewer than the 6 used for Qwen2.5-VL

## Relevance

直接映射到"Computation Reliability & Precision Errors"与"LLM Inference Evaluation"两个方向：它针对低比特量化引入的精度误差，揭示并量化了多模态激活在通道层面的分布异质性如何放大量化误差，并给出通道解耦+跨模态校准的工程化缓解方案，对VLM推理阶段的数值可靠性具有直接意义。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- What are the actual memory and latency overheads of the three transformation matrices plus two low-rank branches, and do they erode the deployment benefits at W3A3?
- Does the 2%/3% channel-selection and low-rank recipe transfer to other VLM architectures (InternVL, Llama-Vision, Florence) without re-tuning?
- Can MOCD's text-channel rank-clustering criterion be replaced by a cheaper, training-free proxy without losing stability under small calibration sets?
- How does SplitQ behave when the vision encoder is also quantized, closing the gap to full-model low-bit deployment?
- Is the modality-outlier channel partition stable across training checkpoints or fine-tuning, or does it drift with model updates?
