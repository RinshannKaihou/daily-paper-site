TORQ: Two-Level Orthogonal Rotation for MXFP4 Quantization

Zukang Xu · Xing Hu · Dawei Yang  —  Houmo AI

TORQ decomposes orthogonal rotation into inter-block variance equalization and intra-block codebook alignment, closing the MXFP4-to-BF16 accuracy gap to under 1.2 percentage points.

Why This Matters
MXFP4 is natively supported by NVIDIA Blackwell; TORQ makes 4-bit deployment practical with zero inference overhead and 10× faster calibration.
Critical Edge
Intra-block rotation captures only first-order statistics, missing kurtosis and skewness; the convex error model is an acknowledged approximation.

Methodology

TORQ targets MXFP4 (e2m1) activation quantization where each block of K=32 elements shares a common exponent and values project to a discrete 16-entry codebook. The global orthogonal optimization is decomposed via Kronecker product R = Rinter ⊗ Rintra:

  • Inter-block (Rinter): Schur-Horn theorem → equalize variance across B blocks. Iterative Givens rotations in O(B²). Proven globally optimal under convex error models (Theorem 4.1).
  • Intra-block (Rintra): Codebook occupancy equilibrium loss Lcode maximizes uniform codeword utilization. Alternating S-step (scale) / R-step (exact piecewise-constant angle search) — no gradient descent.
  • Weight fusion: Inverse RT fused offline into linear layer W′ = W(RintraT ⊗ Rinter). Zero runtime overhead. Calibrates on 128 WikiText2 segments.

Strengths

  • Exceptionally well-motivated: two structural imbalances diagnosed with empirical evidence before solutions are proposed.
  • Theoretically grounded: Schur-Horn optimality proof, Karamata’s inequality, full convexity analysis — rare depth for a systems paper.
  • Zero inference overhead via weight fusion is a critical practical advantage.
  • 10× faster calibration than competitors makes it genuinely deployable.
  • Strong generalization to MXINT4, NVFP4, and MoE architectures.

Limitations

  • Intra-block rotation only optimizes first-order statistics; kurtosis, skewness, multimodality not captured.
  • Fixed block size (32) throughout; adaptive sizing unexplored.
  • No specialized MoE expert-heterogeneity optimization.
  • Joint W4A4 evaluation makes it hard to isolate activation-only gains.
  • FlatQuant OOM on LLaMA3-70B may conflate memory with method quality.
  • Convex error model is an approximation at 4-bit precision.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does TORQ perform with mixed-precision configurations (e.g., MXFP4 for attention, FP8 for MLP)?
  2. Can intra-block rotation be enhanced with higher-order statistics for asymmetric or multimodal distributions?
  3. What is the impact of adaptive block sizes on accuracy and hardware efficiency?
  4. How does TORQ scale to ultra-large MoE models (1000+ experts) with cross-expert heterogeneity?
  5. Can the geometric paradigm apply to weight quantization (currently activation-only)?
  6. Numerical stability of fused W′ = W(RintraT ⊗ Rinter) for very deep models?
