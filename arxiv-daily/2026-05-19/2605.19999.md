> **一句话总结：** 本文提出利用Transformer训练与推理流水线的结构性不对称（训练需全部token的隐状态，推理仅需KV缓存+倒数第二层隐状态），将基准数据集以KV缓存形式发布（Contamination-Resistant Dataset），使其在预训练中不可学习但在推理时可用，并通过锚模型子空间对齐和相对表示两种范式实现跨架构互操作。

> **TL;DR:** The paper proposes releasing benchmark datasets as KV caches and penultimate-layer hidden states (Contamination-Resistant Datasets / CRDs) to exploit the asymmetry between Transformer training (needs all token hidden states) and inference (only needs KV cache + penultimate hidden state), making benchmarks unlearnable during pretraining but functional for evaluation, with two interoperability paradigms for cross-architecture support.

## 核心贡献

- 系统梳理了LLM基准数据集污染的严重程度：GPT-3评估时90%以上样本被标记为污染，Llama 2在MMLU上超16%污染，主流LLM污染率最高达91.8%。
- 正式定义了"抗污染数据集"（CRD）概念及其三个必需属性：不可逆性（Irreversibility）、等价性（Equivalence）、互操作性（Interoperability）。
- 揭示了Transformer训练与推理流水线的关键不对称性：训练需要所有token在每一层的完整隐状态来计算损失，而推理仅需KV缓存和最后一个token的倒数第二层隐状态。
- 提出CRD的具体数据发布格式：`{KV cache across all layers, penultimate-layer hidden state of final token, Y (plaintext labels)}`，仅暴露推理所需组件而非原始token序列。
- 提出两种跨模型互操作范式：(1) 近期方案——锚模型子空间对齐（基于Cross-LoRA、Trans-LoRA等技术）；(2) 长期方案——模型无关的相对表示（基于Moschella et al.的相对表示框架）。
- 系统分析了CRD的兼容性：适用于单轮QA、分类、多模态、代码生成、摘要等基准；不适用于多轮对话、动态代理、自适应等输入输出紧密耦合的基准。
- 提出KV缓存压缩策略（PyramidKV仅需保留12%甚至0.7%的KV条目），将100K token的存储需求从50GB降至约350MB。

## Key Contributions

- Provides a comprehensive survey of benchmark contamination prevalence: GPT-3 flagged >90% of examples, Llama 2 showed >16% contamination on MMLU, major LLMs exhibit up to 91.8% contamination.
- Formalizes Contamination-Resistant Datasets (CRDs) with three required properties: Irreversibility (computationally infeasible to reconstruct plaintext), Equivalence (model output on CRD approximates plaintext output), and Interoperability (CRD can be translated across architectures).
- Identifies the critical Transformer training/inference asymmetry: training computes hidden states for all positions via teacher forcing, while inference only needs cached KV pairs and the penultimate-layer hidden state of the final token.
- Proposes the concrete CRD release format: `{K^(l)_{1:t}, V^(l)_{1:t}} for l=1..L, h^{(L-1)}_t, Y` — exposing only inference-sufficient components without the raw token sequence.
- Presents two interoperability paradigms: (1) near-term anchor-model subspace alignment (leveraging Cross-LoRA, Trans-LoRA, LoRA-X), and (2) long-term model-agnostic relative representations (leveraging Moschella et al.'s relative representation framework with angular invariance).
- Analyzes CRD benchmark compatibility: compatible with single-turn QA, classification, multi-modal, code generation, and summarization benchmarks; incompatible with multi-turn dialogue, dynamic agentic, adaptive, and evolving-gold-standard benchmarks.
- Demonstrates KV cache compression feasibility: PyramidKV retains only 12% (even 0.7% with subtle effects) of KV entries, reducing storage from ~50GB to ~350MB for 100K tokens.

## Methodology

The paper is structured as a position paper combining empirical evidence, formal definitions, and theoretical analysis across seven sections:

1. **Problem formalization (Section 2):** Defines CRDs formally (Definition 2.1) — a dataset ϕ(D) is contamination-resistant w.r.t. model M if M(ϕ(D)) yields valid task performance but training on ϕ(D) fails to improve generalization. Establishes three properties (Irreversibility, Equivalence, Interoperability) derived from Theorem 2.1. Describes a three-phase evaluation framework: Curation (anchor models project plaintext into latent KV format), Discovery (evaluators translate projections to target model), and Model Evaluation (target model generates from translated KV cache).

2. **Transformer asymmetry analysis (Section 3):** Provides a detailed derivation of the training pipeline (Eq. 1: standard NLL loss over all positions requiring all hidden states) vs. the inference pipeline (autoregressive generation using only cached KV pairs and penultimate hidden state). Defines the CRD data pair format (Eq. 3): release `{KV cache, h^{(L-1)}_t, Y}` where Y is plaintext ground truth.

3. **Interoperability (Section 4):** Grounds cross-model translation in three lines of research: Platonic Representation Hypothesis (Huh et al., 2024 — representations converge across architectures), Representational Similarity Metrics (CKA — Kornblith et al., 2019), and Model Stitching (Bansal et al., 2021 — heterogeneous models are stitchable). Presents two paradigms:
   - *Anchor-model subspace alignment:* Uses Cross-LoRA's rank-truncated SVD + Frobenius-optimal linear transformation to translate KV caches from anchor to target model. Architectural similarity (shared GQA, SwiGLU, RMSNorm) improves transfer fidelity.
   - *Relative representations:* Projects all models into a shared coordinate frame via anchor samples, using angular invariance to establish geometric correspondence. Requires only k=100-500 anchor samples.

4. **Discussion of CRD properties (Section 5):** Analyzes each property's feasibility. Irreversibility: KV inversion attacks (Luo et al., 2026) are effective on MHA but much less so on GQA; lossy representations and defenses (noise injection, KV-Cloak obfuscation) add protection. Equivalence: Proposes verification protocols including paired evaluations, calibration checks, distillation, prompt tuning, and adaptive temperature scaling. Interoperability: Cites cache-to-cache translation (Fu et al., 2026) showing limited degradation in cross-model transfer.

5. **Comparison with alternatives (Section 6):** Systematically critiques existing approaches — private benchmarking (bottlenecks, cost barriers, weight-sharing infeasibility), dynamic benchmarking (short-lived utility, rapid re-absorption), lexical obfuscation (LLMs circumvent via prior training on obfuscated formats), and decontamination/filtering (fails on paraphrased/transformed content, scale challenges).

## Results & Highlights

- **Contamination prevalence (Figure 1):** Contamination levels increased from 2020 to 2023 across all major benchmarks, with some reaching 45.4% detected contamination (+18.4% growth).
- **CRD format feasibility:** The KV cache + penultimate hidden state format is mathematically sufficient for inference while preventing training usage, as confirmed by the Transformer forward pass derivation.
- **Storage efficiency (Figure 4):** Using PyramidKV compression, CRD projections of major benchmarks (MMLU, GSM8K, HumanEval, etc.) require only 106-353 MB each, with MMLU at ~3,686 MB being the largest.
- **Cross-model transfer viability:** Subspace alignment via Cross-LoRA achieves positive transfer across LLaMA and Gemma families; models sharing GQA, SwiGLU, and RMSNorm exhibit strongest transferability.
- **Benchmark compatibility analysis:** CRDs are broadly compatible with QA, classification, multi-modal, code generation, and summarization benchmarks, but face challenges with multi-turn, agentic, and adaptive evaluations.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** High for the CRD framing and the specific KV-cache-based release format. The Transformer train/inference asymmetry insight is genuinely creative — while the asymmetry itself is well-known, weaponizing it for contamination resistance is novel. The interoperability analysis synthesizes existing work (Cross-LoRA, relative representations) into a coherent pipeline.
- **Rigor:** Moderate. The formal CRD definition (Definition 2.1) is precise, and the Transformer pipeline derivation is mathematically sound. However, the paper lacks end-to-end empirical validation — no actual CRD benchmark is constructed and tested on real LLMs. The irreversibility claims rely partly on the assumption that GQA architectures resist inversion, which may not hold as attacks improve.
- **Reproducibility:** Low for the core proposal. While the theoretical framework is clearly specified, no implementation, dataset, or experimental protocol is released. The interoperability methods reference existing tools (Cross-LoRA, Trans-LoRA) but do not demonstrate their application to CRD specifically.
- **Significance:** Very high. Benchmark contamination is one of the most pressing issues in LLM evaluation. The shift from reactive (detect/decontaminate) to proactive (release in unlearnable form) is a paradigm change. If feasible, CRDs could fundamentally alter how benchmarks are published and used.
- **Red flags:** (1) No empirical validation of the full CRD pipeline — the paper remains at the proposal level. (2) The irreversibility property is not guaranteed — KV inversion attacks on MHA models are demonstrated, and GQA resistance may be temporary. (3) The equivalence property (M(ϕ(D)) ≈ M(D)) is asserted but not empirically verified across diverse benchmarks and models. (4) The approach is fundamentally limited to Transformer-based models and does not extend to non-Transformer architectures (e.g., Mamba).

## Strengths

- Identifies and formalizes a critical problem with a clear, actionable solution framework.
- The Transformer train/inference asymmetry argument is technically sound and elegantly motivates the KV-cache-based release format.
- Thorough comparison with alternative approaches (private benchmarking, dynamic benchmarking, obfuscation, decontamination) clearly positions CRDs' advantages.
- Two interoperability paradigms (anchor-model and relative representations) provide both near-term and long-term practical paths.
- Practical storage analysis demonstrates feasibility — 350MB for 100K tokens is manageable.
- Excellent literature synthesis spanning contamination detection, representation alignment, model stitching, and cross-model transfer.

## Limitations

- **No empirical end-to-end validation:** The entire CRD pipeline (curation → discovery → evaluation) has not been implemented or tested on real benchmarks and models.
- **Irreversibility is not proven:** KV inversion attacks exist for MHA architectures, and GQA resistance may erode. The paper acknowledges this but relies on defenses (noise, obfuscation) that are not evaluated in the CRD context.
- **Equivalence is assumed but unverified:** Whether translated KV caches actually preserve evaluation properties across diverse benchmarks and model families remains an open empirical question.
- **Limited to Transformer architectures:** Does not apply to non-Transformer models (e.g., state-space models like Mamba).
- **Incompatible with important benchmark types:** Multi-turn dialogue, agentic, and adaptive benchmarks — increasingly important evaluation modalities — cannot be straightforwardly adapted to CRDs.
- **Deployment complexity:** The curation-discovery-evaluation pipeline requires anchor model maintenance, translation services, and calibration, which adds significant infrastructure overhead compared to plaintext benchmarks.
- **Potential adversarial circumvention:** Model developers could potentially train specialized layers to learn from KV-cache-format data if the format becomes standardized and widely used.

## Relevance

该论文提出的抗污染基准数据集（CRD）方案通过重新设计数据发布格式从源头解决评估污染问题，与LLM评估方法论和数据治理高度相关。其核心思路——利用模型架构的内在不对称性——为构建更可信的评估体系提供了全新视角，对理解LLM记忆机制和评估公平性具有重要参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- What is the actual evaluation fidelity (equivalence gap) when using KV-cache-based CRDs vs. plaintext benchmarks across diverse model families and task types?
- How robust is the irreversibility property against future inversion attacks targeting GQA and multi-query attention architectures?
- Can the relative representation paradigm achieve sufficient equivalence for fine-grained discrimination between similarly-performing models?
- What is the computational cost of the translation step (anchor-to-target) for state-of-the-art models with billions of parameters?
- How would CRDs interact with quantized, pruned, or distilled model variants where the latent space structure may differ significantly from the anchor?
- Could a hybrid approach combining CRDs with dynamic benchmarking provide both contamination resistance and temporal freshness?
- What governance structures would be needed to maintain anchor models and ensure the CRD ecosystem remains trustworthy and accessible?
