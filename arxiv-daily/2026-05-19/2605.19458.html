Implicit Bias of Mirror Flow in Homogeneous Neural Networks: Sparse and Dense Feature Learning

Tom Jacobs & Guido Montúfar 2026-05-19
implicit-bias mirror-flow max-margin feature-learning homogeneous-networks
arXiv Page PDF Markdown
Key Finding

Different mirror maps in homogeneous neural networks can converge to the same max-margin solution yet produce fundamentally different feature representations — from sparse to dense neuron activations. A novel balance equation, derived from convex duality, unifies these regimes under one theoretical lens for the first time.

Key Metric
2nx
Exponentially slow convergence observed under certain mirror maps — a phenomenon absent in standard gradient flow.
Why This Matters

As alternative optimizers beyond gradient descent gain traction, understanding how they shape learned representations is critical. This framework is the first to connect mirror descent to implicit bias in deep networks, providing principled guidance on how optimizer choice influences both convergence speed and representation geometry.

Critical Edge
  • Restricted to homogeneous activations — excludes Swish, GELU, LayerNorm+ReLU
  • Gap between continuous mirror flow and practical finite-step training is unaddressed
  • No practical guidance on which mirror maps to prefer for specific tasks
  • Experimental scope unclear — "standard vision tasks" not specified
Methodology

The authors study mirror flow (continuous-time mirror descent) in deep networks with homogeneous activations. Starting from convex duality, they derive a balance equation that generalizes the classical gradient flow result, characterize the horizon function governing the induced margin, and validate predictions on synthetic and vision tasks.

Key tools: convex duality, homogeneous function theory, margin analysis, norm growth estimates. The balance equation connects the mirror map to the geometry of the learned classifier, enabling characterization of both convergence behavior and representation structure.

Results & Highlights
  • Distinct non-homogeneous mirror maps can converge to identical max-margin solutions
  • Convergence can be exponentially slow under certain mirror maps
  • All studied mirror maps exhibit feature learning, but representations differ markedly
  • Framework provides both qualitative (sparse vs. dense) and quantitative (rates, bounds) insights
Strengths
  • Principled theoretical framework connecting mirror descent to implicit bias
  • Unifies sparse and dense feature learning under a single mathematical lens
  • Identifies concrete practical implications of optimizer choice
  • Combines theory with experimental validation
Limitations
  • Homogeneous activations only — excludes modern architectures
  • Continuous flow vs. discrete training gap not addressed
  • No task-specific mirror map selection guidance
  • Experimental details sparse in abstract

Relevance

Provides a unified theoretical framework for understanding how non-standard optimizers (e.g., mirror descent) shape feature learning in deep networks. Directly valuable for researchers studying the interplay between optimization algorithms and representation geometry.

Questions for Further Exploration
  1. How do results extend to non-homogeneous activations (LayerNorm+ReLU, Swish)?
  2. Can the framework predict sparse vs. dense representations a priori?
  3. What is the relationship between mirror flow implicit bias and Adam's implicit bias?
  4. Are there mirror maps that consistently outperform gradient flow on generalization?
