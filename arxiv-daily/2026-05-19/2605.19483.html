A Dynamical Systems View of Training Generative Models and the Memorization Phenomenon

Memorization in generative models is not a static flaw — it is an emergent, intermittent collapse driven by the two-time-scale structure of constant-step-size SGD in over-parameterized spaces.

arXiv 2605.19483
Date 2026-05-19
Venue Automatica (submitted)
Theory
Generative Models
Stochastic Approximation
Key Finding

Memorization is intermittent collapse: the fast SGD variable locks to a local minimum for extended stretches while the slow variable drifts, producing repeated/similar outputs. This only happens under constant step size — it vanishes with decreasing step sizes.

The paper formalizes this as Theorem 11. In over-parameterized networks, spatial scale separation (Austin's theorem) naturally creates two time scales in SGD. The fast dynamics drive collapse to a Dirac measure; the slow drift modulates transitions between collapse phases, making memorization intermittent but systematic.

Key Metric
4
Main theorems building a compositional proof: Theorems 5, 7, 10, 11 — from collapse definition to intermittent memorization.
Why This Matters
  • First rigorous dynamical systems explanation for memorization, unifying collapse, flat minima preference, and step size effects under a single framework.
  • Identifies constant step size as the precise mechanism that enables memorization — decreasing step sizes converge to a fixed equilibrium and do not produce memorization.
  • Explains the empirical observation that larger step sizes suppress memorization: increased noise in constant-step-size SGD discourages the stationary distribution from concentrating on narrow minima.
  • Connects Freidlin-Wentzell large deviations theory to practical training, showing SGD concentrates on minima with low Hessian determinant (flat minima).
Critical Edge
  • Stylized loss F(z) = f(x, εy) is a strong simplification; real networks have continuous spectra of scales.
  • Zero experimental validation on actual diffusion models or other architectures.
  • Conditional barycenter assumption (Eq. 3.1) acknowledged as an idealization that does not generally hold.
  • Gradient issue with Markov noise sidestepped rather than resolved.
Core Theorems
Thm 5
Generative models induce an absorbing measure-valued Markov chain — collapse is convergence to a random Dirac measure.
Formally defines collapse: the chain {μn} converges to δγ almost surely. This is the building block for memorization.
Thm 7
With decreasing step size, pure collapse occurs asymptotically even under concurrent parameter tuning.
In the convergent regime, the generative model output collapses to a single point. Memorization is not "intermittent" here — it is permanent.
Thm 10
Stationary distribution of constant-step-size SGD concentrates on global minima of Freidlin-Wentzell potential, weighted by 1/√det(∇²V).
This Boltzmann-Gibbs-like weighting explains flat minima preference: minima with smaller Hessian determinant (flatter) receive higher probability mass.
Thm 11
Main result — two-time-scale dynamics exhibit memorization as intermittent collapse, with drift-aided transitions between phases.
The fast variable tracks λ(yn) for extended stretches while y drifts slowly. Transitions between collapse phases are not purely noise-driven but drift-aided, making memorization intermittent but not rare.
Mechanism
1
Scale separation. Austin's theorem: in high-dim over-parameterized spaces, the loss depends mostly on a small parameter subset.
2
Two time scales emerge. Fast variables (strong dependence, step size a) and slow variables (weak dependence, effective step size εa).
3
Fast collapse. The fast subsystem drives the measure-valued chain to a Dirac — the model outputs near-identical samples.
4
Slow drift modulates. The slow variable drifts between minima, causing transitions between collapse phases — intermittent memorization.
5
Step size controls regime. Larger constant step size → more noise → less concentration on narrow minima → less memorization.
Strengths
  • Clean unified framework connecting collapse, memorization, flat minima preference, and step size effects.
  • Intermittent collapse is more nuanced than binary "memorized or not" views.
  • Correctly identifies constant step size as the key enabler — memorization does not occur with decreasing step sizes.
  • Emergent time scales via Austin's theorem rather than manually imposed — elegant motivation.
  • Honest about limitations and idealizations throughout.
Limitations
  • Stylized loss F(z) = f(x, εy) is far from real network landscapes.
  • No experimental validation on actual diffusion models.
  • Conditional barycenter property is a significant idealization.
  • Gradient issue with Markov noise deferred, not fully resolved.
  • Bridging theory to practical step-size guidelines requires further work.
Open Questions
Q1Can intermittent collapse be empirically validated by tracking two-time-scale dynamics during actual diffusion model training?
Q2How does the framework extend to adaptive optimizers (Adam) where effective step size varies per parameter?
Q3What are concrete, theoretically grounded guidelines for step size to balance training speed vs. memorization avoidance?
Q4How does the two-scale model compare with the continuous spectrum of scales in real networks?
Q5Can the Freidlin-Wentzell potential comparison between SGD and Langevin dynamics be made quantitative?
