> **一句话总结：** 本文从动力系统视角出发，利用随机逼近中的多时间尺度理论和坍塌（collapse）现象，从数学上解释了生成模型（如扩散模型）训练过程中出现的"记忆化"现象——即模型在较长时间段内重复输出相同或相似样本的间歇性行为。

> **TL;DR:** This paper provides a rigorous dynamical systems explanation for the memorization phenomenon in generative models (e.g., diffusion models), framing it as intermittent collapse driven by two-time-scale SGD dynamics with constant step size and Markov noise.

## 核心贡献

- 将生成模型的"记忆化"（memorization）现象从动力系统角度重新建模，将其解释为一种间歇性的"坍塌"（collapse）行为，而非纯粹统计力学视角下的现象。
- 利用 Austin (2016) 的定理指出，在高维过参数化空间中，损失函数自然形成强/弱依赖变量的分离，从而在 SGD 中涌现出多时间尺度结构。
- 利用 Azizian et al. (2024) 对常步长随机逼近的理论结果，精确刻画了常步长 SGD 的渐近行为与能量景观的关系。
- 通过 Hwang (1981) 的经典结果解释了 SGD 偏好"更平坦"极小值的现象。
- 分析了噪声和步长对记忆化现象的抑制/促进作用，与已有的实验观察（如大步长抑制记忆化）相吻合。

## Key Contributions

- **Dynamical systems framing of memorization**: Recasts memorization not as a static property of the loss landscape, but as an emergent dynamic phenomenon arising from the temporal evolution of the training algorithm itself.
- **Two-time-scale emergence from spatial scales**: Uses Austin's theorem to show that high-dimensional over-parameterized networks naturally lead to two time scales in SGD — fast variables (strong dependence) and slow variables (weak dependence) — without being manually imposed.
- **Collapse as the building block of memorization**: Formalizes collapse as convergence of a measure-valued Markov chain to a Dirac measure (Theorem 5), and shows memorization is intermittent collapse modulated by slow-scale parameter drift (Theorem 11).
- **Constant step size as the key enabler**: Demonstrates that memorization is explicitly a phenomenon of constant step size SGD — it does not arise in the classical decreasing step size regime where convergence to a fixed equilibrium occurs.
- **Preference for flat minima**: Uses Hwang's result to show that the stationary distribution of constant step size SGD concentrates on minima with low Hessian determinant, explaining the empirical preference for flatter/simpler minima.

## Methodology

The paper builds a layered theoretical argument combining several strands of stochastic approximation theory:

1. **Austin's theorem for spatial scale separation**: In over-parameterized settings, a Lipschitz-bounded function of many variables depends mostly on a small subset. This motivates a stylized loss function $F(z) = f(x, \epsilon y)$ where $x$ captures strong-dependence parameters and $y$ captures weak-dependence ones ($\epsilon \ll 1$).

2. **Two-time-scale SGD with Markov noise**: The SGD iteration naturally splits into a fast subsystem (step size $a$) tracking $x$ and a slow subsystem (effective step size $b = \epsilon a$) tracking $y$. The Markov noise $\{Z_n\}$ comes from the generative model's forward-backward passes and evolves on the fastest "natural" clock.

3. **Collapse model (from Borkar 2025a)**: A generative model induces a measure-valued Markov chain $\{\mu_n\}$ that is absorbing — it converges to a random Dirac measure $\delta_\gamma$, formally defining collapse. This is combined with the two-time-scale structure: the fast variable $x$ converges to a local minimum $\lambda(y)$ while $y$ drifts slowly.

4. **Constant step size analysis (from Azizian et al. 2024)**: For constant step size, the SGD iterates concentrate on critical points of a large deviations rate function (Freidlin-Wentzell potential), with a Boltzmann-Gibbs-like distribution over connected components. Unlike decreasing step sizes, there is no convergence — the iterates jump between equilibria.

5. **Memorization as intermittent collapse (Theorem 11)**: When the slow variable $y$ is not at a minimum of $\phi(\cdot, y)$, it drifts slowly while $x$ remains approximately locked to $\lambda(y_n)$ for extended stretches — producing the repeated/similar outputs characteristic of memorization. The transitions between collapse phases are drift-aided (not purely noise-driven), making memorization intermittent but not rare.

## Results & Highlights

- **Theorem 5**: Establishes that generative models induce an absorbing measure-valued Markov chain, providing a rigorous definition of collapse as convergence to a Dirac measure.
- **Theorem 7**: In the decreasing step size convergent case, pure collapse occurs asymptotically even with concurrent parameter tuning.
- **Theorem 10**: The stationary distribution of constant step size SGD, as $a \downarrow 0$, concentrates on global minima of the Freidlin-Wentzell potential with weights inversely proportional to $\sqrt{\det(\nabla^2 V)}$ — explaining flat minima preference.
- **Theorem 11 (main result)**: Under mild conditions, the two-time-scale dynamics exhibit memorization — the fast variable tracks a local minimum for long stretches while the slow variable drifts, producing intermittent collapse-like behavior.
- **Noise and step size effects**: Larger step sizes increase noise, which discourages memorization (consistent with Wu et al. 2025). The analysis provides a theoretical basis for this empirical observation.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: The dynamical systems viewpoint on memorization is genuinely novel. Most prior work treats memorization through statistical mechanics or information-theoretic lenses. Framing it as intermittent collapse driven by emergent multi-time-scale structure is a fresh contribution.
- **Rigor**: The mathematical framework is rigorous, building on well-established results (Austin 2016, Azizian et al. 2024, Freidlin-Wentzell theory, Hwang 1981). However, the stylized model ($F(z) = f(x, \epsilon y)$) is a significant simplification that the authors acknowledge.
- **Reproducibility**: This is a theoretical paper with no experiments. The claims are mathematical and can be verified by working through the proofs, but there are no empirical validations of the predicted dynamics.
- **Significance**: The unification of collapse, memorization, flat minima preference, and step size effects under a single dynamical systems framework is intellectually satisfying. However, the gap between the stylized model and practical training of large-scale diffusion models is substantial.
- **Red flags**: The conditional barycenter assumption (Eq. 3.1) is acknowledged as an idealization that does not hold in general. The gradient issue with Markov noise (Appendix B) is noted as a genuine difficulty that is sidestepped rather than fully resolved. No experimental evidence is provided to confirm the theoretical predictions.

## Strengths

- Provides a clean, unified theoretical framework connecting multiple observed phenomena (memorization, collapse, flat minima preference, step size effects).
- The intermittent collapse interpretation of memorization is more nuanced than binary "memorized or not" views.
- Correctly identifies constant step size as the key ingredient — memorization does not occur with decreasing step sizes.
- The use of Austin's theorem to motivate emergent time scales (rather than manually imposed ones) is elegant.
- Honest about limitations and idealizations throughout.

## Limitations

- The stylized loss function $F(z) = f(x, \epsilon y)$ is a strong simplification; real networks may have multiple or continuous spatial scales.
- No experimental validation on actual diffusion models or other generative architectures.
- The conditional barycenter property is a significant idealization that may not hold in practice.
- The gradient issue with Markov noise is deferred to the appendix and not fully resolved in the main analysis.
- The paper is purely theoretical; bridging to practical recommendations (e.g., how to set step size to avoid memorization) requires further work.
- Heavy mathematical machinery may limit accessibility for the broader ML community.

## Relevance

本文从随机逼近和动力系统理论出发，为理解扩散模型等生成模型的记忆化现象提供了新的数学框架。对于研究训练动态、泛化与记忆化权衡、以及SGD隐式正则化的研究者具有参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Can the intermittent collapse prediction be empirically validated by tracking the two-time-scale dynamics during actual diffusion model training?
2. How does the framework extend to adaptive optimizers (Adam, etc.) where the effective step size varies per parameter?
3. What are concrete, theoretically grounded guidelines for choosing step size to balance training speed and memorization avoidance?
4. How does the stylized two-scale model compare with the continuous spectrum of scales likely present in real networks?
5. Can the Freidlin-Wentzell potential comparison between SGD and Langevin dynamics be made quantitative, and what does it imply for algorithm design?
