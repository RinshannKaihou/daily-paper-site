Beyond Mode Collapse: Distribution Matching for Diverse Reasoning

Xiaozhe Li, Yang Li, Xinyu Fang, Shengyuan Ding, Peiji Li, Yongkang Chen, Yichuan Ma, Tianyi Lyu, Linyang Li, Dahua Lin, Qipeng Guo, Qingwen Liu, Kai Chen

Tongji University · Shanghai AI Laboratory · Zhejiang University · Fudan University · CUHK

DMPO diagnoses GRPO's mode collapse as a consequence of reverse KL's mode-seeking behavior, then solves it via a group-level MSE distribution-matching term that approximates forward KL. Evaluated on a new multimodal NP-hard benchmark (MM-NP-Bench), DMPO achieves 9–12% relative gains on optimization, +2.0% on math reasoning, and +2.3% on out-of-domain tasks.

Key Finding

Standard policy gradient methods for LLM reasoning (GRPO, PPO) implicitly minimize reverse KL divergence, which is mode-seeking—the policy collapses onto the single highest-reward trajectory. DMPO fixes this by adding a group-level MSE distribution-matching term that approximates forward KL, which is mode-covering. The result: policies that preserve diverse high-quality solutions instead of collapsing to one. This is achieved with a single regularizer added to GRPO, at negligible computational cost.

Why This Matters

GRPO is the dominant RL method for training LLM reasoning (used in DeepSeek-R1, Qwen, and others). Mode collapse—where the model learns one solution and stops exploring—is a fundamental, previously undiagnosed failure mode.

  • Timely: GRPO adoption is accelerating across the industry
  • Simple fix: One MSE regularizer, a few lines of code on top of GRPO
  • Generalizes: Gains beyond optimization into math and visual reasoning
  • New benchmark: MM-NP-Bench enables systematic study of exploration failure
  • Theoretical clarity: Finally explains why GRPO collapses
▷

Method: DMPO in Three Steps

Step 1 — Sample Group
For each prompt, sample G=8 trajectories from the current policy. Compute reward for each using Quality Ratio (not binary), yielding a fine-grained optimization signal.
Step 2 — Build Target Distribution
Construct a Boltzmann distribution over the group: p(oi) ∝ exp(r(oi)/α). This replaces GRPO's implicit Dirac delta with a smooth distribution over all rewarded solutions.
Step 3 — Match via MSE
Align the policy's length-normalized empirical distribution qθ to the target p using MSE loss. Added as λ·LDM to the GRPO objective. λ interpolates between exploitation and exploration.
λ = 2.0 (optimal) α = 1/15 Group size G = 8 LR 1e-6 cosine decay 250 iterations 7B-scale models

Critical Edge

No error bars or multi-seed results reported anywhere. The +2.0% math gain could be noise.

Only 7B-scale models tested. Scalability to 70B+ is completely unverified.

QR-based reward is inherently smooth on NP tasks, benefiting DMPO. Sparse binary reward benefit is unclear.

DMPO fails when no valid solution appears in the sampled group (extremely sparse rewards).

Temperature α = 1/15 is problem-specific; may need tuning per domain.

Strengths & Limitations at a Glance

Strengths

  • Clean theoretical framing with 3 formal propositions and proofs
  • Elegant design: single MSE regularizer, negligible compute overhead
  • MM-NP-Bench is a well-designed, reusable benchmark
  • Consistent gains across text, vision, math, and OOD tasks
  • Pure DMPO (without GRPO loss) matches GRPO—validates the objective

Limitations

  • No error bars or statistical significance tests
  • Only 7B-scale; no evidence of larger-model scaling
  • Fails in extremely sparse-reward settings
  • Temperature α requires per-domain tuning
  • Benefits under truly sparse binary rewards unclear
