Refusal Beyond a Single Direction:
A Preliminary Comparison of Diff-in-Means and INLP

Elisabetta Rocchetti & Alfio Ferrara · Università degli Studi di Milano
The same linear subspace, used two different ways, produces two different geometries: nullspace projection erases the refusal concept, while counterfactual flipping inverts it — a first hint that “absence” and “opposite” may live in different places inside a model.

Key Finding
α=2 flips, α=1 fades

INLP counterfactual flipping (α=2) matches DiM directional ablation for refusal suppression across five models; INLP nullspace projection (α=1) is consistently weaker and partially fails on Qwen and Llama-2.

Key Metric
k0.8

Restricting INLP to leading directions that retain 80% classifier accuracy preserves most refusal suppression while keeping perplexity within 0.05 of baseline — a tunable capability–effect knob.

Why This Matters
Absence ≠ Opposite

If models encode the absence of a concept differently from its opposite, concept-erasure methods must choose whether to remove, flip, or steer — with implications for bias, sentiment, truthfulness, and gender pronouns.

Critical Edge
Qualitative, not proven

The geometry claim is observational; the difference could be operator-induced (P_N vs 2P_N−I). Refusal is a murky testbed because “not harmful” and “opposite of harmful” overlap semantically.

Methods

Four interventions, one extraction layer

  • DiM ActAdd: add or subtract the harmful–harmless mean difference vector at the selected layer.
  • DiM directional ablation: project activations onto the hyperplane orthogonal to the DiM direction at the selected layer only.
  • INLP nullspace (α=1): project onto the nullspace of a trained harmful/harmless linear classifier — erase the concept.
  • INLP counterfactual flip (α=2): reflect across that nullspace — invert the concept while preserving orthogonal components.

Tested on Gemma 2B-IT, Qwen 1.8B-Chat, Yi 6B-Chat, Llama-2 7B-Chat, Llama-3 8B-Instruct. Harmful test: full JailbreakBench. Harmless test: Alpaca.

Results

Headline takeaways

  • Suppression parity: α=2 matches or beats DiM ablation on harmful prompts (e.g. Llama-2 +0.76 vs +0.46; Llama-3 +0.96 vs +0.95).
  • Nullspace lag: α=1 works on Gemma, Yi, and Llama-3 but fails on Qwen (+0.10 at k=n) and Llama-2 (≈0).
  • Harmless-side caveat: ActAdd scores highest, but many outputs are degenerate loops that accidentally contain refusal phrases.
  • Capability preserved: MMLU and ARC-Challenge 5-shot accuracy show no meaningful drop under any intervention.
Geometry

Where the transformed activations land

Directional ablation

Both classes collapse onto the harmless side of the refusal axis — the concept is stripped away one-sidedly.

ActAdd

A clean two-way swap, but the harmful→harmless distribution has a long tail above the centroid — off-manifold and degraded.

Counterfactual flipping α=2

Two-way swap similar to ActAdd, but activations land closer to the natural target-class manifold.

Nullspace projection α=1

Transformed points collapse between the harmful and harmless clusters — neither cluster, but suspended in between.

Critical Assessment

What holds up, what wobbles

  • Novelty: The head-to-head comparison and the absence/opposite geometry are new, but the geometric claim is qualitative.
  • Rigor: Small model sample, single judge, single test set, and a deliberate weakening of the DiM baseline to single-layer ablation.
  • Reproducibility: Methods are well-defined, but key implementation details sit behind an anonymous code link.
  • Significance: A useful reference for steering-method choice and a hypothesis-generating starting point for representation geometry.

Relevance

Why read this now

Refusal steering has been dominated by the “find the direction, ablate or add” story. This paper asks whether the underlying concept is better understood as a direction, a subspace, or a pair of opposable representations. If absence and opposite are encoded differently, the next generation of safety, debiasing, and truthfulness interventions may need to pick between erase, flip, and steer rather than assuming one vector rules them all.
