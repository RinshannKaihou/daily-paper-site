TL;DR

This position paper proposes four symmetry-based axioms for actionable interpretability. Within a Markov-category framework, these turn interpretable models into a "sub-recipe" and unify alignment, interventions, and counterfactuals as Bayesian inversion.

The Four Symmetries

S1
Inference Equivariance
Translation maps τₓ, τᵧ commute between model PY|X and human mental model P[h]Y|X.
S2
Information Invariance
The model retains only task-relevant mutual information: H(Z) ≪ H(X), i.e. sufficient statistics.
S3
Concept-Closure Invariance
Learned concepts share semantics with human concepts via extension–intension fixed points.
S4
Structural Invariance
Model hypothesis class matches the user's mental hypothesis class, so the user can predict model behavior.

What It Unifies

  • Alignment → Bayesian posterior over parameters given concept evidence.
  • Intervention → Posterior update with a constrained likelihood (do-operator).
  • Counterfactual → Abduction → action → prediction as Bayesian inversion.
  • CBM + mechanistic interpretability → Diagnosed as over-indexing on disjoint subsets of the axioms.

Methods Disqualified

post-hoc attribution (LIME/SHAP/IG) sparse autoencoders unconstrained DNN concept predictors decision trees on non-concept spaces

Each fails at least one symmetry, giving reviewers a principled way to say "not interpretable in this strict sense."

Critical Assessment

  • No empirical validation. No user study, benchmark, or case study.
  • Completeness unproven. Independence and joint satisfiability remain open.
  • Category-theoretic barrier. Heavy Markov-category machinery may alienate practitioners.
  • Concept-closure fuzziness. Verifying that a learned concept equals a human concept is the same open problem CAVs face.
  • Self-citation density. Many symmetry "consequences" restate the authors' prior work.

Relevance

Highly relevant to concept-bottleneck models, mechanistic interpretability, and any work trying to make AI systems auditable for safety or regulation. The four symmetries offer a shared vocabulary for asking whether a method is truly interpretable or merely post-hoc decorated.
