> **一句话总结：** 在重尾（$p\in(1,2]$）非凸随机优化设定下，首次证明 Muon 这类非欧氏矩阵优化器在核范数 stationarity 下达到 $\mathcal{O}(\min\{m,n\}\,\Delta_1 L\varepsilon^{-2}(\sigma/\varepsilon)^{p/(p-1)})$ 的样本复杂度——比 stationarity-corrected 欧氏方法省去一个 $(3p-2)/(2(p-1))$ 量级的维度因子，并证明该速率在一阶方法中**严格最优**；在 70M Pythia + FineWeb-Edu 上的实验验证了不同 Schatten-$r$ 几何下的预期性能曲线。

> **TL;DR:** Provides the first **first-order optimal** sample-complexity guarantee for matrix-valued non-Euclidean optimizers (Muon, Scion) under heavy-tailed stochastic gradients ($p\in(1,2]$). By working directly in the native Banach geometry via Ball–Carlen–Lieb duality and Wenzel's martingale-type inequality (Theorem 2.4), the authors avoid the dimension-dependent norm-equivalence penalty. Result: Muon finds an $\varepsilon$-$\|\cdot\|_{S1}$-stationary point in $\mathcal{O}\!\left(\min\{m,n\}\,\Delta_1 L\,\varepsilon^{-2}(\sigma/\varepsilon)^{p/(p-1)}\right)$ samples, vs. $\mathcal{O}\!\left(\min\{m,n\}^{(3p-2)/(2(p-1))}\,\Delta_1 L\,\varepsilon^{-2}(\sigma/\varepsilon)^{p/(p-1)}\right)$ for Euclidean algorithms. A matching lower bound (Theorems 3.7 and 3.8) shows the $\min\{m,n\}$ dependence is unavoidable. LLM pretraining experiments on a 70M Pythia model reproduce the predicted Schatten-$r$ sweep (perplexity drops from 88.9 at $r=1$ to ~33 at $r=3,\dots,\infty$, with $r=2$ giving 40.2), and weight matrices trained by Muon empirically exhibit a Schatten $p$-noise ratio far below the worst case.

## 核心贡献
- 在**重尾**（$p$-th central moment 有界，$p\in(1,2]$）非凸随机优化框架下，给出 Normalized Steepest Descent with Momentum (NSSD-M) 在**一般 Banach 空间**的收敛分析，无需对梯度进行 clipping。
- 关键技术：使用 Ball–Carlen–Lieb 的 $p$-均匀光滑 / $q$-均匀凸对偶（$K_q(\mathcal B) = S_p(\mathcal B^*)$，$q = p/(p-1)$）以及 Wenzel 2005 关于鞅差序列在 $p$-均匀光滑 Banach 空间中的强鞅型不等式 (Theorem 2.4)，从而**绕过**以往把一般范数压回 Hilbert 范数再控制噪声的做法。
- 证明 Muon 在 $\|\cdot\|_{S1}$-stationarity 下达到 $\mathcal{O}\!\left(\min\{m,n\}\,\Delta_1 L\varepsilon^{-2}(\sigma/\varepsilon)^{p/(p-1)}\right)$ 样本复杂度，比核范数 stationarity 下的欧氏方法省去一个 $\min\{m,n\}^{(3p-2)/(2(p-1))-1}$ 量级的维度因子；并支持在更弱的 $(L_0, L_1)$-smoothness 假设下成立（Section C）。
- 给出**一阶最优性下界** (Theorems 3.7 和 3.8)：在 $\min\{m,n\}$ 维度依赖下，所有一阶随机算法对 $\|\cdot\|_{S1}$-stationarity 都不能做得更好。
- 70M Pythia + FineWeb-Edu 预训练实验：(i) Schatten-$r$ 扫描显示 $r$ 从 1 升到 3 时验证困惑度从 88.9 降到 34.5，之后基本平台化，与假设的 $p\approx 1.5$ 重尾一致；(ii) 测得 $\sigma_{S1,p}/\sigma_{F,p}$ 比远小于最坏 $\sqrt{\min\{m,n\}}\!\approx\!22.5$，且初始化时更低，解释 Muon 在训练早期特别有效的观察。
- 旁贡献 (Section D)：把 NSSD-M 的收敛分析扩展到带 weight decay 的随机条件梯度设置；为分析一般范数算法提供独立可用的技术工具。

## Key Contributions
- First **first-order optimal** sample complexity for matrix-valued non-Euclidean optimizers (Muon, Scion) under heavy-tailed noise, in a general Banach-space setting.
- Replaces the standard Hilbert-space martingale reduction with a $p$-uniform-smoothness / $q$-uniform-convexity duality (Ball–Carlen–Lieb) and Wenzel-type martingale inequalities, avoiding dimension-dependent norm-equivalence constants.
- Theorem 3.5: Muon reaches $\varepsilon$-$\|\cdot\|_{S1}$-stationarity in $\mathcal{O}\!\left(\min\{m,n\}\,\Delta_1 L\varepsilon^{-2}(\sigma/\varepsilon)^{p/(p-1)}\right)$ samples — saving a $\min\{m,n\}^{(3p-2)/(2(p-1))-1}$ factor over Euclidean algorithms.
- Optimality (Theorems 3.7, 3.8): a first-order lower bound $\Omega\!\left(\min\{m,n\}(\sigma/\varepsilon)^{p/(p-1)}/\varepsilon^2\right)$ for $\|\cdot\|_{S1}$-stationarity, leaving the $\min\{m,n\}$ unavoidable.
- All upper bounds extend to the weaker $(L_0,L_1)$-smoothness assumption.
- 70M-parameter Pythia pretraining on FineWeb-Edu (Chinchilla-optimal 1.4B tokens) validates: a Schatten-$r$ sweep with $r\in\{1, 8/7, 4/3, 3/2, 2, 3, 4, 8, \infty\}$ shows a sharp perplexity drop from 88.9 (r=1) to 34.5 (r=3) and a plateau thereafter (33.0 at r=8, 33.3 at r=∞), consistent with an effective tail index $p\!\approx\!1.5$.
- Empirically, the $\sigma_{S1,p}/\sigma_{F,p}$ ratio across Muon-trained matrices is well below the worst case $\sqrt{\min\{m,n\}}\!\approx\!22.5$, and is smaller at initialization than at the final checkpoint — offering a plausible explanation for Muon's training-start advantage (J. Liu et al., 2025).
- Embeddings exhibit the opposite pattern in $\ell$-geometry, consistent with the common practice of using Adam (ℓ∞-steepest descent) for embedding layers.

## Methodology

**Problem setup.** Unconstrained stochastic optimization $\min_{x\in\mathcal B} F(x) := \mathbb E_\xi[f(x,\xi)]$ over a Banach space $\mathcal B$ (e.g., $\mathbb R^d$ or $\mathbb R^{m\times n}$), with heavy-tailed stochastic gradients:
$$\mathbb E[\nabla f(x,\xi)] = \nabla F(x), \quad \mathbb E[\|\nabla f(x,\xi)-\nabla F(x)\|_*^p]^{1/p} \le \sigma,\quad p\in(1,2].$$

**Algorithm.** Normalized Steepest Descent with Momentum (NSSD-M):
$$m_t \leftarrow \beta_t m_{t-1} + (1-\beta_t)\nabla f(x_t,\xi_t), \qquad x_{t+1} \leftarrow x_t + \eta_t \arg\min_{\|d\|\le 1}\langle m_t,d\rangle,$$
with $\mathcal B = (\mathbb R^{m\times n},\|\cdot\|_{S\infty})$ recovering (idealized) Muon, and $\mathcal B = (\mathbb R^d,\|\cdot\|_{\ell\infty})$ recovering Signum.

**Key analytical tools.**
- *Geometry of norms.* $p$-uniform smoothness constant $S_p(\mathcal X)$ and $q$-uniform convexity constant $K_q(\mathcal X)$ (Ball–Carlen–Lieb, 1994). For $\ell_r$/Schatten-$r$ spaces, $S_r = 1$ for $r\le 2$ and $K_r = 1$ for $r\ge 2$. The duality $K_q(\mathcal B) = S_p(\mathcal B^*)$ for $1/p+1/q=1$ lets the authors verify smoothness in the dual via a convexity check on the primal — often more natural.
- *Martingale-type inequality (Wenzel, 2005; Theorem 2.4).* For a martingale-difference sequence $(Y_t)$ in $\mathcal B^*$ with $\mathcal B^*$ $p$-uniformly smooth, $\mathbb E\|\sum Y_t\|_*^p \le S_p(\mathcal B^*)^p \sum \mathbb E\|Y_t\|_*^p$. This is the tool that controls the gradient noise in the native geometry, sidestepping norm-equivalence to a Hilbert space.
- *Algorithm-specific moment bound (Lemma 3.3).* Unrolling the momentum recursion decomposes the gradient-estimator deviation $m_t - \nabla F(x_t)$ into a martingale-difference part (controlled by Theorem 2.4) and a deterministic smoothness part.
- *Schedule.* $\eta_t \equiv \sqrt{\Delta_1(1-\beta)/(LT)}$ and a $\beta_t$ that decreases as $T^{-1/p}$ (more aggressive momentum as the budget grows) are used to balance the two error terms.

**Lower bound.** The framework of Arjevani et al. (2023): a "coupon collector" hard instance (Theorem 3.7) on $m\times n$ matrices with $\kappa = \min\{m,n\}$ standard-basis rank-1 elements forces any first-order algorithm to spend $\Omega(\kappa q)$ samples learning all signs before reaching $\varepsilon$-$\|\cdot\|_{S1}$-stationarity. A high-dimensional construction (Theorem 3.8) lifts Arjevani et al.'s $\mathbb R^d$ lower bound to matrices, achieving the optimal $\varepsilon$ dependence.

## Results & Highlights

**Sample complexity (Theorem 3.5 / Cor 3.2).** For $\mathcal B = (\mathbb R^{m\times n},\|\cdot\|_{S\infty})$ (Muon), NSSD-M satisfies
$$\min_t \mathbb E[\|\nabla F(x_t)\|_{S1}] \;\le\; 9\sqrt{\tfrac{\Delta_1 L}{T}} + 14\,\min\{m,n\}\,\tfrac{\Delta_1 L\sigma^{p/(p-1)}}{T^{(3p-2)/(2(p-1))}} + 10\,\min\{m,n\}^{(p-1)/p}\,\tfrac{\sigma}{T^{(p-1)/(2p-1)}},$$
yielding a sample complexity of $\mathcal{O}\!\left(\min\{m,n\}\,\Delta_1 L\,\varepsilon^{-2}(\sigma/\varepsilon)^{p/(p-1)}\right)$ to reach $\varepsilon$-$\|\cdot\|_{S1}$-stationarity.

**Euclidean comparison (Eq. 5).** Under the same heavy-tailed + smoothness assumptions, Euclidean algorithms only imply
$$\mathcal{O}\!\left(\min\{m,n\}^{(3p-2)/(2(p-1))}\,\Delta_1 L\,\varepsilon^{-2}(\sigma/\varepsilon)^{p/(p-1)}\right).$$
For $p=1.5$ and PaLM-scale matrices ($\min\{m,n\}\!\approx\!10^4$), this means Muon's complexity has dimension $\sim 10^4$ vs. the Euclidean $\sim 10^{10}$ — roughly **6 orders of magnitude** smaller.

**Optimality (Theorems 3.7, 3.8).** The $\min\{m,n\}$ factor in the upper bound is shown to be unavoidable for $\|\cdot\|_{S1}$-stationarity: any first-order method requires $\Omega(\min\{m,n\}\,(\sigma/\varepsilon)^{p/(p-1)}/\varepsilon^2)$ samples. So Muon is **first-order optimal** for nuclear-norm stationarity.

**General Banach guarantee (Theorem 3.1, Cor 3.2).** For $q$-uniformly-convex dual norms ($q = p/(p-1)$), NSSD-M attains the optimal rate $\mathcal{O}\!\left(\Delta_1 L\varepsilon^{-2}(\sigma/\varepsilon)^{p/(p-1)}\right)$ for $\|\cdot\|_{*}$-stationarity in arbitrary Banach spaces (possibly infinite-dimensional).

**Experiments (70M Pythia on FineWeb-Edu, 1.4B tokens, 3 seeds).**

| Schatten-$r$ | Final perplexity ↓ |
|---|---|
| 1 | 88.89 ± 0.48 |
| 8/7 | 86.50 ± 0.91 |
| 4/3 | 67.61 ± 5.98 |
| 3/2 | 60.57 ± 4.48 |
| 2 (Euclidean) | 40.18 ± 0.09 |
| 3 | 34.51 ± 0.13 |
| 4 | 33.54 ± 0.04 |
| 8 | 33.02 ± 0.01 |
| ∞ (Muon) | 33.34 ± 0.02 |

Sharp improvement from $r=1$ to $r=3$ and a flat plateau beyond is exactly the predicted shape, with the inflection near $r = p/(p-1) \approx 3$ (for $p\approx 1.5$). Muon's spectral geometry matches the $r\!>\!3$ plateau while being computationally cheap (Newton–Schulz vs. full SVD).

- **Noise-ratio experiment.** Across all Muon-trained weight matrices, the empirically observed $p$-th Schatten-noise-to-Frobenius-noise ratio is far below the worst case $\sqrt{\min\{m,n\}}\!\approx\!22.5$, and is markedly lower at initialization — supporting the theory that Muon's gains arise from its small *native* noise $\sigma_{\|\cdot\|_{S1},p}$, not a dimensional accident.
- **Embedding layers** show the opposite pattern under $\ell$-geometry, consistent with the practical convention of training embeddings with Adam (ℓ∞-steepest descent).

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty: High.** The dimension-dependent penalty $\rho^{(3p-2)/(p-1)}$ in prior Muon guarantees is an artefact of the Euclidean reduction; the paper is the first to show that working in the native Banach geometry (via Ball–Carlen–Lieb duality + Wenzel's inequality) eliminates that penalty up to a single $\min\{m,n\}$ factor that is then shown to be unavoidable. The matching first-order lower bound closes the question.

**Rigor: Strong.** All upper-bound proofs are in the appendix (Sections C and D); the key lemma (Lemma 3.3) is given an explicit proof sketch in the main text. Constants are tracked but not optimized. The lower bounds (Theorems 3.7, 3.8) are stated for distinct algorithmic classes (randomized first-order and zero-respecting) and use the standard Arjevani et al. (2023) framework.

**Reproducibility: Moderate.** The theoretical claims are reproducible by reading. Code is released on GitHub (https://github.com/fhueb/ht-schatten-experiments) for the experiments. The Pythia/FineWeb-Edu setup is standard and well-documented. The Schatten-$r$ sweep with $r\!\notin\!\{2,\infty\}$ requires full SVDs, which the code presumably supports.

**Significance: High.** The paper is the first to *provably* explain Muon's empirical success in terms of an algorithm-geometry-noise match in the heavy-tailed regime. The Schatten-$r$ sweep is a direct, falsifiable prediction and is borne out by the experiments. The "free heavy-tailed lunch" framing is memorable and substantiated by the matching upper and lower bounds.

**Red flags / honest concerns:**
- The empirical validation is a **single 70M model on a single dataset** with 3 seeds. Muon is used to train Kimi K2 and DeepSeek V4 (cited in the paper); whether the same Schatten-$r$ sweep pattern holds at frontier scale is an open empirical question.
- The "weighted-decay ↔ conditional-gradient" remark (Remark 3.4) is sound, but the analysis assumes ideal LMO access — practical Muon uses approximate Newton–Schulz iterations, which introduce an error term the paper does not quantify. This is honestly acknowledged in the limitations.
- The lower bound's $\varepsilon$-dependence is matched only via a **zero-respecting** construction (Theorem 3.8) and lifted to randomized via the rotation argument; a randomized-first-order lower bound with matching $\varepsilon$-dependence is not provided in the main text.
- The "norm change in the noise assumption" — moving from $\sigma_{F,p}$ to $\sigma_{\|\cdot\|_{*},p}$ — is a non-trivial modeling choice. The empirical noise-ratio experiment supports it, but the worst case can still be $\sqrt{\min\{m,n\}}\,\sigma_{F,p}$, so the gain is conditional on this being a faithful noise model for the application.
- The result gives an optimal rate, but optimality is w.r.t. *first-order methods under $\|\cdot\|_{S1}$-stationarity*; the paper does not address whether $\|\cdot\|_{S1}$-stationarity is the right practical target, only that it is provably achievable.

## Strengths
- Tight, first-of-kind theory: optimal upper bound + matching lower bound.
- Native-geometry analysis that avoids the $\rho$ penalty in the existing Muon guarantees.
- General Banach-space result (Theorem 3.1) that recovers Euclidean heavy-tailed results (Hübler et al. 2025; Z. Liu and Zhou 2025) as a special case ($r=2$).
- Concrete, theoretically-motivated experimental design (Schatten-$r$ sweep) with predictions that match observations.
- Code released; experiment hyperparameters and seeds are explicit.
- Honest limitations section (Section 6) acknowledging the 70M-scale empirical restriction, the gap from high-probability bounds, and the ideal-LMO assumption.

## Limitations
- Experiments are at 70M parameters on a single dataset; the scaling question is open.
- Guarantees are in expectation; high-probability bounds under heavy tails are left for future work.
- The analysis uses an ideal LMO; practical Muon relies on Newton–Schulz approximation, whose error is unmodeled.
- The matched lower bound on $\varepsilon$ dependence is for zero-respecting algorithms; the rotation lift to randomized is standard but not proved in the main text.
- The paper is silent on whether other recent non-Euclidean methods (Shampoo, SOAP, PSGD) get comparable improvements under the same machinery.
- The "right" Schatten geometry depends on the unknown tail index $p$; the paper diagnoses but does not prescribe a practical estimator for $p$.

## Relevance
本文直接给出当前最常用矩阵优化器 Muon 的重尾样本复杂度最优性证明，理论—实验—最优性下界三件套齐全，与大规模 Transformer 训练中优化器选择、Adam vs. Muon 分层使用（embedding 用 Adam、其他层用 Muon）等核心问题高度相关，是跟踪 Muon/ScShampoo/Shampoo 等非欧氏优化方法机理的必读文献。

## Questions for Further Exploration
- Does the Schatten-$r$ sweep pattern (sharp drop then plateau) generalize to >1B-parameter models, and where does the optimal $r$ stabilize?
- Can a cheap, online estimator of the tail index $p$ guide the choice of $r$ at the layer level (matrices vs. embeddings)?
- Do Shampoo, SOAP, PSGD enjoy analogous Banach-geometry guarantees under heavy-tailed noise, or is something specific to Muon's spectral update?
- Can the ideal-LMO / Newton–Schulz approximation error be controlled inside the analysis to close the realism gap?
- Does the same machinery give a tight, dimension-aware high-probability bound (not just in-expectation)?
- The concurrent work (Y. Zhang and Lin, 2026) gives $\mathcal O(\rho^{(3p-2)/(p-1)} \Delta_1 L\sigma^{p/(p-1)}/\varepsilon^2)$ under Frobenius-norm assumptions. Is there a practical scenario where this weaker-but-simpler bound is competitive with the present, sharper one?
- Can the lower-bound construction (Theorem 3.8) be sharpened to a fully randomized-first-order bound with the same $\varepsilon$ dependence, removing the zero-respecting qualifier?
