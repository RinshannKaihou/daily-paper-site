> **一句话总结：** 本文提出"沉默失败"(Silent Failures)概念，将联邦基础模型个性化(FedFMP)中因隐私约束导致的六类难以察觉的可信性失效按数据/模型/系统三层因果组织，并通过12项基准的横向分析揭示联邦与集中式可信评估之间的结构性鸿沟，主张隐私保护训练必须与隐私保护行为审计配对。

> **TL;DR:** A KDD '26 position paper that names "Silent Failures" — trustworthiness breakdowns (bias amplification, confidence miscalibration, fairness collapse, adaptation misalignment, OOD degradation, alignment erosion) that emerge in Federated Foundation-Model Personalization (FedFMP) and remain invisible because FL's privacy architecture hides the per-client signals needed to detect them. The paper formalizes all six modes, maps them against three FL constraints (aggregation, limited data observability, statistical heterogeneity), and runs a 12-benchmark landscape analysis (6 federated + 6 centralized) showing a structural divide: federated benchmarks track system performance but ignore behavior, while centralized trust benchmarks need white-box model access incompatible with FL. It argues privacy-preserving training is not enough and lays out a research agenda centered on privacy-preserving behavioral auditing.

## 核心贡献

- 命名并界定"沉默失败"——FL隐私架构降低可观测性，使信任属性退化难以被现有评估工具捕捉
- 在三层因果（数据/模型/系统）下组织六类失败模式，并给出形式化定义（Bias函数、ECE、Disp、Lbase/Llocal权衡等）
- 通过12项代表性基准（LEAF、FedScale、FLamby、FedLLM-Bench、FLHetBench、PFLlib、HELM、BIG-bench、DecodingTrust、JailbreakBench、TrustLLM、HarmBench）的横向打分表，量化联邦与集中式可信基准之间的覆盖空隙
- 揭示三类核心FL约束（聚合、有限数据可观测性、统计异质性）如何放大或掩盖每种失败模式（Table 2）
- 明确区分"沉默失败"与对抗性鲁棒性/越狱研究的范畴差异——前者来自系统内部动力学，后者依赖白盒探针
- 提出可信FedFMP研究愿景：鲁棒聚合与个性化、可靠监测与审计、持续自适应个性化、严格基准与可解释性

## Key Contributions

- Defines "Silent Failures" as a *distinct diagnostic category* (not a single new failure mode) for trust breakdowns that FL's privacy architecture makes hard to detect
- Six failure modes organized by causal layer: **Data-level** (Amplified Bias, Confidence Miscalibration), **Model-level** (Fairness Collapse, Adaptation Misalignment), **System-level** (Out-of-Domain Degradation, Alignment Erosion)
- Formal definitions: e.g., `Bias(F) = |M(F,g_a) - M(F,g_b)|`, amplification when `Bias(F_agg) > Bias(F_pre)`; ECE for miscalibration; cross-client `Disp(F) = std M(F,P_k)` for fairness collapse; `L_base(F_k) ≫ L_base(F_pre)` for adaptation misalignment; concept-drift-based alignment erosion `S(F_k,c) < S(F_pre,c)`
- Table 2 cross-walks each failure mode against three FL constraints (aggregation, limited data observability, statistical heterogeneity) to show *how* the FL architecture conceals each mode
- 12-benchmark landscape analysis (Table 3) rated on a ✓/△/– scale; empirically shows federated benchmarks are biased toward system performance, centralized trust benchmarks require model access
- Clear scope distinction from adversarial robustness: silent failures arise from internal federated dynamics, not external attacks; adversarial evaluation assumes the very access that FL privacy forbids
- Research vision along four axes: robust aggregation/personalization, reliable monitoring/auditing, adaptive personalization + continual learning, rigorous benchmarking + federated explainability

## Methodology

- **Position / landscape-analysis paper.** No new empirical results; the contribution is conceptual organization, formalization, and benchmark audit.
- **Problem formulation.** A federation of K clients personalizes a pre-trained FM `F` (frozen backbone `θ_pre`) via PEFT adapters `Δθ_k`. Clients minimize a local objective on private data `D_k^(t)` drawn from a drifting local distribution `P_k^(t)`; the server runs FedAvg to aggregate adapters. The paper uses vanilla FedAvg as a *baseline for failure analysis*, not as a recommended strategy.
- **Failure-mode formalization.** Each mode is reduced to a measurable inequality (e.g., amplification criterion `Bias(F_agg) > Bias(F_pre)`, fairness collapse criterion `Disp(F_agg) ≫ Disp(F_pre)`, alignment-erosion accumulation across T rounds).
- **Benchmark audit.** Twelve benchmarks (6 federated: LEAF, FedScale, FLamby, FedLLM-Bench, FLHetBench, PFLlib; 6 centralized: HELM, BIG-bench, DecodingTrust, JailbreakBench, TrustLLM, HarmBench) scored on a three-point rating (✓ direct / △ proxy / – not in scope) across the six failure modes. The authors flag the ratings as involving interpretive judgment.
- **FL-constraint matrix (Table 2).** Cross-tabulates each failure mode against the three canonical FL constraints (aggregation rule, data observability, statistical heterogeneity) to specify how each constraint amplifies or conceals the mode.

## Results & Highlights

- **Six failure modes defined**, with formalization of each:
  - *Amplified Bias* — aggregation of conflicting local biases can yield **larger** global bias than any single client; quantified via group-fairness gap.
  - *Confidence Miscalibration* — local ECE rises after fine-tuning on factual noise; aggregation smooths the ECE signal at the server, hiding domain-specific miscalibration.
  - *Fairness Collapse* — cross-client performance dispersion `Disp(F_agg) ≫ Disp(F_pre)` driven by adapters specialized to divergent prompt styles; minority clients are statistically too small to perturb the global metric.
  - *Adaptation Misalignment* — local PEFT updates in safety-critical subspaces can degrade `L_base` of the frozen backbone; aggregation of misaligned adapters produces globally incoherent safety behavior.
  - *Out-of-Domain Degradation* — over-specialized adapters fail on other clients' distributions; FL limits cross-client OOD diagnosis.
  - *Alignment Erosion* — per-round small decrements in compliance `S(F,c)` accumulate across T rounds; per-client compliance is uncomputable at the server under secure aggregation.
- **12-benchmark coverage matrix (Table 3).** Empirical reading:
  - *Amplified Bias* — only HELM, DecodingTrust, TrustLLM measure directly; LEAF/FLamby/FedLLM-Bench give partial proxies.
  - *Confidence Miscalibration* — HELM, BIG-bench measure directly; **no** federated benchmark reports it.
  - *Fairness Collapse* — only LEAF measures it directly; most federated benchmarks give partial or no coverage.
  - *Adaptation Misalignment* — only FedLLM-Bench scores ✓; FLamby gives partial coverage.
  - *OOD Degradation* — DecodingTrust, TrustLLM measure; FLamby/FedLLM-Bench/FLHetBench/FlowerTune give partial coverage.
  - *Alignment Erosion* — DecodingTrust, JailbreakBench, TrustLLM, HarmBench measure; federated benchmarks almost all give –.
- **Three motivating trends:** (i) federated personalization is moving to large client populations where rare failures still matter; (ii) recent evidence that fine-tuning on as few as ~10 examples can compromise safety alignment [Qi et al.], and benchmark contamination can corrupt evaluations; (iii) tightening post-market monitoring regulation (healthcare, finance) where current FL tools offer limited support.
- **Causal-layer organization.** Failures compound across layers: data-level (bias, calibration) → model-level (fairness, adaptation) → system-level (OOD, alignment erosion). Adaptation misalignment feeds OOD degradation; both feed alignment erosion.
- **Distinction from adversarial robustness.** Silent failures come from *internal* FL dynamics; adversarial/red-team studies assume *external* probes with direct model access. The very access pattern that makes adversarial evaluation possible is what FL privacy removes — this is the structural monitoring gap.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty.** The umbrella term "Silent Failures" and the three-layer causal organization are useful conceptual contributions. However, the underlying phenomena (bias amplification under conflicting FL objectives, calibration drift under distribution shift, catastrophic forgetting in PEFT) are not new. The value is in *unifying* them under a deployer-facing label and forcing the conversation about "privacy-preserving training is not enough."
- **Rigor.** The failure modes are formalized with measurable inequalities (e.g., `Bias(F_agg) > Bias(F_pre)`), which is a strength for an opinion paper. But the benchmark ratings are explicitly flagged as involving "interpretive judgment" — so Table 3 is a structured argument, not an objective measurement. There are no controlled experiments and no quantitative claims about prevalence.
- **Reproducibility.** N/A — no code, no experiments. The taxonomy is operationalized only at the level of "what inequality to check"; concrete evaluation protocols are punted to future work.
- **Significance.** Useful for FL researchers, deployers, and regulators as a shared vocabulary and as a research-agenda document. Will likely be most cited in the *position-paper* literature and as a motivation for follow-up empirical work; the field needs the shared vocabulary more than another PEFT algorithm.
- **Red flags.**
  1. The "six modes, three layers" structure is partly aesthetic — the choice of axes is not strongly motivated and feels like a 2×3 grid that was filled out. The cross-layer causal arrows (Fig. 1) are described as "conceptual influence rather than strict guarantees" — i.e., illustrative.
  2. No quantitative evidence that these failures actually occur at non-trivial rates in deployed FedFMP systems. A 1–2 case study tying real FL incidents to the taxonomy would substantially strengthen the paper.
  3. The "privacy-preserving behavioral evaluation" agenda is sketched but not operationalized — no concrete protocol, no candidate probing method (e.g., DP-counterfactual queries, secure-aggregation audits with auxiliary signals), no complexity/cost analysis.
  4. The adversarial-robustness distinction is clear in principle, but in practice the boundary blurs (e.g., a client with adversarial data can trigger Amplified Bias without any external attacker).
  5. The 12-benchmark selection is reasonable but not exhaustive — no mention of FedML, OARF, LEAF-Bench extensions, or the recent federated-LLM benchmarks (FederatedScope-LLM, etc.). The resulting coverage gaps may overstate the divide.
  6. The conclusion ("judge FedFMP systems by whether their failures can be audited under the same privacy constraints that govern training") is a strong claim that is not tested or even partially instantiated.

## Strengths

- Clean, deployer-relevant framing — the "silent" qualifier names a real gap in current monitoring practice
- Six failure modes formalized as testable inequalities, not just slogans
- Honest about the scope of Table 3 ratings ("interpretive judgment") — uncommon in position papers
- Useful distinction between silent failures and adversarial robustness; prevents the paper from being mistaken for a security paper
- Research agenda is concrete enough to seed follow-up work (robust aggregation, federated auditing, continual FL, federated explainability)
- Well-timed: post-market monitoring regulation is actively being drafted, and a vocabulary paper that names the problem class is exactly what the field needs

## Limitations

- Conceptual contribution only — no empirical validation of any of the six failure modes
- The taxonomy's taxonomy (three layers, six modes) is one possible organization; alternatives may be equally valid
- No concrete detection protocol — proxies, audits, or DP-aware probes are discussed only at a high level
- Two-author scope under-represents industry/regulator/auditor perspectives that would be central to a "post-market monitoring" framing
- Benchmark set is incomplete (12 of many); the divide is real but the magnitude of the gap may shift with newer benchmarks
- The paper claims to address deployment-scale concerns but provides no scale-aware analysis (e.g., how often these failures show up at K = 10⁴ vs. K = 10⁶ clients)
- The amplification criteria are inequalities (e.g., `Disp(F_agg) ≫ Disp(F_pre)`) — what `≫` means in practice is left undefined

## Relevance

直接相关：本文聚焦的"联邦学习 + 基础模型个性化 + 可信AI"三角正是用户研究的核心交集，特别是FL中基础模型的隐私-可观测性权衡、对齐退化的形式化、以及PEFT场景下的安全-性能权衡。文中关于隐私保护训练与隐私保护行为审计应配对的主张，对用户目前关注的隐私约束下可信评估方向有直接启发。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Of the six silent failure modes, which are empirically most common in real FedFMP deployments (cross-silo healthcare, cross-device mobile), and at what K and round-count T?
- Can privacy-preserving behavioral probes (DP-counterfactual queries, secure-aggregation audits with auxiliary signal models, on-device shadow evaluation) be designed to detect *each* mode without violating FL privacy guarantees — and what is the privacy/utility budget trade-off?
- How do silent failure modes evolve across communication rounds, and are there early-warning signals in the aggregated adapter statistics (e.g., update-norm variance, gradient disagreement)?
- What is the interaction between client heterogeneity (skewed label, demographic, prompt-style distributions) and the *severity* of each mode — and does adaptive client sampling mitigate without breaking privacy?
- How should emerging "post-market monitoring" regulatory frameworks (EU AI Act, FDA SaMD guidance, China algorithm registry) incorporate the silent-failure diagnostic category, and what evidence would regulators need?
- How much of the benchmark divide is fundamental vs. fixable? Could a federated evaluation harness expose adapter-level statistics (e.g., per-client ECE estimates, fairness variances) without compromising the secure-aggregation contract?
- Does the "Amplified Bias" phenomenon (aggregation producing *larger* bias than any single client) hold empirically, or only theoretically? Prior FL-fairness work suggests the direction depends on initialization and heterogeneity structure.
- How do silent failures interact with PEFT subspace choice — i.e., are some LoRA target modules more prone to alignment erosion than others?
