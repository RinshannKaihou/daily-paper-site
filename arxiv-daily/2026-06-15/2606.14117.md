> **一句话总结：** 把隐性联想测验（IAT）改造为 JSON 强制选择格式并提出两阶段贝叶斯分层模型（先建模格式遵从、再建模任务一致性），在 Claude Sonnet-4 / Gemini 2.5 Pro / GPT-5 上以 960 个 trial 证明联想干扰是**模型特异**而非 LLM 普遍属性：Claude Gender–Career 域 ΔP = 0.086（CrI [0.026, 0.173]）显著，Gemini 衰减，GPT-5 接近零。

> **TL;DR:** Cohen & Kincaid (U. West Florida) adapt the Implicit Association Test to a controlled JSON forced-choice format and evaluate three frontier LLMs across Gender–Career and Gender–Science using a two-stage hierarchical Bayesian model — Stage A models compliance (valid A/B response), Stage B models task-consistency given validity, with item-level random intercepts. Across 960 trials, compliance is ≥98% for all models (GPT-5 lowest at ~98%), so refusal cannot explain the differences. Conditional on validity, Claude Sonnet-4 shows clear interference in Gender–Career (ΔP = 0.086, 95% CrI [0.026, 0.173]), Gemini 2.5 Pro is attenuated, and GPT-5 is essentially null. Permutation falsification confirms effects are not item-driven. The work is a principled measurement contribution rather than a mechanistic one: it cleanly isolates interference from compliance but cannot identify which training/alignment choices produce the model-specific differences.

## 核心贡献

- **将 IAT 改造为 JSON 强制选择格式**：每次 trial 只允许模型输出 "A" 或 "B"，消除自由生成带来的格式噪声，并把"是否参与任务"与"是否完成任务"分离开。
- **两阶段贝叶斯分层建模**：Stage A 用 `Pr(valid) ~ block × model × IAT type + (1 | item)` 估计格式遵从率；Stage B 在有效响应子集上用同样的层级结构估计任务一致性，导出 ΔP 与 95% 可信区间。
- **置换验证（permutation falsification）**：在保持项目身份的前提下随机化 block 标签，refit 模型后所有 ΔP_perm ≈ 0，证明观察到的非对称不是项目组成或随机波动造成的。
- **跨三个前沿模型、两个 IAT 域的实证比较**：960 个 trial 的紧凑数据集，论文代码与数据计划在 Zenodo 上公开。
- **方法论论证**：把"看似中性的输出"与"真正的联想结构"区分开来，提示业界在 LLM 偏见审计中应将遵从/拒绝作为一等公民来建模。

## Key Contributions

- **Forced-choice IAT adaptation.** Standard IAT word blocks (Gender–Career and Gender–Science; 80 items per domain, 4 categories) administered as JSON-prompted binary choices ("A" vs. "B"), with 80 congruent + 80 incongruent trials per IAT per model.
- **Two-stage hierarchical Bayesian model.**
  - Stage A: `Pr(valid) ~ block × model × IAT_type + (1 | item)` — multilevel logistic regression with weakly regularizing priors (β ~ N(0, 1), β0 ~ N(3, 1.5), σ_item ~ Exponential(1)); item-level partial pooling.
  - Stage B: `Pr(task-consistent | valid) ~ block × model × IAT_type + (1 | item)` — same structure on compliant subset.
  - Fit in **brms/Stan** with NUTS, 4 chains × 4,000 iterations (1,500 warm-up), yielding 10,000 post-warmup draws. All R̂ ≤ 1.01; no divergent transitions.
- **Effect size on probability scale.** ΔP = P(consistent | congruent) − P(consistent | incongruent), reported with 95% credible intervals and one-sided posterior probability P(ΔP > 0).
- **Permutation falsification.** Block labels shuffled within model × IAT type, model refit; observed ΔPs far exceed permuted nulls (Claude Gender–Career observed 0.086 vs. permuted ≈ 0.004).
- **Empirical scope.** Three frontier LLMs (Claude Sonnet-4, Gemini 2.5 Pro, GPT-5) × 2 IAT domains × 160 trials = 960 trials total; all queried at default inference parameters.

## Methodology

- **Stimuli.** Adapted from validated Greenwald et al. (1998) / Nosek et al. (2002) IAT materials. Each domain has 4 conceptual categories (Science, Arts, Career, Family) with 20 stimulus words per category (80 unique items). Trials present a stimulus word + two alternative pairings (e.g., Male+Career vs. Female+Family); within each IAT, half the trials are congruent, half incongruent. Trial order randomized within blocks.
- **Response coding.** Outputs classified as **Valid** (clean "A"/"B") or **Noncompliant** (refusal, safety response, malformed, missing). A response is **task-consistent** if the selected pairing matches the stimulus's predefined domain. Coverage checks confirm task consistency is defined for 100% of valid responses.
- **Stage A — Compliance model.** Multilevel logistic regression on the binary "valid vs. noncompliant" outcome, with fixed effects for block, model, IAT type and their full factorial interactions, plus item-level random intercepts. Item-level σ_item = 2.01 (CrI [1.12, 3.12] on log-odds) indicates substantial lexical heterogeneity in which words trigger noncompliance.
- **Stage B — Conditional interference model.** Same structure fitted to the conditional outcome (task-consistent | valid). The primary estimand is the **block × model × IAT_type** posterior contrast ΔP, with P(ΔP > 0) reported as a one-sided posterior probability analogous to a Bayes factor–like summary.
- **Effect size reporting.** ΔP on the probability scale (interpretable) plus log-odds contrast S (compact), both with 95% credible intervals from the posterior draws.
- **Falsification.** Within-model, within-IAT block-label permutations refit to confirm that observed ΔPs are not driven by item composition. Under permutation, all ΔP_perm ≈ 0; observed effects (especially Claude Gender–Career 0.086) far exceed null.
- **Implementation.** Stan/brms with NUTS; 4 chains × 4,000 iterations; weakly informative priors (β ~ N(0,1), β0 ~ N(3, 1.5), σ_item ~ Exp(1)); convergence: all R̂ ≤ 1.01, no divergent transitions.

## Results & Highlights

**Compliance (Stage A).** Across 960 trials, compliance was uniformly high and did **not** differ by block or IAT type, validating Stage B:

| Model | Gender–Career | Gender–Science |
|---|---|---|
| Claude Sonnet-4 | 99.8–99.9% | 99.7–99.8% |
| Gemini 2.5 Pro | 99.9% | 99.9–100.0% |
| GPT-5 | 98.4–98.6% | 98.1–98.3% |

GPT-5 had slightly but credibly lower compliance (log-odds difference vs. Claude = −1.86, CrI [−3.06, −0.68]; ≈ 1–2 percentage points), and 5 of 80 items per block were noncompliant across all GPT-5 conditions. Item-level random intercepts capture substantial lexical heterogeneity (σ_item = 2.01).

**Conditional interference (Stage B).** The headline table:

| Model | IAT domain | ΔP | 95% CrI | P(ΔP > 0) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Claude Sonnet-4 | Gender–Career | **0.086** | [0.026, 0.173] | 1.000 |
| Claude Sonnet-4 | Gender–Science | 0.020 | [0.003, 0.057] | 0.995 |
| Gemini 2.5 Pro | Gender–Career | 0.017 | [0.002, 0.049] | 0.992 |
| Gemini 2.5 Pro | Gender–Science | 0.002 | [−0.002, 0.011] | 0.793 |
| GPT-5 | Gender–Career | 0.004 | [−0.003, 0.017] | — |
| GPT-5 | Gender–Science | 0.001 | [−0.003, 0.007] | — |

**Interpretation.**
- **Claude Sonnet-4** is the only model with a clearly non-zero ΔP in Gender–Career; the 95% CrI excludes zero and the posterior probability of a positive effect is essentially 1.
- **Gemini 2.5 Pro** shows a small but credible Gender–Career effect (CrI excludes zero); Gender–Science is null.
- **GPT-5** is essentially flat in both domains; both CrIs span zero and posterior point estimates are < 0.005.
- **Permutation falsification**: refitting on shuffled block labels yields ΔP_perm ≈ 0 across the board; observed Claude Gender–Career 0.086 vs. permuted ≈ 0.004 — a >20× ratio. The asymmetry is structurally block-driven, not item-driven.
- **Headline conclusion.** Associative interference under IAT-style constraints is **not** a universal LLM property; it is model-specific and varies by domain.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty — moderate.** The two-stage compliance-then-consistency decomposition is the real methodological contribution; prior LLM-IAT papers (Caliskan et al. 2017 is the spiritual ancestor, plus several 2023–2025 LLM bias probes) generally do not separate refusal from performance. Forced-choice with JSON is a reasonable if not entirely novel adaptation.
- **Rigor — strong, within scope.** Bayesian hierarchical model with item-level random effects, weakly regularizing priors, permutation falsification, convergence diagnostics, and per-condition 95% CrIs all check the right boxes. The sample size (960 trials = 80 items × 2 blocks × 2 IATs × 3 models) is small in absolute terms but appropriate for a controlled evaluation. **However**, the entire inferential engine rests on a **single prompt template** and **default decoding parameters** per model — there is no prompt-paraphrase robustness, no temperature sensitivity, no block-order counterbalancing (IAT order effects are huge in human work), no chain-of-thought or system-prompt manipulation. A single prompt per cell means any apparent model difference could be an idiosyncratic prompt-model interaction.
- **Reproducibility — partial.** Code is planned for Zenodo, but no GitHub link is provided in v1; the "data availability" section defers the public release to publication and points reviewers at an unspecified URL. Stimulus lists and model versions are concrete enough to replicate, but temperature / top-p / system-prompt settings are glossed as "default" — a reproducibility hazard.
- **Significance — meaningful for measurement, modest for theory.** The framework is genuinely useful for anyone auditing LLM bias; the headline "model-specific, not universal" finding is informative for practitioners deciding whether to spend effort on IAT-style probes. However, the paper offers no mechanistic account of *why* Claude shows stronger interference than GPT-5 — the discussion lists alignment, tokenization, corpus, and decoding as plausible drivers and explicitly disclaims disentangling them. The "GPT-5 ≈ 0" result is striking but could reflect alignment-driven masking rather than absence of underlying associations, and the paper acknowledges this only implicitly.
- **Red flags.**
  - **ΔP ≈ 0.086 in Claude Gender–Career is statistically credible but practically small.** A 9 percentage-point gap on a binary forced-choice task is well within the range that 5–10 paraphrased prompts could move substantially. The headline should be read as "robustly non-zero under this single prompt template," not "large effect."
  - **Two IAT domains only.** All claims about "model-specific" vs. "universal" are made on Gender–Career and Gender–Science. There is no race, age, religion, nationality, or intersectional domain.
  - **No prompt robustness.** IAT scores in human research are famously sensitive to order, instruction wording, and modality. None of these are probed here.
  - **Default decoding parameters** are explicitly used; nothing is said about how sensitive the null GPT-5 result is to temperature or system-prompt variation.
  - **The "ΔP = 0 in GPT-5" claim is not the same as "no bias."** The paper itself notes this in §4.1, but the abstract's framing ("associative interference can be substantially mitigated") leans closer to an alignment-success story than the data alone support.
  - **Compliance gap, while small (~1–2 pp), is interpretively interesting and underexplored.** GPT-5's slightly lower compliance might reflect the same alignment process that suppresses its Gender–Career interference — i.e., the model may be refusing a small fraction of items where associations are strongest, mechanically driving the conditional ΔP toward zero. This is not investigated.

## Strengths

- Clean two-stage modeling: refusal artifacts are quarantined from interference estimates, a real methodological improvement over prior LLM-IAT work.
- Permutation falsification is the right sanity check for block-dependent effects and is rarely included in LLM bias papers.
- Item-level random intercepts prevent any single stimulus from dominating estimates.
- Bayesian framework with credible intervals (rather than point estimates) makes uncertainty visible.
- Honest about what the design cannot say (mechanism, generalization to other domains, prompt sensitivity).
- Compactly scoped: 960 trials, three models, two domains — feasible to read and evaluate.

## Limitations

- **Two IAT domains only** (Gender–Career, Gender–Science); no race, age, religion, nationality, or intersectional domains.
- **Single prompt template per cell** — no paraphrase robustness, no order-effect counterbalancing, no temperature sweep.
- **Default inference parameters** only — no ablation over decoding strategies.
- **One model version per vendor** — no within-vendor version drift; results are a snapshot, not a stable property of model classes.
- **n = 3 models** — "model-specific" is established for these three families, not for LLMs generally.
- **GPT-5's slightly lower compliance (~98% vs. ~100%) is not interrogated** as a possible mechanism for its null interference.
- **IAT validity is contested in human psychometrics** (e.g., Schimmack 2021, Gawronski et al. on D-scores); the paper inherits this debate without addressing it.
- **Data not yet public** (gated to publication on Zenodo); reviewer-only link not specified.
- **No mechanistic explanation** for the Claude > Gemini > GPT-5 ordering; alignment, tokenization, corpus, and decoding are listed as plausible drivers but not separated.

## Relevance

该工作直接对应"LLM 公平性/偏见测量"与"评测方法论"两条主线。其将心理学经典范式严谨移植到 LLM、并显式建模了过往 LLM-IAT 研究中普遍被忽视的遵从/拒绝混淆问题，对关注 LLM 评测可靠性与审计方法的读者具有较强参考价值；其中贝叶斯项目级随机效应与置换验证也是可借鉴的统计实践。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the same ranking (Claude > Gemini > GPT-5) hold under **paraphrased prompts**, **different system prompts**, and **temperature/top-p sweeps**, or is it an artifact of one prompt template?
- Does GPT-5's slightly lower compliance (~98%) drive its null interference? Specifically: condition on the items GPT-5 *did* answer and compare to its rejected items — do rejected items have stronger stereotypical mappings?
- Can the two-stage framework be extended to **multimodal** IATs (image-text associations) and to non-gender domains (race, age, nationality, disability)?
- Are the model differences stable across **languages other than English**, where cultural association strengths (e.g., Gender–Career in Mandarin or Arabic) differ substantially?
- Does the result generalize to **non-frontier / open-weight models** (Llama, Mistral, Qwen) — would the ranking align with model scale, training corpus, or alignment recipe?
- Can the design be extended to **mechanistically distinguish** alignment-driven masking from absence of underlying associations — e.g., by probing hidden states or fine-tuning to "un-mask" associations?
- What is the relationship between **ΔP magnitude** and other established bias benchmarks (BBQ, StereoSet, CrowS-Pairs)? Are IAT-style scores predictive of, or orthogonal to, downstream bias behavior?
- Does the Gender–Science effect in Claude (ΔP = 0.020, just barely credible) replicate at larger n, or is it a marginal finding driven by 1–2 items?
