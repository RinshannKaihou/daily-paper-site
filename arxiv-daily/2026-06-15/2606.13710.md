> **一句话总结：** 提出 HOTE（Hybrid Open-Ended Tri-Evolution）框架，以 GRPO 在"工具使用 + 无工具"双模式下协同进化提议者、求解者、评判者三模块；8B 模型在三个长文深度研究基准上以更少训练时间超越 DR Tulu-8B-RL、Tongyi DeepResearch-30B-A3B 等 8–32B 强基线。

> **TL;DR:** HOTE is a tri-role co-evolution framework (proposer + solver + judge) trained with GRPO under a hybrid of tool-use and no-tool modes for open-ended deep-research agents. The judge dynamically evolves rubrics and emits "meta rubrics" to the proposer, who generates challenging but learnable synthetic queries; only half of training tasks in the latter phase invoke tools, cutting tool-use cost. An 8B HOTE model reaches 54.4 / 76.9 / 45.9 on HealthBench / ResearchQA / DeepResearchBench (avg 59.1), beating DR Tulu-8B-RL (56.0), Tongyi DeepResearch-30B-A3B (51.2), and other RL/self-play baselines, with ~34% lower wall-clock per step than DR Tulu. Ablations confirm all three modules are indispensable and that hybrid mode is the key to avoiding the parametric-knowledge over-reliance of pure no-tool training.

## 核心贡献
- **首个面向开放式环境的深度研究智能体进化框架 HOTE**，明确把"deep research"和"agent evolution"两条通向 AGI 的路径结合，针对无标准答案的长文报告生成任务。
- **三模块协同进化设计**：提议者（基于元评分细则提出难而可学的查询）、求解者（ReAct 风格的多轮检索-推理并产出带引用的报告）、评判者（动态更新评分细则并打分；采用 0/±1 的二值奖励以避免评分膨胀）。
- **Meta-Rubrics 压缩**：评判者对每条评分细则的逐样本打分过长，作者设计"元评分细则"将多个样本的评估聚合成共享评分集合，既驱动提议者训练，又作为合成任务的持久评分。
- **Diverse Proposing**：提议者基于同一组评估结果和前一步 N 个不同的任务组合生成 N 组查询，缓解训练数据主题塌缩。
- **双模式混合训练（Hybrid Modes）**：每步一半任务配置为 tool-use、一半为 no-tool，避免纯 no-tool 训练导致模型过度依赖参数化知识；同时通过附录 B 给出的期望最大生成时间上界证明 hybrid 模式更优。
- **训练效率**：HOTE hybrid 模式每步 753.3s（DR Tulu 为 1136.8s），总步数 1300 步；上限工具调用量显著低于纯工具训练。
- **经验结果**：HOTE-8B 平均分 59.1，超过 Dr. Zero（56.3）、SPICE（55.4）、GSPO（56.6）等 8B RL/自演化基线；用 Llama3.1-8B-Instruct 做底座也能保持领先。
- **可持续进化**：1200→1500 步 HOTE 仍维持上升趋势（55.4→59.9），而各基线早已收敛甚至下行。

## Key Contributions
- **First deep-researcher evolution framework targeting open-ended tasks and environments**, closing the gap between deep research (static, parametric agents) and agent self-evolution (mostly validated on verifiable tasks).
- **Tri-role co-evolution**: a solver (ReAct agent with think / tool / answer / cite actions), a proposer (generates challenging synthetic queries), and a judge (updates rubrics and assigns 0/±1 rewards); all trained with GRPO under hybrid modes.
- **Rubric evolution + meta-rubrics**: the judge maintains persistent + active rubrics per query, prunes / adds rubrics based on solver rollouts, and compresses per-rubric rewards into "meta rubrics" shared across a proposed query group, making proposer training tractable.
- **Hybrid-mode dual training**: per step, half of the batch runs in tool-use mode, the other half in no-tool mode; the paper attributes the gap to noise in search tools and the difficulty of joint tool-use + report generation.
- **Open evolving deep research** category: an 8B HOTE model achieves 59.1 average on HealthBench/ResearchQA/DRB, surpassing open baselines up to 32B and prior deep-research training pipelines, with 33% lower per-step wall-clock.
- **Generalizable**: switching the base model to Llama3.1-8B-Instruct (SFT'd on dr-tulu-sft-data) still beats the same baselines retrained with that base.
- **Sustained evolution**: while RL/self-play baselines plateau by 1200 steps, HOTE continues improving through 1500 steps (~252 GPU-hours).
- **Code release**: https://github.com/IQuestLab/ote.

## Methodology

### Problem formulation
A deep-research LM is a policy πθ with action space {think, tool, answer, cite}. The think action emits internal reasoning (`<think>...</think>`); tool invokes one of several search tools with arguments; cite embeds citations inside the final `<answer>...</answer>`. The solver rollout continues until `answer`. Both proposer and solver use the same paradigm; the proposer omits `cite` since it produces queries, not reports.

### Solver evolution
Solver πθ_s is initialized from DR Tulu-8B-SFT and trained with **GRPO + token-level loss aggregation** (following DAPO, Yu et al. 2025). For each query s₀ and a group of G rollouts {o_i}, advantages are group-normalized; the reward r_i is the rubric-weighted judge score from judge πθ_j:

  r_i = Σ_{(R,w)∈R_{s₀}} w · Judge_πθ_j(o_i, R) / Σ_{(R,w)} |w|

### Judge evolution
The judge is a *fixed instruction model* (Qwen3-235B-A22B-Instruct-FP8) for compute efficiency. Evolution is performed on the rubric set, not the judge weights. Each query has **persistent rubrics** (kept across steps) + **active rubrics** (updated via `R^active = Update_πθ_j(s₀, {o_i}, R^persi)`). The judge is prompted to add **positive rubrics** (new strengths explored by solver rollouts) and **negative rubrics** (common reward-hacking patterns), tracking both rubric gaps and solver weaknesses.

### Proposer evolution
Proposer πθ_p (initialized from Qwen3-8B) is also trained with GRPO. Given judge assessments and a group of solver responses, the judge emits **meta rubrics** R^{op_i} shared across proposed queries {op_i}. The proposer's reward combines (a) whether each meta rubric is satisfied and (b) the solver's max achievable reward, encouraging queries that are challenging yet learnable:

  r_i^p = (1/M) · Σ_{(R,w)∈R^{op}} [ 1 · (1 − E_{o_j ~ πθ_s(·|op_i)} Judge_πθ_j(o_j, R)) ]

Two issues addressed: (1) full per-rubric judge scores are too long for proposer input → meta rubrics summarize them; (2) different proposer queries lack shared rubrics → meta rubrics double as persistent rubrics for the synthetic tasks.

### Dual-mode hybrid training
- **Hybrid data**: per training step with batch B, B/2 are real tasks (from the 9K DR-Tulu ODC-BY dataset) and B/2 are synthetic tasks generated by the proposer in the previous step.
- **Diverse proposing**: N groups of synthetic queries are sampled using N distinct combinations of previous-step tasks to avoid topic collapse.
- **Hybrid modes**: half of each step is tool-use, half is no-tool (no-tool = single think + answer, no retrieval). For fairness on synthetic task scoring, the tool/no-tool assignment is randomized across the N proposing groups.
- **Curriculum**: 600 steps of pure no-tool mode followed by 700 steps of hybrid mode (1300 total).
- **Theorem (Appendix B)**: the hybrid schedule provably bounds the expected maximum generation time below a single-mode schedule.

### Training setup
- Base: solver ← DR Tulu-8B-SFT, proposer ← Qwen3-8B.
- Judge: Qwen3-235B-A22B-Instruct-FP8 (fixed).
- Hyperparams: batch B = 48, solver group G = 8, proposer group G' = 6, lr = 5e-7, max tool uses T = 10, temperature = 1, max response length = 16384.
- Compute: hybrid mode ≈ 753.3 s/step × 700 steps; no-tool mode ≈ 382.0 s/step × 600 steps; no-tool phase dominates the budget but is cheap.

## Results & Highlights

### Main benchmark (Table 1, full 1000 HealthBench + 776 ResearchQA)
| Method | HealthBench | ResearchQA | DRB | Avg |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| GPT-5 + Search (closed) | 59.5 | 78.2 | 50.7 | 62.8 |
| Gemini 3 Pro + Search | 38.0 | 74.3 | 46.3 | 52.9 |
| OpenAI Deep Research | 53.8 | 79.2 | 46.9 | 60.0 |
| Tongyi DeepResearch-30B-A3B | 46.2 | 66.7 | 40.6 | 51.2 |
| WebThinker-32B-DPO | 11.1 | 48.6 | 23.3 | 27.7 |
| DR Tulu-8B-RL | 50.2 | 74.3 | 43.4 | 56.0 |
| Dr. Zero-8B | 52.1 | 73.2 | 43.7 | 56.3 |
| GSPO-8B | 51.0 | 75.1 | 43.6 | 56.6 |
| **HOTE-8B** | **54.4** | **76.9** | **45.9** | **59.1** |

HOTE-8B beats the strongest open 8–32B baselines on every benchmark, and is within ~3.7 avg points of GPT-5 + Search and ~0.9 points of OpenAI Deep Research, while using an 8B backbone.

### Time overhead (Table 2)
- DR Tulu: 1136.8 s/step × 3.8 GPU·h
- SPICE: 1313.7 s/step
- **HOTE no-tool**: 382.0 s/step × 1.5 GPU·h
- **HOTE hybrid**: 753.3 s/step × 2.6 GPU·h
Combined HOTE 1300-step run is cheaper than DR Tulu's 1900-step run; theoretical bound in Appendix B shows hybrid mode gives lower expected max generation time than single-mode.

### Ablations (RQ2, Figure 5)
HOTE vs HOTE w/o judge-evolution (je) vs HOTE w/o proposer-evolution (pe) vs SPICE on the no-tool mode: SPICE/je/pe converge by ~1200 steps; HOTE keeps climbing. Proposer evolution also keeps synthetic task scores stable (Figure 6a), confirming proposer evolution maintains task difficulty (otherwise solver would memorize).

### Cross-base-model (RQ4, Table 3)
With Llama3.1-8B-Instruct SFT'd on dr-tulu-sft-data as both solver and proposer base: HOTE 33.1 / 64.2 / 35.1 vs DR Tulu 28.8 / 60.0 / 32.3 — still the leader, though absolute scores drop due to weaker base.

### Sustainable evolution (RQ5, Table 4)
Average score across the three benchmarks from 1200→1500 steps:
- GRPO 55.4 → 55.2 (plateau/slight decay)
- GSPO 56.4 → 56.4 (plateau)
- REINFORCE++ 56.2 → 56.0
- SPICE 55.5 → 55.4
- Dr. Zero 56.3 → 56.1
- **HOTE 58.0 → 59.9** (still rising)

### Dual-mode progression (RQ3, Figure 4)
- Hybrid-mode HOTE outperforms DR Tulu and HOTE-tool-only on both no-tool and tool-use eval.
- After 600 no-tool steps + 700 hybrid steps, the no-tool and tool-use scores track each other — collaborative progress, not trade-off.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty (★★★½)**: The framework cleanly unifies three lines of work — verifiable-task self-evolution (R-Zero, SPICE, Dr. Zero), rubric-based reward (DR Tulu, Rubrics-as-Rewards), and dual-mode training. The "meta-rubrics" compression trick for the proposer is the most original technical contribution. However, the overall *recipe* (proposer+solver+GRPO+self-play) is now a fairly well-trodden pattern; HOTE's edge is mostly in the rubric-evolution + hybrid-mode details.

**Rigor (★★★)**: The empirical sweep is unusually thorough for this paper type: 3 benchmarks × 11+ baselines × 4 baseline categories, plus cross-base-model and sustainable-evolution tables, plus time/GPU-hour accounting and a theoretical bound. **Weakness**: evaluation is entirely LLM-judge-based (judge is Qwen3-235B-A22B-Instruct-FP8), and the same model family is also used as the proposer backbone. This creates a **judge-model bias risk** — the judge may systematically prefer outputs stylistically aligned with Qwen-family models. The paper does not report inter-judge agreement, human spot-checks, or correlation with other judges. Citation accuracy / hallucination rates are not reported at all.

**Reproducibility (★★★½)**: Code repo is linked; training data is the public DR Tulu ODC-BY 9K set; base models are public; hyperparameters are listed. **Weakness**: the rubric prompts and judge-prompt templates (which dominate behavior) live in the appendix only and would need careful study to reproduce exact numbers. Judge prompts and tool wrappers are presumably in the repo.

**Significance (★★★★)**: If reproduced, this would be one of the strongest open-source deep-research results at 8B scale, and the time-overhead argument (33% cheaper than DR Tulu) makes the recipe practical. The sustainable-evolution finding (still rising at 1500 steps) is the most interesting result for the broader "self-evolving agents" thesis.

**Red flags**
1. **Closed-source + closed-weight judge** (Qwen3-235B) means rubric evolution is partially tied to that judge; swapping judges could shift results. No judge-robustness table.
2. **Dual-judge / cross-judge evaluation missing** — without it, the +3.1 avg over DR Tulu on HealthBench could partly reflect judge-family bias, since HealthBench is a Qwen-authored benchmark.
3. **No human evaluation, no citation accuracy / faithfulness metric.** Deep-research quality hinges on whether citations actually support claims; the paper does not measure this.
4. **"Less time overhead" claim needs careful reading**: HOTE uses 1300 steps; DR Tulu uses 1900 steps; per-step time is lower, but the absolute end-to-end compute is roughly comparable. The appendix-B theoretical bound only covers expected *generation* time, not training.
5. **DR Tulu-8B-RL is from 1900 steps** (footnote 1) — slightly unfair comparison since the paper only trains HOTE for 1300 steps. Could HOTE still rise further with more steps?
6. **Meta-rubrics cap M** = |R^{op}| can become very large; the paper doesn't show how it scales.
7. **No tool-use error analysis** — why does no-tool evaluation beat tool-use evaluation on HealthBench? The paper attributes this to "search noise" but does not measure retrieval precision or how often tool errors propagate.

## Strengths
- **End-to-end practical**: real ReAct tool-using agent + ReAct-style proposer, not toy.
- **Comprehensive baselines**: covers closed (GPT-5, OAI Deep Research, Gemini), fixed-pipeline, open static, open RL (GRPO/GSPO/REINFORCE++), and open evolving (SPICE, Dr. Zero).
- **Time/efficiency accounting**: most papers in this space ignore wall-clock; HOTE makes a strong cost argument.
- **Strong ablations**: w/o judge evolution vs w/o proposer evolution vs both, plus cross-base-model generalization (Llama-3.1).
- **Sustainable-evolution evidence**: the rising trend at 1500 steps is rare and important for the AGI-via-self-play thesis.
- **Open code**: github.com/IQuestLab/ote.

## Limitations
- **No human evaluation / faithfulness metric**: long-form report quality is judged by an LLM; citation accuracy and hallucination are not measured.
- **Single judge model family** (Qwen3) for both rubric generation and final scoring; judge bias is unmeasured.
- **Search-tool-dependent**: the paper admits no-tool evaluation often beats tool-use; real-world deployment depends on retrieval quality.
- **Dataset still anchors training**: even with proposer evolution, the first 600 no-tool steps use the DR-Tulu 9K set; "transcending" original data is future work.
- **Scaling unknown**: paper says HOTE evolution slows as training progresses; benefits at 30B+ not demonstrated.
- **3 long-form benchmarks are all English, written-report-style**; multilingual deep research is untested.

## Relevance
本文直接面向开放式深度研究智能体的自演化训练，与 agentic LLM、GRPO/RFT 训练范式、rubric-as-reward 以及双模式 SFT/RL 等核心方向高度契合。对关注 RAG agent 训练、长文报告生成、以及"自演化-自博弈"AGI 路径的读者价值很高。

## Questions for Further Exploration
- How robust are HOTE results to **swapping the judge model** (e.g., to GPT-4o or Claude Sonnet)? If the +3.1 margin over DR Tulu collapses, much of the gain is judge-family bias.
- What is the **citation precision / faithfulness** of HOTE-generated reports? Long-form reports without verified citations can score high on rubric judges while being wrong.
- Does HOTE benefit from **scaling beyond 8B** (e.g., to 30B-A3B), or does the rubric-based reward saturate?
- Can the **meta-rubrics be made fully data-driven** instead of judge-LLM-generated, to reduce dependence on a strong closed-weight judge?
- How does HOTE handle **multilingual or code-output** deep research, where current benchmarks (HealthBench, ResearchQA, DRB) are English-dominant?
- Is the **no-tool > tool-use** gap a property of the benchmark (HealthBench noise) or the model (parametric knowledge)? Could a retriever fine-tune close the gap?
- Can HOTE be made **offline (no judge LLM at training time)** by training a smaller rubric predictor jointly?
- Does the **sustainable-evolution** trend continue past 1500 steps, or does it plateau like the baselines? The paper's Figure 1(c) hints it might, but longer runs would settle this.
- Is there a **reward-hacking mode** the meta-rubrics fail to catch (e.g., verbose but empty reports)?
