arXiv:2605.16077 Clinical NLP Data Augmentation GPT-5

Can Large Language Models Imitate Human Speech for Clinical Assessment?

Si-Belkacem Yamine Ketir · Lenard Paulo Tamayo · Shohei Hisada · Shaowen Peng · Shoko Wakamiya · Eiji Aramaki

一句话总结： 针对日语小规模自发言语临床数据样本极少、HDS 分数严重右偏失衡的问题，本文用 GPT-5 以患者书面叙述为语义锚点生成 7 种口语风格合成独白，再经 SBERT 嵌入 + 余弦相似度挑选风格相近者做类均衡扩充，在 PLS 回归上将低分组（HDS 22–27）MAE 从 ~2.38 降到 1.85，且不损害多数组表现。
TL;DR: GPT-5 turns written clinical narratives into seven oral-style synthetic monologues per patient. Similarity-guided, class-balanced augmentation drives the low-score minority MAE from 2.381 down to 1.849 on HDS-R regression, while preserving majority-group performance.
Key Finding
−22%
Relative MAE reduction on the clinically critical low-score group (HDS 22–27) with similarity-guided augmentation vs. no augmentation. 2.381 → 1.849
Key Metric
MAE 1.849
Achieved with the best configuration: similarity-guided selection adding 5 synthetic samples per patient. RMSE = 1.726, R² = 0.482.
Why This Matters
Minority Group
Low HDS scores are rare, clinically important, and easy for models to ignore. The method improves exactly where errors matter most without regressing the majority class to the mean.
Critical Edge
n = 30
Tiny dataset, no significance tests, proprietary GPT-5, and the cohort sits in a healthy-aging HDS range — limiting generalization to true dementia detection.

Pipeline

30 Japanese elderly patients from GSK2018-A provide both handwritten and spontaneous oral narratives on the same prompt, annotated with HDS-R scores.

1. Written narrative
2. GPT-5 generates 7 styles
3. SBERT embedding
4. PLS regression

Oral markers (hesitations, syntactic simplification) are conditioned on HDS score band. Generated samples are filtered either randomly or by cosine similarity to the patient's real oral transcript.

  • emotional
  • fragmented
  • storytelling
  • concise
  • humorous
  • paraphrastic

Random LLM selection also improves over baselines, but is noisier and smaller than similarity-guided selection. Performance plateaus beyond 5 synthetic samples per patient.

Critical Assessment

The written → oral cross-modal framing is a clean twist on LLM text augmentation, but the underlying mechanism (style-conditioned paraphrasing) is well-trodden. The strongest conceptual point is that semantic fidelity, not volume, drives the gain.

Patient-level leakage control is done correctly. However, LOOCV on 30 patients yields high-variance estimates, no significance tests are reported, and the Gaussian-noise baseline is a single hand-picked σ = 0.02.

Replication is risky: GPT-5 is proprietary, prompt details are deferred to an unseen appendix, and outputs are non-deterministic. The Japanese SBERT model and GSK2018-A corpus are public, but preprocessing details are sparse.

Sample size: n = 30; low-score analysis rests on ~6 patients.
Clinical scope: HDS 22–30 is closer to healthy aging than dementia.
Missing baselines: No EDA, back-translation, or open-LLM paraphrasing comparison.
Style taxonomy: Seven styles are hand-picked, not derived from dementia speech data.

Bottom Line

This is a pragmatic, carefully evaluated clinical-NLP augmentation study. Its durable insight is that semantically faithful synthetic samples beat random ones in low-data clinical regression — especially for under-represented, high-stakes subgroups. The 22% MAE drop on the low-score minority is meaningful, but the evidence base is small and the clinical population only mildly impaired. Treat it as a promising proof-of-concept rather than a deployable pipeline.
