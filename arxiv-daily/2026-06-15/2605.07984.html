Where's the Plan? Locating Latent Planning in Language Models

Nicole Ma · Nick Rui · Stanford University

TL;DR: Using rhyming-couplet completion as a controlled testbed, the authors find that future-rhyme information is decodable at the newline in every model family — yet only Gemma-3-27B causally uses that newline encoding. Methodology

The paper builds a lightweight toolkit to distinguish two notions of planning: representations that merely could support planning (probe-decodable) versus representations that do causally drive generation (patchable).

Task. Rhyming-couplet completion: given a first line ending in r₁, the model must emit a second line whose final word r₂ rhymes, validated against the CMU Pronouncing Dictionary.
Dataset. 1,200 synthetic couplets (1,000 train / 200 validation) plus a Pile-derived negative control of 1,200 general-text sequences.

Three probes of planning

  • Linear probing — train classifiers to predict r₂ from hidden states at each (layer, position), with Wilson 95% CIs.
  • Activation patching — swap hidden states from a corrupt prompt into the clean forward pass and measure how often the corrupt rhyme is generated.
  • Two-stage path patching — isolate the path “last word → head → newline → output” by caching head outputs under a partial corruption and replaying them in a clean run.

Results & Highlights

Probe signal emerges everywhere. Future-rhyme information at the newline grows with scale in all three families.
Causal use does not. Only Gemma-3-27B shows strong newline patching; Qwen3-32B and Llama-3.1-70B stay flat at the newline.

The representational handoff

In Gemma-3-27B, last-word patching peaks early and collapses near layer 30, while newline patching rises to a peak corrupt-rhyme rate of 0.63 [95% CI 0.48, 0.78] around layer 33. This crossover is the handoff.

Controls hold

Zero-vector and donor-prompt baselines stay near zero (Wilson 95% upper bound ≤ 0.17), confirming the effect is rhyme-specific rather than a perturbation artifact. Steering-vector replication in Gemma-3-27B shows the same handoff pattern.

Sparse Circuit Localization

Attention-weight ranking from the newline position back to the last word isolates a small set of heads. Simultaneous patching at the newline plateaus at k = 5; two-stage path patching peaks at 57% corrupt rhyme rate, ~90% of the full-residual reference.

Attention-mediated. Top-k MLP patches at the newline yield zero corrupt rhyme rate (UB ≤ 0.04), so the handoff is not FFN-driven.
Not an exhaustive search. Attention-weight ranking is a heuristic; an all-pairs path-patching sweep could refine or extend the set.

Critical Edge

Strengths

  • Clean, controlled task isolating a forward-looking structural constraint.
  • Joint probing + patching standard plus zero-vector, donor-prompt, and Pile negative controls.
  • Cross-family × cross-scale sweep with appendices for every layer and size.
  • Replication under steering vectors strengthens the causal claim.

Limitations

  • Single task. Rhyme couplets are phonological; generalization to prose, code, or multi-step reasoning is unproven.
  • Architecture/scale confound. Gemma-3-27B is the only positive case, so “scale” and “Gemma-3 architecture” are not disentangled.
  • Small per-cell N. 5 prompt pairs × 20 samples yield wide CIs (e.g., 0.48–0.78).
  • Residual 10%. The five-head patch recovers ~90%; the missing 10% hints at a secondary, uncharacterized mechanism.
  • No code release as of v2 (11 Jun 2026).

Authors & Context

Nicole Ma and Nick Rui (equal contribution, Stanford) report correspondence at {manicole, nick-rui}@stanford.edu. The work is accepted to the Mechanistic Interpretability Workshop at ICML 2026 in Seoul.

Relevance

This paper is directly relevant to debates about whether large language models form genuine planning representations. It provides a falsifiable template for separating correlation from causation in interpretability and challenges the assumption that scaling alone produces latent planning.

Open questions

  • Does the handoff generalize to non-phonological constraints (JSON schema, code indentation, regex)?
  • Is it architecture-determined or learnable via fine-tuning?
  • How does it interact with chain-of-thought prompting?
  • Do other 27B+ architectures (Mistral-Large, DeepSeek-V3) show the same behavior?
