Be My Tutor: On-Policy Co-Distillation for Mutual LLM Improvement via Peer Feedback

Two specialist LLMs act as peer tutors. Each conditions its self-distillation on its own correct rollouts plus natural-language critique from the other — gated by competence and anchored to problem concepts.

👥 Woohyeon Byeon · Jiwon Jeon · Jeonghye Kim · Youngchul Sung 🏛️ KAIST 📅 2026-06-15
Headline Result
6/6
domain-student configurations achieve mutual Pareto improvement on SciKnowEval pairs.
Safety Margin
2.4×
lower "break rate" for cognizant tutors vs. incognizant tutors.
Key Finding

Peer tutoring can be algorithmically safe

OPCoD is the first on-policy co-distillation framework that guarantees mutual Pareto improvement across all three SciKnowEval domain pairs, with no native-domain regression.

Key Metric

SDPO fails 3/6 times; OPCoD fails 0/6

While SDPO degrades native-domain accuracy in half of all pairings, OPCoD strictly Pareto-dominates both GRPO and SDPO on every tested column.

Why This Matters

No more negative transfer

Practitioners can merge complementary domain expertise without catastrophic forgetting — the holy grail of multi-task LLM post-training.

Critical Edge

Still only Qwen3-8B × SciKnowEval

Pairwise only, narrow domain scope, ~27% feedback discarded, and no compute-cost analysis. Generalization is the open question.

The Two Safety Mechanisms

🛡️ Cognizance-Based Gating

Per-round relative competence check: a tutor only gives feedback when its cognizance gap Δᵢ ≤ τ. If the gap is too large, the tutee falls back to plain self-distillation, cutting off harmful cross-domain noise.

⚓ Feedback Anchoring

Tutor must extract a <concept> from the question and write critique without leaking the answer. Rule-based verification discards off-topic or answer-contaminated feedback.

Core Idea

"Reframe multi-domain LLM training as peer tutoring between two specialists."

Each student's on-policy self-distillation is conditioned on its own correct rollout plus natural-language feedback from the other student. Roles swap every round.

Ablations

  • Always give feedback: Pareto-improves, but is dominated by cognizance gating.
  • Never give / domain-only: Phys score drops below initial — fails Pareto.
  • Schedule: 2 rounds × 50 steps beats both 5×20 and 1×100.
  • Feedback kept rate: rises from 72.8% to 74.6% over training.

Case Study

A physics-stronger tutee mis-counts vibrational heat-capacity degrees. A chemistry tutor supplies the missing physical-chemistry insight — isolate vibrational contribution from measured CV — without giving the answer. The tutee fixes its reasoning and answers correctly.

Strengths

  • Clean, falsifiable Pareto objective.
  • Cheap closed-form gate: one validation pass per round.
  • Deterministic, rule-based feedback filter (not another LLM).
  • Honest ablations that disagree with initial hypotheses.

Limitations

  • Only Science Q&A; no math/code/open-ended generation.
  • Pairwise only; >2 students unexplored.
  • Base model: Qwen3-8B only.
  • ~27% of feedback discarded; cost of discarding unquantified.
  • No FLOPs/$ analysis.

Open Questions

  • Does OPCoD transfer to smaller students (≤3B) or other families (Llama, Mistral)?
  • How does it compare to model-merging baselines like TIES / DARE on the same pairs?
  • Can cognizance gating be replaced by a single forward-pass uncertainty estimate?
  • Does the framework scale to reasoning benchmarks (math, code) where rollout verification is harder?
  • What is the theoretical boundary on when mutual Pareto improvement is achievable?
