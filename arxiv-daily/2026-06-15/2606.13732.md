> **一句话总结：** 该论文证明在数据孤岛（医院联盟、专有金融机构等）这类"原始数据无法集中、verifier 只能看到本地有偏参考"的低资源场景下，递归合成数据训练中的样本选择机制会从"防坍缩"变成"加速坍缩"，并以幂律速率抹去多样性；作为初步缓解，作者提出在多孤岛间不共享原始数据、用 Wasserstein 测地插值与 Wasserstein 重心构造协作式代理参考。

> **TL;DR:** This ICML 2026 paper formalizes a subtle failure mode in recursive synthetic-data training: under *siloed* verification (each verifier only sees a small, biased slice of the target manifold — e.g., a hospital or a bank), the very data-selection mechanism that is supposed to defend against model collapse becomes the thing that **drives** it. The authors prove three results: (i) under top-α selection toward a local target, the variance collapses to zero a.s. (Theorem 1); (ii) the diversity decays at an asymptotic **power-law** rate, $\mathrm{Tr}(\bar{\Sigma}_t) \sim \mathcal{O}(t^{-\psi})$ (Theorem 2); and (iii) a Wasserstein-based generalization bound $\mathbb{E}[W_2^2(\mathcal{N}_t,\mathcal{N}_0)]$ and excess-risk bound $\leq 2\ell\epsilon W_p(D_t, D^*) + R_{D_t}(h_t; g_t) + \mathcal{O}(\ell\delta)$ (Theorem 3) show that any downstream predictor is hurt proportionally. They then propose two **Wasserstein-geometry-based collaborative schemes** that build a shared reference across silos *without sharing raw data* — Scheme I (geodesic interpolation) and Scheme II (Wasserstein barycenter) — and on CIFAR-10/STL-10/CelebA + DDPM, their methods beat Random/K-means/CenterMatch/CovMatch baselines, with Scheme I reaching FID 71 on CIFAR-10 (vs. 102–116 for baselines) and FID 65 on STL-10.

## 核心贡献

- **概念反转：把"选择诱发坍缩"形式化。** 在数据孤岛/低资源验证场景下，本地参考本质上是有偏且碎片化的，选择机制反而成为塌缩驱动器，而非保护机制。
- **三种定理严格刻画坍缩动力学。** 定理 1（top-α selection 下协方差几乎处处坍缩到 0）、定理 2（$\mathrm{Tr}(\bar{\Sigma}_t)/\mathrm{Tr}(\bar{\Sigma}_0)\sim \mathcal{O}(t^{-\psi})$ 的幂律衰减速率）、定理 3（基于 Wasserstein 的下游泛化上界）。
- **Wasserstein 几何 + 数据孤岛的桥梁。** 利用 McCann 插值、Wasserstein 重心、Wasserstein 测地线三大性质，在不交换原始数据的前提下实现多方协作。
- **两种互补的代理参考构造。** Scheme I（测地插值代理 + 贪心子模选择，近似比 $(1-1/e)$）+ Scheme II（Wasserstein 重心，离线可分摊、迭代合成数据生成时更便宜）。
- **多数据集 + 多基线实验。** 在 CIFAR-10、STL-10、CelebA 上以 DDPM 训练，对照 Random / K-means / CenterMatch / CovMatch，并报告 FID / Precision / Recall。
- **可量化、可分析的计算复杂度。** 给出并行 wall-clock 复杂度 $\mathcal{O}(RL(N+M+S)S+nNK)$ 与 $\mathcal{O}(TLMS+LNS)$，并实测随候选集 / 参考集 / 客户端数线性扩展。
- **凸优化安全保证。** Scheme I 的协同选择被建模为单调子模最大化问题，给出近似比保证；Scheme II 的重心迭代被证明单调递减并收敛。

## Key Contributions

- **Reframes selection as a potential *cause* of collapse, not just a cure.** In low-resource verification regimes, the verifier sees only a small, fragmented, biased slice of the target manifold; selection under such a reference preferentially retains samples aligned with the local manifold and prunes globally relevant tail modes.
- **Three theoretical results (Replace vs. Accumulate paradigms):**
  - Proposition 1 (Shumailov et al.): Replace paradigm → covariance $\Sigma_t \xrightarrow{a.s.} 0$, $E[W_2^2(\mathcal{N}_t,\mathcal{N}_0)] \to \infty$.
  - Proposition 2 (Kazdan et al. 2025): Accumulate paradigm → no collapse, $E[\bar{\Sigma}_t]\to \alpha_n \bar{\Sigma}_0$.
  - Theorem 1 (this paper): Accumulate + top-α selection → mean aligns with $u^*$, but $\bar{\Sigma}_t \xrightarrow{a.s.} 0$ and $E[W_2^2(\mathcal{N}_t,\mathcal{N}_0)]\to \|u^*-\bar{\mu}_0\|^2 + \mathrm{Tr}(\bar{\Sigma}_0)$.
  - Theorem 2: Asymptotic power-law decay $\mathrm{Tr}(\bar{\Sigma}_t) = \mathcal{O}(t^{-\psi})$ for a uniform spectral gap $\psi > 0$ of the dissipation matrix $\Psi_{t-1} = I_d - (B_{t-1} + a_{t-1}a_{t-1}^\top)$.
  - Theorem 3: Generalization bound $R_{D^*}(h_t; g^*) \leq 2\ell\epsilon W_p(D_t, D^*) + R_{D_t}(h_t; g_t) + \mathcal{O}(\ell\delta)$, with $(\epsilon, \delta)$-probabilistic cross-Lipschitzness between local and global supervision.
- **Wasserstein-geometry-based collaborative framework.** Uses three OT properties — Wasserstein barycenter, McCann interpolation, Wasserstein geodesics — to compute scores/proxies *without exchanging raw data*.
- **Two schemes with different cost profiles:**
  - **Scheme I (Collaborative Geodesic Interpolation):** online, more accurate, but must recompute the interpolation proxy when the candidate pool $P$ changes.
  - **Scheme II (Collaborative Wasserstein Barycenter):** offline barycenter estimation, decoupled from the candidate pool; reusable across rounds of synthetic-data generation.
- **Submodular selection formulation (Eq. 24).** $\max_{|I|\le n} \sum_k g(\sum_{i\in I}(1 - \tilde{S}_k(x_i)))$ with a non-decreasing concave $g$ (e.g., $\log(1+z)$), greedy $(1-1/e)$ approximation (Nemhauser et al., 1978).
- **Empirical validation across 3 image datasets and 4 baselines.** Table 1: Scheme I reaches FID **71** on CIFAR-10 (vs. 102/116/115/106 for K-means/CenterMatch/CovMatch/Random), **65** on STL-10 (vs. 89/111/131/95), and **69** on CelebA. The contrast with CelebA — where all methods do reasonably well because face data is highly structured — is itself an interesting diagnostic.

## Methodology

**Setup.** Two recursive-training paradigms are studied: **Replace** (each generation replaces the prior) and **Accumulate** (each generation is added to the cumulative pool). For tractability, samples are modeled as $X_{i,t} = \bar{\mu}_{t-1} + \bar{\Sigma}_{t-1}^{1/2} z_{i,t}$ (Gaussian), with selection defined via a score function $U(x)$ that is locally concave around a target state $x=u^*$.

**Selection mechanism (Section 4.1).** Wasserstein-gradient-based scoring: for each silo $k$ holding real data $Q_k$ and a candidate pool $P$, the score is
$$S_k(x_i) = \frac{\partial W_p(P, \xi^*_k)}{\partial P(x_i)} = f^*(x_i) - \frac{1}{N-1}\sum_{j\neq i} f^*(x_j),$$
where $\xi^*_k$ is a **proxy interpolant** along the Wasserstein geodesic between $P$ and $Q_k$, *not the raw distribution $Q_k$ itself*.

**Three key OT properties used:**
- **Wasserstein Barycenter** $Q^* = \arg\min_Q \sum_k \lambda_k W_p^p(Q, Q_k)$.
- **McCann Interpolation:** $P_t = ((1-t)I_d + tT)_{\#}P_0$ via the OT map $T$.
- **Wasserstein Geodesics:** $W_p(P,Q) = W_p(P,\xi^*) + W_p(\xi^*,Q)$ for any $\xi^*$ on the geodesic.

**Scheme I — Collaborative Geodesic Interpolation (Section 4.3).** For each silo $k$, build the proxy $\xi^*_k$ on the geodesic between $P$ and $Q_k$ via a round of barycentric projection $\xi^{(r)}_k$ (Figure 2). Score candidates with $S_k$, then solve a **monotone submodular maximization** (Eq. 24) to pick a non-redundant batch, with $(1-1/e)$ approximation.

**Scheme II — Collaborative Wasserstein Barycenter Estimation (Section 4.4).** Iteratively estimate a single shared proxy $\xi^{(r)}$:
1. Broadcast $\xi^{(r)}$ to all silos.
2. Each silo computes its own geodesic interpolation $\xi^{(r)}_k$ between $\xi^{(r)}$ and $Q_k$.
3. Server updates $\xi^{(r+1)} = \sum_k (1/K) \xi^{(r)}_k$.
**Theorem 5 (Monotonicity & Convergence):** under standard regularity, the surrogate loss $F^{(r)} = \sum_k W_p^p(\xi^{(r)}, Q_k)$ is monotonically non-increasing and the iterates converge to the true Wasserstein barycenter $Q^*$.

**Complexity (Theorem 6).** With Sinkhorn OT, parallel wall-clock:
- Scheme I: $T_{\text{I}} = \mathcal{O}(RL(N+M+S)S + nNK)$.
- Scheme II: $T_{\text{II}} = \mathcal{O}(TLMS + LNS)$.

The key practical insight is **decoupling**: Scheme II's barycenter proxy is *independent of the candidate pool $N$*, so it can be amortized across many rounds of synthetic-data generation, whereas Scheme I must recompute per round.

**Experimental protocol.** DDPM (Ho et al., 2020) trained on CIFAR-10, STL-10, CelebA. The training set ($n=50{,}000$) is *not* added to the pool; instead, the data is distributed across $K=10$ parties to serve as local reference sets (STL-10: 5,000 labeled across 10). Main paradigm: **Accumulate-Subsample** — select $n$ from $N=4n$ generated candidates, then subsample $n$ for training. ExDir$(\alpha=1.0)$ partitions model non-IID clients. Metrics: FID, Precision, Recall (Inception-V3, Kynkäänniemi et al., 2019).

## Results & Highlights

- **Headline: Scheme I wins on the difficult, non-IID setting.** Table 1 (after 10 recursive iterations with ExDir$(1,0.1)$ reference):

  | Method | CIFAR-10 FID↓ | STL-10 FID↓ | CelebA FID↓ |
  |---|---|---|---|
  | Random | 106 | 95 | 96 |
  | K-means | 102 | 89 | 87 |
  | CenterMatch | 116 | 111 | 87 |
  | CovMatch | 115 | 131 | 92 |
  | Scheme II | 85 | 69 | 75 |
  | Scheme I | **71** | **65** | **69** |

  Precision/Recall also favor Scheme I (0.60/0.58 on CIFAR-10, 0.66/0.71 on STL-10). On CelebA all methods are competitive, which the authors attribute to faces being "highly structured" so biased filtering still retains basic characteristics.

- **A counter-intuitive empirical confirmation of the paper's central claim (Figure 5):** "While selection baselines mitigate collapse in IID scenarios, they surprisingly **lag behind Random** in non-IID scenarios." On skewed partitions, even well-known methods (K-means, CenterMatch, CovMatch) are *worse than no selection at all* — a striking visual demonstration that selection can drive collapse.

- **Diversity erosion illustrated directly (Figure 5 left):** when CenterMatch is fed only the Airplane class as the local reference, a VGG11 classifier tracks the predicted class proportions over iterations. The Airplane proportion grows rapidly, while the other 9 CIFAR-10 classes are progressively pruned — exactly the "confirmation bias" failure mode the theory predicts.

- **Scaling (Figure 4).** Wall-clock time scales approximately linearly with $N$, $M$ (candidate size, reference size). The two schemes diverge in $K$ (number of clients): Scheme I grows with $K$ because of repeated client-specific interpolation, while Scheme II stays nearly flat because barycenter estimation is decoupled from $N$.

- **Power-law collapse (Figure 1, Multivariate Gaussian Modeling).** $\mathrm{Tr}(\bar{\Sigma}_t)/\mathrm{Tr}(\bar{\Sigma}_0)$ decays as a clear power law under the Accumulate + Selection regime, matching Theorem 2's prediction.

## Critical Assessment

- **Strong conceptual contribution.** The "selection as cause of collapse" inversion is genuinely novel and well-motivated by a realistic setting (healthcare consortia, banks). The paper frames an important but under-discussed failure mode in the model-collapse literature, which has mostly assumed a global verifier.
- **Theory is more detailed than typical for this area.** Three separate theorems (existence, rate, generalization) cover different angles. The power-law rate is a stronger empirical prediction than the usual exponential-decay claims — and the dissipation-matrix spectral-gap argument is a clean technique. However:
  - Theorems 1 and 2 are proven under **Gaussian** assumptions (Assumption 1 is "locally concave around $u^*$, initialization in local basin"). This is a real restriction, though the authors show empirically (Section 6, Figure 1) that the power-law trend holds in multivariate Gaussian simulation. The authors do **not** provide a non-asymptotic bound or a finite-sample statement.
  - Theorem 3 is an upper bound on *excess risk*, not a lower bound — so it tells you that collapse is bad for downstream performance, but not how fast downstream performance actually degrades.
- **Experiments are decent but limited.**
  - **Architecture:** only **DDPM** is tested (Appendix C.2). The paper makes claims about "Generative AI" broadly; the failure mode is most plausible for diffusion-style models, but it's unclear how it translates to LLMs (which the paper references for the "self-improvement" framing).
  - **Baselines:** Random + K-means + CenterMatch + CovMatch. These are reasonable but not the strongest. Notably absent: **oracle selection** (selection with the *true* full-data reference as a sanity-check upper bound), and any **non-OT** principled selection (e.g., influence-function-based or self-verification methods like Fu et al. 2025). The paper references those works but does not compare to them.
  - **Datasets:** three image datasets; the paper's framing applies to language models too, but no text experiments. CelebA results suggest the effect may weaken on highly structured data, which is a partial concession.
  - **Number of silos / silo heterogeneity:** $K=10$ clients with ExDir$(1,0.1)$ is one setting. The impact of extreme heterogeneity (one silo sees 90% of a class, others see 1%) is not systematically studied.
- **The "fails on skew" finding is the most striking result and is honestly reported** — seeing CenterMatch underperform Random in non-IID scenarios (Figure 5) is a powerful demonstration.
- **Red flags / things to watch:**
  - The mitigation is explicitly "**initial**" — the paper is upfront about this. Scheme I and II are *proxy* references, not true ground truth.
  - The authors explicitly disclaim **adversarial robustness** of the proxy (page 10, "if the collaborative proxy is poisoned or biased by a majority of participating nodes, the selection mechanism could enforce a collective bias rather than a true ground truth") and leave this for future work. In practice, this is a serious deployment concern.
  - The Wasserstein barycenter's convergence guarantee (Theorem 5) requires the local $Q_k$ to be the *same kind* of distribution; in practice silos may have very different supports or dimensions, and the paper does not discuss what happens in that case.
  - No theoretical guarantee that the proposed proxy is *closer* to the true global distribution than the local references — only empirical evidence.
- **Reproducibility:** the paper includes algorithms, complexity analysis, hardware specs (Appendix C.3), and a detailed Appendix C. Implementation will require the Sinkhorn solver and access to multi-client reference sets; the *Distributed* part is hard to fully reproduce outside a federated setup.

## Strengths

- **Conceptual clarity and motivation.** The "low-resource data silo" framing is realistic, well-formalized, and the paper is unusually honest about its scope and limitations.
- **Strong theoretical machinery.** Three theorems covering existence, rate, and downstream impact — more thorough than typical ML papers in this area.
- **Power-law rate is a useful, falsifiable prediction.** Power-law decay is a stronger claim than exponential and gives reviewers a clear empirical target to check.
- **Privacy-preserving mitigation is genuinely novel.** Using Wasserstein geodesics / barycenters to build collaborative references *without sharing raw data* is a clean idea that has practical relevance for healthcare/finance.
- **Surprising and well-documented empirical finding.** The "selection under skewed references can be worse than Random" result is a memorable contribution even if the baselines are simple.
- **Computational analysis is rigorous.** Parallel wall-clock complexity, plus a clean argument for why Scheme II is preferred in iterative settings.
- **Reproducibility-friendly.** ICML-published, with detailed appendix (algorithm pseudocode, hardware, configurations, additional experiments, non-Gaussian empirical evidence, differentially-private OT extension).

## Limitations

- **Gaussian-theory assumption.** All three theorems rely on locally-Gaussian or locally-concave score functions; the transition to real-world non-Gaussian distributions is left to empirical evidence (Appendix C.4).
- **Limited empirical scope.** DDPM only, image only, $K=10$, three datasets, no LLM or text experiments despite the "Generative AI" framing.
- **Baselines are not the strongest available.** No oracle selection, no influence-function selection, no comparison to Fu et al. 2025's self-verification, no comparison to FedAvg-style aggregations.
- **No theoretical guarantee that the proxy approximates the global distribution better than the local references.** Only empirical evidence on three datasets.
- **Adversarial-robustness concern openly acknowledged but not addressed.** A majority of biased nodes could poison the proxy — relevant for any real deployment.
- **CelebA "highly structured" data caveat partially undermines the universality of the claim.** Highly structured domains may already be robust to selection bias; the paper's narrative depends on the failure mode being broad.
- **The "submodular $(1-1/e)$" guarantee requires recomputing interpolation per round** (Scheme I), which limits scalability precisely where you'd most want it.
- **Privacy claim is unverified.** "No raw data shared" is shown at the algorithmic level, but no formal differential-privacy analysis is included (Appendix C.6 sketches a DP-OT extension but it is not the main contribution).

## Relevance

这篇论文直击当前生成式 AI 领域最受关注的风险议题（合成数据递归训练导致模型坍缩），并把问题放进"数据孤岛下的联邦/低资源选择"这一对医疗、金融、AI 治理都至关重要的现实场景，对研究可信生成式 AI、合成数据治理、隐私保护协作训练的读者具有较高参考价值；同时其 Wasserstein 几何方法对最优传输、联邦学习与数据选择三个子方向都有借鉴意义。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does the predicted power-law diversity decay $\mathrm{Tr}(\bar{\Sigma}_t) \sim \mathcal{O}(t^{-\psi})$ **change with the number of silos $K$ and the partition skewness** (e.g., ExDir $\alpha$)? Is there a critical $K$ or $\alpha$ below which collapse is essentially inevitable regardless of the selection rule?
- Can the framework be **extended to LLM-style training** (autoregressive next-token prediction with RLHF-style or Best-of-N selection) where the "manifold" is discrete and the "Wasserstein distance" is less natural? How would the "$\xi^*$" be computed in discrete token space?
- What is the **adversarial-robustness story**? The paper explicitly punts on "poisoned proxy" scenarios; is there a verifiable aggregation rule (Byzantine-robust barycenter, trimmed-mean transport) that preserves the privacy and convergence guarantees?
- Can the **proxy computation be made differentially private** end-to-end? Appendix C.6 sketches DP-OT, but the impact on Theorem 1's collapse guarantee is unclear.
- The baselines tested are simple — how do the proposed schemes compare to **stronger modern selection methods** (influence functions, self-verification, RLHF reward models) when the reference is *deliberately* incomplete?
- Empirically, **does the power-law rate predict downstream task degradation** (e.g., on long-tail benchmarks like ImageNet-LT) at a quantitative level, or is the relationship between distributional Wasserstein cost (Theorem 3) and task accuracy noisy?
- For **multimodal** foundation models, where "siloed" data is the norm (different modalities in different organizations), does the framework generalize? What is the right "ground metric" $d(x, y)$ (Appendix C.1) for cross-modal comparison?
- How sensitive is the Wasserstein barycenter proxy to **silos with very different supports** (e.g., one silo covers a face, another covers a satellite image)? Does the iterative algorithm still converge, and at what rate?
- The paper's submodular formulation rewards **coverage** across silos; could this be combined with **diversity-within-silo** objectives to balance global and local fidelity?
