Key finding · paper 2606.14673

Braun et al.'s "100 ReLUs in 50 neurons" toy model is an artifact of an unintended mixing matrix M — not genuine superposition.

Compressed Computation is (probably) not Computation in Superposition · Jai Bhagat, Sara Molas-Medina, Giorgi Giglemiani, Stefan Heimersheim et al.
NeurIPS 2025 Workshop on Mechanistic Interpretability · posted 2026-06-15
mechanistic interpretability superposition toy models ReLU networks semi-NMF
Key metric
σ ≈ 0.03
Loss scales monotonically with ‖M‖ up to this noise scale, then rises — U-shaped, minimum at σ ≈ 0.03. Clean data (M = 0) gives loss 0.0833, the naive baseline CC never beats.
Why this matters
If you cite or build on Braun et al.'s compressed-computation toy model, this paper effectively deprecates it: the "superposition" gain is just label noise from the residual stream.
Critical edge
No replacement toy model is proposed — the field is left slightly under-served; SNMF baseline leaves a residual loss gap, and the dense regime is deferred ("we did not explore this further").

Methodology

Sparse inputs x ∈ ℝ¹⁰⁰, nonzero with probability p drawn from [−1, 1]. One-hidden-layer MLP ŷ = W_out ReLU(W_in x), only W_in, W_out trainable. Same architecture across all conditions — only M changes.

CleanM = 0 → pure ReLU targets. Naive baseline 0.0833.
RandomMij ~ 𝒩(0, σ²), symmetric. Closest match to Braun et al.
Embed-inducedM = I − WE WET from the original residual architecture's fixed random WE ∈ ℝ1000×100.
TrainingBatch 2048 · LR 0.003 cosine · 10k non-empty batches · σ sometimes optimized jointly. No train/val/test split — deterministic synthetic data.
AnalysesLoss-vs-p curves (Appendix C / Fig. 7) · SVD of I+M and eigendecomp of M · cosine-similarity profile of W_out W_in projected on top singular vectors · SNMF of M for hand-coded weights.

Results & highlights

Four converging lines of evidence that the CC advantage comes from M, not from computing 100 ReLU targets.

Reproduction (1-layer MLP on y = ReLU(x) + Mx)Qualitatively matches Braun et al.'s CC loss-vs-p curve; symmetric random M matches almost exactly.

Strengths

  • Cleanest possible design: same architecture, three well-separated M conditions.
  • Multi-pronged evidence — loss scaling + geometry + SNMF + fine-tune transplant — all converge.
  • The algebraic simplification (residual CC ≡ 1-layer MLP on mixed labels) is genuinely useful; future work can reason about CC without the residual scaffolding.
  • Reproducible code released; tiny compute footprint (single-GPU not required).
  • Honest epistemic hedging ("probably") consistent with the strength of evidence.

Limitations

  • Does not propose a new toy model of computation in superposition — only invalidates one.
  • SNMF-from-M doesn't fully recover the trained model's loss or sparsity; a residual mechanism remains unexplained.
  • Dense-regime story is hand-wavy and partially deferred ("we did not explore this further").
  • Only a single task family (100 ReLU targets on sparse scalars); generalization to other superposition toy models is asserted but not demonstrated.
  • Small regime (100 features, 50 neurons) where linear-algebraic interpretation may be artificially clean.

Questions for further exploration

Does the same residual-stream-induced mixing shortcut appear in other superposition toy models (Elhage et al., Hänni et al. 2024, Bushnaq & Mendel 2024)?
Can a "clean" CC variant (noise-free residual stream, M = 0) be constructed that genuinely exhibits computation in superposition?
What accounts for the residual loss gap between SNMF baseline and trained model — second-order statistics, dynamics, or a fundamentally different factorization?
How sensitive are these conclusions to embedding dimension (1000) and task count (100) — does the M-shortcut still dominate at, say, 1M features in 1024-dim?
