> **一句话总结：** 针对日语小规模自发言语临床数据集中样本极少且 HDS 量表分数严重右偏失衡的问题，本文用 GPT-5 以患者书面叙述为"语义锚点"生成 7 种不同口语风格的合成独白，再以 SBERT 嵌入 + 余弦相似度挑选风格相近者做类均衡扩充，在 PLS 回归上把少数低分组（22–27）的 MAE 从 ~2.38 降到 1.85，同时不损害多数组表现。

> **TL;DR:** This paper proposes using GPT-5 to convert written clinical narratives from 30 Japanese elderly patients (HDS-annotated) into seven oral-style synthetic monologues per patient — each conditioned on HDS score and a distinct style (conversational, emotional, fragmented, storytelling, concise, humorous, paraphrastic). Two selection strategies are compared on a Sentence-BERT → PLS regression pipeline: random class-balanced selection (noisy but diverse) vs. similarity-guided class-balanced selection (top-k most cosine-similar to the patient's original oral transcript). On Leave-One-Out CV across 30 patients, similarity-guided augmentation reduces MAE on the clinically critical low-score minority group (HDS 22–27) from 2.381 to 1.849 while preserving majority-group performance — outperforming both no-augmentation and Gaussian-noise baselines. Random selection gives gains that are noisier and smaller.

## 核心贡献

- 提出"跨模态灵感"的 LLM 文本增强框架：将同一患者的书面叙述当作语义锚点，用 GPT-5 生成保留事实内容但带有口语化标记（犹豫、停顿、句法简化）且风格可控的合成独白，用于扩充极小的临床口语转写数据集。
- 系统对比了两种类均衡筛选策略——随机抽样与基于与原始口语句余弦相似度的相似度引导抽样——并证明后者在 HDS 量表回归任务上更稳定，对临床关键的低分少数类预测改进最显著。
- 在 Hasegawa Dementia Scale（修订版）连续分数回归任务上，以 SBERT 日语嵌入 + PLS 回归为基线，验证了语义保真型合成样本在 LOOCV 协议下不会引起过拟合（RMSE 与 R² 同步改善），并能实质降低低分组预测误差。
- 给出对 7 种语言风格（对话型、情绪型、碎片型、叙事型、简明型、幽默型、改写型）在高低分组上的分布分析，揭示相似度选择更依赖嵌入语义而非风格偏好。

## Key Contributions

- A clinically-grounded LLM data-augmentation framework that treats patients' written narratives as **semantic anchors** and prompts GPT-5 to produce synthetic oral-style monologues with controlled styles and HDS-conditional oral markers (hesitations, fillers, syntactic simplification).
- A side-by-side evaluation of **random vs. similarity-guided class-balanced selection** for picking synthetic samples, with the conclusion that semantic fidelity beats volume.
- Empirical validation on a regression (not classification) task — predicting the HDS-R cognitive score — on a severely class-imbalanced dataset (n=30 patients, 7 HDS classes with as few as 1 sample each).
- A style-distribution analysis showing that the chosen styles are governed by embedding similarity rather than by intrinsic style quality, and that the benefit comes mostly from reducing MAE on the low-score (HDS 22–27) minority group.

## Methodology

**Dataset.** 30 elderly Japanese participants (ages 72–86) from the GSK2018-A corpus. Each provides (a) a spontaneous oral narrative on "Please tell us about the last good thing that happened to you" and (b) a handwritten response to the same prompt, plus an HDS-R clinical score. The HDS distribution is severely right-skewed: most scores cluster at 28–30 (healthy), with scores 22–27 under-represented (some classes have a single patient).

**Synthetic data generation.** For each patient, the handwritten narrative is fed to **GPT-5** with a prompt that conditions on the patient's HDS score and a stylistic configuration. Seven styles are produced: (1) conversational retelling, (2) emotional/reflective, (3) fragmented with short segments, (4) chronological storytelling, (5) concise, (6) humorous/playful, (7) structural paraphrase. Score-conditional oral-marker density is hard-coded into the prompt: 28–30 → fluent/rich vocabulary; 25–27 → mild hesitations and simplified syntax; 22–24 → slower rhythm, vaguer expressions, more hesitations. Global constraints cap length at 150–1300 Japanese characters and require natural Japanese speech markers. This yields 30 original + 210 synthetic = 240 monologues.

**Selection strategies.**
- *Random*: from each patient's 7 generated samples, samples are randomly drawn to balance classes to 8 per HDS class.
- *Similarity-guided*: samples are ranked by cosine similarity of their SBERT embedding to the patient's original oral transcript, and only the top-k most similar are kept.

**Downstream pipeline.** Each (original or synthetic) transcription is encoded with `sonoisa/sentence-bert-base-ja-mean-tokens-v2` (768-dim). Embeddings are standardized and fed to **Partial Least Squares (PLS) regression** — chosen because of high dimensionality vs. tiny n. The optimal number of PLS components (~7) is determined by nested LOOCV on the no-augmentation baseline.

**Evaluation.** Leave-One-Out CV at the **patient level** (to prevent leakage: synthetic samples derived from the test patient are excluded from training). Metrics: MAE, RMSE, R². Random-selection experiments are repeated 30 times and 95% CIs are reported. Effect of augmentation scale is swept by progressively adding synthetic samples until each class has 8 examples.

**Baselines.** (1) No augmentation — PLS on original 30 transcriptions only. (2) Gaussian noise (σ=0.02) on standardized SBERT embeddings, fixed 7 components.

## Results & Highlights

- **All augmentation methods beat no-augmentation.** LLM-driven augmentation consistently outperforms Gaussian noise; Gaussian noise gives only modest RMSE/R² gains and slightly worsens MAE.
- **Best result: similarity-guided with 5 synthetic samples per patient** (RMSE = 1.7261, R² = 0.4824). Beyond 5 samples, performance plateaus or degrades — "fewer, well-targeted samples beats larger volume of less-relevant data."
- **Stratified MAE** (clinically most important comparison):
  - Low-score group (HDS 22–27): Baseline 1 = 2.381, Baseline 2 (Gaussian) = 2.378, **Proposed = 1.849** (~22% relative MAE reduction).
  - High-score group (HDS 28–30): 1.236 / 1.255 / **1.237** — proposed method matches no-augmentation on the majority group rather than regressing to the mean.
- **Random LLM selection** improves over baselines but is inferior to similarity-guided and shows higher variance.
- **No overfitting signal**: RMSE and R² improve in parallel, indicating genuine generalization gains.
- **Style distribution analysis**: high-score patients get a skewed, consistent style distribution (driven by embedding distinctiveness), while low-score patients get a more balanced distribution — suggesting the method works by preserving semantic proximity rather than by picking a "best style."
- Scatter plot of true vs. predicted HDS for the best model shows points reasonably tracking the diagonal across both groups, including the previously under-predicted low-score range.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty.** The core idea — using an LLM to transform written narratives into oral-style synthetic speech for dementia data augmentation — is a reasonable but not particularly surprising application of LLM-based text augmentation to clinical NLP. The cross-modal framing (written → oral) is a clean twist, but the underlying mechanism (LLM paraphrasing with style conditioning) is well-trodden in the broader NLP augmentation literature.

**Rigor.**
- Dataset is tiny (n=30 patients), and **LOOCV on 30 patients gives extremely high variance estimates**; reported confidence intervals may understate this.
- The 7 PLS components are chosen via nested LOOCV on the *no-augmentation* baseline and then frozen for all augmentation runs. This fixes model capacity but does not re-tune it for the augmented setting — a small concern.
- **No statistical significance tests** are reported between the three methods; the authors rely on visual inspection of curves and the stratified MAE table. With n=30 LOOCV folds, the apparent gap between 2.381 and 1.849 MAE on the minority group is suggestive but not rigorously tested.
- **Patient-level leakage control is properly handled** (synthetic monologues from the held-out patient are excluded from training) — this is good practice that is often missing in similar work.
- **Gaussian noise baseline is weak**: σ=0.02 is a single hand-picked value with no sensitivity analysis. The choice makes Gaussian noise look deliberately uncompetitive.

**Reproducibility.** GPT-5 is proprietary and the exact prompt templates / style description strings are deferred to "Appendix A" (not visible in the main text). Since GPT-5 generations are non-deterministic without a fixed seed/temperature and may drift over time, exact replication will be hard. The Japanese SBERT model and GSK2018-A corpus are public, but the full preprocessing pipeline (transcription cleaning, hesitation-marker normalization) is not detailed in the visible portion.

**Significance.** A niche contribution to a small clinical NLP subfield. The most defensible insight — *semantically faithful synthetic samples beat random ones in low-data clinical regression* — is useful but not surprising. The low-score MAE improvement (~22%) is meaningful for the task but should be read in the context of (a) n=30 and (b) only one dataset.

**Red flags.**
- The 7-style prompt design feels somewhat ad-hoc; the choice of styles is not justified against any prior psycholinguistic taxonomy of dementia speech.
- The "low-score minority group" analysis reports MAE over **6 patients** (HDS 22–27) under LOOCV; conclusions drawn from this group rest on very few held-out subjects.
- The paper frames GPT-5 output as "oral-style" with marker density conditioned on HDS, but does not provide any quantitative validation that the generated samples actually exhibit the claimed markers at the right rates (e.g., disfluency counts per minute). The validation is purely downstream regression performance.
- The dataset's relevance to "real dementia" is questionable: the HDS range 22–30 with a mean near 28–30 means **most participants are not clinically impaired**; this is closer to a healthy-aging speech corpus than a dementia-detection benchmark.
- No comparison to non-LLM augmentation baselines that are obvious and standard: **EDA (Easy Data Augmentation)**, **back-translation**, or **paraphrasing via smaller open models** — all listed as future work rather than baselines. The Gaussian-noise baseline is the wrong strawman.

## Strengths

- Clean problem framing: regression on a continuous clinical score (HDS-R) rather than the more common binary dementia/control classification.
- Honest stratified evaluation: separately reports MAE on the clinically critical minority group (HDS 22–27) rather than hiding behind global metrics dominated by the majority class.
- Proper patient-level data-leakage control in LOOCV — a frequent failure mode in this literature.
- Simple, well-motivated pipeline (Japanese SBERT + PLS) that is appropriate for n=30 and avoids overfitting.
- The "semantic anchor" idea (written → oral) is intuitive and is supported by the comparison between random and similarity-guided selection.

## Limitations

- **n=30 patients**: conclusions are statistical estimates under extreme data scarcity; generalization beyond GSK2018-A is unproven.
- **Single dataset, single language (Japanese)**: cross-corpus and cross-language transferability unknown.
- **GPT-5 dependency**: closed-source model with non-deterministic outputs; replication and longitudinal stability are at risk.
- **No comparison to standard text-augmentation baselines** (EDA, back-translation, paraphrasing) — the proposed method's advantage over simpler approaches is asserted but not demonstrated.
- **Synthetic-data fidelity is not directly validated** — no human evaluation or marker-density analysis confirms that generated monologues actually look like dementia speech.
- **Style set is hand-picked** and not derived from any empirical analysis of authentic dementia speech patterns.
- **HDS 22–27 "low group" may be largely cognitively healthy** — the clinical interpretability of the improvements is limited.
- **PLS is a linear model** with limited capacity to exploit any complex structure the synthetic data introduces; stronger nonlinear regressors (e.g., small neural nets, gradient boosting on richer features) are unexplored.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does the proposed LLM augmentation compare head-to-head with **EDA, back-translation, and open-LLM paraphrasing** baselines under the same LOOCV protocol — and on a larger corpus?
- Can **human raters or automatic marker detectors** (e.g., fillers per minute, parse-tree depth, type-token ratio) confirm that the generated monologues actually carry the intended oral-marker profile, and that marker density correlates with the assigned HDS score?
- Does the approach **transfer to a different language** (e.g., English Pitt Corpus or ADReSS) and to **dementia-positive cohorts** (more patients in the HDS ≤ 21 range), not just the healthy-aging regime of GSK2018-A?
- Would a **nonlinear regressor** (e.g., gradient-boosted trees on richer acoustic + lexical features, or a small fine-tuned Japanese BERT regression head) extract more signal from the augmented dataset than PLS?
- How sensitive are the gains to **which LLM** generates the synthetic data? Does GPT-5 → open-source Japanese LLM (e.g., Swallow, ELYZA) preserve the similarity-guided advantage, and how does this change the clinical-deployment story?
- Is there a principled way to **select or discover the style set** from authentic dementia speech data, rather than hand-crafting seven styles?
