> **一句话总结：** 本文提出用"对称性"为可解释性提供可形式化验证的公理化定义——通过推理等变性、信息不变性、概念闭包不变性与结构不变性四条对称性，将可解释模型表征为马尔可夫子范畴上的概率模型，并统一将对齐、干预、反事实推理为贝叶斯反演。

> **TL;DR:** This position paper proposes four symmetry-based axioms for actionable interpretability: (1) inference equivariance (commutation of translation maps between model and human), (2) information invariance (model retains only task-relevant info — sufficient statistics), (3) concept-closure invariance (model concepts share semantics with human concepts), and (4) structural invariance (model lies in a hypothesis class the user can reason about). Within a Markov-category framework, these turn interpretable models into a "sub-recipe" and unify alignment, interventions, and counterfactuals as Bayesian inversion. The paper subsumes well-known desiderata (sparsity, compactness, modularity, faithfulness) as consequences of specific symmetries, and disqualifies feature-attribution methods, unconstrained DNNs as concept predictors, and SAEs as failing at least one axiom.

## 核心贡献

- 指出当前可解释性研究是"病态问题"——既无可测试性也无设计原则
- 提出以"对称性"为核心的 Erlangen-Program 式公理化框架，给出四条对称性
- 用马尔可夫范畴（Markov category）将可解释模型形式化为 BorelStoch 的子范畴
- 证明对齐、干预、反事实推理都可以统一为贝叶斯反演
- 显式给出哪些方法被排除（post-hoc feature attribution、sparse autoencoders、unconstrained DNN 概念预测器等）
- 为可解释 AI 的安全/法规合规性验证提供形式化工具

## Key Contributions

- Diagnoses the ill-posedness of current interpretability definitions — they cannot be formally tested or designed for
- Posits an Erlangen-Program-style (Klein 1893) approach: define interpretability via structure-preserving transformations (symmetries)
- Introduces four symmetries that jointly characterize interpretable models w.r.t. a target human user `h`:
  - **S1 Inference Equivariance**: A commutative diagram between model `P_{Y|X}`, human mental model `P^{[h]}_{Y|X}`, and translation maps `τ_X, τ_Y`
  - **S2 Information Invariance**: A marginalization `P_{Y|Z} ∘ P_{Z|X}` preserves the task-relevant mutual information (`H(Z) ≪ H(X)`)
  - **S3 Concept-Closure Invariance**: Translation maps preserve concept closure (extensional–intensional fixed points, à la Goguen/Formal Concept Analysis)
  - **S4 Structural Invariance**: There exist embeddings `E1: Im ↪ BorelStoch` and `E2: Hm ↪ BorelStoch` such that a functor `F: Im → Hm` commutes — the model's hypothesis class matches the user's mental hypothesis class
- Formalizes the **category of interpretable models** `Im` as a sub-Markov category of `BorelStoch`, with objects = concept spaces, morphisms = concept-based conditionals + copy/discard maps, composition = sequential/parallel
- Shows that **alignment, interventions, and counterfactuals are all Bayesian inversions** in this categorical setting (with ground-truth and do-interventions as special cases)
- Derives existing desiderata (sparsity, compactness, completeness, modularity, faithfulness, fidelity) as consequences of specific symmetries
- Disqualifies: post-hoc feature attribution (LIME/SHAP/Integrated Gradients), sparse autoencoders, unconstrained DNNs as concept predictors — and even classical decision trees / additive models when operating on non-concept spaces

## Methodology

The paper is a **theoretical position paper** (accepted to the 2nd Workshop on Compositional Learning at ICML 2026) that uses **categorical probability theory** as its formal language. The methodological apparatus:

1. **Probabilistic foundation.** Following Murphy (2023), the authors work in the category `BorelStoch` of standard Borel spaces and Markov kernels (Lawvere 1962, Giry 2006, Panangaden 2009, Fritz 2020).
2. **Markov categories** provide a compositional language for probabilistic models; the sub-Markov category `BorelStoch≤1` is used to support Bayesian inversion.
3. **String diagrams** (Joyal & Street 1991, Selinger 2010, Cho & Jacobs 2019) are the visual/syntactic representation for both model structure and probabilistic inference — boxes are stochastic morphisms, wires are value spaces.
4. **Concepts** are formalized à la Goguen (2005) and Ganter & Wille (1996) as fixed points of `β: P(S) → P(U)` (extension) and `γ: P(U) → P(S)` (intension).
5. **Bayesian inversion** is the unifying primitive: alignment = posterior over parameters given concept evidence; intervention = posterior update with a likelihood; counterfactual = three-step abduction–action–prediction sequence.
6. The four symmetries are introduced in a logical order — each addresses a concrete challenge (C1 intractability, C2 translation soundness, C3 behavior characterization) of the preceding one, producing a tight inductive argument.

## Results & Highlights

This is a position paper, so "results" are mostly **definitional and structural**:

- **Result 1 (Conclusion 2):** Verifying inference equivariance for `P_{Y|Z}` is equivalent but exponentially more tractable than for `P_{Y|X}` (information invariance reduces the conditional probability table size).
- **Result 2 (Conclusion 3):** Verifying inference equivariance for `P_{Y|C}` (concept-based) is equivalent to that for `P_{Y|Z}` but supports sound translations (concept-closure invariance).
- **Result 3 (Conclusion 4):** Structural invariance implies that if `P_{Y|C}` is compatible with a user's mental hypothesis space, that user can predict model results and verify inference equivariance.
- **Result 4 (Conclusion 5):** Alignment, interventional, and counterfactual inference are all forms of Bayesian inversion — so a single inference engine (e.g., belief propagation) suffices in principle.
- **Result 5:** The CBM literature has over-indexed on concept-closure invariance while neglecting structural invariance (→ arbitrary/weak predictors), while mechanistic interpretability has neglected information and concept-closure invariance (→ concepts unaligned with human semantics, information overload).
- The paper includes a worked XOR example showing how a piecewise-linear-mental-model user can verify equivariance that a linear-only user cannot — a clean illustration of how `Hm` choice changes what counts as "interpretable."

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty.** The paper's novelty is moderate-but-real, not a paradigm shift. Most individual ingredients have prior art: inference equivariance traces to Marconato, Passerini & Teso (2023) "Interpretability is in the mind of the beholder"; concept-closure invariance is a repackaging of Goguen's formal concept analysis + concept bottleneck models (Koh et al. 2020); the Bayesian inversion unification of alignment/interventions/counterfactuals appears in the CBM literature (Koh et al. 2020, Espinosa Zarlenga et al. 2023); the Erlangen-Program analogy for ML has been used before (Bronstein et al. 2021, for geometric DL). The contribution is in **assembling these into a single axiomatic framework with a Markov-category backbone**, which is genuinely useful as a *unifying* lens even if no single piece is new.

**Rigor.** The formal apparatus is appropriately precise given the venue (ICML workshop). However:
- The paper does **not prove that the four symmetries are independent** (i.e., that none implies the others), nor that they are **jointly satisfiable** in non-trivial cases.
- The "disqualified approaches" list is sometimes too strong: post-hoc attribution methods (LIME/SHAP) are disqualified for failing information invariance, but gradient-based attributions on a concept space `Z` could in principle satisfy it — the paper doesn't carefully delineate.
- The XOR example is convincing but a single toy example; a robustness check across multiple non-trivial compositional structures would strengthen the claim.
- The Markov-category machinery is presented opaquely — the paper repeatedly says "basic notions in category theory are presented elsewhere," which pushes the verification burden onto the reader.

**Reproducibility.** N/A for a position paper. No code, no benchmarks, no user studies. The "testability" of the framework is asserted (each symmetry can be checked) but no concrete verification procedure is given for, e.g., structural invariance in a real network.

**Significance.** High *as a structuring lens* for the CBM / interpretability community. The actionable call-to-action — "researchers should state which symmetry their method relies on" — is the kind of community-organizing move that could genuinely help de-fragment the field. Whether the field actually adopts it is a sociological question.

**Red flags.**
- Self-citation concentration: many of the "consequences" of symmetries are properties named by the same authors' prior work.
- The "ill-posed" framing of mainstream interpretability is rhetorically strong but somewhat over-stated; many authors (Doshi-Velez & Kim 2017, Murdoch et al. 2019) are explicitly aware of the lack of formal definitions and have proposed partial fixes.
- The Erlangen-Program analogy is a stretch: Klein's program unified *existing geometric theories* by identifying their symmetry groups; here, the symmetries are *proposed* rather than *extracted from* an established body of theory.
- The Bayesian-inversion unification of alignment/interventions/counterfactuals is a restatement of standard probabilistic-graphical-model results (Pearl 1988, Jacobs et al. 2025) dressed in category-theoretic language — useful for unification, but not a new algorithmic or empirical claim.
- Concept-closure invariance in particular relies on a fixed point of extension/intension, which is a strong assumption about concept stability that prior FCA work has shown is brittle to noisy data.

## Strengths

- Tackles a real and well-recognized problem (interpretability has no formal definition)
- Provides a **single, modular framework** that subsumes at least 6 widely-cited desiderata
- The Erlangen-Program framing is a memorable organizing metaphor
- Each symmetry has a **concrete intuitive gloss** alongside the formal statement, lowering the entry barrier
- The disqualification list is useful for reviewers and practitioners to argue why certain methods don't qualify as "interpretable" in a strict sense
- Identifies a real **asymmetry in the field**: CBM people ignore structural invariance, mechanistic interp people ignore concept-closure — actionable diagnosis
- Strong, credible author list

## Limitations

- **No empirical validation.** No user study, no benchmark, no case study. The "testability" claim is theoretical only.
- **Heavy reliance on category theory** may alienate the target audience (interpretability researchers, many of whom are application-driven).
- **Symmetry completeness unproven.** It's plausible that the four symmetries are *necessary but not sufficient* — e.g., robustness, fairness, calibration, and stability are conspicuously absent as required properties.
- **"Interpretability" remains user-dependent** (Symmetry 4 explicitly), so the framework gives a definition *parameterized by* `h`, not a universal one. This is acknowledged but partially undermines the "actionable" claim.
- **Concept-closure invariance is operationally fuzzy** in practice: verifying that a learned concept "red" matches a human's "red" is exactly the open problem in CAVs (Kim et al. 2018) and similar work.
- **Bayesian-inversion unification is more reformulation than new method.** The paper does not provide a new inference algorithm.
- Workshop venue (ICML Workshop on Compositional Learning) — not a top-tier main-conference result.

## Relevance

与"可解释 AI"和"概念瓶颈模型（CBM）"方向高度相关；论文给出的四条对称性为评估 CBM、SAE、post-hoc attribution 等方法的可解释性提供了一个统一的理论框架，对研究 CBM 对齐、干预与反事实推理的读者尤其有参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Are the four symmetries **independent**? For instance, does structural invariance imply concept-closure invariance, or vice versa?
- Can a deep neural network with **random** weights vacuously satisfy the axioms? (A vacuity test is missing.)
- What is the precise relationship to **Marconato et al. (2023)**, which also defined an "inference equivariance" principle but using causal notation?
- Does the framework yield any **quantitative, testable prediction** that distinguishes "interpretable" from "non-interpretable" models on standard benchmarks (e.g., a measurable gap on a held-out causal-intervention task)?
- How do the four symmetries interact with **fairness** and **robustness** properties? Are those additional symmetries, or derivable?
- For the concept-closure invariance, can a **practical, scalable verifier** be designed (e.g., via counterfactual probing) that doesn't require enumerating all concept instances?
- Does the Markov-category view enable a **generic inference algorithm** (e.g., a categorical message passing) that works uniformly for alignment, intervention, and counterfactuals?
- Is the framework compatible with **stochastic** or **non-deterministic** user mental models, or does it require a deterministic `H_m`?
- How does structural invariance relate to **information-theoretic** generalization bounds (e.g., mutual-information bounds)? Could they be derived as a 5th symmetry?
- Can the disqualified status of **sparse autoencoders** be revisited — modern SAEs (e.g., monosemantic features) seem closer to satisfying structural invariance than the paper suggests.
