Key metric
3.33ppl
Llama-2-7B on WikiText: same summed cross-entropy, two different denominators — token-perplexity vs. byte/word-perplexity drift by 3.33 points (1.43 for OPT-6.7B). The schema's metric_config exists exactly to keep this from being silently averaged.
Key finding
A unifying schema — not a new harness — finally makes cross-framework LLM scores auditable.

EEE = versioned JSON Schema + Pydantic v2 validators + automatic converters (HELM, lm-eval-harness, Inspect AI, +community). Records are immutable with explicit retraction; one _samples.jsonl sidecar carries per-instance data.

Field inclusion required: reported in ≥1 framework · necessary for interpretability · anticipated to be available to others.
Why this matters

LLaMA-65B's MMLU score has lived in the literature as 63.7 and 48.8 simultaneously — not because either is wrong, but because harnesses, prompts, and normalization disagreed and nobody had a record-level audit trail. EEE is the first cross-org attempt to make that drift visible rather than resolved.

llama-65B · MMLU · paper A 63.7  vs  48.8
Critical edge
Coverage ≠ Comparability

Co-locating 22,235 models under one schema does not normalize them. The perplexity case proves it: the same numeric label can mean different things across harnesses. EEE prevents silent aggregation — it does not produce a valid cross-harness leaderboard.

No silent defaults · no canonical dedup

01Schema anatomy · five blocks

01

source_metadata

source_type: documentation vs. evaluation_run. evaluator_relationship: first_party / third_party / self.

02

model_info

developer/name standardized; access mode splits inference_platform (openai, anthropic) from inference_engine (vLLM) — serving stack is a named confound.

03

generation_config

First-class fields: temperature, n_samples, stop sequences. Unknowns are omitted — never silently defaulted.

04

evaluation_results

One entry per scored metric. metric_config carries lower_is_better, type, range, SE/CI.

05

instance_level · optional

Sidecar _samples.jsonl: single_turn, multi_turn, or agentic (messages + nested tool_calls + sandbox config).

Identity via per-run UUID — not a canonical fingerprint — so duplicates surface instead of being merged away. Records are immutable; corrections are new records.

03Four empirical case studies · what the unified corpus buys you

§7.1 · agentic

Cost vs. accuracy in agentic evals

On CocoaBench: Codex and OpenClaw (both on GPT-5.4) reach the same accuracy — Codex is cheaper and faster. On CORE-Bench Hard (HAL): Claude Code beats CORE-Agent on cost for both Opus 4.5 and 4.1, but accuracy moves in opposite directions across the two backbones. Scaffold and backbone effects are entangled and direction-dependent.

scaffold ≠ backbone
§7.2 · perplexity

The denominator nobody flagged

GPTQ-style token-perplexity vs. lm-eval's byte/word-perplexity — same summed cross-entropy, different denominator. The 3.33 gap on Llama-2-7B is the headline; OPT-6.7B shows 1.43. metric_config prevents silent aggregation.

§7.3 · reproducibility

HELM audit · 3 models × 14 benchmarks

After EEE conversion, agreement between official HELM and local re-runs ranged from ~78% (BoolQ · Falcon-7B) to 100%. Entity-Matching drifted incomparably from a row-order change; SyntheticReasoning-Natural revealed a serving artifact (empty official completions).

78% ↔ 100% agreement
§7.4 · meta-analysis

Cross-benchmark IRT

Unidimensional IRT fit on GPQA Diamond / JudgeBench / Wordle Arena. Wordle Arena has the widest item-difficulty spread and the highest difficulty estimates; GPQA's distribution suggests impending saturation — only visible because per-item outputs are co-located.

05Governance · three roles, immutable record

A

Core maintainers

Schema evolution, release gates, PR merge authority on eval.schema.json.

B

Contributors

Submit new format converters and evaluation records via PR — gated by EvalEvalBot (Pydantic v2).

C

Community reviewers

Dispute resolution, schema-change review, retraction flow — the LLaMA-65B 63.7-vs-48.8 discrepancy stays as two records with metadata, not silently resolved.

Coalition framing: Evaluating Evaluations (EvalEval) Coalition · evalevalai.com. 47 authors from TUM, MIT, Stanford, Yale, EleutherAI, HF, IBM, Meta FAIR, ETH, Mila, Oxford, Cambridge-area, Northeastern, Comenius, Hebrew U.

06Strengths

  • Genuine, well-targeted problem. Cross-harness irreproducibility is documented & acknowledged; the LLaMA-65B-on-MMLU example lands.
  • Right architectural shape. Modular five-block schema + sidecar instance-level data + UUID-per-run + Pydantic validation — defensible for an evolving artifact.
  • Real cross-community coalition. 47 authors, 28 orgs, explicit roles, public governance doc — not a single-lab overreach.
  • Concrete empirical value. All four case studies use the unified corpus to surface findings (cost-accuracy, ppl denominator, HELM gaps, IRT difficulty) that were hard to obtain before.
  • Honest positioning. Explicitly not a new evaluation harness; explicit limitations; concurrent-work citation and merging.
  • Low contribution & consumption friction. HF hosting + Pydantic validators + EvalEvalBot annotations.

07Limitations

  • Coverage ≠ comparability. Co-locating results does not normalize them; cross-harness score comparisons remain unsafe without case-by-case justification.
  • Strong text / single-model bias. Multimodal, human-preference (Chatbot Arena Elo), and multi-agent evaluations are “partially supported”.
  • Adoption-dependent value. If HELM / lm-eval-harness / Inspect AI do not adopt EEE export natively, the schema is reduced to a downstream aggregator.
  • No formal lossiness / audit per converter. Field drop-rates HELM → EEE, etc., are not systematically reported.
  • Schema governance is unproven. Three-role model is sensible but has no track record of resolving contested schema-change proposals.
  • Participation-based bias. 28-org concentration on US / English / text benchmarks is a selection effect, not a true cross-section of AI evaluation.
  • Deduplication is left to consumers. UUID-per-run preserves information but shifts duplicate-detection burden to the analysis layer.
  • Cannot capture serving-stack confounds. Surfaces mismatches (empty HELM completions) but does not always attribute them to a specific runtime cause.

08Questions for further exploration

  1. How lossy is each converter in practice — what fraction of HELM / lm-eval-harness / Inspect AI fields are dropped or transformed into additional_details, and how is this measured?
  2. Will HELM, lm-eval-harness, and Inspect AI ship first-party EEE export, or only downstream converters? (Long-term adoption hinges on this.)
  3. Does the schema capture sufficient metadata to distinguish, e.g., vLLM 0.4.x vs. vLLM 0.5.x serving the same weights? Serving-stack confounds were a major source of HELM mismatches in §7.3.
  4. How does EEE interact with existing meta-evaluation work (BIG-bench, OpenCompass, MLPerf) — complement, overlap, or compete?
  5. For multimodal and agentic evaluations, where is the schema thin, and what concrete field additions would close those gaps?
  6. Can EEE instance-level data be combined across datasets to produce cross-benchmark difficulty priors (IRT / Rasch as in §7.4) at scale — enabling a kind of “global LLM ability calibration”?
  7. What is the empirical duplication rate of records once contributed (same model, slightly different prompts) — and is there a canonical deduplication reference implementation?
  8. How does the immutability + correction / retraction design interact with EU AI Act audit-trail requirements? Is EEE's record format a viable substrate for regulatory compliance logging?
  9. Coverage bias: how representative are the 28 contributing orgs of the global AI evaluation ecosystem (industry, academia, non-English, government)?
  10. Cost trajectory: the paper estimates hundreds of thousands of dollars to reproduce current EEE records — how does this scale as more contributors add agentic / multimodal runs?
