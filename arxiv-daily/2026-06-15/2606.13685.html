arXiv · 2606.13685 / LLM Evaluation & Reliability / Repeated-trial empirical

LLM judges are not random — but a single verdict hides 4 layers of hidden noise that flip outcomes ~14% of the time.

The Coin Flip Judge? Reliability and Bias in LLM-as-a-Judge Evaluation · Abel Yagubyan · 2026-06-15
LLM-as-a-Judge evaluation-reliability position-bias intraclass-correlation pairwise-vs-pointwise noise-budget
Asset extraction: 一句话总结 → TL;DR → Results §5, Critical Assessment §6
Key finding

A 10,080-judgment repeated-trial study decomposes LLM-judge unreliability into four layers — stochastic flip rate, position bias, prompt sensitivity, judge identity — and proves that 11 repeated trials are enough to recover the 50-trial reference verdict with 95% fidelity.

Key metric
13.6% mean flip rate
Across 50 pairwise trials per question, on competitive high-quality pairs (A 9.3 vs B 9.4 / 10), with max 56% and 28% of questions above 20% — an upper bound on single-trial noise.
Why this matters

Every LLM leaderboard, every RLHF preference label, every reward-model benchmark is built on these verdicts. A noise budget of ~14 wrong outcomes per 100-question single-trial benchmark means a single inference is the wrong unit of evidence — and the paper hands practitioners a tiered protocol (10/20/50 trials) to spend compute deliberately.

Critical edge

The "coin flip" headline is oversold by the title: mean 13.6% FR is far from 50% randomness, and the loudest result (GPT-4o-mini 72% A-majority) is contaminated by a self-preference confound — Response A is generated by the same model family as the judge. The position-bias claim cannot be cleanly separated from self-enhancement on this design, and the per-category trends (η²=0.31) rest on 2–4 questions per category, statistically underpowered.

01Methodology

  1. Stress-test dataset. 29 question–response pairs across 10 categories, each with two *deliberately competitive* responses (A from GPT-4o-mini 9.3±0.9, B from GPT-4o 9.4±0.6) — trivial pairs would inflate consistency.
  2. Two judges. GPT-4o-mini and GPT-4.1-mini via OpenAI API, default t=1.0 for the main experiment.
  3. Experiment 1 (main). Per (judge, question): 50 pairwise trials (randomized A/B) + 50 pointwise trials per response. 8,700 API calls. 50 trials give binomial power (≥33/50 for p<0.05).
  4. Experiment 2 (temperature ablation). 10 pairwise trials at t=0 per (judge, question) — tests if deterministic decoding kills the noise. 580 extra calls.
  5. Experiment 3 (prompt sensitivity). Two semantically equivalent prompts ("impartial judge" vs "fair and unbiased evaluator") on 10 questions × 2 judges × 20 trials. 800 calls.
  6. Metrics. Flip rate, Shannon entropy, position-bias index, pairwise–pointwise gap (PPG), ICC(2,1), Cohen's κ, reliability curves (Monte Carlo 500 reps per K trials). Bootstrap 10,000 iterations for 95% CIs.

  • Pairwise–pointwise paradox: mean scalar gap PPG = 0.19–0.36 / 10 with non-significant Wilcoxon (p = 0.827 / 0.126) — judges declare winners without scalar evidence.
  • Bimodal difficulty: 14/29 "easy" (mean FR 2.9%) vs 15/29 "hard" (mean FR 23.6%), Cohen's d = 2.96; coding/writing/reasoning dominate the hard bucket.
  • Temperature matters asymmetrically: t=0 cuts FR by 79% for GPT-4o-mini (13.3% → 2.8%) but only 43% for GPT-4.1-mini (13.9% → 7.9%) — API-level nondeterminism in the latter.
  • Prompt sensitivity: 5/20 (25%) majority verdicts flip on semantically equivalent prompts; mean |ΔFR| = 13.4 pp.
  • Pointwise MoE: within-question σ ≈ 0.60 → single-trial 95% margin of error ±1.2 / 10; 44.7% of pointwise variance is within-question noise.

03Strengths

  • Plug-into-pipeline numbers. 11 trials for 95%, 15+ for hard items, 13.6% mean FR, κ = 0.51, ±1.2 pt single-trial MoE — directly actionable for benchmark designers.
  • Multi-dimensional stress test. Stochastic noise, position bias, temperature, prompt template, judge identity — five failure modes in one study.
  • Pairwise–pointwise paradox named. Forced-choice amplifying sub-threshold scalar differences into unstable ordinal verdicts — a distinct, articulable failure mode.
  • Psychometric grounding. ICC(2,1) + variance decomposition + Cohen's d give principled interpretation, not just descriptive flip rates.
  • Code, data, prompts, per-question tables released. Uncommon for single-author evaluation work; supports direct replication.
  • Honest enumeration of six limitations in Section 6.5 (single-provider, self-preference, small N, no in-task human baseline, etc.).
  • Noise-budget framing. Translates findings into benchmark-design language: "100-question single-trial benchmark ≈ 14 wrong outcomes."

04Limitations

  • Two judges, both OpenAI. No Claude, Gemini, Llama, or Qwen replication — cross-provider generalization is asserted, not tested.
  • Self-preference confound. Response A is generated by GPT-4o-mini (same family as one judge), so the 72% A-majority headline cannot separate position bias from self-enhancement.
  • 29 questions, 10 categories (2–4 per category) — underpowered for per-category statistics; η² = 0.31 effect is suggestive but unsupported.
  • No human baseline on the same instances. Table 8 comparisons borrow κ values from prior papers with different tasks and protocols — "LLM ≈ human" is imprecise.
  • Competitive stress-test pairs (both responses high-quality, A 9.3 vs B 9.4) — real-world lopsided comparisons may have lower FR, so 13.6% is an upper bound, not typical.
  • No cost/latency analysis for the 20–50-trial tiered protocol — leaderboard-scale deployment is recommended without a compute budget.
  • Headline mean is bimodal-skewed: 14 easy questions (FR ≈ 0%) and 15 hard ones (FR ≈ 23.6%) shouldn't be aggregated into a single 13.6% number.
  • Prompt sensitivity tested on 10 questions × 2 templates — the 25% flip claim rests on a small subsample and a narrow prompt pair.

05Questions for further exploration

  1. How do flip rates and reliability curves change across diverse providers (Claude, Gemini, Llama, Qwen) and across model sizes within a family? Single-provider scope is the paper's biggest open gap.
  2. Can a per-question variance predictor (difficulty proxy: length, hedging, formatting, response similarity) be learned so evaluation budgets are spent adaptively?
  3. How does multi-trial aggregation affect downstream reward-model training when the judge provides preference labels? Is the 13.6% noise floor below or above the signal-to-noise threshold for RLHF stability?
  4. Can a panel-of-judges (multi-model majority vote) recover the 50-trial reference with fewer total inferences than naïve within-judge repetition? Would weighted aggregation by per-judge ICC improve efficiency?
  5. Does the pairwise–pointwise paradox replicate on out-of-family response pairs (Llama A vs Claude B judged by GPT-4), or does it shrink when self-enhancement is removed?
  6. What is the true self-enhancement vs position-bias decomposition for GPT-4o-mini on the same 29 tasks with responses generated by a non-OpenAI model placed randomly in position A?
  7. How do fine-tuned judge models (JudgeLM, Prometheus) compare on these reliability metrics — does fine-tuning for consistency improve ICC, or just shift the failure modes?
  8. Does the bimodal easy/hard split persist at scale, or is it an artifact of the small question set? With 100+ items per category, can we build a category-conditional reliability model?
