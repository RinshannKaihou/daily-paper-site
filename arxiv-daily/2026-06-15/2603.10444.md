> **一句话总结：** 作者诊断出 FP4 LLM 训练中激活极端值的根源是 **秩一均值偏置**（feature-wise mean 与主导各向异性谱分量对齐并在训练中不断增强），并由此提出 **Averis** —— 在量化前仅用 reduction + 减法把均值与残差拆分独立量化；它在 Qwen3-0.6B Dense (100B tokens) 与 Qwen3-7B-A1.5B MoE (50B tokens) 上以约 2% 的端到端开销把 W4A4G4 NVFP4 的 BF16 loss gap 压到 1.19% / 0.81%，并超越 NVIDIA 同期发布的 Hadamard 异常平滑方法 (2.05% / 1.10%)，二者可叠加。

> **TL;DR:** This paper pinpoints the failure mode of FP4 LLM training. Through SVD-based spectral analysis of activations across Qwen3-0.6B, the authors show that the dominant activation outliers are *not* random sparse events but are largely induced by a coherent **rank-one mean bias**: the feature-wise mean vector `μ_X = (1/l) Xᵀ·1` aligns tightly with the leading right singular vector `v₁` (cosine similarity up to 0.99), strengthens monotonically through training, and is amplified/reshaped by attention and FFN operators. A Gaussian-residual tail theorem (Theorem 1) shows the mean shift drives exponential tail amplification `∝ exp((2t|m_j| − m_j²)/2τ_j²)`. From this, the authors design **Averis (Averaging-Induced Residual Splitting)**: split activations and output gradients into column-mean and residual, quantize each independently with blockwise NVFP4, and combine the two in the GeMM. Across Qwen3-0.6B Dense (100B tokens) and Qwen3-7B-A1.5B MoE (50B tokens) on DCLM with W4A4G4 NVFP4, Averis reduces BF16 loss gaps to **1.19% / 0.81%** vs. NVIDIA's Hadamard baseline at 2.05% / 1.10% and limits downstream gaps to **0.89 / 0.71 points**. On Blackwell, end-to-end overhead is **2.01% / 2.20% over vanilla NVFP4** (≈29% of Hadamard's overhead), and Averis+Hadamard further shrinks the 0.6B loss gap to 0.94% — i.e. the two methods are complementary, not redundant.

## 核心贡献

- **结构性诊断**：通过 SVD / 谱分析证明，FP4 训练中"激活极端值"并非任意稀疏事件，而是由一个 **秩一均值偏置** `M_X = 1_l μ_Xᵀ` 驱动；`μ_X` 与主导各向异性谱分量 `v₁` 的 cosine similarity 接近 1.0，并随训练单调增强。Attention 和 FFN 算子在局部都对该均值做放大和方向重塑。
- **理论解释 (Theorem 1)**：在经验可验证的"近似高斯残差 + 对角方差 + 均值主导"假设下，列向极值超出概率被均值偏移指数级放大 `∝ exp((2t|m_j| − m_j²)/2τ_j²)`，从机制上解释了为什么移除均值能压缩长尾、降低块级量化 scale 的膨胀。
- **Averis 方法**：在 forward 与 backward GeMM 中均把 `X` / `D`（输出梯度）显式分解为列均值与残差，独立量化再合并；权重梯度利用"残差列中心化使交叉项消失"的性质简化为 `X̄_Rᵀ D̄_R + l μ̄_X μ̄_Dᵀ`。整个流程只需要 reduction + elementwise subtraction，零学习参数、零旋转矩阵。
- **经验上击败 NVIDIA Hadamard 方案**：在 Qwen3-0.6B/7B-A1.5B 两个架构、100B/50B tokens 规模上，BF16 loss gap 从 NVIDIA Hadamard 的 2.05% / 1.10% 降到 1.19% / 0.81%；下游平均精度 gap 0.89 / 0.71 分。
- **极低硬件开销 + 可组合性**：相对 vanilla NVFP4 的端到端开销仅 2.01% / 2.20%，约为 Hadamard 方案开销的 29% / 30%；且与 Hadamard 正交，组合后 0.6B loss gap 进一步降到 0.94%。

## Key Contributions

- **Structural diagnosis of FP4 outliers** — the dominant activation outliers are not arbitrary sparse events but are largely induced by a coherent **rank-one mean bias** `M_X = 1_l μ_Xᵀ`, where the feature-wise mean `μ_X` aligns with the leading anisotropic right singular vector `v₁` (cosine ≈ 0.99) and grows through training; attention and FFN operators both amplify and reshape this mean during forward propagation.
- **Theoretical account (Theorem 1)** — under empirically verified assumptions (Gaussian marginal residual, diagonal-variance approximation, mean-dominance `|m_j|/τ_j ≫ 1`), the columnwise exceedance probability is `Q((t−|m_j|)/τ_j) + Q((t+|m_j|)/τ_j)`, and the far-tail amplification relative to a zero-mean baseline is **exponential** in `(2t|m_j| − m_j²)/2τ_j²` — providing a concrete mechanism for why mean removal contracts the tail and restores blockwise-scale fidelity.
- **Averis (Averaging-Induced Residual Splitting)** — a pre-quantization transform that (i) computes column-mean `μ_X` by reduction, (ii) subtracts it to form residual `X_R`, (iii) quantizes `μ_X`, `X_R`, and `W` independently with blockwise NVFP4, (iv) recombines as `Ŷ = 1_l(μ̄_X W̄) + X̄_R W̄` in forward and analogous forms in backward. The weight-gradient GeMM simplifies to `X̄_Rᵀ D̄_R + l μ̄_X μ̄_Dᵀ` because centered residuals kill the cross terms. No learned parameters, no rotation matrices.
- **Empirical W4A4G4 stability at non-trivial scale** — Qwen3-0.6B Dense (100B tokens) and Qwen3-7B-A1.5B MoE (50B tokens) on DCLM with NVFP4 simulation on Hopper: BF16 loss gaps of **1.19% / 0.81%** vs. NVIDIA's Hadamard baseline at 2.05% / 1.10%, with downstream accuracy gaps of **0.89 / 0.71 points**.
- **Hardware-efficient and composable** — on Blackwell, end-to-end training-step overhead is only **2.01% (0.6B) / 2.20% (7B)** over vanilla NVFP4, i.e. **~29% / ~30% of the Hadamard design's overhead**; the mean-extraction kernel itself is **4.47×–4.72× faster** than tiled Hadamard preprocessing on the evaluated activation shapes. Combining Averis with Hadamard further reduces the 0.6B loss and downstream gaps to **0.94%** and **0.73 points**, showing the two methods are complementary, not redundant.

## Methodology

### Diagnosis — anisotropic spectral analysis of activations

- **Notation**: For an activation matrix `X ∈ R^{l×m}` (`l = b·s`, `m` = hidden), define the feature-wise mean `μ_X = (1/l) Xᵀ 1 ∈ R^m`, the rank-one mean matrix `M_X = 1_l μ_Xᵀ`, and the centered residual `X̃ = X − M_X`. Let SVD be `X = Σ_k σ_k u_k v_kᵀ` and the alignment coefficient `β_k = ⟨u_k, e⟩` where `e = 1/√l` is the normalized all-ones token direction.
- **Three pieces of evidence (Figure 1, layer-27 FFN-input of Qwen3-0.6B at step 170k)**:
  1. The singular spectrum is sharply anisotropic, with a dominant leading singular value.
  2. Token-wise cosine similarities with the mean direction are almost uniformly positive (a *shared offset*), whereas with a non-mean direction (e.g. `v₂`) they alternate in sign.
  3. `μ_X` aligns with `v₁` (cosine similarity up to 0.99), far more than with any other right singular direction.
- **Training dynamics (Figure 2)**: The mean-bias strength ratio `R = ‖μ_X‖₂ / (‖X‖_F / √l)` increases monotonically with training step, especially in early/middle layers, while the cosine alignment between `μ_X` and `v₁` stays close to 1 across layers and stages.
- **Operator-level amplification (Figure 3)**: Both attention and FFN increase `R`; at the late checkpoint, amplification is concentrated in the FFN path while attention contributes more to directional reshaping.
- **Outlier attribution (Figure 4)**: For top-0.1% activation entries, the squared mean-share `ρ^(mean)` vs. residual-share `ρ^(res)` shifts from residual-dominated at early checkpoints to mean-dominated at late checkpoints, with median mean-share rising to **~95% in deep late layers**.

### Theory — Theorem 1 (Mean-bias amplification of columnwise outliers)

- **Setup**: Fix column `j`; let `m_j = (μ_X)_j`, let `η_{Ij}` be the row-sampled centered residual with I ~ Unif({1..l}), and let `Y_j = X_{Ij} = m_j + η_{Ij}`.
- **Assumptions (all empirically validated)**:
  1. **Marginal Gaussian residual**: `η_{Ij} ~̇ N(0, τ_j²)` — Figure 5 shows raw activations deviate from Gaussianity while residuals fit well.
  2. **Diagonal variance approximation**: `τ_j² ≈ (1/l) Σ_k σ_k²(1 − β_k²) v_{kj}²` — Appendix B shows cross-mode covariance contributes median 0.006 / 95th-percentile 0.036.
  3. **Mean-dominant aligned columns**: `|m_j|/τ_j ≫ 1` and the column mean is dominated by the leading aligned spectral mode `m_j^{(1)} = (σ_1 β_1 / √l) v_{1j}`.
- **Result**: For threshold `t > 0`,
  `P(|Y_j| > t) = Q((t − |m_j|)/τ_j) + Q((t + |m_j|)/τ_j)`,
  and in the far-tail regime `t|m_j|/τ_j² → ∞` the amplification relative to a variance-only `N(0, τ_j²)` baseline is **exponential** in `(2t|m_j| − m_j²) / 2τ_j²`.
- **Implication**: A non-zero column mean is not a minor perturbation — it shifts the tail by `|m_j|` and makes the top activations *mean-driven* once `|m_j|/τ_j` is large, which is exactly the regime the authors observe in late-stage deep layers.

### Method — Averis quantization

- **Forward (activation mean–residual splitting)**:
  `μ_X = (1/l) 1_lᵀ X`, `X_R = X − 1_l μ_X`, then `Ŷ = 1_l (μ̄_X W̄) + X̄_R W̄`, where `μ̄_X = Q_b(μ_X)`, `X̄_R = Q_b(X_R)`, `W̄ = Q_b(W)`. The mean matrix is never materialized — `μ_X` is computed and subtracted on the fly.
- **Backward — input gradient**: `μ_D = (1/l) 1_lᵀ D`, `D_R = D − 1_l μ_D`, then `∂̂L/∂X = 1_l (μ̄_D W̄ᵀ) + D̄_R W̄ᵀ`.
- **Backward — weight gradient**: Exact expansion gives
  `∂L/∂W = X_Rᵀ D_R + X_Rᵀ (1_l μ_D) + (1_l μ_X)ᵀ D_R + (1_l μ_X)ᵀ (1_l μ_D)`.
  Since `X_R` and `D_R` are column-centered, the two cross terms vanish, leaving
  `∂̂L/∂W = X̄_Rᵀ D̄_R + l μ̄_X μ̄_Dᵀ`.
- **No learning, no rotations**: The mean can be kept in higher precision (e.g. FP16/BF16) or absorbed into an adjacent operator's accumulator; only reductions and elementwise subtractions are added to the FP4 kernel.

### Experimental setup

- **Models**: Qwen3-0.6B Dense (100B tokens) and Qwen3-7B-A1.5B MoE (scaled-down variant following Qwen3-235B-A22B; 50B tokens).
- **Data**: DCLM (Li et al., 2024a).
- **Precision**: W4A4G4 NVFP4 (E2M1) for weights, activations, and gradients; **stochastic rounding** on backward gradient GeMMs to mitigate quantization bias (orthogonal to Averis).
- **Hardware**: FP4 **simulation** on **Hopper GPUs** for training-quality runs; end-to-end runtime measured on **Blackwell GPUs**.
- **Baselines**: BF16 (reference), vanilla NVFP4, NVIDIA's recently released Hadamard-based outlier-smoothing (NVFP4-Hadamard), Averis, Averis-Hadamard.
- **Downstream suite** (NVFP4 forward eval): question answering (ARC-C/E, RACE, BoolQ), classification (HellaSwag, PIQA), cloze (LAMBADA).

- On 0.6B, Averis shrinks the loss gap by **~42%** relative to Hadamard (1.19 vs. 2.05). On 7B MoE, all low-bit variants stay closer to BF16, with Averis still the best FP4 method at 0.81%.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty — high, but partly narrative reframing**: "Subtract the mean before quantizing" is a textbook variance-stabilization trick (cf. mean-centering in vector quantization, zero-centering in classical lossy compression). The genuine novelty here is the *diagnostic story*: (i) attributing FP4 outliers specifically to a rank-one mean component that aligns with the leading anisotropic spectral direction, (ii) showing that the alignment *strengthens* through training, and (iii) proving that this mean shift drives *exponential* (not just linear) tail amplification. That story is non-obvious and could redirect a community's research agenda by providing a unifying lens for why so many ad-hoc outlier defenses (SmoothQuant-style scaling, magnitude clipping, Hadamard rotations) all help at all — they implicitly tame the mean.
- **Rigor — strong with one notable gap**:
  - **Strong**: Head-to-head against NVIDIA's released Hadamard baseline at 100B- and 50B-token scales; both dense and MoE architectures; joint reporting of loss gap, downstream gap, and per-kernel / end-to-end overhead; a Gaussian-tail theorem with all assumptions explicitly listed and *empirically validated* (Figures 4–5, Appendix B); clean pseudocode for forward and both backward GeMMs.
  - **Missing ablations**: The paper does not isolate the contribution of (i) the precise reduction dimension (per-token? per-block? per-head?), (ii) what to do with the extracted mean (keep in FP16 vs. absorb into adjacent op vs. discard), (iii) sensitivity to block size of the underlying NVFP4 quantizer, (iv) learning-rate and batch-size interaction (both known to affect mean growth in transformers), (v) interaction with stochastic rounding (described as "orthogonal" but not jointly ablated).
  - **Theoretical tightness**: Theorem 1 requires `t|m_j|/τ_j² → ∞` for the one-sided dominance approximation, and the empirical far-tail contraction in Appendix C is qualitative (histogram eyeballing) rather than a quantitative KS / tail-index comparison. The diagonal-variance Assumption 2 has median cross-term 0.006 but 95th percentile 0.036 — small, but not negligible in a paper whose central claim is structural.
- **Reproducibility — medium-good**:
  - **Code link is real but ephemeral** (anonymous-4open.science hosting, not a permanent GitHub repo); reviewers will be able to verify, downstream users may struggle to re-pin the exact baseline implementation.
  - **No error bars** on training runs (NeurIPS checklist #7 explicitly answers "No" with the reason "high computational cost of large-scale LLM training") — the headline 1.19% / 0.81% numbers are single-seed.
  - **The 0.6B (100B tokens) vs. 7B (50B tokens) comparison is not apples-to-apples in token budget**, so the per-model headline numbers cannot be directly compared. The paper does not normalize.
  - **FP4 simulation on Hopper** for the training-quality results — kernel-level fidelity is not the same as native NVFP4 hardware execution; the authors are honest about this in their stated limitations, but it is still a gap.
- **Significance — high for the field**: If the rank-one mean-bias story generalizes, it is a *unifying* explanation for the family of ad-hoc outlier-handling tricks that have proliferated since 2023. That is the kind of paper that gets cited in related-work sections for years. Practically, a 2% overhead vs. 6.8% (0.6B) / 7.6% (7B) for the current best industrial recipe is also a real win for anyone running NVFP4 training at scale.
- **Red flags / things to watch**:
  - **Averis + Hadamard being even better than Averis alone** (0.94% vs. 1.19% loss gap on 0.6B) is sold as a virtue — and it is — but it also implies the mean-bias diagnosis is *partial*. The paper does not propose a second-axis correction, so the "Averis alone" result is not the ceiling.
  - **NVFP4-Hadamard is *worse* than vanilla NVFP4 on 7B downstream** (1.48 vs. 1.37 point gap). The paper mentions this in passing; it is actually a meaningful data point suggesting Hadamard is not a free lunch at scale, and it strengthens the case for Averis — but a sceptical reader might also ask whether the Hadamard baseline was carefully tuned.
  - **The "0.81% loss gap at 7B" is reported without a Hadamard co-result on the 7B MoE Averis-Hadamard combination** (Table 1 only shows 3 FP4 rows for 7B). We do not know whether the composability claim holds at 7B.
  - **Output-gradient mean bias is described as "much weaker"** (Appendix D shows mean centering only reduces NVFP4 quantization error from 13.6% to 13.5%) — the paper still applies the decomposition to gradients for consistency, but the gain there is mostly cosmetic. The "Averis" effect is essentially a forward-pass phenomenon; the backward contribution is marginal.
  - **No comparison to other recent low-bit-training baselines** beyond NVIDIA's Hadamard method — the paper does not run against MXFP4, FP4-All-the-Way, SVDQuant-for-training, Metis itself (the same group's prior work), or any per-block scaling variant. The claim of "best FP4 method" rests on a single named competitor.

## Strengths

- **Clean, falsifiable diagnostic claim** (rank-one mean bias explains FP4 outliers) backed by a spectral-analysis lens that anyone with PyTorch + NumPy can re-run.
- **Genuinely minimal method**: only reductions and elementwise subtractions — no learned modules, no rotation matrices to maintain, no per-channel statistics to track, no new kernel contracts.
- **Empirically strong on the right baseline**: outperforms NVIDIA's *just-released* Hadamard-based method on both loss and downstream gaps, at ~30% of its overhead, on two architecturally distinct models at non-trivial token budgets (100B / 50B).
- **Composability with Hadamard** is an unusual and useful property: a method that is "another tool" rather than "yet another incompatible scheme."
- **A unifying lens for prior outlier-handling tricks** — explains, ex post, why SmoothQuant, magnitude clipping, Hadamard rotations, and SVD-based methods (including the authors' own Metis) all help: they all implicitly tame the mean component.
- **Honstly disclosed limitations** (FP4 simulation, single-seed runs, narrower output-gradient effect) in both the limitations paragraph and the NeurIPS checklist.

## Limitations

- **Limited scale**: results shown only at 0.6B dense and 7B-A1.5B MoE; behavior at 70B+ dense or larger MoE, and at trillion-token pretraining, is not demonstrated. The 1.61× BF16 → NVFP4 speedup cited in the introduction is for Qwen3-8B on B300 — also not the model class used for the training-quality experiments.
- **No ablations**: reduction dimension (per-token / per-channel / per-block), what to do with the extracted mean, block-size sensitivity, learning-rate and batch-size interaction, and joint ablations with stochastic rounding are all absent.
- **Reproducibility concerns**: anonymous-4open-science link (not a stable GitHub repo), no hyper-parameter sheet for 100B-token runs, no error bars, no per-token-budget normalization between the 0.6B and 7B results.
- **Partial diagnosis**: Averis + Hadamard still beats Averis alone on 0.6B, suggesting the rank-one mean story is not the whole story; the paper does not propose a second-axis correction.
- **Output-gradient effect is marginal**: per Appendix D, mean centering on output gradients reduces NVFP4 quantization error only from 13.6% to 13.5% — the Averis gain is essentially a forward-pass phenomenon.
- **Simulation-based training results**: quality numbers come from FP4 *simulation* on Hopper, not native end-to-end execution on Blackwell; the kernel-level story is plausible but unverified at full pretraining scale.
- **No comparison to other recent baselines**: only NVIDIA's Hadamard method is the named competitor; no MXFP4, FP4-All-the-Way, Metis (the same group's prior work), or per-block scaling variants are evaluated head-to-head.

## Relevance

直接命中**大模型高效训练 / 低比特量化**研究主线：把 FP4 训练从经验调参推进到结构性诊断（秩一均值偏置 + 谱分析），并给出在 Blackwell 硬件上仅 2% 开销即可部署的方案。结论对 LLM 训练系统工程师、量化算法研究者、以及设计下一代低精度 GEMM 内核的 GPU/加速器架构师都有参考价值，也提供了一个统一视角来重新审视 SmoothQuant / Hadamard / Metis 等已有方法为何有效。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the rank-one mean-bias phenomenon also appear in vision transformers, diffusion models, or speech LMs, or is it specific to autoregressive language modeling? If universal, it would justify treating the mean as a first-class quantity in any low-bit training stack.
- What is the right granularity for the mean reduction (per-token, per-channel, per-block, per-head) and how does it interact with NVFP4's blockwise quantization tile size? A clean ablation across `(reduction axis) × (NVFP4 block size)` would convert the "Averis recipe" from a single point to a Pareto curve.
- Can the extracted mean be absorbed into a neighboring op (e.g. the prior residual add, the LayerNorm affine, or a downstream GEMM's epilogue) so that the subtraction falls off the critical path entirely? This is the obvious next step toward turning the 2% overhead into negative overhead (i.e. a *faster* training step than vanilla NVFP4).
- How does Averis compose with other orthogonal outlier defenses (SmoothQuant, magnitude-aware clipping, Outlier Suppression, SVDQuant) and with mixed-precision gradient scaling (e.g. OFT, mxfp8) that the community has explored? The composability claim is currently shown only against Hadamard.
- Does the diagnosis predict a regime in which Hadamard-like rotations become *unnecessary* — i.e. when the spectral anisotropy is already small, or when `|m_j|/τ_j < 1`? If so, can that regime be identified cheaply at runtime so that the Hadamard kernel can be skipped on a per-block basis?
- What is the per-kernel cost on NVIDIA Blackwell B200 / GB200 vs. AMD MI300X, and can the mean reduction be fused into existing GEMM epilogues (CUTLASS, Triton) without a separate launch?
- How sensitive is the 1.19% / 0.81% loss gap to the optimizer (AdamW vs. Lion vs. Shampoo) and to the warmup schedule, given the paper's claim that the mean component "strengthens during training"? Optimizers that bias the gradient toward smaller norms might change the rate at which `R` grows.
- Why does NVFP4-Hadamard *hurt* the 7B downstream average (1.48 points) relative to vanilla NVFP4 (1.37 points), and does Averis+Hadamard recover at 7B? The paper's 7B Table 1 row for Averis-Hadamard is conspicuously absent.
- Can the rank-one mean component be predicted cheaply (e.g. a per-layer EMA of `μ_X`) and pre-subtracted in the optimizer state, reducing on-the-fly reduction cost?
