> **一句话总结：** 本文提出 DSGNAR——一种基于"先找良态区域、再快速下降"思想的二阶优化框架，用 CountSketch（行）+ SRCT（列）双重草图化 Gauss-Newton 模型，配合按目标下降比 ϱ⋆ 选步长的自适应策略，在 Burgers、Kuramoto–Sivashinsky、高维 Poisson、Navier–Stokes 等问题上把 PINN 精度提升 3–8 个数量级（相对 ℓ₂ 误差最低达 3×10⁻¹⁶），且对网络结构、精度、超参数鲁棒，单精度下 10 秒内解 Burgers 到 4.75×10⁻⁷。

> **TL;DR:** DSGNAR is a scalable second-order PINN optimiser that reframes training as an ill-conditioning problem: it builds a *doubly-sketched* Gauss–Newton model (CountSketch on rows, SRCT on columns → a small square sketch whose single SVD yields cheap Levenberg–Marquardt steps), and selects each step by probing for a *target decrease ratio* ϱ⋆ in a two-stage scheme that first drives the regularisation down to find a well-conditioned region, then descends. It beats recent strong second-order PINN baselines by 3–8 orders of magnitude across Burgers, KS, high-dim Poisson, Navier–Stokes, etc., reaching relative ℓ₂ errors as low as 3×10⁻¹⁶, while being robust to architecture/precision/hyperparameters and solving Burgers in <10 s in single precision. Worth reading if you care about PINN optimisation or randomised second-order methods.

## 核心贡献

- **"良态优先"的优化哲学**：首次明确论证 PINN 的精度天花板由收敛区域的**有效正则化 λ**（即让 Gauss–Newton 步仍能产生下降所需的最小正则化）决定，而非损失下降速度；据此提出两阶段策略——Stage 1 用小目标比 ϱ≤0.2 驱动 λ 下降寻找良态区域，Stage 2 自动切换到大目标比 ϱ≥0.5 快速下降。
- **双重草图 Gauss–Newton 模型**：用 **CountSketch**（行压缩，OSNAP 类，K=2–4 个哈希）压缩残差维度，用 **SRCT（子采样随机余弦变换）** 压缩参数维度，得到 s×s 小方阵（s ∈ [⌊dθ/3⌋, ⌊dθ/2⌋]）；一次 SVD 即可对任意 λ 廉价给出 Levenberg–Marquardt 步及下降比预测。这是区别于以往"无矩阵"（仅 Jacobi–向量积）PINN 二阶方法的关键。
- **密集残差采样**：CountSketch 的线性可累加性使得残差数 N 可任意大而不受内存约束（实测 1,048,576×252,503 的 Jacobian 在 s=10,000 下 7.8s 构建），绕开了 PINN 中"如何选择少量配点"的难题。
- **按目标比 ϱ⋆ 选步长**：在当前信赖域半径 Δ 的几何间隔探针 [Δ/3, 3Δ] 上（24 个探针），用 PCHIP 插值找到恰好达到 ϱ⋆ 的步，提供"对当前景观敏感、不依赖历史"的正则化规则。
- **自适应加权 + 自动阶段切换**：UpdateWeights（残差基，参数 α=0.05）保证多条件残差尺度一致（CountSketch 把多条件聚合进共享桶所必需）；UpdateTargetRatio 通过对 λ 历史的滑动窗口对数线性拟合自动切换两阶段。
- **全面且诚实的 SOTA 结果**：在 8 个 PDE（非线性、混沌、多尺度、高维、Navier–Stokes）上比近期强二阶基线提升 3–8 个数量级，并公开代码。

## Key Contributions

- **A conditioning-first optimisation philosophy.** Argues a PINN's accuracy ceiling is set by the *effective regularisation* at the converged iterate — the minimum λ needed for a Gauss–Newton step to still yield a decrease — rather than by the rate of objective decrease. Operationalised as a two-stage method: Stage 1 (small target ratio ϱ ≤ 0.2) drives λ down to locate a well-conditioned region; Stage 2 (large ϱ ≥ 0.5) descends aggressively. The stage switch is automatic.
- **A doubly-sketched Gauss–Newton model.** Compresses the residual dimension with **CountSketch** (OSNAP family, K=2–4 hashes; linear and batch-accumulable) and the parameter dimension with **SRCT** (a subsampled randomised cosine transform / SRTT, near-isometric so the lifted step is faithful). The result is a small s×s square sketch (s ∈ [⌊dθ/3⌋, ⌊dθ/2⌋]) whose single SVD cheaply yields Levenberg–Marquardt steps, predicted reductions, and the λ↔Δ secular map for *any* λ — a decisive departure from the matrix-free (JVP-only) second-order PINN line.
- **Cheap, dense residual sampling.** CountSketch's linearity lets the number of collocation points N grow without memory bound (demonstrated on a 1,048,576 × 252,503 Jacobian built in 7.8 s with s=10,000), sidestepping the residual/collocation-point selection problem endemic to PINNs.
- **Target-ratio step selection (ρ⋆).** Probes 24 geometrically spaced radii within [Δ/3, 3Δ] (centred on the stable trust-region radius Δ, not the volatile λ, which swings 25 orders of magnitude), computes each probe's λ from the secular equation via Newton iterations, evaluates the achieved ratio, and PCHIP-interpolates to the step attaining ϱ⋆. This yields a landscape-aware, history-independent regularisation rule.
- **Supporting machinery.** A residual-based adaptive condition reweighting (UpdateWeights, α=0.05) — necessary because CountSketch mixes all conditions into shared buckets — and automatic stage switching via a sliding-window log-linear fit on λ.
- **Comprehensive, honestly-reported results.** 3–8 orders of magnitude improvement over *recent strong second-order* baselines (not just vanilla Adam/L-BFGS) across 8 PDEs, with code released.

## Methodology

DSGNAR casts PINN training as a weighted nonlinear least-squares problem `L(θ) = ½‖F u_θ‖²` (over PDE residual P, initial condition I, boundary B operators) and solves it with a Gauss–Newton-type method, taking the standard Gauss–Newton Hessian approximation `JᵀJ` (positive semi-definite, and coincident with the energy-natural-gradient step for the PINN objective). The novelty is in *how* the Gauss–Newton machinery is made scalable and *how* steps are chosen:

1. **Conditioning-first step philosophy.** Rather than controlling the regularisation λ (Levenberg–Marquardt) or trust-region radius Δ directly, DSGNAR targets a *decrease ratio* ϱ = (actual objective reduction) / (reduction predicted by the sketched quadratic model). A counter-intuitive claim motivates this: a very "successful" step (ϱ ≈ 1) taken early, before conditioning is fixed, can land in a poor minimum. So Stage 1 uses a *small* target (ϱ_Stage1 = 0.075 double / 0.1 single, ≤ 0.2), which promotes large steps that demand minimal regularisation λ — i.e., it hunts for a well-conditioned region. Once λ bottoms out and starts rising (detected automatically), Stage 2 raises the target (ϱ_Stage2 = 0.5, ≥ 0.5) for aggressive descent.

2. **Doubly-sketched Jacobian.** The full Jacobian J ∈ ℝ^{N×dθ} is never formed. It is compressed on **both** sides:
   - **Rows (residual space) — CountSketch** `C ∈ ℝ^{s×N}`: a sparse oblivious subspace embedding (OSNAP, K=2–4 hashes). Crucially, C is *linear and accumulable over batches/conditions* (`C·J = Σ_batches C·J_batch`), so collocation points can be sampled arbitrarily densely into s fixed buckets; "troublesome" regions dominate their buckets naturally, and noise averages out. The left singular vectors are never lifted back, so row geometry need only be preserved in expectation.
   - **Columns (parameter space) — SRCT** `ΩS = (D·Π·F)·S`: a subsampled randomised cosine transform (DCT-II), an SRTT with FFT-type `O(dθ log dθ)` construction and near-isometric guarantees, so the step solved in sketch space lifts faithfully back to θ.
   The product is a small square `J̃ ∈ ℝ^{s×s}` (s ≈ dθ/3…dθ/2). A single thin SVD `J̃ = UΣVᵀ` then gives the Levenberg–Marquardt step `p̃(λ) = −V·diag(σ_i/(σ_i²+λ))·Uᵀr̃` for *any* λ at O(s²), and the λ↔Δ mapping via the secular equation, without ever forming `J̃ᵀJ̃` (which would square the condition number).

3. **Step selection by probing for ϱ⋆ (LambdaSolve).** Each iteration draws 24 geometrically spaced radius probes in [Δ/3, 3Δ] (centred on Δ because it varies <1 order of magnitude across iterations, whereas λ swings ~25 orders). For each probe, Newton iterations solve the secular equation for λ, the step is computed and lifted, and its true ratio ϱ is evaluated in full space. A PCHIP interpolant (with forced monotonicity for robustness) selects the radius achieving ϱ⋆. Steps are accepted only on objective decrease; the radius shrinks on failure or over-aggressive overshoot; training halts when Δ < Δ_min (10⁻⁴ single, 10⁻⁸ double).

4. **Adaptive reweighting & stage switching.** UpdateWeights keeps the M conditions' residuals on a comparable scale (essential given the shared CountSketch buckets); UpdateTargetRatio fits λ's log-history to flip Stage1→Stage2.

5. **Implementation.** JAX (v0.9.1) / XLA JIT, single NVIDIA H100 (96 GB). A *hybrid-precision* trick runs the first 10 iterations in single precision to correct bad initialisations before switching to double. Time marching (warm-started parameter perturbations across intervals) is used for long-horizon problems like KS. Wall-clock times exclude JIT compile time.

- **Headline accuracy floor:** `ℓ₂^rel = 3.03×10⁻¹⁶` on 5D Poisson and `1.20×10⁻¹⁵` on Wave — at the double-precision round-off limit.
- **Burgers shock resolved well:** counter to typical PINN behaviour, errors are *smallest* along the developing shock, attributed to dense CountSketch-driven sampling in the hard region.
- **Single precision is viable:** Burgers to `4.75×10⁻⁷` in 9.8 s (~100 iterations); on KdV, their *single*-precision result (9.55e-7) beats the best reported *double*-precision literature result.
- **Robustness:** fixed hyperparameters (ϱ_Stage1=0.075/0.1, ϱ_Stage2=0.5, α=0.05, ∆₀=1) across SIREN, MLP, GaborNet, SPINN, LSTM architectures and single/double precision.
- **Sketch size = accuracy/cost knob:** Navier–Stokes with s=1,000 vs s=4,000 trades 6.34e-4/80.4s against 1.13e-4/402.9s.
- **Consistent optimisation signature:** across all problems, λ falls ~25 orders in Stage 1 (well-conditioning the iterate), then Stage 2 collapses the error by ~9 orders in far fewer iterations; ∆ stays within one order of magnitude throughout.
- **Linear convergence observed** throughout the run (vs the sublinear rates typical of prior PINN work) — though this lacks formal theory.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty — High.** The framing of PINN accuracy as a conditioning pathology is well-motivated, but the *specific* combination is distinctive and under-explored: (i) an *explicitly built* doubly-sketched Jacobian (CountSketch × SRCT) rather than the matrix-free JVP line, enabling a single reusable SVD; (ii) replacing λ/Δ tuning with *target-ratio probing*. The conditioning-first two-stage philosophy (seek minimal regularisation *before* descending) is a genuinely thoughtful, somewhat counter-intuitive idea, well-argued.
- **Rigor — Good, with caveats.** The baselines are *recent strong second-order methods* ([Kiy+25] hybrid Adam+quasi-Newton; [Guz+25] randomised Nyström Gauss–Newton; [Dai+26], [And+26], [Chi+26]) — not straw-man Adam/L-BFGS, so the orders-of-magnitude gains are meaningful. The optimisation machinery (secular equation, trust-region acceptance, PCHIP interpolation) is implemented in the textbook-correct way. **However:** (a) the headline extreme-precision numbers (3e-16, 1e-15) are achieved on specific problems/setups and effectively measure proximity to the round-off floor rather than genuinely new physics — they are real but should be read as "DSGNAR can saturate fp64," not "every PDE now solves to 1e-15"; (b) the Navier–Stokes result is the honest weak spot — DSGNAR is *slower* than the [Chi+26] baseline (402.9s vs ~90s) for higher accuracy, and the authors themselves note the bottleneck there has shifted to the *objective formulation*, not the optimiser.
- **Reproducibility — Strong signal.** Public JAX code, fixed default hyperparameters, hardware clearly specified (single H100 96GB). The H100 is high-end, though, and wall times exclude JIT compile time — replicating on consumer GPUs may differ.
- **Significance — High if it generalises.** If PINNs can routinely reach classical-solver precision with a robust, tuning-light optimiser, it materially strengthens the case for PINNs as scientific-computing tools. The sketch-size-as-knob result and the single-precision viability are practically useful even today.
- **Theory — Acknowledged gaps (honestly stated).** The authors explicitly flag that the *composed* doubly-sketched construction lacks a clean subspace-embedding guarantee (the individual CountSketch/SRCT guarantees don't automatically transfer); the ratio-targeting step-selection rule has no convergence proof; and the observed *linear* convergence (better than the sublinear rates of prior PINN work) is unexplained theoretically. These are open, not hidden.
- **Red flags / things to scrutinise:**
  - All experiments use the *basic* PINN objective only — no inverse/noisy-data, no stiff industrial problems; the authors concede the Navier–Stokes objective itself is now the limiter.
  - KS required 4,300 iterations / 16 time-marching intervals / 5,226s — genuinely long-horizon chaotic problems remain expensive despite the gains.
  - Single H100 (96 GB) for all timing; memory-heavy sketch builds may behave differently on smaller GPUs (though Table 2 shows CountSketch avoids OOM down to 1M×250k Jacobians).
  - The "8 orders on high-dimensional Poisson" gain is against `~1e-7` (5D) and `~1e-5` (10D) baselines that are already modest; the *absolute* achievement (3e-16) is the more defensible headline than the relative ratio.

## Strengths

- Principled, well-articulated optimisation philosophy (conditioning-first, two-stage) backed by textbook-correct trust-region/Levenberg–Marquardt machinery.
- The doubly-sketched explicit Jacobian is a clean, scalable idea with a genuinely useful side-effect: dense collocation sampling that dissolves the residual-selection problem.
- Strong, honestly-reported gains over *recent strong* second-order baselines, not weak straw-men.
- Tuning-light robustness across architectures, precision, and problem classes — directly attacking PINNs' most painful practical weakness.
- Public code; Oxford optimisation pedigree; authors candid about open theoretical questions and the Navier–Stokes limitation.

## Limitations

- Second-order sketching carries per-step SVD cost `O(s²·…) + O(s³)`; while amortised by few iterations, scalability to very large 3D / long-time-dependent PDEs beyond the tested suite is not yet demonstrated.
- All experiments use only the basic PINN objective; inverse problems with noisy data, hard industrial constraints, and genuinely stiff systems are untested — the authors concede the bottleneck is shifting to the objective.
- Theory lags practice: no subspace-embedding guarantee for the composed sketch, no proof for the ratio-targeting rule, no explanation of the observed linear convergence.
- Headline near-round-off accuracies are problem/setup-specific and partly measure precision-floor saturation rather than universally transferable accuracy.
- Hardware (H100, 96GB) is high-end; wall times exclude JIT compile time.

## Relevance

对于关注高效数值优化、二阶/随机化（草图化）方法、以及深度学习求解 PDE（PINN 精度瓶颈）的研究方向，本文将"病态优化"作为 PINN 精度根因并给出可扩展、调参少的二阶解法，与高效优化、随机数值线性代数、科学机器学习的研究兴趣高度契合，值得精读并在自有 PDE 基准上复现验证。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does a clean subspace-embedding / convergence guarantee exist for the *composed* CountSketch × SRCT square sketch, and does it predict the empirically sufficient s ≈ dθ/3?
- How does DSGNAR behave on **inverse problems with noisy/sparse data**, where the Hessian's second-order term `Σ r_j ∇²r_j` (discarded by Gauss–Newton) is no longer negligible?
- Can the linear-convergence regime observed empirically be proven, or tied to a specific property of the well-conditioned region Stage 1 locates?
- On Navier–Stokes (where the objective, not the optimiser, is the limiter), does pairing DSGNAR with FBPINNs / domain decomposition push accuracy further, as the authors speculate?
- What is the true wall-clock cost (including JIT compile) and memory profile on consumer-grade GPUs vs the H100, especially for the large-sketch solves in Table 2?
- Could the target-ratio probing replace λ/Δ heuristics in *other* randomised second-order settings (e.g., Newton Sketch for general least-squares), beyond PINNs?
