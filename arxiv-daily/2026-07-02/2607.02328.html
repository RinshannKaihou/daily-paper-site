The Counterintuitive Finding

K-fold cross-validation can estimate class-specific risks worse — not better — than the naive empirical estimator.

Under binary empirical-risk minimization, a cube-root asymptotic analysis shows CV's class-specific discrepancy decays at the sluggish n−1/3 rate, slower than the empirical estimator's n−1/2. The proposed Cross-Audit Projection (CAP) projects that bias out — and stays first-order equivalent to the empirical risk.

§ 01 — METHODOLOGY A cube-root rate explains the failure. Under ERM, the classifier converges at Kim–Pollard's cube-root n1/3 rate. That single fact propagates: the empirical risk's over-optimism is a second-order n−2/3 bias, while CV's class-specific tracking decays at the slower n−1/3.
Empirical estimator
The benchmark it should beat
n−1/2
Converges at the parametric rate. Carries a second-order over-optimism bias of order n−2/3 (Theorem 3) — small, but the reason for CAP's correction.
K-fold cross-validation
The headline failure
n−1/3
CV is unbiased for the CV-ensemble risk — but that ensemble differs from the model risk. The class-specific gap (Corollary 1) closes only at n−1/3: too slow to keep up with the empirical estimator.

Problem setting. Binary classification with an m-vector feature Xd per class; a linear classifier I(b⊤Xd°>0); class-specific risks ψd(b) and overall ψ(b)=ωψ₀+(1−ω)ψ₁ with user weight ω. ERM (Eq. 1) is solved via the Huang & Sanda (2022) algorithm — penalty reformulation + indicator smoothing + concave-convex procedure.

The asymptotic backbone. Conditions 1–5 give Theorem 1 (strong consistency) and Theorem 2 (cube-root weak convergence): n1/3(β̂−β) ⇝ U = argmin Z(s), for Z(s)=W(s)+s⊤Hs/2.

The negative result. Corollary 1 is the crux: although CV is unbiased for the ensemble risk, that ensemble differs from the model risk. The class-specific discrepancy (10) decays at only n1/3 — so ψd,cv → ψd(β̂) at order n−1/3, slower than the empirical estimator's n−1/2.

Overall vs class-specific. For overall risk the CV discrepancy is n−2/3 — CV is first-order equivalent to the empirical estimator but with a positive (conservative) second-order bias. The pathology is specific to class-specific risk, exactly where it hurts most in diagnostics.

§ 01b — THE REMEDY CAP: diagnose with the same resampling, then project out. A two-step procedure. The cross-audit reuses CV's own K-fold split to measure over-optimism; the projection shrinks that bias by the theory-derived {(K−1)/K}2/3 factor — never tuned.

Projection
Shrink the bias estimate by {(K−1)/K}2/3 — matching the n−2/3 order of the second-order asymptotic bias (Theorem 3). A monotone transform Q (standard-normal quantile function) preserves the [0,1] range.

What CAP inherits. First-order equivalence to the empirical risk (so it inherits the fast n−1/2 baseline) while removing the second-order n−2/3 bias — instead of inheriting CV's n−1/3 drag.

Inference, not just points. Class-specific bounds mimic the Wilson score interval for binomial proportions: the larger solution q to nd(ψ̂d,cap−q)²/[q(1−q)] = z²δd. An overall bound combines (q₀,q₁) via a quadratic inference function — joint coverage δ₀δ₁.

Insensitive to K — and extends to LR-ERM.
Unlike CV, CAP barely moves with K. Repeated 2-fold CAP (16 reps) is the adopted config; results extend to LR-ERM classifiers and to repeated K-fold CV/CAP.
§ 03 — STRENGTHS / LIMITATIONS A derived fix, in a narrow proven window. The intellectual core is theory-derived, not tuned. The honest scoping is equally clear — and the author states it openly.
Strengths05
  • Identifies and theoretically explains (not just observes) a failure mode of the single most-used validation technique.
  • A principled, asymptotic-theory-grounded remedy whose key parameter — the {(K−1)/K}2/3 shrinkage — is derived, not tuned.
  • Delivers both point estimation and inference (risk bounds); the latter is notably hard with raw K-fold CV.
  • Insensitivity to K (Remark 3): repeated 2-fold CAP is cheaper than 10-fold CV.
  • Honest about limitations — generalization and data-driven bias-order estimation flagged openly.
Limitations06
  • Proven results are narrow: linear ERM binary classification with cube-root asymptotics. Extension to regression, multiclass, or parametric-rate learners is conjectural.
  • Severe class imbalance — where class-specific risk matters most — is never stress-tested in simulations.
  • No released code/data pipeline; reproducibility depends on reimplementation.
  • Baselines exclude 0.632 / 0.632+ bootstrap and leave-one-out CV.
  • The asymptotic-bias order — central to CAP's projection — is not always known for modern complex algorithms, forcing a "working" factor that forfeits the formal guarantee.
  • Single-author, single-real-dataset (n=116) evaluation.
§ 04 — OPEN QUESTIONS Where the cube-root story might — or might not — travel.
Q · 01
Does the phenomenon and the CAP fix extend to multiclass, regression, and survival models — or is it tightly tied to cube-root asymptotics?
Q · 02
How does CAP behave under severe class imbalance and highly immissible-class weighting, where class-specific risk estimation is most consequential?
Q · 03
Can the asymptotic-bias order be estimated from data reliably enough to make CAP "fully automatic," or is the working factor the pragmatic ceiling?
Q · 04
How does CAP compare against 0.632 / 0.632+ bootstrap, leave-one-out CV, and Bates–Hastie–Tibshirani (2024) CV-inference — not just plain 10-fold CV?
Q · 05
Is repeated 2-fold CAP (16 reps) actually cheaper than 10-fold CV in practice, and does it scale to high-dimensional feature spaces (m≫6)?
Q · 06
Could the cross-audit diagnosis serve as a generic flag for when standard CV is unreliable for a given dataset/algorithm pair?
