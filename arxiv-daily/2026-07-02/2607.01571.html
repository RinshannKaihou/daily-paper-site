Why This Matters

A training-free, inference-time geometric measure of CoT complexity that hints at when to stop reasoning — squarely relevant to test-time compute allocation.

Distilled from Relevance
Critical Edge

The 0.93 AUC rests on ~27 questions and a single 0.5B model with pseudo-replicated t-tests; the theory proves only flatness ⇒ high dρ, not that hardness causes flatness.

No scaling story — one model, one domain, no baselines.

02

Methodology — the spectral pipeline

STEP 01

Trajectory as a curve

Each generated token yields a hidden state Ht(ℓ); a reasoning chain becomes a discrete curve γ=(h₁…hₙ) in ℝ⁸⁹⁶.

STEP 02

Covariance & eigen-spectrum

Center the trajectory, form covariance C, compute eigenvalues ν₁≥…≥νd.

C(γ)=⅟ₙ·H̄ᵀH̄
STEP 03

Effective dimension dρ

Fewest principal components capturing a ρ-fraction of variance — training-free, per-instance.

dρ=min{k: Σνᵢ ≥ ρ·tr(C)}
STEP 04

Linear-algebra theory

Prop 1 spectral bounds · Prop 2 majorization ⇒ flat spectrum maximizes dρ · Thm 3 Weyl-based stability.

STEP 05

Kinematic features → classifier

Top-15 PCA: mean position, dispersion, init/final state, mean velocity, speed, speed dispersion → LR / MLP / GRU.

Results & highlights

AUC vs. layer — effective dimension dρ
Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct · leave-one-question-out CV · easy (diff-1) vs hard (diff-5)
dρ AUC per layer peak layer 21 · 0.93 chance = 0.50

Hardness from effective dimension

AUC climbs from ~0.81 at layer 0 to 0.93 at layer 21; every layer carries signal. Hard problems mean dρ≈170 vs easy ≈121.6 (40% gap).

Early correctness prediction

From just 20% of tokens: cross-question LR AUC 0.806±0.132, within-question 5-fold ≈0.90–0.91. Signal saturates early.

Geometry beats the recurrent net

A logistic regression on coarse geometric features outperforms a GRU on the full sequence — the useful signal lives in low-dimensional summaries, not token dynamics.

Anomaly

GRU degrades with more tokens (0.754 @ 70% → 0.577 @ 100%) — unexplained, flagged as a robustness risk.

04

Strengths & limitations — side by side

◆ Strengths

  • Clean geometric/spectral formalization with a genuinely useful, training-free, per-instance quantity dρ.
  • Honest, self-aware Limitations section that names the missing causal link and single-model scope.
  • Thoughtful positioning against concurrent work (Sun, Zhou, Prasad, Javanmard) — clarifies what is new.
  • Within- vs cross-question ablation is an informative design for isolating transferable signal.
  • Clear headline metrics; AUPRC used well for the best-of-n ranking motivation.

◆ Limitations

  • Single 0.5B model; no larger models, other families, or non-math domains — generalization entirely open.
  • ~27 questions total; AUC is high-variance and the t-test p-value is invalid via pseudo-replication.
  • Theory links flatness→dρ but offers no mechanism for why hard tasks flatten the spectrum.
  • Correctness results are AUC-only; no end-to-end early-stopping or best-of-n compute trade-off shown.
  • GRU degradation unexplained; no baselines vs trivial proxies (chain length, token count, embeddings).
05

Open questions for further exploration

Q1

Does dρ predict hardness and enable early stopping on larger models (7B/70B) and non-math domains — code, multi-hop QA, commonsense?

Q2

How much of the 0.93 AUC survives after controlling for trivial confounds: chain length, token count, prompt-embedding norms?

Q3

Why does the GRU degrade with more tokens while LR improves — genuine early geometry, or a low-dimensional offset artifact?

Q4

Can a mechanistic theory causally link task hardness to spectral flatness of the internal trajectory, beyond flatness⇒dρ?

Q5

Can the kinematic predictor become a calibrated best-of-n ranker / early-exit policy with measured accuracy-vs-compute Pareto gains?

Q6

Is the easy/hard split an artifact of MATH500's human labels, or does it hold against a behavioral definition (per-question pass@k)?
