> **一句话总结：** kNNGuard 是一种无需训练的 LLM 防护栏，它将冻结 LLM 的多层隐藏激活与 MiniLM 嵌入分别做 kNN，再通过置信度自适应融合（Fisher 判别加权 + 胜者通吃/置信度混合）做安全/离题分类；仅用每类 50 条样本的标签库，在 6 个领域（含 3 个安全领域）上平均 F1 达 87.4%、FPR 12.9%，比同类 SOTA 防护栏快 2.7 倍，且域适应仅需 7.95 秒（比 LoRA 微调快 3156 倍）。

> **TL;DR:** kNNGuard is a training-free LLM guardrail that reuses a frozen LLM as a feature extractor. It runs kNN over multi-layer hidden activations (weighted by Fisher discriminant scores) and over MiniLM sentence embeddings, then fuses the two risk scores via a confidence-adaptive rule (winner-takes-all when the branches disagree strongly, otherwise confidence-weighted blending). With only 50 labeled examples per class, it attains 87.4% average F1 / 12.9% FPR across 6 domains (topical + security), beats or matches fine-tuned SOTA guardrails (Llama Nemotron Topic/Safety Guard, Llama Guard 3, Prompt Guard 2), runs at 46.8 ms/prompt (2.7× faster than Nemotron Topic Guard V1, ~10× faster than Nemotron Safety Guard V2), and adapts to a new domain in 7.95 s by just swapping the bank — a claimed 3156× speedup over LoRA fine-tuning. The clearest wins are on topical domains (≥95% F1); security domains are harder (73–82% F1) but kNNGuard still gives the most balanced FPR/ASR trade-off.

## 核心贡献

- 提出无训练的防护栏框架 kNNGuard，直接在冻结 LLM 的激活空间做非参数分类，满足"低延迟、无需微调、快速域适应"三项实际部署需求。
- 设计两个变体：(1) **LE（Layer Ensemble）** 用 Fisher 判别分数对多层（9 层，间隔均匀含 layer 0 与末层）激活做 softmax 加权拼接；(2) **FE（Fused Ensemble）** 通过置信度自适应机制融合激活空间 kNN 与 MiniLM 嵌入 kNN——两路置信度差距 > γ=0.1 时取更确定的一路，否则做置信度加权混合。
- 证明仅 50 条/类的极小标签库即可工作，且域适应只需替换标签库（+可选系统提示），100 样本建库 7.95 秒、13854 样本 413 秒，分别比 LoRA 微调快 3156× 与 60.8×。
- 在 6 个领域（代码指令、代码输出、医学、安全、越狱、提示注入）上，与 5 个微调/轻量 SOTA 防护栏对比，平均 F1 最高（87.4%）、FPR 最低（12.9%），且推理 46.8 ms（比 Nemotron Topic Guard V1 快 2.7×、比 Nemotron Safety Guard V2 快约 10×）。
- 系统分析了系统提示对激活几何的影响（FPR 降低 66%、F1 0.842→0.874）、激活空间 vs 嵌入空间的可分性（t-SNE + Silhouette，相对提升 89.0% 与 53.2%）、6 种骨干模型（含 abliterated 模型）的鲁棒性，并给出生产集成与成本指引。

## Key Contributions

- A training-free guardrail framework that classifies prompts directly in the activation space of a frozen, off-the-shelf LLM, explicitly targeting three deployment requirements: low latency, no fine-tuning, and rapid domain adaptation.
- Two variants: **kNNGuard LE** (Layer Ensemble) aggregates last-token hidden activations from 9 evenly-spaced transformer layers (incl. layer 0 and the final layer), weighted by a Fisher-discriminant separability score via softmax; **kNNGuard FE** (Fused Ensemble) combines the activation-space kNN score with a MiniLM embedding-space kNN score via a confidence-adaptive rule (winner-takes-all when the confidence gap Δ > γ=0.1; otherwise confidence-weighted blending).
- Demonstrates that a tiny 50-per-class labeled bank suffices, and that domain adaptation reduces to replacing the bank (+ optional system prompt), with bank-construction times of 7.95 s (100 samples) and 413 s (13,854 samples) — claimed 3156× and 60.8× faster than LoRA fine-tuning.
- A broad evaluation across 6 domains and 16 datasets against 5 SOTA baselines (Nemotron Topic Guard V1, Nemotron Safety Guard V2, Llama Guard 3, Prompt Guard 2, Embedding-kNN), reporting the best average F1 (87.4%) and lowest FPR (12.9%) at 46.8 ms/prompt (2.7× and ~10× faster than the two fine-tuned 8B guards).
- Empirical analyses of: system-prompt conditioning (cuts FPR by 66%, F1 0.842→0.874), activation- vs embedding-space separability (t-SNE + Silhouette, +89.0% / +53.2% relative), backbone robustness across 6 LLMs (incl. an abliterated model), and bank-size sensitivity (n=50 optimal; larger banks hurt via borderline neighbours).

## Methodology

**Goal & formulation.** Given a labeled reference bank D = {(xᵢ, yᵢ)} (yᵢ=1 if off-topic/unsafe), build a training-free decision g_FE(x)→{0,1}. Inputs are optionally wrapped with a domain-specific system prompt: x̃ = P(x; π). Both the LLM Θ_F and embedding model Θ_E stay frozen (∇L = 0 for both).

**Two-phase pipeline (Fig. 1).**
- *Bank building (once):* Each bank prompt is formatted with the system prompt, passed through the frozen LLM, and the **last-token hidden activations** from the selected layers are extracted and cached. The same prompts are also encoded by a frozen MiniLM and cached. Domain adaptation = replace/update these banks.
- *Inference (per prompt):* The query is formatted identically, passed through the LLM and MiniLM, and compared against the cached banks.

**Activation-based Layer Ensemble (LE).** For each layer ℓ, compute class means μ₀⁽ˡ⁾, μ₁⁽ˡ⁾; between-class separation B_ℓ = ‖μ₀−μ₁‖²/(2d_ℓ); within-class dispersion W_ℓ (averaged per-feature variance + ε); Fisher score J_ℓ = B_ℓ/W_ℓ; softmax-normalized weight α_ℓ. The activation representation is the weighted concatenation ϕ_act(x̃) = ⊕ α_ℓ·norm(h⁽ˡ⁾(x̃)).

**Embedding representation.** ϕ_emb(x̃) = norm(E(x̃)) with MiniLM.

**kNN risk scores.** Cosine distance in each space; k=13 nearest neighbours (chosen via leave-one-out CV on the bank). Branch risk s_r(x) = fraction of unsafe labels among the k neighbours, r∈{act,emb}.

**Fused Ensemble (FE, the main method).** Fixed α-blending is the trivial baseline: s_α = λ·s_act + (1−λ)·s_emb. The adaptive rule (Fig. 2) measures each branch's confidence as distance from threshold τ=0.5: c_act=|s_act−τ|, c_emb=|s_emb−τ|; gap Δ=|c_act−c_emb|; if Δ>γ (=0.1) take the more-confident branch (winner-takes-all), else confidence-weighted blend; final decision I[s_FE(x)≥τ].

**Hyperparameters.** k=13 (LOOCV), n=50/class, 9 layers, τ=0.5, γ=0.1, λ=0.5, all selected on a held-out validation set while models stay frozen. Bank size study showed 50/class is best; 250/1000 introduced more borderline neighbours and higher FPR.

## Results & Highlights

**Aggregate (Table 2, 6 domains):** kNNGuard FE — F1 87.4%, Recall 86.6%, FPR 12.9%, FNR 13.4%, 46.8 ms. vs. Nemotron Topic Guard V1 82.7% F1 / 126.0 ms; Nemotron Safety Guard V2 79.2% F1 / 454.6 ms; Llama Guard 3 74.2% / 104.5 ms; Prompt Guard 2 70.4% / 9.7 ms; Embedding-kNN 79.6% / 4.0 ms.

**Topical domains (Table 3):** kNNGuard FE reaches 99.1% (Code Instructions), 99.3% (Code Outputs), 95.3% (Medical) F1, all with FPR ≤0.9%. Nemotron Topic Guard V1 collapses on Code Instructions (83.4% F1, 39.1% FPR) while kNNGuard stays ≥99.1% on both coding sub-domains with the same system prompt — evidence of distributional robustness.

**Security domains (Table 4):** kNNGuard FE — Safety 73.7% F1 / 34.6% FPR / 14.3% ASR; Jailbreak 75.0% / 25.8% / 29.6% ASR; Prompt Injection 82.2% / 15.3% / 26.2% ASR. It delivers the most *balanced* FPR/ASR profile: every baseline collapses on at least one error component (Nemotron Topic Guard 97.4% FPR on jailbreak; Prompt Guard 2 lets 50.2% of jailbreaks through; Embedding-kNN 60%+ FPR on safety/jailbreak). No single fine-tuned guard beats kNNGuard on FPR *and* ASR simultaneously.

**System prompt effect (Fig. 6):** Adding the prompt cuts FPR from 0.385→0.129 (−66%) and raises F1 0.842→0.874, but raises FNR 0.065→0.134 and latency 32.7→46.8 ms. I.e., conditioning makes the guard more conservative (higher precision, lower recall).

**Representation separability (Fig. 7):** t-SNE + Silhouette scores show kNNGuard's activation space yields +89.0% (Coding) and +53.2% (Medical) relative Silhouette improvement over MiniLM embedding space, geometrically supporting the activation-space claim.

**Domain-adaptation cost (Fig. 8, Table 5):** 100-sample bank = 7.95 s (3156× vs LoRA); 13,854-sample bank = 413 s (60.8× vs LoRA's 25,092 s ≈ 7 h).

**Backbone robustness (Tables 6–7, Figs. 10–11):** Tested on Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, Mistral-7B, Phi-4-mini, Gemma-4-12B (base+instruct), Llama-3-8B-abliterated. F1 is broadly consistent on topical domains; prompt-injection is the most backbone-sensitive (e.g., Mistral drops to 50.3% F1 / 65.5% ASR), consistent with injection interacting with instruction-following mechanisms.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty — moderate, incremental.** Using internal LLM activations for safety/topical detection is an established family (refusal-direction work: Arditi et al. 2024; activation monitoring: Patel & Wang 2025; SafeSwitch: Han et al. 2025; embedding-kNN guardrails: NeMo). kNNGuard's specific contribution is the *engineering combination* of (a) multi-layer Fisher-weighted activation ensembling + (b) adaptive confidence fusion with embedding-kNN, packaged as a training-free, rapidly reconfigurable system. The fusion logic and the confidence-adaptive rule are reasonable but not conceptually new; the value is in the systematization and the empirical demonstration that this beats fine-tuned guards on latency + balanced error.
- **Rigor — mostly sound, with caveats.**
  - *Apples-to-apples concerns:* The headline "competitive or superior F1" mixes domains. kNNGuard is evaluated on all 6, but Nemotron Safety Guard V2, Llama Guard 3, and Prompt Guard 2 are marked with "*" as evaluated on "relevant domains only" (Table 2 footnote) — so the averaged comparison is not strictly like-for-like. The paper flags this, but casual readers will still over-read the headline.
  - *Security numbers are modest, not strong:* 73.7% Safety F1 with 34.6% FPR and 14.3–29.6% ASR is operationally borderline for a real safety guardrail. The "best balanced FPR/ASR" framing is true but partly reflects that competitors are *broken* rather than that kNNGuard is *good* in absolute terms on adversarial inputs.
  - *Single fixed bank per domain:* Results use one fixed bank + eval set "to ensure reproducibility"; the random-resampling robustness check (Fig. 3) is reported only for one configuration (kNNGuard FE) on 2000 prompts/class. No per-domain variance/error bars on the main tables.
  - *No adaptive-adversary evaluation:* The paper cites Nasr et al. 2025 ("attacker moves second") showing static benchmarks underestimate vulnerability, but does **not** test kNNGuard against adaptive white-box adversaries crafted to push safe-looking activations into the unsafe cluster — the single biggest threat to any activation-space method. This is a notable gap.
  - *Hyperparameters (k=13, τ=0.5, γ=0.1, λ=0.5, layer set) are tuned per-domain on a validation set,* which is legitimate but means "training-free" is not "hyperparameter-free"; per-domain tuning is a form of light supervision.
  - *Latency measurement:* 46.8 ms includes activation extraction; the comparison hardware is consistent (RTX 6000 Ada), but the fine-tuned baselines run full 8B forward passes while kNNGuard also runs a full 8B forward pass (to get activations) plus MiniLM plus kNN — the 2.7× win is plausible because kNNGuard stops at hidden states without autoregressive generation, but the exact measurement protocol (warm-up, batching, median vs mean) is underspecified.
- **Reproducibility — promising but code-release unclear.** Training-free + small bank + public datasets (MBPP, Alpaca, MedMCQA, Aegis, WildJailbreak, BIPIA, etc.) makes reproduction feasible. The paper does not explicitly state code/bank release. Layer indices, system prompts (Appendix A.1), and fusion equations are fully specified, which is good.
- **Significance — real for production, limited for adversarial-defense research.** The fast adaptation story (seconds vs hours) and the balanced-error profile are genuinely useful for ops teams that need reconfigurable, low-latency guards. As an *adversarial* defense, the absence of adaptive-attack evaluation caps the security claims.
- **Red flags / overclaiming watch:**
  - Headline "competitive or superior F1 compared to fine-tuned SOTA" is true on topical domains and on the balanced-error argument, but the security F1s (73–82%) and the *-marked non-comparable baselines temper it.
  - "10× faster than a fine-tuned safety classifier" is vs Nemotron Safety Guard V2, an unusually slow 8B model (454 ms) — a faster fine-tuned guard (e.g., Prompt Guard 2 at 9.7 ms) would invert the latency story.
  - The 3156× speedup vs LoRA conflates *bank building* (forward passes) with *training* (backward passes) — directionally valid but a marketing-flavored framing.

## Strengths

- Genuinely training-free; the two-phase cache design makes domain adaptation a bank-swap (~8 s for 100 samples), a strong practical property.
- Strong, clean wins on topical domains (≥95% F1, ≤0.9% FPR), with explicit demonstration of distributional robustness across two related coding sub-domains where the fine-tuned baseline collapses.
- Thoughtful dual-view design (activation + embedding) with an interpretable confidence-adaptive fusion rule, well motivated by the t-SNE/Silhouette analysis.
- The most balanced FPR/ASR operating point among all evaluated guards on security domains — operationally the most usable profile when both availability and safety matter.
- Good experimental breadth: 6 domains, 16 datasets, 6 backbones, system-prompt ablation, bank-size study, LOOCV hyperparameter selection, and an honest footnote about non-comparable baselines.

## Limitations

- **No adaptive-adversary evaluation** — the central vulnerability of activation-space methods is white-box attacks that shift activations; this is precisely the scenario the paper itself cites (Nasr et al. 2025) but does not test.
- **Modest absolute security performance** — 73–82% F1 with 14–30% ASR is not yet production-grade for high-stakes safety; the "best balanced" framing partly depends on competitors being broken.
- **Backbone-coupled** — performance (especially prompt injection) varies across LLMs; a model upgrade or quantization change can require re-extracting banks and re-tuning layer selection.
- **Not hyperparameter-free** — per-domain selection of k, τ, γ, λ, layer set on a validation set is light supervision, slightly at odds with the "training-free/configurable" emphasis.
- **Latency headline is fragile** — the 10× claim relies on an unusually slow baseline; against lightweight fine-tuned classifiers (Prompt Guard 2, 9.7 ms) kNNGuard is slower.
- **Bank-size sweet spot may not generalize** — n=50 being optimal is interesting but the explanation ("larger banks add borderline neighbours") suggests the kNN vote is noisy; a smarter retrieval/weighting might change this.
- **Single fixed bank + eval per domain** in the main tables; limited variance reporting across bank draws.
- **Code/bank release not explicitly stated** in the body.

## Relevance

对于关注"高效、低成本、可快速适配的 LLM 安全防护"以及"基于模型内部表征的可解释/轻量检测"方向的研究者，kNNGuard 提供了一个无需训练、亚百毫秒延迟、几秒即可换域的实用方案，与 LLM 安全部署、防护栏工程、激活探针/可解释性监控等议题高度相关；但其对抗鲁棒性尚未经自适应攻击验证，若研究重点在强对抗防御则需谨慎看待其安全结论。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does kNNGuard hold up against **adaptive white-box adversaries** that optimize prompts to land in the safe-activation cluster (the Nasr et al. "attacker moves second" threat)? Can a bank-aware gradient attack easily evade it?
- The fixed τ=0.5 / γ=0.1 / λ=0.5 are tuned per-domain — how much does performance degrade if these are set **once globally**, and is there a principled (validation-free) way to set them?
- Is the layer-weighting (Fisher softmax) robust to **model updates/quantization**, and how often must the bank + layer set be refreshed in a live deployment?
- Why does **larger bank size hurt** — is the kNN vote dominated by noise, and would distance-weighted kNN or a small learned projection (still training-light) recover the gains at n=250/1000?
- Can the embedding branch (MiniLM) be replaced by the LLM's own final-layer embedding to remove the separate model and its deployment cost, without losing the fusion benefit?
- Does the system-prompt conditioning result generalize — i.e., can a **learned/optimized** "conditioning vector" (à la activation steering) replace the hand-written system prompt to push separability further?
- On the security domains, what is the per-attack-*family* breakdown of the 14–30% ASR — are certain jailbreak families (e.g., encoding tricks vs. roleplay) systematically missed?
