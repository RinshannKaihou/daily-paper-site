Why This Matters

A new paradigm — cooperative-game-theory attribution inside the RL reward loop — gives path-level credit assignment for GRPO/RLVR reasoning where outcome rewards get hacked by free riders.

Critical Edge

The +42.3% is the upper-bound Pass@16, not single-shot accuracy — Mean@16 gains are "more moderate" and on AIME25 it regresses (17.2 vs 17.7). The abstract downplays the trade-off.

01 Methodology · Shapley Reward Attribution

Built on GRPO with three components. The core move: treat the K parallel paths as players in a cooperative game and reward each by its marginal contribution, not by whether the summary's final answer happened to be right (a surface that free-riding or misleading paths exploit).

1 · Tag-guided structure. The model emits <Parallel>/<Path>×K/<Summary>; cold-start SFT on Parallel-GSM8K (230 steps) instills the format.
2 · Shapley via GRM. A Generative Reward Model scores any path coalition U(S)∈[0,5] (default qwen-plus). Exact Shapley is 2^K, so a Monte Carlo permutation estimate averages marginal gains Δj over M random orderings, converging O(1/√M).
3 · Two-level token rewards. Outcome reward Rout∈{0,1} lands on the last token; each path Shapley value ϕ̃j lands on its closing <Path_j> token. GRPO group-normalizes the advantage. Trained 40 steps on DAPO (VERL codebase).
combined token reward rt = λo·Rout·𝟙[t=T] + λp·Σj ϕ̃j·𝟙[t=ej]

Exact M and λo/λp weights are not reported in the main text — a reproducibility gap.

02 Attribution > Evaluator Strength

The same-evaluator ablation is the cleanest result: holding the GRM fixed, coalition-aware Shapley beats Independent-Path and Leave-One-Out attribution — so the win is the attribution design, not a stronger reward model.

04 Strengths

  • Principled, original use of cooperative game theory — directly targets a real reward-hacking failure mode in summary-based parallel reasoning.
  • Monte Carlo permutation makes Shapley tractable (O(1/√M)); two-level reward cleanly separates correctness from marginal contribution.
  • Strong training efficiency: 40 steps match/exceed a 200-step baseline, with a cleaner curve and earlier plateau.
  • Same-evaluator ablation (Independent 46.5 vs Shapley 48.3) rules out "it's just a stronger GRM" — and the masking diagnostic is clever.

05 Limitations

  • Single backbone only (Qwen3-4B-Base); no scaling-law evidence or larger-model validation.
  • GRM query count grows with K — a compute bottleneck; exact M and λo/λp weights unreported.
  • Pass@16-vs-Mean@16 trade-off: raises the upper bound but not single-generation accuracy; AIME25 Mean@16 underperforms (17.2 vs 17.7).
  • Top results lean on the proprietary qwen-plus GRM; peak numbers are API-contingent.
  • Generalization beyond math rests on an appendix-only multi-hop QA teaser; code-gen is future work.

06 Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does Parallel Shapley scale beyond 4B — does the Pass@16-vs-Mean@16 trade-off widen, narrow, or invert on 14B/32B backbones?
  2. Can on-policy self-distillation (their planned direction) convert Pass@16 gains into Mean@16 gains without losing path diversity?
  3. How sensitive are results to GRM calibration — would a reward model trained specifically for coalition-level utility outperform general instruct LLMs?
  4. What is the wall-clock / dollar overhead of M permutations × coalition GRM calls vs plain Parallel-R1, and can stratified Shapley approximation reduce it?
  5. Does the "free-rider" diagnosis hold for non-math domains (code-gen, multi-hop QA, agentic tool use) where complementarity is less obvious?
