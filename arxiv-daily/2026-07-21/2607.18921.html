Circuit Claims Depend on What Is Extracted and How It Is Compared

Yang Sheng · Jie Fu | Fudan · Shanghai Innovation Institute · IQuest Research | 2026-07-21

01 Methodology

A controlled stability audit — not a new extraction method

A controlled stability audit of circuit extraction. The task is known by construction, so extraction/comparison choices become the only free variable.

Benchmark. Nine synthetic Lean tactic-prediction tasks — four atomic (single proof rule, e.g. exact And.intro h_p h_q) and five compositional (multi-rule). Examples are sampled dynamically from fixed propositional rules with randomized names and distractor premises, suppressing fixed-string memorization. Behaviour is reported on two independently sampled 100-example test suites.

Model. One 8-layer transformer (~415.7M params, d_model=2048, 128 heads) trained at five weight-sparsity levels (dense, 25/50/75/90%) × 3 seeds. Supervised: 5B tokens. RL: GRPO, 500 composition-only prompts/task, 8 generations, 10 epochs. Three data families × three RL init conditions (Curriculum / Atomic-only / Composition-only).

Audited axes. Three circuit objects (core circuit, extended support graph, fixed-loss pruning graph) × two Q/K representations (coupled vs. factorized) × a loss-budget sweep (τ ∈ {0.04…0.20}). Each comparison asks whether the qualitative takeaway survives changing one choice while holding checkpoints and tasks fixed.

03 Strengths

  • Clean experimental design that genuinely isolates extraction/comparison choices from task-mechanism differences — the synthetic rule-generated benchmark is the right tool for the question.
  • Central negative result reported openly — exact edge lists not reproducible, sometimes at chance — with a p-value against a random baseline rather than spin.
  • Comprehensive sweep across 3 graph objects, 2 Q/K representations, 5 loss budgets, 5 sparsity levels, 3 seeds, 3 training-distribution families.
  • Bridge-fraction metric and the read → Q/K-selector → value-mixture → write → MLP functional path (Fig. 4) tie the abstract stability argument to a concrete mechanistic picture.
  • Distilled reporting practice is a concrete, low-cost field-level contribution.

04 Limitations

  • Single architecture, single scale (415.7M params, 8 layers), single synthetic benchmark family — larger scale, other architectures, and heterogeneous benchmarks flagged as future work.
  • Graph evidence confined to chosen checkpoints (dense + 75%-sparse, 16+16 entries) — representative exemplars, not population-level frequency estimates.
  • Behaviour–structure association non-causal — bridge fraction supported by only two high-leverage curriculum cells.
  • Circuit-level stability under format shift unvalidated — boundary tests measure behaviour, not circuit structure.
  • Shared proof-state rules may inflate format-cue sharing, making the synthetic setting more favourable to coarse-level stability than realistic natural-language tasks.

05 Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does the "exact-edge low / routing-head high" pattern hold for large natural-language models (indirect-object identification, entity tracking), or is it partly an artifact of the synthetic Lean family's shared format cues?
  2. At what model scale does edge-level reproducibility break down or recover — is there a regime where exact edges become stable?
  3. Can the bridge-fraction ↔ compositional-gain association be turned into a causal claim via selective ablation of bridge nodes?
  4. How do extraction choices not audited here (ablation type, reference distribution, attribution method) interact with the reported-vs-stability trade-off?
  5. Would the recommended reporting practice, if adopted, change the conclusions of existing published circuit analyses when their extraction parameters are re-stated and re-checked?
