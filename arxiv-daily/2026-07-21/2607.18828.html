The evaluator is itself a confound

Evaluating medical AI under missing information — same-provider judges and human raters change apparent safety

Koyar Afrasyab, M.D. · Kinvectum AB, Sweden · sole-author, founder-funded

llm-as-a-judge self-preference bias missing-information robustness clinical AI safety

Key finding

Using a same-provider LLM as judge silently inflates a medical model's apparent safety — and reorders the leaderboard.

Key metric · GPT-5.5 self-judging

0.14 → 0.30

Inappropriate-confident rate on HealthBench open-ended probe (n=50). Under leave-own-provider-out judging it more than doubles, dropping GPT-5.5 from 2nd-best to near-worst.

same-provider · permutation p = 0.04 · Fleiss κ = 0.65

Why this matters

Don't rank a model with its own sibling judge; LLM-judged open-ended safety reads optimistic vs. clinicians, and reliability ≠ calibration ≠ validity.

Critical edge

Core safety claim rests on n=50 non-stratified items (first 50 of HealthBench consensus) — and the GPT-5.5-specific same-provider effect is not individually significant (CI crosses zero).

01 Methodology

Extends Gu et al.'s (Nature Medicine 2026) missing-information stress-test from closed-ended/multimodal tasks to open-ended clinical conversation, then treats the evaluator as a measured confound.

Four frontier models are scored in real time via the verifiers harness: Claude Opus 4.8, GPT-5.5, Grok 4.3, Gemini 3.5 Flash (a mid-tier substitute — Gemini 3.1 Pro was rate-limited out at 13/100 calls).

For each of the first n=50 HealthBench consensus conversations, the latter half of the final user turn is deleted. A robust reply acknowledges the gap, qualifies, or asks a clarifying question; a confident definitive answer is scored inappropriate-confident.

The same completions are re-scored by a four-provider LLM-judge panel. A vote-level logistic regression (subject FE + judge FE + same-provider term, prompt-clustered bootstrap) and an exact permutation over all 24 bijective own-judge assignments separate same-provider preference from general leniency. A blinded 50-item clinician subsample (two independent clinicians O, G as co-primary + author R1) anchors validity; a MedQA n=100 anchor (McNemar / ±5pp equivalence) isolates calibration from knowledge.

+ Strengths

  • Operationalizes readiness as behavior under input degradation, not accuracy with the answer present.
  • Treats the evaluator as a first-class object — same-provider preference is separated from general leniency, not merely reported as a raw gap.
  • Explicitly declines to publish a leaderboard; demotes model ordering to a sensitivity analysis.
  • Transparent prespecified-vs-post-hoc declaration; post-hoc analyses labeled throughout.
  • Exemplary artifact release — per-item outputs, judge votes, every rater's labels, perturbation_audit.csv, named scripts — every rate recomputable.
  • Four genuinely useful framing points: three kinds of "missing information" aren't equivalent; same-provider ≈ exact-model pairing; clinician disagreement may be policy variation; reliability/calibration/validity are distinct axes.

− Limitations

  • Small, non-stratified samples (n=50 open-ended, first 50 of HealthBench consensus); most pairwise model contrasts not statistically separable.
  • Sole author = founder-funder = one of three raters — bounded and disclosed, but not independent.
  • Gemini is a Flash-tier substitute, not a flagship — Google data point is a tier mismatch.
  • Baseline and probe use different judges (GPT-4.1-mini vs GPT-5.5) due to provider moderation — not a matched pair.
  • Author-performed perturbation audit found substantial artifacts (7/33 administrative, 14/33 still-answerable); cleaner variable-level deletion not implemented.
  • Open-ended criterion is a single coarse binary; prudent safety-netting can be mis-scored as over-commitment.
  • Same-provider effect is an association; mechanism (identity vs. style familiarity) unresolved — one model+judge per provider.

Open questions for further work

  1. Q1Would the same-provider association survive a larger stratified sample (~150–200) with prespecified human validation, given GPT-5.5's effect isn't individually significant at n=50?
  2. Q2Can a variable-level deletion design (remove a named clinical variable, keep the prompt fluent) isolate clinically-realistic failure modes from the mid-word-truncation cue?
  3. Q3Is the same-provider effect provider loyalty or output-style familiarity — answerable only with multiple models per provider and a style-diverse panel.
  4. Q4Does clinician O vs. G disagreement (0.52 vs. 0.70) reflect noise or a genuine normative policy split, and should benchmarks make that policy explicit?
  5. Q5Which judge-aggregation rule (unanimity / supermajority / provider-excluded) generalizes as most clinician-valid beyond this 50-item sample?
  6. Q6Do deployment-time guardrails that detect missing-information regimes outperform trusting the model to self-flag — and how do over-commitment rates map to an acceptable clinical error budget?
