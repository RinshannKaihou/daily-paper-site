Key finding

For log-smooth, LSI targets, sub-Gaussian score error is exactly the tractability boundary — any weaker error makes sampling impossible.

The Tractability Landscape of Sampling with Inexact Scores

2026-07-21

Key metric

>2/3

TV separation between two Gaussians that share one score oracle — forcing a ≥1/3 failure for any algorithm.

ruled-out: β=2, r<1/18 sε is 5-Lipschitz d = 1

Why this matters

Pinpoints exactly when an inexact score oracle stays sampleable — a foundational boundary for score-based sampling theory, though it does not govern practical DDPM training.

↯ caveat

Critical edge

The impossibility is orthogonal to real diffusion models — DDPM assumes vanishing score error at every convolution scale (R→0), where plain L2 error already suffices.

01 Methodology

A self-contained lower bound — no algorithms, no experiments.

(1) Two error notions are fixed: Lp error and MGF error, where β=2 recovers sub-Gaussianity [YW26] and β<2 is heavier-tailed sub-Weibull. Lemma 1 converts MGF→Lp, so hard instances double as Lp lower bounds.

(2) The hard instance lives in d=1: two unit-variance Gaussians π1=N(0,1), π2=N(R,1) — both 1-log-smooth and 1-LSI — share one approximate score sε(x) = −x + R·χ(x/R). The C∞ bump χ equals each true score where that Gaussian carries mass. Direct calculus gives s′ε ∈ [−1, 5] — a benign 5-Lipschitz field.

(3) Gaussian tails decay like exp(−R²/18) while the MGF term grows like exp(rRβ); for β<2 the tail wins for any ε, so sε fools both targets despite their TV > 2/3 separation.

(4) Any score-only algorithm cannot distinguish the two targets; by the triangle inequality it must fail (TV > 1/3) on at least one. Remark 1: because R must grow like √log(1/ε), the bound does not extend to the multi-scale DDPM regime.

03 Strengths

What lands ▲

  • One clean takeaway. Sub-Gaussian score error is exactly the tractability threshold — in tail-type β and MGF parameter r, up to constants.
  • Two-axis strengthening of [CCSW26]. Algorithm-agnostic (any s-querying algorithm) and a broader refuted error family, with a short proof.
  • Genuinely minimal counterexample. One dimension, two Gaussians, one bump function — transparent enough to teach.
  • Honest scope. Remark 1 explicitly excludes DDPM, pre-empting overclaiming.

04 Limitations

What doesn't ▼

  • No DDPM applicability. Needs R ≈ √log(1/ε) to grow; diffusion assumes accurate scores at all scales, where L2 suffices.
  • No experiments or numerical validation — expected for theory, but zero immediate empirical impact.
  • Narrow target class. Only strongly log-concave / 1-LSI Gaussians; non-log-concave or dissipative cases are open.
  • Constant-factor gap in critical r: r<1/18 impossible vs. r≈>1/μ sufficient — exact threshold unpinned.
  • Title oversells scope. Promises a "landscape" but delivers a single theorem, not a survey.

05 Open questions for further exploration

Q1.

Can the bound be repaired for multi-scale DDPM with R fixed (or R→0) — or is sub-Gaussianity genuinely unnecessary there?

Q2.

What is the exact critical r*(2,μ) between [YW26]'s sufficiency and this impossibility? Closing the constant gap makes the boundary two-sided.

Q3.

Does the algorithm-agnostic impossibility extend to KL, Rényi, or W2, or can richer algorithms recover vanishing error there?

Q4.

Can the hard instance extend beyond strongly log-concave / LSI targets to merely dissipative or non-log-concave families?

Q5.

Is there a matching upper bound — a concrete algorithm achieving TV→0 under exactly the paper's tight sub-Gaussian r — certifying a two-sided boundary?

Q6.

Does a different construction keep R fixed while still fooling every score-querying algorithm, breaking the √log(1/ε) dependence?
