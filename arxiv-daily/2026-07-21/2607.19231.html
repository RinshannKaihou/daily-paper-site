Selection Shapes the Boundary: A Preregistered Replication of Monotonicity and Label Agreement in Unselected NLI Populations

Haram Choi · University of Bremen 2026-07-21 Self-correcting replication of arXiv:2607.15870

A preregistered replication finds the monotonicity–agreement boundary vanishes and reverses in unselected NLI — the published effect is an artifact of ChaosNLI's 3-of-5 selection rule.

Key metric · sign reversal
+0.045δ
MNLI matched Cliff's δ, registered as δ < 0. Observed sign is opposite; Holm p = 7.1 × 10−5. Earlier ChaosNLI study reported δ = −0.284.

Why this matters
A structure claim built on a disagreement-selected re-annotation resource can vanish — even faintly reverse — once you look at the unselected population. HLV/perspectivism conclusions must state their selection conditional explicitly.
Critical edge
The four-level ordinal outcome is non-commensurable with the earlier 100-label entropy scale — so the δ = −0.284 is refuted only by sign and magnitude class, not by interval overlap, and there is no independent human-human agreement estimate.
01 Methodology & Evidence

How the boundary was re-tested preregistered

SNLI dev · 9,986→ MNLI matched · 10,000→ MNLI mismatched · 9,946

Under a frozen rule-based monotonicity tagger (v0.3) and a pre-registered directional hypothesis δ < 0, each hypothesis is labeled upward / downward / non-monotone / mixed via a surface trigger scan. The contrast is binary: purely upward vs. non-upward.

The outcome is a four-level agreement ordinal built from five labels: no-majority < 3/5 < 4/5 < 5/5. Statistics use tie-corrected Cliff's δ, Mann-Whitney U, 10k-resample bootstrap CIs (seed 20260717), Holm correction, and a pre-registered SESOI of |δ| = 0.10. Registration was committed to version control on 2026-07-17, before confirmatory analysis.

A three-tier validity defense backs the null result: Tier 2 simulates label flips over a 36-cell grid (misclassification shrinks δ, never manufactures it — max 97.5th pct ≤ 0.064); Tier 3 is a blind 200-item manual re-tagging audit reaching four-class agreement 0.875 (κ = 0.607); confound models M0/M1 control for length, parse depth, and genre.

Confound model M1 keeps the monotonicity coefficient non-zero (matched −0.202, p = 2.4×10⁻⁶), confirming the reversal is not driven by length, parse depth, or genre.
02 Critical Assessment

Strengths

  • Exemplary preregistration discipline: frozen hypothesis, tagger, SESOI, and correction procedure, with a full registration-vs-result concordance table in the appendix.
  • Intellectual honesty: the author reports a failed prediction and a sign-reversed significant contrast without spin, and openly documents the undefined bridge analysis.
  • Three-layer validity defense — simulated misclassification, blind manual audit, confound models — more thorough than typical for a short replication.
  • Actionable methodological takeaway: state selection conditionals for HLV claims built on disagreement-selected resources.
  • Fully reproducible via public repository with per-run fixed seeds.

Limitations

  • Single-author, single-rater tagging — κ = 0.607 is tagger-vs-human, not inter-human; no independent human-human agreement on the monotonicity judgment.
  • Outcome scale mismatch — the four-level ordinal is non-commensurable with the earlier 100-label entropy; comparison with δ = −0.284 is by sign/magnitude class only.
  • Contested operator semantics (e.g., only, many) make part of the tagger-human disagreement irreducible by lexicon engineering.
  • Surface-scan tagger with measured coverage gaps; MED anchor rates are measured on MED sentences, not the SNLI/MNLI dev domain.
  • English-only; coarse ordinal limits resolution; preregistration is a self-controlled version-control record, not a third-party registry.
03 Open Questions
  1. Would a human-human inter-annotator agreement study on monotonicity tagging (rather than tagger-vs-human) change the residual-disagreement decomposition, especially for the contested operators only and many?
  2. If the four-level ordinal were replaced by a finer-grained outcome — re-annotating a sample with many labels per item as in ChaosNLI but without the 3/5 selection rule — would any non-trivial monotonicity effect reappear in the unselected population?
  3. Does the selection-conditional artifact generalize to other disagreement-selected resources (e.g., Kadasi & Singh 2023's annotation-quantity scaling), or is it specific to ChaosNLI's exact 3-of-5 rule?
  4. Can the formal-semantics layer of irreducible disagreement be turned into a quantitative measurement target itself, e.g., modeling annotator uncertainty over operator readings?
  5. How do these findings interact with model-based evaluations — does a model trained on ChaosNLI's selected distribution inherit the same selection-conditional bias when scored on unselected dev sets?
