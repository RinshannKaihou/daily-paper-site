> **一句话总结：** 针对通用 CPU 上 CKKS 全同态加密易受瞬态硬件故障导致的静默数据损坏（SDC）问题，本文提出一种面向 CPU 的校验和容错方案，通过模数感知桶校验和、算子内数据流融合校验、跨算子校验融合三层优化，在 OpenFHE 中将平均运行时开销降至 6.8%（相比直接校验和方案降低 4.9×），并在 15 万个非崩溃故障案例上达到 100% 经验检测率。

> **TL;DR:** The paper presents the first fault-tolerance scheme specifically designed for CPU-based CKKS fully-homomorphic-encryption computation, protecting NTT/INTT, EWM, and BConv polynomial operators via three CPU-aware optimizations (modulus-aware bucket checksum, dataflow-fused in-operator checking, cross-operator check fusion). Implemented in OpenFHE, it achieves a 100% empirical detection rate over 150,000 non-crashing corrupted cases while adding only 6.0%–8.4% runtime overhead (avg 6.8%) on encrypted LoLA/MLP/ResNet-20/VGG-16 inference and bootstrapping — a 4.9× reduction versus direct checksum-based protection (33.4%) and far below dual modular redundancy (>2.15×), all while keeping application accuracy near the fault-free baseline across fault rates up to 10⁻⁶.

## 核心贡献

- **CPU 上 CKKS 的故障注入特征化：** 通过 PinFI 在 LoLA、MLP、ResNet-20、VGG-16 四个加密推理负载上进行随机单比特瞬态故障注入，证明加密执行比明文执行对硬件故障敏感得多，且故障脆弱点广泛分布在 (I)NTT、BConv、EWM 等多个多项式算子中，单一算子保护不足。
- **首个面向 CPU 的 CKKS 容错方案：** 识别出 CPU 上校验和保护开销的三大来源（频繁模归约、分离的多项式扫描、算子间冗余边界检查），并提出三种对应优化技术予以消除。
- **三项协同优化技术：** 模数感知桶校验和（利用 128 位 CPU 累加器空闲位宽，将 Barrett 模归约次数从 N 降到 v(q)）、算子内数据流融合校验（将 encode/decode 累加嵌入原始算子数据流，消除两次额外内存遍历）、跨算子校验融合（合并 BConv→NTT、EWM→INTT 等相邻算子共享的边界校验）。
- **OpenFHE 实现与系统评估：** 在 OpenFHE 中实现该方案，在 Intel Xeon Platinum 8358P 上对四个加密应用、两种 HMult 配置和 CKKS 自举进行评估，平均开销仅 6.8%，并将检测率与精度维持在高水平。

## Key Contributions

- **Fault-injection characterization of CPU-based CKKS**, showing encrypted CKKS execution is far more vulnerable to single-bit transient faults than the equivalent plaintext execution, with vulnerable points spread across the entire (I)NTT / BConv / EWM polynomial pipeline rather than concentrated in a single operator.
- **The first fault-tolerance scheme explicitly designed for CPU-based CKKS computation**, identifying and attacking three distinct sources of checksum overhead that do not arise on ASIC accelerators: frequent Barrett modular reductions, detached scans of long RNS polynomials, and redundant operator-boundary checks.
- **Three CPU-aware optimizations:** (1) modulus-aware bucket checksum that reuses the unused 8–28 bits of the 128-bit multiply intermediate to accumulate up to s(q) products before a single Barrett reduction; (2) dataflow-fused in-operator checking that folds encode/decode accumulation into the first/last butterfly stage of NTT (and the single EWM loop), eliminating two extra memory passes; (3) cross-operator check fusion that removes the redundant Dec-then-Enc pair at BConv→NTT and EWM→INTT boundaries while preserving the end-to-end checksum invariant.
- **A complete OpenFHE implementation and evaluation**, covering four encrypted inference applications, two ciphertext-multiplication parameter sets, and CKKS bootstrapping, with both reliability and performance results, hardware-counter profiling, and a progressive ablation isolating each optimization's contribution.

## Methodology

The scheme treats polynomial operators (NTT/INTT, EWM, BConv) as the basic detection granularity and verifies the algebraic input-output consistency invariant of each operator, recovering by re-executing only the affected operator (or short fused segment) when a mismatch is found. The detection logic is reshaped at three levels to fit CPU resource constraints:

1. **Modulus-aware bucket checksum (arithmetic level).** In OpenFHE, each RNS coefficient is a 64-bit word whose product already needs a 128-bit intermediate; for the typical 50–60-bit RNS moduli used in CKKS, a single product occupies only ~100–120 bits, leaving 8–28 unused bits. The authors reuse this existing intermediate (no wider types) to accumulate multiple unreduced products in a "bucket," performing one Barrett reduction per bucket of size s(q)=⌊(2^D−1)/(q−1)²⌋ (D=128), rather than one per coefficient. Bucket sizes are pre-computed per modulus at parameter initialization. Because modular addition is linear, this preserves the original encode/decode checksum invariant while reducing checksum-side Barrett reductions from N to v(q).

2. **Dataflow-fused in-operator checking (operator-dataflow level).** Instead of a detached pass that re-traverses the long RNS polynomial to compute input-side and output-side checksums, the encode accumulation is folded into the first butterfly stage of NTT (when input coefficients are loaded) and the decode accumulation into the final butterfly stage (when output coefficients are produced); EWM's single multiply loop fuses both sides natively. This reuses data already live in the operator dataflow and removes the two extra cache-unfriendly memory passes that dominate EWM overhead.

3. **Cross-operator check fusion (pipeline level).** When the decode vector of operator f equals the encode vector of its successor g over the same intermediate RNS tower, Dec_f(y)=Enc_g(y), so the intermediate comparison is removed and the two independent checks are composed into a single end-to-end check Enc_f(x)?=Dec_g(z). This applies at the BConv→NTT boundary (both are coefficient-sum checks) and the EWM→INTT boundary, removing redundant boundary computations while preserving the end-to-end invariant. The trade-off is that the recovery scope expands to a short fused segment of two operators, still tiny relative to a full FHE task.

The scheme is implemented in OpenFHE and evaluated on an Intel Xeon Platinum 8358P using four encrypted inference workloads (LoLA, MLP, ResNet-20, VGG-16), two ciphertext-multiplication configurations, and CKKS bootstrapping. Reliability is measured under a random single-bit transient fault model injected with PinFI (at most one fault per polynomial-operator invocation); performance is averaged over 1,000 runs per workload and compared against dual modular redundancy (DMR) and a re-implementation of the ReliaFHE checksum scheme on CPUs (basic checksum).

## Results & Highlights

- **Detection rate:** The proposed scheme achieves a **100% empirical detection rate over 150,000 collected non-crashing corrupted-result cases** (50,000 each for NTT/INTT, BConv, and EWM) under the random single-bit transient fault model (Table II).
- **End-to-end runtime overhead:** Across all seven benchmarks the scheme adds only **6.0%–8.4%** (averaging **6.8%**) runtime overhead, versus **130.7%–135.3% (avg 33.4%)** for basic checksum-based protection and **215.3%–222.3%** for DMR — a **4.9× reduction** in average protection overhead versus the direct checksum baseline (Fig. 14).
- **Per-benchmark normalized runtime (proposed):** LoLA 107.2%, MLP 108.4%, ResNet-20 106.3%, VGG-16 106.5%, HMult set1 107.2%, HMult set2 106.0%, Bootstrapping 106.3%.
- **Application-level reliability:** On LoLA, MLP, ResNet-20, and VGG-16, the full-protection scheme maintains near-baseline accuracy across fault rates from 10⁻¹² to 10⁻⁶, while unprotected CKKS execution collapses toward random accuracy (with ResNet-20 and VGG-16 degrading earliest because of their longer ciphertext flows).
- **Operator-level hardware-counter profile:** Protected NTT runtime drops from 138.5% (basic checksum) to **108.6%**, and protected EWM from 231.1% to **116.0%**, with instruction count, L1D loads, and L1D misses brought close to unprotected execution; BConv protection overhead is only ~3%.
- **Ablation:** Removing cross-operator check fusion raises runtime to 110.4%–112.5%; additionally removing the bucket checksum raises it to 124.1%–125.7%; additionally removing dataflow-fused in-operator checking degenerates to the basic detached checksum baseline at 130.7%–135.3% — confirming all three optimizations are necessary.
- **Recovery cost:** A single mismatch-triggered re-execution of the affected operator or fused segment costs **<1.0% of end-to-end runtime** across all evaluated workloads.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (high).** This is genuinely the first systematic fault-tolerance design tailored to *CPU-based* CKKS software, and the core insight — that low algorithmic complexity does not imply low CPU overhead, so checksum logic must be reshaped to CPU arithmetic and dataflow constraints — is non-obvious and well-motivated by the hardware-counter profile. The three optimizations (bucket checksum exploiting the 128-bit multiply intermediate's unused headroom, butterfly-stage fusion, and the BConv→NTT / EWM→INTT boundary fusion) are concrete and specific to the CKKS pipeline.
- **Rigor (good, with caveats).** The evaluation is broad (4 applications, 2 HMult configs, bootstrapping, 1,000 runs each) and the progressive ablation cleanly isolates each optimization. However, the headline **100% detection rate is qualified**: it applies only to the collected *non-crashing* cases under a *single-bit, single-fault-per-operator-invocation* model. The authors honestly acknowledge that "specially structured multiple errors that preserve the checksum relation may escape detection," so the 100% figure should not be read as an absolute detection guarantee — checksum-based schemes are by construction blind to linear-dependency-preserving multi-bit adversaries. The cross-operator fusion also deliberately expands the recovery scope from one operator to a two-operator segment, a real though small granularity loss that is only briefly discussed.
- **Reproducibility (moderate).** The algorithm is described in enough detail (pseudocode in Algorithm 1, bucket-size formulas in Eq. 2) to reimplement, and OpenFHE is open-source. But the baseline is a *re-implementation* of the ReliaFHE checksum scheme without its ASIC hardware support — the paper explicitly notes ReliaFHE is not open-sourced — so the basic-checksum overhead numbers (33.4% avg) depend on the fidelity of that reproduction. PinFI configuration and the exact dynamic-instruction eligibility filter for fault injection are not fully specified, and only a single CPU platform (Xeon Platinum 8358P) is tested.
- **Significance (moderate-to-high).** As long as CPU execution remains the baseline for FHE software stacks (development, prototyping, lightweight deployment), cheap CPU-side SDC protection is a real gap; a 6.8% average overhead with full-pipeline coverage is a strong practical result. The techniques are CKKS/RNS-specific, so transfer to BGV/BFV/TFHE is plausible but not demonstrated.
- **Red flags.** None severe, but: (1) only one CPU microarchitecture, so the cache-behavior claims (the L1D-load/miss reductions central to the EWM win) are not validated across Intel/AMD/ARM; (2) the fault model excludes multi-bit and correlated faults, which are increasingly cited as realistic for SDC studies; (3) no comparison against stronger linear-algebraic codes that could detect checksum-preserving multi-errors; (4) "near fault-free accuracy" in Fig. 13 is shown qualitatively without tabulated accuracy deltas at representative fault rates.

## Strengths

- First systematic treatment of efficient SDC protection for CPU-based CKKS, with a clear and well-evidenced motivation that accelerator-oriented schemes do not port directly to CPUs.
- Three complementary optimizations that are individually simple, provably invariant-preserving, and validated by a clean ablation showing all three are needed.
- Strong practical result: 6.8% average overhead with full-pipeline (NTT/INTT, EWM, BConv) coverage and a 100% empirical detection rate on the evaluated fault model, plus sub-1% recovery cost.
- Implementation on the mainstream OpenFHE library and evaluation on realistic workloads including bootstrapping, increasing deployment relevance.
- Intellectually honest limitations section that explicitly states the single-fault-model and library/platform scope.

## Limitations

- Restricted to the random single-bit, single-fault-per-operator-invocation model; multi-bit, correlated, or multi-fault injections within one operator are explicitly out of scope and could defeat the checksum.
- 100% detection rate is empirical and only over non-crashing cases; checksum-preserving structured multi-errors are undetectable in principle.
- Evaluated on a single CPU platform (Intel Xeon Platinum 8358P); the cache-locality benefits central to the EWM win are not demonstrated on AMD/ARM or under multi-threaded execution.
- Only CKKS in OpenFHE is covered; generalization to BGV/BFV/TFHE, GPU libraries, or other software stacks is left to future work.
- The basic-checksum baseline is a self-reproduction of the closed-source ReliaFHE scheme without its hardware support, so the 4.9× headline depends on that reproduction's fidelity.
- Application-accuracy results are reported as curves without tabulated deltas at fixed fault rates, making fine-grained comparison harder.

## Relevance

本文聚焦全同态加密（CKKS）在通用 CPU 上的可靠性与容错，对于关注隐私计算、加密推理（ResNet/VGG/MLP）及系统软件可靠性的研究方向具有直接价值；其将算法级校验和重塑以适配 CPU 算术/访存数据流的设计思路，对加密计算系统的工程化部署有参考意义。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does the scheme behave under multi-bit or correlated transient fault models, and what would a stronger linear-code-based or randomized-checksum variant cost in overhead to close the checksum-preserving-multi-error gap?
- Do the cache-locality gains (especially the EWM win) hold on AMD Zen, ARM Neoverse, or under multi-threaded/SIMD-parallel CKKS execution where the "live in operator dataflow" assumption may change?
- Can the three optimizations transfer to other FHE schemes (BGV, BFV, TFHE) and other libraries (Microsoft SEAL, Zama Concrete), where operator pipelines and RNS representations differ?
- Is there an automated way to discover cross-operator fusion opportunities and construct encode/decode vectors across arbitrary ciphertext primitive compositions, rather than hand-instantiating the BConv→NTT and EWM→INTT cases?
- What is the interaction with GPU/accelerator offload: when CKKS pipelines span CPU and accelerator, where should the checksum boundary live, and does the bucket-checksum headroom analysis still apply?
