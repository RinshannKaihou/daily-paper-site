Why This Matters

A new two-level LLM-as-judge rubric paradigm plus a multimodal factuality benchmark — transferable to any open-ended generation evaluation.

Critical Edge

Scoring hyperparameters (tier weights, λ, µ) are heuristically set with no sensitivity analysis — ranking stability under alternative parameterizations is unverified.

⚠ Unvalidated calibration
KEY FINDING → Factual recall is far from solved: even the best frontier model captures only 58.7% of a complete answer, and the dominant failure mode is omission, not contradiction.

01 Methodology

Describe a good answer in structured space · grade it in binary space

Level 1 — Structured meta-rubric
Annotators (LLM proposes, humans verify/revise over rounds) author a structured rubric: 5 item types, 3 importance tiers, plus meta-insights & evidence citations grounded in retrieved web pages — not the model's parametric memory.
Level 2 — Mechanical compilation
Deterministic, auditable rules flatten the structure into self-contained binary pass/fail checks. Simple knowledge → 1 check; strict list → 1/item; process → presence + sequence checks; etc. Reconciles expressiveness with low-variance grading.

Contradiction is penalized more than omission — a cautious answer outranks a confidently wrong one. Avg 15.2 binary checks/question across 9,400+ cited pages.

03 Strengths

  • Sharp problem framing: diagnoses the shared blind spot (boolean-checklist reduction) in both precision- and recall-focused factuality work.
  • The two-level design is elegant and general — offered as a recipe beyond factuality for any rubric-based eval where flat criteria fall short.
  • Heavy human-verification (every question & rubric, multi-round, expert annotators) + evidence grounding (9,400+ pages, 97%/94% citation rates).
  • Thorough judge-robustness study with explicit self-favoritism controls — benchmark hygiene many papers skip.
  • Text-only ablation cleanly separates perception from knowledge; yields a genuine insight (visual ID is a constant tax).
  • Practical signal: omission is the dominant failure → points to retrieval/coverage work, not hallucination suppression.

04 Limitations

  • Scoring hyperparameters (tier weights, λ, µ) set heuristically — no sensitivity analysis; ranking stability under alternatives unverified.
  • Heavy reliance on a single proprietary LLM (Gemini 3.1 Pro) across generation, rubric construction, and judging — mitigated, not eliminated.
  • Annotation reproducibility constrained: rubrics needed a small in-house expert team; pipeline not easily re-run by others.
  • English-only, everyday-domain questions; no technical/scientific/professional coverage where the framework's value is unproven.
  • No dedicated limitations section; some design choices (stranger test, one question per image, tier-weight calibration) lack formal validation.
  • Measures a specific "completeness"; genuinely divergent-but-valid answers (e.g., a different cultural framing) still squeezed into the flexible-list scheme.

05 Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How sensitive is the ranking to scoring hyperparameters (w_AC/w_V/w_C, λ, µ)? Does a reasonable sweep preserve the proprietary-vs-open-weight ordering?
  2. Can the rubric-construction pipeline be reproduced by an independent team using only open-weight models — and how much would the resulting benchmark differ?
  3. Does the two-level meta-rubric transfer to expert domains (medicine, law, science), where "completeness" is harder to enumerate and annotator expertise scarcer?
  4. Since omission dominates, does explicitly optimizing for rubric-style coverage (RAG guided by a predicted meta-rubric) close the gap more efficiently than scaling?
  5. How would Gamut scores correlate with downstream user satisfaction in a real wearable assistant, where "good enough" coverage may differ from the rubric tiers?
  6. Could the framework incorporate genuinely divergent-but-valid answers (multiple correct cultural framings) beyond the flexible-list coverage scheme?
