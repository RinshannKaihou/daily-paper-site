> **一句话总结：** 针对长文本生成评估中长期忽视的"事实完整性（recall/completeness）"问题，本文提出两层元评分标准框架（结构化 meta-rubric 机械编译为二值 checklist），并构建 GAMUT 多模态基准（1,813 个真实可穿戴图像问题，覆盖 10 个领域，全部由专家人工核验）；在 14 个前沿模型上，最强模型 Gemini 3.1 Pro 仅得 58.7%，且排名对裁判模型选择高度稳健。

> **TL;DR:** GAMUT (Meta AI) tackles the under-studied "factual completeness / recall" half of long-form factuality, which the dominant decompose-search-verify paradigm (e.g., FActScore) ignores. It introduces a two-level meta-rubric (structured representation mechanically compiled into a flat binary checklist) and builds a 1,813-question multimodal benchmark grounded in real wearable imagery across 10 domains, all human-verified. On 14 frontier/open-weight models, even the best (Gemini 3.1 Pro) scores only 58.7% (vs. 33.0% for the strongest open-weight Qwen3-VL 235B — a ~25.7-point gap), with the dominant failure mode being omission rather than contradiction, and rankings robust across three different LLM judges; a text-only variant shows visual identification is a roughly constant ~10-20-point tax, so the ranking reflects knowledge completeness rather than perception.

## 核心贡献

- **首次将"事实完整性（factual completeness）"作为一等公民目标提出**，并明确指出当前主流的精度（precision）与召回（recall）评估方法的共同缺陷：把事实性简化为"独立的二元事实核查清单"，无法表达开放式集合的覆盖度、有序流程、事实间关系与重要性差异。
- **提出两层元评分标准表示法（two-level meta-rubric）**：用结构化的 meta-rubric（包含 Simple Knowledge / Strict List / Flexible List / Process / Relationship 五种类型，以及 Answer-Critical / Valuable / Context 三个重要性层级，外加 meta-insight 与证据引用）来描述"完整答案"应有的组织结构；再通过确定性、可审计的机械规则编译为扁平的二值 pass/fail 检查清单。这样既保留了结构化标注的表达力，又继承了二值 LLM-as-judge 打分的低方差特性。
- **构建 GAMUT 基准**：1,813 个面向"日常深度研究（everyday deep research）"的开放问题，源自 CRAG-MM 的真实可穿戴/公共图像，覆盖 10 个领域；每个问题配有人工核验、证据支撑的 rubric（平均 15.2 个二值检查，引用 9,400+ 个独立网页）。同时发布模态无关的 text-only 变体（1,806 问）。
- **大规模基准评测 14 个前沿与开源模型**，证明事实召回远未解决：最强 Gemini 3.1 Pro 仅 58.7%，主要失败模式是遗漏而非出错，且排名对不同裁判高度稳健。

## Key Contributions

- **Frames factual completeness (recall) as a first-class, under-studied evaluation target**, and identifies a shared limitation of existing precision- and recall-oriented methods: both reduce factuality to a flat list of independent boolean fact-checks that cannot express open-ended coverage, ordering, relationships, or importance tiers.
- **Introduces a two-level meta-rubric representation**: a structured meta-rubric (five item types — Simple Knowledge, Strict List, Flexible List, Process, Relationship; three importance tiers — Answer-Critical, Valuable, Context; plus meta-insights and evidence citations) that is mechanically and deterministically compiled into a flat binary checklist. This reconciles annotation expressiveness with grading reliability, since structure lives where a good answer is described while low-variance binary checks live where the judge operates.
- **Builds the GAMUT benchmark**: 1,813 "everyday deep-research" questions grounded in real wearable imagery from CRAG-MM across 10 domains, each paired with an expert-verified, evidence-backed rubric (avg 15.2 binary checks/question; 9,400+ distinct cited web pages). Releases a modality-agnostic text-only variant (1,806 questions).
- **Benchmarks 14 frontier and open-weight models**, showing factual recall is far from solved (best Gemini 3.1 Pro only 58.7%), that the dominant failure is omission not contradiction, and that the ranking is robust to the choice of LLM judge.

## Methodology

The framework rests on a deliberate separation between *describing* a good answer and *grading* one.

**Level 1 — Structured meta-rubric.** For each question, annotators (frontier LLM proposes, humans verify/revise over multiple rounds) build a structured meta-rubric. Each meta-rubric item carries a **type**:
- *Simple Knowledge* — a single discrete fact (e.g., entity identity, a date).
- *Strict List* — a finite set where every item is required (e.g., core ingredients of a dish).
- *Flexible List* — a pool of valid options requiring sufficient coverage but no specific item (e.g., "name at least 2 of these 6 supporting ingredients"); specified with a baseline threshold.
- *Process* — an ordered sequence where order carries meaning (mandatory + optional steps).
- *Relationship* — a connection/contrast between entities.

Each item also carries an **importance tier**: Answer-Critical (required for correctness), Valuable (meaningfully improves the answer), Context (helpful background). Lists/processes may carry a *meta-insight* — a synthesis no single item captures. Items cite supporting web snippets (evidence grounding), so the rubric rests on retrieved evidence, not on the parametric memory of the rubric-writing LLM.

**Level 2 — Mechanical compilation into binary rubric.** Fixed deterministic rules convert the meta-rubric into a flat checklist of self-contained pass/fail checks (the conversion is auditable): simple knowledge → one check; strict list → one check per item; flexible list → baseline threshold checks ("mention at least N of…") plus per-item/threshold coverage checks one tier down; process → presence checks per step plus sequence checks over required (and full) ordering; relationship/meta-insight → their own checks. Secondary/optional items drop one importance level.

**Scoring (Gamut score).** An LLM judge grades each check independently with four verdicts: *meets* (1), *partially meets* (λ = 0.5), *missing* (0), *contradicts* (µ = −2). Crucially, contradiction is penalized more heavily than omission (a cautious answer outranks a confidently wrong one). Per-tier score formula: `s = (M + λP + µC) / (M + P + S + |µ|C)`, ranging in [−1, 1]. The headline Gamut score is a weighted average across tiers with global weights w_AC = 0.6, w_V = 0.3, w_C = 0.1 (chosen so that covering Answer-Critical content alone reaches roughly a passing score; weights are global so questions with many Context checks can't drown out few critical ones).

**Dataset construction (two stages, each LLM + expert human).** (1) *Question creation*: 1,938 images/entities from CRAG-MM (~80% egocentric smart-glasses photos, often low-light/occluded); a frontier multimodal LLM proposes image-dependent questions that need multi-step research; expert annotators (2 independent + adjudicator) accept/revise/discard over multiple rounds; a "stranger test" rejects leading questions that presuppose the entity's identity. (2) *Rubric creation*: LLM gathers web evidence, drafts a grounded meta-rubric, self-refines (audit phase re-verifies snippets; independent search phase re-researches to fill gaps), then a small in-house expert team revises the binary rubric directly (12 upstream CRAG-MM entity errors corrected by hand). Result: 1,813 fully human-verified question/rubric pairs.

## Results & Highlights

- **Headline difficulty:** On the 1,813 multimodal questions, the best model **Gemini 3.1 Pro reaches a Gamut score of only 58.7%** and *meets* at most **55.2%** of rubric elements, leaving substantial headroom; the weakest (Llama 3.2 11B) scores just **5.1%**.
- **Proprietary vs. open-weight gap:** The strongest open-weight model **Qwen3-VL 235B scores 33.0%**, ~25.7 points behind Gemini 3.1 Pro's 58.7%; the open-weight cohort (Llama 4 / Llama 3.2 / Qwen3-VL 8B) clusters between 5.1% and 33.0%, well below every proprietary model.
- **Discriminative ranking recovers known model regularities:** newer/larger > older/smaller, proprietary > open-weight; the two Qwen3-VL variants show the highest contradiction rates (8.7% and 11.5%) while Claude Opus variants show the lowest (~2.6-3%), consistent with prior characterizations.
- **Dominant failure is omission, not error:** *missing* verdicts account for roughly two-thirds of rubric elements for the weakest models and over a quarter (29.2%) even for Gemini 3.1 Pro; contradiction rates stay low for strong models and climb only for weak ones — so what separates models is largely how much of a complete answer they supply.
- **Judge robustness (3 judges, all 14 models, all 1,813 questions):** Gemini 3.1 Pro and Claude Opus 4.8 produce an **identical ranking** with per-model scores within 1.8 points, and neither favors its own answers (each scores itself within 0.3 points of the other); the weaker Qwen3-VL 235B judge is uniformly more lenient (+4 to +11 points) but preserves the same broad ordering with only adjacent swaps.
- **Text-only variant isolates perception from knowledge:** removing the image raises every model's score by a roughly constant **~10-20 points** — Gemini 3.1 Pro 58.7% → 74.1% (+15.4), GPT-4o 34.2% → 58.1% (+23.9), Llama 4 Scout 14.1% → 31.8% (+17.7) — so visual identification behaves like a constant tax and the multimodal ranking reflects knowledge-completeness differences, not perception alone.
- **Structure is pervasive, not incidental:** 98% of questions require at least one structured component beyond Simple Knowledge (avg 1.6 such components); Flexible Lists appear in 86%, Strict Lists in 49%, Processes in 17%, Relationships in 6%; 97% of meta-rubric items and 94% of compiled checks cite a supporting web snippet.
- **Rubric density:** avg 15.2 binary checks/question (median 15), split ~5.9 Answer-Critical / 5.8 Valuable / 3.5 Context, drawing on ~10 web snippets per rubric across 9,400+ distinct pages.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (genuinely above incremental):** The two-level split — author in structured space, grade in binary space — is a clean and well-motivated reconciliation of a real tension (expressiveness vs. judge reliability). The articulation of *why* flat checklists fail (open-ended coverage, ordering, relationships, importance tiers) and the meta-rubric type system are a substantive conceptual contribution, not just another benchmark.
- **Rigor (strong on validation, weaker on hyperparameter justification):** Judge robustness is tested rigorously (3 judges × 14 models × 1,813 questions, with explicit self-favoritism checks). However, the scoring parameters are chosen heuristically — weights `w_AC=0.6, w_V=0.3, w_C=0.1` are "set so that covering Answer-Critical content alone reaches roughly a passing score," and `λ=0.5, µ=−2` are stated without sensitivity analysis. A reader cannot tell how much the headline ranking would shift under reasonable alternative weightings/penalties.
- **Potential circularity, mitigated but worth noting:** The same frontier model family (Gemini 3.1 Pro) is used for question generation, rubric construction, *and* as the headline judge — and is also the top-scoring system. The authors address this directly (judge self-favoritism within 0.3 points vs. Claude; identical ranking across Gemini and Claude judges), which is reassuring, but the construction pipeline still depends heavily on a single proprietary model's worldview.
- **Reproducibility concern (annotation process):** Rubric revision was done by "a small in-house team working closely with the authors" because "a general annotation pool could not improve on the LLM-generated rubrics." This is honest but limits third-party reproducibility of the rubric-construction process and raises the specter of annotator-author groupthink on what counts as a "complete" answer.
- **Scope limits:** Questions are English and "everyday" (plants, food, vehicles, local places) — explicitly *not* academic/report-level depth; the framework's value on technical or domain-expert answers is untested. Only one question per image; the "stranger test" filter is enforced by LLM + human rather than formally validated.
- **No explicit limitations section** in the paper; the above are inferred from the text. The absence of a self-critical limitations discussion is itself a (minor) red flag for a benchmark paper.

## Strengths

- Sharp, well-argued problem framing: correctly diagnoses a shared blind spot (boolean-checklist reduction) in both precision- and recall-focused factuality work.
- The two-level design is elegant and general — the meta-rubric is explicitly offered as a recipe beyond factuality, for any rubric-based evaluation of open-ended generation where flat criteria fall short.
- Heavy human-verification investment (every question and rubric, multi-round, expert annotators) plus evidence grounding (9,400+ cited pages, 97%/94% citation rates) make the reference standard unusually trustworthy.
- Thorough judge-robustness study with explicit self-favoritism controls — a benchmark hygiene practice many papers skip.
- The text-only ablation cleanly separates perception from knowledge, yielding a genuine insight (visual ID is a constant tax, not the discriminating axis).
- Practical signal for model developers: omission is the dominant failure, pointing to retrieval/coverage improvements rather than hallucination suppression.

## Limitations

- Scoring hyperparameters (tier weights, λ, µ) are heuristically set with no reported sensitivity analysis; ranking stability under alternative parameterizations is unverified.
- Heavy reliance on a single proprietary LLM (Gemini 3.1 Pro) across generation, rubric construction, and judging — mitigated but not eliminated by the multi-judge study.
- Annotation reproducibility is constrained: rubrics required a small in-house expert team, so the construction pipeline is not easily re-run by others.
- English-only, everyday-domain questions; no coverage of technical, scientific, or professional expert settings where the framework's value is unproven.
- No dedicated limitations section; some design choices (the "stranger test," one question per image, the precise tier-weight calibration) lack formal validation.
- The benchmark measures a specific notion of "completeness" defined by human-authored rubrics; genuinely divergent-but-valid answers (e.g., a different cultural framing of a dish) are still squeezed into the flexible-list/coverage scheme.

## Relevance

该工作直接落在用户的 cs.CL / cs.AI / 大模型评估兴趣范围内：它提出了一个新的 LLM-as-judge 评测范式（两层 rubric）和一个多模态长文本事实性基准，对关注大模型可信度、长文本生成评估与多模态智能体（可穿戴/深度研究场景）的研究者具有高度参考价值，其评分框架也可迁移到其他开放生成评测任务。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How sensitive is the model ranking to the scoring hyperparameters (w_AC/w_V/w_C, λ, µ)? Does a reasonable sweep preserve the proprietary-vs-open-weight ordering, or could specific choices be inflating/deflating particular models?
- Can the rubric-construction pipeline be reproduced by an independent team using only open-weight models, and how much does the resulting benchmark differ from the Meta-built one?
- Does the two-level meta-rubric transfer to expert/technical domains (medicine, law, science), where "completeness" is even harder to enumerate and annotator expertise is scarcer?
- The dominant failure mode is omission — does explicitly optimizing models for rubric-style coverage (e.g., retrieval-augmented generation guided by a predicted meta-rubric) close the gap more efficiently than scaling?
- How would Gamut scores correlate with downstream user satisfaction in a real wearable assistant, where "good enough" coverage may differ from the rubric's Answer-Critical/Valuable/Context partition?
- Could the framework incorporate genuinely divergent-but-valid answers (multiple correct cultural framings) beyond the flexible-list coverage scheme, to avoid penalizing legitimate alternative answers?
