01

Methodology

PPT factors the elicitation objective J(z)=E[U(Y)] through the latent posterior of a Bayes-filtered transformer: the one-step predictive is a Bayes mixture over latent kernels, so J becomes a posterior expectation (the "tower identity").

Instead of GCG's discrete gradient-guided search through the embedding layer, PPT optimizes a continuous surrogate in latent space, then estimates its gradient by self-normalized importance sampling against prior samples drawn once via predictive Monte Carlo. Because those samples are utility-independent, every gradient step costs zero transformer calls.

1Tilt factor W(k̃;κ) — probability latent k̃ assigns to a prompt from kernel κ.
2Tilted posterior Π_tilt ∝ W·Π₀, reweighting kernels by prompt-compatibility.
3Marginalized utility µ̄(k̃;κ)=Ez∼κ[µ(k̃;z)].
4Surrogate J_tilt(κ)=∫µ̄·Π_tilt — gradient = covariance + expectation.
5PMC sampling: L=5000 rollouts × R=2000, drawn once, reused for every utility.
6PPT-RB: closed-form µ(k̃;s) replaces noisy single-rollout estimate (Rao–Blackwell).

A hard prompt is recovered by snapping: rounding per-token frequencies (k=0), or constructing an Eulerian path on the de Bruijn graph of the transition table (k≥1).

+ Strengths

  • Reduces elicitation to a posterior expectation; derives a usable gradient entirely in latent space.
  • Genuinely zero backprop through πθ during optimization; utility amortization is a real efficiency win across many utilities.
  • Rigorous, well-instrumented experiments — exact enumeration ground truth + analytic-vs-PMC diagnostic variants.
  • Honest, non-rubberstamp discussion of when PPT loses (long-prompt Dyck, some frequency-match cells).
  • Unifies interpretability (belief-state geometry) and adversarial prompting (GCG, persona modulation) under one latent-posterior lens.

– Limitations

  • Requires the latent-posterior factorization to hold; real LLMs approximate it only loosely.
  • Narrow scope: binary vocab, k∈{0,1}, N=4, prefix-only (no side-data conditioning).
  • IS estimator degenerates at long prompts (ESS/L 0.22 → 0.07), causing the m=50 inversion.
  • Snapping is post-hoc with no optimality guarantee vs the continuous optimum.
  • No demonstration on a realistic large non-BFT transformer — the alignment pitch is prospective.
03

Questions for further exploration

  • Can the IS estimator be stabilized at long prompts (tempered tilting, sequential Monte Carlo, a learned surrogate for W) to avoid the m=50 ESS collapse?
  • Does the factorization hold well enough in real pretrained transformers, perhaps after a probing step to recover an approximate Π₀?
  • How does PPT extend to conditioning on observed data alongside the prompt, not just prefixes?
  • Why does PPT stall at rank 11 for Beta–Bernoulli q*=0.6 while GCG reaches rank 4 — snapping, or κ-optimization?
  • Can persona-selection / emergent misalignment be operationalized as utilities and predicted by where PPT places the posterior?
  • Beyond k-Markov exchangeability, which latent classes admit closed-form W and µ̄, broadening applicability?
