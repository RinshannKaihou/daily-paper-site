Why This Matters

Cheap, full-pipeline silent-data-corruption protection finally makes CPU-based CKKS encrypted inference deployable on commodity hardware — where privacy workloads actually run before any accelerator exists.

Critical Edge

The headline 100% detection is empirical, scoped only to non-crashing single-bit faults — checksum-preserving multi-bit adversaries remain undetectable in principle.

Methodology

The scheme treats each polynomial operator — NTT/INTT, EWM, BConv — as the basic detection unit, verifying an algebraic input→output invariant and recovering by re-executing only the affected (or fused) segment. Three optimizations reshape checksum logic to CPU constraints:

Modulus-aware bucket checksum

Reuse the unused 8–28 bits of the existing 128-bit multiply intermediate to accumulate up to s(q) products before a single Barrett reduction — cutting checksum-side reductions from N to v(q), with no wider types.

Dataflow-fused in-operator checking

Fold encode/decode accumulation into the first/last NTT butterfly stage and EWM's single multiply loop, removing two cache-unfriendly memory passes that dominate EWM cost.

Cross-operator check fusion

At BConv→NTT and EWM→INTT boundaries, compose two independent checks into one end-to-end invariant (Encf?=Decg), removing redundant Dec-then-Enc pairs at the cost of a slightly wider recovery scope.

Evaluated in OpenFHE on an Intel Xeon Platinum 8358P across four encrypted inference apps, two HMult configurations, and CKKS bootstrapping; reliability measured via PinFI single-bit transient fault injection.

How the three cuts stack up

A progressive ablation isolates each optimization's contribution to normalized runtime (100% = unprotected):

03 / TRADEOFFS

Strengths vs. limitations

▲ Strengths

  • First systematic treatment of efficient SDC protection for CPU-based CKKS, with hard evidence that accelerator schemes don't port directly.
  • Three complementary optimizations that are individually simple, invariant-preserving, and validated by a clean ablation.
  • Strong practical headline: 6.8% average overhead with full NTT/INTT + EWM + BConv coverage and sub-1% recovery cost.
  • Built on the mainstream OpenFHE library (also the SPEC CPU 2026 crypto backend) and tested on realistic workloads including bootstrapping.
  • Intellectually honest limitations section that explicitly states single-fault-model and single-platform scope.

▼ Limitations

  • Restricted to single-bit, single-fault-per-operator injection — multi-bit, correlated, or multi-fault cases are explicitly out of scope.
  • 100% detection is empirical and only over non-crashing cases; checksum-preserving structured multi-errors are undetectable in principle.
  • Single CPU platform (Intel Xeon Platinum 8358P) — the cache-locality EWM win is not validated on AMD/ARM or under multi-threading.
  • Only CKKS in OpenFHE; transfer to BGV/BFV/TFHE, GPU libraries, or other stacks is left to future work.
  • The 4.9× headline rests on a self-reproduction of the closed-source ReliaFHE scheme without its hardware support.
  • Application accuracy shown as curves, with no tabulated deltas at fixed fault rates for fine-grained comparison.
04 / OPEN

Questions for further exploration

  1. How does the scheme behave under multi-bit or correlated transient fault models — and what would a stronger linear-code-based or randomized-checksum variant cost in overhead to close the checksum-preserving-multi-error gap?
  2. Do the cache-locality gains (especially the EWM win) hold on AMD Zen, ARM Neoverse, or under multi-threaded / SIMD-parallel CKKS execution where the "live in operator dataflow" assumption may shift?
  3. Can the three optimizations transfer to other FHE schemes (BGV, BFV, TFHE) and libraries (Microsoft SEAL, Zama Concrete), where operator pipelines and RNS representations differ?
  4. Is there an automated way to discover cross-operator fusion opportunities across arbitrary ciphertext primitive compositions, rather than hand-instantiating the BConv→NTT and EWM→INTT cases?
  5. What is the interaction with GPU / accelerator offload: when CKKS pipelines span CPU and accelerator, where should the checksum boundary live, and does the bucket headroom analysis still apply?
