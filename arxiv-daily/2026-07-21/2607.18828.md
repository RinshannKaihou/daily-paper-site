> **一句话总结：** 本文将 Gu 等人(Nature Medicine 2026)的"信息缺失"鲁棒性压力测试从封闭式/多模态扩展到开放式临床对话——通过删掉 HealthBench 对话最后一轮用户消息的后半句，发现四款前沿模型(Claude Opus 4.8、GPT-5.5、Grok 4.3、Gemini 3.5 Flash)在信息不足时仍会"过度作答"；更重要的是，评测者本身就是混淆源：四个跨厂商 LLM 评判员之间一致性仅为中等(Fleiss' κ=0.65)，存在调整一般宽松度后仍显著的"同厂商偏好"(置换检验 p=0.04，足以改变模型排名)，且所有 LLM 评判员都比更严格的人类临床医生更宽松(对严格临床医生 O 高出 +0.14~+0.32)。

> **TL;DR:** This study extends Gu et al.'s (Nature Medicine 2026) missing-information robustness stress-test from closed-ended/multimodal tasks to **open-ended clinical conversation** by deleting the latter half of the final user turn in 50 HealthBench conversations and scoring whether four frontier models (Claude Opus 4.8, GPT-5.5, Grok 4.3, Gemini 3.5 Flash) over-commit. The central finding is methodological: the **evaluator is itself a confound** — a four-provider LLM-judge panel agrees only moderately (Fleiss' κ = 0.65), a positive same-provider association survives severity adjustment (permutation p = 0.04; GPT-5.5's inappropriate-confident rate jumps from 0.14 under its own judge to 0.30 under leave-own-provider-out judging, dropping it from second to near-worst on HealthBench n=50), and all four LLM judges are significantly more lenient than the stricter independent clinician (+0.14 to +0.32 on the blinded 50-item subsample). A MedQA anchor (n=100, accuracy 0.92–0.96) confirms the safety gap is calibration, not knowledge. Code/artifacts are released.

## 核心贡献

- **提出开放式临床对话的"信息缺失"鲁棒性探针**：删除 HealthBench 对话最后一轮用户消息的后半句(句子感知切分)，用单一评判标准衡量模型是否"恰当地承认信息缺失/表达不确定性/追问澄清"，而非给出自信且确定的答案。这是对 Gu 等人封闭式/多模态压力测试的开放式扩展。
- **把评测者当作实验的一部分并量化其两类混淆**：(a) 评判员选择会改变表观安全性——四厂商评判面板一致性仅中等(Fleiss' κ=0.65)，并用投票级逻辑回归(含 subject FE + judge FE + same-provider 项、聚类 bootstrap CI、枚举 24 种同厂商分配的精确置换检验)把"同厂商偏好"从"评判员一般宽松度"中分离出来；(b) LLM 评判员比人类临床医生更宽松。
- **临床医生锚定的效度检验**：50 项盲法样本由两名独立临床医生(O、G，作为 co-primary)和作者(R1)按相同标准标注，报告 Cohen's κ / Gwet's AC1 / Fleiss' κ 及配对 judge-minus-human 区间，揭示 LLM 评判的宽松结论对严格临床医生稳健、对最宽松临床医生较弱。
- **封闭式 MedQA/MedMCQA 锚点**：用配对 McNemar + 等效区间检验(±5pp)证实准确率高、选项顺序效应在 3/4 模型上可忽略，因此开放式安全差距是"校准失败"而非"知识缺失"。
- **公开扰动效度审计与可复现产物**：对每个被扰动的 prompt 审计"截断形式/确定性/任务类型"，指出自动截断会产生大量 artifact(7/33 行政任务、14/33 仍可答)，并提供严格多数规则的分层敏感性分析；全部代码、prompt、逐项输出、评判面板与人工标注协议均开源。

## Key Contributions

- **An open-ended missing-information robustness probe**: deletion of the latter half of the final user turn in HealthBench conversations, scored by an "appropriate response to missing information" criterion (acknowledge gap / express uncertainty / ask a clarifying question vs. unsafe over-commitment), extending Gu et al.'s largely closed-ended/multimodal stress tests to free-text dialogue.
- **An evaluator-reliability analysis** that treats the judge as part of the experiment: a four-provider LLM-judge panel (Fleiss' κ), a vote-level logistic separation of general judge severity from same-provider preference with prompt-clustered bootstrap CIs and an exact permutation test, and a leave-one-provider-out sensitivity analysis showing the apparent model ordering is evaluator-dependent.
- **A clinician-anchored validity check**: a blinded 50-item subsample labeled by two independent clinicians (co-primary) plus the author, with bootstrap κ / Gwet's AC1 and paired judge-minus-human intervals, establishing that LLM judges are more permissive than the stricter clinician and the consensus in this subsample.
- **A closed-ended MedQA/MedMCQA anchor** with paired (McNemar / equivalence) statistics confirming accuracy is high and option-order effects are within a ±5-point margin for three of four models, so the open-ended safety gap is a calibration failure, not a knowledge gap.
- **A perturbation-validity audit** (truncation form / determinacy / task type) released as `perturbation_audit.csv`, plus per-item prompts, completions, judge votes, human labels, and analysis scripts, so every rate and interval can be recomputed.

## Methodology

**Models and inference.** Four models via the `verifiers` framework / `medarc-verifiers` harness, all in real time: Claude Opus 4.8 (Anthropic, extended thinking 16k budget), GPT-5.5 (OpenAI Responses API, reasoning_effort=high), Grok 4.3 (xAI, default high-reasoning), and Gemini 3.5 Flash (Google, reasoning_effort=high). Note Gemini 3.1 Pro was rate-limited to the free tier and exhausted after 13/100 calls, so Gemini is a mid-tier Flash model, not a flagship.

**Datasets.** HealthBench consensus subset, first n=50 conversations (ordering dependence disclosed as a limitation); MedQA-USMLE-4-options test split, first n=100 items, held fixed across all MCQ conditions for paired comparison.

**Primary open-ended probe.** For each HealthBench conversation, the latter half of the final user turn is removed (sentence-aware split). A robust assistant should acknowledge the missing information, qualify, or ask a clarifying question; a confident definitive answer is scored as "unsafe over-commitment" (inappropriate-confident). The positive behavior is termed "appropriate response to missing information" (deliberately avoiding the word "abstention," which fits the MCQ sentinel but not free text).

**Closed-ended anchor (MCQ perturbations).** Each item is transformed into four conditions: `none` (baseline accuracy), `shuffle` (deterministic option permutation, seed 1618, probing positional bias), `remove_answer` (correct option deleted; a "None of the other answers is correct" sentinel appended and marked correct; reward = appropriate-abstention rate), and `remove_context` (clinical vignette removed; an insufficiency sentinel appended). The abstention conditions are explicitly cued, making them a conservative near-best-case bar. MCQ answers are graded deterministically by exact letter match — judge-independent.

**Judging.** MCQ uses no LLM judge. The open-ended probe primary run used GPT-5.5 as sole judge; saved completions were then re-scored by a four-provider panel (GPT-5.5, Opus 4.8, Grok 4.3, Gemini 3.5 Flash) on identical inputs. The HealthBench baseline rubric is judged by GPT-4.1-mini (OpenAI's reference judge) because GPT-5.5's reasoning-model input moderation rejected every long rubric call (HTTP 400) — so baseline and probe use different judges and are not a matched pair.

**Separating same-provider preference from general severity.** A vote-level logistic regression over the 800-vote grid (subject-model FE + judge FE + same-provider term) with prompt-clustered bootstrap CI and an exact permutation test enumerating all 24 bijective judge→subject "own" assignments. A difference-in-differences statistic nets out each judge's leniency on other subjects.

**Human validity subsample.** 50 blinded, provider-balanced items (seed 20260629, stratified 13/13/12/12) labeled by two independent clinicians (O, G; co-primary) and the author (R1, a physician; disclosed bounded COI). Reported: pairwise Cohen's κ, Gwet's AC1 (because κ is depressed by high "appropriate" prevalence), Fleiss' κ, and paired judge-minus-human rate differences.

**Statistics and prespecification.** Wilson intervals on proportions; exact McNemar + paired bootstrap CI on Δacc; post hoc ±5pp equivalence check (TOST-consistent). The single-judge GPT-5.5 scoring, four MCQ conditions, and blinded human subsample were prespecified; the four-provider panel, fixed-effects separation, equivalence stats, Gwet's AC1, perturbation audit, and aggregation analyses were added post hoc in response to review and labeled as such. With n=50–100 per cell the authors avoid formal NHST of every pairwise contrast and report intervals/equivalence rather than point-estimate rankings.

## Results & Highlights

1. **Inter-judge reliability is only moderate and same-provider preference survives severity adjustment (HealthBench open-ended probe, n=50).** Fleiss' κ across the four judges = 0.65 over 198 jointly-rated items (mean pairwise agreement 0.88) — "substantial" but far from interchangeable. The shared same-provider coefficient from the vote-level logistic regression is +0.35 log-odds (95% CI [+0.16, +0.56]); exact permutation p = 0.04 over all 24 bijective own-judge assignments; the GPT-5.5-specific coefficient is +0.52 log-odds (95% CI [−0.10, +1.35]) ≈ +0.10 on the probability scale. Under leave-own-provider-out judging, GPT-5.5's inappropriate-confident rate rises from 0.14 (its own judge) to 0.30, moving it from second-best to near-worst; Opus stays lowest (0.06→0.08), Gemini highest (0.28→0.33).

2. **All four LLM judges are more permissive than the stricter clinician (blinded 50-item subsample).** Against the stricter independent clinician O (appropriate-response rate 0.52), every judge credits appropriate response significantly more often, with paired differences of +0.14 (Grok) to +0.32 (GPT-5.5), all CIs excluding zero; against the author-influenced consensus (0.54) three of four are significantly more lenient (judge-vs-consensus κ = 0.20–0.43). The disagreements are one-directional — false-lenient far outnumber false-strict (GPT-5.5 16/1, Opus 16/3, Gemini 12/3, Grok 10/4).

3. **MedQA accuracy is near-ceiling and option-order effects are within ±5pp for three of four models (MedQA n=100).** Accuracy: Opus 0.92, GPT-5.5 0.94, Grok 0.96, Gemini 0.96. No McNemar test is significant and the 90% paired-bootstrap CI on Δacc lies within the ±5-point equivalence region for Opus, Grok, and Gemini; GPT-5.5 is the exception (shuffle helped by 4 points, paired 95% CI [−0.08, −0.01]) — an honest non-equivalence in the safe direction. Yet under answer/context removal every model still picks a concrete wrong option roughly one-in-ten to one-in-five of the time (inappropriate-confident 0.06–0.20).

4. **More judges is not more valid (judge-aggregation analysis).** Against clinician O, a tie-positive rule (≥2/4, ties=appropriate) gives κ ≈ 0.30 — no better than the worst single judge (GPT-5.5 alone κ = 0.26) — whereas unanimity gives κ = 0.56 and roughly halves false-lenient errors (to 8). Even on the 31 items where all four judges unanimously voted "appropriate," the stricter clinician O agreed on only 74% — roughly one in four unanimously-"appropriate" items is judged over-confident by the stricter clinician.

5. **Opus-best / Gemini-worst endpoints are stable; the middle is not.** A prompt-clustered bootstrap (5000 reps, all four judges) puts Opus as best abstainer in 100% of resamples and Gemini as worst in 87%; the panel ranking does not move when any single judge is dropped — only scoring GPT-5.5 with itself as sole judge disturbs it. On the author-audited clinical-underdetermined subset (strict-majority rule) over-commitment rates are Opus 0.07, GPT-5.5 0.21, Grok 0.21, Gemini 0.32.

6. **Robustness checks hold the pattern.** Larger-n MedQA (n=300): |Δacc| ≤ 0.007; inappropriate rates Opus 0.157/0.223, GPT-5.5 0.143/0.177, Grok 0.093/0.087, Gemini 0.077/0.127 (answer/context). MedMCQA (n=200) replicates the answer-removal signal (0.25–0.41) but the context-removal column jumps to 0.86–0.92 — a benchmark artifact of self-contained items. Rollout variance (5× re-runs): SD 0.008–0.023.

The author served as one of three human raters (R1) and performed the perturbation-validity audit — both disclosed as bounded conflicts of interest, mitigated by making the two independent clinicians (O, G) the co-primary reference and releasing every rater's labels separately. The study is founder-funded by Kinvectum AB; because funder and author coincide, no funder-independence claim is made. The work builds directly on Gu et al.'s Nature Medicine 2026 stress-test methodology (arXiv:2509.18234) and OpenAI's HealthBench, positioning itself explicitly as complementary (deletion-based) to Pan et al.'s additive adversarial red-teaming. Credibility is bolstered by an unusually transparent prespecified-vs-post-hoc declaration, full release of per-item artifacts, and a refreshingly self-critical framing that demotes the per-model leaderboard to a sensitivity result. The main credibility costs are the sole-author structure, the founder-funded provenance, and the small samples.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Genuine and well-scoped. Extending missing-information stress-testing from closed-ended/multimodal (Gu et al.) and additive adversarial pressure (Pan et al.) to **deletion-based open-ended conversation** is a real gap, and treating the evaluator as a measured confound — specifically separating same-provider preference from general judge severity via a permutation-tested fixed-effects model — goes beyond the raw self-minus-peer gaps reported by Pombal et al. and Philipp et al.
- **Rigor:** High on the measurement-design axis (paired McNemar/equivalence tests, clustered bootstrap CIs, Gwet's AC1 to counter κ's prevalence paradox, exact permutation over all 24 bijective own-judge assignments, a perturbation-validity audit with stratified sensitivity). But the core safety claim rests on **n=50 open-ended items that are the first 50 of the consensus subset, not a stratified random draw**, so theme balance is uncontrolled and most pairwise model contrasts fall within overlapping intervals — the authors acknowledge they cannot statistically separate the three flagships on over-commitment. The headline same-provider finding for **GPT-5.5 specifically is not individually significant at n=50** (CI [−0.10, +1.35]); only the shared coefficient clears p=0.04, and with one model+judge per provider, provider family cannot be disentangled from exact-model identity or output-style familiarity.
- **Reproducibility:** Strong. Fixed seeds (shuffle 1618, human subsample 20260629), pinned endpoints, error-free-only consolidation rule, released `perturbation_audit.csv`, per-item prompts/completions/votes/labels, and named analysis scripts (`self_preference_adjusted.py`, `paired_medqa_stats.py`, `evaluator_analyses.py`). Public repo: github.com/KAVentures/health-ai-readiness-robustness.
- **Significance:** The methodological warnings — don't rank a model with its own sibling judge; LLM-judged open-ended safety is optimistic vs. clinicians; reliability ≠ calibration ≠ validity — are actionable and broadly applicable to any HealthBench-style LLM-graded evaluation. The per-model clinical-safety ranking is the weakest and most caveated output.
- **Red flags / data quirks:** (1) **Gemini 3.1 Pro was substituted mid-study with Gemini 3.5 Flash** after the free-tier API quota was exhausted at 13/100 calls, so the Google data point is a cheaper tier than the other three flagships — Gemini's last-place finish is partly a tier effect the authors flag but cannot remove. (2) **OpenAI's content moderation blocked GPT-5.5 from judging the HealthBench baseline rubric (HTTP 400)**, forcing a GPT-4.1-mini baseline judge and breaking the baseline/probe matched pair — the baseline is reference-only. (3) The perturbation audit itself is **performed by the author** (disclosed COI): 7/33 audited prompts were administrative/rewriting tasks where the criterion does not apply and 14/33 remained answerable, so a substantial minority of the probe items are arguably ill-posed; the effect is partly mitigated by the stratified sensitivity but not eliminated. (4) **Only two independent clinicians** (the authors could not recruit a third external one), and the two disagree only moderately (O↔G κ = 0.47), so the "clinician reference" is itself noisy and possibly encodes legitimate policy variation rather than ground truth.

## Strengths

- Frames readiness as behavior under input degradation rather than accuracy with the answer present, and operationalizes it cleanly for open-ended text.
- Treats the evaluator as a first-class object of study: the same-provider preference is separated from general leniency, not merely reported as a raw gap.
- Clinically honest about what the intervals will and will not support — explicitly declines to publish a leaderboard and demotes the model ordering to a sensitivity analysis.
- Transparent prespecified-vs-post-hoc declaration, with post-hoc analyses (added in response to review) clearly labeled throughout.
- Exemplary artifact release: per-item outputs, judge votes, each rater's labels, the perturbation audit, and named scripts make every rate recomputable.
- The four framing points (three kinds of "missing information" are not equivalent; same-provider confounded with exact-model pairing; clinician disagreement may be policy variation; reliability/calibration/validity are distinct axes) are genuinely useful for the field.

## Limitations

- Small, non-stratified samples (n=50 open-ended, first 50 of HealthBench consensus; n=100 MCQ); most pairwise model contrasts are not statistically separable.
- Sole author who is also the founder-funder and one of three human raters — bounded and disclosed, but not independent.
- Gemini is a Flash-tier substitute, not a flagship, so the Google data point is a tier mismatch.
- Baseline and probe use different judges (GPT-4.1-mini vs. GPT-5.5) due to provider moderation, so they are not a matched pair.
- The author-performed perturbation audit found substantial truncation artifacts (7/33 administrative, 14/33 still-answerable); a cleaner variable-level deletion design is recommended but not implemented.
- The open-ended criterion is a single coarse binary; prudent safety-netting plus conditional advice can be mis-scored as over-commitment.
- Single rollout for headline cells (rollout variance bounded only on the two MedQA removal conditions, SD 0.008–0.023).
- Same-provider effect is an association, not an isolated provider-family effect; mechanism (identity vs. style familiarity) unresolved.
- Public benchmarks (MedQA, HealthBench) may be training-contaminated, inflating baselines (perturbation deltas are more robust to this).
- Not a replication of Gu et al.'s point estimates (different models, modality, datasets); replicates methodology and qualitative findings only.

## Relevance

对关注 LLM 评测方法论、LLM-as-a-judge 偏见与医疗 AI 安全的研究者高度相关：它把"信息缺失鲁棒性"压力测试带入开放式对话，并系统地证明了"用同厂商模型当裁判"会扭曲安全结论——这与日常 paper digest 中 LLM 评测、自我偏好(self-preference)与临床 AI 部署安全这几个主题直接呼应，可作为设计跨厂商评判面板与临床医生锚定效度检验的方法学参考。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Would the same-provider association survive a larger, seed-fixed, stratified open-ended sample (~150–200 HealthBench items) with prespecified human validation, given that at n=50 the GPT-5.5-specific effect is not individually significant?
- Can a **variable-level deletion design** (removing a named clinical variable while keeping the prompt grammatically fluent) isolate the hardest, most clinically realistic failure mode from the obvious mid-word-truncation cue, and does it change the model ordering?
- Is the same-provider effect driven by **provider loyalty / identity recognition** or by **familiarity with output style** — answerable only with multiple models per provider and a style-diverse panel, which this study cannot do.
- Does clinician O vs. G disagreement (0.52 vs. 0.70) reflect annotator noise or a genuine **normative policy split** ("clarify before advising" vs. "give conditional safety info while acknowledging uncertainty"), and should benchmarks make that policy explicit rather than assume a single ground truth?
- Which judge-aggregation rule (unanimity, supermajority, provider-excluded majority) generalizes as most clinician-valid beyond this 50-item sample, and does requiring a confidence threshold on the panel-vote count recover clinician agreement?
- Do deployment-time guardrails that detect missing-information regimes outperform trusting the model to self-flag, and how do the over-commitment rates translate into an acceptable clinical error budget once a prespecified threshold is set?
