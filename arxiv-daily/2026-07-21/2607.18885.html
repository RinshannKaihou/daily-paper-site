KALE: Kernel Alignment with Loss Equilibration for Stable CLIP–DINOv2 Alignment at Web Scale

Michał Pawłowicz · single-author · CC12M-3.3M · CLIP ViT-L/14 → DINOv2 ViT-L/14 · 8×H100

Key finding

KALE adaptively rescales CLIP→DINOv2 alignment weight ~104× at web scale — reviving gradients that KUEA's fixed weight lets go inert, and beating its zero-shot gain.

Why this matters

A practical controller keeps a small auxiliary loss effective — and stops runaway CLIP drift — at web scale, transferable to alignment, distillation, and multi-loss training.

Critical edge

Attribution is incomplete. The fixed-weight KUEA baseline was never run downstream on CC12M — so KALE's gains may come from web data, not the controller. The comparison conflates method with data regime.

§ 01

Method & Results

controller · equilibration · 3.3M CC12M

KALE sits on top of KUEA: align a CLIP visual student to a frozen DINOv2 teacher in degree-3 polynomial kernel space, regularised toward the original CLIP by a clean loss.

The total objective L = λc·Lclean + λa·Lalign is the same; the problem is that on CC12M the weighted alignment term collapses to ~0.2% of the clean loss (ρ≈2×10-3, decaying to 10-5) — its gradient goes inert. No single fixed scalar works: the rebalancing factor swings 7,000×–47,000× and shifts with LR/schedule.

λa ← λa·√(τ/ρ), ρ = (λa·ℓ̂align)/(λc·ℓℏclean), every Δ=100 steps

The KALE controller maintains EMAs of both losses (α=0.99) and applies square-root damping every 100 steps to hold ρ at target τ=0.8. The closed-form fixed point λ★a = τ·λc·ℓclean/ℓalign contracts geometrically (factor ½ in log-weight coords), converging from any positive init with no per-dataset tuning.

§ 02

Strengths & Limitations

what holds up · what doesn't

+ Strengths

  • Quantifies a concrete failure mode — alignment term at ~0.2% of clean loss — with a falsifiable analytical counterfactual, not a vague "fixed weight is suboptimal."
  • Clean closed-form controller analysis: fixed point λ★a and geometric contraction factor ½ predict the observed escalate-then-decline of λa and bounded-then-receding CLIP drift.
  • Honest variance reporting over 7 replicates; CLIP baseline reproduced exactly (65.26) matching KUEA's published numbers, so gains are directly comparable.
  • Transferable lesson: the same equilibration may help wherever a small auxiliary loss must stay influential against a dominant one.

! Limitations

  • No fixed-weight baseline on CC12M — controller-vs-data attribution is incomplete (explicitly acknowledged).
  • Large LP variance (σ=2.03; CLEVR σ≈4.3–5.3) from no fixed seed / no deterministic kernels; single-best-run LP headlines could mislead.
  • Brittle regime: small deviations from the effective LR/controller window cause meaningful regressions; robustness across dataset and batch sizes untested.
  • Narrow scope — only 3.3M CC12M subset, one polynomial kernel, one teacher (DINOv2), and only zero-shot/LP/retrieval (no VQA, dense prediction, or MLLM).
  • Unresolved LP-vs-retrieval trade-off: no single model maximises both; broader-coverage hypothesis unsupported (EuroSAT regresses).
§ 03

Open Questions

for further exploration
  1. What are KALE's numbers when fixed-weight KUEA is also trained on the identical 3.3M CC12M subset, cleanly isolating the controller from the data-regime change?
  2. Does the equilibration principle generalise to other small auxiliary losses — dense-pixel or contrastive regularisers in CLIP finetuning, or distillation in multimodal LLMs?
  3. How do KALE dynamics and the 2×10-4 collapse boundary shift at full CC12M, DataComp-1B, or LAION scale and different batch sizes?
  4. Would a controller parametrised by relative training progress or loss ratios (not raw step count) be more robust across dataset sizes?
  5. Could multi-objective / Pareto-aware checkpoint selection resolve the linear-probing-vs-retrieval trade-off instead of a single convergence checkpoint?
  6. Are the volatile CLEVR / PCAM gains reproducible across seeded runs and genuinely attributable to DINOv2 texture features?
