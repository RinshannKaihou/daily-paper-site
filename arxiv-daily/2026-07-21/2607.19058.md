> **一句话总结：** 提出 SkewAdam 优化器，按"主干/专家/路由器"三类参数的梯度特性差异化分配优化器状态（主干保留 fp32 动量，专家仅保留分解二阶矩，路由器保留精确二阶矩），在 6.78B MoE 上将优化器状态从 AdamW 的 50.6 GB 压到 1.29 GB（2.6%），峰值显存从 81.4 GB 降到 31.3 GB，且在 OpenWebText 上验证困惑度 108.4 优于 AdamW(126.8)/Muon(120.2)/Lion(393.7)/Adafactor(149.5)。

> **TL;DR:** SkewAdam treats an MoE not as a homogeneous bag of parameters but as three tiers with distinct gradient statistics, allocating fp32 momentum to the dense backbone (5% of params), only a factored second moment to the 95%-of-params experts, and an exact second moment to the 0.01% router. On a 6.78B-parameter MoE trained over 82M OpenWebText tokens, it cuts optimizer state from AdamW's 50.6 GB to 1.29 GB (2.6%) and peak training memory from 81.4 GB to 31.3 GB (fitting a 40 GB accelerator), while reaching validation perplexity 108.4 versus 126.8 for AdamW, 120.2 for Muon, and 149.5 for Adafactor — a lead that survives tuning AdamW to 118.5 and Adafactor to 139.7.

## 核心贡献

- **按角色分层的优化器状态策略（SkewAdam）**：首次明确指出 MoE 的三类参数——稠密主干（5%）、专家（95%）、路由器（<0.01%）——在规模与梯度统计上差异巨大，不应共享同一份状态。主干保留 fp32 动量+分解二阶矩，专家只保留分解二阶矩（去掉占据最大内存的动量），路由器保留精确非分解二阶矩（仅需 2 MB）。
- **显式的内存解析模型**：给出封闭式内存公式，在该 6.78B MoE 上状态仅 1.29 GB（AdamW 为 50.55 GB 的 2.6%），峰值训练显存 31.3 GB，落在 40 GB 加速器预算内。
- **严格匹配条件的对照实验**：单一 GPU 上与 AdamW / Lion / Muon 共享初始化、相同数据顺序、相同 bfloat16 写回路径（抖动随机舍入），后续在 H100 与 MI300X 上补充 Adafactor 与 GaLore 基线、分层消融、以及基线学习率扫描。
- **关键结论——分层换来的是内存而非精度**：分层消融显示各变体困惑度都在 108.2–108.9 内（单种子噪声范围内），但状态相差 20 倍；精度来源于"保留动量"这一性质（均匀带动量优化器也能达到），专家动量是 24 GB 的死重。
- **路由稳定性分析**：以平衡损失相对其解析下限（α=0.05）的偏离作为校准的不平衡度量，发现 Lion 在训练全程都学不出语言模型却仍能平衡负载，Muon 在最后千步突跳 22%。

## Key Contributions

- **Tiered state allocation policy (SkewAdam)**: Identifies that the three parameter populations of an MoE — dense backbone (5%), experts (95%), router (<0.01%) — differ enough in size and gradient statistics that they should not receive the same state, and assigns fp32 momentum + factored second moment to the backbone, factored second moment only to the experts, and an exact second moment to the router.
- **Closed-form memory model and large savings**: On a 6.78B-parameter MoE, SkewAdam's state is 1.29 GB (2.6% of AdamW's 50.55 GB), and peak training memory falls from 81.4 GB to 31.3 GB, within a 40 GB accelerator budget. The saving is structural (independent of quantization and would compound with it).
- **Rigorously controlled comparison**: Single-GPU evaluation against AdamW, Lion, and Muon from identical initialization, identical data order, and an identical bfloat16 update path with dithered stochastic rounding, plus same-protocol follow-ups on H100 and MI300X adding Adafactor and a GaLore-style baseline, a tier ablation, and baseline learning-rate sweeps.
- **The tiers buy memory, not accuracy**: A tier ablation shows every variant reaches the same validation perplexity (108.2–108.9, within single-seed noise) while state varies twentyfold; expert momentum is dead weight costing 24 GB, and uniform allocation with momentum matches the tiered policy at 20× the state.
- **Routing-stability analysis**: Uses deviation from the analytic load-balancing floor (α=0.05) as a calibrated imbalance measure, showing Lion balances load without learning and Muon drifts 22% above the floor in the last thousand steps.

## Methodology

SkewAdam is built on three design choices applied to a standard Adam-style update.

**Factored second moments (Adafactor's estimator).** For a weight matrix W with gradient Gₜ, it maintains exponential moving averages of row-wise (Rₜ) and column-wise (Cₜ) mean squared gradient and reconstructs the second-moment matrix as the rank-one estimate V̂ₜ = RₜCₜᵀ / R̄ₜ. Storage falls from nm to n+m floats per matrix (e.g., a 4096×4096 expert matrix goes from 64 MB to 32 KB). The paper explicitly claims no novelty for this estimator or for update-RMS clipping — both are Adafactor's.

**The tiers (Algorithm 1).** Allocation is by parameter role:
- *Backbone* (embeddings, attention, dense FFN, norms): float32 momentum + factored second moment. Gradients are dense (every token contributes), and at 5% of parameters the buffer costs only 1.27 GB.
- *Experts*: only the factored second moment. They are 95% of parameters, and under top-2-of-128 routing each sees ~1/64 of tokens, so a momentum buffer would cost 24 GB to smooth sparse, high-variance gradients.
- *Router*: full, unfactored second moment and no weight decay. Factoring a 128×4096 gate would pool scale statistics across the very logits whose relative magnitudes determine load balance; exactness costs only 2 MB.

**Low-precision updates.** Master weights are bfloat16; each step is computed in float32 and written back through dithered rounding (uniform noise of one-ULP width added before the cast), approximating unbiased stochastic rounding. Critically, every optimizer in the comparison uses this identical write-back path so none is privileged by precision handling.

**Model and protocol.** A 2-block decoder-only transformer (block 1 dense SwiGLU FFN, block 2 MoE with 128 SwiGLU experts of hidden 4096, top-2 routing), width 4096, GQA (32 query / 8 KV heads), 6.78B params, ~440M active per token. Trained on OpenWebText with a document-hash 95/5 split (no shared validation docs), 10,000 steps at batch 64×128 tokens (81.9M tokens, single epoch), cosine schedule with 3% warmup, gradient clipping at 1.0, bf16 autocast with per-block activation checkpointing, Switch-style balance loss (α=0.05) and z-loss 1e-4. The shallow 2-block design is deliberate: it concentrates 95% of parameters in one expert bank to stress optimizer state.

## Results & Highlights

- **Memory**: SkewAdam uses 1.29 GB of optimizer state on the 6.78B MoE, versus 50.55 GB for AdamW, 25.27 GB for Lion and Muon — i.e. 2.6% of AdamW's state. Peak training memory falls from 81.4 GB (AdamW) to 31.3 GB (SkewAdam), clearing the 40 GB accelerator budget; Lion/Muon still peak near 57 GB and do not.
- **Convergence on OpenWebText (validation perplexity)**: SkewAdam reaches 108.4, beating AdamW 126.8 (−14.5% / −18.4 points), Muon 120.2 (−9.8%), Lion 393.7, and Adafactor 149.5 (about 40 points behind on the same H100 protocol). SkewAdam overtakes AdamW and Muon by step 4,000.
- **Throughput**: SkewAdam sustains 5,000 tokens/s — 6.6% above AdamW (4,692) and only 1.5% below the cheapest-buffer Lion (5,075); Muon pays a 32% throughput penalty (3,409 tokens/s) for Newton–Schulz iterations over 6.4B expert parameters every step.
- **Tier ablation (MI300X)**: All four variants land at perplexity 108.2–108.9 and balance loss ≈0.050 (within single-seed noise), but state varies twentyfold — restoring momentum to experts costs 24 GB for a 0.2 perplexity change, and uniform momentum+factored allocation matches the tiered policy at 25.29 GB. The contribution is memory at parity, not a perplexity ordering.
- **Baseline tuning does not close the gap**: After sweeping learning rates (SkewAdam deliberately left untuned), best AdamW reaches 118.5 ± 0.5 (3 seeds) and best Adafactor 139.7 (2 seeds), still 10 and ~30 perplexity points behind untuned SkewAdam (108.4–109.0 across three GPUs).
- **Routing stability**: SkewAdam and AdamW stay within 1% of the 0.05 balance floor from step 4,000 onward (final 0.0505 and 0.0502); Lion is stable but 7–9% elevated throughout while failing to learn; Muon jumps to 0.0608 (22% above floor) in the final thousand steps.
- **Cross-platform reproducibility**: SkewAdam's perplexity is 108.4 (H200), 109.0 (H100), 108.9 (MI300X) — within 0.6 across three GPUs and two vendors.
- **Zero-shot (PIQA/WinoGrande/HellaSwag/ARC-Challenge)**: At only 82M tokens all four models are near chance on three tasks and a few points above chance on PIQA (53.5–55.9%), with no optimizer separation beyond 1–2 standard errors — reported only as a completeness check.

## Authors & Credibility

Single-author work by Nuemaan Malik, listed as an Independent Researcher (self-funded, run on rented GPU time). The honesty of the limitations section is notable: the author explicitly frames SkewAdam as "memory, not a better optimizer," discloses that weight decay was inert under bf16 rounding in all runs, and that most configurations are single-seed. Code, per-step training logs, and figures are released at https://github.com/nuemaan/skewadam with a single-file trainer and exact reproduction commands. The methodology follows best practices for controlled optimizer comparisons (shared initialization, identical data order, identical precision path), and the paper distinguishes carefully between what is novel (the allocation policy) and what is reused from Adafactor (the factored estimator, update clipping). The work has not (yet) been peer-reviewed.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: Moderate and well-scoped. The factored second-moment estimator and update-RMS clipping are explicitly Adafactor's; the genuine contribution is the *allocation question* — deciding tier by tier where momentum and factoring earn their memory — which the related work shows uniform recipes (Adafactor, SM3, 8-bit, ZeRO, GaLore, Lion, Muon) all leave open. The naming and framing as a design principle rather than a single optimizer is honest.
- **Rigor**: Strong on the controlled-comparison axis (shared init, identical data order, identical bf16/dithered-rounding write-back path so no optimizer is precision-privileged) but weak on statistical power. The headline perplexity comparisons rest on single seeds for the main H200 run; only the Table 4 LR sweeps use repeated seeds, and SkewAdam itself was *not* tuned while the baselines were. The author acknowledges this asymmetry but it tempers the headline perplexity lead (the tier ablation's own 0.6-point spread is within run-to-run noise, undermining any perplexity-ordering claim among variants).
- **Reproducibility**: High. Complete hyperparameters in Appendix A, analytic memory accounting in Appendix B, per-step logs, exact CLI commands, deterministic document-hash data split, and released code. Cross-platform agreement (H200/H100/MI300X, two vendors) is a real positive.
- **Significance**: Practically meaningful for memory-constrained MoE training — a 61% peak-memory reduction that moves a 6.78B MoE out of an 80 GB-class device into a 40 GB-class one — but the scope is narrow. The 2-block model is shallow by intent (to concentrate 95% of params in one expert bank), leaving multi-layer MoE behavior untested. The 82M-token / 128-context horizon is three to four orders of magnitude below production budgets, and downstream evals are explicitly near-chance.
- **Red flags**: (1) The GaLore baseline (1,839.9 perplexity) is a single untuned configuration of the author's own implementation and the paper itself says it "should not be read as more than that" — readers should not treat it as a verdict on GaLore. (2) Lion's catastrophic 393.7 reflects one learning rate and one seed; the magnitude-blindness interpretation is suggestive, not proven. (3) Weight decay rounding to a no-op in all runs means the recipe is effectively unregularized — anyone scaling it must re-fuse decay into the fp32 update ahead of the stochastic cast, a configuration "not yet validated at length." (4) Single-author, non-peer-reviewed, self-funded on rented compute; the compute budget, not the experimental design, set the scale.

## Strengths

- Principled, role-aware allocation that directly targets the dominant cost (expert momentum) rather than uniformly shrinking state.
- Large, structural memory savings (97.4% state reduction, 61% peak-memory reduction) that are independent of quantization and would compound with it.
- Exceptionally controlled optimizer comparison — shared initialization, identical data order, identical bf16/dithered-rounding path — which is what licenses the matched-conditions claim.
- Intellectual honesty: the paper states its central result is memory-at-parity, not a perplexity win, and pre-empts over-reading the near-chance downstream numbers.
- Open code, logs, exact reproduction commands, and cross-vendor replication.

## Limitations

- Only a 2-block-deep model; how tiered allocation behaves when routing decisions compose across many MoE layers is untested.
- Most configurations are single-seed; only the LR sweeps use repeated seeds, and SkewAdam was itself never tuned while baselines were.
- Small training horizon (82M tokens, 128-token contexts) and inert weight decay — the recipe is effectively unregularized and not validated at production scale.
- Downstream evaluations (PIQA/WinoGrande/HellaSwag/ARC-Challenge) are near chance and serve only as a completeness check.
- The GaLore baseline is a single untuned configuration of the author's own implementation; the tier ablation rests on one seed per variant with a 0.6-point spread within noise.
- Single-author, self-funded preprint; the author explicitly invites collaboration/compute to extend to deeper models, longer horizons, and multi-seed replication.

## Relevance

与 mixture-of-experts 训练、内存高效优化器设计直接相关，适合关注大模型训练系统、MoE 架构优化与 optimizer 工程的研究者：它给出了一个即插即用的"按角色分配状态"原则，可在显存受限的 MoE 训练中显著降低峰值显存，且代码与日志已开源便于复现。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the tiered allocation preserve its perplexity/memory advantage on deeper MoE models (e.g., 16–32 blocks) where routing decisions compose across many expert layers?
- Would restoring a *cheap, low-rank* momentum to the experts (rather than full fp32 momentum) close any gap at longer training horizons where expert momentum might "begin to earn its 24 GB"?
- How does SkewAdam interact with quantized optimizer state (8-bit, FP8) — does factoring's lack of a scale ceiling compound with quantization as the paper speculates?
- Does the Muon load-balance drift in the final thousand steps foreshadow a real instability, and does it worsen at longer horizons or larger expert counts?
- Can the tiered principle be extended to other heterogeneous-parameter architectures (e.g., retrieval-augmented models, multi-modal towers) where gradient density also varies sharply by component?
