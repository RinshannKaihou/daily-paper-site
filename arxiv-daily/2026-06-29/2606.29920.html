Methodology, distilled

§Methodology

Rubric verification is cast as a binary meta-evaluation: a judge receives a task context m (a Q–A pair in Deep Research, or a serialized trajectory in Agentic Coding) plus a single rubric r_i, and predicts ŷ_i ∈ {0,1}, scored against a human gold label.

A multi-stage funnel builds the benchmark: pool rubrics from existing agent benchmarks → pair each with a fixed judged output → manually strip subjective / unverifiable / multi-condition rubrics and downsample for affordability → categorize by the verifier ability required → label everything binary under one evidence-based guideline.

Sources→ Fixed Output→ Filter + Taxonomy→ Double Annotation + Adjudication

Quality uses Balanced Accuracy (BAcc) per category to absorb class imbalance; the headline ranking is Avg BAcc across all K categories. 18 frontier LLMs are evaluated; all strategy probes run on a fixed 20% subset using 6 representative models.

Strengths & Limitations

§Critical Assessment

Strengths

  • First benchmark explicitly targeting LaaJ reliability for rubric verification in agentic scenarios.
  • Realistic, high-stakes domains with genuine long context (up to ~49K-token trajectories).
  • Robust ground truth: large-scale human annotation, κ = 0.808, 90.4% agreement.
  • Category-level taxonomy turns an opaque aggregate score into actionable diagnostics.
  • Practitioner-relevant strategy analysis (prompting, batching, voting) gives deployment guidance.
  • Open dataset & code maximize reuse and reproducibility.

Limitations

  • Two domains only — transfer to tool-use, GUI, or multi-agent settings is untested.
  • Strategy analyses run on a 20% subset that may underrepresent rare categories (Planning, Tools).
  • “Strong but noisy” results suggest a ceiling effect that can obscure finer-grained failures.
  • “Fixed” judged outputs depend on generator models, biasing which rubrics are satisfiable.
  • Findings are model-snapshot-specific (GPT-5.4, Gemini-3.1…) and need re-evaluation as judges evolve.

Open questions for further exploration

§Questions
Q1 · ENSEMBLING

Given near-zero error overlap, can ensembling different judges beat simple self-voting for reliability?

Q2 · RUBRIC DESIGN

What rubric designs (atomic vs. compound, evidence-cited vs. abstract) most reduce residual judge noise?

Q3 · CALIBRATION

Is there a calibration that corrects “partial satisfaction” & “requirement expansion” without majority-vote cost?

Q4 · SCALE

Do the batching / voting trade-offs hold at 100K+ trajectories and across GUI / multi-agent domains?
