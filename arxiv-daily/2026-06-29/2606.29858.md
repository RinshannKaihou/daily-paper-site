> **一句话总结：** 本文提出一种 token 级别分析框架，用 sigmoid 拟合每个 contextualized token 的损失轨迹，证明平滑的 power-law scaling law 本质上是由"token 学习时间谱"（learning-time spectrum）这一重尾分布主导的；该谱可沿训练步数 $T$、数据规模 $D$、模型规模 $M$ 三轴定量重建损失导数，并据此重排训练数据分布，实现 11% 的验证损失下降加速。

> **TL;DR:** This paper decomposes smooth LLM scaling laws into per-token sigmoid-shaped learning events, showing that the aggregate power-law loss is governed primarily by a heavy-tailed "learning-time spectrum" of when individual tokens become learnable; this spectrum quantitatively reconstructs loss derivatives along the $T$, $D$, and $M$ axes across 110+ runs (up to 6B params, 300B tokens, 1,178 A100 GPU-days), and reshaping the data distribution by token learnability yields 11% faster validation-loss reduction.

## 核心贡献

- **token 级别的 Scaling Law 分解框架**：首次在大规模真实数据训练中，将聚合的平滑损失曲线分解为单个 contextualized token 的局部学习事件，用 sigmoid 参数化拟合 token 损失轨迹，从微观层面解释 power-law 的来源。
- **学习时间谱 (Learning-Time Spectrum) 的发现与解耦**：把每个 token 的学习分解为"局部脉冲形状" $g$ 与"学习时间分布" $p(\tau)$ 两个因子；脉冲形状跨 token 近似共享，而学习时间谱强烈重尾、本身近似 power-law，正是它主导了宏观损失导数的形状。
- **三轴定量重构**：在 110+ 次预训练运行（290M–6B 参数、1B–300B token、1,178 A100 GPU-days）上，用测得的学习时间谱沿训练步数 $T$、数据规模 $D$、模型规模 $M$ 三个轴定量重建了验证损失导数，与经验值高度吻合。
- **可操作的训练优化**：按 token 变得可学的时机对训练样本打分、分桶并重新塑形训练分布（提升目标区间内可学样本权重、降低其他样本权重），改变后续优化轨迹，实现 11% 的验证损失下降加速——证明该信号不仅是解释性的，更是可干预的。

## Key Contributions

- **Token-level decomposition of scaling laws**: Introduces a framework that breaks the smooth aggregate loss into localized learning events of individual contextualized tokens, each fitted with a sigmoid — the first direct token-level validation of the heavy-tailed-difficulty hypothesis in industrial-scale real-data training.
- **Decoupling pulse shape from learning-time spectrum**: Separates each token's learning into a local pulse shape $g(a)$ (approximately shared across tokens) and a learning-time spectrum $p(\tau)$ (strongly heavy-tailed, itself ~power-law). Under $L'(a) \approx (p * g)(a)$, the macroscopic loss-derivative shape is governed primarily by $p(\tau)$ rather than token-specific shapes.
- **Quantitative multi-axis reconstruction**: Across 110+ pre-training runs (290M–6B params, 1B–300B tokens, 1,178 A100 GPU-days), the measured learning-time spectrum reconstructs the validation-loss derivative along the training-step ($T$), data-scale ($D$), and model-scale ($M$) axes with close agreement to empirics.
- **Actionable training improvement**: Shows the signal is not just explanatory — reshaping the training distribution by when tokens become learnable (sample-level bucketing by learning-time fraction in a target interval) alters the optimization trajectory and yields 11% faster validation-loss reduction.

## Methodology

**Core decomposition.** The aggregate validation loss is written as $L(a) = \frac{1}{|I|}\sum_{i \in I} \ell_i(a)$, where $\ell_i(a)$ is the loss of contextualized token instance $i$ along an axis $a \in \{T, D, M\}$. To find what drives the power-law, the authors study the loss *derivative* $L'(a)$, which reveals *where learning occurs*.

**Three hypotheses tested.** (A) the power law comes from overall training dynamics/optimizer noise; (B) it comes from individual token trajectories themselves being power laws; (C) it comes from *different tokens being learned at different points along the axis*. Cross-run comparison of the same token instances (same descent timing/shape across random seeds) rules out (A); non-power-law token trajectories rule out (B), pointing to (C).

**Sigmoid parameterization.** Each token's loss is fit as $\ell_i(a) \approx \ell^{post}_i + \Delta_i[1 - \sigma(\lambda_i(a - \tau_i))]$, with interpretable parameters: $\tau_i$ = learning time (descent center), $\lambda_i$ = sharpness/width, $\Delta_i$ = total loss reduction, and pre/post plateaus. The derivative forms a localized "learning pulse" $\ell'_i(a)$ centered at $\tau_i$.

**Decoupling shape vs. time.** The centered pulse template $g(a) = \frac{1}{|I|}\sum_i \ell'_i(a+\tau_i)$ captures the (shared) local shape, while the learning-time spectrum $p(\tau) = \frac{1}{|I|}\sum_i \delta(\tau - \tau_i)$ captures the distribution of *when* learning happens. The aggregate derivative is then a convolution $L'(a) \approx (p * g)(a)$, so if $g$ is shared, the macroscopic shape is governed primarily by $p(\tau)$.

**Experimental scale (industrial pipelines).**
- *Step axis ($T$)*: 1.5B MoE trained on 300B tokens (mixed Chinese/English/math/reasoning/books/papers/code), token-level losses recorded across checkpoints; 133 A100 GPU-days.
- *Data axis ($D$)*: 290M MoE, training budget swept 1B→35B tokens; a 50-run configuration sweep builds an envelope frontier; 358 A100 GPU-days.
- *Model axis ($M$)*: uses non-embedding FLOPs/token $M$ (more faithful than parameter count for MoE) rather than $N$; 60 variants spanning 0.42–5.00 GFLOPs/token (~400M–6B params) at fixed 10B tokens; 422 A100 GPU-days.
- *Reshaping experiment*: a 290M MoE reference model estimates learning times for 1B candidate tokens; samples are ranked by the fraction of tokens whose learning events fall in a target step interval (steps 2000–3800), partitioned into 10 buckets, then down/up-weighted before continuing training from step 2000.
- Validation also includes controllable synthetic-data experiments (Appendix A.3) where the difficulty distribution is known exactly.

## Results & Highlights

- **Token learning is non-uniform and transition-like**: individual token losses sit on a high plateau, drop sharply in a localized interval (well fit by sigmoids), then settle on a lower plateau; trajectories are reproducible across seeds. Tokens are grouped into *learned / early-learning / late-learning / unlearned*.
- **Token-wise sigmoid fits recover the aggregate loss**: the loss implied by fitted sigmoids closely matches empirical validation loss on the step axis (e.g., 1.5B model, 300B tokens), with a clear power-law regime after the initial transient.
- **Shared pulse, heavy-tailed spectrum**: after alignment by learning time, learning pulses are highly similar; the learning-time spectrum is strongly heavy-tailed and well approximated by a power law itself.
- **Spectrum reconstructs the loss derivative on all three axes** ($T$, $D$, $M$): the spectrum-dominated reconstruction, the sigmoid-implied derivative, and a power-law fit all closely agree with the empirical derivative.
- **Generalizes across scaling axes**: the same pulse-spectrum mechanism persists when the horizontal axis changes from training steps to data scale to model scale, including under recipe-optimized frontier runs.
- **Model scale uses FLOPs/token, not parameter count**, avoiding MoE distortions when comparing small/medium models.
- **Actionable 11% acceleration**: reshaping the training distribution by learning-time signal produces faster training-loss decline and an 11% validation-loss-reduction speedup vs. the original distribution, despite a higher irreducible-loss floor — demonstrating controllable intervention on the scaling curve.
- **Total compute**: 110+ pre-training runs, ~1,178 A100 GPU-days.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty — High.** The heavy-tailed-difficulty / data-distribution explanation of scaling laws is well established (Hutter, Michaud et al.'s quantization model, Cagnetta et al.), but prior validations were largely theoretical or on synthetic/toy data. Providing *direct token-level empirical evidence* at 6B/300B scale on real data is a meaningful advance, as is explicitly bridging token-level dynamics to macroscopic scaling in one decomposition.
- **Rigor — Strong.** Reconstructing the loss derivative on three *independent* axes ($T$, $D$, $M$) is a demanding quantitative test; using 110+ runs and recipe-optimized frontiers (rather than a single fixed recipe) guards against confounds. Cross-seed reproducibility checks and allowing both descending/ascending (forgetting) trajectories in the fits are thoughtful touches.
- **Reproducibility — Good.** Code is public; core setup (Adam β1=0.9, β2=0.95, 10% warmup, MoE architectures, axes definitions) is described. Full reproducibility still depends on access to the proprietary industrial corpus, but the methodology is transferable.
- **Significance — Potentially high.** For theory, it offers a concrete micro-mechanism for *why* aggregate loss is power-law. For practice, the learning-time signal offers a principled (non-heuristic) basis for data curriculum / mixture control.
- **Red flags / caveats.** (1) The 11% speedup is demonstrated at small scale (290M reference model, a narrow step interval 2000–3800) and over a short horizon; whether it compounds at much larger scale or over full training is not shown. (2) The "irreducible-loss floor is higher" under reshaping — the gain is in descent speed, not necessarily final loss, which should be examined for downstream-task effects. (3) $p(\tau)$ is empirically estimated from checkpoints, so using it as an *a priori* predictor (without first measuring) remains open. (4) The shared-pulse assumption is an approximation; the decomposition's accuracy hinges on its validity, which is shown visually/qualitatively rather than via a formal goodness metric across all axes.

## Strengths

- Long-sought token-level empirical grounding for scaling laws at genuinely industrial scale (1,178 A100 GPU-days).
- Unifies explanation across three scaling axes within a single convolution-based framework $L'(a) \approx (p*g)(a)$.
- Cleanly rules out competing hypotheses (optimizer-noise; per-token power-law) via cross-seed evidence.
- Bridges mechanistic insight and practical value — the spectrum is both explanatory and actionable.
- Uses real multilingual + code corpora and MoE architectures, and adopts FLOPs/token as a more faithful model-scale axis.
- Public code release lowers the barrier to follow-up work.

## Limitations

- The 11% acceleration is shown at 290M scale over a narrow window; durability and magnitude at larger scale / longer horizons / downstream tasks are unverified.
- Sigmoid fitting is an idealization; trajectories that are noisy, multi-step, or non-monotone may be only coarsely captured, and goodness-of-fit is largely shown qualitatively.
- Computing and maintaining per-token learning-time spectra adds overhead; the cost/benefit of doing this online during training is not quantified.
- $p(\tau)$ must currently be *measured* from checkpoints, limiting its use as a pure *a priori* curriculum signal.
- Generalization to non-MoE / very different architectures, modalities (vision/multimodal), and post-training (SFT/RL) settings is not tested.
- The higher irreducible-loss floor under reshaping is a noted but under-explored trade-off.

## Relevance

对于关注大模型 scaling law、训练数据配比与高效预训练的研究者而言，本文从 token 级机制出发解释了平滑 scaling law 的成因，并给出可直接指导数据课程设计的可操作信号，与 scaling laws、data-centric 预训练及 MoE 训练实践等核心方向高度相关。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the 11% speedup persist, amplify, or vanish at much larger scales (e.g., tens of B params, full-scale pretraining) and over the entire training run rather than a narrow window?
- Can the learning-time spectrum $p(\tau)$ be *predicted* a priori from cheap linguistic/data features (frequency, n-gram dependency length, domain) — which the paper notes correlate with it — so curriculum design doesn't require running a reference model first?
- How does the spectrum behave across different architectures (dense vs. MoE, non-transformer) and modalities (vision, multimodal, speech)?
- Is the shared-pulse assumption $g$ robust enough that a single template generalizes across corpora, or does $g$ itself shift with data mixture?
- How does the reshaping intervention interact with downstream task performance and final convergence loss (given the higher irreducible floor)?
- Does the learning-time signal connect mechanistically to known phenomena like grokking, emergence, or catastrophic forgetting observed at the token level?
- What is the compute overhead of estimating and maintaining the spectrum online, and can it be amortized via cheap proxy measurements?
