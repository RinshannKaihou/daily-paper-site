Why this matters

Gives the eval-and-alignment community a shared vocabulary, an evidence map, and a practical split: the open–closed safety gap is governance-driven, not behavior-driven.

Critical edge

Headline stats are genuinely underpowered (n = 10, CIs spanning ±0.5) — and the sharpest contrast rests on one model's classification: dropping Grok 4 (5/7 estimated cells) inflates the composite-safety gap from d = 1.69 → 3.48.

01Methodology

A three-part design: a hybrid literature survey, an original conceptual framework, and a structured 10-model audit — explicitly framed as diagnostic, not rank-generating.

The survey pairs PRISMA-style systematic search with narrative synthesis and separately-tracked grey evidence (model cards, blogs), spanning 2018–2026 across eight evidence streams: benchmark validity, dynamic evaluation, LLM-as-judge reliability, safety evaluation, jailbreak robustness, reward hacking, mechanistic interpretability, and governance/auditability.

Two constructs organize the field: the Instability Decomposition (G_target ~ f(β, ε, σ, m, d, M) — a study-design checklist, not a theorem) and the Alignment Trilemma (Optimization effectiveness · Value fidelity · Robust generalization). Ten models are then scored on seven harmonized [0,1] indicators, split into Core Safety (behavioral) and Governance (disclosure) composites.

Four Goodhart failure modes (the shared lens)

RegressionalSelection on a noisy proxy shifts the mean downward.
ExtremalOptimization pushes past the region where proxy ≈ target.
CausalIntervention on the proxy breaks its link to the goal.
AdversarialA capable agent actively games the metric.

03Strengths & limitations

Strengths

  • Genuinely unifies two separate literatures (benchmark validity ⇄ alignment failure) under a precise Goodhart taxonomy with a cross-domain mapping table.
  • Exceptional epistemic discipline: four-level evidence taxonomy, falsification requirements, parity reporting, no causal overclaims.
  • Transparent provenance engineering (traceable / recomputable / common-protocol; distinct missingness states).
  • The behavioral-vs-governance decomposition is a practically useful insight for evaluators and policymakers.
  • Prespecified, actionable research agenda (four Expectations; longitudinal & causal designs).

Limitations

  • Tiny purposive sample (n = 10); headline correlation indeterminate with wide CIs and no power.
  • Heterogeneous public protocols undermine comparability; no common-protocol measurement.
  • Composite Safety deliberately mixes behavioral + disclosure — not a validated unidimensional construct.
  • Governance/auditability rubric is ad hoc and unvalidated (no Cronbach's α / PCA).
  • Estimated cells (Grok 4: 5/7) materially drive results; leave-one-out conclusions are fragile.
  • Cross-sectional only — no causal identification; open/closed grouping conflates license, governance, and checkpoint heterogeneity.

04Questions for further exploration

  1. How does the capability–robustness association change under a version-locked, common-protocol re-evaluation (single attack generator, judge, attempt budget) with a power-justified sample?
  2. Can the Instability Decomposition's components (β, ε, σ, m, d, M) be empirically isolated via controlled optimization-pressure experiments within one model family?
  3. Does the "governance-driven open–closed gap" persist once disclosure is measured by a validated instrument (e.g., official FMTI) rather than a project-specific rubric?
  4. How does evaluation awareness / strategic deception interact with adversarial-Goodhart as models grow more capable?
  5. Can a longitudinal repeated-measures design detect the shortening "benchmark discriminative lifetime" hypothesized in Expectation 3?
