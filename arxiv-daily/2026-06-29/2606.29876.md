> **一句话总结：** 本文提出"临床推理图"（Clinical Reasoning Graphs）方法——用包含5类节点、7类边的领域本体将LLM诊断推理文本转化为结构化有向图，并在50个NEJM CPC病例、5个LLM、3种提示条件下的750条轨迹上发现：尽管LLM诊断准确率高达60–70%，但其推理结构在临床相似病例间并未表现出稳定可复用的"诊断schema"（相似/不相似病例的图相似度几乎相等，无一通过多重检验校正），揭示了LLM"有能力但缺乏一致性"，主张用过程级评估补充最终准确率。

> **TL;DR:** The author introduces **clinical reasoning graphs** — directed graphs extracted from free-text LLM diagnostic traces via a domain-grounded ontology (5 node types, 7 edge types) — and applies them to **750 traces from 5 frontier LLMs (GPT-5.4, GPT-5.2, Claude Opus 4.5, Claude Sonnet 4.5, Gemini 3 Pro) across 50 NEJM Clinicopathological Conference cases under 3 prompt conditions**. The headline finding is "competence without consistency": despite 60–70% diagnostic accuracy, LLM reasoning graphs show **no schema-scale cross-case consistency** (within-cluster ≈ between-cluster similarity; no comparison survives Bonferroni correction). Graph similarity is also nearly identical for both-correct (0.488) and both-incorrect (0.484) model pairs, meaning accuracy and reasoning structure are decoupled. Structured reflection prompting increases within-trace discriminating structure (+33%) but does *not* improve cross-case consistency.

## 核心贡献

- **提出"临床推理图"形式化本体**：基于经典诊断推理理论（Charlin 2000；Bowen 2006；Dhaliwal 2017），定义了包含5类节点（临床特征、诊断、语义限定词、鉴别特征、证据引用）与7类边（支持、反对、鉴别、触发反思、以及反思后的更新——提升/降级/不变）的有向图schema，将LLM自由文本推理转化为可计算、可审计的结构化对象。
- **构建可扩展且经过验证的抽取管线**：用GPT-5.4作为结构化抽取器，在750条轨迹上达到100%抽取成功率，平均每图47.0节点、58.7边；作者本人（临床医生）审阅30个样本图，边精确率98.4%、临床显著召回率94.8%，并提供test-retest信度（0.825）、noise floor（0.466）与跨抽取器一致性（0.593）作为标定基准。
- **大规模实证检验"诊断schema"假设**：在15个"模型–条件"组合上以分层置换检验+Bonferroni校正系统检验"相似病例图相似度更高"的假设，结论是否定的——这是论文最具份量的负面结果。
- **"有能力但无一致性"的核心发现**：跨相似病例的图相似度与跨不相似病例几乎相等（pooled 0.475 vs 0.472），且答案都对与都错的模型对图相似度也几乎相同（0.488 vs 0.484，p=0.25），证明图结构捕捉的是与最终准确率正交的维度。
- **结构化反思提示的剖析**：发现结构化反思能显著提升单条轨迹内的鉴别结构（+33%，p<10⁻⁶），却反而降低4/5模型的跨病例一致性，说明反思带来的是病例特异的差异化分析而非可复用模板。
- **方法论工具化与开源**：将本体、抽取管线、验证协议、抽取出的推理图与相似度产物全部开源（github.com/nisargpatel/clinical-reasoning-graphs），可作为其他领域"结构化推理评估"的基础设施。

## Key Contributions

- **A formal clinical-reasoning-graph ontology** grounded in classical diagnostic-reasoning theory, with **5 node types** (clinical features, diagnoses, semantic qualifiers, discriminating features, evidence references) and **7 edge types** (evidential support, evidence against, discrimination, triggered reflection, and post-reflection updates: promoted / demoted / unchanged).
- **A validated, scalable extraction pipeline**: GPT-5.4 as extractor yields 100% extraction over 750 traces (mean 47.0 nodes, 58.7 edges/graph, edge-to-node ratio 1.26, discriminating edges in 76% of graphs); the author (a physician) audited 30 sampled graphs, achieving **98.4% edge precision** and **94.8% clinically salient recall**, with published test-retest reliability (0.825), noise floor (0.466), and inter-extractor agreement (0.593) as calibration anchors.
- **A large-scale empirical test of the "diagnostic schema" hypothesis**: case-level permutation tests with Bonferroni correction across 15 model–condition cells find no schema-scale cross-case consistency.
- **The "competence without consistency" result**: graph similarity is nearly identical for clinically similar vs. dissimilar cases (pooled 0.475 vs 0.472), and for both-correct vs. both-incorrect model pairs (0.488 vs 0.484, p=0.25), showing reasoning structure is orthogonal to accuracy.
- **A nuanced dissection of reflection prompting**: structured reflection increases within-trace discriminating structure by +33% (p<10⁻⁶) yet reduces cross-case consistency in 4/5 models — diversification, not standardization.
- **Open infrastructure**: ontology, pipeline, validation protocol, extracted graphs, and similarity artifacts released for reuse, including adaptation to multi-step agent trajectories.

## Methodology

**Study design.** Analysis of 750 diagnostic reasoning traces generated under a preregistered protocol (Patel, 2026), spanning 50 NEJM Clinicopathological Conference (CPC) cases stratified by physician-assigned difficulty (15 easy / 20 moderate / 15 hard) and grouped into 20 clinical clusters. Five frontier LLMs evaluated: **GPT-5.4, GPT-5.2, Claude Opus 4.5, Claude Sonnet 4.5, Gemini 3 Pro**. Three prompting conditions: (i) *baseline* — differential + probability estimates; (ii) *adversarial* — phased, argue against leading diagnosis before a revised answer; (iii) *structured reflection* — phased protocol requiring problem representation, balanced stress test, and explicit defend-or-update decision per diagnosis (Dhaliwal ART framework). Each trace from a single autoregressive pass at temperature 1.0; phased conditions sequence phases within one prompt. Final diagnoses scored against the CPC reference by an LLM judge (Claude Opus 4.6).

**Ontology (5 node types / 7 edge types).** Nodes: clinical features (signs/symptoms/labs/imaging), diagnoses (with probability estimates), semantic qualifiers (e.g., "acute-onset"), discriminating features (findings used to distinguish diagnoses), evidence references (guidelines, rules, studies). Edges: evidential support, evidence against, discrimination between diagnoses, triggered reflection, and post-reflection diagnosis updates (promoted / demoted / unchanged).

**Graph extraction.** GPT-5.4 with a structured extraction prompt enforcing the ontology, schema-valid JSON, connected graphs, phase tags on all edges, and constraints on reflection edges in multi-phase traces. 100% extraction success. Because GPT-5.4 is both an evaluated model and the extractor, the author checked for source-model bias (extraction stats did not differ across source models; a subset independently re-extracted with Claude Opus 4.7). Fidelity: a physician (the author) reviewed 30 sampled graphs → 98.4% edge precision, 94.8% clinically salient recall (baseline 92.8%, adversarial 95.5%, structured 96.2%); no hallucinated or missed discriminating edges in the sample. Gold diagnosis was provided to disambiguate edge direction; gold-included vs. gold-excluded similarity was 0.862 (final diagnosis unchanged in all 20 re-extractions), exceeding test-retest reliability.

**Clustering & similarity.** Diagnostic-schema consistency is operationalized as: graphs of clinically similar cases should be more similar than graphs of dissimilar cases. Two-stage clustering — organ-system classification then embedding-based agglomerative subclustering (text-embedding-3-large) — yields 20 clusters (44/50 cases in clusters of size ≥2), author-reviewed for coherence. Pairwise graph similarity is a **5-component composite** (with pre-fixed weights, not re-optimized): motif similarity (1−JSD over edge-type triplets) 0.35; diagnosis-overlap Jaccard 0.30; feature-overlap Jaccard 0.15; qualifier-overlap Jaccard 0.10; reasoning depth (discriminating-edge ratio, 1−|r_a−r_b|) 0.10. Same-case pairs (same case, different models) are excluded.

**Calibration anchors.** Test-retest (same trace, same model) = 0.825 (SD 0.073, n=10); noise floor (shuffled node labels) = 0.466 (SD 0.058, n=50); inter-extractor (same trace, different extractor) = 0.593 (n=20) — all above the within-cluster level, confirming the metric detects similarity when it exists.

**Statistics.** Within- vs. between-cluster composite similarity per model–condition cell via **case-level permutation tests** (10,000 permutations, preserving model/condition structure), bootstrap 95% CIs, Cohen's d, **Bonferroni correction across 15 comparisons (α = 0.0033)**. Component-level and content-channel analyses guard against aggregation artifacts.

## Results & Highlights

- **No schema-scale consistency (primary result).** Within- vs. between-cluster composite similarity statistically indistinguishable across all 5 models × 3 conditions. Pooled similarity 0.475 (within) vs. 0.472 (between). All 15 comparisons: |∆| < 0.014, all p > 0.02, **none survive Bonferroni**; bootstrap 95% CIs within [−0.024, +0.028]; effect sizes negligible (d = −0.16 to 0.26).
- **Structured-reflection condition (Table 1)** — per-cell pooled 0.460/0.460, all p > 0.22:
  | Model | W | B | d | p |
  |---|---|---|---|---|
  | GPT-5.4 | 0.452 | 0.448 | 0.06 | 0.30 |
  | GPT-5.2 | 0.459 | 0.465 | −0.11 | 0.80 |
  | Sonnet 4.5 | 0.460 | 0.463 | −0.05 | 0.63 |
  | Opus 4.5 | 0.474 | 0.469 | 0.09 | 0.22 |
  | Gemini 3 Pro | 0.457 | 0.453 | 0.06 | 0.29 |
- **Within-cluster under structured reflection (0.456) did not even exceed the noise floor (0.466)**, implying cross-case similarity is driven by shared graph form, not shared clinical content.
- **Accuracy does not predict graph similarity.** Among 1,112 same-outcome model pairs, both-correct similarity 0.488 (n=738) vs. both-incorrect 0.484 (n=374); Mann-Whitney U=143,797, p=0.25, 95% CI for ∆ [−0.003, +0.012].
- **The composite is structure-dominated.** Decomposition shows motif similarity (≈0.88) and reasoning depth (≈0.98) — both near ceiling — contribute ~89% of the composite's value while carrying only 45% of its weight; ~11% comes from qualifier Jaccard scoring empty-vs-empty pairs as identical (587/750 graphs have no semantic qualifier); realized clinical-content overlap contributes only ~0.3%.
- **Content-channel analysis isolates a real but marginal signal.** On the content channel alone (mean of feature/diagnosis/qualifier Jaccard, empty-vs-empty scored 0): within 0.0021 vs. between 0.0006 — positive in all 15 cells, 7/15 survive Bonferroni, but the within level sits only ~0.12–0.37 floor-SDs above the noise floor and reaches just 0.5% of the content channel's own test-retest ceiling (0.43). A Mantel regression of content similarity on gold-diagnosis embedding similarity gives mean r = 0.118 (~1.4% of variance), positive in all 15 cells, pooled slope +0.0037 (95% CI [+0.0020, +0.0057]). This is the cleanest evidence for *any* residual content signal — real, but far below schema scale.
- **Structured reflection increases within-trace discriminating structure (+33%, more Phase-2 reasoning edges and discriminating motifs, p < 10⁻⁶)**, confirming the pipeline detects genuine cross-condition structural variation. Yet it did *not* improve cross-case consistency: per-model within-cluster similarity fell for 4/5 models (change −0.041 to +0.005, all p > 0.58). Reflection introduces case-specific discriminating analysis that diversifies rather than standardizes the graph.
- **Robustness.** Null holds for both pre-2023 cases (∆ < 0.001) and post-2023 cases (∆ = +0.005), reducing concern about LLM memorization bias.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty — Moderate-to-high.** Process-level, graph-structured evaluation of LLM clinical reasoning is timely and complements accuracy-centric benchmarks (MedQA, MedPaLM, HealthBench) and step-level CoT-faithfulness work (Lanham, Turpin, Basu & Chakraborty). The "competence without consistency" framing is memorable and falsifiable; the orthogonal-dimension claim (0.488 vs 0.484) is a genuinely useful conceptual contribution.
- **Rigor — Strong for a workshop paper, with honest caveats.** Multiple-testing correction (Bonferroni, α=0.0033), permutation tests, bootstrap CIs, Cohen's d, calibration anchors (test-retest, noise floor, inter-extractor), and a component-level decomposition that preempts the "aggregation artifact" objection. The author is unusually transparent that the content-channel metric has test-retest reliability of only 0.43 and explicitly bounds the claim accordingly ("evidence against detectable schemas, not proof that no organizing structure exists").
- **Reproducibility — High.** Released: ontology, extraction pipeline, validation protocol, extracted graphs, pairwise similarity arrays, gold-diagnosis embeddings, case metadata (code: github.com/nisargpatel/clinical-reasoning-graphs); traces released with verbatim NEJM text removed. Preregistered protocol.
- **Significance — Conceptually important, empirically modest.** If findings hold, they meaningfully challenge accuracy-only evaluation in high-stakes clinical AI and provide reusable infrastructure for process-level auditing. However, the practical takeaway ("audit on one case may not generalize to nearby cases") follows more from the null than from any positive capability demonstration.
- **Red flags / concerns.**
  1. *Extractor–model circularity*: GPT-5.4 is both an evaluated model and the extractor; mitigations (inter-extractor 0.593 with Claude Opus 4.7; stats constant across source models) are reasonable but the dependency is intrinsic.
  2. *Single-author + single-physician auditor*: all fidelity judgments (precision/recall, cluster coherence) come from one clinician; no independent multi-annotator agreement is reported.
  3. *Negative-result fragility*: the central null is partly an artifact of a content metric at the noise floor (test-retest 0.43). The paper is commendably explicit about this, but readers must weigh whether the instrument is reliable enough to *conclude* "no schemas."
  4. *Fixed composite weights* (motif 0.35, diagnosis 0.30, …) inherited from the prior study and not re-optimized; the structure-dominated result is therefore partly by construction — the component ablation addresses this but the headline number depends on the chosen weighting.
  5. *Workshop scale*: 50 cases, 5 models, no human-clinician baseline for graph consistency, limiting subgroup power and the ability to claim "unlike experts."
  6. Models named (GPT-5.4, Claude Opus 4.5, Gemini 3 Pro) reflect a mid-2026 frontier snapshot; generalization to future models is open.

## Strengths

- Tackles a genuinely important and under-served problem: evaluating *how* LLMs reason in medicine, not just whether they are right.
- Rigorous statistics (permutation tests + Bonferroni + bootstrap CIs + Cohen's d) and pre-registered, openly described design.
- Excellent calibration hygiene: test-retest, noise floor, and inter-extractor baselines let readers judge whether observed similarities are meaningful.
- Counterintuitive, decision-relevant findings (competence without consistency; accuracy ⟂ graph structure; reflection helps within-trace but not across-case).
- Strong artifact release and clear adaptation path to agentic multi-step trajectories.

## Limitations

- Operationalizes **expressed** reasoning, not internal model cognition; the ontology may miss schema structure clinicians would recognize.
- 50-case corpus limits subgroup power; no consensus human-clinician graph-consistency baseline (so "unlike experts" is hypothesized, not directly shown).
- Single-author / single-physician validation; no multi-annotator inter-rater reliability on graph extraction.
- Composite weighting is fixed and structure-heavy, making the headline null partly structural by design (mitigated but not eliminated by the content-channel analysis).
- Content-channel metric reliability is low (test-retest 0.43), attenuating any true correlation; the strongest defensible claim is "no detectable schemas under this metric," not "no latent structure."
- Scope limited to rare NEJM CPC cases; generalization to routine clinical reasoning is untested.
- Reflection prompting's failure to improve consistency needs follow-up to understand whether different elicitation (e.g., output graphs directly) could recover schemas.

## Relevance

对于关注**LLM评估方法学**与**可信临床AI**的研究而言，本文提供了一种结构化、过程级的评估范式，明确揭示"仅看准确率"会高估临床LLM的可靠性（有能力但缺一致性），其"临床推理图"本体与抽取管线可作为评估、审计与跨模型/跨病例对比的通用基础设施，对LLM评估方法论与可信AI部署都有直接借鉴价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How reliable is the free-text → graph extraction under *independent* multi-annotator review, and how much of the "no-schema" null is attributable to extraction noise vs. true model behavior?
- Would constraining the LLM to **emit reasoning directly in graph format** (rather than extracting post hoc) recover a schema-scale consistency signal?
- Do these findings generalize beyond rare NEJM CPC cases to common, bread-and-butter clinical reasoning, or to longitudinal/multi-turn consultations?
- Could a richer ontology (temporal/causal edges, severity, certainty attributes) and embedding-based soft matching surface latent schemas currently hidden by the 7-edge, label-Jaccard design?
- Why does structured reflection *increase* within-trace discriminating structure yet *decrease* cross-case consistency — and is there an elicitation regime that achieves both?
- Does the "accuracy ⟂ graph structure" decoupling (0.488 vs 0.484) hold for multimodal clinical LLMs that integrate imaging and labs, not just text?
- How does LLM graph consistency compare to a human-expert graph-consistency baseline on the same cases — the key missing comparison for the "unlike experts" claim?
