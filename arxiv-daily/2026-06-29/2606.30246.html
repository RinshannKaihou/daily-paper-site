OVER-CLAIM RISK

Methodology

A typed research graph, four layers, swappable everything

Clarus models the research network as a typed graph of three primitives — Projects, Agents, Resources — joined by a deliberately minimal edge set, where an agent may be an AI system, a human, a team, or an organization. A six-phase Idea→Paper lifecycle decomposes into task DAGs; every subtask emits trace, audit, credit, and evidence records as first-class data.

The architecture splits across four layers — Research Application, Digital Collaboration, Physical Substrate, Physical World — so trust, audit, and credit flow upward while task flow moves downward. Crucially, every variable decision (planning, routing, negotiation, audit, impact update, physical access) is a pluggable, formally-defined strategy, turning Clarus into a testbed rather than a fixed pipeline.

Strengths

What it gets right

  • Genuinely reframes autonomous science as collaboration infrastructure with a clean, minimal, well-formalized ontology.
  • Treats trust, audit, credit attribution, and physical-resource grounding as first-class design requirements.
  • Four-layer separation + pluggable strategies make it a credible testbed for comparing orchestration/audit/credit schemes.
  • Honest framing of “web-scale” as design stress conditions, not current deployment.
  • Strong provenance story: notary chain, credit-aligned authorship, full artifact taxonomy.

Limitations

Where it falls short

  • No quantitative evaluation — no baselines, no success-rate or quality metrics, no ground-truth credit validation.
  • Physical Substrate not implemented — the layer that distinguishes Clarus is only a mock interface.
  • Self-demonstration — the generated paper and its benchmark were produced by the system itself, with no independent validation.
  • “Web-scale” unproven — no federation, no cross-org pilot, no Sybil/collusion resistance or scalability tests.
  • Reproducibility gaps — minimal implementation, LLM, and prompt details; code availability unclear in-body.

Open Questions

Worth probing further

  1. What objective metric proves process-derived credit matches true contribution, and how does it compare to human-authored authorship?
  2. How does each pluggable strategy (LLM- vs rule-based audit; centralized vs hybrid negotiation) quantitatively move output quality and credit stability?
  3. Can the lease + evidence-package protocol integrate real wet-lab / robotic resources while preserving owner control and reproducibility?
  4. What are the empirical failure modes under Sybil, collusion, audit manipulation, and prompt injection — and what governance mitigates them?
  5. Can Clarus become a standardized benchmark testbed for multi-agent research frameworks on attribution, provenance, and cross-project impact?
