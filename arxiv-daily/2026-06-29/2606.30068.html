Predictive Objectives Discard Exogenous Control-Relevant Features: A Controlled Mechanistic Study Pendharkar et al. · arXiv:2606.30068 · cs.LG · 2026-06-29
Key finding

Predictive objectives like JEPA systematically discard features that are exogenous yet reward-relevant — they optimize predictability, not control-relevance.

A 2×2 design with a “predictability knob” isolates the one cell prior distractor work never measured: uncontrollable but reward-deciding. Every reward-free predictive objective leaves it at chance.

Why this matters

Names a structural blind spot of JEPA-style world models on control-relevance, with a cheap 2%-label fix directly relevant to SSL-RL representation work.

Critical edge

All results live in tiny synthetic environments — no Atari, robotics, or real pretrained JEPA models, so the failure’s frequency in natural data is unknown.

01Methodology

2 axes × 4 cells · predictability knob · byte-identical encoder

Two binary axes — controllability (does the agent’s action move the feature?) and relevance (does the feature decide reward and the optimal action a*?) — carve feature space into four cells. The first three are well-trodden; cell 4 (uncontrollable yet reward-relevant) had been named as a risk but never isolated. A predictability knob p_repeat ∈ [0.5,1.0] independently varies how predictable an exogenous feature is while freezing its control-relevance — ruling out “the objective dropped an irrelevant feature.”

Three deliberately tiny synthetic environments (12×12–32×32) run under a byte-identical encoder shared across six objectives plus two references, scored by linear-probe accuracy, an InfoNCE mutual-information lower bound, and effective rank (to separate selective dropping from representational collapse).

03Strengths & Limitations

side-by-side

+ Strengths

  • Genuinely clean causal design — the predictability knob decouples predictability from control-relevance, turning a vague intuition into a measured, orthogonal phenomenon.
  • Names & isolates cell 4, and explains why cell-3 distractor fixes are precisely the ones that also kill cell 4.
  • Three corroborating metrics + an effective-rank check separate selective dropping from collapse (rank stays 38–42).
  • Axis-correct remedy (relevance, not controllability) with concrete numbers: 2%, dims 16–1024, two environments.
  • Exceptional self-awareness about scope (dedicated “what this does not imply” + limitations sections).

− Limitations

  • Confined to small synthetic envs — no Atari, robotics, real pretrained JEPA, or natural data; practical prevalence of cell-4 failures is unknown.
  • Not a benchmark: no task-suite ranking; only 3 seeds (justified but limited); bisimulation comparison is a single-seed estimate.
  • GridWorld’s low intrinsic effective rank (~3) means the anti-collapse check is calibrated for QuadrantEnv and must be read differently there.
  • Objective set is not exhaustive — claim holds for the evaluated reward-free objectives, not every conceivable one.
  • The remedy needs some reward labels — it does not solve the fully reward-free setting it diagnoses.

04Open Questions

for further exploration

1
Does cell-4 failure appear in a frozen V-JEPA 2-AC or DINO-WM encoder on a goal-not-given task — and can a weakly reward-conditioned adapter prevent it?
2
Can a fully reward-free auxiliary objective close the gap, or is reward grounding structurally necessary for cell 4?
3
How does the required labeled fraction scale with environment complexity and the number of simultaneous exogenous-but-relevant features?
4
Could the whitened separation gap (1.893) serve as a general, objective-agnostic diagnostic for control-relevant information loss in trained world models?
5
Is the 2% recovery threshold stable across seeds and p_repeat, or does it degrade as features become only partially predictable?
