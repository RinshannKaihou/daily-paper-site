Extrapolating regularised solves toward nugget = 0 gives a differentiable, tuning-free solver for ill-conditioned linear systems.

Why this matters

A drop-in fix for a real pain point. For GP, kernel-method and Bayesian-ML workflows, brittle non-differentiable nugget selection becomes one stable call — improving both prototyping and end-to-end gradient training.

Critical edge

Proven only on toys. Comparisons are against generic baselines on synthetic kernel Gram matrices plus one toy GP task — no real-world, large-scale, or structure-exploiting benchmarks. "Out-performs" is scoped to prototyping.

Methodology — how it works

Solving Ax=b for symmetric positive-definite but severely ill-conditioned A — the regime behind Gaussian processes, Bayesian optimisation and probabilistic numerics — practitioners add a Tikhonov nugget x_σ=(A+σI)⁻¹b and hope σ→0. autonugget turns that hope into a procedure.

It performs n∈{2,3,4} Tikhonov solves on a geometric nugget grid, then extrapolates per-coordinate toward σ=0 with a Richardson polynomial. Cost stays within a constant factor of a single direct solve and parallelises to O(1) wall-clock. Two theorems frame the trade-off: Theorem 1 bounds extrapolation error at rate O(hⁿ); Theorem 2 bounds finite-precision amplification via the Lebesgue constant and condition number. Equating them yields an automatic scale h⋆ (≈ ε_MP^{1/(n+1)} near singularity), with the critical λ_min(A) estimated stochastically.

A custom JAX forward-differentiation rule factors ∇_θ x_θ into three independent protected solves, so gradients inherit the same stability — and the API is literally autonugget(A,b) replacing np.linalg.solve.

中文一句话：对若干不同 nugget 的 Tikhonov 解做 Richardson 多项式外推至 σ→0，自动选 nugget 并兼容 JAX 自动微分，在求解与导数精度上均优于单一 nugget 的常规做法。

Strengths vs. Limitations

Strengths

  • Solves a widely-felt pain point (brittle, non-differentiable nugget choice) with a clean drop-in API.
  • Theoretically grounded: error bounds justify both the extrapolation and the automatic nugget pick.
  • Full JAX compatibility via a custom forward-diff rule — strong for kernel / GP workflows.
  • Intellectually honest about novelty and scope; separates "regularisation" from "ill-conditioning" roles of the nugget.
  • Low marginal cost (a few direct solves), parallelisable, robust defaults in experiments.

Limitations

  • Restricted to symmetric positive-definite matrices and direct solvers; no non-symmetric / large-scale iterative regimes.
  • Accuracy hinges on a stochastic minimum-eigenvalue estimate that can mis-identify the critical σ⋆.
  • Higher-order extrapolation (n>2) gives little benefit and can amplify noise — capped near linear in practice.
  • Evaluation is synthetic kernel Gram matrices plus one small toy GP problem; no real-world benchmark.
  • Compared only against generic baselines; not positioned vs. modern structure-exploiting kernel solvers.

Open questions

  1. How sensitive is the automatic h⋆ (hence σ⋆) to errors in the stochastic λ_min(A) estimate — can a poor estimate drive extrapolation over unreliable solves?
  2. Can the approach extend to non-symmetric or highly non-normal systems, where conditioning and eigenstructure behave differently?
  3. How does autonugget compare — in accuracy and wall-clock — against structure-exploiting solvers (HODLR, inducing-point, stochastic-trace) rather than generic baselines?
  4. For very large d, do multiple direct solves plus eigenvalue estimation stay competitive with preconditioned CG or iterative refinement?
  5. Could the custom differentiation rule generalise beyond SPD A_θ to settings where A depends nonlinearly on parameters?
  6. What is the practical ceiling on extrapolation order n before numerical-error amplification dominates — is there an adaptive per-problem choice?
