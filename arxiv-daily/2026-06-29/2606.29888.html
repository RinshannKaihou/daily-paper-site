Same Concept, Different Directions: Cross-Modal Feature Heterogeneity in Sparse Autoencoders

Same concepts occupy different feature directions across image and text in VLM sparse autoencoders — so train them apart, then align latent coordinates by permutation.

Why this matters

Decouples cross-modal alignment from reconstruction — a cheap, adoptable recipe for probing and steering the internal structure of multimodal models.

Critical edge

No raw-CLIP retrieval baseline. Absolute R@1 of 16.0 is low for CLIP ViT-B/32 — so it's unclear whether the method is genuinely good or merely least-bad among SAEs.

— Critical Assessment, red flag #1
01

Methodology

Same concept, two directions. Even pairs with high coactivation correlation (c ≥ 0.8) keep a cosine distance centred well above zero — directly contradicting the assumed ϕᵢ = ψᵢ.

The authors model VLM image and text embeddings as sparse linear combinations of monosemantic feature directions — but, unlike prior joint-embedding SAE work, they do not impose ϕᵢ = ψᵢ. The same latent code can be carried by different decoder columns per modality.

They then prove the split is unavoidable: Corollary 1 shows reconstruction alone pushes a distinct direction into a distinct coordinate, while Theorem 1 shows limited capacity collapses distinct directions into shared columns. Propositions 1–2 go further — the two existing auxiliary-loss remedies (group-sparse, Iso-Energy) can only align features by collapsing them into one direction, which breaks reconstruction past a critical λ.

The fix is deliberately cheap: train one SAE per modality (preserving each geometry), then solve a Hungarian assignment over the coactivation-correlation matrix to permute — not rotate — the text SAE's latent indices onto the image SAE's. A permutation only reindexes coordinates, so reconstruction is provably preserved and alignment is gained for free.

Corollary 1 Theorem 1 Prop. 1–2 — closed-form account of why split happens & why auxiliary losses fail.

03

Strengths & limitations

Strengths

  • Clean, well-motivated core idea — names and tests an assumption everyone quietly made.
  • Theory genuinely predicts the empirical trade-offs (Props. 1–2 map onto Fig. 6's curves).
  • Constructive: offers a simple, cheap fix (permutation alignment), not just a diagnosis.
  • Honest reporting — explicitly "competitive," not best, on zero-shot classification.
  • Multi-axis evaluation: reconstruction, retrieval, steering, monosemanticity.

Limitations

  • Full method validated on one VLM (CLIP ViT-B/32); generalization rests on appendix.
  • No code/data release stated; key hyperparameters deferred to appendix.
  • No no-SAE embedding baseline — absolute (not relative) performance is hard to judge.
  • Coactivation-correlation proxy is heuristic; misalignments silently degrade matching.
  • Hungarian is one-to-one — no counterpart or many-to-one concepts are unsupported.
04

Open questions for further work

Q1 · scaling

Is heterogeneity intrinsic, or a training artifact that shrinks with model size, data composition, or a weaker modality gap?

Q2 · transfer

Is the Hungarian permutation stable and transferable across VLM families, or must it be re-fit per backbone and dictionary?

Q3 · ceiling

Would a no-SAE / raw-embedding baseline confirm SAE compression is the main ceiling on absolute retrieval scores?

Q4 · training signal

Can heterogeneity itself become a regularizer that nudges VLMs toward natively aligned joint embeddings?

Q5 · many-to-one

How does the method behave for concepts with no cross-modal counterpart, or mappings a one-to-one permutation can't express?

Q6 · downstream

Does reducing heterogeneity improve real downstream tasks (fine-tuning, generation), or only interpretability & steering?
