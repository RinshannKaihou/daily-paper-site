Curvature-Weighted Gradient Diversity: A Noise Measure for Geometry-Adaptive SGD Schedules

The key finding

Weighting gradient noise by curvature — H⁻¹ᐟ² — proves a 2× lower asymptotic floor for cosine SGD schedules on convex quadratics.

A principled, geometry-aware noise signal for adaptive SGD schedules — a useful theoretical baseline, though current gains are confined to synthetic convex quadratics.

The ~20% gain holds only on d=50 synthetic quadratics; on the only realistic non-convex MLP tests, Hessian staleness makes the method degrade performance by 2–14%.

01

How CWGD reshapes the noise floor

SGD's standard convergence floor, ησ²/(2µ), charges the full gradient variance at the smallest eigenvalue — no matter where the noise actually sits in the spectrum. But a high-curvature direction (large λₖ) already forces a tiny step η ≤ 1/L, so its noise-induced wobble, of order η²σ²/λₖ², is small precisely because λₖ is large. Standard bounds ignore this.

CWGD exploits it. It rescales per-sample gradient diversity within the mini-batch by H⁻¹ᐟ², down-weighting stiff directions and yielding a tighter "effective noise" σ²_eff = σ²·tr(H⁻¹)/d — up to ρ(κ) ≈ κ/log κ tighter than the usual σ²/µ. Plugged into a cosine schedule as ηₜ = η̄/(1 + α·rₜ) with rₜ = CWGDₜ/CWGD₀, this provably shrinks the floor by 1/(1+α) — exactly 2× at the recommended α=1 (Theorem 4.1).

A Hutchinson diagonal estimator supplies H via Rademacher probes at <10⁻⁸ relative error on quadratics — at a cost of ~20 extra gradient evaluations (~40% overhead) per step. An early "normalize-variance-by-variance" estimator was caught collapsing to the uninformative constant d and replaced.

Theory → practice gap

Bars scaled to the aspirational ρ(κ) ≈ κ/log κ ceiling. The proved 2× guarantee is never reached empirically; the observed 1.25× is roughly the asymptotic-vs-finite-T shortfall the authors flag but do not close.

03

Strengths vs. Limitations

Strengths

  • Principled reformulation. Clean closed-form link E[CWGD]=2σ²tr(H⁻¹) to the standard σ²/µ bound — geometry-aware, not ad hoc.
  • Tight theorem. Self-contained convergence guarantee with an explicit 1/(1+α) floor reduction (2× at α=1).
  • Exceptional honesty. Explicit non-claims, a self-detected degenerate estimator, stress tests of failure modes, and quantification of the theory-practice gap.
  • Statistically rigorous. 20 seeds, paired t-tests, and a compute-normalized fairness check — not cherry-picked.
  • Reusable signal. Negligible per-step overhead; usable as a landscape diagnostic beyond LR scheduling.

Limitations

  • Net-negative where it matters. Hessian staleness causes 2–14% degradation on non-convex MLPs — the only realistic test.
  • Theory-practice gap. The proved 2× is asymptotic; only ~1.25× (20%) appears at finite T; aspirational ρ(κ)=2.0–3.9× is further off.
  • Narrow scope. Positive results restricted to strongly-convex diagonal quadratics with isotropic/aligned noise.
  • Hard failures. Breaks at extreme curvature (κ=500) and on pathological non-convexity (Rosenbrock).
  • Unverified scale. Only d=50 tested; behavior at d=10³–10⁴ unknown, where diagonal-estimator error may accumulate.
  • Real cost. ~40% compute overhead only partly justified by controlled gains on tiny problems.
04

Open questions to test

  1. Can an online EMA curvature proxy kill Hessian staleness? An Adam-style running estimate of diag(H) might restore CWGD's benefit on non-convex deep models — the make-or-break test for practical relevance.
  2. Does a full-matrix / Kronecker Hutchinson estimator rescue coupled landscapes? The Rosenbrock failure is blamed on a static diagonal proxy; does paying for off-diagonal curvature recover the gain?
  3. Can a full-trajectory analysis close the 2× → 1.25× gap? The proved floor is asymptotic; a finite-T bound might explain the shortfall — or push toward the aspirational ρ(κ) ≈ κ/log κ.
  4. How does it scale to d=10³–10⁴? Diagonal-estimator error accumulates with dimension; does the <10⁻⁸ accuracy survive, or does the signal wash out?
  5. Is CWGD better as a diagnostic than a scheduler? Repurposed for spike detection / warm-restart triggers, the convergence theory need not apply — possibly the most practical use.
