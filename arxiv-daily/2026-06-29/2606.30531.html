The core finding

The right tool, the wrong target: agents bind a correct tool call to the wrong real-world entity 24–26% of the time.

24–26%

Action-oriented methods act on the wrong entity despite 0% wrong-tool error — entity-aware gating drives it to 0.0%, with 0% over-clarification.

Why this matters

Wrong-target harms — wrong recipient, wrong account, wrong deletion — are exactly what blocks enterprise agent deployment, yet no mainstream tool-use benchmark measures them.

⚠ Critical edge

Baselines are forced into direct execution while entity-aware methods may defer — so the 26%-vs-0% gap overstates what a fair, clarification-enabled baseline comparison would show.

Methodology, distilled

Every executed agent action is decomposed as ⟨tool, entity-bindings, other-args⟩. The authors formally split two correctness axes — did the agent pick the right tool, and did it bind that tool to the right real-world entity? An entity binding failure is precisely the conjunction: right tool, wrong target (e.g. the correct send_email dispatched to the wrong "Alex"). They taxonomize 8 enterprise ambiguity conditions — name collisions, document versions, temporal, account conflicts, near-duplicates, cross-system links, true ambiguity — across 4 risk tiers from read-only up to delete / cancel.

Their fix, the entity-aware action gate, sits between tool choice and execution. It enforces per-tool entity preconditions, retrieves a recall-oriented candidate set, and binds the top candidate only when both an absolute threshold s ≥ τ and a margin over the runner-up s − s₂ ≥ δ clear. Otherwise it returns a metadata-grounded clarification ("Alex Chen from launch vs. Alex Kumar from CS") and logs provenance for audit. The same idea is instantiated as an entity-aware extension of Causal Minimal Tool Filtering (CMTF).

Strengths & limitations

▲ Strengths

  • "Right tool, wrong target" framing is immediately intuitive and genuinely under-measured by existing benchmarks.
  • Clean formal decomposition (tool vs. entity correctness, multi-entity, ambiguity, risk-weighting) that others could adopt directly.
  • Honest about the safety↔completion tradeoff — no free-lunch overclaim.
  • Public artifacts (code, benchmark, prompts) plus a reusable ambiguity/risk taxonomy.
  • 0% over-clarification: gating adds no friction to clear, unambiguous requests.

▼ Limitations

  • Only 60 tasks; excludes payments, infra, medical, legal, security — single-step only.
  • Asymmetric comparison: baselines lack clarification rules, inflating the 26%-vs-0% gap.
  • Gating via structured prompting, not a calibrated scorer; τ/δ thresholds may be testbed-tuned.
  • Steep cost — completion drops ~75% → 26–40%; possibly over-deferring.
  • No human study on whether clarifications are actually understandable or low-burden.
  • Heavy same-month self-citation (4/10 core refs); method is incremental over prior CMTF.
04

Open questions

How much of the 26–40% completion drop is avoidable with learned or adaptive τ/δ thresholds instead of fixed values?
Would a simple clarification rule for baselines close most of the 0%-vs-26% gap, isolating the gate's unique value?
How do entity binding failures compound across multi-step workflows (early wrong binding → downstream errors)?
Do frontier models with stronger built-in entity resolution still hit 24–26%, or does the effect shrink with scale?
Can provenance records drive online correction — an agent re-grounding after a failed low-risk probe?
Do the 8 ambiguity categories and 4 risk levels transfer to higher-stakes domains (payments, medical, security)?
