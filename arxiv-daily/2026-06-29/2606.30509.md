> **一句话总结：** 本文从理论上分析 Muon 优化器在矩阵分解问题上的动力学，揭示其相比梯度下降避免了缓慢的鞍点到鞍点学习、对学习率鲁棒（不受问题条件数限制）、并守恒不同的矩阵不变量（核范数差而非平方范数差），据此构造出仅需两步即可完成对齐的"尖峰学习率"调度。

> **TL;DR:** This paper theoretically dissects the Muon optimizer (spectral/polar-decomposition-based gradient updates) on matrix factorization, identifying three dynamical differences from gradient descent — uniform singular-value growth that avoids saddle-to-saddle dynamics, stability beyond the sharpness-based critical learning rate, and conservation of $\sqrt{P^\top P} - \sqrt{Q^\top Q}$ (nuclear-norm difference) instead of $P^\top P - Q^\top Q$ — and engineers a "spiked" learning-rate schedule that aligns weights to the target in just two optimization steps.

## 核心贡献

- 系统刻画了 Muon（无动量版，极分解正交化梯度）在矩阵分解 $\min_{P,Q}\|M^\star - PQ^\top\|_F^2$ 上的参数动力学，与梯度下降形成清晰、可对照的三重差异。
- 证明 Muon 避免了小初始化下缓慢的"鞍点到鞍点"动力学：因为更新幅度固定为 $\eta$，所有奇异值以相同速率线性增长（小的先收敛），而 GD 由于乘性动力学呈现阶梯式逐模式学习。
- 揭示 Muon 在学习率超过尖锐度临界阈值 $2/\lambda_{\max}$ 时仍稳定——更新幅度恒定无法发散——从而把学习率与条件数解耦，支持指数级学习率退火实现快速线性收敛。
- 指出对齐后 Muon 流守恒 $\Delta_1 := \sqrt{P^\top P} - \sqrt{Q^\top Q}$（等价于核范数差 $\|P\|_* - \|Q\|_*$），且该流是 Hamiltonian 流（$H(P,Q)=\mathrm{Tr}\sqrt{\Delta_1}$，Noether/经典力学视角）；而梯度流守恒 $P^\top P - Q^\top Q$ 且流线为双曲型。两者守恒量不同，但都能从衰减初始化收敛到平衡解。
- 在最小化"外积分解"和"内积分解"两个玩具问题上推导出对齐速率：欠参数下目标对齐按 $(t/\alpha_{\text{eff}})^{-2(1-s^{-1})}$ 衰减（$s$ 为目标谱间隙），过参数下内部对齐按 $(t/\alpha_{\text{eff}})^{-4}$ 衰减，其中 $\alpha_{\text{eff}}=\max(\eta,\alpha)$，理论与一般情形实验高度吻合。
- 利用 Muon 的结构性质构造出"尖峰学习率调度"：第一步用 $\eta^{(0)}=\alpha$ 使 $P,Q$ 一步对齐，第二步用大尖峰 $\eta^{(1)}\gg\eta^{(0)}$ 同时对齐目标并推向解流形，随后几何退火实现线性收敛——概念验证性地展示理论可指导实践。

## Key Contributions

- Provides a systematic, mechanistic characterization of the (momentum-free, polar-orthogonalized) Muon optimizer on matrix factorization, organized around three crisp contrasts with gradient descent.
- **Dynamics #1 (Growth):** Because the Muon update magnitude is fixed at $\eta$, every singular value grows uniformly and linearly; there are no intermediate saddles, so the slow saddle-to-saddle staircase of GD is avoided and smaller modes converge first.
- **Dynamics #2 (Stability):** Muon cannot diverge since its update is bounded; it remains stable even when $\eta$ exceeds GD's critical threshold $2/\lambda_{\max}(\nabla^2 L)$, decoupling the usable learning rate from the problem's condition number and enabling rapid convergence via exponential (geometric) annealing.
- **Dynamics #3 (Conservation):** Once weights are aligned, Muon flow conserves $\sqrt{P^\top P} - \sqrt{Q^\top Q}$ and is a Hamiltonian flow with $H(P,Q)=\|P\|_* - \|Q\|_*$, giving affine/parallel flow lines; gradient flow instead conserves $P^\top P - Q^\top Q$ with hyperbolic geometry. Both nonetheless reach the balanced solution from vanishing initialization.
- **Alignment-rate theory:** In under-parameterized regimes target misalignment decays as $(t/\alpha_{\text{eff}})^{-2(1-s^{-1})}$ (derived on the tied-weight outer-product problem with target spectral gap $s$), while in over-parameterized regimes internal misalignment decays as $(t/\alpha_{\text{eff}})^{-4}$ (derived on the inner-product problem via Noether's angular-momentum invariant); both match general-case empirics, with $\alpha_{\text{eff}}:=\max(\eta,\alpha)$.
- **Practical payoff (spiked LR schedule):** A three-stage schedule — a tiny initial step $\eta^{(0)}=\alpha$ aligning $P$ with $Q$ in one step, a large spike $\eta^{(1)}\gg\eta^{(0)}$ that aligns the model with the target and lands on the solution manifold, then geometric annealing by $1/2$ — achieves near-perfect alignment in just two optimization steps (proof-of-concept, exactly/over-parameterized regime).

## Methodology

The authors study the matrix factorization objective $L(P,Q)=\tfrac12\|M^\star - PQ^\top\|_F^2$ for a square target $M^\star\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ and weights $P,Q\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times d}$, updated by the momentum-free Muon rule $\Delta P = -\eta\,\widehat{\nabla_P L}$, $\Delta Q = -\eta\,\widehat{\nabla_Q L}$, where the hat denotes polar orthogonalization (matrix sign) of the gradient via its SVD $G=USV^\top\mapsto UV^\top$. Initialization is isotropic Gaussian scaled by $\alpha^2/\max(n,d)$ so the relative update size depends only on $\alpha$ and $\eta$, not on dimensions (a Frobenius-norm convention consistent with large-scale Muon practice, though noted as inconsistent with µP's spectral-norm scaling).

The analysis proceeds by **decoupling the dynamics into an alignment phase and a post-alignment growth phase**, and rotating into the SVD basis of $M^\star$ so the residual is diagonal.

1. **Post-alignment dynamics (Sec. 3):** With weights aligned, the problem splits into $n$ independent scalar problems $L(p,q)=\tfrac12(m^\star-pq)^2$ with Muon updates $\Delta p=\eta\,\mathrm{sign}(r)$, $\Delta q=\eta\,\mathrm{sign}(r)$. The authors show (a) the update magnitude is fixed → uniform growth; (b) "effective sharpness" is zero a.e. → all learning rates are stable, though constant-$\eta$ iterates oscillate across the solution manifold and require annealing (geometric $1/2$ annealing gives linear convergence, proven by induction: $|p^{(t)}-\sqrt{m^\star}|<\eta^{(t-1)}$); (c) $p-q$ is conserved in the flow limit, and the matrix generalization $\sqrt{P^\top P}-\sqrt{Q^\top Q}$ is an invariant generated by the Hamiltonian $H(P,Q)=\|P\|_*-\|Q\|_*$, with parallel flow lines (vs. GD's hyperbolic $p^2-q^2$ invariant).
2. **Alignment dynamics (Sec. 4):** A principled alignment metric $a\in[0,1]$ based on shared subspace overlap (averaged squared cosine similarity, robust to degenerate spectra) is defined for internal ($V_P$ vs $V_Q$), left-target ($U_{\text{mod}}$ vs $U^\star$), and right-target alignment. Closed-form rates are derived on two toy problems:
   - *Under-parameterized* (outer-product factorization $L(p)=\tfrac12\|\mathrm{diag}(s,1)-pp^\top\|^2$): Muon flow $\dot p \approx M^\star p/\|M^\star p\|$ reduces to power iteration, giving target misalignment $\sim(t/\alpha_{\text{eff}})^{-2(1-s^{-1})}$.
   - *Over-parameterized* (inner-product factorization $L(p,q)=\tfrac12(m^\star-p^\top q)^2$): Hamilton's equations yield a conserved angular momentum $L^2=x^4\varepsilon$, integrating to internal misalignment $\sim(t/\alpha_{\text{eff}})^{-4}$.
3. **Spiked schedule (Sec. 5):** For $d\ge n$ with small orthogonal init, $\widehat{\nabla L}\approx \pm Q/\alpha$ initially; setting $\eta^{(0)}=\alpha$ gives $P^{(1)}\approx Q^{(1)}$ (one-step internal alignment). A large spike $\eta^{(1)}$ then makes $P^{(2)}\approx\eta^{(1)}\widehat{M^\star P^{(1)}}\approx Q^{(2)}$ with mutually orthogonal rows, achieving target alignment; geometric annealing thereafter yields linear convergence.

Throughout, theory is matched against numerical experiments on targets with planted low-rank-signal-in-noise structure $M^\star = G+\lambda UV^\top$.

## Results & Highlights

- **No saddle-to-saddle staircase (Fig. 1):** GD singular values escape the origin one at a time (multiplicative growth, stepwise loss); Muon grows all singular values uniformly by $\eta$ per step with no intermediate saddles, so smaller modes converge first.
- **Condition-number-free stability (Fig. 1):** GD diverges for $\eta>2/\lambda_{\max}$; Muon's bounded update makes it stable at any learning rate (it just oscillates faster around the solution manifold until annealed), so convergence is not bottlenecked by the condition number.
- **Distinct invariant, same balanced solution:** Muon conserves $\sqrt{P^\top P}-\sqrt{Q^\top Q}$ (affine/parallel flow lines, Hamiltonian); GD conserves $P^\top P-Q^\top Q$ (hyperbolic flow lines). Both reach the balanced solution $\{P,Q:PQ^\top=\mathrm{top}_d(M^\star),\, P^\top P=Q^\top Q\}$.
- **Alignment-rate predictions (Fig. 3):** Target misalignment decays polynomially with exponent $\ge -2$ (slowing as the target spectral gap closes $s\to1^+$); internal misalignment decays as $(t/\alpha_{\text{eff}})^{-4}$. Alignment occurs earlier with smaller effective initialization scale $\alpha_{\text{eff}}=\max(\eta,\alpha)$, and the closed-form toy-problem rates predict general empirics well across aspect ratios $d/n\in\{0.25,1,4\}$ ($n=128$).
- **Two-step alignment schedule (Fig. 4):** In the exactly-parameterized setting ($n=d=25$, orthogonal init at $\alpha=10^{-4}$), $\eta^{(0)}=\alpha$ aligns $P,Q$ in one step, $\eta^{(1)}=\sqrt{s^\star_{\max}/2}\approx0.707$ aligns the model to the target in the second step, and $1/2$ geometric annealing yields rapid linear convergence.
- **Caveats observed:** constant-$\eta$ Muon oscillates forever around the solution manifold (annealing is necessary); and "oscillation traps" near the origin can occur if weights are catastrophically misaligned with too-large $\eta$, avoidable by deeper flow regime (small $\eta/\alpha$) or heavy over-parameterization.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (Moderate-High):** The three-way contrast (uniform growth, sharpness-free stability, nuclear-norm conservation law with a Hamiltonian interpretation) is a genuinely new and clean mechanistic synthesis for Muon on this problem. However, the uniform-growth phenomenon and condition-number-independent linear convergence were identified concurrently by Ma et al. (2026) and Kang et al. (2026); this paper's distinctive value is the alignment-phase analysis and the conserved-quantity/Hamiltonian lens rather than a brand-new empirical phenomenon.
- **Rigor (Moderate):** The paper is explicit and honest that its derivations are scaling/flow-limit arguments ("less rigorous, but intuitive") rather than the formal convergence proofs of the concurrent works. The scalar-problem reductions are clean, but several matrix-generalization steps and the spiked-schedule argument rest on approximations (e.g., $M^\star \gg PQ^\top$, small orthogonal init) that are validated empirically rather than proven in full generality. The induction-based proof of linear convergence under geometric annealing is a solid, self-contained result.
- **Reproducibility (High):** Code is public; the setup (diagonal $M^\star$, Gaussian init, planted-signal targets) is standard and cheap to reproduce; experimental details are deferred to an appendix but the main figures specify $n,d,\alpha$.
- **Significance (Moderate):** Strong as theory of a hot optimizer on a canonical toy model; practical significance is limited because matrix factorization is far from real LLM training and the analysis deliberately drops momentum, weight decay, and Newton-Schulz iteration — the very ingredients used in practice. The spiked schedule is explicitly a proof-of-concept, not a recommended recipe.
- **Red flags:** None severe. The main intellectual caveat is scope: the studied "Muon" is the idealized momentum-free polar-descent update, not the production Muon optimizer, so the title's claims should be read as applying to this idealization. No overclaiming of real-world LLM gains; limitations are stated openly.

## Strengths

- Exceptionally clear, well-organized exposition: the three-dynamical-differences framing (Fig. 1) and the GD-vs-Muon flow-line pictures (Fig. 2) make the core intuition immediately graspable.
- Mechanistic depth: links Muon dynamics to classical mechanics (Hamiltonian flow, Noether's theorem, conserved angular momentum) in a way that explains *why* alignment and conservation happen, not just *that* they do.
- Honest positioning relative to concurrent rigorous work — complements rather than competes.
- Alignment-rate theory that genuinely predicts empirical behavior across parameterization regimes, plus a constructive, surprising payoff (two-step alignment schedule).
- Open code and self-contained toy-problem derivations.

## Limitations

- **Scope of "Muon":** Analyzes only the momentum-free, no-weight-decay, no-Newton-Schulz spectral gradient update — the practical Muon optimizer used in LLM training adds all three, so direct applicability to production training is unproven.
- **Toy model only:** Matrix factorization lacks the depth, nonlinearity, and data-dependent structure of real networks; transfer of the three dynamical differences to deep nets is asserted by analogy, not demonstrated.
- **Less rigorous than concurrent work:** Flow-limit and scaling arguments, not formal theorems; matrix generalizations and the spiked schedule rely on approximations validated only empirically.
- **Schedule is a proof-of-concept:** The authors themselves caution it is "not advisable in general settings."
- **Initialization assumptions:** Cleanest results assume small (semi-)orthogonal initialization and square $M^\star$; the orthogonal-init assumption for the spiked schedule is somewhat special.
- **Oscillation/trap phenomena** (constant-$\eta$ non-convergence, origin oscillation traps) mean Muon is not a drop-in "always faster" optimizer — annealing and regime care are required.

## Relevance

与深度学习优化器理论（尤其 Muon 这类基于谱/极分解的新兴优化器）和表示学习动力学高度相关。它用最简洁的矩阵分解模型揭示了 Muon"拉平更新谱"带来的隐式偏差与守恒律，对理解现代大模型训练中优化器的收敛行为、学习率调度设计以及条件数鲁棒性有直接的理论参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Do the three dynamical differences (uniform growth, sharpness-free stability, nuclear-norm conservation) survive when momentum, weight decay, and Newton-Schulz approximation are added back — i.e., for the *production* Muon optimizer?
- Can the alignment-rate theory (especially the $(t/\alpha_{\text{eff}})^{-4}$ internal-alignment law) be extended to deep linear networks or even single nonlinear layers, where "silent alignment" is known to matter?
- Is there a principled, generally-advisable analog of the spiked schedule for realistic (non-orthogonal, data-dependent) initializations, or does the trick collapse outside the clean regime?
- Does the Hamiltonian / nuclear-norm conservation law imply any new inductive bias or generalization guarantee for solutions found by Muon vs GD?
- How does the origin "oscillation trap" interact with stochasticity, minibatching, and warmup in practice — could it explain any observed Muon instability early in training?
