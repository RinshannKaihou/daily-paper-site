> **一句话总结：** 本文将循环 Transformer（Looped Transformer）在算法任务上的长度外推脆弱性归因于"序列长度与训练循环步数"之间的虚假相关——它制造了一个训练时的轨迹选择问题，并证明在训练时对循环步数引入随机性（含基于 REINFORCE 的学习式调度 RL-Halting）能大幅降低 OOD 方差、稳定不同推理循环数下的预测；但稳定性并不等同于最优外推，RL-Halting 有时会锁死次优计算。

> **TL;DR:** The paper traces the brittle length generalization of Looped Transformers to a training-time selection problem created by the spurious correlation between sequence length and number of training loops, and shows that randomizing the loop depth during training (including a REINFORCE-based learned schedule, RL-Halting) sharply reduces out-of-distribution variance and stabilizes predictions across inference-time loop counts—though low variance and best extrapolation remain distinct, sometimes conflicting objectives.

## 核心贡献

- **诊断外推-稳定性鸿沟**：通过"oracle-over-iterations"诊断指标证明，确定性循环 Transformer 确实"内含"可外推的计算（某些 run 在远超训练长度时仍准确），但梯度训练并不能可靠地选中它——run 间 OOD 行为差异极大（有的外推、有的刚出训练区间即崩溃）。
- **定位根因——循环步数 vs. 长度的虚假相关**：将不稳定性归因于训练时的"轨迹选择问题"：循环次数不仅是算力预算，更决定了被选中的计算轨迹。确定性调度只在单一深度施加监督，使学到的计算对该深度脆弱。
- **训练时随机停止稳定循环模型**：从长度匹配调度 K=n 出发，在邻域窗口内采样 K=clip(n+Δ,1,Tmax)（Δ∼Unif{-w,...,w}），同时降低 run 间 OOD 方差、降低对推理循环数的敏感性（更宽的高精度区间、更低的"预测翻转率"）。
- **学习式随机调度 RL-Halting**：用轻量停止头参数化停止分布 πstop,ϕ(τ|x)，每步采样一个停止深度 τ，以负任务损失为奖励 R=-ℓ，用 REINFORCE（移动平均基线 + 熵正则）训练停止策略，把"何时停"从手工窗口变成损失驱动的学习量。
- **诚实揭示局限——稳定≠最优**：在二进制加法、Dyck-1、Unique Set 上 RL-Halting 同时取得最高 OOD 准确率与最低方差；但在 Copy 任务上它把模型稳定在了一个次优计算上（OOD 仅 43.2，劣于 K=n 的 68.7）。
- **概念重新定位**：明确主张"何时停止"对循环架构而言是核心的训练时设计选择，而不仅是推理时的算力分配规则。

## Key Contributions

- **Diagnosing the extrapolation–stability gap**: Using an "oracle-over-iterations" metric, the authors show deterministic Looped Transformers *contain* extrapolating computations (some runs stay accurate far beyond training lengths), but gradient training does not reliably *select* them—run-to-run OOD behaviour varies qualitatively (some extrapolate, others collapse at the training boundary).
- **Root cause — a training-time selection problem**: Instability stems from the spurious correlation between sequence length and the number of training loops. The loop count is not just a compute budget; it determines which computation trajectory is selected. A deterministic schedule supervises only one depth, making the learned computation brittle to that choice.
- **Stabilization via stochastic schedules**: Starting from length-matched K=n, sampling K=clip(n+Δ,1,Tmax) with Δ∼Unif{-w,...,w} reduces run-to-run OOD variance *and* inference-time loop-count sensitivity (wider high-accuracy region, lower prediction flip rate between adjacent loop counts).
- **RL-Halting — a learned stochastic stopping rule**: A lightweight stopping head parameterizes π_stop,ϕ(τ|x); each step samples one stopping depth τ, supervises only that depth, and trains the policy with REINFORCE using reward R=-ℓ, a moving-average baseline, and an entropy regularizer. This turns the hand-designed window into a loss-driven, length-dependent (but non-degenerate) learned distribution.
- **Honest limitation — stability ≠ best extrapolation**: RL-Halting achieves the best OOD accuracy *and* lowest variance on binary addition, Dyck-1, and Unique Set; but on Copy it stabilizes a *suboptimal* computation (OOD 43.2 vs. 68.7 for K=n), demonstrating low variance and high extrapolation are distinct objectives.
- **Conceptual reframing**: "When to stop" is argued to be a central *training-time design choice* for looped architectures, not merely an inference-time compute-allocation rule.

## Methodology

**Architecture.** A Looped Transformer reuses a single shared 3-layer Transformer block across iterations (fixed parameter count, variable depth), following Fan et al. (2024). Hidden states evolve as **H^(t) = Block_θ(H^(t-1) + H^(0))** for t=1..K, where H^(0)=E(x) is the token embedding; the addition of H^(0) implements *input injection* (the original input is reintroduced each iteration). After K loops a readout map produces token logits, trained with standard token-level cross-entropy on supervised positions.

**Stopping schedules.** The loop count is treated as K ∼ π_stop(K|x), yielding the schedule-induced objective **L_π(θ;x,y) = E_{K∼π_stop}[ ℓ(θ;x,y,K) ]**. Each SGD step samples one depth per example (an unbiased estimate of the depth-averaged objective). Three families are compared:
- *Deterministic*: fixed K=20 (latent-reasoning style) and length-matched K=n.
- *Hand-designed stochastic*: K=clip(n+Δ,1,Tmax), Δ∼Unif{-w,...,w} (windows ±1/±5/±10).
- *Learned stochastic (RL-Halting)*: a stopping head parameterizes π_stop,ϕ(τ|x); per step one depth is sampled, supervised, and the policy is updated with REINFORCE, (R-b)∇_ϕ log π_stop,ϕ(τ|x), reward R=-ℓ, moving-average baseline b, plus an entropy regularizer for exploration. Compared against a PonderNet-style baseline (Appx. C.5).

**Tasks & length-generalization protocol.** Models train on lengths n<20 (in-distribution, ID) and are evaluated on n≥20 (OOD), with OOD summaries over {20,25,30,...,60}. Tasks: **binary addition** (main diagnostic; n=#digits per operand, e.g. 1011+0110→10001<eos>), **Dyck-1** (nested formal language), **Unique Set** (first-appearance deduplication, [a,b,a,c,b]→[a,b,c,<eos>]), and **Copy**. A prediction is correct only if the *entire* output sequence (incl. <eos>) matches.

**Baselines.** A 3-layer fixed-depth Transformer (parameter-matched to one looped block) and a 60-layer Transformer (higher-compute baseline).

**Metrics.** Primary diagnostic is **oracle-over-iterations accuracy**: Acc_oracle(n) = E_{(x,y)}[ max_{K∈K_eval} acc(ŷ_K(x), y) ] — uses ground truth to pick the best depth, so it diagnoses *whether* an extrapolating computation exists separately from whether a stopping rule finds it. Summary metrics: **OOD** (avg oracle accuracy over lengths 20–60), **Front.@90** (largest OOD length where mean accuracy ≥90%), **Std.** (std across runs of per-run average OOD accuracy).

**Theoretical explanation (Section 5).** Two informal mechanisms, with formal toy statements in Appxs. D & E:
1. *Gradient averaging*: with G_k=∇L_k and each gradient written as G_k = Ḡ + ξ_k (shared + depth-specific residual), E_K[G_K] = ∇J_w, so sampling over a local window averages out weakly-aligned residuals, pushing updates toward the shared direction Ḡ — explains reduced run-to-run spread (does *not* guarantee better extrapolation).
2. *Loop-trajectory consistency*: supervising the same input at multiple nearby depths asks the model for compatible answers across a depth window, lowering prediction flip rates and broadening the useful-K region (again, stabilizing but not guaranteeing extrapolation).

## Results & Highlights

- **Looping reveals extrapolation potential but unreliably.** On binary addition, fixed-depth Transformers degrade sharply past the training boundary (OOD Std 0.009 for 3-layer, 0.005 for 60-layer), while Looped Transformers show extrapolation potential but with large OOD variability (**Std 0.087 for K=20, 0.122 for K=n**). Larger loop budgets widen the gap between *potential* and *reliability* (Fig. 2).
- **Stochastic schedules reduce both axes of instability.** The ±5 and ±10 windows around K=n produce concentrated OOD outcome distributions with smaller std (Fig. 3). They also broaden the high-accuracy region across inference-time loop counts and lower prediction flip rates (Fig. 4). Effect is *stabilization*, not monotonic accuracy gain: ±5 helps the central OOD accuracy but ±10 can reduce it.
- **RL-Halting improves the accuracy–stability trade-off on 3 of 4 tasks.** Table 1 (OOD acc / Front.@90 / Std):

  | Task | Fix K=20 | K=n | K=n,±5 | RL-Halt |
  |------|----------|-----|--------|---------|
  | Addition | 34.2 / 25 / 7.4 | 22.1 / 20 / 9.9 | 30.9 / 25 / 5.9 | **45.0 / 30 / 2.7** |
  | Dyck-1 | 84.6 / 30 / 23.8 | 66.6 / 25 / 33.3 | 88.2 / 35 / 18.7 | **97.5 / 60 / 3.6** |
  | Unique | 66.1 / 20 / 34.0 | 78.5 / 35 / 17.5 | 73.2 / 40 / 9.3 | **82.8 / 40 / 3.3** |
  | Copy | 62.3 / 25 / 27.7 | 68.7 / 35 / 13.2 | 55.9 / 30 / 8.5 | 43.2 / 25 / **2.7** |

  RL-Halting dramatically cuts variance everywhere (Std ≤3.6) and wins accuracy on addition/Dyck-1/Unique, but on **Copy it collapses accuracy (43.2, worst of all)** while being the most stable — it stabilized a suboptimal computation.
- **Controlling initialisation/data order does not remove the instability** (Appx. C.1), confirming the issue is intrinsic to deterministic looping, not an artefact of randomness.
- **RL-Halting learns a length-dependent but non-degenerate stochastic schedule** (Appx. C.4), and matches the sampled-depth protocol so it is comparable to (and distinguished from) the hand-designed windows and a PonderNet baseline (Appx. C.5).
- **Take-away observation**: low variance and high extrapolation accuracy are *distinct* objectives — a stable schedule can select a non-extrapolating computation.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (Moderate-High).** The core insight — that loop-count vs. length decoupling creates a *training-time selection problem* and that supervising across a depth window regularizes it — is a fresh, actionable framing specific to looped architectures. RL-Halting itself borrows from adaptive-computation/REINFORCE lineage (ACT, PonderNet), so the *mechanism* is not novel; the novelty is in (a) the diagnosis via oracle-over-iterations, (b) applying learned sampled-depth stopping to *stabilize length extrapolation*, and (c) the conceptual reframing of "when to stop" as a training-time design choice.
- **Rigor (Strong).** Clean experimental design: parameter-matched baselines, controlled train/test length split, a diagnostic metric (oracle-over-iterations) deliberately separated from the stopping-rule metric, controlled initialisation/data-order ablations, cross-task validation (4 tasks), and an explicit PonderNet comparison. Run-to-run variance is reported as a first-class quantity. The Copy failure case is reported honestly rather than hidden.
- **Reproducibility (Likely Good, pending code).** Tasks are simple and fully specified; architectures follow a cited prior design (Fan et al. 2024); hyperparameters/seeds are described with detail deferred to appendices (Appxs. A & B). No explicit code-release link is visible in the main text — this is the main uncertainty; reproducibility hinges on the appendix/code availability.
- **Significance (Moderate-High).** Directly relevant to the growing recurrent-depth / latent-reasoning / looped-LLM line (Geiping et al. 2025, Zhu et al. 2025). The "stabilize but don't over-trust stability" lesson is a useful caution for anyone deploying adaptive computation for length generalization.
- **Red flags / caveats.** (1) Evaluation is confined to synthetic algorithmic tasks; generalization to realistic language/code is untested. (2) RL-Halting can lock in a suboptimal computation (Copy) — so the method is not universally safe and needs task-dependent validation. (3) Oracle-over-iterations is a *diagnostic*, not an inference policy, so headline OOD numbers are optimistic upper bounds (the authors acknowledge this and provide a selected-rule comparison in Appx. C.3, which should be checked). (4) The theoretical Section 5 is explicitly "toy"/informal — it explains stabilization but makes no extrapolation guarantee.

## Strengths

- Crisp, falsifiable root-cause diagnosis (length–loop-count spurious correlation → training-time selection problem) supported by a dedicated diagnostic metric.
- Simple, drop-in intervention (depth-window randomization) plus a principled learned upgrade (RL-Halting) on a clean formulation.
- Rigorous variance-first evaluation with controlled ablations and cross-task validation.
- Intellectual honesty: the Copy failure is foregrounded, and the theoretical section carefully scopes what it does/doesn't prove.
- Conceptually useful take-away with clear applicability to the broader recurrent-depth / latent-reasoning literature.

## Limitations

- **Scope**: only synthetic algorithmic tasks (binary addition, Dyck-1, Unique Set, Copy); no realistic NLP/code/vision evaluation, so external validity is unknown.
- **Stability ≠ quality**: the central finding also bounds the method's safety — RL-Halting can stabilize a bad computation (Copy), requiring per-task validation.
- **Diagnostic optimism**: headline OOD/accuracy uses oracle-over-iterations (ground-truth depth selection); practical selected-rule accuracy is lower (Appx. C.3).
- **Theory is informal**: Section 5 provides intuition, not guarantees; formal statements (Appxs. D, E) rest on toy assumptions (bounded, weakly-aligned residuals).
- **Added complexity/cost**: RL-Halting introduces a stopping head, REINFORCE training, and hyperparameters (baseline, entropy weight) that need tuning; no explicit compute-cost analysis is given in the main text.
- **Code release** not confirmed from the main text.

## Relevance

对关注 Transformer 架构改进、长度泛化与训练范式设计的读者，本文提供了针对循环/递归深度模型 OOD 脆弱性的清晰诊断与可操作方案，并将"何时停止循环"提升为训练阶段的核心设计选择，对 latent reasoning / looped LLM 方向有直接的方法论启发；其"稳定不等于最优外推"的告诫也具有普遍警示价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does RL-Halting transfer beyond synthetic algorithmic tasks to realistic language, code, or math reasoning where "when to stop" matters for chain-of-thought-style latent reasoning?
- How does the variance reduction scale to sequence lengths *much* farther beyond the training distribution (e.g., 2×–10×), and does the stabilized computation eventually collapse?
- Is the length–loop-count spurious correlation the *only* source of OOD variance, or do positional encoding / attention-variance mechanisms interact with it? Could combining stochastic stopping with randomised positional encodings compound the gains?
- Can the RL-Halting policy be interpreted — does it converge to task-structured, interpretable step counts (e.g., proportional to algorithmic complexity)?
- What is the training cost / sample efficiency of RL-Halting versus simple uniform window randomization, and is there a cheap heuristic that captures most of the benefit without RL?
- Why does RL-Halting stabilize a *suboptimal* computation on Copy specifically — is it a property of the task (trivial algorithm) or a general failure mode of loss-driven stopping on easy tasks?
