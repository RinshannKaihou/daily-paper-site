Why This Matters

A faithful-looking, high-accuracy CBM can hide a completely broken concept layer.

If the learned classifier masks unfaithfulness, the “interpretable” decisions that CBMs are deployed for in medical and safety-critical settings may be illusory — and trust misplaced.

Critical Edge

The most incriminating metrics need ground-truth concepts — exactly what’s missing in real post-hoc settings.

Sim∢, surrogate-error |Δ| and correlation |ρ| all require annotated concepts; only the H∆H covariate-shift proxy is annotation-free on the target task.

§ Method How a CBM hides the truth

A frozen backbone f feeds a learned concept projection πθ, then a classifier h.

x→ f (backbone)→ πθ : A→C→ h : C→Y→ ŷ

Faithfulness (Def. 1). πθ is faithful iff it minimizes expected risk over the true task distribution, recovering E[c|x] in expectation.

Why accuracy lies. Under the manifold hypothesis the Johnson–Lindenstrauss lemma preserves geometry with far fewer than d random projections. The authors construct an h that inverts πθ (Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse) to exactly reconstruct activations — so high accuracy says nothing about whether the concepts mean anything.

Two failure modes, each with a metric. (1) Covariate shift when πθ is trained on auxiliary data (Broden) then applied to a target task — bounded by a domain-adaptation H∆H-divergence term, estimable without target concept labels. (2) Systematic surrogate-label noise from VLMs — a GLM gradient analysis shows unfaithfulness needs errors that are both large and correlated with activations; random, orthogonal errors cancel out and stay faithful.

§ Results Evidence the illusion is real

Synthetic Elements dataset (ground-truth concepts = shape × color × texture). Oracle h* uses true concepts; learned h is what you’d normally report.

+ Strengths

What the paper gets right.

  • Memorable core demo — random projections ≈ real CBMs makes the point viscerally.
  • Clean two-mode split — covariate shift and surrogate noise, each with a tailored, theory-grounded metric.
  • Practical proxy — H∆H needs no target-task concept labels, the hard constraint in real use.
  • Honest oracle h* — unmasks what the learned classifier conceals.
  • Orthogonality insight — elegantly explains why some noisy supervision is tolerated and some is fatal.

− Limitations

Where the argument is thinnest.

  • Concept-label dependency — the sharpest metrics (sim∢, |Δ|, |ρ|) need ground-truth concepts absent in practice.
  • Synthetic-heavy evidence — cleanest results come from Elements (shapes on white); natural-image generalization is less direct.
  • Assumption-heavy theory — manifold, linear-subspace, linear-backbone, GLM/EDF; tightness on real CNN/ViT activations is asserted more than proven.
  • Diagnosis, not cure — evaluation tools only; no method to fix unfaithful projections.
  • Partial overlap — the accuracy-illusion partly re-derives known JL / random-projection results.

? Open Questions

Where this work points next.

Can the systematic-error metric |ρ| be estimated without ground-truth concepts — via self-consistency, multi-VLM agreement, or probe-set holdouts?
How do these failure modes behave on modern large VLM backbones (SigLIP-2, Qwen3-VL) and end-to-end intrinsic / label-free CBMs?
Can the H∆H bound become a correction — an importance-weighting or adversarial objective realigning πθ to the target domain?
Is there a principled way to constrain h so it cannot mask unfaithfulness — forcing the independent-bottleneck property h* embodies?
Do the orthogonality findings extend to multi-modal systematic bias (e.g., VLM cultural/geographic bias) leaking into concept labels?
Can theory–empirics agreement (>0.9) survive when the linear-subspace / GLM assumptions break on real activations?
