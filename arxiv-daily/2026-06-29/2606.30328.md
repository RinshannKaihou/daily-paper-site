> **一句话总结：** 本文提出 `autonugget`，一个兼容 JAX 自动微分的 Python 包，它通过对多个不同 nugget 大小的 Tikhonov 正则化求解结果做 Richardson 多项式外推，自动且稳定地求解机器学习中常见的病态线性方程组，并在求解精度与导数精度上均优于单一 nugget 的常规做法。

> **TL;DR:** The paper introduces `autonugget`, a JAX-compatible Python package that automatically and stably solves ill-conditioned symmetric-positive-definite linear systems by performing several Tikhonov-regularised ("nugget") solves and combining them via Richardson polynomial extrapolation toward nugget = 0, replacing brittle non-differentiable nugget selection while beating single-nugget baselines on both solution and derivative accuracy.

## 核心贡献
- 提出 `autonugget` Python 包（已开源，适用于快速原型开发），用于病态对称正定线性方程组 `Ax=b` 的自动、稳定数值求解，使用时仅需将 `np.linalg.solve(A,b)` 替换为 `autonugget(A,b)`。
- 首次将 Richardson 多项式外推系统性地应用于现代机器学习中的 Tikhonov 正则化求解：对若干 nugget 值 `Σ={σi}` 各做一次直接法求解，再逐坐标做多项式外推至 `σ→0`，得到比单一 nugget 更精确的解。
- 给出严格的理论分析：在外推误差（Theorem 1，收敛速率 `O(h^n)`）与有限精度下的数值误差（Theorem 2，受 Lebesgue 常数与条件数控制）之间取得平衡，据此自动选择 nugget 缩放因子 `h⋆`（等式 8–10），无需用户手动调参。
- 提供自定义的 JAX 前向微分规则：利用 `∇θ xθ` 的解析公式（公式 12）将导数计算分解为三次独立的 `autonugget` 调用，各自独立自适应选 nugget，从而绕开了含条件分支/终止判据的常规自动微分带来的不稳定性，支持端到端梯度训练。
- 论证传统"数据自适应选 nugget 但只返回单次求解结果"的做法是浪费的——`autonugget` 复用了这些中间求解来提升精度。

## Key Contributions
- **`autonugget` package**: a drop-in, open-source Python tool for rapid prototyping; standard usage simply replaces `np.linalg.solve(A,b)` with `autonugget(A,b)`.
- **Richardson extrapolation over nuggets**: combines `n` Tikhonov-regularised solves at a geometric reference grid `Σ_ref={2^j σ_min}` via coordinatewise polynomial extrapolation toward `σ=0`, improving accuracy beyond any single-nugget solve.
- **Principled theory**: Theorem 1 bounds extrapolation error (rate `O(h^n)`, accelerated with more data `n`); Theorem 2 bounds numerical-error amplification via the Lebesgue constant and condition number. Balancing the two (Eq. 8) yields an automatic, unique scale factor `h⋆` — for near-singular `A`, `h⋆ ∍ ε_MP^{1/(n+1)}`.
- **Differentiable by construction**: a custom JAX forward-differentiation rule analytically decomposes `∇θ xθ` into three separate `autonugget` solves, each with its own adaptive nugget — avoiding the non-differentiability and instability of branching/tolerance-based nugget selection.
- **"Stop wasting solves"** argument: standard data-adaptive nugget selection performs multiple solves but discards all but one; `autonugget` repurposes them via extrapolation.

## Methodology
The setting is solving `Ax=b` for symmetric positive-definite (but possibly severely ill-conditioned) `A`, as arises in Bayesian optimisation, Gaussian processes, emulation, and probabilistic numerics. Standard prototyping adds a Tikhonov *nugget*: `xσ = (A+σI)^{-1} b`, with `xσ → x` as `σ→0`. The paper targets the ill-conditioning role of the nugget (the goal is the exact `σ=0` solution), explicitly disentangling it from regularisation-for-generalisation.

`autonugget` is built on three pieces:
1. **Extrapolation estimator (Sec. 2.1, Def. 1).** Given polynomial space `P=span{1,σ,…,σ^{n-1}}` and a `P`-unisolvent grid `Σ`, fit a polynomial (per coordinate) to the reliable solves `x̂σ` at `σ≥σ_min`, then evaluate at `σ=0`. Cost is `n` direct solves (negligible polynomial-interpolation overhead); `n∈{2,3,4}` keeps total cost on the order of a single direct solve, and parallelisable to `O(1)` wall-clock with `n` processors.
2. **Error analysis (Sec. 2.2–2.3).** The total error decomposes into extrapolation error + numerical error (Eq. 4). Theorem 1 gives `‖f(0)−f_n(0)‖ ≤ (1+λ(0;Σ_ref))·σ_max^n·λ_min(A)^{-(n+1)}‖b‖·h^n` — convergence accelerates as `n` grows. Theorem 2 bounds numerical-error amplification by the Lebesgue constant `λ(0;Σ_ref)` times the input data error, which for direct solvers scales as `ε_MP·κ(A+hσ_min I)`.
3. **Automatic nugget selection (Sec. 2.4).** The grid is `Σ=h·Σ_ref`; `h⋆` is chosen to balance extrapolation vs. numerical error (Eq. 8). The smallest eigenvalue `λ_min(A)` needed for the bound is approximated stochastically via `λ_min(M)^{-1}=sup_{‖v‖=1}‖Mv‖^{-1}` over ~`min(100,0.1d)` random vectors.
4. **Differentiation (Sec. 3).** `∇θ xθ = −A_θ^{-1}(∇θ A_θ)A_θ^{-1}b_θ + A_θ^{-1}(∇θ b_θ)` (Eq. 12) is computed via three autonomous `autonugget` calls, registered as a custom JAX forward rule so each sub-solve gets independent nugget protection.

## Results & Highlights
- **Solver accuracy (Sec. 4.2, Fig. 3):** Across Gram matrices from a squared-exponential kernel (varying dimension `d∈[20,1000]` and length-scale `ℓ∈[10^{-2},10^4]`), `autonugget` (default `n=2`, linear extrapolation) out-performs LU and SVD on most systems, especially severely ill-conditioned ones, and beats CG, LSTSQ, TSVD to a lesser extent. The fixed-nugget `LU-fix` baseline is *worse on both ends* — biased on well-conditioned systems, unstable on ill-conditioned ones — illustrating the need for adaptivity. Crucially, `autonugget` also beats `LU-adapt` (which uses the same auto-selected `σ⋆` but no extrapolation), directly attributing the gain to extrapolation. Ablations with `n>2` give marginal benefit, not justifying the extra cost as default.
- **Derivative accuracy (Sec. 4.3, Fig. 4):** On parametrised systems, `autonugget`'s custom differentiation is the most accurate, with the gap widening for large `ℓ` because differentiation triples the number of solves (each of which must be stabilised). LSTSQ/SVD/TSVD frequently fail to produce finite derivatives for ill-conditioned systems.
- **GP regression application (Sec. 4.4, Fig. 5):** Inferring a length-scale `ℓ=50` on a 5×5 grid via leave-one-out cross-validation. Only `autonugget` yields a smooth loss with a clean minimum near the true `ℓ=50` and converges correctly; LU/SVD give non-smooth losses and the optimiser diverges; LSTSQ/TSVD barely move; `LU-fix` converges to a far-too-small biased length-scale; `LU-cond`/`LU-adapt` converge but exhibit an incorrect *increasing* loss as `ℓ→∞` (an artefact of growing regularisation), whereas `autonugget` correctly captures the constant tail.
- **Reproducibility:** Code at https://github.com/hegdedisha/autonugget; experiments include seed-variation checks (Fig. 10) and tables of mean/std absolute errors (Tables 1–2).

## Critical Assessment
- **Novelty:** *Moderate, honestly scoped.* The authors are explicit (Sec. 1.1) that applying Richardson extrapolation to Tikhonov regularisation dates back to Groetsch & King (1979) and that the core numerical-analysis concepts are not new. The genuine novelty is (a) the *differentiable, tuning-free software artefact* for modern ML, and (b) the principled automatic `h⋆` selection balancing extrapolation vs. numerical error. This is a "right idea, right time, packaged well" contribution rather than a conceptual breakthrough.
- **Rigor:** *Strong.* Two theorems with proofs in appendices, careful finite-precision error decomposition, Lebesgue-constant analysis, and the balancing argument for `h⋆` are technically sound and standard for this community.
- **Reproducibility:** *Good.* Open-source JAX package, code-linked, seed variations reported, ablations in supplement (different kernel, non-kernel matrices, varying `n`).
- **Significance:** *Practical, narrow.* It is a useful engineering/prototyping tool, not a new capability. The scope is restricted to *symmetric positive-definite* `A` and to *direct* solvers; it deliberately does not compete with bespoke or iterative methods that exploit structure.
- **Red flags / caveats:** (1) Extrapolation over noisy solves is inherently fragile — the authors acknowledge higher-order polynomials amplify numerical error, and they default to the conservative `n=2`; the benefit of `n>2` was found to be marginal. (2) `λ_min(A)` estimation via a handful of random vectors is crude and can mis-estimate `σ⋆`; the paper calls the estimator "slightly risk-averse," but correctness hinges on it. (3) Comparisons are against *generic* baselines only — the paper explicitly excludes structure-exploiting or preconditioned methods, so "out-performs" claims are scoped to the prototyping use-case. (4) Empirical evaluation is exclusively on synthetic Gram/kernel matrices plus one toy GP task; no large-scale or real-data benchmark.

## Strengths
- Genuinely solves a real, widely-felt pain point (brittle, non-differentiable nugget selection) with a clean drop-in API.
- Theoretically grounded: error bounds justify both the extrapolation and the automatic nugget choice.
- Full JAX compatibility via a custom forward-diff rule is a strong practical differentiator, especially for kernel/GP workflows.
- Intellectually honest about novelty and scope; clear separation of "regularisation" vs. "ill-conditioning" roles of the nugget.
- Marginal cost (a few direct solves), parallelisable, and defaults that are robust in experiments.

## Limitations
- Restricted to symmetric positive-definite matrices and direct solvers; no treatment of non-symmetric/non-normal systems or large-scale iterative/preconditioned regimes.
- Accuracy depends on a stochastic minimum-eigenvalue estimate that can mis-identify the critical nugget `σ⋆`.
- Higher-order extrapolation (`n>2`) offers little benefit and can amplify noise, capping the method near linear/quadratic extrapolation in practice.
- Evaluation is synthetic (kernel Gram matrices) plus one small toy GP problem; no real-world or large-scale benchmarks.
- Compared only against generic baselines; does not position against modern structure-exploiting kernel solvers.

## Relevance
对于涉及大规模线性代数求解的机器学习研究与工程实践（尤其是高斯过程、核方法、贝叶斯推断与概率数值方法），该方法提供了一个可微、免调参的病态线性系统求解工具，可直接提升原型开发与端到端梯度训练的稳定性与精度。

## Questions for Further Exploration
- How sensitive is the automatic `h⋆` (and hence `σ⋆`) to errors in the stochastic `λ_min(A)` estimate — can a poor estimate cause extrapolation over unreliable data?
- Can the approach be extended to non-symmetric or highly non-normal systems, where conditioning and eigenvalue structure behave differently?
- How does `autonugget` compare (in accuracy *and* wall-clock) against structure-exploiting kernel solvers (e.g. HODLR, inducing-point, or stochastic-trace methods) rather than only generic baselines?
- For very large `d`, does the cost of multiple direct solves plus eigenvalue estimation remain competitive with preconditioned conjugate gradient or iterative refinement?
- Could the custom differentiation rule be generalised beyond SPD `A_θ` to settings where `A` depends nonlinearly on parameters?
- What is the practical ceiling on extrapolation order `n` before numerical-error amplification dominates — is there an adaptive, per-problem choice of `n`?
