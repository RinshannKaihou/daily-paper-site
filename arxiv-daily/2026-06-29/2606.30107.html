Structural Certification for Reliable Physical Design with Language Models

Nakul Vyas & Iliya D. Stoev et al. · Heysuvi Labs / Karlsruhe Inst. of Technology
PHACT LLM-verification neuro-symbolic formal-verification propose–certify loop reliable reasoning
Key finding · the headline claim

An ML-free physics engine derives — never accepts — the certified quantity from fixed inputs, stripping the language model's authority to assert. Forgery becomes impossible by construction.

PHACT reframes reliability as an interface-design choice, not a statistical hope: the model only proposes; a deterministic engine alone certifies, refuses, or abstains.

Why this matters

A transferable, fully reproducible template for trustworthy LLM-aided scientific design — proof that reliability is wiring, not wishful calibration, available wherever a deterministic forward model exists.

Critical edge

Closed-world test. Goals, engines, and the feasible/impossible boundary are all author-defined, with no held-out benchmark — an adversarial third party authoring goals might probe edges the structural contract does not anticipate.

Sharpest caveat
01

Methodology

PHACT pairs an unreliable language model with a deterministic, ML-free physics engine. The LM proposes a fully specified design and invokes the engine; the engine alone computes the relevant quantities exactly and issues a verdict. There is no path by which the model resubmits the value being checked.

The core device is the structural contract — a directed dependency graph of bound nodes (goal-fixed, read-only to the proposer), free design-variable nodes (the model's only writable inputs), and derived nodes whose leaf is the certified quantity. Because that quantity is computed forward from bound inputs rather than accepted from the model, a forged answer is structurally unattainable — a property of the interface, independent of decoding or sampler.

On failure the engine returns the specific violated law plus a targeted correction (the minimal root-input change that crosses threshold). Engines implement peer-reviewed equations — SantaLucia DNA melting, post-Newtonian GW inspiral, RC transfer, DLVO colloidal stability, drone momentum theory — calibrated as a correctness check, deliberately never scored on famous quantities the LM might merely recall.

03

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Sharp forgery diagnosis with a simple, transferable fix — derive, don't accept.
  • Clean control: same model bare vs. with-engine isolates the loop as the sole variable.
  • Capability-vs-safety separation demonstrated across a 10× model-size ladder.
  • Honest, non-overclaiming prose — repeatedly states what the guarantee is not.
  • Fully open, credential-free archive; the certification path is ML-free.

Limitations

  • Closed-world evaluation; no held-out external benchmark.
  • Only three proposers; only closed-form engines shown (surrogates proposed, untested).
  • Soundness is relative to the engine's physics model, not physical reality in full.
  • Safety sometimes bought via loop-fails (weaker proposers stall silently) — a real utility cost.
  • Modest N; zero-forgery reported descriptively, not statistically.
  • High per-domain engineering cost: each domain needs a hand-built engine + contract.
04

Open Questions

  1. Does non-forgeability hold when the engine is a learned PINN surrogate rather than closed-form code — and is exactness preserved?
  2. Against an adversarial third party authoring impossible goals, does the zero-forgery result survive — and where are the contract's blind edges?
  3. Would a model-agnostic loop-closure supervisor recover Llama 3.3 70B's lost feasible-certification rate without touching the engine's authority?
  4. Can the pattern extend to high-dimensional / stochastic design spaces (real MD, CFD) where a single deterministic forward evaluation is infeasible?
  5. What is the engineering cost to instantiate a new domain, and can any of that wiring be automated or templated?
