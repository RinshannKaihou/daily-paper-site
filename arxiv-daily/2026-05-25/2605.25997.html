Core Contributions

  • Formalizes deployment completeness as a factorization D = ϕ ∘ E — benchmark evidence must determine action on every fiber
  • Introduces mixed fibers as mathematical witnesses: candidates indistinguishable by benchmarks but requiring different actions
  • Designs completion curves ΓE(b; D) that quantify certified fraction vs. evidence budget
  • Proves no statistic T = h(E) can repair non-factorization — you cannot post-process away incompleteness

Strengths & Limitations

  • Striking inversion result: best MAE model certified only 6% of top-100 candidates; worse-MAE model certified all 100
  • Practical workflow: certify-then-acquire with demonstrated reductions across five domains
  • Requires specifying D upfront — may be ambiguous or evolving in practice
  • Linear response-space assumption — highly nonlinear deployments may need more complex analysis
  • Operational replays use held-out splits — not true prospective deployment with distribution shift

Methodology — Three Mathematical Objects

Object 1
Evidence Map E
E: S → Z partitions candidates into fibers — groups indistinguishable by benchmark output.
Object 2
Deployment Action D
D: S → A represents the operational decision (deploy / reject / threshold). Completeness requires D constant on every fiber.
Object 3
Completion Curve
ΓE(b; D) reports the certified fraction as a function of additional evidence budget b. Greedy probe maximizes ⟨r*, q⊥⟩² / (‖q⊥‖² · cq).
Relevance
A new benchmark evaluation paradigm that audits whether scores support deployment decisions. For any scenario relying on benchmarks for model selection, procurement, or deployment — including materials science AI, drug screening, and safety assessment — this framework provides rigorous mathematical tools and a practical certify-then-acquire workflow.

Significance
Potentially High
The factorization framing (D = ϕ ∘ E) is elegant and, to our knowledge, has not been applied to benchmark evaluation before. If adopted, this would change how benchmarks report results — from scores to evidence-action-completeness packages. However, adoption faces practical barriers: most benchmark organizers are not accustomed to this framing, and the framework's value is most apparent in high-stakes deployment where the cost of wrong decisions justifies the overhead. The JARVIS break-even of 20.7% means the approach is cost-effective only when false decisions are expensive relative to measurement.

Open Questions

  1. How does the framework extend to multi-action deployment claims (e.g., ranking with ties, continuous policies)?
  2. Can completion curves be estimated without deployment labels, using only benchmark evidence and structural assumptions?
  3. How does the framework interact with distribution shift — when calibration fibers are not exchangeable with deployment candidates?
  4. What are the computational costs of mixed-fiber audits for benchmarks with millions of submissions (e.g., LLM leaderboards)?
  5. Could the factorization diagnostic be integrated into benchmark design as a constraint, ensuring new benchmarks are deployment-complete by construction?
