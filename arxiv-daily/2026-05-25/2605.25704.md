> **一句话总结：** 本文提出 PowLU 激活函数，通过有理幂函数 x^(1+m/√(x+1))·sigmoid(x) 替代 SwiGLU 的近似 x² 放大特性，在 FP8 低精度训练中将激活值和梯度异常值显著抑制，在 Ling 架构 7.9B 和 124B MoE 模型上取得了与 SwiGLU 可竞争的基准性能，同时消除了 loss spike。

> **TL;DR:** Proposes PowLU, an activation using x^(1+m/√(x+1))·sigmoid(x) for positive inputs that grows sub-quadratically (≈linear for large x), reducing activation/gradient outliers in FP8 training. Evaluated on Ling MoE models (26M–124B params), it matches SwiGLU on 17 benchmarks while eliminating loss spikes that plague SwiGLU and SwiGLU-Clip at scale.

## 核心贡献

- 提出 PowLU 激活函数：正输入区间采用 x^(1+m/√(x+1))·sigmoid(x)，负输入区间与 SwiGLU 一致（x²·sigmoid(x)），从根源抑制 SwiGLU 的近似二次放大问题
- 从理论上严格证明了 PowLU 的连续性、可微性、单调递增性（m∈(0,10)）和有界增长性质（正方向近似线性增长而非二次增长）
- Scaling law 实验覆盖 26M 到 368M 激活参数，PowLU 与 SwiGLU 的 loss 曲线几乎重合，证明性能一致性
- 在 FP8 精度下训练 7.9B 和 124B MoE 模型，PowLU 完全消除了 loss spike，而 SwiGLU 和 SwiGLU-Clip 均出现严重 spike
- 通过 P99 分布可视化和 outlier channel 分析，定量展示了 PowLU 在前向和反向传播中对异常值的有效抑制

## Key Contributions

- Introduces PowLU: for x>0, uses x^(1+m/√(x+1))·sigmoid(x) (sub-quadratic growth); for x≤0, identical to SwiGLU (x²·sigmoid(x))
- Rigorously proves continuity, differentiability at x=0, monotonicity for m∈(0,10), and bounded sub-linear growth as x→+∞
- Scaling law experiments (26M–368M activated params) show PowLU and SwiGLU loss curves nearly overlap
- Eliminates loss spikes entirely in FP8 training of 7.9B and 124B Ling MoE models, where both SwiGLU and SwiGLU-Clip suffer severe spikes
- Quantitative outlier analysis via P99 distribution plots and channel-wise L2 norm visualization confirms PowLU suppresses extreme values in both forward and backward passes

## Methodology

**Activation function design.** PowLU is defined as:

- For x > 0: `PowLU(x) = x^(1 + m/√(x+1)) · sigmoid(x)` where m ∈ (0, 10) is a hyperparameter (default m=3)
- For x ≤ 0: `PowLU(x) = x² · sigmoid(x)` (identical to SwiGLU)

The key insight: as x → +∞, the exponent 1 + m/√(x+1) → 1, so PowLU grows approximately linearly rather than quadratically like SwiGLU. The √x factor in the exponent slows convergence to linearity, preserving nonlinearity. The "+1" in the denominator prevents the exponent from blowing up as x → 0⁺.

**Implementation.** Uses the standard GLU gating pattern: `PowLU(x₁, x₂) = x₁ · f(x₂)` where x₁ and x₂ are two linear projections of the same input.

**Theoretical analysis.** Proves four properties: (1) continuity at x=0 via left/right limit matching, (2) differentiability at x=0 via left/right derivative matching, (3) monotonicity for m<10 through auxiliary function analysis, (4) bounded growth — unbounded but sub-quadratic (≈x) as x→+∞.

**Experimental setup.** All experiments use the Ling MoE architecture. Scaling law: 26M–368M activated params, seq length 4096. Large-scale: 7.9B total params trained on 600B tokens; 124B total params trained on 800B tokens. FP8 precision for PowLU and SwiGLU-Clip comparisons; BF16 for SwiGLU baseline. Evaluated on 17 benchmarks across World Knowledge, Language & Reasoning, and Math & Code.

## Results & Highlights

**Scaling law (Table 1, Figure 3):** Loss curves of PowLU(m=3) and SwiGLU nearly overlap across 26M–368M activated parameters, confirming PowLU does not sacrifice expressiveness.

**7.9B model (Table 2):** PowLU achieves competitive results on 17 benchmarks vs. SwiGLU and SwiGLU-Clip (600B tokens). Notable wins: MMLU-Pro 24.00 vs. 21.56/21.79, ARC-challenge 55.93 vs. 51.53/51.86, WinoGrande 65.90 vs. 63.14/64.56, GSM8K 33.74 vs. 30.40/32.30, MATH 23.98 vs. 22.98/22.64.

**124B model (Table 3):** PowLU competitive with SwiGLU (800B tokens). Wins on: MMLU-Pro 40.12 vs. 40.75 (slightly behind), MMMLU 48.10 vs. 46.27, ARC-challenge 83.05 vs. 77.29, HumanEval 55.49 vs. 54.27, MATH 44.98 vs. 42.22, CMATH 83.33 vs. 80.69. Overall parity with marginal improvements on several benchmarks.

**Loss spike analysis (Figure 4):** In FP8 training, SwiGLU shows severe loss spike at ~76,200 steps; SwiGLU-Clip delays but still spikes at ~77,000 steps; PowLU maintains smooth loss trajectory around 1.32 with no significant deviations.

**Numerical distribution (Figures 5–7):** P99 distribution and outlier channel analysis show PowLU dramatically compresses the red band (min-max range) in both forward and backward passes, with more uniform channel-wise distributions.

**Ablation (Table 4):** m=2/3/4 yield losses of 1.913/1.912/1.914 vs. SwiGLU 1.910 — performance is not sensitive to m. Ablation on core components confirms both √x and sigmoid contribute to optimal performance.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Moderate-to-good. The specific form x^(1+m/√(x+1)) is novel and well-motivated. Power-function activations exist (PReLU, etc.), but applying rational exponents to address SwiGLU's quadratic amplification in LLM training is a genuine contribution.
- **Rigor:** Strong theoretical analysis with formal proofs of continuity, differentiability, monotonicity, and bounded growth. The monotonicity proof for m<10 is thorough (auxiliary function ϕ(t) analysis with transcendental equation solving). However, the non-linearity proof in Appendix A.2 is surprisingly hand-wavy — "product of two non-linear functions is non-linear" is not generally true without further conditions.
- **Reproducibility:** Good. The activation function is simple to implement. Hyperparameter m=3 is given. However, the Ling architecture is not fully open-sourced, and the 124B-scale experiments are not trivially reproducible.
- **Significance:** Practically significant for FP8 training stability. The elimination of loss spikes is a meaningful result for practitioners. However, the performance gains on benchmarks are marginal (often <1 point), so the primary value is stability, not capability.
- **Red flags:** (1) The 7.9B SwiGLU-Clip and PowLU models are initialized from a SwiGLU checkpoint mid-training rather than trained from scratch — this introduces a confound. (2) The SwiGLU baseline uses BF16 while PowLU uses FP8, making the loss spike comparison somewhat unfair (different precision). (3) Only tested on Ling MoE architecture; no validation on dense or other MoE architectures. (4) The ablation study is only at 47M scale, which may not generalize.

## Strengths

- Clean, well-motivated activation function design with strong theoretical grounding
- Genuinely large-scale validation (124B params) — rare in activation function papers
- Comprehensive stability analysis with multiple visualization methods (P99 distribution, outlier channels, loss curves)
- Scaling law experiments provide confidence that results extrapolate
- Ablation studies systematically validate each design choice

## Limitations

- Only validated on the Ling MoE architecture; generalizability to LLaMA, Qwen, or dense transformers is unknown
- The BF16 vs. FP8 comparison confound in loss spike analysis makes it hard to isolate the activation function's contribution from precision effects
- Mid-training switching from SwiGLU to PowLU/SwiGLU-Clip introduces distribution shift that complicates fair comparison
- Benchmark improvements are marginal (typically 1–3 points); the primary benefit is stability, not performance
- No wall-clock or throughput comparison — the computational cost of computing x^(1+m/√(x+1)) vs. standard SwiGLU is not discussed
- Non-linearity proof in Appendix A.2 is incomplete

## Relevance

PowLU 针对大规模 LLM 预训练中 SwiGLU 的数值不稳定问题提出了简洁的解决方案，特别适用于 FP8 低精度训练场景。对于关注训练稳定性、MoE 架构设计和高效训练技术的研究者具有直接参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- What is the actual FLOPs/throughput overhead of computing x^(1+m/√(x+1)) compared to SwiGLU in practice?
- Can PowLU be combined with other stabilization techniques (QK-norm, logit soft-capping, gradient clipping) for additive benefits?
- How does PowLU behave in dense (non-MoE) transformer architectures at comparable scale?
- Does PowLU improve quantization quality for post-training quantization (e.g., GPTQ, AWQ) due to reduced outlier channels?
- Is the m=3 optimal across different training data mixtures, sequence lengths, or vocabularies?
- What happens when PowLU is used from-scratch vs. switched mid-training — do the stability benefits hold in both cases?
