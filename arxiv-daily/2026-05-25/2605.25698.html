Key Finding

Drop batch size at the quality transition,
hold stable, then ramp up.

A quality-aware functional scaling law reveals that high-quality data plays a dual role — signal amplifier and noise suppressor — depending on regime. The resulting Drop-Stable-Rampup schedule improves a 15B MoE model by +2.98 over cosine decay across 14 benchmarks with no architectural changes.

Why it works: Lower batch size during high-quality phases increases the effective step count, letting the model extract more signal per token. Later ramp-up suppresses terminal noise. The stable-phase ratio (24.9%) is tuned via validation loss — but every ratio from 0%–62% beats both baselines.

Two Regimes from Theory

Noise-Limited (s > scrit)

Quality placement barely matters. What matters: drop batch size during high-quality phases to convert cleaner gradients into more learning steps. High-quality data acts as a signal amplifier.

Signal-Limited (s < scrit)

Strict ordering emerges: low-quality first → high-quality last. Late placement suppresses terminal noise. High-quality data acts as a noise suppressor. Batch drop still helps but is secondary.

Derived from a variational optimization over training horizon T, batch schedule b(t), and quality mix p(t) under feature-space linear regression with power-law spectral decay.

Theoretical model is a simplification. Feature-space linear regression with Gaussian features does not capture nonlinear feature learning or attention dynamics. Binary good/bad quality is coarse. Only tested on one 15B MoE architecture (2.4B active, 108B tokens). Pretraining checkpoint and midtraining data are proprietary (Meituan). The 24.9% stable ratio is empirically tuned, not derived. Multi-domain heterogeneous schedules remain open.

Strengths

  • Clean theory yielding actionable, interpretable insights — two regimes, dual role of quality data
  • DSR requires zero architectural changes — only schedule tweaks
  • Every DSR stable-ratio variant (0%–62%) outperforms both baselines — robust to hyperparameter choice
  • Largest gains on math reasoning (GSM8K +7.85), aligning with theory's prediction for signal-limited capabilities
  • Scaling-law exponents match predictions (−0.81 vs theory −0.80; −0.51 vs theory −0.50)

Limitations

  • Linear regression model ignores nonlinear feature learning, multi-layer composition, attention
  • Binary quality abstraction — real corpora have continuous quality spectra
  • Single scale: 15B MoE only. Dense models, other scales, and budgets are untested
  • 1.1× wall-clock slowdown from low-batch stable phase
  • Single scalar quality dimension ignores per-domain differences (math vs code vs knowledge)
  • No principled method to set stable ratio at new scales

Open Questions

Q1
Does DSR transfer to dense transformers (7B–70B LLaMA-scale) and pretraining-scale budgets (trillions of tokens)?
Q2
Does the bang-bang optimal schedule persist under a continuous (rather than binary) quality spectrum?
Q3
How should per-domain schedules be jointly optimized when math, code, and reasoning have different signal/noise profiles?
Q4
Can the regime (noise- vs signal-limited) be inferred from observable training metrics for adaptive scheduling?
