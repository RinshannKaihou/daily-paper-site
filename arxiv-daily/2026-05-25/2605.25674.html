A single-pass stochastic Hessian trace estimator detects label memorization with 99.4% power during neural-network training.

Stochastic Estimation of the Layer-wise Hessian Trace for Monitoring Neural-network Training

Bolshim & Kugaevskikh·ITMO University·2026-05-25

Key Metric
179/180
noisy runs correctly flagged (99.4% power)
Why This Matters

Catches training anomalies that loss and gradient norms miss, at ≤10% overhead — practical for online monitoring.

Critical Edge

13.3% false-alarm rate; requires clean-label calibration runs; no transformer or large-scale validation.

Methodology

The algorithm (Section 3.3) runs in three steps: (1) compute the gradient while retaining the computational graph; (2) for each of K Rademacher probe vectors, compute a Hessian-vector product and accumulate per-layer dot products; (3) average over K probes. Because cross-layer probe components are independent, a single HVP delivers unbiased trace estimates for every layer simultaneously.

Weight-sharing correctness

The true layer Hessian under shared weights sums all cross-instance blocks, not just the diagonal. Pearlmutter's method on the original graph captures these automatically; unrolling shared weights discards them, introducing systematic bias.

Variance & probe count

The total variance decomposes as Var(T̂) = VH(K) + VB. The critical probe count K* balances probe randomness against batch randomness; empirically K* ∈ [5, 10].

Results

99.4% detection power 13.3% false-alarm rate ≤10% overhead
  • 179/180 noisy runs correctly flagged; sole miss is VGG-11/CIFAR-10 at η=0.25 (9/10 detected)
  • False-alarm rate 16/120 consistent with theoretical 11.0%; Wilson 95% CI covers it
  • Mean detection epoch decreases monotonically with noise level η across all six configurations
  • Zero false alarms in the first five snapshot steps; first-order autocorrelation under H₀ indistinguishable from zero
  • Stable under perturbation of k and ARL₀ hyperparameters

Strengths

  • Elegant single-pass algorithm with clear theoretical justification
  • Weight-sharing bias analysis is an important practical finding for Hutchinson estimation on CNNs
  • Thorough variance analysis with actionable guidance (K ∈ [5, 10])
  • Strong detection statistics with principled statistical calibration
  • Low computational overhead — practical for online monitoring

Limitations

  • Only validated on CIFAR-10/100 with ResNet-18/34 and VGG-11; no transformer or large-scale experiments
  • Requires a clean-label calibration ensemble (Phase I), which may not be available in practice
  • Trace loses informativeness in saddle regions (Tℓ ≈ 0, κℓ ≫ 1)
  • No comparison with simpler baselines (validation-loss monitoring, gradient-norm CUSUM)
  • 13.3% false-alarm rate may be too high for production use
  • Fully online (calibration-free) variant left to future work

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does the layer-wise trace evolve during LLM pretraining vs. fine-tuning? Does anisotropy κℓ differ between attention and FFN blocks?
  2. Can the CUSUM detector be adapted to detect other training anomalies (loss spikes, gradient explosion, data corruption) beyond label memorization?
  3. What is the interaction between this estimator and mixed-precision training — does float16 introduce additional bias?
  4. Could per-layer trace trajectories serve as early stopping criteria or adaptive learning-rate signals?
  5. How many clean calibration runs are needed for stable μ₀(t) and σ₀(t) estimates — is 20 sufficient for larger models?
