When In-Distribution Gains Fail: Evaluating Weak-to-Strong Reward Models under Preference Shift

Khoi Le, Tri Cao, Phong Nguyen, Cong-Duy Nguyen, Anh Tuan Luu, Chunyan Miao, See-Kiong Ng, Thong Nguyen
Weak-to-strong reward models appear successful in-distribution but fail under preference-domain shift. ANCHOR — a representation-anchoring regularizer — constrains hidden-state drift from the pretrained model, consistently improving OOD transfer with no inference cost.
Key Finding
75.74% → 54.42%
ID accuracy on HelpSteer3 collapses to near-random when transferred to Anthropic Helpful — a 21-point drop revealing brittle generalization.

Key Contributions

  • Zero-shot preference-domain shift evaluation protocol for W2S reward modeling — train on one preference domain, test on held-out datasets within the same category
  • Empirical proof that weak-supervised fine-tuning causes representational drift toward source-domain features, explaining why ID gains fail to transfer
  • ANCHOR: constrains response-token hidden states toward a frozen pretrained reference during training, discarded at inference — zero overhead at serving time
  • Three transfer-aware metrics: WRG (in-distribution gain), AOG (absolute OOD gain), NTS (net transfer score accounting for source regression)

Methodology — ANCHOR Pipeline

01 / Weak Teacher

Train weak reward model wS on gold source-domain labels. Generate soft preference labels qi for student training data.

02 / Anchored Fine-Tuning

Combined loss: Lw2s (match soft teacher labels) + λLanchor (MSE between student and frozen reference hidden states at last transformer layer).

03 / Cross-Domain Eval

Within each preference category (Helpful / Harmless), train on one dataset, evaluate on all — including held-out source (ID) and other datasets (OOD).

Results & Highlights

  • ANCHOR achieves best or second-best on majority of WRG, AOG, and NTS metrics across both model families and both preference categories
  • SEAM can achieve competitive OOD (AOG) but often at severe ID degradation — e.g., ID PGR drops to −1315.4% in Qwen/Anthropic Helpful
  • Naive and confidence-based methods show strong ID but unstable OOD transfer, with negative NTS in several settings
  • Weaker anchoring (λ=10−4) consistently outperforms stronger anchoring (λ=10−2) — over-constraining hinders adaptation
  • Last-Layer ANCHOR provides better overall balance than Middle-Layer variant

Critical Assessment

  • Only offline pairwise accuracy evaluated — no downstream RLHF policy quality tested
  • ANCHOR is conceptually simple: hidden-state MSE regularizer reminiscent of continual learning techniques
  • PGR values can be extremely large and negative, confusing interpretation
  • Full reference model forward pass adds VRAM overhead during training — acknowledged but not quantified
  • Limited to 1B/1.7B teachers and 8B students

Strengths

  • Identifies a real, previously underappreciated failure mode in W2S reward modeling
  • Clean evaluation protocol with well-defined transfer-aware metrics
  • Simple to implement — no inference overhead
  • Comprehensive experiments: 2 families, 6 datasets, 2 preference categories, 3 seeds
  • Honest about limitations

Limitations

  • No downstream RLHF policy optimization or generation quality evaluation
  • Reference model forward pass increases training memory and time
  • Only helpful/harmless categories tested; specific model scales only
  • λ requires manual tuning — no automatic selection method

Open Questions

  • Does improved OOD transfer lead to better downstream policy quality in online RLHF?
  • How does ANCHOR interact with DPO methods that bypass explicit reward modeling?
  • Can λ be auto-calibrated from source-domain signal strength?
  • Does representational drift also affect strong-supervised reward models under distribution shift?
