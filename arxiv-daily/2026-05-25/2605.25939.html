From Latent Space to Training Data: Explainable Specialization in Minimal MLPs

Enrique Alba & Ezequiel Lopez-Rubio

Key Finding

Coverage regularization consistently drives hidden neurons to specialize as training prototypes — while overlap penalties are systematically harmful due to degenerate equilibria.

Controlled Runs 480 Systematic sweep across N = 3 … 100
Best Method Coverage Lowest reconstruction error at every tested size
Failure Mode Degenerate Eq. Overlap pushes prototypes outside data convex hull
Design Principle Attractor &harr; Repulsor Every repulsive loss needs a compatible attractor
Key Contributions
  • Systematic comparison of three structural losses (coverage, separation, overlap) in Gaussian-activation MLPs
  • 480 controlled runs proving coverage regularization wins at every dataset size (N = 3 to 100)
  • Identified the degenerate equilibrium mechanism that makes overlap penalties harmful
  • Proposed a prototype-recoverability design principle for regularizer construction
Methodology
  • Gaussian-activation one-hidden-layer MLPs, width = dataset size
  • Three structural losses: coverage, separation (temperature-swept), overlap
  • Uniformly sampled 1D datasets, N = 3 to 100
  • Prototype-position visualization confirms the degenerate equilibrium
Results & Highlights
  • Coverage: lowest mean reconstruction error across all sizes
  • Coverage raises the prototype-usage specialization ratio vs. baseline
  • Separation: mixed effects depending on temperature τ
  • Overlap: fits data equally well but pushes prototype centers outside the convex hull

Strengths

  • Explains a concrete failure mechanism instead of merely reporting negative results

Limitations
  • Experiments restricted to 1D uniformly sampled data only
  • Width = dataset size is unrealistic for practical applications
  • No validation on real-world or higher-dimensional datasets
  • Analysis is largely empirical rather than formally theoretical
Questions for Further Exploration
  1. How do findings generalize to higher-dimensional data (2D, 10D, 100D)?
  2. Does the attractor-repulsor principle hold for deeper networks?
  3. Can coverage regularization scale to practical prototype-based classification?
  4. What happens with non-uniform distributions (multimodal, heavy-tailed)?
  5. Is there a formal proof for why coverage acts as an attractor?
Relevance to Your Research

Theoretical value for understanding hidden-neuron specialization in simple networks, but the highly constrained setup (1D data, width = dataset size) limits direct practical application. Useful reference for researchers in prototype learning and neural network interpretability.
