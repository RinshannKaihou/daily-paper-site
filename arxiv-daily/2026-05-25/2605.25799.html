Addressing Exacerbated Attention Sink for Source-Free Cross-Domain Few-Shot Learning

S. Yi, Y. Zou, Y. Li, R. Li — HUST

Key Finding
Standard few-shot fine-tuning exacerbates attention sink in CLIP's ViT — the model learns a shortcut by pushing "simple tokens" closer while suppressing discriminative ones. TIR fixes this and hits 58.20% on 1-shot CDFSL.
Standard target-domain fine-tuning causes sink tokens in ViT to grow dramatically in norm, degrading class discriminability. The proposed TIR method dynamically re-weights tokens to suppress sinks and enhance discriminative tokens, achieving new SOTA across four benchmarks with consistent gains on multiple backbones and adaptation strategies.

Methodology — TIR (Token Importance Reweighting)
01 / Classify
For each image token, compute how many of the K classes rank it as their top-attended token ("Sum"). Tokens with Sum=K are sink tokens; Sum=1 are discriminative tokens.
02 / Inhibit
At layers 8 & 10, reduce value magnitudes of the top-k fraction of sink tokens (Sum=K) that all classes attend equally, breaking the attention shortcut.
03 / Enhance
Amplify value magnitudes of discriminative tokens (Sum=1) attended by only one class. Temper ratio (0.5) controls reweighting strength. Simplest form: zero-out Sum=K, boost Sum=1.
Strengths
  • Elegant problem identification: connecting attention sink with CDFSL is novel and well-motivated
  • "Shortcut learning" interpretation provides clear mechanistic understanding of failure modes
  • Strong empirical results across agriculture, remote sensing, medical imaging, radiology
  • Simplified strategy (zero-out Sum=K, boost Sum=1) makes it practical and easy to implement
  • Comprehensive analysis: layer-wise CKA, attention visualizations, hyperparameter sensitivity
Limitations
  • Only evaluated on ViT-B/16; scalability to ViT-L/H is untested
  • Medical imaging improvements are modest — may struggle with extreme domain shifts
  • The "Sum" heuristic for token classification is somewhat arbitrary
  • Hyperparameter sensitivity beyond top-k ratio of 0.5
  • Requires applying reweighting during inference, adding test-time overhead

Relevance
Important implications for understanding VLM fine-tuning behavior in cross-domain settings. Attention sink is likely overlooked in practical deployments. TIR provides a simple plug-and-play solution for researchers using CLIP for few-shot domain adaptation. The shortcut learning interpretation offers a new lens for analyzing VLM fine-tuning failures.
Questions for Further Exploration
  1. How does TIR interact with register tokens (Darcet et al., 2024)? Could they combine for additive gains?
  2. Does attention sink exacerbation generalize beyond CDFSL — e.g., domain adaptation with more shots or full fine-tuning?
  3. Can the Sum-based classification be replaced with a learned selector that adaptively identifies sink vs. discriminative tokens?
  4. What is the computational overhead at inference time, and can reweighting be distilled into model weights?
  5. Why weaker gains on ChestX specifically — inherent to grayscale radiology, or could modality-aware reweighting help?
