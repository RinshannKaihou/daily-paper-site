AI Cartography: Mapping the Latent Landscape of AI Benchmark Ecosystems

Hardy, Reuel, Zhang, Casabianca, Truong, Dave, Lee, Domingue, Koyejo

A psychometric framework (CFA + Generalizability Theory + latent regression) decomposes noise in AI benchmark leaderboards across 4,000+ models, revealing that a single general factor dominates and manifest-score scaling laws are deeply unreliable.

ICML 2026 · Seoul · Stanford / BroadMetrics

Methodology
Three interlocking psychometric methods
Method 1
CFA Structural Discovery
Tests six competing latent structures (independent, unidimensional, hierarchical, correlated, bifactor, correlated bifactor) using meta-analytic bootstrapping with permutation controls. Out-of-sample AUC/MAE validates the winner.
Method 2
Generalizability Theory
Fully crossed mixed-effects model decomposes variance into architecture, benchmark, contributor, deployment, and all interactions. Introduces Slope SNR and PSIS as reliability diagnostics for scaling laws.
Method 3
Mixed-Effects Latent Regression
Hierarchical model: bifactor IRT measurement layer (items → latent traits) plus structural layer (traits → covariates). Corrects measurement-error attenuation that biases naïve OLS scaling laws.
Results & Highlights
What the latent landscape reveals
g
Bifactor structure dominates
A single general factor g captures most cross-benchmark covariance. Benchmark-specific factors are largely residual noise after removing g.
β
Scaling is a vector, not a scalar
MMLU-Pro scales strongly beyond g (knowledge). GPQA and BBH specific factors show trivial size effects after controls (reasoning stagnates).
↔
Alignment tax discovered
Instruction-following (IFEval) gains come from tuning, not scale. MuSR-specific factor shows a small but significant negative relationship with size — instruction tuning hurts soft reasoning.
#1
Rank instability at the top
Only 1 model stays in the top 1% after conditioning on g. Spearman ρ=0.86 masks a Kendall τ of only 0.66 — the leaderboard reshuffles dramatically.
Critical Assessment
Novelty High
First combined application of CFA + G-theory + latent regression to AI benchmarking. Permutation controls and out-of-sample CFA diagnostics are novel even within psychometrics.
Rigor Very High
Bayesian estimation, multiple estimators, permutation controls, bootstrap aggregation, careful handling of pseudo-replication. Unusually thorough limitations section.
Reproducibility Moderate
Public leaderboard data with referenced code repository, but bootstrapped CFA/IRT on 4,000+ models is computationally expensive. Metadata quality is acknowledged as noisy.
Significance High
Challenges fundamental assumptions of AI evaluation. Rβ=0.53 vs Rg=0.97 has major implications for how the field interprets benchmark results and scaling laws.
Limitations
Single leaderboard snapshot with 6 benchmarks; generalizability unknown
Contributor metadata is an operational grouping, not a clean causal variable
Bifactor model may over-extract general factors; semantics of g depend on item pool
Leaderboard models are not representative of all LLMs
Latent factors are statistical abstractions, not definitive constructs of intelligence
Framework does not address temporal instability as ecosystems evolve
