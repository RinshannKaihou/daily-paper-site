Blue Sky Vision Paper

Causal Methods for LLM Development and Evaluation

Frauen, D. · Brockschmidt · Hess · Ma · Ma · Maarouf · Schröder · Schweisthal · Wang · Deviyani · Parbhoo · Krishnan · Feuerriegel, S.

Key Finding

LLM development is riddled with confounding—and causal inference (identifiability, DML, off-policy evaluation) is the principled, underused fix across the entire pipeline.

TL;DR: Maps causal inference methods—identifiability, double/debiased ML, off-policy evaluation—across five LLM pipeline stages (pretraining, alignment, deployment, evaluation, safety), showing where purely predictive approaches fail and causal thinking is essential.

Anchor Fact
~105 references spanning causal inference × LLM training × preference learning × evaluation, unified under potential outcomes framework Y(a)
So What
Every major LLM decision—data mixture, alignment, routing, judging—is an observational intervention. Without causal correction, evaluation and deployment are systematically biased.
Critical Edge
No empirical validation. Identifiability assumptions (ignorability, overlap) are often unrealistic at LLM scale. Theory–practice gap remains large.

1 LLM Pipeline → Causal Mapping

Pretraining Data curation (toxicity filtering, deduplication, domain weighting) are confounded interventions. Continuous treatment effect estimation can predict downstream performance without full retraining.
Alignment / RLHF Bradley-Terry model underlying RLHF/DPO is often misspecified (cyclic preferences, position bias). DML-based doubly robust alignment and semiparametric BT extensions offer robustness.
Deployment LLM routing logs are confounded by query difficulty. Counterfactual estimation from observational data corrects deployment bias. RAG/agent systems require off-policy evaluation with structured mediators.
Evaluation LLM-as-a-judge exhibits verbosity preference, self-enhancement, position effects. Prediction-powered inference (PPI) + DML debiases by combining large-scale LLM annotations with small gold-standard human samples.
Safety Audits Multiple reward functions can produce identical outputs—reward identification is fundamentally under-determined. Partial identification bounds the probability of harmful actions.

2 Methodology

  1. Formal framing — each pipeline decision cast as causal estimand E[Y(a)] or E[Y(a)−Y(a')] under potential outcomes.
  2. Identifiability analysis — backdoor adjustment, ignorability, and overlap conditions mapped per stage.
  3. Estimation toolbox — DML, efficient influence functions, doubly robust estimators, off-policy evaluation.
  4. Pipeline walkthrough — pretraining → alignment → deployment → evaluation → safety, identifying confounders, treatments, and open problems at each step.

3 Open Questions

  1. How do DML-based methods scale to millions of comparisons in LLM arenas?
  2. Can the causal RAG framing extend to multi-step agentic workflows with combinatorial action spaces?
  3. What practical sensitivity analysis exists when ignorability is violated in preference data?
  4. How does partial identification for reward recovery compare empirically to red-teaming?
  5. Can causal discovery automatically identify confounders in LLM evaluation logs?
