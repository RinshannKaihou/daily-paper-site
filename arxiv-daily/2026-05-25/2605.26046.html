When Gradients Collide: Failure Modes of Multi-Objective Prompt Optimization for LLM Judges

Parth Darshan · Abhishek Divekar · IIT Jodhpur / Amazon
6/10 configurations never improve over a naive "Rate 1–5" prompt

Two separable failure modes — gradient dilution at optimization time and instruction interference at inference time — explain why multi-criteria textual gradient optimization breaks down for LLM judges.

TL;DR — The authors systematically study five decomposition modes for multi-criteria textual gradient prompt optimization on SummEval. They discover that combining tasks dilutes textual gradients (specificity drops 59%) and that even oracle-optimal instructions degrade when co-located (−0.053 Spearman). The optimizer faithfully follows suggestions — the bottleneck is gradient quality, not execution.

Strengths
  • Clean experimental design spanning the full decomposition space
  • Two-failure-mode decomposition is conceptually clear and well-supported
  • Oracle cherry-pick elegantly isolates inference-time interference
  • Honest reporting of negative results with appropriate effect-size claims
  • Process-level diagnostics (specificity, adherence) are algorithm-agnostic and reusable
Limitations
  • Single benchmark (SummEval, 4 criteria) limits generalizability
  • N=3 runs — statistical power constrained
  • No proposed solution — diagnoses failure without mitigation
  • LLM-based diagnostics scored by Claude introduces evaluator-bias confound
  • Only TextGrad-style optimization; OPRO, GEPA, evolutionary methods untested
Open Questions
  1. Would 8+ criteria amplify dilution linearly or cause a phase transition?
  2. Can length-aware normalization fully mitigate inference-time interference?
  3. Do population-based methods (NSGA-II) avoid textual gradient pitfalls?
  4. Is the 59% specificity drop model-dependent across GPT-4 / Claude / Gemini?
