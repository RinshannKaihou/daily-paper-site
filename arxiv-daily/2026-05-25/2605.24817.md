> **一句话总结：** RouteScan 提出了一种非侵入式的 MoE 大模型安全审计框架，通过 prefilling 阶段 GPU 级专家路由遥测数据（活跃线程数分配）检测有害输入，在未见过的有害领域上 AUROC > 0.93、在新型越狱包装下 AUROC > 0.96，且遥测数据无法重建原始提示，兼顾安全审计与用户隐私。

> **TL;DR:** RouteScan audits MoE LLM safety non-intrusively by analyzing GPU-level expert routing telemetry — specifically active GPU thread counts allocated to expert modules during the prefilling phase. Evaluated on DeepSeek-V2-Lite-Chat and Qwen1.5-MoE-A2.7B-Chat across NVIDIA RTX A6000/5880 GPUs, it achieves AUROC > 0.93 on unseen harmful domains and > 0.96 on novel jailbreak wrappers, while empirical inversion tests confirm aggregated telemetry resists prompt reconstruction.

## 核心贡献

- **首次识别 MoE 执行侧审计面**：发现 MoE 模型的稀疏路由机制会将输入依赖的 token 路由到不同专家，产生可测量的 GPU 底层执行信号（活跃线程数），形成有害/无害输入的判别性指纹
- **非侵入式审计框架 RouteScan**：仅依赖 prefilling 阶段的 GPU 级专家路由遥测数据，不访问用户提示、模型输出或内部表示，即可检测有害风险输入
- **分层特征表示 + 自适应特征选择**：构建细粒度专家负载分布与粗粒度层级结构统计的统一表示，通过混合评分与自适应支持机制选择跨域鲁棒的遥测维度
- **三角色隐私审计生态**：定义了用户-服务商-监管者的三方威胁模型，仅通过聚合遥测数据实现安全审计，经验性验证了隐私边界

## Key Contributions

- **Execution-side auditing surface**: First to identify that MoE sparse routing produces input-dependent expert workload patterns reflected in low-level GPU execution telemetry (active SIMT thread counts), providing a non-content signal for harmful-risk detection.
- **RouteScan framework**: A complete non-intrusive auditing pipeline that collects aggregated GPU-level expert routing telemetry during the prefilling phase, extracts hierarchical features (expert-load distributions + layer-level structural statistics), selects robust dimensions via hybrid scoring with adaptive support, and classifies via a calibrated linear detector — all without accessing user content or model internals.
- **Cross-domain generalization**: Demonstrates strong transferability to unseen harmful-content benchmarks (AUROC > 0.93) and unseen jailbreak wrappers including PAIR and CodeChameleon (AUROC > 0.96), evaluated on DeepSeek-V2-Lite-Chat and Qwen1.5-MoE-A2.7B-Chat.
- **Empirical privacy boundary validation**: Medical privacy stress tests show aggregated telemetry achieves Token F1 of only 0.1601 for prompt reconstruction, zero exact sensitive-field recovery, and leave-one-scenario-out probing AUROC below 0.45 for most attributes — confirming practical privacy preservation.

## Methodology

**Telemetry acquisition**: RouteScan uses NVTX (NVIDIA Tools Extension) range markers injected around the SiLU activation in each expert module, then captures active thread counts via `nsys` profiling during the prefilling phase. The active thread count `n_{l,e}` for expert `e` in layer `l` is linearly proportional to the expert load (sum of routing indicators across all input tokens).

**Feature representation**: Two complementary views are concatenated per request:
1. *Fine-grained expert-load distributions*: Normalized per-layer load vectors `p^l` (proportion of tokens per expert).
2. *Coarse-grained layer-level structural statistics*: Four statistics per layer — activation coverage rate `v_act`, effective activation rate `v_eff` (entropy-based), coverage gap `g_cov`, and coverage concentration `c_cov`.

**Hybrid scoring and adaptive support**: Each feature dimension receives a reliability score `rho_j = M_disc(j) * M_cons(j) * M_prior(j)`, combining discriminative evidence, cross-source stability, and conservative priors. Shannon entropy of the score-mass distribution determines the support set width adaptively — concentrated signals yield narrow support, diffuse signals yield broader support.

**Linear detection with calibration**: A logistic regression detector operates on selected/weighted features. Source-adaptive regularization selects the `C` parameter based on weakest positive separation and positive-subset disparity. Platt scaling calibrates raw margins to a source-calibrated harmful-risk score.

**Evaluation protocols**:
- *Cross-domain harmful auditing*: Leave-one-target-out over AdvBench, StrongREJECT, CatHarmfulQA, HarmBench-Std.
- *Mixed-positive family transfer*: Source pool contains harmful + AutoDAN jailbreak; target tests PAIR and CodeChameleon jailbreak wrappers.
- *Privacy boundary*: End-to-end telemetry-to-text inversion and targeted coarse-attribute probing on synthetic medical prompts.

## Results & Highlights

**Cross-domain harmful auditing** (Table 1):
| Model/Hardware | Target | AUROC | F1@0.5 | Acc@0.5 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| DeepSeek/A6000 | AdvBench | 0.9979 | 0.9346 | 0.9385 |
| DeepSeek/A6000 | StrongReject | 0.9706 | 0.8811 | 0.8866 |
| DeepSeek/A6000 | CatHarmfulQA | 0.9726 | 0.8980 | 0.9009 |
| DeepSeek/A6000 | HarmBench-Std | 0.9859 | 0.8869 | 0.8725 |
| Qwen/A6000 | AdvBench | 0.9863 | 0.8868 | 0.8971 |
| Qwen/5880 | CatHarmfulQA | 0.9336 | 0.8596 | 0.8536 |

**Mixed-positive family transfer** (Table 2): Unseen wrappers PAIR and CodeChameleon achieve AUROC up to 1.0000 and consistently > 0.96. The strongest results use AdvBench + AutoDAN as source; StrongReject + AutoDAN source is harder but still AUROC > 0.95.

**Privacy boundary** (Tables 3–4):
- Prompt inversion: Token F1 = 0.1601, ROUGE-L = 0.1194, zero exact sensitive-field recovery, 0/54 LLM-based sensitive-fact hits.
- Coarse-attribute probing: For acute/recent and high severity, LOSO AUROC drops below 0.45 (random-split performance driven by dataset bias). Oncology-related shows residual LOSO AUROC of 0.746 but low high-confidence precision (0.516).

**Deployment overhead**: Online annotation interface ~2.7% overhead; current nsys-based trace collection ~17.8% overhead (future work to reduce via custom telemetry).

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: High. Repurposing GPU micro-architectural side channels from offensive (MoEcho-style privacy attacks) to defensive (safety auditing) is creative and well-motivated. The insight that aggregated prefilling-phase telemetry loses enough information to preserve privacy but retains enough for harmful/benign separation is non-obvious.
- **Rigor**: Strong. Leave-one-target-out cross-domain evaluation, multiple jailbreak wrappers, two models, two GPU types, and dedicated privacy stress tests with both random-split and LOSO controls. The EDA in Section 4.2 (UMAP vs LDA) provides clean evidence that harmful signals exist beyond syntactic structure.
- **Reproducibility**: Moderate. Requires NVTX instrumentation, nsys profiling, and access to specific GPU hardware. The paper provides code abstractions (Listing 1) but the full pipeline depends on GPU-specific profiling infrastructure.
- **Significance**: Potentially high impact for AI governance. The three-party (user–provider–regulator) model maps well to real regulatory frameworks (e.g., EU AI Act). If productionized with lower-overhead telemetry collection, this could enable privacy-preserving compliance auditing of deployed MoE models.
- **Red flags**: (1) Evaluated only on relatively small MoE models (DeepSeek-V2-Lite, Qwen1.5-MoE-A2.7B) — unclear how well the approach scales to models with hundreds of experts. (2) The 17.8% trace-collection overhead from nsys is non-trivial for production. (3) Non-adaptive attacker assumption: gray-box adversaries could potentially craft prompts that mimic benign routing distributions. (4) The oncology-related attribute showing LOSO AUROC of 0.746 suggests some coarse information does leak through aggregated telemetry, which warrants caution.

## Strengths

- Elegant conceptual framing: turns a side-channel "bug" into an auditing "feature."
- Privacy-by-design: aggregated prefilling telemetry is structurally lossy, preventing exact prompt reconstruction with zero sensitive-field recovery.
- Strong empirical results across multiple dimensions: cross-domain (4 benchmarks), cross-wrapper (3 jailbreak types), cross-model (2 MoE architectures), cross-hardware (2 GPU types).
- Thorough privacy evaluation with both reconstruction attacks and attribute probing, including LOSO controls that expose dataset bias.
- Lightweight linear detector keeps the system interpretable and avoids overfitting to source-domain patterns.

## Limitations

- **MoE-only**: The approach fundamentally depends on sparse expert routing; dense LLMs cannot be audited this way. Even among MoE models, the telemetry signal depends on routing granularity.
- **Prefilling-phase only**: Decoding-phase traces are excluded (per-token traces would leak privacy, as shown by MoEcho with >91% token reconstruction). This limits applicability to streaming/multi-turn settings.
- **Scalability untested**: Only tested on ~2.7B–16B parameter MoE models. Large-scale models (DeepSeek-V3 with 256+ experts, Llama 4 Scout) may exhibit different routing dynamics.
- **Production overhead**: ~17.8% overhead from nsys profiling is significant; production deployment requires custom lightweight telemetry collection.
- **Adaptive attacks**: The paper acknowledges gray-box adversaries could use surrogate models to craft prompts mimicking benign routing patterns — this remains an open challenge.
- **Privacy is empirical, not formal**: The guarantee relies on inversion experiments with specific attackers, not information-theoretic bounds. Stronger adversaries with side information may extract more.

## Relevance

与 MoE 模型安全审计和 AI 治理直接相关。RouteScan 提出的非侵入式审计范式对云服务商和监管机构在保护用户隐私前提下进行安全合规具有重要意义。对 MoE 模型部署的安全监控、越狱检测和监管框架设计均有参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does RouteScan perform on larger-scale MoE models (DeepSeek-V3 with 256 experts, Llama 4 Scout with 16 experts) — does the higher expert count improve or degrade discriminability?
- Can adaptive adversaries with surrogate models craft prompts that produce benign-looking expert routing patterns, and how robust is the linear detector to such evasion?
- What is the minimum prompt length for reliable detection — do short adversarial prompts (e.g., single-sentence jailbreaks) produce sufficient routing fingerprint variation?
- How could the framework be extended to multi-turn conversations where harmful intent emerges gradually across turns?
- Is a formal differential-privacy-style bound possible for the aggregated telemetry, going beyond empirical inversion tests?
- What custom GPU telemetry collection mechanisms (beyond nsys) could reduce the 17.8% overhead to production-viable levels?
