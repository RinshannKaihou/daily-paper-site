LLMs encode the semantic operations behind NLI labels — not just the labels themselves — in partially separable internal subspaces.

Is Inference Mediated by Distinct Semantic Structures in LLMs? A Mechanistic Interpretation
Nura Aljaafari·Marco Valentino·André Freitas ·2026-05-25
mechanistic-interpretability natural-language-inference activation-steering representation-engineering transformer-internals
Key Metric
84.8–99%
Transformation-type subspace decoding accuracy across four open-weight decoder models, well above random-subspace baselines.
Why This Matters
Shifts focus from label-level to operation-level interventions, opening new paths for mechanistic analysis and targeted model control.
Critical Edge
High decodability may reflect artificial simplicity of controlled pairs — generalization to naturalistic inference is unproven.
Methodology

The authors design controlled NLI datasets where premise-hypothesis pairs differ by exactly one semantic transformation (negation, antonymy, synonym substitution). For each transformation type:

  1. Collect layer-wise activations from the model for both original and transformed pairs.
  2. Compute difference vectors and apply SVD to extract a low-rank subspace capturing the transformation effect.
  3. Train linear probes to classify which transformation was applied (84.8–99% accuracy).
  4. Test causal relevance via activation steering: adding operation-direction vectors to intermediate activations and measuring changes in predicted labels.
  5. Perform cross-operation steering to assess whether operation subspaces are independent or interfere with each other.
Results & Highlights
  • Subspace decodability: 84.8–99% accuracy across models, well above random baselines.
  • Causal shift: Activation steering along operation directions causally shifts model predictions — these subspaces are not merely correlational.
  • Model variance: Steerability varies across models; some are more amenable to directional intervention.
  • Structured interference: Cross-operation steering affects predictions for related but distinct operations.
  • Dissociation found: Subspace selectivity (isolation of an operation's effect) is decoupled from cross-operation independence (whether operations interfere).
Authors & Credibility

Nura Aljaafari, Marco Valentino, and André Freitas — likely affiliated with a UK/European NLP group with prior work on explainable NLI and logical inference.

  • Principled experimental design with controlled stimuli and proper baselines.
  • Claims are calibrated: authors acknowledge partial encoding rather than asserting full operational representation.
Strengths
  • Clean experimental design with controlled stimuli isolating individual semantic transformations.
  • Proper baseline comparisons (random subspaces) rather than relying solely on high decoding accuracy.
  • Causal validation via activation steering, not just correlational probing.
  • Calibrated claims — authors acknowledge partial encoding and variability.
Limitations
  • Controlled NLI pairs may not reflect the complexity of natural inference; generalization to real-world text is uncertain.
  • Abstract does not disclose the four model architectures, transformation types, or dataset sizes.
  • Steerability varying across models is reported but not explained — architectural or training differences may confound results.
  • Overlap between operation subspaces suggests representations are not fully factorized, limiting precision of operation-level control.
Questions for Further Exploration
  1. How do operation-level subspaces generalize from controlled NLI pairs to naturally occurring inference contexts?
  2. Does scaling model size increase or decrease the separation between operation subspaces?
  3. Can operation-level steering be combined with existing representation engineering techniques for more targeted model control?
  4. Are the identified subspaces robust to prompt formatting changes or domain shifts?
  5. What role does fine-tuning on NLI-specific data play in the formation of operation-level subspaces?
