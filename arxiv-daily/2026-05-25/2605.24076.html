Causality as the Statistical Conscience of Artificial Intelligence

Ernest Fokoué — Rochester Institute of Technology

A Statistical Necessity Theorem formally proves that any predictor exploiting spurious features inevitably fails under distribution shift — and more data cannot save it. The paper unifies four causal estimation frameworks into a single family and diagnoses hallucination, reward hacking, and distribution shift as symptoms of the same disease: causal blindness.

Three AI Failure Modes — Causal Diagnosis
  • LLM Hallucination — Confounding in reward signal → Remedy: Backdoor adjustment
  • RLHF Reward Hacking — Spurious mediators (e.g. response length) → Remedy: Instrumental variables
  • Distribution Shift — Reliance on spurious features → Remedy: Invariant Risk Minimization
Critical Assessment

Moderate — clean formalization of known intuitions; CSE unification is taxonomic
Rigor
Strong — precise theorems, correct proofs, proper citations

Strong — pure NumPy, explicit SCMs, code provided
Significance
Moderate-High — compelling case; but synthetic-only experiments
Strengths
  • Crisp theorem-driven narrative connecting statistical theory to pressing AI failures
  • CSE unification table is an elegant pedagogical contribution
  • Honest about theory-practice gap — lists five genuinely open statistical problems
  • Excellent writing — one of the clearest expositions of Pearl → IRM → DML available
Limitations
  • No real-world experiments — all demonstrations use synthetic data with known SCMs
  • Causal structure assumed, not discovered — DAG G is given; causal discovery is the hard step
  • Scalability gap — no path from moderate-dimensional DML to billion-parameter LLMs
