## 一句话总结

本文提出 SoundnessBench 基准，用 1,099 个从 ICLR 论文中提取的研究提案测试 12 个前沿 LLM 能否在执行实验前判断研究方案的方法论合理性，发现当前模型普遍存在"乐观偏差"：标准提示下 74% 的低质量提案被误判为合理，而激进提示则导致过度拒绝，表明 LLM 尚无法作为独立的科学研究"守门人"。

## TL;DR

SoundnessBench is a benchmark of 1,099 ML research proposals reconstructed from ICLR submissions, designed to test whether frontier LLMs can judge methodological soundness before experiments are run. Across 12 models, the authors find a pervasive optimism bias (74% false-positive rate under standard prompting) that shifts to over-conservatism under aggressive prompting (high-soundness recall collapses to 36.1%), demonstrating that current LLMs are not reliable standalone first-gate evaluators for scientific rigor.

## 核心贡献

1. **SoundnessBench 基准数据集**：包含 1,099 个经过验证的机器学习研究提案，覆盖 ICLR 2022-2026 的 16 个子领域，是目前最大规模的同类数据集之一。

2. **高精度数据构建流程**：五阶段流程包括专家一致性过滤、原子声明审计（Atomic-Claim Auditing）和结果遮蔽（Outcome Masking），确保提取的提案可追溯到源论文。

3. **广泛的混淆因子控制**：针对数据污染、论文标识符识别、表面特征启发式、年份/子领域/写作质量分层以及对抗性内容注入进行了系统性控制实验。

4. **揭示"乐观-脆弱"权衡**：系统性地量化了 12 个前沿 LLM 在研究提案评估中的乐观偏差及其对提示策略的极度敏感性。

## Key Contributions

- **SoundnessBench**: A curated benchmark of 1,099 validated ML research proposals spanning 16 subfields from ICLR 2022-2026, processed from over 35,209 submissions and 137,940 expert reviews.
- **Rigorous curation pipeline**: Five-step pipeline with reviewer-agreement filtering (mean confidence >= 3, soundness std < 0.15), near-verbatim proposal extraction using Gemini 2.5 Pro, and retrieval-backed atomic-claim verification (threshold 0.7).
- **Comprehensive robustness controls**: ICLR 2026-only split for contamination control, identifier removal, surface-feature baselines, year/subfield/writing-quality slicing, and adversarial injection of methodological flaws.
- **Empirical characterization of the optimism-fragility tradeoff**: Systematic evaluation of 12 frontier LLMs documenting that optimism bias is not a small-model artifact (larger Qwen3.5 models become more permissive) and persists across multiple control conditions.

## Methodology

### Benchmark Construction

The benchmark is built in five stages:

1. **Data Collection**: Papers from the ICLR corpus (2022-2026), covering 16 ML subfields (RL, NLP, Generative Models, Optimization, CV, etc.). Desk-rejected papers are removed. Only papers with strong reviewer agreement (mean confidence >= 3, soundness score std < 0.15) are retained.

2. **Label Assignment**: Labels derived from reviewer soundness sub-scores (not overall ratings or acceptance decisions). High soundness: mean score >= 3; Low soundness: mean score <= 2; ambiguous middle scores excluded.

3. **Proposal Extraction**: Research proposals (abstract, related work, risk factors, hypothesis, experiment design) extracted from source PDFs using Gemini 2.5 Pro with source-grounded prompts, excluding experimental results and outcome cues. Format follows The AI Scientist-v2.

4. **Verification Audit**: Atomic-claim decomposition followed by BM25 retrieval (chunk size 3000, overlap 200, top-k=3) and LLM-based verification against source papers. Threshold: 0.7. 66.93% of candidates pass, yielding 1,099 proposals (458 low-soundness, 641 high-soundness).

5. **Final Dataset Assembly**: 1,099 proposals with auditable hypothesis-experiment designs.

### Evaluation Protocol

- **Models**: 12 frontier LLMs including GPT-4o, GPT-5.4-Mini, GPT-5.4, Claude-Opus-4.6, Claude-Sonnet-4.6, Gemini-2.5-Pro, Gemini-3-Flash, Gemini-3.1-Pro, Qwen3.5-27B, Qwen3.5-122B-A10B, LLaMA-3.3-70B, Kimi-Linear-48B-A3B.
- **Two prompting regimes**: (1) Standard prompt -- structured justification then classification; (2) Aggressive prompt -- stricter instruction to default to low soundness unless clearly strong.
- **Setup**: max_tokens=8192, temperature=0.2. Open-source models deployed on 2x NVIDIA H200 GPUs via vLLM.

### Robustness Controls

- **Label/leakage audit**: 92.3% of leakage checks match expected "No" answer; 84.6% of label-validity checks match expected "Yes."
- **Contamination control**: ICLR 2026-only split shows similar optimism bias (77.47% vs. 73.88% false positives).
- **Identifier removal**: Changes aggregate confusion matrix by ~1 percentage point.
- **Surface-feature baselines**: Simple baselines (length, experiment count, risk factors) over-reject in the opposite direction from LLMs.
- **Adversarial injection**: Injecting severe hypothesis-experiment mismatches into 100 high-soundness proposals drops GPT-5.4's approval rate from 77.0% to 1.0%.

## Results & Highlights

### Main Findings

| Metric | Standard Prompt | Aggressive Prompt |
|--------|----------------|-------------------|
| Mean Low-Soundness Recall | 26.0% (74.0% FPR) | 80.1% (19.9% FPR) |
| Mean High-Soundness Recall | 91.8% | 36.1% |
| Mean Macro F1 | 54.9 | 49.3 |

- **Optimism bias is pervasive**: 9/12 models label more than 70% of low-soundness proposals as high soundness under standard prompting. LLaMA-3.3-70B and GPT-4o are the worst offenders (98.0% and 94.5% false positives).

- **Aggressive prompting over-corrects**: GPT-5.4 and GPT-5.4-Mini move to near-always-low behavior (0% and 0.2% high-soundness recall). Qwen3.5-122B-A10B rejects 95.6% of low-soundness but identifies only 16.8% of high-soundness.

- **Scale does not help**: Across six Qwen3.5 models (2B-122B), larger models become more optimistic under standard prompting (low-soundness recall decreases from 31.0% at 2B to 19.2% at 35B). No monotonic improvement under aggressive prompting either.

- **Best single-model performance**: GPT-5.4 achieves 69.7% Macro F1 under standard prompting but collapses to 29.5% under aggressive prompting. Claude-Opus-4.6 is more stable (60.5% vs. 68.6%).

- **Robustness**: The optimism pattern persists across contamination controls, identifier removal, surface-feature analyses, year/subfield/writing-quality slices, and adversarial injection experiments.

## Critical Assessment

This work addresses a genuinely important and under-explored problem: whether LLMs can serve as pre-execution filters for research quality. The benchmark construction is rigorous, with multiple layers of verification and an impressive array of control experiments. The central finding -- that LLMs exhibit an optimism bias that cannot be easily prompted away -- is both practically relevant and intellectually interesting.

However, several points warrant caution:

1. **Label proxy limitation**: The ground truth relies on ICLR reviewer soundness sub-scores, which are noisy, subjective, and based on full papers rather than proposals alone. The authors acknowledge this but it fundamentally limits the benchmark's claims. Reviewers may assign low soundness scores for reasons (poor writing, missing details) that would not be visible in a well-structured proposal.

2. **Binary classification is reductive**: Reducing soundness judgment to a binary high/low classification loses important nuance. Real scientific triage involves continuous assessment, domain-specific criteria, and cost-benefit reasoning.

3. **Prompt space is undersampled**: Only two prompts (standard and aggressive) are tested. The authors note this limitation, but it leaves open the possibility that better-calibrated prompts or few-shot approaches could improve results substantially.

4. **Temporal contamination concerns**: Although the ICLR 2026-only split is used as a control, the paper does not fully address whether pre-training on scientific corpora (including papers discussing the reviewed works) could influence model behavior in subtler ways.

5. **Evaluation is classification-only**: The paper does not explore whether models could provide useful calibrated scores, nuanced feedback, or whether their justifications contain valuable information even when binary judgments are wrong.

## Strengths

- **Exceptionally thorough benchmark construction**: Five-stage pipeline with atomic-claim verification is a model for future benchmark work.
- **Comprehensive robustness controls**: The multiple control experiments (contamination, identifiers, surface features, adversarial injection) substantially strengthen the conclusions.
- **Scale analysis across Qwen family**: Isolating scale effects within a single model family is a clean experimental design choice.
- **Honest framing**: The paper is commendably clear about what SoundnessBench measures (recoverable proposal-stage soundness) and does not claim to predict full-paper review outcomes.
- **Timely and practically relevant**: As AI scientist agents proliferate, understanding their blind spots in research quality assessment is crucial.

## Limitations

- **ICLR-only scope**: All proposals come from ICLR, limiting generalizability to other venues, other ML sub-communities, and especially other scientific domains entirely.
- **Reviewer scores as ground truth**: Reviewer soundness scores are imperfect proxies with known noise, inter-reviewer disagreement, and potential biases (e.g., toward well-known authors or institutions).
- **Proposal extraction introduces potential drift**: Despite near-verbatim extraction claims, using an LLM (Gemini 2.5 Pro) for extraction introduces its own potential errors and biases.
- **No human ceiling established**: The human audit is preliminary. Without a proper inter-annotator agreement study and human baseline, it is hard to contextualize model performance.
- **Binary evaluation misses nuance**: The 1-4 soundness scale is collapsed to binary (<=2 vs. >=3), discarding information and potentially amplifying noise at the boundary.
- **Static snapshot**: The benchmark is fixed in time. As LLMs improve and training data changes, the benchmark may become stale or contaminated.

## 相关性

本文对于关注 **AI 自动化科研** 的研究者具有高度相关性。它直接揭示了当前 AI Scientist 系统（如 AI Scientist-v2、Agent Laboratory）的一个关键短板：无法在执行实验前可靠地判断研究方案的方法论合理性。这对于设计更安全的自主研究代理、开发 LLM 辅助的同行评审工具、以及理解 LLM 在科学判断中的局限性都有直接参考价值。此外，该基准对于研究 LLM 校准（calibration）、阿谀奉承行为（sycophancy）和提示脆弱性的研究者也具有借鉴意义。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. **Can fine-tuning or RLHF specifically targeting scientific soundness judgment improve model calibration, or is the optimism bias a more fundamental limitation of current architectures?**

2. **How would models perform on a continuous (rather than binary) soundness scoring task, and would ranking-based evaluation reveal different patterns?**

3. **Does the optimism bias persist when models evaluate proposals from domains outside ML (e.g., biology, physics), and is it amplified or reduced for unfamiliar domains?**

4. **Could ensemble methods (e.g., combining multiple models or prompting strategies) produce more stable and calibrated soundness judgments than any single model?**

5. **What is the human expert ceiling on this task -- how well can experienced ML researchers predict reviewer soundness scores from proposals alone, and how does it compare to the best model performance?**
