Why This Matters
Quantifies how English safety training fails in Chinese — a gap no machine-translated benchmark could expose.
Critical Edge
No LLM evaluation was performed — the dataset's ability to expose model weaknesses remains entirely unvalidated.

Methodology

All 1,897 prompts were hand-crafted by a native Chinese speaker deeply embedded in Chinese social-media culture — not algorithmically generated or translated. The dataset spans four high-stakes domains:

Each instance carries: original prompt, English translation, intent type, obfuscation type, risk level, gold-action label, safety rationale, and annotator notes. A 3-class gold-action schema (REFUSE / SAFE-REDIRECT / RESPOND) replaces the crude safe/unsafe binary, explicitly encoding the pragmatic distinctions a well-aligned model must handle.

Results & Proposed Protocol Resource Paper

No LLM experiments are reported. As a resource paper, the contribution is the dataset and annotation framework itself. The authors propose a standardized three-metric evaluation protocol for future benchmarking:

  • Gold Action Accuracy — overall correctness against the 3-class schema
  • Domain Accuracy — per-domain breakdown to catch uneven safety alignment
  • Obfuscation-Stratified Accuracy — accuracy conditioned on each evasion tactic, revealing which cultural obfuscation strategies most effectively bypass filters

The authors recommend baseline comparisons pitting an English-dominant model (e.g., GPT-4o) against a Chinese-native model (e.g., Qwen or ChatGLM) to isolate the language-alignment gap.

Assessment

Strengths

  • Grounded in authentic Chinese internet culture, not machine translation
  • 3-class gold-action schema is finer-grained than the safe/unsafe binary
  • 9-category obfuscation taxonomy has clear empirical and cultural roots
  • Explicit discussion of epistemic independence in safety evaluation
  • Strong ethics coverage: release governance, annotator wellbeing, sensitive-domain protocols

Limitations

  • Self-harm domain is only 29.4 % annotated — the highest-stakes area is the least complete
  • Single annotator constructed all prompts; limited regional, generational, and platform coverage
  • No inter-annotator agreement (IAA) reported
  • Zero LLM evaluation results — the benchmark's discriminative power is unproven
  • Relatively small scale (1,544 fully annotated instances)
  • Gated access via application, limiting reproducibility
  • "Other" obfuscation category is 17.7 % of self-harm data, signaling an incomplete taxonomy

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How large is the safety gap between Chinese-native models (Qwen, ChatGLM) and English-dominant models (GPT-4o) on ChiSafe-PAS, and how does it vary across obfuscation strategies?
  2. Can the 9-class obfuscation taxonomy be expanded into a hierarchical structure to capture combinatorial evasion tactics?
  3. Will inter-annotator agreement be lower for pragmatic categories (tone softening, slang) than for lexical ones (pinyin, emoji)?
  4. Can a safety classifier fine-tuned on this dataset generalize to unseen obfuscation combinations — critical for staying ahead of rapidly evolving internet evasion?
  5. Is labeling all sarcasm as SAFE-REDIRECT justified? Should the boundary between sarcasm and genuinely harmful content admit finer gradations?
