> **一句话总结：** 本文通过固定点处的传输-信息不等式和Fisher光滑性条件，严格证明了坐标上升变分推断(CAVI)算法在Wasserstein距离下的指数收敛性，并将理论应用于贝叶斯高斯混合模型（揭示了后验相变现象）、高维Probit回归和Logistic回归（Jaakkola-Jordan算法），给出了首个后两者的收敛速率保证。

> **TL;DR:** This paper proves that CAVI (Coordinate Ascent Variational Inference) contracts exponentially in Wasserstein distance under two conditions: (1) Fisher-smoothness of the optimality maps (related to cross-smoothness of the target potential), and (2) transport-information inequalities at the fixed points (weaker than log-Sobolev inequalities). The main theorem (Theorem 1) gives a contraction rate L_{1|2}L_{2|1}/(lambda_{1|2}lambda_{2|1}) that is sharp for Gaussian targets (recovering the spectral norm of the correlation matrix). A data-augmentation variant (Theorem 4) enables convergence analysis when one block is discrete or non-smooth. Applications to Bayesian Gaussian Mixtures reveal a posterior phase transition governing convergence; Probit regression yields a global rate equal to the Bayesian fraction of missing information; and Logistic regression with Polya-Gamma augmentation provides the first known convergence rate for the Jaakkola-Jordan algorithm. High-dimensional scaling results show the rate stays bounded away from 1 as n,p -> infinity under standard priors.

## 核心贡献

- 建立了CAVI在Wasserstein距离下的严格指数收敛理论，仅需固定点处的传输-信息不等式和Fisher光滑性条件，显著弱于现有的强对数凹假设
- 提出了数据增强框架下的收敛定理（Theorem 4），允许辅助变量空间为离散或非光滑（如高斯混合模型中的超立方体{0,1}^n），仅需对参数分布的收敛性做分析
- 揭示了高斯混合模型中CAVI收敛的后验相变现象：收敛速率受后验模式分离度控制，模式分离越大收敛越快；平衡混合(p=1/2)在数据充分时全局收敛不可能
- 给出了Probit回归CAVI的全局收敛速率（等于贝叶斯缺失信息比），以及Logistic回归Polya-Gamma增强下Jaakkola-Jordan算法的首个收敛速率
- 证明了高维情形下(n,p→∞)收敛速率始终严格小于1，不随维度退化
- 提供了坐标下降算法在交叉光滑性和梯度二次增长条件下的新型收敛分析（附录A），可能具有独立价值
- 发现了"精度-收敛对偶性"：CAVI收敛越快，变分近似质量也越高

## Key Contributions

- Establishes exponential Wasserstein contraction for CAVI under transport-information inequalities (weaker than log-Sobolev) and Fisher-smoothness of optimality maps, significantly relaxing the strong log-concavity assumptions of prior work
- Introduces a data-augmentation theorem (Theorem 4) that enables convergence analysis for the parameter-of-interest block alone, without requiring conditions on the auxiliary variable space -- critical for discrete augmentations (Gaussian Mixture Models) and Polya-Gamma augmentations
- For Gaussian Mixtures: identifies a posterior phase transition r = (tau^2)/(tau_0 + tau*n) * lambda_max(sum y_i y_i^T) governing global vs. local convergence; shows local convergence rate depends positively on posterior mode separation, paralleling EM theory
- For Probit Regression: proves global exponential convergence with rate equal to lambda_max((Q_0 + X^T X)^{-1} X^T X), the Bayesian fraction of missing information
- For Logistic Regression (Jaakkola-Jordan algorithm): provides the first convergence rate guarantee, with explicit high-dimensional scalings under g-prior and p-scaled priors
- Shows the convergence rate stays bounded away from 1 as n,p -> infinity (e.g., lim r = 1/(1+1/g) < 1 for g-prior in Probit regression), using random matrix theory
- Identifies an "accuracy-convergence duality": conditions ensuring fast CAVI convergence also guarantee high-quality variational approximations, and vice versa
- Provides a novel coordinate descent convergence analysis under cross-smoothness and gradient quadratic growth (Appendix A), potentially of independent interest

## Methodology

The paper develops its theory in three layers:

**Layer 1: General CAVI contraction (Theorem 1).** For a target distribution pi = e^{-f} on Z x B, CAVI iterates optimal distributions mu_star[nu] and nu_star[mu] that minimize KL divergence coordinate-by-coordinate. The paper defines two key conditions:

1. **Fisher-smoothness** (Definition, Eq. 5): The optimality maps satisfy I(gamma_star[qho] || gamma_star[qho']) <= L^2 W_p^2(qho, qho'). When p=1, this reduces to Lipschitz conditions on the cross-gradients of f (Lemma 3).

2. **Transport-information inequality** (Definition, Eq. 6): lambda^2 W_p^2(qho, gamma) <= I(qho || gamma) at the fixed points. This is weaker than log-Sobolev inequalities, which are weaker than strong log-concavity (Lemma 2), and tensorizes for product distributions.

The contraction rate is L_{1|2}L_{2|1} / (lambda_{1|2}lambda_{2|1}), requiring the "small interaction" condition that cross-smoothness products are dominated by curvature products. This rate is sharp for Gaussians, recovering the spectral norm of the correlation matrix (Example 1).

**Layer 2: Data augmentation (Theorem 4).** When Z houses auxiliary variables and only beta matters inferentially, convergence of (nu_k) alone can be established under Fisher-smoothness of the composed map nu_star[mu_star[.]] and transport-information inequality for nu_star only. This bypasses any requirement on Z's geometry or mu_star's curvature.

**Layer 3: Applications.** Each application uses the appropriate theorem layer:

- **Gaussian Mixtures (Proposition 5):** Uses Theorem 4 with p=2 since z in {0,1}^n is discrete. The rate involves a sech^2 term that activates with large posterior mode separation, creating a phase transition.
- **Probit Regression (Proposition 6, Corollary 7):** Uses Theorem 1 with strongly log-concave conditional posteriors. The rate equals the Bayesian fraction of missing information. High-dimensional analysis uses Bai-Yin's random matrix theory.
- **Logistic Regression (Proposition 8, Corollary 9):** Uses Theorem 4 with Polya-Gamma augmentations. The rate involves the function phi(c) = tanh(c/2)/(2c) and its derivative. High-dimensional bounds use phi(x) <= 1/4 and spectral mapping.

## Results & Highlights

- **Theorem 1 is sharp**: For Gaussian targets, the contraction rate recovers exactly the spectral norm of the correlation matrix A = Q_{11}^{-1/2} Q_{12} Q_{22}^{-1/2}, matching known optimal rates from Bhattacharya et al. (2025)
- **Gaussian Mixture phase transition**: In the balanced case (p=1/2), global convergence requires r = (tau^2)/(tau_0 + tau*n) * lambda_max(sum y_i y_i^T) < 1, which corresponds to the posterior being unimodal (beta=0 being a strict local maximum). Under correct model specification with growing n, r > 1 almost surely, so global convergence is impossible but local convergence holds with rate depending on mode separation
- **Probit rate is global and dimension-free**: The rate lambda_max((Q_0 + X^T X)^{-1} X^T X) < 1 always holds. Under g-prior with i.i.d. design, lim r = 1/(1+1/g) < 1 as n,p -> infinity
- **Logistic regression is faster than Probit**: The bounds suggest CAVI converges faster for logistic link than probit link, corroborated by numerical experiments. Under g-prior, the limiting rate is lim r* <= 1/(1+4/g), which is strictly smaller than the probit rate
- **Accuracy-convergence duality**: The conditions ensuring CAVI convergence (small cross-smoothness relative to curvature) are the same conditions ensuring the variational approximation quality is high
- **Coordinate descent bonus**: The proof technique extends to standard coordinate descent under cross-smoothness and gradient quadratic growth, providing a new convergence result under assumptions different from classical block-smoothness analyses

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: High.** This is the first work to establish Wasserstein contraction for CAVI using functional inequality-based assumptions rather than strong log-concavity. The transport-information inequality framework is strictly weaker than log-Sobolev (and hence strong log-concavity), enabling analysis of models like Gaussian Mixtures and Logistic Regression that are not strongly log-concave. The data-augmentation theorem (Theorem 4) is a clever technical contribution that handles discrete/non-smooth auxiliary variable spaces. The accuracy-convergence duality observation is elegant.

- **Rigor: Very high.** The proofs are short and sharp (Theorem 1's proof is essentially a clean 4-line argument chaining Fisher-smoothness and transport-information inequalities). The connection to Gaussian correlation matrices (Example 1) provides a precise benchmark. The high-dimensional scaling results use standard random matrix theory (Bai-Yin theorem) rigorously.

- **Reproducibility: Moderate.** The paper is primarily theoretical with minimal code/experiments. The 3 numerical figures show convergence plots that could be reproduced from the closed-form CAVI updates given in the paper. However, the theoretical results are the main contribution, and these are self-contained with complete proofs.

- **Significance: High for theoretical foundations.** Provides convergence guarantees for widely-used algorithms (CAVI, Jaakkola-Jordan) under significantly weaker assumptions than prior work. The phase transition insight for Gaussian Mixtures connects variational inference convergence to the statistical structure of the posterior in a novel way.

- **Red flags / Concerns:**
  - The analysis is restricted to the **two-block** (m=2) setting. Multi-block CAVI (m > 2) is acknowledged as future work but is the practically more relevant setting for complex models.
  - The convergence is **local** in general (requires epsilon-close initialization). The global convergence results are limited to specific models (Probit, Gaussian case with r < 1). For Gaussian Mixtures, local convergence is the typical scenario.
  - The **transport-information inequality** at fixed points, while weaker than log-concavity, may still be hard to verify for complex models beyond those studied. The paper provides good sufficient conditions (log-Sobolev, strong log-concavity) and references Lyapunov methods, but practical verification for arbitrary models remains non-trivial.
  - The paper mentions multiple fixed points are possible, but the theory does not address **which** fixed point the algorithm converges to from a given initialization, beyond saying it depends on initialization.
  - The **Fisher-smoothness** condition requires uniform bounds (sup over z or beta), which could be restrictive for unbounded spaces, though the paper handles this via the structure of specific models.

## Strengths

- Significantly relaxes assumptions compared to prior CAVI convergence work (no strong log-concavity needed)
- Sharp rates demonstrated for Gaussian targets; the framework connects naturally to classical concepts (correlation matrices, Bayesian fraction of missing information)
- The data-augmentation theorem is particularly valuable for practical Bayesian computation where auxiliary variables are computational tools
- Clean, short proofs -- the core argument is elegant and transparent
- Explicit convergence rates for three important model classes with interpretable expressions
- High-dimensional scaling guarantees using random matrix theory
- The accuracy-convergence duality provides practical guidance: if CAVI converges fast, the approximation is also good
- The coordinate descent result in Appendix A is a bonus contribution

## Limitations

- Restricted to two-block CAVI; multi-block extension is non-trivial and left for future work
- Local convergence guarantees in general; global results require model-specific analysis
- The Fisher-smoothness and transport-information conditions may be difficult to verify for models beyond those studied
- Limited empirical validation (3 figures showing convergence curves); no comparison with alternative algorithms or speed benchmarks
- Does not address the question of which fixed point is reached from a given initialization
- The theory does not directly suggest practical strategies for choosing the variational family or improving convergence speed
- No discussion of stochastic or mini-batch CAVI variants

## Relevance

本文为变分推断的核心算法CAVI提供了基于Wasserstein距离的严格收敛保证，对于理解变分推断在贝叶斯混合模型、分类模型中的收敛行为和设计可靠的高维贝叶斯推断方法具有重要理论价值，尤其适合关注变分推断理论和贝叶斯计算方法学的研究者。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can the two-block analysis be extended to multi-block CAVI (m > 2) while maintaining the functional inequality-based approach?
- For Gaussian Mixtures with r > 1, what is the basin of attraction of each fixed point, and can the theory guide initialization strategies?
- Can the Fisher-smoothness condition be verified for more complex models (e.g., hierarchical models, deep generative models)?
- How do the Wasserstein contraction rates compare with KL divergence contraction rates from prior work (Bhattacharya et al. 2025)?
- Can the transport-information inequality framework be applied to stochastic variational inference or black-box VI methods?
- Is there a practical diagnostic based on the cross-smoothness constants L_{1|2}, L_{2|1} to predict CAVI convergence speed before running the algorithm?
- The coordinate descent result (Appendix A) under cross-smoothness -- does it improve upon existing rates for specific optimization problems of practical interest?
