# When, why, and how do diffusion posterior samplers fail? A finite-sample lens

**Authors:** Benjamin A. Burns (Georgia Institute of Technology), Sara Fridovich-Keil (Georgia Institute of Technology)
**Published:** 2026-05-28 | **arXiv:** [2605.30330](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30330)
**Code:** [github.com/voilalab/diagnosing-posterior-sampling](https://github.com/voilalab/diagnosing-posterior-sampling)

---

## 一句话总结

本文从有限样本视角系统分析了扩散后验采样器中似然近似误差如何导致后验分布失败，揭示了欠/过估计中间时间步后验展布是造成早停敏感性、模态权重失准和幻觉现象的根本原因，并证明这些失败仅需多模态先验即可触发，无需非线性测量或多模态后验。

## TL;DR

This paper introduces a finite-sample lens to diagnose why diffusion posterior samplers fail. By treating the prior as a discrete empirical distribution over training samples, the authors can compute exact posteriors at any intermediate diffusion timestep. Using this ground-truth proxy, they show that popular moment-matching approximations (DPS, PiGDM, TMPD) systematically under- or over-estimate the posterior spread at intermediate timesteps, causing three failure modes: early stopping sensitivity, inaccurate mode weighting, and hallucination -- all of which can arise from multimodal priors alone, even with linear measurements and unimodal posteriors.

## 核心贡献

1. **有限样本后验采样框架：** 将先验分布视为有限训练集上的离散经验测度，使得中间时间步的后验分布可以解析计算。该近似随训练集增大而以 O(N^{-1/2}) 的蒙特卡洛速率收敛至真实后验。
2. **精确的似然和后验计算：** 在有限样本体制下，似然 p(y|xt) 为高斯混合，后验 p(xt|y) 亦为加权高斯混合，可在任意时间步直接求值（命题4-6），无需迭代去噪。
3. **三类失败模式的系统识别：** 高斯近似（PiGDM、TMPD）过早锁定先验模态，导致后验协方差过估和先验幻觉；Dirac近似（DPS）的超参数 ζ 引起似然/先验权重失衡，导致模态遗漏和幻觉。
4. **反直觉的根因发现：** 失败不依赖于非线性测量模型或多模态后验，仅凭多模态先验配合不准确的中间时间步后验展布即可触发。

## Key Contributions

- Introduces a **finite-sample posterior sampling framework** where the prior is an empirical discrete measure, yielding closed-form posterior density access at all intermediate timesteps (Propositions 4-6).
- Shows convergence at the **expected Monte Carlo rate** O(N^{-1/2}), requiring more samples as t approaches zero but providing accurate posteriors at moderate timesteps with reasonable N.
- Provides the **first finite-sample analysis for understanding posterior sampling** (prior work used finite-sample regimes only for unconditional/conditional generation, not posterior sampling diagnostics).
- Identifies that **Gaussian approximations (PiGDM, TMPD) overinflate posterior covariance** when the prior is multimodal, causing sampling from prior-inconsistent modes. **Dirac approximations (DPS)** explore low-probability regions with incorrect mean/variance at intermediate timesteps.
- Demonstrates that tuning the DPS hyperparameter zeta can partially correct mode weighting but cannot always avoid hallucination of either prior-consistent or likelihood-consistent modes.

## Methodology

The authors develop a rigorous analytical framework built on three pillars:

### Finite-Sample Diffusion Framework (Section 4)
- Replace the continuous prior p_pr with an empirical distribution over N i.i.d. samples. Under the VP-SDE, the marginal p_t^N becomes a Gaussian mixture with N components (Proposition 4).
- The denoiser p_{0|t}^N is a discrete measure with analytically computable weights w_i(x_t, t) based on proximity of each training sample's expected position to the current noised state.
- The likelihood p_{y|t}^N reduces to a weighted Gaussian mixture over training data (Proposition 5), computable for any (even nonlinear) forward model A.
- The posterior p_{t|y}^N is also a Gaussian mixture with weights combining the measurement likelihood of each training point and the diffusion-time proximity (Proposition 6).

### Analyzed Methods
The paper studies four moment-matching methods:
1. **sigma-DPS** (Chung et al., 2023): Dirac denoiser approximation with unmodified noise prefactor.
2. **zeta-DPS** (Chung et al., 2023): Dirac approximation with a tuned step-size replacing the noise prefactor -- effectively approximating the Gaussian measurement likelihood with a Laplace distribution.
3. **PiGDM** (Song et al., 2023): Gaussian denoiser with inflated scalar covariance r_t^2 * I, only applicable for affine A.
4. **TMPD** (Boys et al., 2024): Gaussian denoiser with the true denoiser covariance C_{0|t} (KL-optimal), also affine A only.

### Experimental Testbed (Section 5)
- **Prior types:** Discrete (multi-atom), Gaussian (unimodal), Gaussian mixture (GMM, multimodal).
- **Forward models:** Linear A(x) = Ax + b, and nonlinear A(x) = x^2, A(x) = x^3.
- **Ground truth:** For discrete/Gaussian/GMM priors with affine A, all scores and posteriors are derived analytically. For nonlinear A with continuous priors, the finite-sample regime serves as proxy.
- **Evaluation:** 400-point time grid, 600-point spatial grid, total variation (TV) distance as primary metric, with heatmap visualizations of the full reverse process.

## Results & Highlights

### Convergence Validation (Figure 1)
- The finite-sample posterior converges at the expected O(N^{-1/2}) Monte Carlo rate across all three prior types (discrete, Gaussian, GMM).
- Accuracy degrades near t=0 for fixed N (the discrete measure cannot approximate a continuous density), but remains excellent at moderate timesteps with N=4096.

### Gaussian Approximation Failures (Figures 2-3)
- **PiGDM and TMPD** both overestimate posterior covariance when the prior is multimodal, producing samples from prior modes inconsistent with measurements.
- At intermediate timesteps, samples drift to low-probability regions where the prior score dominates, effectively behaving like unconditional generation.
- Even TMPD (which uses the true denoiser covariance) suffers from this, because the single-Gaussian denoiser approximation cannot capture the multi-component structure of the true denoiser.

### Dirac Approximation Failures (Figures 2, 4)
- **sigma-DPS** regularly samples from prior modes absent in the true posterior and/or fails to capture true posterior modes entirely.
- **zeta-DPS** with tuned zeta can partially correct mode weighting and reduce hallucination, but cannot always avoid either prior-consistent or likelihood-consistent hallucination.
- In some cases, overly large zeta causes mode collapse to a single hallucinatory mode.

### Nonlinear Forward Models (Figure 4)
- For A(x) = x^2 and A(x) = x^3, only DPS methods apply (Gaussian approximations are restricted to affine A).
- sigma-DPS misses modes, incorrectly weights modes, and hallucinates prior-inconsistent modes across all tested nonlinear settings.
- The finite-sample regime accurately tracks the (intractable) true posterior even for nonlinear A.

### Key Insight
Even when terminal-time samples look reasonable (and existing benchmarks would deem a method "accurate"), the intermediate-time distributions can be severely wrong. This is dangerous because (a) learned score networks may be uncontrolled in the low-probability regions the sampler visits, and (b) downstream tasks using intermediate states (e.g., OOD detection) are affected.

## Critical Assessment

### Strengths

- **Fills a fundamental gap.** While the field has focused on proposing new posterior samplers and benchmarking them at terminal time, this paper provides the first rigorous framework for understanding *why* they fail at intermediate timesteps -- which is where the errors originate.
- **Mathematically rigorous.** Three formal propositions with complete proofs establish the finite-sample framework. Analytical derivations for discrete, Gaussian, and GMM priors are thorough.
- **Counterintuitive and impactful finding.** The result that multimodal priors alone (with linear A and unimodal posteriors) can cause failure challenges the common belief that nonlinear measurements are the primary difficulty. This has direct implications for MRI, CT, and other applications with inherently multimodal image priors.
- **Comprehensive experimental design.** Testing across discrete/Gaussian/GMM priors with linear/quadratic/cubic forward models, with both analytical ground truth and finite-sample proxies, provides strong empirical support.
- **Practical diagnostic value.** The framework is agnostic to likelihood approximation type and forward model, making it immediately usable by practitioners to evaluate any posterior sampler.

### Limitations

- **One-dimensional experiments only.** All experiments are in 1D with small training sets (up to N=4096). The authors acknowledge that computational cost grows significantly for high-dimensional imaging, where both the training set and state space are much larger.
- **No learned scores tested.** All experiments use analytically known unconditional marginal scores. The authors note that issues should amplify with learned scores (due to uncontrolled behavior in low-probability regions), but this conjecture is not tested.
- **No proposed fix.** The paper diagnoses failures but does not propose corrections. The finite-sample posterior is positioned as a diagnostic tool, not a practical sampler -- it cannot generalize beyond the training set at small t.
- **Limited scalability discussion.** The paper does not quantify the constant factors in the N^{-1/2} convergence rate or provide guidance on required N for specific problem classes and dimensionalities.
- **zeta tuning is manual.** The DPS hyperparameter zeta is chosen by grid search with visual inspection (Appendix E), which limits reproducibility and scalability of the DPS comparison.

## Relevance (相关性)

本文对理解和改进扩散模型在成像逆问题中的后验采样具有基础性价值。其分析框架和失败模式识别对于研究条件生成模型、贝叶斯推理和计算成像的研究者具有直接参考意义。核心发现——多模态先验本身即可导致后验采样失败——对所有使用扩散先验进行不确定性量化的应用（如MRI重建、CT成像、相位恢复等）具有重要的警示作用。此外，中间时间步后验失真对下游任务（如分布外检测、主动测量采集）的影响也是一个值得关注的实践问题。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How does the finite-sample diagnostic scale to high-dimensional imaging problems (e.g., 256x256 MRI)? What is the effective minimum N needed for useful posterior access at moderate timesteps?
2. The paper shows that learned scores amplify the identified failure modes -- can this be quantified? Would score regularization in low-probability regions mitigate the issue?
3. Can the finite-sample posterior itself be used as a correction signal? For instance, could one train a likelihood score approximation to match the finite-sample ground truth at intermediate timesteps?
4. Do the identified failure modes extend to other generative posterior samplers (flow matching, consistency models, guided diffusion with classifier networks)?
5. The paper focuses on VP-SDE -- how do the results transfer to other diffusion formulations (VE-SDE, flow matching, rectified flow)?
6. For real imaging applications where the prior is a large-scale pre-trained diffusion model, what practical workflow would use the finite-sample diagnostic? Would one need to store the full training set?
