Diffusion models achieve near-minimax optimal sample complexity for learning distributions on unions of low-dimensional subspaces

Diffusion Models Are Statistically Optimal for Learning Low-Dimensional Multi-Modal Distributions

Jingda Wu & Changxiao Cai ICML 2026 · May 28
Statistical Theory Diffusion Models Minimax Optimality
Key Metric
O(ε−(k∨2))
Sample complexity for ε-accurate sampling in Wasserstein-1 distance — depends only on intrinsic dimension k, not ambient dimension d.
Why This Matters

Explains why diffusion models succeed on high-dimensional multi-modal data: they automatically adapt to intrinsic low-dimensional structure, with no smoothness assumptions.

Critical Edge

The kernel-based score estimator is a theoretical proof device, not a practical algorithm — the gap to neural network estimators used in practice remains unbridged.

Methodology

The target distribution p* lives on a union of M linear subspaces with maximum intrinsic dimension k. The proof constructs an explicit score estimator and bounds its convergence.

1
Subspace recovery. Use O(M²k log n) samples for clustering to recover each subspace Vi and assignment.
2
Score estimation. Per subspace, estimate the low-dimensional tangent score via truncated Gaussian kernel density estimation with clipping regularization.
3
Sampling. Plug estimated scores into the continuous-time reverse SDE with early stopping τ = n−2/k and terminal time T = log n.
st(x) = Σi wt(i,x) · st(i,x)
Normal component: closed-form Gaussian  |  Tangent component: ki-dimensional estimation

Results

Two theorems govern the pipeline, plus empirical validation on synthetic data.

Result Rate
Score estimation (Thm 1) L2 error ≤ C · dM³ · (1/Nt + σk∨2 / t(k∨2)/2+1) polylog(N)
Sampling (Thm 2) W1(p*, pŷ) ≤ C · dM3/2 · n−1/(k∨2) polylog(n)
Minimax lower bound n−1/(k∨2) (Chewi et al. 2024)
Experiments d=48, M=128, k=3, N=50,000 confirm scaling with k, not d

The rate exponent depends only on intrinsic dimension k. The prefactor carries a linear d dependence the authors conjecture may be a proof artifact.

Strengths

  • First minimax-optimal sample-complexity result for diffusion models on multi-modal low-dimensional distributions
  • Weak assumptions: subgaussian tails only; no smoothness, bounded density, or log-concavity required
  • Elegant normal-tangent score decomposition cleanly separates known (Gaussian) from estimated (tangent) components
  • Constructive proof with explicit algorithm and numerical validation of theoretical scaling
  • Honest discussion of the neural-network gap and a clear roadmap for future work

Limitations

  • Kernel-based estimator is a theoretical proof device, not a practical training algorithm
  • Assumes exact subspace recovery, which is a strong idealization
  • Linear subspaces only; real data lives on nonlinear manifolds
  • Noiseless support assumption (data exactly on subspaces, not near them)
  • Single synthetic experiment (d=48, M=128, k=3); limited empirical breadth
  • Continuous-time analysis; discretization error under mild conditions is deferred

Questions for Further Exploration

1 Can the normal-tangent decomposition framework extend to nonlinear manifolds (not just linear subspaces) while preserving minimax optimal rates?
2 How can we construct neural network approximators with intrinsic-dimension-dependent complexity so that practically trained diffusion models enjoy similar guarantees?
3 When the number of subspaces M is large or unknown, does the subspace clustering step become a sample bottleneck? Can it be made adaptive?
4 Does the discretization error in practical samplers (DDPM, DDIM) still depend only on intrinsic dimension under these weak assumptions?
5 When data concentrates near subspaces rather than exactly on them, how does noise tolerance affect the convergence rate?
