Native-language accuracy lags English by ~0.50 on Indian languages; code-mixed inputs close the gap to ~0.05.
Evaluating Cross-lingual Knowledge Consistency in Code-Mixed vis-à-vis Indian Languages using IndicKLAR · Mazumder, Pathak, Kodali, Joshi et al. · 2026-05-28 multilingual-LLM indian-languages code-mixing cross-lingual-consistency
Key Metric
0.50 → 0.05
Accuracy gap to English: native-script queries versus code-mixed inputs, averaged across 18 Indian languages and 9 open-weight models.
Why This Matters
TinT is a lightweight, no-retraining fix that makes LLMs fairer for 1 billion+ Indian-language speakers.
Critical Edge
All evidence comes from open-weight models up to 14B with synthetic code-mixing; proprietary LLMs and real-world variability remain untested.

Methodology

IndicKLAR Benchmark

Extends KLAR-CLC to 18 Indian languages (of 22 scheduled) with 11 code-mixed variants verified by native speakers. Each factual query is aligned in three forms: English, native script, and code-mixed (English content words interleaved into native grammar).

Prompting Strategies

  • Baseline — direct query in native script.
  • 2-Step — translate to English / code-mixed / Roman, then answer.
  • 1-Step — generate translation alongside answer.
  • TinT (proposed) — single-prompt internal conversion; emits only the final answer, no intermediate text.

Setup

9 models (Llama 3.1/3.2, Gemma 3, Qwen 2.5; 1B–14B), greedy decoding, 3-shot ICL, A100 80GB.

Flip Point: A consistent boundary between incorrect and correct predictions was observed along the native → code-mixed → English spectrum, holding for both input-driven and model-driven (TinT) trajectories.

Strengths

  • Unprecedented scale: 18 Indian languages + 11 code-mixed variants.
  • Actionable insight — code-mixed inputs nearly match English without model changes.
  • TinT is lightweight, single-step, and faster than multi-step baselines.
  • Rigorous stats (McNemar), scaling analysis, and generalization to a second benchmark.
  • Flip Point concept is a novel lens on multilingual knowledge organization.

Limitations

  • Open-weight models only (1B–14B); proprietary LLMs untested.
  • Code-mixed variants are synthetically constructed — organic real-world mixing may differ.
  • Factoid-only evaluation; reasoning, summarization, and generation are out of scope.
  • Only 7 of 18 languages have code-mixed variants (Dravidian under-represented).
  • Single-seed reporting; no multi-seed variance.
  • Odia outlier identified but not resolved.

Relevance

English: For researchers tracking LLM fairness in low-resource language ecosystems, IndicKLAR provides a much-needed evaluation scaffold. TinT's simplicity and zero-retraining design make it immediately deployable in production multilingual pipelines. The discovery that surface-level code-mixing can approximate English performance opens a practical path for improving equity without infrastructure overhaul.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does TinT scale to 70B+ and 405B models? Does the flip point shift toward native-script inputs?
  2. Is the code-mixed advantage rooted in tokenization efficiency? Native-script tokenization may be a hidden bottleneck.
  3. Can fine-tuning teach a model internal conversion paths superior to TinT's prompt-level directive?
  4. Do these findings transfer to non-Indic pairs — e.g., Chinese-English or Arabic-French code-mixing?
  5. For severely under-resourced languages like Odia, are architectural interventions (beyond data and script fixes) necessary?
