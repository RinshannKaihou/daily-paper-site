Median-of-three interpolators achieves optimal O(dγ/ε) sample complexity, strictly outperforming any proper learning algorithm.

The Interplay Between Interpolation and Aggregation in Regression: Optimal Sample Complexity Høgsgaard, Larsen & Zou 2026-05-28 Learning Theory Regression Sample Complexity
Key Metric
O(dγ/ε)
Sample complexity for median-of-three interpolators — matching the lower bound up to constants.
Proper learners need Ω(dγ·log(1/ε)/ε) — a log(1/ε) factor worse.
Why This Matters
Explains why ensemble methods work: even the simplest aggregation (median-of-three) provably beats any single-hypothesis learner in sample complexity — validating bagging and random forests in theory.
Critical Edge
Analysis assumes noiseless realizable regression; extension to agnostic settings with label noise remains an open and potentially much harder problem.

Methodology

Realizable regression framework (Attias et al., 2023). Performance measured by cutoff loss Lγ = P[|h(x) − y| > γ].

  • γ-graph dimension (dγ): largest shatterable set where the class realizes all match/far-from-witness patterns.
  • Lower bounds: Construct hard distributions on γ-graph-shattered sets with asymmetric mass. Proper / interpolating aggregation rules cause aligned errors on unseen points.
  • Upper bound: Leave-one-out argument — median errs only if ≥2 of 3 interpolators err. Independence + improved bound (ln2(n/dγ) vs ln2(n)) yields tight O(dγ/n) rate.
  • Separation proof: Coupon-collector argument shows proper learners need an extra log(1/ε) factor.

Strengths

  • Complete theoretical picture — lower bounds, matching upper bound, and strict separations form a cohesive landscape.
  • Algorithmic simplicity — the optimal method (median-of-three) is trivially implementable.
  • Clear hierarchy: proper learning < aggregation of interpolators < general learning.
  • Genuine technical innovation — improved interpolator bound and elegant Cantor-class constructions.
  • Accessible presentation — proof overviews in Section 3 convey key ideas without full technical burden.

Limitations

  • Realizable setting only — noiseless labels assumed; agnostic extension is open and likely much harder.
  • Cutoff-loss focus — direct Lp analysis may yield different optimality conclusions.
  • No computational guarantees — interpolators themselves may be expensive to compute.
  • Gap to general learning remains — when dγ >> dγ,OIG, median-of-three is suboptimal vs. one-inclusion-graph algorithms.
  • Abstract hard instances — constructed hypothesis classes may not resemble natural learning problems.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can these results extend to the agnostic (noisy-label) setting? Does median aggregation retain its advantage?
  2. For natural hypothesis classes (neural nets, linear predictors), how large is the gap between γ-graph and γ-OIG dimensions?
  3. Does median-of-three optimality extend to other losses (e.g. L2) or aggregation rules (e.g. weighted average)?
  4. Are there computationally efficient infinite or non-interpolating aggregation algorithms for the hard classes?
  5. Does the improved interpolator bound (ln2(n/dγ) vs ln2(n)) have downstream impact on online learning or multi-class classification?
