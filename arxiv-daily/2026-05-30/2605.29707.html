Domino decouples causal modeling from autoregressive drafting, then injects it back with a lightweight head. Result: up to 5.49× end-to-end speedup on Qwen3.
Domino: Decoupling Causal Modeling from Autoregressive Drafting in Speculative Decoding Huang et al. · SJTU / HUST / Fudan / Huawei · 2026-05-28
Speculative Decoding LLM Inference SGLang
Key Metric
5.49×
End-to-end speedup on Qwen3-8B (Transformers backend, greedy decoding). Peak single-task: 7.92× on GSM8K.
Why This Matters
Domino's decoupled causal injection on parallel compute offers a practical leap for LLM serving teams, especially at high concurrency where EAGLE-3 degrades below baseline.
Critical Edge
Only evaluated on Qwen3-4B and Qwen3-8B; generalization to Llama, Gemma, Mistral is untested, and block size is fixed at 16 with no scaling analysis.

Methodology

1
Parallel draft backbone (DFlash)
One non-autoregressive forward pass produces base logits for an entire 16-token block using frozen target-model features.
2
Domino head — causal encoder
A lightweight GRU (hidden dim 1024) sequentially consumes preceding draft-token embeddings to build a causal state Si−1.
3
Low-rank logit correction
Concatenate hidden state + causal state, project through a bottleneck (r=256) with SiLU, and add the residual in logit space — never re-running the full LM head.
4
Base-anchored training
Teacher forcing with ground-truth prefixes; joint loss annealed from base-only to final-logit to prevent backbone collapse.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Elegant insight — decoupling causal modeling from autoregressive execution cleanly addresses a genuine quality-cost tradeoff.
  • Logit-space correction — avoids re-applying the expensive full LM head; the parallel backbone can still function independently.
  • Thorough evaluation — same-data ablations, multiple backends, concurrency sweep (2–32), and detailed latency breakdowns.
  • Practical impact — SGLang integration shows real serving relevance, with gains holding at high concurrency.

Limitations

  • Model coverage — only Qwen3-4B/8B tested; Llama, Gemma, Mistral generalization is unknown.
  • Fixed block size — block size = 16 only; no analysis for 8, 32, or 64, which matters for deployment tuning.
  • Framework scope — mainly adapted to SGLang; vLLM and TensorRT-LLM compatibility not evaluated.
  • Hardware specificity — all on A100; balance of head overhead vs. backbone cost may shift on H100 or consumer GPUs.
  • Training cost — overhead of Domino head training and curriculum is not reported.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does Domino head overhead grow linearly with block length? Would GRU sequential processing become a bottleneck at block size 32 or 64?
  2. How sensitive is performance to the λt annealing schedule? Would cosine annealing outperform linear?
  3. Can logit-space residual correction be combined with EAGLE-3's tree-structure verification for even higher acceptance rates?
  4. Does the relative advantage hold on larger models (Qwen3-32B/72B), where speculative decoding typically yields greater absolute gains?
  5. What is the motivation for GRU over a small Transformer encoder? Could a stronger causal encoder further improve draft quality?
  6. Can Domino transfer across checkpoints or fine-tuned variants of the same model without retraining?
