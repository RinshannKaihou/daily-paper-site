First sharp row-wise ℓ₂ perturbation bound for matrix completion enables HTE estimation from panel data without propensity scores.

Improved Guarantees for Heterogeneous Treatment-Effect Estimation via Matrix Completion Mehrotra, Tran, Vu & Zampetakis (Yale) — et al. 2026-05-30
Causal Inference Matrix Completion Panel Data Perturbation Theory
Key Metric
Õ(√(1/n + n/m²))
Row-wise ℓ₂ error bound for HTE estimation; approaches Õ(1/√n) when m ≫ √n, near-optimal for row averages.
Why This Matters
Bridges causal inference and matrix completion by enabling per-unit treatment effect estimates where prior theory only guaranteed averages.
Critical Edge
The estimator has only been validated on synthetic data, with no demonstration on real-world panel datasets where practical benefits would matter most.

Methodology

The authors cast heterogeneous treatment effect estimation as a matrix completion problem over n units and m time periods.

1
Model: Outcome Y = L* + τ* ⊙ W + noise, where L* is low-rank baseline, τ* is treatment effects, W is assignment.
2
Completion: Estimate combined matrix M* = L* + τ* ⊙ W via nuclear-norm regularization or SVD from partial observations.
3
Row average: HTE for unit i is the row mean of recovered τ*. This demands a row-wise guarantee, not merely global Frobenius control.
4
Analysis: Leave-one-out decomposition balances matrix completion error against bias from non-uniform assignments, yielding the first sharp row-wise bound.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • First sharp row-wise ℓ₂ perturbation bound — a fundamental contribution to matrix perturbation theory.
  • Elegant bridge between causal inference and matrix completion.
  • No propensity score knowledge required.
  • Transparent about assumptions and scope.
  • Strong author team with deep random matrix expertise.

Limitations

  • Heavy reliance on exact low-rank structure; approximate low-rank robustness unexplored.
  • Experiments limited to synthetic data; no real-world validation.
  • Bound meaningful only when m ≫ √n; short panels get weaker guarantees.
  • No comparison against causal forests, R-learners, or BART.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How do guarantees degrade under approximate low-rankness (fast-decaying singular values)?
  2. How does the estimator compare empirically with causal forests or R-learners on real data?
  3. Can the framework extend to continuous or multi-valued treatments?
  4. What happens when treatment effects evolve dynamically over time?
  5. How does computational scalability behave for very large panels (n, m in millions)?
  6. Can similar sharp bounds be obtained for row-wise ℓ∞ norms?
