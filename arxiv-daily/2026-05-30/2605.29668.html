GRASP: Gated Regression-Aware Skill Proposer for Self-Improving LLM Agents

Johannes Moll, Jean-Philippe Corbeil, Jiazhen Pan, Martin Hadamitzky, et al. · 2026-05-28 · LLM Agents Self-Improvement Clinical AI

Why This Matters

Regression-gated skill editing generalizes beyond clinical tasks and reveals a parameter-free knowledge distillation effect across models.

Critical Edge

The “no parameter updates” framing understates the inference cost: ~440 agent calls per batch, roughly 3× more than simple baselines.

Methodology

Three stages per training batch, capacity-bounded and reversible.

Propose → Validate → Commit
  • Failure-Driven Proposal. An LLM classifier labels failing traces with mechanism-specific tags. Failures are grouped by label; the skill writer cycles from most to least frequent, receiving failing traces, passing samples, active skill summaries, and other failure labels. Returns ADD, MODIFY, or REMOVE edits. ADD is blocked at capacity unless paired with a REMOVE.
  • Regression-Aware Selection. A balanced probe (N/2 previously-failing + N/2 previously-passing) is drawn from earlier batches. Acceptance requires net improvement and a hard regression budget: R(c) ≤ R₀. If regressions occur, a contrastive revision narrows the trigger or adds a guard clause.
  • Skill Representation. Structured Markdown with YAML frontmatter (trigger conditions, behavioral rules, verification steps, contrastive examples). Libraries are versioned and reversible.

Training: 5 epochs, batch size 48, 36-example probe, 4 candidate edits per batch. Best validation checkpoint restored.

Results & Highlights

  • Every base model improves by 17.2–40.3 points on MedAgentBench.
  • OOD margins are even larger: gpt-oss-120b reaches 56.3% vs 31.3% best baseline (+25.0).
  • Learning is stable and near-monotonic; baselines regress as context accumulates.
  • Cross-model transfer is asymmetric and unique to GRASP: strong writers improve weak executors, but not vice versa.
  • Non-clinical: strong gains on ALFWorld (+28.4) and WebShop (+20.6); OS Interaction shows no significant improvement.

Strengths

  • Conceptually simple yet powerful regression-aware gate
  • Exceptional ablation with matched-compute controls
  • Honest reporting of mixed results on MedAgentBench-v2
  • Novel asymmetric cross-model transfer finding
  • Compact libraries: ~5 skills, 5.6k tokens
  • Rigorous statistics (bootstrap CIs, Cohen's d)
  • Clear applicability boundaries stated upfront

Limitations

  • Validation probe dominates cost (~440 calls/batch, ~3× baselines)
  • Probe compute not budget-matched across methods
  • Clinical eval limited to English FHIR with exact-match scoring
  • Skills are benchmark-tuned, not vetted medical expertise
  • Narrow applicability: structured, verifiable tasks only
  • No principled method to detect writer-executor mismatch
  • GPT-4.1/GPT-5.4 show no benefit on MedAgentBench-v2

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Would adding the acceptance gate to baselines (ExpeL, Evo-MedAgent, SkillX) narrow the gap, or is the gate tied to GRASP's specific representation and failure-grouping?
  2. How does probe size scale with task complexity? Is there a minimum below which the gate becomes unreliable?
  3. Can the asymmetric cross-model transfer be predicted a priori — e.g., by measuring a "procedural capability gap" between writer and executor?
  4. How do skills degrade in non-stationary environments where task distributions shift?
  5. What is the overhead of human-in-the-loop skill review? Could clinician vetting improve reliability, and at what cost in iteration speed?
  6. Could a two-layer skill architecture — abstract procedural patterns plus interface-specific implementations — improve cross-environment transfer?
