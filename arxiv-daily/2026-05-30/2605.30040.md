## 一句话总结

本文揭示了LLM按token计费模型中存在的"信任悖论"——审计只能检查提供商自己提供的证据，而提供商恰恰最有动机篡改这些证据——并通过对三种审计框架（Coln、PALACE、基于鞅的统计审计）的实证攻击，证明提供商可在不被发现的情况下将计费token数虚报50.85%至1,469%，呼吁采用可信执行环境或密码学证明等提供商不可控的验证机制。

## TL;DR

The paper formalizes the "trust paradox" in LLM per-token billing: audits must trust artifacts from the provider who has the strongest financial incentive to manipulate them. Through empirical attacks on three recent auditing frameworks (Coln, PALACE, martingale-based statistical auditor), the authors show a dishonest provider can inflate billed tokens by 50.85% (visible reasoning) to 1,469% (hidden reasoning) without detection. The structural fix requires provider-independent verification such as TEE attestation, cryptographic proofs of inference, or third-party re-execution.

## 核心贡献

- **"信任悖论"的形式化定义**：LLM提供商为保护IP、安全性和用户隐私而隐藏模型、分词器和执行细节，但正是这种不透明性使得审计无法获取独立证据，只能依赖提供商提供的制品进行一致性检查——而这些制品恰恰是提供商最有动机篡改的
- **对三种审计框架的系统性攻击**：
  - **Coln**（Sun et al. 2025a）：通过复用或伪造语义上合理的推理块，平均虚报1,469%而不触发检测
  - **PALACE**（Wang et al. 2025）：通过改写答案风格（+30.30%）、触发token（+82.4 tokens）、训练数据投毒（+38.58%）和条件后门（+247.16%）四种方式突破其两个关键假设
  - **鞅统计审计**（Yefremova et al. 2023）：即使推理文本完全可见，利用tokenization歧义仍可实现50.85%虚报
- **经济影响量化**：按当前前沿推理模型价格，100美元的诚实账单可在同一查询上被虚报至约1,569美元
- **指出解决方案方向**：需要从"对提供商可控制品的一致性检查"转向"提供商独立的验证机制"

## Key Contributions

- **Trust Paradox Formalization**: The opacity that creates the need for auditing (IP protection, safety, user privacy) is the same opacity that prevents auditors from collecting independent evidence. Every audit rests on trust assumptions that sit precisely on the artifacts a financially motivated provider is incentivized to manipulate.
- **Three Framework Attacks**:
  - **Coln**: Provider reuses or fabricates semantically plausible reasoning blocks to inflate reported reasoning length by 1,469% on average. Shorter traces admit larger relative inflation. The highest-scoring-block attack turns Coln's own similarity scores into an attacker target.
  - **PALACE**: Four attack modes break PALACE's two assumptions — (1) predictions from (P,A) are robust to provider control over A, and (2) provider-supplied auxiliary data is faithful. Attacks include answer-style rewriting (+30.30%), trigger-token appending (+82.4 tokens/82% success), targeted poisoning (10% of data, 47.16% of samples inflated by 38.58%), and conditional backdoor ("Think harder" trigger, 99.91% of triggered samples inflated, mean rate 247.16%).
  - **Martingale Statistical Auditor**: Even with visible reasoning, tokenization ambiguity (Monte Carlo estimation bias) creates systematic slack the provider can exploit, permitting 50.85% total inflation below detection threshold.
- **Structural Diagnosis**: The failure is not in any specific model, threshold, or estimator — it is that each audit replaces direct verification with a consistency check over provider-controlled artifacts.
- **Solution Direction**: Provider-independent verification through TEEs, cryptographic proofs of inference, or third-party re-execution.

## Methodology

The paper proceeds through a systematic adversarial analysis of three auditing frameworks:

**Threat Model**: An opaque LLM service where a user submits prompt P, receives answer A, and is billed per provider-reported token count m. Internally, the provider may generate hidden reasoning trace R. The user observes (P, A, m) but not the model, tokenizer, or execution.

**Attack on Coln** (Section 3): Coln audits hidden reasoning by checking semantic similarity between reasoning blocks and the final answer. The provider exploits this by (a) reusing existing high-scoring reasoning blocks as padding, (b) fabricating new blocks using the same embedding model Coln uses for verification, and (c) selecting blocks that maximize Coln's similarity scores. The provider has white-box access to Coln's embedding model and can search against it.

**Attack on PALACE** (Section 4): PALACE trains a domain-adapted auditor on provider-supplied auxiliary data to predict hidden reasoning-token counts from (P, A). The authors break two assumptions:
- Assumption 1 (inference-time): Appending trigger tokens (e.g., "boxed" +82.4 tokens, 82% success) or rewriting answer style (long variant +30.30%, verbose variant +16.62%) shifts the auditor's estimates upward.
- Assumption 2 (training-time): Poisoning 10% of auxiliary data inflates 47.16% of samples by 38.58%. A conditional backdoor with trigger "Think harder" inflates 99.91% of triggered samples with 247.16% mean rate, while leaving untriggered prompts near baseline.

**Attack on Martingale Auditor** (Section 5): The auditor estimates expected token counts via Monte Carlo sampling and aggregates deviations into a martingale statistic. Tokenization is not unique — distinct token sequences decode to the same string. Monte Carlo sampling introduces systematic negative bias (underestimation), creating slack the provider can exploit by reporting inflated counts that stay within the estimator's uncertainty range.

**Experimental Setup**: PALACE experiments use the official domain-adapted auditor on Glaive reasoning-v1-20m. Answer rewriting uses a separate Qwen3-14B model. The Coln experiments use semantic similarity scoring with matching heads.

## Results & Highlights

- **Coln: 1,469% average inflation** without triggering detection. Shorter reasoning traces admit larger relative inflation. The provider's own similarity scores become attack targets.
- **PALACE: Four attack modes** with success rates from 16.62% to 247.16%:
  - Answer-style rewriting (long): +30.30% predicted reasoning length
  - Trigger token "boxed": +82.4 tokens, 82% success rate
  - Targeted poisoning (10% of training data): 47.16% of sub-600-token samples inflated by 38.58%
  - Conditional backdoor: 99.91% of triggered samples inflated, 247.16% mean rate
- **Martingale auditor: 50.85% total inflation** below the detection threshold, even with fully visible reasoning traces
- **Economic impact**: $100 honest bill becomes ~$1,569 on the same query (Coln scenario, frontier reasoning model prices)
- **Key insight**: Upgrading embedding models or using learned discriminators does not fix the problem — the provider has white-box access to whatever discriminator is deployed. A decade of adversarial ML shows white-box access enables cheap evasion.

## Critical Assessment

The paper makes a structurally important argument that goes beyond individual attack demonstrations. The "trust paradox" framing is elegant and likely durable — it captures a fundamental tension between the opacity needed for commercial LLM services and the transparency needed for billing verification.

**Strengths of the analysis**: The paper does not merely show that specific auditors fail; it identifies the *common structural cause* across all three frameworks. The adaptive attack methodology (showing that natural defenses like pre-normalization or stronger embedding models fail under adaptive adversaries) follows best practices from the adversarial ML literature.

**Honest limitations**:
- The PALACE backdoor attack assumes the provider can control the auxiliary training data — while this is realistic per PALACE's design, it raises the question of whether PALACE's designers intended this data to be independently sourced (a limitation of PALACE, not the attack).
- The proposed solutions (TEEs, cryptographic proofs) receive only high-level discussion. The authors themselves acknowledge that proving a response contains n tokens "does not, by itself, certify the GPU time, memory use, energy consumption, batching overhead, or serving cost" — a significant gap.
- The threat model focuses exclusively on provider-side inflation; the symmetric problem of users understating usage or the interplay between inflation and model substitution is not explored.
- No real-world validation against actual commercial APIs (understandable but limits practical impact assessment).

## Strengths

- Identifies a fundamental structural problem in LLM billing that will only grow as reasoning models with hidden traces become dominant.
- The trust paradox is a clean, transferable theoretical contribution applicable beyond LLM billing.
- Systematic adversarial analysis of three frameworks provides comprehensive coverage of the current auditing landscape.
- Adaptive attack methodology demonstrates that incremental defenses (stronger embeddings, canonicalization, larger datasets) are insufficient.
- Clear economic quantification makes the case compelling for both researchers and policymakers.

## Limitations

- Proposed defenses are high-level directions without implementation or evaluation.
- No empirical validation against actual commercial LLM providers.
- Does not address whether major providers already employ undisclosed verification mechanisms.
- The TEE and cryptographic proof solutions face their own scalability challenges at frontier model inference throughput — these are acknowledged but not resolved.
- Does not explore semi-honest or negligent provider behaviors, only fully dishonest ones.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. What is the practical latency and throughput overhead of cryptographic proof-of-inference at frontier model scale (e.g., 405B+ parameters, streaming decoding)?
2. Can a tokenization standard with canonicalization rules eliminate the ambiguity that enables 50.85% inflation in the visible-reasoning setting, or is ambiguity inherent to subword tokenization?
3. How would a regulatory framework for verifiable LLM billing work — would it require model weight disclosure, standardized tokenizers, or something less invasive?
4. Is there a game-theoretic equilibrium where market pressure (reputation, competition) sufficiently deters token inflation, or is the opacity too great for market mechanisms alone?
5. Could federated or distributed auditing (multiple independent parties cross-checking each other's API bills) provide a practical near-term verification mechanism without requiring TEEs or cryptographic proofs?
