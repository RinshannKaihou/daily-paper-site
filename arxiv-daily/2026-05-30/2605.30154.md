## 一句话总结

RL2ML提出了一个连续的代理目标函数族，通过参数gamma在有限rollout预算下将强化学习目标平滑连接到最大似然目标及更远的区域，并给出了无偏梯度估计器的闭式解和基于方差感知的外层优化框架来选择最优gamma。

## TL;DR

This paper develops RL2ML, a one-parameter family of finite-rollout surrogate objectives for correctness-based RLVR that continuously interpolates between standard RL, maximum-likelihood-like training, and beyond-ML objectives. The key contribution is a closed-form exactly unbiased gradient estimator along with a principled one-dimensional outer optimization for selecting the objective parameter, showing that neither proximity to maximum likelihood nor population-level weighting alone determines the best training objective -- the choice depends jointly on the evaluation metric, local sensitivity, and estimator variance.

## 核心贡献

1. **RL2ML代理目标族**: 定义了以gamma为参数的截断代理目标族，在有限rollout预算N下，通过闭式系数提供精确无偏的梯度估计器。gamma=0恢复普通RL，gamma=1恢复最大似然（MaxRL），gamma>1进入"超临界"区域。
2. **群组级更新尺度(group-level update scale)**: 引入alpha_K概念，揭示了亚临界-超临界的转换边界（gamma=1），区分了总体级权重函数和实际随机更新的几何结构，这一区别在总体级目标表示中被隐藏。
3. **校准度量增益分析**: 通过有效学习率校准，证明最优gamma的选择取决于评估指标、局部敏感度和估计器方差的联合作用，而非仅仅取决于与最大似然的接近程度。
4. **一维外层优化**: 将gamma的选择形式化为方差感知的一维优化问题（带投影Newton步求解器），而非无约束的超参数调优。

## Key Contributions

1. **RL2ML surrogate objective family**: Defines a truncated power-likelihood surrogate objective parameterized by gamma, with closed-form coefficients yielding an exactly unbiased gradient estimator under finite rollout budget N. gamma=0 recovers RL, gamma=1 recovers MaxRL/ML, and gamma>1 enters the "supercritical" regime.
2. **Group-level update scale analysis**: Introduces the group-level update scale alpha_K to characterize how a rollout group is reweighted after observing its empirical success count, revealing a subcritical-supercritical transition at gamma=1 that is hidden by population-level objective notation alone.
3. **Calibrated metric-gain criterion**: After effective learning-rate calibration, shows that the best surrogate objective depends jointly on the evaluation metric, local prompt sensitivity, and estimator variance -- not on proximity to maximum likelihood alone.
4. **One-dimensional outer optimization**: Formulates gamma selection as a variance-aware one-dimensional optimization problem rather than an unconstrained hyperparameter, with a projected Newton step solver using digamma/trigamma derivatives.

## Methodology

### Problem Setup

The paper considers correctness-based RLVR where a language model samples N rollouts per prompt, receives binary correctness rewards r_i in {0,1}, and updates parameters. The single-sample success probability is p_theta(x), and the key quantity is K = sum of binary rewards in a rollout group. The score function S(x,z) = grad_theta log m_theta(z|x) forms the basis of REINFORCE-style estimation. When K >= 1, the average score over successful samples S_bar_K estimates the ML gradient direction.

### Bernstein Representation (Theorem 2.1)

Any K-only estimator (depending on rollout outcomes only through success count K) has a population-level weight that is a Bernstein polynomial of degree N-1. The paper shows that the population-level weight w_f(p) and the sample-level update scale alpha_K are distinct concepts that are often conflated:

- w_f(p) = expected gradient weighting (what the estimator optimizes in expectation)
- alpha_K = how strongly a realized rollout group is amplified/attenuated (what the optimizer actually sees)

### RL2ML Objective Family (Section 3)

The family is defined as J_gamma^RL2ML(x) = phi_gamma(p_theta(x)), where phi_gamma'(p) = p^{-gamma}. The antiderivative is phi_gamma(p) = (p^{1-gamma} - 1)/(1-gamma) for gamma != 1, and log(p) for gamma = 1.

For finite rollout budget N, the truncated surrogate objective is:

J_{gamma,N}^RL2ML(x) = sum_{k=1}^{N} ((gamma)_{k-1} / ((k-1)! k)) * pass@k(x)

The closed-form coefficient for the unbiased estimator (derived via Bernstein basis expansion and Chu-Vandermonde identity):

beta_{K-1}^{(gamma,N)} = Gamma(N+gamma)/Gamma(N) * Gamma(K)/Gamma(K+gamma)

The group-level update scale:

alpha_K^{(gamma,N)} = (K/N) * beta_{K-1} = Gamma(N+gamma)/Gamma(N+1) * Gamma(K+1)/Gamma(K+gamma)

A control variate form is also provided: subtracting (1/N) sum_i S_i preserves unbiasedness while reducing variance. For gamma=1, this recovers the MaxRL control-variate advantage A_i = (N/K)r_i - 1.

### Subcritical-Supercritical Transition (Proposition 3.3)

The ratio alpha_{K+1}/alpha_K = (K+1)/(K+gamma) reveals:

- gamma=0: alpha_K = K/N (ordinary RL, attenuates low-K groups proportionally)
- 0 < gamma < 1: K/N < alpha_K < 1 (subcritical, attenuates but less severely)
- gamma=1: alpha_K = 1 (MaxRL boundary, removes success-count dependence entirely)
- gamma > 1: alpha_K > 1 for K < N (supercritical, amplifies rare-success groups beyond MaxRL)

### Finite-Horizon gamma Selection (Section 4)

**Effective learning-rate calibration**: Changing gamma changes coefficients, update scale, and population-level weight simultaneously. To compare surrogate objectives rather than update magnitudes, the paper normalizes the expected update direction for each gamma to the same target update length c. The calibrated first-order metric-gain criterion is:

U(gamma) = A(gamma) / sqrt(B(gamma))

where A(gamma) = sum_x v_x'(p_x) * w_{gamma,N}(p_x) * l_x measures alignment with the validation metric, and B(gamma) = sum_x w_{gamma,N}(p_x)^2 * l_x measures squared norm of the expected update. Under prompt-separable approximation, V(theta_gamma^+) - V(theta) = c*U(gamma) + O(c^2).

**Key result (Corollary 4.2)**: If B'(1)/B(1) > 2*A'(1)/A(1), then U'(1) < 0, meaning a subcritical gamma slightly below 1 outperforms MaxRL. This provides a verifiable condition.

**Variance decomposition (Theorem 4.3)**: Total estimator covariance decomposes into:
1. Count variance: Var(a_K | x) * mu_x * mu_x^T (from random K)
2. Within-success variance: E[a_K^2/K | x] * Sigma_x (from trajectory score randomness)

Both scale quadratically with the update scale, so supercritical gamma carries explicit finite-rollout noise cost.

**Outer objective**: gamma* minimizes a variance-penalized objective over a candidate interval [gamma_min, gamma_max], solved via projected Newton steps using digamma/trigamma derivatives of the weighting function.

### Implementation Details (Appendix B)

The paper provides concrete Python code for Verl-style on-policy RLVR training, including:
- Coefficient table precomputation via gammaln
- Group advantage construction for both direct and control-variate forms
- Prompt-level success count collection from on-policy batches or calibration shards
- Projected Newton solver for gamma* selection

### Extension: Triad Family (Appendix C)

A more general triad family introduces truncation order M <= N, but M is pushed to boundary M=N unless stability or fidelity tradeoffs penalize larger M.

## Results & Highlights

- **No empirical experiments**: This is a purely theoretical paper. All results are mathematical theorems, propositions, and corollaries with formal proofs in appendices.
- **Key theoretical results**:
  - Theorem 2.1: Bernstein representation -- any K-only estimator's population weight is a Bernstein polynomial of degree N-1
  - Theorem 3.2: Exactly unbiased estimator for the truncated RL2ML objective with closed-form coefficients
  - Proposition 3.3: Four subcritical-supercritical regimes controlled by gamma
  - Proposition 4.1: Calibrated metric gain under fixed update length equals c*U(gamma) + O(c^2)
  - Corollary 4.2: Verifiable sufficient condition for when subcritical gamma improves over MaxRL
  - Theorem 4.3: Exact conditional variance decomposition into count variance and within-success variance
- **Central insight**: MaxRL (gamma=1) is a structural boundary and reference point, not a universal optimum. The best gamma depends on the specific evaluation metric, current prompt success distribution, and rollout budget.

## Critical Assessment

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical framework for understanding and improving the surrogate objectives used in correctness-based RLVR training. The mathematical development is sound and carefully organized, with clear separation between population-level expectations and finite-rollout update geometry. The identification of the subcritical-supercritical transition at gamma=1 is a genuinely useful conceptual contribution that clarifies what MaxRL actually does at the sample level -- it removes success-count dependence of the successful-score average, rather than simply being "closer to ML."

However, the paper has significant limitations. It is entirely theoretical with no experimental validation -- a notable gap given that the central claim is that the optimal gamma is metric-dependent and calibration-dependent. Without experiments, the reader cannot assess whether the theoretical advantages of RL2ML over MaxRL materialize in practice, nor whether the proposed one-dimensional outer optimization is stable and effective during training. The practical impact hinges on the accuracy of the prompt-separable approximation used in the finite-horizon analysis, which assumes gradient contributions from different prompts are orthogonal -- an assumption that is false for large-scale LLM training where prompts share virtually all model parameters. The assumption is acknowledged but its quantitative impact is not analyzed. Additionally, the paper does not address computational overhead of the gamma* selection procedure (requiring collection of per-prompt success counts, evaluation of digamma-based derivatives, and Newton iteration) or its sensitivity to the calibration shard size. The single-author status and lack of institutional affiliation, while not invalidating the work, reduce the prior on correctness of such extensive mathematical derivations. The paper's contribution over MaxRL is a generalization that is mathematically non-trivial but practically untested -- the key question of whether tuning gamma away from 1 provides meaningful gains in real LLM training remains open.

## Strengths

1. **Clean theoretical framework**: The Bernstein representation provides an elegant unifying view of K-only estimators, making the connection between population objectives and finite-rollout updates transparent.
2. **Precise conceptual separation**: The distinction between population-level weight w_f(p) and group-level update scale alpha_K is genuinely clarifying and resolves a real conflation in prior work.
3. **Constructive results**: Closed-form coefficients and exactly unbiased estimators make the framework immediately implementable, with concrete Python pseudocode provided.
4. **Honest about scope**: The paper clearly positions itself as theoretical and provides implementation details for future experimental work rather than overselling claims.
5. **Well-organized proofs**: Appendices are thorough (A.1 through A.8) and the main text maintains narrative flow despite heavy mathematical content.

## Limitations

1. **No experimental validation**: The entire framework is untested on any real language model or benchmark. Claims about metric-dependent gamma selection and improvements over MaxRL remain theoretical predictions only.
2. **Prompt-separable approximation**: The finite-horizon analysis assumes orthogonal gradient contributions across prompts, which is unrealistic for large models where nearly all parameters are shared across all prompts. The quantitative error from this approximation is not bounded.
3. **Computational overhead unclear**: The gamma* selection procedure requires collecting success counts, computing digamma/trigamma derivatives, and running Newton iteration at each (or periodic) training steps, but the cost and convergence properties are not analyzed.
4. **Limited novelty over MaxRL for practitioners**: The practical question is whether tuning gamma away from 1 provides meaningful gains that justify the additional complexity. Without experiments, there is no evidence that it does.
5. **Single author, no peer review**: The extensive mathematical derivations across 20 pages would benefit from independent verification.

## Relevance

这篇论文对理解RLVR训练中代理目标的设计具有理论指导意义。它揭示了有限rollout场景下总体级目标与实际随机更新之间的关键区别，为选择训练目标提供了原则性框架。对于从事语言模型RL后训练的研究者，该工作提供了：(1) MaxRL的理论推广与统一视角；(2) 理解不同reweighting策略（GRPO、RLOO等）效果的 Bernstein 表示框架；(3) gamma选择的结构化方法而非ad hoc调参。但由于缺乏实验验证，实际效果尚不确定，更适合作为理论参考而非直接指导实践的论文。
