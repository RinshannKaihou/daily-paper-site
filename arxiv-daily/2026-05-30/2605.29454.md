> **一句话总结：** 本文提出了一个覆盖数据准备、模型架构、训练算法和后训练（微调/机器遗忘）全流水线的MIA评估框架，形式化了审计/攻击两种威胁模型和三种互补指标，通过大规模实验揭示了MIA方法有效性高度依赖场景选择，并给出了基于过拟合程度的实用选型指南。

> **TL;DR:** This paper presents the first full-pipeline evaluation framework for Membership Inference Attacks (MIAs), spanning data, architectures, training algorithms, and post-training modules (fine-tuning, machine unlearning). It formalizes two threat models (Audit Mode with ground-truth labels vs. Attack Mode without) and three complementary metrics (Balanced Accuracy, TPR@low FPR, TNR@low FNR). Extensive experiments across 9 MIA methods, 6 architectures, 5 datasets, DP-SGD, fine-tuning, and unlearning show that MIA efficacy is highly sensitive to threat model, metric choice, and pipeline configuration. Metric MIA excels in audit mode, Quantile MIA is most stable in attack mode, RMIA dominates in low-FPR regimes, and BlindMI One-class is best for cross-model comparisons. A key meta-finding: the generalization gap is a consistent predictor of MIA vulnerability across all settings.

## 核心贡献

- 提出首个覆盖ML全流水线的MIA评估框架：数据准备、架构配置、训练算法（含DP-SGD）、后训练（微调+机器遗忘），每阶段独立分析其对隐私泄露的影响
- 形式化两种威胁模型：**审计模式**（有真实成员标签，用于评估最坏情况泄露）和**攻击模式**（无标签，仅用影子模型估计），并为每种MIA方法构造对应变体以实现公平比较
- 引入三种互补评估指标：平衡准确率（对称代价）、TPR@0.1%FPR（高置信泄露场景）、TNR@0.1%FNR（高召回筛选场景如版权检测）
- 统一框架公式化9种MIA方法为 `S_mem = C(v(x, f_θ; K))`，按表示函数、辅助知识、分类器三组件系统分类
- 揭示过拟合（泛化差距）是MIA脆弱性的核心驱动力，跨数据集、架构、训练算法均成立
- 为从业者提供明确的MIA选型指南：审计模式选Metric MIA，攻击模式选Quantile MIA，低FPR场景选RMIA，跨模型场景选BlindMI One-class

## Key Contributions

- Introduce the first systematic full-pipeline evaluation framework for MIAs, decomposing the ML lifecycle into data processing, architecture configuration, training algorithms (including DP-SGD), and post-training modules (fine-tuning and machine unlearning)
- Formalize two threat models -- **Audit Mode** (ground-truth membership labels available for hyperparameter optimization) vs. **Attack Mode** (relying solely on shadow models) -- and create cross-compatible variants for 9 MIA methods
- Employ three complementary metrics: Balanced Accuracy for symmetric costs, TPR@0.1%FPR for high-confidence leakage auditing, and TNR@0.1%FNR for high-recall screening (e.g., copyright enforcement)
- Unify 9 MIA methods under a single framework `S_mem = C(v(x, f_theta; K))`, systematically characterizing each by its representation function, auxiliary knowledge, and classifier
- Demonstrate empirically that the generalization gap is a consistent predictor of MIA vulnerability across datasets, architectures, and training algorithms
- Provide actionable practitioner guidelines: Metric MIA (Confidence/Modified Entropy) for audit mode, Quantile MIA for attack mode, RMIA for low-FPR regimes, BlindMI One-class for cross-model comparisons

## Methodology

The paper proposes a unified framework formalizing MIA as `S_mem = C(v(x, f_theta; K))` where `v` is the representation function (logit/posterior, scalar metric, calibrated metric, or loss landscape measure), `K` is auxiliary knowledge (shadow models or reference data), and `C` is the classifier (parametric, static threshold, adaptive threshold, or geometric).

**Threat Models:**
- **Audit Mode:** Auditor has oracle access to ground-truth membership labels to derive optimal hyperparameters, establishing worst-case leakage risk.
- **Attack Mode:** Attacker relies solely on auxiliary dataset `D_aux` with shadow models to estimate hyperparameters, reflecting realistic adversarial constraints.
- Cross-compatibility is achieved via Oracle Variants (attack-to-audit) and Shadow Variants (audit-to-attack).

**Evaluation Metrics:**
- Balanced Accuracy on equal member/non-member splits for symmetric cost scenarios.
- TPR@0.1%FPR for high-stakes auditing (e.g., medical records) where false accusations must be minimized.
- TNR@0.1%FNR for high-recall screening (e.g., copyright enforcement) where missed detections are costlier than false alarms.

**Full-Pipeline Structure:**
1. Data Splitting: `D_source` into target data (`D_train`, `D_test`) and auxiliary `D_aux`; members subsampled from `D_train`, non-members from `D_test`.
2. Training Pipeline: Architecture selection, training (vanilla SGD or DP-SGD), post-training (fine-tuning or machine unlearning with SalUn/SFRon).
3. MIA Execution: 5 shadow models trained with sampling rate r=0.8.
4. Evaluation: Under both threat models with all three metrics.

**Baseline Setting:** ResNet-18 on CIFAR-100, with controlled ablations varying one pipeline component at a time.

**MIA Methods Evaluated:** ShadowMIA, ML-Leaks (1 & 3), Merlin, Metric MIA (Entropy, Modified Entropy, Confidence), LiRA, Quantile MIA, RMIA, BlindMI (One-class, Diff-w, Diff-single, Diff-bi) -- totaling 14 variants.

**Datasets:** CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100 (fine-grained, superclass, mislabeled), ImageNet-100.
**Architectures:** 4-layer CNN, VGG-11, ResNet-18/34/50, WideResNet-28-2, Swin Transformer-Tiny.

## Results & Highlights

**Threat Model Sensitivity (Table 2):** Many methods suffer large audit-attack gaps, especially Metric MIA which degrades substantially in attack mode. Quantile MIA maintains the smallest gap across all conditions, demonstrating superior stability.

**Data Impact (Table 3):**
- Mislabeled data (10% noise) increases MIA vulnerability: accuracy rises from ~82% to ~87% for Metric MIA (Confidence).
- Superclass CIFAR-100 (fewer classes, lower-dimensional outputs) is harder to attack than fine-grained CIFAR-100.
- Strong correlation between generalization gap and MIA success rate across all datasets.

**Architecture Impact (Table 4):**
- Overparameterized models (ResNet-34/50) are slightly more vulnerable than smaller ones (4-layer CNN: ~67% vs. ResNet-34: ~84% for Metric MIA).
- Swin-T shows lower vulnerability (~68%) despite comparable training accuracy, attributed to its different inductive biases.
- Metric MIA consistently outperforms across all architectures.

**Training Algorithm Impact (Table 5):**
- DP-SGD effectively reduces MIA accuracy: Metric MIA drops from ~82% to ~70%, and LiRA/RMIA collapse near random guess (~52-57%).
- Fine-tuned models are significantly harder to attack than scratch-trained models: most MIA methods produce near-random results on fine-tuned models.
- Quantile MIA uniquely maintains non-trivial performance on fine-tuned models (~72.6% accuracy).

**Machine Unlearning (Tables 5-6, Figure 5):**
- Fixed-data audit: BlindMI One-class achieves best performance in distinguishing membership across different models on the same dataset.
- Fixed-model audit: The choice of MIA method can change the relative ranking of unlearning algorithms (SalUn vs. SFRon), raising concerns about fair evaluation of unlearning methods.

**Overfitting as Meta-Predictor (Figure 7):** Across all pipeline dimensions (dataset, architecture, training algorithm), the generalization gap shows strong positive correlation with MIA accuracy. This provides a practical heuristic for assessing privacy risk without running attacks.

**Practitioner Takeaways:**
1. Metric MIA (Confidence/Modified Entropy): Best overall in audit mode but sensitive to hyperparameters; degrades in attack mode and fine-tuning scenarios.
2. Quantile MIA: Most stable across threat models and challenging scenarios (attack mode, DP-SGD, fine-tuning); slightly lower baseline performance.
3. RMIA: Excels in low-FPR regimes (high-confidence leakage) due to likelihood ratio amplification, but underperforms in high-recall screening.
4. BlindMI One-class: Best for cross-model membership comparison (e.g., unlearning auditing).

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** The paper's primary contribution is systematization rather than a new attack or defense. While this is inherently less novel than proposing a new method, the field badly needs this kind of rigorous benchmarking. The unified framework `S_mem = C(v(x, f_θ; K))` is clean and useful, and the formalization of audit vs. attack threat models addresses a real gap in the literature where methods developed under different assumptions were being compared unfairly.

**Rigor:** The experimental design is commendable in its controlled ablation approach (varying one pipeline component at a time while holding others at the ResNet-18/CIFAR-100 baseline). The use of three complementary metrics is a genuine improvement over prior work that relied solely on AUC or accuracy. However, several concerns remain:
- The evaluation is restricted to **image classification** tasks. NLP, tabular data, and generative models are not covered, which limits generalizability.
- The shadow model setup (5 models, r=0.8 sampling rate) is reasonable but not extensively ablated -- how sensitive are conclusions to this choice?
- Some MIA methods (notably Quantile MIA) are excluded from certain experiments due to "prohibitive computational complexity," which introduces gaps in the comparison.
- The machine unlearning evaluation only covers two methods (SalUn, SFRon) and one of them (SFRon) is co-authored by the paper's own authors, which could introduce bias in the selection.

**Reproducibility:** The promise of a "ready-to-use auditing toolkit" is a strong positive, though the paper does not include a link to code. The detailed algorithm pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and comprehensive hyperparameter descriptions aid reproducibility.

**Significance:** The finding that MIA method choice can flip the ranking of unlearning algorithms (Figure 5) is arguably the most impactful result -- it directly challenges how the unlearning community evaluates its methods. The overfitting correlation provides a practical shortcut for practitioners.

**Red Flags:**
- The paper's title claims "full-pipeline" but the pipeline is limited to classification tasks with standard architectures. No evaluation on foundation models, LLMs, or diffusion models.
- The claim of providing a "ready-to-use auditing toolkit" lacks a code link in the paper.
- Some tables are extremely dense and hard to parse, with the bottom-line takeaways buried in text rather than highlighted.

## Strengths

- First truly comprehensive full-pipeline MIA benchmark covering data, architectures, training algorithms, and post-training stages in a unified framework
- Clean formalization of audit vs. attack threat models that resolves a long-standing comparability issue in the MIA literature
- Three complementary evaluation metrics that map to distinct real-world deployment scenarios (high-confidence auditing, high-recall screening, general-purpose evaluation)
- Strong empirical evidence linking overfitting (generalization gap) to MIA vulnerability across all pipeline dimensions
- Critical finding that MIA choice can change unlearning algorithm rankings, which has immediate implications for the machine unlearning community
- Actionable, concrete practitioner guidelines for MIA method selection based on deployment context

## Limitations

- Restricted to image classification; no coverage of NLP, tabular data, generative models, or foundation models
- Quantile MIA excluded from temporal and mislabeling experiments due to computational cost, creating gaps in the comparison
- The unlearning evaluation includes SFRon, a method co-authored by the paper's own Xinwen Cheng and Xiaolin Huang, raising potential selection bias concerns
- No code or toolkit link provided in the paper despite the "ready-to-use auditing toolkit" claim
- Shadow model count (5) and sampling rate (0.8) are not ablated; sensitivity of conclusions to these choices is unknown
- The framework does not address adaptive adversaries who may tailor their attack strategy to the target model's properties
- Datasets are limited to CIFAR and ImageNet-100; no evaluation on larger-scale or domain-specific datasets (e.g., medical, financial)

## Relevance

该工作对研究机器学习隐私保护和机器遗忘的研究者具有直接参考价值。它不仅提供了一个系统性的MIA评估基准，更重要的是揭示了不同MIA方法在不同流水线配置下的行为差异，特别是发现MIA选择会改变机器遗忘算法的排名这一结论，对如何公平评估机器遗忘方法提出了重要警示。对于关注模型隐私审计实际部署的研究者，文中给出的过拟合-MIA脆弱性关联和MIA选型指南具有很高的实用价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How do these findings transfer to foundation models (LLMs, diffusion models) where the training data scale and model complexity are orders of magnitude larger?
- Can the generalization gap heuristic be used proactively as a privacy regularization target during training?
- What is the sensitivity of the audit-attack gap to the number and architecture of shadow models?
- How should the unlearning community standardize MIA-based evaluation given that method choice affects algorithm rankings?
- Does the overfitting-MIA vulnerability relationship hold under distribution shift or adversarial training?
- Could the framework be extended with adaptive threat models where the adversary selects the MIA method based on observable model properties?
