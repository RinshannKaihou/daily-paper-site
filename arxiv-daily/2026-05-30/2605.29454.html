MIA method choice can flip unlearning algorithm rankings, while overfitting predicts vulnerability across every pipeline stage.

A Full-Pipeline Framework for Evaluating Membership Inference Attacks in Machine Learning
Ding Chen, Xinwen Cheng, Xuyang Zhong et al. 2026-05-28 membership-inference privacy-auditing machine-unlearning benchmark
Key Metric
84vs67%
ResNet-34 MIA accuracy versus 4-layer CNN — overparameterization directly amplifies privacy leakage.
Why This Matters
Reveals that MIA method choice changes unlearning algorithm rankings — a critical warning for fair privacy auditing.
对研究机器学习隐私保护和机器遗忘的研究者具有直接参考价值，对如何公平评估机器遗忘方法提出了重要警示。
Critical Edge
The "full-pipeline" claim is limited to image classification — no NLP, generative models, or foundation models evaluated.

Methodology

All MIA methods are unified under a single formalism:

Smem = C( v(x, fθ; K) )

where v is a representation function, K is auxiliary knowledge, and C is a classifier. The paper evaluates 14 variants across 9 base methods, 6 architectures, and 5 datasets.

  • Threat Models: Audit Mode (oracle labels, worst-case) vs. Attack Mode (shadow models only)
  • Metrics: Balanced Accuracy, TPR@0.1%FPR, TNR@0.1%FNR
  • Training: Vanilla SGD or DP-SGD; post-training via fine-tuning or unlearning (SalUn, SFRon)
  • Shadows: 5 models, sampling rate r = 0.8

Results & Highlights

Scenario Finding
Threat model Metric MIA degrades in attack mode; Quantile MIA is most stable across both modes
Data noise 10% mislabeled data raises Metric MIA accuracy ~82% → ~87%
Architecture ResNet-34 ~84% vs. 4-layer CNN ~67%
DP-SGD LiRA / RMIA collapse to ~52–57% (near-random)
Fine-tuning Most MIAs fail; Quantile MIA alone holds ~72.6%
Unlearning BlindMI One-class best for cross-model membership comparison

Strengths

  • First truly comprehensive full-pipeline MIA benchmark covering data, architectures, training, and post-training stages
  • Clean formalization of audit vs. attack threat models resolves long-standing comparability issues
  • Three complementary metrics map directly to real-world deployment scenarios
  • Strong empirical link between overfitting and MIA vulnerability across all pipeline dimensions
  • MIA choice can flip unlearning algorithm rankings — a direct challenge to current evaluation practice
  • Actionable practitioner guidelines for method selection based on deployment context

Limitations

  • Image classification only; no NLP, tabular data, generative models, or foundation models
  • Quantile MIA excluded from some experiments due to computational cost
  • SFRon co-authored by paper authors raises potential selection bias in unlearning evaluation
  • No code or toolkit link despite the "ready-to-use auditing toolkit" claim
  • Shadow model count (5) and sampling rate (0.8) are not ablated
  • Framework does not address adaptive adversaries

Questions for Further Exploration

How do these findings transfer to foundation models (LLMs, diffusion models) where scale and complexity are orders of magnitude larger?
Can the generalization-gap heuristic be used proactively as a privacy regularization target during training?
How should the unlearning community standardize MIA-based evaluation given that method choice affects algorithm rankings?
What is the sensitivity of the audit-attack gap to the number and architecture of shadow models?
Does the overfitting-MIA vulnerability relationship hold under distribution shift or adversarial training?
Could the framework be extended with adaptive threat models where the adversary selects the MIA method based on observable model properties?
