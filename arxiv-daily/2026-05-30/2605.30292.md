> **一句话总结：** 本文提出"留窗法"（Leave-a-Window-Out），通过在Jackknife重采样时移除时间窗口而非单点，结合循环嵌入系数（cyclic embedding coefficient）和OOS稳定性条件，为时间序列预测推断提供有理论保证的有效覆盖，同时产生比分裂保形预测更窄的置信区间。

> **TL;DR:** The vanilla leave-one-out jackknife suffers arbitrary coverage loss in time series with mild temporal dependence (e.g., even a simple MA(1) process). This paper proposes the leave-a-window-out (LWO) method, which removes a window of tau neighboring points instead of a single point during jackknife resampling, thereby breaking the "two-sided vs one-sided" information asymmetry. Under mild out-of-sample stability assumptions, LWO achieves provable approximate coverage via novel cyclic embedding coefficients. Experiments on synthetic MA(1) data, a sticky Markov chain, and real traffic/solar datasets show LWO maintains valid coverage where jackknife fails and produces substantially narrower intervals than split conformal prediction.

## 核心贡献

- 证明经典留一法Jackknife在温和时间依赖下（如简单MA(1)过程）即可遭受任意覆盖损失，根本原因是"双面信息不对称"：内部代理测试点可获得前后信息，而最终测试点只能获得单面信息
- 提出留窗法（LWO）：移除长度为tau的窗口而非单点，打破上述信息不对称，使校准分数更好地反映最终测试点的预测难度
- 引入循环嵌入系数（cyclic embedding coefficient）这一新概念，量化时间序列偏离循环可交换性的程度，构成理论分析的核心工具
- 在近似马尔可夫+beta混合条件下（推论1）和仅beta混合条件下（推论2）分别证明LWO的近似覆盖保证
- 引入新的beta条件混合系数（beta-conditional-mixing coefficient），推广了经典beta混合的概念以刻画近似马尔可夫性
- 实验覆盖合成MA(1)数据、粘性马尔可夫链和真实数据集（交通流量、太阳能），验证LWO在Jackknife失效时保持覆盖且区间远窄于分裂保形预测

## Key Contributions

- Demonstrates that the vanilla leave-one-out jackknife can suffer arbitrary coverage loss even in canonical time series models (e.g., MA(1) processes), due to an information asymmetry: interior proxy test points have "two-sided" information from neighbors while the actual test point has only "one-sided" information
- Proposes the leave-a-window-out (LWO) method, which removes a window of tau consecutive points after each proxy test point during jackknife resampling, ensuring calibration scores match the information structure at test time
- Introduces the cyclic embedding coefficient rho_tau(Z), a novel measure of how far a dependent sequence is from being embeddable into a cyclically exchangeable longer sequence
- Proves two main coverage guarantees: Theorem 1 via direct cyclic embedding (giving Corollary 1 for approximately Markovian + mixing processes), and Theorem 2 via embedding of masked sequences (giving Corollary 2 for general beta-mixing processes without Markovian structure)
- Provides explicit stability examples: ridge regression, kernel ridge regression, k-nearest neighbors, and bagged predictors all satisfy the required OOS stability condition with concrete parameter bounds
- Introduces the beta-conditional-mixing coefficient as a new measure of approximate Markovianity, complementing the classical beta-mixing coefficient

## Methodology

The paper operates in a general time-series predictive inference setting with memory-based predictors. A predictor with memory L uses a window of L past observations plus the current covariate to predict the response. The key methodological contributions are:

**The LWO Procedure:** For each proxy test point k, instead of removing only point k from training (as in jackknife), LWO removes a window of points k, k+1, ..., k+tau. The predictor is trained on the remaining "pre-window" and "post-window" data, and the score is computed at point k. The final prediction set uses the full predictor evaluated at the test point, with the quantile of LWO scores as the radius. When tau=0, LWO reduces to the vanilla jackknife.

**Theoretical Framework:** The analysis introduces cyclic exchangeability (a sequence is invariant under cyclic rotations) and the cyclic embedding coefficient rho_tau(Z) = inf over cyclically exchangeable sequences of the TV distance between Z and the truncated sequence. Coverage is proved by: (1) embedding the dependent sequence into a cyclically exchangeable longer sequence, (2) using exchangeability of "circular scores" on the embedded sequence, and (3) controlling the gap between circular scores and actual LWO scores via OOS stability.

**OOS Stability (Definition 2):** The (score, Alg) pair is stable if the test-point score does not change too much when a random block of length ell is masked from training. Concrete examples: ridge regression satisfies this with nu=0 and t = O(ell/n); k-NN satisfies it with nu = k*ell/n and t=0.

**Two Coverage Regimes:** Corollary 1 handles approximately Markovian + mixing sequences (requires only single-block stability). Corollary 2 handles general beta-mixing sequences (requires two-block stability but no Markovian assumption). Both give coverage of the form 1 - alpha - O(sqrt(nu + mixing_coefficient + tau/n)).

## Results & Highlights

- **MA(1) synthetic data (d=50):** Vanilla jackknife severely undercovers for Ridge, KNN, and Kernel Regression predictors. LWO (tau=5) achieves near-nominal 90% coverage across all five predictors, with prediction radii comparable to jackknife and significantly smaller than split CP.
- **Sticky Markov chain:** A particularly challenging setting where the jackknife undercovers dramatically due to epoch-based feature construction. LWO successfully recovers coverage by censoring the local future window.
- **Real data (Traffic & Solar Energy):** LWO maintains near-nominal coverage consistently, while jackknife undercovers for some predictors (especially KNN on Solar Energy). LWO produces narrower intervals than split CP across all predictors and datasets.
- **Window size tau=5** suffices for the MA(1) process (memory q=1, but cyclic embeddability requires tau>=2). Real data uses tau=20 with lag L=24.
- Code available at https://github.com/scotthanyang/LWO

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** The LWO method itself is a conceptually natural modification of the jackknife, but the theoretical analysis is genuinely novel and substantial. The cyclic embedding coefficient is a new tool that provides a principled framework. The insight about "two-sided vs one-sided" information asymmetry as the root cause of jackknife failure in time series is both elegant and practically important. The beta-conditional-mixing coefficient appears to be new to the literature.

**Rigor:** The proofs are detailed and complete (Section 5 + Appendix A). The framework carefully handles edge cases (dummy data points, masking conventions). The stability assumption is clearly defined and verified for concrete algorithm classes. The two coverage regimes (Markovian+mixing vs. mixing-only) are well-organized with clear relationships between them (Proposition 3 showing the switch coefficient sandwich).

**Reproducibility:** Code is publicly available. Experimental setup is clearly described with specific hyperparameters. Real datasets (Traffic, Solar Energy) are publicly available benchmarks. The methods are straightforward to implement.

**Significance:** This fills an important gap in the conformal prediction literature. While split conformal was recently shown to be robust to temporal dependence, it pays a statistical efficiency cost. LWO provides a principled alternative that avoids data splitting while maintaining coverage, which is especially valuable in low-data regimes.

**Potential concerns:**
- The window size tau is a hyperparameter that must be chosen. The authors acknowledge this as an open question and provide only heuristic guidance (tau should exceed the effective dependence range). A fully data-driven choice with valid guarantees is missing.
- The coverage bounds involve square roots of the stability and mixing parameters (e.g., 1 - alpha - 3*sqrt(nu + ...)), which may be loose in practice. The paper does not discuss tightness of these bounds.
- The computational cost of LWO is O(n) times the cost of fitting the predictor (same as jackknife), which is more expensive than split CP but comparable to the jackknife.
- For Theorem 2 (general mixing case), the stability requirement is stronger (stability with respect to masked sequences), and it is not clear how much stronger this is in practice.
- The real-data experiments use fixed tau=20 without sensitivity analysis to this choice.

## Strengths

- Identifies and explains a clean mechanistic reason for jackknife failure in time series (information asymmetry), not just a generic "dependence is bad" argument
- Provides two complementary theoretical results covering different dependence structures
- Introduces the cyclic embedding coefficient as a potentially broadly useful tool for studying other problems with temporal dependence
- Extensive experiments with five different predictors across three data settings
- Recovers the jackknife as a special case (tau=0) and recovers existing jackknife+ guarantees under exchangeability
- Careful discussion of open questions in Section 6

## Limitations

- No principled, data-driven method for choosing the window size tau
- The coverage bounds are conservative (square-root dependence on mixing/stability parameters)
- Stability assumption may not hold for all black-box predictors (e.g., decision trees are acknowledged as unstable)
- The paper does not compare against other time-series conformal methods like EnbPI, SPCI, or KOWCPI -- the comparison is limited to split CP and vanilla jackknife
- Theoretical results are for fixed-batch prediction (not online/streaming)
- No extension to multi-step-ahead prediction

## Relevance

本文对时间序列的保形预测提出了有理论保证且无需数据分割的方法，对序列数据的预测不确定性量化具有重要实用价值。LWO方法尤其适合数据量有限、需要高效利用全部训练数据的时序预测场景，是保形预测在实际时序应用中的重要推进。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can the window size tau be chosen in a fully data-driven way while maintaining valid coverage guarantees?
- How does LWO compare computationally and statistically to EnbPI, SPCI, and other time-series-specific conformal methods?
- Can the LWO principle be combined with cross-conformal prediction, jackknife+-after-bootstrap, or other resampling-based methods?
- Does the cyclic embedding coefficient have applications beyond predictive inference (e.g., in goodness-of-fit testing or dependence measurement)?
- Can the framework be extended to online/streaming settings where data arrives sequentially?
- How sensitive is LWO's performance to misspecification of tau (too small vs. too large)?
