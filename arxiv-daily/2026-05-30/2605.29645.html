Tight bounds for sparse contextual bandits eliminate the spurious |A|⁹ gap in prior sample-complexity results.
The Sample Complexity of Multiclass and Sparse Contextual Bandits · Liad Erez, Fan Chen, Alon Cohen, Tomer Koren et al. · 2026-05-28 · contextual-bandits sample-complexity online-learning
Key Metric
|A|⁹ → s + |A|
Sample-complexity gap closed: prior Õ(|A|⁹/ε²) reduced to optimal Õ(s/ε² + |A|/ε).
Why This Matters
Eliminating the |A|⁹ gap makes theoretical guarantees meaningful in large action spaces like multiclass classification.
Critical Edge
Purely theoretical — no empirical evaluation; practical performance of the proposed algorithms remains untested.

Methodology

The paper studies stochastic i.i.d. contextual bandits under an s-sparse reward assumption: reward vectors have L₁-norm ≤ s (often s ≪ |A|), motivated by multiclass classification where only the correct class yields reward. Two complementary algorithmic paths are developed.

1. DEC-Based (Information-Theoretic)

Frames the problem via the Decision-Estimation Coefficient (Foster et al.). Key insight: s-sparse rewards induce a model class with sharp DEC scaling in s rather than |A|, yielding the optimal rate through exploration-by-optimization. Involves solving complex min-max problems.

2. Low-Variance Exploration (Practical)

Avoids the computational burden of DEC by using importance-weighted estimators with controlled variance. Produces concrete, tractable algorithms and extends naturally to contextual combinatorial semi-bandits.

A matching lower bound of Ω(s/ε² + |A|/ε) confirms optimality. Results generalize to Natarajan classes (beyond finite policy classes) and to combinatorial semi-bandits with per-action feedback.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Resolves a major open problem — closing the |A|⁹ gap is a significant theoretical advance.
  • Dual perspective — DEC provides clean information-theoretic understanding; low-variance method yields practical algorithms.
  • Natural sparsity assumption — well-motivated by multiclass classification, leading to an elegant framework.
  • Broad extensions — Natarajan classes and combinatorial semi-bandits extend impact beyond the base setting.
  • Matching lower bounds — confirm optimality and give a complete characterization.

Limitations

  • No empirical evaluation — practical performance remains entirely untested.
  • Computational tractability — DEC min-max optimization may be intractable for large policy classes.
  • I.i.d. assumption — more restrictive than adversarial or non-stationary settings.
  • Finite policy focus — infinite/continuous classes (e.g. neural nets) need additional work.
  • Hidden constants — Õ() notation conceals logarithmic factors that may matter in practice.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does the low-variance algorithm perform empirically against Thompson Sampling or UCB in real multiclass tasks?
  2. Can the s-sparse assumption be made adaptive or data-dependent while retaining near-optimal complexity?
  3. Do the O(s/ε²) rates survive in adversarial (non-i.i.d.) contextual bandit settings?
  4. Can Natarajan-dimension bounds be made competitive with uniform convergence for neural network policy classes?
  5. Are there efficient min-max approximations that preserve DEC guarantees at scale?
