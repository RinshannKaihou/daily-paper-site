> **一句话总结：** 本文提出了一种名为 GROKtimizer 的双阶段优化器：第一阶段关闭权重衰减以快速达到插值（零训练误差），第二阶段切换到临界阻尼动量（CDM）并结合权重衰减，在插值后的平坦方向（Hessian 零空间）上高效最小化参数范数，从而在高维低样本场景下选择具有更好泛化能力的低复杂度解，并在理论上证明了相比经典梯度下降的二次加速。

> **TL;DR:** This paper proposes GROKtimizer, a biphasic optimizer that first disables weight decay to rapidly reach interpolation (zero training loss), then switches to Critically Damped Momentum (CDM) with weight decay to efficiently minimize parameter norm along the flat directions (Hessian null space) of the post-interpolation basin. Under a local quadratic model, it achieves a quadratic speedup over gradient descent for selecting low-complexity, generalizing solutions, with empirical validation on grokking benchmarks, biomedical datasets, and language models.

## 核心贡献

- **双阶段优化框架**：明确将训练过程分为两个阶段——(1) 无正则化的快速经验风险最小化阶段（快速达到插值）；(2) 插值后的复杂度最小化阶段，利用临界阻尼动量（CDM）高效降低参数范数。
- **理论保证**：在插值后 basin 的局部二次模型假设下，证明了 CDM 相比经典梯度下降在平坦方向上的收敛具有**二次加速**（$E_{\text{CDM}} \sim \sqrt{E_{\text{GD}}}$），并证明了一阶优化器中的信息论最优性。
- **最优学习率定理**：给出了 CDM 阶段的最大稳定学习率公式 $\eta_{\text{max}} = 4(\sqrt{\lambda_{\text{max}}+2\lambda} - \sqrt{\lambda})^2 / (\lambda_{\text{max}}+\lambda)^2$，并提出了基于 Aitken 修正的幂迭代方法来在线估计该学习率。
- **广泛的实验验证**：在 6 个合成 grokking 基准、3 个真实世界高维数据集（白血病基因表达、TCGA、QM9 分子性质预测）、MNIST 低样本效率实验以及 WikiText2 / BabyLM 语言模型预训练上均取得最优或接近最优的表现。
- **与平坦极小值假说的联系**：将插值后动力学与平坦极小值假说联系起来，提出使用 Hessian 零空间的维度作为对 reparameterization 不变的平坦度度量。

## Key Contributions

- **Biphasic optimization framework**: Explicitly separates training into (1) unregularized fast empirical risk minimization (rapid interpolation), and (2) post-interpolation complexity minimization using Critically Damped Momentum (CDM) to efficiently reduce parameter norm.
- **Theoretical guarantees**: Under a local quadratic model of the post-interpolation basin, proves a **quadratic speedup** of CDM over classical gradient descent along flat directions ($E_{\text{CDM}} \sim \sqrt{E_{\text{GD}}}$), with information-theoretic optimality among first-order methods.
- **Optimal learning rate theorem**: Derives the maximal stable learning rate for the CDM phase as $\eta_{\text{max}} = 4(\sqrt{\lambda_{\text{max}}+2\lambda} - \sqrt{\lambda})^2 / (\lambda_{\text{max}}+\lambda)^2$, and proposes an Aitken-corrected power iteration method to estimate it online.
- **Broad empirical validation**: Achieves best or near-best performance on 6 synthetic grokking benchmarks, 3 real-world high-dimensional datasets (Leukemia gene expression, TCGA, QM9 molecular property prediction), MNIST low-data sample efficiency, and WikiText2 / BabyLM language model pretraining.
- **Connection to flat minima**: Links post-interpolation dynamics to the flat-minima hypothesis, proposing the dimensionality of the Hessian null space as a reparameterization-invariant measure of flatness.

## Methodology

### Theoretical Framework

The paper formalizes the post-interpolation regime under **Assumption 1**: near an interpolating solution $w^\star$, the $L_2$-regularized loss can be locally approximated as a quadratic form:

$$L_{\text{reg}}(w) \approx L_{\text{unreg}}(w^\star) + \frac{1}{2}(w-w^\star)^\top H (w-w^\star) + \frac{\lambda}{2}\|w\|_2^2$$

where $H$ is the Hessian at $w^\star$. In this regime, the optimization dynamics along the eigenbasis $\{(\lambda_i, v_i)\}$ of $H$ decouple into independent harmonic oscillators. For zero-eigenvalue directions (the null space / flat directions), the dynamics reduce to a damped oscillator.

### Critically Damped Momentum (CDM)

The key insight is that for the flat directions ($\lambda_i = 0$), the continuous-time momentum dynamics are:

$$\ddot{\tilde{w}}_i + \gamma^u \dot{\tilde{w}}_i + \lambda \tilde{w}_i = 0$$

**Critical damping** ($\gamma^u = 2\sqrt{\lambda}$) yields the fastest non-oscillatory decay. Discretizing this gives the momentum parameter:

$$\beta = 1 - 2\sqrt{\lambda \eta}$$

**Theorem 1** proves that with this setting, the number of epochs needed along flat directions scales as $E_{\text{CDM}} \sim \sqrt{E_{\text{GD}}}$, a quadratic speedup over gradient descent. This is information-theoretically optimal among first-order methods due to Nesterov's $O(1/k^2)$ lower bound.

### Optimal Learning Rate

**Theorem 2** derives the maximum stable learning rate for CDM given the largest Hessian eigenvalue $\lambda_{\text{max}}$:

$$\eta_{\text{max}} = \frac{4(\sqrt{\lambda_{\text{max}}+2\lambda} - \sqrt{\lambda})^2}{(\lambda_{\text{max}}+\lambda)^2} \sim 4\lambda_{\text{max}}^{-1}$$

To estimate $\lambda_{\text{max}}$ without computing the full Hessian, the authors propose using the **Perlmutter trick** for Hessian-vector products combined with **Aitken-corrected power iteration** (only ~10–20 steps needed).

### GROKtimizer Algorithm

1. **Phase 1 (Interpolation)**: Train with standard optimizer (e.g., Adam or SGD) **without weight decay** ($\lambda = 0$) to rapidly reach zero training loss.
2. **Phase 2 (Norm Minimization)**: Switch to CDM with:
   - Weight decay $\lambda > 0$
   - Momentum $\beta = 1 - 2\sqrt{\lambda \eta}$
   - Learning rate $\eta$ near $\eta_{\text{max}}$ (estimated via power iteration)

The switching criterion is when training loss falls below a threshold (i.e., interpolation is achieved).

## Results & Highlights

### Synthetic Grokking Benchmarks (Table 1, n=10 seeds)

GROKtimizer achieves **best mean performance on all 6 benchmarks**:

| Task | Adam | AdamW | Muon | SGD | GROKtimizer |
|------|------|-------|------|-----|-------------|
| Gaussian (Val. Loss ↓) | 121.7 | 151.4 | 94.2 | 94.2 | **4.3×10⁻⁵** |
| Binary Addition (Acc. ↑) | 82.9% | 36.6% | 0.31% | 0.04% | **89.5%** |
| Modular Addition (Acc. ↑) | 70.1% | 0.0% | 0.27% | 1.83% | **95.1%** |
| RF Teacher Linear (Acc. ↑) | 78.6% | 55.5% | 50.7% | 56.1% | **84.3%** |
| Sparse Parity (Acc. ↑) | 60.5% | 69.6% | 52.1% | 50.3% | **89.1%** |
| Two-Subspace Linear (Acc. ↑) | 79.5% | 66.3% | 59.2% | 58.3% | **79.7%** |

On Gaussian, GROKtimizer reduces validation loss by **several orders of magnitude** relative to the best baseline.

### Real-World High-Dimensional Datasets (Table 2, n=5 seeds)

| Dataset | Task | Best Baseline | GROKtimizer |
|---------|------|---------------|-------------|
| Leukemia | Gene expression classification | 57.2% (Adam/SGD) | **80.3%** |
| TCGA | Cancer classification | 89.9% (Adam) | **97.5%** |
| QM9 | Molecular property prediction (GNN) | 0.28 (Adam) | **0.16** |

Gains are strongest on biomedical datasets where features ≫ samples, consistent with the hypothesis that explicit norm minimization is most beneficial when the solution space is underdetermined.

### MNIST Sample Efficiency (Figure 4)

With an overparameterized MLP (~1.46M parameters) and varying training images per class (10–50):
- GROKtimizer provides the **largest advantage in the most data-scarce setting** (10 images/class).
- The gap shrinks as more data is added, consistent with the solution space becoming more constrained.

### Language Model Pretraining (Figure 5)

On data-constrained WikiText2 (14M params) and BabyLMStrictSmall (38M params):
- The biphasic pattern **persists** in transformers: after reaching low training loss, switching to norm minimization changes the norm trajectory and produces small further validation loss reductions.
- However, the norm–generalization relationship is **less direct** than in tabular/settings, due to scale symmetries, adaptive updates, and noisy next-token objectives.
- The SGD-based interpolation phase without weight decay is better suited than applying weight decay early.

### Ablation: Learning Rate is Not the Full Story

When Adam uses the same elevated learning rate as GROKtimizer's Phase 2, it becomes **highly unstable** and fails to match performance (Figure 8). The gains come from the combination of entering the post-interpolation regime, exploiting the flatter landscape, and using appropriate CDM dynamics—not just a larger learning rate.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** High. While biphasic training and momentum methods are well-studied, the explicit decomposition into "reach interpolation fast, then minimize complexity optimally via critically damped momentum" is a novel and principled framing. The connection to classical mechanics (damped harmonic oscillators) applied to the post-interpolation null space is elegant and original. The paper also distinguishes itself from prior grokking-acceleration methods (Grokfast, NeuralGrok, Muon) by providing theoretical convergence guarantees rather than purely empirical speedups.

**Rigor:** Strong. The theoretical claims are carefully scoped under Assumption 1 (local quadratic model), which is standard for post-interpolation analysis. The proofs for Theorem 1 (quadratic speedup) and Theorem 2 (maximal learning rate) are provided in the main text and appendix. The empirical evaluation is extensive: 6 synthetic benchmarks + 3 real-world datasets + sample efficiency + language models, with multiple random seeds and error bars reported.

**Reproducibility:** Good. The algorithm is deterministic and algorithmically simple. The switching criterion (training loss < threshold) is straightforward. The Aitken-corrected power iteration for learning rate estimation is well-described. However, exact hyperparameters for all experiments are not fully tabulated in the main text, which may require some tuning to reproduce exact numbers.

**Significance:** Potentially high for data-scarce learning domains (genomics, medicine, materials science). If the post-interpolation dynamics consistently lead to better-generalizing solutions with theoretical guarantees, this could influence training practices. The language model results suggest the principle scales, even if the norm–generalization relationship becomes more complex.

**Red flags / caveats:**
- The local quadratic assumption (Assumption 1) may break down for deep networks with highly non-quadratic null spaces or rapidly changing eigenbases. The authors acknowledge this and propose a "slow evolution of eigenbasis" relaxation.
- The switching criterion requires detecting interpolation, which can be noisy in stochastic training. The paper does not deeply analyze sensitivity to the switching threshold.
- The method assumes the model has sufficient capacity to reach interpolation — not always true in constrained architectures.
- Parameter norm is treated as a proxy for complexity, but this is known to be affected by reparameterization and scale symmetries in deep networks (acknowledged in the Discussion).
- The language model results show only modest improvements, suggesting the method's primary value may be in data-scarce regimes rather than large-scale pretraining.
- The flat-minima hypothesis itself remains debated; the reconciliation offered is suggestive but not conclusive.

## Strengths

- Elegant theoretical framework connecting post-interpolation optimization to damped harmonic oscillators.
- Clean, practically implementable algorithm with minimal overhead.
- Strong empirical validation across a diverse set of domains (synthetic algorithmic tasks, biomedical genomics, molecular property prediction, image classification, language modeling).
- Information-theoretic optimality proof among first-order methods.
- Principled approach to learning rate selection for the post-interpolation phase.
- Honest discussion of limitations, including the breakdown of norm–generalization correlation in transformers.
- Reputable author team with strong track record.

## Limitations

- Theoretical results are local (quadratic approximation); global behavior in non-convex deep network landscapes may differ.
- Requires the model to have sufficient capacity to reach interpolation.
- The norm–generalization relationship weakens in large-scale language models due to scale symmetries and other transformer-specific phenomena.
- Switching threshold for the biphasic schedule is not deeply analyzed for sensitivity.
- Limited to L2 regularization / weight decay; does not address other regularization mechanisms.
- Primary value is in data-scarce regimes; large-scale pretraining gains are modest.

## Relevance

本文提出的双阶段优化策略直接针对高维低样本场景下的泛化问题，与 grokking 现象、隐式正则化以及优化动力学的研究高度相关。对于关注如何在过参数化模型中通过训练过程选择更好泛化解的研究者——尤其是在基因组学、医学和材料科学等数据稀缺的科学领域——该方法提供了兼具理论保证和实用性的工具。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How robust is GROKtimizer to imperfect interpolation detection in large-batch or noisy training? Can the switching criterion be made adaptive based on gradient variance?
2. Can the CDM framework be extended to adaptive optimizers (e.g., Adam-like CDM) rather than being restricted to SGD-like momentum?
3. How does GROKtimizer interact with other regularization techniques such as dropout, data augmentation, or label smoothing in the post-interpolation phase?
4. Does the method retain its advantages in mixed-precision training, where gradient noise and quantization effects may alter the ideal damping regime?
5. Can the Hessian-null-space dimensionality be efficiently estimated during training to serve as a practical, reparameterization-invariant flatness measure for model selection?
6. What is the behavior of GROKtimizer in non-interpolating regimes (e.g., label noise, architecture constraints) where exact interpolation is impossible?
