Neuron Populations Exhibit Divergent Selectivity with Scale

arXiv: 2606.03990

Date: 2026-06-03

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03990

一句话总结： 该研究通过对语言模型（至30B）和视觉模型（至5B）的跨模型神经元匹配分析，发现"罗塞塔神经元"（Rosetta Neurons）的数量随模型规模呈次线性幂律增长，且随规模扩大这些共享神经元变得更具选择性和单语义性，而非共享神经元则保持多语义状态，形成"神经元极化效应"。
TL;DR: This paper establishes that Rosetta Neurons—units with recurrent activation patterns across independently trained models—follow a sublinear power-law scaling in model size (exponents ~0.5–0.7), growing in absolute count but shrinking as a fraction of total neurons. The authors further discover the Neuron Polarization Effect: Rosetta Neurons become increasingly selective and monosemantic with scale, while the non-Rosetta background remains polysemantic. An analytical capacity-allocation model explains both phenomena, and a data-filtering case study shows a single Rosetta neuron can recover a code domain with 0.98 F1.

核心贡献

  • 次线性幂律扩展规律：在语言模型（Pythia、GPT-2、OPT、Qwen-2.5，80M–30B）和视觉模型（OpenCLIP、DINOv2、Pixio，80M–5B）中，罗塞塔神经元的数量随模型规模遵循次线性幂律增长（指数约0.5–0.7），R²≈0.99；即绝对数量增加，但在总神经元中的占比逐渐缩小。
  • 神经元极化效应（Neuron Polarization Effect）：罗塞塔神经元随规模扩大变得更具选择性和单语义性，而非罗塞塔神经元群体则保持较低的选择性和多语义状态，形成两极分化。
  • 解析理论模型：基于特征重要度谱（幂律分布）与有限神经元容量之间的权衡，建立了容量分配模型，成功解释了次线性增长和极化效应。高重要性特征获得更多独立神经元表示，低重要性特征则混杂在超位置（superposition）中。
  • 领域专门化趋势：罗塞塔神经元的激活模式随规模从通用文本（散文、对话）向专门领域（代码、数学、科学文本）转移。
  • 数据过滤功能验证：在CodeSearchNet上的JavaScript代码过滤实验中，单个JavaScript选择性罗塞塔神经元以0.98的F1分数几乎完全恢复了目标域数据，持续预训练后的困惑度（3.02）接近Oracle水平（3.01），显著优于随机过滤（3.59）和非罗塞塔神经元过滤（3.23）。

Key Contributions

  • Sublinear power-law scaling of Rosetta Neurons: Across language models (Pythia, GPT-2, OPT, Qwen-2.5; 80M–30B parameters) and vision models (OpenCLIP, DINOv2, Pixio; 80M–5B parameters), the number of discovered Rosetta Neurons follows a sublinear power law in model size with fitted exponents in the ~0.5–0.7 range and R² ≈ 0.99. Absolute count grows, but the fraction of total neurons shrinks.
  • Neuron Polarization Effect: Rosetta Neurons become increasingly selective and monosemantic with scale, as measured by vocabulary-space excess kurtosis (language) and VLM-judged monosemanticity rates (vision). In contrast, the non-Rosetta population remains weakly selective and polysemantic.
  • Analytical capacity-allocation model: A phenomenological model balancing feature utility (power-law importance spectrum wr ∝ r⁻ᵝ) against limited neuron capacity predicts both the sublinear scaling (Rτ(N) = Θ(N^(1/β))) and the polarization effect (average isolation of Rosetta neurons increases while non-Rosetta isolation vanishes).
  • Domain specialization with scale: Rosetta Neuron firing shifts from broad text categories (general prose, conversational) toward specialized domains (code, math, formal/science) as model size increases, even when all models are trained on the same data mixture.
  • Functional data-filtering demonstration: A single JavaScript-selective Rosetta Neuron from Pythia-6.9B filters CodeSearchNet with 0.98 F1, yielding continued-pretraining perplexity of 3.02—near the oracle at 3.01 and substantially better than random (3.59) or non-Rosetta neuron filtering (3.23).

Methodology

Identifying Rosetta Neurons. The authors extend the Rosetta Neuron framework from Dravid et al. (2023): neurons whose activation patterns recur across independently trained models. For two Transformer models A and B of the same modality, they pass the same dataset through both models, align token/patch positions, and compute Pearson correlation between all pairs of MLP neurons across the datasets. They retain only mutual nearest-neighbor matches (k=1 by default) as Rosetta Neurons.

Scaling experiments. Language models are evaluated on ~10M tokens sampled from The Pile. Vision models use 50,000 class-balanced images sampled from a diffusion model conditioned on ImageNet-1k labels, passed through both a generative and a discriminative model. Pairwise matching is performed across model families at approximately matched scales, producing scaling curves. Curves sharing a common model are collapsed by intersecting Rosetta sets for a stricter universality definition.

Selectivity measurement. In language models, neuron selectivity is measured via vocabulary-space projection excess kurtosis—higher values indicate concentration on a small token set. In vision models, GPT-5.4 judges monosemanticity from top-20 activating images, activation maps, and overlays.

Data-filtering case study. On CodeSearchNet (multilingual code corpus), a single JavaScript-selective Rosetta Neuron from Pythia-6.9B scores training functions by activation and selects top-scoring functions up to a 16M-token budget. This is compared against random sampling, a non-Rosetta JavaScript neuron, and the ground-truth Oracle JavaScript subset. Downstream utility is tested by continuing pretraining GPT2-1.5B and measuring JavaScript test-set perplexity.

Results & Highlights

  • Robust scaling across modalities and objectives: The sublinear power-law trend holds across contrastive image-text supervision (OpenCLIP), self-distillation (DINOv2), masked autoencoding (Pixio), diffusion/flow-based generative modeling, and multiple language model families trained on different datasets. Collapsed curves (stricter universality) show the same qualitative behavior.
  • Scaling is learned, not artifactual: When applied to untrained randomly initialized networks, the matching pipeline yields only a marginal number of matches with no systematic scaling trend. This confirms the observed scaling laws are a property of learned representations, not an artifact of the matching procedure or larger search pools.
  • Polarization validated in both modalities: In Pythia language models, mean excess kurtosis of Rosetta Neurons increases with scale while non-Rosetta neurons remain near zero. In OpenCLIP vision models, VLM-judged monosemanticity increases for Rosetta Neurons and decreases for non-Rosetta neurons as scale grows.
  • Specialization trend is data-independent: Because all Pythia models were trained on the same data mixture and token budget, the shift toward code/math cannot be explained by greater data exposure. Scale itself changes how models organize internal representations.
  • Single-neuron functional utility: The Rosetta Neuron data filter achieves 0.98 F1 on JavaScript recovery and downstream perplexity of 3.02 ± 0.05 (mean ± 95% CI over three runs), nearly matching the oracle (3.01 ± 0.04). The non-Rosetta neuron achieves only 0.09 F1 and 3.23 perplexity, suggesting it fires on broader web-programming syntax rather than JavaScript specifically.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: High. While Rosetta Neurons were introduced in prior work (Dravid et al., 2023), this paper is the first to systematically characterize their scaling behavior across model families, modalities, and scales up to 30B parameters. The Neuron Polarization Effect and the capacity-allocation analytical model are genuinely new contributions that bridge scaling laws with mechanistic interpretability.

Rigor: Strong. The empirical methodology is careful: mutual nearest-neighbor matching with ablations, null baselines on untrained networks, collapsed curves for stricter universality, and modality-appropriate selectivity metrics. The analytical model is formally derived from a Gaussian channel perspective and makes testable predictions (sublinear scaling, polarization) that are empirically validated. However, the monosemanticity judgments in vision rely on a VLM-as-a-judge proxy (GPT-5.4), which introduces some subjectivity and potential evaluator bias. The authors acknowledge this limitation.

Reproducibility: Moderate to Good. The paper uses publicly available model families (Pythia, GPT-2, OPT, Qwen-2.5, OpenCLIP, DINOv2, Pixio) and standard datasets (The Pile, ImageNet-1k, CodeSearchNet). The matching procedure is clearly described. However, some diffusion-model sampling details and the exact VLM prompting protocol for monosemanticity judgments are only partially specified in the main text. The substantial compute requirements (up to ~24 hours on 8 A100s for a single 30B model-pair match) may limit independent replication.

Significance: High for the interpretability and scaling-law communities. The paper provides one of the first systematic bridges between macroscopic scaling behavior (loss, compute) and microscopic internal organization (individual neuron properties). It suggests that interpretable structure does not vanish with scale but becomes increasingly concentrated in a specialized, selective subpopulation.

Red flags / concerns:

  • The VLM-judged monosemanticity metric, while practical, is a proxy. The authors are transparent about this but it remains a weaker link in the evidentiary chain.
  • The data-filtering case study (Section 5.3) is limited to a single neuron and a single domain (JavaScript). While compelling as a proof of concept, broader validation across more domains and neurons would strengthen the functional claims.
  • DINOv3 (mentioned in appendix) does not exhibit a clear Rosetta scaling trend, suggesting the phenomenon may depend on specific training objectives or regularization strategies. This limits universal generalization.
  • The analytical model assumes a power-law feature-importance spectrum and a superposition regime; while well-motivated, these are phenomenological assumptions rather than first-principles derivations from gradient dynamics.

Strengths

  • Cross-modal robustness: The same scaling trend and polarization effect are observed in both language and vision, across heterogeneous architectures and training objectives, suggesting a domain-general principle.
  • Theory-experiment alignment: The analytical model makes quantitative predictions (sublinear exponent, increasing vs. vanishing selectivity) that are empirically confirmed, lending credibility to the capacity-allocation explanation.
  • Appropriate baselines: Untrained-network null tests and corrupted-alignment controls effectively rule out procedural artifacts.
  • Functional validation: The data-filtering case study moves beyond descriptive statistics to demonstrate that Rosetta Neuron selectivity has genuine downstream utility.
  • Transparent limitations: The discussion section honestly addresses scope (Rosetta Neurons are only one form of internal structure) and measurement limitations (proxy metrics for monosemanticity).

Limitations

  • Scope of structure: Rosetta Neurons capture only single-neuron shared structure. Distributed computation (circuits, attention heads, subspaces) is not addressed. The Rosetta population should not be viewed as a complete map of shared internal function.
  • Monosemanticity proxy dependence: Vision-model monosemanticity relies on VLM judgments, which are subject to evaluator granularity and prompting choices. Language-model selectivity uses excess kurtosis of vocabulary projections, which may miss certain forms of selectivity.
  • Limited functional validation: The data-filtering demonstration uses a single neuron in one domain (JavaScript). Generalizing to other domains, multimodal settings, or neuron ensembles remains open.
  • Training-dependency: DINOv3 does not show clean Rosetta scaling, indicating the phenomenon is sensitive to training objectives, regularization, or architectural choices. This limits universal predictive power.
  • Compute barrier: Reproducing the full scaling curves, especially at the 30B scale, requires substantial GPU resources (up to ~192 A100-hours per data point).

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does the Neuron Polarization Effect manifest in multimodal models where language and vision representations interact? Do cross-modal Rosetta Neurons exist, and do they follow the same scaling law?
  2. Can the analytical capacity-allocation model be extended to predict Rosetta Neuron emergence in newer architectures such as state-space models (Mamba), mixture-of-experts (MoE), or linear attention transformers?
  3. What are the practical implications of the shrinking Rosetta fraction for automated interpretability pipelines at very large scales (100B+ parameters)? Does interpretability become harder or merely more targeted?
  4. How do post-training interventions (fine-tuning, RLHF, quantization, pruning) affect the Rosetta Neuron population and its selectivity properties?
  5. Can ensembles of Rosetta Neurons be used for systematic, scalable domain-specific data curation beyond the single-neuron proof of concept demonstrated here?
  6. The DINOv3 deviation suggests training-objective sensitivity: can we derive from first principles which training objectives or regularizers promote vs. suppress Rosetta Neuron formation?
