Expert-Aware Causal Tracing of Factual Recall in Sparse MoE Language Models

arXiv: 2606.03780

Date: 2026-06-02

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03780

一句话总结： 本文将因果追踪从密集Transformer扩展到稀疏MoE模型，提出两阶段专家感知追踪协议：在Qwen3-30B-A3B中事实回忆可精确定位到第44层的单个专家L44E069，而在Mixtral-8x7B中同一层级的信号却分散在多个专家的协同更新中，表明MoE中的专家级因果定位具有显著的模型依赖性。
TL;DR: This paper extends causal tracing of factual recall from dense transformers to sparse MoE models via a two-stage expert-aware protocol. On Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base, factual recall localizes cleanly to a single expert (L44E069) in layer 44, but on Mixtral-8x7B-v0.1, the validated mid-layer signal requires routed multi-expert coalitions to recover and does not localize to the selected singleton expert. The key insight is that MoE-block-level rescue and single-expert localization can come apart, and expert-level causal localization is model-dependent rather than universal.

核心贡献

  • 提出了首个专家感知的因果追踪框架，将MoE模型中的事实召回分析从聚合的MoE块级别推进到单个专家粒度，填补了稀疏MoE可解释性研究的关键空白。
  • 设计了严格的两阶段协议：第一阶段通过MoE块输出补丁进行层扫描并验证；第二阶段在选定层内通过消融差分估计专家贡献，并将纯净的专家级更新补丁注入噪声运行中。
  • 在Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base上，层扫描锁定第44层（验证集救援分数+0.901），专家级追踪进一步识别出专家L44E069，其在验证集上展现出显著的正特异性（+0.400），优于同层其他活跃专家。
  • 在Mixtral-8x7B-v0.1上，层级追踪验证了第19层的中层信号（救援分数+0.457），但被选中的单一专家L19E006表现出负特异性（−0.175）；多专家联盟检查（纯净Top-2或路由并集）成功恢复了层级信号。
  • 揭示了MoE事实追踪中聚合块救援、单专家定位和多专家路由恢复三种现象可能分离，专家级定位的有效性取决于具体模型和协议，而非普适规律。

Key Contributions

  • Proposes the first expert-aware causal tracing framework for sparse MoE language models, advancing factual-recall analysis from aggregate MoE-block granularity to individual expert granularity.
  • Designs a rigorous two-stage protocol: Stage 1 sweeps MoE layers via block-output patching and validates on held-out cases; Stage 2 estimates expert contributions by ablation difference and patches clean expert-level updates into the noised run.
  • On Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base, layer sweep identifies layer 44 (validation rescue +0.901), and expert-level tracing isolates expert L44E069, which shows robust positive specificity (+0.400) over other same-layer active experts.
  • On Mixtral-8x7B-v0.1, layer-level tracing validates a mid-layer signal at layer 19 (rescue +0.457), but the selected singleton expert L19E006 exhibits negative specificity (−0.175); multi-expert coalition checks (clean top-2 or routing-union) successfully recover the layer-level signal.
  • Reveals that aggregate MoE-block rescue, single-expert localization, and multi-expert routed recovery can dissociate in MoEs, and that expert-level localization is model- and protocol-dependent rather than universal.

Methodology

The study adapts the corrupt-then-restore causal tracing paradigm to sparse MoE architectures through a carefully controlled two-stage protocol.

Dataset and setup. The authors use filtered single-token CounterFact facts (Meng et al., 2022). Each record provides a subject, a relation-specific prompt template, a true object, and a counterfactual foil object. Factual preference is measured as the true-vs-foil next-token logit difference ∆. For each model, they collect 256 usable cases (128 discovery, 128 validation) with model-specific filters ensuring single-token true/foil objects and sufficient clean/noise margins. A relaxed-filter scale-up to 512 cases preserves the main qualitative pattern.

Stage 1: Corruption and MoE-block output patching.

  1. Corruption: Gaussian noise (σ = 3.0 × embedding-matrix standard deviation) is added to subject-token embeddings, degrading the true-vs-foil logit contrast.
  2. MoE-block patching: The final-position output of each MoE block (the aggregate routed hidden-state update vector) from the clean run is patched into the noised run. A rescue score measures the change in logit difference: Rescueℓ = ∆patched,ℓ − ∆noised. Positive rescue indicates the clean MoE-block output restores factual preference.
  3. Layer selection: Layers are selected only on discovery cases and evaluated as fixed hypotheses on validation cases.

Stage 2: Expert-contribution patching.

At the selected layer, the routed update of a clean-active expert e is estimated by ablation difference: ce(x) = MoEOut(x) − MoEOut\e(x), where MoEOut\e suppresses expert e under the original top-k routing decision without rerouting or renormalization. The expert patch vector δe = ce(xclean) − ce(xnoised) is added to the noised MoE-block output.

To avoid confounds, experts are selected via recurrence-first selection: a candidate must be clean-active in at least half of discovery cases, and the recurrent candidate with the highest mean rescue is selected. On validation, specificity is measured against same-source active-random controls (other clean-active experts selected by the router for the same prompt).

Models. Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base: 48 MoE layers, 128 experts per layer, top-8 routing. Mixtral-8x7B-v0.1: 32 MoE layers, 8 experts per layer, top-2 routing.

Evaluation. The authors report mean effects, positive fractions, bootstrap confidence intervals, and two-sided sign-flip tests. Coalition checks for Mixtral patch either the clean-routed top-2 experts or the union of clean- and noised-routed experts.

Results & Highlights

  • Qwen3 layer-level validation (Table 1, Figure 1a): Layer 44 shows a strong, sharp rescue effect (+0.901, 95% CI [+0.752, +1.053]) on held-out validation cases, clearly standing out from other layers.
  • Qwen3 expert-level validation (Table 1, Figure 1b): Expert L44E069, selected by recurrence-first discovery, validates with a rescue of +0.463 and positive specificity of +0.400 (95% CI [+0.276, +0.533]), meaning it outperforms other active same-layer experts. This expert is repeatedly selected in the clean run.
  • Mixtral layer-level validation: Layer 19 validates with rescue +0.457 (95% CI [+0.331, +0.579]), but nearby layers are comparable, indicating a broader mid-layer band rather than sharp single-layer localization.
  • Mixtral expert-level validation (Table 1, Figure 1b): The selected singleton expert L19E006 shows a small positive rescue (+0.099) but negative specificity (−0.175, 95% CI [−0.284, −0.072]), underperforming active-random controls.
  • Mixtral coalition recovery (Figure 1c, Table 16): Clean top-2 coalition patching achieves rescue +0.461 (positive fraction 0.75), and routing-union coalition achieves +0.490 (positive fraction 0.75), both recovering the L19 MoE-block signal. This demonstrates that the factual signal in Mixtral is distributed across routed expert coalitions rather than concentrated in a single expert.
  • Scale-up robustness (Table 14): A relaxed-filter expansion to 512 cases per model preserves the same qualitative pattern: Qwen3 L44E069 maintains positive specificity (+0.413), while Mixtral L19E006 remains negative (−0.155).

Critical Assessment

Novelty (7/10): The extension of causal tracing to expert granularity in MoEs is a natural but important advance. The core empirical finding—that Qwen3 localizes to a singleton expert while Mixtral requires coalitions—is genuinely informative and challenges naive assumptions about expert specialization. However, the paper covers only two models, and the architectural differences (top-8 vs. top-2 routing, 128 vs. 8 experts, different training recipes) are fully confounded with model family, so one cannot cleanly attribute the divergence to any single architectural choice.

Rigor (8/10): The experimental design is notably careful. The two-stage protocol, discovery/validation split, recurrence-first selection to avoid low-N confounds, and active-random specificity controls all demonstrate methodological sophistication. Bootstrap confidence intervals and sign-flip tests are reported. One concern is that the 256-case (strict) sample, while standard for CounterFact tracing studies, is modest for drawing robust conclusions about rare expert behaviors. The relaxed-filter 512-case check helps but is still a filtered diagnostic.

Reproducibility (6/10): The models are publicly available, CounterFact is standard, and the paper provides detailed methodological descriptions. However, no code is released at submission time (the artifact contains scripts but the paper notes "compute requirement remains nontrivial"). Implementing expert-level ablation under fixed routing requires non-trivial MoE introspection infrastructure. The exact hook points and implementation details would be needed for full reproduction.

Significance (7/10): For the mechanistic interpretability community, this work establishes a new granularity of analysis and provides a cautionary lesson: MoE-block rescue does not imply expert-level localization. This has direct implications for model editing and steering in MoEs—practitioners cannot assume that editing a single expert will suffice. The impact is somewhat bounded by the narrow focus on factual recall (a single capability) and the small model sample.

Red flags:

  • The paper is honest about negative results (Mixtral's singleton expert fails), which strengthens credibility. However, the Qwen3 result names "L44E069" specifically; without systematic analysis across more facts and layers, one might worry this is an anecdote rather than a general pattern, though the validation split and controls mitigate this.
  • The model comparison is confounded: Qwen3 and Mixtral differ in tokenizer, training data, routing design (top-8 vs. top-2), number of experts per layer (128 vs. 8), and overall scale. The authors acknowledge this and treat the results as "two controlled case studies," but readers may still be tempted to overgeneralize.
  • No investigation of whether the identified Qwen3 expert is consistently important across different relation types or only for specific subjects.
  • The coalition check in Mixtral is somewhat coarse—only top-2 and routing-union are tested; intermediate coalition sizes and compositions are not systematically explored.

Strengths

  • Methodologically rigorous two-stage protocol with well-designed controls (recurrence-first selection, active-random specificity, discovery/validation splits).
  • Honest and transparent reporting of both positive (Qwen3) and negative (Mixtral) results, enhancing scientific credibility.
  • Clear distinction between three levels of localization: MoE-block rescue, single-expert localization, and multi-expert coalition recovery—a useful conceptual contribution.
  • Bootstrap confidence intervals and sign-flip tests provide statistical grounding for the reported effects.
  • Scale-up check with relaxed filters (512 cases) confirms the main pattern is robust to case-selection thresholds.
  • Well-written and accessible to readers familiar with causal tracing; figures effectively communicate the core empirical pattern.
  • Appropriate scope claims: the authors carefully frame results as model-specific controlled diagnostics rather than universal laws.

Limitations

  • Only two MoE models evaluated, limiting generalizability to other architectures (e.g., DeepSeek-MoE, Switch Transformer, DBRX).
  • Controlled diagnostic setting: filtered single-token CounterFact facts with final-position interventions only; natural multi-token factual recall and non-final positions are not examined.
  • Model differences are fully confounded—routing policy (top-8 vs. top-2), expert count (128 vs. 8), training data, and tokenizer all vary simultaneously.
  • No systematic exploration of coalition compositions in Mixtral beyond top-2 and routing-union.
  • No code publicly released at submission; reproduction requires substantial MoE introspection infrastructure.
  • The two-stage design decomposes only the selected layer, so useful experts in non-selected layers may be missed.
  • Subject-noise corruption with fixed σ = 3.0 may not capture all corruption regimes; no sensitivity analysis on noise level is reported.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does the singleton-expert localization in Qwen3 generalize to other fact categories (e.g., multilingual knowledge, commonsense reasoning, syntactic agreement) beyond CounterFact-style factual recall?
  2. Which architectural factor primarily drives the divergence between Qwen3 and Mixtral—routing topology (top-8 vs. top-2), expert count (128 vs. 8), or training recipe? A systematic ablation on a single architecture would isolate the cause.
  3. Can the identified L44E069 expert in Qwen3 be successfully edited to modify factual behavior, and does editing a singleton expert produce cleaner, more specific updates than dense-model editing or coalition editing in Mixtral?
  4. Do the same patterns hold in other widely deployed MoEs such as DeepSeek-V2/V3, DBRX, or recent Switch Transformer variants?
  5. Is there a systematic relationship between expert selection frequency and causal importance across layers, or is L44E069's importance idiosyncratic to the CounterFact relation distribution?
  6. How do subject-position and relation-position interventions (not just final-position) affect expert-level localization in MoEs compared to dense models?
  7. Can the coalition-recovery pattern in Mixtral be exploited for distributed model editing that modifies factual behavior by editing multiple experts simultaneously with smaller per-expert perturbations?
