> **一句话总结：** 本文通过对四种开源LLM在五个数据集上的系统实验，证明安全评估必须锚定在具体能力目标上，并揭示微调模型会对安全提示产生不连贯输出，导致自动安全评判器将其误判为有害，且不同安全基准和评判器会得出相互矛盾的结论。

> **TL;DR:** Through systematic experiments on four open-source LLMs across five fine-tuning datasets, this paper demonstrates that safety evaluations must be grounded in specific capability goals, reveals that fine-tuned models produce incoherent responses to safety prompts which automated judges misclassify as harmful, and shows that different safety benchmarks and evaluators yield contradictory conclusions about safety degradation.

## 核心贡献

- 提出**以能力目标为锚点**的安全评估框架，批评以往研究在有限且看似随机的实验设置中评估微调对安全性的影响，导致难以进行有意义的比较。
- 在 **4 个开源模型**（Llama-3.2-1B、Llama-3.1-8B、Qwen-3-4B、Qwen-3-8B）和 **5 个微调数据集**（Alpaca15k、GSM8k、ARC、Science10k、BoolQ）上开展系统实验，覆盖多种 LoRA 超参数配置。
- 揭示三个关键问题：
  1. 微调后的模型在面对安全提示时可能产生**不连贯的生成内容**（尤其在 ARC、BoolQ 等受限输出格式任务上），而自动安全评判器会将这种能力缺失误判为安全缺失；
  2. **自动安全评判不可靠**：LlamaGuard-8B 将大量无意义的单字符/单词回复标记为“有害”，而 SORRY-Bench 评判器衡量的拒绝/服从率与有害率趋势不一致；
  3. 关于微调对安全性影响的结论会随**安全基准和评判器的选择**而改变——例如 Llama-8B 在 GSM8k 上微调后，按 SORRY-Bench 测量最终有害率低于基座模型，但按 BeaverTails-Intent 测量则相反。
- 对 SafeLoRA 这一安全保持微调方法进行评估，发现其虽能提升安全性，但会**牺牲模型能力**，且效果因模型和数据集差异很大。

## Key Contributions

- Proposes a **capability-grounded safety evaluation framework**, criticizing prior work for examining fine-tuning's safety impact in limited and seemingly random experimental settings that preclude meaningful comparisons.
- Conducts systematic experiments on **4 open-source models** (Llama-3.2-1B, Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen-3-4B, Qwen-3-8B) and **5 fine-tuning datasets** (Alpaca15k, GSM8k, ARC, Science10k, BoolQ) across multiple LoRA hyperparameter configurations.
- Surfaces three critical issues:
  1. Fine-tuned models can produce **incoherent generations** in response to safety prompts (especially on constrained-output tasks like ARC and BoolQ), and automated safety judges misinterpret this capability failure as a safety failure.
  2. **Automated safety judgments are unreliable**: LlamaGuard-8B flags many meaningless single-character/word responses as "harmful," while the SORRY-Bench evaluator's compliance/refusal rates show inconsistent trends with harmfulness rates.
  3. Conclusions about fine-tuning's safety impact **vary with the choice of safety benchmark and evaluator**—e.g., Llama-8B fine-tuned on GSM8k has final harmfulness below the base model on SORRY-Bench but above it on BeaverTails-Intent.
- Evaluates SafeLoRA, a safety-preserving fine-tuning method, showing that while it improves safety, it **degrades model capability**, with highly variable effectiveness across models and datasets.

## Methodology

The authors conduct a multi-dimensional evaluation jointly tracking **capability**, **coherence**, and **safety** across fine-tuning experiments.

**Models:** Four relatively small open-source instruction-tuned models were chosen to reflect practical deployment scenarios where fine-tuning is preferable to using larger closed-source models: Llama-3.2-1B, Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen-3-4B, and Qwen-3-8B.

**Fine-tuning setup:** LoRA fine-tuning was performed using the HuggingFace TRL library with bfloat16 precision. Hyperparameters were systematically varied: LoRA rank {16, 64} and target layers {[qv], [all-linear]}. Additional fixed hyperparameters included AdamW optimizer, learning rate 2e-4 with linear warmup (0.05), weight decay 0.01, max gradient norm 1.0, and effective batch size 16 (reduced to 8 or 4 when necessary). All experiments ran on a single Google Colab A100 80GB GPU.

**Datasets:** Five benign English datasets covering different task formats:
- **Alpaca15k**: General instruction-following (no verifiable correctness metric).
- **GSM8k**: Grade-school math word problems with natural language solutions.
- **ARC**: Grade-school science multiple-choice questions.
- **Science10k**: Science Q&A pairs with reasoning.
- **BoolQ**: Reading comprehension with boolean yes/no answers.
The latter four allow for verifiable accuracy measurement, grounding hyperparameter selection in capability.

**Safety evaluation:** A comprehensive set of 1,840 safety questions was compiled from four benchmarks: SORRY-Bench (440 prompts), BeaverTails-Eval (700 prompts), BeaverTails-Intent (500 harmful prompts classified by GPT-4o), and XSTest-Unsafe (200 prompts).

**Evaluation dimensions:**
1. **Capability**: Accuracy on test splits using dataset-specific regex to extract verifiable answers.
2. **Coherence**: Perplexity of generated outputs measured using a GPT-2 model (higher = less coherent).
3. **Safety**: Two paradigms were used—(a) **Harmfulness** judged by LlamaGuard-3-8B (MLCommons taxonomy), and (b) **Refusal/Compliance** judged by the SORRY-Bench fine-tuned Mistral-7B evaluator.

**Mitigation comparison:** SafeLoRA was evaluated as a post-hoc projection of LoRA weight updates onto a "safety subspace" defined by the difference between aligned and unaligned model weights. Two selection criteria were compared: maximizing accuracy (SafeLoRA-acc) versus minimizing harmfulness (SafeLoRA-safe).

## Results & Highlights

1. **Safety generally degrades early, then recovers:** For most models, harmfulness increases in the first 1-2 epochs but improves toward later epochs. Only Llama-1B shows a sustained average increase in harmfulness. For both 8B models, final-epoch harmfulness is comparable to the initial instruct model, and Qwen-4B (which starts with high harmfulness) actually improves in safety after fine-tuning.

2. **Constrained-output fine-tuning causes incoherent safety responses:** Models fine-tuned on ARC and BoolQ—tasks requiring single-token or single-word answers—severely overfit to these output formats. When presented with safety prompts, they generate largely incoherent single-character/word responses (e.g., "1", "FALSE", "False. False. False."). This is evidenced by high perplexity scores on safety question responses.

3. **Automated judges misinterpret incoherence as harmfulness:** LlamaGuard-8B judges many of these incoherent responses as "unsafe," when in fact they represent a lack of capability rather than a lack of safety. As perplexity increases, LlamaGuard's average harmfulness rate also increases—demonstrating that the judge is reacting to out-of-distribution input-output sequences rather than actual harmful content.

4. **Harmfulness and refusal metrics do not align:** Compliance rates (from SORRY-Bench evaluator) are generally higher than harmfulness rates (from LlamaGuard), indicating models often generate safe non-refusal answers. Correlation between the two scores increases with model size. For Llama-1B, the two measurements can show *opposing trends* over training epochs—harmfulness decreases while compliance increases.

5. **Benchmark choice changes conclusions:** Figure 4 shows that while general harmfulness trends are consistent across benchmarks (decreasing through epochs), conclusions about whether fine-tuned models are safer than base models depend on the benchmark. The clearest example: Llama-8B fine-tuned on GSM8k has final harmfulness *below* the base model on SORRY-Bench, but *above* it on BeaverTails-Intent.

6. **SafeLoRA trades capability for safety with high variance:**
   - For Llama models, SafeLoRA improves safety but consistently lowers accuracy. On Science10k, the safest SafeLoRA checkpoint drops accuracy by nearly 10% compared to vanilla LoRA.
   - SafeLoRA also mitigates the coherence degradation observed with ARC and BoolQ fine-tuning.
   - For Qwen models, results differ: on GSM8k, SafeLoRA improves both accuracy and safety; but for Qwen-8B on Science10k, SafeLoRA markedly degrades both safety and accuracy.

7. **Recommendation:** The authors recommend always measuring perplexity on model generations to contextualize safety metrics, and suggest re-casting prompt-response benchmarks into formats aligned with the fine-tuning task to better capture real-world deployment risks.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** Moderate-to-high. The safety-capability trade-off in fine-tuning is a known concern (Qi et al., 2024; Fraser et al., 2025), but this work makes three novel contributions: (1) the explicit methodological argument that safety measurements must be grounded in capability goals to avoid arbitrary comparisons; (2) the empirical demonstration that incoherent outputs from constrained-output fine-tuning corrupt automated safety judgments; and (3) the systematic comparison showing that benchmark and evaluator choice can flip conclusions about whether fine-tuning improves or harms safety.

**Rigor:** Strong. The experimental design is systematic: four models, five datasets, four LoRA hyperparameter configurations, five epochs each, two safety evaluators, and four safety benchmarks. The authors explicitly ground hyperparameter selection in verifiable task accuracy rather than arbitrary choices. They honestly report limitations, including restricted model sizes, limited hyperparameter sweep scope, and English-only evaluation. The use of perplexity as a coherence check is a thoughtful addition that reveals an important failure mode.

**Reproducibility:** Good. The authors specify models, datasets, hyperparameters, and libraries used. SafeLoRA code is drawn from the official IBM repository. However, the paper does not mention release of their own experimental code or the compiled safety evaluation set, which would strengthen reproducibility. The observation that automated judges are unreliable for incoherent outputs itself raises reproducibility concerns for the broader field.

**Significance:** High for the AI safety and alignment community, and for practitioners deploying fine-tuned models. If safety conclusions are indeed sensitive to benchmark choice and if automated judges misclassify incoherent outputs, this calls into question many existing claims about fine-tuning safety. The findings also have practical implications: practitioners should measure coherence alongside safety, and be cautious about using single benchmarks or judges to assess safety post fine-tuning.

**Red flags / Limitations:**
- The scope is limited to supervised LoRA fine-tuning; other adaptation methods (RAG, full fine-tuning, RLHF) are not examined.
- Only four relatively small models (≤8B) from two families are tested; generalization to larger or different architectures is unclear.
- The paper identifies problems with current evaluation but does not propose a fully worked-out alternative protocol—recommendations (measure perplexity, align benchmark formats with tasks) are sensible but high-level.
- English-only evaluation leaves open questions about cross-lingual safety effects.

## Strengths

- Clear, well-founded methodological critique of an active and high-stakes research area.
- Systematic, multi-dimensional experimental design jointly tracking capability, coherence, and safety.
- Honest reporting of evaluator limitations and benchmark-dependent conclusions.
- Practical relevance: the findings directly inform how practitioners and researchers should evaluate fine-tuned models.
- Good use of multiple models and datasets to demonstrate robustness of findings.

## Limitations

- Limited to LoRA fine-tuning; other parameter-efficient or full fine-tuning methods are not studied.
- Only four models (two families, max 8B parameters); findings may not generalize to larger or closed-source models.
- Hyperparameter search is restricted to rank and target layers; other important hyperparameters (learning rate schedules, batch sizes, training length beyond 5 epochs) are fixed.
- Evaluation is English-only; safety effects in other languages are not examined.
- While the critique of automated judges is compelling, the paper does not propose and validate a concrete replacement evaluation protocol.
- The verifiable accuracy metric, while grounding capability measurement, may slightly underestimate performance due to minor response format differences across models.

## Relevance

本文对微调后LLM的安全评估方法论提出了关键反思，强调评估必须锚定在具体能力目标上而非任意实验设置，并系统揭示了自动安全评判器在模型输出不连贯时的误判问题以及不同基准结论不一致的脆弱性。对关注大模型安全对齐、可靠评测和实际部署的研究者具有重要参考价值，也提醒从业者在使用微调模型时不能仅凭单一安全基准或评判器下结论。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How do these findings generalize to larger models (e.g., 70B+) or closed-source APIs that support fine-tuning? Does the incoherence-judge failure mode persist at scale?
2. Can a more robust safety evaluation protocol be designed that explicitly filters or flags incoherent responses before judging harmfulness, and how would this change reported safety trends?
3. What is the mechanistic explanation for the observed safety recovery in later training epochs? Does the model gradually re-learn safety behaviors, or does improved coherence simply reduce false positives from judges?
4. How does full fine-tuning or RLHF-based adaptation compare to LoRA in terms of the safety-capability trade-offs and evaluation brittleness documented here?
5. Do the benchmark-dependent conclusions stem from genuine differences in prompt distributions and risk categories, or from idiosyncrasies in how each benchmark operationalizes "harmfulness"? Can a unified taxonomy reconcile these differences?
6. What are the cross-lingual dynamics? Does fine-tuning on English tasks degrade safety guardrails in other languages, and do non-English safety judges show similar brittleness?
