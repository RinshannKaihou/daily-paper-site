Clustered Self-Assessment: A Simple yet Effective Method for Uncertainty Quantification in Large Language Models

arXiv: 2606.03846

Date: 2026-06-02

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03846

一句话总结： 本文提出了一种基于语义聚类的自评估方法，通过NLI模型将LLM采样生成结果聚类后转化为结构化的多选题选项，再利用模型对选项标签的Softmax概率作为置信度估计，在TriviaQA、Natural Questions和XSum上持续优于Semantic Entropy、SAR、P(True)等基线，且仅需两个额外样本即可达到竞争性能。
TL;DR: This paper proposes Clustered Self-Assessment, a method that groups sampled LLM generations into semantic clusters via an NLI model, converts them into structured multiple-choice options, and uses the model's softmax probability over choice labels as a confidence estimate. It consistently outperforms baselines (Semantic Entropy, SAR, P(True), etc.) across TriviaQA, Natural Questions, and XSum on seven open-source models, achieving competitive performance with as few as two additional samples.

Key Contributions

  • Proposes a novel self-assessment uncertainty quantification method that leverages the LLM's own evaluation capability on structured multiple-choice questions, rather than relying on indirect signals like entropy.
  • Designs an NLI-based semantic clustering mechanism that groups sampled generations according to bidirectional entailment relationships, ensuring multiple-choice options are semantically distinct and preventing compatible answers from splitting probability mass.
  • Consistently outperforms a comprehensive set of baselines across seven models and three datasets, including Perplexity, Probability, P(True), Predictive Entropy, LN-Entropy, Semantic Entropy, Ecc/EigV/Deg, and SAR variants.
  • Demonstrates exceptional sample efficiency: achieves competitive performance with only two additional samples, compared to up to sixteen for baseline methods, substantially reducing computational overhead.
  • Exhibits strong calibration (lower Brier scores than probability-based baselines) and enables training hidden-state probes that generalize well to out-of-distribution settings, substantially outperforming Semantic Entropy Probe.

Methodology

The proposed method consists of two stages:

Stage 1: Answer Clustering. For each question, the LLM generates multiple sampled answers plus a greedy-decoded answer to be assessed. An external NLI model estimates pairwise semantic relationships among all sampled answers, predicting labels in {entailment, neutral, contradiction} for each ordered pair. Using bidirectional NLI results, answers are grouped into clusters via a rule-based criterion: two answers are merged if their bidirectional labels contain no contradiction or include at least one entailment in both directions. The first answer assigned to each cluster becomes its representative. This ensures clusters correspond to semantically distinct candidate answers while reducing redundancy.

Stage 2: Self-Assessment via Multiple-Choice Question (MCQ). The clustered answers are formatted as a structured MCQ, where each cluster becomes one option (labeled A, B, C, etc.). An additional option "None of the above" is included to handle cases where all sampled answers may be incorrect. The original LLM is then presented with this MCQ, and the softmax probability assigned to the choice label token (e.g., P("A")) is used as the confidence score for the answer being assessed.

The clustering stage is critical: without it, semantically equivalent answers would appear as separate choices and split the model's probability mass, degrading uncertainty estimation reliability.

Results & Highlights

  • Main Results (AUROC): Across TriviaQA (9,960 samples), Natural Questions (3,610 samples), and XSum (11,334 samples), the proposed method consistently ranks first or near-first across all seven evaluated models: Qwen2.5-7B/14B/32B, Qwen3-14B/30B-A3B, and Gemma-3-12B/27B. On TQA, AUROC values range from ~0.924 to ~0.940; on NQ, from ~0.821 to ~0.853.
  • Sample Efficiency: Using Qwen2.5-32B, the method achieves strong AUROC with only 2 additional samples on both TQA and NQ, while baselines such as SAR, Semantic Entropy, EigV, Deg, and Ecc require 8–16 samples to approach comparable performance. This is particularly significant because the computational overhead primarily comes from generating additional samples—the NLI clustering is lightweight, and obtaining choice logits requires only single-token generation.
  • Ablation Study: Removing answer clustering causes substantial performance drops (e.g., on TQA with Qwen2.5-7B: 0.927 → 0.903; on NQ with Gemma-3-12B: 0.843 → 0.787). Removing sampling entirely degenerates the method to P(True), with even larger drops (e.g., TQA Qwen2.5-7B: 0.927 → 0.832; NQ Gemma-3-12B: 0.843 → 0.609). Both components are essential.
  • Calibration (Brier Score): The method consistently achieves lower Brier scores than P(True), Probability, and normalized Semantic Entropy across all model-dataset combinations. For example, on TQA with Qwen2.5-32B: Ours 0.0843 vs. P(True) 0.1172 vs. Probability 0.2267. On NQ with Gemma-3-27B: Ours 0.1736 vs. P(True) 0.2354 vs. Probability 0.3503.
  • Probe Generalization: A probe trained on hidden states using the method's score as soft labels achieves performance comparable to an accuracy probe (trained on correctness labels) in in-distribution settings, and substantially outperforms both Semantic Entropy Probe and accuracy probe in out-of-distribution settings. This suggests the score aligns well with the LLM's internal uncertainty representations.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The core insight—converting open-ended generation uncertainty into a multiple-choice self-assessment task—is conceptually elegant and well-motivated. While sampling-based uncertainty quantification is an active research area, the specific combination of NLI-based semantic clustering + MCQ reformulation + choice-label probability as score represents a meaningful and practical innovation over prior work.
  • Rigor: The experimental design is commendable. The authors test across three diverse datasets (two QA, one summarization), seven models from three families, and ten baseline methods. Metrics include both discrimination (AUROC) and calibration (Brier score). The ablation study clearly isolates the contribution of each component. However, the paper does not report statistical significance tests for the performance differences, which would strengthen claims of consistent superiority.
  • Reproducibility: The method is straightforward to implement given an LLM with logit access and an NLI model. The authors state code will be released. Prompts for generation and MCQ formatting are provided in the appendix. The main ambiguity is the exact NLI model used (not explicitly named in the extracted text, though likely a standard model like DeBERTa-NLI).
  • Significance: The method's simplicity, strong empirical results, and minimal sample requirements make it highly practical for real-world deployment. The finding that MCQ-formatted self-assessment outperforms entropy-based discrepancy measures suggests an important direction for the field—leveraging LLMs' trained capabilities on structured formats rather than inventing new heuristics. The probe results showing alignment with internal representations are particularly interesting.
  • Red Flags: (1) The method requires logit access, which limits applicability to closed-source APIs. (2) Dependence on an external NLI model introduces a potential failure mode if the NLI model misclusters nuanced or domain-specific answers. (3) The "None of the above" option handles cases where all samples are wrong, but the paper does not explicitly analyze its effectiveness in such scenarios. (4) Performance gains, while consistent, are sometimes modest (e.g., on NQ for some models, the gap to strong baselines like P(True) is relatively small).

Strengths

  • Simplicity and elegance: The method requires no model fine-tuning, no architectural changes, and no complex post-processing—just clustering, MCQ formatting, and logit extraction.
  • Exceptional sample efficiency: Competitive performance with only 2 additional samples makes it feasible for latency-sensitive applications where generating 8–16 samples is prohibitively expensive.
  • Strong empirical breadth: Evaluated across 3 datasets, 7 models, and 10 baselines with multiple evaluation metrics.
  • Human interpretability: Confidence scores are derived from structured multiple-choice probabilities, which are more intuitive than entropy-based discrepancy scores.
  • Practical extensibility: The confidence scores can train hidden-state probes that generalize to out-of-distribution settings, offering a path to efficient uncertainty estimation without sampling at inference time.

Limitations

  • Logit access requirement: The method requires access to output logits to compute choice-label probabilities. This is unavailable for many closed-source API-only models (e.g., GPT-4, Claude), limiting deployment scenarios.
  • External NLI dependency: Answer clustering relies on an external NLI model, which adds computational overhead and may struggle with nuanced semantics, out-of-distribution content, or highly domain-specific terminology. The authors note that using the LLM's own internal representations for clustering is a promising future direction.
  • No post-hoc calibration: The method uses raw softmax probabilities without temperature scaling or other calibration techniques. While performance is already strong, the authors acknowledge that adding calibration could further improve reliability.
  • Limited analysis of failure modes: The paper does not deeply analyze cases where all sampled answers are incorrect (despite the "None of the above" option) or where the NLI model produces erroneous clustering.
  • Evaluation scope limited to extractive QA and summarization: While TQA, NQ, and XSum are standard benchmarks, the method's effectiveness on more complex reasoning tasks (math, code, multi-hop reasoning) or longer-form generation remains to be validated.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can the method be adapted for closed-source models that only provide token probabilities or ranked outputs, rather than full logits? Would approximate choice probabilities suffice?
  2. How does the method perform on complex reasoning tasks (mathematical reasoning, code generation, multi-hop QA) where answers are not simple factual strings but structured outputs or reasoning chains?
  3. Would replacing the external NLI model with the LLM's own hidden-state similarity or self-evaluation for clustering improve robustness on out-of-distribution or domain-specific inputs?
  4. How effective is the "None of the above" option in practice when all sampled generations are hallucinated or incorrect? Can the model reliably detect this condition?
  5. Could integrating post-hoc calibration (e.g., temperature scaling, Platt scaling) on top of the MCQ probabilities further improve Brier scores and expected calibration error?
