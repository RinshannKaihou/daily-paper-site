The Reliability Gap in Benchmark Auditing: Distribution Shift and Scale as Failure Modes of Contamination Detection

arXiv: 2606.03305

Date: 2026-06-03

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03305

一句话总结： 本文通过在27个多家族多规模模型（含前沿工业模型）上进行335次评估，系统检验了LLM Dataset Inference、Post-Hoc Dataset Inference和CoDeC三种基准污染检测范式，发现分布偏移和规模限制导致这些方法在实际审计场景中仅约60%的判定正确，揭示了受控验证与实用基准审计之间的系统性可靠性差距。
TL;DR: This paper systematically evaluates three leading benchmark contamination detection paradigms across 27 models and 335 evaluations, identifying distribution shift and scale constraints as critical failure modes that create a systematic reliability gap—yielding only ~60% correct outcomes—between controlled academic validation and practical benchmark auditing.

核心贡献

  • 指出了两个被忽视但实际关键的失效模式：分布偏移（验证集与嫌疑训练集违反IID假设）和规模限制（基准数据比预训练语料小多个数量级，导致生成式对照数据质量不足）。
  • 在27个模型上系统评估了三种主流检测范式（LLM Dataset Inference、Post-Hoc Dataset Inference、CoDeC），覆盖多个模型家族（Pythia、OLMo 2、文化与医学专用LLM）及规模（最高27B），并进一步扩展至前沿工业模型（Gemma 3、Qwen 3、Llama 3.2）。
  • 在335次评估中，仅199次（约60%）得到正确结果，揭示了统计检测方法从受控环境迁移到实际审计场景时的系统性可靠性差距。
  • 提出了针对各方法的实用建议：LLM DI仅在拥有真正未见过且近似IID验证集时可用；Post-Hoc DI对标准基准规模数据集不适用；CoDeC更适合作为粗粒度的比较性污染指标而非认证工具。
  • 开源了评估基准与代码，为后续研究提供可复现的测试平台。

Key Contributions

  • Identifies two under-studied but critical failure modes: distribution shift (violation of IID assumptions between suspect and validation sets) and scale constraints (benchmarks being orders of magnitude smaller than pre-training corpora, leading to insufficient synthetic held-out data quality).
  • Systematically evaluates three leading detection paradigms (LLM Dataset Inference, Post-Hoc Dataset Inference, and CoDeC) across 27 models from multiple families (Pythia, OLMo 2, cultural and medical LLMs) and scales (up to 27B), with further extension to frontier industry models (Gemma 3, Qwen 3, Llama 3.2).
  • Reveals that only 199 out of 335 evaluations (~60%) yield correct outcomes, exposing a systematic reliability gap when moving statistical detection methods from controlled environments to practical benchmark auditing.
  • Provides practical recommendations for each method: LLM DI is only usable with a genuinely unseen and approximately IID validation set; Post-Hoc DI is unsuitable for standard benchmark-sized datasets; CoDeC is best interpreted as a comparative contamination indicator rather than a certification tool.
  • Open-sources the evaluation benchmark and code to enable reproducible follow-up research.

Methodology

The authors evaluate three state-of-the-art contamination detection paradigms under realistic "in-the-wild" benchmark auditing conditions:

  1. LLM Dataset Inference [Maini et al., 2024]: Aggregates 52 membership inference attack (MIA) features (perplexity, Min-k%, perturbation scores, reference-model comparisons, compression ratios) across samples from a suspect set and an unseen validation set. A linear regressor is trained on a subset to learn feature correlations, then a one-sided t-test (p < 0.1) on held-out samples determines whether the suspect set exhibits systematically stronger membership evidence. The method critically assumes the validation set is unseen and IID with the suspect set.
  1. Post-Hoc Dataset Inference [Zhao et al., 2025]: Relaxes the IID validation set requirement by generating a synthetic validation set. A small generator model is trained on one pool of text snippets, then used to produce artificial suffixes conditioned on prefixes from a second pool. Two classifiers are trained—one text-only, one combining text with MIA features—and a one-sided t-test determines whether MIA features provide additional separability beyond text alone (p < 0.1 threshold, mean over 3 runs).
  1. CoDeC [Zawalski et al., 2025]: A context-based method measuring how same-dataset in-context examples affect next-token likelihood. For each sample, it compares average log-likelihood in a baseline setting versus an in-context setting (preceded by one random sample from the same dataset). The contamination score is the fraction of samples where adding context reduces confidence (Δ(x) < 0). Scores > 0.8 indicate strong contamination; < 0.6 indicate no contamination; 0.6–0.8 are inconclusive.

Experimental Design: Four tasks were conducted:

  • Task 1 (Benchmark-Scale Auditing): Pythia models (0.4B–6.9B) evaluated on Pile subsets (CC, Europarl, Hacker News, Stack Exchange) with only n=2,000 documents per subset.
  • Task 2 (Split-Level Exposure): OLMo 2 instruction-tuned models (1B/7B/13B) audited on GSM8K, Competition Math, MMLU, and DROP train/test splits.
  • Task 3 (Specialized Post-Training): Medical LLMs (MedGemma 4B/27B, Meditron3 8B/9B, Meerkat 7B/8B, Neeto-1.0 8B) on MedQA-USMLE and MedMCQA; Polish PLLuM models on Automatic/Manual SFT and Alignment splits.
  • Task 4 (Industry Models): Comparative auditing of Gemma 3 (1B/4B/12B), Qwen 3 (1.4B/3B/14B), and Llama 3.2 (1B/3B) on GSM8K, MMLU, and DROP.

Outcomes are coded as: + (true positive), − (true negative), + (false positive), − (false negative), ? (uncertain).

Results & Highlights

  • Overall reliability is poor: Across all 335 evaluations, only 199 (~60%) yielded correct outcomes, with false positives and false negatives roughly balancing successful detections (Figure 1).
  • Task 1 — Benchmark-scale reference data:
  • LLM DI correctly distinguished train from test splits across all Pythia model sizes (15/16 correct on Stack Exchange; 12/16 on Pile CC/Europarl).
  • Post-Hoc DI failed to reject the null hypothesis in most cases (only 4/16 and 8/16 correct). At benchmark scale, the generator trained on 2,000 samples was too weak to produce realistic synthetic validation data.
  • CoDeC assigned high contamination scores to both train and test splits when distributions were similar, reflecting sensitivity to distributional overlap rather than precise membership (8/16 correct).
  • Task 2 — Split-level exposure on OLMo 2:
  • LLM DI produced false positives on DROP and MMLU (train splits flagged despite no training exposure), likely driven by split-level distribution differences in difficulty or style rather than memorization (11/18 correct).
  • Post-Hoc DI again failed to cleanly separate train and test splits; synthetic validation artifacts dominated the statistical test (14/18 correct, but many unstable outcomes).
  • CoDeC could not reliably distinguish seen from unseen splits within the same benchmark. Scores decreased consistently with model size but were indifferent to whether a split was used for training (6/18 correct).
  • Task 3 — Specialized post-training datasets:
  • Performance degraded substantially. For medical QA, LLM DI succeeded on MedMCQA but consistently failed on MedQA-USMLE. CoDeC was unstable across models.
  • For PLLuM Polish models, both methods were limited by small evaluation splits. LLM DI produced repeated false negatives on train splits and false positives on Alignment data. CoDeC showed partial success for PLLuM-12B but performed worse for Llama-PLLuM-8B.
  • Task 4 — Industry models:
  • Signals were suggestive rather than definitive. Most model families received elevated CoDeC scores and were flagged by LLM DI on at least some benchmarks.
  • Gemma 3 stood out as the only family not flagged by LLM DI on GSM8K and having consistently lower CoDeC scores, suggesting it was likely not trained on the GSM8K training set.
  • Qwen models showed patterns suggestive of GSM8K training exposure.
  • Failure mode diagnosis:
  • LLM DI: False positives arise when benchmark train/test splits differ in difficulty or construction, violating the IID assumption. The test captures distribution differences, not just training exposure.
  • Post-Hoc DI: At benchmark scale, the text-only classifier already achieves high AUC (>0.78–0.9 for most Pile subsets), indicating that real-versus-synthetic distribution shift dominates the signal, leaving little room for MIA features to contribute meaningful membership information.
  • CoDeC: Effectively separates pre-training corpora from evaluation-only benchmarks in aggregate (coarse provenance), but train and test splits within the same dataset yield nearly identical scores, lacking split-level resolution.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The explicit framing and systematic quantification of "distribution shift" and "scale constraints" as failure modes for contamination detection represents a meaningful conceptual contribution. While these issues are intuitively understood, the comprehensive empirical validation across multiple methods, model families, and scales (including industry models) is valuable and timely.
  • Rigor: The experimental design is thorough, with controlled ablations (Task 1 vs. full-scale appendix comparisons), multi-domain coverage (general, medical, low-resource language), and careful outcome notation. The use of ground-truth training data for open models (Pythia, OLMo 2) strengthens internal validity. The decision to increase the p-value threshold from 0.05 to 0.1 for LLM DI (to match Post-Hoc DI) and 0.14 for Post-Hoc DI is justified by the authors but could be seen as lenient.
  • Reproducibility: Strong. The authors open-source their benchmark and code (linked in footnote 5). The use of publicly available models and benchmarks further aids replication. The paper provides detailed methodological descriptions and appendix tables.
  • Significance: Highly relevant to the LLM evaluation community. Benchmark contamination is a pressing concern, and showing that existing statistical tools fail in realistic settings challenges current practices and leaderboard integrity claims. The practical recommendations are actionable.
  • Red Flags / Caveats:
  1. For industry models (Task 4), ground-truth contamination is unavailable, so conclusions are inherently speculative and comparative rather than definitive.
  2. The ~60% aggregate accuracy figure conflates different task difficulties and method configurations; readers should examine per-task breakdowns rather than relying solely on the headline number.
  3. The threshold choices (p < 0.1, CoDeC 0.6/0.8) are somewhat arbitrary and sensitive; different thresholds could shift outcomes.
  4. The paper focuses on diagnosing failures but does not propose novel solutions or mitigations, leaving the reader with a problem statement rather than a path forward.

Strengths

  • Large-scale empirical evaluation with 27 models and 335 total evaluations.
  • Multi-family coverage (Pythia, OLMo 2, medical LLMs, Polish LLMs, industry models) enhances generalizability.
  • Explicit identification, naming, and diagnosis of two practical failure modes (distribution shift and scale constraints).
  • Four well-structured tasks progressing from controlled settings to realistic and industry scenarios.
  • Detailed failure mode analysis with quantitative evidence (AUC tables, p-value tables, score visualizations).
  • Open-sourced benchmark and code promote reproducibility and follow-up research.
  • Intellectually honest conclusions that avoid overstating capabilities and explicitly acknowledge limitations.

Limitations

  • No silver bullet identified: The paper demonstrates that current methods fail but does not propose improved alternatives or robust mitigation strategies.
  • Industry model uncertainty: Task 4 lacks ground-truth labels for closed models, making all conclusions inferential and potentially confounded by unknown training procedures.
  • Threshold sensitivity: Detection outcomes depend on somewhat arbitrary thresholds (p < 0.1 for DI methods, CoDeC score bands of 0.6/0.8). The paper does not extensively explore threshold robustness.
  • Post-Hoc DI generator quality: The failure of Post-Hoc DI at benchmark scale is attributed to weak generators, but the paper does not explore whether stronger generators or alternative generative approaches could salvage the method.
  • Limited to English-centric and selected domains: While medical and Polish-language models are included, the evaluation does not cover multilingual or vision-language settings where contamination dynamics may differ.
  • arXiv preprint: Not peer-reviewed; some claims and methodological choices may benefit from external scrutiny.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can the reliability gap be closed by developing detection methods that are explicitly robust to distribution shift, or is transparent data provenance truly the only viable solution?
  2. How sensitive are the aggregate results to the choice of p-value and contamination-score thresholds? Would a Bayesian or calibration-aware approach improve reliability?
  3. Could stronger synthetic data generators (e.g., based on larger foundation models) rescue Post-Hoc DI at benchmark scale, or is the approach fundamentally limited?
  4. How do these failure modes manifest in multimodal or vision-language model benchmarks, where contamination may involve image-text pairs rather than text alone?
  5. For the industry model analysis, can external evidence (e.g., training data disclosures, temporal benchmarking) be combined with statistical signals to increase confidence in contamination claims?
