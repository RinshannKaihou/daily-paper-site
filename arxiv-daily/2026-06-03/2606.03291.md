> **一句话总结：** 本文将TOFU基准扩展至英、中、德、俄、土五种语言，系统揭示了大模型多语言知识遗忘的跨语言迁移规律（与文字系统和语系相似性高度相关），并通过逐层表征分析与推理时 steering 向量干预证明：当前遗忘方法并未真正擦除知识，而是在后期解码层施加可逆的表层抑制，单一致 steering 方向即可恢复50%（Qwen）至90%（Gemma）的遗忘知识。

> **TL;DR:** This paper extends the TOFU benchmark to five languages (English, Chinese, German, Russian, Turkish) and systematically characterizes cross-lingual unlearning transfer in multilingual LLMs. The authors find that transfer strength is highly variable and strongly correlated with shared scripts and language families. Through layer-wise representational analysis and inference-time steering vector interventions, they demonstrate that current unlearning methods do not truly erase knowledge but instead induce superficial suppression in later decoding layers. A single steering direction recovers 50% of unlearned knowledge in Qwen2.5-7B and 90% in Gemma2-9B, with the effect transferring across languages.

## 核心贡献

- 首次对多语言大模型的跨语言知识遗忘迁移进行系统性研究，将TOFU基准扩展至五种语言（英、中、德、俄、土）。
- 发现遗忘迁移性高度可变：与共享文字系统和语系的语言间迁移最强，且高预训练覆盖率语言（英语、中文）作为遗忘源语言时迁移效应更强。
- 通过跨语言提示实验表明，语言间的隐藏映射强度与遗忘迁移显著相关（Pearson r=0.50, p<0.05; Spearman ρ=0.60, p<0.01）。
- 逐层余弦相似度与PCA分析揭示：遗忘基本保留早期和中间层的跨语言对齐，主要作用于后期解码层，未真正擦除知识。
- 构建基于表征差异的 steering 向量，在不使用遗忘数据、不进行额外微调的情况下，推理时注入即可恢复大量遗忘知识，且该抑制方向具有跨语言通用性。

## Key Contributions

- First comprehensive study of cross-lingual unlearning transfer in multilingual LLMs, extending the TOFU benchmark to five languages.
- Discovers that unlearning transfer is highly variable: strongest between languages sharing scripts and families, and asymmetric with high-pretraining-coverage languages (English, Chinese) transferring more strongly.
- Shows through cross-lingual prompting that hidden mapping strength between languages significantly correlates with unlearning transfer (Pearson r=0.50, p<0.05; Spearman ρ=0.60, p<0.01).
- Layer-wise cosine similarity and PCA analysis reveal that unlearning largely preserves early- and middle-layer cross-lingual alignment, operating primarily in later decoding layers without true erasure.
- Constructs steering vectors from representational differences that recover substantial unlearned knowledge at inference time without relearning or access to forget data, with the suppression direction generalizing across languages.

## Methodology

The authors build on the TOFU (Task of Fictitious Unlearning) benchmark, which contains synthetic facts about 200 fictitious authors described by 20 QA pairs each. They extend TOFU to four additional languages—Chinese, German, Russian, and Turkish—using Gemini-flash2.5 translation, yielding parallel QA pairs across all five languages. The forget/retain partition is kept identical across languages so that unlearning always targets the same underlying facts.

Experiments are conducted on Qwen2.5-7B and Gemma2-9B. For each configuration, three model variants are produced: (1) the base model \(f_{base}\), (2) the fine-tuned model \(f_{ft}\) trained on all authors in chosen fine-tuning languages \(L_{FT}\), and (3) the unlearned model \(f_{un}\) obtained by applying an unlearning objective to remove knowledge about forget-set authors in chosen unlearning languages \(L_{unl}\).

The primary unlearning objective is Direct Preference Optimization (DPO): for each forget prompt, the model is encouraged to prefer an "I don't know" refusal over the ground-truth answer. Gradient Ascent (GA) and Negative Preference Optimization (NPO) are also evaluated in ablations. Evaluation uses a multilingual NLI model (xlm-roberta-large-xnli) to score semantic equivalence between generated and ground-truth answers, rather than relying solely on lexical overlap.

To localize the unlearning effect, the authors extract pooled hidden states at each layer and compute (i) cross-lingual cosine similarity curves, (ii) PCA projections of forget-set representations across model variants and layers, and (iii) per-layer steering vectors as the normalized difference between fine-tuned and auxiliary-unlearned model activations on retain-set data. These vectors are then subtracted (with tunable strength α and span N) during inference to test reversibility.

## Results & Highlights

**Cross-lingual unlearning transfer (Table 1, Qwen2.5-7B).** Unlearning in the same language as querying yields near-complete forgetting (e.g., English→English drops 90 NLI points). Cross-lingual transfer is highly variable: English unlearning transfers strongly to German (−17) and Russian (−13), weakly to Chinese (−7); Turkish unlearning transfers strongly to English (−55) despite the model's poor English performance on the forget set (11%). Both language family and script influence transfer: Indo-European languages transfer more strongly even across scripts; shared script boosts transfer even across families; maximum transfer occurs when both are shared (e.g., English↔German).

**Asymmetry and high-coverage dominance.** Languages with high pretraining coverage (English, Chinese) are stronger unlearning sources than low-coverage languages (German, Russian, Turkish). However, even weak-source languages can substantially impact high-coverage targets, suggesting a shared language-agnostic concept space.

**Cross-lingual prompting (Table 2).** Instructing the model to answer in the fine-tuning language when queried in another language yields large gains (up to +61 for German query→English answer). The pairwise gains correlate strongly with unlearning transfer scores, supporting the hypothesis that inputs in different languages access similar internal representations and that unlearning disrupts this shared space.

**Layer-wise representational analysis (Figures 1–2).** Cosine similarity between semantically equivalent questions in different languages remains high through early and middle layers for both \(f_{ft}\) and \(f_{un}\), diverging only in later decoding layers. PCA shows that \(f_{un}\) representations separate from \(f_{ft}\) early (by layer 10 for Chinese) and do not revert toward \(f_{base}\), indicating suppression rather than erasure.

**Steering-vector recovery (Figure 3).** Injecting extracted steering vectors (computed only on English retain-set data) recovers over 50% of lost performance on English forget set for Qwen, with substantial recovery in Chinese, German, Russian, and Turkish as well. Random Gaussian baselines produce only small, inconsistent gains, confirming that the extracted vectors capture a structured unlearning direction. The same qualitative patterns hold for Gemma2-9B, where recovery reaches ~90%, and for GA/NPO unlearning methods.

## Authors & Credibility

All authors are affiliated with the School of Computing and Information Systems, University of Melbourne. The paper is published at ICML 2026 (PMLR 306). The code is publicly available. The work builds rigorously on established benchmarks (TOFU) and models (Qwen2.5-7B, Gemma2-9B), uses native-speaker validation for NLI evaluation, and reports confidence intervals over 5 random forget-set shufflings. The evaluation metric (multilingual NLI) is well-motivated to capture semantic leakage beyond lexical overlap.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** High. This is the first systematic study of cross-lingual unlearning transfer across multiple languages with controlled linguistic relatedness. The combination of behavioral evaluation, representational analysis, and steering-based reversibility testing is comprehensive.

**Rigor:** Strong overall. The authors control for language family and script explicitly, use multiple forget-set shufflings with confidence intervals, validate NLI predictions with native speakers, and test two models and three unlearning objectives. The steering vectors are carefully constructed from auxiliary retain-set data to avoid data leakage. A limitation is that the TOFU benchmark uses synthetic, post-injected facts rather than naturally pretrained knowledge; the authors acknowledge this and note recent evidence that pretraining-acquired knowledge may respond differently to unlearning.

**Reproducibility:** Good. Public code, standard models, and a well-defined benchmark facilitate reproduction. Hyperparameters for steering (α, N) are reported, though optimized on the forget set to estimate worst-case leakage upper bounds.

**Significance:** High for LLM safety, privacy, and regulatory compliance (e.g., GDPR "right to be forgotten"). The finding that unlearned knowledge is easily recoverable across languages via simple steering vectors raises serious practical concerns about the robustness of current unlearning methods in deployed multilingual models.

**Red flags:** The ease of reversibility is a critical red flag for practitioners relying on unlearning for sensitive data removal. The small-scale setting (1% forget split, simple factual QA) may not generalize to larger unlearning fractions or complex reasoning content. Translation via Gemini-flash2.5, while treated as an intentional design feature to preserve semantic alignment, may not capture all linguistic variation in real multilingual documents.

## Strengths

- First systematic multilingual unlearning study with controlled linguistic variables (family, script, pretraining coverage).
- Multi-faceted methodology combining behavioral evaluation, cross-lingual prompting, layer-wise cosine similarity, PCA, and steering interventions.
- Strong empirical rigor: two models, three unlearning methods, five languages, multiple shufflings with confidence intervals.
- Well-motivated multilingual NLI evaluation metric with native-speaker validation.
- Careful construction of steering vectors from auxiliary data to avoid circularity.
- Clear, honest discussion of limitations and scope.

## Limitations

- TOFU facts are synthetically generated and post-injected via fine-tuning; behavior for naturally pretrained knowledge may differ.
- Small unlearning scale (1% forget split) may not reflect scenarios requiring removal of larger content fractions.
- Simple factual QA format does not capture complex reasoning or long-form generation settings.
- Translation-based multilingual extension, while controlled, may lack the linguistic variation of naturally occurring multilingual documents.
- Steering interventions assume white-box access; black-box adversaries would face additional constraints.
- Only five languages are studied; broader linguistic diversity (especially low-resource languages) remains unexplored.

## Relevance

本文对多语言大模型的安全部署与隐私合规具有重要警示意义：当前知识遗忘方法在多语言环境下不仅存在显著的跨语言泄漏风险，而且其抑制效应可通过简单的推理时 steering 向量被大量逆转，表明现有技术在真正擦除敏感知识方面仍远远不够可靠。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How does multilingual unlearning behave for knowledge acquired during pretraining rather than post-training fine-tuning?
2. Can the observed reversibility be mitigated by designing unlearning methods that operate across all layers, including early and middle representations?
3. Do these findings generalize to low-resource languages and to larger unlearning fractions beyond 1%?
4. What defense mechanisms can be deployed to detect or prevent steering-based recovery attacks in production multilingual LLMs?
5. How do complex reasoning tasks and long-form generation settings alter the cross-lingual transfer and reversibility patterns observed for simple factual QA?
6. Can alternative unlearning objectives (e.g., representation-misdirection methods) achieve more robust, irreversible multilingual forgetting?
