TL;DR: This paper rigorously proves that momentum in the Muon optimizer acts as a spectral filter: exponential moving average suppresses gradient noise and preserves the coherent signal before orthogonalization, enlarging the spectral gap and stabilizing singular subspaces. The "denoise first, orthogonalize later" ordering is theoretically shown to strictly dominate both the reversed order and the no-momentum baseline.

Key Contributions

  • Theoretical bridge: First theoretical explanation of why momentum helps in Muon, filling a gap where prior analyses either removed momentum or retained it without explaining its benefit.
  • Spectral-filter perspective: Proves momentum in Muon acts as a spectral filter—under a structured signal-plus-perturbation gradient model, it suppresses noise while preserving the dominant signal, thereby enlarging the spectral gap.
  • Ordering matters: Rigorously shows that Pre-polar (momentum before orthogonalization) strictly dominates Post-polar (orthogonalize before momentum) and Polar-only (no momentum) in signal alignment, explaining why Muon outperforms Orthogonal-SGDM.
  • Quantitative separation: Under the low-SNR rank-1 spiked Gaussian model, Polar-only signal alignment vanishes as matrix size grows, while Pre-polar continues to recover the signal given a sufficiently large momentum window.
  • Multi-scale empirical validation: Synthetic models, CIFAR-10, NanoGPT stationary/trajectory probes, and end-to-end NanoGPT and LLaMA 350M pretraining all support the theoretical predictions.

Methodology

The authors formulate the gradient matrix $G_t \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ at step $t$ as a structured signal-plus-perturbation decomposition: $G_t = G_t^{\text{sig}} + \Xi_t$, where $G_t^{\text{sig}}$ is a temporally coherent low-rank signal with deterministic orthonormal singular vectors, and $\Xi_t$ is a bounded-variance mean-zero orthogonal perturbation sequence (BVMZOS) that allows time-dependent and heavy-tailed noise beyond standard i.i.d. sub-Gaussian assumptions.

Momentum is modeled as an EMA buffer $M_t = \beta M_{t-1} + (1-\beta) G_t$, whose effective window size is $T = 1/(1-\beta)$. The polar factor is $O(X) = UV^\top$ from the thin SVD $X = U\Sigma V^\top$.

Three update pipelines are compared:

  1. Pre-polar (standard Muon): $\Delta W_t \propto O(M_t)$ — momentum first, then orthogonalize.
  2. Post-polar (Orthogonal-SGDM): $\tilde{M}_t = \beta \tilde{M}_{t-1} + (1-\beta) O(G_t)$, then $\Delta W_t \propto \tilde{M}_t$ — orthogonalize each gradient, then momentum.
  3. Polar-only: $\Delta W_t \propto O(G_t)$ — no momentum, direct orthogonalization.

The theoretical analysis proceeds in two stages: (1) characterizing the spectral gap and subspace reliability opened by momentum alone (Theorem 1 & Corollary 1); and (2) proving the signal-alignment separation between the three pipelines (Theorem 2 & Theorem 3).

Empirical validation uses three protocols:

  • Stationary probe: Fix model weights, collect $K$ mini-batch gradients, and apply momentum pipelines to isolate the stochastic gradient stream around a training checkpoint.
  • Trajectory probe: Maintain a sliding $K$-step gradient buffer alongside live training to test robustness to parameter drift.
  • End-to-end training: NanoGPT (GPT-2-style 12-layer / width-768 on FineWeb-10B) and LLaMA 350M (24-layer / width-1024 on C4) with all three pipelines.

Results & Highlights

Theoretical:

  • Theorem 1 (Spectral gap): Under Assumptions 1–2, with probability $\geq 1 - (2T-1)^{-1/2}$, the momentum buffer satisfies:
  • Signal preservation: $\sigma_k(M_t) \geq c_{\text{sig}} \lambda_k - \sqrt{\eta}/(2T-1)^{1/4}$ for $k \leq r$
  • Perturbation attenuation: $\sigma_{r+1}(M_t) \leq \sqrt{\eta}/(2T-1)^{1/4}$

The spectral gap widens with heavier momentum (larger $T$), in contrast to standard optimization theory where large $\beta$ hurts guarantees.

  • Corollary 1 (Subspace reliability): The top-$r$ singular subspaces of $M_t$ converge to those of the coherent signal at rate $O(T^{-1/4})$ in $\sin\Theta$ distance.
  • Theorem 2 (Pre-polar dominance): For any fixed signal and absolutely continuous perturbation, there exists $T_0$ such that for all $T \geq T_0$, Pre-polar achieves strictly higher expected signal alignment than both Post-polar and Polar-only.
  • Theorem 3 (Quantitative separation): In the rank-1 spiked Gaussian model under low SNR, Polar-only alignment vanishes as $m \to \infty$, while Pre-polar continues to recover the signal.

Empirical:

  • NanoGPT & LLaMA 350M end-to-end: Pre-polar (standard Muon) consistently achieves lower validation loss than Post-polar and Polar-only across training (Figure 1).
  • Stationary probe (NanoGPT step 3000, h.0.attn.c_proj): As $\beta$ increases, tail singular values and filtering ratios are suppressed more than head values, opening the predicted spectral gap (Figure 2). Noise-suppression ratio $R(T)$ lies above the $(2T-1)^{1/4}$ floor predicted by theory.
  • Subspace alignment: At ranks $r \in \{1,5,10\}$, $\sin\Theta$ errors decrease with momentum window $T$ at rate consistent with $T^{-1/4}$ (Figure 3).
  • Signal alignment ordering: Pre-polar monotonically improves with $\beta$; Post-polar and Polar-only remain flat and low (Figure 4). This persists across training checkpoints including warmdown phases.
  • Trajectory probe: Pre-polar advantage survives live training non-stationarity, with Post-polar flatness confirming the noncommutativity of momentum and orthogonalization (Figure 6).

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: Very high. This is the first work to rigorously explain why momentum helps in Muon and why the Pre-polar ordering is essential. The "spectral filter" reframing is conceptually elegant and distinct from classical momentum-as-acceleration or momentum-as-low-pass-filter narratives.
  • Rigor: Strong within the assumed model. The proofs use min-max singular value theory, Wedin's $\sin\Theta$ theorem, and careful concentration bounds for the momentum-filtered perturbation. The BVMZOS assumption is more general than i.i.d. sub-Gaussian noise. However, the signal persistence assumption (Assumption 2) and time-invariance requirement for Theorem 2 are idealizations—real training dynamics involve drifting signals.
  • Reproducibility: Good. The experiments use standard public models (NanoGPT, LLaMA 350M) and datasets (FineWeb-10B, C4). Detailed hyperparameters are provided in Appendix F. The stationary/trajectory probe protocols are clearly described and could be replicated on other architectures. Code and project page are referenced.
  • Significance: High for the optimizer-design community. Muon is emerging as a serious alternative to AdamW for LLM training, and understanding its components theoretically can guide future variants. The design rule "denoise first, then orthogonalize" may generalize to other matrix-aware optimizers with nonlinear spectral steps.
  • Red flags / limitations: (1) The analysis does not cover Nesterov momentum (common in practical Muon implementations), though Appendix B.4 discusses its effective sample size. (2) Theorem 3 is restricted to rank-1 spiked Gaussian models. (3) The time-invariant signal assumption is relaxed empirically but not theoretically. (4) The paper does not address how spectral filtering interacts with learning rate schedules, weight decay, or the Newton–Schulz approximation error. (5) No analysis of computational cost trade-offs versus AdamW.

Strengths

  • First rigorous theoretical explanation of momentum's role in Muon, resolving a clear contradiction between theory (heavy momentum hurts) and practice (heavy momentum helps).
  • Clean, intuitive core insight: momentum as spectral filter → denoise first, orthogonalize later.
  • Comprehensive empirical validation spanning synthetic models, small-scale image classification, probe-based deep learning analysis, and full LLM pretraining.
  • Elegant proof techniques connecting EMA variance reduction, singular value perturbation theory, and Wedin's subspace bounds.
  • Identifies a general design principle applicable beyond Muon.

Limitations

  • Theoretical guarantees rely on idealized signal-plus-perturbation gradient models; real-world gradients may have more complex structure (e.g., non-stationary signals, heavy-tailed noise with non-finite variance, correlation across steps).
  • Does not formally analyze time-varying momentum schedules (e.g., warmup), which are common in practice.
  • Nesterov momentum variants are not covered by the main theoretical results.
  • The rank-1 quantitative separation (Theorem 3) does not extend to general rank-$r$ signals or non-Gaussian perturbations.
  • Does not explore whether there exists an optimal momentum coefficient as a function of signal-to-noise ratio, or how to adapt it online.
  • No discussion of memory or computational overhead relative to AdamW, which remains a practical concern for Muon adoption.
