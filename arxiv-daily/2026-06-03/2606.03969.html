Quantifying Faithful Confidence Expression in Large Reasoning Models

Areeb Gani, Asal Meskin, Gabrielle Kaili-May Liu, Arman Cohan

arXiv: 2606.03969

Date: 2026-06-02

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03969

一句话总结： 本文提出了首个系统量化大型推理模型（LRMs）忠实校准（FC）的框架，基于token概率、隐藏状态和采样一致性三种内部不确定性估计器，配合前缀条件采样控制推理迹的结构变化，发现当前LRMs的cMFG*得分仅0.64–0.78，推理训练本身无法改善FC，且针对非推理模型的提示干预在推理设置中完全失效。
TL;DR: This paper introduces the first systematic framework to quantify Faithful Calibration (FC) in Large Reasoning Models (LRMs). Using three complementary intrinsic-confidence estimators (RCC for hidden states, DeepConf for token log-probabilities, and prefix-conditioned sampling consistency) and an LLM-as-a-Judge decisiveness scorer (Gemini-2.5-Flash), the authors evaluate 7 models across 5 reasoning datasets (AIME, HLE, SuperGPQA, LegalBench, MuSR). Key findings: (1) LRMs achieve only moderate FC (cMFG* 0.64–0.78) while remaining highly decisive even when wrong; (2) FC is decoupled from accuracy and barely improves with model scale; (3) reasoning training (SFT on SYNTHETIC-1 CoT traces) actually degrades FC by making models sound more cautious without lowering internal confidence; (4) distillation reshapes FC behavior in ways that diverge from the teacher; (5) prompt interventions that improve FC in non-reasoning LLMs fail entirely in LRMs; (6) the three confidence estimators produce divergent faithfulness profiles on identical traces, with DeepConf showing the greatest alignment and RCC the least. Code: https://github.com/yale-nlp/faithful_lrm.

核心贡献

  • 提出了首个专门针对大型推理模型（LRMs）忠实校准（FC）的系统量化框架，解决了长链式推理输出缺乏清晰步骤边界、步骤结构不一致、条件依赖复杂等评估难题。
  • 引入前缀条件采样（prefix-conditioned sampling）方法，通过对原始提示和先前步骤进行条件化来控制推理迹中的条件依赖和跨采样的结构变化，为基于一致性的置信度估计提供了可行的黑盒信号。
  • 采用三种互补的内部不确定性估计器——基于表示的RCC、基于token对数概率的DeepConf、以及基于前缀条件采样的响应一致性——从不同层面捕获模型的内在不确定性。
  • 在7个模型（涵盖蒸馏、原生推理、指令基座及同架构推理微调变体）和5个数据集（数学、科学、法律、多步软推理）上进行了大规模实证研究。
  • 揭示了多个重要发现：推理训练本身不能改善FC；模型规模对FC帮助有限；蒸馏会差异化重塑FC行为；非推理模型的提示干预无法迁移到推理模型；不同估计器对同一推理迹给出分歧的忠实度评估。

Key Contributions

  • Presents the first framework to systematically measure and analyze faithful confidence expression in LRMs, addressing the unique challenges of long-form, dynamically evolving reasoning traces that lack clean step boundaries and encode complex conditional dependencies.
  • Introduces prefix-conditioned sampling for consistency-based confidence estimation in LRMs, conditioning on the original prompt and prior steps to control for conditional dependencies and step-structure variation across sampled responses.
  • Employs three complementary intrinsic-confidence estimators spanning representation-based (RCC), token log-probability (DeepConf), and sampling-consistency paradigms, requiring progressively weaker model access (white-box hidden states → white-box logprobs → black-box outputs).
  • Conducts a large-scale empirical study across 7 models and 5 reasoning-intensive datasets (AIME, HLE, SuperGPQA, LegalBench, MuSR), characterizing how FC varies with task, model design, scale, and prompt strategy.
  • Provides actionable insights: reasoning training degrades FC; distillation differentially reshapes uncertainty expression; prompt interventions for non-reasoning LLMs fail in LRMs; and different confidence estimators yield divergent faithfulness profiles, suggesting no single estimator captures the full picture.

Methodology

Problem Formulation. Given a query x, a reasoning model M generates a CoT trace T = (s₁, s₂, ..., sₙ) of n steps followed by a final response R. Each step sᵢ has an internal confidence C(sᵢ) ∈ [0, 1] (estimator-dependent proxy for intrinsic certainty) and a linguistic decisiveness D(sᵢ) ∈ [0, 1] (certainty conveyed through surface form). Faithful calibration measures alignment between C and D. Step-level faithfulness is defined as F(sᵢ) = 1 − |D(sᵢ) − C(sᵢ)|, aggregated across steps for trace- and dataset-level scores.

Internal Confidence Estimation (three estimators):

  1. RCC (Representation-based Confidence Chain) [Mao & Venkat, 2026]: Uses final-layer hidden states to compute an inter-step relevance filter between previous and current segments. Token probabilities within each step are aggregated through this filter to obtain a local step confidence qᵢ, combined recurrently with prior confidence via pᵢ = δqᵢ + (1−δ)pᵢ₋₁. This captures the intuition that reasoning confidence is non-local — uncertainty in earlier steps affects later ones even when local token distributions appear confident.
  1. DeepConf (Token Log-Probability Confidence) [Fu et al., 2025]: Computes per-token confidence as the negative mean log-probability of the top-k candidates, aggregated to the step level. A bounded transform f(x) = clamp(x/8, 0, 1) maps unbounded scores to [0, 1]. This captures local surface-form certainty from the generation process.
  1. Sampling Consistency (Prefix-Conditioned): For each step sᵢ, conditions on the original prompt and prior steps s₁...sᵢ₋₁, then samples k=10 continuations of up to 200 tokens. Each continuation is judged for consistency with the original step; step-level confidence is the fraction judged consistent. Due to compute constraints, at most 20 steps per trace are evaluated (first, last, and uniformly subsampled), which robustness analysis confirms preserves aggregate metrics. This provides a tractable black-box confidence signal.

Linguistic Decisiveness Estimation. Uses LLM-as-a-Judge (Gemini-2.5-Flash) with few-shot examples mapping hedging language to numerical scores in [0, 1], derived from systematic studies of human-perceived decisiveness. The judge scores each reasoning step holistically (rather than individual factual assertions), and step scores are averaged to the example level. Validated against human annotations: Pearson = 0.884, Spearman = 0.869 on short-form text.

Faithfulness Metrics. Uses cMFG* (width-weighted variant of conditional mean faithfulness gap), which replaces fixed-width confidence bins with equal-mass bins over the empirical confidence range the model actually uses, providing stable estimates even when trace-level confidence occupies a narrow range.

Prompt Interventions. Tests three conditions: (i) baseline task prompt, (ii) perception prompt prepended to elicit faithful linguistic confidence, (iii) MetSens+Hedge system prompt paired with perception prompt (the most effective intervention from Liu et al.'s non-reasoning LLM study).

Models. DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen3-8B, QwQ-32B, DeepSeek-R1 (671B AWQ), Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, plus reasoning-tuned variants of the latter two (SYNTHETIC-1 SFT on 2M math/STEM CoT traces distilled from DeepSeek-R1), and Qwen3-8B as a base-model comparison.

Datasets. Hard: AIME (math olympiad), SuperGPQA (graduate-level science), HLE (broad-domain expert questions). Medium/easy: LegalBench (legal reasoning), MuSR (multi-step soft reasoning). n = 1000 per configuration (AIME: n=933; MuSR: n=756).

Results & Highlights

Moderate FC with high decisiveness even when wrong. LRMs achieve cMFG* scores of 0.64–0.78 across settings, indicating only moderate faithful calibration. Models maintain relatively high decisiveness even when final answers are frequently incorrect — a tendency exacerbated for smaller distilled models (DeepSeek-R1-8B).

Model scale helps FC only slightly. DeepSeek-R1-8B achieves higher cMFG under RCC and DeepConf than QwQ-32B, while QwQ-32B is slightly higher under Sampling Consistency. This contrasts with Liu et al.'s finding that scale improves FC in non-reasoning LLMs. The stability of cMFG despite fluctuating accuracy shows FC is decoupled from accuracy.

Temporal dynamics are model-dependent. Later reasoning steps are not uniformly more faithful. DeepSeek-R1-8B becomes slightly less faithful over the trace (linguistic decisiveness drifts from internal confidence), while QwQ-32B shows consistent improvement. The intuition that later reasoning is more "final and accurate" does not extend to FC.

Estimator divergence is substantial. cMFG* is highest under DeepConf (~0.77), followed by RCC (~0.73), and lowest under Sampling Consistency (~0.66). Spearman correlation between decisiveness and confidence at the step/trace level confirms DeepConf yields the greatest alignment (trace-level gap 0.631), while RCC shows the weakest (trace-level gap 0.081). This suggests no single estimator fully captures the uncertainty expressed by LRMs.

Reasoning training degrades FC. Comparing instruction-tuned vs. reasoning-tuned checkpoints on the same backbone (Llama-3.1-8B and Qwen2.5-7B): reasoning-tuned models produce substantially longer traces (9–10k vs. 0.8–1.3k tokens) while maintaining high internal confidence, but their linguistic decisiveness drops sharply, producing clear cMFG* degradation — most pronounced on SuperGPQA. The models sound more hesitant and self-questioning, but this surface caution does not correspond to lower internal confidence. Reasoning tuning makes models sound more cautious without making that caution faithful.

Distillation reshapes FC differently from the teacher. DeepSeek-R1 (671B teacher) is less decisive and less internally confident than its distilled 8B student, yet achieves stronger task performance. The distilled model expresses greater decisiveness and higher DeepConf confidence, yielding higher faithfulness on HLE. Similar aggregate FC scores can arise from very different underlying behaviors. Comparing the distilled model to its base Qwen3-8B: Qwen3-8B shows very high DeepConf confidence but low decisiveness (large gaps, low FC); distillation lowers token-level confidence, increases decisiveness, and brings the two into closer alignment.

Prompt interventions fail in LRMs. The perception and MetSens+Hedge prompts improve LRM accuracy (somewhat contrasting with non-reasoning LLM findings) but produce approximately zero change in mean cMFG* across all three estimators. Faithful confidence expression in LRMs is harder to steer — reasoning behavior, verbal style, and internal confidence shift independently.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The paper addresses a genuinely underexplored and timely gap. While both LLM calibration and CoT reasoning have been extensively studied, their intersection — whether LRMs faithfully express confidence through their reasoning traces — has received little systematic attention. The prefix-conditioned sampling approach is a practical methodological innovation for handling LRM trace structure, and the three-estimator decomposition provides multi-dimensional insight.

Rigor: The methodology is careful and self-critical. The authors validate their decisiveness judge against human annotations, perform robustness checks on the 20-step sampling cap, and compare their cMFG* metric to its predecessor. The controlled same-backbone comparisons (instruction-tuned vs. reasoning-tuned) are particularly strong for causal attribution. However, a few concerns: (1) The DeepConf clamp normalization (divide by 8) is somewhat ad hoc and may compress differences at the high-confidence end; the authors acknowledge this in limitations. (2) The sampling consistency estimator is necessarily approximate due to the 20-step cap and compute constraints. (3) Decisiveness scoring by an external LLM judge inherits potential biases, though validation against humans mitigates this. (4) The paper does not attempt to establish ground-truth "true intrinsic confidence" — it treats estimator-dependent proxies as the best available signals, which is reasonable but means the divergence across estimators could reflect either model-level FC failure or estimator inadequacy (or both).

Reproducibility: The work is highly reproducible. All evaluated models are open-weight, datasets are public, and code is released. The methodology is described in sufficient detail (including extensive appendices) to replicate.

Significance: High. As LRMs are deployed in high-stakes contexts where users interpret reasoning traces as signals of deliberation and reliability, uncalibrated linguistic confidence poses real trust and safety risks. The finding that reasoning training degrades FC is counterintuitive and important — it challenges the assumption that "more reasoning = more reliable." The failure of prompt interventions to transfer is a valuable negative result.

Red flags:

  • The paper's main claim — that different estimators produce divergent assessments — raises a meta-methodological question: if estimators disagree, how do we know which (if any) reflects "true" intrinsic confidence? The authors sidestep this by treating multi-estimator evaluation as a feature, but it slightly weakens the normative force of any single FC score.
  • The reasoning-tuned checkpoints (SYNTHETIC-1) were trained only on math/STEM traces, so the comparison is restricted to AIME and SuperGPQA. Whether this finding generalizes to reasoning training on broader domains is unclear.
  • DeepSeek-R1-671B was evaluated with AWQ quantization, which may subtly affect confidence distributions compared to the full-precision model.

Strengths

  • Addresses a timely, practically important, and understudied problem at the intersection of reasoning and uncertainty communication.
  • Methodologically thorough: three complementary confidence estimators, validated decisiveness judge, controlled same-backbone comparisons, and robustness checks.
  • Claims are appropriately bounded — the authors quantify and diagnose FC rather than claiming to solve it.
  • The finding that reasoning training degrades FC (models sound more cautious without actually being more uncertain internally) is counterintuitive and clinically important.
  • The failure of prompt interventions to transfer from non-reasoning to reasoning models is a valuable negative result that prevents wasted research effort.
  • The distillation analysis reveals that similar aggregate FC scores can hide fundamentally different underlying behaviors, which is a subtle and important insight.
  • Code release and use of public models/datasets make the work transparent and reproducible.

Limitations

  • DeepConf's normalization (clamp(x/8, 0, 1)) may introduce scale compression at the high-confidence end, attenuating fine-grained differences.
  • The sampling consistency estimator caps evaluation at 20 steps per trace for tractability; while robustness is verified, this is necessarily an approximation on long traces.
  • Decisiveness is scored by an external LLM judge and may inherit its biases, although validation against human annotations mitigates this.
  • Reasoning-tuned checkpoints were trained only on math/STEM traces, limiting the generalizability of the "reasoning training degrades FC" finding to other domains.
  • DeepSeek-R1-671B was evaluated with AWQ quantization, which could affect confidence distributions.
  • The paper focuses on quantification and diagnosis, not remediation — it does not propose training methods to improve FC.
  • Does not evaluate proprietary reasoning systems (OpenAI o-series, Gemini Flash Thinking, Claude Extended Thinking), so findings may not generalize to closed-weight models with different training paradigms.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can FC be improved through targeted fine-tuning or RL-based training that rewards calibrated linguistic expressions alongside reasoning correctness — e.g., using the three-estimator divergence itself as a regularization signal?
  2. How do proprietary reasoning systems (OpenAI o-series, Gemini Flash Thinking, Claude Extended Thinking) perform on this framework? Do their training paradigms (which may include RL for reasoning quality) inadvertently improve or degrade FC?
  3. How does FC vary across reasoning domains beyond math/STEM and legal — e.g., coding, commonsense reasoning, or scientific hypothesis generation? Are certain reasoning types inherently harder to calibrate?
  4. Can the prefix-conditioned sampling framework be extended to interactive or multi-turn reasoning settings where context accumulates dynamically?
  5. If different confidence estimators disagree, can a meta-estimator or ensemble approach provide a more robust internal-confidence signal? Or does the divergence itself indicate a fundamental ambiguity in what "intrinsic confidence" means for LRMs?
  6. Does the degradation of FC under reasoning training persist when the training data includes diverse domains beyond math/STEM, or when models are explicitly trained to verbalize uncertainty during reasoning?
