Beyond False Stability: High-Noise Drift Gating for Test-Time Adversarial Defenses in Vision-Language Models

arXiv: 2606.03730

Date: 2026-06-02

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03730

一句话总结： 该论文通过系统分析CLIP视觉表征在不同强度随机扰动下的漂移行为，发现对抗样本在弱噪声下呈现虚假稳定性、但在高噪声下显著比干净样本更不稳定这一被忽视的噪声-区域转变，并据此提出一种仅需一次额外前向传播的高噪声漂移门控机制，选择性触发现有测试时防御，在13个数据集上显著改善了干净精度与对抗鲁棒性的权衡。
TL;DR: This paper systematically analyzes how CLIP's visual representations respond to stochastic perturbations across noise strengths, discovering an overlooked noise-regime transition where adversarial examples flip from falsely stable (weak noise) to markedly more unstable than clean samples (high noise). Building on this, the authors propose a training-free, high-noise drift-gated mechanism that selectively triggers existing test-time defenses only when adversarial-like instability is detected, substantially improving the clean-robust trade-off across 13 datasets with just one additional forward pass.

核心贡献

  • 噪声-区域转变的发现： 揭示了CLIP视觉表征空间中一个被先前工作忽视的系统性转变——在弱扰动下，对抗样本表现出虚假稳定性（漂移小于干净样本），但随着扰动强度增加，这一行为发生反转，对抗表征变得显著比干净表征更不稳定，产生更清晰的分离信号。
  • 一致性与广泛性验证： 证明该转变在不同噪声分布（均匀噪声、高斯噪声）、不同变换类型（光度变换、几何变换）、多种攻击预算（ε ∈ {1/255, 4/255, 8/255}）、多种攻击类型（PGD、EOT-PGD、CW、MI-FGSM、图像特征攻击）以及多个数据集上均保持一致。
  • 几何解释： 从表征几何角度解释了该现象——对抗样本位于远离干净语义流形的脆弱局部 basin 中，弱噪声不足以将其推出，而强噪声则能有效破坏这些局部区域，使对抗表征重新对齐回干净流形附近。
  • 高噪声漂移门控防御机制： 提出一种无需训练、即插即用的门控机制，通过在高噪声下测量特征漂移 τ(x) = ||Fv(x) − Fv(T(x))||₂，以阈值 γ ≈ 0.85 作为判断标准，仅在检测到对抗式不稳定时才激活防御（如TTC、AOM），从而避免对干净样本的不必要干扰。
  • 显著的性能提升： 在8个细粒度数据集上，将TTC的平均（干净+对抗）准确率从65.7%提升至71.4%，AOM从63.8%–68.4%提升至72.7%–73.2%，R-TPT+TTC从68.92%提升至73.16%；在ImageNet及4个分布偏移变体上，TTC从56.1%提升至66.2%，AOM从62.1%提升至67.6%。

Key Contributions

  • Noise-regime transition in CLIP representations: Identifies and characterizes a consistent transition in CLIP's visual feature-space response to stochastic perturbations that prior work overlooked. Under weak perturbations, adversarial examples exhibit false stability (smaller latent drift than clean inputs, as noted by TTC). As perturbation strength increases, this ordering reverses: adversarial inputs become markedly more unstable than clean ones, yielding a substantially clearer separation signal.
  • Broad consistency: Demonstrates that this transition is consistent across noise types (uniform, Gaussian, photometric, geometric), multiple attack budgets (ε ∈ {1/255, 4/255, 8/255}), diverse attack types (PGD, EOT-PGD, CW, MI-FGSM, vision-only feature attacks), and multiple datasets.
  • Geometric interpretation: Explains the phenomenon through the geometry of CLIP's representation space—adversarial examples reside in fragile, off-manifold local basins that strong perturbations destabilize, pushing them back toward the clean semantic manifold. Weak noise leaves them trapped in these local regions, producing the false stability effect.
  • Drift-gated selective defense: Proposes a training-free, plug-in mechanism that uses high-noise latent drift as a lightweight adversarial detector to selectively trigger existing test-time defenses (TTC, AOM, R-TPT) only when adversarial-like instability is detected. This avoids unnecessary corruption of clean representations with no additional training cost.
  • Substantial empirical improvements: Achieves large gains across 13 datasets. On eight fine-grained datasets: TTC improves from 65.7% to 71.4% average accuracy; AOM improves from 63.8%–68.4% to 72.7%–73.2%; R-TPT+TTC improves from 68.92% to 73.16%. On ImageNet and four OOD variants: TTC improves from 56.1% to 66.2%; AOM improves from 62.1% to 67.6%.

Methodology

Preliminaries

CLIP performs zero-shot classification by computing cosine similarity between an ℓ₂-normalized visual feature fv = Fv(x)/||Fv(x)|| and text features ftk = Ft(Pk)/||Ft(Pk)||, followed by a temperature-scaled softmax. Adversarial examples are generated via PGD-100 with ℓ∞ budget ε = 4/255 by maximizing cross-entropy on CLIP's zero-shot logits. The paper also evaluates vision-only feature attacks that maximize layer-wise feature discrepancy.

Noise-Regime Analysis

The core empirical analysis measures latent drift in CLIP's visual embedding space:

τ(x) = ||Fv(x) − Fv(T(x))||₂

where T(·) is a stochastic perturbation. Under increasing uniform noise strength:

  • Weak-noise regime (ε ≈ 4/255): Adversarial samples exhibit slightly lower drift than clean samples — reproducing the "false stability" effect that TTC [50] exploited.
  • High-noise regime (ε ≥ 24/255): The curves cross. Adversarial drift rises steeply and becomes substantially larger than clean drift, revealing stronger clean-adversarial separability.

This transition is not specific to uniform noise — identical crossovers appear under Gaussian noise and under photometric/geometric stochastic augmentations (brightness, contrast, rotation, etc.).

Geometric Interpretation

The authors interpret this through representation-space geometry:

  • Clean images map to a broad semantic manifold learned from large-scale pretraining. Moderate perturbations produce local movements around the same semantic region.
  • Adversarial examples are optimized to induce misclassification while remaining visually close to clean images, pushing representations into fragile, locally optimized regions off the clean semantic manifold.
  • Under weak noise, adversarial representations remain trapped within their local adversarial basin, producing limited displacement (false stability).
  • Under strong noise, the adversarial basin destabilizes; perturbations push representations away from this fragile region, causing large displacement. Clean representations, by contrast, continue local manifold movement.

This is supported by PCA visualizations of drift vectors and by convergence metrics (cosine similarity, ℓ₂ distance to clean distribution) that show adversarial features monotonically realigning with clean features as noise strength increases.

Drift-Gated Defense

The defense mechanism (Algorithm 1) is remarkably simple:

  1. Compute latent drift τ(x) under a high-strength stochastic transformation T (uniform noise at ε = 24/255, or Gaussian noise at σ = 0.12).
  2. Apply a binary gate: if τ(x) > γ (γ ≈ 0.85), activate defense D(x); otherwise use standard CLIP inference.

The probe strength and threshold are selected by evaluating gated noise injection across a range of values and choosing the configuration that maximizes clean-adversarial average performance. These values remain near-optimal across different intervention types and datasets.

The gate is combined with three existing defenses:

  • TTC [50]: Replaces TTC's weak-noise false-stability trigger with the high-noise drift gate; counterattack (PGD-based ℓ₂ distance maximization) fires only when τ(x) > γ.
  • AOM [43]: Anchor construction and feature interpolation occur only when high drift is detected. The paper also shows that uniform noise anchors of sufficient strength yield comparable gains to Gaussian anchors, supporting the claim that perturbation strength — not distribution — is the key factor.
  • R-TPT [37]: Drift-gated TTC counterattack is applied before passing the sample into the prompt tuning pipeline, correcting adversarial displacements before test-time adaptation.

Experimental Setup

  • Model: CLIP ViT-L/14 pretrained on DataComp-1B (stronger generalization than original CLIP). Also evaluated on adversarially trained variants FARE [35] and DeltaCLIP-L [47].
  • Datasets: 8 fine-grained (Caltech101, Pets, Flower102, Cars, Aircraft, DTD, EuroSAT, UCF101) + ImageNet + 4 OOD variants (ImageNet-V2, ImageNet-Sketch, ImageNet-A, ImageNet-R).
  • Baselines: TTC [50], AOM [43], R-TPT [37].
  • Evaluation metric: Clean accuracy, adversarial accuracy, and their average.

Adversarially Trained Models

A critical finding: FARE [35] and DeltaCLIP-L [47] exhibit no meaningful clean-adversarial drift separation. This supports the geometric interpretation—adversarial training aligns clean and adversarial representations in feature space, eliminating the fragile local basins. As a consequence, the drift gate is not applicable to these models; they achieve strong inherent robustness (DeltaCLIP-L: 49.86% adversarial on fine-grained) and defensive interventions can be applied directly without gating.

Computational Efficiency

  • The gate requires only one additional forward pass to compute τ(x).
  • It prevents defensive intervention on 90.34% of clean samples, substantially reducing average inference cost compared to unconditional defense application.
  • Cost depends solely on the defense applied, plus this single lightweight probe.

Key Insight on AOM

The paper clarifies that AOM's Gaussian-specific suppression narrative is incomplete. By showing that uniform noise anchors of sufficient strength (ε = 48/255) yield comparable gains to Gaussian anchors (σ = 0.12), the authors establish that perturbation strength, not the specific corruption distribution, is the key factor in noise-based defense effectiveness.

Prompt Tuning Contribution

An interesting auxiliary finding: in R-TPT, the prompt tuning objective itself contributes negligibly to adversarial robustness beyond weighted ensembling alone. The robustness gains are driven almost entirely by ensembling across augmented views. The drift-gated TTC intervention bridges this gap by directly correcting adversarial feature displacements before prompt tuning.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The core contribution — identifying the noise-regime transition from false stability to high-noise instability — is a genuinely novel empirical observation that reframes the prevailing understanding in stochastic test-time defense literature. Prior work (TTC) built defenses around weak-noise false stability; this paper shows that was only half the story, and the high-noise regime offers a much stronger signal.
  • Rigor: The experimental design is thorough. Consistency checks across noise types, transformations, attack budgets, attack objectives, and datasets lend strong credibility. The ablation on adversarially trained models (FARE, DeltaCLIP-L) elegantly supports the geometric hypothesis. The threshold and probe strength selection procedure is clearly described.
  • Reproducibility: The defense is training-free and algorithmically simple (Algorithm 1), which aids reproducibility. The paper specifies exact hyperparameters (probe noise ε = 24/255, threshold γ = 0.85, αAOM = 1.4). The promise of public code release further supports reproducibility. One minor concern: the exact method for selecting γ across datasets is described as grid search over a range, but the range boundaries are not explicitly stated.
  • Significance: High. The drift-gating mechanism is broadly applicable to any existing test-time defense, offering a principled way to avoid the clean-robustness trade-off that has plagued this line of work. The insight that perturbation strength matters more than distribution is conceptually important and could guide future defense design.
  • Red Flags / Limitations:
  • Adaptive attacks: The paper does not evaluate adaptive attacks specifically designed to fool the drift gate. An attacker with knowledge of the gate could potentially craft perturbations that exhibit low drift even under high noise.
  • Dependence on non-robust CLIP: The phenomenon largely disappears in adversarially trained models. While this validates the geometric interpretation, it also means the gate is unnecessary (and ineffective) for models that are already robust. This limits applicability if the field moves toward adversarially trained VLMs as the default.
  • Clean accuracy ceiling: Even with the gate, clean accuracy on some defenses (e.g., AOM at α=1.4) does not fully recover to zero-shot levels (~76%), suggesting residual distortion from the intervention itself.
  • Single model architecture: All main experiments use CLIP ViT-L/14 (DataComp-1B). Generalization to other VLM architectures (e.g., SigLIP, LLaVA-style models) is not tested.
  • No certified robustness: The paper does not claim or evaluate certified robustness bounds; the defense is purely heuristic.

Strengths

  • Identifies a fundamental and previously overlooked phenomenon in CLIP's representation geometry with broad implications for stochastic test-time defenses.
  • Proposes an exceptionally simple, training-free mechanism that can be plugged into existing defenses with minimal implementation effort.
  • Provides a principled geometric explanation (fragile off-manifold basins) that is supported by multiple empirical observations, including the elegant ablation on adversarially trained models.
  • Demonstrates broad empirical consistency across diverse experimental conditions (noise types, transforms, datasets, attacks, OOD variants).
  • Achieves substantial practical improvements on well-established benchmarks, often recovering 5–9 percentage points in average accuracy.
  • Computationally efficient: only one additional forward pass, and avoids expensive defense computation on ~90% of clean inputs.
  • Intellectually honest about the phenomenon's disappearance in adversarially trained models, framing it as validation rather than a weakness.

Limitations

  • Adaptive attack vulnerability: The defense has not been tested against adaptive attacks that explicitly target the drift gate. An attacker aware of the mechanism might optimize for low high-noise drift.
  • Limited to non-robust models: The drift separation signal disappears in adversarially trained CLIP variants (FARE, DeltaCLIP-L), making the gate unnecessary for such models. This limits applicability as the community potentially shifts toward inherently robust VLMs.
  • Architecture specificity: All main experiments focus on CLIP ViT-L/14. Whether the phenomenon generalizes to other vision encoders (CNN-based, other transformer variants) or other VLMs remains open.
  • Threshold sensitivity: While the paper shows γ ≈ 0.85 works well across settings, the robustness of this threshold to domain shifts, other perturbation types, or adversarially optimized inputs is not deeply explored.
  • Clean accuracy gap: Even with gating, some methods (e.g., AOM at high α) still exhibit a clean accuracy gap relative to undefended zero-shot CLIP, indicating the interventions themselves carry some unavoidable cost when activated.
  • No theoretical analysis: The geometric interpretation, while compelling, is empirically motivated rather than theoretically derived. Formal bounds on when/why the drift crossover occurs are not provided.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Adaptive attack robustness: How would the drift gate perform against an adaptive attacker who explicitly optimizes perturbations to minimize high-noise drift? Can the gate be made robust to gradient masking concerns?
  2. Cross-architecture generalization: Does the noise-regime transition persist in other vision encoders (e.g., ConvNeXt, Swin Transformer) or in other VLMs (SigLIP, LLaVA, Qwen-VL)? Is the phenomenon tied specifically to CLIP's contrastive pretraining?
  3. Theoretical characterization: Can the drift crossover be formally analyzed? For instance, is there a relationship between the crossover noise strength and properties of the adversarial basin (e.g., basin volume, curvature, distance to clean manifold)?
  4. Dynamic thresholding: Could the fixed threshold γ = 0.85 be replaced with an input-dependent or dataset-dependent adaptive threshold? Would this improve robustness under distribution shift?
  5. Multi-scale probing: Would probing at multiple noise strengths simultaneously (rather than a single high-noise probe) provide additional discriminative signal or better resistance to adaptive attacks?
  6. Beyond classification: Can the drift-gating principle be extended to other CLIP-based downstream tasks such as retrieval, segmentation, or object detection, where adversarial perturbations may have different feature-space effects?
