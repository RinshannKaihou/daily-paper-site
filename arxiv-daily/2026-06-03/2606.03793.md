> **一句话总结：** 该研究通过对12种语言下三种代表性开源MLLM（PALO、PARROT、Qwen3-VL）的系统评估，发现对抗图像扰动具有强跨语言可迁移性，并揭示了浅层多语言适配模型中存在的"因失败而安全"（safety-by-failure）现象——低资源语言看似更安全实则是理解与视觉编码失败的产物，而深度整合多语言训练的Qwen3-VL展现出真正的跨语言安全对齐。

> **TL;DR:** This paper systematically evaluates adversarial robustness and multimodal safety across 12 typologically diverse languages in three open-source MLLMs (PALO, PARROT, and Qwen3-VL). It reveals strong cross-lingual transferability of gradient-based adversarial perturbations and identifies "safety-by-failure" in shallowly multilingual models—where low-resource languages appear safer due to comprehension and visual-grounding failures rather than genuine alignment. In contrast, Qwen3-VL, which deeply integrates multilingual training across all stages, demonstrates genuine cross-lingual safety alignment.

## 核心贡献

- 首次对多语言多模态大语言模型（MLLM）的对抗鲁棒性与安全性进行系统性跨语言研究，覆盖12种语言、超过60,000个评测样本
- 构建了首个多语言多模态对抗鲁棒性与安全性评测基准套件，通过"翻译-验证"流程将COCO、Flickr30k、LLaVA-Bench、RealToxicityPrompts和MM-SafetyBench适配到12种语言
- 发现梯度对抗攻击具有强跨语言可迁移性：针对一种语言优化的对抗图像在查询其他语言时仍能显著降低模型性能，说明对抗脆弱性根植于共享的多语言多模态表征空间
- 提出并验证"safety-by-failure"现象：仅在指令微调阶段获得多语言能力的模型（PALO、PARROT）在低资源语言中看似更安全，实则是因为模型无法理解有害指令（产生幻觉、重复填充文本或不相关内容），而非真正学会了拒绝
- 对比证明在全训练阶段深度整合多语言能力的Qwen3-VL展现出真正的跨语言安全对齐——在所有语言中保持主动拒绝，且安全模式呈反向分布（英语最安全，低资源语言暴露最高残余脆弱性）
- 视觉排版攻击揭示强英语偏向：英文脚本在图像中被可靠识别并执行，而非英语脚本几乎无法被视觉编码器解析，导致虚假的安全表现

## Key Contributions

- First systematic cross-lingual study of adversarial robustness and safety alignment in Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs), covering 12 typologically diverse languages and over 60,000 adapted evaluation instances
- Construction of the first multilingual multimodal benchmark suite for adversarial robustness and safety, adapting COCO, Flickr30k, LLaVA-Bench, RealToxicityPrompts, and MM-SafetyBench into 12 languages via a rigorous translate-then-verify pipeline with back-translation consistency filtering and human validation
- Discovery of strong cross-lingual adversarial transferability: adversarial images optimized in one language degrade performance when the model is queried in other languages, indicating that adversarial vulnerabilities arise from shared multilingual multimodal representations rather than language-specific decoding
- Identification and validation of "safety-by-failure": instruction-tuned multilingual MLLMs (PALO and PARROT) exhibit illusory safety in low-resource languages, where lower unsafe response rates actually co-occur with low refusal rates—harmful instructions are missed rather than rejected, producing hallucinated visual descriptions, repetitive filler text, or irrelevant content
- Comparative demonstration that Qwen3-VL, which incorporates multilingual data across all training stages (pretraining, multimodal alignment, and instruction tuning), achieves genuine cross-lingual safety alignment with substantial refusal rates across all languages and an inverted cross-lingual vulnerability pattern
- Revelation of strong English typographic bias: English scripts embedded as text within images are reliably recognized and followed, whereas non-English scripts are rarely parsed by the vision encoder, producing near-zero unsafe rates that reflect grounding failure rather than alignment

## Methodology

The study evaluates three multilingual MLLMs that differ in how they acquire multilingual capability:

**Models:**
- **PALO** [27]: LLaVA-style architecture with CLIP vision encoder + LLaMA-2-7B backbone, trained on translated multilingual multimodal instruction data across 10 languages. Predominantly English-centric pretraining with limited non-English coverage.
- **PARROT** [40]: LLaVA-style architecture with CLIP vision encoder + Qwen-1.5-7B backbone, uses a text-guided Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) adapter for cross-lingual representation alignment, trained on 6 languages. Benefits from stronger bilingual (English/Chinese) pretraining.
- **Qwen3-VL** [7]: Built on the strongly multilingual Qwen-3 backbone, incorporates high-quality multilingual multimodal data across pretraining, multimodal alignment, and instruction tuning stages—not relying solely on translated English instruction data.

**Gradient-Based Adversarial Evaluation (white-box, PALO & PARROT only):**
- **Captioning attacks:** Auto-PGD (APGD) for 100 iterations with ℓ∞-bounded perturbation budget ε = 8/255, maximizing cross-entropy loss between image and ground-truth caption. Evaluated on COCO, Flickr30k (short captioning), and LLaVA-Bench (long-form reasoning).
- **Cross-lingual transferability:** Perturbations optimized in a source language lsrc are evaluated by querying the model with prompts and reference outputs in a different target language ltgt.
- **Adversarial visual jailbreak:** Unconstrained PGD optimization for 5,000 iterations starting from random noise, targeting 66 derogatory sentences. Resulting adversarial images paired with 1,200 toxicity-inducing prompts from RealToxicityPrompts (all translated per language). Safety violations judged by Llama Guard 4 (12B).
- **Evaluation:** GPT-4.1 Nano as LLM-as-a-judge for captioning similarity; English-only CIDEr scoring for precise comparison.

**Non-Adversarial Multimodal Safety (PALO, PARROT, Qwen3-VL):**
- **Text-only safety:** Harmful instructions expressed purely in each target language, paired with a benign black image. Probes the LLM backbone's multilingual comprehension and safety guardrails.
- **Typographic safety (TYPO):** Harmful content rendered as visible text within images, with a benign textual prompt asking the model to follow the image instruction. Probes the vision encoder's multilingual OCR and visual-semantic grounding.
- **SD+TYPO:** Stable-Diffusion generated images overlaid with typographic cues (detailed in Appendix).
- **Judgment:** GPT-4.1 Nano classifies each response as safe or unsafe following MM-SafetyBench refusal-safety judgment prompts.

**Multilingual Benchmark Adaptation (12 languages):**
Arabic, Bengali, Chinese, English, French, Hindi, Japanese, Portuguese, Russian, Spanish, Turkish, and Urdu. A translate-then-verify pipeline with multi-model translation (GPT-3.5 Turbo, GPT-4.1 Nano, GPT-5 Nano, Apertus-8B/70B), back-translation consistency filtering, and human verification by native/proficient speakers. Manual visual inspection for typographic rendering, especially for complex scripts (Arabic, Urdu).

## Results & Highlights

**Cross-Lingual Adversarial Transferability:**
- Adversarial perturbations optimized in one source language transfer broadly across all 12 evaluation languages for both PALO and PARROT, on both COCO/Flickr30k (short captioning) and LLaVA-Bench (long-form reasoning).
- Even attacks crafted in lower-resource languages (e.g., Bengali) successfully transfer to high-resource languages (English, French), confirming vulnerability arises from the shared multilingual multimodal representation space.
- Under English-only CIDEr evaluation, attacks from high-resource or LLM-dominant languages (English, Chinese) produce the largest performance drops, while attacks from lower-resource languages transfer less strongly.
- PARROT shows a clearer high-resource bias in transferability than PALO, likely due to its stronger English/Chinese bilingual pretraining base.

**Adversarial Visual Jailbreak:**
- Both PALO and PARROT are consistently vulnerable across all languages, including lower-resource ones (Urdu, Bengali for PALO).
- PALO shows notable variability in harm category distribution across attack languages; PARROT exhibits more uniform harm composition.

**Text-Only Non-Adversarial Safety:**
- **PALO & PARROT:** High-resource languages (English 0.24–0.33, Spanish, French, Chinese) produce substantially higher unsafe rates. Lower-resource languages (Bengali 0.06, Urdu 0.04 for PALO; Turkish 0.11 for PARROT) show markedly lower unsafe rates.
- **Critical insight:** Lower unsafe rates co-occur with *low* refusal rates, not high ones. The model does not refuse harmful queries in low-resource languages—it fails to comprehend them, producing hallucinated visual descriptions, repetitive filler text, or irrelevant content. This is "safety-by-failure."
- **Qwen3-VL:** Overall unsafe rates are substantially lower (0.03–0.16) with an *inverted* cross-lingual pattern: English has the lowest unsafe rate (0.03), while Bengali (0.16) and Hindi (0.13) are highest. This confirms genuine comprehension across languages—unsafe rates reflect actual alignment gaps rather than comprehension failures. The languages that appeared "safest" under PALO (Bengali, Urdu) are precisely where Qwen3-VL exposes residual vulnerability previously masked.

**Typographic (TYPO) Non-Adversarial Safety:**
- **PALO & PARROT:** English typography yields high unsafe rates (up to 0.35 for PALO, 0.39 for PARROT). Non-English scripts collapse to near-zero unsafe rates (0.00–0.04 for Bengali, Hindi, Urdu in PALO). Refusal rates are also near-zero for non-English scripts, confirming the harmful cue is *missed*, not *rejected*.
- **Qwen3-VL:** Unsafe rates are moderate and uniformly distributed across language pairs (0.05–0.16), without the sharp collapse for non-English scripts. Substantial refusal rates (0.04–0.21) across languages confirm it recognizes harmful typographic content in multiple scripts and actively refuses.

The paper evaluates open-source models with fully disclosed experimental protocols, making claims independently verifiable. ## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** High. This is the first systematic study of adversarial robustness and safety alignment across multiple languages in the MLLM space. The "safety-by-failure" conceptualization is a valuable and actionable framing that reframes apparent multilingual safety as a potential failure mode.
- **Rigor:** Strong. The experimental design carefully controls for confounds: fixed ℓ∞ perturbation budget ensures attack strength is held constant across languages; back-translation consistency filtering and human verification ensure benchmark quality; evaluation judges operate within each target language to avoid cross-lingual comparison bias. The comparison between shallow (PALO/PARROT) and deep (Qwen3-VL) multilingual integration provides a natural experimental control for the safety-by-failure hypothesis.
- **Reproducibility:** Good. Open-source models, explicit hyperparameters (APGD 100 iterations, ε = 8/255, PGD 5,000 iterations for jailbreak), and planned public release of code and benchmarks support reproduction. The exact set of 12 languages is clearly specified.
- **Significance:** High for MLLM safety and evaluation communities. The findings directly challenge the sufficiency of English-centric safety evaluation and provide empirical grounding for requiring multilingual adversarial and safety testing as a deployment prerequisite. The distinction between genuine safety and safety-by-failure has implications for how low-resource language performance should be interpreted in safety benchmarks.
- **Red flags / Limitations:** (1) The gradient-based adversarial evaluation is restricted to fully open-source PALO and PARROT; whether similar cross-lingual transferability holds for proprietary models remains an open question. (2) The study focuses on LLaVA-style architectures with CLIP vision encoders—results may not generalize to other architectural families. (3) While the authors control for translation quality, it is difficult to fully rule out cultural or contextual differences in harmfulness perception across languages. (4) The lower-resource languages in the study (Bengali, Urdu) still have substantial online text presence; truly endangered or extremely low-resource languages may exhibit even more extreme safety-by-failure.

## Strengths

- Addresses a critical gap in MLLM safety research by moving decisively beyond English-centric evaluation
- Introduces a precise, actionable conceptual distinction between genuine safety alignment and "safety-by-failure"
- Methodologically rigorous: controlled attack parameters, multi-stage translation verification, language-internal evaluation, and three independent runs
- Provides both quantitative metrics and rich qualitative examples that clearly illustrate the three safety outcomes (safe refusal, unsafe compliance, safety-by-failure)
- The Qwen3-VL comparison provides compelling evidence that safety-by-failure is a property of shallow multilingual adaptation, not multilingual MLLMs in general
- The typographic safety evaluation cleverly disentangles linguistic comprehension failures from visual-grounding failures
- Public release commitment for code and benchmarks will enable follow-up research

## Limitations

- Gradient-based adversarial evaluation limited to fully open-source models (PALO, PARROT); proprietary multilingual MLLMs (GPT-4o, Gemini) are not evaluated for white-box adversarial robustness
- Architectural scope limited to LLaVA-style models with CLIP vision encoders; generalization to other architectures (e.g., Flamingo, BLIP-2, unified encoder-decoder designs) is not established
- The 12 languages, while typologically diverse, still include primarily languages with substantial digital text resources; safety-by-failure may be even more pronounced in endangered or extremely low-resource languages
- The study does not quantitatively disentangle tokenizer coverage effects from model comprehension effects when explaining cross-lingual differences
- Focus on gradient-based attacks may not capture the full spectrum of real-world adversarial threats (e.g., query-based black-box attacks, natural adversarial examples)
- The use of GPT-4.1 Nano as an LLM-as-a-judge introduces a potential evaluator bias, though the use of Llama Guard 4 for safety classification provides a secondary verification

## Relevance

该论文与用户的ML可靠性、鲁棒性与失败模式分析研究兴趣高度相关，特别是在多模态大模型的安全评估与对抗鲁棒性方面提供了跨语言视角的重要洞察。论文提出的"safety-by-failure"概念本质上是一种被误标为安全性的模型失效模式，对于理解模型在不同语言环境下的真实鲁棒性边界和进行严谨的失败模式分析具有直接参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How do proprietary closed-source MLLMs (e.g., GPT-4o, Gemini) compare in cross-lingual adversarial robustness? Can transfer-based or query-based attacks reproduce the cross-lingual transferability observed under white-box access?
2. What specific architectural and training data choices enable Qwen3-VL's genuine cross-lingual safety? Can the same depth of multilingual integration be retrofitted to LLaVA-style architectures, or is it contingent on the backbone LLM's pretraining?
3. Can "safety-by-failure" be precisely quantified and automatically detected using mechanistic interpretability tools (e.g., SAEs, probing of hidden states, attention pattern analysis) to distinguish comprehension failure from active refusal?
4. How does the vision encoder's multilingual OCR capability specifically contribute to typographic safety outcomes? Would adversarial fine-tuning of the vision encoder [29, 38] improve multilingual typographic parsing without compromising clean accuracy?
5. What are the implications of these findings for safety evaluation standards and deployment regulations—should multilingual adversarial and safety testing become a mandatory certification requirement for multilingual MLLM deployment?
6. Do truly endangered or extremely low-resource languages (below the Bengali/Urdu threshold studied here) exhibit even more extreme safety-by-failure, and what are the ethical implications of deploying MLLMs in such linguistic communities?
