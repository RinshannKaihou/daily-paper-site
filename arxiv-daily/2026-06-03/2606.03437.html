Large Language Models Are Overconfident in Their Own Responses

arXiv: 2606.03437

Date: 2026-06-02

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03437

一句话总结： 本文首次将指令微调后的校准退化与聊天模板效应解耦，发现对话式LLM存在"所有权偏差"——模型对自己生成的答案比对用户提供的相同答案置信度高最多26%，并提出一种简单的推理时重框定策略（将模型答案视为用户输入来引出置信度）来改善校准，无需重新训练。
TL;DR: This paper decouples the effects of instruction tuning and chat templates on LLM calibration, discovers an "ownership bias" where conversational LLMs are up to 26% more confident in their own answers than in identical user-provided ones, and proposes a simple inference-time reframing strategy — presenting the model's answer as user input during confidence elicitation — that improves calibration without retraining.

核心贡献

  • 首次系统性地将指令微调（instruction tuning）与聊天模板（chat template）对LLM校准的影响解耦分析。
  • 发现"所有权偏差"（ownership bias）：模型对自己生成的答案比对用户提供的相同答案表现出系统性更高的置信度，偏差幅度最高达26%（语言表达式法）。
  • 在六个开源权重LLM（Llama 3.1 8B/70B、Qwen3 4B/30B、Gemma 3 4B/27B）、三个基准（MMLU、GSM8K、TruthfulQA、开放MMLU）和三种置信度引出方法（P(True)、百分比、语言表达式）上进行了广泛验证。
  • 提出一种零成本的推理时干预策略：在引出置信度时将模型答案重新框定为用户输入，可将校准误差（ECE）最多降低约26%，显著缩小基础模型与指令微调模型之间的校准差距。
  • 将发现推广至闭源模型GPT-5.2，证明所有权偏差不仅限于开源模型。

Key Contributions

  • First systematic decoupling of instruction tuning and chat template effects on LLM calibration.
  • Discovery of "ownership bias": conversational LLMs assign systematically higher confidence to their own generated answers compared to identical user-provided answers, with deviations up to 26% (linguistic elicitation method).
  • Extensive validation across six open-weight LLMs (Llama 3.1 8B/70B, Qwen3 4B/30B, Gemma 3 4B/27B), four benchmarks (MMLU, GSM8K, TruthfulQA, open-ended MMLU), and three confidence elicitation methods (P(True), Verbalized Percentage, Verbalized Linguistic).
  • Proposal of a zero-cost inference-time intervention: reframing the model's answer as user input during confidence elicitation reduces Expected Calibration Error (ECE) by up to ~26% and substantially narrows the calibration gap between base and instruction-tuned models.
  • Generalization to the proprietary model GPT-5.2, showing ownership bias is not limited to open-weight models.

Methodology

The study follows a four-step experimental design to isolate and characterize the drivers of miscalibration in conversational LLMs.

Step 1 — Decoupling instruction tuning and chat template effects: For each of six models (three families, two scales each), the authors compare three variants: (a) the base pre-trained model prompted without chat template, (b) the instruction-tuned model prompted without chat template (i.e., as a base model), and (c) the instruction-tuned model with the standard chat template. Logit-based confidence is extracted from answer-token probabilities on MMLU. Calibration is measured via Expected Calibration Error (ECE, 10 equal-width bins) and Brier score.

Step 2 — Explicit confidence elicitation: The same three variants are evaluated with three established confidence elicitation methods: (1) P(True) — probability of the "True" token after asking whether a given answer is correct; (2) Verbalized Percentage — asking the model to output a confidence percentage (0–100%); and (3) Verbalized Linguistic — asking the model to select from a 7-point Likert scale ("very low" to "very high"), mapped to [0, 1]. For each question, the model is forced to evaluate all four answer options independently.

Step 3 — Ownership bias test: To test whether models are overconfident in their own answers, the authors construct two prompt variants that differ only in whether the answer is presented as an assistant message (model's own response) or a user message (external input). The same three confidence methods are applied. Statistical significance is assessed via Wilcoxon signed-rank test (Brier, raw confidence) and paired bootstrap resampling (ECE, K = 1000).

Step 4 — Mitigation validation and generalization: The reframing strategy is tested across additional tasks (GSM8K, TruthfulQA, open-ended MMLU) and on the proprietary model GPT-5.2 to assess generalizability.

Results & Highlights

Instruction tuning vs. chat template (Table 1): Instruction tuning improves accuracy (+3.7% on average) but harms calibration, increasing ECE by 13.1% and Brier score by 6.5%. Adding the chat template yields a further modest accuracy gain (+1.1%) but aggravates miscalibration: ECE increases by an additional 2.74% and Brier by 1.5%. The combined effect of instruction tuning + chat template raises ECE by 15.8% relative to base models. Instruction tuning is the main driver of miscalibration; the chat template is a secondary aggravator.

Explicit confidence elicitation (Table 2): Across all three verbalized methods, instruction-tuned models remain significantly worse calibrated than base models, with or without the chat template. This confirms that the post-training algorithm — not merely the prompting format — is the root cause of miscalibration.

Ownership bias (Table 3, Figure 4): When the answer is presented as the assistant's own response, models show consistently worse calibration and higher raw confidence than when the identical answer is presented as user input. All Δ values are positive. Average differences across models:

  • P(True): ΔECE +9.8%, ΔBrier +8.8%, ΔConfidence +15.8%
  • Verbalized Percentage: ΔECE +17.9%, ΔBrier +19.5%, ΔConfidence +18.1%
  • Verbalized Linguistic: ΔECE +26.1%, ΔBrier +25.2%, ΔConfidence +26.8%

The linguistic expression method shows the largest ownership bias; P(True) shows the smallest but still substantial effect. Reliability diagrams (Figure 4) visually confirm better calibration for user-framed answers across all six models.

Breakdown by correctness (Figure 6): The overconfidence is largely independent of whether the answer is actually correct or incorrect. Even for incorrect answers where user-framed confidence is near zero, assistant-framed confidence can erroneously reach up to 60%.

Total confidence (Figure 7): In the multiple-choice setting, total confidence across four options should ideally sum to 100%. Instead, models show aggregate overconfidence: assistant-framed totals range from ~198% to 315%, while user-framed totals range from ~135% to 243%.

Generalization to other tasks (Table 4): The ownership bias replicates on GSM8K (math), TruthfulQA (common misconceptions), and open-ended MMLU. Effect sizes vary by task and method but the directional trend holds consistently.

Generalization to proprietary models (Table 5): GPT-5.2 shows the same directional pattern on MMLU: assistant-framed answers produce higher ECE, Brier score, and raw confidence than user-framed answers across all three elicitation methods.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The paper makes a genuinely novel empirical contribution. Prior work had established that instruction tuning harms calibration and that chat templates affect first-token behavior, but no prior work had isolated the chat template as an independent variable or identified the specific "ownership bias" mechanism. The conceptual framing is clean and actionable.

Rigor: The methodology is sound. The use of three model variants per architecture cleanly separates the effects of instruction tuning and chat format. Cross-model validation (three families, two scales each), three confidence elicitation methods, four benchmarks, and statistical significance testing (Wilcoxon, bootstrap) lend robustness. The proprietary-model validation (GPT-5.2) strengthens external validity.

Reproducibility: Very high. The study uses publicly available open-weight models and standard benchmarks. The inference-time intervention is trivial to implement — it requires only a prompt change. Full prompts are provided in the appendix.

Significance: The finding is practically important. Any system that relies on LLM self-reported confidence — retrieval-augmented generation, human-in-the-loop decision support, safety-critical filtering — may be systematically misled by ownership bias. The zero-cost mitigation is immediately deployable.

Red flags / limitations:

  • The 26% figure is the maximum average difference (linguistic method), not a uniform effect. P(True) shows a smaller but still meaningful ~16% confidence inflation.
  • The mechanism behind ownership bias is described interpretively ("self-consistency" assumption) but not mechanistically validated through, e.g., attention analysis or representation probing.
  • The mitigation strategy reframes the answer as user input, which may not generalize cleanly to multi-turn conversations where the model has already generated the answer in context.
  • Scope is limited to question-answering tasks with relatively objective correctness; subjective generation tasks are not tested.
  • The authors acknowledge they cannot fully guarantee generalization to all proprietary models with different post-training recipes.

Strengths

  • Elegant experimental design that cleanly isolates two previously confounded sources of miscalibration.
  • Broad empirical validation across model families, scales, benchmarks, and confidence elicitation methods.
  • Practical, zero-cost inference-time mitigation that is immediately applicable.
  • Transparent reporting with full prompts in appendices and open-weight models throughout.
  • Direct confrontation with the sycophancy alternative hypothesis — the finding that models are more confident in their own answers (not the user's) is theoretically informative.

Limitations

  • Mechanism remains descriptive, not mechanistic: The "self-consistency" interpretation of ownership bias is plausible but not validated via attention patterns, hidden-state analysis, or causal intervention.
  • Multi-turn generalization unclear: The reframing strategy works when the answer is presented in a single turn, but its effectiveness in extended conversations — where the model has already generated and committed to an answer — is not tested.
  • Scope limited to QA: The study focuses on question-answering with objective correctness. Whether ownership bias persists in open-ended generation, summarization, or creative writing — where "correctness" is ill-defined — remains unknown.
  • Proprietary-model coverage narrow: Only GPT-5.2 is tested among proprietary models, and the full suite of benchmarks and methods was not run on it (likely due to cost).
  • Chat-template granularity: The study treats the chat template as a binary variable (with/without) but does not dissect whether specific role tokens, turn separators, or positional effects within the template drive the bias.
  • No training-time solution: The paper focuses entirely on inference-time mitigation. It does not explore whether calibration-aware fine-tuning or RLHF reward shaping could eliminate ownership bias at the source.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Mechanistic source: Can causal tracing or attention-head analysis pinpoint which components of the network drive ownership bias? Is it tied to role-token embeddings, positional bias, or a deeper self-referential computation?
  2. Multi-turn dynamics: Does the reframing strategy remain effective when the model has already generated the answer in a prior turn of a conversation? Does repeated self-generation amplify or attenuate the bias?
  3. Training-time mitigation: Can calibration-aware fine-tuning (e.g., Xiao et al. 2025) or reward-model modifications (e.g., Leng et al. 2025) be adapted to specifically penalize ownership bias during post-training?
  4. Subjective tasks: Does ownership bias extend to open-ended generation, creative writing, or summarization — tasks where correctness is not binary and confidence estimation is inherently more ambiguous?
  5. Broader proprietary validation: Do the effect sizes and directional trends hold consistently across the full range of frontier proprietary models (e.g., Claude, Gemini, o-series reasoning models) with diverse post-training recipes?
  6. Template design guidelines: Can chat templates be redesigned (e.g., via explicit meta-cognitive prompts or neutral role framing) to inherently reduce ownership bias without requiring answer reframing at inference time?
