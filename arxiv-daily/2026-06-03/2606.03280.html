A Negative Result on Cross-Model Activation Transfer in a Pythia Multi-Hop Setting

arXiv: 2606.03280

Date: 2026-06-03

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03280

一句话总结： 本文在Pythia-160M到Pythia-410M的多跳推理设置中测试了跨模型激活传递，发现尽管线性翻译层在归一化空间中的对齐度极高（余弦相似度≈0.97），但将翻译后的隐藏状态注入接收模型进行推理时，无法提升下游任务表现， replacement式注入甚至具有破坏性，说明离线表征对齐不等于接收器端的因果可用性。
TL;DR: This paper tests cross-model activation transfer from Pythia-160M to Pythia-410M in a multi-hop reasoning setting. Despite a linear translation layer achieving high normalized-space alignment (cosine similarity ≈ 0.97, R² ≈ 0.88), injecting translated hidden states into the receiver at inference time yields no downstream improvement. Replacement-style injection is actively harmful, and even rescaling to match receiver L₂ norms does not rescue performance. The core finding is that offline representational alignment is separable from receiver-side causal usability.

核心贡献

  • 首次在严格控制的语言模型设置中，系统检验了推理时跨模型隐藏激活直接传递的可行性（Pythia-160M → Pythia-410M，多跳问答）。
  • 发现线性翻译层在归一化空间能学得极强的映射（平均归一化R² ≈ 0.88，余弦相似度 ≈ 0.97），但注入接收模型后无法带来稳定的下游性能提升。
  • 通过消融链区分了两种失效因素：(1) 翻译向量与接收模型隐藏状态之间存在约两个数量级的L₂范数不匹配；(2) 即使通过缩放消除范数差距，残余的方向/分布误差仍使表现远低于基线，且后者是主导因素。
  • 提供了严格的诊断控制实验（B-to-B自注入、同范数随机向量、乱序翻译等），排除了钩子机制缺陷、向量幅度问题和信号特异性的简单解释。

Key Contributions

  • First systematic test of inference-time cross-model hidden-activation transfer in a controlled language-model setting (Pythia-160M → Pythia-410M, multi-hop QA).
  • Demonstrates that a linear translation layer achieves strong normalized-space alignment (mean normalized R² ≈ 0.88, cosine similarity ≈ 0.97), yet fails to produce any stable downstream improvement when injected into the receiver.
  • Decomposes the failure into two quantifiable factors via an ablation chain: (1) an ~two-orders-of-magnitude L₂-norm mismatch between translated vectors and native receiver hidden states; (2) a residual direction or distribution error that remains destructive even after norm correction, with the latter being the dominant factor.
  • Provides rigorous diagnostic controls (B-to-B self-injection, same-norm random vectors, shuffled translation) to rule out trivial explanations such as hook bugs, magnitude-only effects, or non-specific signal properties.

Methodology

The experiment uses Pythia-160M as the sender and Pythia-410M as the receiver, both sharing the GPT-NeoX architecture and tokenizer. Activations are extracted at relative depth 0.67 (sender layer 8, receiver layer 16) and a learned linear translation layer maps sender hidden states to receiver hidden dimensions. The layer is trained against L2-normalized receiver activations.

Injection protocols include:

  • No injection: receiver baseline.
  • Natural-language relay: sender generates text, receiver answers from text + question.
  • Additive injection: low-strength perturbation added to receiver hidden state.
  • Replacement injection: receiver hidden state substituted with translated sender activation.
  • Scale-corrected replacement: translated vectors rescaled to match receiver native L₂ norm.
  • Best-alpha blend (α = 0.30): partial substitution from a historical pilot.

Controls:

  • B-to-B self-injection: receiver's own native activations routed through the hook path.
  • Same-norm random: random vectors with receiver-scale norms injected.
  • Shuffled translation: translated states paired with wrong samples.
  • Strict matched shuffled: shuffled control excluding singleton prompt-length buckets (n = 383).

Evaluation uses a locked clean-eval set of 396 rows (SHA-256 hash frozen). The primary metric is word-boundary answer containment (lenient but avoids substring artifacts); exact match and legacy substring contains-match are also reported. Decoding is greedy with 64 new tokens, repetition penalty 1.3, and no-repeat 3-gram constraint.

  • Replacement-style conditions (uncorrected, scale-corrected, shuffled) are all strongly below baseline, with word-boundary accuracy near zero.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The paper addresses a genuinely under-explored question at the intersection of model stitching, representation alignment, and multi-agent LLM communication. While prior work shows that representations are structurally similar across models (CKA, cross-model SAE feature transfer) and that activations can be causally manipulated within a single model (activation steering, patching), the direct test of whether offline-aligned cross-model activations can be causally substituted inside a receiver at inference time is new. The framing as a "negative result" is intellectually honest and adds value by delineating boundaries.

Rigor: High. The experimental design includes: (1) a locked evaluation set with cryptographic hash verification; (2) sequence-alignment and token-mismatch checks for every sample; (3) multi-seed replication with pooled bootstrap confidence intervals; (4) a rich ablation chain and diagnostic controls that each isolate exactly one rival explanation; (5) transparent reporting of best-alpha as a historical pilot value. The metric hierarchy (exact match → word-boundary containment → legacy contains) is clearly explained.

Reproducibility: Good. The paper provides the frozen eval hash, model pair, layer depths, decoding hyperparameters, and validation checks. However, the actual code and data are not released in the arXiv PDF, so full reproduction depends on author release. The methodology is described in sufficient detail that an experienced practitioner could reimplement it.

Significance: Moderate-to-high for the mechanistic interpretability and multi-agent LLM communities. It directly challenges the implicit assumption that "representations are similar" implies "representations are interchangeable." For multi-agent systems, it provides empirical evidence that natural-language tokens may not be replaceable by learned linear activation bridges without receiver-side adaptation.

Red Flags:

  • Single model family, single direction, single task, single injection depth: the author is explicit about this scoping, but readers should not overgeneralize.
  • Low baseline performance (< 10% word-boundary accuracy): the negative result is obtained in a weak-receiver, hard-task regime. A stronger receiver or easier task might yield different results.
  • Best-alpha uses a historical pilot value (α = 0.30) predating the clean-eval rerun; this is disclosed but means the alpha was not tuned on the locked set.
  • Strict matched shuffled control drops 13 samples due to singleton prompt-length buckets; this is transparently reported but reduces n from 396 to 383.
  • Single author, no institutional affiliation: not a methodological flaw, but peer review and community scrutiny would strengthen confidence.

Strengths

  • Exceptionally transparent and self-critical writing; every limitation is disclosed before a reader can raise it.
  • Clean separation of offline alignment metrics (R², cosine similarity) from downstream causal metrics (word-boundary accuracy).
  • The ablation chain (additive → best-alpha → scale-corrected replacement → uncorrected replacement) elegantly decomposes the failure.
  • Diagnostic controls are well-designed and each isolates exactly one confound.
  • Fixed evaluation set with SHA-256 hash prevents eval-set hacking.
  • The distinction between "representational alignment" and "causal usability" is conceptually sharp and well-motivated.

Limitations

  • Narrow scope: one model family (Pythia), one sender-receiver pair (160M → 410M), one task (multi-hop reasoning), one injection depth (layer 16). The result does not generalize to larger models, different architectures, or different tasks.
  • No receiver adaptation: the receiver is never trained or fine-tuned to consume translated activations. The paper itself notes that joint optimization might change the outcome.
  • Low baseline regime: the raw models perform poorly on the task (< 10%), so there is limited dynamic range to observe improvement.
  • Linear translation only: non-linear translators, attention-based routing, or learned protocol heads are not tested.
  • Single direction: 160M → 410M is tested; 410M → 160M or same-scale transfer are not.
  • Automatic metrics only: no human evaluation of answer quality or reasoning traces.

Relevance

该论文直接回应了多智能体大语言模型系统中信息传递方式的核心问题：能否绕过自然语言文本瓶颈，通过隐藏激活直接传递推理状态。对于关注表征对齐、模型拼接（model stitching）以及跨模型因果干预的研究者来说，这是一个边界明确的负结果，提示未来工作必须优化接收器端的因果使用目标，而非仅追求发送器-接收器之间的表征拟合。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Would receiver-side adaptation (e.g., fine-tuning the receiver to consume translated activations) rescue performance? The paper hypothesizes that a causal-use training objective is needed, but this remains untested.
  2. Does the negative result hold for larger models or different architectures? Testing Llama → Llama or GPT-2 → GPT-2 at larger scales would clarify whether the phenomenon is size-dependent.
  3. Can a non-linear translator or a learned attention-based bridge succeed where linear fails? The L₂-norm mismatch and direction error might be better handled by more expressive mappings.
  4. What is the geometric structure of the "residual direction/distribution error"? Analyzing the translated vectors in PCA or SAE feature space could reveal which dimensions carry the destructive signal.
  5. Does the result change for easier tasks or stronger base models? The <10% baseline leaves little room for improvement; a higher-baseline regime might reveal whether the bridge is merely insufficient or fundamentally incompatible.
