Neural Networks Provably Learn Spectral Representations for Group Composition

arXiv: 2606.02993

Date: 2026-06-03

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.02993

一句话总结： 该研究通过将投影梯度流提升到傅里叶域，严格证明了在任意有限群上的群合成任务中，两层神经网络几乎必然收敛到单个不可约表示，并在跨层傅里叶系数间实现秩一旋转对齐，从而将模块化加法中的傅里叶特征机制推广到了非阿贝尔群。
TL;DR: By lifting projected gradient flow to the Fourier domain, the authors prove that on the group composition task for arbitrary finite groups, two-layer neural networks almost surely converge to single irreducible representations with rank-one rotational alignment across layers, generalizing the Fourier-feature mechanism from modular addition to non-Abelian groups.

核心贡献

  • 从循环群到任意有限群的严格理论推广：将模块化加法（Zp）中观察到的傅里叶特征专门化现象，通过群表示论和调和分析严格推广到任意有限群（包括非阿贝尔群）的群合成任务。
  • 单表示收敛（Single Representation Convergence）：证明在随机初始化下，梯度流几乎必然驱动每个神经元收敛到单个非平凡不可约表示（irreducible representation）及其共轭，所有其他谱分量趋于零。
  • 秩一旋转对齐（Rank-one Rotational Alignment）：对于存活的矩阵值表示，三层参数（两个输入嵌入和一个输出嵌入）的傅里叶系数均为秩一矩阵，并满足跨层的循环比例对齐关系。
  • 阿贝尔群的完整刻画：对阿贝尔群提供了完整的人口层面描述——随机初始化促进在非平凡表示上的均匀多样化，并诱导Haar均匀相位，通过多数投票机制联合逼近指示函数。
  • 指数收敛速率：证明了相位对齐和表示竞争均以指数速率出现，并刻画了"彩票 ticket"机制——初始主导表示几乎必然获胜，竞争表示被指数级抑制。

Key Contributions

  • Rigorous generalization from cyclic to arbitrary finite groups: Extends the empirically observed Fourier-feature specialization in modular addition (Zp) to arbitrary finite groups (including non-Abelian ones) via group representation theory and harmonic analysis.
  • Single-representation convergence: Proves that under random initialization, gradient flow drives each neuron almost surely to converge to a single non-trivial irreducible representation and its conjugate, while all other spectral components vanish.
  • Rank-one rotational alignment: For the surviving matrix-valued representations, the Fourier coefficients of all three parameter layers (two input embeddings and one output embedding) become rank-one matrices satisfying cyclic proportional alignment relations across layers.
  • Complete population-level characterization for Abelian groups: Shows that uniform spherical initialization promotes uniform diversification across non-trivial representations and induces Haar-uniform phases, which jointly approximate the indicator via a majority-vote mechanism.
  • Exponential convergence rates: Establishes that both phase alignment and representation competition emerge with exponential convergence rates, and characterizes a lottery-ticket mechanism where the initially dominant representation wins almost surely.

Methodology

The paper studies the group composition task g1 ⋆ g2, where a two-layer neural network with quadratic activation is trained to predict the composition of two elements from a finite group G. The network logits take the form:

f_NN(g1, g2; Θ) = (1/M) Σ_m a_m · ξ_m · σ(θ^1_{m,g1} + θ^2_{m,g2})

where θ^1_m, θ^2_m, ξ_m : G → R are separate input and output embeddings for each neuron m, a_m > 0 is a scaling factor, and σ is the quadratic activation. Training uses the complete composition table with cross-entropy loss.

The analysis proceeds in two stages:

  1. Stage I — Representation Learning: Scaling factors a_m are fixed at a small initialization value. Only directional parameters (θ^1_m, θ^2_m, ξ_m) evolve under projected gradient flow on the unit sphere. In the small-logit regime, the cross-entropy risk is approximated by a quadratic Taylor expansion (Proposition 4.2), and the dynamics are lifted to the Fourier domain via the group Fourier transform.
  2. Stage II — Scale Growth: Directional parameters are frozen, and only scaling factors are optimized. Under a tied-scalar simplification, the mean-field predictor's functional direction is fixed by Stage I structures; growing the scale sharpens the softmax toward correct labels.

The key technical tool is a Riemannian gradient ascent on a representation-theoretic energy functional Ω_m in the spectral manifold. The authors classify all critical points, prove that non-target equilibria are either measure-zero traps or strict saddles, and extend saddle-avoidance results to continuous Riemannian gradient flows (Theorem 4.4), ensuring almost-sure convergence to the desired spectral patterns.

Results & Highlights

  • Theorem 4.3 (Main Result): Under random initialization on the sphere, gradient flow almost surely converges to a state where:
  • Single Representation: Each neuron m keeps only one non-trivial irreducible representation ρ̌_m and its conjugate; all other Fourier coefficients vanish.
  • Rank-one Rotational Alignment: For the surviving representation, the three Fourier coefficient matrices are each rank-one and satisfy cyclic proportionality relations: ξ̂_m[ρ] ∝ θ̂^2_m[ρ] θ̂^1_m[ρ], with analogous relations for the other layers.
  • Theorem 4.5 (Stage II Growth): If the mean-field predictor already assigns the highest logit to the correct group product (perfect accuracy condition), then growing the scalar scale a(t) drives the cross-entropy loss to zero.
  • Theorem 5.1 (Abelian Diversification): For Abelian groups under uniform spherical initialization, the learned representation is uniformly distributed over non-trivial irreps, and the absolute phase is Haar-distributed on the unit circle, independent of the representation.
  • Theorem 5.3 (Convergence Rate): Phase alignment occurs exponentially fast. In the representation competition regime, the initially dominant representation wins almost surely while competitors are exponentially suppressed.
  • Empirical Validation: Experiments on generalized modular addition (Z_3 ⊕ Z_5) and non-Abelian groups verify the predicted spectral structures (Figures 1, 5, 6), including single-frequency concentration and phase alignment.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. This is the first work to rigorously prove that neural networks learn the irreducible representations of arbitrary finite groups, not just cyclic groups. The extension to non-Abelian groups—where representations are matrix-valued rather than scalar frequencies—is conceptually and technically significant.
  • Rigor: Very high. The paper contains extensive proofs (~55 pages of appendices), including Riemannian lifting, critical point classification, saddle avoidance on manifolds, and convergence rate analysis. The two-stage decomposition (representation learning → scale growth) is a standard but effective simplification.
  • Reproducibility: Good. The code is publicly available, the task is fully supervised on small finite groups (deterministic datasets), and the network architecture is simple (two-layer with quadratic activation). Reproducing the theoretical proofs requires deep expertise in representation theory and differential geometry.
  • Significance: High for mechanistic interpretability and deep learning theory. It provides a representation-theoretic account of why neural networks discover structured, low-dimensional solutions from massively overparameterized initializations.
  • Red flags / Caveats:
  • The analysis is restricted to two-layer networks with quadratic activation and full batch training on the complete composition table—far from practical deep architectures.
  • Stage II assumes tied scaling factors for analytical tractability; while experiments suggest independent scaling works, this is a theoretical simplification.
  • The population-level (full-data) setting removes sampling noise, making the problem more tractable but less realistic than stochastic mini-batch training.
  • The non-Abelian results characterize the limiting spectral structure but do not provide the same closed-form ensemble predictor as the Abelian case.
  • Some citations (e.g., "He et al., 2026"; "Marchetti et al., 2026") appear to be forward-citations or preprints, which may not yet be peer-reviewed.

Strengths

  • First rigorous proof of irreducible representation learning for arbitrary finite groups, including non-Abelian ones.
  • Elegant use of harmonic analysis on finite groups and Riemannian gradient flows to analyze training dynamics.
  • Strong connection between empirical mechanistic interpretability observations (e.g., Power et al., 2022; Nanda et al., 2023) and formal representation theory.
  • Complete, rate-accompanied characterization for Abelian groups (diversification + Haar phases + exponential convergence).
  • Extensive experimental verification of predicted spectral patterns.
  • Open-source code provided.

Limitations

  • Architecture is highly simplified (2-layer, quadratic activation, scalar readout) compared to transformers or deep CNNs used in practice.
  • Analysis assumes access to the full composition table (population risk), not a sampled training set.
  • The non-Abelian characterization is structural but lacks the closed-form predictive ensemble available for Abelian groups.
  • Tied scaling factors in Stage II are a theoretical convenience, not a training protocol.
  • The results apply to algebraic tasks with rigid symmetries; transfer to natural data (images, text) is non-trivial.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can the two-stage analysis (representation learning → scale growth) be unified into a single continuous-time framework without fixing scales?
  2. How do the spectral emergence results extend to deeper architectures (e.g., 3+ layers) or non-quadratic activations (e.g., ReLU, softmax attention)?
  3. Do similar irreducible representation structures emerge when training on sampled subsets of the composition table rather than the full population?
  4. Can the rank-one rotational alignment principle be leveraged for network compression or efficient parameterization of group-equivariant layers?
  5. How do these findings translate to continuous or infinite groups (e.g., Lie groups), where the discrete Fourier transform is replaced by Peter-Weyl theory or representation theory of compact groups?
  6. Is there a connection between the lottery-ticket representation competition discovered here and the broader lottery ticket hypothesis in neural network pruning?
