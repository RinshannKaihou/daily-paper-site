CoEval: Ranking Language Models for Custom Tasks Without Labeled Data or Trustworthy Benchmarks

arXiv: 2606.03650

Date: 2026-06-02

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03650

一句话总结： CoEval 是一个开源框架，仅通过任务描述即可自动生成无标签、零污染的属性分层评测数据，并利用跨厂商的裁判模型 ensemble 对候选模型进行可靠排序，在无需人工标注和可信基准的情况下实现了与真实标签高达 ρ=0.86 的一致性。
TL;DR: CoEval is an open-source framework that generates fresh, attribute-stratified, contamination-free benchmarks from a task description alone and ranks candidate models via a cross-family judge ensemble—achieving ρ = 0.86 correlation with ground-truth correctness without any human labels or trusted public benchmarks.

核心贡献

  • 端到端自动生成评测数据：仅需任务或领域描述，教师模型（teacher）即可自动合成带有属性分层的评测题目与参考答案，无需任何人工标注。
  • 零污染保障：由于评测项在每次运行时全新生成，与五个主流公开基准的 13-gram 完全无重叠，从根本上杜绝了数据泄露和记忆化作弊。
  • 跨厂商裁判 ensemble 设计：引入跨模型家族的裁判组合，发现 panel 的可靠性由组成（composition）决定，而非规模（size）——一个小型但 vendor-diverse 的 panel 比大型同质 panel 更可靠。
  • 偏见自动抵消：ensemble 平均机制可将单个裁判存在的 verbosity bias（长度偏见）从平均 |r| = 0.153 降低到 r = +0.010（95% CI 包含零），减少 93%；同时 structurally 消除同家族 self-preference。
  • 极低成本、可复用：四任务研究共产生 7,978 条评估，总成本仅 USD 5.89，足够便宜以至可在每次模型发布时重新运行。

Key Contributions

  • End-to-end automated benchmark generation: From only a task or domain description, teacher models synthesize attribute-stratified evaluation items and reference answers with no human labels.
  • Contamination-free guarantee: Because items are generated fresh on every run, there is zero verbatim 13-gram overlap with five major public benchmarks, structurally preventing memorization-based gaming.
  • Cross-family judge ensemble design: The reliability of the judge panel is governed by composition (vendor diversity), not size—a small, well-chosen cross-family panel is more reliable than a large homogeneous one.
  • Automatic bias cancellation: Ensemble averaging reduces verbosity bias from a per-judge mean |r| = 0.153 to r = +0.010 (95% CI includes zero), a 93% reduction, and structurally precludes same-family self-preference.
  • Extremely low cost and reusable: A four-task study produced 7,978 evaluations for USD 5.89, cheap enough to re-run on every model release.

Methodology

CoEval executes a five-phase declarative pipeline with explicit role separation:

  1. Teacher phase: A teacher LLM maps declared target attributes (e.g., difficulty, topic, input length, reasoning type) to an attribute-stratified benchmark. Each stratum receives at least ⌊N/|S|⌋ items, ensuring edge-case coverage. The teacher also auto-generates a task-specific rubric.
  2. Student phase: Candidate models under evaluation generate responses to the synthesized prompts.
  3. Judge phase: A cross-family panel of judges (drawn from distinct vendors such as OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and open-weight models) scores each student response against the auto-generated rubric.
  4. Aggregation phase: Raw scores are normalized to [0,1] and aggregated. The default is an unweighted mean; an optional unsupervised reliability-weighted aggregator weights each judge by its mean agreement with the panel consensus.
  5. Reporting phase: Reliability is reported via ICC(3,k), Spearman–Brown prophecy, Cohen's κ, and residual bias via Pearson correlation with nonparametric bootstrap 95% CIs.

Role separation is critical: the judge panel is explicitly vendor-disjoint from the student under test, structurally precluding self-preference. The entire pipeline is specified in a single declarative configuration file and is fully reproducible.

Results & Highlights

  • Ground-truth correlation (Section 5.1): On exact-match QA (SciQ, ARC-Challenge, n=191 items, 573 responses), the frontier cross-family ensemble (gpt-4o, claude-sonnet-4, gemini-2.5-flash) achieves Spearman ρ = 0.859 (95% CI [0.77, 0.94]) with ground-truth correctness. The ensemble reproduces the true model ranking (gpt-4o-mini > gpt-3.5-turbo > llama-3.2-3b) within 0.02 accuracy.
  • Composition over size (Section 5.2): Average-measures reliability ICC(3,k) peaks at 0.70 for a selected two-judge panel but falls to 0.40 when low-agreement judges are added, matching Spearman–Brown predictions exactly. A small selected panel (k=2) already recovers most of the full-panel consensus (Spearman ρ = 0.93 for k=4).
  • Judge-choice regret (Section 5.2): On benchmark-grounded tasks, individual judges range from anti-correlated (gpt-3.5-turbo, ρ = −0.04) to ρ = 0.31 (claude-haiku)—a regret of 0.35. The ensemble mean (ρ = 0.238) is never anti-correlated. The unsupervised reliability-weighted aggregator slightly outperforms plain mean (0.246 vs. 0.238) and substantially beats median/trimmed mean (0.222) and Dawid–Skene (0.178).
  • Verbosity bias cancellation (Section 5.3): Individual judges carry mixed-sign length biases (gpt-3.5-turbo: r = −0.177; SmolLM2: r = +0.234). The ensemble collapses this to r = +0.010 with 95% CI [−0.039, +0.057] spanning zero—a 93% reduction in bias magnitude.
  • Self-preference neutralization (Section 5.4): Vendor-disjoint design structurally precludes same-family judging. Measured residual same-family preference across three verticals is small and inconsistent in sign (|effect| ≤ 0.04). Vendor-disjoint aggregation leaves all rankings unchanged (score shifts ≤ 0.015).
  • Contamination resistance (Section 5.5): Zero verbatim 13-gram overlap with 491 items from five major benchmarks (XSum, CNN/DailyMail, CodeSearchNet, SciQ, ARC-Challenge). A controlled memorization study shows a fine-tuned 0.5B model scores 1.00 on memorized public items (ranked above gpt-4o-mini at 0.845), but on fresh items the correct ranking is restored (gpt-4o-mini 0.81 > memorizer 0.74).
  • Cost (Section 5.6): 7,978 evaluations across four tasks for USD 5.89 (~USD 0.00074 per evaluation).
  • Domain case studies (Section 5.7): On three custom verticals (drug–drug interaction, clinical reasoning, legal analysis) with no labeled data, CoEval generates 40 attribute-stratified items per vertical and produces unanimous or near-unanimous rankings with non-overlapping confidence intervals.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. While individual components (synthetic benchmark generation, LLM-as-judge, judge panels) exist, CoEval is the first to close the full loop: contamination-free generation + cross-family ensemble scoring + label-free aggregation in a single declarative framework aimed at the no-data, untrusted-benchmark regime. The "composition over size" finding for judge panels is a genuine conceptual contribution that reframes how ensembles should be assembled.
  • Rigor: Strong. The authors ground their central claims against objective exact-match QA labels (SciQ, ARC), use proper clustered bootstrap CIs, control FDR, and validate the Spearman–Brown prediction for panel reliability empirically. The contamination study includes both a structural n-gram test and a controlled memorization experiment.
  • Reproducibility: Excellent. The entire pipeline is declarative, captured in a single configuration file, and released as open-source. All artifacts are regenerable. The paper provides detailed experimental configurations and appendices with exact prompts, strata, and rubrics.
  • Significance: High for practitioners. The problem—choosing a model when you have no labels and can't trust public benchmarks—is extremely common. The solution is cheap ($5.89 for ~8K evaluations), automated, and domain-agnostic.
  • Red flags / limitations:
  • Ground-truth validation is limited to exact-match QA (objective, unambiguous). On subjective open-ended tasks, the authors acknowledge they have no gold ranking and must rely on the ensemble itself.
  • Only three student models are evaluated (gpt-4o-mini, gpt-3.5-turbo, llama-3.2-3b). Broader model pools would further test generality.
  • The "contamination-free" guarantee is structural (fresh synthesis) + n-gram verification, not a cryptographic membership test against any model's full pretraining corpus.
  • The verbosity bias cancellation is observed on a specific heterogeneous panel (OpenAI + small open-weight models); sign-diversity is correlated with both vendor family and model capability, so the mechanism is bias-sign diversity rather than vendor diversity per se.
  • Some of the domain case studies show very high absolute scores (e.g., legal: gpt-4o-mini at 0.982), which may reflect ceiling effects or lenient rubrics rather than genuine capability differentiation.

Strengths

  • Solves a real, frequently-encountered practitioner problem with an elegant end-to-end solution.
  • Rigorous empirical grounding against objective labels where available; transparent about limitations where they are not.
  • The "composition over size" result is counter-intuitive and actionable—it directly changes how one should build judge panels.
  • Structural (not post-hoc) protection against both contamination and self-preference is a strong design.
  • Extremely cheap: at ~1,350 evaluations per dollar, it is 2–3 orders of magnitude cheaper than human annotation.
  • Fully declarative, reproducible, and open-source.

Limitations

  • Ground-truth validation relies on exact-match QA; subjective tasks lack an external gold standard.
  • Small student model pool (n=3) limits generalization claims.
  • The n-gram overlap test is a necessary but not sufficient condition for contamination; it does not prove non-membership in a model's training set.
  • Bias cancellation depends on having judges with mixed-sign biases, which in this study correlated with capability differences—may not generalize to panels of similarly-capable models.
  • Ceiling effects in some vertical case studies reduce discriminative power.
  • The framework assumes API access to multiple model families, which may be a barrier for some users.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does CoEval perform when all judges are similarly capable frontier models (e.g., GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet, Gemini Pro)? Would bias-sign diversity still hold, or is the cancellation effect dependent on mixing strong and weak models?
  2. Can the framework be extended to evaluate multi-turn conversational capabilities or agentic tool-use tasks, where the attribute-stratified generation space is much larger and more complex?
  3. What is the sensitivity of the ranking to rubric design? If the auto-generated rubric is perturbed or simplified, how much does the model ranking change?
  4. How does CoEval behave on adversarial or safety-critical domains (e.g., medical diagnosis, legal compliance) where incorrect rankings carry high stakes? Can the confidence intervals be calibrated to provide reliable "do not rank" regions?
  5. Could the reliability-weighted aggregator be improved by incorporating inter-item agreement patterns (e.g., items where judges disagree most might be poorly specified or inherently ambiguous)?
