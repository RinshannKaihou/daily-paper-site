Exploiting Verification-Generation Gap: Test-Time Reinforcement Learning with Confidence-Conditioned Verification

arXiv: 2606.03608

Date: 2026-06-02

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03608

一句话总结： 本文提出TTRL-CoCoV框架，利用"验证能力领先于生成能力"的核心洞察，通过置信度条件化的验证机制在无需标签的测试时强化学习中同时提升Pass@1和Pass@k性能，并在六个推理基准上超越全监督GRPO基线。
TL;DR: This paper proposes TTRL-CoCoV, a confidence-adaptive test-time RL framework that exploits the verification-generation gap to improve both Pass@1 and Pass@k coverage in label-free reasoning. It outperforms prior methods by large margins across six benchmarks and even surpasses fully supervised GRPO baselines after a two-stage annealing strategy.

核心贡献

  • 揭示Pass@k优化的根本性障碍： 首次系统诊断了完全无监督测试时强化学习（TTRL）中Pass@k生成覆盖优化的瓶颈，包括低置信度样本的伪标签噪声、高置信度样本的轨迹多样性坍塌，以及直接套用监督式Pass@k目标函数的经验性失败。
  • 提出置信度自适应框架TTRL-CoCoV： 引入基于置信度的条件分类机制（Confidence-Conditioned Classifying），将样本划分为高/中/低三个置信度区域，分别采取不同的验证与奖励策略；同时设计探索增强奖励（Rdiv）以维持高置信度样本的推理轨迹多样性。
  • 实现生成器-验证器协同进化： 使用共享权重的单一模型同时承担生成与验证双重角色。高置信度区域为验证器提供高质量训练信号，低置信度区域借助验证器过滤伪标签噪声，形成正向循环。
  • 超越全监督性能天花板： 在六个复杂推理基准（MATH500、AIME24/25、AMC、GPQA、DAPO）上，相比TTRL平均绝对提升Pass@1达+9.8%、Pass@16达+18.7%；通过"探索-利用"两阶段退火策略，在部分基准上Pass@1甚至超过全监督GRPO基线最高达+5.0%。

Key Contributions

  • Empirical Diagnosis of Label-Free Pass@k Dynamics: First systematic investigation of Pass@k optimization in completely unsupervised TTRL, identifying fundamental bottlenecks including noisy exploration signals from low-confidence samples, latent diversity collapse on high-confidence problems, and objective formulation mismatches.
  • Novel Confidence-Adaptive Framework (TTRL-CoCoV): Introduces a Confidence-Conditioned Classifying mechanism that adaptively allocates verification resources based on per-sample consensus confidence. High-confidence samples bootstrap the verifier and receive a length-diversity reward; low-confidence samples delegate pseudo-label selection to the verifier; medium-confidence samples bypass verification to save compute.
  • Generator-Verifier Co-evolution: Employs a single shared-weight model as both generator and verifier. High-confidence problems provide clean training data for the verifier, which in turn filters spurious pseudo-labels on uncertain problems, establishing a virtuous cycle of mutual improvement.
  • State-of-the-Art and Supervised-Ceiling Breaking Results: Achieves average absolute gains of +9.8% in Pass@1 and +18.7% in Pass@16 over TTRL across six benchmarks on Qwen3-4B. A two-stage exploration-to-exploitation annealing strategy further pushes Pass@1 beyond fully supervised GRPO by up to +5.0% on several benchmarks.

Methodology

TTRL-CoCoV is built on the key empirical insight that verification capability generally leads generation capability, but the verifier’s advantage varies asymmetrically across the confidence spectrum.

Problem Setup and Base RL. The framework operates on a standard GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization) base. For each prompt, the model samples N reasoning trajectories. Rewards are binary (correct/incorrect) and advantages are computed via intra-group normalization.

Three-Regime Confidence Conditioning. For each problem, the model computes majority confidence Cmaj (proportion of samples agreeing with the majority answer). Based on thresholds τlow and τhigh, samples are classified into three regimes:

  • Region A — High-Confidence Exploration Zone (Cmaj ≥ τhigh): The majority answer is treated as a reliable pseudo-label. Verification is activated (Iverify = 1) not for correction, but to provide high-quality positive examples for training the verifier. Crucially, an exploration-enhancing reward Rdiv is injected: Rdiv(yi) = λ · min(|li − µL| / (σL + ε), Cmax), where li is trajectory length. This rewards trajectories that deviate from the mean length, explicitly sustaining solution-strategy diversity on mastered problems and preventing length variance collapse. The final advantage combines Pass@k advantage with Rdiv.
  • Region B — Low-Confidence Verification Zone (Cmaj < τlow): Pseudo-labels from majority voting are unreliable. The model switches to verifier mode, generating m independent verification trajectories for each Top-K candidate answer. The Verification Pass Rate (VPR) is computed for each candidate. Only candidates with VPR > 0.5 enter the trusted set; the highest-consensus trusted candidate becomes the pseudo-label. If no candidate passes, the sample is skipped entirely to avoid gradient corruption.
  • Region C — Medium-Confidence Transition Zone (τlow ≤ Cmaj < τhigh): The verifier offers no exploitable advantage over majority voting. Verification is bypassed (Iverify = 0) to save compute, and only the first-stage generation loss is applied using the majority label.

Asymmetric Verifier Reward. The verifier is shaped with an asymmetric soft reward matrix: +1.0 for true positives/negatives, −0.3 for false negatives (lenient), and −0.8 for false positives (strict). This embodies the principle of being "lenient to false negatives while strict with false positives," prioritizing the interception of erroneous reasoning over endorsing potentially correct but uncertain paths.

Two-Stage Annealing for Exploitation. After the initial exploratory training, a brief second phase removes Rdiv and transitions to single-path Pass@1 greedy maximization. This "explore-then-exploit" paradigm leverages the diverse reasoning pool accumulated during exploration to trigger a secondary surge in single-pass accuracy, ultimately surpassing supervised GRPO ceilings.

Results & Highlights

Main Results (Table 1). TTRL-CoCoV was evaluated on Qwen3-4B-Base and Qwen3-8B-Base across six benchmarks: MATH500, AIME24, AIME25, AMC, GPQA-Diamond, and DAPO-14k-MATH-test.

  • On Qwen3-4B, TTRL-CoCoV achieves absolute gains over TTRL of +9.8% Pass@1 and +18.7% Pass@16 averaged across all six benchmarks. Notable per-benchmark gains include +14.9% Pass@1 on AIME25 and +11.8% on MATH500.
  • On Qwen3-8B, the gains are +6.2% Pass@1 and +13.0% Pass@16 on average, with +6.6% on GPQA and +6.2% on DAPO.
  • Critically, standard TTRL often suffers severe late-stage mode collapse (e.g., AIME25 Pass@16 dropping from 31.6% base to 17.6%), while TTRL-CoCoV completely reverses this degradation.

Approaching and Surpassing Supervised Upper Bounds. TTRL-CoCoV (without annealing) achieves an average Pass@16 of 70.0% on Qwen3-4B, nearly matching GRPO’s 70.2%. With the two-stage annealing (Ours*), it surpasses GRPO on AIME24 (+1.4%), MATH500 (+1.0%), and GPQA (+5.0%), achieving an average Pass@1 of 50.5% vs. GRPO’s 48.0%.

Training Dynamics.

  • Reward accuracy remains stable at ~0.8 throughout training, whereas standard TTRL collapses to ~0.4 in later stages due to confirmation bias.
  • Label accuracy converges smoothly to ~0.7, with a widening gap of ~0.2 over TTRL in late training.
  • Verifier correct rate steadily climbs while error rate declines under joint updates; freezing the verifier causes a capability mismatch and degrades downstream performance (AIME25 Pass@1 drops from 18.2% to 9.8%).

Ablations and Scalability.

  • Removing the verifier entirely causes consistent degradation, especially on hard tasks (AIME25 Pass@1 drops from 18.2% to 8.5%).
  • Removing Rdiv triggers dramatic diversity collapse (AIME25 Pass@16 drops from 47.0% to 32.4) and impairs exploitation (AIME24 Pass@1 drops by 6.4).
  • Performance gains increase with model size (consistent with RL scaling laws), but smaller models (0.6B, 1.7B) show mixed results.
  • Cross-architecture experiments (OctoThinker-8B, Qwen2.5-Math-7B) confirm generalization, with gains most pronounced on domain-matched base models.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: High. The paper is the first to systematically diagnose Pass@k optimization in fully unsupervised TTRL and proposes a principled, confidence-conditioned solution rather than ad-hoc heuristic adjustments. The explicit framing of the verification-generation gap as a confidence-dependent asymmetric phenomenon is a fresh conceptual contribution.

Rigor: Strong. The work includes controlled empirical analyses to identify failure modes, detailed training dynamics tracking, extensive ablations, scalability studies across model sizes (0.6B to 8B), and cross-architecture generalization tests. However, the evaluation is heavily concentrated on mathematical and scientific reasoning benchmarks with deterministic answers. Broader evaluation on open-ended or process-critical tasks (e.g., formal proofs) is absent.

Reproducibility: Good. The authors disclose all key hyperparameters (τlow = 0.4, τhigh = 0.6, λdiv = 0.05, etc.), hardware setup (4× H100), sampling procedures, and provide pseudocode. The codebase is publicly linked. The training uses standard open-weight models and publicly available datasets.

Significance: High for the test-time compute and label-free RL communities. The method effectively narrows the performance gap between unsupervised TTRL and supervised RLVR, suggesting that high-quality self-improvement without human labels is increasingly viable. The co-evolution mechanism and confidence stratification provide reusable design patterns for future TTRL systems.

Red Flags:

  • Base-model dependency: Gains are highly correlated with the base model's domain expertise. On general-domain tasks (e.g., GPQA for generalist models), improvements are more modest, and small models (0.6B/1.7B) sometimes degrade.
  • Outcome-oriented verification limitation: The verifier checks final answers, not intermediate reasoning steps. This risks validating spurious reasoning that coincidentally yields correct answers—a known failure mode in mathematical reasoning.
  • Benchmark concentration: All main benchmarks are math/science QA with short, verifiable answers. Generalization to longer-form reasoning, code generation, or multi-modal tasks remains unproven.
  • Annealing overhead: The two-stage annealing requires training schedule management; the paper does not quantify the extra compute or wall-clock time cost versus single-stage training.

Strengths

  • Principled mechanism: The confidence-conditioned three-regime design is well-motivated by empirical observations and cleanly separates the roles of exploration, exploitation, and noise filtering.
  • Strong empirical gains: Large, consistent improvements across multiple benchmarks and model sizes, with some results surpassing fully supervised methods.
  • Co-evolution design: Using a single shared-weight model for both generation and verification is elegant and avoids the overhead of training separate reward models.
  • Comprehensive analysis: The paper goes beyond final metrics to analyze internal dynamics (reward accuracy, label accuracy, length variance, verification error rates), providing diagnostic value.
  • Detailed ablations: Each component (verifier, Rdiv, asymmetric rewards, joint updates) is systematically isolated and validated.

Limitations

  • Ceiling of co-evolution: If the base model has weak foundational reasoning, the high-confidence region becomes too sparse to provide reliable verifier training signals, starving the co-evolutionary cycle. The ultimate ceiling is still fundamentally constrained by base model capacity.
  • Outcome-oriented verification scope: The verifier evaluates extracted final answers rather than full reasoning rollouts. For tasks where logical process correctness is paramount (e.g., complex mathematical proofs), this may overlook spurious reasoning steps that happen to reach the correct answer.
  • Limited task diversity: The evaluation focuses on deterministic, short-answer mathematical and scientific reasoning. Generalization to open-ended generation, code synthesis, or natural language tasks with non-binary correctness is not demonstrated.
  • Mixed small-model performance: While the method scales well to 4B and 8B models, experiments on 0.6B and 1.7B models show inconsistent or negative results on some benchmarks, suggesting the framework may require a minimum capability threshold to function effectively.
