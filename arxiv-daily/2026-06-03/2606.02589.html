TL;DR: This paper proposes Rashomon-seeded annealing, a framework that repurposes Rashomon sets as strategic warm starts for annealed importance sampling (AIS) in factorial designs. It achieves full Bayesian model averaging (BMA) inference without exhaustive enumeration, with theoretical consistency guarantees and strong empirical performance in both accuracy (~70% runtime reduction vs. MCMC) and uncertainty quantification.

Key Contributions

  • Rashomon-seeded annealing framework: Repurposes Rashomon sets as strategic warm starts for annealed importance sampling (AIS), transforming the Rashomon certificate from an inferential truncation into a proposal mechanism.
  • Full-support initial distribution: Proposes a three-component mixture initial density q0 comprising (i) the seed set S weighted by posterior probabilities, (ii) its unit neighborhood L1(S) sampled uniformly, and (iii) a global uniform component over M, guaranteeing strictly positive support everywhere.
  • Theoretical consistency guarantees: Proves almost-sure consistency of self-normalized AIS estimators (Theorem 1), uniform convergence of posterior CDFs, and quantile consistency (Corollaries 1–2). These results require only full support of q0 and valid MH kernels, not special properties of the seed set.
  • Certified instantiation via RPS: In factorial designs, leverages Rashomon Partition Sets as a rigorous seed constructor, using edit distance on canonical cut-incidence Σ-matrices to define the model-space metric.
  • Empirical superiority: Demonstrates on simulations and two real datasets (NHANES telomere length, charitable donations) that the method achieves superior inferential accuracy (L1 error, IoU) and computational efficiency (~70% runtime reduction vs. MCMC) compared to both truncated RPS inference and PAC-Bayesian competitors.

Methodology

The paper addresses Bayesian model averaging (BMA) over factorial designs, where the combinatorial explosion of interpretable interaction effects creates a multimodal posterior over the model space M. Standard MCMC struggles with poor mixing in such spaces.

General framework (Section 2):

The authors define a model space with structural geometry and a distance metric d (e.g., Hamming distance for variable selection, Robinson–Foulds for trees, cut-swap edit distance for factorial partitions). The k-th level set of a seed set S is Lk(S) = {M ∈ M : d(M, S) = k}.

The unnormalized initial proposal q0 is constructed as a mixture over three components:

q0(M; S) = α1 · [p̃(M|D) / Σ_{M′∈S} p̃(M′|D)] · 1_S(M) + α2 · [1_{L1(S)}(M) / |L1(S)|] + (1 − α1 − α2) · Uniform(M)

This guarantees q0(M; S) > 0 for all M ∈ M. Sampling from q0 is straightforward: with probability α1, sample from S proportionally to posterior weights; with probability α2, sample uniformly from L1(S); otherwise sample uniformly from M.

Annealed importance sampling:

Given a temperature ladder 0 = b0 < b1 < ... < bT = 1, intermediate densities are ft(M) = q0(M; S)^{1−bt} · p̃(M|D)^{bt}. At each step, a Metropolis–Hastings kernel with symmetric proposal on N1(M) targets ft. The importance weight is updated recursively by ft(M^{t−1}) / ft−1(M^{t−1}). Running J independent trajectories yields weighted samples {(Mj, wj)} targeting the full posterior.

Bayesian factorial design instantiation (Section 3):

Each feature m has Rm levels, yielding K = Π Rm cells. A permissible partition Π pools cells with statistically indistinguishable expected outcomes while respecting partial order constraints. The hierarchical model uses a g-prior on pool means γ and an ℓ0-penalty prior p(Π) ∝ exp(−λ|Π|). The MAP partition minimizes (1/n)SSE_Π + λ̃|Π|.

The RPS (Rashomon Partition Set) enumerates all partitions within ϵ of the MAP. Using S = R_ϵ as the seed set and the Σ-matrix edit distance as d, the general framework applies directly. The conditional posterior p(β | Π, D) is Gaussian (Equation 7), so the AIS estimate for p(β | D) is a Gaussian mixture weighted by importance weights.

Theoretical results:

  • Theorem 1: Under finite-moment conditions, the self-normalized estimator ψ̂_J converges almost surely to ψ = E_{M|D}[ζ(M)].
  • Corollary 1: The estimated posterior CDF converges uniformly to the true posterior CDF.
  • Corollary 2: Under continuity and strict monotonicity, empirical quantiles converge to true quantiles.

Results & Highlights

Simulation studies (Section 4):

Two scenarios were evaluated across 50 replications:

  • Scenario 1: M = 2 features (4 × 3 levels, K = 12 cells) — exact posterior computable by exhaustive enumeration.
  • Scenario 2: M = 3 features (4 × 3 × 3 levels, K = 36 cells) — MCMC with ~10^6 iterations as ground truth surrogate.

Key findings:

  • Inferential accuracy: AIS consistently outperforms both RPS-truncated inference and PAC-Bayesian (PB) across all posterior summaries (mean, 2.5% and 97.5% quantiles). Its L1 deviation remains stable as the Rashomon threshold ϵ increases, indicating robustness to seed set volume.
  • Uncertainty quantification: AIS achieves substantially higher Intersection-over-Union (IoU) for credible intervals versus PB and RPS, effectively restoring model uncertainty lost to truncation.
  • Runtime efficiency: In Scenario 2, AIS requires ~180 seconds on average (~70% reduction vs. MCMC at ~600s), while PB is the most expensive at >800 seconds. PB's overhead stems from greedy search for localized concentration sets.

Real-data applications (Section 5):

  • NHANES telomere length data: Analyzing socioeconomic/demographic determinants of cellular aging. RPS-truncated inference suggested counterintuitive findings (e.g., Black males with longer telomeres than females, positive age-telomere correlation in White populations). AIS with 300 trajectories recalibrated these conclusions by exploring beyond the 2,435 RPS models, revealing these as artifacts of narrow inferential structure. PB failed to capture these global shifts, yielding results nearly identical to truncated RPS.
  • Charitable donation dataset: Analyzing treatment effect heterogeneity in fundraising. AIS achieved L1 error of 0.01 and IoU of 0.99 relative to MCMC, significantly outperforming truncated RPS. PB achieved slightly higher accuracy but at nearly double the runtime of AIS.

Code availability: All code and datasets are available at an anonymous repository (https://anonymous.4open.science/r/RashomonSeededAnnealing-DE5F).

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: ⭐⭐⭐⭐☆ The synthesis of Rashomon sets with AIS for BMA is conceptually elegant and genuinely novel. While both Rashomon sets and AIS are established tools, their combination — using certified high-evidence regions as warm starts while recovering full posterior support — represents a meaningful innovation. The paper also extends AIS's traditional use (marginal likelihood estimation) to discrete model-space sampling for BMA.
  • Rigor: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ The theoretical development is thorough: Theorem 1 proves almost-sure consistency of self-normalized estimators, with Corollaries establishing posterior CDF and quantile convergence. The proofs rely on standard AIS unbiasedness and SLLN, which is methodologically sound. The factorial design instantiation with g-priors and closed-form posterior derivations (Equations 6–8) is carefully executed.
  • Reproducibility: ⭐⭐⭐⭐☆ The algorithm is well-specified (Algorithm 1), and code is publicly available. Key hyperparameters (α1, α2, temperature ladder, L MH steps) are discussed with practical recommendations (log-equispaced ladder, adaptive pilot runs). The anonymous repository link facilitates reproduction. Minor gap: no explicit discussion of how α1, α2 were chosen in experiments.
  • Significance: ⭐⭐⭐⭐☆ Factorial designs are fundamental in experimental design, economics, and social sciences. The combinatorial explosion of interaction terms is a real bottleneck. The method's ~70% runtime reduction with maintained accuracy is practically significant. The generalization claim to other combinatorial spaces (trees, DAGs) is promising but remains speculative without empirical validation.
  • Red flags:
  • The NHANES application shows AIS completely recalibrating RPS findings (L1 error drops from 0.734 to 0.000, IoU improves from 0.901 to 0.965). This dramatic difference raises the question of whether the RPS threshold ϵ was set too restrictively, potentially creating an artificially large gap.
  • The PB method performs surprisingly poorly in NHANES (identical to RPS) despite being theoretically motivated — this may reflect implementation choices rather than inherent weakness.
  • The effective sample size (ESS) ratio for AIS is 0.129 (NHANES) and 0.324 (Charitable Donation), indicating substantial weight variance despite temperature ladder optimization.
  • The general claim that "any high-posterior seed set can provide computational leverage" may be overstated — performance likely depends on the geometry of the posterior and the quality of the seed set.

Strengths

  • Conceptually elegant bridge between deterministic model discovery (Rashomon sets) and full stochastic Bayesian inference.
  • Theoretical guarantees are clean and do not depend on seed set optimality — only on full support and valid kernels.
  • Strong empirical validation across two simulation scenarios and two real datasets with multiple baselines.
  • Practical runtime improvements (~70% vs. MCMC) make the method attractive for applied researchers.
  • Transparent uncertainty quantification: AIS credibly restores uncertainty lost to truncation, while PB fails to do so in key cases.
  • Method is general and extensible to other combinatorial model spaces with well-defined metrics and proposal distributions.

Limitations

  • The method is designed for discrete, structurally geometric model spaces; direct extension to continuous parameter spaces or deep neural architectures is non-trivial.
  • Constructing Rashomon sets (especially RPS) can itself be computationally expensive — the net gain depends on this upfront cost.
  • Performance depends on the posterior geometry: if high-evidence models are scattered across the space, the warm start advantage diminishes.
  • ESS ratios indicate room for improvement in weight variance management; more sophisticated temperature ladders or adaptive schemes might help.
  • The real-data comparison uses MCMC as ground truth, but MCMC itself may suffer from poor mixing in these multimodal spaces, potentially biasing the "reference."
  • No sensitivity analysis for the choice of α1, α2 mixture weights or Rashomon threshold ϵ is reported.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How sensitive are the results to the choice of mixture weights (α1, α2) in the initial proposal? Is there an optimal or adaptive strategy for selecting these weights based on the seed set size or posterior geometry?
  2. Can the framework be extended to neural architecture search or other deep learning model spaces where defining a meaningful distance metric d is challenging?
  3. What is the breakdown of computational cost between RPS construction and AIS sampling? For what problem sizes does the net speedup become marginal or negative?
  4. How does the method perform when the true posterior has many well-separated modes that are not captured by any reasonable Rashomon threshold ϵ?
  5. Could more advanced annealing schedules (e.g., adaptive temperature ladders based on ESS monitoring) further reduce the weight variance and improve the ESS ratio?
  6. The NHANES results show dramatic differences between RPS and AIS — would a larger ϵ (more expansive Rashomon set) reduce this gap, and what is the trade-off between RPS enumeration cost and inference quality?
