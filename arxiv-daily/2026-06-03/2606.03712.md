> **一句话总结：** 本文通过对LLaGA和TEA-GLM两种代表性图语言模型的机制分析，发现图token中存在"图sink token"现象——它们在特定隐藏维度上产生极大的激活值，但干预实验和logit lens分析表明这些高激活token并非图信息的主要载体，揭示了激活显著性与图语义效用之间的深层解耦。

> **TL;DR:** This paper presents a mechanistic analysis of two representative Graph Language Models (LLaGA and TEA-GLM), identifying "graph sink tokens" that exhibit massive activation outliers on specific hidden dimensions. Through pruning, swapping, repositioning, and logit lens analyses, the authors show these highly salient tokens are not the main carriers of graph-structural or semantic information, revealing a severe decoupling between activation-level saliency and graph-semantic utility.

## 核心贡献

- 首次对图语言模型（GLMs）中的图token行为进行系统性机制分析，以LLaGA（节点对齐图token）和TEA-GLM（编码器生成图token）为代表
- 发现"图sink token"现象：某些图token在隐藏状态的少量维度上表现出极大的激活值（如维度1512），且偏向早期图token位置
- 证明这种激活层面的显著性不等于图信息的实际利用——图sink token不一定吸引最大的查询注意力权重
- 通过剪枝、交换、重定位等干预实验证明，移除高激活的sink token对下游任务性能影响很小，而移除非sink token有时损害更大
- 利用logit lens分析揭示，sink token解码后主要暴露通用领域术语（如"paper"），而非任务特定的标签或拓扑信息
- 揭示当前GLM中图结构映射到LLM token空间后，图token表示未能形成完全可用的拓扑感知内部表示

## Key Contributions

- First systematic mechanistic analysis of graph-token behavior in Graph Language Models (GLMs), studying LLaGA (node-aligned graph tokens) and TEA-GLM (encoder-generated graph tokens)
- Discovery of "graph sink tokens": activation outliers with massive values concentrated on specific hidden dimensions (e.g., dimension 1512 in both models, plus 2533 in LLaGA), biased toward early graph-token positions
- Demonstration that activation-level saliency does not equate to actual graph information utilization—graph sink tokens do not necessarily attract the largest query-to-graph attention weights
- Through pruning, swapping, and repositioning interventions, showing that removing top-2 sink tokens causes minimal performance degradation, while removing random non-sink tokens can be more harmful
- Logit lens analysis revealing that sink-token hidden states decode to generic domain terms (e.g., "paper") rather than task-specific labels or topology-aware concepts
- Identification of a severe decoupling between activation-level saliency and graph-semantic utility in current GLM architectures

## Methodology

The authors analyze two representative GLM designs on standard graph benchmarks (Cora, Arxiv, PubMed) for node classification and link prediction tasks:

**Models studied:**
- **LLaGA**: Uses node-aligned graph tokens with a Neighborhood Detail (ND) template. The center node is placed at index 0, followed by k-hop neighborhood samples organized in a fixed-shape tree. [PAD] tokens fill unused slots to maintain fixed-length sequences (K = 111 for node classification, K = 222 for link prediction).
- **TEA-GLM**: Uses a GNN encoder to aggregate neighborhood structure into a central-node representation, then projects it into K = 5 learnable graph-token embeddings via a linear projector, aligned with LLM hidden space through contrastive learning.

**Analytical techniques:**
1. **Sink detection**: Define graph sink tokens using activation magnitude on identified "sink dimensions" (e.g., dimension 1512), with threshold τ = 15.0, measured after RMSNorm.
2. **Attention analysis**: Average query-to-graph attention weights across heads and test samples, examining both pre-fill attention maps and layer-wise patterns.
3. **Interventions**: Three controlled interventions per sample—(a) prune top-2 sink tokens, (b) prune two random non-sink tokens (15 seeds), (c) swap sink and non-sink positions (5 seeds).
4. **Post-pruning sink detection**: Re-run sink detection after removing all original sinks to test whether sink behavior is redistributed or eliminated.
5. **Logit lens**: Decode graph-token hidden states at each layer into LLM vocabulary space to inspect what information they expose.

## Results & Highlights

**Graph sink tokens emerge as activation-level outliers**
- Across datasets and tasks, only a few hidden dimensions show large activation spikes while most remain near a low range.
- Dimension 1512 repeatedly appears as a dominant sink dimension in both LLaGA and TEA-GLM, suggesting graph-specific sink behavior emerges after graph information enters the LLM token space—not merely inherited from LLaMA backbones.
- Graph sink tokens are heavily biased toward early graph-token positions. For LLaGA, the top-2 sink tokens with the largest activation magnitudes are consistently [PAD] tokens—not the center node at index 0.

**Activation saliency does not imply attention dominance**
- In TEA-GLM, attention is often stronger on later graph-token indices (2–4) than on sink-token positions 0–1, with this separation visible across layers.
- In LLaGA, stable vertical attention bands appear at fixed positions, but these overlap only partially with sink regions and often correspond to [PAD] tokens.
- This distinction is important: graph sink tokens should not be treated as direct graph-domain versions of classical attention sinks.

**Sink tokens are not the main information carriers**
- Table 1 (node classification): Pruning top-2 sink tokens causes minimal or no performance drop for both models. For LLaGA on Cora, accuracy barely changes (88.40 → 88.00). For TEA-GLM on Cora, accuracy stays flat at 13.33.
- In contrast, pruning random non-sink tokens sometimes causes larger drops (e.g., LLaGA on Arxiv drops from 77.00 to 74.36).
- Swap interventions change performance only mildly, indicating sink tokens are not strongly tied to sample-specific graph content or uniquely important positions.
- In LLaGA, sink-token attention correlates inversely with graph-token sparsity: when fewer real neighborhood tokens are present, more attention flows to [PAD] sink tokens; when more real tokens are present, attention shifts away.

**Post-pruning behavior differs across architectures**
- **LLaGA**: After pruning all sinks, new sink tokens appear but are broadly redistributed across the graph-token sequence. The activation-sink pattern is not eliminated—just relocated.
- **TEA-GLM**: After pruning, overall activation magnitudes decrease and remaining tokens rarely satisfy the sink criterion. Sink behavior is more localized and eliminated once removed.

**Logit lens reveals weak topology awareness**
- Decoded graph-token states mostly produce fragmented subwords, punctuation, or generic terms rather than class labels or structural concepts.
- At sink-token positions (g0, g1) from layer ~20 onward, the most frequent decoded token is "paper" across all three datasets (Cora, Arxiv, PubMed).
- Since TEA-GLM is pretrained on Arxiv and applied zero-shot to all datasets, this suggests sink-token states retain broad citation-domain signals from pretraining rather than encoding dataset-specific topology information.
- Decoded probabilities remain generally low, indicating weak alignment with stable vocabulary predictions.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** High — The paper is among the first to systematically apply mechanistic interpretability tools (activation analysis, attention mapping, causal interventions, logit lens) to graph language models. The discovery of graph-specific sink tokens with decoupled semantic utility is a genuinely new observation that extends prior work on attention sinks in text and vision-language models.

**Rigor:** Strong — The authors go beyond correlational analysis by using three distinct intervention types (pruning, swapping, repositioning) and post-pruning re-detection to test causal claims. They control for sequence-length effects by comparing sink-pruning against non-sink-pruning baselines. The layer-wise attention analysis and logit lens provide converging mechanistic evidence. A minor limitation: the choice of τ = 15.0 is not motivated with sensitivity analysis, though the patterns appear robust across models and datasets.

**Reproducibility:** Moderate — The exact models, checkpoints, and intervention code are not mentioned as publicly released. The paper uses standard benchmarks (Cora, Arxiv, PubMed) and published GLM architectures (LLaGA, TEA-GLM), so reproduction is feasible for researchers with domain expertise, but not turnkey.

**Significance:** High for GLM and interpretability communities — If the decoupling between activation saliency and graph-semantic utility is widespread, it has direct implications for graph-token construction, placement, and alignment in future GLM architectures. The finding that [PAD] tokens become the most activation-salient tokens in LLaGA is particularly concerning for practical deployment.

**Red flags:**
- The study is limited to two GLM architectures on three citation-network datasets. Generalization to other graph domains (e.g., molecular graphs, knowledge graphs, recommendation systems) is not established.
- TEA-GLM uses only K = 5 graph tokens, which may be too few to expose richer structural behavior. The compact representation could artificially suppress the importance of individual tokens.
- The logit lens is applied only to TEA-GLM, not LLaGA, because LLaGA's longer variable-length sequences make position-wise comparison harder.
- The authors acknowledge they do not test whether improved graph-token construction can mitigate the decoupling—this is left as future work.

## Strengths

- Tackles an underexplored intersection: mechanistic interpretability × graph language models
- Uses causal interventions (pruning, swapping, repositioning) rather than purely correlational analysis
- Challenges the assumption that activation outliers are the most important tokens—a lesson relevant beyond graph modalities
- Provides converging evidence from activation analysis, attention mapping, interventions, and logit lens
- Identifies a concrete failure mode ([PAD] tokens becoming sinks in LLaGA) with practical implications
- Clearly distinguishes graph sink tokens from classical attention sinks, advancing the conceptual vocabulary of interpretability

## Limitations

- Limited architectural scope: only two GLM designs evaluated; other graph-tokenization strategies may behave differently
- Dataset scope restricted to citation networks (Cora, Arxiv, PubMed); molecular, social, and knowledge graphs are not studied
- TEA-GLM's compact K = 5 token block may be too small to capture rich structural behavior
- Logit lens analysis only applied to TEA-GLM due to LLaGA's variable-length sequence complexity
- No code or checkpoint release mentioned, hindering exact reproduction
- No evaluated remedies: the paper diagnoses the decoupling but does not test whether alternative graph-token constructions, placement strategies, or alignment methods can fix it
- Threshold τ = 15.0 for sink detection lacks sensitivity analysis

## Relevance

本文与"LLM Hidden States & Interpretability"研究兴趣高度相关：它直接应用了机制可解释性工具（激活分析、注意力映射、因果干预、logit lens）来分析图语言模型中隐藏状态的表示行为，揭示了图token在LLM内部表示中的结构性缺陷。对于关注表示几何、信息编码和探测（probing）的研究者来说，本文提供了图模态下激活显著性与语义效用解耦的重要实证证据。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Do the findings generalize to other GLM architectures such as GraphGPT, InstructGLM, or GNN+LLM fusion designs with different tokenization strategies?
- Can the decoupling be mitigated by alternative graph-tokenization approaches (e.g., edge-centric tokens, hierarchical neighborhood encoding, or learned adaptive token budgets)?
- What happens when K (number of graph tokens) is scaled up or down in TEA-GLM? Does a larger token budget distribute sink behavior or intensify it?
- Do graph sink tokens correlate with any specific graph properties (e.g., node degree, centrality, feature entropy) that could predict which nodes become sinks?
- What would be the downstream task performance impact of explicitly suppressing or normalizing graph sink tokens through architectural modifications?
- Can the logit lens analysis be extended to LLaGA, perhaps by analyzing fixed structural roles (center node, 1-hop neighbors, 2-hop neighbors) rather than absolute positions?
- How do these findings compare to recent work on attention sinks and massive activations in multimodal models (e.g., Luo et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2026)?
- Would fine-tuning or instruction-tuning the GLM backbone reduce the domain-level "paper" signal in sink-token decodings, or would it simply shift to other generic terms?
