Dead on Arrival: Characterizing and Protecting Against Dead-Entry TLB Misses in GPU Microarchitectures

arXiv: 2606.00486

Date: 2026-06-03

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.00486

一句话总结： 本文在24个GPU工作负载上系统表征了"死条目"L2 TLB缺失现象（recently-evicted translation被立即重新页表遍历），发现其可占敏感应用99%的TLB缺失，并提出仅3.5KB存储开销的DEPOT机制（1KB Bloom filter + 逐条目保护定时器），在干扰驱动型负载上实现高达72%的IPC提升，且与LatPC预取机制正交互带来额外2-7%增益。
TL;DR: This paper characterizes "dead-entry" L2 TLB misses in GPUs — recently evicted translations immediately re-walked at full page-walk cost — across 24 workloads, finding they dominate misses in TLB-sensitive applications. The authors identify a two-class taxonomy (Class A: interference-driven shared-VPN access vs. Class B: capacity-driven unique-VPN access per warp), validated by 2MB huge page experiments. They propose DEPOT (Dead-Entry PrOTection), a 3.5KB hardware mechanism (1KB Bloom filter + 2.5KB per-entry protection timers) that prevents reinstalled entries from immediate re-eviction, achieving up to 72% IPC improvement on Class A workloads with graceful zero-overhead fallback on Class B, and composing additively with LatPC prefetching for an additional 2-7% gain.

核心贡献

  • 首次对GPU L2 TLB中的"死条目"缺失进行了大规模系统表征，覆盖24个工作负载，发现其可占TLB敏感应用超过99%的L2 TLB缺失。
  • 提出了基于内存访问结构的两分类法：Class A（干扰驱动型，多个warp共享同一虚拟页，导致MSHR合并放大）与Class B（容量驱动型，每warp访问不同页面集合，工作集超出TLB覆盖范围），并通过2MB大页实验验证了Class B的诊断。
  • 设计了DEPOT机制：一个仅3.5KB存储开销的硬件方案（1KB Bloom filter记录最近逐出条目 + 2.5KB的1024×20bit逐条目保护定时器），通过检测-填充-逐出三阶段流水线阻止重新安装的条目被立即再次逐出。
  • 证明了DEPOT与LatPC（最先进的TLB预取与压缩机制）正交互，在Class A负载上额外带来2-7%的IPC提升；但在Class B负载上与LatPC组合可能产生回归（如gesummv -28.5%）。

Key Contributions

  • First large-scale characterization of "dead-entry" L2 TLB misses in GPUs across 24 workloads, establishing that over 99% of L2 TLB misses in the most TLB-sensitive applications are dead-entry re-walks.
  • A two-class taxonomy of TLB-sensitive workloads by access structure: Class A (interference-driven, multiple warps share the same virtual page, causing MSHR-merge amplification) vs. Class B (capacity-driven, each warp accesses distinct pages, working set exceeds TLB reach), with Class B validated through 2MB huge page experiments.
  • DEPOT (Dead-Entry PrOTection): a lightweight hardware mechanism requiring only 3.5KB total storage (1KB eviction-history Bloom filter + 2.5KB of 1024×20-bit per-entry protection timers), implementing a three-phase pipeline (detection → fill → eviction) within the L2 TLB.
  • Demonstrated composability with LatPC (state-of-the-art TLB prefetching and compaction): additive 2-7% IPC improvement on Class A workloads, though composition can regress on Class B workloads (e.g., gesummv −28.5%, nw −10.0%, kmeans −8.3%) due to contention between protection windows and prefetch fills.

Methodology

The study uses architectural simulation on the NVIDIA Ampere SM86 microarchitecture (46 SMs, 1024-entry shared L2 TLB, 16 hardware page-table walkers, 32-entry per-SM fully-associative L1 TLB). Twenty-four GPU workloads from PolyBench, Lonestar, Rodinia, and other benchmark suites are evaluated. The authors use MPKI ≥ 1 as a threshold to identify TLB-sensitive workloads (9 of 24).

Characterization approach: Each L2 TLB miss is tagged by eviction history — whether the missed VPN was previously installed, subsequently evicted, and then re-accessed without any intervening hit. The authors measure dead-entry ratio, MPKI, and MSHR burstiness (peak dead-slot occupancy) to classify workloads. The two-class taxonomy is validated using 2MB huge pages: Class B workloads show dramatic speedup (up to 127×) under huge pages, confirming capacity-driven root causes, while Class A workloads show modest gains (2-6×), confirming interference-driven eviction.

DEPOT design: The mechanism adds three components to the L2 TLB datapath:

  1. Detection path: On every L2 TLB miss, the missed VPN is hashed through three independent hash functions into an 8192-bit Bloom filter recording recently evicted VPNs. A filter hit registers the VPN in a ≤16-slot Pending Dead-Entry Set (PDS).
  2. Fill path: When the page-table walk completes, a PDS hit triggers the Protection Timestamp Writer to set the newly installed entry's protection timer to expire at now + W cycles (default W = 500K cycles).
  3. Eviction path: The replacement logic uses a three-priority cascade: (P1) invalid slots, (P2) LRU among unprotected entries, (P3) plain LRU fallback when all entries are protected. The victim's VPN is inserted into the Bloom filter. The filter is reset every 1024 insertions to keep false-positive rate below 5%, and protection timers are flushed at kernel boundaries.

Results & Highlights

  • Dead-entry prevalence: In the most TLB-sensitive applications, over 99% of L2 TLB misses carry an eviction-history signature (dead-entry ratio near 100%).
  • MPKI is necessary but not sufficient: Workloads with large IPC improvements and near-zero improvements are indistinguishable by MPKI alone. MSHR burstiness (peak merged dead slots) is the discriminating predictor.
  • DEPOT standalone performance:
  • atax: 0.354 → 0.610 IPC (+72%), reflecting near-complete elimination of dead-entry stalls.
  • mvt: 0.574 → 0.596 IPC (+3.8%), smaller gain due to lower baseline stall fraction.
  • All 15 non-TLB-sensitive workloads: near-zero improvement; minor regressions on sad (−5.1%) and spmv (−2.9%) from second-order LRU displacement effects.
  • All 7 Class B workloads: near-zero gain (−1.4% to +2.7%), within simulation noise.
  • Robustness to false positives: Under fully saturated Bloom filter (worst-case false positive), atax IPC remains identical at 0.610 — the LRU fallback absorbs all mismatch.
  • Parameter insensitivity: Sweeps of protection window (100K–2M cycles) and Bloom filter size (2048–16384 bits) show nearly flat performance, indicating robustness to parameter choice.
  • Composition with LatPC:
  • LatPC alone achieves massive gains: atax +1227% (0.354→4.70), bicg +667%, mvt +729%.
  • LatPC+DEPOT adds +5.4% on atax (4.70→4.95) and +4.0% on mvt, because LatPC's stride prefetcher cannot catch dead-entry misses where the prefetched next stride displaces the current VPN via LRU.
  • Regressions on Class B with LatPC+DEPOT: gesummv −28.5%, nw −10.0%, kmeans −8.3% — DEPOT's protected entries block prefetch fill slots.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The core insight — that dead-entry ratio alone is a poor predictor of performance impact, and that MSHR burstiness distinguishes actionable from inactionable dead-entry misses — is a genuine advance over prior TLB characterization work. DEPOT's specific design as an eviction-history Bloom filter with per-entry protection timers is novel in the GPU TLB context, though the general concept of protecting recently evicted entries has lineage in cache dead-block prediction. The observation that LatPC and DEPOT compose additively because they address different miss sources (access-pattern prediction vs. replacement-boundary protection) is technically well-reasoned.

Rigor: The methodology is sound: 24 workloads, validated simulation model (Ampere SM86), huge page validation for the taxonomy, false-positive saturation tests, and parameter sensitivity sweeps. The distinction between Class A and Class B is empirically grounded and validated. One concern: the minor regressions on non-sensitive workloads (sad −5.1%, spmv −2.9%) are attributed to "second-order LRU displacement" but could be more deeply analyzed — these are workloads where DEPOT should ideally be completely inactive.

Reproducibility: The simulation infrastructure and workload suite are not publicly released in the paper, which is typical for architecture simulation studies but limits independent reproduction. The workload names, baseline parameters (1024-entry L2 TLB, 16 PTWs), and DEPOT design details are sufficiently specified to allow reimplementation in another simulator.

Significance: For GPU microarchitecture researchers and hardware designers, this work isolates a major, avoidable source of performance loss and provides a lightweight, deployable solution. The composability analysis with LatPC is practically important because it tells designers when to deploy DEPOT (always, for standalone use) but when to be cautious about combining it with prefetchers (avoid on Class B workloads).

Red flags:

  • The abstract states "1KB Bloom filter mechanism" but the full design requires 3.5KB total — the 2.5KB of per-entry protection timers is essential and should not be omitted in high-level descriptions.
  • "Zero overhead on others" is mostly true but not perfectly: non-sensitive workloads show small regressions.
  • The Class B regression under LatPC+DEPOT (up to −28.5%) is substantial and could mislead practitioners who deploy both mechanisms indiscriminately. The authors appropriately flag this in the Discussion section.
  • The evaluation is on a single microarchitecture (Ampere SM86); generalization to other GPU generations with different TLB capacities or PTW counts is discussed but not empirically validated.

Strengths

  • Clear, empirically validated two-class taxonomy that explains the divergent performance impact of dead-entry misses.
  • Lightweight hardware mechanism with precisely quantified storage cost and graceful fallback behavior.
  • Honest and thorough discussion of limitations, including cases where DEPOT provides no benefit and cases where it actively regresses when composed with prefetchers.
  • Robustness analysis (false-positive saturation, parameter sweeps) demonstrates the mechanism is tolerant of real-world operating conditions.
  • The MSHR-burstiness metric is a practical diagnostic tool for identifying Class A vs. Class B behavior without requiring huge page experiments.

Limitations

  • DEPOT fundamentally cannot help Class B (capacity-driven) workloads because the TLB simply cannot hold the working set regardless of replacement policy. This is acknowledged but means the mechanism's applicability is limited to roughly 2 of 9 TLB-sensitive workloads in the evaluated suite.
  • Total storage is 3.5KB, not 1KB as the abstract suggests. While still small, the per-entry protection timers (2.5KB) are a non-negligible addition to the tag array.
  • Small regressions on non-TLB-sensitive workloads (sad −5.1%, spmv −2.9%) indicate the mechanism is not completely transparent when it misfires.
  • Composition with aggressive prefetchers (LatPC) causes substantial regressions on three Class B workloads, limiting DEPOT's deployment in systems already using prefetching unless workload class is known a priori.
  • Single-microarchitecture evaluation (Ampere SM86) — quantitative thresholds and absolute IPC improvements are calibrated to specific parameters (1024-entry L2 TLB, 16 PTWs).
  • No discussion of energy or power impact beyond storage area; the dynamic energy of Bloom filter hashing and protection-timer comparison is not quantified.

Relevance

虽然本文聚焦于GPU微架构层面的TLB优化，但其核心方法论——通过系统表征区分"可避免的冗余计算"与"根本性的容量瓶颈"——与关注计算可靠性和硬件级效率的研究方向高度共鸣。DEPOT以极小存储开销（3.5KB）解决高达72%性能损失的设计理念，展示了在资源受限场景下通过精准硬件机制提升系统可靠性的典范路径，对于关心底层计算效率、内存系统瓶颈以及硬件-软件协同优化的读者具有参考价值。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How would DEPOT behave on newer GPU architectures (e.g., Blackwell, Hopper) with larger L2 TLBs, more PTWs, or different MSHR merging policies? Would the Class A/Class B threshold shift?
  2. Can the small regressions on non-sensitive workloads (sad, spmv) be eliminated entirely by a more conservative activation threshold (e.g., requiring both Bloom filter hit and MPKI > threshold before triggering protection)?
  3. What is the dynamic energy cost of Bloom filter hashing and protection-timer comparison on every L2 TLB access, and how does it compare to the energy saved from eliminated page walks?
  4. Could a runtime-adaptive policy that dynamically suspends DEPOT protection when an aggressive prefetcher is actively filling entries resolve the LatPC+DEPOT regressions on Class B workloads?
  5. Does the dead-entry phenomenon and the Class A/Class B taxonomy apply to CPU TLB hierarchies as well, or is it fundamentally tied to GPU warp-level execution and MSHR-merge amplification?
