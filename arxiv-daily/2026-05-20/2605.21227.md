> **一句话总结：** 本文构建了 LexNeo-Bench（3,050 个实例的卢森堡语词汇借用分类基准），发现多语言 LLM 在零样本下接近随机猜测，但通过注入语言学知识图谱子图可将分类准确率从 25–35% 提升至 71–81%，且最小模型受益最大。

> **TL;DR:** Introduces LexNeo-Bench, a 3,050-instance Luxembourgish borrowing classification benchmark. Off-the-shelf multilingual LLMs (Gemma 3 12B/27B, Llama 3.3 70B) barely exceed chance (~25–35%) without context, but injecting instance-specific linguistic knowledge-graph subgraphs into prompts boosts accuracy to 71–81%, with the smallest model gaining the most (+56.5 pp). Neology detection remains difficult and is best handled by few-shot prompting, not KG context, since the graph encodes structural donor information but not diachronic recency signals.

## 核心贡献

- 构建了 **LexNeo-Bench**，一个基于卢森堡语新闻语料库 LuxBorrow 的 3,050 实例 token 级词汇借用分类基准，标注了原生词、法语借词、德语借词三类标签。
- 设计并构建了**语言学知识图谱（LKG）**，编码形态模式、借词词典和同义词对，通过多跳检索生成实例特定的子图注入 prompt。
- 系统评估了 3 个多语言 LLM 在 34 种 prompt 配置下的表现，发现实例特定的知识图谱 prompt 可将借用分类准确率提升 36–57 个百分点，且**最小模型受益最大**（Gemma 3 12B: +56.5 pp）。
- 揭示了大型模型在 KG-graph 条件下的**原生词过度借用归因偏差**：模型越大，越倾向于将原生卢森堡语词误判为借词。

## Key Contributions

- Introduce **LexNeo-Bench**, a 3,050-instance token-level benchmark derived from the LuxBorrow corpus with balanced labels across NATIVE, FR_LOAN, and DE_LOAN classes, plus 50 diagnostic CODE_SWITCH tokens.
- Construct a **Linguistic Knowledge Graph (LKG)** integrating morphological patterns, a loanword lexicon from the Lëtzebuerger Online Dictionnaire (LOD), and hand-curated loanword–native synonym pairs, with multi-hop retrieval to produce instance-specific subgraphs.
- Conduct a comprehensive evaluation of three multilingual LLMs (Gemma 3 12B, Gemma 3 27B, Llama 3.3 70B) across 34 prompt settings × 3 models = 102 evaluation conditions on two tasks: borrowing classification and binary neology detection.
- Demonstrate that **instance-specific KG prompting** dramatically improves borrowing classification (from ~25–35% to 71–81% accuracy) while the coarser KG-flat baseline provides minimal benefit, showing that token-level structural context — not merely reminding models that borrowing exists — is critical.
- Identify an **inverse scaling effect under KG-graph**: the smallest model (Gemma 3 12B) achieves the highest accuracy (81.0%), likely because larger models' stronger parametric priors about French/German compete with and override KG evidence.
- Provide detailed **ablation analysis** of KG components, showing etymology chains and analogical examples carry the most signal, while synonym links and contrastive patterns are marginal.

## Methodology

**Benchmark Construction:** LexNeo-Bench is derived from LuxBorrow, a corpus of 259,305 RTL Luxembourgish news articles (1999–2025, 43.7M tokens). After removing punctuation, function words, and low-confidence labels, 1,000 instances per class are drawn (NATIVE, FR_LOAN, DE_LOAN) plus 50 CODE_SWITCH tokens, yielding 3,050 balanced instances. EN_LOAN is excluded (< 50 instances in source). A reduced subset of 674 items with full LOD dictionary entries is used for lexicon-assisted conditions.

**Linguistic Knowledge Graph:** The LKG integrates three resources: productive morphological patterns, a loanword lexicon from LOD, and loanword–native synonym pairs. Nodes are typed (PATTERN, LANG, LOANWORD, NATIVE_SYN, POS) with edges encoding relations like `follows_pattern`, `from_donor`, `has_synonym`, `contrastive`. For each benchmark instance, multi-hop retrieval produces a compact subgraph (≤30 lines) forming a donor form → adaptation pattern → Luxembourgish form chain, plus native synonyms, analogues, and contrastive patterns.

**Prompt Strategies:** 17 prompt setups per task (zero-shot, few-shot with 5 demonstrations, minimal, KG-flat with global pattern list, KG-graph with instance-specific subgraph, and 6 ablation variants removing individual KG components). Applied to both borrowing classification (4-way: NATIVE/FR_LOAN/DE_LOAN/EN_LOAN, evaluated on 3 classes) and binary neology detection (YES/NO), yielding 34 settings total.

**Models:** Gemma 3 12B, Gemma 3 27B, and Llama 3.3 70B Instruct, all accessed via OpenRouter API with temperature 0.0, treated as frozen black boxes with no fine-tuning.

**Evaluation:** Post-processing strips reasoning blocks and normalizes labels. Metrics include accuracy, balanced accuracy, macro/weighted F1, per-class precision/recall/F1, confusion matrices, and temporal robustness (pre-2015 vs. post-2015).

## Results & Highlights

- **Zero-shot performance is near random:** Accuracy ranges from 24.5% (Gemma 12B) to 34.7% (Llama 70B) on a 4-label task where random = 25%. Parametric multilingual knowledge alone barely helps.
- **KG-graph is transformative:** Accuracy jumps to 81.0% (Gemma 12B), 71.4% (Gemma 27B), 71.3% (Llama 70B). KG gain decreases monotonically with model size (+56.5, +40.1, +36.6 pp).
- **Inverse scaling under KG-graph:** The smallest model achieves the highest accuracy. Larger models over-attribute borrowing to native words (NATIVE recall drops: 0.639 → 0.447 → 0.349 as scale increases).
- **Donor bias:** FR→DE misclassification is 4.6× more frequent than DE→FR. Models systematically overpredict German-origin borrowing.
- **EN_LOAN distractor reveals English bias:** Without KG context, Gemma 12B assigns EN_LOAN to 32.8% of tokens; KG-graph reduces this to 4.2%. Most false EN_LOAN predictions fall on genuinely NATIVE tokens.
- **Binary native vs. borrowed is easier:** Under KG-graph, accuracy reaches 85.5% (Gemma 12B), showing the main difficulty lies in donor discrimination, not borrowing detection per se.
- **Neology detection fails with KG:** Few-shot prompting is best (~48–49% accuracy), while KG-graph degrades performance to ~30–34% because the graph encodes structural origin, not recency.
- **Temporal robustness:** KG-graph shows the smallest gap between pre-2015 and post-2015 items (0.7–2.8 pp), indicating it captures structural regularities that generalize to recent borrowings.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** The benchmark construction is solid and fills a genuine gap — there are few borrowing-annotated benchmarks for small contact languages, and none systematically evaluate LLMs on morphologically integrated borrowings vs. native words. The LKG design and instance-specific retrieval approach is a meaningful methodological contribution. The inverse scaling finding is novel and counterintuitive.

**Rigor:** The evaluation is thorough: 102 evaluation conditions, detailed ablations, confusion analysis, temporal robustness checks, and EN_LOAN distractor diagnostics. The balanced benchmark design (1,000 per class) is appropriate. However, borrowing labels come from an automatic pattern pipeline, not human annotation — borderline cases may be mislabeled. The paper acknowledges this limitation.

**Reproducibility:** Prompt templates and evaluation scripts are promised on GitHub. Models are accessed via OpenRouter API, which is commercially available. The LuxBorrow corpus itself is under RTL copyright and not redistributable, which limits full reproducibility of the benchmark construction step.

**Significance:** The paper makes an important practical point: community-curated lexical resources remain essential for LLM performance on low-resource contact languages, and structured context can close gaps that scale alone cannot. The neology detection results are negative but informative, showing the limits of structural knowledge without temporal signals.

**Red flags:** Single-author paper; the automatic labeling pipeline's accuracy is not quantified against a human-annotated gold standard; only 3 models evaluated (all accessed via one API provider); the LuxBorrow corpus itself is a concurrent submission (arXiv:2603.10789) that serves as both data source and prior work by the same author.

## Strengths

- Extremely thorough evaluation grid (102 conditions) with well-designed ablations.
- The inverse scaling result is compelling and well-analyzed — larger models' stronger parametric priors actually hurt when structured external evidence is available.
- The LKG design is elegant: multi-hop retrieval producing instance-specific subgraphs is more principled than global prompt augmentation.
- Honest reporting of negative results on neology detection, with clear explanation of why KG context hurts (orthogonality of structural vs. diachronic information).
- Strong ethical considerations section addressing data provenance, privacy, and appropriate use.

## Limitations

- Benchmark derived from a single professionally edited news source (RTL), under-representing informal registers and spoken Luxembourgish.
- Borrowing labels are automatically generated (pattern pipeline + dictionary), not human-validated — error propagation from the labeling system is unquantified.
- Only 3 instruction-tuned models evaluated, all via OpenRouter; conclusions about scaling may not generalize to other model families.
- The benchmark focuses on classification, not generation — does not measure how LLMs handle borrowing in downstream tasks like spell-checking or writing assistance.
- Neology detection remains essentially unsolved (best F1 ~0.52), suggesting the binary innovation proxy may not be well-suited to this setup.
- The paper does not compare against traditional baselines (e.g., CRF taggers, dictionary lookup) to contextualize the absolute performance level.

## Relevance

本文的核心关注点（多语言 LLM 在低资源语言上的词汇知识评估）与"LLM Hidden States & Interpretability"和"LLM Inference Evaluation"方向有一定交叉：它通过探针实验揭示了 LLM 对低资源语言词汇结构的内部表征不足，并展示了结构化外部知识如何补偿预训练数据稀疏问题。但从研究兴趣匹配度看，该论文更偏向计算语言学和 NLP 资源构建，与训练稳定性、数值可靠性等核心关注点关联较弱。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How would the LKG approach transfer to other low-resource contact languages with dense borrowing (e.g., Maltese, Romansh, or Yiddish)?
2. Does the inverse scaling effect persist with frontier models (GPT-4-class), or is it specific to the 12B–70B range evaluated here?
3. Could the LKG subgraph retrieval be framed as a retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) pipeline, and would embedding-based retrieval outperform the rule-based multi-hop approach?
4. What is the inter-annotator agreement on the LuxBorrow borrowing labels, and how does label noise affect the benchmark's ceiling performance?
5. Can the morphological pattern knowledge encoded in the LKG be injected via fine-tuning rather than prompting, and would that change the scaling dynamics?
