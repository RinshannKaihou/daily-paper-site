> **一句话总结：** 本文揭示了LLM部署中torch.compile等编译优化引入的数值偏差可被恶意利用，通过LoRA注入后门，使模型在eager模式下表现正常但编译后触发恶意行为，在4个主流LLM上平均攻击成功率达90%。

> **TL;DR:** This paper uncovers a novel attack surface where inference compilation (torch.compile) introduces exploitable numerical deviations. The authors propose two backdoor strategies — ISBS (input-specific boundary shaping via LoRA) and CTB (compilation-triggered backdoor with a universal continuous trigger) — that remain dormant under eager execution but activate malicious behavior when the model is compiled. Across Llama-3.2 and Qwen2.5 models (1B–3B), the attacks achieve ~90% average ASR under Inductor compilation while preserving ~100% clean accuracy and high stealth under eager evaluation. The paper also shows compiler-triggered deviations can jailbreak safety-aligned models.

## 核心贡献

- 首次系统揭示了LLM推理编译优化（如torch.compile Inductor）引入的数值副作用可被恶意利用，构成新型攻击面
- 提出两种互补的后门攻击策略：ISBS（针对特定输入，通过LoRA将决策边界推至编译偏差翻转点）和CTB（通用连续触发器，三阶段攻击：触发器优化、偏差构造、微调）
- 无需修改编译器或硬件，攻击完全在模型权重层面实现，且在标准eager模式安全评估下完全不可见
- 实验覆盖4个主流开源LLM和4个任务领域（Agent、具身、医疗、情感分析），Inductor后端下平均ASR约90%
- 提出并评估了四种防御措施（输入扰动、批次大小变化、精度切换、额外微调），并指出双后端验证是最可靠的防御策略
- 额外展示了编译偏差可被用于绕过安全对齐（jailbreak），使同一LoRA适配器在eager模式下拒绝有害请求但在编译模式下输出有害内容

## Key Contributions

- First systematic demonstration that compiler-introduced numerical deviations in LLM inference (torch.compile Inductor/CUDAGraphs) create an exploitable attack surface
- Two complementary backdoor attacks: ISBS (per-input LoRA boundary shaping, 100% ASR on targets) and CTB (universal continuous trigger with 3-phase pipeline, ~90% average ASR across arbitrary inputs)
- Neither attack requires modifying the compiler or hardware — all manipulation happens at model weight level
- Both attacks are invisible under standard eager-mode safety evaluation, preserving ~100% clean accuracy
- Evaluation across Llama-3.2-1B/3B and Qwen2.5-1.5B/3B on Agent, Embodied, Medical, and SST tasks
- Demonstrates compiler deviations can jailbreak safety-aligned models — same LoRA adapter refuses harmful requests under eager but complies under compiled execution
- Proposes four practical defenses and advocates for dual-backend inference consistency validation

## Methodology

**Attack I — ISBS (Input-Specific Boundary Shaping):**
- Adds LoRA adapters to the last N MLP layers of the model
- Optimizes LoRA parameters under eager execution using a composite loss: boundary proximity loss (minimizes logit gap between correct and malicious tokens to push the input to the decision boundary) plus weight regularization
- The key insight: compilation introduces small but systematic numerical shifts; by positioning a target input exactly at the decision boundary, these shifts are sufficient to flip the prediction only under compiled execution
- Training terminates when the eager prediction is correct and the compiled prediction is incorrect

**Attack II — CTB (Compilation-Triggered Backdoor):**
- Phase 1: Profiles all layers to find the critical layer and top-N dimensions with maximum numerical divergence between eager and compiled execution. Optimizes a continuous trigger in embedding space to produce concentrated activations at the critical layer.
- Phase 2: Computes scalar bias values for each divergent dimension based on the tightly clustered uncompiled activations. Injects these biases before the SiLU activation function, collapsing uncompiled activations near zero (suppressed by SiLU) while compiled activations remain slightly displaced.
- Phase 3: Splits the model at the critical layer, freezes lower layers, fine-tunes upper layers with a four-loss objective that preserves correct behavior for clean inputs (both backends) and triggered inputs under eager mode, while forcing triggered inputs under compiled mode to output the adversarial target.

**Additional jailbreak demonstration:** A multi-objective LoRA training that minimizes NLL of safe refusal under eager execution and NLL of harmful output under compiled execution, showing that numerical deviations can break safety alignment.

## Results & Highlights

- **ISBS (Attack I):** 100% ASR on targeted inputs across all settings; clean accuracy fully preserved
- **CTB (Attack II) under Inductor:** ~90% average ASR across 16 model-task settings; peak 100% ASR on 6 settings; clean accuracy perfectly preserved at 1.000 everywhere
- **CTB stealth:** Trigger(Eager) averages 93.3%, meaning triggered inputs preserve correct labels under eager inspection
- **CTB under CUDAGraphs (transfer):** ~74% average ASR without retraining; Qwen models transfer better (~85%) than Llama models
- **Ablation:** Boundary shaping alone achieves 62.5% ASR; all three CTB phases are necessary — removing the bias construction phase drops stealth from 0.800 to 0.750 even with 92.5% ASR
- **Causal analysis:** FFN blocks contribute 70–80% of cumulative logit deviation from compilation; attention layers contribute smaller but non-negligible deviations in deeper layers
- **Jailbreak:** Same LoRA adapter produces safe refusal under eager execution but harmful output under compiled execution on policy-violating prompts
- **Overhead:** No additional runtime modules or latency after attack deployment

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** High. This is the first paper to systematically weaponize compiler-introduced numerical deviations as a backdoor trigger for LLMs. While prior work (Möller et al. 2026 on hardware-triggered backdoors, Chen et al. 2025 on compiler inconsistency) has touched on related themes, this paper provides a complete attack framework with two complementary strategies and demonstrates it on modern instruction-tuned LLMs.

**Rigor:** Strong. The evaluation covers 4 models × 4 tasks × 2 compilation backends with ablation studies, cross-backend transferability analysis, causal layer-wise analysis, and defense evaluation. The four-loss objective for CTB is well-motivated and the ablation confirms each component's necessity.

**Reproducibility:** Good. Code is publicly available, algorithms are fully specified, and experimental details are comprehensive. The attack relies on standard PyTorch compilation, making it straightforward to replicate.

**Significance:** High. This identifies a genuinely new attack surface at the intersection of the ML deployment pipeline and compiler infrastructure. The implication — that the optimization stack itself must be part of the security perimeter — has practical consequences for all LLM deployment pipelines using torch.compile or similar technologies.

**Red flags:**
- Experiments limited to 1B–3B parameter models; behavior in larger models (70B+, MoE architectures) is unexplored and may differ significantly
- CUDAGraphs transferability is notably weaker (~74% vs ~90%), suggesting the attack is somewhat backend-specific
- Attack I requires per-target optimization, limiting practical scalability
- The continuous trigger in CTB operates in embedding space rather than discrete token space, which may limit real-world delivery mechanisms
- Defense evaluation is preliminary — the paper advocates dual-backend validation but does not rigorously quantify its cost or failure modes

## Strengths

- Identifies a genuinely novel and practically relevant attack surface (compiler numerical deviations)
- Two complementary attack strategies covering both targeted and universal scenarios
- Extremely thorough evaluation: multiple models, tasks, backends, ablations, transferability, jailbreak extension
- Clean accuracy is perfectly preserved — the backdoor is truly invisible without compiled execution
- Strong ethical responsibility: no release of backdoored checkpoints
- Well-structured paper with clear threat model, detailed algorithms, and comprehensive appendices

## Limitations

- Only tested on small models (1B–3B); scaling to larger models is unconfirmed
- Only PyTorch torch.compile backends evaluated; other compilers (XLA, TensorRT, ONNX Runtime) may behave differently
- Attack I is per-target and does not generalize to unseen inputs
- CTB's continuous trigger requires embedding-level access, which may be impractical in some deployment scenarios
- CUDAGraphs transferability is inconsistent, particularly for Llama-1B on SST (as low as 11.3%)
- Defense evaluation is shallow — only four simple defenses tested, and the advocated dual-backend approach lacks formal analysis
- The paper does not explore whether the attack survives quantization (INT8/INT4) or other post-training optimizations commonly applied in deployment

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How do these attacks interact with quantization (INT8/INT4, GPTQ, AWQ) — does quantization break or amplify the compiler-triggered backdoor?
- Would the attack surface generalize to larger models (70B+) or MoE architectures where numerical deviations may be diluted across more parameters?
- Can compiler-level defenses (e.g., deterministic mode, numerical consistency checks during graph lowering) mitigate the attack without sacrificing optimization benefits?
- Is the attack detectable through differential testing — comparing outputs across multiple compilation backends at scale?
- What is the minimum numerical deviation threshold needed for a successful attack, and can compilers be designed to stay below it?
- How does the attack interact with serving frameworks like vLLM, TensorRT-LLM, or TGI that use custom CUDA kernels rather than torch.compile?
