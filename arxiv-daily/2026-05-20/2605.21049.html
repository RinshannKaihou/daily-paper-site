Cross-lingual Robustness of LLM-Brain Alignment and Its Computational Roots

Ni Yang, Rui He, Philipp Homan, Iris Sommer, Davide Staub, Wolfram Hinzen brain-llm-alignment cross-lingual interpretability representation-geometry
Key Finding
Transformer-brain alignment is cross-linguistically robust across Mandarin, English, and French — spanning not just cortex but also subcortical structures. Yet contrary to the prevailing narrative, contextual embeddings do not outperform static ones, and the alignment is not driven by predictive processing (surprisal) or information compression (intrinsic dimensionality). The neural predictivity arises from distributed lexical-semantic correspondences instead.
Key Metric
3 / 3
Languages with stable whole-brain alignment (Mandarin, English, French) — typologically distinct, same result.
Alignment covers distributed cortical networks (limbic, ventral attention, DMN) and subcortical structures. Spatial overlap across languages is substantial, with stable patterns across model layers.
Why This Matters
This directly challenges the dominant "shared hierarchical computation" story. The field has assumed that deeper transformer layers map neatly onto cortical hierarchies, and that surprisal or compression geometry explains why LLMs predict brain activity. This paper shows neither holds — suggesting we've been overfitting our theoretical narratives to English-only, cortex-only, contextual-embedding-only evidence. The implication: LLM-brain alignment may be more about lexical-semantic grounding than about computational architecture mirroring.
Critical Edge
The null results — contextual embeddings matching static ones, surprisal and intrinsic dimensionality failing to explain alignment — could reflect underpowered experiments rather than true null effects. Sample sizes and statistical correction methods are not reported in the abstract. It is also unclear whether these findings hold for larger models (LLaMA-3-class, GPT-4-class) or different architectures. The naturalistic story-listening paradigm, while ecologically valid, may not capture the full spectrum of language processing where contextual effects could emerge more clearly.

Methodology

  • Multilingual whole-brain fMRI encoding
  • Participants heard naturalistic stories in 3 languages
  • Voxel-wise brain activity predicted from transformer representations
  • Contextual vs. static embeddings compared directly
  • Surprisal & intrinsic dimensionality tested as explanatory factors

Strengths

  • Three typologically diverse languages, not just English
  • Whole-brain analysis including subcortical regions
  • Systematic elimination of candidate computational explanations
  • Challenges hierarchical brain-LLM correspondence assumptions

Open Questions

  • Do results hold for larger, more capable models?
  • What specific lexical-semantic features drive cross-lingual alignment?
  • Would MEG or ECoG with higher temporal resolution show different patterns?
  • Is model surprisal the right proxy for brain prediction error?
