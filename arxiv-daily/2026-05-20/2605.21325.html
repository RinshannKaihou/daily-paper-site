Fast and Stable Triangular Inversion for Delta-Rule Linear Transformers

Sobczyk, Gottardo, Matzoros, De Vita, Skogh, Zouzias, Zhuang Huawei Computing Systems Lab 2026-05-20 linear-attention delta-rule NPU-kernels numerical-stability
Key Metric
4.3× kernel speedup
2.5× full layer
Measured on Huawei Ascend NPU vs SGLang Triton baseline. Triangular inversion accounts for ~40% of GDN layer runtime — making it the critical optimization target.
Key Finding

MXR (Mixed Recursion) uses the unstable Cayley-Hamilton method only for small 16×16 diagonal blocks where errors stay bounded, then switches to a log-stable Bunch-Hopcroft recursive algorithm — achieving both speed and numerical reliability.

Six families of triangular inversion algorithms (VCS, MCS, MBH, MCH, Newton-Schulz, Iterative Refinement) were systematically analyzed. The key theoretical insight: DeltaNet matrices have condition number bounded by n², far better than random triangular matrices, which justifies low-precision computation. MXR optionally applies one step of iterative refinement to recover the 2–3 lost digits of precision.

Why This Matters
75.6% → 0.2%

A single unstable inversion algorithm (MCH) collapses Qwen3.6 MMLU accuracy to near-zero. This is the first systematic stability analysis for Delta-Rule models, providing the theoretical and practical foundation needed to safely deploy Qwen3.5, Kimi Linear, and RWKV-7 at low precision.

Critical Edge
NPU-only benchmarks

All speedups measured on Huawei Ascend. No GPU (NVIDIA/AMD) results provided. Algorithmic analysis is hardware-agnostic but performance claims are not.

Fusion portability

The 2.5× layer speedup relies on mega-kernel fusion combining MXR with 5 other GDN kernels. This fusion strategy is hardware-specific engineering that may not transfer.

Open questions

Newton-Schulz stability is conjectured, not proven. Only chunk sizes up to 128 tested. Behavior at 128K+ context lengths is unknown.
