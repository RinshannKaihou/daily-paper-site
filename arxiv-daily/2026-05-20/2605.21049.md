> **一句话总结：** 该研究在中文、英文、法文三种类型学差异显著的语言上用全脑编码框架检验 LLM-大脑对齐，发现对齐覆盖皮层与皮层下结构且跨语言高度重叠、跨模型层基本稳定，并发现上下文嵌入（mBERT）并未优于静态嵌入（FastText），surprisal 与内在维度均无法解释层级对齐模式。

> **TL;DR:** Using a multilingual whole-brain encoding framework on the Le Petit Prince fMRI corpus (49 English, 35 Mandarin, 28 French participants) with mBERT, monolingual BERTs, and FastText, this paper shows transformer-brain alignment spans distributed cortical networks (limbic, ventral attention, DMN) and subcortical structures (caudate, amygdala) with substantial cross-lingual overlap and stability across all 12 mBERT layers. Contrary to prior work, FastText static embeddings significantly outperform mBERT contextual embeddings (mixed-effects fixed-effect estimate negative, p < 10⁻⁴⁰), and neither layer-wise surprisal nor intrinsic dimensionality mirrors the U-shaped brain-score profile.

## 核心贡献

- 首次在三种类型学差异显著的语言（中文、英文、法文）上系统检验 LLM-大脑对齐的跨语言鲁棒性，使用公开的 Le Petit Prince 多语言 fMRI 语料（49 名英文、35 名中文、28 名法文被试）。
- 将对齐证据从经典语言皮层扩展到皮层下结构（尾状核、杏仁核、丘脑、海马）与跨皮层功能网络（边缘系统、腹侧注意网络、默认模式网络），并以 400 个 Schaefer 皮层分区 + 16 个 Melbourne 皮层下分区进行量化。
- 用配对置换检验与混合效应模型重新检验上下文 vs. 静态嵌入之争，发现 FastText 显著优于 mBERT，挑战了以往"上下文嵌入必然更优"的结论。
- 系统排除两种候选计算机制——surprisal（预测处理）与内在维度（信息压缩）——作为层级脑分数模式的解释，提出对齐更可能源于跨语言泛化的分布式词汇-语义对应。

## Key Contributions

- A cross-lingual whole-brain encoding study across three typologically distinct languages (Mandarin, English, French) using the public Le Petit Prince fMRI corpus, with 49 English, 35 Mandarin, and 28 French native speakers.
- Demonstrates that mBERT-based encoding significantly predicts activity beyond classical perisylvian language cortex, extending to subcortical structures (caudate, putamen, amygdala, thalamus, hippocampus) and transmodal cortical networks (DMN, ventral attention, limbic), with substantial spatial overlap shared across all three languages.
- Revisits the contextual-vs-static embedding question with paired sign-flip permutation tests and a pooled linear mixed-effects model, finding FastText static embeddings significantly outperform mBERT contextual embeddings (fixed-effect estimate for BERT−FastText negative, p < 10⁻⁴⁰), contradicting prior reports.
- Rules out two candidate computational explanations — layer-wise surprisal (predictive processing) and intrinsic dimensionality (information compression) — as neither metric mirrors the U-shaped layer-wise brain-score profile, suggesting alignment arises from distributed lexical-semantic correspondences rather than shared hierarchical computation.

## Methodology

The multilingual fMRI dataset is the publicly available "Le Petit Prince" corpus (OpenNeuro ds003643, v2.0.5). Participants: 49 healthy native English speakers (30 females; mean age 21.3, SD 3.6), 35 right-handed native Mandarin speakers (15 females; mean age 19.3, SD 1.6), and 28 native French speakers (15 females; mean age 24.4, SD 4.6). Stimuli were audiobook versions of The Little Prince (~94 min English, ~99 min Chinese, ~97 min French), split into 9 runs of ~10 min each; participants passively listened and answered 4 comprehension questions per run.

Imaging: English and Chinese acquired on a 3T GE Discovery MR750 with 32-channel head coil; French on a 3T Siemens Magnetom Prisma Fit. Multi-echo EPI (TR=2000 ms; EN/CN TEs=12.8/27.5/43 ms; FR TEs=10/25/38 ms; FA=77°; matrix 72×72; FOV 240mm; 2× acceleration; voxel 3.75×3.75×3.8 mm). Preprocessed with fMRIPrep 21.0.1, normalized to MNI152NLin2009cAsym; confound regression removed head motion, white matter, CSF, plus DCT basis regressors for low-frequency drift. Data parcellated into 400 Schaefer cortical parcels + 16 Melbourne Subcortex Atlas (S1) subcortical areas (416 ROIs total).

Embeddings: 7 models — multilingual BERT (mBERT-base-cased), 3 monolingual BERTs (BERT-base-uncased, Chinese-BERT-wwm, camembert-base), and 3 FastText models (cc.en.300, cc.zh.300, cc.fr.300). BERT contextual embeddings extracted from all 12 transformer layers (768-dim, embedding layer excluded); sub-token embeddings averaged to word level. Context window = entire current sentence (~15 words on average). FastText gives static 300-dim word vectors.

Encoding: ridge regression / banded ridge regression mapping embeddings to fMRI time series, temporally aligned to word onsets via canonical HRF. Leave-one-run-out 9-fold cross-validation respecting temporal order (to avoid overfitting flagged by Feghhi et al. 2024). Performance = Pearson r between predicted and observed BOLD in held-out run, averaged across folds per subject per ROI.

Statistics: group-level one-sample one-sided t-tests across subjects per ROI, FDR-corrected (Benjamini-Hochberg, q<0.05) across 416 ROIs. Layer differences tested with paired sign-flip permutation tests across subjects, FDR-corrected. mBERT-vs-FastText compared via ROI-wise paired permutation tests (one-sided BERT>FastText) and a pooled linear mixed-effects model (Model as fixed effect, random intercepts for subject and ROI). Surprisal computed as layer-wise pseudo-log-likelihood (PLL) from mBERT via masked-token scoring; intrinsic dimensionality estimated via two-nearest-neighbor ML estimator (2NN-ML, DADApy) on up to 8,000 L2-normalized tokens per language per layer.

## Results & Highlights

- **Cross-lingual whole-brain alignment including subcortex:** All three mBERT-based models significantly predicted activity in distributed cortical regions (PCC, mPFC, ACC, MTL, IFG, STG, MTG, TP, IPL) and subcortical structures (caudate, putamen, amygdala) across English, Mandarin, and French (FDR q<0.05 across 416 ROIs). Highest brain scores consistently in primary auditory cortex. Shared cross-lingual overlap observed in pSTG, MTG, IFG, IPL, PCC, mPFC, ACC, and amygdala.
- **No systematic layer-wise hierarchy:** Paired sign-flip permutation tests across all 12 mBERT layer pairs found most pairs showed no significant difference across the majority of ROIs; preferred-layer maps did not map cleanly onto canonical cortical hierarchies and showed no cross-lingual consistency. Mandarin showed structured deviations only for mid-level (layers 5–6) vs. later (layers 10–12) comparisons.
- **FastText static embeddings significantly outperform mBERT contextual embeddings:** One-sided permutation tests for BERT>FastText were non-significant (all p>0.95, effects in the opposite direction). The pooled linear mixed-effects model fixed-effect estimate for Model (BERT−FastText) was negative and highly significant (p < 10⁻⁴⁰) across all three languages, confirming higher average encoding performance for FastText.
- **U-shaped brain-score profile across functional networks:** Peak brain scores by network were DMN max=0.038, subcortical max=0.035, ventral attention max=0.026, limbic max=0.025, versus consistently lower visual max=0.019, sensory-motor max=0.019, dorsal attention max=0.016. This U-shape was nearly universal across all 12 mBERT layers and three languages, showing no clear layer-wise differentiation.
- **Surprisal and intrinsic dimensionality do not mirror brain scores:** Surprisal decreased progressively across layers with a marked drop in the final layer; ID peaked at the 10th layer. Neither metric mirrored the U-shaped brain-score profile, failing to explain the superior predictive performance of middle layers — ruling out predictive processing and information compression as primary drivers.

## Critical Assessment

- **Stimulus confound weakens the contextual-vs-static null:** The authors themselves note The Little Prince is a children's narrative with simple grammar and linear structure "designed to be easily followed," so "lexical-semantic information alone may account for substantial variance in BOLD responses." The FastText-beats-mBERT result may therefore be specific to this easy stimulus rather than a general refutation of contextual advantage — a generalization threat the paper acknowledges but does not empirically test with harder materials.
- **Single model architecture limits computational-root claims:** Surprisal and ID are computed only from mBERT (bert-base-multilingual-cased). The authors cite that ID profiles "vary substantially across model architectures and scales" and "only a subset of models show precise correspondence between ID curves and brain scores" — yet they test only one architecture, so the negative computational-root conclusion is under-sampled.
- **fMRI temporal resolution caps sensitivity to layer differences:** The paper concedes BOLD's sluggish hemodynamic response may "attenuate differences between model layers or architectures," and cites that ECoG/MEG studies have reported hierarchical effects fMRI misses. The null layer-hierarchy finding may thus reflect modality limits rather than absence of hierarchy.
- **Modest sample sizes for cross-lingual claims:** 28–49 participants per language with 416 ROIs and FDR correction leaves limited power for detecting language-specific effects; null cross-lingual differences in layer pairs could be underpowered.

## Strengths

- Rigorous cross-lingual design spanning three typologically distinct languages (tonal Mandarin vs. Indo-European English/French) on a single matched narrative stimulus.
- Whole-brain analysis including subcortical structures via two parcellation atlases, avoiding the cortex-centric bias of prior work.
- Strong statistical framework: FDR correction across 416 ROIs, paired sign-flip permutation tests for layer and model comparisons, plus a pooled linear mixed-effects model with random intercepts for subject and ROI as a robustness check.
- Systematic evaluation of two mechanistic candidate explanations (surprisal, intrinsic dimensionality) rather than only reporting descriptive alignment.

## Limitations

- Single fMRI modality with limited temporal resolution; hierarchical effects reported in ECoG/MEG may be missed.
- Only mBERT used for surprisal/ID; generalization across architectures (GPT-2, LLaMA-class) untested.
- Stimulus (The Little Prince) is a simple children's narrative; contextual advantages may emerge with materials requiring fine-grained disambiguation or stronger predictive processing.
- mBERT training data composition is not balanced across languages; cross-lingual comparisons may be confounded by corpus imbalance or tokenization.
- No code/data release explicitly stated in the PDF beyond the public OpenNeuro dataset.

## Relevance

直接映射到「LLM Hidden States & Interpretability」与「ML Reliability & Mathematical Foundations」两个研究方向：该工作通过对比静态与上下文嵌入的脑预测能力、检验 surprisal 与内在维度这两种 LLM 内部表征指标是否能解释脑对齐，为理解 LLM 隐藏状态的信息编码方式与表征几何提供了神经科学外部验证，并揭示了"模型层级≠皮层层级"这一可靠性相关的归因警示。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Would FastText-beats-mBERT hold on materials requiring contextual disambiguation (e.g., conversational corpora, ambiguous pronouns) or higher-capacity models (GPT-2, LLaMA-3-class)?
- Do surprisal/ID fail to mirror brain scores across all architectures, or only mBERT — would autoregressive models with next-token prediction yield different layer-wise alignment?
- Can the shared cross-lingual regions (pSTG, MTG, IFG, IPL, PCC, mPFC, ACC, amygdala) be replicated with higher-temporal-resolution modalities (ECoG, MEG) where hierarchical effects are more detectable?
- What specific lexical-semantic features drive the cross-lingual alignment, and can they be isolated via probing or causal interventions on hidden states?
- Does the U-shaped network brain-score profile (DMN/subcortical peaks) reflect narrative-level event segmentation rather than lexical-semantic content per se?
