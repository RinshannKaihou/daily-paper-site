The General Theory of Localization Methods.

2605.20635 Congwei Song 2026-05-20 cs.LG / math.ST / stat.ML
A single-author theoretical paper proposing "localization methods" as a unifying framework: almost every ML model — kernel regression, MeanShift, Hopfield networks, self-attention, DAEs, score-based models, and diffusion models — can be expressed as a "local mean" parameterized by a "localization kernel" with relaxed conditions (no symmetry or positive-definiteness required). The Transformer is shown to be constructible from hierarchical local models.
Key Finding

Self-attention is a temporal self-local mean; the Transformer is hierarchical local models.

A single abstraction — the localization kernel (any non-negative, normalized, concentrating similarity) combined with the local mean (kernel-weighted average) — subsumes over a dozen classical and modern ML methods under one mathematical roof. The query-key-value decomposition in Transformers arises naturally from discretizing the kernel via feature maps.

Critical Edge

Unified but no practical payoff. Overreaching claims risk misleading.

  • "Any model is a local model" excludes decision trees, full Bayesian GPs
  • No new algorithms or empirical improvements
  • Missing connections to Mamba, linear attention, state-space models
  • Single author, no code released
Why This Matters

Retrieves the Transformer from engineering folklore and places it on formal statistical footing. If attention is just a discrete kernel local mean, then designing better attention means designing better kernels — a well-studied problem with decades of theory to draw from. The relaxed kernel conditions (no symmetry, no positive-definiteness) genuinely expand the design space beyond what classical kernel methods permit.
