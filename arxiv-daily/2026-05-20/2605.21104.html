Key Finding

HORST: Composing Optimizer Geometries for Sparse Transformer Training

Steepest descent and mirror descent operators are structurally non-commutative. By composing them in the correct order — AdamW step first, then hyperbolic entropy mirror map — HORST inherits both L∞ stability and L1 sparsity bias, gaining up to +8.0 accuracy points over AdamW at 90% sparsity on ImageNet.

Jacobs, Jain, Burkholz CISPA Helmholtz Center 2026-05-20 DeiT-base/small · GPT-2 Small
Key Metric
+8.0 pp

DeiT-small @ 90% sparsity, ImageNet top-1. HORST: 69.41 vs AdamW: 59.64. At 80% sparsity the gap is still +8.8 pp on DeiT-base.

Why This Matters

AdamW's implicit bias is inherently hostile to sparsity — its sign-based steepest-descent geometry pushes weights toward uniform magnitudes (L∞ bias). The operator composition framework reveals this is not a tuning problem but a geometric bottleneck: the order in which you apply optimization operators determines which biases survive. HORST proves that mirror-after-steepest preserves stability while injecting L1 sparsity, opening a principled path for optimizer-aware model compression.

Critical Edge
  • Implicit bias theorem covers continuous-time Lp (p ≥ 2), not the discrete AdamW (p → ∞) regime.
  • Largest model tested: GPT-2 Small (124M). Billion-parameter scaling is untested.
  • Only AC/DC and one-shot magnitude pruning validated; RigL / SparseGPT compatibility unknown.
  • Exponential update prevents sign flips by construction — shared limitation with HAM.
