Do LLMs Know What Luxembourgish Borrows? Probing Lexical Neology in Low-Resource Multilingual Models

Introduces LexNeo-Bench, a 3,050-instance borrowing classification benchmark. Off-the-shelf LLMs barely exceed chance — but injecting linguistic knowledge-graph subgraphs into prompts boosts accuracy to 71–81%, with the smallest model gaining the most.

Nina Hosseini-Kivanani · University of Luxembourg & RTL Luxembourg

Key Finding
Instance-specific knowledge-graph prompting transforms near-random LLM borrowing classification (24–35%) into strong accuracy (71–81%). The gain is driven by token-level structural context — etymology chains and analogical examples — not merely reminding models that borrowing exists. The coarser KG-flat baseline provides almost no benefit.

Why This Matters
Community-curated lexical resources remain essential for LLM performance on low-resource contact languages. Structured external knowledge closes gaps that model scale alone cannot — revealing a fundamental limit of parametric memorization for languages underrepresented in training data.
Critical Edge
Inverse scaling: the smallest model (Gemma 3 12B) achieves the highest accuracy (81.0%) under KG-graph. Larger models' stronger parametric priors about French and German actively compete with and override the structured KG evidence, causing systematic over-attribution of borrowing to native Luxembourgish words. NATIVE recall drops: 0.639 → 0.447 → 0.349 as scale increases.
Benchmark
LexNeo-Bench: 3,050 token-level instances from 259k RTL Luxembourgish news articles (1999–2025, 43.7M tokens). Balanced across NATIVE, FR_LOAN, DE_LOAN + 50 CODE_SWITCH tokens.
Models
Gemma 3 12B, Gemma 3 27B, Llama 3.3 70B Instruct. All accessed via OpenRouter API, temperature 0.0. 102 total evaluation conditions (34 prompt settings × 3 models).
Donor Bias
FR→DE misclassification is 4.6× more frequent than DE→FR. Models systematically overpredict German-origin borrowing. Without KG, Gemma 12B assigns EN_LOAN to 32.8% of tokens; KG-graph reduces this to 4.2%.

Methodology
The Linguistic Knowledge Graph (LKG) integrates morphological patterns, a loanword lexicon from the Lëtzebuerger Online Dictionnaire, and synonym pairs. For each benchmark instance, multi-hop retrieval produces a compact subgraph (≤30 lines) forming a donor form → adaptation pattern → Luxembourgish form chain. 17 prompt setups per task, 34 total, including zero-shot, few-shot, KG-flat, KG-graph, and 6 ablation variants.
Ablation Insight
Etymology chains and analogical examples carry the most signal; synonym links and contrastive patterns are marginal. Neology detection is best handled by few-shot prompting (~48–49%), not KG context (~30–34%), because the graph encodes structural donor information but not diachronic recency signals. Binary native vs. borrowed reaches 85.5% under KG-graph.

Critical Assessment

Strengths: Extremely thorough evaluation grid (102 conditions). The inverse scaling result is compelling and well-analyzed. Honest reporting of negative results on neology detection with clear causal explanation. The LKG's instance-specific subgraph retrieval is more principled than global prompt augmentation.

Limitations: Benchmark derived from a single professionally edited news source (RTL), under-representing informal registers. Borrowing labels are automatically generated, not human-validated — error propagation is unquantified. Only 3 instruction-tuned models evaluated. No comparison against traditional baselines (CRF taggers, dictionary lookup). Neology detection remains essentially unsolved (best F1 ~0.52).

Red flags: Single-author paper; automatic labeling pipeline accuracy not quantified against human gold standard; LuxBorrow corpus is a concurrent submission by the same author.

Open Questions

  1. Would the LKG approach transfer to other low-resource contact languages (Maltese, Romansh, Yiddish)?
  2. Does inverse scaling persist with frontier models (GPT-4-class), or is it specific to the 12B–70B range?
  3. Could LKG subgraph retrieval be framed as RAG, and would embedding-based retrieval outperform rule-based multi-hop?
  4. What is the inter-annotator agreement on LuxBorrow borrowing labels, and how does label noise affect ceiling performance?
  5. Can morphological pattern knowledge be injected via fine-tuning rather than prompting, and would that change scaling dynamics?
