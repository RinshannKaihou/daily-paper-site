Towards Understanding Self-Pretraining for Sequence Classification

Omar Coser, Loredana Zollo, Paolo Soda, Antonio Orvieto 2026-05-20 ICLR-lineage Transformers Optimization

Why does self-pretraining (SPT) work so well for Transformers on sequential data? This paper isolates the mechanism: masked reconstruction reshapes Query-Key attention to learn proximity-biased patterns that standard label supervision simply cannot discover from random initialization.

Key Finding

The central bottleneck in Transformer sequence classification is not depth or generalization — it is the inability of label supervision to learn useful Query-Key attention patterns from random initialization. Freezing attention at random barely hurts from-scratch performance on 4/5 LRA tasks. Self-pretraining breaks through this by converting absolute positional encodings into a proximity-biased attention structure (resembling ALiBi) via the WQ WK interaction with positional embeddings.

Why This Matters

Reveals a fundamental optimization blind spot in Transformer training: mean-pooled label supervision has zero gradient along certain attention-score directions at uniform initialization (Proposition 1), while masked reconstruction has non-zero gradient in those same directions. This means supervised training can be structurally unable to discover useful attention patterns — a constraint that applies broadly to any attention-based model on sequential data, not just SPT-specific setups.

Critical Edge

Initializing only WQ and WK from SPT while leaving every other weight random recovers the full SPT gain in 1-layer models (CIFAR10: +12%, PathFinder: +3%). Attention weights are the sole carrier. MLP initialization is negligible. This is confirmed by the freezing experiment: locking attention at random values costs only 0–3.2% accuracy on 4/5 tasks — supervised training never learned those weights anyway.

Methodology — Progressive Ablation Cascade
  1. Replication of Amos et al. (2024) SPT on LRA (200-epoch pretraining → 100-epoch finetune vs. 100-epoch scratch).
  2. Duration: 1–200 epoch sweep; gains visible after 1 epoch on CIFAR10/ListOps.
  3. Depth + cross-data: SPT helps even 1-layer models and across different datasets.
  4. Freezing: Lock attention at random → barely hurts supervised training.
  5. Hybrid init: Only QK weights from SPT matter; MLP is irrelevant.
  6. Synthetic task: 2D elliptical trajectories visualize SPT reshaping attention into diagonal patterns.
  7. Positional encoding analysis: Zero-content input through SPT attention still shows proximity bias.
  8. Theory (Proposition 1): Supervised loss gradient is zero along certain directions at uniform attention; reconstruction loss is not.
