Key Finding

Q-ARVD: Quantizing Autoregressive Video Diffusion Models

First quantization framework purpose-built for autoregressive video diffusion models. Tackles exponential frame sensitivity decay and heterogeneous weight outliers via frame-weighted reconstruction and adaptive dual-scale quantization — achieving near-lossless W8A8 quality.

Why This Matters
  • ARVDs are emerging as the dominant paradigm for long-form, controllable video generation — but their autoregressive rollout makes inference prohibitively expensive for real-time or edge deployment.
  • Early frames determine the quality of the entire generated video due to exponential error compounding across chunks. Q-ARVD is the first to formalize and exploit this structural property.
  • The dual-scale quantizer isolates outlier channels automatically, eliminating manual per-layer tuning — making PTM quantization practical for new ARVD architectures without expert intervention.
  • Near-lossless W8A8 quantization means production deployment of ARVDs on consumer GPUs is feasible for the first time, with a 1.34 GB footprint.
  • The proposed Discriminability Score (DS = CV × BOA) reveals that FVD-FP and LPIPS-FP are far more reliable than VBench for evaluating quantization quality — a methodological contribution beyond the main technique.
Critical Edge
  • Frame sensitivity calibration requires N full autoregressive rollouts (one per frame) — a one-time cost that may be prohibitive for longer videos or frequent re-quantization.
  • The 1.30× W8A8 speedup falls well short of the theoretical 2× INT8 gain, suggesting kernel-level overhead (Triton) remains a bottleneck. Custom CUDA kernels may close the gap.
  • Evaluated on only two ARVD architectures (self-forcing, causal-forcing). Generalization to other autoregressive video models — including commercial-scale systems — is claimed but unverified.
  • No human-perception study; relies entirely on automated metrics (FVD-FP, LPIPS-FP). The authors' own DS analysis shows metric choice matters — but human judgment is still absent.
  • Mixed-precision across frames (high precision early, low precision late) is a natural extension the authors identify but leave entirely to future work.
