AGPO: Adaptive Group Policy Optimization with Dual Statistical Feedback

Miaobo Hu, Shuhao Hu, Bokun Wang, Ruohan Wang, Xin Wang, Xiaobo Guo, Daren Zha, Jun Xiao — Chinese Academy of Sciences / UCAS

Key Finding

Adaptive clipping + bidirectional temperature sampling, driven by shared group statistics, consistently outperforms PPO and GRPO on 9 benchmarks across 3 model families—with only ~3% throughput overhead.

AGPO replaces GRPO's fixed clipping radius and static decoding temperature with two adaptive controllers fed by a single probe phase. The probe computes reward dispersion (σ̂), safeguarded skewness (κ̃3), vote entropy (Eprobe), and step-wise KL drift. Adaptive clipping uses these to set the trust-region size; adaptive temperature sampling heats or cools decoding based on centered uncertainty against a running EMA baseline. Both controllers share one probe, coupling exploration with stability without a value critic.

Why This Matters

No value critic needed. Near-zero overhead. Broad gains.

The dual-controller design gives RLHF practitioners a drop-in GRPO upgrade that is simultaneously more stable (adaptive trust region) and more exploratory (bidirectional temperature). The ~3% throughput cost is negligible compared to the 1.5× speed advantage over PPO.

Critical Edge
  • 7+ tunable coefficients (α, γ, δ, ζ, wr, we, wk, λ, ρU) with limited sensitivity analysis beyond leave-one-out ablations
  • Math-only evaluation: all 9 benchmarks require verifiable answers; no open-ended generation, alignment, or dialogue tasks
  • Group size dependency: robustness with G<8 or sparse/noisy rewards is untested
  • Reward hacking: not analyzed; acknowledged as open direction

Strengths

  • Principled integration of two controllers through shared probe phase
  • Consistent gains under strictly equal token budgets
  • Thorough ablation: leave-one-out signals, robust dispersion substitutes (MAD/IQR), stability metrics
  • Public code, fully specified hyperparameters

Limitations

  • 15 hyperparameters vs GRPO's single ε
  • No open-ended / non-verifiable-reward evaluation
  • Probe phase adds per-step generation overhead (accounted for but present)
  • Convergence proof is for idealized proxy, not the actual objective

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does AGPO behave with non-verifiable rewards (learned reward models)? Does adaptive clipping help prevent reward hacking?
  2. Is there a simpler parameterization that retains most of the gains?
  3. Can the probe phase be amortized or approximated for large models where generation is expensive?
  4. How does AGPO scale to 70B+ models with smaller group sizes?
  5. Does bidirectional ATS interact well with best-of-n or tree-of-thought sampling?
