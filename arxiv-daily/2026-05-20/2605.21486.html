Quantifying Hyperparameter Transfer and the Importance of Embedding Layer Learning Rate

Kalra & Barkeshli — UMD / Meta FAIR 2026-05-20 arXiv 2605.21486
The authors introduce a three-metric framework (E, κ, R(∞)) to rigorously quantify HP transfer quality. Across 16 ablations on GPT-style Transformers (128–2048 width, ~1B params), they find μP's advantage comes almost entirely from its Θ(1) embedding layer learning rate. Scaling SP's embedding LR by width eliminates instabilities and matches μP—making the full μP recipe unnecessary.
Key Finding
μP's entire advantage is the embedding layer learning rate. The full maximal-update parameterization—with its four changes to SP—is unnecessary. Only one correction matters: set the embedding layer LR to Θ(1) instead of Θ(1/n). In a systematic 16-ablation study (all 2⁴ combinations), SP+Embd matches μP on every transfer quality metric. Conversely, removing just the embedding fix from μP (μP-Embd) collapses transfer quality back to SP levels. Dynamic switching experiments confirm the embedding LR is causally decisive early in training—a persistent ~0.2 loss gap appears when switching from fast to slow embedding LR at step 100.

Why This Matters
Practitioners can recover μP-quality transfer by correcting one hyperparameter in standard parameterization—no framework migration needed. The three-metric framework (E, κ, R(∞)) also provides a reusable diagnostic for evaluating any future transfer method.
Critical Edge
Experiments cap at ~1B params, depth 12. Untested at 70B+, MoE, or beyond AdamW + FineWeb-Edu. Single seed per config. Depth scaling is unaddressed. Weight-tied embeddings need an extra output multiplier.

Methodology

  • GPT-style Transformers, depth 12, width 128–2048, ~1B params
  • Pre-trained on FineWeb-Edu with AdamW (WSD schedule)
  • 20 LR × 8 WD × 8 widths × 16 parameterizations = ~160K H100 GPU hours
  • Three-metric framework: loss predictability (E), transfer robustness (κ), asymptotic degradation (R(∞))
  • Joint ansatz fit via Huber loss with 200 random initializations

Additional Insights

  • LayerNorm LR should stay Θ(1/n)—faster rates destabilize training (E ≈ 10× SP)
  • Last-layer init variance is negligible—warmup absorbs the difference
  • Weight decay lowers E but degrades κ in compute-optimal (fixed TPP) regime
  • The η·λ = Θ(1) convention is inadequate when T ∝ Θ(n²)
  • CNN validation on CIFAR-100 confirms: first-layer LR effect generalizes beyond Transformers
