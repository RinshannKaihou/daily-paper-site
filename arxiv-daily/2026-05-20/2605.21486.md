> **一句话总结：** 本文提出了基于三个指标（缩放定律拟合质量 E、外推鲁棒性指数 κ、渐近损失惩罚 R(∞)）的超参数迁移量化框架，并通过 16 组消融实验发现 μP 相比标准参数化的核心优势仅来源于最大化嵌入层学习率——将 SP 的嵌入层 LR 放大 width 倍即可消除训练不稳定性并获得与 μP 相当的迁移质量。

> **TL;DR:** The authors introduce a three-metric framework (loss predictability error E, transfer robustness exponent κ, asymptotic loss degradation R(∞)) to rigorously quantify hyperparameter transfer quality. Using 16 ablations between standard parameterization (SP) and μP on GPT-style Transformers (width 128–2048, ~1B params, trained with AdamW on FineWeb-Edu), they find that μP's advantage comes almost entirely from its Θ(1) embedding layer learning rate. In SP, the embedding LR acts as a bottleneck that induces training instabilities near the optimal global LR; simply scaling it up by a factor of width eliminates these instabilities and matches μP's transfer quality. Weight decay improves scaling law fits (lower E) but degrades extrapolation robustness (higher κ) in the compute-optimal fixed-TPP regime. The full μP recipe is excessive—practitioners using SP can recover comparable transfer by correcting only the embedding layer LR.

## 核心贡献

- 提出了三个互补指标量化超参数迁移质量：损失可预测误差 E、迁移鲁棒性指数 κ = α − 2β + γ、渐近损失退化 R(∞)
- 通过 SP 与 μP 之间全部 16 组消融实验，定位嵌入层学习率为 μP 优势的根本来源
- 发现 SP 中嵌入层学习率过慢不仅拖慢训练，还会在最优全局学习率附近引发训练不稳定性
- 将 SP 嵌入层 LR 提升 width 倍（SP+Embd）即可在所有三个指标上匹配 μP
- 揭示 weight decay 改善缩放定律拟合但损害 compute-optimal 设定下的外推鲁棒性

## Key Contributions

- A principled three-metric framework for quantifying hyperparameter transfer: (1) loss predictability error E measuring how well a parametric scaling ansatz fits observed loss curves, (2) transfer robustness exponent κ = α − 2β + γ controlling whether the loss landscape flattens (κ < 0, robust) or sharpens (κ > 0, brittle) with width, and (3) asymptotic loss degradation R(∞) measuring the irreducible loss gap relative to the best parameterization at infinite width
- A unified "abcd" parameterization expressing SP and μP in a common form, showing they differ in exactly four ways: embedding layer LR, last-layer initialization variance, LayerNorm LR, and attention scaling (Table 1)
- Systematic 16-ablation study between SP and μP isolating the embedding layer learning rate as the dominant factor: SP+Embd (SP with Θ(1) embedding LR) matches μP across all three metrics; μP-Embd (μP with Θ(1/n) embedding LR) degrades to SP-like instability
- Dynamic switching experiments showing the embedding LR matters most early in training—switching from fast to slow embedding LR in μP creates persistent loss gaps, while switching from slow to fast in SP eliminates instabilities
- Analysis showing weight decay primarily reduces E (smoother scaling law fits) but degrades κ in the compute-optimal fixed token-per-parameter regime, with the standard η·λ = Θ(1) convention becoming inadequate when training steps scale as Θ(n²)

## Methodology

The paper operates at two levels: a theoretical framework and large-scale empirical ablations.

**Framework.** The authors model the loss landscape as a function of width n and log-learning-rate ν using three coupled power-law scaling laws: (1) optimal loss L*(n) = L*(∞) + An^(−α), (2) optimal log-LR ν*(n) = ν*(∞) + Bn^(−β), and (3) loss Hessian H(n) = Cn^γ. Substituting into a local quadratic approximation yields a joint ansatz L(ν;n) = L*(∞) + An^(−α) + ½Cn^γ(ν − ν*(∞) − Bn^(−β))². The three metrics—E, κ = α − 2β + γ, and R(∞)—are extracted from fits to this ansatz. Fitting uses Huber loss with 200 random initializations and handles degeneracies (near-constant ν* across widths) via a β-min sweep procedure.

**Experiments.** GPT-style Transformers (depth 12, head dim 64, context 1024, GPT-2 tokenizer with 50,304 vocab) are pre-trained on FineWeb-Edu with AdamW (β₁=0.9, β₂=0.95, WSD schedule: 20% warmup / 60% stable / 20% decay). Width is swept n ∈ {128, 256, 512, 768, 1024, 1536, 2048}. For each width, 20 peak learning rates × 8 weight decay values × 16 parameterizations are swept—totaling ~160,000 H100 GPU hours. Two training regimes are studied: fixed-step (T=10,000 steps, batch 1024, ~10B tokens) and compute-optimal (20 tokens per parameter, batch 256). Loss curves are filtered (retain runs within 1.35× of per-width optimum), smoothed with cubic splines, and the three metrics are estimated from individually fitted scaling laws plus a joint ansatz fit.

**Ablation design.** Starting from SP, four changes produce μP: (1) embedding LR: Θ(1/n) → Θ(1), (2) last-layer init: Θ(1/n) → Θ(1/n²), (3) LayerNorm LR: Θ(1/n) → Θ(1), (4) attention scale: 1/√d → 1/d. All 16 combinations (2⁴) are tested, enabling identification of which change(s) drive transfer quality.

## Results & Highlights

- **Embedding LR dominates.** SP+Embd achieves E = 0.0005, κ = −2.333, R(∞) = 0.268—essentially matching μP (E = 0.0034, κ = −2.705, R(∞) = 0.241). μP-Embd degrades to E = 0.0623 with severe instability. This one factor explains the overwhelming difference.
- **Attention scaling is subtle but matters.** Both SP+Attn and μP-Attn produce large positive κ (~0.5–0.9), making transfer brittle. This suggests the attention scaling convention interacts with other layer scaling choices.
- **LayerNorm LR: slower is better.** SP+LN (Θ(1) LayerNorm LR) severely destabilizes training (E ≈ 10× SP). μP-LN (Θ(1/n) LayerNorm LR) has negligible effect. LayerNorm parameters are best trained slowly—a small performance penalty for significant stability.
- **Last-layer init is negligible.** SP+Last and μP-Last barely change metrics. Warmup mitigates initialization differences by reducing Hessian sharpness early in training.
- **Dynamic switching confirms early-training importance.** Switching embedding LR from Θ(1) to Θ(1/n) in μP at step 100 creates a ~0.2 persistent loss gap. Switching from Θ(1/n) to Θ(1) in SP at step 100 eliminates instabilities entirely.
- **Weight decay tradeoff.** Increasing λ consistently reduces E (better fits) but in the compute-optimal regime, κ degrades sharply toward 0 or positive values, making extrapolation less robust. The authors trace this to μP's η·λ = Θ(1) convention being derived under fixed steps, not the T ∝ n² scaling of compute-optimal training.
- **CNN generalization.** On CIFAR-100 with CNNs, SP with Θ(1) input-layer LR matches μP's transfer, confirming the first-layer LR effect generalizes beyond Transformers.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: High.** The three-metric framework (E, κ, R(∞)) is a genuine contribution that goes beyond prior work's binary "transfer works/doesn't" assessments. The finding that embedding LR alone explains μP's advantage is both surprising and impactful—it challenges the prevailing narrative that μP's full parameterization is necessary.
- **Rigor: Very high.** The ablation design is exhaustive (all 2⁴ = 16 combinations), the fitting procedure handles edge cases (degeneracy resolution via β-min sweep, Huber loss, 200 random inits), and results are consistent across fixed-step and compute-optimal regimes. However, single-seed training for each configuration is a limitation acknowledged by the authors.
- **Reproducibility: Moderate-high.** The paper specifies architecture, optimizer, dataset, and sweep ranges in detail. The nanoGPT codebase is public. However, the full 160K GPU-hour sweep is expensive to reproduce, and some fitting procedure details (e.g., the exact f=1.35 filtering threshold choice) require careful implementation.
- **Significance: Very high.** If confirmed at frontier scale, the result that SP+Embd suffices could save practitioners from adopting the full μP recipe. The framework itself is a reusable diagnostic tool. The weight decay inadequacy in compute-optimal training raises important open questions.
- **Red flags:** (1) Scale limitation—experiments go up to ~1B parameters; it is unclear if findings hold at 70B+ scale. (2) Single optimizer (AdamW) and single dataset (FineWeb-Edu); generalization is not guaranteed. (3) The fixed-depth assumption leaves depth scaling unaddressed. (4) The three metrics can trade off against each other, and the paper does not propose a single scalar objective—this is intellectually honest but leaves practitioners without a clear "best" ranking.

## Strengths

- The three-metric framework cleanly decomposes transfer quality into complementary axes, enabling nuanced comparison beyond "does transfer work?"
- The 16-ablation design is the gold standard for isolating causal factors in a multi-variable system
- The dynamic switching experiments provide strong mechanistic evidence that embedding LR matters causally, not just correlatively
- Honest about limitations (single seed, single optimizer, single dataset, scale ceiling)
- The CNN validation on CIFAR-100 demonstrates generality of the first-layer LR finding
- Identifies a concrete, actionable prescription: just increase your embedding layer LR in SP
- Raises important open questions about weight decay scaling in compute-optimal regimes

## Limitations

- Experiments limited to ~1B parameter decoder-only Transformers at fixed depth 12; generalization to frontier-scale models (70B+, MoE, multimodal) is untested
- AdamW only; findings may differ for SGD, Muon, or other optimizers with different update geometries
- Single dataset (FineWeb-Edu); domain-specific effects are possible
- Single random seed per configuration introduces statistical noise, especially for the noisier SP/SP+LN parameterizations
- Depth scaling is not addressed—the abstractions only consider width scaling
- The compute-optimal regime analysis reveals weight decay scaling issues but leaves them unresolved
- Weight-tied embedding (sharing embedding and output layer) requires an additional 1/n output multiplier, adding complexity to the practical prescription

## Relevance

本文对 LLM 训练实践有直接指导意义：揭示 μP 的核心优势仅来自嵌入层学习率，大幅降低了超参数迁移的采纳门槛。三个指标框架也为评估未来的迁移方法提供了标准化工具，对关心训练效率和规模化训练的研究者都很有价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the SP+Embd finding hold at 70B+ scale, with mixture-of-experts, or with multi-modal architectures?
- How does the embedding LR effect interact with learning rate warmup schedules and cosine decay?
- Can the three-metric framework be extended to batch size, context length, or depth as scaling dimensions?
- What is the correct weight decay scaling convention for compute-optimal training where T ∝ Θ(n²)?
- Does the first-layer LR insight generalize to other boundary layers (e.g., the output projection in encoder-decoder models)?
- For weight-tied embeddings, is there a principled way to set the output multiplier without μP's full parameterization?
- How do these results change with Muon or other orthogonal optimizers that have different update geometries from AdamW?
