Hack-Verifiable Environments: Towards Evaluating Reward Hacking at Scale

Amit Roth, Ankur Samanta, Matan Halevy, Yoav Levine, Yonathan Efroni 2026-05-20
reward-hacking LLM-evaluation alignment agent-benchmark robustness
Key Finding
Instead of detecting reward hacking after the fact by inspecting agent trajectories, you can embed deliberately detectable hack opportunities into evaluation environments. When an agent exploits the planted shortcut, the hack is verified deterministically by design — no human judgment required.
Key Metric
0 manual
Trajectory inspections eliminated. Hack rates are measured automatically and deterministically across environments.
Why This Matters
A paradigm shift from post-hoc forensics to verifiable-by-design evaluation. If the benchmark proves comprehensive, it could become a standard tool for alignment auditing — making reward-hacking measurement as routine as accuracy benchmarks.
Critical Edge
Synthetically planted hack opportunities may not capture the full spectrum of real-world reward hacking. Ecological validity remains an open question — the benchmark risks measuring only the exploits its authors thought to embed, missing emergent or unfamiliar strategies.
Methodology

The authors construct "hack-verifiable" environments on top of TextArena, a text-based game suite. Certain shortcuts or exploits are deliberately planted in each environment with a clear signature. If an agent's trajectory matches the exploit signature, it is unambiguously flagged as reward hacking. This enables controlled, reproducible experiments across models and conditions without requiring any manual annotation or human judgment of trajectories.

Strengths

  • Clean paradigm shift from post hoc analysis to verifiable-by-design evaluation
  • Open-source benchmark lowers the barrier for community adoption
  • Enables automated, deterministic measurement at scale

Limitations

  • Ecological validity of planted hacks vs. organic reward hacking is unproven
  • Dependent on TextArena's diversity and representativeness
  • Abstract-only analysis; full paper may reveal gaps in environment design

Open Questions

  1. Do planted hacks capture the full taxonomy of real-world reward hacking behaviors?
  2. How do hack rates scale with model capability — do stronger models hack more or less?
  3. Can this paradigm extend to tool-use and code-generation settings?
  4. What is the correlation between hack-verifiable exploit rates and real deployment failures?
