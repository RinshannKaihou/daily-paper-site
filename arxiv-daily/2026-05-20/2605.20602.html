Self-Training Doesn't Flatten Language — It Restructures It: Surface Markers Amplify While Deep Syntax Dies

Ming Liu · Amazon 2026-05-20 arXiv: 2605.20602

Iterative self-training restructures language by syntactic depth. Surface markers (discourse connectives, hedges, em-dashes) amplify >2× while deep syntactic structures (questions, passives, subjunctives) collapse ~50–90%. Aggregate complexity metrics paradoxically rise during this collapse, systematically misleading LLM-text detectors.

Key Finding

Language doesn't flatten — it bifurcates by syntactic depth

Self-training decay rate is predicted primarily by structural depth — the number of nested syntactic dependencies a feature requires — not by how common the feature is. The result: a linguistic ecosystem where surface-level richness masks deep structural poverty.

Why This Matters

The Superficial Complexity Paradox

  • ↑ Dependency tree depth +45%
  • ↑ Type-token ratio +10%
  • ↑ Avg word length +25%
  • ↑ Clause embedding +33%
  • ↓ Actual clause-structural diversity −50–90%

Perplexity drops 68% (41.2 → 13.1) — standard diagnostics call this “convergence” while the structural inventory degrades.

⚠
Critical Edge
  • Only tested on small models (≤2.8B params). Whether the depth gradient holds at frontier scale (7B–70B+) is unknown — though correlation strengthens with scale (ρ: 0.373 at 124M → 0.705 at 2.8B).
  • The d=3 depth tier contains a single feature (subjunctive), making cross-clause claims illustrative rather than statistically grounded. Authors show significance holds without d=3.
  • Pure self-training regime — zero human data mixing. Real contamination involves partial synthetic data at varying ratios.
  • Feature detection relies on surface heuristics (regex for passives: ~31% recall, mean F1=0.69). Noise attenuates rather than inflates effects, but limits per-feature interpretability.
