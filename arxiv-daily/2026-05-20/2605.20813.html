PulseCol: Periodically Refreshed Column-Sparse Attention for Accelerating Diffusion Language Models

Yanyi Lyu, Letian Chen, Futing Sun, Miao Zhang, Weili Guan, Liqiang Nie Harbin Institute of Technology (Shenzhen) 2026-05-20 diffusion-language-models sparse-attention inference-acceleration

Replaces coarse block-sparse attention with fine-grained column-sparse attention in diffusion LLMs. Discovers that early denoising steps already exhibit column-wise sparsity—enabling sparsification from step one. With periodic index refresh and an optimized GPU kernel, achieves up to 1.95× end-to-end speedup at 64K context while matching full-attention quality.

Key Finding

Early denoising attention is column-sparse, not block-sparse

Prior work assumed early diffusion steps need full dense attention. PulseCol visualizes LLaDA-1.5 attention maps and finds high-scoring interactions concentrate on a small subset of key columns from step one. Block-sparse methods (SparseD) waste capacity on block boundaries; column-level selection preserves critical interactions with far less computation. This reframes when sparsification can begin—not after warmup, but immediately.

Critical Edge

Only validated on small dLLMs

Results cover LLaDA-1.5 and Dream only. Scaling to 100B+ models or multimodal diffusion architectures is unknown. Refresh hyperparameters (η=30%, R=16/8, group size 32) are fixed—no guidance for new models. No comparison with KV-cache methods (Fast-dLLM, dLLMCache) that attack the same bottleneck differently. The GPU kernel is unreleased and non-trivial to replicate.

Why This Matters

Breaks the “early steps must be dense” assumption

The field believed early denoising needed full attention for quality. PulseCol proves otherwise: column-sparse attention from step one loses only 1.0 points average while cutting compute by 80%. This shifts the design space for all dLLM inference systems. The 22.98-point gap over SparseD at 80% sparsity shows the right sparsity structure matters more than the sparsity ratio. Practical wall-clock gains (not just FLOPs) come from the tiled GPU kernel that avoids materializing full attention matrices.

Three-Stage Pipeline
STEP 1 Initial Pattern Full attention computed once to construct column-sparse indices
STEPS 2 – Twin Periodic Refresh Indices refreshed at R uniformly-spaced points; reused between refreshes
STEPS > Twin Persistent Reuse Last refresh indices fixed and reused for all remaining denoising steps

Assessment
  • Novelty Moderate-to-high
  • Rigor Good
  • Reproducibility Moderate (no code)
  • Significance High for dLLM efficiency
Strengths
  • Clean motivation: explains why block sparsity fails early, not just that it does
  • Column sparsity is a natural fit for observed attention structure
  • Real wall-clock speedups with optimized kernels, not just FLOPs reduction
  • Strong at high sparsity (80%) where prior methods collapse
  • Periodic refresh is simple yet well-justified by ablation
Limitations
  • Only two dLLMs tested; generalization to other architectures is unclear
  • Refresh hyperparameters likely tuned on held-out data; sensitivity underexplored
  • No comparison with KV-cache methods (Fast-dLLM, dLLMCache)
  • Full-attention overhead at refresh steps not analyzed for short sequences
  • Group size 32 is a tuned sweet spot with no guidance for new models
Open Questions
Combination? Can PulseCol stack with KV-cache methods (Fast-dLLM, dLLMCache) for compounding gains?
Scaling? Does column sparsity hold for 100B+ diffusion models or multimodal variants?
Adaptive refresh? Can the refresh schedule respond to attention entropy rather than fixed intervals?
Auto-tuning? What is the optimal group size as a function of sequence length and model dimension?
Schedule sensitivity? How do results change with different denoising step counts or confidence thresholds?
