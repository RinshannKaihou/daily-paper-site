Stimulus Symmetries Can Confound Representational Similarity Analyses

Farhad Pashakhanloo & Jacob A. Zavatone-Veth · Harvard University · 2026-05-20 · tags: representational-similarity-analysis, neural-codes, symmetry, representation-learning, neuroscience

Stimulus symmetries (e.g., rotation) make functionally equivalent neural representations yield qualitatively different RSMs — RSA/CKA can misclassify identical codes as different when gauge transformations are not orthogonal.
Key Metric
r = −0.91 CKA drops from ~1 to ~0.55 as gauge difference increases (p < 0.001)
Why This Matters
Many published RSM comparisons between networks trained on image data — which harbors latent symmetries — may be confounded. The discrepancy reflects gauge, not genuine functional difference.
Critical Edge
All experiments use shallow (two-layer) networks. Practical impact on large-scale deep model comparisons (ResNet-50 vs. ViT on ImageNet) remains unquantified. No concrete fix is proposed.

Methodology

  • ReLU encoder toy model with n=4 neurons tiling a ring S¹; global rotation φ acts as gauge variable
  • General formalism: symmetry groups G acting on latent space Z, proving decoding error is gauge-invariant but RSM is not (unless encoding is an orthogonal linear representation of G)
  • Scaling analysis: RSM variability Δ = tan(π/n)²/n, decaying O(n²−)
  • SGD + weight decay & L1 regularization on two-layer autoencoders — two paths to sparse/collapsed codes with high Δ
  • Extension to Sd−1 with d(d−1)/2 structured off-diagonal clusters
  • Empirical validation: 1000 independently trained autoencoders on rotated Kuzushiji-MNIST; pretrained ResNet, ConvNeXt, DINOv2

Results

  • n=4 ring: off-diagonal RSM element ranges 0 (φ=0) to ½ (φ=π/4) — a functionally irrelevant transformation produces a qualitatively different matrix
  • SGD collapse: 15 neurons → 4 effective dominant directions; RSM drifts without convergence
  • KMNIST: normalized off-diagonal RSM vs. inferred gauge φ shows clear U-shape (r=0.76, p<0.001); reconstruction loss remains flat (r=0.03)
  • Pretrained models (ResNet, ConvNeXt, DINOv2): CKA varies systematically with relative rotation angle
  • Required neuron count n scales exponentially with intrinsic dimensionality of the symmetric manifold

Strengths

  • Elegant theoretical framework cleanly separating functional equivalence from RSM equivalence
  • Rigorous proofs in appendices; closed-form exact results for the toy model
  • Systematic experimental progression: toy → learned codes → higher dimensions → image data
  • Insightful connection to representational drift in neuroscience

Limitations

  • Shallow networks only; gauge dependence in deep architectures with many layers is unknown
  • No concrete fix proposed beyond acknowledging the problem
  • G-invariant metric assumption may not hold for real data distributions
  • Pretrained model experiments show correlation but do not establish functional equivalence under rotation

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does gauge dependence scale with network depth? Does composing layers amplify or attenuate RSM variability?
  2. Can practical symmetry-invariant comparison metrics be constructed without full knowledge of the stimulus distribution?
  3. Do modern equivariant architectures (e.g., equivariant ViTs) fully eliminate this confound, or do residual nonlinearities still cause issues?
  4. How does gauge dependence interact with other known RSA confounds (choice of trial stimuli, dimensionality)?
  5. Is the RSM variability observed in pretrained models correlated with functional differences, or is it purely a gauge artifact?
