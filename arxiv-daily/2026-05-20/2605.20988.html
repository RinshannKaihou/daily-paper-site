Key Finding

A Sharper Picture of Generalization in Transformers

Paul Lintilhac & Sair Shaikh

Transformer generalization on Boolean functions is governed by Fourier spectral sparsity ω and maximum degree Df. A constructive PAC-Bayes method yields a bound of O(ω Df³), and proves chain-of-thought collapses Parity error from exponential to linear in sequence length T.

Why This Matters
  • First constructive PAC-Bayes approach: bounds sharpness of an explicit construction, not just capacity
  • Connects Fourier spectral structure to loss landscape curvature (Hessian trace)
  • Gives a precise complexity-theoretic answer to why expressible ≠ learnable
  • CoT result: exponential → linear error for Parity, a clean mechanistic explanation for reasoning benefits
  • Learned solutions have ~100× lower sharpness and norm than the explicit construction

Vacuous Analytic Bound

Domination Assumption

Core assumption that learned interpolators dominate the construction's norm and sharpness is validated only empirically at small scale.

┌

Narrow Scope

Boolean domains only, all-positive Fourier coefficients, constant degree, ω ≤ T. Far from real-world NLP/CV.

∼

Perturbation Looseness

P(σ) dominates the bound's looseness; isotropic Gaussian noise is overly conservative. Per-matrix variances deferred to future work.

Methodology
  1. Explicit construction — a 1.5-layer, single-head transformer that represents any sparse Boolean function via Fourier-Walsh expansion, with bounded parameter norm and Hessian trace.
  2. PAC-Bayes bound — if a learned interpolator's norm and sharpness are dominated by the construction, it inherits the generalization guarantee (adapted from Alquier 2023).
  3. CoT decomposition — Parity (Df=T) is split into T intermediate degree-2 sub-tasks, collapsing the bound from exponential to linear in T.
Chain-of-Thought Impact

For Parity (the canonical hard Boolean function), intermediate reasoning steps decompose a degree-T problem into T degree-2 problems.
