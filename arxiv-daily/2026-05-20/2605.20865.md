> **一句话总结：** 本文提出NFPO算法，通过引入N步前向迹（forward trace）对PPO代理目标进行似然比校正，在局部近似和精确策略梯度之间建立连续插值，以原理性方式控制偏差-方差权衡，在数学推理基准上一致优于GRPO和DPPO。

> **TL;DR:** The paper identifies that PPO surrogate objectives in RLVR discard the "forward trace" (cumulative future likelihood ratios) from the exact policy-improvement identity, introducing structural bias bounded by O(T²δ²). The proposed NFPO retains only the next N−1 future likelihood ratios, creating a tunable bias-variance trade-off. Theorem 1 proves truncation bias decreases as O((T−N)²δ²) while variance grows as O((1+ε)^{N−1}). With N=4 as a sweet spot, NFPO improves Qwen3-1.7B average accuracy by 16.3% over GRPO and 10.0% over DPPO across 7 math reasoning benchmarks, with gains confirmed at 8B scale.

## 核心贡献

- 揭示了PPO代理目标丢弃精确策略改进等式中的前向迹（forward trace），导致O(T²δ²)的结构性偏差
- 提出N步前向迹，在PPO代理目标和精确策略梯度之间建立连续插值，通过迹长度N控制偏差-方差权衡
- 证明截断偏差以O((T−N)²δ²)递减、方差以O((1+ε)^{N−1})增长，存在最优中间N值
- 提出NFPO算法，将N步前向迹集成到掩码策略梯度框架中，在多个推理基准上一致优于GRPO和DPPO
- 机制分析表明前向迹选择性增强推理token的校正信号，并充当语义低通滤波器抑制token级噪声

## Key Contributions

- Identifies that PPO surrogate objectives in RLVR discard the forward trace Γ_{t+1} (cumulative future likelihood ratios) from the exact policy-improvement identity (Lemma 1), introducing structural bias bounded by O(T²δ²) under a uniform trust region
- Proposes the N-step forward trace that retains only the next N−1 future likelihood ratios after each token, creating a continuous interpolation between the PPO surrogate (N=1) and the exact policy gradient objective (N=T)
- Proves a formal bias-variance trade-off (Theorem 1): truncation bias decreases as O((T−N)²δ²) while variance grows as O((1+ε)^{N−1}), implying an optimal intermediate N exists
- Introduces NFPO, a practical RLVR algorithm integrating the N-step forward trace into the masked policy gradient framework with clipped trace and TV-based token masking
- Demonstrates consistent improvements over GRPO and DPPO across 7 math reasoning benchmarks on both Qwen3-1.7B and Qwen3-8B
- Provides mechanistic analysis showing the forward trace selectively amplifies correction on reasoning tokens (e.g., "suppose", "since", "wait") and acts as a semantic low-pass filter, reducing switch frequency from 20.18% to 10.08%

## Methodology

The paper builds on the exact policy-improvement identity (Lemma 1), which decomposes policy improvement into token-level deviations (ρ_t − 1) weighted by a forward trace Γ_{t+1} = ∏_{j=t+1}^T ρ_j. Standard PPO/GRPO discards this trace (Γ = 1), introducing bias.

The N-step forward trace truncates this product to the next N−1 tokens: Γ_{t+1}^{(N)} = ∏_{j=t+1}^{min(t+N−1,T)} ρ_j. This yields the N-step surrogate objective:

L_μ^{(N)}(π) = E_{y∼μ}[R(y) Σ_t (ρ_t − 1) Γ_{t+1}^{(N)}]

For practical implementation, NFPO:
1. Clips token-level likelihood ratios ρ_{t,β} = clip(ρ_t, 1/β, β) with β=3.0
2. Computes the clipped N-step forward trace Γ̄_{t+1}^{(N)} = clip(∏_{j=t+1}^{min(t+N−1,T)} ρ_{j,β}, 1−ε_low, 1+ε_high)
3. Applies stop-gradient to the forward trace for numerical stability
4. Uses total-variation-based token masking from DPPO to enforce trust region
5. Optimizes the masked objective: L = (1/G) Σ_{i,t} M_t · Â_t^{(i)} · ρ_t^{(i)} · Γ̄_{t+1}^{(N,i)}

Training uses 128 prompts per step, 8 responses per prompt, mini-batch size 256, with MATH level 3−5 for 1.7B and DAPO-Math-17k for 8B models.

## Results & Highlights

- **Qwen3-1.7B-Base**: NFPO achieves 30.7% average accuracy across 7 benchmarks, vs. 26.4% (DPPO, +10.0%) and 26.4% (GRPO, +16.3%). Largest gains on AIME26 (8.1% vs 5.6%) and AMC23 (49.6% vs 42.4%).
- **Qwen3-8B-Base**: NFPO achieves 51.5% average, vs. 50.1% (DPPO) and 46.1% (GRPO). Strong on AIME24 (36.4% vs 34.7%) and AMC23 (82.0% vs 79.1%).
- **Optimal N=4**: Ablation across N∈{1,2,4,8,16} shows N=4 as the sweet spot. All N>1 outperform N=1 (DPPO baseline).
- **Low-pass filtering**: Forward trace halves switch frequency (20.18% → 10.08%), suppressing noisy token-level oscillations while preserving segment-level signals.
- **Reasoning token concentration**: Correction strength on reasoning tokens is 2× higher than average (0.1078 vs 0.0722 for trace-corrected ratio).
- **Robustness**: Performance is robust to clip range [0.6,1.2]−[0.8,1.4] and log-prob recompute choices.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: High.** The paper identifies a previously underappreciated structural bias in PPO-style RLVR objectives and provides a principled correction via truncated forward traces. The theoretical framework connecting local surrogates to exact policy gradients through N-step traces is clean and insightful.
- **Rigor: Strong.** Theorem 1 provides formal bias-variance bounds with complete proofs. The toy Token MDP experiment validates the theory in a controlled setting. The main experiments cover 7 benchmarks with proper evaluation protocol (pass@1 over 32 samples).
- **Reproducibility: Good.** Code is publicly available. Training details (batch sizes, datasets, hyperparameters) are specified. However, exact compute budgets and training duration are not reported in the main paper.
- **Significance: High.** If the gains hold across more models and tasks, this could become a standard improvement to PPO/GRPO-style RLVR training. The insight that PPO discards useful future correction is broadly applicable.
- **Red flags**: (1) Gains over DPPO are modest at 8B scale (~1−3% on most benchmarks), suggesting the advantage may diminish with scale. (2) The paper only evaluates on math reasoning — generalization to coding, science, or other RLVR domains is unknown. (3) The clip range and TV mask choices interact with N in ways not fully disentangled. (4) The N=4 sweet spot is found empirically but the theory does not predict it a priori — choosing N remains a hyperparameter search.

## Strengths

- Clean theoretical framework that precisely characterizes the bias introduced by PPO's local approximation and how forward trace correction addresses it
- The bias-variance trade-off is intuitive, well-formalized, and empirically validated at multiple levels (toy example, ablation, mechanistic analysis)
- Consistent improvements across two model scales and seven diverse reasoning benchmarks
- Mechanistic analysis provides interpretable insights: reasoning token concentration and low-pass filtering effects explain *why* NFPO works, beyond just showing *that* it works
- The method is a modular addition to existing masked policy gradient frameworks, easy to implement

## Limitations

- Evaluation is restricted to mathematical reasoning tasks; it remains unclear whether forward trace correction benefits coding, multilingual, or open-ended generation tasks
- The improvement over DPPO narrows at the 8B scale (e.g., Minerva: 39.0% vs 39.9%), raising questions about scaling behavior
- The optimal trace horizon N=4 is determined empirically; the theory provides qualitative guidance but no closed-form prescription for choosing N
- The paper does not analyze computational overhead of computing the forward trace during training
- No comparison with more recent RLVR methods beyond GRPO and DPPO (e.g., REINFORM, various advantage estimation variants), though a comparison with FIPO is included in the appendix

## Relevance

本文对RLVR训练中PPO代理目标的结构性偏差进行了深入分析，提出的N步前向迹校正方法具有坚实的理论基础和一致的实验验证。对于关注强化学习与大模型推理能力训练的研究者而言，这项工作提供了一个简单且可即插即用的改进方案，值得在自身的RLVR流程中尝试。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does the optimal N scale with response length T? Does N=4 remain optimal for much longer reasoning chains (e.g., 32k+ tokens)?
- Can the forward trace framework be combined with other RLVR improvements (e.g., advantage estimation, entropy regularization) for additive gains?
- Does forward trace correction help in non-reasoning RLVR tasks such as code generation, where the token-level structure differs from mathematical reasoning?
- Is there a principled way to adaptively choose N per-token or per-segment based on local policy deviation, rather than using a fixed global N?
- How does NFPO interact with reward shaping or process reward models, where the per-step reward structure might already capture some of the future-correction signal?
