Sparse atoms inside a vision transformer reveal whether a landing system actually sees the runway.

Mechanistic Interpretability for Learning Assurance of a Vision-Based Landing System

R. Valentin, O. Beyer Bruvik, M. R. Schlichting, M. J. Kochenderfer — Stanford 2026-05-20
Key Metric
AUROC 0.96
Out-of-model-scope detection with only 34 sparse atoms; 81.7% of regression weight on contentful atoms.
Why This Matters
First concrete mechanistic-interpretability tool that directly addresses EASA HF-01/HF-03 — monitoring a neural network's internal situation representation in safety-critical aviation.
Critical Edge
OOMS evaluation is a synthetic binary task (runway present vs. absent). Content reliance ≈82% for both pretrained and scratch models — the metric may not capture what drives generalisation.

Methodology — Four-Stage Pipeline

  1. ViT Keypoint Regressor — DINOv2-S backbone outputs 16×16 patch embeddings (384-d). A soft-argmax head (~1.5k params) maps them to 4 runway keypoint coordinates. Huber loss on LARDv2 with BOGO crop + L/R/C cue in the top-left patch.
  2. Sparse Decomposition — K-SVD learns 512 atoms from importance-sampled patches (top-4 per image by head attention) at sparsity 8. Reconstruction explains >90% variance. Each patch is sparse-coded via Matching Pursuit.
  3. Content vs. Style Classification — Atoms are classified by cross-subset coefficient of variation (CV) across LARDv2's four domains. Low CV = contentful (runway structure); high CV = stylistic (domain appearance). Median-CV split; no tuned threshold. ~82% of effective regression weight falls on contentful atoms.
  4. OOMS Detection — Constrained logistic regression on binary atom-activation vectors (bias ≤ 0, β ≥ 0, L1 regularisation). Classifies inputs as in-model-scope vs. out-of-model-scope. Default: flag OOMS.

Results & Highlights

  • Regression: 1.91 px median error (±0.03), 4.39 px MAE (±0.14) on LARDv2 test — pretrained model.
  • Content reliance: 81.7% ±5.4% of effective regression score on contentful atoms (pretrained); 79.8% ±4.4% (scratch) — indistinguishable.
  • OOMS AUROC: 0.96 ±0.01 with 34 ±6 selected atoms; 78% ±8% of those are contentful (vs. 50% random baseline).
  • Dictionary quality: >90% variance explained at sparsity 8 from 512 atoms.
  • Contentful atoms consistently activate on runway structure (edges, threshold markings, horizon); stylistic atoms fire on domain appearance (rendering artifacts, color palettes).

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Directly addresses a regulatory gap (EASA HF-01/HF-03)
  • Elegant minimal SAM head forces interpretability into embeddings
  • Cross-subset invariance is label-free and qualitatively meaningful
  • OOMS concept complementary to ODD/OOD
  • Multi-seed evaluation with variance reporting

Limitations

  • OOMS tested on synthetic binary task only
  • Content/style split depends on LARDv2 domain structure
  • No per-atom causal ablation
  • Single architecture and task demonstrated
  • K-SVD dictionary is offline; model updates require refit

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does OOMS compare to established OOD methods (Mahalanobis, energy-based, ensemble disagreement) on the same data split?
  2. Would per-atom ablation (zeroing individual contentful atoms) causally degrade regression, or is the content-reliance score merely correlational?
  3. Can cross-subset invariance generalise to settings where multiple visual domains are unavailable (e.g., real flight data only)?
  4. How stable is the dictionary across retraining seeds — are the same semantic atoms recovered each time?
  5. Could SAEs replace K-SVD in this pipeline, and would the resulting atoms be more or less interpretable for certification?
