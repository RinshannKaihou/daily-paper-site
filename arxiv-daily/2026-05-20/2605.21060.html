Divide et Calibra: Multiclass Local Calibration via Vector Quantization

Compositional calibration maps built from shared codeword-dependent factors — enabling reliable uncertainty estimation in sparse, low-density regions of the latent space.

Barbera, Perini, De Toni, Passerini, Pugnana calibration vector-quantization uncertainty-estimation
Key Finding
By partitioning the latent space into Voronoi cells via vector quantization and composing region-specific calibration maps from shared codeword-dependent factors (an indexed parameterization trick), the method reduces Local Calibration Error by 25–60% over the strongest local baselines — without sacrificing global calibration.
The indexed parameterization trick reuses VQ discrete indices as selectors into sender/receiver codebooks A and B, collapsing parameter complexity from O(|Y|²·|C|w) to O(2·|C|·|Y| + w). This enables compositional generalization across 6464 possible regions with only ~8K parameters.

Why This Matters
Standard calibration methods average errors globally — hiding catastrophic mis-calibration in minority subpopulations.
In medical diagnosis, autonomous driving, and selective classification, per-region reliability is the quantity that actually determines safe deployment. This work directly tackles the sparse-data regime where existing methods collapse, offering frequency-weighted convergence guarantees tied to codeword usage rather than region density.
Critical Edge
  • Requires a frozen encoder — calibration quality is bounded by latent space geometry
  • Does not uniformly beat global methods on ECCE or NLL
  • Experiments limited to image classification; no NLP or large-scale evaluation
  • diag(σ²) = I simplification removes the slot-dependent scaling the theory describes
Methodology
Stage 1 — VQ Partition

Latent vector z = E(x) is split into w slots. A shared codebook maps each slot to its nearest codeword, yielding a discrete index sequence s that induces a Voronoi tessellation.

Stage 2 — Compositional Calibration

Index sequence s selects rows from sender codebook B and receiver codebook A. Combined via bilinear form: α(V) = ϕ(A(V)ᵀ diag(σ²) B(V)), producing a Dirichlet-calibrated posterior.
