LOSCAR-SGD: Local SGD with Communication-Computation Overlap and Delay-Corrected Sparse Model Averaging

Maziane, Mahran, Maranjyan, Richtárik — KAUST 2026-05-20 distributed-training communication-efficiency local-sgd

Key Finding

LOSCAR-SGD is the first method to unify three communication-saving techniques — local training, sparse model averaging, and communication-computation overlap — in a single algorithm with convergence guarantees for smooth non-convex objectives. A delay-corrected merge rule preserves local optimization progress during communication instead of discarding it.

Pre-comm: Ni local steps
Overlap: sparse sync + Qi steps
Merge

Each round splits into preparation, overlapped communication+compute, and a delay-corrected merge. Workers with slower hardware naturally perform fewer steps — heterogeneity is handled natively.

Key Metric

p = 0.001 sparsity ratio

Aggressive sparsification communicates only 0.1% of model coordinates while maintaining competitive accuracy. Delay correction consistently beats naive overwriting across LIBSVM, CIFAR-10, and Tiny ImageNet.

  • Long-compute / short-delay: small persistent gain
  • Short-compute / long-delay (ζ=48): large gain
  • Highly heterogeneous workers: moderate gain

Why This Matters

Communication overhead is the dominant bottleneck in large-scale distributed training. LOSCAR-SGD gives principled, theoretically grounded guidance on combining three widely-deployed techniques that were previously analyzed only in isolation or in pairs.

Wall-clock complexity (Corollary 4.1) explicitly accounts for heterogeneous worker speeds — going beyond round complexity to reflect real system behavior.

Critical Edge

The convergence theory covers only the data-homogeneous setting. A label-Dirichlet (α=0.03) stress test reveals aggressive overlap actively hurts performance when data is strongly non-i.i.d.:

Negative result: With M=1, ζ=96, p=0.01 under non-i.i.d. data, overlap methods plateau below the blocking baseline — the method can fail in the federated setting it ostensibly targets.

  • No large-scale LLM or modern architecture experiments
  • Tiny ImageNet shows little separation between merge strategies
  • No comparison with fully asynchronous SGD methods

Methodology

1 Worker i runs Ni = Mτ/τi local SGD steps, producing iterate yir
2 Workers send sparse messages via shared Rand-K mask Sr; server averages them
3 During communication, worker i takes Qi = ζ/τi extra steps, reaching zir
4 Delay-corrected merge: xi,jr+1 = mjr + (zi,jr − yi,jr) for j ∈ Sr — keeps overlap progress

Theorem 4.1: Overlap staleness appears as a controlled O(η3) higher-order term, negligible for small step sizes.

Results & Highlights

  • Clear ordering: Overlap delay-corrected > Overlap overwrite > Local Sparse across accuracy and gradient norm
  • Delay-corrected overlap shows the strongest invariance to local computation budget M (curves nearly collapse across M∈{1,4,16,64})
  • Larger communication delay ζ makes overlap more valuable — at ζ=48 delay correction has visibly larger advantage
  • CIFAR-10 & Tiny ImageNet: overlap improves over blocking in both normal and communication-stress regimes
  • Processed-example plots confirm extra local steps are useful optimization, not just timing artifact

Authors & Credibility

Peter Richtárik (KAUST) is a leading researcher in distributed optimization and federated learning. The paper builds directly on prior work by the same group (Maranjyan et al., 2025; Mahran et al., 2026).

Credibility strengthened by honest inclusion of the negative non-i.i.d. stress test and thorough ablations.

Open Questions

  • Can delay correction extend to data-heterogeneous (federated) settings with client-drift control (FedProx, gradient correction)?
  • Performance at truly large scale (hundreds of GPUs training transformers)?
  • Replace random mask Sr with structured or learned sparsity?
  • Combine with gradient quantization for further savings?

TL;DR

LOSCAR-SGD combines local SGD steps, sparse coordinate-wise model averaging, and communication-computation overlap in a single method for distributed learning with heterogeneous workers. The key innovation is a delay-corrected merge rule that preserves local optimization progress made during communication instead of naively overwriting with stale averages. This is the first convergence theory covering all three techniques together, with experiments on LIBSVM, CIFAR-10, and Tiny ImageNet confirming that overlap reduces wall-clock training time and delay correction consistently outperforms naive overwriting. Limitations include data-homogeneous-only theory and no large-scale validation.
