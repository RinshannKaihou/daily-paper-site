Key Finding

From Circuit Evidence to Mechanistic Theory: An Inductive Logic Approach

Nura Aljaafari, Danilo S. Carvalho, Andre Freitas — 2026-05-20

Circuit interpretation is not just pattern discovery — it is inductive theory construction. Causal Functional Signatures + ILP-learned architectural signatures make mechanistic claims explicit, formally comparable via θ-subsumption, and portable across model scales & architectures.

Key Metric

ILP-learned architectural signatures achieve substantially better structural separation than graph-kernel and feature-vector baselines when distinguishing circuit types.

Why This Matters

Most circuit-level findings are isolated, ad-hoc, and incomparable. This framework provides a shared formal language for cumulative mechanistic science — the missing infrastructure layer.

Critical Edge

ILP can be computationally expensive at scale. Practical usability for non-ILP researchers is unproven. Empirical breadth (how many models/tasks/scales) remains unclear from the abstract alone.

Methodology

A dual-level characterization framework:

  • Causal Functional Signatures (CFS) — ground-level component behavior via causal attribution evidence and token role profiles. Captures what a circuit computes.
  • Architectural Signatures (τarch) — higher-level structural characterization learned via Inductive Logic Programming (ILP) from scale-invariant structural predicates. Enables cross-scale & cross-architecture transfer.
  • Together they form a formal coherence layer: claims become explicit, comparable (θ-subsumption), and portable.
  • Reveals qualitatively distinct computational strategies: attention-mediated copying vs. MLP-mediated binding.

Results & Highlights

  • CFS reveals qualitatively distinct computational strategies across task types.
  • ILP signatures outperform graph-kernel and feature-vector baselines on structural separation.
  • Signatures support principled transfer across model scales and architecture families.
  • The framework enables cumulative mechanistic science via shared formal representations.

Strengths

  • Addresses a fundamental meta-scientific gap — lack of cumulative, comparable findings in mechanistic interpretability.
  • Grounded in established formal methods (inductive logic, θ-subsumption) rather than ad-hoc heuristics.
  • Dual-level approach cleanly separates functional from structural characterization.
  • Scale-invariance and cross-architecture transfer are highly desirable properties.

Limitations

  • Empirical breadth (number of models, tasks, scales tested) cannot be assessed from the abstract.
  • ILP approaches can be computationally expensive; scalability to very large models is unclear.
  • Practical usability for researchers unfamiliar with ILP may be a barrier.
  • Handling of partially overlapping or hierarchical circuits is not addressed.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does CFS scale to circuits in very large language models (e.g., 70B+ parameters)?
  • What specific structural predicates does the ILP system use, and how sensitive are results to predicate choice?
  • Can the framework handle nested or hierarchical circuits where sub-circuits interact in complex ways?
  • How does θ-subsumption comparison relate to existing representational similarity methods (CKA, SVCCA)?
  • What is the computational cost of learning ILP signatures for circuits with hundreds of components?
