Where Do Self-Supervised Speech Models Become Unfair?

arXiv: 2604.18249

Date: 2026-04-21

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.18249

一句话总结： 本文首次对自监督语音模型进行逐层公平性分析，发现说话人识别(SID)和自动语音识别(ASR)任务在层级偏置模式上呈现截然相反的趋势——SID偏置在性能最优的浅层最小，而ASR偏置在性能最优的深层最大，且这种偏置在预训练阶段就已固化，微调难以消除。
TL;DR: This paper presents the first layerwise fairness analysis of pretrained self-supervised speech models (S3Ms), revealing that SID bias is lowest at layers with best SID performance, while ASR bias is highest at layers with best ASR performance. The bias is baked in during pretraining and persists even after ASR finetuning with fairness-enhancing algorithms.

核心贡献

  • 首次对自监督语音模型(S3Ms)进行逐层公平性探针分析，在每个嵌入层上分别训练轻量级线性解码器，测量说话人识别(SID)和自动语音识别(ASR)的总体误差和各说话人群体(SG)的相对误差。
  • 发现SID和ASR任务在层级偏置模式上完全相反：SID偏置与总体误差成正比（低误差层偏置也低），而ASR偏置与总体误差成反比（低误差层偏置反而最高）。
  • 证明SG级别的偏置在预训练阶段就已经根深蒂固：即使使用领域增强/对抗训练(DET/DAT)进行ASR微调，也无法有效消除预训练中已形成的偏置模式。
  • 偏置从第一个自注意力层就存在：所有测试的S3M在最浅层就已对特定说话人群体产生差异化表示。
  • 跨模型、跨语言的普适性：多语言模型(XLS-R)、仅法语模型(W2V2-FR)和纯英语模型展现出相似的非母语说话人偏置模式，表明偏置可能源于数据集内在的建模挑战。

Key Contributions

  • First layerwise fairness analysis of pretrained S3Ms, probing each embedding layer with lightweight linear decoders for both SID and ASR to measure overall error and per-speaker-group relative error rates.
  • Discovery of opposing bias-performance relationships for SID vs ASR: SID bias scales directly with SID error (best layers are fairest), while ASR bias scales inversely with ASR error (best layers are most biased).
  • Evidence that SG-level bias is established during pretraining: ASR finetuning, even with fairness-enhancing domain-enhancing/adversarial training (DET/DAT), has negligible effect on the bias patterns already present in pretrained models.
  • Bias originates at the very first self-attention layer across all tested S3Ms, indicating deep-rooted unfairness.
  • Cross-model and cross-lingual generality: Multilingual (XLS-R), French-only (W2V2-FR), and English-only S3Ms exhibit similar non-native speaker bias patterns, suggesting inherent dataset-level modeling challenges.

Methodology

The authors train lightweight linear probes (single linear layers, following the SUPERB framework) at every layer of each pretrained S3M for two tasks: speaker identification (SID) and automatic speech recognition (ASR). The use of simple linear decoders is deliberate -- any observed bias must originate from the pretrained representations rather than being learned by the decoder itself.

Models studied include WavLM-base+, WavLM-large, BEST-RQ-large, wav2vec 2.0 large variants (LibriSpeech, LibriVox, XLS-R, French 7K), and Whisper-medium as a non-SSL baseline. All are approximately 300M parameters.

Evaluation datasets are the Sonos Voice Control Bias Assessment Dataset (166 hours, 1038 speakers) and Meta's Fair-speech corpus (56 hours, 593 speakers), both annotated with demographic metadata including gender, age, native language, dialect/ethnicity, and socioeconomic background.

Bias metrics: The authors define relative error rate (Err_rel) for each speaker group as the difference between group-average and population-average error, and DV-bias as the mean absolute relative error across all groups within a demographic variable. All experiments are replicated five times for robustness.

Finetuning experiments: S3Ms are finetuned for ASR on CommonVoice 16.0 with vanilla CTC and CTC + DET + DAT (fairness-enhancing), followed by the same layerwise probing to assess whether finetuning changes bias patterns.

Results & Highlights

  • SID bias-performance relationship is direct: Layers with lowest SID error rate (typically early layers) also have lowest SG-level bias. As SID performance degrades in deeper layers, bias increases. Statistically significant positive Pearson correlation (p < 0.05) between SID error rate and SID bias across all S3Ms.
  • ASR bias-performance relationship is inverse: Layers with lowest WER (typically later layers) have the highest SG-level bias. Early layers with poor ASR performance are the most fair. Statistically significant negative Pearson correlation (p < 0.05) between WER and ASR bias across all S3Ms.
  • Most disadvantaged groups: Non-native speakers and children suffer the most ASR bias; children and women suffer the most SID bias. Negligible gender-level bias for ASR.
  • Finetuning does not fix bias: Both vanilla CTC finetuning and CTC + DET + DAT finetuning improve overall ASR performance but have negligible effect on SG-level bias compared to pretrained models.
  • Multilingual and non-English models show similar bias patterns: XLS-R (53 languages) and W2V2-FR-7K (French-only) exhibit the same non-native speaker bias trends as English-only models.

Authors & Credibility

The paper is authored by Felix Herron, Maja Hjuler, Solange Rossato, Alexandre Allauzen, and François Portet, affiliated with MILES Team at Université Paris Dauphine-PSL, GETALP Team at Université Grenoble Alpes, and Queensland University of Technology. The authors have prior publications on speech fairness (including prior work on DET/DAT). The code is based on SpeechBrain, a widely-used speech processing toolkit. The methodology is grounded in the established SUPERB probing framework.

Critical Assessment

This paper makes a genuinely novel contribution by being the first to systematically probe fairness at every layer of pretrained speech models. The key finding -- that ASR performance and fairness are inversely related across layers -- is both surprising and consequential. It suggests that the features S3Ms learn to improve ASR come at the expense of equitable treatment across speaker groups.

However, several limitations deserve attention. First, the use of linear probes, while principled for attributing bias to pretrained representations, may understate or mischaracterize the bias that manifests with more powerful (nonlinear) decoders used in practice. The authors acknowledge this but dismiss it somewhat quickly. Second, the study is limited to English evaluation datasets (Sonos, Fair-speech) even when probing multilingual and French-only models, leaving open whether the same bias patterns hold across languages. Third, the paper identifies correlation between layerwise performance and bias but does not establish causation -- it remains unclear what specific pretraining mechanisms or data properties drive the observed patterns. Fourth, the fairness-enhancing baseline (DET/DAT) tested in finetuning is only one of many possible approaches; other methods (e.g., data balancing, prompt-based approaches) might fare differently.

Strengths

  • First-of-its-kind layerwise fairness analysis filling a clear gap in the literature.
  • Clean experimental design: linear probes ensure observed bias comes from pretrained representations.
  • Surprising and impactful finding that ASR performance and fairness trade off across layers.
  • Comprehensive model coverage spanning different architectures, sizes, pretraining data, and languages.
  • Replicated experiments (5 runs) and statistical significance tests (Pearson correlation) support robustness of claims.

Limitations

  • Linear probes may not capture the full picture of bias that emerges with deeper downstream decoders.
  • Evaluation is limited to English test sets even for multilingual and non-English models.
  • Only one fairness-enhancing finetuning method (DET/DAT) is tested; broader comparison would strengthen claims about finetuning futility.
  • Correlational analysis without mechanistic explanation of what drives the bias patterns.
  • The paper does not propose concrete solutions for fairer pretraining despite motivating the need for it.

Relevance

Questions for Further Exploration

  • What specific pretraining objectives or data distributions causally drive the inverse relationship between ASR performance and fairness across layers?
  • Would nonlinear probes (e.g., small MLPs or attention-based decoders) reveal different layerwise bias patterns, or is the linear probe approximation sufficient?
  • Do the same layerwise bias patterns hold when evaluating on non-English languages, or are they artifacts of the English-centric evaluation datasets?
  • Can pretraining-time interventions (e.g., balanced sampling, contrastive objectives that are fairness-aware) prevent the early-layer bias that this paper identifies?
  • What acoustic or linguistic properties of disadvantaged speaker groups (e.g., dialect density, speaking rate, pitch range) correlate with the observed layerwise bias, and can these be used as more precise predictors of model unfairness?
