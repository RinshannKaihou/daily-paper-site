TL;DR: This paper formulates LLM failure analysis as contrastive attribution using LRP (Layer-wise Relevance Propagation), attributing the logit difference between incorrect and correct output tokens to input tokens and internal model states. It introduces an efficient extension for building cross-layer attribution graphs on long-context inputs and conducts a systematic empirical study across benchmarks, model sizes, and training checkpoints, finding that token-level contrastive attribution yields informative signals in some failure cases but is not universally applicable.

Key Contributions

  • Formulates LLM failure analysis as contrastive attribution: attributing the logit difference between an incorrect output token and a correct alternative to input tokens and internal model states using LRP-based propagation, providing fine-grained causal explanations for model failures.
  • Introduces an efficient extension for constructing cross-layer attribution graphs on long-context inputs, addressing the computational scalability challenge of traditional LRP methods for realistic long-sequence inputs.
  • Conducts a systematic empirical study across multiple benchmarks, comparing attribution patterns across datasets, model sizes, and training checkpoints, providing empirical evidence on how attribution signals vary under different conditions.
  • Provides an honest assessment of both utility and limitations of token-level contrastive attribution, demonstrating that while the method yields informative signals in some failure cases, it is not universally applicable, thereby advancing practical understanding of interpretability tool boundaries.

Methodology

The paper builds on Layer-wise Relevance Propagation (LRP), adapting it for the contrastive attribution setting to analyze LLM failures on realistic benchmarks.

Core technical framework:

  1. Contrastive Attribution Formulation: Given an input that produces an incorrect output token, the method computes the logit difference between the incorrect token and a known correct alternative token, then propagates this difference backward through the network using LRP to assign attribution scores to each input token and internal neuron.
  1. Efficient Cross-Layer Attribution Graphs: To handle long-context inputs common in realistic benchmarks, the paper introduces computational optimizations that enable the construction of attribution graphs spanning multiple transformer layers without the prohibitive memory/compute costs of naive LRP application.
  1. Experimental Design: The study evaluates contrastive attribution across multiple dimensions: (a) different benchmark datasets covering various task types; (b) models of varying sizes to study scaling effects on attribution patterns; (c) different training checkpoints to examine how attribution evolves during training.
  1. Analysis Protocol: For each failure case, the paper examines where attribution concentrates (input tokens, specific layers/heads), whether attribution patterns align with human intuition about the failure cause, and how consistently attribution signals appear across similar failure types.

Results & Highlights

  • Contrastive attribution yields meaningful signals in some cases: The method successfully identifies input tokens and internal states that contribute to specific failures, with attribution patterns that are interpretable and align with plausible failure explanations.
  • Non-universal applicability: A key finding is that token-level contrastive attribution does not consistently produce informative explanations across all failure types. Some failure cases yield noisy or uninformative attribution patterns, highlighting the method's boundaries.
  • Cross-dataset variation: Attribution patterns differ meaningfully across benchmarks, suggesting that the nature of the task and input characteristics influence the reliability and interpretability of contrastive attribution signals.
  • Scaling and training dynamics: The study reveals how attribution patterns change with model size and across training checkpoints, providing insights into when and why models fail during the training process.
  • Practical guidance: The results offer concrete guidance for practitioners on when contrastive attribution is a useful diagnostic tool versus when other interpretability approaches may be needed.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty (Moderate): The idea of using contrastive explanations with attribution methods is not entirely new -- contrastive explanations have been explored in vision models and earlier NLP work. The main novelty lies in: (a) systematically applying and evaluating contrastive LRP-based attribution on realistic LLM benchmarks (rather than toy settings), and (b) the efficient extension for long-context inputs. However, the formulation itself is a relatively straightforward adaptation of existing LRP methodology.
  • Rigor (Moderate-High): The empirical study is systematic, covering multiple datasets, model sizes, and training checkpoints. The paper provides quantitative evaluations of attribution quality. However, the assessment of whether attribution signals are "meaningful" relies partly on qualitative judgment, and the paper lacks a formal theoretical framework for predicting when the method will or will not work.
  • Reproducibility (High): Code is publicly available at https://aka.ms/Debug-XAI. The paper describes the experimental setup across 16 tables of results, providing sufficient detail for replication.
  • Significance (Moderate): The paper's most significant contribution may be its honest negative finding -- that contrastive attribution is not universally applicable. This is valuable for the interpretability community as it tempers expectations about attribution methods and motivates the development of complementary approaches. However, the paper does not propose a fundamentally new method that overcomes the identified limitations.
  • Red flags: (1) The paper's title promises "in the wild" analysis but the benchmarks, while more realistic than toy settings, are still standard evaluation benchmarks rather than truly deployed production systems. (2) The lack of a principled criterion for when the method works versus fails means the results are largely descriptive rather than predictive. (3) The comparison across conditions (datasets, model sizes, checkpoints) is broad but may lack the depth needed to draw strong causal conclusions about what drives the observed differences.

Strengths

  • Honest and balanced reporting: The paper's willingness to report both successes and failures of the contrastive attribution approach is refreshing and scientifically valuable. Many interpretability papers oversell their methods; this one does not.
  • Systematic evaluation scope: Covering multiple benchmarks, model sizes, and training checkpoints provides a comprehensive picture of the method's behavior that goes beyond anecdotal case studies.
  • Practical relevance: By focusing on realistic benchmarks rather than toy settings, the paper provides findings that are directly relevant to practitioners working with LLMs in production.
  • Code availability: Public code release supports reproducibility and enables other researchers to build on the work.
  • Long-context handling: The efficient extension for long inputs addresses a real practical bottleneck and makes the method applicable to realistic use cases.
  • Clear formulation: The mathematical formulation of contrastive attribution via logit differences is clean and well-motivated.

Limitations

  • Descriptive rather than predictive: The paper identifies cases where contrastive attribution works and does not work, but does not develop a theory or heuristic for predicting applicability a priori. Practitioners must still run the analysis to discover if it will be informative.
  • Limited methodological innovation: The core technique is an adaptation of existing LRP methodology to a contrastive setting. The efficient long-context extension is useful but incremental.
  • Qualitative assessment reliance: Evaluating whether attribution signals are "meaningful" or "informative" involves subjective judgment. The paper would benefit from more rigorous automated evaluation metrics.
  • Benchmark scope: While more realistic than toy settings, the benchmarks used are standard evaluation datasets. The analysis of truly "wild" failures -- production deployment errors, adversarial attacks, or distribution shift scenarios -- remains future work.
  • Single attribution method: The study focuses exclusively on LRP-based attribution. Comparing with other attribution methods (gradient-based, attention-based, probing-based) would strengthen the conclusions about the general utility of contrastive attribution versus the specific limitations of LRP.
  • Limited failure type taxonomy: The paper does not develop a systematic taxonomy of LLM failure types, which would help organize the findings about when contrastive attribution is most and least useful.
