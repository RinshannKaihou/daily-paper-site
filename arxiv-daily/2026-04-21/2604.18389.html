Understanding the Prompt Sensitivity

arXiv: 2604.18389

Date: 2026-04-21

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.18389

一句话总结

本文通过一阶泰勒展开和柯西-不等式推导出LLM提示敏感性的理论上界，揭示大模型对相似输入在隐藏空间中呈现分散而非聚类行为，且提示模板对logit的影响大于问题本身。

TL;DR

This paper provides a theoretical and empirical investigation of why LLMs are sensitive to prompt wording. Using first-order Taylor expansion and Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, the authors derive upper bounds on log-probability differences caused by prompt variations. They find that LLMs disperse semantically similar inputs in hidden state space (unlike smaller networks such as ResNet that cluster them), misaligned prompt tokens cause more sensitivity than modified tokens, and prompt templates exert greater influence on logits than the questions themselves. Activation steering experiments confirm the causal role of hidden state dispersion in prompt sensitivity.

核心贡献

  1. 理论框架：将LLM视为多元函数，利用一阶泰勒展开和柯西-不等式推导出提示变化导致log概率差异的理论上界，上界与PromptSensiScore (PSS)在同一模型系列内呈正相关。
  2. 隐藏状态分散现象：发现LLM对语义相似的输入在隐藏空间中呈现分散行为，而非像ResNet等较小网络那样聚类，这是提示敏感性的几何解释。
  3. 提示组件影响分析：系统性地证明了提示模板（template）对logit的影响大于问题（question）本身，且错位（misalignment）比修改（modification）造成更大的敏感性。
  4. 因果验证：通过在不同Transformer层深度（L/4, L/2, 3L/4）进行激活导向（activation steering）实验，因果性地验证了隐藏状态差异在提示敏感性中的作用。

Key Contributions

  1. Theoretical Framework: Treats LLMs as multivariate functions and applies first-order Taylor expansion with Cauchy-Schwarz inequality to derive upper bounds on log-probability differences from prompt variations. These upper bounds are positively correlated with PromptSensiScore (PSS) within the same model series.
  1. Hidden State Dispersion Phenomenon: Discovers that LLMs disperse semantically similar inputs in hidden state space rather than clustering them -- a striking contrast to smaller networks like ResNet-101 which exhibit strong clustering behavior. This provides a geometric explanation for prompt sensitivity.
  1. Prompt Component Influence Analysis: Systematically demonstrates that prompt templates exert greater influence on output logits than the questions themselves. Misalignment causes more sensitivity than modification, and among modification types, typos cause the most sensitivity, followed by orthographic changes, then paraphrases.
  1. Causal Validation via Activation Steering: Conducts activation steering experiments at different Transformer layer depths (L/4, L/2, 3L/4) to causally confirm the role of hidden state differences (||Delta h||) in mediating prompt sensitivity, showing that steering at intermediate layers is most effective.

Methodology

The paper addresses four research questions through a combination of theoretical derivation and empirical experimentation:

RQ1: Hidden State Geometry -- The authors compare how LLMs and smaller neural networks (ResNet-101 on CIFAR-10) represent semantically similar inputs in hidden state space. They compute pairwise cosine similarities between hidden states of meaning-preserving prompt variants across 12 templates from Zhuo et al. (2024).

RQ2: Prompt Modification Types -- They create 4 custom templates that introduce three types of meaning-preserving modifications (typo, orthographic, paraphrase) and one misalignment condition. They measure sensitivity using the PromptSensiScore (PSS) metric, which captures how model outputs change across prompt variants.

RQ3: Theoretical Upper Bounds -- The core theoretical contribution treats an LLM as a function f mapping prompt representations to log-probability distributions. Using first-order Taylor expansion around a reference prompt:

  • f(x') - f(x) ~ nabla f(x)^T (x' - x)
  • By Cauchy-Schwarz: |f(x') - f(x)| <= ||nabla f(x)|| * ||x' - x||
  • The upper bound decomposes into a model-dependent term (||nabla f(x)||) and a prompt-dependent term (||Delta x||)

They verify that these upper bounds correlate with empirical PSS scores within the same model series (e.g., LLaMA family).

RQ4: Template vs. Question Influence -- They perform logit variance analysis by decomposing logit variation into contributions from prompt templates versus questions, using ANOVA-style decomposition across multiple datasets.

Activation Steering Experiments -- To establish causality, they perform activation steering by adding scaled difference vectors (derived from prompt variant pairs) to hidden states at specific Transformer layers (L/4, L/2, 3L/4), measuring how this intervention reduces prompt sensitivity.

Models tested: 11 models including Qwen1.5-4B, LLaMA-2-7B, LLaMA-2-13B, LLaMA-3-8B, Mistral-7B, and others.

Datasets: ARC Challenge, MMLU, OpenBookQA, CommonSenseQA.

Results & Highlights

  • RQ1 -- Dispersion vs. Clustering: LLMs show significantly lower pairwise cosine similarities between hidden states of meaning-preserving variants compared to ResNet-101. While ResNet clusters inputs from the same class tightly (high cosine similarity), LLMs spread similar prompts across a wider region of hidden state space. This dispersion increases with model size within the LLaMA family.
  • RQ2 -- Sensitivity Ranking by Modification Type: Misalignment causes the highest sensitivity, followed by typo > orthographic > paraphrase among modification types. This suggests that tokens with different semantics (misalignment) are more disruptive than tokens with the same semantics in different surface forms.
  • RQ3 -- Upper Bound Validity: The Cauchy-Schwarz upper bounds are positively correlated with empirical PSS within the same model series (e.g., LLaMA-2-7B, LLaMA-2-13B, LLaMA-3-8B). The correlation holds across all four datasets. However, the bounds do not generalize well across different model families.
  • RQ4 -- Template Dominance: Logit variance analysis reveals that prompt templates account for a larger proportion of logit variance than questions across all datasets and models. This indicates that how a question is framed (template) matters more than what is asked (question content) for model output stability.
  • Activation Steering: Steering at L/2 (middle layers) is most effective at reducing prompt sensitivity, suggesting that intermediate Transformer layers play a critical role in mediating prompt sensitivity. Steering at L/4 and 3L/4 also reduces sensitivity but to a lesser degree.

Critical Assessment

The paper makes a commendable effort to bridge theoretical analysis (Taylor expansion, Cauchy-Schwarz bounds) with empirical validation across multiple models and datasets. However, several limitations merit consideration:

  1. Upper bound tightness: The Cauchy-Schwarz upper bounds correlate with PSS within model families but are likely loose bounds -- the paper does not quantify how tight these bounds are in absolute terms, only that they correlate directionally. A loose upper bound has limited predictive utility.
  1. Generalization across model families: The positive correlation between upper bounds and PSS does not hold across different model families (e.g., comparing LLaMA with Mistral or Qwen), which significantly limits the practical applicability of the theoretical framework.
  1. First-order approximation limitation: The first-order Taylor expansion is only accurate when prompt variations are small. For larger prompt modifications (which are common in practice), higher-order terms may dominate, and the analysis does not address this regime.
  1. Limited model scale range: The models tested range from 4B to 13B parameters. The trends observed may not extrapolate to much larger models (70B+, frontier models), where scaling behaviors could differ qualitatively.
  1. Dataset scope: All four datasets are multiple-choice QA benchmarks. It is unclear whether the findings generalize to generation tasks, code tasks, or longer-context scenarios where prompt sensitivity may manifest differently.
  1. Activation steering interpretation: While activation steering reduces sensitivity, the paper does not fully disentangle whether this is because it reduces harmful dispersion or simply constrains the model toward a specific output distribution regardless of input quality.

Strengths

  • Novel theoretical framing of prompt sensitivity through Taylor expansion and Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, providing formal grounding for an otherwise empirical phenomenon.
  • Creative comparison with ResNet-101 to highlight LLM-specific hidden state behavior (dispersion vs. clustering).
  • Comprehensive experimental design: 11 models, 4 datasets, multiple modification types, and activation steering for causal validation.
  • Open-source code and detailed appendix with all prompt templates and experimental details, supporting reproducibility.
  • Clear research question structure (RQ1-RQ4) makes the paper easy to follow despite its technical depth.

Limitations

  • The Cauchy-Schwarz upper bounds do not generalize across model families, limiting practical applicability of the theoretical contribution.
  • First-order Taylor expansion may not capture the full nonlinear dynamics of prompt variations in LLMs.
  • Evaluation is restricted to multiple-choice QA benchmarks; generalization to open-ended generation tasks is untested.
  • The model scale range (4B-13B) leaves open questions about behavior at larger scales.
  • The paper does not propose concrete methods to mitigate prompt sensitivity beyond the diagnostic activation steering experiments, which are primarily a validation tool rather than a practical solution.

  1. Can the activation steering approach be developed into a practical inference-time technique for reducing prompt sensitivity in deployed systems?

  1. Could the theoretical framework be extended to incorporate higher-order Taylor terms for larger prompt modifications, and would this improve cross-model-family generalization?
