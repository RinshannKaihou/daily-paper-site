> **一句话总结：** 本文提出了一种因子化线性投影（FLiP）模型，通过从预训练的多模态多语言句子嵌入中恢复词汇内容（准确率超过75%），揭示了SONAR、LaBSE和Gemini等编码器的模态偏差和语言偏差，为嵌入空间的可解释性提供了诊断工具。

> **TL;DR:** FLiP is a factorized log-linear model that extracts lexical keywords from pretrained sentence embeddings (SONAR, LaBSE, Gemini) with over 75% accuracy, revealing modality and language biases in embedding spaces and significantly outperforming prior baselines like SpLiCE.

## 核心贡献

- 提出因子化线性投影（FLiP）模型，将嵌入解释任务转化为线性关键词提取任务，证明语义概念在嵌入空间中呈线性表示
- FLiP 能够从句子嵌入中恢复超过 75% 的词汇内容，显著优于非因子化的基线方法（约 59%）和 SpLiCE（约 29%）
- 利用 FLiP 作为诊断工具，系统性地揭示了 SONAR、LaBSE 和 Gemini 三种编码器的模态对齐能力和语言偏差
- 发现 SONAR 嵌入空间中英语是线性概念表示的首选语言，且对于语言差异较大的语言对（如 EN-TA），嵌入空间对齐质量显著下降
- 提出了跨模态和跨语言的训练方案，通过混合系数 alpha 平衡文本和语音（或两种语言）的贡献

## Key Contributions

- Introduces the Factorized Linear Projection (FLiP) model that decomposes W = AB, where A is a word embedding matrix and B is a modality-to-latent projection, framing embedding interpretation as a linear keyword extraction task
- Demonstrates that a well-trained FLiP can recall 75-80% of lexical content from sentence embeddings, proving that semantic concepts are linearly represented in the embedding space
- Uses FLiP as a diagnostic tool to systematically analyze modality alignment, language alignment, and concept-language effects across SONAR, LaBSE, and Gemini embedding spaces
- Reveals that SONAR exhibits strong English-centric bias in its linear semantic structure, and that cross-lingual alignment degrades for linguistically distant language pairs
- Shows FLiP achieves approximately twice the span-aware accuracy of SpLiCE (58.83% vs. 28.21% on speech embeddings), establishing it as a superior interpretability tool

## Methodology

FLiP formulates embedding interpretation as keyword extraction via linear projection. The model learns a projection matrix W and bias b that map a sentence embedding to a probability distribution over a vocabulary V via softmax. The key innovation is factorizing W = AB, where A (|V| x r) is a word embedding matrix and B (r x d) is the modality-to-latent projection. This factorization imposes implicit regularization that significantly boosts performance even at full rank.

The model supports three training schemes: (1) text-only, where text embeddings are projected to vocabulary; (2) cross-modal, where both speech and text embeddings are trained jointly with a mixing coefficient alpha; and (3) cross-lingual, where text embeddings from two languages are combined. The objective maximizes regularized log-likelihood, with L1 regularization on A for sparsity via proximal gradient descent.

At inference, keyword extraction requires only a single linear projection: z = b + A(Bu), followed by selecting the top-k entries. The bias vector b learns a log-prior over vocabulary, enabling stop-word compensation for named entity recall.

The authors evaluate on SONAR (1024-dim, 200+ languages), LaBSE (768-dim, 109 languages), and Gemini Embedding (768-dim, API-based), using Mozilla Common Voice for cross-modal experiments and Europarl/Samanantar for cross-lingual experiments, with vocabulary sizes of up to 100K unigrams.

## Results & Highlights

- **Factorization matters:** Full-rank FLiP achieves 77.29% on text vs. 59.45% for non-factorized LiP -- a 18-point gap attributable to implicit regularization from factorization. Even rank-512 FLiP (76.77%) closely matches full-rank performance.
- **Cross-modal alignment:** SONAR shows good speech-text alignment within a single language. Training on speech transfers well to text and vice versa, with speech-trained models showing slightly better cross-modal transferability (Jaccard indices around 87-90% for EN).
- **English-centric bias:** SONAR consistently favors English for linear concept representation. Training on EN yields good accuracy on DE (67.51%) and FR (65.18%), but the reverse direction shows a significant gap for the non-EN language.
- **Language distance effect:** For linguistically distant pairs (EN-BN, EN-HI, EN-TA, EN-TE), cross-lingual alignment degrades substantially. EN-TA shows only 48.70% accuracy when using EN vocabulary.
- **SpLiCE comparison:** FLiP achieves 61.45% and 58.83% span-aware accuracy on SONAR text and speech embeddings respectively, nearly doubling SpLiCE's 29.58% and 28.21%.
- **Named entity recall:** Strict entity recall increases monotonically with k. Removing the bias term improves entity recall because the bias up-weights frequent stop words that displace named entity terms.
- **Vocabulary language effect:** Cross-lingual models perform better with EN vocabulary, especially for dissimilar language pairs. SONAR shows a pronounced proclivity for representing concepts in English.

## Authors & Credibility

The authors are all from Speech@FIT at Brno University of Technology, Czechia -- a well-established speech processing research group with strong publication records in speech recognition, speaker verification, and embedding models. Santosh Kesiraju, the lead author, has prior work on speech embeddings and multilingual systems. The group has contributed to SONAR and related multilingual speech-text systems. The code is publicly available at https://github.com/BUTSpeechFIT/FLiP, enhancing reproducibility. The paper is currently under review.

## Critical Assessment

The paper makes a solid contribution to understanding sentence embedding spaces through a simple yet effective linear probing approach. The factorization insight -- that W = AB with implicit regularization outperforms a direct linear probe by ~18 points -- is practically valuable, though the authors acknowledge this is primarily due to regularization rather than expressiveness. The English-centric bias findings for SONAR are interesting but perhaps not surprising given the predominance of English in training data. The comparison with SpLiCE is somewhat limited by using only a 10K vocabulary for that specific experiment, while other experiments use 100K. The paper does not explore whether the extracted keywords correspond to semantically meaningful concepts beyond surface-level lexical matching, leaving open the question of whether linear decoding captures deeper semantic structure. Additionally, the evaluation is primarily intrinsic; the paper does not demonstrate how FLiP-based diagnostics could directly improve downstream model training or selection.

## Strengths

- Clean, well-motivated methodology that is simple to implement and computationally efficient
- Comprehensive analysis across three different embedding architectures (SONAR, LaBSE, Gemini)
- Novel diagnostic perspective that goes beyond conventional downstream benchmarks
- Strong empirical results with clear ablations on factorization rank, modality alignment, and language alignment
- Public code release for reproducibility
- Multi-metric evaluation (accuracy, span-aware accuracy, Jaccard index, named entity recall)

## Limitations

- Evaluation relies solely on intrinsic metrics; no demonstration of downstream utility for the diagnostic insights
- Vocabulary is limited to unigrams and bigrams; semantic concepts beyond lexical level are not captured
- The paper covers only high- and mid-resource languages, leaving low-resource settings unexplored
- Named entity analysis relies on automatic tagging with Gemini-2.5-Flash-Lite, introducing potential annotation noise
- The approach cannot distinguish between semantically equivalent words in different contexts (polysemy is not addressed)
- Limited exploration of why factorization provides such large gains beyond the implicit regularization argument

本文与隐藏状态可解释性研究高度相关：FLiP 通过线性探测揭示了预训练句子嵌入中词汇信息的编码方式，为理解模型内部表示的线性结构假设提供了实证支持，其因子化投影方法也可启发对 Transformer 隐藏状态的类似分析。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does FLiP performance correlate with downstream task performance (e.g., retrieval accuracy, translation quality)?
- Can the FLiP diagnostic insights be used to guide or improve the training of sentence encoders?
- What happens when FLiP is applied to layer-wise hidden states of the encoder rather than the final pooled embedding?
- Could a nonlinear probe (e.g., a small MLP) recover additional lexical or semantic content that FLiP misses?
- How does the English-centric bias manifest in the attention patterns or intermediate representations of the encoder?
- Is the implicit regularization benefit of factorization specific to the log-linear formulation, or does it generalize to other probing architectures?
