> **一句话总结：** SIREN 通过 L1 正则化线性探测识别 LLM 内部层中的安全神经元，结合自适应层加权聚合构建轻量级有害内容检测器，无需修改底层模型权重即可实现流式、即插即用的安全监控，在多个基准上超越 LlamaGuard3 和 Qwen3Guard。

> **TL;DR:** SIREN identifies safety-critical neurons across all transformer layers via L1-regularized linear probing, aggregates them with adaptive layer-weighted scoring (weighted by per-layer F1), and achieves state-of-the-art harmful content detection that is streaming-compatible, plug-and-play, and requires no modification to the underlying LLM.

## 核心贡献

- 提出 SIREN（Safety Internal REpresentation Neurons）框架，通过 L1 正则化线性探测在 LLM 所有 transformer 层中识别与安全性最相关的神经元，无需修改模型权重
- 设计自适应层加权聚合策略（adaptive layer-weighted aggregation），按每层验证 F1 分数（alpha_l = (f_l - f_min)/(f_max - f_min)）对各层安全神经元激活进行加权，自动发现最有利于有害内容检测的层
- 实现流式检测（streaming detection）：通过前缀级别均值池化（prefix-level mean pooling）对安全神经元激活进行监控，实现 token-by-token 的零样本有害内容检测
- 提出跨模型集成（cross-model ensemble）方案，通过堆叠泛化（stacked generalization）融合不同骨干 LLM 的 SIREN 预测，进一步提升检测鲁棒性
- 在 ToxicChat、OpenAIModeration、Aegis、Aegis2.0、WildGuard、SafeRLHF、BeaverTails 等八个基准上全面超越 LlamaGuard3（1B/8B）和 Qwen3Guard（0.6B/4B）

## Key Contributions

- Introduces SIREN, a lightweight guard model framework that detects harmful content by probing internal LLM representations, requiring no changes to model weights or architecture
- Develops an adaptive layer-weighted aggregation mechanism that automatically discovers which transformer layers carry the strongest safety signals, weighting each layer's contribution by its validation F1 score
- Enables streaming zero-shot detection via prefix-level mean pooling of safety neuron activations, allowing real-time token-by-token monitoring of harmful content during generation
- Proposes cross-model ensembling via stacked generalization, combining SIREN predictions from different backbone LLMs for improved robustness
- Achieves state-of-the-art results across eight safety benchmarks, outperforming dedicated guard models (LlamaGuard3, Qwen3Guard) while being significantly more parameter-efficient

## Methodology

**Overview:** SIREN treats an existing LLM as a frozen feature extractor. For each transformer layer, it trains L1-regularized logistic regression probes on the hidden states to identify neurons whose activations are most predictive of harmful vs. harmless content. These "safety neurons" are then aggregated across layers for classification.

**Safety Neuron Identification (Section 3.2):**
- For each layer l, hidden state representations H_l are extracted for all training prompts
- An L1-regularized linear probe (logistic regression) is trained to classify harmful vs. harmless
- The L1 penalty induces sparsity, selecting a small subset of neurons most relevant to safety
- Probing is performed on every transformer layer independently, rather than only the final layer

**Adaptive Layer-Weighted Aggregation (Section 3.3):**
- Each layer l receives a weight alpha_l proportional to its validation F1 score: alpha_l = (f_l - f_min) / (f_max - f_min)
- Layers with higher discriminative power for harmfulness detection contribute more to the final prediction
- The weighted safety neuron activations are aggregated and fed to a meta-classifier

**Streaming Detection:**
- Safety neuron activations are monitored at the prefix level during token generation
- Mean pooling over token positions within each prefix yields a fixed-dimensional safety signal
- This enables real-time detection without waiting for full sequence completion

**Cross-Model Ensemble:**
- SIREN instances trained on different backbone LLMs (e.g., Llama-3.2-1B and Qwen-3.0-0.6B) are combined
- Stacked generalization trains a meta-learner on top of individual SIREN predictions
- This leverages complementary safety representations across model families

**Backbone Models:** Llama-3.2-1B, Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen-3.0-0.6B, Qwen-3.0-4B

## Results & Highlights

**Main Results (F1 scores across benchmarks):**
- SIREN consistently outperforms LlamaGuard3-1B, LlamaGuard3-8B, Qwen3Guard-0.6B, and Qwen3Guard-4B on the majority of benchmarks
- On Aegis and Aegis2.0 (diverse harm taxonomies), SIREN shows especially strong performance
- On WildGuard and SafeRLHF, SIREN maintains high detection rates with lower false positive rates compared to dedicated guard models

**Generalization (Think benchmark):**
- SIREN generalizes to novel harm categories not seen during training, demonstrating that the identified safety neurons capture broadly applicable safety representations

**Efficiency:**
- SIREN adds negligible overhead to the base LLM: only a sparse linear probe needs to be evaluated
- Compared to running a separate 1B-8B parameter guard model, SIREN is far more parameter-efficient
- FLOPs analysis shows SIREN requires orders of magnitude less compute than standalone guard models

**Streaming Evaluation:**
- Streaming SIREN maintains competitive detection quality compared to full-sequence SIREN
- Detection accuracy improves monotonically with prefix length, enabling early detection of harmful content

**Plug-and-Play on Guard Models:**
- SIREN probes can be applied to existing guard models (e.g., LlamaGuard3) to further boost their detection capability, demonstrating the generality of the approach

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** The idea of probing internal LLM representations for safety properties builds on the linear representation hypothesis and prior work in representation engineering. The key novelty lies in the systematic identification of sparse safety neurons across all layers (rather than only the final layer) and the adaptive layer-weighted aggregation that automatically balances layer contributions. The streaming detection extension and cross-model ensembling are practical contributions.

**Rigor:** The evaluation is extensive, covering 8 benchmarks, 4 backbone models, and comparisons with 4 dedicated guard models. Ablation studies examine the contribution of individual components (layer selection, neuron sparsity, aggregation strategy). The FLOPs analysis provides concrete efficiency comparisons. However, the paper relies on L1-regularized probes which may not capture non-linear safety representations, and the approach is evaluated primarily on English content.

**Reproducibility:** The method is described with sufficient algorithmic detail. Training hyperparameters for probes are provided in Appendix A. The framework is conceptually simple enough to reproduce, though exact reproduction requires access to the specific training data splits and model checkpoints.

**Significance:** The central claim -- that safety information is linearly encoded in sparse sets of neurons across multiple transformer layers -- has important implications for AI safety. If validated, it suggests that efficient safety monitoring can be built directly into LLM inference without additional heavy models. The plug-and-play nature of SIREN makes it immediately deployable.

**Red flags:**
- The approach relies on the linear representation hypothesis holding for safety concepts; non-linear safety features may be missed
- L1 regularization selects neurons based on linear separability, which may not capture interaction effects between neurons
- The training data for probes could introduce biases in which neurons are identified as "safety-critical"
- Evaluation is primarily on English benchmarks; cross-lingual generalization is not addressed

## Strengths

- Extremely parameter-efficient: only requires sparse linear probes on top of existing LLM hidden states
- No modification to underlying LLM weights, preserving model capabilities
- Streaming-compatible design enables real-time safety monitoring during generation
- Strong empirical results across diverse benchmarks and backbone models
- Cross-model ensembling provides a principled way to combine safety signals from different model families
- Plug-and-play design allows application to any transformer-based LLM
- The layer-wise probing analysis provides valuable insight into where safety information resides in transformer architectures

## Limitations

- Relies on linear probes, potentially missing non-linear safety representations
- Performance depends on the quality and coverage of training data for probe training
- Not evaluated on multilingual or code-mixed content
- The approach requires access to internal model states, which may not be available for closed-source APIs
- Cross-model ensemble adds complexity and requires running multiple backbone LLMs
- The relationship between identified safety neurons and actual model behavior (causal rather than correlational) is not fully established
- Streaming detection accuracy degrades for very short prefixes where insufficient context is available

## Relevance

本文直接面向 LLM 安全性检测问题，利用模型内部隐含状态中的线性安全表征实现轻量级有害内容检测，与 LLM 可解释性、隐含状态分析和安全对齐研究高度相关。其关于安全信息在多层神经元中稀疏分布的发现，为理解 LLM 安全机制和构建高效安全监控系统提供了重要启示。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How do the identified safety neurons relate to mechanistic interpretations of safety circuits in LLMs -- are they causally involved in safety-related computations or merely correlated with safety labels?
- Can safety neurons identified via L1 probing be used for activation steering or safety-focused fine-tuning, and would this improve or degrade model behavior?
- How stable are the identified safety neurons across different training data compositions and different random seeds for probe training?
- Does the linear representation hypothesis hold for more nuanced safety categories (e.g., subtle manipulation, multi-turn persuasion) beyond the binary harmful/harmless distinction?
- Can SIREN be extended to multilingual settings, and do safety neurons exhibit cross-lingual transfer?
- How does the approach perform on adversarial inputs specifically designed to evade safety neuron detection?
