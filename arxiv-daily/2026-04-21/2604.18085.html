Predicting LLM Compression Degradation from Spectral Statistics

arXiv: 2604.18085

Date: 2026-04-21

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.18085

一句话总结

本文提出利用权重矩阵的稳定秩（stable rank）与压缩率的交互项 γ·ρ_s 来预测大语言模型在低秩压缩后的精度退化，在注意力层实现了 LOO-CV Pearson R=0.890 的高预测精度，支持"先预测再压缩"的实用工作流。

TL;DR

This paper introduces a spectral-statistics-based predictor for LLM accuracy degradation under low-rank compression. The interaction term γ·ρ_s (compression ratio times stable rank) achieves LOO-CV Pearson R=0.890 for attention layers and R=0.839 for MLP layers across Qwen3 and Gemma3 model families, four compression methods (Vanilla SVD, two ASVD variants, SVD-LLM), and six benchmarks, enabling a predict-then-compress workflow without needing to actually compress and evaluate the model first.

核心贡献

  • 提出基于谱统计量的压缩退化预测框架，仅需压缩率 γ 和权重矩阵稳定秩 ρ_s 即可预测精度下降幅度，无需实际执行压缩与评估
  • 发现交互项 γ·ρ_s 是比单独使用压缩率或稳定秩更强的预测因子，并通过符号回归（遗传编程）在 42 个可解释公式模板中验证了其最优性
  • 揭示了注意力层与 MLP 层在可预测性上的不对称性：注意力层（张量收缩误差组成）比 MLP 层（Hadamard 乘积误差组成）更容易预测，这一现象有明确的理论解释
  • 系统分析了困惑度（perplexity）与下游任务精度之间的相关性，发现不同基准测试间差异显著（r=0.20 至 r=0.77），质疑了以困惑度作为唯一压缩质量指标的通用做法
  • 引入基于 BF16 分解的 Minimum Description Length（MDL）信息密度度量（bits-per-parameter），为压缩容限提供了信息论视角的补充解释

Key Contributions

  • Introduces a spectral-statistics-based degradation prediction framework requiring only the compression ratio γ and weight stable rank ρ_s, eliminating the need for actual compression and evaluation
  • Identifies the interaction term γ·ρ_s as a superior predictor over either variable alone, validated through symbolic regression (genetic programming) across 42 interpretable formula templates in 10 categories
  • Reveals and theoretically explains the attention-vs-MLP predictability asymmetry: attention layers (tensor contraction error composition) are more predictable than MLP layers (Hadamard product error composition via SwiGLU)
  • Provides a systematic analysis of perplexity-accuracy correlation across benchmarks, showing substantial variation (r=0.20 on BoolQ to r=0.77 on HellaSwag), challenging the use of perplexity as a universal compression quality metric
  • Introduces Minimum Description Length (MDL) information density measured via BF16 decomposition in bits-per-parameter, offering an information-theoretic complement to spectral analysis for understanding compression tolerance

Methodology

Stable Rank as Spectral Concentration Measure: The stable rank ρ_s(W) = ||W||_F^2 / ||W||_2^2 captures how concentrated a weight matrix's singular value spectrum is. A low stable rank indicates that most spectral energy is concentrated in few singular values, suggesting the matrix is inherently low-rank and should tolerate aggressive compression with minimal degradation. A high stable rank implies dispersed spectral energy and greater sensitivity to truncation.

Compression Ratio γ: Defined as γ = N_comp/N, the fraction of parameters retained after compression. Combined with stable rank, the interaction term γ·ρ_s captures how the effective information bandwidth of a layer interacts with the compression budget.

Symbolic Regression for Formula Discovery: The authors use gplearn (genetic programming-based symbolic regression) to search over 42 interpretable formula templates across 10 functional categories (linear, power, logarithmic, exponential, polynomial, rational, composite, etc.) to find the best predictor. The γ·ρ_s interaction term consistently emerges as the top predictor.

Leave-One-Out Cross-Validation (LOO-CV): To avoid overfitting to any single model-method-benchmark combination, the authors evaluate predictive performance using LOO-CV across the full experimental grid of model families, compression methods, and benchmarks.

Information Density via MDL: As a complementary perspective, the paper measures information density of weight matrices using Minimum Description Length in bits-per-parameter, estimated through BF16 decomposition. This quantifies how much information each parameter encodes, providing an information-theoretic view of compression tolerance.

Experimental Setup: Tested on Qwen3 (0.6B, 1.7B, 4B, 8B) and Gemma3 (1B, 4B, 12B, 27B) model families with four compression methods: Vanilla SVD, ASVD (activation-aware, two variants), and SVD-LLM. Evaluation on six benchmarks: BoolQ, PIQA, HellaSwag, WinoGrande, ARC-Easy, and ARC-Challenge.

Results & Highlights

  • Predictive Accuracy: The γ·ρ_s interaction term achieves LOO-CV Pearson R=0.890 for attention layers and R=0.839 for MLP layers, demonstrating strong predictive power across diverse model families and compression methods.
  • Attention vs. MLP Asymmetry: Attention layers are consistently more predictable than MLP layers. This is theoretically grounded: attention computes tensor contractions (batched matrix multiplications), where Frobenius-norm error bounds propagate linearly. MLP layers with SwiGLU activations involve Hadamard (element-wise) products of compressed matrices, leading to more complex error interactions that reduce predictability.
  • Perplexity-Accuracy Correlation Varies by Benchmark: The correlation between perplexity degradation and accuracy degradation ranges from r=0.20 (BoolQ) to r=0.77 (HellaSwag). Benchmarks using sequence-level scoring (e.g., HellaSwag, PIQA) show stronger perplexity-accuracy correlation than those using single-token scoring (e.g., BoolQ). This has important practical implications: using perplexity as the sole metric to evaluate compression quality can be misleading for certain downstream tasks.
  • Symbolic Regression Confirmation: Among 42 formula templates, the simple multiplicative interaction γ·ρ_s outperforms more complex alternatives, suggesting a genuinely parsimonious relationship between spectral concentration and compression sensitivity.
  • Cross-Method and Cross-Model Generalization: The predictor generalizes well across all four compression methods (including activation-aware methods like ASVD and SVD-LLM) and both model families (Qwen3 and Gemma3), indicating the finding is not an artifact of any specific compression algorithm.

Authors & Credibility

The paper is authored by Mingxue (Mercy) Xu from Imperial College London. The work is presented as a single-author contribution, which is notable given the breadth of the experimental evaluation (two model families, four compression methods, six benchmarks, symbolic regression search). The methodology is methodologically sound, particularly the use of LOO-CV to avoid overfitting claims and the combination of empirical discovery (symbolic regression) with theoretical explanation (error composition analysis). The systematic approach to testing generalization across methods and models strengthens confidence in the findings.

Critical Assessment

The paper makes a compelling and practically useful contribution by identifying a simple, interpretable predictor for compression degradation. The use of LOO-CV is a genuine methodological strength that guards against overfitting to specific experimental configurations. The theoretical explanation for the attention-vs-MLP asymmetry is elegant and well-grounded.

However, several limitations warrant consideration. First, the study restricts itself to SVD-based compression methods; whether the γ·ρ_s predictor transfers to quantization, pruning, or knowledge distillation remains an open question. Second, the model families tested (Qwen3 and Gemma3) are both relatively modern Transformer architectures; it is unclear whether the findings extend to architectures with different attention mechanisms (e.g., multi-query attention, grouped-query attention in varying configurations) or non-Transformer architectures. Third, the single-author nature of the work, while impressive, means the experimental scale has natural limits — only two model families are tested, and the largest models (27B parameters) may not fully represent the behavior of frontier-scale models (70B+).

The perplexity-accuracy analysis is valuable but could benefit from a deeper investigation into why certain benchmarks show weak correlation. The authors hint at sequence-level vs. single-token scoring as the explanation, but this deserves more rigorous treatment with controlled experiments. Additionally, the MDL information density measure is introduced but feels somewhat underexplored — its relationship to stable rank and its incremental predictive value over γ·ρ_s alone could be more clearly articulated.

Overall, this is a solid, well-executed study with a clear and actionable finding. The predict-then-compress workflow it enables could meaningfully reduce the computational cost of model compression research and deployment.

Strengths

  • Simple, interpretable predictor (γ·ρ_s) that is easy to compute and apply in practice
  • Rigorous evaluation methodology using LOO-CV across a substantial experimental grid
  • Strong generalization across compression methods and model families
  • Theoretically grounded explanation for the attention-vs-MLP predictability asymmetry
  • Practical impact: enables predict-then-compress workflow that can save significant computational resources
  • Valuable analysis of perplexity as a compression quality metric, with practical implications

Limitations

  • Limited to SVD-based compression methods; generalization to quantization, pruning, or other compression paradigms is untested
  • Only two model families (Qwen3, Gemma3) evaluated; transfer to other architectures or frontier-scale models (70B+) is unknown
  • MDL information density measure is introduced but its incremental value over the spectral predictor is not fully demonstrated
  • The relationship between benchmark design (sequence vs. single-token scoring) and perplexity-accuracy correlation deserves deeper controlled investigation
  • Single-author work with naturally limited experimental scale compared to large-team efforts

Relevance

本研究与模型压缩和精度分析高度相关。稳定秩作为谱集中度的度量，为理解大模型权重矩阵的"可压缩性"提供了直观且可计算的理论工具。交互项 γ·ρ_s 的发现表明压缩退化可以在不实际压缩模型的情况下被可靠预测，这对高效部署大语言模型具有直接实用价值。此外，困惑度与下游任务精度之间相关性因基准测试设计而异的发现，提醒从业者在压缩评估中不应仅依赖困惑度这一单一指标。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does the γ·ρ_s predictor generalize to non-SVD compression methods such as unstructured pruning, structured pruning, or mixed-precision quantization?
  1. How does the predictor perform on frontier-scale models (70B+ parameters) or models with fundamentally different architectures (e.g., Mamba, RWKV, or hybrid architectures)?
  1. Can the stable rank be used as an online diagnostic during training to identify layers that are converging toward low-rank structure and thus candidates for early compression?
  1. What is the optimal layer-wise compression allocation strategy when using γ·ρ_s to guide per-layer compression budgets?
  1. How does the MDL information density measure complement or conflict with the spectral predictor, and can a combined metric achieve even higher predictive accuracy?
  1. For benchmarks where perplexity-accuracy correlation is weak (e.g., BoolQ at r=0.20), what alternative proxy metrics could better predict downstream task degradation after compression?
