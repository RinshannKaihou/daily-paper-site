> **一句话总结：** 本文将随机种子稳定性形式化为(ε,δ)浓度条件，证明子袋装(subbagging)可对任意有界输出的回归算法保证种子稳定性，并提出自适应交叉袋装(adaptive cross-bagging)算法同时消除去偏机器学习(如AIPW)中来自算法训练和样本划分两方面的种子依赖，在AIPW仿真中达到r(δ̂,δ)=1.04接近目标，而无袋装的2折交叉拟合r=90.5。

> **TL;DR:** Formalizes random seed stability as an (ε,δ) concentration condition (Definition 1) and proves (Theorem 1, via Bernstein's inequality) that subbagging with V sufficiently large guarantees seed (ε,δ)-stability for any regression algorithm bounded in [0,1]; extends this to scalar debiased-ML estimators via a Lipschitz corollary (Corollary 1) and proposes adaptive cross-bagging that simultaneously eliminates seed dependence from both nuisance estimation and sample splitting. In simulation (Schader et al. 2024 DGP, n=100, AIPW with random forest 500 trees), adaptive cross-bagging achieves r(δ̂_0.01, 0.01)=1.04 vs. 90.5 for 2-fold cross-fitting without averaging, 79.12 for 10-fold, and 31.52 for LOO; it reaches (0.01,0.01)-stability in ~20s on an Apple M3 Max vs. ~10 min for the LOO-averaging alternative (roughly 20× slower).

## 核心贡献

- 将**随机种子稳定性**形式化为(ε,δ)浓度条件（Definition 1）：算法在同一数据上用不同种子训练，预测差异超过ε的概率不超过δ，与经典算法稳定性概念平行但扰动源是种子而非训练数据。
- 证明**子袋装(subbagging)保证任意有界输出回归算法的种子稳定性**（Theorem 1）：基于Bernstein不等式，给出V的充分下界 V ≥ max[ν²(x;D,ρ)·log(2k/δ) + (4/3)log(2k/δ)·ε] / ε²，其中ν²可用Popoviciu上界1/4替代得到最坏情况可计算上界。
- 将结果扩展到**标量去偏机器学习估计量**（Corollary 1）：当Ψ关于η Lipschitz连续（常数L）时，给出V的充分条件，适用于AIPW/TMLE/DML等估计器。
- 提出**自适应交叉袋装(adaptive cross-bagging)算法**：通过OOB(out-of-bag)预测池化同时消除算法种子依赖和样本划分随机性，并用Theorem 2的渐近正态性驱动自适应增袋策略，回收已有预测以降低计算开销。

## Key Contributions

- Formal definition of random seed (ε,δ)-stability (Definition 1) mirroring classical algorithmic stability but with the perturbation applied to the seed rather than the training data, plus Lemma 1 reducing stability to controlling point-wise prediction variance σ²(x;D_n) over seeds.
- Theorem 1 proving that subbagging (m=⌊ρn⌋ subsamples without replacement, V independent seed-and-bag pairs) makes any regression algorithm bounded in [0,1] seed (ε,δ)-stable, with an explicit V lower bound derived from Bernstein's inequality and a worst-case computable bound via Popoviciu's inequality (ν²≤1/4).
- Corollary 1 extending stability to scalar debiased-ML estimators Ψ(D_n,η) that are Lipschitz in η (constant L w.r.t. ‖·‖_∞), giving V ≥ max[ν_l²(x_i;D_n,ρ)·log(2np/δ) + (4/3)log(2np/δ)·(ε/L)] / (ε/L)²; holds for AIPW ATE when c<π_i<1−c.
- Adaptive cross-bagging algorithm that pools OOB nuisance predictions across V† bags (each observation OOB at least V times), eliminating both seed and sample-splitting variability, with Theorem 2 establishing V^{1/2}(Ψ(D_n,η̄_ζ)−Ψ(D_n,η̄_ζ')) → N(0,2τ²) to drive adaptive V selection.

## Methodology

**Stability definition (Definition 1).** A regression algorithm 𝔉 is random seed (ε,δ)-stable on training data D_n and test set T if pr_{s,s'}(max_{x∈T} |𝔉(D_n,s)(x) − 𝔉(D_n,s')(x)| ≥ ε) ≤ δ. Lemma 1 shows this reduces to bounding the point-wise prediction variance σ²(x;D_n): via Chebyshev/Markov, stability holds when σ²(x;D_n) ≤ ε²δ/(2|T|).

**Subbagging (Section 3).** Fix ρ∈(0,1), m=⌊ρn⌋; draw V independent (seed, m-size subsample) pairs from the product measure P_S ⊗ P_B. The subbagged prediction is the average over V bags. Theorem 1 (Bernstein's inequality) gives the sufficient V lower bound; because the V bags are independent, the variance of the subbagged average is ν²(x;D_n,ρ)/V. Popoviciu's inequality (y∈[0,1] ⟹ ν²≤1/4) yields a worst-case computable bound.

**Debiased ML extension (Section 4).** For a scalar estimator Ψ(D_n,η) (e.g., AIPW ATE with η={π,μ_1,μ_0}), Corollary 1 requires Ψ Lipschitz in η (holds for AIPW when propensity c<π_i<1−c). The cross-bagging algorithm: (1) fix ρ and choose V per Corollary 1; (2) draw m=⌊ρn⌋ subsamples until each observation is OOB ≥V times (total V† subsamples); (3) train each nuisance model on each bag; (4) pool OOB predictions per observation; (5) compute Ψ(D_n,η̄_ζ). Theorem 2 gives the asymptotic normality V^{1/2}(Ψ(D_n,η̄_ζ)−Ψ(D_n,η̄_ζ')) → N(0,2τ²), enabling an adaptive scheme that increments V while recycling previous predictions.

**Numerical experiments (Section 5).** Simulation 1: DGP with binary Y and 20 features (generated via Claude Opus 4.6), n=100, single test observation, 1000 seeds; single-layer neural network (20 neurons) with and without subbagging (ρ=2/3, V=320 chosen for (0.1,0.1)-stability under worst-case ν²=1/4). Simulation 2: Schader et al. (2024) DGP (4 confounders, binary A, binary Y), n=100, 1000 seeds, AIPW ATE with random forest (500 trees); comparators: 2-fold/10-fold/LOO cross-fitting, each with and without 80-seed final-estimate averaging, vs. adaptive cross-bagging (ρ=1/2, target (ε,δ)=(0.01,0.01)). Hardware: Apple M3 Max, 36 GB RAM. Code at https://github.com/nt-williams/bagging-random-seed-stability. All simulations in R.

## Results & Highlights

- **Simulation 1 (neural network, seed (0.1,0.1)-stability):** Subbagged NN (V=320) achieves r(δ̂_0.1, 0.1)=0 (conservative, i.e., zero empirical violations), whereas the non-bagged NN yields r(δ̂_0.1, 0.1)=7.5 — i.e., the empirical instability is 7.5× the tolerated δ level.
- **Simulation 2 (AIPW ATE, seed (0.01,0.01)-stability, without averaging):** 2-fold cross-fitting r=90.5, 10-fold r=79.12, LOO r=31.52 — all far exceeding the target r≤1, showing increased folds alone do not control seed stability.
- **Simulation 2 (AIPW ATE, with 80-seed final-estimate averaging):** 2-fold r=90.35, 10-fold r=78.33, LOO r=0 — only LOO+averaging meets the target, but at ~10 min runtime.
- **Adaptive cross-bagging:** r(δ̂_0.01, 0.01)=1.04 (near the target) with a median of 396 bags selected, in ~20 s on an Apple M3 Max — roughly 20× faster than the LOO+averaging alternative that also achieves stability.
- **Worst-case V bound is conservative but computable:** V=320 was chosen using the Popoviciu worst-case ν²=1/4 for (0.1,0.1)-stability; the adaptive scheme uses the estimated variance to avoid the worst-case over-allocation.

## Critical Assessment

- **The sufficient V bound (Theorem 1 / Corollary 1) relies on the worst-case Popoviciu variance ν²≤1/4, which is conservative.** The authors acknowledge this: V=320 for (0.1,0.1)-stability yields r=0 (overly conservative), and the adaptive scheme is introduced precisely because the worst-case bound "may be prohibitively large." Practitioners without the adaptive machinery are left with either an over-large fixed V or no computable guarantee if the true ν² is unknown.
- **The Lipschitz condition for Corollary 1 is restrictive in practice.** It trivially holds for AIPW only when propensities are bounded away from 0 and 1 (c<π_i<1−c); for TMLE/DML estimators with more complex nuisance maps or unbounded outcomes, the condition requires case-by-case verification, limiting the generality of the debiased-ML extension.
- **Experiments are extremely small-scale.** Both simulations use a single dataset with n=100 and a single test observation (Simulation 1) or a single ATE estimate (Simulation 2), drawn from one DGP each. There is no variance estimate over datasets/DGPs, so it is unclear whether r=1.04 for adaptive cross-bagging is a stable result or a single-draw artifact, and whether the method scales to the n=10⁴–10⁶ regimes common in applied causal ML.
- **The [0,1] bounded-output assumption excludes common regression settings.** Real-valued outcomes (continuous treatment effects, continuous biomarkers) require a transformation or extension not provided; the theory as stated does not directly cover unbounded regression.
- **No comparison to alternative stabilization methods beyond averaging/folds.** Other approaches (e.g., deterministic training, snapshot ensembles, variance-reduced training) are not evaluated, so the relative advantage of cross-bagging is established only against two specific baselines.

## Strengths

- Provides the first formal (ε,δ) definition of random seed stability and a theorem-level guarantee (Theorem 1) that subbagging enforces it for any bounded regression algorithm — a genuine theoretical contribution over the prior purely-Monte-Carlo literature.
- The cross-bagging algorithm is principled: it unifies nuisance stabilization and sample-splitting elimination through OOB pooling, and Theorem 2's asymptotic normality justifies the adaptive V-incrementing scheme.
- Honest about limitations: explicitly states the worst-case bound is conservative, the Lipschitz condition is required, and the adaptive scheme recycles predictions to control cost.
- Code is publicly available, and the runtime comparison (20 s vs. 10 min) is concrete and reproducible on commodity hardware.

## Limitations

- Experiments use n=100, one dataset per simulation, and one or two DGPs — no Monte Carlo over datasets, no confidence intervals on r, and no demonstration at realistic sample sizes.
- Theory requires bounded outcomes in [0,1]; unbounded or real-valued regression is not covered without transformation.
- The Lipschitz condition for the debiased-ML corollary is non-trivial to verify beyond AIPW-with-bounded-propensity.
- The worst-case V bound can be prohibitively large, and the adaptive scheme, while practical, lacks a finite-sample guarantee on the number of iterations needed.
- Only two baselines (final-estimate averaging, increased folds) are compared; no comparison to deterministic-training or other variance-reduction alternatives.

## Relevance

直接映射到 **ML Reliability & Mathematical Foundations**——本文从算法稳定性理论出发，给出随机种子依赖（训练非确定性的一个具体来源）的形式化浓度条件和袋装保证，是ML可靠性的数学基础工作。同时与 **LLM Training Stability & Observability** 间接相关：种子依赖是训练可复现性的核心观测与控制问题，本文的(ε,δ)框架可为训练稳定性监控提供可借鉴的形式化范式。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does adaptive cross-bagging maintain r≈1 at realistic sample sizes (n=10⁴–10⁶) and across many DGPs, or does the median V=396 result change substantially with n?
- Can the bounded-output assumption be relaxed to unbounded regression via clipping, winsorization, or a sub-Gaussian extension of Bernstein's inequality?
- How does cross-bagging interact with modern gradient-boosted nuisance learners (XGBoost, LightGBM) whose seed dependence differs from random forests?
- Can the (ε,δ)-stability framework be adapted to monitor LLM training reproducibility, where "seeds" include data-ordering, initialization, and dropout randomness?
- Is the r=1.04 result stable across dataset draws, or does the single-dataset experiment mask high variability in the achieved stability ratio?
