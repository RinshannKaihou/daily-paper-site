GSQ: Highly-Accurate Low-Precision Scalar Quantization for LLMs via Gumbel-Softmax Sampling

arXiv: 2604.18556

Date: 2026-04-21

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.18556

一句话总结： GSQ 通过 Gumbel-Softmax 松弛将标量量化中的离散网格分配问题转化为可微优化问题，在 2/3 bit 精度下以纯对称标量格式接近向量量化方法的精度前沿，并成功扩展至万亿参数 MoE 模型 Kimi-K2.5。
TL;DR: GSQ reformulates per-weight grid assignment in scalar quantization as a differentiable discrete optimization via Gumbel-Softmax relaxation, closing most of the accuracy gap to vector-quantized methods (e.g., QTIP) at 2-3 bits while remaining fully compatible with existing scalar inference kernels and scaling to trillion-parameter MoE models.

核心贡献

  • 提出 GSQ 方法，利用 Gumbel-Softmax 松弛联合优化标量量化中每个权重的离散网格分配和分组缩放因子，将量化从贪心/局部决策提升为全局梯度优化
  • 针对低位宽（ternary/2-bit）场景，松弛的基数仅为 3-4 个候选值，天然适配；对于更高位宽（3-bit+），提出局部偏移（local-shift）参数化，将每权重可训练参数从 O(2^b) 降至 O(5)，保持线性开销
  • 在 Llama-3.1-8B/70B-Instruct 上，2-bit GSQ 比最佳标量基线 EfficientQAT 平均准确率高 4.14-4.76 个百分点，仅落后 QTIP 约 1.3-1.7 个百分点
  • 首次将低位标量量化成功应用于万亿参数 MoE 模型 Kimi-K2.5（2-bit），在数学/代码推理任务上几乎无损，而向量量化方法难以在此规模部署
  • 使用 Lion 优化器替代 AdamW 以缓解 Gumbel-Softmax 温度退火导致的梯度消失问题

Key Contributions

  • Introduces GSQ, a post-training scalar quantization method that jointly learns per-coordinate grid assignments and per-group scales via Gumbel-Softmax relaxation, converting discrete rounding into a differentiable optimization problem
  • Matches the cardinality of the relaxation to the small number of grid levels (3-4 for ternary/2-bit), making the relaxation naturally tight; proposes a local-shift parameterization for higher bit-widths (b>2) that reduces per-weight trainable parameters from 2^b to 5
  • Achieves SOTA among scalar quantization methods: at 2 bits, improves over EfficientQAT by 4.76 pp (8B) and 4.14 pp (70B) on average zero-shot accuracy, trailing QTIP by only 1.33-1.68 pp despite using symmetric quantization without zero-points
  • First method to apply low-bit close-to-lossless quantization to trillion-parameter MoE models (Kimi-K2.5 at 2 bits) in a fully scalar, kernel-compatible format where VQ methods are infeasible
  • Adopts the Lion optimizer to address gradient vanishing under Gumbel-Softmax temperature annealing, and uses gradient accumulation as explicit variance reduction for Gumbel noise resampling

Methodology

GSQ targets the standard layer/block-wise reconstruction objective: minimize the Frobenius norm between the full-precision and quantized module outputs over calibration data. The key innovation is making the discrete grid assignment differentiable.

Gumbel-Softmax Relaxation: For each weight coordinate, GSQ introduces trainable logits over candidate grid values. A soft quantized weight is computed as a probability-weighted sum of all candidates, where probabilities come from Gumbel-perturbed softmax. Temperature annealing gradually collapses the soft distribution to a hard one-hot assignment.

Ternary Quantization (1.58-bit): Each ternary weight {-s, 0, +s} is parameterized by two binary decisions (mask and sign), each relaxed via binary Gumbel-Softmax (equivalent to sigmoid). Logits are warm-started from the GPTQ solution with injected Gaussian noise to encourage exploration.

2-bit Quantization: Uses the full grid {-2, -1, 0, 1} with 4 logits per weight. The scale s can be negative, removing directional bias. Initialization follows a Gaussian prior centered on the GPTQ solution.

Higher Bit-widths (3-bit+): Uses a local-shift formulation: instead of 2^b logits per weight, only 5 logits parameterize a discrete shift delta in {-2, -1, 0, +1, +2} relative to the GPTQ-initialized grid index. This keeps memory linear in the number of weights regardless of bit-width.

Optimization Pipeline:

  1. Block-wise training with staged within-block schedule: Q/K projections independently under linear loss, then V/O jointly under attention output loss, then MLP under full block loss
  2. End-to-end scale-only fine-tuning: discrete assignments are frozen, only per-group scales are updated globally
  3. Temperature annealed linearly from 2.0 to 0.05; noise scale kappa from 100 to 500
  4. Lion optimizer used instead of AdamW to handle vanishing gradients in saturated Gumbel-Softmax regimes

Initialization: Logits are warm-started from GPTQ solutions with injected isotropic Gaussian noise, controlled by a relative strength parameter alpha.

Results & Highlights

Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (2-bit):

  • GSQ: 53.80 avg accuracy; GPTQ: 41.61; QuIP: 40.12; EfficientQAT: 49.04; QTIP: 55.13
  • GSQ improves over best scalar baseline (EfficientQAT) by +4.76 pp

Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct (2-bit):

  • GSQ: 62.62 avg accuracy; GPTQ: 52.33; QuIP: 51.48; EfficientQAT: 58.48; QTIP: 64.30
  • GSQ improves over best scalar baseline by +4.14 pp, trails QTIP by only 1.68 pp

3-bit results: GSQ matches or surpasses all scalar baselines and is essentially on par with QTIP on the 70B model.

Ternary (1.58-bit): GSQ at ternary precision already exceeds all scalar baselines run at higher 2-bit precision on the 8B model.

Non-uniform bit allocation: Mixed 2/3-bit configurations at 2.37 and 2.62 average bpp retain most 3-bit accuracy while reducing model size, with 4.99-5.46x speedup over BF16 on L40s GPUs.

Kimi-K2.5 (1T MoE, 2-bit):

  • MATH500: 96.68 -> 97.32 (improvement); LiveCodeBench v6: 61.37 -> 69.37 (improvement)
  • AIME25: 95.33 -> 93.00 (minor drop); GPQA Diamond: 89.29 -> 76.57 (noticeable drop, attributed to calibration data bias)
  • Long-context: strong preservation up to 32k, modest degradation at 128k-256k

Runtime: 8B in 10 hours, 70B in 68 hours, Kimi-K2.5 in 39 hours on 8xH200 GPUs.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The core idea of applying Gumbel-Softmax to quantization grid assignment is conceptually clean and well-motivated, building directly on MaskLLM's use of the same technique for sparsity. The local-shift parameterization for higher bit-widths is a practical engineering contribution. However, the Gumbel-Softmax relaxation itself is not new, and the method can be seen as a natural extension of existing differentiable discrete optimization ideas to the quantization setting.

Rigor: The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against both scalar and VQ baselines across multiple benchmarks and model scales. The paper is transparent about where GSQ still falls short (e.g., the remaining gap to QTIP at 2 bits, the GPQA degradation on Kimi-K2.5). The ablation on scale-only fine-tuning (Appendix A.2) provides useful insight. However, only single runs are reported for most configurations due to cost, which limits statistical confidence. The within-block staging strategy is motivated heuristically rather than formally.

Reproducibility: The method is described in sufficient algorithmic detail (pseudocode for ternary, 2-bit, and general cases). Key hyperparameters are provided in appendix tables. However, no code release is mentioned, and the experiments require significant compute (8xH200 or 8xB300 GPUs). The Gumbel noise sampling introduces stochasticity, though the authors report low variance in preliminary tests.

Significance: The central claim -- that the scalar-vs-VQ accuracy gap is largely an optimization gap rather than representational -- is important and well-supported by evidence. If validated by the community, this shifts the research emphasis from format design to optimization methodology for scalar quantization. The Kimi-K2.5 results demonstrate unprecedented scalability.

Red flags:

  • The 2-bit GSQ on Llama-3.1-8B skips the down_proj of the second layer (left in FP), which slightly inflates the effective bit-width and complicates fair comparison.
  • The ternary GSQ results are only shown for the 8B model, not 70B.
  • The paper does not compare against AWQ, one of the most widely deployed scalar methods, despite citing it prominently.
  • The GPQA drop on Kimi-K2.5 (12.7 pp) is substantial and is somewhat dismissed as a calibration data issue without rigorous verification.
  • The local-shift parameterization with delta in {-2, -1, 0, +1, +2} limits the maximum grid movement, which could be a hard constraint in certain optimization landscapes.

Strengths

  • Strong empirical results that convincingly close the scalar-VQ gap at 2-3 bits
  • Symmetric quantization (no zero-points) yet outperforms asymmetric baselines -- clean ablation showing gains come from optimization, not format flexibility
  • Full compatibility with existing scalar inference kernels (llama.cpp, vLLM + Humming), enabling immediate practical deployment
  • Scalability to trillion-parameter MoE models where VQ methods cannot operate
  • Well-structured within-block staged optimization that is thoughtful about gradient signal propagation
  • The Lion optimizer choice for handling Gumbel-Softmax gradient vanishing is a practically useful insight
  • Non-uniform bit allocation is a natural byproduct of the scalar format, offering flexible accuracy-compression trade-offs

Limitations

  • Significant computational overhead: GSQ requires 2-5x the memory of standard PTQ due to auxiliary logits, and block-wise training takes 10-68 hours on 8xH200 GPUs
  • Still trails QTIP and PV-Tuning at 2 bits by 0.7-1.7 pp on average, meaning the VQ gap is not fully closed
  • The local-shift formulation restricts each weight to move at most 2 grid positions from its initialization, which may be limiting for outlier weights
  • Long-context performance degrades noticeably at 128k-256k sequence lengths on Kimi-K2.5
  • Calibration data composition has an outsized effect on downstream task performance (GPQA degradation), suggesting sensitivity to data selection
  • No comparison with AWQ despite its wide deployment, and no evaluation on generation quality metrics (e.g., perplexity, human evaluation)
  • The method is currently limited to weight-only PTQ; extending to activation quantization or KV-cache quantization is mentioned as future work

Relevance

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does the local-shift window size (currently fixed at {-2,...,+2}) affect optimization quality, and can it be adapted per-layer or per-group based on weight distribution characteristics?
  • Can the GSQ framework be extended to joint weight-activation quantization or KV-cache quantization while maintaining differentiability?
  • What is the sensitivity of GSQ to the choice of calibration dataset, and can a more principled calibration data selection strategy mitigate the domain-specific degradation observed on GPQA?
  • How does GSQ interact with rotation-based methods (e.g., QuaRot, SpinQuant) that pre-process weights to be more quantization-friendly?
  • Is the remaining gap to QTIP at 2 bits truly a representational limitation of scalar formats, or could a more expressive optimization (e.g., multi-round GSQ with re-initialization) close it further?
  • What are the theoretical convergence guarantees for the Gumbel-Softmax relaxation under the Lion optimizer with temperature annealing?
