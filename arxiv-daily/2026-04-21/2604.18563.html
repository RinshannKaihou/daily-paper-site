Dual Alignment Between Language Model Layers and Human Sentence Processing

arXiv: 2604.18563

Date: 2026-04-21

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.18563

Key Contributions

  • Identification of a "dual alignment" phenomenon: The paper systematically demonstrates that different types of human sentence processing behavior align with different LM layers -- early layers for naturalistic reading and later layers for syntactic ambiguity resolution.
  • Evidence for distinct human processing modes: Naturalistic reading is characterized by "weak prediction" akin to early LM layers, while syntactically demanding constructions require more fully-contextualized representations resembling later LM layers.
  • Probability-update measures: Proposes novel measures capturing the probability shift between shallow and deep LM layers, demonstrating complementary value over single-layer surprisal for predicting human reading times.
  • Refinement of surprisal theory: Challenges the assumption that final-layer surprisal serves as a universal proxy for cognitive effort, showing that internal layers provide more nuanced and accurate models.
  • Empirical bridge between interpretability and cognitive science: Provides concrete evidence that LM internal representations carry cognitively meaningful structure, advancing both mechanistic interpretability and psycholinguistics.

Methodology

The authors investigate the alignment between LM layer-wise representations and human sentence processing through two main experimental paradigms:

  1. Layer-wise surprisal extraction: The authors extract surprisal estimates from every layer of multiple large language models (including LLaMA family models) by computing the output distribution at each intermediate layer. This allows them to measure how well each layer's predictive distribution matches human behavioral data.
  1. Naturalistic reading time data: They use established eye-tracking and self-paced reading corpora to correlate layer-wise surprisal with human reading times on naturalistic text. Prior work (Kuribayashi et al., 2025) showed early layers best predict such data.
  1. Syntactic ambiguity processing data: They collect or use existing datasets of human processing difficulty on syntactically ambiguous constructions (e.g., garden-path sentences, relative clause attachments), where traditional surprisal has been shown to underestimate human cognitive effort.
  1. Correlation analysis: For each dataset type, the authors compute the predictive accuracy (e.g., using linear mixed-effects models with delta log-likelihood) of surprisal from each LM layer, identifying which layer best captures human behavior in each condition.
  1. Probability-update measures: Drawing on the dual alignment finding, the authors define measures that capture the shift in predicted probability between a shallow layer and a deep layer. These measures quantify how much the model "revises" its prediction as contextualization deepens, which may analogously reflect human reanalysis processes during difficult syntactic processing.
  1. Compositional analysis: The probability-update measures are tested alongside single-layer surprisal in regression models predicting reading times, demonstrating that they capture distinct variance.

Results & Highlights

  • Naturalistic reading aligns with early layers: Replicating prior findings, surprisal from early-to-middle LM layers best predicts reading times on naturalistic text, while final-layer surprisal performs worse.
  • Syntactic ambiguity processing aligns with later layers: For garden-path sentences and other syntactically challenging constructions, later layers provide surprisal estimates that better correlate with human processing difficulty. This constitutes the "dual" part of the alignment.
  • Persistent underestimation: Even the best-performing later layers still systematically underestimate the magnitude of human processing difficulty on challenging constructions, suggesting a fundamental gap between LM and human processing in these cases.
  • Probability-update is complementary: Measures capturing the probability shift between shallow and deep layers explain additional variance in reading time data beyond what single-layer surprisal can account for, providing a more complete cognitive model.
  • Cross-model consistency: The dual alignment pattern is observed across multiple LLM architectures and scales, suggesting it is a robust phenomenon rather than an artifact of a particular model.

Critical Assessment

The paper makes a genuinely insightful contribution by identifying and characterizing the dual alignment phenomenon. The core finding -- that different processing regimes in humans correspond to different LM layers -- is elegant and has clear implications for both cognitive science and model interpretability.

However, several points merit scrutiny:

  1. Causal claims vs. correlational evidence: The paper frames the dual alignment as revealing "different modes of sentence processing," but the evidence is fundamentally correlational. The observed layer-wise correspondence does not necessarily imply that humans switch between processing strategies analogous to LM layers; it could simply reflect that easier constructions require less contextual integration (captured by early layers) while harder ones require more (captured by later layers), without any deeper architectural parallel.
  1. Scope of "syntactically challenging" constructions: The paper focuses primarily on syntactic ambiguity resolution (garden-path effects). Whether the dual alignment extends to other types of processing difficulty (semantic anomalies, pragmatic inferences, discourse-level phenomena) remains unclear. The generalizability of the finding is an open question.
  1. Residual underestimation gap: The fact that even later layers systematically underestimate human difficulty on challenging constructions is an important negative result. This suggests that the "alignment" is partial at best and that fundamental aspects of human reanalysis and repair mechanisms remain unmodeled. The paper acknowledges this but could engage more deeply with what the gap implies.
  1. Probability-update interpretation: While the probability-update measures show complementary predictive power, the interpretation of these measures as reflecting human "reanalysis" is somewhat speculative. Alternative explanations (e.g., that the measure simply captures higher-order statistical information) are not fully ruled out.
  1. Model dependency: Although tested across multiple models, all are transformer-based LLMs. Whether the dual alignment holds for other architectures (e.g., state-space models, recurrent architectures) is unknown.

Strengths

  • Clear and novel research question: The extension from naturalistic reading to syntactically challenging constructions is a natural but important step that yields surprising findings.
  • Well-motivated experimental design: The two-paradigm comparison (naturalistic vs. ambiguity processing) provides a clean setup for revealing the dual alignment.
  • Practical contribution: The probability-update measures offer a concrete tool for improving cognitive models of reading.
  • Theoretical significance: The findings have implications for both surprisal theory in psycholinguistics and for understanding what information is encoded at different depths in LMs.
  • Thorough evaluation: Testing across multiple models, datasets, and analysis methods strengthens the robustness of claims.

Limitations

  • English-only evaluation: All experiments are conducted on English data, leaving cross-linguistic validity untested.
  • Limited construction types: The "syntactically challenging" category is primarily defined by ambiguity resolution; other types of syntactic complexity are not covered.
  • Correlational nature: As noted in the critical assessment, the evidence cannot establish causal mechanisms linking LM layers to human processing.
  • Persistent underestimation: The gap between even the best LM-layer predictors and human data on difficult constructions indicates that current models still miss key aspects of human sentence processing.
  • Architectural specificity: The dual alignment may be specific to transformer architectures and may not generalize to other model families.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Does the dual alignment pattern hold for languages with different typological properties (e.g., head-final languages, free-word-order languages)?
  • Can the probability-update measure be interpreted as a proxy for "reanalysis cost" in human parsing, and how does it relate to existing parsing models (e.g., constraint-based or garden-path models)?
  • Are there specific neural mechanisms in the human brain (e.g., differential activation in Broca's area vs. temporal regions) that correspond to the early-layer vs. later-layer distinction?
  • How does the dual alignment interact with model scale -- do larger models show sharper or more graded layer-wise differences?
  • Can the probability-update framework be extended to model other cognitive phenomena beyond reading time, such as sentence acceptability judgments or recall accuracy?
  • Do the findings generalize to non-transformer architectures such as Mamba or other state-space models?
