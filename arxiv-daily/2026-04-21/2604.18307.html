Reasoning Models Know What's Important, and Encode It in Their Activations

arXiv: 2604.18307

Date: 2026-04-21

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.18307

一句话总结： 本文通过训练探针（probe）发现，推理语言模型在激活值中编码了推理步骤重要性的内部表示——这一信号早于后续步骤的生成即已存在、跨模型泛化、分布在多层且不与表面特征（位置、长度等）相关，表明分析模型内部激活比仅看 token 更能揭示推理的本质。
TL;DR: Reasoning models encode an internal notion of step importance in their activations before subsequent steps are generated. Activation-based probes achieve >=80% accuracy in predicting which reasoning steps are important (removable vs. non-removable), outperforming token-based methods including Gemini-2.5-Pro as a judge (<=60%). This importance signal is distributed across layers, transfers across models, and is not captured by surface-level features.

核心贡献

  • 定义了基于"可移除性"（removability）的推理步骤重要性度量：一个步骤若能在移除后仍使模型保持正确回答且推理链在分布内，则该步骤不重要。
  • 提出基于梯度的激活归因方法来识别核心推理子序列，在五个模型和两个数据集上均比 token-based（Gemini-2.5-Pro）和随机方法产生更小的核心子序列。
  • 训练 MLP 探针（layerwise-MLP, h=128）从模型激活中预测步骤重要性，准确率达 80%+，且该信号在后续步骤生成之前即已存在（pre-hoc）。
  • 发现这种重要性表示跨模型可迁移：在一个模型的激活上训练的探针可以预测另一个模型的重要性标签，准确率与模型内设置相当。
  • 证明重要性信号分布在所有层和位置，不与表面特征（相对位置、步骤长度、数字数量、算术符号数量、步骤类别等）相关。

Key Contributions

  • Define a causal notion of reasoning step importance via removability: a step is important if removing it (from a sufficient, attainable subsequence) prevents the model from reaching the correct answer.
  • Propose an activation-based greedy method using gradient attribution to identify core reasoning subsequences that are smaller (18--31% of prefix) than those found by token-based (Gemini-2.5-Pro) or random methods across 5 models and 2 math benchmarks (HARP, MATH-500).
  • Train probes on full-context activations to predict step importance with >=80% accuracy; the signal is pre-hoc (exists before subsequent steps are generated), requiring both full context and strong latent representations.
  • Show cross-model transferability: probes trained on one model's activations predict another model's importance labels with comparable accuracy, suggesting importance is a universal concept rather than model-specific.
  • Demonstrate the importance signal is distributed across layers and token positions, and not reducible to surface-level features (position, length, numeric content, step categories).

Methodology

The paper studies reasoning step importance through a three-stage pipeline applied to mathematical reasoning across five models (GPT-OSS-20B, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen 1.5B/7B/14B, OLMo-3-7B-Think) on two benchmarks (HARP with 4,780 problems and MATH-500 with 500 problems).

Step 1 -- Preprocessing. For each problem, the model generates a greedy reasoning chain, which is segmented into steps. Chains are filtered to keep only those that are correct, necessary (model cannot answer without reasoning), and free of shortcut steps (steps that alone reveal the answer). A sufficient prefix (shortest contiguous subsequence with high sufficiency and attainability) is identified for each chain.

Step 2 -- Identifying core reasoning subsequences. A greedy procedure finds the minimal core subsequence that preserves both sufficiency (fraction of correct completions >= 0.8) and attainability (normalized perplexity within bounds). Three variants are compared:

  • Activation-based: Uses gradient attribution (Frobenius norm of gradient of downstream step probabilities w.r.t. input embeddings) to measure inter-step influence, then greedily removes low-influence steps.
  • Token-based: Uses Gemini-2.5-Pro to identify removable steps and rank importance via prompting.
  • Random: Random ranking baseline.

The activation-based variant consistently produces the smallest core subsequences, and is adopted for labeling steps as "removable" or "non-removable."

Step 3 -- Probing and analysis. Binary classification probes (2-layer MLP, h=128, trained on full-context activations averaged over tokens, flattened over layers and hidden dim) predict step importance. Multiple baselines are evaluated: context-free activations, TF-IDF, sentence embeddings (all-MiniLM-L6-v2), and Gemini-2.5-Pro as LLM-as-a-judge. Cross-model transfer is tested by training probes on one model's activations to predict another model's labels. Layer-wise and position-wise probes assess localization, and correlation with surface-level features is measured.

Results & Highlights

  • Core subsequence size: The activation-based method identifies core subsequences of 18--31% of the sufficient prefix, consistently smaller than token-based (31--43%) and random (22--33%) methods across all model-dataset pairs (Table 1).
  • Probe accuracy: Full-context activation probes achieve >=80% accuracy on HARP and >=83% on MATH-500 for larger models, substantially outperforming all baselines.
  • LLM-as-a-judge fails: Gemini-2.5-Pro achieves <=60% accuracy in classifying step importance, even with full context, suggesting token-level access is fundamentally insufficient.
  • Context is critical: Removing context from activation probes causes a 10--20% accuracy drop; context-free methods (TF-IDF, sentence embeddings) also perform substantially worse.
  • Cross-model transfer: Probes trained on model A's activations predict model B's importance labels with accuracy matching within-model performance. Agreement ratios between cross-model and self-model probes are consistently high across all model pairs.
  • Distributed representation: No single layer or position matches the full probe's accuracy. Individual surface features show low correlation with probe predictions, and a linear regression on all surface features substantially underperforms the activation probe.
  • Most steps are removable: Only 17--30% of prefix steps are non-removable (important), depending on model and dataset, suggesting reasoning chains are highly redundant.
  • Negative results: Logit lens analysis of probe weights yields uninterpretable tokens; single-step interventions (removal, filler, paraphrase) produce highly correlated outcomes due to reasoning chain redundancy.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The paper makes a genuine contribution by shifting the analysis of reasoning step importance from tokens to activations, and by demonstrating that models encode a pre-hoc internal representation of importance. The cross-model transfer finding is particularly novel and thought-provoking.

Rigor: The experimental design is thorough -- five models from different families, two benchmarks, multiple baselines with fair comparisons (same classifier architecture), and 3-seed evaluation. The greedy subsequence identification procedure is well-defined with sufficiency and attainability constraints. However, the definition of importance via removability has limitations: it conflates multiple reasons a step might be removable (redundancy, recoverability from context), and the binary labeling (removable vs. non-removable from stage 1 only) discards intermediate-importance steps from stage 2.

Reproducibility: Code and data are publicly available. The methodology is clearly described with pseudocode. However, the activation-based variant requires computing gradient attribution matrices for all step pairs, which is computationally expensive. The Gemini-based baselines require API access (~$200 estimated cost), partially limiting reproducibility.

Significance: The finding that importance is encoded pre-hoc in activations has important implications for understanding how reasoning models plan their outputs. The cross-model universality suggests something fundamental about how reasoning is represented. However, the practical impact is currently limited: the probes are trained post-hoc and not used to improve model behavior or efficiency at inference time.

Red flags: (1) The paper focuses exclusively on mathematical reasoning; generalization to other domains is unverified. (2) The "ground truth" importance labels are themselves derived from the activation-based method, creating a potential circularity -- the probes predict labels that were generated using activations. (3) The random-model baseline achieves above-random accuracy in cross-model experiments, suggesting some probe performance may come from statistical structure rather than meaningful importance encoding. (4) The negative results (uninterpretable logit lens, failed single-step interventions) suggest the importance signal, while detectable, may not correspond to a human-interpretable mechanism.

Strengths

  • Comprehensive evaluation across 5 models from different families and 2 datasets, lending strong support to the generality of findings.
  • Clean experimental design with fair baselines (same classifier architecture across representation methods) isolates the effect of representation quality.
  • The pre-hoc finding -- that importance exists before subsequent steps are generated -- is a strong and surprising result with clear implications.
  • The cross-model transfer experiment is elegant and the universality finding is compelling.
  • Honest reporting of negative results (logit lens, single-step interventions, step taxonomy) increases trustworthiness.
  • Well-structured paper with clear progression from importance identification to probing to analysis.

Limitations

  • Restricted to mathematical reasoning only; whether findings transfer to code, natural language reasoning, or open-ended problems is unknown.
  • The importance labels are derived from the same activation-based method used to evaluate the probes, raising concerns about circularity in the probing experiments.
  • The probes require full-context activations from the generating model, limiting practical applicability (e.g., cannot be used by an external observer without model access).
  • Binary importance classification (removable vs. non-removable from stage 1) ignores steps with intermediate importance, oversimplifying the continuous nature of importance.
  • The paper does not leverage the importance signal to fine-tune models or improve reasoning efficiency, leaving the practical implications unexplored.
  • No analysis of what makes a step important from a semantic or functional perspective -- the probes detect importance but do not explain it.

Relevance

本文与 LLM 可解释性研究高度相关，直接研究了推理模型隐藏状态中编码的抽象概念（步骤重要性）。其核心方法——从模型激活中训练探针预测推理属性——是 hidden states 分析的经典范式，结果支持了"模型内部表示比 token 输出更忠实反映推理过程"这一关键假设。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Can the pre-hoc importance signal be used at inference time to dynamically prune reasoning chains and improve efficiency without sacrificing accuracy?
  • Does the cross-model universality of importance hold for non-mathematical reasoning domains (code generation, logical deduction, scientific reasoning)?
  • What is the causal role of the importance-encoding directions in the residual stream? Can perturbing these directions change which steps the model treats as important?
  • Can importance probes be trained without ground-truth labels (e.g., via self-supervised objectives) to enable importance detection without the expensive greedy removal procedure?
  • How does the importance signal relate to the model's confidence calibration and self-verification capabilities?
  • Is the pre-hoc importance signal related to the model's internal planning or lookahead mechanisms? Could analyzing its development during training shed light on how reasoning capabilities emerge?
