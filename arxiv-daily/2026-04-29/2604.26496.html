Robust Alignment: Harmonizing Clean Accuracy and Adversarial Robustness in Adversarial Training

arXiv: 2604.26496v1

Date: 2026-04-29

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.26496v1

一句话总结： 本文发现对抗训练中边界样本的扰动强度变化对模型鲁棒性几乎没有影响，揭示了输入空间与隐空间之间的语义不对齐是精度-鲁棒性权衡的关键原因，据此提出Robust Alignment目标，通过降低边界样本扰动强度和引入域插值一致性对抗正则化(DICAR)实现精度与鲁棒性的协同提升。
TL;DR: This paper reveals that varying perturbation intensities for boundary samples in adversarial training has minimal impact on robustness, identifying input-latent space misalignment as a root cause of the accuracy-robustness trade-off. The authors propose Robust Alignment Adversarial Training (RAAT), which combines reduced perturbation for boundary samples with a novel Domain Interpolation Consistency Adversarial Regularization (DICAR) that regularizes all-order derivatives, achieving improved trade-offs across CIFAR-10/100 and Tiny-ImageNet against 14 SOTA methods.

核心贡献

  • 发现关键现象： 首次揭示在对抗训练中，对决策边界附近的样本施加不同强度的扰动几乎不影响最终模型鲁棒性，表明这些扰动信息未被模型有效学习到隐空间表示中，而是变成了无意义的噪声。
  • 提出Robust Alignment新目标： 将对抗训练的目标从单纯追求预测一致性重新定义为"鲁棒对齐"——在保持最终标签预测不变的前提下，鼓励模型感知随输入扰动而变化，从而对齐输入空间与隐空间。
  • 提出DICAR正则化方法： 基于严格理论推导，提出域插值一致性对抗正则化(DICAR)，鼓励两组增强变体的对抗样本插值的预测与两组预测的插值保持一致，理论证明DICAR等价于对所有阶导数的正则化。
  • 构建RAAT训练框架： 综合上述两个思路，提出RAAT和RAAT++两种具体方法，分别使用基础和更高级的损失函数来优化新的鲁棒对齐目标。
  • 全面实验验证： 在CIFAR-10、CIFAR-100和Tiny-ImageNet上使用ResNet-18、PreActResNet-18和WideResNet-28-10，超越4个通用基线和14个SOTA方法。

Key Contributions

  • Novel empirical finding: Demonstrates that varying adversarial perturbation intensities for boundary samples (correctly classified but near the decision boundary) has negligible effect on final model robustness, exposing input-latent space misalignment as a core issue.
  • Robust Alignment formulation: Defines a new adversarial training target that encourages model perception to change proportionally with input perturbations while maintaining correct label predictions, moving beyond the conventional view of adversarial perturbations as pure noise.
  • DICAR regularization: Proposes Domain Interpolation Consistency Adversarial Regularization with rigorous theoretical grounding; Theorem 2 proves it regularizes derivatives of all orders, enabling stronger inductive bias than point-based AT or edge-based consistency methods.
  • Theoretical foundation via Gaussian CR: Extends the Gaussian Model sample complexity framework to consistency regularization (CR), proving (Theorem 1) that the standard-robust generalization gap of O(sqrt(d)/log d) can be filled with O(sqrt(d)) unlabeled data points.
  • RAAT/RAAT++ methods: Two concrete implementations that combine reduced boundary perturbation with DICAR, evaluated comprehensively against 14 SOTA methods across three datasets and three architectures.

Methodology

The paper proceeds in three stages:

1. Boundary Sample Analysis (Section 2): The authors conduct proof-of-concept experiments on CIFAR-10 with ResNet-18, training a PGD-AT baseline and then systematically varying perturbation intensities for boundary vs. non-boundary samples. The key finding is that perturbation variations for boundary samples barely affect robustness, while non-boundary sample perturbations are critical. This leads to the identification of input-latent space misalignment as a root cause.

2. Robust Alignment via DICAR (Section 3): The theoretical framework extends the Gaussian Model (Schmidt et al.) to define Gaussian Consistency Regularization (Gaussian CR). Theorem 1 establishes that CR can bridge the generalization gap between standard and robust classification. The proposed DICAR term takes two augmentations of an input, generates adversarial samples from each, and encourages the prediction of their interpolation to match the interpolation of their individual predictions. Theorem 2 proves this acts as an all-order derivative regularizer.

3. RAAT Training Framework (Section 4): The full method combines: (i) reduced and fixed perturbation intensity (by a ratio eta) for boundary samples identified via a threshold epsilon/10, and (ii) the DICAR regularization term weighted by lambda. Two variants are proposed: RAAT using basic loss functions and RAAT++ using more advanced ones.

Results & Highlights

  • CIFAR-10 (ResNet-18, L_inf): RAAT achieves 89.17% clean accuracy (+0.46% over best baseline) and 67.95% AA robustness; RAAT++ reaches 70.84% PGD-10 robustness (+0.90% improvement).
  • CIFAR-100 (ResNet-18): RAAT++ achieves 44.95% PGD-10 (+2.34% over best baseline) with 62.43% clean accuracy; consistent improvements across all attack evaluations.
  • Tiny-ImageNet: Improvements are more modest, with RAAT gaining +0.57% on C&W attack and marginal gains elsewhere.
  • SOTA comparison: When implemented on top of ReBAT, RAAT achieves new SOTA performance beyond 11 additional SOTA methods on PreActResNet-18, demonstrating plug-and-play compatibility.
  • L2 threat model: Also evaluated under L2 perturbations, showing consistent improvements across all three datasets.
  • Ablation studies: Confirm the individual effectiveness of both ideas (reduced boundary perturbation and DICAR), show moderate sensitivity to boundary range threshold and regularization weight.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty (Moderate-High): The finding that boundary sample perturbation intensity has minimal impact is genuinely surprising and the DICAR formulation is novel. However, the individual components (reduced perturbation for boundary samples, consistency regularization) each have precedents in prior work (MMA, HAT, TE for reduced perturbation; ICT, Cons-AT for consistency). The main novelty lies in the synthesis and the misalignment framing.
  • Rigor (Moderate): The theoretical results are built on the Gaussian Model assumption, which is a significant simplification of real neural network behavior. The DICAR all-order derivative regularization result (Theorem 2) is a straightforward extension of the ICT theorem. The boundary sample experiments, while illustrative, use a single threshold (epsilon/10) and a simple identification criterion that may not generalize.
  • Reproducibility (Good): Code is provided. Experiments use standard architectures (ResNet-18, WRN-28-10) and standard datasets. Three runs with standard deviations are reported.
  • Significance (Moderate): The improvements over baselines are consistent but often modest (1-2% on most metrics). On Tiny-ImageNet, gains are marginal or even slightly negative on clean accuracy (-0.47%). The SOTA comparison in Section 5.2 uses numbers reported from prior papers rather than unified re-implementation, making fair comparison difficult. The method introduces two new hyperparameters (boundary perturbation ratio eta and DICAR weight lambda) that require tuning.
  • Red flags: The paper is quite long (19 pages) with substantial appendix material. The claimed "harmonization" of accuracy and robustness still shows the fundamental trade-off -- RAAT improves clean accuracy and robustness simultaneously compared to some baselines, but RAAT++ (the stronger variant) still sacrifices clean accuracy for robustness (e.g., 86.54% clean vs 89.17% for RAAT on CIFAR-10). The boundary sample identification criterion is somewhat arbitrary and may not be robust across different settings.

Strengths

  • Provides a novel and empirically grounded perspective on the accuracy-robustness trade-off through the lens of input-latent space misalignment.
  • The boundary sample analysis is a genuine empirical contribution that challenges existing assumptions.
  • DICAR is theoretically well-motivated with the all-order derivative regularization interpretation.
  • Comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets, architectures, threat models, and against 14+ SOTA methods.
  • Good reproducibility with code release and detailed appendices.

Limitations

  • Theoretical results rely on the restrictive Gaussian Model assumption, which may not transfer to practical deep networks.
  • Improvements on Tiny-ImageNet are marginal, raising questions about scalability to more complex datasets.
  • Introduces additional hyperparameters (eta, lambda, boundary threshold) that require careful tuning.
  • The method still exhibits the fundamental accuracy-robustness trade-off -- it mitigates but does not eliminate the tension.
  • The boundary sample identification relies on a fixed threshold (epsilon/10), which is heuristic and may not be optimal across different settings.
  • No evaluation on larger-scale datasets (e.g., full ImageNet) or with more modern architectures (Vision Transformers).

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does RAAT perform on larger-scale datasets (ImageNet) and with modern architectures (ViTs, ConvNeXt)?
  2. Can the boundary sample identification be made adaptive rather than relying on a fixed threshold?
  3. Is the input-latent space misalignment hypothesis testable beyond the perturbation intensity experiments -- for instance, via direct measurements of representation alignment (e.g., CCA, SVCCA)?
  4. How does DICAR interact with other regularization techniques such as weight perturbation (AWP) or label smoothing?
  5. Does the finding about boundary sample perturbation insensitivity hold for other threat models (e.g., L1, spatial perturbations) or only for L_inf and L2?
