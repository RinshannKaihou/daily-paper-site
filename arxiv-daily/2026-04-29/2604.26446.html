Near-Optimal Cryptographic Hardness of Learning With Homogeneous Halfspaces Under Gaussian Marginals

arXiv: 2604.26446v1

Date: 2026-04-29

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.26446v1

一句话总结： 本文基于LWE（Learning With Errors）问题的亚指数困难性假设，证明了在高斯边际分布下学习齐次半空间（homogeneous halfspaces）的三个问题——不可知学习、单侧可靠学习和公平性审计——均不存在多项式时间的 (1, 1/log^{1/2+eps} d)-近似算法，将先前针对一般半空间的下界严格推广到了齐次情形，并缩紧了已有上下界之间的间隙。
TL;DR: This paper proves near-optimal computational hardness for three problems involving homogeneous halfspaces under Gaussian marginals -- agnostic learning, one-sided reliable learning, and subgroup fairness auditing -- by reducing from the Learning With Errors (LWE) problem. The authors show that no polynomial-time (1, 1/log^{1/2+eps} d)-approximation algorithm exists for any of these problems, strictly generalizing prior lower bounds that only applied to general (non-homogeneous) halfspaces. The key technical novelty is a direct argument that the homogeneous halfspace defined by the LWE secret vector has significant advantage over random guessing, replacing the indirect two-halfspace construction used in prior work.

核心贡献

  • 证明了在高斯边际分布下，不可知学习齐次半空间不存在多项式时间的 (1, 1/log^{1/2+eps} d)-近似算法，严格推广了 Diakonikolas et al. [2023] 的下界结果（原结果仅适用于一般半空间）。
  • 证明了单侧可靠学习（positive-reliable learning）齐次半空间同样不存在上述近似算法，推广了 Huang and Juba [2025a] 的负面结果，且去除了原先对人口规模参数的约束。
  • 证明了齐次半空间子群上的统计奇偶性公平性审计（SPSF auditing）不存在多项式时间的乘法或加法近似算法，推广了 Hsu et al. [2024] 的不可近似性结果。
  • 提出了新的技术路线：直接论证 LWE 秘密向量定义的齐次半空间在目标损失度量下具有显著优势（advantage），通过方波与高斯密度的部分卷积及傅里叶展开进行分析，避免了先前工作中需要非零阈值的平行双半空间构造。

Key Contributions

  • Proved that no polynomial-time (1, 1/log^{1/2+eps} d)-approximation algorithm exists for agnostically learning homogeneous halfspaces under Gaussian marginals, assuming sub-exponential hardness of LWE -- strictly generalizing the cryptographic lower bound of Diakonikolas et al. [2023] from general to homogeneous halfspaces.
  • Established the same inapproximability for one-sided (positive-reliable) learning of homogeneous halfspaces, removing the population-size constraints required by prior work of Huang and Juba [2025a].
  • Extended the SPSF (Statistical Parity Subgroup Fairness) auditing hardness results from general halfspaces to homogeneous halfspaces, showing both additive and multiplicative inapproximability.
  • Introduced a new reduction technique: instead of constructing two parallel halfspaces with small agreement (which requires non-zero thresholds), directly argues that the homogeneous halfspace defined by the LWE secret vector has advantage over random guessing by analyzing a partial convolution between a square wave and Gaussian density via Fourier series expansion.

Methodology

The paper establishes cryptographic hardness results through reductions from the continuous Learning With Errors (cLWE) problem, which is known to be as hard as the standard LWE problem under the sub-exponential LWE assumption (Assumption 10, due to Lindner and Peikert [2011]).

The core methodology proceeds in two stages for each of the three problems:

1. Indistinguishability (Theorems 13, 14, 18): The authors design a label transformation that maps continuous LWE samples (x, y') to binary-labeled samples (x, y) by thresholding y' at T/2. They then prove that:

  • Under the alternative hypothesis (LWE instance with a secret vector s), the homogeneous halfspace defined by s has Omega(1/sqrt(eta * log d)) advantage over random guessing in the relevant loss measure.
  • Under the null hypothesis (random labels), no homogeneous halfspace has any advantage (loss equals random guessing baseline, e.g., 1/2 for classification).

The key technical innovation is in bounding the advantage term. The objective loss for each problem can be rewritten as: loss = baseline_loss + advantage_term, where the advantage term is a partial convolution of a square wave (period q) with the Gaussian density. By expanding the square wave in its Fourier series and computing the partial convolution on each basis element, the authors show the advantage is dominated by the first Fourier coefficient, yielding a lower bound via Gaussian tail analysis.

2. Inapproximability (Corollaries 15, 16, 17, 19, 20): Given the indistinguishability result, any algorithm achieving the assumed approximation guarantee could distinguish between the alternative and null hypotheses, contradicting the cLWE hardness. This yields both additive (1, 1/log^{1/2+eps} d)-inapproximability and multiplicative (no 1/poly(d)-approximation for fairness auditing) lower bounds.

The parameter regime considered: dimension d, with the hardness holding for c/(sqrt(d) log d) <= eps <= 1/log^7(d), and running time d^{O(1/(sqrt(log d))^{2alpha})}.

Results & Highlights

  • Agnostic learning (Theorem 5 / Corollary 17): No d^{O(1/(sqrt(log d))^{2*alpha})}-time (1, eps)-approximation algorithm exists for agnostically learning homogeneous halfspaces under Gaussian marginals for any constant alpha in (0,1) and eps = 1/log^{1/2+c}(d) (for any c > 0). This matches the quasi-polynomial upper bound of O(1/sqrt(log d))-approximation up to the exponent on log d.
  • Positive-reliable learning (Theorem 6 / Corollary 16): The same inapproximability holds for one-sided reliable learning, removing the population-size constraints of prior work.
  • Fairness auditing (Theorems 7 & 8 / Corollaries 19 & 20): Neither (1, eps)-approximation nor 1/poly(d)-multiplicative approximation is achievable in polynomial time for auditing SPSF over homogeneous halfspace subgroups.
  • Strict generalization: All results strictly generalize prior work by Diakonikolas et al. [2023], Huang and Juba [2025a], and Hsu et al. [2024], which were limited to general (non-homogeneous) halfspaces.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The extension from general to homogeneous halfspaces is significant because homogeneous halfspaces are a strictly simpler concept class. Prior techniques (two parallel halfspaces with small agreement) fundamentally required non-zero thresholds and could not be applied here. The new direct argument via Fourier analysis of the advantage term is a genuine technical contribution. However, the overall proof strategy (reduce from cLWE, establish indistinguishability, derive inapproximability) follows the established paradigm.
  • Rigor: The proofs are structured clearly with the indistinguishability results established first and inapproximability derived as corollaries. The parameter settings and conditions are carefully specified. The paper provides complete proofs in the main text and appendices (Sections A, B, C).
  • Reproducibility: The theoretical results should be reproducible given the detailed proof sketches. No code or experiments are involved (pure theory paper).
  • Significance: These results narrow the gap between upper and lower bounds for agnostic learning of homogeneous halfspaces to near-optimal. The fact that homogeneous halfspaces are as hard to learn as general ones (in the cryptographic sense) is a somewhat surprising finding that deepens our understanding of the complexity landscape.
  • Red flags: (1) The hardness results are conditional on the sub-exponential hardness of LWE, which while widely believed, is not proven. (2) The paper does not address whether a stronger lower bound could be obtained for general vs. homogeneous halfspaces, leaving this as future work. (3) The restriction to Gaussian marginals is notable -- the hardness may not hold under other distributions.

Strengths

  • The paper successfully extends three independent hardness results from general to homogeneous halfspaces, which is a non-trivial technical achievement given that prior methods were inapplicable.
  • The new proof technique (direct advantage argument via Fourier analysis of partial convolutions) is conceptually cleaner than the indirect two-halfspace construction.
  • The results are near-optimal, tightly matching the best known quasi-polynomial upper bounds for agnostic learning.
  • Clear paper organization with well-defined problems, thorough discussion of prior work, and clean separation between indistinguishability and inapproximability arguments.

Limitations

  • All results are restricted to Gaussian marginal distributions on the input space; extending to other distributions remains open.
  • The hardness is conditional on the sub-exponential LWE assumption; an unconditional proof would be much stronger (but likely out of reach given current techniques).
  • The paper does not provide matching upper bounds -- it shows near-optimality by matching against known algorithms, but does not construct new algorithms.
  • The gap between general and homogeneous halfspace hardness is not fully resolved; the authors conjecture a stronger lower bound may hold for halfspaces with larger thresholds but do not prove it.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Can the hardness results be extended to other well-behaved marginal distributions beyond Gaussian, such as log-concave or sub-Gaussian distributions?
  • Is there a fundamental complexity separation between learning homogeneous and general halfspaces, or are they equally hard in all regimes?
