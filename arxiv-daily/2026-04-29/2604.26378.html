CoQuant: Joint Weight-Activation Subspace Projection for Mixed-Precision LLMs

arXiv: 2604.26378v1

Date: 2026-04-29

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.26378v1

一句话总结： CoQuant提出了一种联合权重-激活子空间投影方法，通过理论建模量化输出的期望误差，推导出闭式加权PCA解来选择最优高精度子空间，在Llama-3.2和Qwen2.5上4.5-bit混合精度量化中一致超越ResQ等强基线。
TL;DR: CoQuant addresses a key limitation of subspace-based mixed-precision LLM quantization: prior methods (e.g., ResQ) select high-precision subspaces using activation statistics alone, ignoring the joint nature of output perturbation from both weight and activation quantization noise. CoQuant derives a closed-form weighted PCA objective that fuses activation and weight covariance matrices to select the optimal high-precision subspace. On Llama-3.2 (1B/3B) and Qwen2.5 (1.5B/7B/14B/72B) models at 4.5-bit average precision, CoQuant consistently achieves the best WikiText perplexity and zero-shot accuracy among PTQ baselines, with particularly strong gains on the hard-to-quantize Qwen2.5 family (e.g., +1.93% zero-shot accuracy over ResQ on Qwen2.5-1.5B). At ultra-low 3.375-bit precision, CoQuant reduces perplexity by up to 11.1 points over ResQ on Llama-3.2-1B.

  • 联合权重-激活误差建模： 首次在子空间级混合精度量化中，同时考虑权重和激活的量化噪声对输出误差的联合影响，而非仅依赖激活统计量。

Key Contributions

  • Joint weight-activation error modeling for subspace MPQ: CoQuant is the first to explicitly model the expected output error as a function of both activation and weight quantization noise in the subspace decomposition framework, correcting the activation-only bias of prior work.
  • Closed-form weighted PCA solution: Under first-order approximation and isotropic quantization noise assumptions, the method derives a weighted PCA objective whose solution identifies the optimal high-precision subspace by eigendecomposition of a mixed covariance matrix M = lambda_X Sigma_X + lambda_W Sigma_W.
  • Fused output covariance for shared-input layers: For grouped linear layers (e.g., W_Q/W_K/W_V or W_gate/W_up), CoQuant constructs a fused output covariance Sigma_W to better exploit weight structure.
  • Consistent improvements across model families and scales: Experiments on Llama-3.2 (1B, 3B) and Qwen2.5 (1.5B, 7B, 14B, 72B) at 4.5-bit and 3.375-bit average precision show CoQuant outperforms all PTQ baselines in perplexity and zero-shot accuracy.
  • Robustness under extreme quantization stress: At 3.375-bit average precision, CoQuant reduces perplexity by 11.1 on Llama-3.2-1B over ResQ, while QuaRot completely collapses (perplexity > 950).

Methodology

CoQuant builds on the subspace-wise mixed-precision quantization framework, where the feature space is partitioned into high-precision and low-precision subspaces via an orthogonal transformation. The key insight is that prior methods (ResQ) select the high-precision subspace by running PCA on activation covariance alone, but the output error of Y = XW depends on both activation and weight quantization noise.

Step 1 -- Joint Error Modeling: Given subspace-decomposed outputs, the quantized output error is expanded to first order, dropping the second-order cross term E_X * E_W. This yields an expected squared Frobenius norm that separates into activation-error and weight-error contributions per subspace.

Step 2 -- Isotropic White Noise Approximation: Since random orthogonal rotations within subspaces suppress outliers and make projected tensors approximately Gaussian, the quantization noise is modeled as proportional to the signal energy: E[||E_X||_F^2] ~ alpha^2 * ||X||_F^2 and similarly for weights. This enables a trace-identity simplification of the cross-error terms.

Step 3 -- Weighted PCA Objective: The total expected output error becomes a weighted sum of subspace energy products, with coefficients gamma_k = (alpha_k^2 + beta_k^2) / d_k encoding bit-width-dependent quantization noise. Minimizing this objective reduces to maximizing Tr(P_h^T M P_h), where M = lambda_X Sigma_X + lambda_W Sigma_W is a mixed covariance matrix. The optimal P_h consists of the top-r eigenvectors of M.

Step 4 -- Fused Output Covariance: For grouped linear layers sharing the same input (e.g., QKV projections), the weight covariance Sigma_W is constructed from the concatenated output WW^T, capturing inter-layer weight interactions.

Step 5 -- Calibration and Quantization: Activation covariance is estimated from 128 calibration sequences (WikiText). Subspaces are determined per-layer, and quantization is applied with different bit-widths to the high- and low-precision subspaces.

Critical Assessment

Novelty (Moderate): The core idea -- jointly modeling weight and activation quantization error for subspace selection -- is conceptually straightforward but well-motivated. The derivation from first-order error approximation to a weighted PCA objective is clean and principled. However, the method is a direct extension of the ResQ framework, sharing the same rotation-based quantization pipeline and changing only the subspace selection criterion.

Rigor (Good): The paper includes detailed mathematical derivations in the appendix, ablation studies validating each design choice, and calibration sensitivity analysis. The isotropic white noise assumption is justified by the Gaussianizing effect of random rotations, though it remains an approximation that may break at very low bit-widths.

Reproducibility (Strong): Code is publicly available. The method uses standard calibration (128 WikiText sequences), standard models (Llama-3.2, Qwen2.5), and well-established benchmarks. The weighted PCA computation is a single eigendecomposition per layer, making implementation straightforward.

Significance (Moderate-to-Good): The improvements over ResQ are consistent but modest on Llama models (e.g., 0.43 PPL improvement on Llama-3.2-1B). The gains are more substantial on Qwen2.5 and under ultra-low bit settings, where the joint modeling becomes more critical. The method's importance is primarily in correcting a conceptual flaw (activation-only subspace selection) rather than achieving dramatic performance leaps.

Red Flags:

  • The improvements over ResQ on Llama models are relatively small, raising the question of whether the added complexity of joint covariance estimation is always justified.
  • The paper does not report actual inference latency or throughput numbers, only quantization accuracy. The practical efficiency impact of subspace-wise mixed-precision is not validated.
  • Testing is limited to models up to 3B (Llama) and 72B (Qwen, mentioned but not shown in detail), with most detailed results on smaller models.
  • The fixed 1/8 subspace ratio for high-precision channels is a heuristic not extensively justified.

Strengths

  • Clean theoretical motivation: the observation that output error is jointly driven by weight and activation quantization noise is correct and underexplored in the subspace MPQ literature.
  • Principled closed-form solution: the weighted PCA objective naturally integrates both sources of quantization error with bit-width-aware coefficients.
  • Strong performance on hard-to-quantize models: CoQuant shows particularly robust results on Qwen2.5, where competing methods catastrophically fail.
  • Low calibration overhead: only 128 calibration sequences needed, and performance is stable even with 16 samples.
  • Comprehensive ablation: validates joint vs. activation-only vs. weight-only objectives, ultra-low bit robustness, and calibration sensitivity.

Limitations

  • Modest gains on some models: On Llama-3.2, the improvements over ResQ are relatively incremental (0.25 PPL, 0.2% accuracy on the 3B model).
  • No inference speed benchmarks: The paper lacks end-to-end latency or throughput measurements, which are critical for deployment decisions.
  • First-order approximation may be coarse: The isotropic white noise assumption and dropping of second-order cross terms may lose accuracy at very low bit-widths, which the authors acknowledge.
  • Fixed subspace ratio: All layers use the same 1/8 high-precision ratio, which is suboptimal given that different layers have varying sensitivity to quantization.
  • Limited model scale diversity: Detailed results focus on 1B-3B models; larger-scale validation (e.g., 70B+) would strengthen the claims.
  • No comparison with non-subspace mixed-precision methods: The comparison set focuses on rotation-based methods; comparison with other mixed-precision strategies (e.g., SVDQuant) would be informative.
