Recipes for Calibration Checks in Safety-Critical Applications

arXiv: 2604.26479v1

Date: 2026-04-29

TL;DR: This paper introduces a modular four-slot framework (data model, metric, hypothesis, testing) for regression calibration testing in safety-critical systems. It unifies disparate threads from ML, weather forecasting, Bayesian computation, and biostatistics into a single composable grammar. Two safety-critical refinements -- one-sided hypotheses that reject only overconfidence (accepting benign conservatism) and tolerance bands that admit small engineering-acceptable deviations -- transform calibration from a subjective continuous-score exercise into a deployable accept/reject decision. The framework is validated on two complementary problems (weather forecasting via offline KS test on PIT uniformity, and robot localization via online e-value monitor on particle-filter projections), demonstrating that recipes differing on every slot yield operationally distinct, engineer-readable verdicts on the same data.

Key Contributions

  • A modular four-slot decomposition for regression calibration testing that organizes the probabilistic-forecast evaluation literature into a single grammar with independently swappable slots: (i) data model (parametric density, particle cloud, conformal set), (ii) metric (per-level coverage, PIT uniformity, rank histogram), (iii) hypothesis formulation (two-sided, one-sided, equivalence), and (iv) testing procedure (offline p-value, sequential e-value).
  • Two safety-critical refinements promoted consistently across all four slots: (a) one-sided hypotheses that reject only overconfidence while accepting benign conservatism, and (b) tolerance bands drawn from equivalence testing that admit small, operationally irrelevant deviations within an explicit engineering budget.
  • The folded PIT transformation $V_i = |2U_i - 1|$ that establishes the mathematical equivalence between the empirical coverage curve and the folded PIT's empirical CDF, enabling the KS test to serve as a single joint replacement for Bonferroni-corrected per-level Binomial tests and recovering the statistical power lost to worst-case multiple-testing correction.
  • Empirical composability validation on two complementary safety-critical problems at opposite corners of the design space: a univariate Gaussian weather forecaster (offline, two-sided KS on PIT) and a multivariate particle-filter localizer (online, one-sided e-value on half-plane projections), with four slot-swap ablations demonstrating that different recipes yield operationally distinct verdicts on identical data.
  • Deployment-oriented accept/reject decisions rather than continuous scores (ECE, NLL, CRPS), directly embeddable into safety cases and certification workflows.

Methodology

The paper organizes calibration checking into a modular pipeline with four independently swappable slots:

  1. Data Model (Slot 1): Describes the form of forecasts and observations. Options include parametric densities (univariate or multivariate Gaussian with known CDF), particle clouds from filters (no closed-form CDF, requiring coverage-based approaches), and conformal prediction sets.
  1. Metric (Slot 2): Quantifies the discrepancy between forecasts and outcomes. The paper develops two parallel views:
  • Per-level coverage: For each coverage level $\lambda_k$, count how many outcomes fall inside the prediction interval; under calibration, $c_{\lambda_k} \sim \text{Binomial}(N, \lambda_k)$. Requires Bonferroni correction across K levels.
  • PIT uniformity: Compute $U_i = \text{cdf}(\hat{p}_Y, Y_i)$; under calibration, $U_i \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$ i.i.d. The folded PIT $V_i = |2U_i - 1|$ is shown to be mathematically equivalent to the empirical coverage curve via the identity $\hat{c}(\lambda) = \hat{F}_v(\lambda)$. This enables a single KS test to replace K Bonferroni-corrected Binomial tests, recovering power by exploiting the Brownian-bridge correlation structure between coverage levels.
  1. Hypothesis Formulation (Slot 3): Turns calibration into a testable statement with three variants:
  • Two-sided: Rejects both over-confidence and under-confidence (classical).
  • One-sided (safety-critical): Rejects only over-confidence (intervals too narrow); accepts benign conservatism. Uses the folded PIT to make the one-sided KS test clean: $D_N^+ = \sup_\lambda [\hat{F}_v(\lambda) - \lambda]$.
  • With tolerance: Admits deviations up to $\varepsilon_{\text{tol}}$ (from equivalence testing / TOST), solving the large-N problem where exact-null tests reject operationally benign miscalibration.
  1. Testing Procedure (Slot 4): Produces the accept/reject decision:
  • Offline p-value: Fixed-sample tests (Binomial, KS) on a collected validation set.
  • Sequential e-value (anytime-valid): Likelihood-ratio test martingale $E_t = E_{t-1} \cdot e_t$ where $e_t$ is the per-step likelihood ratio. Rejection when $E_t > 1/\alpha$ via Ville's inequality. Valid under temporal dependence without i.i.d. assumption, requiring only $\mathbb{E}[e_t | \mathcal{F}_{t-1}] \leq 1$ under $H_0$.

For multivariate/non-parametric settings, the paper introduces a half-plane projection method: at each step, a fresh random direction $d$ is drawn on the unit circle, both the particle cloud and the observation are projected onto $d$, and coverage is assessed on the 1D projection. Because the direction is independent of the data, the per-step indicator remains $\text{Bernoulli}(\lambda)$ under $H_0$ regardless of direction.

Results & Highlights

  • Weather forecasting experiment (Section 8.1): An AR(1) forecaster with a small conditional-mean bias produces predictions over 365 days. The two-sided KS test on the raw PIT rejects ($p = 0.001$), detecting the location bias. The one-sided folded KS test with 2% tolerance passes, confirming that prediction intervals are wide enough for safety despite the bias. This demonstrates that the same data can yield legitimately different conclusions depending on the operational question.
  • Robot localization experiment (Section 8.2): A particle filter (500 particles) tracks a 2D robot with unmodeled drift. The half-plane e-value monitor uses a sequential likelihood-ratio martingale. Under baseline drift, 3/20 seeds cross the rejection threshold; under high drift, 9/20 cross with median first crossing at $t \approx 143$. The monitor scales predictably with model-mismatch magnitude.
  • Slot-swap ablations (Appendix A): Four ablations hold the data fixed and vary one slot at a time, demonstrating:
  • Folded one-sided KS accepts even at zero tolerance; unfolded two-sided KS rejects (location bias signal); Bonferroni coverage rejects at zero tolerance but accepts at 2% tolerance.
  • Swapping the testing slot from offline p-value to online e-value on the same weather data produces an anytime-valid monitor that temporarily crosses the rejection threshold before retreating.
  • Swapping the data model from particle cloud to fitted Gaussian on the robot data yields the same pipeline structure with minimal code changes.
  • Power recovery: The KS test on the folded PIT exploits the exact Brownian-bridge correlation between coverage levels, yielding uniformly tighter bounds than Bonferroni in the central $\lambda$ range.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty (moderate): The individual components -- PIT transforms, KS tests, one-sided hypotheses, tolerance bands from equivalence testing, e-value martingales, half-plane projections -- are all drawn from prior literature. The paper's novelty lies in the systematic integration of these components into a unified, composable grammar, and in consistently applying the safety-critical modifications across all four slots. The folded PIT equivalence with the coverage curve, while known implicitly (Gneiting et al., 2007), is made explicit and operationalized here in a way that is genuinely useful. The contribution is primarily one of framework design and unification rather than new statistical methodology.
  • Rigor (high): The paper is methodical in its exposition, walking through each slot with clear notation, explicit null hypotheses, and careful attention to when assumptions (i.i.d. vs. conditional calibration) matter. The distinction between marginal and conditional calibration, and the correct use of test martingales under temporal dependence, is handled properly. The ablation studies cleanly isolate the effect of each design choice.
  • Reproducibility (moderate-high): The algorithms are described in pseudocode (Recipes 1 and 2). The experiments use standard models (AR(1) forecaster, particle filter in 2D) that should be straightforward to replicate. However, no code repository is referenced, and some experimental details (e.g., the specific weather dataset, random seeds) may require clarification. The paper's length (36 pages) and tutorial style make it accessible for replication.
  • Significance (moderate-high for practitioners): For safety-critical domains (autonomous driving, aviation, medical devices), this paper fills an important gap: how to certify that a probabilistic predictor is calibrated, rather than just measuring calibration. The accept/reject framing with controlled false-rejection rates is exactly what a certification workflow requires. The framework is more of an engineering methodology contribution than a fundamental statistical advance.
  • Red flags:
  • The paper is quite long (36 pages) and tutorial-like, which may limit its impact as a research publication while increasing its value as a reference.
  • The two demonstration experiments are relatively simple (synthetic/simulated). Validation on real-world safety-critical deployment data (e.g., actual autonomous vehicle fleets, operational weather forecasting systems) would strengthen the practical claims.
  • The one-sided test that accepts conservatism could be misused: a system that is extremely conservative (very wide prediction intervals) would pass the test, but such over-conservatism may itself be a safety issue in time-critical applications where overly cautious predictions lead to unnecessary interventions or degraded performance. The paper does not discuss this failure mode in depth.
  • The half-plane projection for multivariate calibration uses random directions, which introduces stochasticity into the test outcome; different random directions could yield different accept/reject verdicts, and the paper does not fully explore the sensitivity of this choice.

Strengths

  • Unifies fragmented literatures (ML calibration, conformal prediction, weather forecast verification, Bayesian computation, robotics, biostatistics, sequential analysis) into a single coherent framework.
  • The four-slot decomposition is genuinely useful as a design tool: it makes explicit the design decisions that are often left implicit in calibration studies.
  • The safety-critical modifications (one-sided tests, tolerance bands) address real operational needs that the standard two-sided exact-null framework does not.
  • Excellent pedagogical quality: the paper walks through the simplest instance first (Section 3) and then refines each slot in turn, with clear figures and worked examples.
  • The slot-swap ablations convincingly demonstrate that the framework is not just a taxonomy but an operational tool that yields different engineering decisions on the same data.
  • The use of e-values and anytime-valid inference for the sequential setting is timely and correctly applied.

Limitations

  • The demonstration experiments are synthetic/simulated; real-world deployment validation would be more compelling.
  • The framework does not address conditional calibration (calibration conditioned on specific input features or operating regimes) beyond simple variance binning; this is acknowledged but not developed.
  • No discussion of how to set the tolerance parameter $\varepsilon_{\text{tol}}$ in practice beyond "engineering judgment," which may be difficult to specify rigorously.
  • The paper does not address computational cost of the half-plane projection method for high-dimensional state spaces.
  • Extreme conservatism is not flagged as a potential problem, which could be relevant in time-critical or resource-constrained deployments.
  • The single-author scope results in a 36-page document that may be more suitable as a monograph or technical report than a standard conference/journal paper.
