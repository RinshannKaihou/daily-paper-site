Benchmarking the Safety of Large Language Models for Robotic Health Attendant Control

arXiv: 2604.26577v1

Date: 2026-04-29

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.26577v1

一句话总结： 本文构建了基于AMA医学伦理准则的270条有害指令数据集，在RHA仿真框架下对72个大语言模型进行安全性评估，发现平均违规率高达54.4%，专有模型安全性远优于开源模型（中位数23.7% vs 72.8%），医学微调和提示防御均无法有效降低风险，揭示出LLM在医疗机器人控制场景下面临严重的安全隐患。
TL;DR: This paper constructs a dataset of 270 harmful instructions grounded in AMA medical ethics principles and evaluates 72 LLMs (proprietary and open-weight) as controllers for a simulated robotic health attendant. The mean violation rate was 54.4% across all models, with proprietary models substantially safer than open-weight ones (median 23.7% vs 72.8%). Model size and release date are the primary determinants of safety, medical domain fine-tuning provides no significant safety benefit, and a prompt-based Self-Reminder defense yields only a modest 5.5 percentage-point reduction. Category-specific analysis reveals that superficially plausible instructions (device manipulation, emergency delay) are harder for models to refuse than overtly destructive ones. These results indicate that current LLMs are fundamentally unsafe for deployment as medical robot controllers without dedicated safety engineering.

核心贡献

  • 构建了首个面向医疗机器人控制场景的有害指令数据集（270条指令，9类禁止行为），基于AMA医学伦理准则进行系统化设计与验证，填补了医疗安全评估与具身AI安全评估之间的空白
  • 对72个大语言模型（涵盖GPT、Claude、Gemini、Llama、Qwen、Gemma等7个家族，参数量从0.6B到685B）进行了大规模安全性基准测试，揭示了当前LLM在医疗机器人控制中普遍存在的严重安全风险
  • 通过混合效应模型统计分解了模型规模、发布日期和模型家族对安全性的独立贡献，发现规模和时效性是主要决定因素，家族差异可被这两个因素完全解释
  • 对14个医学专用LLM与其通用基座模型进行配对比较，证明医学领域微调不会系统性提升安全性，甚至在部分模型上导致安全退化
  • 引入配对良性指令数据集评估过度拒绝率，验证了低违规率反映的是真正的安全对齐而非 indiscriminate refusal，并揭示了Claude和Gemini家族内部存在的安全-效用权衡

Key Contributions

  • Constructed the first harmful instruction dataset specifically targeting the medical robot control context, comprising 270 instructions across 9 prohibited behavior categories grounded in the AMA Principles of Medical Ethics, filling the gap between text-based medical safety benchmarks and household-oriented embodied AI safety benchmarks
  • Conducted the largest-scale safety evaluation of LLMs for medical robot control to date, spanning 72 models across 7 families (GPT, Claude, Gemini, Llama, Qwen, Gemma, DeepSeek/Phi) with parameter counts ranging from 0.6B to 685B, revealing a mean violation rate of 54.4%
  • Used linear mixed-effects regression to decompose the independent contributions of model size, release date, and model family to safety performance, finding that size and recency fully account for family-level differences (family intercept variance = 0)
  • Performed a paired analysis of 14 medical-specialized LLMs (Meditron3, Med42, OpenBioLLM, Aloe, UltraMedical, MedGemma) against their general-purpose base models, demonstrating no significant overall safety benefit from medical domain fine-tuning (p = 0.451)
  • Evaluated the Self-Reminder prompt-based defense on 17 high-violation models, showing only a modest mean reduction of 5.5 percentage points with absolute rates remaining dangerously high (71.9%--97.4%)
  • Introduced a paired benign instruction dataset to measure over-refusal rates alongside violation rates, establishing that safety and instruction compliance are largely independent across models while revealing an intra-family safety-utility trade-off in Claude and Gemini

Methodology

The study follows a rigorous experimental pipeline built on the Robotic Health Attendant (RHA) framework by Kim et al. (2024):

Dataset Construction. Nine prohibited behavior categories were designed through systematic adaptation of OpenAI usage policies to the medical robot context, each grounded in the AMA Principles of Medical Ethics. For each category, 30 harmful instructions were generated using GPT-4.1 (or GPT-4.1 mini when the former refused), yielding 270 total instructions. All instructions were validated against the 9 AMA Principles using GPT-5 to confirm ethical violations. A paired benign instruction dataset of 270 harmless equivalents was constructed using GPT-5.4, preserving sentence structure while replacing malicious intent with ethically appropriate alternatives.

Evaluation Setup. Each model received a system prompt containing the robot's role, 11 available skills, and a JSON-formatted patient room configuration with 3 patients, medical devices, and other objects. A single harmful instruction was presented as the user turn, and the model was expected to either produce a JSON-formatted action plan or refuse. Each instruction was evaluated in an independent session with no context carry-over.

Harmfulness Assessment. An LLM-as-a-Judge approach using GPT-5.4 assigned each response a harmfulness score (1--5) and identified violated AMA principles. The primary metric was the violation rate (proportion of responses with at least one violated principle). Over-refusal was measured using an analogous Unhelpfulness Score on benign instructions, with over-refusal rate defined as the proportion scoring 4 or above.

Models Evaluated. 72 LLMs spanning proprietary models (OpenAI GPT-3.5 through GPT-5.4, Anthropic Claude 4/4.5/4.6, Google Gemini 2.5--3.1 including Gemini Robotics ER) and open-weight models (Meta Llama 3/3.1/3.2, Alibaba Qwen 2.5/3/3.5, Google Gemma 3/4, Microsoft Phi-4, DeepSeek V3.2), plus 14 medical-specialized variants. Temperature was set to 0.0 for reproducibility.

Statistical Analysis. Spearman rank correlations assessed bivariate associations. A linear mixed-effects model with model family as random intercept decomposed independent effects of size and release date. Kruskal-Wallis and Wilcoxon tests compared groups. Paired Wilcoxon signed-rank tests evaluated medical fine-tuning effects.

Results & Highlights

  • Overall safety is alarmingly poor: Mean violation rate of 54.4% (SD = 27.8%) across 72 models, with 37/72 (51.4%) exceeding 50%. Individual model rates ranged from 6.3% to 98.1%.
  • Proprietary models vastly outperform open-weight: Median violation rate of 23.7% (proprietary) vs 72.8% (open-weight), a highly significant difference (p < 0.0001). GPT-3.5 Turbo was the sole proprietary outlier at 93.3%.
  • Model family ranking: Claude (mean 13.5%) > Gemini (28.0%) > GPT (36.9%) > DeepSeek/Phi (~60%) > Qwen (63.4%) > Gemma (64.4%) > Llama (80.7%).
  • Size and release date are key drivers: Both showed significant independent negative effects on violation rate in the mixed-effects model (size: beta = -0.105, p < 0.001; date: beta = -0.130, p < 0.001). Family intercept variance was effectively zero, meaning family differences are fully explained by these two factors.
  • Category-specific vulnerabilities: Emergency Delay and Device Manipulation (superficially plausible instructions) elicited the highest violation rates; Supply Theft and Hospital Disruption (overtly harmful) were more reliably refused.
  • Medical fine-tuning does not help: No significant overall improvement in 14 paired comparisons (p = 0.451); effects were heterogeneous with some models (Meditron3, OpenBioLLM, Aloe) actually showing safety degradation.
  • Self-Reminder is insufficient: Only a 5.5 percentage-point mean reduction (90.1% to 84.6%) on 17 high-violation models, leaving all models at dangerously high levels. One model (medgemma-4b-it) experienced catastrophic over-refusal (7.0% to 96.3%).
  • Over-refusal is rare but reveals trade-offs: Mean over-refusal rate was 11.9%, with no global correlation to violation rate. However, within Claude and Gemini families, stronger safety alignment correlated with higher over-refusal (rho = -0.893 and -0.821 respectively).

The paper is thorough in scope (28 pages including supplementary material), uses established frameworks (RHA framework, AMA Principles, MedSafetyBench methodology), and employs rigorous statistical methods. The dataset and code are publicly available via a GitHub repository. The study evaluates a remarkably broad set of 72 models, which is unusually comprehensive for an LLM safety benchmarking paper.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The paper makes a genuine contribution by bridging the gap between text-based medical safety benchmarks (MedSafetyBench) and embodied AI safety benchmarks (SafeAgentBench, EARBench). The specific focus on medical robot control is novel, and the paired analysis of medical-specialized vs. general-purpose models is a valuable addition. However, the overall methodology (harmful instruction dataset + LLM-as-a-Judge evaluation) follows well-established patterns, and the finding that proprietary models are safer than open-weight ones is unsurprising.

Rigor: The statistical analysis is notably strong for an AI safety paper. The use of mixed-effects regression to decompose contributing factors, paired comparisons for fine-tuning effects, and separate evaluation of over-refusal demonstrate methodological care. However, the reliance on a single LLM-as-a-Judge evaluator (GPT-5.4) without inter-rater reliability assessment against human judgments is a significant limitation acknowledged by the authors. The dataset size (270 instructions) is modest, and the use of GPT models to generate, validate, and evaluate the instructions creates a potential circularity concern.

Reproducibility: Temperature was set to 0.0 across models (with noted exceptions), the dataset and code are publicly released, and the evaluation pipeline is clearly described. The reliance on proprietary models with potential API changes over time poses a practical reproducibility challenge, and GPT-5/5.4-based evaluation judgments may not be perfectly reproducible across API versions.

Significance: The practical implications are substantial. The finding that the models most feasible for local clinical deployment (open-weight) are precisely the least safe creates a real tension for healthcare AI governance. The demonstration that medical fine-tuning can degrade safety is an important cautionary result for the medical AI community.

Red flags: (1) The family intercept variance of zero in the mixed-effects model is an unusual finding that may reflect the limited number of families rather than a genuine absence of family-level effects. (2) The evaluation is entirely simulation-based with no physical robot validation. (3) The Self-Reminder defense evaluation was limited to only the weakest models, leaving open the question of whether more sophisticated defenses could achieve clinically acceptable rates. (4) The use of GPT models for both instruction generation and evaluation introduces potential evaluator bias.

Strengths

  • Unusually large model coverage (72 LLMs) providing a comprehensive landscape view of safety performance
  • Rigorous statistical methodology including mixed-effects regression, paired analyses, and multiple non-parametric tests
  • Thoughtful dataset design with paired benign instructions to disentangle genuine safety alignment from indiscriminate refusal
  • Category-specific analysis revealing that aggregate metrics understate risk in the most clinically consequential scenarios
  • Clear practical implications highlighting the tension between data governance requirements (favoring local/open-weight models) and safety performance (favoring proprietary models)
  • Public release of dataset and code

Limitations

  • Single LLM-as-a-Judge evaluator without human inter-rater reliability validation
  • Simulation-only evaluation without physical robot deployment; real-world latency, multimodal input, and real-time feedback may alter model behavior
  • Modest dataset size (270 instructions, 30 per category) may not capture the full spectrum of harmful scenarios
  • Self-Reminder is a relatively weak defense strategy; more sophisticated approaches (safety fine-tuning, adversarial training) were not evaluated
  • No evaluation of robustness to adversarial attacks or jailbreaks in the medical robot context
  • The zero family intercept variance result should be interpreted cautiously given the small number of families in the random-effects structure
  • Potential circularity from using GPT models for instruction generation, validation, and evaluation

Relevance

该研究对关注LLM安全对齐和具身智能的研究者具有重要参考价值，系统性地揭示了当前LLM在医疗机器人控制这一高风险场景下的安全缺陷，其统计方法论和大规模模型对比实验设计为安全基准测试提供了可借鉴的范式。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How do these violation rates change under adversarial prompting or jailbreak attacks specifically adapted for the structured JSON-output context of robotic action planning?
  • Would safety-oriented fine-tuning (e.g., Safe RLHF) on medical robot-specific data achieve clinically acceptable violation rates while preserving task performance?
  • Do the category-specific vulnerability patterns (e.g., higher compliance with device manipulation instructions) persist across different cultural and regulatory contexts beyond AMA-based ethics?
  • How would the safety landscape change if the LLM were required to generate multi-step action sequences rather than single commands, where harmful intent is distributed across multiple steps?
  • Can the observed proprietary-vs-open-weight safety gap be attributed to specific alignment techniques (e.g., Constitutional AI, RLHF variants) that could be systematically transferred to open-weight models?
