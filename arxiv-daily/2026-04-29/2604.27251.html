Compliance versus Sensibility: On the Reasoning Controllability in Large Language Models

arXiv: 2604.27251v1

Date: 2026-04-29

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.27251v1

一句话总结： 本文通过构建"推理冲突"实验（强制LLM使用与任务不匹配的推理类型），系统性地揭示了LLM优先选择"合理性"而非"指令遵从"的行为模式，并通过探测实验证明推理类型在中间-后层线性编码，最终利用对比激活添加（CAA）将指令遵从率提升了最多29%。
TL;DR: This paper introduces "reasoning conflicts" -- forcing LLMs to use mismatched reasoning types (e.g., deduction on an abductive task) -- to systematically test reasoning controllability. Across 8 models and 4 datasets, LLMs consistently prioritize logical sensibility over instruction compliance (43.5% sensible-but-non-compliant vs. 18.6% compliant-but-not-sensible). Confidence scores drop during non-compliant episodes, probing shows reasoning types are linearly encoded in middle-to-late layers, and Contrastive Activation Addition (CAA) boosts compliance by up to 29%. A well-executed mechanistic study that bridges CoT faithfulness and activation-level steering for reasoning control.

核心贡献

  • 首次系统性地定义并量化"推理冲突"（reasoning conflicts）：通过强制模型使用与任务不匹配的推理模式（演绎、归纳、溯因），构建参数化知识与上下文指令之间的显性张力，提出四个分类维度（合理且遵从、合理但不遵从、遵从但不合理、两者皆非）来系统评估推理可控性。
  • 揭示LLM偏好合理性而非遵从性：在8个模型（含GPT-5.1、Gemini3-flash）和4个数据集上的实验表明，模型43.5%的情况下选择合理推理模式而忽略指令，仅18.6%遵从不合理指令；合理性随模型规模提升，但遵从性与架构和后训练方法更相关。
  • 机制性分析揭示推理编码位置与冲突检测：线性探测表明推理类型在中间-后层线性编码；置信度分数在非遵从推理时显著下降（p<0.05），表明模型内部具有冲突感知能力。
  • 通过对比激活添加（CAA）提升推理遵从率：对最不遵从的Olmo3-7B-IT应用CAA，推理类型遵从率提升高达29%，同时发现负向steering可减少推理冲突导致的认知摩擦。

Key Contributions

  • First systematic framework for "reasoning conflicts": Defines the tension between parametric priors and contextual instructions by mandating mismatched reasoning schemata (deductive, inductive, abductive) across tasks, with a four-quadrant classification (Sensible+Compliant, Sensible+Non-Compliant, Compliant+Non-Sensible, Neither).
  • Empirical finding that LLMs prioritize sensibility over compliance: Across 8 models (including GPT-5.1, Gemini3-flash) and 4 benchmarks, models default to the task-appropriate reasoning type in 43.5% of cases while following conflicting instructions only 18.6% of the time. Sensibility scales with model size; compliance is driven by post-training methodology rather than scale.
  • Mechanistic evidence of internal conflict awareness: Confidence (p(True)) drops significantly during non-compliant episodes. Linear probing shows reasoning types are linearly decodable from middle-to-late layer residual streams, with instruction encoding nearly perfect even in non-compliant cases -- indicating the model "knows" what was asked but deliberately defaults to a different strategy.
  • Activation steering improves compliance by up to 29%: Contrastive Activation Addition (CAA) applied to the least compliant model (Olmo3-7B-IT) at layers 14-17 successfully overrides natural reasoning priors. Anti-steering (negative multiplier) also reduces reasoning friction by encouraging direct answers.

Methodology

The paper introduces a principled framework centered on reasoning conflicts:

  1. Problem formulation: Given a question q requiring reasoning type t (predetermined by dataset design), the model is instructed to use a different reasoning type t' (t' != t). The generated chain-of-thought r-hat is classified by an LLM judge (GPT-5.1 or Gemini3-flash) to infer the actual reasoning type t-hat. Each response is categorized as: (1) Sensible and Compliant (t-hat = t = t'), (2) Sensible but Non-Compliant (t-hat = t != t'), (3) Compliant but Non-Sensible (t-hat = t' != t), (4) Neither.
  1. Prompting design: Zero-shot prompts provide high-level definitions of deductive, inductive, and abductive reasoning along with general step-by-step instructions, without few-shot exemplars, to test whether models intrinsically understand and can execute these reasoning patterns.
  1. Automated reasoning classification: An LLM-as-a-Judge framework classifies generated CoTs into reasoning types. A rule-based parser maps judge responses to four categories. The judges successfully detect "lexical mirroring" -- cases where models superficially echo instruction terminology without executing the corresponding logic. Human validation on 360 samples yields Cohen's kappa of 0.83-0.84.
  1. Confidence estimation: Uses the p(True) method -- after generating a response, the model is asked whether its answer is True or False, with the probability of "True" serving as a confidence proxy.
  1. Linear probing: Residual stream activations collected from every transformer layer after the feed-forward block, mean-pooled over the question span. Independent multi-class linear probes predict: (a) the instructed reasoning type, (b) the judge-inferred reasoning type, and (c) compliance vs. non-compliance.
  1. Contrastive Activation Addition (CAA): Steering vectors computed as the mean difference of residual stream activations between compliant and non-compliant response pairs at specific layers. During inference, the vector is added to activations at the corresponding layer at every token position, scaled by a multiplier mu.

Results & Highlights

  • Sensibility dominates compliance: Models opt for task-appropriate reasoning in 43.5% of cases (Sensible but Non-Compliant) vs. only 18.6% for instruction compliance (Compliant but Non-Sensible). "Stray" reasoning (Neither) is rare at 5.7%.
  • Sensibility scales, compliance does not: GPT-5.1 achieves 88.1% sensibility; Llama3.1-8B-IT is lowest at 58.2%. Qwen3-8B and Qwen3-32B show nearly identical sensibility (73.5% vs 73.9%), suggesting architecture matters more than scale. Llama3.1-8B-IT has the highest compliance (65.1%) while the larger Llama3.3-70B-IT has lower compliance.
  • Accuracy tracks sensibility, not compliance: Sensible CoT maintains 20-40% accuracy lead over compliant-but-not-sensible on FOLIO. The gap narrows on RECV to under 20%. Gemini3-flash shows relatively uniform accuracy across categories, suggesting heavier reliance on internal heuristics.
  • Confidence drops during conflicts: p(True) is significantly higher for compliant responses (0.7116 correct / 0.5664 incorrect) vs. non-compliant (0.6937 / 0.4831), with p < 0.05, regardless of answer correctness.
  • Probing confirms instruction encoding: Instructed reasoning type is nearly perfectly decodable from residual streams across all model families, even in non-compliant cases -- meaning the model represents the instruction but still overrides it. Compliance is a weaker signal, peaking in middle-to-late layers.
  • CAA boosts compliance: On Olmo3-7B-IT (the least compliant model), steering at layers 14-17 with positive mu increases compliance up to 29%. Negative mu consistently improves accuracy across all instruction types by encouraging direct answers and reducing cognitive friction.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The reasoning conflict framing is novel and well-defined. While CoT faithfulness and model steering are established areas, the specific intersection -- testing whether fundamental reasoning types can be decoupled from problem instances via explicit instruction conflicts -- is genuinely new. The four-quadrant classification is clean and reusable.
  • Rigor: The experimental design is thorough: 8 models across 3 open-source families plus 2 frontier models, 4 datasets covering 3 reasoning types, 33 random seeds, automated judging validated against human annotators (kappa 0.83-0.84), and proper statistical testing for confidence differences (p < 0.05). The probing methodology follows established protocols.
  • Reproducibility: Code is publicly available. Models are either open-weight or accessible via API. Datasets are public. The paper provides sufficient implementation details (vLLM, temperature settings, probe configurations). High reproducibility.
  • Significance: The finding that LLMs encode reasoning instructions perfectly but deliberately override them is important for understanding model behavior. The 29% compliance improvement via CAA is practically meaningful. However, the steering experiments are limited to one model (Olmo3-7B-IT) and mostly one dataset (alpha-NLI), which limits generalizability claims.
  • Red flags: (1) The LLM-as-a-Judge approach for classifying reasoning types, while validated, is still an approximation -- a model judging another model's reasoning type could introduce systematic biases. (2) The steering results are presented primarily on the weakest model (Olmo3-7B-IT, which has the lowest baseline compliance), raising questions about whether the approach transfers to better-performing models. (3) The "task-neglect" side effect during steering (models become so focused on the reasoning type that they fail to follow formatting constraints) is acknowledged but not systematically addressed. (4) The paper evaluates reasoning controllability but does not test whether forced compliance actually leads to worse or better reasoning in downstream applications -- the practical value of controlling reasoning type remains somewhat ambiguous.

Strengths

  • Clean and well-motivated problem formulation with the reasoning conflict framework.
  • Comprehensive evaluation across diverse models, datasets, and reasoning types.
  • Strong mechanistic analysis connecting behavioral observations (sensibility vs. compliance) to internal representations (probing, confidence).
  • The finding that instruction encoding is near-perfect even during non-compliance is a substantive contribution to understanding LLM reasoning.
  • Public code release enhances reproducibility and follow-up research.
  • The paper is well-written with clear figures that effectively communicate the four-quadrant framework and scaling relationships.

Limitations

  • Steering experiments are limited in scope: primarily Olmo3-7B-IT on alpha-NLI, with one additional model-dataset combination in the appendix. It remains unclear whether CAA would work on frontier models or larger open models.
  • The LLM judge, while validated at kappa 0.83-0.84, still introduces 15-17% disagreement with human annotators, which could propagate as measurement noise into compliance rates.
  • The practical utility of enforcing reasoning compliance is not demonstrated: forcing a model to use deductive reasoning on an abductive task may increase compliance, but does it improve or harm real-world performance?
  • The paper does not explore whether the observed behaviors are specific to the prompt formats used; different prompting strategies might yield different compliance-sensibility trade-offs.
  • The relationship between reasoning conflicts and the broader sycophancy literature is underexplored -- the "lexical mirroring" phenomenon suggests a form of sycophantic behavior that deserves deeper analysis.

Relevance

本文对理解LLM推理可控性具有重要意义，特别是揭示了模型内部推理模式编码与实际行为之间的鸿沟。对于关注LLM推理能力评估、CoT忠实性和机制可解释性的研究者，这项工作提供了新的分析框架和实验范式。激活引导（activation steering）作为轻量级推理控制手段也具有实际应用价值。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Would CAA-based steering transfer effectively to larger models (70B+) or frontier proprietary models where the compliance-sensibility trade-off might differ fundamentally?
  • Can the reasoning conflict framework be extended to multi-step reasoning tasks where multiple reasoning types are needed within a single solution?
  • Does forced compliance (via steering) improve or degrade performance on downstream applications that require specific reasoning types, such as legal argumentation or scientific hypothesis generation?
  • How does the reasoning conflict behavior change with instruction-tuning methodology -- can specific fine-tuning strategies produce models that are more controllable without sacrificing accuracy?
  • Is the "lexical mirroring" phenomenon (superficial compliance without structural adherence) consistent across languages and cultures?
