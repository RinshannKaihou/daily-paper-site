> **一句话总结：** 本文提出基于知识蒸馏（KD）的框架，将YOLOv8-L教师模型（43.7M参数）蒸馏到YOLOv8-S学生模型（11.2M参数），在INT8量化下实现3.9倍压缩和2.37倍加速，KD学生模型在INT8下精度达0.748（比直接训练高14.5%），误报率比退化的教师模型降低44%，揭示了KD传递的是置信度校准（精度）而非检测能力（召回率）。

> **TL;DR:** This paper demonstrates that knowledge distillation transfers confidence calibration (precision) rather than raw detection capacity (recall) from a YOLOv8-L teacher (43.7M params) to a YOLOv8-S student (11.2M params). The KD student retains only -5.6% mAP degradation under INT8 quantization versus -23.4% for the teacher, achieves 0.748 precision at INT8 (exceeding the teacher's FP32 precision of 0.718), reduces false alarm rate by 44% versus the collapsed teacher, and delivers 3.9x compression with 2.37x speedup -- making it the only configuration combining real-time inference, high precision, and low false alarm rate suitable for edge-deployed VRU safety systems.

## 核心贡献

- **首次系统验证KD对INT8量化的鲁棒性传递效应**：KD学生模型在INT8量化后仅下降5.6% mAP，而大型教师模型下降23.4%，表明KD训练是边缘部署中不可或缺的步骤。
- **揭示KD传递的是置信度校准而非检测能力**：KD学生模型在INT8下精度达0.748（比直接训练高14.5%），但召回率在所有模型间基本等价（0.416-0.423），说明KD无法突破架构容量限制。
- **验证任务损失衰减（alpha=0.5）是关键机制**：通过降低ground-truth监督权重、增强教师软标签影响，使学生在量化后保持更好的校准性能。
- **在BDD100K数据集上进行全规模验证**：使用70K训练图像，对行人、骑车人和摩托车手三类VRU进行专门评估，具有实际部署参考价值。

## Key Contributions

- **First systematic demonstration that KD confers INT8 quantization robustness**: KD student degrades only -5.6% mAP under INT8 PTQ versus -23.4% for the large teacher, establishing KD as essential for edge-deployable VRU detection.
- **Discovery that KD transfers precision calibration, not detection capacity**: KD student achieves 0.748 precision at INT8 (+14.5% over direct training) with equivalent recall (0.416-0.423 across all models), proving KD cannot overcome architectural capacity limits.
- **Identification of task-loss dampening (alpha=0.5) as the enabling mechanism**: Down-weighting ground-truth supervision relative to teacher soft distributions preserves calibration under quantization.
- **Full-scale validation on BDD100K (70K training images)** with VRU-specific evaluation across pedestrian, cyclist, and motorcyclist classes, yielding practical deployment guidance.
- **Demonstration of deployment viability**: 3.9x parameter compression, 2.37x inference speedup, and 44% false alarm reduction versus the collapsed teacher at INT8.

## Methodology

The paper employs a controlled experimental design comparing three training configurations:

1. **Teacher (YOLOv8-L, 43.7M params)**: Trained directly on ground-truth labels.
2. **Direct Student (YOLOv8-S, 11.2M params)**: Trained directly on ground-truth labels, identical architecture to KD student.
3. **KD Student (YOLOv8-S, 11.2M params)**: Trained with teacher guidance via knowledge distillation.

The KD framework uses a task-dampened formulation: L_total = L_task + gamma * L_distill, where the task loss weight (alpha=0.5) down-weights ground-truth supervision. Logit distillation uses KL divergence with temperature T=10, and feature distillation uses L2 distance between backbone feature maps with low weight (gamma=0.02). All three models are evaluated at FP32 and INT8 using NVIDIA TensorRT Post-Training Quantization (PTQ). The experiment uses BDD100K with standard splits (70K train / 10K val / 20K test), and 1,024 calibration images sampled from the test split. Training uses AdamW with cosine annealing for 50 epochs on NVIDIA RTX PRO 6000 Blackwell GPUs. Evaluation metrics include mAP50, mAP50-95, precision, recall, and False Alarm Rate (FAR = 1 - Precision).

## Results & Highlights

- **INT8 quantization collapse of large model**: Teacher degrades from 0.595 to 0.456 mAP50 (-23.4%) under INT8, making it unsuitable for edge deployment despite superior FP32 performance.
- **KD student matches teacher precision at FP32**: KD student achieves 0.716 precision vs. 0.633 for direct student at equivalent recall (~0.480), matching the teacher's 0.718.
- **KD student exceeds teacher precision at INT8**: 0.748 vs. 0.718 (teacher FP32) in a model 3.9x smaller.
- **False alarm reduction**: KD student achieves FAR of 0.252 at INT8, a 44% reduction versus the collapsed teacher's 0.454 and 27% reduction versus the direct student's 0.347.
- **VRU-specific analysis**: Teacher leads by 6.3-7.2 mAP50 points across all VRU groups at FP32, but KD and direct students achieve equivalent VRU performance, confirming the capacity gap is architectural.
- **Throughput**: KD student achieves 464 FPS on RTX 5070 at INT8 (2.37x speedup), all models exceed 30 FPS real-time threshold.
- **Ablation**: Task loss weight alpha=0.5 is validated as optimal; higher values reduce KD benefit, lower values sacrifice task learning.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (Moderate)**: The primary finding -- that KD transfers calibration/precision rather than recall and confers INT8 robustness -- is a meaningful empirical observation, though the KD methodology itself (vanilla logit + feature distillation) is not novel. The insight about the task-loss dampening mechanism adds mechanistic understanding.
- **Rigor (Moderate-High)**: Controlled experimental design with identical architectures differing only in training supervision. Proper use of standard splits and calibration procedures. However, evaluation on a single GPU (RTX 5070) rather than actual edge hardware (Jetson) limits deployment conclusions. Single dataset (BDD100K) and single architecture family (YOLOv8) limit generalizability claims.
- **Reproducibility (Moderate)**: Hyperparameters are fully specified (alpha=0.5, beta=0.3, gamma=0.02, T=10). Training configurations are detailed. However, code is not mentioned as publicly available. The TensorRT quantization pipeline adds an engineering dependency.
- **Significance (Moderate)**: The finding that KD is essential (not optional) for edge VRU detection is practically important for the autonomous driving community. However, the paper does not test on actual edge hardware, and the "edge" evaluation uses a desktop GPU.
- **Red flags**: (1) Training on RTX PRO 6000 Blackwell (96GB VRAM) but evaluating "edge deployment" on RTX 5070 (8GB) -- neither is automotive-grade edge hardware. (2) The paper claims KD "establishes KD as a requirement" based on one dataset and one architecture, which is an overstatement. (3) INT8 calibration uses 1,024 images from the test split -- while the authors note this is separate from evaluation, the test split is used for both calibration (sampling) and evaluation (validation), which could be cleaner. (4) No comparison with Quantization-Aware Training (QAT), which is acknowledged but limits the practical contribution.

## Strengths

- Well-designed controlled experiment isolating the effect of knowledge distillation from architectural differences.
- Clear and important finding: KD transfers calibration, not capacity, with practical implications for safety-critical systems.
- Comprehensive evaluation including VRU-specific analysis, quantization impact, precision/recall decomposition, and throughput.
- Honest discussion of limitations (single dataset, single architecture, desktop GPU evaluation).
- Task loss weight ablation provides actionable guidance for practitioners.

## Limitations

- Single dataset (BDD100K) and single architecture family (YOLOv8); generalization to nuScenes, KITTI, Waymo, or transformer-based detectors is unvalidated.
- Evaluation on desktop GPU (RTX 5070, 8GB) rather than automotive-grade edge hardware (NVIDIA Jetson); power consumption and thermal profiling are absent.
- Post-Training Quantization only; Quantization-Aware Training comparison is missing and may yield better results.
- No analysis stratified by environmental conditions (day/night, weather), which matters for VRU safety in practice.
- Code availability is not mentioned, limiting reproducibility.
- The 7.9-hour KD training time (vs. 2.7 hours for direct student) represents significant overhead that is not thoroughly discussed in terms of cost-benefit tradeoff.

## Relevance

本文对自动驾驶边缘部署场景下的目标检测模型压缩与量化具有直接参考价值，尤其在VRU安全检测的精度-召回率权衡分析方面提供了实用见解。对于关注YOLO系列模型在资源受限硬件上的实际部署效果的研究者而言，文中关于KD传递置信度校准而非检测能力的发现值得关注。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the calibration transfer property of KD generalize to transformer-based detectors (e.g., RT-DETR, YOLO-World) and other quantization schemes (QAT, mixed-precision)?
- How does the KD student perform on actual automotive-grade edge hardware (Jetson Orin) under continuous operation with thermal and power constraints?
- Would stratified evaluation by lighting (day/night), weather, and VRU distance reveal deployment-critical failure modes that aggregate metrics mask?
- Can the task-loss dampening mechanism (alpha=0.5) be adaptively tuned during training to optimize the precision-recall tradeoff for different deployment scenarios?
- What is the interaction between KD and more advanced quantization techniques (e.g., QAT, GPTQ) -- could KD+QAT yield further robustness gains beyond what KD+PTQ achieves?
