Semantic Structure of Feature Space in Large Language Models

arXiv: 2604.27169v1

Date: 2026-04-29

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.27169v1

一句话总结： 通过构建360个词的特征向量并投影到32条语义轴上，本文系统性地证明了LLM隐藏状态中语义特征的几何结构（余弦相似度、低维子空间、转向溢出效应）与人类心理语义关联高度一致，揭示了特征之间并非独立正交而是通过有意义的几何关系相互纠缠。
TL;DR: This paper demonstrates that the geometric relationships between semantic features in LLM hidden states closely mirror human psychological associations. Using 360 words projected onto 32 semantic axes (e.g., beautiful-ugly, soft-hard) across Llama 3.2 3B and Llama 3.1 70B, the authors show: (1) feature projections correlate strongly with human survey ratings (r > 0.3 to r > 0.8), (2) cosine similarities between semantic axes predict inter-scale correlations in human data, (3) 32 axes reduce to a low-dimensional subspace (first 3 PCs explain >45% variance in 3B, >33% in 70B), closely matching the classic EPA (Evaluation-Potency-Activity) structure from psychology, and (4) steering along one axis produces predictable spillover effects on others proportional to their cosine similarity. This is a rigorous, well-motivated study that bridges psychometrics and mechanistic interpretability, arguing that LLM features must be understood through their geometric relations rather than in isolation.

核心贡献

  • 揭示LLM语义轴投影与人类评分的高度相关性：将360个词的特征向量投影到32条语义轴上，发现投影值与人类语义评分的Pearson相关系数最高可达r > 0.8，最低也超过r > 0.3，证明LLM内部表征编码了与人类高度一致的语义关联。
  • 证明语义轴间的余弦相似度预测人类心理关联结构：LLM中语义轴之间的余弦相似度能有效预测人类调查中对应量表之间的相关性，说明特征之间的非正交几何关系是有意义的。
  • 发现LLM语义特征空间呈低维结构，再现经典EPA模型：对32条语义轴做PCA，前三个主成分解释了3B模型中>45%的方差和70B模型中>33%的方差，且通过CCA证实该子空间与人类调查数据派生的子空间高度对齐，再现了心理学中经典的评价-力量-活跃（EPA）三维结构。
  • 揭示特征转向的溢出效应受几何对齐程度调控：沿一条语义轴进行转向干预时，会在其他语义轴上产生与余弦相似度成正比的溢出效应，表明特征间的几何关系直接影响力学行为。
  • 跨模型验证（Llama 3.2 3B / 3.1 70B / Qwen3 1.7B / 32B）：四个模型的结果一致，增强了结论的鲁棒性。

Key Contributions

  • High correlation between LLM semantic axis projections and human ratings: Feature vectors for 360 words projected onto 32 semantic axes show Pearson correlations ranging from r > 0.3 to r > 0.8 with human survey ratings on the same scales, demonstrating that LLM internal representations encode human-like semantic associations.
  • Cosine similarities between semantic axes predict human inter-scale correlations: The geometric alignment (cosine similarity) between semantic axes in LLM activation space is highly predictive of the correlation between the corresponding scales in the human survey, establishing that non-orthogonality between features is semantically meaningful.
  • Low-dimensional subspace structure mirrors classic EPA model: PCA on 32 semantic axes reveals that the first 3 components explain >45% of variance in the 3B model and >33% in the 70B model. CCA confirms this subspace closely aligns with the Evaluation-Potency-Activity (EPA) structure derived from human survey data -- a classic finding from psychometrics.
  • Feature steering produces predictable spillover effects: Steering along one semantic axis induces off-target effects on other axes proportional to their cosine similarity with the intervention axis, demonstrating that geometric relations between features have direct behavioral consequences.
  • Cross-model robustness: Results are consistent across Llama 3.2 3B, Llama 3.1 70B, Qwen3 1.7B, and Qwen3 32B.

Methodology

The paper uses two parallel data sources: (1) a human semantic association survey (Boutyline & Johnston, 2025) with 1,750 respondents rating 360 words on 32 semantic scales, and (2) LLM activations from Llama 3.2 3B (layer 8, 30% depth) and Llama 3.1 70B (layer 40, 50% depth).

Feature vector construction: For each of the 360 words, four prompts (e.g., "Tell me about {word}", "Define the word {word}") are passed through the LLM. Mean activations across all tokens are computed per prompt, then averaged across the four prompts to produce a single robust feature vector per word.

Semantic axis construction: Using a contrastive pair design, 10 antonym pairs are compiled for each of the 32 semantic scales. Feature vectors for each antonym word are computed, and the axis is defined as the mean of the 10 pairwise differences (positive antonym minus negative antonym).

Semantic structure measurement: (1) Word feature vectors are projected onto semantic axes and correlated with human ratings. (2) Cosine similarities between semantic axes are computed and correlated with inter-scale survey correlations. (3) PCA is applied to both survey data and LLM features; CCA measures alignment of the resulting 3D subspaces.

Steering interventions: A forward hook additively applies the normalized semantic axis vector to the residual stream at target word positions. The intervention modifies the hidden state as w' = w + alpha ||w|| f_hat, where alpha = 0.33. For each prompt, all 31 off-target axes are tested in both positive and negative directions. Spillover effect is the difference in forced-choice probability between steered and baseline conditions.

Results & Highlights

  • Projection correlations: Across 32 semantic axes, correlations between LLM projections and human ratings range from ~0.3 to >0.8, with the 3B model slightly outperforming the 70B model. Results are consistent but marginally stronger in smaller models.
  • Axis-axis geometry: Both cosine similarity between raw axis vectors and correlation between word projections on axes strongly predict inter-scale correlations in the survey data. This holds across all four tested models.
  • Low-dimensional structure: The first 3 PCA components of the raw semantic axis vectors explain >45% variance (3B) and >33% (70B), versus 3.1% expected under orthogonality. CCA between survey and LLM 3D subspaces yields high canonical correlations, confirming structural alignment.
  • Steering spillover: Off-target spillover effects are strongly correlated with cosine similarity between axes (r ~ 0.6-0.7). The 70B model shows smaller spillover magnitudes than 3B, but on-target steering effects are also proportionally weaker in 70B, suggesting the model is generally more resistant to steering rather than having more independent features.

Critical Assessment

Novelty (Moderate-High): While the idea that distributed representations encode semantic structure is not new (word2vec era), applying this rigorously to LLM hidden states at middle layers with human survey validation is a genuine contribution. The steering spillover analysis is particularly novel and has practical implications.

Rigor (High): The methodology is carefully designed. Using 360 words, 32 scales, multiple models (4 total), multiple prompts per word (4), and 10 antonym pairs per axis provides substantial robustness. The comparison to human survey data is well-matched in structure. The PCA and CCA analyses are appropriate and well-motivated by the psychological literature.

Reproducibility (Moderate-High): Methods are clearly described. The survey data is from a published source. The LLM models (Llama, Qwen) are open-weight. However, exact antonym pair lists and code are not explicitly mentioned as released, which could limit exact reproduction.

Significance (Moderate-High): The findings have clear implications for mechanistic interpretability and AI safety. If safety-relevant features (true-false, good-bad, safe-dangerous) lie on a low-dimensional subspace, this could simplify auditing and control. The spillover finding cautions against treating feature steering as independent knob-turning.

Red flags: (1) The PCA analysis is inherently limited by having only 32 axes, constraining the maximum number of meaningful principal components. (2) The first three PCA components of the LLM data are not individually well-aligned with survey components (only the joint 3D subspace aligns via CCA), which slightly weakens the structural correspondence claim. (3) The steering magnitude (alpha=0.33) is chosen heuristically based on coherence trials, which is practical but not principled. (4) The paper does not address whether these findings generalize beyond English or beyond the selected word/scale sets.

Strengths

  • Elegant experimental design that directly pairs LLM feature geometry with a matched human survey benchmark.
  • Cross-model validation across 4 models (2 Llama, 2 Qwen) strengthens generalizability.
  • The steering spillover analysis is a creative and practical contribution that bridges geometric analysis with behavioral consequences.
  • Clear writing and strong connection to classic psychological literature (Osgood's semantic differential, EPA structure).
  • The conceptual framing -- features should be understood through their geometric relations, not in isolation -- is an important and timely message for the interpretability community.

Limitations

  • Only 32 semantic axes are studied, which constrains the PCA analysis and may not capture the full complexity of LLM feature spaces that may contain thousands of features.
  • The analysis focuses on a specific set of 360 English words and semantic scales; generalization to other languages, domains, or feature types (e.g., syntactic, task-specific) is untested.
  • The individual PCA components do not align well between LLM and survey data (only the joint 3D subspace does via CCA), which means the interpretability of individual dimensions is limited.
  • The steering analysis uses a single fixed magnitude (alpha=0.33) and a single layer; sensitivity to these choices is not thoroughly explored.
  • The paper does not investigate whether the geometric structure holds in the context of actual generation tasks or only under the specific prompt formats used.

Relevance

本文对理解LLM内部表征的语义几何结构具有重要意义，其发现——特征之间的非正交关系是有意义的且影响模型行为——为可解释性研究和AI安全领域的特征审计与控制提供了新的理论视角和实证基础。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How do these geometric relations change across layers? Do middle layers show the strongest alignment with human semantic structure, and does this degrade in later layers?
  • Can the low-dimensional subspace structure be exploited for more efficient multi-feature auditing or control in safety-critical applications?
  • Do these findings hold for more abstract or domain-specific features (e.g., features related to reasoning, factual knowledge, or instruction-following) beyond the concrete semantic scales studied here?
  • How does fine-tuning or RLHF alter the geometric relationships between semantic features? Does alignment training increase or decrease the correlation with human semantic structure?
  • What happens when the number of semantic axes is scaled up by orders of magnitude -- does the low-dimensional structure persist, or does it dissolve into a higher-dimensional arrangement?
