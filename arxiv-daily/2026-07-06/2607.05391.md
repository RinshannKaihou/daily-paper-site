> **一句话总结：** 本文提出 LLM-as-a-Verifier 通用验证框架，通过对打分 token logits 分布取期望得到连续奖励分数（而非传统 LM Judge 的离散打分），并沿"打分粒度、重复评估、判据分解"三个维度进行验证缩放，无需任何额外训练即在 Terminal-Bench V2（86.5%）、SWE-Bench Verified（78.2%）、RoboRewardBench（87.4%）和 MedAgentBench（73.3%）四个跨领域基准上达到 SOTA。

> **TL;DR:** LLM-as-a-Verifier replaces the discrete top-token scoring of standard LM judges with a continuous reward computed as the expectation over the distribution of scoring-token logits, enabling verification to scale along three training-free axes (score granularity, repeated evaluation, criteria decomposition). Paired with a cost-efficient Probabilistic Pivot Tournament ranking algorithm, it reaches state-of-the-art on Terminal-Bench V2 (86.5% vs. GPT-5.5 baseline 84.7%), SWE-Bench Verified (78.2% vs. Opus 4.5 76.8%), RoboRewardBench (87.4% vs. RoboReward-8B 81.4%), and MedAgentBench (73.3% vs. Opus 4.8 70.2%), and doubles as a dense RL reward signal yielding ~1.8× better sample efficiency on LIBERO.

## 核心贡献

- **提出"验证即缩放轴"的新范式**：将 verification 从离散的 LM Judge 打分提升为基于 scoring token logits 分布期望的连续概率验证框架，形式化为 Bradley-Terry 偏好模型，无需任何额外训练。
- **刻画验证缩放的三个维度**：(1) 打分粒度 G（1→20）、(2) 重复评估次数 K、(3) 判据分解 C，并证明三者互补——粒度提高信噪比、重复降低方差、分解减少提示偏差，在 Terminal-Bench V2 上将验证准确率从 73.1% 提升到 78.3%。
- **提出 Probabilistic Pivot Tournament（PPT）排序算法**：通过随机哈密顿环（Ring Pass）消除位置偏差并选出 top-k 锚点，将候选选择代价从 O(N²) 降至 O(Nk²)，在相同预算下优于 V1 基线。
- **跨四个基准的 SOTA 验证**：在编程（Terminal-Bench V2、SWE-Bench Verified）、机器人（RoboRewardBench）、医疗（MedAgentBench）三领域统一框架达到 SOTA，全部 training-free、plug-and-play。
- **验证信号即任务进度估计器**：细粒度分数与 agent 执行步骤的时序顺序强相关（Spearman VOC 在机器人任务上达 0.966，代码任务成功轨迹 0.848），据此构建 Claude Code / Codex 的 TurboAgent 扩展用于实时监控 agent 行为。
- **作为 RL 密集奖励**：在 LIBERO 上对 π₀+DSRL-SAC 实现 ~1.8× 样本效率提升（最终成功率 0.76 vs 0.69），在 MATH 上对 Qwen3-8B+GRPO 实现 ~1.1× 提升。

## Key Contributions

- **Reframes verification as a distinct scaling axis** alongside pre-training, post-training, and test-time compute, arguing that standard LM judges collapse scoring distributions into coarse discrete tokens (producing a 27% tie rate on Terminal-Bench), which bottleneck best-of-N selection even when oracle Pass@K headroom reaches 98.9%.
- **Introduces a probabilistic, training-free verifier** that computes a continuous reward as the expectation over the distribution of scoring-token logits (Eq. 3.1), converted to pairwise preference via the Bradley-Terry model (Eq. 3.2), eliminating ties entirely rather than merely reducing them.
- **Systematically characterizes three complementary scaling axes**: score granularity (improves calibration/SNR within a single forward pass), repeated evaluation (Monte-Carlo variance reduction, ~1/K), and criteria decomposition (decomposing "correctness" into Specification / Output / Errors sub-rubrics that reduce prompt bias).
- **Proposes the Probabilistic Pivot Tournament (PPT)** ranking algorithm: a random Hamiltonian ring pass cancels positional bias and ranks candidates, the top-k form pivots, and all non-pivot-vs-pivot and pivot-vs-pivot pairs are scored, cutting cost from O(N²) to O(Nk²) while concentrating budget on uncertain top candidates.
- **Achieves SOTA across four cross-domain benchmarks** (Terminal-Bench V2, SWE-Bench Verified, RoboRewardBench, MedAgentBench) with the *same* training-free framework and no per-domain fine-tuning.
- **Shows the verifier score is a calibrated proxy for task progress** (Value-Order Correlation), powering open-sourced TurboAgent extensions for Claude Code and Codex that let developers monitor/pause/rollback long-running agentic jobs.
- **Demonstrates utility as a dense reward for RL**, improving sample efficiency of both off-policy (SAC) and on-policy (GRPO) algorithms without reward-model training.

## Methodology

The framework models an agent as a finite-horizon MDP and verifies candidate trajectories produced by any generation policy. The core innovation replaces discrete top-token scoring with a **fine-grained reward estimate**:

Given a task prompt `x`, a criterion `c`, two candidate trajectories, and an ordered set of score tokens `V_score = {v_1, ..., v_G}`, the verifier extracts the conditional logprob distribution `p_θ(v_g | x, c, τ)` over score-level tokens. The continuous reward is the expectation:

**R(x, τ) = (1 / CK) · Σ_c Σ_k Σ_g p_θ(v_g | x, c, τ) · φ(v_g)**

where C = number of criteria, K = repeated evaluations, G = granularity (number of score tokens, up to 20 via Gemini 2.5 Flash logprobs), and φ maps each token to a scalar. The reward is normalized to [0,1] and converted to a pairwise preference via the Bradley-Terry model. A practical detail: they use letter-based (A–T) rather than digit score tokens specifically to enable logprob extraction for granularity scaling.

**Three scaling axes** each target a distinct error source:
1. *Granularity (G)* — finer score tokens give the decoder a finer projection space for the model's internal belief, raising the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR grows from 0.775 at G=1 to 0.799 at G=20 on Terminal-Bench).
2. *Repeated evaluation (K)* — a Monte-Carlo estimator whose variance shrinks as O(1/K); a single-pass verifier (K=1) already matches a K=16 ensembled judge.
3. *Criteria decomposition (C)* — replaces a monolithic "is this correct?" rubric with an ensemble over C simpler sub-criteria (e.g., Specification / Output / Errors), preventing the verifier from latching onto whichever factor is most salient.

**Probabilistic Pivot Tournament (PPT)** selects the best of N candidates under a constrained budget: (1) a random Hamiltonian ring pass scores N adjacent pairs so every candidate appears once in slot A and once in slot B, cancelling positional bias; (2) candidates ranked by ring-pass win-mass give the top-k pivot set P; (3) pivot rounds score all non-pivot-vs-pivot and pivot-vs-pivot pairs. The total is N + k(N−k) + C(k,2) ≈ O(Nk²) comparisons, normalized by participation count c_i to remove pivot bias.

The default configuration is G=20, K=8, three-criterion decomposition, with Gemini 2.5 Flash as the coding verifier and Qwen 3.6 35B as the robotics VLM verifier. The framework is training-free and applied identically across all four benchmarks.

## Results & Highlights

- **Terminal-Bench V2 (coding)**: LLM-as-a-Verifier reaches **86.5%**, beating GPT-5.5 + NexAU-AHE (**84.7%**), Claude Opus 4.7 + WOZCODE (**80.2%**), and Gemini 3.1 Pro + TongAgents (**80.2%**), recovering a large portion of the oracle Pass@5 upper bound of 92.1% from a Pass@1 of 83.1%.
- **SWE-Bench Verified (coding)**: **78.2%**, outperforming Claude Opus 4.5 (**76.8%**), Gemini 3 Flash (**75.8%**), and MiniMax M2.5 (**75.8%**); mean Pass@1 across the heterogeneous candidate pool was 76.1% with oracle Pass@3 of 84.4%.
- **RoboRewardBench (robotics preference accuracy)**: **87.4%**, beating RoboReward-8B (**81.4%**, trained on ~45k episodes), Robometer-4B (**78.8%**, trained on ~1M comparisons), TOPReward (**74.7%**), and the discrete LLM-as-a-Judge baseline (**70.8%**) — zero-shot, no fine-tuning.
- **RoboRewardBench MAE vs. human labels**: LLM-as-a-Verifier reduces Mean Absolute Error from **1.11** (RoboReward-8B discrete) to **0.72**.
- **MedAgentBench (medical EHR tasks)**: **73.3%**, outperforming Claude Opus 4.8 (**70.2%**), Gemini 3.5 Flash (**66.3%**), and GPT-5.5 (**65.1%**).
- **Tie-rate elimination**: On Terminal-Bench V2 the discrete judge produces ties in **26.7%** of comparisons at K=1 and still **5.5%** at K=16, whereas the continuous verifier yields **0.0% ties** at all budgets; pairwise accuracy rises from 74.7% (K=1) to 77.5% (K=16).
- **Verification-scaling ablation (Terminal-Bench pairwise accuracy)**: improves from **73.1%** (G=1) to **77.5%** (G=20) on granularity; from **74.7%** (K=1) to **77.4%** (K=16) on repetition; from 75.2–76.4% (single criterion) to **78.3%** (three-criteria ensemble).
- **Case study (query-optimize)**: over 100 repeated evaluations, the discrete 1–5 judge ties in **88/100** runs and ranks correctly in only 12/100; taking the expectation over the same 5-point distribution ranks correctly in **69/100**; scaling to G=20 ranks correctly in **77/100**.
- **Value-Order Correlation (progress tracking)**: robotics VOC of **0.966** (Qwen 3.6) vs. RoboReward-8B 0.877, Robometer-4B 0.780, TOPReward 0.565; code-generation Spearman VOC of **0.848** on successful trajectories vs. 0.769 on failed ones.
- **RL sample efficiency**: on LIBERO, π₀ + DSRL-SAC with the verifier's dense progress reward reaches the same success rate in ~1.8× fewer environment steps and a higher final success rate (**0.76 vs. 0.69**); on MATH, Qwen3-8B + GRPO with the reasoning reward yields ~1.1× improvement (~10% fewer optimizer steps).
- **ORM results (Appendix B.3)**: as an outcome reward model under Best-of-N, improves pass@1 by +9.5% on SWE-Bench Lite (23.5%→33.0%), +18.5% on AIME (71.5%→90.0%), and +21.3% on HMMT (52.0%→73.3%) over the base model, beating V1 by +6.2 points at 1.5× less compute.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: The core trick — taking the expectation over scoring-token logprobs instead of the argmax — is a relatively small, clean idea, but the paper's contribution is the *systematic scaling characterization* and the pivot-tournament algorithm that makes it practical. The framing of "verification as a scaling axis" is conceptually appealing though somewhat rhetorical: granularity and repetition are essentially "use more logits" and "sample more," which are well-known variance-reduction levers repackaged under one equation.
- **Rigor**: Strong on controlled ablations (the SNR decomposition in Table 1 and the query-optimize case study in Table 2 are genuinely illuminating about *why* the expectation beats the argmax). However, several headline numbers rest on curated candidate pools: Terminal-Bench results pool trajectories only from GPT-5.5, and SWE-Bench draws exactly N=3 from three hand-picked frontier models, so the SOTA claims are conditional on the specific sampling harness rather than the official leaderboard protocol. The authors acknowledge baseline accuracies are "extracted from the official leaderboard and model cards," meaning candidate-pool composition differs across methods.
- **Reproducibility**: Mixed. The method itself is reproducible (logprob extraction + Bradley-Terry + ring tournament), and extensions are open-sourced. But the headline benchmarks depend on access to expensive frontier models (GPT-5.5, Claude Opus 4.5/4.7/4.8, Gemini 3/3.5), and the *exact* candidate trajectories are not all released, so independent SOTA replication is costly. The Appendix B.6 "two-stage workaround" for models that do not expose logprobs is mentioned but under-specified.
- **Significance**: If the claims hold, the impact is real — a training-free verifier that beats trained robotics reward models (Robometer-4B trained on ~1M comparisons) zero-shot is notable, and the progress-tracking / RL-reward applications add practical value beyond a ranking benchmark.
- **Red flags / data quirks**: (1) The 27% tie-rate motivation and 98.9% oracle Pass@K are computed on Terminal-Bench specifically and may not generalize; (2) robotics preference-accuracy gains (87.4% vs. 81.4%) are measured on 500 randomly sampled pairs, with no per-task difficulty breakdown; (3) the RL improvements (1.8× / 1.1×) are reported on single tasks (LIBERO ketchup; MATH) with small seed counts (n=5, n=3), so the RL section reads as a proof-of-concept rather than a robust evaluation; (4) the comparison against V1 (ref [5], a 2026 concurrent work) uses different budgets, making the "fewer comparisons" claim budget-dependent.

## Strengths

- Genuinely training-free and modality-agnostic: the *same* equation (Eq. 3.1) and tournament algorithm are applied to text, multi-frame video, and EHR-tool trajectories without per-domain tuning.
- The SNR / tie-rate analysis (Tables 1–2, Fig. 7) provides a crisp mechanistic explanation for why the expectation over logprobs dominates the argmax — a contribution that generalizes beyond this paper.
- The Probabilistic Pivot Tournament is a clever, well-motivated algorithm: positional-bias cancellation via the Hamiltonian ring is elegant, and the O(N²)→O(Nk²) reduction is concretely benchmarked (Table 9, PPT k=7 at 8,242 pairs beats V1's 7N-budget at 9,800 pairs).
- Practical downstream artifacts (TurboAgent extensions for Claude Code/Codex, progress-monitoring VOC metric) make the work immediately usable by practitioners.
- Broad empirical coverage across coding, robotics, and medical domains strengthens the generality claim.

## Limitations

- Requires **logprob access** to the verifier model (up to G=20 top logprobs); for frontier closed models that withhold token logprobs, only an approximate "two-stage workaround" (Appendix B.6) is offered, limiting portability to the strongest models.
- Headline SOTA numbers are **conditional on curated candidate pools** (N=5 from GPT-5.5 on Terminal-Bench; N=3 heterogeneous on SWE-Bench), not on a standardized leaderboard sampling protocol, so cross-paper comparison is not strictly apples-to-apples.
- The RL section is a **proof-of-concept on single tasks** (LIBERO ketchup, MATH) with small seed counts (n=5, n=3); the ~1.1× gain on MATH is modest and could be within noise without more seeds.
- **Compute cost**: although PPT reduces comparisons, the framework still runs K=8 repeated evaluations × G=20 granularity × C criteria per pair, so per-task verifier cost is non-trivial and the cost-vs-accuracy frontier is only partially characterized.
- The paper does not deeply analyze **failure modes** of the verifier itself (e.g., systematic biases that survive the ring pass, or domains where finer granularity *fails* to help), and the criteria-decomposition rubrics are hand-designed per domain.
- Reliance on a very recent benchmark ecosystem (Terminal-Bench V2, RoboRewardBench, many 2026 references) means baselines are a moving target.

## Relevance

与你的研究兴趣中"LLM Inference Evaluation"和"LLM Hidden States & Interpretability"高度相关——该方法本质上是通过读取 scoring token 的完整 logits 分布（一种隐状态信号）来构建更可靠、更低方差的评估器，直接服务于 LLM 评估的可靠性与可观测性；其将验证信号用作任务进度估计器和 RL 密集奖励的思路，也触及"LLM Training Stability & Observability"与"Self-Evolving Agents & Automated Research"（agent 自我监控、自我改进）。对追求验证可靠性与计算精度误差缓解的工作有直接借鉴价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does LLM-as-a-Verifier behave when the verifier model is *weaker* than the generator (e.g., Gemini 2.5 Flash judging GPT-5.5 outputs) — is the SNR/tie-rate advantage preserved, or does the calibration degrade?
- Can the granularity/repetition/decomposition axes be predicted theoretically from verifier calibration, or tuned adaptively per-task to hit a latency budget without manual grid search?
- Does the Bradley-Terry preference assumption hold for very large candidate pools (N≫20), and where does PPT's pivot-selection heuristic start to fail (e.g., near-tied top candidates)?
- How sensitive are the cross-domain SOTA numbers to the specific candidate-pool composition — would a standardized, pooled-from-leaderboard protocol erase the margins over GPT-5.5 / Opus 4.5?
- Could the verifier's logprob-distribution signal be used as a **silent-failure / SDC detector** during long agentic runs, given that VOC already separates successful (0.848) from failed (0.769) trajectories?
- For the RL application, does the dense verifier reward introduce reward-hacking or verifier-imitation pathologies when scaled beyond single-task fine-tuning?
