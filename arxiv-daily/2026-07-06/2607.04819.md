> **一句话总结：** 本文研究结构化牛顿层并行（SNLP）能否降低加密Transformer推理的逐层非线性深度——在0.5B IDN模型上，SNLP将符号化CKKS自举次数从53降至20（2.65×），困惑度仅退化1.2%，同时误差放大更低（1.36× vs. 1.42×），且发现mHC等线性残差混合架构天然更利于加密推理。

> **TL;DR:** The paper applies Structured Newton Layer Parallelism (SNLP) to fully homomorphic encryption (FHE) Transformer inference, showing on an 8-model / 4-architecture-family simulation (Chebyshev polynomial approximation, ClimbMix-trained Nanochat models) that SNLP cuts symbolic CKKS bootstraps from 53 to 20 (2.65×) on a 0.5B IDN model with only +1.2% perplexity degradation and lower error amplification (1.36× vs. 1.42× sequential), positioning SNLP as complementary (not a replacement) to per-block FHE-friendly operator design.

## 核心贡献

- **提出NFE（Nonlinear Forward Evaluations）作为FHE成本代理指标**：定义 `NFE = (L−N)+K`（N为并行后缀层数，K为SNLP迭代数），并构建符号化CKKS成本模型证明NFE与自举次数的相关比在0.99–1.02×之间，为加密推理成本提供可计算的衡量标准。
- **将SNLP（层并行推理）从GPU墙钟时间优化迁移到FHE非线性深度优化场景**：发现IDN（Identity Newton）校正是纯加法运算，零FHE乘法深度，因此能在不增加加密开销的前提下重组层间计算图，将逐层非线性深度从L阶段压缩到 (L−N)+K。
- **建立基于Chebyshev多项式近似的FHE误差模拟框架**：通过在PyTorch前向传播中用多项式替换softmax/RMSNorm/激活等非线性算子，在8个Nanochat模型上系统测量误差累积，发现SNLP在所有模型上误差放大均低于顺序推理。
- **揭示FHE最优配置与墙钟时间最优配置的分离**：n24-K8配置以2×更少自举次数达到接近顺序推理的质量（+0.1% PPL），但在GPU上反而更慢——说明FHE的成本由序列深度而非墙钟时间主导，开辟了一个GPU优化无法覆盖的独特操作点。
- **架构级FHE友好性发现**：mHC（manifold-constrained HyperConnection）架构因残差混合为纯线性矩阵运算，顺序误差放大仅1.24×（vs. 标准0.5B的1.42×），与3B模型相当，表明线性残差混合架构天然适配加密推理。

## Key Contributions

- **Introduces NFE (Nonlinear Forward Evaluations) as an FHE cost proxy:** Defines `NFE = (L−N)+K` and builds a symbolic CKKS cost model showing NFE tracks the FHE bootstrap count within a 0.99–1.02× ratio, giving a computable handle on encrypted-inference cost.
- **Transfers SNLP (layer-parallel inference) from a GPU wall-clock objective to an FHE nonlinear-depth objective:** Recognizes that the IDN (Identity Newton) correction is purely additive (zero FHE multiplicative depth), so it can restructure the inter-layer computation graph to compress sequential nonlinear depth from L stages to (L−N)+K without extra encrypted overhead.
- **Builds a Chebyshev-polynomial FHE error simulation framework:** Replaces nonlinear operators (softmax, RMSNorm, activations) with polynomial approximations inside the PyTorch forward pass, measuring error accumulation across 8 Nanochat models and finding SNLP has lower error amplification than sequential inference in every case.
- **Demonstrates the separation of FHE-optimal vs. wall-clock-optimal configurations:** The n24-K8 point reaches near-sequential quality (+0.1% PPL) with 2× fewer bootstraps yet is slower on GPU — evidence that FHE cost is governed by sequential depth, not wall-clock time, opening an operating point invisible to standard speed-quality tradeoffs.
- **Identifies architecturally FHE-friendly designs:** mHC (manifold-constrained HyperConnection) models achieve sequential amplification of only 1.24× (vs. 1.42× for standard 0.5B models) because their residual mixing is purely linear matrix operations, matching the 3B model's robustness.

## Methodology

The paper investigates whether SNLP (Structured Newton Layer Parallelism, Han et al. 2026) can reduce the FHE cost of Transformer inference. The core problem is that under CKKS-style fully homomorphic encryption, every nonlinear operation (softmax's `exp`, RMSNorm's `rsqrt`, activations like GELU/SiLU) must be approximated by polynomials, and these per-block approximations compound sequentially across L layers — consuming the modulus budget and forcing frequent bootstrapping (the most expensive FHE operation, ~600× a plaintext multiply, 50–86% of total latency).

**SNLP reframes the inter-layer graph.** Rather than composing L nonlinear blocks sequentially, SNLP solves the first S layers as a prefix, then iteratively solves the remaining N = L−S suffix layers over K solver iterations. Within each iteration, all suffix blocks are evaluated in parallel (batchable), then a structured Newton correction propagates information across depth. Two surrogates are used for the correction:
- **IDN (Identity Newton):** `A = I` → purely additive prefix-sum propagation, **zero FHE multiplicative depth**.
- **HCN (HC Newton):** `A = Hres_attn · Hres_mlp` → a small S×S linear matrix over streams.

**NFE cost model.** The authors define NFE = (L−N)+K for SNLP vs. NFE = L for sequential, and verify via a symbolic CKKS model (each Transformer block has nonlinear multiplicative depth ≈ 4d+7 for degree-d Chebyshev softmax, 15 usable CKKS levels per bootstrap) that NFE tracks bootstrap count within 1–2%.

**Simulation framework (not real CKKS).** Rather than full CKKS encryption (computationally prohibitive at these scales), the authors replace each nonlinear operation with a degree-d Chebyshev polynomial approximation in the PyTorch forward pass: `exp(x)` on [−20,0] for softmax, simulated Goldschmidt iteration for rsqrt in RMSNorm, Chebyshev on calibrated intervals for sigmoid/tanh, with ReLU² kept exact and optional CKKS Gaussian arithmetic noise. Both sequential (`forward_sequential_he`) and SNLP (`forward_snlp_he`) paths share identical approximation code so any output difference is purely structural. A sanity check confirms bit-identical results (max abs diff = 0.0) when approximations are bypassed.

**Models:** 8 Nanochat models (L=32 layers) across 4 families — Standard (3B/0.5B, baseline & IDN-regularized), w/o x₀/VE (0.5B), and mHC (0.5B) — all trained from scratch on ClimbMix, evaluated on a fixed validation split at seq_len 2048 (mostly 200k tokens; verified stable at 1M within 0.3%).

## Results & Highlights

- **Bootstrap reduction (headline, 0.5B IDN model, Table 1):** SNLP n24-K4 reduces the symbolic CKKS bootstrap count from **53 (sequential) to 20** — a **2.65× reduction** (NFE 32→12, multiplicative depth 768→288) — with only **+1.2% perplexity degradation** and lower error amplification (**1.36× vs. 1.42×** sequential).
- **Per-operation ablation isolates softmax as the bottleneck (Table 2, 0.5B IDN, degree-12):** Approximating RMSNorm or sigmoid alone changes PPL by <0.2%, tanh by +12.8%, but softmax alone at degree 12 raises PPL by **+43.7%** (exact PPL 13.08 → 18.80); all-operations degree-12 (+43.4%) is nearly identical to softmax-only, confirming softmax dominates the error budget.
- **Cross-model consistency (Table 5, degree-12, 200k tokens):** SNLP achieves lower amplification than sequential on all 8 models; the largest gap is on the 0.5B IDN model (m4): **1.419× → 1.355× (−6.4pp)**. The mHC HCN model (m8) records the lowest sequential amplification among 0.5B models at **1.238×**, close to the 3B baseline (1.246×), versus 1.472× for the standard 0.5B baseline — because mHC's linear HC connections add zero nonlinear FHE depth.
- **CKKS arithmetic noise is negligible (Table 4):** Adding simulated CKKS noise from 40-bit down to an aggressive 20-bit precision leaves amplification unchanged (1.419× / 1.355× at all settings); polynomial approximation error (~1% per operation) dominates arithmetic noise by ~10,000× even at 6-digit precision.
- **NFE–PPL Pareto frontier (Figure 1):** SNLP-aware (IDN) training shifts the frontier — the IDN model reaches NFE=12 with +1.2% PPL degradation, whereas the baseline cannot achieve practical quality below NFE=20; below NFE=10, quality drops rapidly (+14–50%).

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Moderate and honestly scoped. The core SNLP method is inherited (Han et al., 2026); the genuine novelty here is (a) the *transfer* of SNLP's GPU wall-clock objective to an FHE nonlinear-depth objective, and (b) the observation that IDN's zero-multiplicative-depth correction is FHE-native. The "FHE-optimal ≠ wall-clock-optimal" finding is the most conceptually fresh contribution.
- **Rigor:** Reasonable but with a major caveat — **the entire evaluation is a *simulation*, not real CKKS encryption.** The symbolic cost model (NFE ↔ bootstraps) is validated only internally (ratio 0.99–1.02×), never against an actual FHE library, so absolute bootstrap/latency numbers are estimates. The relative sequential-vs-SNLP comparison is defended on the grounds that both paths share identical approximations, which is fair, but absolute cost claims should be read as projections.
- **Reproducibility:** Good on the ML side (Nanochat, ClimbMix, degree sweeps, public model recipes) but the Chebyshev fitting code and the SNLP forward passes are not released as referenced artifacts; the cost-model assumptions (4d+7 depth, 15 usable levels) are stated but not validated against a real CKKS implementation. The softmax `[−20,0]` interval, calibrated via 20 validation batches, is a specific knob others must replicate.
- **Significance:** Potentially meaningful for the FHE-ML community as a *direction* (restructure the computation graph, not just the operators), but practical impact is gated on whether the simulated advantages survive real CKKS — especially because the paper itself flags that real softmax needs comparison circuits for max-subtraction and RMSNorm needs reciprocal approximation, both **omitted** from the NFE metric. These omitted costs could erode the relative advantage.
- **Red flags / weak points:**
  - The headline softmax approximation is the dominant error source and is also the operation whose *real* FHE cost (comparison circuits) is excluded from the cost model — a circular vulnerability where the metric understates the very operation that matters most.
  - Models are small (0.5B–3B Nanochat); the "larger models are more robust" trend is encouraging but unverified beyond 3B.
  - Baseline (non-IDN) models tolerate only conservative SNLP configs (NFE reduction limited to 1.14×, <1pp amplification gain), meaning the strong headline result *requires* SNLP-aware training — a non-trivial pretraining-time dependency under-stated in the abstract.
  - No comparison to actual prior encrypted-Transformer systems (THE-X, Iron, BOLT, THOR) on a common benchmark — only conceptual positioning.

## Strengths

- Clean, well-motivated problem framing: FHE's sequential nonlinear depth is a genuine and under-appreciated bottleneck distinct from per-operator approximation quality.
- The NFE metric is simple, intuitive, and (internally) well-validated as a bootstrap proxy.
- Comprehensive ablations: per-operation, per-degree, per-precision-bits, cross-architecture, and per-layer error traces collectively pin down *where* the advantage comes from (additive parallel correction vs. repeated sequential re-feeding).
- Intellectually honest about scope — explicit "Limitations" section naming the simulation caveat, the omitted comparison-circuit cost, and the model-scale boundary.
- The architecture-level finding (mHC/linear residual mixing is FHE-friendly) is a transferable, actionable insight independent of SNLP itself.

## Limitations

- **Simulation, not deployment:** No real CKKS encryption is run; absolute bootstrap/latency numbers are projections from a symbolic model. Real-FHE comparison circuits (for softmax stability) and reciprocal approximation (for normalization) are excluded from NFE.
- **Scale:** Only 0.5B–3B Nanochat models; generalization to production-scale LLMs is untested.
- **Softmax dependence:** Because softmax dominates the error budget, the SNLP advantage is bounded by softmax approximation quality; the paper's own future-work note is that the gain may be larger or smaller with softmax-replacing architectures (gated/linear attention).
- **Requires SNLP-aware training:** The strongest result (n24-K4, NFE=12) needs IDN-regularized pretraining; vanilla baselines get <1pp improvement, so adoption requires pretraining-time changes.
- **No end-to-end latency benchmark** against existing FHE-Transformer systems.

## Relevance

This paper sits squarely in **Computation Reliability & Precision Errors** and **ML Reliability & Mathematical Foundations**: it quantifies how polynomial-approximation and CKKS arithmetic noise accumulate across depth, and shows structured Newton corrections (a mathematical re-derivation of the forward pass) can tame that accumulation. It is also relevant to **LLM Inference Evaluation** in the encrypted-inference regime, and its per-precision-bit / per-operation error-budget analysis connects to precision-error observability themes.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the SNLP error-amplification advantage survive a **real CKKS implementation** (e.g., OpenFHE/seal) where softmax max-subtraction needs comparison circuits and RMSNorm needs reciprocal approximation — both currently excluded from NFE?
- How does SNLP interact with **softmax-replacing architectures** (gated attention, linear/SSM attention) where the dominant error source shifts away from softmax?
- Can the Pareto frontier at **production scale (>7B, 64+ layers)** preserve the depth savings, or does error amplification grow with depth in a way that narrows the useful NFE window?
- Is the **"FHE-optimal ≠ wall-clock-optimal"** operating point robust to FHE hardware accelerators (e.g., HEAP-style bootstrap units) that may change the depth/iteration cost ratio?
- Could SNLP-aware training be made **post-hoc / adapter-based** so that existing pretrained models can access the low-NFE regime without full retraining?
