> **一句话总结：** 本文报告了一个反直觉的负面结果：为思维链模型提供特权上下文（如参考答案）的在线自蒸馏（OPSD），反而会破坏其长链推理能力——在 Qwen3/OLMo 五个思维模型上，特权上下文导致 avg@16 准确率最高相对下降约 17%，其根因在于特权教师信号在"分叉位置"上把验证/回溯/对冲类 token 的优势符号翻转，从而抑制了思维模型赖以探索的审议行为。

> **TL;DR:** Privileged-context on-policy self-distillation (OPSD) — where a self-teacher is given a gold solution the student cannot see — **degrades** long-budget thinking models rather than improving them: across five Qwen3/OLMo thinking models on AIME24/AIME25/HMMT25 it cuts avg@16 by up to ~17% relative (e.g., Qwen3-1.7B 0.372→0.308 avg@16), while the *same* recipe with no privileged context (vanilla OPD from a larger teacher) *improves* the student (0.372→0.392). The authors trace the failure to "fork suppression": privileged teacher context lowers fork rates at high-entropy decision points and flips the per-token advantage on self-correction markers (wait/but/maybe), so trained students emit fewer verification/backtracking/hedging tokens — precisely the behaviors that power thinking models' long-rollout gains.

## 核心贡献

- **首次系统报告特权自蒸馏对思维模型的负面结果：** 在 5 个 Qwen3 / OLMo 思维模型、3 个数学竞赛基准（AIME24、AIME25、HMMT25）上，特权上下文 OPSD 一致降低 avg@16 准确率，相对下降最高约 17%，且问题出在长推理预算场景。
- **通过精巧对照排除了"训练/评估长度不匹配"的简单解释：** 在相同的 4096 token 短预算在线训练设定下，不带特权上下文的 vanilla OPD（用更大教师）反而提升学生（0.372→0.392），而一旦给教师注入特权上下文，增益立刻翻转（gold-demo OPD 降到 0.350，自教师 OPSD 降到 0.308）。
- **提出"分叉抑制（fork suppression）"机制：** 借助 fork/lock 诊断，发现特权上下文会显著降低思维模型 rollout 中的 fork 率（中位从 ~0.083 降到 ~0.035），而对 instruct 模型几乎无影响——解释了为何特权蒸馏帮助 instruct 模型却伤害思维模型。
- **在 token 级别定位到优势符号翻转：** 固定同一条 rollout、切换两个教师打分时，特权教师把 "But wait"、"but"、"Maybe" 等自纠错/对冲 token 的对数比从正翻为负（如 "but" 从 +0.86 → −12.70），把正向信用推向"局部流畅但缺乏探索"的续写。
- **量化了审议行为的系统性退化：** 配对 rollout 分析显示，OPSD 在长度归一化后仍使验证类标记（1.63→1.35/1k tokens）、回溯类（6.45→6.29）和对冲类（3.79→3.62）均下降，说明损失不只来自"输出变短"。

## Key Contributions

- **A negative result for a trending method.** Privileged-context OPSD, previously reported to help instruction-tuned models, is shown to **uniformly degrade** five thinking models (Qwen3-1.7B/4B/8B, Qwen3-4B-Think-2507, OLMo-7B-Think) on AIME24/AIME25/HMMT25, with avg@16 drops of 0.8–6.4 absolute points (up to ~17% relative for Qwen3-1.7B).
- **Causal dissection via controlled comparisons.** Vanilla OPD from a larger *unprivileged* teacher improves the same student (0.372→0.392 avg@16), while adding gold-demo privileged context reverses the gain (0.350) and full OPSD drops it to 0.308 — isolating *privileged context*, not on-policy distillation per se, as the culprit.
- **A budget-curve characterization.** The degradation is concentrated at long rollout budgets (32k–38k tokens); at short budgets (4k–8k) OPSD can even help via response compression, which is exactly where it is easy to miss the harm in a single-cap evaluation.
- **A mechanistic account — fork suppression.** Using the SSD-style fork/lock diagnostic, privileged context monotonically lowers fork rate and raises lock rate in *thinking* rollouts but leaves *instruction* rollouts flat, explaining the instruct-vs-thinking dichotomy.
- **Token-level sign-reversal evidence.** On identical trajectories scored by privileged vs. unprivileged teachers, the privileged teacher flips the per-token log-ratio negative on self-correction cues ("But wait": +0.95→−1.96; "but": +0.86→−12.70), even on rollouts that ultimately reach the correct answer.
- **Behavioral confirmation in trained students.** Paired-rollout analysis (7,200 pairs) shows OPSD students produce significantly fewer verification, backtracking, and hedging markers per 1k tokens even after length normalization.

## Methodology

The authors write on-policy distillation in an explicit-context form. Given a problem *x*, the student policy π_S samples an on-policy rollout *y ∼ π_S(·|x)*, and the student is updated by minimizing a token-level divergence between teacher and student next-token distributions along that sampled rollout:

L_OPD = Σ_t D( π_T(·|x, c, y_<t), π_S(·|x, y_<t) )

where *c* is **teacher-only privileged context**. *c = ∅* gives vanilla OPD; *c* = final answer gives "sparse" privileged OPD; *c* = full gold demonstration gives "dense" OPSD. The teacher and student share architecture and typically the same initial checkpoint (the teacher is a copy given *c*; the student is not).

- **Distillation:** JSD divergence, 1 epoch, effective batch size 64, training rollouts capped at **4,096 completion tokens** (shared across all OPD/OPSD variants).
- **Training data:** 15k OpenThoughts math subset (prompt = problem, privileged context = reference solution); 15k Countdown examples plus a 500-example held-out Countdown split.
- **Models:** Instruct vs. thinking pairs from Qwen3-4B and OLMo-3-7B families on Countdown; five thinking models across sizes/families on OpenThoughts.
- **Evaluation:** Transfer to AIME24/AIME25/HMMT25 (30 problems each) and held-out Countdown; **16 samples/problem, up to 38,912 tokens**; metric is **avg@16** (mean per-sample correctness), distinct from unbiased pass@16. Budget sweeps at 4k/8k/16k/32k/38k tokens; controls include training-with-thinking-disabled (eval with thinking on) and a conciseness-prompt teacher.
- **Diagnostics:** (1) fork/lock classification via top-candidate dominance (top prob < 0.25 = fork, > 0.65 = lock); (2) per-token teacher–student log-ratio along fixed trajectories; (3) epistemic/deliberation-marker densities per 1k tokens, with clustered-bootstrap CIs over model–benchmark–problem clusters.

## Results & Highlights

- **Privileged OPSD degrades all five thinking models (Table 1, avg@16 averaged over AIME24/AIME25/HMMT25).** Qwen3-1.7B Thinking: 0.372→0.308 (−6.4 pts, the largest, ~17% relative drop); Qwen3-4B: 0.591→0.534; Qwen3-8B: 0.635→0.578; Qwen3-4B-Think-2507: 0.720→0.683; OLMo-7B-Think: 0.612→0.604. Bootstrap intervals exclude zero for four of five comparisons (all Qwen variants).
- **Privilege — not on-policy distillation — is the cause (Table 4, Qwen3-1.7B, avg@16 avg).** Base 0.372; **vanilla OPD (larger Qwen3-8B teacher, no privilege) → 0.392 (improves)**; +OPD gold-demo (privileged larger teacher) → 0.350; +OPSD (privileged self-teacher) → 0.308. Same 4,096-token training cap in all rows; eval at 38,912 tokens.
- **The harm is specific to thinking rollouts (Table 3, Qwen3-4B).** OPSD with thinking enabled during training: avg 0.591→0.532; OPSD with thinking **disabled** during training but re-enabled at eval: 0.591→0.590 (preserved). All rows evaluated in thinking mode.
- **The effect is budget-dependent and concentrates at long budgets (Fig. 1).** At 4k–8k tokens OPSD matches or beats base; by 32k–38k tokens it falls below, and OPSD rollouts become substantially shorter than base — OPSD "removes the gains that thinking models obtain from longer rollouts."
- **Instruct models still benefit (Table 2, Countdown).** Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 0.527→0.559 avg; OLMo-7B-Instruct 0.474→0.494; matched thinking models are flat or mixed (Qwen3-4B-Think-2507 0.776→0.765, OLMo-7B-Think 0.679→0.695).
- **Sparse privilege is safer than dense (Fig. 3).** Averaged over Table 1 models, dense gold demonstrations give the largest short-budget gains but the largest long-budget reversals and ~0.8× length compression at 38k; final-answer-only context stays closer to base at long budgets.
- **Privileged context lowers fork rate in thinking rollouts only (Fig. 4).** Median fork rate drops ~0.083→~0.035 and lock rate rises ~0.69→~0.77 for the thinking model; instruct-model fork (~0.06) and lock (~0.78) rates stay essentially flat across conditions.
- **Trained students lose deliberation markers (Table 6, paired rollouts, per 1k tokens, OPSD−base with 95% CI).** Verification 1.63→1.35 (Δ −0.28 [−0.32, −0.25]); Backtracking 6.45→6.29 (Δ −0.16 [−0.32, −0.02]); Hedging 3.79→3.62 (Δ −0.17 [−0.25, −0.10]) — reductions survive length normalization.
- **Token-level sign reversal is consistent (Fig. 5).** Privileged teacher assigns negative advantage to "But wait" (−1.96, −4.97), "but" (−12.70), and "Maybe" (−1.64), shifting positive credit toward locally fluent continuations — even on rollouts that reach the correct answer.

## Authors & Credibility

Simran Kaur, Narutatsu Ri, Yinghui He, Liam Fowl, and **Sanjeev Arora** (Princeton Language and Intelligence / Princeton University). Sanjeev Arora is a highly cited theorist (machine learning and algorithms), and the group has authored the SD-Zero self-distillation work (He et al., 2026) cited here — giving the authors direct first-hand stakes in the methods they now show can fail. The paper is a careful preprint (arXiv:2607.05184v1, 6 Jul 2026) that engages closely with concurrent work (Kim et al. 2026 on epistemic-verbalization suppression), positions itself as complementary rather than competing, and is unusually transparent about its own limitations.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** High and well-targeted. "Privileged self-distillation is bad *specifically* for strong thinking models at long budgets" is a genuinely surprising, well-scoped negative result. The fork-suppression framing is the paper's distinctive lens relative to the concurrent Kim et al. account, which is restricted to epistemic/uncertainty vocabulary — the authors argue (and show via fixed-trajectory token scoring) that the branch-level sign reversal is broader than lexical hedging tokens.
- **Rigor:** Strong experimental hygiene. The vanilla-OPD-improves / privileged-OPD-reverses control (Table 4) under the *same* 4,096-token cap is the key control that defeats the obvious "short-training / long-eval mismatch" objection, and the thinking-disabled training control (Table 3) cleanly ties the failure to thinking rollouts. Clustered-bootstrap CIs over model–benchmark–problem clusters (Table 6) and problem-level bootstrap intervals (Appendix C.1) are appropriate for a 30-problem-per-benchmark eval.
- **Reproducibility:** Good. Code/data/checkpoints are named (Ashkchamp/OpenThoughts filtered 30k, jasonrqh/Countdown-CoT-20k, Qwen3/OLMo checkpoints, vLLM), hyperparameters are in an appendix, and prompt templates (including dense vs. sparse {Answer} insertion) are given verbatim. The main cost barrier is compute: 16 samples × 38,912-token rollouts across 5 models and a 5-point budget sweep is expensive.
- **Significance:** Timely and consequential. It directly challenges a currently trending recipe (OPSD/SDFT/SDPO), and the discussion explicitly connects to production stacks like Cursor's Composer 2.5 "targeted textual feedback" — implying long-horizon agent OPSD may be viable only if privileged hint *density* and *placement* are controlled. For practitioners, the practical takeaway (use sparse final-answer hints, not full gold demos; evaluate at the *full* intended budget, not the training budget) is immediately actionable.
- **Red flags / honesty:** The authors are admirably explicit that the fork-suppression analysis is **correlative, not proven causal** (§4.3: "These diagnostics do not establish a causal explanation for the accuracy drop"). The 30-problem AIME/HMMT benchmarks are small and noisy (note OLMo-7B-Think's near-zero drop 0.612→0.604 sits within noise, and its CI in Appendix C.1 plausibly includes zero). The paper offers **no fix** — it deliberately scopes itself to diagnosing failure — which is intellectually honest but leaves the most useful contribution (a fork-preserving distillation loss) to future work. A missing ablation the reader wants: does *up-weighting* the loss at fork positions, or masking the loss on self-correction tokens (a move recent OPSD updates reportedly make — noted in Appendix A), recover the long-budget gains? That is hinted at but not tested here.

## Strengths

- Clean, causal-leaning experimental design: the privileged-vs-vanilla OPD and thinking-on-vs-thinking-off controls isolate the mechanism far better than a single before/after table.
- The budget-sweep framing is the paper's quiet strength — it explains *why* prior OPSD work missed this (they evaluated at short budgets where compression looks like a free win).
- Token-level sign-reversal evidence on fixed trajectories (Fig. 5) is a compelling, intuitive demonstration that is hard to explain away.
- Honest scoping: complementary positioning vs. concurrent work, explicit non-causal caveat, clear statement that no remedy is proposed.

## Limitations

- **No causal proof** that fork suppression causes the accuracy drop — the authors only establish a consistent associated behavioral pattern.
- **No proposed fix** for the failure mode; how to use privileged information in thinking-model distillation is left open.
- **Narrow domain:** only verifiable math (AIME/HMMT) + Countdown; continual-learning and open-ended agent settings are not tested, even though OPSD is marketed for continual learning (Shenfeld et al. 2026).
- **Small benchmarks:** 30 problems each for AIME24/AIME25/HMMT25; some deltas (esp. OLMo-7B-Think) are within noise.
- The fork/lock classification uses a simple top-candidate-dominance heuristic (0.25/0.65 thresholds); sensitivity to these thresholds is not explored.

## Relevance

这项工作与你的多个研究兴趣高度契合：它在 token 级别揭示了思维模型训练中一种隐蔽的"信号翻转"失效模式（特权教师上下文在分叉位置把自纠错 token 的优势符号反转），既属于 **LLM 训练稳定性与可观测性**（一种需要靠 budget sweep 和 token 级诊断才能察觉的退化），也触及 **隐藏状态/可解释性**（fork/lock 与审议标记的机制分析）。它还提示了 **计算可靠性/精度误差**式的问题——即表面指标（短预算 avg@16）会掩盖长预算下真实推理能力的损失，是一种值得纳入评测协议的"沉默退化"。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Would a fork-aware distillation loss — e.g., masking or down-weighting the loss at high-entropy fork positions, or preserving positive advantage on self-correction tokens — recover long-budget performance while keeping privileged-context gains?
- The paper notes recent OPSD updates *disable per-token divergence feedback on deliberation tokens* to stabilize training — does that modification eliminate the fork-suppression effect, and at what cost to short-budget compression?
- Does the privileged-OPSD failure generalize beyond verifiable math to continual-learning and open-ended agent/long-horizon tasks, where OPSD-style "targeted textual feedback" (Composer 2.5) is being deployed?
- Can the fork/lock diagnostic be turned into a real-time training *monitor/observability* signal that flags fork suppression as it happens, before eval reveals the accuracy drop?
- Is the instruct-vs-thinking dichotomy purely a function of base fork density — i.e., would any model above a fork-rate threshold (including future stronger instruct models that "think") be vulnerable?
- How does fork suppression interact with precision/numerical issues in the per-token log-ratios at extreme magnitudes (e.g., the −12.70 value on "but")?
