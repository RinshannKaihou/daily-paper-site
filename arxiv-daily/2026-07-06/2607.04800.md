> **一句话总结：** 本文以50神经元单隐层ReLU网络计算100个稀疏特征的ReLU（压缩计算toy模型）为研究对象，发现仅将损失函数从L2换为L4即可让网络自发涌现"超叠加计算"（所有特征均被近似等价地处理），并逆向工程出其机制——网络为每个特征分配稀疏二进制码字（K≈5.5神经元）、用伪逆解码器读取，仅3个标量即可恢复训练网络约1.13×的损失。

> **TL;DR:** Studying the compressed-computation toy model (50 ReLU neurons must compute the elementwise ReLU of 100 sparse features), the authors show that simply swapping L2 loss for L4 loss elicits computation in superposition: L4 spreads error evenly across all 100 features (coefficient of variation of per-feature MSE 0.031 vs. 0.999 for L2's naive half-and-half solution), and reverse-engineering reveals a sparse-binary-code encoder (K=5-7 neurons/feature) read out by a near-pseudoinverse decoder (Frobenius cosine 0.996), with a 3-scalar ansatz recovering ~1.13× the trained network's loss — far below the non-superposition baselines (~26-56×).

## 核心贡献

- **首次在训练（而非手工设计）中涌现出计算超叠加（Computation in Superposition）的toy模型**：针对Braun et al. (2025)的压缩计算toy模型，仅通过把损失函数从L2改为L4，便让50神经元的单隐层ReLU网络学会用超叠加方式计算全部100个稀疏特征的ReLU，填补了此前缺乏"野外涌现"超叠加计算toy模型的空白。
- **识别出损失指数是触发超叠加的关键变量**：系统扫描损失指数后发现，凡指数大于2（2.5/3/4/6/8）均涌现超叠加解（CV在0.028–0.046），唯独L2给出naive解（CV=0.999）；说明L4只是代表性选择而非特殊选择，L2才是异常。
- **完整逆向工程出涌现机制**：编码器权重呈双峰分布（on-code +0.34±0.06，off-code −0.02±0.01），每个特征被赋予K=5–7神经元的稀疏二进制码字，码字近似双正则（每神经元参与10–12个码字），解码器是编码器的近伪逆（Frobenius余弦0.996），并经码字交换测试100%验证。
- **用3标量参数化+人工设计码字验证机制描述的完备性**：给定码字后仅用(on-code值、off-code值、解码器缩放)三个标量即可恢复训练网络1.13×的损失；用人工设计的双正则K=5码字（经重叠最小化边交换）更达1.12×，略胜训练网络自身码字的三标量版本，且无任何合成码字能超越训练网络。
- **系统拆解各组件对性能的贡献**：通过Table 1逐步放松约束，证明网络仅在编码器与解码器均自由时达到1.00×；单独放松编码器达1.05×、单独放松解码器达1.06×，定位了尚未完全解释的残差结构。

## Key Contributions

- **First toy model where computation in superposition (CiS) emerges through training rather than hand-design:** Taking Braun et al. (2025)'s compressed-computation setup (50 ReLU neurons computing the ReLU of 100 sparse features), the authors elicit genuine CiS purely by switching the loss from L2 to L4 — addressing the absence (until very recent concurrent work) of in-the-wild CiS toy models amenable to full reverse-engineering.
- **Identifies the loss exponent as the variable that triggers superposition:** A systematic loss-exponent sweep shows every exponent above 2 (2.5, 3, 4, 6, 8) yields a superposition-like solution (CV 0.028–0.046), while L2 alone gives the naive half-and-half solution (CV 0.999) — framing L4 as representative rather than special, and L2 as the outlier.
- **Fully reverse-engineers the emergent mechanism:** Encoder weights are bimodal (on-code +0.34±0.06, off-code −0.02±0.01); each feature is assigned a sparse binary codeword of K=5–7 neurons that is near-biregular (every neuron in 10–12 codewords); the decoder is a near-pseudoinverse of the encoder (Frobenius cosine 0.996); a codeword-swap test passes 100%, confirming codeword position (not magnitude) determines the decoded feature.
- **Validates the description via a 3-scalar ansatz and hand-designed codes:** Given codewords, just three scalars (on-code value, off-code value, decoder scale) recover ~1.13× the trained network's loss; a hand-designed biregular K=5 code with overlap-minimizing edge swaps reaches ~1.12×, marginally beating the 3-scalar version of the trained network's own code — and no synthetic code beats the full trained network.
- **Systematically decomposes what each ingredient accounts for:** A constraint-relaxation table shows the network reaches 1.00× only when both encoder and decoder are free (free-per-entry encoder → 1.05×; freely-trained decoder on the ansatz → 1.06×), localizing the unexplained residual to the encoder's per-entry values and the decoder's small leftover structure.

## Methodology

The paper studies the **compressed-computation toy model** of Braun et al. (2025), modified in two ways motivated by Bhagat et al. (2025): (1) the residual connection is removed, so the target is exactly `y = ReLU(x)` and any improvement over the naive "ignore half the features" baseline must come from superposition; (2) the loss is changed from L2 to L4.

**Task and architecture.** Input is 100-dimensional; each feature is zero with probability 1−p and drawn uniformly from (−1, 1) with probability p, with p = 0.02 (≈2 active features in expectation). A single hidden layer of 50 ReLU neurons without bias acts on the input via trainable `W_in ∈ R^{50×100}` and `W_out ∈ R^{100×50}`; the task is to compute the elementwise ReLU of the input. (The original model's random 1000-dimensional embedding is shown to be incidental and deferred to Appendix A.)

**Training.** L4 loss (mean fourth power of elementwise error); Adam optimizer (learning rate 0.01, cosine annealing), batch size 8,192, for 100,000 steps; `W_in`/`W_out` initialized from U(−0.1, 0.1)/U(−0.15, 0.15). The intuition: L4 penalizes outlier errors more than L2, pushing the network to approximate all features roughly equally (superposition) rather than computing some perfectly and ignoring others.

**Reverse-engineering procedure.** (1) Inspect encoder-entry distribution (bimodal → classify on/off code). (2) Extract binary codeword matrix M, characterize regularity (biregularity). (3) Test the "codeword as identifier" hypothesis via a swap test (transplant feature i's hidden magnitudes onto feature j's codeword neurons, check decoder outputs j). (4) Compare decoder to a scaled pseudoinverse of the encoder. (5) Build a 3-scalar ansatz (on-code a, off-code b, decoder scale c) and validate with hand-designed codes (biregular/random, with/without overlap-minimizing edge swaps).

## Results & Highlights

- **L4 elicits superposition where L2 fails (Figure 2, per-feature MSE coefficient of variation):** L4-trained networks spread error evenly across all 100 features with **CV = 0.031**, versus **CV = 0.999** for the L2 naive solution (half the features perfect, half ignored). Every exponent above 2 (2.5/3/4/6/8) gives CV in **0.028–0.046**; the strongest non-superposition baseline (emulate-bias) sits **~26× higher** in L4 loss than the L4 solution.
- **3-scalar ansatz recovers most performance, far below baselines (Section 4.3):** Given the trained codewords, the 3-parameter (a, b, c) ansatz reaches **~1.13×** the trained network's L4 loss, compared to **naive ~28×**, **emulate-bias ~26×**, and **random network ~56×** — i.e., the codewords + 3 scalars capture essentially all of the superposition advantage.
- **Hand-designed biregular K=5 code marginally beats the trained code's 3-scalar version (Figure 7, Table 1):** With overlap-minimizing edge swaps, the best designed code (biregular K=5) reaches **~1.12×** the trained loss, versus the trained-support 3-scalar ansatz at **1.13×**; without edge swaps both families stay within **~1.7–2.0×**, and no synthetic code beats the full trained network (1.00×).
- **Codeword regularity and decoder structure (Figures 3–4):** Encoder entries split into an on-code cluster **+0.34±0.06** and an off-code cluster **−0.02±0.01**; every feature uses **K = 5–7 neurons (mean 5.5)** and every neuron participates in **10–12 codewords (mean 10.9)** — near-biregular. The decoder's Frobenius cosine with a scaled pseudoinverse of the encoder is **0.996**, and the codeword-swap test passes **100%**.
- **Output attenuation as an interference response (Figure 12):** The trained network systematically under-shoots the true ReLU with **mean slope ≈ 0.81** on the active branch, because shrinking active outputs reduces leaked cross-talk onto the far more numerous inactive features — and the 5–95% band across features is too narrow to see, confirming every feature is handled almost identically.
- **Robustness to embedding and model size (Appendix A):** Adding Braun et al.'s random 1000-dim embedding changes all headline quantities within 1% (Table 2); at larger scale (F=1000, N=500) CV remains low (0.06), and using the exact pseudoinverse unembedding `W_E^+` instead of the transpose gives the same L4 loss within 1%.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Moderate but genuinely fresh in framing. The architecture is inherited (Braun et al. 2025) and the residual-removal insight is credited to Bhagat et al. (2025); the core new lever is the **loss-exponent observation** (any exponent >2 elicits CiS, L2 is the outlier) and the **complete reverse-engineering** of the resulting sparse-code + pseudoinverse mechanism. This is a real, if narrow, contribution to the CiS literature.
- **Rigor:** Strong on internal consistency. Multiple independent validation angles (per-feature CV across exponents and seeds, codeword-swap test, decoder-vs-pseudoinverse cosine, 3-scalar ansatz, hand-designed-code substitution, embedded-vs-axis-aligned equivalence, model-size sweeps) cross-check the same conclusion. The coefficient-of-variation metric is scale-free and well-justified for comparing across loss exponents.
- **Reproducibility:** Good. Hyperparameters (lr 0.01, batch 8192, 100k steps, init ranges, p=0.02), the loss-exponent sweep, and the edge-swap procedure are fully specified; code is public. The toy scale (50 neurons, 100 features) is trivially cheap to reproduce.
- **Significance:** Conceptually valuable as a clean, fully-reverse-engineered instance of CiS arising from training — filling a gap the field explicitly wanted filled — and as a usable ground-truth testbed for parameter-decomposition methods (APD/SPD). However, the result is confined to a *single-hidden-layer, elementwise-ReLU, extremely sparse* (p=0.02) toy setting; the paper is honest that extension to deeper/realistic networks and richer computations is open.
- **Red flags / weak points:**
  - The 3-scalar ansatz plateaus at **1.13×**, not 1.00× — and the authors admit they do *not* fully understand the per-entry encoder values (on-codeword values are non-uniform; the degree–value correlation r≈−0.82 accounts for only ~67% of variance), nor the decoder's residual structure. The headline "3 scalars recover most performance" oversells slightly: the unexplained ~13% gap is non-trivial and explicitly named as unfinished.
  - The **sparsity p=0.02** (≈2 active features) is extreme; CiS here is demonstrated only in a regime where interference is minimal by construction. The smaller-embedding variant (d=50) shows a notably weaker CV (0.20 vs 0.03), hinting the phenomenon is fragile to the compression ratio / embedding geometry.
  - The "no synthetic code beats the trained network" claim is true only for the *full* trained network; a designed biregular K=5 code actually beats the *3-scalar* version of the trained code (1.12× vs 1.13×), so the trained network's specificity resides almost entirely in its per-entry values and decoder — precisely the parts left unexplained.
  - Only a single loss-exponent *type* (power loss) is explored; whether other loss families (e.g., quantile, Huber) elicit CiS, or whether the >2 threshold generalizes, is untested.

## Strengths

- Exceptionally clean experimental design: a single, well-motivated change (loss exponent) flips the qualitative solution, and the conclusion is buttressed by ~6 independent cross-checks rather than one headline number.
- The reverse-engineering is unusually complete for a mechanistic-interpretability paper — it identifies the mechanism (binary code + pseudoinverse), validates it (swap test, cosine, ansatz), and honestly quantifies what remains unexplained (Table 1).
- Intellectual honesty throughout: concurrent work (Gibson 2026) and prior null results (Bhagat et al. 2025) are credited rather than obscured; the paper repeatedly distinguishes "likely CiS" from "proven CiS" (note the hedged title).
- The loss-exponent sweep is a simple, cheap, and broadly applicable diagnostic that other CiS/representation researchers can immediately adopt.
- Public code and a trivially reproducible toy scale lower the barrier to follow-up work.

## Limitations

- **Toy-scale only:** A single-hidden-layer network computing elementwise ReLU on extremely sparse (p=0.02) inputs; no demonstration in deeper networks, realistic architectures, or richer nonlinear computations.
- **Incomplete mechanistic account:** ~13% of performance (1.13× vs 1.00×) is unexplained — specifically the encoder's non-uniform per-entry on-code values and the decoder's residual deviation from a scaled pseudoinverse.
- **Fragility signals:** The phenomenon weakens substantially under a tighter embedding (CV 0.20 at d=50 vs 0.03 at d=1000), suggesting sensitivity to the compression/embedding regime that is not fully characterized.
- **"Likely" rather than "proven":** The title itself hedges; the argument for CiS is circumstantial (even error spread + reverse-engineered shared-neuron mechanism) rather than a formal proof that more functions than neurons are computed.
- **Single loss family:** Only power-law losses are swept; generality of the exponent->2 threshold to other loss forms is untested.
- **No downstream application:** The paper is foundational/mechanistic; it does not connect the finding to improved training, better representations, or practical interpretability tools beyond naming APD/SPD as future testbeds.

## Relevance

本文与"LLM Hidden States & Interpretability"高度相关——它直接研究超叠加（superposition）表示与计算的涌现机制，并提供了一个可完整逆向工程的toy模型，这正是机制可解释性领域构建ground-truth检验台所急需的。同时对"ML Reliability & Mathematical Foundations"也有意义：它揭示了损失函数的数学形式（指数>2）如何从根本上改变训练所收敛的解的性质，提示损失选择是一种被低估的、影响网络内部计算结构的可靠性旋钮。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the **exponent->2 threshold generalize beyond power-law losses** — e.g., do quantile, Huber, or log-cosh losses also elicit CiS, and is there a unifying property (convexity, tail-weight) that predicts it?
- Can the **sparse-binary-code + pseudoinverse mechanism survive deeper networks** (2–3 hidden layers, multiple bottlenecks)? Does anything resembling the error-correction layers of Hänni et al. (2024) emerge when interference compounds across depth?
- The unexplained ~13% gap (1.13× ansatz vs 1.00× trained) is concentrated in **non-uniform per-entry encoder values** — what do these values encode, and is the degree→value correlation (r≈−0.82) a load-balancing heuristic that could be designed in directly?
- How does the phenomenon depend on **sparsity p and compression ratio F/N**? The CV degradation at d=50 (0.20 vs 0.03) suggests a phase boundary — where is it, and does CiS disappear in denser regimes relevant to real LLM activations?
- Can this reverse-engineered network now serve as a **calibrated ground-truth testbed for parameter-decomposition methods (APD, SPD)** — do those methods actually recover the binary-code mechanism the authors identified, and where do they fail?
- Is the **output attenuation (slope ≈0.81)** a generic signature of interference-limited CiS that could be detected in real LLM activations as a diagnostic for superposed computation?
