Interpreting Neural Combinatorial Optimization via Evolving Programmatic Bottlenecks

arXiv: 2606.19741

Date: 2026-06-19

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19741

一句话总结： 提出 EPB（Evolving Programmatic Bottlenecks）框架，利用 LLM 自动演化一组人类可读的启发式程序，把黑箱神经组合优化（NCO）策略蒸馏为程序组合，蒸馏后的代理在保持性能的同时揭示了 POMO/LEHD 的阶段性决策逻辑，并在大规模 OOD 实例上反而显著超越教师模型。
TL;DR: EPB is the first Concept-Bottleneck-Model-style interpretability framework for sequential decision policies: an LLM autonomously evolves a bank of executable heuristic programs whose per-step action distributions form the bottleneck, distilled to match black-box NCO teachers (POMO, LEHD) on TSP/CVRP with negligible loss — and the readable surrogate actually outperforms the teacher on out-of-distribution large-scale instances.

核心贡献

  • 首次将 Concept Bottleneck Model（CBM）思想迁移到序列决策策略上：用 LLM 自动生成的"可执行程序"取代 CBM 中预先定义的静态概念，作为神经组合优化（NCO）策略的瓶颈层。
  • 提出双块迭代优化框架：Block I 在固定容量下做混合"文本-数值"梯度下降（用 SGD 更新路由网络，用 TextGrad 经 LLM 修订程序代码）；Block II 通过"面向故障的 Add"和"冗余剪枝的 Drop"动态调整程序库容量。
  • 提出输出条件动态路由（OCDR，scaled dot-product attention）作为状态相关的 student router，并使用 stop-gradient 切断路由键路径，使归因信号只反映程序在混合路径上的直接作用。
  • 在 TSP/CVRP 上对 POMO 与 LEHD 做蒸馏，蒸馏代理与教师差距很小（搜索后 0.03%–2.16%），并揭示出可解释的阶段性/几何性决策模式。
  • 实证发现：程序化瓶颈作为归纳偏置可"剥离过拟合噪声"，使 student 在 500 规模 OOD 实例上比教师好 −13% ~ −15%，把可解释性重塑为"研究的催化剂"而不仅是可信度工具。

Key Contributions

  • First CBM-style interpretability paradigm for sequential decision policies, where "concepts" are executable programs generated/evolved by LLMs rather than a fixed a-priori vocabulary.
  • A novel iterative two-block optimizer that handles three coupled but incompatible search spaces (symbolic programs, continuous routing weights, bank cardinality) via alternating block coordinate descent.
  • Hybrid Textual-Numerical Gradient Descent: jointly trains routing network with SGD and rewrites the heuristic bank with TextGrad under a shared KL distillation loss, with a failure-attribution score that selects which heuristic-sample pairs receive textual feedback.
  • Output-Conditioned Dynamic Routing (OCDR) attention router whose keys are tied to each program's recommended action, avoiding per-heuristic learned keys and enabling stable Add/Drop without re-initialization.
  • Empirical evidence that an interpretable programmatic bottleneck can preserve and even improve a black-box teacher's OOD generalization (−15% gaps on POMO-TSP-500 / CVRP-500).

Methodology

EPB distills a teacher NCO policy π_T into a student π_S(·|s; θ, B) composed of (i) a heuristic bank B = {(c_m, d_m)} of executable code strategies plus natural-language descriptions, and (ii) a routing network θ that selects which heuristics drive the prediction per state.

Forward pass (3 steps): each heuristic c_m evaluates the state to produce a per-action distribution V_m(s) via temperature softmax; the OCDR attention router computes state-conditional weights w_m(s; θ, sg(V)) (stop-gradient on V); the final prediction is the weighted mixture Σ w_m(s)·V_m(s).

The router query Q encodes the current decision state; each key K_m is the expected node embedding under program m's output, so attention measures alignment between "what the state asks for" and "what program m offers." Tying keys to outputs makes the router treat similar heuristics similarly and lets newly Added heuristics inherit a well-defined key.

Training is a nested two-block scheme (Algorithm 1):

  • Block I (fixed capacity, Hybrid Textual-Numerical Gradient Descent): router θ updated by SGD on the KL distillation loss; the bank updated periodically via TextGrad. A failure-attribution score (routing weight × top-action disagreement × KL deviation from teacher) selects the top-K failing states per heuristic to drive textual revision, so mixture-level errors are routed to the responsible program rather than naively to all.
  • Block II (capacity revision): Add collects states where every heuristic fails, asks the LLM to partition them into K hypothesized failure modes with candidate specialists, refines candidates on their fault subset (accepted only if held-out agreement improves); Drop performs greedy leave-one-out pruning, removing the heuristic whose removal least increases validation loss within a tolerance.

The scheme approximates block coordinate descent with heterogeneous blocks: fast SGD on the router, slow LLM-based TextGrad rewrites on the symbolic bank.

Results & Highlights

Distillation fidelity (Table 1, mean tour cost): student stays close to teacher — greedy gaps 1.0–3.9%; after search (multi-start for POMO, random-reconstruction for LEHD) gaps shrink to 0.03–1.25% (POMO) and 0.40–2.16% (LEHD).

Out-of-distribution (Table 3, zero-shot to much larger sizes) — the headline result: the distilled surrogate beats its teacher, with the programmatic bottleneck acting as a transfer-friendly inductive bias:

  • POMO-TSP-500: −15.01% (greedy), −14.32% (search)
  • POMO-CVRP-500: −13.57% (greedy), −15.22% (search)
  • LEHD (a model designed for cross-size generalization): student stays on par.

Interpretability insights:

  • POMO on TSP behaves phase-adaptively (heuristics condition on tour progress / unvisited count, shifting from early hull/outlier exploration to late start-aware closure). On TSP-100, only 16/99 steps deviate from nearest-neighbor, each in regions consistent with the firing heuristic (hull-sweep, isolated-rescue, far-from-depot-first).
  • LEHD on TSP is geometry-driven (local-geometry, insertion-cost, angular-sweep, pocket-cleaning corrections), consistent with its stronger OOD generalization since local rules transfer better than fixed tour-progress schedules.
  • POMO on CVRP shows a clear stage transition: sector-structured exploration → savings/proximity and capacity-fit → depot-oriented closure picking up on-the-way customers.

Ablations: numerical or textual gradients alone saturate at fixed bank size; activating capacity management at plateau breaks the plateau with a clear accuracy jump; OCDR uniformly beats a static-key baseline.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty — High. This is the first CBM-style interpretability paradigm for sequential decision policies in NCO. The reframing of CBM "concepts" as LLM-evolved executable programs, plus the dual-block hybrid textual-numerical optimizer, is genuinely new and not a trivial port of vision CBMs (which the authors justify by pointing out the object-mismatch and setting-mismatch problems).
  • Rigor — Moderate-to-High. Two teachers, two problem types, multiple sizes, in-distribution + OOD, and three targeted ablations (gradients, capacity management, routing). Distillation gaps are reported honestly and include search variants. The failure-attribution derivation (stop-gradient, mixture-path sensitivity) is worked through.
  • Reproducibility — Moderate. Built on public POMO/LEHD code and the published TextGrad library, which helps. But the LLM-in-the-loop pipeline (Add/Drop/TextGrad rewrites) introduces model-version and seed sensitivity that is not characterized. No code at submission; prompts shown only partially (one LEHD-TSP-100 example in §I).
  • Significance — Moderate. Strong within NCO interpretability (a niche but growing subfield); the OOD-superiority result is the most broadly interesting claim and recasts interpretability as a generalization tool. Broader ML impact depends on whether the EPB recipe extends beyond routing.
  • Red flags / things to probe: (a) the OOD "improvement" is largely a story about POMO generalizing poorly at scale — the student looks great partly because the teacher is bad there; LEHD (designed for cross-size) shows the student is only on par, not better. (b) LLM non-determinism and cost of TextGrad/Add/Drop are undisclosed. (c) The router is explicitly acknowledged as still a black box, so the surrogate is only partially interpretable. (d) Bank size hyperparameters and convergence criteria (plateau detection, drop tolerance) are tuning-sensitive but under-specified in the main text.

Strengths

  • Genuinely novel framing: program-as-concept + state-dependent router elegantly resolves the object/setting mismatches that block naive CBM application to NCO.
  • The hybrid textual-numerical optimization is technically clean, and the failure-attribution score is a sensible way to allocate mixture-level error to individual symbolic heuristics.
  • The distilled surrogates yield concrete, human-readable operational insights (phase-based vs geometry-based; sector→capacity→depot transition) that look like real OR concepts rather than post-hoc rationalizations.
  • The OOD-generalization gain is a compelling, non-obvious finding that elevates the work beyond pure interpretability.
  • Honest, useful ablations isolating the contribution of textual gradients, capacity management, and dynamic routing.

Limitations

  • Router network remains a black box; the student is only semi-interpretable (acknowledged by the authors).
  • Distilled surrogate is an approximation, not a guaranteed faithful proxy of the teacher.
  • LLM-in-the-loop optimization: cost, latency, and determinism are not quantified; results may be sensitive to the underlying LLM and sampling seed.
  • Scope limited to routing (TSP, CVRP) and two teachers; constrained NCO (time windows, pickups-deliveries, scheduling) is left to future work.
  • Capacity-management hyperparameters (plateau thresholds, drop tolerance, Add candidate count K) are likely brittle and under-detailed in the main text.
  • The most striking OOD gains hinge on POMO's known weak large-scale generalization; on a teacher built for cross-size transfer (LEHD), the student merely matches.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How sensitive is the discovered program portfolio to the choice and seed of the underlying LLM used by TextGrad/Add/Drop? Is there a stable "core" heuristic set across runs?
  • The router is still neural. Can it be replaced by a transparent/programmatic routing rule (e.g., a decision tree over state features) without losing the OOD gains?
  • The OOD win is largest for POMO, which generalizes poorly at scale. Does EPB still help when the teacher is already strong OOD (e.g., does it ever hurt LEHD at 1000+ nodes)?
  • Does the failure-attribution score (routing weight × disagreement × KL) bias the bank toward teacher quirks rather than true problem structure — i.e., is the surrogate faithful or just a good mimic?
  • Can EPB extend to constrained NCO (VRPTW, PDPTW, scheduling), and can the distilled banks expose specific teacher failure modes (e.g., constraint-violation-prone states) for targeted retraining?
  • Could the same "evolving programmatic bottleneck" recipe be applied to autoregressive LLM policies or RL agents to surface composable, interpretable decision rules?
  • What is the compute/cost breakdown of the LLM calls vs. neural training, and does the method scale to problems with very large action sets where per-step softmax over actions is expensive?
