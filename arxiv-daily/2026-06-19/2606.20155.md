> **一句话总结：** 提出一个无需参考照片、训练数据或白盒访问的全黑盒行为探针，利用生成图像的分散度 δ 与跨名字质心相似度 scen 来判别 T2I 模型是否记忆了某人身份；并发布覆盖 1269 个公众人物、跨知名度层级的 NAMESAKES 基准（SDXL-Base 上预测 R²=0.58、高知名度名字二分类 AUC=0.95）。

> **TL;DR:** A fully black-box, reference-free behavioral probe distinguishes memorized from fabricated identities in text-to-image models using inter-generation dispersion (δ) and cross-name centroid similarity (scen), benchmarked on a new 1,269-name NAMESAKES dataset of public figures stratified by fame.

## 核心贡献
- 提出全黑盒行为探针，无需 ground-truth 照片、训练数据或白盒模型内部访问，即可区分 T2I 模型对某名字的生成是「身份记忆」还是「身份虚构」。
- 设计两个互补的无参考探针分数：单一名字内的生成分散度 δ，以及跨名字比较的质心相似度 scen。
- 构建 NAMESAKES 数据集：1269 个公众人物的真实姓名、ground-truth 人脸、知名度（基于 Wikipedia 页面浏览量）以及经扰动构造的近似虚构姓名（保留首字母、最小非零 Levenshtein 距离）。
- 在 SDXL-Base/Turbo、Flux1-Dev/Schnell 四个模型上验证探针预测 reference similarity 的有效性，并区分真实姓名与扰动姓名。
- 揭示不同模型家族在身份记忆行为上的显著差异（SDXL 记忆更强，Flux 更倾向虚构，与社区关于 Flux 可能使用图像重标注/姓名屏蔽训练的观察一致）。

## Key Contributions
- A fully black-box behavioral probe requiring no reference photos, training-data access, or white-box model internals to detect identity memorization vs. fabrication — applicable to commercial closed T2I models.
- Two complementary reference-free probe scores: dispersion δ (within a name's generations) and centroid similarity scen (across names), which are near-orthogonal (Pearson 0.26 for SDXL-Base, −0.02 to −0.06 for others).
- The NAMESAKES benchmark of 1,269 public figures with names, ground-truth faces, fame estimates (Wikipedia log-pageviews), and orthographically-matched perturbed names; designed for 5-fold cross-validation with held-out centroid computation to prevent leakage.
- Empirical validation across four SOTA T2I models showing the probe substantially predicts reference similarity and separates real from perturbed names, with per-model fitting accounting for each model's memorization behavior and generation diversity.
- Cross-model-family insights: SDXL reproduces celebrity likenesses more faithfully (sgt > 0.37) while Flux fabricates generic faces (sgt < 0.07), consistent with community reports about Flux name handling.

## Methodology
- **Reference similarity (sgt):** mean cosine similarity between the ground-truth face embedding and generated face embeddings (k = 4 images per name), via ArcFace (Deng et al., 2019) after facial alignment. This operationalizes identity memorization and is used only for post-hoc evaluation — the probe itself never accesses ground-truth photos.
- **Dispersion (δ):** mean squared distance of generation embeddings from their centroid (trace of the sample covariance matrix). Memorized identities produce tight clusters (low δ).
- **Centroid similarity (scen):** maximum cosine similarity between a name's normalized centroid and the centroids of all other NAMESAKES names. Fabricated names converge to generic, demographically-driven centroids (high scen).
- **Dataset construction:** ~190K Wikipedia entries with structured person info; log-pageview fame f is approximately log-normal, so stratified-uniform sampling over binned f (filtered for freely-licensed photos) yields 1,269 entries with even fame coverage.
- **Name perturbation:** each name component is replaced by a Wikipedia-pool candidate with minimum nonzero Levenshtein edit distance subject to matching the first letter (e.g., Jodie Foster → Jolie Fuster); validated to produce more dispersed generations than randomly shuffled names on SDXL-Base (δ: 0.58 vs 0.55, p=0.008).
- **Evaluation:** OLS linear regression to predict sgt from probe scores, and a logistic-regression real-vs-perturbed classifier, both fitted per-model under 5-fold cross-validation with held-out test centroids.

## Results & Highlights
- Predicting reference similarity: R² = 0.581 for SDXL-Base (above Cohen's large-effect benchmark), 0.438 for SDXL-Turbo, 0.349 for Flux1-Dev, 0.325 for Flux1-Schnell using both probes.
- Real-vs-perturbed separability: AUC 0.859 / Acc 0.791 for SDXL-Base; AUC > 0.77 across all models. Restricted to high-fame names: AUC 0.947 and >90% accuracy for SDXL-Base.
- Fame (log-pageviews) moderately correlates with reference similarity (r = 0.36–0.53, all p << 0.001), validating stratified sampling.
- Distilled/aesthetic models (SDXL-Turbo, Flux1-Schnell) show weaker dispersion signal due to lower generation diversity; the centroid-similarity probe partially compensates.
- Demographic breakdown reported transparently: NAMESAKES is majority White (938) and male (757), inherited from English Wikipedia's skew — an acknowledged limitation.
- Cross-model qualitative comparison (Casey Wilson): SDXL reproduces the identity (sgt > 0.37) while Flux produces generic faces (sgt < 0.07).

## Critical Assessment
- **Novelty:** Genuinely novel framing. Most prior identity-leakage and memorization work requires ground-truth photos (Webster 2021; Vora 2025), training-data access (Carlini 2023; Somepalli 2023), or white-box gradient/internal access (Wen 2024; Hintersdorf 2024b). This probe is fully black-box and targets identity-level (not image-level) memorization.
- **Rigor:** Solid protocol: 5-fold CV, held-out centroid computation to prevent leakage, balanced real/perturbed design, fame stratification, and per-model probe fitting. Honest reporting of demographic skew and face-embedding bias.
- **Reproducibility:** Likely high if code/data are released as promised; pipeline uses standard components (ArcFace, SDXL, Flux). Only internal-only API usage is Gemini 3.1 Pro for demographic annotation (99% validated on 100 items).
- **Significance:** Practically valuable for privacy auditing and evaluating identity-unlearning methods on T2I models whose training data is undisclosed — a real-world regulatory gap.
- **Red flags:** Reference similarity is itself a noisy proxy (limited by face-embedding accuracy and generation variance), imposing an inherent ceiling on achievable R²; performance on distilled models is materially weaker (R² as low as 0.325); dataset is small (1,269) and demographically skewed; the statistical relationship is not guaranteed to hold for individual samples and should not be used as a standalone diagnostic without human validation. Cross-model R² differences confound "probe effectiveness" with underlying model memorization strength.

## Strengths
- Fully black-box and reference-free — directly applicable to closed commercial T2I systems where training data and weights are unavailable.
- Elegant dual-probe design with intuitive geometric justification; the two probes are complementary and near-orthogonal for most models.
- A thoughtfully constructed benchmark with fame stratification, paired perturbed names enabling separability testing, and a cross-validation protocol that avoids leakage.
- Transparent, ethically careful reporting: datasheet, opt-out mechanism, non-commercial licensing, and explicit acknowledgment of inherited demographic biases.

## Limitations
- Relies on neural face embeddings (ArcFace) with documented gender/racial bias; accuracy of the proxy is bounded by embedding quality.
- Small dataset (1,269) skewed toward English-Wikipedia demographics (majority White/male); no handling of name collisions (e.g., "Michael Jordan"), name changes, or dramatic appearance change over time.
- Probe underperforms on low-diversity/distilled models, where δ provides weak signal.
- Statistical predictions are aggregate — not reliable per-sample — so the probe is not a conclusive individual diagnostic.
- Ground-truth reliance during evaluation means sgt is only a proxy for "true" memorization, capping achievable R².

## Questions for Further Exploration
- How would the probe generalize to newer closed T2I systems, video/multimodal generative models, or text-to-3D avatars?
- Can mechanistic/white-box signals (e.g., denoising intermediate features, attention maps) complement the black-box scores to push past the R² ceiling imposed by reference-similarity noise?
- Is identity memorization unequally distributed across demographic groups after controlling for fame, and how does probe reliability vary by subgroup?
- Could the probe be adapted to quantitatively verify identity-unlearning methods, and to detect partial vs. full memorization?
- What is the effect of prompt phrasing, number of generations k, and choice of face encoder (e.g., FaceNet vs ArcFace vs VGG-Face2) on probe stability?
- Does the centroid-similarity probe's reliance on a reference name-pool create vulnerabilities (e.g., adversarial name selection) when used as an auditing tool?
