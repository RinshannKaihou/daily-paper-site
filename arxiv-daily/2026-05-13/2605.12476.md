> **一句话总结：** 本文从梯度动力学角度揭示SMoE模型中路由器与专家之间存在几何耦合——匹配的路由器权重和专家输入权重沿相同输入方向累积梯度——并证明辅助负载均衡损失会破坏这种耦合，同时提出一种无需参数的在线K-Means路由器来验证这一机制。

> **TL;DR:** This paper reveals that in Sparse Mixture-of-Experts (SMoE) models, matched router-expert pairs receive gradients along the same input direction during backpropagation, causing them to co-evolve as coupled accumulators of their shared routed-token history. Empirically, higher router scores predict stronger expert neuron activations (ρ=0.43). Auxiliary load-balancing losses break this coupling by making distinct router directions ~3× more similar. A parameter-free online K-Means router based on cosine similarity to EMA centroids achieves the lowest load imbalance among all variants (MaxVio=0.037 vs. 0.084 for loss-free), with only 2.6% perplexity increase, confirming that centroid tracking captures a substantial part of what learned routers acquire.

## 核心贡献

- **揭示路由器-专家几何耦合机制：** 从梯度层面证明，对于每个被选中的专家，其对应的路由器权重向量和专家输入侧权重矩阵在反向传播时沿相同的输入方向更新，仅标量系数不同。这意味着匹配的路由器-专家对会累积相同的被路由token历史。
- **实证验证耦合在推理时的体现：** 在1B参数SMoE模型中，更高的路由器分数确实对应更强的专家门控神经元激活（相关系数ρ=0.43），证明路由决策在专家内部计算中被"镜像"反映。
- **揭示辅助负载均衡损失的负面效应：** 辅助损失向所有路由器权重向量（无论是否被选中）注入输入方向的梯度，破坏了专家特定的累积结构，使不同路由器方向的余弦相似度提高近3倍（μ=0.63 vs. 0.13）。
- **提出并验证在线K-Means路由器：** 用非可学习的EMA质心替代可学习的路由器权重，基于余弦相似度路由，无需辅助损失即可实现最低的负载不均衡（MaxVio=0.037），仅带来2.6%的困惑度增加。

## Key Contributions

- **Gradient-level geometric coupling:** Proves that matched router weight vectors and expert input-side weight matrices receive backpropagation updates proportional to the same hidden-state direction x, differing only in scalar coefficients — a consequence of the chain rule, not an added constraint.
- **Empirical activation-level evidence:** In a 1B SMoE trained from scratch (~50B tokens), higher router scores monotonically predict stronger expert gate-neuron activations (ρ=0.43, p=1.2×10⁻⁸¹), demonstrating that routing decisions are mirrored inside selected experts.
- **Auxiliary loss breaks coupling:** Shows that the standard auxiliary load-balancing loss sends input-directed gradients to every router weight vector on every token (regardless of selection), making distinct router directions nearly 3× more similar (off-diagonal mean cosine similarity μ=0.63 with aux-loss vs. μ=0.13 without).
- **Parameter-free K-Means router:** Introduces a router using non-learnable EMA centroids of routed hidden states with cosine similarity scoring and sign-updated biases. Achieves the lowest load imbalance (MaxVio=0.037) among all variants with only 2.6% training perplexity cost, confirming that centroid tracking captures a substantial component of what learned routers acquire.

## Methodology

The paper combines theoretical gradient analysis with controlled experiments on a 1B-parameter SMoE model (L=9 SMoE layers, d=1024, N=64 routed experts, top-K=6, 2 shared experts, expert hidden width 512) trained from scratch on OLMoE-mix-0924 for ~50B tokens.

**Theoretical framework (Section 3):** The authors derive the gradients for both the router weight vector rᵢ and the expert input-side weight matrix Wᵢᵘᵖ. For a token x routed to expert i, both receive updates of the form ∝ x, differing only in scalar coefficients (δᵢ,ₖ for expert rows, γᵢ for router). Over training, matched pairs accumulate the same routed-token history. Critically, experts outside the top-K receive no gradient, preserving expert-specific accumulation.

**Empirical coupling measurement (Section 4):** The authors compare router scores zᵢ(x) = rᵢᵀx with average gate-neuron activations σ(Wᵢᵍᵃᵗₑx) for the same token-expert pairs. Both are computed independently in the same forward pass. Scores and activations are normalized per-layer and per-expert before pooling.

**Auxiliary loss analysis (Section 5):** Two 1B SMoEs are trained from the same initialization — one with Switch-style auxiliary load-balancing loss + router z-loss, one with bias-only loss-free balancing. Pairwise cosine similarities between router weight vectors are compared at three stratified layers.

**K-Means router (Section 6):** Replaces learned routing weights with EMA centroids (α=0.99) updated per micro-batch. Tokens are scored by cosine similarity to centroids plus sign-updated biases (γ=10⁻³). Four variants are compared: Aux-Loss, Loss-Free, Loss-Free+Seq-Aux, and K-Means.

## Results & Highlights

| Method | Train PPL | C4-en PPL | Pile PPL | MaxVio | Router Params |
|--------|-----------|-----------|----------|--------|---------------|
| Aux-Loss | 15.09 | 20.54 | 11.82 | 0.526 | 0.59M |
| Loss-Free + Seq-Aux | 15.03 | 20.44 | 11.77 | 0.102 | 0.59M |
| Loss-Free | 15.01 | 20.40 | 11.76 | 0.084 | 0.59M |
| K-Means (ours) | 15.40 | 21.01 | 12.09 | **0.037** | **0** |

- Router scores and expert activations are significantly correlated (ρ=0.43, p=1.2×10⁻⁸¹), providing direct functional evidence of geometric coupling.
- Auxiliary loss causes router weight vector collapse: off-diagonal cosine similarity μ=0.63 with aux-loss vs. μ=0.13 without (at middle layers).
- K-Means achieves 14× lower MaxVio than Loss-Free (0.037 vs. 0.526 for Aux-Loss, 0.084 for Loss-Free) with no trainable routing parameters.
- The 2.6% training PPL gap between K-Means and Loss-Free quantifies the contribution of centroid tracking vs. other degrees of freedom in learned routers.

## Authors & Credibility

The authors are from the Blavatnik School of Computer Science and AI at Tel Aviv University. **Mor Geva** is a well-established researcher in mechanistic interpretability of language models, known for work on knowledge localization and causal tracing in transformer layers. The paper provides complete mathematical derivations in appendices, releases code at `github.com/sagearc/router-expert-geometry`, and uses standard, reproducible experimental setups (OLMoE-mix-0924 dataset, publicly documented architectures). Experiments were run on NVIDIA H100 and AMD MI325X nodes.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty (High):** The gradient-level analysis of router-expert coupling is original and provides a clean mechanistic explanation for phenomena previously only observed empirically (e.g., correlated router-expert weights in pretrained models). The connection between auxiliary load-balancing and router geometry collapse is a new insight. The K-Means router is a constructive validation rather than a primary contribution.

**Rigor (High):** Theoretical derivations are clean and follow directly from the chain rule. The experimental design is well-controlled — models differ only in the balancing rule, starting from the same initialization. The activation-level evidence (router scores vs. expert activations measured independently) is a strong empirical test of the theory.

**Reproducibility (High):** Code is released. All architectural details, training hyperparameters (AdamW, β₁=0.9, β₂=0.95, lr schedule, batch size), and datasets are specified. The 1B configuration follows Wang et al. [7].

**Significance (Medium-High):** The findings have practical implications for SMoE design — they explain *why* auxiliary losses degrade specialization and suggest that future routing methods should preserve natural router-expert geometry. The K-Means router, while not intended as a practical replacement, provides strong evidence for the theory. Impact is somewhat limited by the 1B scale.

**Red flags:**
- All empirical results are at 1B scale only; it remains unclear whether geometric coupling strengthens, weakens, or changes qualitatively at larger scales (7B, 70B+).
- The K-Means router's 2.6% PPL gap is not trivial — at production scale this could be significant.
- The analysis focuses exclusively on SwiGLU experts with gate-branch activations; other expert architectures are not considered.

## Strengths

- Provides a principled, gradient-level explanation for router-expert co-evolution that is simpler and more general than prior empirical observations.
- Clean experimental design: controlled comparisons isolating the effect of balancing rules.
- The K-Means router is an elegant constructive test — replacing learned weights with explicit centroids validates the theory directly.
- Dual theoretical + empirical approach makes the argument compelling from both angles.
- Clear, well-structured writing with helpful figures (especially Figure 1 illustrating the coupling mechanism).

## Limitations

- **Scale:** Only tested on a single 1B configuration. Geometric coupling may not hold, or may manifest differently, at the scales where SMoEs are most impactful (e.g., DeepSeek-V3 at 671B).
- **Expert architecture:** Analysis is specific to SwiGLU experts. Other FFN variants or non-FFN experts are not covered.
- **Activation scope:** Only gate-branch activations (σ(Wigate x)) are analyzed; the up-projection branch and other weight matrices are left for future work.
- **K-Means router:** Not presented as a practical solution — the PPL gap indicates learned routers exploit additional degrees of freedom beyond centroid tracking that are not yet understood.
- **Static analysis of dynamics:** The coupling is analyzed through accumulated weight statistics rather than tracking gradient dynamics step-by-step during training.

## Relevance

本文直接关联"LLM Hidden States & Interpretability"研究方向——从梯度动力学和表征几何角度揭示了SMoE路由器与专家之间的内在耦合机制。对理解稀疏MoE模型中专家的专业化形成过程、以及辅助损失如何影响模型内部表征结构具有重要意义。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Does geometric coupling strengthen or weaken as model scale increases from 1B to 70B+? Does the ratio of centroid-tracking vs. other learned routing components change?
2. Can hybrid routers be designed that preserve centroid geometry while recovering the 2.6% PPL gap — e.g., by adding a small learnable residual on top of EMA centroids?
3. How does geometric coupling interact with other routing pathologies such as representation collapse and expert dominance, which the authors note but do not analyze?
4. Does the coupling hold for non-SwiGLU expert architectures (e.g., plain ReLU FFNs or attention-based experts)?
5. Can the geometric lens predict *which* experts will collapse or dominate early in training, enabling proactive intervention?
