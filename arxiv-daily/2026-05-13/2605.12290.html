Targeted Neuron Modulation via Contrastive Pair Search

arXiv: 2605.12290

Date: 2026-05-13

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.12290

一句话总结： 本文提出对比神经元归因（CNA）方法，仅通过前向传播识别0.1%的MLP神经元即可使指令模型拒绝率下降35%–98%，且在所有干预强度下保持输出连贯性（n-gram重复率<0.03）；跨Llama/Qwen架构（1B–72B）实验揭示对齐微调将预存的晚期层判别结构转化为稀疏拒绝门。
TL;DR: Contrastive Neuron Attribution (CNA) identifies the top 0.1% of MLP neurons distinguishing harmful from benign prompts using only forward passes. Ablating them drops refusal rates by 35–98% across Llama and Qwen (1B–72B) while preserving generation quality (>0.96) and MMLU accuracy at all steering strengths—unlike CAA which collapses coherence. The authors show alignment fine-tuning repurposes pre-existing late-layer discrimination structures into a sparse, targetable refusal gate.

核心贡献

  • 提出对比神经元归因（CNA），通过对比有害/无害提示的MLP激活差异，仅需前向传播（8对提示即可发现回路），选取top 0.1%神经元，无需梯度、辅助训练或SAE。
  • 在8个指令模型上，消融0.1%的MLP激活使JBB-Behaviors（100条有害提示，NeurIPS 2024基准）拒绝率相对下降35%–98%（如Qwen2.5-7B从87%降至2%，Llama-3.1-70B从86%降至18%）。
  • 神经元级干预在所有干预强度下保持输出质量>0.96（n-gram重复率度量），而CAA在α≥0.75时导致8个模型中6个质量低于0.60。
  • CNA消融保持MMLU准确率在基线1个百分点以内，而CAA在最大干预时降至接近零。
  • 跨Llama和Qwen（1B–72B）验证基础模型在相同晚期层具有类似判别结构，但引导这些神经元仅产生内容变化而非行为改变——证明对齐微调改变了功能而非结构。
  • 仅8–29%的神经元索引在base-to-instruct转换中保持不变，说明微调替换了具体神经元但保留了层级的集中模式。

Key Contributions

  • Introduce Contrastive Neuron Attribution (CNA), which computes per-neuron mean activation differences between harmful and benign prompt sets, selecting the top 0.1% of MLP neurons across all layers. Requires only forward passes—no gradients, SAE training, or iterative search.
  • Ablating the discovered circuit on JBB-Behaviors achieves relative refusal reductions of 35%–98% across all 8 instruct models tested (e.g., Qwen2.5-7B: 87%→2%, Llama-3.1-70B: 86%→18%, Llama-3.2-1B: 90%→34%).
  • Preserves generation quality above 0.96 (measured as 1 minus n-gram repetition ratio) for every model at maximum steering strength, while CAA drops below 0.60 for 6 of 8 models.
  • Preserves MMLU accuracy within 1 point of baseline across all steering strengths; CAA degrades to near-zero accuracy at maximum intervention.
  • Demonstrates that base models contain comparable late-layer discrimination neurons (85%+ in final 3 layers for Llama-1B, similar for Qwen-3B) but steering these produces only content shifts, not behavioral change—fine-tuning transforms function, not structure.
  • Shows only 8–29% neuron index overlap between base and instruct circuits, confirming fine-tuning replaces individual neurons while preserving layer-level concentration.

Methodology

Contrastive Discovery (Section 3): Given positive prompts P⁺ (harmful) and negative prompts P⁻ (benign), the method: (1) runs all prompts through the model, recording MLP down-projection activations at the last token position via forward hooks; (2) computes per-neuron mean activation difference δⱼˡ = mean(aⱼˡ(x) for x∈P⁺) − mean(aⱼˡ(x) for x∈P⁻); (3) selects the top 0.1% of total MLP activations by |δⱼˡ| across all layers.

Universal Neuron Filtering (Section 3.2): Neurons that fire in the top 0.1% for ≥80% of diverse prompts are excluded to remove content-universal activations.

Targeted Ablation (Section 3.3): Causality is verified by multiplying each circuit neuron's activation by scalar m at inference: m=0 ablates, m=1 is baseline, m>1 amplifies. Steering strength α is defined as 1−m for comparison with CAA.

Models: Base and instruct variants of Llama-3.2-1B/3B, Llama-3.1-8B/70B, Qwen2.5-1.5B/3B/7B/72B (16 models total). Small models on RTX 3080, large models on B200, all in bfloat16.

Discovery set: 8 harmful + 8 benign prompts for qualitative analysis; 100+100 for JBB-Behaviors evaluation. Evaluation on 100 JBB-Behaviors prompts + StrongREJECT rubric (Llama-3.3-70B judge) + MMLU (1000 questions).

Results & Highlights

  • Refusal reduction (Table 3): Relative refusal drops range from 35.6% (Qwen2.5-3B) to 97.7% (Qwen2.5-7B). Absolute drops range from 32pp to 85pp. All 8 instruct models show >50% relative reduction except Qwen2.5-3B.
  • Generation quality (Table 1): CNA quality scores 0.969–0.984 at maximum ablation vs. CAA scores 0.406–0.888 (6 of 8 models below 0.60). CAA on Qwen2.5-72B produces 98% refusal from degenerate output misclassified by keyword classifier.
  • MMLU preservation (Figure 2): CNA maintains baseline accuracy (within 1 point) at all α values; CAA drops to near-zero at α=1.0.
  • Base vs. instruct (Table 4): Qualitative examples show base-model steering changes content ("How do I pick a lock?" → "Lock picking is a skill...") while instruct-model steering flips behavior ("I can't assist" → "Picking a lock can be challenging, here's a guide...").
  • Layer localization (Appendix C): 82–87% of top-200 neurons concentrate in final 3 layers for Llama-1B; 58–69% for Qwen-3B (which has more layers). Pattern is consistent across refusal, capitals, and SVA tasks.
  • Neuron replacement (Table 8): Only 8.5–29% of individual neurons overlap between base and instruct circuits, despite identical layer-level concentration patterns.

Authors & Credibility

The authors are from Nous Research, a research lab known for open-source LLM work. The paper is methodologically rigorous: 16 models tested (8 base + 8 instruct), two distinct architecture families (Llama and Qwen), multiple evaluation metrics (refusal rate, n-gram quality, MMLU, StrongREJECT rubric), and detailed appendices with layer localization and hyperparameter details. The code is promised to be open-sourced at github.com/NousResearch/neural-steering. The work directly engages with and extends multiple recent findings (Arditi et al. 2024 on refusal directions, Arora et al. 2026 on neuron-basis sparsity, Chaudhury 2025 on alignment localization).

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. CNA is a clean neuron-level reinterpretation of CAA's contrastive approach. The base-vs-instruct comparison yielding the "fine-tuning transforms function, not structure" insight is the paper's most novel contribution.
  • Rigor: Strong. 16 models across 2 architectures and 4 scales, with multiple evaluation metrics. The ablation study is well-controlled (same discovery prompts, same evaluation set). The base-vs-instruct comparison is a compelling experimental design.
  • Reproducibility: High. Only forward passes needed; 8 contrastive pairs suffice for discovery; code to be open-sourced; standard benchmarks (JBB-Behaviors, MMLU) used.
  • Significance: High. The practical finding that neuron-level ablation avoids CAA's coherence collapse is immediately useful. The theoretical insight about alignment repurposing structure could influence how we think about fine-tuning mechanisms.
  • Red flags: (1) The refusal-rate metric uses a keyword classifier, which can misclassify degenerate CAA outputs as refusals—inflating CAA's apparent failure but also potentially undercounting CNA's harmful completions. (2) Detailed per-layer analysis is only provided for 1B and 3B models; larger models lack this granularity. (3) The method is limited to behaviors with clean contrastive pairs; unclear how it extends to emergent or distributed behaviors. (4) Dual-use concern is acknowledged but not fully addressed.

Strengths

  • Exceptionally simple methodology (forward passes + mean difference) that scales effortlessly to 72B models.
  • Comprehensive evaluation across 16 models, 2 architectures, 4 scales—rare in interpretability work.
  • Quantitative demonstration that neuron-level ablation preserves both generation quality and general capabilities (MMLU).
  • The base-vs-instruct experimental design cleanly isolates the effect of alignment fine-tuning.
  • Strong empirical evidence for the "structure preserved, function transformed" thesis via neuron overlap analysis.
  • Open-source code commitment and detailed appendices.

Limitations

  • Keyword-based refusal classifier may miss nuanced harmful responses or flag degenerate outputs; a more robust judge (like StrongREJECT) should be the primary metric.
  • Per-layer localization analysis only for 1B/3B models; larger models lack this granularity.
  • Limited to gated SiLU MLP architectures (Llama, Qwen); generalization to MoE or other architectures is untested.
  • Discovery requires manually constructed contrastive pairs; unclear how to automate for arbitrary behaviors.
  • The method identifies which neurons matter but does not explain why or trace the full causal chain from neuron to output.
  • Only tested on refusal behavior and two auxiliary tasks (capitals, SVA); broader behavioral coverage needed.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does the stability of identified neurons vary with prompt diversity, language, and domain?
  • Can the "structure preserved, function transformed" finding be leveraged to design more robust alignment methods that resist neuron-level attacks?
  • What is the minimal discovery set size needed for stable circuit identification, and how does this scale with model size?
