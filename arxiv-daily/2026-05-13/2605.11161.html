Interpretability Can Be Actionable

arXiv: 2605.11161

Date: 2026-05-13

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.11161

一句话总结： 本文提出可解释性研究应以"可操作性"为核心评估标准——即解释是否能够驱动对模型的实际干预和决策，而不仅仅是"理解"本身，并提出了包含具体性（concreteness）和验证性（validation）两个维度的评估框架。
TL;DR: A position paper arguing that interpretability research should be evaluated by actionability—the extent to which insights enable concrete decisions and interventions beyond interpretability research itself. The authors define two dimensions (concreteness, validation), identify five domains of unique interpretability leverage, and propose evaluation criteria and a practical checklist for researchers.

核心贡献

  • 提出以可操作性（actionability）作为可解释性研究的核心评估标准，而非仅关注方法新颖性或"理解"本身
  • 定义了可操作性的两个维度：具体性（concreteness）——行动描述的精确程度；验证性（validation）——行动效用的实证支持程度
  • 识别了可解释性研究缺乏实际影响的三大障碍：激励不对齐、方法论局限、部署挑战
  • 提出五个可解释性具有独特优势的领域：规模无法解决的问题（幻觉、偏见）、对齐验证、概念翻译、架构设计、精准干预
  • 给出了面向研究者的可操作性检查清单和评估标准框架

Key Contributions

  • Redefines success criteria for interpretability: shifts from "does it explain?" to "what does the explanation enable us to do?"
  • Two-dimensional actionability framework: concreteness (vague suggestion → exact specification) and validation (untested hypothesis → systematically evaluated with evidence)
  • Diagnoses three barrier categories: misaligned incentives (publication norms don't reward actionability), methodological limitations (oversimplified setups, lack of comparative analysis), and deployment challenges (technical complexity, open-weights assumption)
  • Identifies five domains of unique interpretability leverage: problems scaling doesn't solve, alignment verification, translation to meaningful concepts, architectural design guidance, and surgical interventions
  • Actionable checklist for researchers: define a clear goal, identify your audience, propose concrete actions, validate empirically, evaluate in realistic settings, use actionable metrics

Methodology

This is a position paper rather than an empirical study. The authors synthesize observations from the ICML 2025 Workshop on Actionable Interpretability, analyze publication and citation patterns in the interpretability literature, and construct a conceptual framework. They ground their arguments in concrete examples from prior work—including model editing (Meng et al., 2022), representation finetuning (Wu et al., 2024), SAE-based steering, and influence functions—demonstrating that actionable interpretability is already achievable in select cases. The paper also analyzes reviewer feedback from the ICML 2025 workshop, noting that 21.8% of submissions were flagged as insufficiently actionable.

Results & Highlights

  • Citation impact analysis: Mosbach et al. (2024) showed interpretability papers are cited predominantly for conceptual reasons—most citations do not credit changes to training, architecture, or evaluation
  • Workshop evidence: At the ICML 2025 Actionable Interpretability Workshop, over one-fifth of submissions were explicitly flagged by reviewers as insufficiently actionable
  • Baseline comparison gap: AxBench (Wu et al., 2025) demonstrated that prompting and finetuning often outperform interpretability methods for LLM steering—a critical finding suggesting interpretability methods must clear a higher bar
  • Success cases documented: Model editing methods leveraging MLP key-value store view, representation finetuning inspired by interpretability findings, induction heads informing Mamba architecture design, and concept vectors uncovering novel chess strategies from AlphaZero

Authors & Credibility

This is a large, multi-institutional collaboration spanning 13 authors from Harvard (Kempner Institute), Oxford, Technion, USC, Mila/McGill, Boston University, Northeastern, University of Maryland, University of Pennsylvania, Google DeepMind, and Tel Aviv University. Several authors (Mor Geva, Hadas Orgad, Fazl Barez) are established figures in mechanistic interpretability. The paper emerged partly from the ICML 2025 Workshop on Actionable Interpretability, lending it community legitimacy. Ian Tenney (Google DeepMind) brings industry perspective.

Strengths

  • Practical and immediately useful: The checklist (Figure 1) and evaluation criteria (Section 6) can be adopted by researchers and reviewers right away
  • Balanced perspective: Explicitly states that not all interpretability work must be immediately actionable; values exploratory research as necessary groundwork
  • Concrete examples: Each abstract claim is backed by specific prior work (model editing, SAE steering, Mamba design, influence functions)
  • Addresses a real community concern: The gap between interpretability research volume and practical impact is widely acknowledged but rarely systematized
  • Multi-stakeholder framing: Distinguishes audiences (developers, domain experts, policymakers) and tailors evaluation criteria accordingly

Limitations

  • No empirical validation of the framework itself: The paper proposes evaluation criteria but does not test whether adopting them actually improves research impact
  • Potential for unintended consequences: Over-indexing on actionability could discourage foundational mechanistic work whose applications are not yet foreseeable (the paper acknowledges this but doesn't fully resolve it)
  • Open-weights elephant in the room: The paper correctly identifies the tension between interpretability's safety motivation and frontier models being proprietary, but offers no concrete path forward
  • Selection bias in success cases: The cited examples of actionable interpretability are cherry-picked; a systematic survey would be more convincing
  • Lacks quantitative benchmarks: While proposing evaluation criteria, the paper does not define specific metrics or thresholds for "sufficiently actionable"

Relevance

本文与可解释性研究的方法论评估直接相关，尤其涉及LLM内部机制分析（如SAE、circuit分析）的实际应用价值问题。作者提出的评估框架对任何从事机制可解释性研究的人都有参考意义——它要求研究者在报告"发现了什么机制"之外，还必须回答"这个发现能让人们做什么"。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can the actionability framework be operationalized into a quantitative review scoring rubric, and would adopting it change what gets published at top venues?
  2. How should the community handle the tension between the open-weights requirement of most interpretability methods and the proprietary nature of frontier models where safety concerns are most acute?
  3. Does the "comparative utility against standard baselines" criterion inadvertently penalize early-stage mechanistic discoveries whose applications require years to materialize?
  4. What institutional changes (reviewer guidelines, paper templates, dedicated tracks) would most effectively incentivize actionable interpretability without stifling foundational research?
  5. How can interpretability insights be translated into domain-appropriate concepts at scale, particularly for high-stakes domains like healthcare where the paper identifies the largest gap?
