> **一句话总结：** 本文提出Freon（基于Schatten quasi-norm的优化器族）和Kaon（用随机噪声替代奇异值的"荒谬"优化器），两者均能匹敌Muon的表现，证明Muon的成功并非源于其精确的谱几何结构，而是由批次梯度对齐度和下降潜力两个局部量的交互决定，配合合适的步长即可实现最优性能。

> **TL;DR:** This paper debunks the prevailing geometric explanation for the Muon optimizer's success. The authors introduce **Freon** (a family of Schatten quasi-norm optimizers interpolating between SGD and Muon and beyond, using a novel QDWH-based rational approximation with provable optimality) and **Kaon** (which replaces gradient singular values with chaotic random noise yet still matches Muon). Both match Muon on GPT-2 and NanoGPT benchmarks. The paper identifies that optimization performance is actually governed by two local quantities — batch gradient alignment (γ) and directional descent potential (Φ) — and that Muon's advantage lies in guaranteeing step-size optimality (constant optimal step size), not in tracking any ideal spectral geometry. The quasi-norm regime (c > 1/2) empirically dominates, which is provably outside any unitarily invariant norm LMO theory.

## 核心贡献

- **提出Freon优化器族**：基于Schatten (quasi-)norm的更新形式 $(GG^\top)^{-c}G$，自然插值于SGD ($c=0$)、Muon ($c=1/2$)以及pseudo-inverse-like端点 ($c=1$)，开发了基于QDWH的可证明最优迭代近似算法
- **提出Kaon"荒谬"优化器**：用混沌随机噪声替换梯度奇异值，完全不具备相干几何结构，却在GPT-2和NanoGPT上匹敌Muon表现，并保留了经典收敛保证（O(1/K)速率）
- **证明最优参数位于quasi-norm区域**：GPT-2上最优的Schatten参数 $c \in [1/2, 1]$，严格超出了任何unitarily invariant LMO能表示的范围，从根本上否定了LMO理论框架
- **揭示性能的真正驱动因素**：通过精确的局部Taylor展开分解出批次梯度对齐度 $\gamma_k$ 和方向下降潜力 $\Phi_k$，证明Muon的成功在于保证步长最优性（最优步长为常数），而非追踪理想全局几何
- **发现Freon($c=1$)具有层对称性不变量**：该更新在缩放和正交层对称下是等变的，意味着优化轨迹不依赖于参数化方式

## Key Contributions

- Introduces **Freon**, a family of Schatten (quasi-)norm optimizers of the form $(GG^\top)^{-c}G$, smoothly interpolating SGD ($c=0$) → Muon ($c=1/2$) → pseudoinverse ($c=1$), powered by a novel QDWH-based rational iteration with doubly exponential convergence and provable optimality (Theorem 2.7)
- Introduces **Kaon**, a deliberately "absurd" optimizer that replaces singular values with chaotic random noise from a logistic map iteration — yet matches Muon on NanoGPT and GPT-2 benchmarks while retaining classical O(1/K) convergence guarantees (Theorem 2.5)
- Demonstrates empirically that optimal Schatten exponents for GPT-2 lie strictly in the quasi-norm regime ($c > 1/2$), which is **provably outside** any unitarily invariant norm ball — conclusively showing LMO theory is insufficient to explain performance
- Derives a unified local descent condition decomposing optimization into two fundamental quantities: **batch gradient alignment** ($\gamma_k$) and **directional descent potential** ($\Phi_k$), showing performance is governed by their interplay with the step size rather than geometric structure
- Proves Freon at $c=1$ is equivariant under scaling and orthogonal layerwise symmetries (Theorem 2.6), making the optimization trajectory invariant to parameterization
- Shows via a random feature model that Muon's core advantage is **step-size stability**: its optimal step size is constant throughout training, while GD requires a highly oscillatory step-size schedule that is practically unrealizable

## Methodology

The paper proceeds in three methodological stages:

1. **Generalizing spectral updates (Freon):** Starting from the LMO/steepest-descent framework, the authors generalize to Schatten $p$-(quasi-)norm updates parameterized by a dual exponent $q$. The update direction is $D = G_n(G_n^\top G_n)^{-c}G_n$ where $c = 1 - q/2$ and $G_n$ is the dual-normalized gradient. A mean-norm scaling trick ($\|\cdot\|_{p,\text{mean}} = r^{1/p}\|\cdot\|_p$) ensures hyperparameter transfer across exponents by anchoring all updates to the spectral ball. For efficient computation, they develop a QDWH-based rational iteration using Zolotarev best rational approximants, achieving doubly exponential convergence ($C|1 - l^b|^{(n+d+1)^T}$).

2. **Destroying geometric structure (Kaon):** To test whether geometry matters at all, they replace the gradient's singular values with output from a chaotic logistic recurrence ($x_{t+1} = \lambda x_t(1-x_t^2)^2$ with $\lambda = 4.1$). This "optimizer" has no target spectrum, no LMO, no norm ball — yet they prove it converges at O(1/K) by verifying the sufficient descent and boundedness conditions of their generalized convergence theorem.

3. **Mechanistic analysis (γ and Φ):** Extending Davis & Drusvyatskiy (2026), they decompose the exact local Taylor expansion of the loss into alignment $\gamma_k = \langle G_k, D_k\rangle / \langle \hat{G}_k, D_k\rangle$ and descent potential $\Phi_k = \langle \hat{G}_k, D_k\rangle^2 / \langle D_k, \nabla^2 f(Z_k)[D_k]\rangle$. The descent condition becomes $f(X_{k+1}) - f(X_k) = -\Phi_k(\gamma_k - \frac{\alpha_k \lambda_k}{2})\alpha_k\lambda_k$, revealing that changing descent direction sacrifices $\gamma$ but amplifies $\Phi$, and the optimal step size is $\alpha_k^* = \gamma_k/\lambda_k$ (independent of $\lambda_k$ at optimum).

Experiments span: (i) a random feature regression model with exact asymptotic analysis (proportional limit $n/b \to \delta$), (ii) WikiText-2 language modeling with GPT-2 (124M params), and (iii) NanoGPT with learning rate sensitivity sweeps across 84 runs on 8×H100 GPUs.

## Results & Highlights

- **Kaon matches Muon:** On both NanoGPT (Figure 3) and WikiText-2 (Figure 4), Kaon achieves validation loss statistically indistinguishable from Muon, despite using random singular values
- **Freon quasi-norm regime dominates:** The best Freon exponents for GPT-2 concentrate in $c \in [1/2, 1]$ (Figures 6 and I.7), outside any proper Schatten norm — with $c = 2/3$ and $c = 3/4$ closely matching Muon
- **TruncatedSGD improves over SGD but falls short of Muon:** Zeroing out the top 5% of singular values helps but is insufficient (Figure 4a), confirming that suppression of large singular values is necessary but not sufficient
- **Step-size stability is the key differentiator:** In the RF model, GD achieves greater per-step loss reduction than spectral descent under exact line search, but requires a wildly oscillating step-size schedule. Muon's optimal step size remains constant throughout training (Figure 5)
- **Optimal exponents fluctuate across batches:** Per-step optimal $c$ values in GPT-2 are highly stochastic and vary dynamically over training (Figure 6), explaining why greedy adaptive schedules fail
- **Trading γ for Φ explains the c > 1/2 advantage:** Increasing $c$ suppresses alignment $\gamma$ but massively amplifies descent potential $\Phi$, with the net effect favoring the quasi-norm regime

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty (High):** The paper delivers a genuinely surprising result — that random spectra work as well as carefully designed spectral whitening. The Freon family and its QDWH-based computation are technically novel. The γ/Φ decomposition provides a new mechanistic lens. The quasi-norm optimality finding directly contradicts the prevailing LMO narrative.

**Rigor (High):** Theoretical claims are formally proven (convergence theorems, equivariance, optimality of QDWH iteration). The random feature asymptotics use standard random matrix theory tools. Empirical methodology is sound with proper baselines, sweeps, and statistical reporting.

**Reproducibility (Moderate):** Algorithms are clearly specified (pseudocode provided). Training details (learning rates, hyperparameters, hardware) are documented. However, code is not released, and the QDWH-based Freon implementation requires careful engineering (coupled Cholesky, block-QR). The Kaon chaotic iteration involves specific constants (λ = 4.1, scaling factor 1/175) that may be sensitive.

**Significance (High):** This paper has the potential to redirect the research community's understanding of spectral optimizers. If geometry doesn't matter, then pursuing better LMOs or spectral structures may be a dead end. The γ/Φ framework could open new research directions focused on step-size optimality rather than update geometry.

**Red flags:**
- Empirical validation is limited to relatively small-scale experiments (GPT-2 124M, NanoGPT). No large-scale (1B+) training results are provided, though the authors acknowledge this limitation
- Kaon's practical utility is limited — it is explicitly a pedagogical counterexample, not a recommended optimizer
- The framework is primarily diagnostic/post-hoc — it cannot predict optimal settings a priori, limiting its prescriptive power
- The random feature model relies on mean-zero Gaussian activations, excluding ReLU and other practical settings

## Strengths

- Delivers a clean, compelling refutation of the geometric narrative behind spectral optimizers through multiple complementary lines of evidence (Freon, Kaon, γ/Φ analysis)
- Strong theoretical foundations with formal convergence guarantees even for the "absurd" Kaon optimizer
- The γ/Φ decomposition elegantly unifies diverse optimizers under a single mechanistic framework
- Excellent experimental design with controlled comparisons, learning rate sensitivity sweeps, and multi-seed runs
- The insight about step-size stability being Muon's true advantage is practically actionable

## Limitations

- Only tested on GPT-2 (124M) and NanoGPT — no vision models, no billion-scale training, leaving generalization uncertain
- The random feature model's mean-zero Gaussian assumption limits the theoretical analysis
- The framework cannot predict optimal hyperparameters a priori — it is diagnostic, not prescriptive
- Attempts to dynamically track optimal Schatten exponents failed due to batch-to-batch variability
- Freon's QDWH implementation is computationally involved and may not justify the engineering effort given similar performance across all optimizers
- The paper does not address interactions with other training components (weight decay, learning rate schedules, data parallelism)

## Relevance

本文直接关联LLM训练稳定性和ML可靠性研究方向：它从根本上挑战了Muon等谱优化器的理论基础，揭示了训练中梯度奇异值处理的真正作用机制，对理解大规模模型训练的数值稳定性和优化器选择具有重要指导意义。γ/Φ框架为监控和诊断训练动态提供了新的量化工具。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Do Kaon and Freon's conclusions hold at billion-parameter scale, or does precise geometry become necessary at larger model sizes?
2. Can the γ/Φ framework be used to design adaptive step-size schedules that automatically exploit the constant-optimal-step-size property of spectral methods?
3. How does the stochasticity of optimal exponents across batches relate to gradient noise structure — and could per-layer or per-block exponent tuning improve over a global choice?
4. If geometry is irrelevant, what is the minimal sufficient preconditioning for matrix gradients — is it simply "suppress the largest singular values and normalize"?
5. Could the alignment/potential tradeoff framework extend to understanding why Adam works well for attention layers while spectral methods may be preferred for MLP layers?
