Grid Games: The Power of Multiple Grids for Quantizing Large Language Models

arXiv: 2605.12327

Date: 2026-05-13

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.12327

一句话总结： 本文提出PO2多网格量化框架，允许每16个数值的量化组从2-3个4-bit网格中自适应选择最优者，理论证明小分组格式（如MXFP4/NVFP4）可显著受益而大分组无增益，提出的PO2(Split87)在7个模型W4A4量化中达到97.85%精度恢复率，SFP4通过NVFP4+偏移实现在现有TensorCore上的部署（约50%额外开销）。
TL;DR: The paper introduces "Grid Games" — a systematic framework for adaptive multi-grid 4-bit LLM quantization. Each block of 16 values selects the best among 2–3 quantization grids, with the grid choice encoded in the scale byte (no sideband storage). Theoretical analysis proves small-group formats (g=16, 32) benefit significantly from multiple grids while the advantage vanishes for large groups (via a Glivenko–Cantelli argument). The authors propose four grid families: PO2(NF4) (Normal prior + residual), PO2(Split87) (asymmetric explicit-zero + residual, best overall), MPO2 (fully learned), and SFP4 (NVFP4 + two shifted variants, TensorCore-implementable at ~50% overhead). Across 7 models in W4A4 post-training quantization, PO2(Split87) achieves 97.85% average accuracy recovery (vs. 96.97% for NVFP4). In pre-training of 30M–100M Llama-like models, dual-grid methods consistently close the C4 validation loss gap versus BF16.

核心贡献

  • PO2理论框架： 形式化了Power-of-Two-Grids问题，证明当分组大小g→∞时双网格优势消失（Theorem 1），但对g=16的微缩放格式，归一化后的分组呈现双峰分布（spiky vs. flat），多网格可通过凹性（concavity）论证超越任意单一网格。
  • 竞争性分析（Competitive Analysis）： 提出将IF4（INT4/FP4逐组选择）与最优单网格对比的分析框架，证明IF4仍比最优单网格差10–15%（β≈1.10–1.13），揭示了其结构性局限。
  • 四种网格族设计： PO2(NF4)固定NF4+学习残差网格；PO2(Split87)为8+0+7显式零非对称网格+残差（总体最优）；MPO2完全从数据学习双网格；SFP4保持E2M1编码+偏移实现TensorCore兼容。
  • SFP4硬件可部署方案： 将NVFP4与两个±0.5偏移网格组成三元组，通过两次GEMM（主NVFP4矩阵乘 + BF16校正矩阵乘）分解，在RTX 5090上实测额外开销约50–60%。
  • 大规模实验验证： 在7个模型（Llama-3.2-3B到Qwen3.5-27B）的W4A4量化中，PO2(Split87)达到97.85%平均精度恢复率；在30M/50M/100M Llama预训练中，双网格方法显著缩小与BF16的C4验证损失差距。

Key Contributions

  • Formalize the PO2 (Power-of-Two-Grids) problem with rigorous theory: Theorem 1 proves the asymptotic advantage vanishes as group size g→∞, while a concavity argument (Lemma 2 + Corollary 3) shows specialized grids for spiky vs. flat blocks beat any single grid at practical g=16.
  • Provide a competitive analysis framework showing IF4 (INT4/FP4 per-block selection) is 10–15% worse than the best single learnable grid (competitive factor β≈1.10–1.13), revealing its structural limitations.
  • Propose four grid families: PO2(NF4) (Normal prior + learned residual), PO2(Split87) (asymmetric explicit-zero 8+0+7 + residual, best overall), MPO2 (fully learned pair), SFP4 (NVFP4 + ±0.5 shifts, TensorCore-compatible).
  • SFP4 decomposes the matmul into a standard NVFP4 GEMM plus a BF16 correction GEMM (1/16 the size), achieving ~50–60% overhead on RTX 5090 while using no sideband storage (grid selector bits stored in the scale byte).
  • Extensive evaluation: 7 models (Llama-3.2-3B to Qwen3.5-27B) in W4A4 PTQ (PO2(Split87) at 97.85% recovery vs. NVFP4 at 96.97%); pre-training of 30M/50M/100M Llama-like models shows consistent C4 loss improvements over single-grid baselines.

Methodology

The paper operates at the intersection of theory, algorithm design, and hardware implementation:

Theoretical Framework (Section 3). The authors consider absmax-normalized blockwise quantization with group size g. A block X = (X₁,…,Xₘ) ∈ ℝᵍ is normalized by Mₘ = max|Xᵢ|, and each normalized magnitude is rounded to the nearest grid point. The PO2 risk is defined as the expected MSE when each block can choose between two grids B₁, B₂. Theorem 1 shows that as g→∞, the per-block maximum converges a.s. to the essential supremum, so all blocks have the same normalized profile and per-block selection becomes useless. The key insight for small groups is that real transformer weights exhibit bimodal normalized block statistics (Student-t fits with ν≈7–11): some blocks are "spiky" (dominated by outliers, low average normalized magnitude μ) while others are "flat" (values of comparable magnitude, high μ). Lemma 2 shows the best single-grid risk V_G(p) is concave in the spiky fraction p, so two specialized grids (one per block type) beat any single compromise grid via the concavity gap.

Grid Training Protocol (Section 4). All learned grids are trained on absmax-normalized groups (g=16) from a pooled corpus of weights and activations from Qwen3-8B, Qwen3.5-9B, Mistral-7B-v0.3, and Phi-4 (2M sampled groups). The PO2 algorithm (Figure 1) takes a primary grid B₁, collects blocks where B₁ incurs above-median error as a residual pool, initializes B₂ on this residual via weighted Lloyd iterations, then alternates block assignment with Lloyd updates. Grids are snapped to FP8 E4M3 after training (negligible accuracy loss, verified in Appendix G).

SFP4 Matmul Decomposition (Section 4.4). For weights with scale s and E2M1 codes cₖ, three grids are offered: Grid A (standard NVFP4: ŵₖ = s·g(cₖ)), Grid B⁺ (ŝhift +0.5), Grid B⁻ (shift −0.5). The matmul y = Wx decomposes as y = NVFP4_MMA(W̃, X) − c·Σ[σ[m,b]·sW[m,b]·Xsum[b,n]], where the correction GEMM is 1/16 the main GEMM size. The 2-bit grid selector is stored in bits 7:6 of the scale byte (reducing scale format from UE4M3 to E3M3 with negligible loss).

Experimental Setup (Section 5). PTQ: 7 models quantized with RTN + absmax scaling at g=16; metrics include KL divergence on WikiText-2/C4, EAR (Expected Acceptance Rate), and 9 downstream tasks. Pre-training: Llama-like 30M/50M/100M models on C4 with straight-through gradient quantization. SFP4 kernels benchmarked on RTX 5090.

Results & Highlights

  • PO2(Split87) is the best overall method. In W4A4 PTQ across 7 models, it achieves 97.85% average accuracy recovery (Table 3), the lowest KL divergence (0.0364 on C4 top-10, Table 4), and highest EAR (0.9235). It consistently outperforms all single-grid methods and other multi-grid variants.
  • PO2(NF4) is a close second at 97.74% recovery, confirming the validity of the Normal-distribution prior for transformer weights — even with a fixed NF4 primary grid, the learned residual grid provides substantial gains.
  • SFP4 bridges theory and hardware. As an E2M1-native method, SFP4 reduces MSE by 11–21% vs. FP4 on synthetic distributions and achieves 97.31% recovery in PTQ (competitive with IF4 at 97.47%). With Four-Over-Six scale search, SFP4-2c+4/6 improves Qwen3.5-0.8B KL by 23% over NVFP4.
  • IF4 is structurally limited. Competitive analysis confirms IF4 (INT4/FP4 pair) is 10–15% worse than the optimal single grid, and empirically ranks below PO2(NF4) and PO2(Split87). Its advantage over NVFP4 is real but suboptimal.
  • Pre-training results confirm gains. In 30M/50M/100M Llama-like pre-training (Figure 3), PO2(Split87) consistently achieves the lowest C4 validation loss gap vs. BF16 among all methods. BOF4 (best single-grid) outperforms IF4 but loses to PO2(Split87).
  • Hadamard transforms provide mixed results. In W4A4, Hadamard rotation gives a small improvement; in W4A16, results are mixed (Appendix D). Grid choice effects are orthogonal to transform choice.
  • SFP4 kernel overhead is ~50–60%. On RTX 5090, for large shapes (M=4096, N=5120, K=27648), SFP4 takes 1.53ms vs. 0.79ms for NVFP4 baseline. The correction GEMM is the dominant overhead component.
  • Software PO2 kernels are efficient. For memory-bound decoding (M=1), a lookup-table kernel achieves 91% of peak bandwidth on RTX 5090. For compute-bound cases, a tensor-core kernel with 128×128 tiling hides lookup overhead, achieving ~20% overhead vs. BF16 at large batch sizes.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. The adaptive multi-grid idea is simple yet powerful and has been underexplored. The theoretical formalization (Theorem 1 + concavity argument + competitive analysis) provides genuine insight beyond empirical recipe-building. The Split87 grid (8+0+7 asymmetric explicit-zero) is a non-obvious construction.
  • Rigor: Strong. Full proofs in appendix; competitive analysis provides quantitative bounds; experimental protocol covers 7 models × 9 tasks for PTQ, 3 model sizes for pre-training, plus synthetic distributions. Multi-seed experiments (Figure 5) show robustness.
  • Reproducibility: High. Source code is publicly available. Training protocol is clearly described (pooled models, 2M groups, Lloyd iterations, FP8 snapping). Exact grid values listed in Appendix C. Hardware benchmarks specify exact shapes and GPU.
  • Significance: High. The paper makes a convincing case that the "free" bit in microscaled formats (the unused sign information in the scale) should be exploited. SFP4 demonstrates this is feasible on current hardware. The recommendation for future hardware to support configurable LUTs rather than fixed formats is well-argued.
  • Red flags / concerns:
  • Pre-training experiments are limited to small models (30M–100M); scaling to billions of parameters is unverified.
  • Among the PO2 variants, differences on downstream tasks are often close to noise level (acknowledged by the authors), making it hard to definitively rank them.
  • MPO2 (fully learned) overfits model-specific distributions (Table 2 shows good MSE but poor KL transfer), suggesting the approach is sensitive to training data composition.
  • The 50–60% SFP4 kernel overhead is significant; the authors acknowledge this is a "compromise" path and that a fused production kernel would be needed for practical deployment.
  • The paper does not evaluate interaction with popular quantization methods like GPTQ or AWQ (only mentions composability in passing).
  • Largest PTQ model is Qwen3.5-27B; no evaluation on 70B+ or MoE models.

Strengths

  • Elegant theoretical framework that explains when and why multiple grids help, not just that they help.
  • The competitive analysis of IF4 is a particularly insightful contribution — it quantifies the structural limitation of a concurrent work's approach.
  • SFP4 is a creative engineering solution: reusing NVFP4 tensor cores via a decomposition that adds only a small correction GEMM.
  • No sideband storage needed — grid selector bits fit in the existing scale byte, avoiding memory format changes.
  • Comprehensive evaluation spanning synthetic distributions, PTQ (7 models), pre-training (3 sizes), and hardware benchmarks.
  • The paper is self-aware about limitations and avoids overclaiming (e.g., acknowledges near-noise-level differences between PO2 variants).

Limitations

  • Pre-training experiments are small-scale (max 100M parameters); the gap between PO2 variants and single grids may narrow at larger scales.
  • SFP4's 50–60% overhead on current hardware is non-trivial; a fused kernel would be needed for production use but is not provided.
  • No evaluation of composability with GPTQ, AWQ, or other weight-adjustment methods beyond Hadamard transforms.
  • No results on MoE models, very large models (70B+), or quantization-aware fine-tuning.
  • MPO2 overfits to training model distributions, suggesting that fully learned grids may not generalize well.
  • The paper argues for hardware LUTs in future silicon, but this is a proposal without silicon validation.

Relevance

本文提出的自适应多网格4-bit量化方法直接对LLM压缩与推理加速具有高实用价值。PO2框架与当前NVFP4/MXFP4硬件设计紧密相关，SFP4方案可在现有Blackwell TensorCore上部署，对模型部署和推理优化研究具有重要参考意义。理论分析为理解分组量化中网格设计提供了新的视角。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How do PO2 grid pairs interact with GPTQ's Hessian-aware weight correction or AWQ's activation-aware salience protection?
  • Does the PO2 advantage persist at 70B+ scale, where weight distributions may be more homogeneous due to increased parameter count?
  • Can the grid selection mechanism be learned jointly with model weights during full-scale pre-training (not just straight-through estimation)?
  • How does the approach interact with MoE architectures, where expert-specific weight distributions may benefit from expert-specific grid pairs?
  • Would per-tensor or per-layer grid optimization (rather than global grids) yield significant further gains without overfitting?
  • What is the optimal number of grids beyond two? The paper briefly considers three (SFP4) but does not systematically study the k-grid tradeoff.
  • Can the SFP4 correction GEMM be fused into a production kernel to reduce the 50–60% overhead, and what silicon-level changes would be needed for native multi-grid support?
