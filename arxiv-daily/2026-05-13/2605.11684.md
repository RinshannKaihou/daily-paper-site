> **一句话总结：** 本文将部分模型共享（PSO-Fed）与鲁棒联邦共形预测（Rob-FCP）相结合，在训练阶段通过仅交换30%的模型参数天然削弱恶意更新的影响并降低通信量，在校准阶段通过基于直方图特征向量的恶意性评分检测并排除拜占庭客户端，最终在三种攻击场景下实现了接近名义覆盖率（~90%）且比标准FCP紧致2.2倍的预测区间。

> **TL;DR:** Integrates PSO-Fed's partial parameter sharing with Rob-FCP's histogram-based Byzantine detection to defend both training and calibration phases of federated conformal prediction, achieving near-nominal 90% coverage with intervals 1.8–2.2× tighter than existing methods under 20% Byzantine clients.

## 核心贡献

- 提出首个同时保护联邦训练和共形校准两个阶段的拜占庭鲁棒联邦共形预测框架，弥补了现有方法（Rob-FCP）仅防御校准阶段的不足。
- 利用PSO-Fed的部分模型共享机制（每次仅交换M/D=30%的参数），天然限制拜占庭客户端的攻击面、削弱恶意更新对聚合模型的影响，同时显著降低通信开销。
- 在校准阶段，客户端将非一致性分数压缩为H维直方图特征向量，服务器通过ℓ₂距离计算恶意性评分（与最近邻的平均距离），识别并排除拜占庭客户端后仅用良性客户端估计共形分位数。
- 在100个客户端、20%拜占庭节点的设置下，三种攻击（效率攻击、覆盖攻击、随机攻击）实验均表明该方法覆盖率接近90%目标，预测区间比FCP紧致2.2倍、比Rob-FCP紧致1.8倍。

## Key Contributions

- First framework to defend both the federated training and conformal calibration phases against Byzantine attacks in FCP, addressing a gap in Rob-FCP which only hardens calibration.
- Leverages PSO-Fed partial model sharing (M/D = 0.3) to inherently attenuate poisoned updates during training while achieving significant communication savings — only 30% of parameters are exchanged per round.
- Introduces histogram-based characterization vectors (H = 100 bins) for local non-conformity scores during calibration; the server computes pairwise ℓ₂ distances to assign maliciousness scores and excludes Byzantine clients before quantile estimation.
- Empirically demonstrates near-nominal coverage (~90%) with prediction intervals 1.8× tighter than Rob-FCP and 2.2× tighter than standard FCP across efficiency, coverage, and random Byzantine attack scenarios with 20% malicious clients.

## Methodology

The proposed method builds on two existing lines of work — PSO-Fed (partial sharing online federated learning) and Rob-FCP (Byzantine-robust federated conformal prediction) — and integrates them into a unified dual-phase defense:

**System model:** K = 100 clients collaboratively estimate a linear model w* ∈ ℝ^D (D = 50) via federated least-mean-squares. Data is non-IID across clients (client-specific input variances ς²_k ~ U(0.2, 1.2), noise variances σ²_k ~ U(0.005, 0.025)).

**Training phase (PSO-Fed):** At each iteration, the server selects |S_n| = 10 clients. Each client exchanges only M = 15 out of D = 50 parameters (M/D = 0.3), determined by a diagonal selection matrix S_{k,n}. Byzantine clients (|S_B| = 20, p_a = 0.25 attack probability) inject Gaussian noise (σ²_B = 0.1) into their partial updates. Partial sharing inherently limits the adversary's influence because only the shared coordinates can be corrupted.

**Calibration phase:** After training, each client computes non-conformity scores (residuals) on a local calibration set of 1,000 samples and compresses them into an H = 100-bin histogram vector v_k. Three attack types are considered:
- *Efficiency attack:* Byzantine clients report all-zero scores → deflates quantile → undercoverage.
- *Coverage attack:* Byzantine clients report all-one scores → inflates quantile → overly wide intervals.
- *Random attack:* Byzantine clients report uniform random scores from U(0.8, 1).

**Byzantine detection:** The server computes pairwise ℓ₂ distances between characterization vectors (Eq. 12). Each client receives a maliciousness score m_k equal to the average distance to its K_b − 1 nearest neighbors (Eq. 13), where K_b = K − |S_B|. The K_b clients with lowest scores are classified as benign (set B), and only their scores are used for quantile estimation.

**Prediction intervals:** The conformal quantile q̂_{1−α} is estimated from benign clients only, and prediction intervals C(x) = [ŵᵀx − q̂, ŵᵀx + q̂] are constructed with target coverage 1 − α = 90%.

## Results & Highlights

- **Maliciousness detection:** Violin plots (Fig. 3) show clear separation — Byzantine clients have significantly higher maliciousness scores across all three attack types, enabling reliable identification and exclusion.
- **Coverage (Fig. 4a):** The proposed method consistently achieves ~90% coverage (matching the target), while FCP under coverage attack reaches ~93% (inflated) and under efficiency/random attacks drops below target. Rob-FCP also achieves ~90% coverage but at the cost of wider intervals.
- **Interval width (Fig. 4b):** The proposed method produces intervals that are:
  - **2.2× tighter than standard FCP** under coverage attack (FCP's intervals are inflated by adversarial scores).
  - **1.8× tighter than Rob-FCP** because partial sharing during training yields a lower-MSE model, which naturally produces smaller residuals and thus tighter conformal intervals.
- **Communication efficiency:** Only 30% of model parameters are exchanged per round, reducing communication by 70% compared to full-sharing FL.
- **Dual-phase resilience:** Unlike Rob-FCP which only defends calibration, the proposed method's training-phase defense via partial sharing directly reduces model MSE, which compounds with calibration-phase detection for tighter intervals.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Moderate. The core contribution is the integration of two existing methods (PSO-Fed + Rob-FCP) rather than a fundamentally new algorithm. However, the insight that partial sharing simultaneously provides training-phase robustness *and* communication efficiency is valuable and well-motivated.
- **Rigor:** The experimental setup is reasonable (100 clients, 20% Byzantine, three attack types) but limited to synthetic linear regression data with D = 50. No experiments on real-world datasets (e.g., image classification, NLP) or deep neural networks are presented. The theoretical analysis of PSO-Fed's convergence under Byzantine attacks is deferred to prior work [21, 22].
- **Reproducibility:** The method description is detailed with specific hyperparameters (M/D = 0.3, H = 100, K_b = 80, p_a = 0.25, σ²_B = 0.1). No code is mentioned. Reproduction is feasible but would require implementing both PSO-Fed and the calibration pipeline from scratch.
- **Significance:** Practical relevance is high for uncertainty-aware federated learning in adversarial settings. However, the evaluation on synthetic linear regression limits the claimed generality. The approach is not evaluated on any standard FL benchmark.
- **Red flags:**
  - No formal coverage guarantees are established for the combined framework — the paper explicitly lists this as future work. The coverage results are purely empirical.
  - The assumption that the server knows |S_B| (number of Byzantine clients) is strong. The authors briefly mention MAD as an alternative but do not evaluate it.
  - The linear regression setting (D = 50) is far from the typical deep FL scenarios with millions of parameters. Scaling behavior is unknown.
  - The calibration phase requires 1,000 samples per client, which may be unrealistic in privacy-sensitive FL applications.

## Strengths

- Holistic dual-phase defense (training + calibration) is more principled than single-phase approaches.
- Partial sharing provides a "free" robustness boost during training with built-in communication savings.
- Histogram-based characterization is privacy-preserving (no raw scores shared), communication-efficient, and effective for detection.
- Clear empirical separation between benign and Byzantine clients in maliciousness scores.
- Tighter prediction intervals than both FCP and Rob-FCP, which is practically valuable.

## Limitations

- Evaluated only on synthetic linear regression (D = 50); generalization to deep networks and real-world datasets is untested.
- Assumes the server knows the exact number of Byzantine clients |S_B| for the nearest-neighbor threshold.
- No formal finite-sample coverage guarantees; robustness and coverage properties are purely empirical.
- Partial sharing's impact on convergence speed in non-Byzantine settings is not discussed.
- The histogram compression may lose fine-grained distributional information; sensitivity to bin count H is not analyzed.
- Scalability to large K, high D, or heterogeneous model architectures is unexplored.

## Relevance

该论文在分布式场景下将联邦学习的通信效率、拜占庭鲁棒性与共形预测的不确定性量化相结合，对研究可靠的不确定性估计方法和对抗环境下的分布式机器学习具有直接参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does the method perform on real-world datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10/100, medical imaging) with deep neural networks where D >> 50?
- Can formal finite-sample marginal coverage guarantees be established when partial sharing modifies the trained model's distribution?
- What is the sensitivity of Byzantine detection accuracy to the histogram resolution H and the number of calibration samples per client?
- How does the method perform under adaptive attacks where Byzantine clients craft characterization vectors specifically to evade ℓ₂-distance-based detection?
- Does the partial sharing ratio M/D have a sweet spot balancing convergence speed, model accuracy, and Byzantine robustness?
- Can the assumption of knowing |S_B| be relaxed effectively using MAD or other outlier detection methods without performance degradation?
