Solve the Loop: Attractor Models for Language and Reasoning

arXiv: 2605.12466

Date: 2026-05-13

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.12466

一句话总结： 本文提出 Attractor Model，通过将循环精修建模为输出嵌入空间中的不动点求解问题（而非有限次展开），配合隐函数微分实现常数训练内存，在大规模语言建模和极小模型推理任务上均大幅超越 Transformer 和现有循环模型，并观察到"均衡内化"现象——训练后骨干网络的初始预测已接近均衡点，推理时可几乎无需迭代精修。
TL;DR: Attractor Models treat latent recurrent refinement as a fixed-point problem in output embedding space, using implicit differentiation for constant-memory training. A 770M Attractor Model beats a 1.3B Transformer trained on 2x tokens; a 27M model achieves 91.4% on Sudoku-Extreme and 93.1% on Maze-Hard where frontier LLMs score 0%. The model learns "equilibrium internalization" — the backbone proposal converges near the fixed point, making the solver unnecessary at inference.

核心贡献

  • 提出一种新的架构范式——Attractor Model：先用骨干 Transformer 生成初始输出嵌入提案，再用较小的吸引子模块通过不动点迭代精修至均衡，最终通过绑定的 unembedding 解码为词表分布。
  • 利用隐函数定理（IFT）进行反向传播，训练内存不随精修步数增长（O(1) 内存），相比循环语言模型（如 Parcae）节省 25–31% 训练 FLOPs。
  • 精修步数由收敛容差 ε 自适应决定，而非固定的循环次数或学习的停止机制，推理时可灵活调整计算预算而不引起训练-推理失配。
  • 发现并命名"均衡内化"（equilibrium internalization）现象：训练过程中骨干网络的初始预测逐渐逼近不动点，使得推理时 T=0（无迭代）即可达到或超越需要 8 步循环的基线模型。
  • 在极小模型（27M 参数、~1000 样本）上，Sudoku-Extreme 91.4%、Maze-Hard 93.1%，远超 DeepSeek R1 / Claude / o3-mini（均为 0%）和专门递归推理器 TRM/HRM，且不像 TRM 那样在放大时性能崩溃。

Key Contributions

  • Introduce Attractor Models: a backbone module proposes an initial output embedding, an attractor module refines it to a fixed point, decoded via tied unembedding — treating recurrent refinement as an equilibrium computation rather than finite unrolling.
  • Achieve constant training memory (O(1) in refinement depth) via implicit differentiation through the fixed point, with a cheap one-step gradient approximation losing only 0.14 PPL vs. full IFT while being 4.8x more memory-efficient.
  • Demonstrate that solver iterations are adaptive (convergence-based), decoupling inference compute from training depth and avoiding the train-test mismatch that plagues looped LMs.
  • Identify "equilibrium internalization": during training, the backbone progressively moves its initial prediction toward the fixed point, so the solver becomes largely unnecessary at inference — the model self-distills the iterative process into its feed-forward pass.
  • Show that a 770M Attractor Model matches or outperforms a 1.3B Transformer trained on twice as many tokens on language modeling benchmarks.
  • Achieve 91.4% on Sudoku-Extreme and 93.1% on Maze-Hard with only 27M parameters and ~1000 training examples, where frontier LLMs (DeepSeek R1, Claude 3.7, o3-mini-high) all score 0% and specialized recursive reasoners (TRM) collapse at larger sizes.

Methodology

Architecture. Attractor Models consist of three components: (1) a tied input/output embedding E, (2) a backbone module T_θb (a standard causal Transformer) that maps input embeddings to an initial output embedding proposal ỹ₀, and (3) an attractor module T_θa (a smaller Transformer-based network) that iteratively refines the proposal via ỹ_{t+1} = T_θa(ỹ_t, ỹ₀) with persistent additive injection of the backbone proposal at every step.

Fixed-point solving. Rather than unrolling for T steps, the method solves A_θa(ỹ⋆, ỹ₀) = T_θa(ỹ⋆, ỹ₀) − ỹ⋆ = 0 using Anderson acceleration, exiting when the relative residual ‖A‖/‖ỹ_t‖ < ε or after T_max steps. The equilibrium embedding is decoded via tied unembedding: p = Softmax(ỹ⋆ E^T).

Training. Gradients are obtained via the implicit function theorem. A one-step approximation (u ≈ v) avoids the expensive linear solve for the full IFT backward, reducing memory to constant in iteration count while losing minimal quality. The standard cross-entropy next-token prediction loss is applied to the fixed-point output.

Key design differences from DEQ. (i) The fixed point lives in the tied output-embedding space (not a hidden state), so every iterate is a decodable prediction. (ii) The solver is warm-started from the backbone's semantically meaningful proposal ỹ₀, not from zero or noise. (iii) The backbone can be an arbitrarily deep Transformer, while DEQ replaces the entire network with a single equilibrium layer.

Reasoning task adaptation. For Sudoku/Maze tasks, deep supervision provides initialization (previous step's (y, z) initializes the next step), and phantom gradients (rather than one-step) are used for the backward pass due to the small-data sensitivity.

Results & Highlights

Language Modeling (FineWeb-Edu, nanochat recipe):

  • Val PPL: 770M Attractor = 12.09 vs. Transformer 13.08 (−7.6%) vs. Parcae 12.49
  • Lambada PPL: 770M Attractor = 15.21 vs. Transformer 22.37 (−32.0%) vs. Parcae 19.71
  • CORE accuracy: 770M Attractor = 26.83% vs. Transformer 22.42% (+19.7%) vs. Parcae 25.07%
  • 770M Attractor matches 1.3B Transformer (Val PPL 11.95, Lambada 17.26) trained on 2x tokens
  • Training FLOPs: 25–31% lower than Parcae across all scales
  • Peak memory: constant vs. Parcae's linear growth with loop count (OOM at high T)

Hard Reasoning (Sudoku-Extreme / Maze-Hard, ~1000 examples):

  • 27M Attractor: 91.4% / 93.1%
  • TRM 7M: 74.7% / 85.3%; TRM 27M: 0.0% / 0.0% (collapses at scale!)
  • HRM 27M: 55.0% / 74.5%
  • DeepSeek R1 / Claude 3.7 / o3-mini-high: 0% / 0%
  • Standard Transformer 27M: 0% / 0%

Equilibrium Internalization:

  • At inference, T=0 (backbone-only) already matches Parcae at T=8 for all model sizes
  • Solver iterations decrease during training (unlike DEQ which requires increasing iterations)
  • 99.7% of tokens converge within T_max (vs. 12.4% without persistent injection)

Ablations:

  • Backbone proposal initialization beats zero init (Val PPL 34.05 vs. 43.87) and Gaussian noise (41.26)
  • Additive persistent injection beats concatenation (34.05 vs. 36.81) and initial-only (51.92)
  • One-step backward approximation vs. full IFT: 34.05 vs. 33.91 PPL, but 4.8x less memory and 2.7x faster

Critical Assessment

Novelty: High. The core idea — placing the fixed point directly in output embedding space with a backbone proposal as warm-start — is a meaningful departure from both DEQ (hidden-state equilibrium from zero) and looped LMs (finite unrolling). The "equilibrium internalization" phenomenon is genuinely novel and has implications beyond this specific architecture.

Rigor: Strong. Theoretical grounding via Banach fixed-point theorem and implicit function theorem. Comprehensive ablations isolate each design choice. Fair experimental comparisons with matched parameters, data, and training recipes.

Reproducibility: Good. Public code repository, detailed hyperparameters in appendix, standard training recipe (nanochat/FineWeb-Edu). The use of Anderson acceleration and one-step IFT are well-described implementation details.

Significance: Potentially high. If the results hold at larger scales (1B+), this could influence how recurrent/iterative computation is incorporated into LLMs. The equilibrium internalization finding — that a model can learn to make its own iterative refinement unnecessary — challenges assumptions about the necessity of test-time compute scaling.

Red flags:

  • Largest model tested is 770M; it remains unclear whether the gains persist at 7B–70B scale.
  • The one-step gradient approximation, while efficient, is a significant simplification; its adequacy at very large scale is untested.
  • The reasoning experiments use very small models and datasets (~1000 examples); it's unclear how this transfers to general-purpose reasoning at scale.
  • No comparison with recent latent reasoning methods like Coconut or other continuous CoT approaches in the language modeling setup.

Strengths

  • Clean architectural design with clear motivation from mechanistic analysis of looped LMs converging to fixed points.
  • Strong empirical results across two very different regimes (large-scale LM and tiny reasoning models).
  • Constant memory training is a genuine practical advantage over looped LMs.
  • The equilibrium internalization phenomenon is fascinating and well-documented with PCA trajectory visualizations.
  • Comprehensive ablations cover initialization, injection mechanism, backward pass variants, and comparison to DEQ.
  • Theoretical appendix provides formal guarantees (well-posedness, convergence, gradient validity).

Limitations

  • Scale ceiling at 770M parameters; extrapolation to production-scale models (7B+) is uncertain.
  • The one-step backward approximation may limit quality at larger scales where gradient fidelity matters more.
  • No investigation of how equilibrium internalization interacts with instruction tuning, RLHF, or other post-training.
  • Reasoning results are limited to synthetic algorithmic tasks (Sudoku, Maze); no evaluation on natural reasoning benchmarks (GSM8K, MATH, etc.).
  • The attractor module adds architectural complexity and a hyperparameter (solver tolerance ε, T_max) that may require tuning.
  • Comparison with DEQ could be stronger — the DEQ baseline does not use the same training recipe (nanochat), making the gap partly attributable to the training setup rather than architecture alone.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does equilibrium internalization persist at 7B–70B scale, or does the backbone become saturated before it can absorb the attractor's refinement?
  2. How does Attractor Model training interact with instruction tuning and RLHF — does the internalized equilibrium survive post-training alignment?
  3. Can the attractor module be selectively applied (e.g., only on "hard" tokens) to reduce average inference cost while retaining quality gains?
  4. What happens when multiple attractor modules are stacked at different layers — does this enable hierarchical equilibrium internalization?
  5. How does the one-step gradient approximation compare to phantom gradients at larger scales for language modeling (where it currently works well only for reasoning tasks)?
