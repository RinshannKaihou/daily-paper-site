FeatMap: Understanding image manipulations in the feature space and its implications for feature space geometry

arXiv: 2605.11203

Date: 2026-05-13

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.11203

一句话总结： 本文通过在图像输入空间施加多种变换（几何、光度、局部掩码、扩散模型语义编辑），然后在ConvNeXt和SwinV2的特征空间学习从原始到变换后特征图的映射，发现即使是复杂的语义操作也可通过共享线性模型（单个特征向量上的线性变换）近似实现，SVD和偏差分析表明特征空间中的语义概念以线性子空间结构组织。
TL;DR: FeatMap systematically probes feature space geometry by learning mappings from original to manipulated feature maps across geometric, photometric, masking, and diffusion-based semantic transformations on ConvNeXt and SwinV2 backbones. A shared linear model on individual feature vectors achieves near-parity with global transformer mappers even for non-trivial semantic edits, with SVD analysis revealing that concept transformations correspond to rotations+scalings within linear subspaces rather than additive offsets.

核心贡献

  • 系统性地设计了三类输入空间变换（几何/光度、局部掩码、基于扩散模型的语义编辑），应用于Stanford Cars和CUB-200-2011两个数据集，探测ConvNeXt和SwinV2两种骨干网络的中间特征表示。
  • 设计了从线性到非线性、从局部到全局的四种映射模型（linear/MLP/CNN/transformer），同时评估重建质量（LPIPS、SSIM、MdnCS）和语义保真度（Top-1准确率、预测一致性、JSD）。
  • 发现共享线性模型在单个特征向量上即可逼近全局Transformer映射器的效果，即使在高度非平凡的语义操作（如车身颜色、轮毂、车灯编辑）上也仅产生很小的质量退化。
  • 通过SVD分析权重矩阵和偏差贡献比，发现映射主要由Wx主导（bias影响可忽略）、奇异值偏离1表明存在缩放成分，这一结果支持特征空间以线性子空间而非仿射/凸结构组织的假设。
  • 提出利用生成式图像编辑模型（Qwen-Image-Edit）作为探针来研究特征空间几何的新范式，发现特征层越深，线性映射效果越好。

Key Contributions

  • Comprehensive framework probing feature space geometry of ConvNeXt and SwinV2 across three categories of input manipulations (geometric/photometric, local masking, diffusion-based semantic editing) on Stanford Cars (16,185 images, 196 classes) and CUB-200-2011 datasets.
  • Systematic evaluation of four mapping architectures (linear, MLP, CNN, transformer) with dual assessment: reconstruction quality (LPIPS, SSIM, masked median cosine similarity) and semantic fidelity (Top-1 accuracy, prediction agreement, Jensen-Shannon divergence via frozen-backbone classifiers).
  • Key finding that shared linear models operating on individual feature vectors achieve competitive reconstruction with global transformer mappers — especially in deeper layers (feat2/feat3) — even for non-trivial semantic manipulations.
  • SVD-based geometric analysis reveals: (1) bias contribution is negligible (input dominance ratio > 0.99, MdnCS between Wx and Wx+b > 0.9992), ruling out affine subspaces; (2) singular values significantly deviate from 1 with non-trivial scaling, inconsistent with bounded concept definitions but consistent with linear subspace structures; (3) spectral entropy ranges 5.4–6.5 across manipulations, indicating broad subspace utilization.
  • Introduction of generative image editing models (Qwen-Image-Edit) as probes for feature space geometry, enabling controlled semantic perturbations that were previously inaccessible.

Methodology

The framework proceeds as follows:

  1. Backbone models: ConvNeXt-Base and Swin Transformer V2-Base, initialized with official ImageNet-pretrained weights. ConvNeXt features extracted from all four stages (128→1024 channels, 72×72→9×9 spatial); SwinV2 from the final stage (1024-dim, 12×12).
  1. Input manipulations:
  • Geometric: rotation, mirroring (with feature vector reordering to align spatial positions).
  • Photometric: grayscale, hue shift, Gaussian noise, color jitter.
  • Local masking: fixed rectangular regions occluded with solid color.
  • Semantic: prompted edits via Qwen-Image-Edit (body color change, rim color change, headlight toggle, part removal, etc.).
  1. Mapping architectures:
  • Linear: shared Wx+b per feature vector, no cross-spatial interaction.
  • MLP: 1 hidden layer (512 units), ReLU, dropout 0.2.
  • CNN: 2 conv layers (3×3), hidden 512, BatchNorm, ReLU, Dropout2d 0.2.
  • Transformer: 4 layers, 8 heads, hidden 512, input/output projections, dropout 0.1.
  1. Training objective: Weighted combination of MSE (λ=0.3) and median cosine similarity loss (λ=0.7), chosen empirically.
  1. Feature reconstruction: FeatInv (conditional diffusion model) maps feature-space outputs back to image space for visual evaluation.
  1. Semantic evaluation: Frozen backbone + linear classification head fine-tuned on Stanford Cars (196 classes). Metrics: Top-1 accuracy, Top-1 agreement with original predictions, JSD between probability distributions.
  1. Geometric analysis: SVD of linear weight matrices; bias vs. weight dominance analysis; spectral entropy computation; effective rank assessment across layers and manipulation groups.

Results & Highlights

  • All transformations are mappable across both backbones and all feature stages.
  • Linear model is depth-dependent: substantial improvement from feat0→feat3; deeper layers are more linearly mappable. Non-linear models show less pronounced layer dependence, indicating earlier layers need non-linear corrections.
  • Quantitative results (ConvNeXt feat3, semantic editing): Linear achieves LPIPS 0.21 / MdnCS 0.83 vs. Transformer LPIPS 0.16 / MdnCS 0.86 — a small gap for a drastically simpler model.
  • Semantic fidelity is preserved: Top-1 accuracy and agreement remain largely stable across semantic, photometric, and masking manipulations; geometric transformations show the largest gap due to spatial reordering challenges.
  • Bias is negligible: Input dominance ratio > 0.99 across all settings; MdnCS(Wx, Wx+b) > 0.9992. This argues against affine subspace structure.
  • SVD reveals non-trivial scaling: Singular values deviate significantly from 1, concentrated in early ranks at deeper layers. This is inconsistent with bounded geometric structures (convex bodies) and most consistent with linear subspaces.
  • Architecture-independent findings: ConvNeXt and SwinV2 show qualitatively similar results, suggesting geometric regularity is a property of the feature space rather than an inductive bias of either architecture.
  • Mirror-h is an outlier: Highest spectral entropy (~6.5) among all manipulations, while other geometric transforms stay below 5.8.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty (High): The use of generative image editing models to probe feature space geometry is genuinely novel. The systematic comparison across mapping architectures and transformation categories goes well beyond prior work that focused on single transformation types. The argument chain from mapping feasibility → SVD analysis → linear subspace conclusion is creative.
  • Rigor (Good): Dual evaluation (reconstruction + semantic), two backbones, two datasets, four mapping architectures, and multiple metrics provide strong empirical coverage. However, the core claim about linear subspace structure is based on indirect evidence — mapping feasibility and weight matrix properties — rather than direct geometric measurement of the feature manifold.
  • Reproducibility (Good): Standard architectures (ConvNeXt, SwinV2 from timm), public datasets (Stanford Cars, CUB-200-2011), well-specified hyperparameters. The generative editing model (Qwen-Image-Edit) is identified. Code release would make it fully reproducible.
  • Significance (High): The finding that linear models suffice for semantic manipulations has implications for concept-based XAI, representation learning theory, and mechanistic interpretability. It challenges manifold-based concept definitions and supports the linear representation hypothesis.
  • Red flags: (1) The conclusion that feature spaces are organized as linear subspaces relies on the assumption that mapping feasibility reflects underlying geometry — this is a strong inferential leap. (2) The study uses relatively small training sets (1,000 train / 300 test per semantic manipulation) though the authors note stability at larger scale. (3) Reconstruction metrics can be "overly optimistic" — the authors themselves note that masked MdnCS may report high similarity despite partial semantic changes. (4) It remains unclear whether observed effects come from the mapping, FeatInv, or their interaction.

Strengths

  • Exceptionally systematic experimental design covering multiple transformation categories, architectures, datasets, and mapping models.
  • Surprising and impactful central finding that linear models suffice for complex semantic edits, with strong empirical support.
  • Creative use of generative editing models as scientific instruments for probing representations.
  • Thorough geometric analysis (SVD, bias analysis, spectral entropy) that provides mechanistic insight beyond mere performance metrics.
  • Architecture-agnostic finding (ConvNeXt ≈ SwinV2) strengthens the generality of conclusions.
  • Honest and thoughtful discussion of limitations and alternative explanations.

Limitations

  • Indirect evidence: Mapping feasibility is a proxy for, not a direct measurement of, feature space geometry. The linear subspace conclusion is one plausible interpretation but not a formal proof.
  • Vision-only: All experiments are on image classification models. Generalization to LLMs, multimodal models, or other domains is untested.
  • Semantic manipulation constraints: Depend on the quality of the generative editing model (Qwen-Image-Edit), which sometimes produces incomplete or off-target edits. Finding universally applicable semantic manipulations is difficult, especially for CUB-200-2011.
  • Reconstruction metric caveats: Masked MdnCS can be overly optimistic; cosine similarity is less sensitive to fine-grained differences in early layers.
  • Confounded evaluation: The interaction between the learned mapping and the FeatInv reconstruction model is not disentangled.
  • Feature normalization effects: Different normalization strategies were needed for different layers, introducing an additional variable.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How would these mapping experiments generalize to LLM hidden states — can semantic input perturbations (e.g., paraphrasing, style transfer) be captured by linear mappings in transformer language model representations?
  • The spectral entropy analysis reveals manipulation-dependent subspace utilization; could this be developed into a diagnostic tool for measuring the "linearity" of different layers or model architectures?
  • The finding that linear mappings involve non-trivial scaling (not pure rotations) challenges additive offset models like word analogy arithmetic — how does this reconcile with observations in LLM representation steering?
  • Could the FeatMap probing methodology be combined with sparse autoencoder (SAE) features to identify which SAE latents correspond to the linear subspaces discovered by the mapping analysis?
  • The authors note that positional information in SwinV2 may help local models with masking — how does implicit positional encoding in different architectures affect the feasibility of local linear mappings?
  • What is the relationship between the effective rank of learned mappings and the intrinsic dimensionality of the corresponding concept subspaces?
