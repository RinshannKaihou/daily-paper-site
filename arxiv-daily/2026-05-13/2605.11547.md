> **一句话总结：** 提出SharpEuler，一种免训练的流匹配采样器，通过离线标定轨迹加速度（速度场变化率）构建锐度分布，经分位数变换生成优化的时间步网格，在固定NFE预算下显著提升FLUX等大模型的低步数采样质量。

> **TL;DR:** SharpEuler is a training-free sampler for flow matching that profiles a pretrained model offline by estimating where the learned velocity field changes most rapidly (trajectory acceleration), then uses a quantile transform to produce an optimized timestep grid. At the same NFE budget, it dramatically improves low-step sampling — e.g., reducing FLUX.1-dev FID from 90.32 to 32.76 at B=16 — by changing only where (not how many) Euler evaluations are placed.

## 核心贡献

- 提出SharpEuler采样器，仅通过改变Euler积分的时间步放置位置（不改模型、不重训练、不增加NFE）即可提升流匹配模型采样质量
- 离线标定阶段：使用细粒度Euler轨迹计算速度场的有限差分加速度，平均后得到锐度分布 S̄(t)
- 引入指数参数 γ 控制锐度分布的形状：γ=0.5 对应分布匹配最优，γ=1.0 对应保真度-覆盖率权衡最优
- 从三个理论原则为方法提供支撑：数值原理（加速度控制Euler截断误差）、变分原理（幂律时间步密度）、统计稳定性保证
- 在合成分布和FLUX.1-dev（12B参数文生图模型）上验证，低NFE（8-20步）下大幅优于均匀调度和ETS

## Key Contributions

- Proposes SharpEuler, a training-free, solver-aware timestep schedule for flow matching that changes only *where* Euler evaluations are placed — not the model, solver, or NFE count
- Offline calibration: runs fine-grained Euler trajectories on a small calibration set (e.g., 64 prompts), computes finite-difference velocity accelerations, and averages to obtain a sharpness profile S̄(t)
- Converts the sharpness profile into a monotone timestep grid via quantile transform (inverse CDF interpolation) for any desired inference budget B
- Three theoretical justifications: (1) numerical principle — trajectory acceleration ∥ẍ(t)∥ controls both Euler local truncation error and leading modified-equation bias; (2) variational principle — natural allocation objectives yield power-law densities ρ*(t) ∝ I(t)^{1/(β+1)}; (3) statistical guarantee — calibrated schedule is near-oracle with finite-sample stability at rate O(√(log(N/δ)/M))
- Demonstrates large improvements on synthetic 2D distributions (mode coverage + fidelity) and FLUX.1-dev text-to-image generation (e.g., FID 32.76 vs 90.32 at B=16), winning 50/50 pairwise GPT-5.5 vision judge comparisons at all budgets

## Methodology

SharpEuler has two stages:

**Offline calibration (Algorithm 1):**
1. Select M calibration inputs (e.g., 64 prompts for conditional models)
2. Run fine-grained N-step Euler trajectories (N >> inference budget B) on a uniform reference grid
3. Record consecutive velocity evaluations V_{j,i} along each trajectory
4. Estimate finite-difference accelerations â_{j,i} = (V_{j,i+1} - V_{j,i}) / d_i, where d_i averages adjacent step sizes
5. Average over calibration set to get global sharpness profile S̄_i = (1/M) Σ ∥â_{j,i}∥
6. Form exponent-shaped signal I^γ_i = (S̄_i + ε_a)^γ (γ > 0, ε_a small floor)
7. Smooth with Gaussian kernel (bandwidth σ), normalize to discrete masses, build empirical CDF
8. Store inverse-CDF map for online use

**Online sampling (Algorithm 2):**
1. For budget B, compute B+1 quantiles via the stored inverse-CDF map
2. Convert to descending reverse-time schedule {s_k}
3. Run standard Euler integration on this non-uniform grid — exactly B model evaluations

The approach is justified by three principles:
- **Numerical (Proposition 1):** Euler's local truncation error is h²/2 · ∥ẍ(t)∥ + O(h³). Backward error analysis confirms the same quantity controls the leading modified-equation bias.
- **Variational (Proposition 2):** Minimizing F_β(ρ; I) = ∫ I(t)ψ_β(ρ(t))dt over normalized densities yields ρ*(t) ∝ I(t)^{1/(β+1)}. The Euler-risk proxy (β=1) selects γ=1/2 (square-root law).
- **Statistical (Theorem 2):** With M calibration trajectories on an N-interval grid, W₁ distance between calibrated and population-optimal terminal distributions is bounded by C_Eul · J(w*)/B + O(√(log(N/δ)/M)) + bias terms.

## Results & Highlights

**Synthetic 2D distributions (Branched Manifold & Rotated Grid):**
- At B=8, SharpEuler γ=1.0 achieves Density 0.913 and Coverage 0.872 on Rotated Grid (vs Uniform: 0.426 / 0.581)
- γ=0.5 consistently minimizes W₂² (best distributional match), while γ=1.0 gives best coverage-fidelity tradeoff
- At B=16, the gap narrows but SharpEuler remains superior across all metrics

**FLUX.1-dev (12B params, 1024×1024, 80 held-out prompts):**
- Calibration: 64 prompts, 50-step reference grid (one-time cost)
- At B=12: RMSE drops 39.97→16.51, FID drops 104.77→48.65 (vs uniform)
- At B=16: FID drops 90.32→32.76; CLIP scores preserved (~0.33)
- GPT-5.5 vision judge: SharpEuler wins 50/50 pairwise comparisons at all budgets (B=8 to B=24)
- At B=16: SharpEuler scores 9.04 vs Pipeline 6.82 (mean Δ = +2.22, p < 10⁻²¹)
- Shifted schedule (α=3) shows negligible improvement over uniform, confirming the gain is not from a generic monotone shift

**Key ablation findings:**
- γ=0.5: best distributional agreement (minimizes W₂²), recommended when goal is overall sample quality
- γ=1.0: best coverage-fidelity tradeoff, recommended for practical generation
- γ=1.5: highest density but can lose coverage (over-concentrates samples)

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: High.** First timestep scheduler for learned flow samplers that explicitly derives its schedule from a solver-aware Euler error proxy (trajectory acceleration) estimated via offline finite differences. The connection between Euler LTE, backward error analysis, and the sharpness profile is clean and original.
- **Rigor: Very strong.** Three independent theoretical justifications (numerical, variational, statistical) with formal propositions and a statistical stability theorem. The variational framework elegantly explains why power-law densities arise naturally.
- **Reproducibility: High.** Code is publicly available. Method uses only standard operations (finite differences, Gaussian smoothing, quantile transform, Euler integration). No special hardware beyond the base model required.
- **Significance: High for practical deployment.** Directly addresses the inference cost bottleneck in flow matching without retraining. The FLUX.1-dev results are compelling — near-50-step quality at 16 steps is a meaningful speedup.
- **Red flags / concerns:**
  - The method is solver-specific (designed for Euler only); extending to higher-order solvers requires re-derivation
  - Calibration cost is not reported (how long does the 64-prompt, 50-step calibration take?)
  - Sensitivity to calibration set size M and smoothing bandwidth σ is not systematically ablated
  - The approach may not work well with distilled models or models tuned to specific sampling grids (acknowledged by authors)
  - Comparison to DPM-Solver, UniPC, or other higher-order solvers at the same NFE is missing — it's unclear if a better solver at B steps would outperform SharpEuler's smarter Euler schedule

## Strengths

- Training-free: no model modification, no retraining, no additional solver stages
- Principled: grounded in numerical analysis, variational optimization, and statistical learning theory
- Simple implementation: offline calibration + standard Euler integration on a non-uniform grid
- Strong empirical results: consistent improvements across synthetic and large-scale (12B param) settings
- Deterministic and fixed-cost at inference: exactly B evaluations, same as uniform Euler
- Flexible exponent γ allows tuning for different objectives (distributional match vs. fidelity)

## Limitations

- Solver-specific: designed for first-order Euler; not directly applicable to higher-order or adaptive solvers without re-derivation
- Requires offline calibration step with computational overhead (cost not quantified in the paper)
- May not transfer well to distilled models or models co-tuned with specific schedules
- Only compared against uniform, shifted, and ETS baselines — no comparison with higher-order solvers (DPM-Solver, UniPC) at equivalent NFE
- Sensitivity to calibration set composition and size not thoroughly explored
- The smoothing bandwidth σ and floor ε_a are hyperparameters that may need tuning per model

## Relevance

该方法通过优化Euler积分的时间步分配来提升流匹配模型的采样效率，与扩散/流匹配模型推理加速、数值ODE求解器设计等方向直接相关，对实际部署大规模生成模型（如FLUX）有直接应用价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does SharpEuler compare to DPM-Solver or UniPC at the same NFE budget on FLUX.1-dev?
- What is the wall-clock cost of the offline calibration step, and how does quality scale with the number of calibration prompts M?
- Can the sharpness-aware principle be extended to higher-order solvers (midpoint, RK4) by deriving the appropriate local error proxies?
- How robust is the calibrated schedule across different conditioning distributions (e.g., different prompt styles or domains)?
- Could SharpEuler be combined with model distillation or consistency training for further acceleration?
- Does the approach transfer to video generation models (e.g., Pyramidal Flow Matching) where NFE budgets are even more critical?
