TL;DR: This paper applies Sparse Autoencoders to Whisper's encoder to extract interpretable monosemantic features from frame-level audio embeddings. It discovers features spanning phonetic, lexical, and non-linguistic boundaries, and demonstrates cross-lingual feature steering — the first mechanistic interpretability study of an audio/Speech Recognition model using SAEs.

核心贡献

  • 首次将SAE应用于语音识别模型：在Whisper编码器的帧级嵌入上训练稀疏自编码器，填补了SAE在音频处理模型中的应用空白。
  • 发现多样化的单语义特征：揭示了跨越语音学、词汇层面和非语言学（如背景噪声、说话人特征）边界的可解释特征。
  • 跨语言特征操控：展示了通过操控SAE潜在特征可以影响模型在不同语言上的行为，证明了Whisper编码器具有跨语言的内部表征。
  • 验证了Whisper编码器的语言信息编码能力：实验表明Whisper编码器内部蕴含丰富的语言学信息，而非仅仅是声学特征。

Key Contributions

  • First application of Sparse Autoencoders to a speech recognition model (Whisper), bridging the gap between mechanistic interpretability research in text LLMs and audio models.
  • Discovery of diverse monosemantic features across phonetic, lexical, and non-linguistic boundaries in Whisper's encoder representations.
  • Demonstration of cross-lingual feature steering, showing that manipulating individual SAE latent dimensions can influence model behavior across different languages.
  • Empirical validation that the Whisper encoder encodes rich linguistic information beyond simple acoustic features.

Methodology

The authors train a Sparse Autoencoder on frame-level embeddings extracted from the Whisper encoder. The SAE projects the dense encoder representations into a high-dimensional sparse latent space, where individual latent dimensions ideally correspond to interpretable monosemantic features. The methodology follows the established SAE interpretability pipeline adapted from text-based LLM research: extract activations, train the SAE with sparsity constraints, then analyze individual features for semantic content. Feature analysis spans linguistic properties (phonemes, words) and non-linguistic properties (noise, speaker characteristics). Cross-lingual steering is performed by activating or deactivating specific latent features and observing changes in ASR output across languages.

Results & Highlights

  • The SAE successfully decomposes Whisper encoder embeddings into interpretable sparse features.
  • Diverse monosemantic features are identified across multiple granularities: phonetic-level, word-level, and non-linguistic features (e.g., background noise types).
  • Cross-lingual feature steering demonstrates that some features have consistent effects across multiple languages, suggesting shared multilingual representations in Whisper.
  • The work establishes that Whisper's encoder contains richer linguistic structure than previously understood.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High — this is the first application of SAEs to ASR models, extending mechanistic interpretability beyond text-based LLMs.
  • Rigor: Moderate — the abstract claims are compelling, but the degree of quantitative evaluation versus qualitative feature inspection needs verification from the full paper.
  • Reproducibility: Likely good — Whisper is open-source, and SAE training is a well-documented procedure. The frame-level embedding extraction should be straightforward to replicate.
  • Significance: Significant for the mechanistic interpretability community, as it opens a new domain (speech) for SAE-based analysis. The cross-lingual steering results are particularly noteworthy.
  • Red flags: The abstract does not report quantitative metrics (e.g., reconstruction loss, sparsity levels, downstream task impact of steering). The scalability of the approach to larger ASR models is unclear.

Strengths

  • Pioneering work applying SAE-based mechanistic interpretability to speech recognition.
  • Cross-lingual feature steering is a novel and potentially impactful finding.
  • Open-source model (Whisper) ensures community accessibility.
  • Bridges two active research areas: speech processing and mechanistic interpretability.

Limitations

  • Unclear how well SAE features scale to more complex speech phenomena (e.g., code-switching, heavy accents).
  • The relationship between discovered features and model performance is not well established from the abstract alone.
  • SAE training hyperparameters (expansion factor, sparsity penalty) may significantly affect feature quality; sensitivity analysis is needed.
  • Limited to Whisper; generalization to other ASR architectures (e.g., Conformer-based models) is not demonstrated.
