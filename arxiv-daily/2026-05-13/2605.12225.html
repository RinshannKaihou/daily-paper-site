Mechanistic Interpretability of ASR models using Sparse Autoencoders

arXiv: 2605.12225

Date: 2026-05-13

Tags: [mechanistic-interpretability, sparse-autoencoder, speech-recognition, whisper, cross-lingual, feature-steering, audio-interpretability]

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.12225

一句话总结： 本文首次将稀疏自编码器（SAE，TopK k=45，16000 维潜空间）应用于 Whisper-base 编码器输出，发现语音学、词汇、形态、语义（亵渎词、数字）等多层次单语义特征，并通过激活操控证明 latent 3584 能跨英/法/西三语插入或移除脏话（法语未参与 SAE 训练），证明 Whisper 编码器蕴含语言无关的抽象语义信息。
TL;DR: This paper trains a TopK (k=45) Sparse Autoencoder (16000 latent dims, 16.4M params) on frame-level embeddings from the Whisper-base encoder (512-dim), trained on 646,769 audio files (~200M frames) from LJSpeech, LibriSpeech, VoxCeleb1, Mozilla Common Voice, SLR, and Musan. On a 28,414-file analysis set it uncovers monosemantic features spanning phonetic (diphone R UW1: precision 88.7%, recall 64.9%), lexical (word "his": precision 99.3%, recall 81.4%), morphological (-ly suffix: precision 87.2%, recall 17.8%), and semantic (profanity latent 3584: recall 35.4%, joint recall with latent 104 of 89.7%; numbers latent 7710: precision 38.1%, recall 79.2%) categories, and demonstrates cross-lingual steering where deactivating latent 3584 removes profanity across English, Spanish, and French — a language absent from SAE training data.

核心贡献

  • 首次将 SAE 机制可解释性方法应用于端到端语音识别模型（Whisper），填补了 SAE 在音频模型中的空白（此前工作仅针对文本 LLM 或说话人识别模型）
  • 在 Whisper-base 编码器最终层输出上训练 TopK（k=45）稀疏自编码器（16000 维潜空间，16.4M 参数），发现跨越语音（phones/diphones）、词汇、形态（-ly 后缀）、语义（数字、亵渎词）、位置与非语言学（噪声）边界的单语义特征
  • 通过激活操控（steering）建立因果关系：对 latent 3584 设大负值可移除脏话、设大正值可插入脏话，证明特征非仅相关而是因果编码
  • 展示跨语言操控：latent 3584 在英语、西班牙语、法语上同样可移除脏话，其中法语完全未参与 SAE 训练，表明该语义抽象来自 Whisper 编码器本身而非 SAE 或训练数据

Key Contributions

  • First application of SAE-based mechanistic interpretability to an end-to-end ASR model (Whisper), extending prior SAE work on text LLMs (Bricken et al. 2023; Huben et al. 2024; Templeton et al. 2024) to the audio modality
  • Trains a TopK SAE (k=45, 16000 latent dims, 16.4M trainable parameters) on frame-level embeddings from Whisper-base's final encoder layer, using ~200M frames from 646,769 audio files across 8 diverse speech/noise corpora
  • Discovers a rich hierarchy of monosemantic features: phonetic (phones, diphones, triphones), lexical (whole words like "his"), morphological (-ly, -ed, -ing suffixes), semantic (numbers, profanity), positional (timestamp-localized), and non-linguistic (noise vs voice)
  • Demonstrates causal feature steering: deactivating latent 3584 replaces profanity with phonetically similar words; activating it inserts profanity — and this generalizes cross-lingually to Spanish and French (French absent from SAE training), showing language-agnostic abstract semantics in the Whisper encoder

Methodology

The SAE is inserted immediately after the final layer of the Whisper-base encoder (512-dim output), chosen because this layer directly feeds the decoder and should contain the most transcription-relevant information. The SAE architecture follows OpenAI's sparse autoencoder implementation: a simple linear encoder (512→16000) with a TopK activation (Makhzani & Frey 2014; Gao et al. 2025) constraining to K=45 active latents, then a linear decoder (16000→512) reconstructing the Whisper embedding. Total trainable parameters: 16.4M (two layers of 512×16000 each). Training loss is mean squared error between SAE input and output. Whisper processes 30-second audio windows (up to 1500 frames), so padded frames are identified and removed during SAE training.

Training data comprises 646,769 audio files yielding ~200 million frame-level data points: LJSpeech, LibriSpeech, VoxCeleb1, Mozilla Common Voice English, SLR39, SLR67, SLR61, SLR71–SLR75, and Musan. Data diversity is intentionally chosen to develop a diverse latent feature set.

Feature identification: for each hypothesized feature (language, phonetic, lexical, syntactic, morphological, semantic), precision and recall are computed over an analysis set of 28,414 files (13,084 LJSpeech-1.1, 7,830 Common Voice English, 7,500 Common Voice Spanish, 331 curated English profanity clips). Phonetic/morpheme/lexical analyses use the LJSpeech subset with TextGrid phone-level alignments; other analyses use forced alignment for character/word-level spans. Additionally, all latents are auto-labeled by GPT-OSS 120B (100 median-activation files per latent, up to 20 shown with activated spans marked), assigned to 7 categories (phonetic, orthographic, morphological, lexical, semantic, syntactic, diffuse); a manual audit of 100 latents yields 76% labeling accuracy.

Steering (causal analysis): a latent's value is modified (set to large negative/positive), the SAE decoder reconstructs a modified embedding ε', which is fed to the Whisper decoder to observe transcript changes. This is applied bidirectionally to latent 3584 (profanity) and cross-lingually to English, Spanish, and French Common Voice clips. The authors argue (Appendix A) that because the SAE operates only on the Whisper encoder's image manifold M, which is invariant to SAE training data composition, evaluating on training-seen data is inconsequential — the SAE cannot recover structure the encoder does not encode.

Results & Highlights

  • Language discrimination: latent index 5106 classifies non-English utterances with precision 74.7% and recall 91.2% using a single-frame activation rule; many false positives are non-native English speakers. (Section 4.1)
  • Phonetic feature: latent 6373 represents the diphone R UW1 with precision 88.7% and recall 64.9% (Figure 2). The SAE encodes phones, diphones, and potentially triphones, reflecting co-articulation. (Section 4.2)
  • Lexical feature: latent index 28 represents the word "his" with precision 99.3% and recall 81.4%. (Section 4.3)
  • Morphological feature: latent index 29 correlates with the adverbial suffix "-ly" with precision 87.2% but recall only 17.8% (low recall attributed to feature splitting — a separate latent 9779 also encodes "-ly" adverbs). (Section 4.4)
  • Semantic — numbers: latent 7710 activates on numerical content with precision 38.1% and recall 79.2%; of 7,881 false positives, 3,069 occur in utterances containing numbers (semantic info is not tightly temporally bounded); of 1,272 false negatives, 52.2% are "one"/"first" and 24.8% are "two"/"second", which have their own dedicated latents (feature splitting). (Section 4.5.1, Table 2)
  • Semantic — profanity: latent 3584 identifies swear words with recall 35.4%; combined with latent 104 (specialized for "damn"), joint recall reaches 89.7% at precision 6.6% (profanity occurs in only 0.11% of the corpus; false positive rate 1.45%). (Section 4.5.2, Table 3)
  • Causal steering: setting latent 3584 to a large negative value replaces "fuck"→"focus/falk", "shit"→"sheet"; setting it to large positive inserts profanity into clean transcripts ("the luckier you are"→"the fuck you are"). (Table 4)
  • Cross-lingual steering: deactivating latent 3584 removes profanity in English, Spanish, and French — notably French was entirely absent from SAE training data, demonstrating language-independent semantic encoding in the Whisper encoder itself. (Section 4.5.3, Table 5)
  • Non-linguistic feature: latent 15019 separates noise-only audio (ESC-50) from speech with 97.7% precision. (Appendix C.1)
  • Positional features: latent 12816 activates at mean 8.44s (SD 0.19), latent 15039 at 10.09s (SD 0.04), latent 3604 at 14.04s (SD 0.37) — tightly temporally localized. (Appendix C.2)
  • Automated labeling: GPT-OSS 120B labels achieve 76% accuracy on a 100-latent manual audit. (Section 3.2.2, Appendix B)

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: Genuine. This is the first SAE mechanistic-interpretability study of an end-to-end ASR model (prior SAE work covered text LLMs and the authors' own speaker-recognition work), and the cross-lingual steering into French — a language unseen during SAE training — is a clean, well-argued demonstration that the abstraction lives in Whisper, not the SAE (Appendix A's manifold-invariance argument is the right framing).
  • Rigor — the metrics are uneven and often weak. Many headline features have low recall that the authors attribute to "feature splitting," but no quantitative method is given to detect or control splitting — it functions as a post-hoc explanation for poor recall (e.g., "-ly" at 17.8% recall, profanity latent 3584 alone at 35.4% recall). The profanity feature's combined precision is only 6.6%, which is hard to call "monosemantic" despite the high recall; the paper leans on the base rate (profanity = 0.11%) to justify this, but a 1.45% false positive rate on a 0.11% base rate means the vast majority of flagged tokens are not profanity.
  • No reconstruction-loss or sparsity-quality tradeoff reported. The paper never reports the SAE's MSE reconstruction loss, the fraction of variance explained, or any comparison against alternative K values, latent dimensions, or SAE architectures (e.g., JumpReLU, BatchTopK). The choice of K=45 and 16000 dims is asserted without justification or ablation. The footnote (Section 5) admits "a smaller dimension would likely yield more monophonic representations" but provides no experiment.
  • Only Whisper-base examined. The authors explicitly note (Section 6.1) that only the smallest Whisper model was studied; whether larger Whisper models or other ASR architectures (Conformer, wav2vec) encode the same features is unknown.
  • Analysis set overlaps training data. The paper argues this is inconsequential via the manifold-invariance argument (Appendix A), which is theoretically sound but empirically unverified — no held-out training data ablation is run to confirm the SAE recovers the same features.
  • Steering evidence is qualitative. The steering results (Tables 4–5) are presented as transcript examples with no automated metric (WER change, semantic similarity) quantifying the effect size or failure rate. It is unclear how often steering produces gibberish rather than the clean substitutions shown.
  • Reproducibility: Reasonable — Whisper is open-source, the SAE follows OpenAI's public implementation, and code/data are at an anonymous repo. But the anonymous URL may not persist, and no permanent DOI or permanent repo is given.

Strengths

  • Genuinely novel domain transfer: first SAE study of an ASR model, opening speech modality to mechanistic interpretability
  • The cross-lingual steering into French (unseen in SAE training) is a strong causal argument that abstract semantics reside in the Whisper encoder, not the SAE — well-supported by the Appendix A manifold-invariance formalization
  • Broad feature taxonomy discovered in a single SAE: phonetic (phones/diphones/triphones), lexical, morphological, semantic (numbers/profanity), positional, and non-linguistic (noise) — a surprisingly rich hierarchy for a model trained only on transcription
  • Causal steering (not just correlational probing) establishes that features like profanity are directly encoded, not merely co-occurring
  • Honest about feature splitting/absorption as a limitation, and points to Chanin et al. 2025 for the relevant theory
  • Code, analysis data, and latent examples released (anonymous repo)

Limitations

  • Only Whisper-base (smallest variant) studied; no larger Whisper or other ASR architectures (Conformer, wav2vec2) — generalization unknown
  • Single SAE configuration (K=45, 16000 dims); no sensitivity analysis over K, latent dimension, or SAE architecture — the authors flag this as a resource limitation
  • Many features have low recall attributed to feature splitting, but splitting is neither detected nor controlled quantitatively
  • No reconstruction-loss, variance-explained, or dead-latent statistics reported — SAE quality is not benchmarked
  • Steering results are qualitative transcript examples without automated effect-size metrics (WER, success rate, failure mode analysis)
  • English is the primary focus; cross-lingual findings rest on a single profanity feature in 2 non-English languages
  • Analysis set overlaps SAE training data; the manifold-invariance argument is theoretically sound but empirically unverified via held-out ablation
  • Automated GPT-OSS labeling accuracy is only 76%, introducing noise into the broader latent taxonomy in Appendix B

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How do the discovered features change with SAE latent dimension (e.g., 4096 vs 16000 vs 65536) and K value — does feature splitting diminish at higher dimensions, and what is the reconstruction-loss / feature-granularity tradeoff?
  • Do larger Whisper models (large-v3, turbo) encode the same profanity/numbers/phonetic features at the same latent indices, or do representations reorganize with scale?
  • Can the cross-lingual profanity feature be used constructively — e.g., to detect or filter toxic speech across low-resource languages, or does steering degrade transcription quality too much?
  • What is the quantitative effect of steering on WER and semantic similarity, beyond the qualitative transcript examples shown?
  • Are the positional latents (Appendix C.2) an artifact of Whisper's sinusoidal positional encoding leaking through, or do they represent learned temporal structure — and do they persist if the positional encoding scheme changes?
  • Could SAE features from the encoder predict downstream decoder errors, enabling targeted failure-mode analysis of ASR systems?
