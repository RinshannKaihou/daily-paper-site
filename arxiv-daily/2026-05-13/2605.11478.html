FibQuant: Universal Vector Quantization for Random-Access KV-Cache Compression

arXiv: 2605.11478

Date: 2026-05-13

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.11478

一句话总结： FibQuant 利用 Haar 旋转后 KV-Cache 坐标服从球形-Beta 分布这一理论发现，设计了径向-角度向量码本替代标量量化表，在保持随机访问接口的同时实现了 5×–34× 的压缩比，并在低于 1 比特/坐标的极端压缩场景下显著优于 TurboQuant 等标量方法。
TL;DR: FibQuant identifies that post-Haar-rotation KV-cache coordinates follow a spherical-Beta distribution (not a product source), then replaces scalar quantization tables with a radial–angular vector codebook matched to this canonical distribution, achieving 5×–34× compression on GPT-2 with strict dominance over scalar TurboQuant at all matched rates, including sub-one-bit operating points that no scalar codec can reach.

核心贡献

  • 严格证明了 normalize + Haar 旋转后任意非零输入分布都映射到相同的球形-Beta 分布 $f_{d,k}$（Theorem 1），因此一个共享的离线码本是所有层、头、提示和模型的通用最优对象。
  • 揭示了标量量化在该场景下次优的根源：$k$ 个连续坐标不是独立乘积源，而是具有径向-角度耦合结构的球形-Beta 源。
  • 提出了 FibQuant 径向-角度码本设计：Beta 分位数径向 + Fibonacci/Roberts–Kronecker 准均匀方向 + 多重启 Lloyd–Max 优化。
  • 证明了向量码在相同码率下严格优于标量乘积码（Theorem 2），增益分解为胞腔形状因子 $\gamma_{\text{cell}} \geq 1$ 和密度匹配因子 $\gamma_{\text{dens}} \geq 1$。
  • 通过 $b = (\log_2 N)/k$ 实现了密集的码率轴，支持分数比特（如 2.5, 3.5 bits）和低于 1 比特的操作点，无需校准或变长编码。
  • 在 GPT-2 小模型上实现了 34.1× 压缩（注意力余弦相似度 0.946），在 TinyLlama-1.1B 上 b=2 时困惑度比 TurboQuant 低 3.6×。

Key Contributions

  • Proves the source-agnostic universality (Theorem 1): norm-then-Haar-rotation maps every nonzero input distribution to the same spherical-Beta density $f_{d,k}$ on $\mathcal{B}_k$, making a single shared offline codebook universally optimal across layers, heads, prompts, and models.
  • Identifies that $k$ consecutive post-rotation coordinates form a spherical-Beta source with radial–angular coupling—not an independent product of scalar marginals—exposing the fundamental inefficiency of scalar quantization in this setting.
  • Designs the FibQuant radial–angular codebook: Beta-quantile radii, Fibonacci spiral ($k{=}2$) / Fibonacci sphere ($k{=}3$) / Roberts–Kronecker ($k{\geq}4$) quasi-uniform directions, plus multi-restart Lloyd–Max refinement.
  • Proves strict matched-rate dominance (Theorem 2): the best $k$-vector code strictly outperforms the scalar product specialization, with high-rate gain factoring into cell-shaping ($\gamma_{\text{cell}}$) and density-matching ($\gamma_{\text{dens}}$) terms. Cell shaping alone yields ~1.53 dB gain as $k \to \infty$.
  • Achieves a dense rate axis $b = (\log_2 N)/k$ enabling fractional-bit (1.25, 1.5, 2.5, 3.5 bits) and sub-one-bit ($b \approx 0.19$ at $k{=}32, N{=}64$) operating points with fixed addresses and independent decoding.
  • Demonstrates 34.1× compression at 0.946 attention cosine similarity on GPT-2 small (the only universal random-access method beyond 10×), and 3.6× lower perplexity than TurboQuant at $b{=}2$ on TinyLlama-1.1B.

Methodology

FibQuant operates within the normalize–rotate–store framework used by rotation-based scalar codecs like TurboQuant, preserving the random-access serving contract (fixed-rate, independently decodable token slots). The key innovation is replacing scalar coordinate tables with a vector codebook matched to the true distribution induced by this interface.

Step 1: Canonical source identification. For any nonzero cached vector $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^d$, the encoder stores the fp16 norm $\nu = \|\mathbf{x}\|_2$ and rotates the unit vector via a shared Haar-random orthogonal matrix $\Pi \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$. The rotated unit vector is partitioned into $d/k$ blocks of $k$ consecutive coordinates. Theorem 1 proves that each block has marginal density $f_{d,k}(\mathbf{x}) = C_{d,k}(1 - \|\mathbf{x}\|^2)^{(d-k-2)/2} \mathbf{1}_{\|\mathbf{x}\| \leq 1}$ on $\mathcal{B}_k$ (the spherical-Beta distribution), with $R^2 \sim \text{Beta}(k/2, (d-k)/2)$ and $\mathbf{X}/R$ uniform on $\mathcal{S}^{k-1}$ independent of $R$. This law is universal—independent of the original KV distribution, layer, head, prompt, or token.

Step 2: Radial–angular codebook construction. Each codeword is $\mathbf{c}_n = r_n \mathbf{u}_n$:

  • Radii: Bennett–Gersho companding gives Beta-quantile boundaries $r_n = \sqrt{\text{BetaInv}(q_n; k/2, \beta_{d,k})}$ with $\beta_{d,k} = \frac{k}{k+2} \cdot \frac{d-k-2}{2} + 1$. For $k{=}2$ this has closed form $r_n = \sqrt{1 - (1-q_n)^{4/d}}$.
  • Directions: Planar Fibonacci spiral at $k{=}2$, Fibonacci sphere with golden-angle azimuth at $k{=}3$, Roberts–Kronecker rank-one sequences at $k{\geq}4$.
  • Refinement: Multi-restart ($R{=}4$) Lloyd–Max iteration (25 iterations per restart) on samples from $f_{d,k}$, with empty-cell repair by splitting high-distortion donors. For $k{=}3$, a multi-shell construction factorizes $N = S \cdot M_a$ with $S$ Beta-quantile radii and $M_a$ Fibonacci-sphere directions per shell, optimized by exhaustive factor-pair enumeration.

Step 3: Encoding and decoding. The encoder performs nearest-codeword lookup per block: $i_m = \arg\min_j \|\mathbf{y}^{(m)} - \mathbf{c}_j\|_2^2$. The decoder sets $\hat{\mathbf{y}}^{(m)} = \mathbf{c}_{i_m}$ and returns $\hat{\mathbf{x}} = \nu \Pi^\top \hat{\mathbf{y}}$. Payload rate is $b = (\log_2 N)/k$ bits per coordinate, uniform for every vector and token. A hierarchical encoder with radius-first search + list decoding reduces encoder cost from $O(N)$ to $O(\sqrt{N})$ per block without changing the bitstream.

Loss functions: Intrinsic MSE $\mathcal{L}_{\text{mse}}(\mathcal{C})$ on the canonical source, and downstream attention-output cosine similarity $\mathcal{L}_{\text{attn}}$ measuring fidelity of reconstructed attention outputs.

Results & Highlights

Canonical source MSE (Section 5.1): On $f_{d,k}$ for $d \in \{64, 256\}$, FibQuant strictly dominates scalar TurboQuant at every integer rate, fills fractional-bit operating points between them, and operates alone in the sub-one-bit regime ($b < 1$). Curves shift down by the predicted $1/d$ variance scaling at $d{=}256$.

GPT-2 small KV cache (Section 5.2): On the full KV cache ($L{=}12, H{=}12, T{=}512, d{=}64$; 18.87 MB fp16):

  • Three regimes emerge: up to ~7× compression, FibQuant/TurboQuant/KIVI are comparable; 10–25×, non-rotation universal baselines fail; beyond 20×, only calibrated low-rank SVD and universal FibQuant remain.
  • Extreme point: $(k, N) = (64, 16384)$ achieves 34.1× compression at 0.946 attention cosine similarity.
  • Full-vector ($k{=}d$) outperforms half-vector ($k{=}d/2$) at lower rate because all bits are spent on angular packing (the quantity read by attention inner products).

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. The spherical-Beta source identification for post-rotation KV-cache coordinates is a genuinely new theoretical insight. The radial–angular codebook construction elegantly combines classical ingredients (Beta companding, Fibonacci lattices, Lloyd–Max) in a novel way matched to this specific distribution.
  • Rigor: Strong. Formal proofs of source universality (Theorem 1), strict matched-rate dominance (Theorem 2) with gain factorization into cell-shaping and density-matching terms, and large-$d$ finite-rate characterization (Theorem 3). The high-rate analysis follows established Bennett–Gersho–Zador methodology correctly.
  • Reproducibility: Moderate. The algorithm is fully specified (pseudocode in Table 2), codebook construction uses standard ingredients, and training parameters are given ($M{=}30N$ samples, $R{=}4$ restarts, $T_{\text{LM}}{=}25$ iterations). However, no public code repository is mentioned, and the hierarchical encoder (Appendix F) is only sketched.
  • Significance: High potential. Sub-one-bit compression for KV caches addresses a real and growing production bottleneck. The dense rate axis is practically valuable: cache budgets are determined by VRAM/latency constraints, not by elegant integer-bit quantizers. The universality claim (no calibration needed) is a major practical advantage over methods like PALU.
  • Red flags:
  • Scale limitation: Largest end-to-end experiment is TinyLlama-1.1B (1.1B params). GPT-2 results measure attention-output fidelity, not generation quality. Scaling to 7B–70B+ models with 100k+ context remains unverified.
  • No wall-clock latency measurements: The paper discusses FLOP counts but does not report actual decoding throughput or latency, which is the practical bottleneck.
  • Encoder complexity: At aggressive operating points ($k{=}64, N{=}16384$), naive encoding is $O(dN) \approx 10^6$ FLOPs per cached vector. The hierarchical scheme (Appendix F) is only sketched, not benchmarked.
  • Bootstrap confidence intervals over rotations and prompts are noted as missing.

Strengths

  • Clean theoretical contribution with formal proofs connecting classical VQ theory to a practical systems problem.
  • Preserves the random-access property critical for deployable KV-cache codecs.
  • The dense rate axis (fractional and sub-one-bit) fills a genuine gap left by scalar codecs like TurboQuant ($b \geq 1$) and RABitQ (1-bit only).
  • Universality (no calibration, one shared codebook for all layers/heads/models) is a major practical advantage.
  • Significant end-to-end improvement at low bitrates: 3.6× perplexity reduction over TurboQuant at $b{=}2$.
  • The full-vector ($k{=}d$) insight—that spending all bits on angular packing aligns better with attention fidelity—is elegant and well-motivated.

Limitations

  • Model scale: Only tested on GPT-2 small (124M) and TinyLlama-1.1B. Behavior at frontier-model scale is unknown.
  • No generation quality metrics: GPT-2 experiments measure attention-output cosine similarity, not downstream task performance or human evaluation.
  • No latency/throughput benchmarks: Wall-clock decoding overhead of vector codebook lookup vs. scalar table access is not measured.
  • Finite-rate optimality gap: The theory characterizes high-rate behavior; finite-rate Lloyd–Max optimality for every $(d, k, N)$ is not characterized in closed form.
  • Shared rotation assumption: A single Haar-random matrix $\Pi$ is shared across all layers, heads, and models. Sensitivity to the specific rotation draw is not analyzed (though universality should mitigate this).
  • No comparison with learned/codecalibrated methods: PALU (calibrated low-rank projection) is mentioned but not directly compared in end-to-end tables; the comparison is mostly against universal (calibration-free) baselines.
