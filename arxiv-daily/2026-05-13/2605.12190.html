Information-Theoretic Generalization Bounds for Sequential Decision Making

TL;DR: This paper extends supersample-based conditional mutual information (CMI) generalization bounds from the batch i.i.d. setting to sequential decision-making problems by introducing a sequential supersample framework (SCMI) that separates the learner filtration from a proof-side enlargement, yielding generalization bounds for online learning, streaming active learning, and stochastic multi-armed bandits with improved regret rates.

Key Contributions

  • Sequential supersample framework: Introduces a causal version of the batch supersample construction that preserves the selected–ghost comparison while respecting the sequential nature of learning. The learner history $\mathcal{H}_t$ contains only selected-path observations, while the proof-side enlargement $\mathcal{G}_{t-1}$ additionally reveals the current paired row for analysis.
  • General SCMI bounds (Theorem 2): The sequential generalization gap decomposes into roundwise selector–loss correlations, controlled by sequential CMI: $|\text{Err}^{\text{seq}}_n| \leq \frac{2}{n}\sqrt{\sum_{t=1}^n I(L_t^+; U_t | \mathcal{G}_{t-1})}$.
  • Bernstein-type fast-rate refinement (Theorem 3): When a conditional second-moment (Bernstein) condition holds, the information term enters linearly rather than through a square root, yielding fast-rate bounds.
  • Online learning and sequential complexity: Connects SCMI with sequential Rademacher complexity and, under pathwise realizability, with the Littlestone dimension — the online analogue of the VC dimension.
  • Streaming active learning (Theorem 4): Derives an evaluated SCMI bound for importance-weighted active learning, where the learner adaptively decides whether to query labels.
  • Stochastic bandits with smoothing (Theorem 5): Achieves $\mathcal{O}(\sqrt{KT\log K / \Delta_{\min}})$ static regret for smoothed exponential-weights policies, improving the horizon dependence from $\mathcal{O}(T^{2/3})$ (existing PAC-Bayes) to $\mathcal{O}(\sqrt{T})$ under the positive-gap assumption.

Methodology

The core methodological innovation is the sequential supersample construction with two information objects at each round $t$:

  1. Learner filtration $\mathcal{H}_t$: Contains only information retained along the selected path (past observations, learner states). The learner never observes the unselected "ghost" coordinate.
  1. Proof-side enlargement $\mathcal{G}_{t-1} = (\mathcal{H}_{t-1}, \tilde{Z}_t)$: A local analysis object that additionally exposes the current latent paired row $(Z_{t,0}, Z_{t,1})$ for the purpose of comparing selected and unselected coordinates. The learner does not observe this.

At each round, a latent supersample row $\tilde{Z}_t = (Z_{t,0}, Z_{t,1})$ is generated, a fair binary selector $U_t$ chooses one coordinate, and the learner updates only from the selected coordinate $Z_t = Z_{t,U_t}$. Under row-wise exchangeability (the conditional law of $(Z_{t,0}, Z_{t,1})$ is invariant under swapping), the train–holdout gap decomposes into roundwise selector–loss correlations (Lemma 1), analogous to the batch correlation identity.

The SCMI term $I(L_t^+; U_t | \mathcal{G}_{t-1})$ measures how much information about the current selector $U_t$ is revealed by the evaluated loss at round $t$, after fixing the selected past and the current paired row. This is the key conceptual difference from terminal CMI: SCMI charges information at the round where it can affect the train–holdout comparison, rather than through a single terminal quantity.

The framework supports predictable learner-side weights $Q_t$ (useful for stopping rules and importance weighting) and applies the Donsker–Varadhan variational representation for the Bernstein refinement.

Results & Highlights

  • Theorem 2 (General SCMI bound): $|\text{Err}^{\text{seq}}_n| \leq \frac{2}{n}\sqrt{\sum_{t=1}^n I(L_t^+; U_t | \mathcal{G}_{t-1})} \leq \frac{2}{n}\sqrt{\sum_{t=1}^n I(W_t; U_t | \mathcal{G}_{t-1})}$, where the first inequality is the evaluated (loss-SCMI) bound and the second uses the data-processing inequality.
  • Theorem 3 (Bernstein-type): Under the nonnegative-excess condition and a Bernstein second-moment condition with parameter $B$, the holdout excess risk is controlled by the training excess risk plus a linear term in the sum of SCMI terms (fast rate).
  • Theorem 4 (Active learning): For streaming active learning with importance weighting and query probability $p_t \geq p_{\min}$, the generalization gap of the terminal predictor is bounded by $\frac{2}{p_{\min} n}\sqrt{\sum_{t=1}^n I(L_{n,t}^+; U_t | \mathcal{G}_{t-1}^{\text{act}})}$.
  • Theorem 5 (Bandits): For smoothed exponential-weights policies with smoothing parameter $\epsilon_t$, the static regret satisfies $\mathbb{E}[\Delta(\tilde{\rho}_t^{\exp})] \leq K\epsilon_{t+1} + \frac{2\log K}{\gamma_t} + \frac{52}{t\epsilon_t\Delta_{\min}}\sum_{s=1}^t I(G_{s,0}(\bar{A}_t); U_s | \mathcal{G}_{s-1}^{\text{bd}})$. With appropriate tuning, this yields $\mathcal{O}(\sqrt{KT\log K / \Delta_{\min}})$ cumulative regret.
  • Connection to sequential complexity: In the online learning specialization, selector prefixes generate a binary tree of counterfactual histories, connecting SCMI with sequential Rademacher complexity. Under pathwise realizability, the SCMI sum is controlled by the Littlestone dimension.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty (4/5): The key insight — separating the learner filtration from a proof-side enlargement to extend CMI to sequential settings — is clean and original. The connection to sequential Rademacher complexity and Littlestone dimension is a nice theoretical contribution. However, the generalization from batch to sequential is a somewhat natural direction given the existing literature.
  • Rigor (5/5): Technically rigorous with careful measure-theoretic treatment. The distinction between $\mathcal{H}_t$ and $\mathcal{G}_{t-1}$ is precisely formalized. All theorems are proved with explicit assumptions and the proofs are detailed in appendices.
  • Reproducibility (4/5): The theoretical framework is self-contained and the construction is clearly described. No experiments are presented, so empirical reproducibility is not applicable. The mathematical definitions are precise enough to be independently verified.
  • Significance (4/5): Unifies generalization analysis across online learning, active learning, and bandits under a single information-theoretic framework. The bandit result improving from $\mathcal{O}(T^{2/3})$ to $\mathcal{O}(\sqrt{T})$ is a concrete improvement. The framework opens routes to PAC-Bayes bounds with data-dependent priors for online learning.
  • Red flags: The paper assumes bounded losses $[0,1]$ throughout. The bandit result requires the positive-gap assumption ($\Delta_{\min} > 0$) and unique optimal arm, which is standard but restrictive. The bounds are in expectation only — no high-probability guarantees.

Strengths

  • Clean and unified framework that generalizes batch CMI to sequential settings via a simple but powerful construction (separating learner filtration from proof-side enlargement)
  • Provides both slow-rate and fast-rate (Bernstein-type) bounds within the same framework
  • Three concrete applications (online learning, active learning, bandits) demonstrate the generality of the approach
  • The bandit application yields a concrete improvement in regret horizon dependence over existing PAC-Bayes bounds
  • Preserves the connection between information-theoretic bounds and classical complexity measures (VC dimension → Littlestone dimension) in the sequential setting
  • The framework is resumable and composable — the SCMI decomposition allows per-round analysis

Limitations

  • Bounded losses only: All results assume losses in $[0,1]$. Extension to heavy-tailed losses is left as open work.
  • Expectation bounds only: No high-probability guarantees are provided. This limits applicability in settings requiring confidence intervals.
  • Positive-gap assumption for bandits: The $\mathcal{O}(\sqrt{T})$ bandit result requires $\Delta_{\min} > 0$ and a unique optimal arm, which does not hold in the minimax regime.
  • No empirical validation: The paper is purely theoretical. While the bounds are non-vacuous in principle (following the batch CMI literature), there is no numerical evaluation of the bound tightness.
  • Open directions not addressed: Contextual bandits, reinforcement learning, dynamic regret, and broader partial-monitoring problems are mentioned as future work but not explored.
  • Computational considerations: The SCMI terms may be difficult to compute or estimate in practice for complex learners.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can the SCMI framework be extended to provide high-probability bounds, perhaps using a PAC-Bayes–style change-of-measure argument combined with the sequential construction?
  2. How do the SCMI bounds behave numerically for practical online learning algorithms (e.g., online gradient descent, follow-the-regularized-leader) — are they non-vacuous and competitive with uniform convergence bounds?
  3. Can the bandit analysis be extended to the minimax (gap-free) regime while retaining the $\sqrt{T}$ dependence, or to contextual bandits where the policy space is structured?
  4. Is there a natural connection between the SCMI framework and the information-theoretic regret bounds for reinforcement learning (e.g., Russo & Van Roy's information ratio)?
  5. Can the row-wise exchangeability assumption be relaxed to accommodate non-stationary environments or adversarial settings while preserving the SCMI decomposition?
