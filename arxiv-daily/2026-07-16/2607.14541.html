Are LLM-Generated GPU Kernels Production-Ready? A Trace-Driven Benchmark and Optimization Agent ↗ · Lingyun Yang†, Yuxiao Wang†, Shenghao Liang, Linfeng Yang, Daocheng Ying, Chunbo You, Rui Zhang, Luping Wang, Yinghao Yu, Guodong Yang, Liping Zhang · ATREX Team · Alibaba · 2026-07-16

Six frontier coding agents reach at best 10.7% of GPU roofline — none beats the deployed production kernel.

On Atrex-Bench — 30 operators, 440 shapes mined from 1,303 production profiles over >10k XPU-A/H20 accelerators and ~20 deployed models — the best agent (GPT-5.5, Sagg=0.107) clears just a tenth of the hardware ceiling. The co-released Atrex-Kernel-Agent (AKA) converts 0%-FlyDSL fallbacks into near-100%-FlyDSL kernels that beat hand-tuned baselines by 1.06x–1.31x on three compute-bound operators. arXiv: 2607.14541

Key metric — best agent vs ceiling

0.107Sagg = 10.7% of hardware roofline

GPT-5.5 leads; Claude Opus 4.7 reaches only 0.059, the other four fall to 0.012–0.047. Closest to production is Opus 4.7 at 0.99x — still no win. Correctness illusion: Qwen3.7-Max posts 84.8% correctness on only 43.8% FlyDSL adoption.

Why this matters

First production-trace GPU-kernel benchmark, weighted by real serving time, with a per-problem roofline ceiling — a deployability signal that rebukes over-optimistic synthetic benchmarks. Both artifacts open-sourced.

from Relevance · ATREX Team, Alibaba

Critical edge

AKA's production-beating margins are single-timed-run point estimates on three hand-picked compute-bound operators — no variance bars, one shape each, no full-benchmark run, no ablation isolating dropout vs GPU-Wiki vs profile loop.

Methodology Results Strengths Limitations Open Questions

01 Methodology trace → contract → roofline

A tracer attaches to live serving on XPU-A / H20 clusters without relaunch, fusing Python-frame context with vendor runtime traces; raw kernels collapse into computation-level operator families so vLLM / SGLang / AITER / RTP-LLM share one problem.

  • Five-file contract per operator. Candidate sees reference.py, input.py, shapes.json, prompt. Hidden from generation: metadata.json + roofline.json — anti-leakage by construction.
  • Per-problem roofline. Troofline = max(F/Pτ, M/β) with dtype-specific compute peak & bandwidth calibrated once per accelerator.
  • Importance weighting (Eq. 3). wi = app card-hour share × per-phase device-time share; top-5 ops hold ≈64% (unified_attention alone 36.1%). Headline Sagg = Σ wi·Si is arithmetic, so one zeroed operator does not erase signal.
  • Three-stage gate. compile → correctness (K=5 seeds, atol=1e-2) → perf (median end-to-end, frequency-lock, L2 flush, per-shape subprocess isolation).
  • FlyDSL deliberately absent from pre-training — probes learning, not recall.
  • AKA agent: 7-stage outer + 8-stage inner loop; profile-driven measure-revise, optimization dropout (partial restart masking stale memory), layered GPU Wiki (298 kernels + 244 docs, every spec cited).

  • No candidate beats production. Opus 4.7 closest at 0.99x, GPT-5.5 0.85x, Qwen3.7-Max 0.19x, DeepSeek-V4-Pro 0.12x. vs torch.compile only Opus (2.29x) & GPT-5.5 (3.06x) win on the median op.
  • Correctness illusion is measurable. Qwen3.7-Max: 84.8% correctness vs 43.8% FlyDSL adoption — the rest hides in scaled_dot_product_attention / precompiled AITER. Opus is the honest exception (negative gap).
  • Memory-bound solved, compute-bound not. Opus memory-bound median S=0.207 is >20x its compute-bound 0.009; GPT-5.5 is the only model with non-trivial compute-bound achievement (0.074).
  • Hardness is operator-specific. Hardest — fp8_blockscale_fused_moe (22.2% mean pass), fused_rmsnorm_quant (34.8%), per_token_group_quant_fp8 (44.7%), attention_forward (45.2%) — uniformly low-precision-fused / matrix-engine.
  • Failure taxonomy (683 units). 53.4% compile (GLM-5.1 alone: 232), 43.0% runtime (257 numeric mismatches), 3.5% correct-but-slow. Opus's 46 failures are uniquely half correct-but-slow — the soft perf signature of the strongest backend.
  • Volume ≠ quality. DeepSeek-V4-Pro emits 6.56M tokens for 18 correct ops (365K/op); GPT-5.5 emits 1.19M for 26 (46K/op).
  • AKA closes both gaps. On Qwen3.7-Max: chunk_gated_delta_rule_state 0.001→0.03 S (0.03x→1.20x prod); attention_forward 0.06→0.40 (0.17x→1.11x); mla_decode 0.10x→1.06x. On Opus lifts attention_forward 0.28→0.42 (0.78x→1.17x).

03 Strengths what holds up

  • First distribution-honest benchmark. Problems, weights and denominator all come from production serving — the score is a deployability proxy, not a synthetic average.
  • FlyDSL-adoption metric turns an invisible "specification shortcutting" pass into a recorded number, exposing the correctness illusion that pass-rate benchmarks miss.
  • Disclosed failure taxonomy — all 683 failing units broken down by stage and model, plus a model-independent operator-difficulty ranking.
  • Concrete anti-gaming engineering — hidden metadata/roofline, restricted tool surface, isolated subprocesses, frequency-lock, L2 flush.
  • AKA design discipline — every hardware spec cited, every kernel edit justified by profiler evidence. Good practice others should adopt.

04 Limitations where it frays

  • Single-hardware empirics. All headline numbers on XPU-A (AMD-class, desensitized); H20 sampled but not benchmarked.
  • Inference-only. Backward / optimizer-step kernels out of scope for this release.
  • Narrow AKA evaluation. Three operators, one shape each, one timed run, no variance, no full-benchmark run, no ablation, no AKO/KDA comparison.
  • FlyDSL-specific. Out-of-distribution DSL ⇒ absolute Sagg not comparable to CUDA/Triton benchmarks; Triton/Gluon/CuteDSL deferred.
  • Weight skew. unified_attention alone holds 36.1%; a different fleet mix could re-rank agents.
  • "Overtake production" partly explained away — the general AITER varlen kernel only reaches ~36% of roof on the awkward head-dim-72 shape, so a shape-specialized win is less surprising than it sounds.

05 Questions for Further Exploration open threads

  1. Does the agent ranking change — and does the correctness illusion shrink, stay, or morph — when the target DSL is Triton or CUDA (in-distribution) instead of FlyDSL?
  2. Does AKA's production-beating margin hold across the full 440-shape benchmark with variance bars, or collapse outside the three hand-picked compute-bound operators?
  3. What is the individual contribution of optimization dropout vs the GPU-Wiki vs the profile loop in an ablation under the same contract?
  4. How transferable are the production-importance weights and operator ranking to an NVIDIA-only or H20-heavy fleet with a different model mix?
  5. Can the memory-bound vs compute-bound diagnostic become an automatic routing signal that picks the AKA optimization category per operator?
  6. Given AKA retrieves from 298 kernels + 244 docs, how sensitive is it to knowledge-base coverage on out-of-scope operators (e.g., training backward kernels)?
