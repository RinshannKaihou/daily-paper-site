> **一句话总结：** 本文首次为 RLVR（强化学习可验证奖励）微调的十亿参数 LLM 给出了非平凡的 PAC-Bayes 泛化界——通过对 token 生成的 Gumbel-max 重参数化将随机性外部化，并配合 Progressive RLVR（RLVR + 在策略蒸馏 + TinyLoRA + 量化）实现极致压缩，使界在四个真实任务上超过基座模型精度 9–51%，且与学生模型经验精度仅相差 6–11%。

> **TL;DR:** The paper proves the first non-vacuous PAC-Bayes compression generalization bounds for RLVR fine-tuned billion-parameter LLMs (Qwen3.5-4B) by reparameterizing stochastic decoding with the Gumbel-max trick, and proposes a Progressive RLVR pipeline (RLVR teacher → TinyLoRA on-policy distillation → quantization) that retains 84–97% of teacher accuracy while being 14,796× more compressible; the resulting bounds exceed the base model's accuracy by 9–51% on math (Eurus-2-RL/NuminaMath-CoT), code (KodCode-V1), general-knowledge (MMLU/MedQA/LogiQA/C-Eval/LegalBench/ARC), and Text-to-SQL (SynSQL-2.5M), and lie within 6–11% of the fine-tuned student's empirical accuracy — versus vacuous bounds for standard LoRA on the same 4B model.

## 核心贡献

- **首个 RLVR 非平凡泛化界**：将经典压缩式 PAC-Bayes 框架（Lotfi 等人用于预训练）扩展到 RLVR 后训练。关键创新是用 Gumbel-max 重参数化把 token 生成的内在随机性外部化为与假设无关的 Gumbel 噪声分布，使奖励函数从"组合爆炸支持集上的不可处理期望"变成"prompt×噪声上的确定性函数",从而获得可计算的界（Theorem 1）。
- **误差分解为外在 + 内在两部分**：把泛化差距分解为 prompt 分布上的外在误差 ε_ext（O(1/√m)）和模型自身生成过程上的内在误差 ε_int（O(1/√mn)）。由于评估时 n 可任意放大，内在项可被压成低阶，使界本质上由外在误差主导。
- **Progressive RLVR 训练流水线**：为同时满足"经验奖励大 + 压缩尺寸小"两个条件，作者设计了"先用标准 RLVR 训练大容量教师 → 用 TinyLoRA 在策略蒸馏压缩到学生 → 量化（默认 5 级，2.3 bits）"三阶段。这利用了 PAC-Bayes 只对最终假设收费、不约束搜索过程的非对称性。
- **四个真实领域的非平凡界**：在数学、编程、通识推理、Text-to-SQL 四个领域上，界比基座模型精度高出 9–51%，与学生模型经验精度仅差 6–11%；Text-to-SQL 上总惩罚仅 8%（受 2.5M 训练样本驱动）。
- **多轮 RLVR 与 OOD 扩展**：Theorem 1 自然扩展到确定性环境的多轮 RLVR（覆盖 SkyRL-SQL 那类交互式 SQL）和可重参数化的随机环境；给出 OOD 评估版本（Theorem 2，仅剩 Hoeffding 项）。实证 12 个跨领域 OOD 对中 9 个非平凡，数学作为源域正向迁移到所有目标域，单点最大提升 +25.9（→General）。

## Key Contributions

- **First non-vacuous PAC-Bayes generalization bound for RLVR.** Adapts the Solomonoff-prior compression framework (à la Lotfi et al.) to RLVR by externalizing the stochasticity of token decoding via the Gumbel-max reparameterization trick, sidestepping the combinatorially large support of generated sequences that makes naive RLVR bounds intractable.
- **Extrinsic/intrinsic error decomposition.** Splits the generalization gap into an extrinsic term over the prompt distribution (rate O(1/√m)) and an intrinsic term over the model's own generation (rate O(1/√mn)); because n is freely chosen at evaluation, the intrinsic term can be made lower-order, so the bound is effectively governed by data size m alone.
- **Progressive RLVR pipeline.** A three-stage recipe — (i) train a high-capacity teacher with standard LoRA-64 RLVR, (ii) on-policy distill into a TinyLoRA student (inner dim u=16, projection rank r=16), (iii) quantize the adapter — that exploits the PAC-Bayes asymmetry: the procedure producing h is unconstrained, only the final compressed hypothesis is charged. Delivers 10% higher training rewards and 15% tighter bounds than directly applying TinyLoRA to RLVR.
- **Non-vacuous bounds at the 4B-parameter scale on four real-world domains** (mathematics, programming, general knowledge, Text-to-SQL), with bounds 9–51% above the base model and within 6–11% of the fine-tuned student accuracy.
- **Multi-turn RLVR and OOD extensions.** Theorem 1 transfers verbatim to multi-turn RLVR with deterministic environments (and to stochastically reparameterizable environments); a separate OOD bound (Theorem 2) drops the compression penalty and retains only a Hoeffding term. Empirically, 9 of 12 cross-domain pairs are non-vacuous, with math→General showing the largest gain (+25.9 points) and SQL→Math the worst (−4.0 points).

## Methodology

**Problem setup.** Given a base pre-trained model π_θ_ref (Qwen3.5-4B) and a finite hypothesis space H of parameter updates, the population reward of a hypothesis is R(h) = E_{x~P0} E_{y~π_θ(h)(·|x)}[r(x,y)], where r is a deterministic bounded reward in [a, a+Δ]. The challenge is that the inner expectation is over a distribution with support combinatorial in sequence length and vocabulary size.

**Step 1 — Gumbel-max reparameterization.** At each decoding step the categorical token sample y_{i,t} ~ Categorical(softmax(ℓ_{i,t}/τ)) is rewritten as y_{i,t} = argmax_v (τ^{-1} ℓ_{i,t}^{(v)} + ξ_{i,t}^{(v)}) with ξ ~ Gumbel(0,1) iid. Collecting noise across all tokens and the n generations per prompt gives ξ_{i,j} ∈ R^{T×V}, all hypothesis-independent. Generation becomes a deterministic function y = g(x, ξ; h), turning R(h) into E_{x~P0} E_{ξ~Pξ}[r(x, g(x,ξ;h))] with both randomness sources explicit and h-independent.

**Step 2 — PAC-Bayes compression bound (Theorem 1).** Adopting the Solomonoff prior P(h) ∝ 2^{−K(h)}, with probability ≥1−δ over the m prompts and the mn Gumbel noise vectors,

R(h) ≥ (1/mn) Σ_{i,j} r(x_i, g(x_i, ξ_{i,j}; h))  −  Δ·√((1/n + 1))  −  √((C(h) log2 + 2 log C(h) + log(2/δ)) / 2m),

where C(h) is the description length (bits) of the compressed hypothesis. The bound scales with C(h), motivating aggressive compression.

**Step 3 — Progressive RLVR pipeline.** (a) Train a teacher with standard LoRA-64 RLVR using an importance-sampling GRPO-style objective without KL penalty. (b) On-policy distill into a TinyLoRA student, which reparameterizes ΔW = U Σ (Σ_i v_i P_i) V^T from the truncated SVD of the frozen weight — compressible to as few as one trainable scalar. (c) Quantize the adapter to 5 levels (2.3 bits). The decoupling "high-capacity search, low-capacity billing" is the design lever.

**Step 4 — Empirical reward estimation (Corollary 1).** Full-dataset evaluation over m prompts is costly, so a uniform subsample of m' prompts is used with a small additive Hoeffding penalty √(Δ² log(3/δ) / 2m'). In experiments m'=4,096 prompts and n=64 noise vectors per prompt.

**Extensions.** Multi-turn RLVR with deterministic environments: substitute the single-step g with a trajectory map G(x, ξ_{1:H}; h), and Theorem 1 applies verbatim. Stochastic but reparameterizable environments add a second hypothesis-independent noise η. OOD evaluation against a labeled target corpus Q0 yields a bound where the compression penalty drops out (Theorem 2).

## Results & Highlights

- **Headline bound (4 domains, δ=0.05).** On Qwen3.5-4B post-trained via Progressive RLVR, the generalization bound **exceeds the base model's accuracy by 9–51%** in every domain and **lies within 6–11% of the student's empirical accuracy** — i.e., it is simultaneously non-vacuous and tight. Standard LoRA on the same 4B model yields vacuous bounds at every quantization level and is omitted from the plots.
- **Compression–accuracy trade-off.** Progressive RLVR **recovers 84–97% of the teacher model's accuracy** while being **14,796× more compressible on average** than the teacher (whose updates are full LoRA-64 adapters).
- **On-policy distillation ablation.** Removing distillation (the "RLVR w/ TinyLoRA" arm) **reduces the bound by 15% on average** versus Progressive RLVR; on the math domain, distillation provides **+10% higher training rewards** than direct TinyLoRA-RLVR at equal step counts.
- **Smaller-model baseline fails.** Substituting a Qwen3.5-2B student with standard rank-1 LoRA produces bounds **below the 2B base model's accuracy and only slightly above random guess**, demonstrating that simply scaling down is not a substitute for Progressive RLVR.
- **Penalty decomposition (Figure 4).** Text-to-SQL has the tightest penalties (total ≈8%, thanks to 2.5M training examples); for the other domains the extrinsic penalty is 7–8% and the distillation+quantization penalty 6–11%. **Intrinsic gaps are negligible in all domains**, confirming 64 generations per prompt suffice.
- **Dataset scale.** Math (456,285 questions from NuminaMath-CoT via Eurus-2-RL), code (504,885 tasks from Eurus-2-RL + KodCode-V1), general knowledge (438,908 points merging MedQA, LogiQA, C-Eval, ARC, LegalBench, MMLU, Webinstruct-verified), Text-to-SQL (2,544,390 queries from SynSQL-2.5M over 16,583 databases).
- **Text-to-SQL under tri-level training reward (Appendix E, Figure 7).** Bound of **0.56** vs. base accuracy **0.04** (Δ=+0.52) vs. student empirical accuracy **0.64** (gap 0.08), consistent with the binary-reward bounds.
- **OOD transfer (Appendix D, Figure 6).** All 12 off-diagonal cross-domain pairs yield non-vacuous bounds; 9 beat the base model. Math is the strongest source (positive transfer to every target, peak +25.9 points to General), while Math is the hardest target; SQL is the most isolated domain (all cross-domain bounds <13%).

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty.** Genuine: the Gumbel-max reparameterization of token decoding is the load-bearing new idea that lets compression-based PAC-Bayes clear the RLVR stochasticity barrier. The extrinsic/intrinsic decomposition is clean and well-motivated. The Progressive RLVR pipeline itself is a sensible composition of existing primitives (on-policy distillation, TinyLoRA, quantization) rather than a new algorithm — its novelty is in being the *vehicle* for non-vacuous bounds, not in the components.
- **Rigor.** Proofs are deferred to appendices; the main text states Theorem 1 with the exact rate decomposition (Remark 1) and the OOD extension (Theorem 2). The extrinsic O(1/√m) and intrinsic O(1/√mn) rates are consistent with standard Hoeffding-style arguments. The use of a tri-level Text-to-SQL reward for training while reporting a binary-accuracy bound is theoretically clean (PAC-Bayes is reward-agnostic to the training objective) but worth flagging — the headline numbers use a friendlier binary reward than the one actually optimized.
- **Reproducibility.** Hyperparameters are listed (batch size 64, 8 rollouts/prompt, LR sweep {1e-5, 5e-5, 1e-4}, u=r=16 TinyLoRA, 5 quantization levels = 2.3 bits, m'=4096, n=64). Code/datasets/models are linked. Hardware is modest: Tinker API on 8×3 GHz vCPUs/80 GB RAM for the teacher, 2× A100/512 GB for distillation — within reach of academic groups. Verifying a bound requires ~262k generations, which is non-trivial but feasible.
- **Significance.** First-of-its-kind result in a hot area (verifying RLVR generalization). The "verified specialization" framing (a deployable lower bound on accuracy before serving) is a concrete practical pitch, not just theory.
- **Red flags / weak points.**
  - The headline 84–97% retention and 14,796× compressibility compare the **TinyLoRA student to the LoRA-64 teacher**, not to standard LoRA on the same model. Standard LoRA on Qwen3.5-4B yields vacuous bounds and is dropped from figures, which is a fair theoretical point but understates how much accuracy the compression costs relative to a normal RLVR run.
  - The compression budget is validated **only at the 4B scale**; the authors themselves flag that deployed models (Kimi-K2.6, Qwen3.5-397B-A17B) are orders of magnitude larger and that the required compression has not been shown achievable there. The practical ceiling of the method is untested.
  - Bounds hold **in-distribution only**; OOD verification requires a labeled target corpus — a strong assumption that limits the "deploy with confidence" framing for genuinely novel deployment data.
  - TinyLoRA's "one trainable scalar" extreme is referenced but the experiments use u=16; the gap between the advertised extreme and what actually produces non-vacuous bounds is not explored as an ablation.
  - The intrinsic-error claim "64 generations per prompt suffice" is supported by Figure 4 only indirectly (small intrinsic bars); a direct n-sweep vs. bound tightness would be more convincing.

## Strengths

- **Theoretically clean resolution of the RLVR stochasticity problem.** Gumbel-max externalization is the right tool and is explained crisply; the extrinsic/intrinsic split makes the rates transparent.
- **Concrete recipe, not just a bound.** Progressive RLVR is reproducible end-to-end and uses only off-the-shelf ingredients.
- **Four diverse real-world domains** including multi-turn Text-to-SQL, not just synthetic toy tasks.
- **Honest negative results reported** (standard LoRA on 4B is vacuous; 2B+LoRA-1 baseline fails; SQL→Math OOD is negative).
- **Useful penalty decomposition** (Figure 4) tells practitioners exactly what to invest in (more data, better TinyLoRA, more quantization) to tighten bounds.

## Limitations

- **Scale.** Only 4B models. Achieving the compression budget at 30B–400B is an open question the authors explicitly defer.
- **Distribution match.** Bounds require deployment distribution = training distribution; OOD requires labeled target data, which is rarely available in the high-stakes settings the authors motivate (medicine, law).
- **Reward mismatch.** Text-to-SQL trains on a tri-level reward but reports binary accuracy bounds; while PAC-Bayes permits this, the binary bounds are noticeably friendlier than the underlying training signal.
- **Cost of bound estimation.** ~4,096 prompts × 64 generations per bound is cheap relative to training but non-trivial to run as a routine post-deployment check.
- **No comparison to alternative generalization frameworks** (e.g., norm-based, information-theoretic, or the RL-specific PAC-Bayes of [62]) beyond a verbal dismissal — a quantitative contrast would strengthen the "first non-vacuous" claim.
- **Non-stationary tool environments** (live APIs) are explicitly out of scope, which excludes a growing fraction of agentic RLVR deployments.

## Relevance

与用户对 RLVR/LLM 推理后训练的研究兴趣高度相关：它给出了衡量"RLVR 到底是泛化还是过拟合"这一当前争议的首个形式化下界工具，且 Progressive RLVR（蒸馏+TinyLoRA+量化）本身就是一个可复用的极致压缩 RLVR 训练配方，对研究高效可验证的领域特化模型具有直接的方法学价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can the compression budget that yields non-vacuous bounds be achieved at the 30B–400B scale (e.g., Qwen3.5-397B-A17B) without sacrificing empirical reward, perhaps via MoE-aware TinyLoRA?
- How do the bounds behave when the deployment distribution genuinely shifts but no labeled OOD corpus is available — can semi-supervised or unlabeled OOD variants of Theorem 2 recover non-trivial guarantees?
- Does replacing Gumbel-max with the Gumbel-softmax (differentiable) relaxation yield tighter or looser bounds, and can it enable bound-aware training rather than post-hoc verification?
- A direct ablation over TinyLoRA inner dimension u (from 1 to 16+) vs. bound tightness would clarify whether the "one trainable scalar" extreme is ever Pareto-optimal in practice.
- How do these bounds interact with the observed RLVR pathologies (diversity collapse, length bias from Dr. GRPO) — can verified specialization detect or rule out overfitting to degenerate trajectories?
- Could the verified-specialization framing be combined with conformal prediction to give distribution-free (rather than PAC-Bayes) deployment guarantees on top of these compression bounds?
