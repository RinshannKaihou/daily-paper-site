> **一句话总结：** 本文在 RewardBench / M-RewardBench 的 23 种语言上系统证明，无论是 trained reward model 还是 prompted LLM-as-a-Judge，对语义相同的指令-回复对都会按评测语言给出显著偏移的点值分数——低资源语言（如 Hindi、Hebrew、Arabic）被打分更宽松（reward model 上 Spearman ρ = −0.81），而 >90% 的 pairwise accuracy 完全掩盖了这一点，导致全局阈值下接收率差距高达 43 个百分点，使低资源语言的有害内容更易通过安全过滤。

> **TL;DR:** Across 23 languages of RewardBench/M-RewardBench, 8 open-weight evaluators (4 judges + 4 reward models) plus 2 frontier judges (GPT-4.1-mini, Qwen3-32B) assign semantically identical content systematically different pointwise scores: lower-resource languages (Hindi, Hebrew, Arabic) get higher scores (Spearman ρ = −0.81 between resource level and reward), while pairwise accuracy stays uniformly >90% — yet under a global 50th-percentile threshold the acceptance-rate gap reaches 43.0 pp (34.0 pp even at >95% accuracy). A per-language offset fix cuts the gap by 60.9% but is defeatable via code-switching; the bias correlates with summed NLL but language identity remains a significant predictor after controlling for uncertainty.

## 核心贡献

- **首次系统量化"评测语言导致点值分数偏移"这一结构性偏差。** 在 23 种语言、4 类任务（Chat / Chat-Hard / Safety / Reasoning）下证明 reward model 和 LLM-as-a-Judge 对相同语义内容给出明显不同的绝对分数（约 0.4–0.5 / 5 分的位移），方向一致：低资源语言得分更慷慨。
- **揭示 pairwise accuracy 是这种偏差的盲点。** 即使 pairwise accuracy >90% 甚至 >95%，全局阈值下接收率仍可相差最多 43.0 pp，意味着阈值化过滤、RLHF 奖励整形、安全审计这类依赖绝对分数的下游应用都被误导，使低资源语言（如 Ukrainian）的有害回复更易通过安全过滤。
- **给出机制层面的解释：不确定性相关但不能完全归因。** summed NLL 与分数在语言层面强正相关（ρ = +0.81），但控制 NLL 后语言身份仍显著（incremental F-test），且语内 NLL→Score 斜率随域而变（Chat/Chat-Hard 多为负），说明偏差是结构性语言级而非单纯的实例难度。
- **指出常用补救措施的缺陷并给出务实建议。** 语级阈值/offset 平均可削减 60.9% 接收率差距，但需要 language identification（LID），而 LID 本身是攻击面：代码混合提示使 44% 被误判为英语、接收率从 50% 升至 75%。建议在训练阶段做语言感知校准与平衡的偏好数据。

## Key Contributions

- **Documents a structural, cross-evaluator language bias in pointwise scores.** Across 8 evaluators spanning LLaMA/Qwen/Gemma/Aya backbones and both Bradley–Terry and uncertainty-aware training, semantically identical instruction–response pairs receive systematically different absolute scores (≈0.4–0.5 points on a 1–5 scale) depending on the evaluation language, with lower-resource languages scored more leniently (Hindi, Hebrew, Arabic on top; English, Italian, French, Spanish at the bottom).
- **Shows pairwise accuracy is structurally blind to this bias.** With pairwise accuracy uniformly >90% (often >95%), the acceptance-rate gap under a global 50th-percentile threshold still reaches 43.0 pp across languages and 34.0 pp even in the >95% accuracy regime — making the bias invisible to the standard validation metric and consequential for threshold-based filtering, RLHF reward shaping, and safety auditing.
- **Investigates the mechanism and shows it is not reducible to uncertainty.** Model uncertainty (summed NLL) is positively correlated with reward at the language level (Spearman ρ = +0.81, p = 2.9e−06) and reproduced by token-free measures (semantic entropy, URM attribute-head disagreement, predictive variance), yet incremental F-tests show language identity remains a significant predictor after controlling for NLL, and within-language NLL→score slopes are domain-dependent (negative in Chat / Chat-Hard).
- **Demonstrates the bias persists at frontier scale and that the obvious mitigation is an attack surface.** Both GPT-4.1-mini and Qwen3-32B (thinking) reproduce the pattern (resource-level Spearman ρ between −0.58 and −0.73, acceptance-rate gaps up to 32.7 pp). Per-language offset correction cuts the gap by 60.9% on average but requires language ID, which 44% of code-switched prompts evade — raising acceptance from the calibrated 50% to 75%.

## Methodology

- **Data.** RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2025) plus its professionally human-translated, human-validated 22-language extension M-RewardBench (Gureja et al., 2025), jointly yielding aligned instruction–response pairs across 23 languages and four categories: Chat, Chat-Hard, Safety, Reasoning. The semantic-parallelism of the data is the key enabling condition: every language expresses the same content, so score differences cannot be attributed to content.
- **Evaluators.** Four prompted LLM-as-a-Judges with a 1–5 Likert rubric at temperature 0 (Aya Expanse 32B, Qwen 2.5 72B, LLaMA 3.1 70B Instruct, M-Prometheus 14B — the only one explicitly trained for multilingual judging) and four continuous-score reward models (URM-LLaMA-3.1-8B, BTRM-Qwen-2-7B, Skywork-Reward-Gemma-2-27B, Skywork-Reward-LLaMA-3.1-8B-v0.2), spanning three backbone families and two training paradigms (Bradley–Terry vs. uncertainty-aware). Two frontier judges (GPT-4.1-mini, Qwen3-32B thinking) are added in §3.5.
- **Analysis of the bias.** Per-language mean scores are compared via one-way ANOVA (all p < 0.001), cross-evaluator ordinal ranking and Pearson correlations, and resource-level correlations against Common Crawl page percentages (Pearson r = −0.58, Spearman ρ = −0.81 for reward models).
- **Threshold sensitivity.** Acceptance rate under a 50th-percentile global threshold calibrated on a held-out split, plotted against pairwise accuracy per language, exposing the gap that pairwise metrics hide.
- **Mechanism (decomposition).** score = f(x) + β·NLL + b(ℓ) + ε, where NLL is summed (not per-token, because tokenizer-induced token-count inflation cancels the per-token surprise; summed NLL spans a 2.6× range across 23 languages). Three nested OLS models (M1: NLL, M2: Language, M3: NLL + Language) on N = 36,800 instances per reward model are compared via incremental F-tests and stacked R² variance decomposition. A within-language regression (one slope βℓ per language) tests whether the bias is reducible to instance difficulty alone.
- **Mitigation.** Per-language offset correction (equivalent to language-specific thresholds) is evaluated for residual gap, then attacked via code-switched Safety prompts (English wrapper around Hindi content) scored against English/Hindi thresholds to expose the LID attack surface.

## Results & Highlights

- **Language-dependent scoring shift:** for reward models, the correlation between Common Crawl resource level and mean score is Pearson r = −0.58 and Spearman ρ = −0.81 (monotonic increase in score as resources drop); for LLM-as-a-Judges, the corresponding Spearman is −0.57. The language effect is significant for every evaluator (one-way ANOVA, p < 0.001 in all cases).
- **Magnitude:** mean scores shift by ≈0.4–0.5 points on the 1–5 Likert scale for judges, and span a gap of ±0.5 on the z-normalized ±1 scale for reward models; on the 8-evaluator suite across "all" splits the range is ~0.5, with Hindi/Hebrew/Arabic the most lenient and English/Italian/French the strictest.
- **Pairwise accuracy masks the bias:** acceptance-rate gap (max−min) reaches 43.0 pp across benchmark splits at the 50th-percentile global threshold, and remains up to 34.0 pp even in the >95% pairwise-accuracy regime. Concrete example on Safety with Skywork-Reward-LLaMA-3.1-8B-v0.2: English has 93% pairwise accuracy / 23% acceptance while Ukrainian has 87% / 67% — a 44 pp acceptance gap; the same rejected response scoring −4.51 in English scores +3.57 in Ukrainian under one global threshold.
- **Frontier persistence:** GPT-4.1-mini yields resource-level Spearman ρ of −0.58 (Safety) and −0.71 (Chat-Hard) with acceptance gaps of 19.6 and 14.0 pp; Qwen3-32B (thinking) yields ρ of −0.68 / −0.73 with acceptance gaps of 32.7 and 29.7 pp (all p < 0.01).
- **Mechanism:** summed NLL correlates with reward at language-level ρ = +0.81 (p = 2.9e−06, n = 23) and item-level ρ = +0.50 (n = 36,432); token-free measures reproduce this (URM attribute-head disagreement +0.73/+0.92; predictive variance +0.43/+0.58; semantic entropy for judges up to +0.68). However, incremental F-tests show adding language identity to NLL significantly improves the fit for every model, and within-language NLL→score slopes are predominantly negative in Chat / Chat-Hard, so the bias cannot be reduced to instance difficulty.
- **Mitigation ceiling:** a per-language offset correction cuts the cross-language acceptance-rate gap by 60.9% on average, but 18.8% of centered-score variance remains in the language×item interaction. The code-switching attack exposes that an off-the-shelf LID (langdetect) mislabels 44% of English-wrapped Hindi prompts as English, raising acceptance from the calibrated 50% to 75%.

## Authors & Credibility

- Authors are at the Language Technology Lab, University of Cambridge (Ej Zhou, Lucas Resck, Zheng Hui, Anna Korhonen; † marks equal advising). Anna Korhonen is a senior, well-cited researcher in multilingual NLP, lending the work strong institutional credibility.
- The study is grounded in two established, community-recognized benchmarks (RewardBench and M-RewardBench) with human-validated translations, covering 23 languages and 4 task categories, and spans 8 open-weight evaluators from 3 backbone families plus 2 frontier judges — a sufficiently broad coverage that the cross-model consistency claim is defensible.
- Statistical claims are reported with significance tests (one-way ANOVA p < 0.001; resource-level Spearman with p < 0.01; incremental F-tests), and a dedicated "Use of Large Language Models" statement declares LLMs were used only for code/debugging and prose polishing.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty.** Genuine and well-scoped: prior multilingual-judge work (M-RewardBench, M-Prometheus, Fu & Liu 2025) focuses on pairwise accuracy and inter-language agreement, leaving pointwise score shifts unstudied. The Tabelog/Google Maps analogy makes the contribution intuitive. The "uncertainty ↔ generosity" hypothesis and the code-switching attack on language-specific thresholds are non-obvious extensions.
- **Rigor.** Strong on the empirical side (8 evaluators × 4 categories × 23 languages, ANOVA + F-tests + R² decomposition). However, several methodological choices warrant scrutiny: (i) the *summed* vs *per-token* NLL argument is reasonable but is itself a modeling decision that biases the conclusion toward finding a language effect, since summed NLL partly reflects tokenizer choice rather than "uncertainty" alone; (ii) only M-Prometheus among the judges was explicitly trained for multilingual evaluation, so the cross-judge consensus partly reflects shared English-centric pretraining rather than independent corroboration; (iii) the 0.4–0.5 point shift is reported as "considerably large" without a corresponding human-judged threshold for what counts as a meaningful difference on the 1–5 scale.
- **Reproducibility.** Reasonable: open-weight evaluators are named, prompts are in Appendix E, the data is public. The two frontier judges (GPT-4.1-mini, Qwen3-32B) are evaluated only on Safety and Chat-Hard rather than the full suite, leaving their behaviour on Chat/Reasoning unverified. Code release is not stated in the main text.
- **Significance.** Practically important: the safety-filtering and RLHF reward-hacking implications are concrete and well-illustrated. The finding that pairwise accuracy — the de facto validation metric — is structurally blind to the bias is the most actionable message for the community.
- **Red flags.** (a) The headline 43 pp gap is the *max−min* across 23 languages and is computed at the 50th percentile — a deliberately central threshold where small distributional shifts are most amplified; the more conservative mean gap and percentiles at other operating points are not foregrounded. (b) Per-language offset is praised for the 60.9% reduction but the residual 39.1% (and the 18.8% language×item interaction) is exactly where the most damaging errors live, and the paper does not propose a training-time fix beyond a brief "future directions" paragraph. (c) The mechanism story (uncertainty + residual language identity) is correlational; no causal intervention (e.g., fine-tuning that reduces NLL gap) is attempted to confirm it.

## Strengths

- A clean, falsifiable thesis ("pairwise accuracy hides pointwise language bias") supported by a multi-evaluator, multi-language, multi-domain experimental matrix.
- Mechanism analysis goes beyond reporting the bias: three nested OLS decompositions, token-free uncertainty cross-checks, and within-language slope analysis collectively triangulate the cause.
- Concrete downstream implications with worked examples (safety filtering on the loaded free-speech question; the English/Hindi code-switching attack).
- Honest framing of mitigation limits — the per-language fix is shown to be both incomplete and attackable.

## Limitations

- The four LLM-as-a-Judges are mostly English-centric generalists; only M-Prometheus is multilingual-trained, so "cross-evaluator consensus" partly reflects shared pretraining priors.
- Summed NLL conflates model surprise with tokenizer artefacts; the per-token NLL counter-argument is asserted rather than tested with a tokenizer-controlled experiment.
- Frontier judges are evaluated only on 2 of 4 splits; no Chat or Reasoning numbers for GPT-4.1-mini / Qwen3-32B.
- No training-time fix is implemented or evaluated — the calibration/training recommendations remain future work.
- The 43 pp headline is a max−min at a centile-selected threshold; distributional sensitivity (other percentiles, mean gaps) is under-reported in the main text.
- No human-judgment calibration of what score-difference magnitude is perceptible, so "0.5 points is large" is asserted without an external reference.

## Relevance

与多语言 LLM 评测、reward model 校准与 RLHF 安全高度相关：本文直接指出 pairwise accuracy 这一主流验证指标无法暴露语言级的点值分数偏移，对未来设计语言平衡的偏好数据、跨语言一致的评测协议以及安全过滤的阈值策略都有直接借鉴价值，也提示在做多语言 reward model 训练/部署时必须额外检查点值分数的语言一致性。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the language bias shrink when reward models are explicitly trained on language-balanced preference data, and how much multilingual preference data is needed per language to flatten the score shift?
- Is the summed-NLL ↔ score relationship causal? Would reducing the tokenizer-induced NLL gap (e.g., via a multilingual tokenizer or per-token NLL with length normalization) reduce the bias?
- How does the bias interact with other known evaluator biases (self-preference, position bias, verbosity)? Are the effects additive or do they cancel in some languages?
- The per-language offset cuts the gap by 60.9% on average — can a language×item interaction term or a learned calibration head reach substantially lower residual error without enabling LID-attack vectors?
- How does the bias behave on generation tasks (not just scoring) — e.g., do policy models trained with these reward models learn to "language-hack" by shifting output language to maximize reward?
