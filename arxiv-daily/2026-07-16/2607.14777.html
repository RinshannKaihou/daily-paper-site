§ 01

How SEED works

A single autoregressive LLM plays every role. Stage 1 bootstraps a skill-writing capability from an external analyzer; Stage 2 closes a self-evolving loop where the same refreshed checkpoint both rolls out trajectories and rewrites them as hindsight skills. At inference the agent is indistinguishable from the base policy.

Stage 1 · Cold start

Hindsight Skill SFT

Roll out 1,440 trajectories (M=180 tasks × K₀=8). An external analyzer (GLM-5.2, T=0, 4096 tok) turns each episode into one policy-facing rule — a workflow for successes, an avoidance rule for failures. The policy is SFT'd 3 epochs to predict the skill from the trajectory. This checkpoint seeds both the RL actor and the analyzer.

Stage 2 · Self-evolving loop

On-Policy Distillation (OPD)

Freeze the current policy as π_old. It samples N=8 trajectories per task and analyzes each into a skill s. Re-score the same sampled tokens under ordinary vs skill-augmented context; the detached log-prob shift Δ = sg[ℓ_skill − ℓ_θ] becomes a gate g = σ(β·Δ), trained jointly with a KL-regularized GRPO loss.

Gate
g = σ(β_opd · Δ), β=5.0
Loss
L_SEED = L_rl + λ_opd·L_opd
λ_opd
0.01 · group N=8
Inference footprint = base policy. No analyzer, no skill bank, no retrieval module, no augmented prompt is deployed. The skill is internalized into weights.

§ 03

Strengths & Limitations

▲ Strengths

  • Inference footprint = base policy. No skill bank, retrieval, or extra prompt — a real deployment edge over Skill-Prompt / Skill-GRPO*.
  • Consistent gains across 3 text benchmarks + a multimodal extension, across three model scales (1.7B / 3B / 7B).
  • Sample efficiency is compelling: 60%-data SEED > 100%-data GRPO; Unseen-set gains (esp. +35 Heat) show transfer, not memorization.
  • Ablations cleanly isolate the three components; largest drop comes from de-syncing the analyzer — directly backing the central claim.
  • Theoretical appendix ties each design choice to a proved proposition — uncommon for agentic-RL papers.

▼ Limitations

  • The OPD mechanism is largely inherited from SDAR. The genuine novelty is narrower: the self-evolving shared-analyzer loop, not a new distillation objective.
  • GLM-5.2 cold-start dependency with no analyzer-quality ablation — a hidden dependency the "self-evolving" branding understates.
  • Self-reinforcement risk (shared blind spots, model self-preference) is acknowledged in App. E but never empirically quantified.
  • Single-seed, no variance, scale capped at 7B. OPSD baseline looks mis-tuned (near-zero on Search-QA across all backbones).
  • SEED does not win everywhere: 7B WebShop exact 78.1 < SDAR 82.8; Unseen-Clean regresses −2.9 — and these non-wins go undiscussed.
  • Training cost is non-trivial: per-update analyzer pass over N=8 trajectories + paired re-scoring of all sampled tokens.
§ 04

Open questions

How much of SEED's gain is the GLM-5.2 cold-start vs the self-evolving loop? A same-size open analyzer, or policy-as-analyzer from step 0, would isolate the teacher's contribution.
Does the shared actor/analyzer parameterization cause observable skill drift or self-preference collapse over 150+ updates? Per-iteration skill novelty and gate statistics would quantify the risk.
Does it scale to 30B+, where the SFT analyzer is suddenly weaker than the student — or to truly long-horizon benchmarks with rare success and dense partial-credit grading?
Is the OPSD baseline's ~0.0 Search-QA performance a reproduction bug? A corrected comparison would clarify whether SEED's margin over the distillation family is real.
Would anchoring skills to verifiable state changes (not free-form NL) mitigate the self-reinforcement failure mode and lift the ceiling under noisy self-supervision?
Tracking analyzer-vs-oracle agreement across iterations would test the framework's ceiling under self-generated noise — the experiment the paper omits.
