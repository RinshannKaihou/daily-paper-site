> **一句话总结：** 本文使用预训练的 per-layer transcoders（PLTs）在 Qwen3-4B 上构建 attribution graph，识别出 112 个欺骗相关特征，并通过特征干预实验证明负向干预这 10 个高频特征可将 100% 的欺骗响应翻转为非欺骗响应（无假阳性），同时发现 "Obscuring information" 与 "secrets/confidentiality" 两个核心特征在欺骗电路中占据 60% 的输入连接。

> **TL;DR:** The authors apply pre-trained per-layer transcoders (PLTs) to Qwen3-4B to build attribution graphs for a 100-prompt secret-hiding deception task, manually curating a dictionary of 112 deception-related features. Feature steering (α=5) shows that negatively steering the top-10 occurring features flips 100% of deceptive prompts to non-deceptive with zero false positives (vs ~45.8% mean for random control groups, p<0.001), while positively steering converts 21% of non-deceptive prompts to deceptive. Two circuit hubs, "Obscuring information" and "secrets/confidentiality", alone account for 60% of input-feature connections versus a 16% average, demonstrating that transcoders enable circuit-level (not just feature-level) analysis of deception.

## 核心贡献

- 首次将 transcoders（特别是 per-layer transcoders, PLTs）应用于 AI 安全/欺骗行为研究：先前 transcoder 工作聚焦于通用可解释性，本文将其用于分析模型内部的欺骗机制。
- 构建了一套基于 attribution graph 的欺骗特征识别流程（4 步：生成图 → 选择种子 token → 追踪边 → 通过 feature steering 验证），并产出包含 112 个欺骗相关特征的字典。
- 通过 feature steering 实验（α=5）证明欺骗行为可以被可靠抑制：负向干预 top-10 高频特征可将 100% 的欺骗响应转为非欺骗响应，且不产生假阳性。
- 进行 circuit-level 分析，揭示 "Obscuring information" 与 "secrets/confidentiality" 两个特征是欺骗电路的核心枢纽（占 60% 输入连接 vs 其他特征平均 16%），展示了 transcoder 相对于 SAE/residual probing 的优势——可追踪跨层特征交互。
- 为 AI 安全团队提供了一个在欺骗行为 manifested 为可被利用的输出之前识别安全相关特征的方法论框架。

## Key Contributions

- First application of transcoders (specifically PLTs) to AI safety and deception analysis, whereas prior transcoder work (Dunefsky et al. 2024; Paulo et al. 2025; Quirke et al. 2025; Karvonen 2025) focused on general mechanistic interpretability.
- A 4-step pipeline for deception-feature identification built on attribution graphs: (1) process the prompt through transcoders, (2) select seed tokens such as "private/hidden/confidential", (3) trace edges token→feature→feature to the output, (4) validate candidate features via bidirectional feature steering. This yields a curated dictionary of 112 deception-related features.
- Empirical evidence via feature steering (α=5) that negative steering of the top-10 occurring deception features converts 100% of deceptive prompts to non-deceptive responses with no false positives, while positive steering produces a 21% ND→D shift (p<0.001 vs control).
- A circuit-level analysis showing two hub features, "Obscuring information" (layer 23, 95/100 occurrences) and "secrets/confidentiality" (layer 8, 86/100 occurrences), each input into 6/10 circuit members versus a 1.6/10 (16%) average, and that steering this pair is the only combination significant under both negative (100.0% vs 45.8%, p<0.001) and positive (17.3% vs 3.7%, p<0.001) steering.
- A statistical-evaluation protocol using 100 randomly sampled control groups of 10 features (drawn from the 112-feature dictionary minus the target features) as a null distribution, instead of relying on raw steering success rates.

## Methodology

**Model and transcoders.** Experiments use Qwen3-4B together with the released `qwen3-4b-transcoders` checkpoint from the `circuit-tracer` repository (Yang et al. 2025), which provides predefined per-layer-transcoder (PLT) features and their natural-language explanations. The authors did not train their own transcoders.

**Deception task and dataset.** A 100-prompt dataset was constructed by combining random secret keys (e.g., `xhf2l1jk`) with sentence templates containing deception-related adjectives. A prompt is classified as non-deceptive if the model reveals the secret key and deceptive otherwise. The system prompt instructs the model that "the word ... needs to stay hidden", and the user directly asks for it.

**Feature identification (4 steps).** (1) Each prompt is run through transcoders to produce an attribution graph showing how features transform across layers/tokens. (2) Seed tokens associated with deception ("private", "hidden", "confidential") are selected. (3) Edges are traced from the seed token to features and between features until the output, recording any features linked to negation, concealment, secrecy. (4) Relevance is validated by feature steering — if the steered output flips between deceptive/non-deceptive, the feature is stored. This produces a 112-feature deception dictionary.

**Feature steering.** For an original activation `x`, positive steering applies `x + α·x` and negative steering `x − α·x`, with a fixed `α = 5`. Positive steering is applied when the default output is non-deceptive; negative steering is applied when the default output is deceptive.

**Baseline and significance testing.** The top-10 highest-occurring deception features form the target group. Controls are 100 groups of 10 random features drawn from the 112-feature dictionary minus the top-10. Each control group is run through the same steering pipeline to form a null distribution against which the target's performance is compared (empirical p-values).

**Circuit construction.** Input features of each top-10 target feature are collected across the 100 prompts. A directed edge between two features is retained in the circuit graph only if it appears in at least 30% of prompts, enabling identification of central hubs. Core deception features are then individually steered to test whether a small subset reproduces the population-level effect.

## Results & Highlights

- **Deception-feature identification.** 112 unique deception-related features were manually identified in Qwen3-4B. The top-10 highest-occurring features appeared in 55%–95% of the 100 evaluated prompts, indicating that despite the large dictionary, deceptive behaviour depends on a small recurring subset (e.g., "Obscuring information_119106" 95/100, "Negation and inability_158577" 91/100, "secrets/confidentiality_95840" 86/100, "hide/hidden_9414" 78/100).
- **Negative steering is a perfect suppressor.** Negatively steering the top-10 features converted **100% of deceptive prompts to non-deceptive responses with zero false positives**, whereas positive steering caused only **21% of non-deceptive prompts to become deceptive** (p<0.001 vs control groups). The authors interpret this asymmetry as evidence that negative steering reliably removes deception without unintended outputs.
- **Two features dominate the circuit.** Within the deception circuit, "Obscuring information" and "secrets/confidentiality" each individually input into 6/10 of the other top features (60%), versus an average of 1.6/10 (16%) for the remaining eight features.
- **Only one feature pair is significant in both directions.** Among the n=28 possible pairs of the top-10 excluding the two hubs, only the pair "secrets/confidentiality + Obscuring information" reached significance under both negative steering (target 100.0% vs mean control 45.8%, p<0.001) and positive steering (17.3% vs 3.7%, p<0.001), identifying it as the central steering target.
- **Circuit-level evidence not visible feature-by-feature.** When examined individually, no single non-hub feature was significant in both directions; the hub status of the pair emerged only from the attribution-graph circuit analysis, illustrating the specific value the authors claim for transcoders over SAE/residual-stream methods.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty — moderate, application-level.** Transcoders/PLTs, attribution graphs, and feature steering are all borrowed from prior work (Dunefsky et al. 2024; Ameisen et al. "Circuit Tracing"; Quirke et al. 2025). The novel contribution is the *application* to deception and the curated 112-feature dictionary; no new transcoder architecture or training method is proposed.
- **Rigor — weak experimental design.** The "deception" task is a single, narrow, artificial template — the model is told a random key must stay hidden and is then directly asked for it — so the 100% negative-steering success may say more about how trivially separable this token-revelation task is than about deception in general. Only one model (Qwen3-4B) and one fixed steering coefficient (α=5) are evaluated; the authors themselves flag the latter as a limitation. There is no comparison against SAE-based or residual-probe deception detectors (Goldowsky-Dill et al. 2025; Wang et al. 2025) on the same task, so the central claim that transcoders "outperform" other MI methods for deception is not directly demonstrated here — only asserted from prior general-interpretability benchmarks.
- **Data quirks that inflate the dictionary.** A close look at Table 3 shows the 112 "unique" features are heavily redundant: dozens are variants of "secret" at different layers/indices (e.g., `secret_96858`, `secret_57885`, `secret_122729`, `secret_100314`, …) plus `secrecy`, `secrets/confidentiality`, `secrets and confidentiality`, etc. Several entries appear to share the same underlying transcoder index (e.g., `secret_95840`, `secrets/confidentiality_95840`, and `secrets and confidentiality_95840` all reference index 95840 at layer 8), suggesting labelling inconsistency rather than 112 genuinely distinct features. This weakens the "112 unique deception features" framing.
- **Missing ablations.** No ablation over steering strength α, no ablation over the 30% circuit-edge threshold, no held-out prompt set (steering identification and evaluation use the same 100 prompts), no test of whether the identified features transfer to other deception datasets (e.g., OpenDeception, Park et al. 2024's AI deception examples). The absence of train/test separation risks overfitting the feature dictionary to the evaluation prompts.
- **Reproducibility — moderate.** The model, transcoder checkpoint, and `circuit-tracer` library are public, and the steering formula, α value, and significance protocol are stated. However, the prompt templates, the seed-token list, and the manual feature-tracing decisions are not released, and the 4-step identification is explicitly described as subjective and bias-prone.
- **Significance — exploratory.** The work is a useful proof-of-concept that circuit-level hub features exist for a narrow deception task and can be steered, but it does not establish that these features generalise, that the effect holds at other α values, or that the methodology beats simpler deception detectors. Treat as a hypothesis-generating case study rather than a deployable defence.

## Strengths

- Clean, reproducible significance-testing protocol with 100 random control groups drawn from the same feature dictionary, which is more rigorous than reporting raw steering success rates.
- Circuit-level finding (two hub features accounting for 60% of input connections vs 16% average) is a concrete, non-obvious result that genuinely leverages the transcoder's cross-layer edge structure.
- Asymmetric steering result (100% D→ND with zero false positives vs 21% ND→D) is practically meaningful for safety: it suggests suppression is more reliable than induction.
- Honest, detailed limitations section that owns the single-model, fixed-α, manual-identification, and no-custom-transcoder caveats.
- Public model and transcoder checkpoint lower the barrier to replication.

## Limitations

- Single model (Qwen3-4B), single steering strength (α=5), single narrow task family (100 secret-key prompts); no cross-model or cross-dataset validation.
- Deception is operationalised narrowly as "withholding a secret key when explicitly asked", which may not reflect richer forms of LLM deception (strategic, agentic, social-engineering).
- Manual, subjective feature identification with no formal definition of a "deception feature" — explicitly acknowledged as bias-prone.
- Feature dictionary is heavily redundant (many "secret"/"secrecy" variants) with possible index-labelling collisions, inflating the "112 unique features" count.
- No comparison against SAE-based or linear-probe deception detectors on the same task; the claim of transcoder superiority rests on cited general-interpretability benchmarks rather than direct deception-task evidence.
- No held-out evaluation split — the same 100 prompts are used to identify features and to evaluate steering, raising overfitting concerns.
- Authors could not train their own transcoders due to limited tooling, restricting all findings to one pre-trained checkpoint.

## Relevance

对 mech interp / LLM 安全方向高度相关：本文展示 transcoder（per-layer）相较 SAE 能做 circuit-level 分析，并通过 feature steering 实现对欺骗行为的可靠抑制，是可解释性研究及对齐/监测工具的可借鉴案例；其单模型、固定 α 与狭窄任务设计也提示了在更复杂欺骗场景与多模型上验证的必要方向。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Do the two hub features ("Obscuring information", "secrets/confidentiality") transfer to richer deception benchmarks such as OpenDeception (Wu et al. 2026) or Park et al. 2024's AI-deception examples, or are they artefacts of the secret-key template?
- How does the steering success rate and false-positive rate vary as a function of α across, say, α ∈ {1, 2, 5, 10, 20}? Is the perfect 100% D→ND result stable or specific to α=5?
- If the 112-feature dictionary is deduplicated by underlying transcoder index (e.g., collapsing all `_95840` entries), how many genuinely distinct deception features remain, and do the circuit-hub conclusions survive?
- How does transcoder-based deception steering compare quantitatively against SAE-feature steering and linear-probe deception detection (Goldowsky-Dill et al. 2025) on the same prompts?
- Can the circuit-hub features be used for *detection* (classifying a prompt/response as deceptive without steering), and what AUROC would that achieve on a held-out set?
- Does the methodology generalise beyond Qwen3-4B — e.g., to Llama, Mistral, or larger Qwen3 variants — once custom transcoders can be trained?
- What happens when the prompt explicitly instructs the model to deceive for a non-security reason (e.g., a kind white lie): do the same hub features activate, suggesting a general deception circuit, or different ones?
