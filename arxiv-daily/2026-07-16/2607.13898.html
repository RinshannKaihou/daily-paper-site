01 Methodology 3-part study

Characterization (§III). The open-source MXFP dot-product RTL (Samson et al., FPL'24) is taken as the soft-logic baseline, augmented with FloPoCo fp32 output conversion, then deeply pipelined to push Agilex-5 frequency from 47–98 MHz → 516–713 MHz. The same k=32 dots are then mapped three ways onto DSPs: packed fixed-point multipliers, native fp16 vector mode, and the floating-point tensor mode (only feasible for E2M1/E2M3). Peak TFLOPS = fill A5EC065BB32AE4S until ALMs/DSPs exhaust. Tool: Quartus Prime Pro 25.3.

DSP modifications (§IV). Area baseline is a simplified Agilex-5 tensor-mode core (two dot-10 units, ping-pong banks, fp32 accumulator, shared-exponent) — so reported overheads are conservative overestimates. Implemented in ASAP7 7nm via HAMMER (Genus 21.17 synth, Innovus 21.17 PnR, 2 ns target clock); full-custom routing sized with COFFE. Two hard constraints per iteration: (i) no new I/O pins, (ii) no drop below 458 MHz.

Systolic case study (§V). An N×k · k×(N·D) matrix-multiplier array (k=32) swept over N until the device exhausts or unroutables. Correctness via RTL sim; Quartus PnR of the full array uses the unmodified Agilex-5 block as a pin-compatible stand-in (legitimate because pin count & fmax are unchanged).

03 Results & Highlights Table V / Fig. 6

baseline DSP proposed DSP
Peak device TFLOPS — optimistic upper bound (no frequency derate). Larger is better.
  • Recommended design (iter 4): +36% DSP-tile area = +1.8% die area; all variants run ≥ 458 MHz, identical latency.
  • DSP count per k=32 dot: cut from 16 → 2–4 blocks; largest arrays use >90% of device DSPs at ~400 MHz, only 22–32% soft logic — freeing fabric is the win.
  • Characterization (Table I): optimized soft logic 1.7–11.5 TFLOPS; packed fixed-point DSP 2.1/3.4/6.5 (E5M2/E4M3/E3M2, +28–45% over soft logic); fp16 vector mode flat 1.5 TFLOPS; tensor mode 12.4 TFLOPS but E2M1/E2M3 only.

04 Strengths

  • Cleanly isolates a real architectural gap — tensor mode covers only E2M1/E2M3 — then closes it.
  • Unusually transparent iterative design narrative (iter 1→4, area 1.28×/1.65×/1.31–1.59×/1.36×), ending in a justified recommendation.
  • Honest about limits: peak TFLOPS labelled optimistic, area labelled overestimate, dot-10→dot-8 breakage disclosed.
  • Block-level change tied to a device-level outcome (4.2× avg, >90% DSP util) — what an FPGA architect actually wants.
  • Full open-source RTL + benchmarks — rare for DSP-block papers.

05 Limitations

  • No silicon validation — all area/timing from a simplified standard-cell model, not a taped-out or vendor-accurate block.
  • No end-to-end DL workload — no model accuracy, no real latency, no memory-bandwidth-bound cases.
  • E5M2 dropped in the recommended design (recoverable only at +59% area); NaN/Inf encodings sacrificed → narrower MX-spec compliance.
  • Backward-incompatible dot-10→dot-8 in iter 4 may break existing tooling/bitstreams; ecosystem impact unanalyzed.
  • Frequency derate under full-device util only partially measured; standalone Table-I/Fig-3 peaks remain optimistic.

06 Open Questions

Q1Does 4.2× peak survive on memory-bandwidth-bound LLM kernels (GEMV in decoding), where arithmetic density isn't the bottleneck?
Q2What is the real accuracy impact of dropping E5M2 and NaN/Inf — are there workloads where the design is functionally unusable?
Q3Can dot-10→dot-8 be made backward-compatible via a mode bit, and at what area cost?
Q4How do results transfer to AMD/Xilinx DSP48E2-class blocks, with their very different MAC structure?
Q5What is the energy/TFLOPS cost hidden by the larger CSA trees (37-bit E4M3, 67-bit E5M2)?
Q6Would a stripped-down testchip confirm the ASAP7-projected area and 458 MHz in silicon?
