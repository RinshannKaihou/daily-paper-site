Evaluating Epistemic Uncertainty: Beyond OOD Detection and Active Learning →

Jakub Paplhám · Willem Waegeman · Eyke Hüllermeier · Vojtěch Franc

Key finding

OOD-detection and active-learning proxy rankings diverge mathematically from regret-minimizing selectors — producing striking pairwise rank inversions on real data.

Why this matters

A challenge to how the field measures uncertainty.

If your UQ benchmark ranks methods by OOD or active-learning proxies, it may be ranking the wrong objective. The paper offers a unified reject framework and an actionable Pareto-gap diagnostic to replace contested rank-correlation tests.

Critical edge

No significance tests. Some flips may be noise.

The headline rank inversions are reported without confidence intervals or significance tests, and several rest on small numerical differences.

e.g. AuReC 0.0030 vs 0.0034 — within plausible seed variance.
01

Methodology

Builds on the decision-theoretic split: total T*, aleatoric A*, epistemic/regret E* = T* − A*. Selectors C:X→[0,1] are scored on three functionals — coverage ρ, risk Ri, regret Re — studied as three coupled constrained problems.

Theorem 1 (KKT): optimal selector ≡ 1{ (1−λ)A* + λE* ≤ τ }, λ ∈ [1/2, 1]
  λ=1/2 → Chow / Geifman-El-Yaniv (total uncertainty)
  λ=1 → Franc-Paplhám epistemic reject option

Evaluation exploits a frequentist–Bayesian hybrid: theory lives where E* is computable; empirics need the true p*(y|x), obtained only from densely human-annotated datasets — CIFAR-10H, APPA-REAL, DCIC (8 tasks). Pure epistemic utility measured by AuReC (+ ex / n variants); joint capacity by the Pareto-gap (IGD+ distance to the oracle (coverage, risk, regret) surface).

A closed-form 1-D Rossellini-function sandbox shows five Bayes-optimal scorers (OOD, EER, BALD, risk-, regret-coverage) reject disjoint regions at 80% coverage — proving the disagreement is structural, not a training artifact. Baselines: MC-Dropout, Deep Ensembles, DDU, Evidential (ResNet-18 / ResNet-101).

03

Strengths & limitations

+ Strengths

  • Clean unification. Theorem 1 subsumes Chow-style selective prediction and the epistemic reject option as λ-endpoints of one convex family, with explicit recovery of classical results.
  • Demonstrates, not asserts. Rank divergence is shown on real human-annotated p*(y|x), not synthetic proxies.
  • Pareto-gap is a principled, decision-theoretic replacement for the contested Spearman disentanglement test; the 0.999-correlation-yet-best-Pareto-gap case is memorable.
  • Multi-loss, multi-domain (CE / 0-1 / squared / absolute across faces, natural images, 8 DCIC tasks) — broader than typical UQ empirics.

− Limitations

  • Scope ceiling. True regret needs dense human labels — the framework cannot touch vanilla CIFAR-10 or ImageNet, so claims hold only on annotation-rich data.
  • Non-deployable diagnostic. Pareto-gap uses oracle test-set tuning of (w1,w2); it measures capacity, not online performance (authors flag this).
  • Inherited baselines. OOD (OpenOOD) and active-learning (OpenML) numbers are adopted from an anonymous concurrent submission, not re-run.
  • No CIs / significance tests; estimation vs approximation error conflated; 1-D sandbox only.
04

Open questions

Q1Do inversions persist on large single-label benchmarks once p*(y|x) is approximated via soft-label distillation or conformal calibration?
Q2Can the Pareto-gap be made deployable — tuning (w1,w2) on a held-out calibration set — without losing discriminative power?
Q3Do second-order / proper-scoring-rule decompositions (Hofman 2024, Kotelevskii 2025) close the Pareto-gap better than the four baselines here?
Q4Are the pairwise inversions statistically significant once seed-level variance is propagated across all DCIC datasets?
Q5Does the framework extend to structured prediction (segmentation, detection) where p*(y|x) is far harder to elicit?
Q6How much of the Evidential-vs-DDU flip is driven by APPA-REAL being reframed as categorical CE over ages 0–100?
