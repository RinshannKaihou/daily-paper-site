02 Why This Matters
Per-layer transcoders expose circuit-level deception hubs that SAEs miss — and let safety teams suppress the behaviour before it surfaces.
03 Critical Edge
The single secret-key template is so trivially separable that the perfect 100% may reflect task simplicity, not a general deception circuit — no SAE/probe baseline, one model, one α, no held-out split.

A Methodology

Model & transcoders. Qwen3-4B with the public qwen3-4b-transcoders checkpoint from the circuit-tracer repo (Yang et al. 2025). No transcoders were trained by the authors.

Task. 100 prompts pairing a random secret key (e.g. xhf2l1jk) with deception-related adjectives. A response revealing the key = non-deceptive; withholding = deceptive.

4-step identification pipeline

  • Run each prompt through transcoders to produce a cross-layer attribution graph.
  • Pick seed tokens: "private", "hidden", "confidential".
  • Trace edges token→feature→feature→output, keeping anything tied to negation, concealment, secrecy.
  • Validate via bidirectional feature steering (α=5): if output flips, store the feature → 112-feature dictionary.

Significance protocol

Top-10 highest-occurring features form the target; 100 random groups of 10 features (drawn from the remaining 102) form the null distribution for empirical p-values.

Circuit construction

A directed feature→feature edge is kept only if it appears in ≥30% of prompts, surfacing central hubs that individual feature inspection cannot.

B Results & Highlights

  • 112 deception features curated; top-10 appear in 55–95% of prompts — deception rides on a small recurring subset.
  • Negative steering is a perfect suppressor: 100% D→ND, 0 false positives; positive steering only 21% ND→D (p<0.001). Asymmetry = suppression is safer than induction.
  • Two hubs dominate the circuit. "Obscuring information" (L23, 95/100) and "secrets/confidentiality" (L8, 86/100) each feed 6/10 other top features vs a 1.6/10 (16%) average.
  • Only one pair is bi-directionally significant among 28 candidate pairs — the two hubs — under both negative (100% vs 45.8%) and positive (17.3% vs 3.7%) steering.
  • Hub status is invisible feature-by-feature; it emerges only from attribution-graph circuit analysis — the authors' headline argument for transcoders over SAEs.

C Assessment — Two Sides

Strengths

  • Rigorous null: 100 random control groups from the same dictionary, not raw success rates.
  • Circuit-hub finding (60% vs 16%) is concrete and genuinely leverages cross-layer edges.
  • Asymmetric steering (100% / 0 FP) is practically meaningful for safety tooling.
  • Frank limitations section; public checkpoint lowers replication cost.

Limitations

  • One model (Qwen3-4B), one α=5, one narrow task family; no cross-dataset validation.
  • Dictionary is heavily redundant — many "secret" variants; possible index-labelling collisions inflate the "112 unique" count.
  • No SAE / linear-probe baseline on the same task; superiority is asserted, not directly shown.
  • No held-out split — identification and evaluation share the same 100 prompts.
  • Manual, subjective feature tracing, acknowledged as bias-prone.

D Open Questions

Do the two hubs transfer to OpenDeception or Park et al. 2024's examples — or are they artefacts of the secret-key template?
Is the 100% D→ND result stable across α ∈ {1,2,5,10,20}, or specific to α=5?
After deduplicating by transcoder index (e.g. all _95840), how many distinct features remain — do the hub conclusions survive?
How does transcoder steering compare quantitatively to SAE-feature steering and linear-probe detection on the same prompts?
Can the hubs do detection (no steering) — what AUROC on a held-out set?
Does the circuit generalise to Llama / Mistral / larger Qwen3 once custom transcoders can be trained?
