LLM Evaluators are Biased across Languages

Published 2026 · 07 · 16

Same content, different language — evaluators hand back systematically different scores, and the standard metric never notices.

Ej Zhou · Lucas Resck · Zheng Hui · Anna Korhonen — Language Technology Lab, University of Cambridge

llm-as-a-judge reward-models multilingual-evaluation cross-lingual-bias reward-calibration rlhf-safety m-rewardbench
Key metric
43.0pp
acceptance-rate gap, 50th-percentile threshold

English scores 23% acceptance on Safety; Ukrainian 67% — a single rejected reply jumps from −4.51 (EN) to +3.57 (UK).

Why this matters

Pairwise accuracy — the de facto validation metric — stays uniformly >90%, making the bias structurally invisible. Low-resource-language harmful content slips through safety filters unnoticed.

Critical edge

The headline 43 pp is a max−min at a centile-selected threshold; the residual 39.1% of error lives where damage concentrates, the fix needs language-ID that 44% of code-switched prompts evade, and the mechanism story is correlational — no causal intervention attempted.

Methodology 8 evaluators · 23 languages · 4 tasks

Aligned instruction–response pairs across RewardBench + M-RewardBench express identical content in every language — so any score difference is attributable to language, not content.

Evaluators

  • 4 LLM-as-a-Judges (1–5 Likert, temp 0): Aya Expanse 32B, Qwen 2.5 72B, LLaMA 3.1 70B Instruct, M-Prometheus 14B.
  • 4 reward models: URM-LLaMA-3.1-8B, BTRM-Qwen-2-7B, Skywork-Reward-Gemma-2-27B, Skywork-Reward-LLaMA-3.1-8B-v0.2 — spanning BT and uncertainty-aware training.
  • 2 frontier judges (§3.5): GPT-4.1-mini, Qwen3-32B (thinking).

Analysis

  • One-way ANOVA per evaluator (all p < 0.001); Pearson/Spearman vs Common Crawl resource level.
  • Threshold sensitivity: acceptance rate at 50th-pct global threshold vs pairwise accuracy.
  • Decomposition: score = f(x) + β·NLL + b(ℓ) + ε; three nested OLS models (N=36,800 / model) compared via incremental F-tests.
  • Mitigation: per-language offset, then attacked via code-switched Safety prompts.

Results highlights

−0.81 Spearman ρ · resource ↔ reward (RMs)
+0.81 Spearman ρ · summed NLL ↔ reward
0.4–0.5 Likert-point shift on 1–5 scale
60.9% gap cut by per-language offset

What the data shows

  • Lower-resource languages scored more leniently: Hindi, Hebrew, Arabic at top; English, Italian, French, Spanish at bottom. Effect significant for every evaluator (ANOVA p < 0.001).
  • Pairwise accuracy masks the bias: gap stays 34.0 pp even in the >95% accuracy regime.
  • Persists at frontier scale: Qwen3-32B ρ = −0.68/−0.73, gaps up to 32.7 pp; GPT-4.1-mini ρ = −0.58/−0.71.
  • Not reducible to uncertainty: token-free measures reproduce it (URM +0.73/+0.92), yet language identity remains significant after controlling for NLL; within-language slopes negative in Chat / Chat-Hard.
  • Mitigation is an attack surface: off-the-shelf LID mislabels 44% of English-wrapped Hindi prompts as English, raising acceptance from 50% to 75%.

▲ Strengths

  • Clean, falsifiable thesis — “pairwise accuracy hides pointwise language bias” — backed by an 8-evaluator × 23-language × 4-domain matrix.
  • Mechanism analysis goes beyond reporting: three nested OLS decompositions, token-free uncertainty cross-checks, within-language slope triangulation.
  • Concrete downstream implications with worked examples (free-speech safety filter; EN/HI code-switching attack).
  • Honest framing of mitigation limits — the per-language fix is shown to be both incomplete and attackable.

▼ Limitations

  • 4 of 5 judges are English-centric generalists; only M-Prometheus is multilingual-trained — “cross-evaluator consensus” partly reflects shared pretraining priors.
  • Summed NLL conflates model surprise with tokenizer artefacts; the per-token counter-argument is asserted, not tested with a tokenizer-controlled experiment.
  • Frontier judges evaluated on only 2 of 4 splits (no Chat / Reasoning for GPT-4.1-mini, Qwen3-32B).
  • No training-time fix implemented; calibration recommendations remain future work.
  • The 43 pp headline is a max−min at a centile-selected threshold; distributional sensitivity is under-reported.
  • No human-judgment calibration of what magnitude of score difference is perceptible on the 1–5 scale.

Questions for further exploration 5 open threads

  1. Does the bias shrink when reward models are trained on language-balanced preference data — and how much per language is needed to flatten the shift?
  2. Is the summed-NLL ↔ score link causal? Would a multilingual tokenizer or length-normalized per-token NLL reduce the bias?
  3. How does it interact with self-preference, position bias, verbosity? Additive, cancelling, or language-dependent?
  4. Can a language×item interaction term or a learned calibration head beat the 60.9% reduction without enabling LID-attack vectors?
  5. On generation: do policy models trained with these reward models learn to “language-hack” by shifting output language to maximize reward?
