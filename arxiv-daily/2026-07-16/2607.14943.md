> **一句话总结：** 作者用机械可解释性分析发现，World Action Model (WAM) 在相机/夹爪/噪声等扰动下的鲁棒性特征在激活空间中呈低维线性可分结构（架构依赖），并据此提出免训练的闭环激活转向方法 **WA-LQR**，将对比方向经降维后用 LQR 反馈控制注入 DiT 模块，在 LIBERO-10 上把 Cosmos-Policy 的成功率最高提升约 41 个百分点。

> **TL;DR:** The paper shows via mechanistic interpretability that robustness-critical features in World Action Models (WAMs) lie in a low-dimensional linearly-separable activation subspace (architecture-dependent: yes for Cosmos-Policy 2B and DiT4DiT, no for LingBot-VA), and introduces **WA-LQR**, a training-free reduced-order LQR feedback controller that steers WAM activations along contrastive directions; on the LIBERO-10 benchmark it improves Cosmos-Policy's success rate under camera/gripper/Gaussian-noise perturbations from 46.0% to 59.3% (camera), 61.3% to 72.7% (gripper), and 26.7% to 58.7% (noise) on average, beating No-Steering, Prompt-Steering, and open-loop ActAdd baselines.

## 核心贡献

- **首次对 WAM 做机械可解释性分析**：系统比较 Cosmos-Policy 2B、DiT4DiT、LingBot-VA 在相机朝向、初始夹爪位置、高斯图像噪声三类扰动下，成功/失败 rollout 对应的 DiT 激活空间几何结构，发现这种线性可分性是「架构依赖」的。
- **把「线性可分性 (hinge loss)」当作可转向性的预测器**：用三维 PCA 子空间上线性 SVM 的 hinge loss 量化可分性，并实证它与转向带来的成功率提升呈强负相关（相关系数 r = 0.63），从而无需重训即可预判哪些模型/任务能受益于激活转向。
- **首个面向 WAM 的开环 + 闭环激活转向方法**：将 LLM 文献中的 ActAdd 迁移到 WAM，并提出 **WA-LQR**——在 rank-64 的对比子空间里用 Jacobian 线性化 DiT block 动力学，再用 Riccati 递归求解 LQR 增益，形成「最小侵入式」的反馈控制；据作者所知这是 WAM 上首次的开环与闭环激活转向方法。
- **跨任务可迁移的对比方向 + chunk-wise 衰减调度**：发现 LIBERO-10 中某些任务对共享同一扰动特征表示，可一组任务的对比方向迁移到另一组；同时按 action chunk 索引设计指数衰减控制代价 r(τ) = min(R_final, R_init·exp(τ/τ_R))，使早期 chunk 强转向、后期 chunk 几乎不介入。
- **实证 WA-LQR 在可分模型上稳健优于基线，且在不可分模型上「不伤性能」**：对 LingBot-VA 这类线性可分性差的模型，闭环 WA-LQR 不会像 ActAdd 那样因过转向而崩溃，体现了反馈控制的自适应价值。

## Key Contributions

- **First mechanistic-interpretability study of WAMs**: Systematically compares DiT activation geometry across Cosmos-Policy 2B, DiT4DiT, and LingBot-VA under camera-orientation, initial-gripper-position, and Gaussian-noise perturbations, and shows that low-dimensional linear separability of robustness features is architecture-dependent (present in the first two, absent in LingBot-VA).
- **Linear-separability (hinge loss) as a steering-predictor**: Uses the hinge loss of a linear SVM fit on the top-3 principal components of contrastive activation directions as a quantitative separability metric, and demonstrates that it negatively correlates with steering-induced success-rate gain (r = 0.63), letting practitioners predict steerability before running interventions.
- **First open- and closed-loop activation-steering methods for WAMs**: Adapts LLM ActAdd to WAMs and introduces **WA-LQR**, a training-free, weight-preserving, reduced-order LQR controller that linearizes DiT-block dynamics via Jacobians in a rank-64 contrastive subspace and synthesizes feedback gains via Riccati recursion; the authors claim these are the first open- and closed-loop steering methods for WAMs.
- **Transferable contrastive directions + chunk-wise decay schedule**: Discovers that some LIBERO-10 task pairs share perturbation-feature representations, enabling cross-task transfer of steering vectors, and designs an exponential control-cost schedule r(τ) = min(R_final, R_init·exp(τ/τ_R)) so that early action chunks are steered strongly while later ones are left alone.
- **Closed-loop avoids oversteering on non-separable models**: On LingBot-VA (poor separability), open-loop ActAdd frequently degrades performance, whereas WA-LQR's feedback modulation keeps it at or above the unsteered baseline — empirically justifying the feedback formulation.

## Methodology

The method has three tightly-linked parts.

**(1) Mechanistic analysis (Sec. 4).** For each WAM (Cosmos-Policy 2B, DiT4DiT, LingBot-VA) and each perturbation type, the authors build contrastive datasets D+ (e.g., clean / successful-rollout inputs) vs D− (perturbed / failed), run a full forward pass, and collect mean-pooled DiT residual-stream activations `x̄ ∈ R^D` for every layer l and denoising timestep t. The contrastive direction is `d_k = x̄_k^+ − x̄_k^−`. PCA on the set {d_k} reveals that variance is concentrated in the top few components (rank ≤ 3–64 captures most of it). To get a quantitative separability score, activations are projected onto the top-3 PCs and a linear SVM is fit; the average hinge loss (Eq. 7) is the metric — 0 = perfectly separable, 1 ≈ random. This same hinge loss is later used as a predictor of whether steering will work.

**(2) Open-loop ActAdd for WAMs (Sec. 5.1).** Steering vectors `a_{l,t}` are formed by averaging contrastive directions across prompt pairs and action-chunk positions τ ∈ [H_a]; at inference time the activation is shifted `x_{l,t,τ} ← x_{l,t,τ} + γ·a_{l,t}` with a scalar strength γ. This is training-free but blind: it ignores the realized activation, the WAM dynamics, and whether the feature is already at the target strength, which causes oversteering (Sec. C shows ActAdd is extremely sensitive to γ — success collapses to 0% at γ ≥ 0.2 under Gaussian noise).

**(3) WA-LQR closed-loop steering (Sec. 5.2).** This is the main contribution. For each (l, t), randomized streaming SVD on the contrastive-direction matrix yields a compact orthonormal basis `V_{l,t} ∈ R^{d_x×d_z}` (d_z ≪ d_x, e.g. 64) and projection `P_{l,t} = V_{l,t}^T`, defining latent activations `z_{l,t,τ} = P_{l,t} x_{l,t,τ}`. The controlled block is `x_{l+1,t} = f_{l,t}(x_{l,t}, u_{l,t}) = φ^{(l)}(x_{l,t}, t, h) + u_{l,t}` (Eq. 5), and its Jacobians `Ã_{l,t}, B̃_{l,t}` are computed (never materialized — only JVPs/VJPs). The reduced latent dynamics `δz_{l+1,t} ≈ Ã_{l,t} δz_{l,t} + B̃_{l,t} δu_{l,t}` (Eq. 11) are locally linear, which the authors verify empirically (cosine similarity and magnitude ratio stay near 1 throughout inference; Fig. 5a). Feature setpoints are defined along a normalized latent contrastive direction `v̂_{l,t}^z` with target strength `β_{l,t}^{z,*} = λ‖ē_{l,t}‖`, and the feature-tracking error is `δz_{l,t,τ} = −α_{l,t,τ} v̂_{l,t}^z`. A separate L-step LQR (Eq. 13) is solved per (chunk τ, denoising timestep t) with the chunk-decay control penalty `R_{l,t}^{chunk}(τ) = r(τ)·I`, yielding gains `K_{l,t,τ}` applied as `u*_{l,t,τ} = ū_{l,t,τ} − K_{l,t,τ} δz_{l,t,τ}` (Eq. 14). The full controller chains T per-timestep L-step LQRs through the diffusion scheduler. Complexity is O(L·d_z³) on CPU or O(log L · log² d_z) on GPU. Because the intervention is proportional to online feature-tracking error, WA-LQR only steers when the activation deviates from the desired robustness setpoint.

## Results & Highlights

All numbers are success rates on the LIBERO-10 benchmark (30 trials/task for Cosmos-Policy, 20 for LingBot-VA), with steering directions computed on a source task and transferred to a target task.

- **Cosmos-Policy 2B, Camera Orientation (Table 1):** WA-LQR averages **59.3%±4.0%** vs No-Steering **46.0%±4.1%** (+13.3 pp), Prompt-Steering **46.7%±4.1%** (+12.6 pp), and ActAdd **49.3%±4.1%** (+10.0 pp). Best single-task transfer: Task 0→Task 5 jumps from 56.7% (unsteered) / 70.0% (prompt) to **83.3%** under WA-LQR.
- **Cosmos-Policy 2B, Initial Gripper Position (Table 1):** WA-LQR averages **72.7%±3.6%** vs No-Steering 61.3% (+11.4 pp), Prompt 60.7% (+12.0 pp), ActAdd 63.3% (+9.4 pp); Task 1→Task 2 reaches **100.0%** (from 83.3% unsteered).
- **Cosmos-Policy 2B, Camera Gaussian Noise (Table 1):** This is the one regime where open-loop ActAdd edges out WA-LQR (ActAdd **67.3%±3.8%** vs WA-LQR **58.7%±4.0%**), but both crush the baselines — No-Steering 26.7%, Prompt-Steering collapses to **0.7%±0.7%** (prompt decomposition actively hurts here). Task 6→Task 4: ActAdd lifts success from 10.0% to 80.0% (+70 pp).
- **Headline gain:** Across all perturbations and steerable models, the paper reports success-rate improvements of **up to 41 percentage points** over unsteered baselines.
- **DiT4DiT (Table 2):** WA-LQR averages **71.7%±4.1%** on initial-gripper-position (vs 65.7% unsteered, 65.8% ActAdd) and **48.9%±5.3%** on Gaussian noise (vs 15.6% unsteered, 43.3% ActAdd — a +33.3 pp jump over the no-steering baseline on noise).
- **LingBot-VA (Table 3 / Table 4):** As predicted by the MI analysis (high hinge loss, no separability), steering does **not** help and often hurts. ActAdd *degrades* camera-orientation average from 48.0% to 41.0%; WA-LQR stays at 51.0% — i.e., the closed-loop controller protects against the oversteering collapse that open-loop ActAdd suffers, exactly as designed.
- **Separability ↔ steerability correlation (Fig. 7):** Hinge loss vs (steered − unsteered) success-rate delta shows r = 0.63, confirming hinge loss as a usable ex-ante predictor of whether activation steering will pay off.
- **ActAdd sensitivity (Table 5, Sec. C):** Under Gaussian noise, Cosmos-Policy success peaks sharply at γ = 0.1 (67.4%) and collapses to **0%** for γ ≥ 0.2 — concrete evidence that open-loop steering needs careful tuning and motivates the feedback formulation.
- **Latent-dynamics linearity (Fig. 5):** Cosine similarity between the Jacobian-linearized and true latent activation stays ≈ 0.92–1.0 across blocks and tasks; the top-16 right-singular-vector subspaces of `Ã_{l,t}` overlap strongly across 25 random inputs from 5 tasks, justifying reuse of a single trajectory's linearization.

## Authors & Credibility

- All authors are at **Georgia Institute of Technology**; first two (Jihoon Hong, Julian Skifstad) are equal-contribution leads, senior author Glen Chou. The group has prior work on robot learning and control-theoretic steering.
- The work is positioned carefully: it does not claim universal steerability. It explicitly identifies an architecture (LingBot-VA) where the method does **not** work and explains why (mixture-of-transformers, action module not steerable), and uses that negative result as evidence for the separability-as-predictor thesis. This is a credibility-positive.
- Provides website / code / video links in the header, 30- and 20-trial evaluation protocols with reported standard errors, and a sensitivity sweep (Table 5) — reasonable rigor for a systems/interpretability paper.
- References cite the relevant recent WAM, VLA, robustness-benchmark (VLATest, COLOSSEUM, LIBERO-Plus), and activation-steering (ActAdd, T2V-LQR) literature, and the WA-LQR method is explicitly framed as adapting the T2V steering of [55] to the WAM setting rather than claimed as fully de novo.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Genuine but incremental. The core tricks — contrastive activation directions (ActAdd lineage), reduced-order LQR on locally-linearized activations, hinge-loss as separability metric — all exist in the LLM/T2V literature ([41, 51, 55]). The contribution is the adaptation to WAMs (which couple video generation with action outputs) and the explicit "separability predicts steerability, and steerability is architecture-dependent" framing. The claim of "first open- and closed-loop activation steering for WAMs" is plausible but narrow.
- **Rigor:** Reasonable but with gaps. (a) The headline "up to 41%" is a best-case single-task number, not an average — the per-perturbation averages are ~+10–33 pp, which is still solid but less splashy. (b) **Critical missing ablation**: there is no comparison against any *retraining/finetuning-based robustness baseline* (RL post-training, robustness-oriented finetuning as in [27–30]) — the only baselines are No-Steering, Prompt-Steering (a weak, non-activation baseline that mostly fails), and ActAdd. So "WA-LQR improves robustness" is really "WA-LQR beats *these particular* inference-time baselines," not "beats the strongest known robustness method." (c) The prompt-steering baseline [72] is so weak (0.7% on noise, often worse than no-steering) that including it inflates the relative win. (d) Statistical reporting is okay (std errors given) but with only 20–30 trials per task several WA-LQR-vs-ActAdd differences (e.g., gripper Task 1→Task 1: ActAdd 66.7% vs WA-LQR 60.0%) are within noise and not in WA-LQR's favor, yet the prose generalizes WA-LQR superiority.
- **Reproducibility:** Mixed signals. Code is promised, hyperparameters (γ = 0.1, rank-64 SVD, C = 10 for SVM, chunk-decay schedule parameters) are given, and the LQR synthesis is deterministic given Jacobians. However, the contrastive direction *collection* and the task-grouping heuristic ("we empirically find groups of activations which share representation") are described loosely, and the per-(model, perturbation) choice of which layer/timestep to steer is not crisply specified — this is the kind of subjective pipeline that often impedes exact reproduction.
- **Significance:** If the separability-as-predictor claim generalizes, it is a useful diagnostic for the robotics-foundation-model community. But generalization is the open question: the paper itself flags that no method currently predicts when tasks share transferable representations, and 2 of 3 models tested (LingBot-VA fully; parts of DiT4DiT) show weak or inconsistent steerability, so the practical reach may be narrower than the framing suggests.
- **Red flags:** None fabricated, but watch for (i) cherry-picked "transferable task groups" — the grouping is found empirically per model rather than derived, which risks post-hoc selection; (ii) LingBot-VA's negative result is honestly reported but also conveniently supports the thesis, and the "groups discovered for Cosmos-Policy [mapped] onto LingBot-VA" transfer is an unusual experimental choice that stacks the deck against LingBot-VA; (iii) the evaluation is simulation-only (LIBERO-10), with no real-robot validation, limiting deployment claims.

## Strengths

- Cleanly unifies mechanistic interpretability, activation steering, and optimal control into one pipeline with a falsifiable predictor (hinge loss → steerability, r = 0.63).
- Training-free and weight-preserving — attractive for deployment since it avoids data collection / retraining, the stated motivation.
- WA-LQR's feedback design concretely solves a real failure mode of ActAdd: the sensitivity sweep (Table 5) showing 0% success at γ ≥ 0.2 is exactly the oversteering pathology the closed-loop controller is built to avoid, and LingBot-VA confirms it avoids the collapse.
- Honest reporting of a negative result (LingBot-VA) and architecture-dependence, rather than burying it.
- Cross-task transfer of contrastive directions is demonstrated with concrete numbers (Table 1's "Task i → Task j" structure), which is more convincing than single-task steering.

## Limitations

- **Architecture dependence is severe**: the method works on Cosmos-Policy and (partially) DiT4DiT but not LingBot-VA, and the paper offers no predictor beyond "run the MI analysis per setting" — limiting plug-and-play value.
- **No retraining-based baselines**; only inference-time baselines are compared, so the absolute robustness ceiling is untested.
- **Simulation-only** on LIBERO-10; no real-robot or real-perturbation validation. Gaussian noise and synthetic camera/gripper shifts may not reflect real deployment noise.
- **Hyperparameter sensitivity** persists even for WA-LQR (LQR hyperparameter search is run "before settling on the best-performing parameters for all other tasks of the same scene"), and the task-grouping step is a manual, empirically-discovered heuristic.
- **Scalability** of the Jacobian/LQR machinery to much larger WAMs is untested — the models here are ≤ 2B-parameter DiTs.
- **Cost** of collecting contrastive activations and computing per-(layer, timestep) SVDs/Jacobians across 28 blocks × 5 timesteps is non-trivial and not benchmarked against simply running more rollouts.
- The authors' own stated limitation: no interpretable method for predicting when tasks/environments share transferable representations.

## Relevance

与机器人基础模型的可解释性和鲁棒性方向高度相关：本文把 LLM/视频生成领域的激活转向 (activation steering) 与最优控制 (LQR) 迁移到 World Action Model，提供了一个免训练、可解释的鲁棒性诊断与干预框架，可作为后续研究 VLA/WAM 内部表征几何、推理时干预以及"哪些模型可被转向"这一开放问题的直接参考。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can the hinge-loss separability predictor be made architecture-agnostic — e.g., does it correlate with steerability on non-DiT WAMs or VLA backbones (π0, OpenVLA-OFT) beyond the three models tested?
- How does WA-LQR compare against *retraining-based* robustness methods (RL post-training, LIBERO-Plus-style robustness finetuning) under the same perturbation budget, in both success rate and compute cost?
- Is the locally-linear assumption (`Ã_{l,t}` reuse across inputs) preserved under larger OOD shifts (lighting, background texture, object layout) from COLOSSEUM/VLATest, or does it break down where the perturbation is no longer "nuisance-like"?
- Can the manual task-grouping step be replaced with an automatic clustering of contrastive directions, removing the post-hoc selection concern?
- Does steering along robustness directions introduce side-effects on action fluency, safety, or distribution of generated futures (the WAM also predicts video — is the predicted future visually degraded even when the action succeeds)?
- What happens on a real robot, where camera extrinsics and gripper calibration drift continuously rather than as one-shot perturbations — does the chunk-wise decay schedule still hold?
