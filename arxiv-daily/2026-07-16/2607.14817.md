> **一句话总结：** 本文证明 OOD 检测与主动学习等代理任务的 Bayes-最优打分器与"以后悔（regret）度量的认知不确定性"在数学上并不对齐，并将选择性预测与认知拒判统一为对覆盖率/风险/后悔的约束优化框架（最优选择子是真实 aleatoric 与 epistemic 分量的阈值化凸组合），进而在 CIFAR-10H、APPA-REAL、DCIC 等带稠密人工标注的数据集上展示出：按代理任务排名与按真实后悔排名会发生显著翻转（如 Evidential 在 OOD 上最差、却在 CIFAR-10H 纯后悔上最优）。

> **TL;DR:** The paper shows that the Bayes-optimal scorers for OOD detection and active learning are mathematically distinct from regret-minimizing epistemic selectors, unifies selective classification and the epistemic reject option into one constrained optimization (the optimal selector thresholds a convex combination of true aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty), and benchmarks standard UQ methods (MC-Dropout, Deep Ensembles, DDU, Evidential) on densely human-annotated datasets (CIFAR-10H, APPA-REAL, DCIC). It reports striking rank inversions: on CIFAR-10H (CE loss), Evidential networks — worst on Near-OOD (AuROC 0.711) and Far-OOD (0.726) — achieve the lowest AuReC = 0.0673, while DDU (best on Far-OOD at 0.935) is worst on pure regret (AuReC = 0.0958); it also argues Spearman rank correlation is a poor disentanglement diagnostic and proposes a Pareto-gap metric instead.

## 核心贡献

- **揭示并形式化了评估错配问题**：指出 OOD 检测的 Bayes-最优打分器是输入似然比 `p_ood(x)/p_id(x)`（完全与预测损失和 `p*(y|x)` 无关），主动学习 (BALD/EER) 优化的是全局误差缩减，而认知不确定性本质上是逐点的后悔（reducible error），三者只在特殊情况下重合。
- **统一约束优化框架与最优选择子定理**：将选择性预测（基于总不确定性 `T*`）与认知拒判（基于 `E*`）统一为对覆盖率、风险、后悔的三个约束优化问题，证明最优选择子恒为 `1{(1-λ)A* + λE* ≤ τ}`，λ∈[1/2,1] 的两个端点恰好恢复 Chow/Geifman-El-Yaniv 与 Franc-Paplhám 的经典结论。
- **1-D 闭沙盒的可视化反例**：基于 Rossellini 函数构造一个解析的回归环境，在固定 80% 覆盖率下展示 OOD、EER、BALD、风险-覆盖、后悔-覆盖五种 Bayes-最优打分器拒绝的是输入空间中完全不同的区域，证明经验排名分歧并非训练缺陷所致。
- **挑战"秩相关=去纠缠"的诊断范式**：用 CIFAR-10H 上的实例说明 Evidential 的学习分量秩相关高达 `r_s(Â,E)=0.999`（按 Mucsányi et al. 2024 会被判为完全纠缠失败），但其联合决策能力反而最接近理论最优（Pareto-gap=0.0379，AuReC=0.0673 都最好）。提出 Pareto-gap（基于 IGD+ 距离）作为更具操作意义的诊断。
- **真实标注数据集上的系统基准**：使用带稠密人类标注、可得到近似真实 `p*(y|x)` 的数据集（CIFAR-10H、APPA-REAL、DCIC 8 个），使得真实 frequentist 后悔可被精确计算，从而以 AuReC、ex-AuReC、n-AuReC、Pareto-gap 为指标对四类主流方法严格排名，揭示代理任务排名与决策论排名之间存在成对翻转。

## Key Contributions

- Formalizes the **evaluation disconnect**: OOD detection's Bayes-optimal scorer is the marginal likelihood ratio (independent of predictive loss and `p*(y|x)`), BALD/EER optimize global error reduction, while epistemic uncertainty is by definition pointwise regret. These coincide only in special cases.
- **Unified constrained-optimization framework (Theorem 1)**: frames selective classification and the epistemic reject option as three coupled problems over coverage, risk, and regret, and proves the optimal selector is always a threshold on `(1-λ)A* + λE*`, with λ∈[1/2,1]; the endpoints exactly recover Chow / Geifman-El-Yaniv (λ=1/2, total uncertainty) and Franc-Paplhám (λ=1, pure epistemic).
- **Closed-form 1-D counterexample**: a Rossellini-function regression sandbox in which five Bayes-optimal scorers (OOD, EER, BALD, risk-coverage, regret-coverage) reject disjoint regions of the input space at a fixed 80% coverage budget, showing that empirical rank disagreement is structural, not a training artifact.
- **Refutes the rank-correlation disentanglement criterion**: demonstrates that learned components with near-perfect Spearman correlation (`r_s(Â,E)=0.999` for Evidential on CIFAR-10H) can still form near-optimal joint decision boundaries, and proposes the **Pareto-gap** (IGD+ distance between the achievable and oracle (coverage, risk, regret) surfaces) as a more meaningful diagnostic.
- **Benchmark on densely human-annotated datasets**: uses CIFAR-10H, APPA-REAL, and the DCIC suite (8 datasets), where dense annotations expose an approximate `p*(y|x)` and thus allow exact computation of true frequentist regret via AuReC/ex-AuReC/n-AuReC, directly comparing MC-Dropout, Deep Ensembles, DDU, and Evidential networks.

## Methodology

The paper builds on the decision-theoretic aleatoric/epistemic split where, for a loss `ℓ` and training data `D`: total `T*_ℓ = E[ℓ(H(x,D),y)]`, aleatoric `A*_ℓ = E[ℓ(h(x,θ),y)]`, and epistemic/regret `E*_ℓ = T*_ℓ − A*_ℓ`, all expectations over `(θ,y) ~ p(θ,y|x,D)`. Selectors `C:X→[0,1]` are evaluated by three functionals — coverage `ρ(C)`, risk `Ri(C)`, regret `Re(C)` — and the authors study three constrained problems: (P_ρ) maximize coverage subject to risk/regret budgets; (PR_i) minimize risk subject to coverage/regret budgets; (PR_e) minimize regret subject to coverage/risk budgets. Applying KKT conditions to each Lagrangian yields Theorem 1: the optimum is always `1{(1-λ)A*_ℓ + λE*_ℓ ≤ τ}` for some λ∈[1/2,1].

For evaluation they exploit a frequentist–Bayesian hybrid: theoretical analysis lives in the Bayesian setup where `E*` is computable, while empirical evaluation requires the true `p*(y|x)`, obtained only from densely human-annotated datasets. Pure epistemic utility is measured by AuReC (area under the regret-coverage curve) and its accuracy-normalized variants ex-AuReC and n-AuReC. Joint representational capacity is measured by the Pareto-gap: search (oracle, on the test set) for the best linear weights `(w1,w2)` in `1{w1 Â_ℓ + w2 Ê_ℓ ≤ τ}`, sweep the resulting 3-D (coverage, risk, regret) surface, and compute its IGD+ distance to the oracle surface `S*` built from the true `(A*,E*)`.

A tractable 1-D regression sandbox inspired by the Rossellini function `y = sin(0.2/(x+0.16)^3) + ε` (well-specified Bayesian RBF linear regression) provides closed-form Bayes-optimal scorers for OOD, BALD, EER, risk-coverage, and regret-coverage, visualizing their disagreement at 80% coverage. Backbones: ResNet-18 (ImageNet-pretrained, fine-tuned) for CIFAR-10H and DCIC; ResNet-101 facial-age features + MLP for APPA-REAL. All tasks are framed as categorical probability estimation; uncertainty is extracted under deployment losses CE/0-1 (CIFAR/DCIC) and squared/absolute (APPA-REAL). Baselines: MC-Dropout, Deep Ensembles, DDU, Evidential Networks. OOD and active-learning numbers are adopted from a concurrent submission (Anonymous, 2026) and provided in the supplement.

## Results & Highlights

- **Rank inversion CIFAR-10H (CE loss)**: Evidential networks, which are *worst* on Near-OOD (AuROC = 0.711) and Far-OOD (AuROC = 0.726), achieve the *lowest* AuReC = 0.0673 and best ranking-quality ex-AuReC = 0.0183; DDU, which is *best* on Far-OOD (AuROC = 0.935), is *worst* on pure regret (AuReC = 0.0958). (Tables 1, 4.)
- **0/1 loss on CIFAR-10H**: ordering persists — Evidential AuReC = 0.0030 vs DDU AuReC = 0.0034 vs Ensemble AuReC = 0.0058; on n-AuReC, Evidential = 0.132 beats Ensemble = 0.379 and Dropout = 0.852, despite DDU/Evidential being statistically tied at the top of the Far-OOD leaderboard.
- **APPA-REAL (squared-error loss)**: Ensemble (AuReC = 11.3519, n-AuReC = 0.453) and Dropout (AuReC = 11.6382) dominate, while DDU is worst with n-AuReC = 1.048 (worse than random). Evidential is worst on raw AuReC = 16.9411 but better than DDU on n-AuReC. This contradicts the OOD/SC leaderboard where Deep Ensembles and DDU are the strongest.
- **DCIC suite (8 datasets, 0/1 loss)**: Ensemble wins on 6 of 8 datasets with the lowest mean n-AuReC = 0.502; Evidential wins 2 with n-AuReC = 0.617; MC-Dropout and DDU win 0 datasets each, with DDU last at n-AuReC = 0.978. Best Pareto-gap is Ensemble (0.0207) and Evidential (0.0226), not DDU (0.0248). (Table 3.)
- **Active-learning proxy vs regret**: on OpenML ID 6, Evidential scores NAUC = 0.177 (near-failure, vs 0.943–0.962 for the other three methods), yet Evidential *leads* on CIFAR-10H pure regret (AuReC = 0.0673) — a direct pairwise rank inversion between the active-learning proxy and the regret objective. (Tables 1, 5.)
- **Disentanglement refutation**: on CIFAR-10H (CE) Evidential shows near-perfect rank correlation `r_s(Â,E)=0.999` yet achieves the *best* Pareto-gap = 0.0379 and best AuReC; conversely the ground-truth `r_s(A*,E*)` itself reaches 0.882 for Dropout, so high correlation may reflect the data-generating process rather than a separation failure. (Table 1.)

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: The strongest novelty is the unification theorem (Theorem 1), which subsumes Chow-style selective prediction and the epistemic reject option as λ-endpoints of one convex family; this is genuinely new and clean. The task-misalignment argument itself partially overlaps with existing critiques — e.g., Li et al. 2025 already argued that supervised classifiers cannot implement the OOD likelihood-ratio scorer, and Jiménez et al. 2026 already flagged the uncomputability of regret. The Pareto-gap diagnostic is the more original methodological contribution.
- **Rigor**: The KKT-based proof is short and verifiable; the closed-form 1-D sandbox is a legitimate constructive demonstration rather than a strawman. Benchmarking spans multiple domains (faces, natural images, 8 DCIC tasks) and multiple losses (CE, 0/1, squared, absolute), which is stronger than single-dataset UQ papers.
- **Reproducibility concerns — data quirk**: the entire frequentist-regret evaluation hinges on datasets with dense human annotations approximating `p*(y|x)` (CIFAR-10H, APPA-REAL, DCIC). The authors exclude ImageNet-ReaL because its multi-label format violates the single-class `p*(y|x)` assumption, and exclude DCIC-Synthetic. This is a genuine restriction of scope: the regret framework cannot be applied to any standard single-label benchmark, so the central claim ("proxy rankings disagree with regret rankings") is demonstrated only on annotation-rich datasets whose `p*(y|x)` may not be representative of typical ML deployments. APPA-REAL is also reframed as categorical (CE over ages 0–100), which is a non-standard treatment that could interact with the regret computation.
- **Missing ablations**: the paper does not separate estimation error from approximation error in the regret (acknowledged in §7), nor does it ablate the loss-consistent extraction of `(Â_ℓ, Ê_ℓ)` or sensitivity to backbone choice. The Pareto-gap is computed by an *oracle* test-set search for optimal `(w1,w2)`, explicitly disclaimed as non-deployable — so the diagnostic measures "capacity" rather than achievable online performance, which somewhat limits its operational claim.
- **Inherited baselines**: the OOD-detection (OpenOOD) and active-learning (OpenML) numbers are adopted wholesale from an anonymous concurrent submission rather than re-run; cross-paper differences in training recipes could quietly contribute to the reported inversions.
- **Red flags**: none severe. The headline pairwise inversions (Evidential, DDU) are striking but on small absolute AuReC differences in some cells (e.g., Evidential vs DDU on CIFAR-10H 0/1 loss: 0.0030 vs 0.0034); the authors do not report confidence intervals or significance tests, so some "rank inversions" may be within noise.

## Strengths

- Provides a clean, provable unification of two previously separate selective-prediction traditions with explicit endpoint recovery of classical results.
- Empirically demonstrates — rather than merely asserts — that OOD and active-learning rankings diverge from regret rankings, using real human-annotated `p*(y|x)` rather than synthetic proxies.
- The Pareto-gap diagnostic is a principled, decision-theoretic alternative to the increasingly contested Spearman-correlation test for disentanglement, and the Evidential 0.999-correlation-yet-best-Pareto-gap counterexample is memorable and convincing.
- Honest about limitations: explicitly states regret metrics are benchmarking-only tools unusable on single-label data, and that estimation vs approximation error are not separated.
- Multi-loss, multi-domain evaluation (CE/0-1/squared/absolute across face, natural-image, and DCIC tasks) is more thorough than typical UQ empirical studies.

## Limitations

- True regret requires dense human annotations; the framework cannot be applied to standard single-label benchmarks like vanilla CIFAR-10 or ImageNet, restricting the scope of the empirical claims.
- Pareto-gap uses oracle test-set tuning of `(w1,w2)`, so it measures capacity for optimal decisions rather than practically deployable performance — the paper itself flags this.
- OOD and active-learning baselines are adopted from an anonymous concurrent submission rather than independently reproduced; cross-paper training differences are not controlled.
- No confidence intervals or statistical significance tests are reported, and some rank inversions rest on small numerical differences.
- Estimation and approximation error in the regret are conflated.
- The closed-form sandbox uses a single 1-D regression problem; the divergence of Bayes-optimal scorers is demonstrated there but not quantified across higher-dimensional tasks.

## Relevance

与不确定性量化、选择性预测以及认知/偶然不确定性分解的研究兴趣高度相关：它对当前以 OOD 检测和主动学习为代理的 UQ 评测范式提出了理论性挑战，并给出可操作的 Pareto-gap 诊断与统一拒判框架，对于从事可靠机器学习评测、拒判预测与人机标注数据建模的研究者具有直接参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the regret-ranking vs OOD-ranking inversion persist on larger single-label benchmarks if `p*(y|x)` is approximated (e.g., via soft-label distillation or conformal calibration), and how sensitive is the conclusion to the quality of that approximation?
- Can the Pareto-gap diagnostic be made *deployable* — e.g., by tuning `(w1,w2)` on a held-out calibration set rather than the test set — without losing its discriminative power?
- How do recent second-order / proper-scoring-rule decompositions (Hofman et al. 2024; Kotelevskii et al. 2025) behave under the unified framework, and do they close the Pareto-gap more effectively than the four baselines tested here?
- Are the pairwise inversions statistically significant once seed-level variance is propagated, and which inversions survive a proper significance test across all DCIC datasets?
- Does the framework extend to structured prediction (segmentation, detection) where `p*(y|x)` is far harder to elicit from human annotators?
