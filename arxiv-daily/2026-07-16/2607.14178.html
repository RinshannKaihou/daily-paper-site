ReasFlow: Assisting Reasoning-Centric Scientific Discovery in Applied Mathematics via a Knowledge-Based Multi-Agent System ↗
— Key finding

A multi-agent system that autonomously writes rigorous applied-math papers — survey, algorithm, proof, experiment, manuscript — with verified convergence guarantees.

01

Methodology

A central MetaAgent clarifies the topic with the user, then drives a chain of specialized sub-agents over a shared workspace with file/terminal utilities.

Two mechanisms separate ReasFlow from prior AI-scientist systems: (1) an internal verification loop — the LemmaVerifierAgent audits context coherence, bound tightness, and explicit justifications under a zero-tolerance policy, looping rejected proofs back to the prover. (2) Knowledge augmentation via "knowledge cards" encoding procedural heuristics, refined by an automatic prompt-update loop until retrieved-card performance matches expert guidance.

MetaAgent→ SurveyAgent→ AlgorithmAgent→ ProverAgent ◯ verify→ ExperimentAgent→ IntroductionAgent→ WritingAgent
02

Results & Highlights

  • End-to-end paper 77.9 avg PaperReviewAgent score; +20.6 over DeepScientist (57.3). Beats AI Scientist-v2, ARIS, Claude Pro, CycleResearcher on every reviewer.
  • ProverAgent 90.7 avg (5-dim ProofReviewAgent), vs 73.0 for QED. A perfect 100 from Gemini-3-Pro across all three backbones.
  • AlgorithmAgent 100% first-run success on 35 MLR-Bench + OR-Bench tasks, vs 97% Claude Code, 60% ARIS, 44% AI Scientist-v2.
  • ExperimentAgent Lowest hallucination suspicion: 9% vs 26% Codex, 46% ARIS, 100% AI Scientist-v2. Leads on design soundness (8.49).
  • WritingAgent 9.31 overall; only system ranking first on 3 of 4 dimensions (faithfulness 9.73, submittability 9.87).
  • CIFAR-100 case ReasFlow-designed method hits 45.43% test accuracy vs FedAvg 34.35%, FedSub 23.17% (ResNet-110).
03

Strengths & Limitations

+ Strengths

  • Tackles a genuinely under-served niche — theory-driven applied mathematics — where empirical-ML AI-scientist systems are structurally inadequate.
  • Verifier + auto-refined knowledge cards are motivated by a concrete failure mode: LLMs mishandling stochastic independence and losing the sharp 1/N factor.
  • Five end-to-end case studies across federated, manifold, decentralized optimization and LLM training, each with novel theory + experiments.
  • Section-level ablations isolate each sub-agent against matched baselines; open-sourced via ReasLab + GitHub.

— Limitations

  • Internal verifier does not guarantee formal correctness; no Lean / Coq / Isabelle integration — "rigorous proof" rests on natural-language checking.
  • Knowledge base is tailored to applied math; the 339-paper FAISS library is not released, and cross-domain extension needs a rebuilt pipeline.
  • Results tied to proprietary foundation models (GPT-5.x, Gemini-3); long-term claims shift as those models change.
  • Headline evaluation is LLM-as-judge with no human cross-validation; some Table-1 baselines (CycleResearcher-ML-12B) are mismatched in scale, inflating margins.
04

Questions for further exploration

  1. How much of the ProverAgent gain (90.7 vs 73.0) is knowledge cards vs verifier vs GPT-5.4 backbone? A clean three-way ablation is missing.
  2. Would the five auto-generated proofs survive Lean 4 formalization by an independent party, or hide gaps natural-language checking cannot catch?
  3. Are the perfect Gemini-3-Pro scores driven by presentation vs mathematical content? Can a human-expert panel reproduce the ranking vs QED / ProofGrader?
  4. Does the automatic prompt-refinement loop converge reliably across proof tasks — and at what iteration / token cost? No convergence statistics reported.
  5. Can the framework generalize beyond optimization theory (PDEs, numerical linear algebra, approximation theory), where procedural heuristics are less codified?
