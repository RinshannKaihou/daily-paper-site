> **一句话总结：** 提出 AIMO 可解释性挑战赛（NeurIPS 2026 竞赛轨道），通过符号推理链生成的对抗性扰动，要求参赛者基于前沿数学大模型（Qwen3.5、GPT-5.2、Gemini-3.1 Pro 等）的内部机制判断其正确作答来自稳健推理还是脆弱捷径，基线探测分类器仅达 58.37%、基于不确定性的分类器达 69.23%。

> **TL;DR:** This is a NeurIPS 2026 Competition Track proposal for the AIMO Interpretability Challenge, which asks participants to classify whether a frontier math reasoning model (from AIMO 3 submissions, including Qwen3.5, GPT-5.2, Gemini-3.1 Pro) answers a problem *robustly* or via *spurious shortcuts*, using the model's internal signals on 180 olympiad-level problems (AIMO/AIME/JMO, 50% newly authored) with symbolic-chain perturbations. Baseline probing reaches only 58.37% accuracy and an uncertainty-based baseline 69.23% (vs. 50% random) on DeepSeek-R1-0528-Qwen3-8B, and frontier models suffer up to 90% accuracy drops under expert perturbations (e.g., Qwen3.5 expert no-solution: 100%→10%).

## 核心贡献

- **提出全新竞赛框架：** 定义了一个面向前沿数学推理模型的可解释性任务——给定 (问题 X, 模型 M, 分布偏移 Δ)，判定模型在原问题答对的情况下是否在扰动下仍然稳健（二分类），将"稳健 vs. 脆弱捷径"从哲学争论操作化为可评测的预测问题。
- **构建高质量数据集：** 汇集 180 道奥赛级数学题（来自 AIMO 2/3、JMO、近两年 AIME），其中 50% 为互联网上从未公开的新题；为每题标注符号推理链（类 GSM-Symbolic 但面向奥赛级），可生成无限量功能变体作为对抗扰动；划分 150 题 train / 30 题 private test，预计配合 ≥8 个模型产生约 1200 训练 / 240 测试实例。
- **设计 7 类对抗扰动体系：** Rephrase、Rename、Domain、Distract、Typos、Expert perturbations、Expert no-solution，覆盖语义重写、变量改名、领域迁移、无关信息注入、排版噪声以及专家级对抗编辑与不可解变体检测。
- **提供完整开源基线与基础设施：** 实现并发布了 3 个基线（探测分类器、不确定性分类器、数据归因方法），配套 Codabench 提交容器、评估代码库、HuggingFace 公开样本、GitHub 起步包；为参赛者提供最高 10,000 H200 GPU 小时算力。
- **双轨设计促进参与公平：** Main track（AIMO 3 全量前沿模型）与 Small models track（<10B 参数，便于 SAE/Transcoder 等计算密集方法）并行，并设立私有测试集、提交次数限制与离线执行以防过拟合。

## Key Contributions

- **A new interpretability task formulation:** Operationalizes "robust vs. spurious reasoning" as a binary classification problem over triples (Problem X, Model M, Distribution shift Δ), where M answers X correctly — predicting whether M remains correct under perturbations of X, judged from the model's internal mechanisms rather than final-answer accuracy alone.
- **A curated olympiad-level benchmark with symbolic annotations:** 180 problems drawn from AIMO 2, AIMO 3, JMO, and two years of AIME, with 50% newly authored and never published online; each problem annotated with a symbolic reasoning chain (GSM-Symbolic-style but at olympiad difficulty) enabling generation of unlimited functional variants under controlled distribution shifts.
- **A reusable robustness benchmark and toolset:** Public train split (150 problems) released under Apache 2.0; a private test split (30 AIMO 3 problems) evaluated only on organizer servers; cross-product with ≥8 top AIMO 3 models yields ~1200 training / ~240 test robustness-labeled instances.
- **Seven perturbation types with demonstrated efficacy:** Rephrase, Rename, Domain, Distract, Typos, Expert perturbations, and Expert no-solution, shown empirically to cause large accuracy drops on frontier models (Table 1).
- **Open baselines, infrastructure, and compute access:** Three starter baselines (probing, uncertainty-based, data attribution) with full lifecycle code on GitHub, a Codabench submission pipeline, exact-match evaluation code, HuggingFace sample data, and up to 10,000 H200 GPU-hours of compute for participants via the Fields Model Initiative.
- **Dual-track design (Main + Small models <10B):** Makes compute-heavy mechanistic methods (SAEs, Transcoders) viable while keeping a frontier-scale track, broadening participation.

## Methodology

This is a **competition design paper**, not a single-method empirical study, so "methodology" describes the competition protocol, data construction, and baseline methods rather than one algorithm.

**Task formulation.** Given a triple (Problem X, Model M, Distribution shift Δ) where M(X) = y_true, participants must build a submission system S that outputs 1 (robust) if M remains correct on a majority of perturbed variants ΔX ∈ Δ, and 0 (spurious) otherwise. Symbolic reasoning chains (Figure 1 shows the annotated Python `solution()` template for AIMO 2 problem 480182) allow unconstrained generation of perturbed problems ΔX, so clear-cut (non-ambiguous) samples can be retained and ambiguous ones discarded.

**Data.** 180 olympiad problems from AIMO 2, AIMO 3, JMO, and two years of AIME, with confirmed licenses (AIMO/AIME are Apache 2.0; private AIMO/JMO confirmed by owners). 50% of train and test problems are newly authored by mathematicians on the team (Fuzhou University, NII Japan, with Peking University network). Annotations of symbolic reasoning chains are near-complete and will be fully released under Apache 2.0 before launch. An LLM-based codebase will also be released for generating adversarial training data from generic (non-symbolic) perturbations.

**Perturbations (Appendix B).** Seven types implemented: Rephrase (semantic-preserving rewrites), Rename (variable/entity renaming), Domain (surface-domain transfer), Distract (irrelevant-but-plausible info), Typos (formatting noise), Expert perturbations (answer-preserving adversarial edits using mathematical conventions / unstated premises), and Expert no-solution (unanswerable variants, e.g., missing premises, to test unsolvability detection).

**Models.** The interpreted set is drawn from top-performing AIMO 3 submissions; the small-models track restricts to <10B-parameter models. Organizers confirm AIMO participation grants access to these models.

**Baselines (already implemented for the first two):**
1. A **probing classifier** on the last-input-token internal representation at the best-performing layer.
2. An **uncertainty/confidence-based classifier** over chain-of-thought generation signals (Grünefeld et al., 2026).
3. A **data attribution method** assessing robustness via memorization impact/scale (Park et al., 2024).
All evaluated with DeepSeek-R1-0528-Qwen3-8B (a top-3 AIMO 3 model) using stratified 10-fold cross-validation.

**Evaluation.** Primary metric is accuracy on a 30-problem held-out private test set, with planned breakdowns by (1) public/private, (2) perturbation type, (3) model, (4) published/unpublished problems. Final report uses nonparametric bootstrap CIs and paired significance testing (McNemar's test). Evaluation runs offline on organizer infrastructure (32 CPU cores, 512GB RAM, 8 large-scale GPUs, 1 hour per problem on 8-GPU nodes, Docker via Codabench).

**Anti-overfitting rules.** No extra labeled data for the same perturbation types as the final validation set (unlabeled data and labels for other perturbations allowed); signals from weights/activations/token probabilities/black-box statistics all permitted; ranked teams must submit a 1–2-page technical report for compliance.

## Results & Highlights

The paper reports three distinct kinds of quantitative evidence:

1. **Baseline accuracy on the proposed task (DeepSeek-R1-0528-Qwen3-8B, stratified 10-fold CV, random baseline = 50%):**
   - Probing classifier: **58.37% (±7.5)** accuracy — only +8.37 points over random, indicating the task is hard and leaves large room for improvement.
   - Uncertainty-based (CoT confidence) classifier: **69.23% (±10.13)** accuracy — +19.23 over random, the strongest baseline.
   - The third baseline (data attribution) is listed as not yet implemented at submission.

2. **Frontier-model accuracy drops under symbolic-chain perturbations on AIMO 2 reference problems (Table 1):**
   - Problem `bbd91e`, **Qwen3.5, expert no-solution: 100% → 10% (90-point drop)**.
   - Problem `a1d40b`, **Qwen3-8B, expert no-solution: 100% → 10% (90-point drop)**.
   - Problem `71beb6`, **GPT-OSS-120B, expert perturbations: 70% → 20% (50-point drop)**.
   - Problem `057f8a`, **Qwen3.5: distract 60%→20% (40 pts), domain 60%→10% (50 pts), rename 60%→20% (40 pts), typos 60%→30% (30 pts)**.
   - Problem `480182`, **GPT-5.2, rephrase: 100% → 60% (40-point drop)**.
   - Problem `349493`, **Gemini-3.1 Pro, typos: 60% → 30% (30-point drop)**.

3. **Scale and engagement signals:** AIMO 3 (the upstream Kaggle competition) attracted **4,000+ teams in 2026**; the challenge targets **20–40 well-performing submissions**; the effective labeled-instance count is **~1200 training / ~240 test** from the cross-product of ≥8 top models and the problem set; participants can request up to **10,000 H200 GPU-hours**.

These numbers establish two facts central to the paper's thesis: (i) the task is *feasible* (baselines beat 50%), and (ii) it is *challenging and well-motivated* (frontier models including GPT-5.2 and Gemini-3.1 Pro collapse by 30–90 points under answer-preserving perturbations, confirming the existence of spurious reasoning worth detecting).

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty — moderate, well-positioned.** This is genuinely a *new competition*, but its intellectual ingredients are familiar: GSM-Symbolic-style perturbations (Mirzadeh et al., 2025), probing (Belinkov, 2022), CoT-uncertainty probing (Grünefeld et al., 2026), and data attribution (Park et al., 2024). The novelty is in *composition and target* — pointing interpretability tooling at frontier olympiad models with a clean robust/spurious binary, rather than at synthetic algorithm-recovery benchmarks (InterpBench, MIB) or attribute disentanglement (RAVEL). The honest framing ("will not build the first interpretability benchmark") is appreciated.

- **Rigor — sound protocol, weak preliminary evidence.** The evaluation protocol is well-designed: private test set, Docker-reproducible Codabench submissions, offline execution, submission-count limits, McNemar paired tests, bootstrap CIs, and stratified 10-fold CV for baselines. However, the headline baseline numbers (58.37%, 69.23%) come from a **single model (DeepSeek-R1-0528-Qwen3-8B)** on a *sample validation set* whose size is unspecified — we cannot tell whether the ±7.5 / ±10.13 SDs reflect few problems or genuine task variance. Table 1's perturbation drops are illustrative paired examples, not a systematic benchmark (no averaging, no CIs, one problem per row), so they motivate but do not yet substantiate broad fragility claims.

- **Reproducibility — high by design, partial in practice.** Code, baselines, evaluation harness, and public sample data are already linked; final-model submissions run in Docker on fixed hardware. But the central scientific asset — the **50% newly-authored, never-published problems** — is private and unverifiable by the community, and the symbolic-chain annotation pipeline (authoring + verification) is described only narratively. Reproducing the *benchmark* externally is impossible until/unless problems are released post-competition.

- **Significance — potentially high if it lands.** If the competition produces a public robustness benchmark and ≥2 baselines that clearly beat 69.23% via internal mechanisms, it would meaningfully advance the "is frontier reasoning real?" debate and feed AI-safety auditing use cases. If participation is thin or winning methods are black-box behavior proxies (which the authors themselves flag as a possible failure mode in footnote 4), the contribution collapses to "another perturbation benchmark."

- **Red flags / concerns:**
  1. **Small test set (30 problems).** With only 240 test instances from the model×problem cross-product, per-perturbation-type and per-model breakdowns will be noisy; McNemar helps but cannot manufacture statistical power that isn't there.
  2. **"50% new problems" is a strong, hard-to-audit claim** relying entirely on the organizing mathematicians' network; the contingency plan (fall back to JMO) is reasonable but does not address verification.
  3. **The strongest baseline (uncertainty, 69.23%) is a black-box signal**, somewhat at odds with the stated goal of using *internal mechanisms* — the competition rules permit any black-box statistic, so winning entries could side-step mechanistic interpretability entirely, undercutting the scientific framing.
  4. **Status risk:** as a proposal, the paper presents *planned* infrastructure (full dataset "within one month from the submission deadline," industrial sponsorship still unconfirmed) — readers should treat quantitative claims as preliminary baselines, not validated findings.

## Strengths

- Clearly motivated by a genuinely open, high-stakes question (robust vs. spurious frontier reasoning), with a precise, falsifiable task formulation.
- Strong, credible organizing team combining interpretability, evaluation, and olympiad-mathematics expertise — rare and well-matched to the task.
- Thoughtful anti-overfitting design: private test set, offline Docker execution, submission limits, compliance reports, perturbation-type data-usage restrictions.
- Dual-track structure (Main + <10B) inclusively accommodates both frontier-scale and compute-heavy mechanistic methods (SAEs, Transcoders).
- Already-shipped artifacts: public website, GitHub baselines, HuggingFace sample, evaluation codebase, Codabench interface — rare readiness for a competition proposal.
- Backed by real compute (10,000 H200 GPU-hours) and access to AIMO 3's top models and private problems.

## Limitations

- **It is a proposal, not a results paper.** All empirical numbers are preliminary baselines on a single model over an unspecified-size sample; the main scientific payoff depends on competition outcomes that have not yet occurred.
- **Small held-out test set (30 problems / ~240 instances)** limits statistical power for the fine-grained breakdowns (per perturbation type, per model) that are advertised as deliverables.
- **Table 1 perturbation drops are illustrative**, not aggregated — one problem per row, no confidence intervals, no control for problem difficulty — so broad fragility conclusions are not yet supported quantitatively.
- **The "internal mechanisms" framing is somewhat undercut** by allowing black-box statistics (token probabilities, confidence) as inputs, and by the fact that the strongest baseline is a black-box uncertainty signal.
- **New-problem authoring is unverifiable** by the community while the dataset is private; symbolic-chain annotation and verification methodology is described only at a narrative level.
- **No human baseline or interpretability-method ceiling** is reported, so it is unclear how much headroom exists above 69.23% or what a "solved" task would look like.
- **Compute and sponsorship partially aspirational** at submission time (industrial 500 USD award "awaiting confirmation").

## Relevance

对研究"大模型是否具备稳健推理能力 vs. 依赖脆弱捷径"这一核心问题高度相关：本竞赛直接将可解释性方法（探测、SAE、注意力分析、数据归因）与前沿数学推理模型的对抗鲁棒性评测对接，并提供公开基准、私有测试集与算力支持，可作为衡量内部机制能否预测 OOD 行为的标准化平台，契合可解释性、推理鲁棒性与 AI 安全审计方向的研究兴趣。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can mechanistic interpretability methods (SAEs, transcoders, circuit-level attribution) substantially exceed the 69.23% black-box uncertainty baseline, or does behavior alone already capture most of the robustness signal?
- How stable are robust/spurious labels across sampling temperature and decoding variation — i.e., is the "clear-cut" filtering criterion (Sec 1.4) robust to generation stochasticity?
- Does robustness on one perturbation type (e.g., Rephrase) predict robustness on others (e.g., Expert no-solution), suggesting a general "robustness factor," or are they orthogonal?
- With only 30 private test problems, what effect size can the competition realistically detect, and would a larger public post-competition release change conclusions?
- Do models that *fail* Expert-no-solution perturbations (e.g., Qwen3.5 dropping 100%→10%) share a common internal failure mode (e.g., missing premise-tracking circuits) that interpretability could localize?
- How do robustness labels transfer across model families (Qwen vs. GPT-OSS vs. Gemini) — is robustness a property of the problem, the model, or their interaction?
- Can the symbolic-chain perturbation framework extend beyond olympiad math to code, scientific QA, or multi-step tool use where "robust reasoning" matters for deployment?
