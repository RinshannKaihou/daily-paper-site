§ 01

Methodology

The authors implement AdaptiveEmbeddedRK3Optimizer: Bogacki–Shampine 3(2) embedded Runge–Kutta stages applied to the gradient flow θ̇ = −∇L(θ), with the 3rd-order averaged gradient driving standard Adam moments. FSAL caches the post-step gradient as the next k₁; a local-error controller ϕ = clip(0.9·err^(−1/3), 0.5, 2.0), h ← clip(h·ϕ, [0.1·lr, h_max]) nominally adapts the step.

s-stage RK step = charged s gradient evals
The compute-matched protocol denominates every optimizer's budget in gradient evaluations, and credits FSAL reuse only in the deterministic full-batch regime where it is mathematically valid. This disallows the two accounting conventions — per-step comparison, and FSAL under minibatch sampling — that quietly flatter the RK side of the literature.

Four experiments

(1) Stochastic minibatch MNIST (784–128–10 MLP, batch 128, 4000 evals/seed). (2) Full-batch on a 1024-example MNIST subset — the autonomous-vector-field regime where FSAL is valid (600 evals/seed), baselines spanning SGD+momentum/Nesterov, Adagrad, RMSprop, Adam, AdamW, NAdam, RAdam. (3) A repaired-controller ablation adding a true accept/reject branch, error measured on the applied map, h_max = 100·h₀. (4) A pre-registered pSGLD temperature sweep testing the tempering hypothesis.

Diagnosis & reproducibility

Direct instrumentation of the step controller plus a 36-point ρ × h_max × h₀ hyperparameter cube upgrades a single anecdote into an exhaustive negative. All runs are CPU-only with deterministic seeding; named scripts (RK4Optimizer.py, fullbatch_experiment.py, fixed_controller_experiment.py, temperature_sweep_experiment.py) with code, logs, and result JSONs released.

§ 03

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Principled compute-matching that exposes two accounting conventions (per-step comparison; FSAL under minibatch) systematically biasing prior RK-optimizer comparisons.
  • Airtight inertness diagnosis — formal saturation condition (Prop. 1), direct instrumentation, and a 36-point hyperparameter cube proving no setting rescues the as-designed controller.
  • Self-critical framing: repairs the very method it debunks, isolates the surviving mechanism with a fixed-step control, and pre-registers tests to rule out alternatives.
  • Actionable reporting standard for adaptive-step optimizers (accepted-h distribution, rejection rate, non-bit-identical-trajectory requirement) the community could adopt.
  • Clean positioning vs acceleration theory, the practical RK-optimizer line, and the SG-MCMC sampling lens; exemplary honesty (eight numbered caveats).

Limitations

  • Scale is minimal: all results on MNIST with a single 784–128–10 MLP; rankings may not transfer to CNNs, transformers, or larger datasets.
  • Baseline tuning asymmetry: Adam got only a "modest lr grid" — and Adam lr=0.01 (90.30%, beating every RK variant) appears almost as an aside in Table 4.
  • One design point: only Bogacki–Shampine 3(2) implemented; IMEX / implicit schemes and AdamNODE not re-implemented, and have a different failure surface.
  • ~40× win is full-batch & fragile to h₀: tighter ρ=0.01 breaks by h₀=0.002 (reject-retry thrashing); no evidence it survives minibatch noise, where the estimator is variance-dominated.
  • n=3 seeds for main sweeps and repaired tables; implicit-regularization observation is post hoc, mechanism identified only by exclusion (curvature not instrumented).
§ 04

Open questions

  1. Does the inertness diagnosis generalize to other embedded RK pairs (Dormand–Prince 5(4), higher-order IMEX), or is it specific to Bogacki–Shampine 3(2) composed with Adam?
  2. Can a variance-corrected embedded error estimator make the repaired controller meaningful under minibatch noise, where the current estimator is sampling-noise-dominated?
  3. Does the repaired controller's ~40× full-batch advantage and emergent warmup-and-growth schedule survive on modern architectures (CNNs, transformers) and datasets (CIFAR, ImageNet)?
  4. Is the implicit-regularization effect of RK gradient averaging (beating lr-matched Adam/AdamW on 10/10 seeds) a disguised form of gradient centralization / EMA — obtainable more cheaply?
  5. Can the controller's emergent warmup schedule be characterized precisely as an implicit error-control policy, and does it match hand-designed warmup heuristics?
  6. Does the per-gradient-evaluation accounting discipline change the conclusions of the IMEX-Adam (Bhattacharjee et al. 2024) and AdamNODE (Cho et al. 2022) results if re-run under this protocol?
