> **一句话总结：** 在严格按梯度计算量对齐的评测协议下，一个有代表性的 Bogacki–Shampine 3(2) 阶 RK–Adam 优化器在 MNIST 的小批量与全批量训练损失上均输给普通 Adam，其所谓"自适应"步长控制器实际上完全失效（步长从第一步就钉在上限、容差跨两个数量级轨迹位级相同）；修复控制器（加入拒绝分支、对实际施加的映射估误差）虽能在全批量上把训练损失再压低约 40×，但收益仅来自一种"涌现式 warmup-and-growth"调度，且无法迁移到测试精度。

> **TL;DR:** Under a strict compute-matched protocol that gives every optimizer the same gradient-evaluation budget (FSAL credited only where mathematically valid), a representative Bogacki–Shampine 3(2) RK–Adam loses to plain Adam on MNIST minibatch (97.26% vs 97.76%) and full-batch training loss, and its "adaptive" step controller is provably inert — the step saturates at its ceiling on step one and tolerances spanning 100× produce bit-identical trajectories. Repairing the controller (real accept/reject branch, error on the applied map) reverses the full-batch training-loss result by ~40× (2.5e-6 vs Adam's 1.0e-4), but a fixed-step control localizes the benefit to an emergent warmup-and-growth schedule rather than higher-order accuracy, and the gain does not transfer to test accuracy (no repaired config reaches the 90.30% of Adam lr=0.01); RMSprop (90.02%) and NAdam (89.94%) match or beat every RK configuration at one third the per-step cost.

## 核心贡献

- **建立严格的"每梯度计算量"对齐评测协议**：把优化器步数折算成梯度求值数（s 阶 RK 一步 = s 次求值），并只在确定性向量场（全批量）下才承认 FSAL 重用——这一核算标准使 RK 文献中常见的"每步比较"与"小批量下滥用 FSAL"两大美化惯例失效。
- **给出 RK3(2)-Adam 在两种机制下都输给 Adam 的反例证据**：小批量 MNIST 上 RK 落后 ~0.5 分，全批量上 Adam 在等计算预算下达到 2.4–3.4× 更低的训练损失。
- **诊断并形式化"自适应"控制器失效的原因**：无拒绝分支、增长因子饱和（命题 1 给出饱和条件 (0.9/2)³≈0.0911）、误差估计与实际施加映射不一致；在 36 点 ρ×h_max×h₀ 立方扫描中，所有容差设置下步长都被钉在上限，98–100% 的步骤位于天花板。
- **修复控制器并隔离机制**：加入真正的拒绝分支、对实际施加的 Adam 预条件映射估误差后，全批量训练损失比调优过的 Adam 低约 40×；再用固定步长对照证明该收益来自"涌现式 warmup-and-growth"调度（即自适应），而非高阶精度本身或大步长。
- **预注册实验排除备选解释**：用 pSGLD 显式温度旋钮检验"冷后验/tempering"假设（H0 成立：T=0 最优，任何 T>0 测试精度都不升反降），并验证"更深深度的极小化本身不会过拟合"，从而把泛化短板归因于"控制器轨迹选了更差的最小值"这种隐式偏置效应。
- **提出最小化报告标准**：建议任何自适应步长优化器都应报告接受步长 h 的分布（天花板占比）、拒绝率，以及至少两组控制器设置产生非位级相同轨迹的证据。

## Key Contributions

- Establishes a strict **per-gradient-evaluation compute-matched protocol**: an s-stage RK step is charged s gradient evaluations, and FSAL reuse is credited only in the deterministic full-batch regime where it is mathematically valid. This disallows the two accounting conventions (per-step comparison; FSAL under minibatch sampling) that quietly flatter the RK side of the literature.
- Provides **compute-matched negative evidence** that a representative RK3(2)-Adam loses to plain Adam on both stochastic minibatch MNIST (Table 1) and full-batch MNIST (Table 2), where Adam reaches 2.4–3.4× lower training loss at equal budget.
- **Diagnoses and formalizes** why embedded-pair step control is inert when composed with Adam preconditioning: no rejection branch, growth-factor saturation, and an error estimate inconsistent with the applied map. Proposition 1 gives the saturation condition; an exhaustive ρ×h_max×h₀ sweep shows the step is pinned at its ceiling in 98–100% of steps in every configuration and tolerances spanning 100× give bit-identical trajectories.
- **Repairs the controller and isolates the mechanism**: a true accept/reject branch plus error measured on the applied map reverses the full-batch training-loss comparison by ~40× (2.5×10⁻⁶ vs Adam's 1.0×10⁻⁴); a fixed-step control at the same final h shows the gain comes from adaptivity (an emergent warmup-and-growth schedule), not from higher-order accuracy per se.
- **Pre-registered experiments rule out off-the-shelf explanations**: a pSGLD temperature sweep refutes the cold-posterior/tempering reading (T=0 is the global optimum; every T>0 hurts by ≥0.6 pts), and a long T=0 run shows pure deeper minimization is generalization-neutral, pinning the generalization shortfall on which minimum the trajectory selects.
- Proposes a **minimal reporting standard** for adaptive-step optimizers (accepted-h distribution / ceiling fraction, rejection rate, non-bit-identical-trajectory demonstration) that would have caught this inertness.

## Methodology

The authors implement `AdaptiveEmbeddedRK3Optimizer`: Bogacki–Shampine 3(2) embedded Runge–Kutta stages applied to the gradient flow θ̇ = −∇L(θ), with the third-order averaged gradient g₃ = (2/9)k₁ + (1/3)k₂ + (4/9)k₃ driving standard Adam moments, FSAL caching the post-step gradient as the next k₁, and a local-error step controller ϕ = min(max(0.9·err_c^(−1/3), 0.5), 2.0), h ← clip(h·ϕ, [0.1·lr, h_max]) with h_max = 2·lr by default. In the stochastic setting FSAL is disabled (the cached k₁ belongs to the previous minibatch's vector field), so each step honestly costs 4 evals; in full-batch FSAL is valid and a step costs 3 fresh evals.

The compute-matched protocol denominates the budget in gradient evaluations across all methods. Four experiments follow: (1) stochastic minibatch MNIST (784–128–10 MLP, batch 128, 4000 evals/seed); (2) full-batch on a 1024-example MNIST subset (the autonomous-vector-field regime where FSAL is valid, 600 evals/seed), with a baseline pool spanning SGD+momentum/Nesterov, Adagrad, RMSprop, Adam, AdamW, NAdam, RAdam; (3) a repaired-controller ablation adding a true accept/reject branch, error measured on the actually-applied map, and h_max = 100·h₀, with two variants (PureRK3(2) raw step, FixedRK3Adam Adam-preconditioned); (4) a pre-registered temperature sweep using pSGLD to test the tempering hypothesis. Diagnosis is supported by direct instrumentation of the step-size controller and a 36-point ρ×h_max×h₀ hyperparameter cube. All experiments are CPU-only with deterministic seeding; code, logs, and result JSONs are released.

## Results & Highlights

- **Minibatch MNIST, lr=0.001:** RK3(2)-Adam reaches 97.26±0.17% test accuracy vs Adam's 97.76±0.14% — RK loses ~0.5 pts, well outside seed noise; the ρ=0.01 and ρ=0.1 rows are bit-identical because the error controller never influences a single step (Table 1).
- **Full-batch MNIST (1024 subset), lr=0.003:** Adam final train loss 0.000103±0.000002 vs RK3(2)-Adam defaults 0.000350±0.000096 — Adam reaches ~3× lower loss at equal compute; Adam's advantage is 2.4–3.4× across learning rates (Table 2).
- **Controller inertness:** maximum normalized error over an entire run is 0.029 (3× below the growth-saturation threshold (0.9/2)³≈0.091 from Proposition 1); h is pinned at the ceiling for 100% of steps with defaults and 98.5% at h_max=8×; across the ρ×h_max×h₀ cube, tolerances ρ∈{0.01,0.1,1.0} are bit-identical to six decimals in all 12 triplets.
- **Test-accuracy wrinkle (n=10, lr=0.003):** RK3(2)-Adam defaults 89.53% vs Adam 89.03% (+0.51 pts, paired t=6.0, wins 10/10 seeds); best-loss config (h_max=8×) 89.83% (+0.80, t=6.5). But it never clears the baseline pool: RMSprop 90.02%, NAdam 89.94%, Adam lr=0.01 90.30% match or beat it at one third the per-step cost.
- **Repaired controller (full-batch):** FixedRK3Adam (h₀=10⁻³, ρ=0.01) reaches train loss 2.5×10⁻⁶±0.6×10⁻⁶ — ~40× below the best Adam (1.0×10⁻⁴) — and ρ now genuinely changes trajectories (rejections occur, 9.3/seed; ρ=0.01 vs 0.1 differ). The fixed-step control at h=0.1 gives loss 0.186, far worse than the adaptive run that ends at the same h, isolating adaptivity as the mechanism (Table 3, Table 4).
- **Tempering refuted (pSGLD sweep):** T=0 is the global optimum at 90.38%; for T≥10⁻⁷ the noise floor dominates and accuracy collapses to 79.79% at T=10⁻³; no T>0 comes within 0.6 pt of T=0 (Figure 4). A long T=0 run shows deeper minimization is generalization-neutral (90.77% at loss 1.6×10⁻³ vs 90.58% at loss 9×10⁻⁷).
- **Fragility boundary:** the repaired win is fragile to the (h₀,ρ) pairing — the tighter tolerance ρ=0.01 breaks by h₀=0.002 (reject-retry thrashing, 79–97 rejects/seed), while ρ=0.1 stays stable through h₀=0.0025 and reaches the study's lowest loss 3.8×10⁻⁷ (Table 5).

The related-work section accurately positions the result against Zhang et al. (2018) acceleration theory, the IMEX/AdamNODE practical line, and the SG-MCMC sampling literature.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** The per-gradient-evaluation accounting argument is not new in spirit (the author credits Zhang et al. 2018 and the HMC-integrator literature for per-force-evaluation accounting), but the specific contribution — instrumenting whether the embedded-pair controller in an RK-optimizer ever actually alters a step, and proving via Proposition 1 + a 36-point sweep that it does not — appears genuinely novel; the author explicitly states they are "not aware of prior work that (a) instruments whether the controller in an RK-optimizer ever alters a step, or (b) repairs it and re-runs the comparison."
- **Rigor:** High. Bit-identical trajectory comparisons across a 100× tolerance range, formal saturation proposition with proof, paired statistics on n=10 seeds for the one effect worth chasing, pre-registered hypothesis tests with automated verdicts. The honesty about limitations (Section 9.1, eight numbered caveats) is exemplary.
- **Reproducibility:** Strong on paper — deterministic CPU seeding, named scripts, released code/logs/JSONs — though the author flags real weaknesses: repaired-controller tables remain n=3; the ~40× result is full-batch only with no evidence it survives minibatch noise (where the author's own diagnosis says the error estimator is variance-dominated); per-step optimizer overhead is ignored, slightly favoring RK methods.
- **Significance:** The headline "RK-Adam loses" is appropriately tempered — the paper itself argues the durable contributions are methodological (the accounting discipline, the reporting standard, the inertness diagnostic) rather than a new optimizer. The implicit-regularization observation is explicitly called "real but not competitive."
- **Red flags / paper-specific concerns:** (1) **Scale is minimal** — all results are on MNIST with a single 784–128–10 MLP; the author acknowledges rankings may not transfer, and a single small-scale architecture is a weak base for broad optimizer claims. (2) **Baseline tuning asymmetry** — Adam received only a "modest lr grid" while RK got a "modest h₀/ρ grid"; crucially, Adam lr=0.01 (the config that beats every RK variant at 90.30%) is reported almost as an aside in Table 4 rather than as a primary comparison cell, which slightly understates how decisively plain Adam wins on the deployed metric. (3) **One design point** — only Bogacki–Shampine 3(2) is implemented; the inertness is argued to be structural for no-reject/raw-map-error relatives, but published variants (IMEX, AdamNODE) were not re-implemented, and implicit schemes have a different failure surface that is not probed. (4) The repaired-controller's ~40× win is confined to the deterministic full-batch regime — precisely the setting where the author's own diagnosis says the error estimator is not corrupted — so the positive result lives in a narrow corner while the negative result covers the practically relevant stochastic setting.

## Strengths

- Principled, clearly stated compute-matching protocol that exposes two accounting conventions (per-step comparison, FSAL under minibatch sampling) that systematically bias prior RK-optimizer comparisons.
- The inertness diagnosis is airtight and the most valuable part: formal saturation condition (Proposition 1), direct instrumentation showing h pinned at the ceiling, and a full hyperparameter cube proving no setting rescues the as-designed controller.
- Honest, self-critical framing — the paper repairs the very method it debunks, isolates the surviving mechanism with a fixed-step control, and pre-registers tests to rule out the obvious alternative explanations rather than leaving them as future work.
- Actionable minimal reporting standard for adaptive-step optimizers (accepted-h distribution, rejection rate, non-bit-identical-trajectory requirement) that the broader optimization community could adopt.
- Clean positioning relative to acceleration theory, the practical RK-optimizer line, and the SG-MCMC sampling lens.

## Limitations

- All experiments are on MNIST (full set stochastic, 1024-example subset full-batch) with a single small MLP; empirical rankings may not transfer to CNNs, transformers, or larger datasets.
- Main sweeps use n=3 seeds per cell; only the secondary test-accuracy effect is chased at n=10. Repaired-controller tables remain n=3.
- Only one RK design point (Bogacki–Shampine 3(2)) is implemented; published IMEX/implicit variants and AdamNODE were not re-implemented, and implicit schemes have a different failure surface.
- The ~40× train-loss win is full-batch, deterministic, and fragile to h₀; no evidence it survives minibatch noise, where the author's own diagnosis says the error estimator is variance-dominated — i.e., repairing the controller does not repair the estimator.
- Baseline tuning is asymmetric (modest grids on both sides); the repaired method's documented h₀ fragility means a finer sweep could move results either way.
- The implicit-regularization observation is post hoc, emerging from inspection of result tables, and the minimum-selection mechanism is identified only by exclusion (loss-surface curvature is not instrumented).

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the inertness diagnosis generalize to other embedded RK pairs (Dormand–Prince 5(4), higher-order IMEX schemes), or is it specific to Bogacki–Shampine 3(2) composed with Adam?
- Can a variance-corrected embedded error estimator make the repaired controller meaningful under minibatch noise, where the author's own diagnosis says the current estimator is sampling-noise-dominated?
- Does the repaired controller's ~40× full-batch train-loss advantage and emergent warmup-and-growth schedule survive on modern architectures (CNNs, transformers) and datasets (CIFAR, ImageNet-scale)?
- Is the implicit-regularization effect of RK gradient averaging (beating lr-matched Adam/AdamW on 10/10 seeds) a disguised form of gradient centralization / EMA, and can it be obtained more cheaply?
- Can the emergent warmup schedule the controller discovers be characterized precisely as an implicit error-control policy, and does it match hand-designed warmup heuristics?
- Does the per-gradient-evaluation accounting discipline change the conclusions of the IMEX-Adam (Bhattacharjee et al. 2024) and AdamNODE (Cho et al. 2022) results if re-run under this protocol?
