> **一句话总结：** 本文在广义 Gauss-Newton (GGN) 近似下，给出了任意宽度/深度线性网络 Hessian 特征值的闭式（或紧上界）表达，并证明在 MSE 分类任务中解的锐度 (sharpness) 正比于最大类别占比 α_max——该结论与 Cohen 等人 Edge-of-Stability 的两条经验论断相矛盾。

> **TL;DR:** Deriving closed-form/bound eigenvalues of the Hessian (via the GGN/NTK approximation) for linear networks of arbitrary width and depth, the authors show on MNIST/FashionMNIST/CIFAR10 that solution sharpness for MSE classification is governed by the maximum class proportion α_max (e.g. α_max = 0.2 fixed while varying depth and input scale), directly contradicting two empirical claims of Cohen et al.'s Edge-of-Stability work, and correcting a top-eigenvalue formula in Singh et al. (2026).

## 核心贡献

- **任意深度/宽度线性网络的 Hessian 谱刻画**：在广义 Gauss-Newton (GGN) 近似下，给出 L=2 浅网络特征值等于两层奇异值平方的成对和（定理 1），并在"强平衡"假设下推广到任意深度（定理 2），得到闭式上界，解释了 Hessian 谱"大量接近零、少数显著"的经验现象。
- **锐度对数据的依赖关系**：在 OLS 解处（定理 3），证明锐度与数据集大小 n 无关，但随深度、特征尺度与标签分布而变化；特别地，对 one-hot 标签，σ_i²(Y)/n 正是各类别占比，解的锐度随最大类别占比 α_max 增大而增大。
- **纠正先前理论错误**：指出 Singh et al. (2026) 关于浅网络最大特征值 max(∥W1∥², ∥W2∥²) 的结论是错的（源于对块对角 Hessian 的假设），正确结果应是 ∥W1∥² + ∥W2∥² 的和形式。
- **挑战 Edge-of-Stability 的两条经验论断**：与 Cohen et al. 关于"锐度随 n 增大"和"越简单的数据集锐度越低"的说法相矛盾，提供了基于数据几何的替代解释。
- **系统消融验证假设鲁棒性**：逐一去除各项假设（白化输入、平衡初始化、线性性），发现去掉白化假设理论立即失效，但用 Kaiming 初始化或 Tanh 非线性时定性结论仍成立。

## Key Contributions

- **Closed-form Hessian spectrum for linear nets of arbitrary size**: Under the GGN approximation, Theorem 1 gives the non-zero eigenvalues of shallow (L=2) linear networks as pairwise sums of squared singular values of the two weight matrices, tight under isotropic features; Theorem 2 extends this to arbitrary depth L under a strong-balance assumption (Assumption 2), yielding a clean rational-function upper bound that recovers the empirical "bulk near zero, few outliers" spectrum.
- **Sharpness as a function of data geometry (Theorem 3)**: At the ordinary-least-squares solution, the spectral norm of the NTK is λ_max = L·(∥X∥²/n)^(1/L)·(∥U_X^T Y∥²/n)^(1−1/L), showing sharpness is independent of dataset size n, increases with feature magnitude and (conditionally) with depth, and—crucially—depends on label distribution.
- **Label-imbalance → sharpness link**: For one-hot labels, {σ_i²(Y)/n} equals the empirical class proportions, so the model learns sharper solutions when one class dominates (large α_max). This is an interpretable, data-only predictor of sharpness for MSE classification.
- **Correction of prior theory**: Demonstrates (Fig. 2) that the top eigenvalue is ∥W1∥²+∥W2∥² (sum), not max(∥W1∥²,∥W2∥²) as in Singh et al. (2026, App. D.3.3), attributing the discrepancy to their block-diagonal Hessian assumption.
- **Two direct contradictions of Edge-of-Stability (Cohen et al., 2021)**: their Figure 18 (sharpness grows with n) and Caveat 2 (simpler datasets yield lower peak sharpness) are both disputed and reinterpreted via loss-landscape geometry rather than optimizer behavior.

## Methodology

The analysis proceeds in four tightly linked steps, all built on the Generalized Gauss-Newton (GGN) approximation of the Hessian, ∂²L ≈ (1/n)J^T J, which becomes exact as training loss → 0 and whose non-zero eigenvalues coincide with those of the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) matrix (1/n)J J^T.

1. **Setup & GGN/NTK reduction (Sec. 2.1)**. The Jacobian of an L-layer linear net f(x)=xW_1^T…W_L^T is factored into per-layer Kronecker blocks J_ℓ = (S_ℓ)⊗(X P_ℓ), letting the NTK be written as a sum of Kronecker products involving the left/right weight "paths" S_ℓ, P_ℓ. This converts a parameter-space Hessian of size p=Σ d_ℓ d_{ℓ−1} into a much smaller d_L·n × d_L·n NTK.
2. **Shallow theorem (Thm. 1)**. For L=2 and an SVD of X with isotropic singular values (Assumption 1: n≥d_0, full rank, Σ=Σ₁₁ I), the non-zero NTK eigenvalues are exactly (1/n)·(∥X∥²/n)·(σ_i²(W_1)+σ_j²(W_2)). This also yields Tr((1/n)JJ^T)=d_2∥W_1∥_F²+d_0∥W_2∥_F², explaining progressive-then-sharpening early-training dynamics.
3. **Deep theorem under strong balance (Thm. 2 + Cor. 1)**. Adding Assumption 2 (consecutive layers share singular values, W_ℓ W_ℓ^T = W_{ℓ+1} W_{ℓ+1})—justified because balanced layers are preserved by gradient flow and approached under weight decay / EoS training—the eigenvalues admit a closed-form rational bound; only min{d_0,…,d_L} of the p eigenvalues can be non-zero. Spectral norm is bracketed by L·(∥X∥²/n)·∥W∥^{2(L−1)} bounds, tight respectively for anisotropic (lower) and isotropic (upper) features.
4. **Data dependence at the OLS solution (Thm. 3)**. Imposing W_1^T…W_L^T = X†Y gives σ_i(W)=Σ₁₁^{−1/L}·σ_i(U_X^T Y)^{1/L}, hence λ_max = L·(∥X∥²/n)^{1/L}·(∥U_X^T Y∥²/n)^{1−1/L} ≤ L·(∥X∥²/n)^{1/L}·(∥Y∥²/n)^{1−1/L}, with equality at zero loss. This is the lever used to read off dependences on n, depth, feature scale, and—via one-hot label structure—class proportions.

**Empirical protocol (Appendix C)**: full-batch gradient descent for 10,000 epochs on n=5,000-sample subsets of MNIST/FashionMNIST/CIFAR10, with learning rate η_max halved on <0.1% loss improvement per 100 epochs. Eigenvalues are computed numerically with LOBPCG; traces with Hutchinson's method. Class imbalance is injected by taking ⌊(1−α_max)n⌋ samples from each of classes 1–9 and the remainder from class 0. Widths: 256 (2-layer), 64 (4-layer), 128 (OLS 2-layer). Ablations in Fig. 7 drop Assumption 1 (raw inputs), Assumption 2 (Kaiming-uniform init), and linearity (Tanh-MLP) one at a time.

## Results & Highlights

- **Top-eigenvalue correction (Fig. 2, MNIST/FashionMNIST/CIFAR10)**: For a 2-layer Linear-MLP (width 256, σ_x=1, η=1e−3), the proposed sum-of-squared-spectral-norms predictor ∥W_1∥²+∥W_2∥² tracks the true Hessian top eigenvalue increasingly well across training, whereas the prior Singh et al. (2026) predictor max(∥W_1∥²,∥W_2∥²) systematically underestimates it—the discrepancy is attributed to their block-diagonal Hessian assumption.
- **Top-10 eigenvalue approximation error (Fig. 1, Thm. 1)**: Percentage error in approximating the top-10 Hessian eigenvalues of a 2-layer MLP falls as training progresses on all three datasets, validating that the GGN-based bound becomes tight near convergence (qualitative; no single aggregate number reported).
- **Deep-net spectrum prediction (Fig. 4, Thm. 2)**: For a 4-layer Linear-MLP (width 64, σ_x=1, η=1e−2), predicted top-10 eigenvalues (black) overlay the numerically computed values on MNIST/FashionMNIST/CIFAR10 with increasing accuracy over training.
- **Trace ↔ parameter-norm agreement (Fig. 3, CIFAR10)**: Tr((1/n)JJ^T) (Hutchinson) closely follows the squared parameter-norm dynamics d_2∥W_1∥_F²+d_0∥W_2∥_F² for both a 2-layer (width 256) and a 4-layer (width 64) MLP, reproducing the early flattening / later sharpening pattern.
- **Sharpness independent of dataset size n (Fig. 8, vs. Cohen et al. Fig. 18)**: Under the balanced-OLS setup, λ_max stays flat as n grows (with X_ij,Y_ij=Θ_n(1) entries, spectral norms scale as Θ(√n) and cancel), contradicting the Edge-of-Stability claim that sharpness increases with n.
- **Sharpness increases with label imbalance (Fig. 6c, vs. Cohen et al. Caveat 2)**: Holding σ_x=1 and varying α_max, the spectral norm of trained 4-layer models rises monotonically with the maximum class proportion and matches the Theorem-3 prediction, contradicting the EoS caveat that "simpler" (more imbalanced) datasets yield lower peak sharpness.
- **Depth scaling (Fig. 6b)**: With α_max=0.2 fixed and ∥X∥²/n swept, sharpness increases with depth L once ∥X∥² ≤ ∥Y∥² or L ≥ 2·ln(∥Y∥²/∥X∥²), matching Theorem 3's L^{1/L}-type scaling.
- **Ablation outcomes (Fig. 7)**: Shedding whitening (raw inputs) breaks the theory—"inconsistent variations" of sharpness with α_max; shedding balance (Kaiming init) leaves qualitative trends intact; adding Tanh nonlinearity keeps results qualitatively similar, supporting extension of the label-distribution conclusion to nonlinear nets.

No quantitative results reported in the form of standard benchmark accuracy tables; all evidence is in the form of matched theoretical-vs-numerical eigenvalue curves and monotonic trends on MNIST/FashionMNIST/CIFAR10 subsets.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: Genuine and specific. The headline data-dependence result—that MSE-classification sharpness is set by the max class proportion α_max via σ_i²(Y)/n—is a clean, non-obvious link between label geometry and Hessian sharpness, and the correction of Singh et al. (2026) (sum vs. max of squared spectral norms, traced to their block-diagonal Hessian assumption) is a concrete, falsifiable theoretical fix rather than a generic restatement.
- **Rigor**: Proofs are given in full (Appendix A) and hinge on standard, clearly stated assumptions. However, the two central assumptions are strong and the authors admit it: Assumption 1 (full-rank, isotropic/whitened features, n≥d_0) is acknowledged as "rather strong, and not realistic in practice," and Assumption 2 (strong balance) only provably holds at minimizers or under specific training regimes (gradient flow, weight decay, EoS). The theory is therefore a characterization of local geometry at special solutions, not of general training trajectories.
- **Reproducibility**: Good on paper—Appendix C specifies widths (256/64/128), n=5,000 subsampling rule (⌊(1−α_max)n⌋ from classes 1–9), 10,000-epoch full-batch schedule with η-halving, σ_x=1 orthogonalization, and per-figure hyperparameters. No code release is referenced in the text, which is a gap; the LOBPCG/Hutchinson eigenvalue computations and the balanced-weight sampling procedure are described in enough detail to reimplement, but the bar for an independent reproduction is non-trivial.
- **Significance**: Moderate-to-high for the theory subcommunity. It sharpens the data-geometry view of sharpness and supplies a usable closed-form for linear/balanced nets; the direct rebuttals of two Edge-of-Stability claims (Fig. 18 and Caveat 2) are provocative and likely to spur follow-up. Significance is capped by the linear-network + MSE-only scope and the workshop format.
- **Red flags / weak baselines**: A specific concern is that all empirical "validation" is qualitative—eigenvalue curves that visually track the theory—rather than tabulated approximation errors or statistical aggregates; Figs. 1–8 report trends, not numbers, making the claim that predictions are "considerably robust in most cases" hard to calibrate. The most damaging ablation (Fig. 7a, raw non-whitened inputs) shows the theory "breaks down immediately" with "inconsistent variations," yet this is exactly the practically relevant regime—so the headline label-imbalance conclusion is, by the authors' own ablation, reliable mainly after input whitening. Missing ablations include: dependence on width scaling, behavior under cross-entropy loss (only MSE is treated), and any comparison against the modern sharpness-prediction baselines beyond the single Singh-et-al. curve in Fig. 2.

## Strengths

- Clean, interpretable closed-form/bound eigenvalue expressions that reduce a large parameter-space Hessian to a small NTK and explicitly tie sharpness to SVD quantities of X and Y.
- A memorable, actionable takeaway: for MSE classification, peak sharpness tracks the maximum class proportion α_max—an easily computed data statistic.
- Intellectual honesty about assumptions and their failure modes (Fig. 7 ablations explicitly show where the theory breaks).
- Corrects a concrete prior error (Singh et al. 2026) and challenges two claims of a highly cited Edge-of-Stability paper, which is scientifically valuable.
- Full proofs and a reproducible-feeling experimental protocol in the appendices.

## Limitations

- Restricted to **linear networks** and **MSE loss**; the Tanh-MLP ablation (Fig. 7c) is only "qualitatively similar," so extension to ReLU networks and cross-entropy is unproven.
- Core theory requires **whitened/isotropic features (Assumption 1)** and **strong layer balance (Assumption 2)**; the former fails on raw real-world inputs (authors' own Fig. 7a), limiting practical applicability without preprocessing.
- All results are about **local geometry at balanced/OLS solutions**, not about the full training trajectory; the dynamics claims (e.g., progressive flattening) are suggestive, not theorems about SGD.
- **Empirical evidence is figure-based and qualitative**—no tabulated approximation errors, confidence intervals, or accuracy metrics, making "robustness" hard to quantify.
- The contradictions with Cohen et al. are presented as conceptual disagreements but **not reconciled quantitatively**; the paper notes the EoS observations "may be better understood through geometry and optimizer behavior, which are beyond the scope of this work," leaving the tension unresolved.
- Workshop length (4 pages + appendix) limits depth; no code or data release is cited.

## Relevance

与深度学习优化理论、损失景观几何及锐度/泛化机制高度相关——本文给出的"锐度由标签占比 α_max 决定"这一闭式结论，对理解 edge-of-stability、sharpness-aware 优化器的设计以及数据不平衡对训练稳定性的影响都有直接启发，属于你应该追踪的"为什么深度网络能学得好"这一主线工作。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the α_max → sharpness relationship survive under **cross-entropy loss** and **ReLU/Transformer** architectures, where the GGN/NTK approximation and the one-hot singular-value interpretation no longer apply directly?
- Can the theory be extended to **non-whitened, anisotropic features** (the regime where Fig. 7a shows it currently fails), e.g., by keeping the full Σ in the SVD rather than collapsing it to Σ₁₁ I?
- How do the predictions interact with **stochastic, mini-batch SGD** rather than full-batch gradient descent—the regime where Edge-of-Stability phenomena are actually observed?
- Is there a quantitative reconciliation with Cohen et al.'s Fig. 18 / Caveat 2, e.g., can both hold if one distinguishes sharpness at the balanced minimizer from sharpness along the optimizer's trajectory?
- Can the label-imbalance predictor be used **diagnostically**, e.g., to set learning rates or sharpness-aware regularization as a function of measured class proportions?
- What happens to the spectrum under **weight decay or adaptive optimizers (Adam)**, which alter the balance and parameter-norm dynamics the theory builds on?
