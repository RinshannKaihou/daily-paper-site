Why this matters

Standardizes whether mechanistic interpretability can predict out-of-distribution robustness of frontier math models — directly serving AI-safety auditing and reasoning-robustness research.

Critical edge
The strongest baseline (69.23%) is a black-box uncertainty signal. Competition rules permit any black-box statistic, so winning entries could bypass mechanistic interpretability entirely — undercutting the paper's scientific framing.

01 Methodology — the task, not an algorithm

This is a competition design paper. The “method” is a protocol that turns a philosophical debate into a measurable binary prediction.

given (Problem X, Model M, shift Δ) where M(X) = y_true predict S(X, M, Δ) = 1 if M stays correct on most ΔX → robust = 0 otherwise → spurious

Data. 180 olympiad problems (AIMO 2/3, JMO, two years of AIME); 50% newly authored, never published. Symbolic reasoning chains enable unlimited functional variants. 150 train / 30 private test; cross-product with ≥8 AIMO 3 models → ~1200 train / ~240 test instances.

Seven perturbation types with demonstrated efficacy:

  • Rephrase · Rename · Domain — semantic-preserving surface edits
  • Distract · Typos — irrelevant info / formatting noise
  • Expert perturbations — answer-preserving adversarial edits
  • Expert no-solution — unanswerable variants (unsolvability detection)

Dual track. Main track (full AIMO 3 frontier models) + Small-models track (<10B) so SAE / Transcoder methods stay viable. Anti-overfitting: private test, offline Docker via Codabench, submission limits, compliance reports. Up to 10,000 H200 GPU-hours via the Fields Model Initiative.

03 Strengths

  • Sharp, falsifiable task on a genuinely open, high-stakes question.
  • Credible 14-person team spanning interpretability, evaluation & olympiad math (NII, LMU, Tübingen, Oxford, UCL).
  • Anti-overfitting by design: private test, offline Docker, submission caps, compliance reports.
  • Dual-track accommodates frontier-scale and compute-heavy mechanistic methods (SAEs, Transcoders).
  • Already-shipped artifacts: website, GitHub baselines, HuggingFace sample, Codabench — rare for a proposal.
  • Real backing: 10,000 H200 GPU-hours + access to AIMO 3 top models and private problems.

04 Limitations

  • A proposal, not a results paper — all numbers are preliminary baselines on one model.
  • Tiny held-out set: 30 problems / ~240 instances → noisy fine-grained breakdowns.
  • Table 1 is illustrative — one problem per row, no CIs, no difficulty control.
  • “Internal mechanisms” framing is undercut by allowing black-box statistics as inputs.
  • New-problem authoring is unverifiable while the dataset stays private.
  • No human baseline or ceiling — headroom above 69.23% is unknown.
  • Sponsorship partially aspirational (500 USD award “awaiting confirmation”).

05 Open questions for further exploration

iCan mechanistic methods (SAEs, transcoders, circuits) substantially beat the 69.23% black-box baseline, or does behavior already capture most of the signal?
iiAre robust/spurious labels stable across temperature & decoding, or is the “clear-cut” filter brittle to generation stochasticity?
iiiDoes robustness on one perturbation predict robustness on others — a general factor — or are they orthogonal?
ivWith only 30 private test problems, what effect size can realistically be detected — would a larger post-competition release change conclusions?
vDo models that fail Expert-no-solution (Qwen3.5 100→10%) share a common internal failure mode (e.g. missing premise-tracking circuits)?
viIs robustness a property of the problem, the model, or their interaction — how do labels transfer across families?
viiCan the symbolic-chain framework extend beyond olympiad math to code, scientific QA, or multi-step tool use?
