Full Attention Strikes Back

RTPurbo — Zhou et al. 2026 — Nanjing University & Alibaba Group

Full-attention LLMs are already intrinsically sparse. Only ~15% of query heads are "retrieval heads"; the rest need only local context. RTPurbo exploits this with a 16-dimensional low-rank projection and dynamic token selection, converting dense models to sparse inference in ~600 training steps.

Nanjing University: Yanke Zhou, Maohua Li, Yuan Yao, Xiaoxing Ma  ·  Alibaba Group: Yiduo Li, Hanlin Tang, Kan Liu, Lan Tao, Lin Qu

Why This Matters
Deploy million-token context LLMs without accuracy loss and with negligible training cost (~600 steps). The intrinsic sparsity finding means most existing dense models are already wasting compute on heads that only attend locally.
#
Method Pipeline
01
Offline Head Calibration
Insert identical "needle" span at both ends of a long document. Measure attention mass from later to earlier needle. Single sequence partitions heads into retrieval vs. local sets.
02
Adaptive Sparse Attention
Local heads: sliding window (8192) + 4 attention sinks. Retrieval heads: dense during prefill, query-aware dynamic top-p during decode. 16-dim pre-RoPE projections index tokens.
03
Two-Stage Training
Stage 1: align compressed attention distributions (~600 steps, FineWeb 32K-80K). Stage 2: end-to-end fine-tuning. Total: ~840K params across 210 retrieval heads.
04
Hardware-Aware Kernel
Single-warp CTA, no shared memory. Register-only state, 2-token unrolled inner loop with half2 vectorized instructions. KV ranges partitioned for parallel CTA processing.

Ab
Key Ablations
  • Retrieval head ratio: 15% vs. 30% negligible difference; 10% causes major drops (MMLU-PRO Math: 88.2 → 79.3)
  • Projection dimension: dim=16 and dim=32 yield identical accuracy; dim=4 has weaker fitting, requires recalling many more tokens
  • top-p vs. top-k: top-16k computes ~8K extra tokens but recovers only 3.8% more attention mass than top-p (0.9)
  • Dynamic budgets: NIAH tasks need ~469 active tokens; multi-K tasks need ~2462 — per-query adaptivity is critical
+
Strengths
  • Clean decomposition into three well-supported sparsity observations
  • Minimal training cost (~600 steps, ~840K params) — very practical to adopt
  • Dynamic top-p elegantly handles variable-density retrieval
  • Strong RULER results at extreme lengths (128K-512K) where baselines collapse
  • Hardware-aware kernel design for memory-bound sparse decoding
!
Limitations
  • Only tested on Qwen3 MoE models (30B total / 3B active) — dense model generalization unproven
  • Speedup is per-layer, not end-to-end — actual wall-clock gains will be lower
  • top-k variant shows severe drops: MMLU-Chem 88.0 → 51.4, MMLU-CS 86.1 → 50.5
  • No principled method for determining retrieval head ratio on new models
  • Indexer overhead during prefill not thoroughly analyzed
/
Critical Edge
Generalization is the biggest open question. Every result comes from two closely related Qwen3 MoE models. The 15% retrieval head ratio was manually tuned for this architecture. The reasoning drops in the top-k variant (Chemistry: 88.0 → 51.4) suggest the sparse approximation can be fragile in certain domains. The 9.36x speedup is at the attention layer level, not full-model inference. Until results appear on Llama, Mistral, or dense transformers, the scope of applicability remains uncertain.
?
Open Questions
  • How does RTPurbo perform on dense models (Llama 3, Mistral) at different scales (7B, 70B)?
  • What is the end-to-end inference speedup including indexer computation overhead?
  • Can retrieval head ratio be automatically determined via calibration score thresholds?
  • Is RTPurbo complementary to KV cache compression (group quantization)?
  • Are there hidden drops in the top-p variant similar to top-k's MMLU-Chem collapse?
  • How stable are retrieval head assignments during fine-tuning or continued pretraining?
