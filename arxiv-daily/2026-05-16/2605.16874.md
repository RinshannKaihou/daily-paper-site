> **一句话总结：** 本文发现大推理模型相比基础模型的优势高度集中在约8%的早期规划决策token上（Gini系数0.936），通过仅在这些高分歧位置让推理模型接管一个token的简单干预方案（约4%干预率即可恢复91%的Pass@8差距），即可大幅恢复甚至超越同尺寸推理模型的性能（0.6B基础模型+13%干预率超越同尺寸Thinking模型157%恢复率）。

> **TL;DR:** The reasoning advantage of large reasoning models over their base counterparts is extremely sparse and concentrated on early planning-related "decision tokens." On Qwen3-0.6B, only ~8% of generated tokens account for salient cross-model disagreement (Gini coefficient 0.936), and these tokens appear early, are 17.6x enriched in planning decisions, and coincide with high base-model uncertainty. The authors propose disagreement-guided token intervention — a simple one-token takeover scheme where the reasoning model intervenes only at high-disagreement positions. At just ~4% intervention rate, this recovers 91% of the Pass@8 gap on Qwen3-0.6B, and at ~13% intervention rate, it surpasses the same-size Thinking model (157% recovery). The paper also shows this generalizes to LLaMA pairs. Published at ICML 2026.

## 核心贡献

- 揭示了基础模型与推理模型之间的性能差距在token级别极度稀疏：Gini系数0.936，仅约8%的token贡献了主要的分布分歧，且这些token集中在生成早期（位置密度在u=0.05处达峰）
- 发现关键决策token富集了17.6倍的规划相关决策（从全局1.9%到干预集33.3%），并与基础模型的内在不确定性高度吻合（IoU验证），证明基础模型主要在早期规划点失败
- 提出了disagreement-guided token intervention方法：使用双部分门控（全局阈值+滑动窗口比率测试），仅在高分歧位置让推理模型接管一个token后立即切回基础模型
- 在六个数学推理基准上验证：0.6B基础模型以13%干预率超越同尺寸Thinking模型157%（Pass@8从36.0提升至80.0，Thinking为64.1）；1.7B基础模型以16%干预率达112%恢复率
- 提供了预算匹配基线对比：引导干预仅用4%预算即超越随机基线和仅-early基线在25%预算下的表现

## Key Contributions

- Quantifies token-level distributional disagreement between base and reasoning models using cross-entropy and reverse KL divergence, revealing extreme sparsity (Gini coefficient 0.936 across six math benchmarks)
- Shows that high-disagreement tokens are front-loaded (density peaks at normalized position u~0.05), enriched 17.6x in planning decisions (from 1.9% global to 33.3% of intervention tokens), and coincide with base-model uncertainty spikes
- Demonstrates that disagreement scores predict reasoning failures: averaging top-K per-token disagreement values yields strong AUROC for separating correct vs. incorrect samples, outperforming entropy-based prediction
- Proposes a two-part runtime gating mechanism: a global threshold (calibrated tail event) combined with a sliding-window ratio test that suppresses noisy toggling, requiring the current score to exceed the local baseline by a scale factor
- Shows generalization beyond Qwen to LLaMA pairs (LLaMA-3.1-8B base + DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B guide), where ~20% intervention recovers ~91% of the Pass@8 gap

## Methodology

The paper operates in two phases: analysis and intervention.

**Analysis phase.** The authors quantify token-level distributional disagreement between a base model (p_b) and a stronger reasoning model (p_r) using cross-entropy D_CE(t) = -sum p_r(y|x_t) log p_b(y|x_t) and reverse KL divergence. They compute these scores along base-model rollouts across six math benchmarks (MATH500, GSM8K, AMC23, OlympiadBench, AIME24, AIME25). They characterize the disagreement distribution using Lorenz curves and Gini coefficients, analyze positional density of top-1% disagreement tokens, compute IoU between disagreement-ranked and entropy-ranked token sets, and use a lightweight heuristic classifier to measure planning enrichment.

**Intervention phase.** The disagreement-guided token intervention uses a two-part gate: g_t = [s_t > tau AND s_t > lambda * s_bar_{t,W}], where tau is a global threshold calibrated as the (1-r)-quantile of disagreement scores, lambda is the tail-to-mean scale factor, and s_bar_{t,W} is a sliding-window average. When g_t=1, the reasoning model generates one token; otherwise the base model generates. The realized intervention rate rho is reported. The authors also note a cheaper alternative: gating on base-model entropy alone, which recovers most of the strong-model Pass@8 at small budgets (Appendix C.3).

**Experimental setup.** Primary analysis uses Qwen3 family: Qwen3-0.6B-Base for rollouts, Qwen3-8B (Thinking) for reasoning guidance. They also test Qwen3-1.7B and LLaMA-3.1-8B / DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B. Evaluation uses Accuracy and Pass@8 across six mathematical reasoning benchmarks.

## Results & Highlights

- **Extreme sparsity:** Average Gini coefficient of 0.936 across six benchmarks — the base-reasoning gap is driven by rare, high-magnitude deviations, not diffuse incremental drift
- **Early concentration:** Top-1% disagreement tokens cluster at normalized position u~0.05, with density rapidly decaying by u~0.2 — inconsistent with the view that reasoning gains come from long post-reasoning trajectories
- **Planning enrichment:** Planning tokens are 17.6x enriched in the intervention set (1.9% global -> 33.3% of takeover tokens); execution tokens are under-represented (0.7x)
- **Failure prediction:** Disagreement-based scores predict reasoning failures with high AUROC, consistently outperforming entropy-based prediction
- **Main results (Table 3):**
  - Qwen3-0.6B-Base at rho~0.04: 29.1/61.4 (Acc/Pass@8) vs base 13.0/36.0 — 91% recovery of Thinking model gap
  - Qwen3-0.6B-Base at rho~0.13: 52.4/80.0, surpassing Qwen3-0.6B-Thinking (43.4/64.1) — 157% recovery
  - Qwen3-1.7B-Base at rho~0.06: 43.6/73.5 — 76% recovery
  - Qwen3-1.7B-Base at rho~0.16: 62.1/83.8, matching Qwen3-1.7B-Thinking (64.1/80.3) — 112% recovery
- **Sharp nonlinearity:** Most recovery arrives within the first few percent replacement; Pass@8 jumps ~20 points from rho~0.5% to rho~3%, with diminishing returns thereafter
- **Budget-matched baselines (Table 4):** Guided intervention at rho~0.04 outperforms both random and early-only baselines at rho=0.25 — a 6x smaller budget. Position selection matters more than strong-model injection
- **Qualitative finding:** Intervention induces a "stop-and-check" planning pattern, resolving upstream ambiguities before returning control to the base model for execution
- **Sample-level analysis:** Across 400 problems, intervention flips 152 examples from incorrect to correct and only 3 in reverse

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** High. The token-level disagreement analysis is a genuinely fresh perspective on understanding why reasoning models outperform base models. The finding that only ~8% of tokens drive the gap, concentrated at early planning points, is striking and well-supported. The intervention method is elegantly simple yet effective.
- **Rigor:** Strong. The analysis uses proper statistical tools (Lorenz curves, Gini coefficients, IoU, enrichment analysis) and spans six benchmarks. The matched-budget baselines (random and early-only) are well-designed ablations that rule out trivial explanations. The "surpassing Thinking model" claim at 157% recovery is genuine and validated with sample-level analysis (152 flipped correct, only 3 flipped incorrect).
- **Reproducibility:** Good — code is publicly available, experimental setup is well-detailed, and the gating mechanism is clearly specified with equations.
- **Significance:** Potentially very high. This work challenges the common intuition that reasoning gains come from long CoT trajectories and instead identifies a sparse set of early planning decisions as the critical bottleneck. If the findings hold across more domains (beyond math), this could reshape how we think about inference-time compute allocation.
- **Red flags / concerns:**
  - The method requires running both the base model and reasoning model at every decoding step to compute disagreement scores, which the authors acknowledge as a practical limitation (Remark in Section 5.1). This means the actual compute cost may be significantly higher than the token intervention rate suggests.
  - The cheaper entropy-only gating alternative (Appendix C.3) is only briefly mentioned — its full performance profile deserves more attention since it would be the practically deployable version.
  - Results are primarily on math reasoning benchmarks; generalization to other reasoning domains (code, science, logic) is untested.
  - The cross-model setup uses a much larger reasoning model (8B) to guide a smaller base model (0.6B, 1.7B) — this is a significant asymmetry that may not reflect practical deployment scenarios.
  - The "157% recovery" headline, while accurate, could be misleading — the 0.6B Thinking model is not a very strong baseline (it was likely under-trained or suboptimal), and the 8B guidance model is substantially larger.

## Strengths

- Beautifully structured analysis that builds from observation (sparsity) to mechanism (early planning) to application (intervention)
- Strong empirical validation with matched-budget baselines that isolate the contribution of position selection
- The Gini coefficient of 0.936 is a compelling, quantitative summary of the sparsity finding
- The qualitative analysis of intervention patterns (stop-and-check behavior) provides mechanistic insight beyond accuracy numbers
- Honest discussion of practical deployment limitations (both-model requirement, compute overhead)
- Generalization demonstration beyond Qwen to LLaMA pairs

## Limitations

- Requires running both models at every step for disagreement computation, raising actual compute cost well above the intervention rate suggests; the authors themselves frame this as a "diagnostic form of sparse control rather than an optimized inference system"
- Limited to mathematical reasoning benchmarks; unclear whether the early-planning-sparsity pattern holds for code generation, scientific reasoning, or other domains
- The cross-model setup uses an 8B reasoning model to guide 0.6B/1.7B base models — a significant model size asymmetry; whether same-size or smaller guides suffice is not fully explored
- The heuristic classifier for planning vs. execution tokens, while functional, lacks validation against human annotations
- The "surpassing Thinking model" result may partially reflect the 0.6B Thinking model being a weak baseline rather than the intervention being exceptionally powerful
- No latency measurements in an actual serving scenario, making real-world efficiency claims premature

## 相关性

本文对理解推理模型在token级别的行为机制具有重要价值，特别是揭示了推理优势集中在早期规划决策点的发现，对推理时计算分配策略和模型部署效率优化具有直接启发意义。其"少量关键token决定推理轨迹"的核心洞察也可为推理模型训练策略设计提供新视角。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can the disagreement gate be approximated using only base-model entropy (as briefly noted in Appendix C.3) without significant performance loss, and what is the full performance-compute tradeoff curve?
- Does the early-planning-sparsity pattern hold in non-math domains such as code generation, multi-hop QA, or scientific reasoning?
- What happens when the guide model is the same size or smaller than the base model — does the intervention still help, or is the 8B->0.6B asymmetry essential?
- Can the "decision token" locations be predicted from the prompt alone (before generation begins), enabling pre-computed intervention plans?
- How does this approach compare to speculative decoding in terms of wall-clock latency and total FLOPs for equivalent accuracy levels?
- Could the sparsity insight be used during training — e.g., focusing RLVR training signal on planning-critical positions rather than uniformly across the trajectory?
