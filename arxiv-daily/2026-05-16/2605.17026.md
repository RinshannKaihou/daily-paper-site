> **一句话总结：** 本文通过受控实验揭示推理模型SFT后训练中pass@k覆盖率下降的根本原因是训练数据中的"决策分叉点"（forks in the road）——模型在面临多条有效推理路径时被迫选择单一路径，导致过拟合于虚假线索；并提出问题级多样性数据设计和前缀token扰动解码两种缓解策略，证明丢失的覆盖率是被抑制而非被遗忘。

> **TL;DR:** Reasoning models fine-tuned with SFT improve pass@1 but shrink pass@k. This paper pinpoints the cause: "forks in the road" decision points in training data where the model must commit to one of multiple valid reasoning paths without knowing which will succeed. Through controlled experiments on (1) graph navigation with indecipherable branching nodes and (2) math reasoning with multiple solution modes (NL vs. code, linear vs. backtracking), the authors show shrinkage is tightly correlated with decision-point prevalence. The model becomes overconfident at decision points -- assigning high probability to both correct and incorrect branches -- and learns spurious surface cues for branch selection. Problem-level diversity in training data (exposing multiple modes per problem) preserves coverage far better than dataset-level diversity. A simple inference-time prefix perturbation (sampling from Top-8 initial tokens) recovers lost coverage without retraining, proving suppressed reasoning capacity can be reactivated. Code available at https://github.com/psunlpgroup/reasoning_forks.

## 核心贡献

- 提出"决策分叉点"（forks in the road）假说：训练数据中存在模型无法区分的多条有效推理路径时，SFT会迫使模型收敛到单一路径，是覆盖率收缩的数据层面根本原因
- 设计两组受控实验验证假说：(1) 基于星图的图导航任务，通过前向/反向遍历设置有无决策点的对比；(2) 数学推理中的多模式选择（自然语言vs代码推理、线性vs回溯推理）
- 发现模型在决策点产生虚假过拟合：对正确和错误分支都赋予高置信度，依赖表面特征（如变量声明顺序、前缀token）做路径选择
- 揭示数据多样性结构的关键作用：问题级多样性（每题包含多种解法）远优于数据集级多样性（全局平衡但每题单一解法）
- 提出前缀token扰动解码：通过采样Top-8初始token恢复被抑制的覆盖率，无需重新训练，证明推理能力是被抑制而非遗忘
- 验证RLVR（GRPO）下同样存在决策点驱动的覆盖率收缩，说明该现象不限于SFT

## Key Contributions

- Identifies "forks in the road" decision points in training data as the data-centric root cause of coverage shrinkage in reasoning post-training
- Designs two controlled case studies to isolate the mechanism: (1) graph navigation with star-shaped dependency graphs and indecipherable branching nodes (forward vs. reverse traversal), and (2) mathematical reasoning with multiple solution modes (NL vs. code, linear vs. backtracking)
- Shows that SFT on data with decision points causes the model to become overconfident at branching points -- assigning high confidence to both correct and incorrect paths -- and to rely on spurious surface cues (variable order, prefix tokens) for branch selection
- Demonstrates that problem-level diversity (multiple reasoning modes per problem) dramatically preserves coverage compared to dataset-level diversity (same global ratio but single mode per problem)
- Proposes prefix token perturbation at inference time (Top-8 sampling of initial tokens) that recovers suppressed coverage without retraining, proving that lost reasoning capacity is suppressed rather than forgotten
- Validates that the same decision-point-driven shrinkage emerges under GRPO/RLVR training, confirming the phenomenon is not SFT-specific

## Methodology

The paper takes a data-centric approach to studying coverage shrinkage through controlled experiments rather than observational benchmark studies.

**Case Study 1: Graph Branching (Section 4.1).** The authors design a synthetic task based on star-shaped dependency graphs (inspired by Bachmann & Nagarajan, 2024), where variables form a central root with multiple branching paths. The model must compute a target variable at the end of one path, but at the branching point has no information about which path leads to the target (an "indecipherable node"). They compare two training regimes: Forward (with decision points -- model must choose a branch) and Reverse (without decision points -- model traces backward from the target). Models tested: Qwen-2.5-0.5B and EvoLM-1B. They also test RLVR (GRPO) on top of both settings to verify the effect extends beyond SFT.

**Case Study 2: Reasoning Mode Selection (Section 4.2).**
- *NL vs. Code reasoning (4.2.1):* Using GSM8K problems from OpenMathInstruct-1 (code solutions) and OpenMathInstruct-2 (NL solutions), they create two datasets with identical 50/50 mode ratios but different structures: data-level diversity (each problem has one mode, balanced globally) vs. problem-level diversity (each problem appears with both modes). They track pass@k across SFT epochs and analyze the model's reasoning mode preference distribution.
- *Linear vs. Backtracking reasoning (4.2.2):* They study off-the-shelf R1-distilled reasoning models (e.g., DeepSeek-R1-Distill), which naturally exhibit both linear and backtracking thinking modes. They probe the brittleness of initial tokens by systematically varying prefix tokens ("Okay", "Let", "To") and measuring the impact on reasoning behavior, response length, and accuracy across GSM8K, MATH-500, AIME24, AIME25.

**Mitigation Strategies (Section 5):**
- Data diversity design: Per-problem coverage of alternative decisions vs. distributing diversity across problems
- First-token manipulation: Uniform sampling from Top-8 candidate prefix tokens at inference time, tested on Evol-Instruct-2-5-0.5B fine-tuned on MetaMathQA subset

## Results & Highlights

- **Decision points cause coverage shrinkage:** In graph branching, the Forward setting (with decision points) shows pass@1 improving but pass@k degrading as training progresses. The Reverse setting (without decision points) maintains near-perfect pass@k across all k values throughout training.
- **Overconfidence at decision points:** The model's confidence at branching nodes increases sharply during training, but is undiscriminating -- it becomes highly confident on both correct and incorrect branches, indicating uncalibrated decision-making.
- **Spurious cue learning:** Perturbing prompt surface features (e.g., shuffling variable dependency order while preserving semantics) significantly shifts the model's branch selection, showing it relies on spurious cues rather than genuine reasoning at decision points.
- **Problem-level diversity is critical:** Despite identical overall diversity ratios, problem-level diversity (multiple modes per problem) maintains stable pass@k, while data-level diversity (single mode per problem, balanced globally) shows severe shrinkage. The model under data-level diversity develops bimodal reasoning mode preferences; under problem-level diversity, the distribution remains balanced and calibrated.
- **Initial token brittleness:** Small changes in the first token of reasoning traces (e.g., "Okay" vs. "Let" vs. "To") lead to drastically different reasoning behaviors -- response length, structure (linear vs. backtracking), and accuracy. Prefixes like "Okay" or "Alright" consistently trigger backtracking behavior with improved math performance.
- **Over-thinking and under-thinking:** Models over-think on simple factual QA (backtracking hurts) and under-think on counterfactual arithmetic (backtracking helps up to 60% improvement), showing they cannot adaptively select reasoning depth.
- **Coverage is suppressed, not forgotten:** Top-8 prefix sampling at inference time recovers lost pass@k without any retraining, demonstrating that the underlying reasoning capacity remains in the model but is locked behind collapsed decision points.
- **RLVR exhibits the same pattern:** GRPO training on forward (decision-point) data shows the same coverage shrinkage, and it is even more pronounced than in SFT. When decision points are absent (reverse setting), RL preserves coverage.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** The "forks in the road" framing is a genuine conceptual contribution. Prior work attributed shrinkage to RLVR optimization dynamics; this paper shifts the lens to data structure, which is both novel and actionable. The controlled experimental design is well-suited for isolating causal mechanisms.
- **Rigor:** The use of controlled case studies (synthetic graph task, controlled data mixtures) is a strength. The forward/reverse ablation cleanly isolates the effect of decision points. The extension to RLVR (GRPO) adds confidence that the finding is general. However, the synthetic graph task is inherently limited in complexity -- real-world reasoning involves far more nuanced and layered decision points.
- **Reproducibility:** Strong. Code and data are publicly available. The experimental setups are described in sufficient detail. The use of standard models (Qwen-2.5-0.5B, EvoLM-1B, R1-distilled models) and benchmarks (GSM8K, MATH-500, AIME) facilitates reproduction.
- **Significance:** High practical relevance. The finding that problem-level diversity is far more effective than dataset-level diversity has immediate implications for how reasoning training data should be curated. The prefix perturbation result -- that suppressed coverage can be recovered without retraining -- is practically useful and theoretically interesting.
- **Red flags:** The models used are relatively small (0.5B-1B for controlled experiments). Whether the same dynamics hold at larger scales (7B, 70B+) is not established. The prefix perturbation mitigation is a proof-of-concept on GSM8K with a 0.5B model; its effectiveness on harder benchmarks and larger models is unclear. The paper studies only two types of decision points (graph branching, reasoning mode selection); real-world reasoning may involve many other types. The "overthinking/underthinking" analysis on factual QA is interesting but feels somewhat tangential to the main thesis.

## Strengths

- Clean experimental design with proper ablations (forward vs. reverse, data-level vs. problem-level diversity)
- Compelling evidence that coverage is suppressed (recoverable) rather than forgotten
- The finding on problem-level vs. dataset-level diversity is immediately actionable for data curation
- The spurious cue analysis is a strong supporting result that deepens understanding of the mechanism
- Honest about limitations: mitigations are partial, focused on controlled settings
- Extension to RLVR confirms the phenomenon is not algorithm-specific

## Limitations

- Small model sizes (0.5B-1B) for controlled experiments; scalability to production-scale models is untested
- The prefix perturbation mitigation is demonstrated only on GSM8K with a 0.5B model -- a relatively easy benchmark
- The paper studies only two types of decision points; the taxonomy of decision points in real reasoning is likely much richer
- No quantitative metric for "decision-point density" in arbitrary datasets, which limits the practical diagnostic value
- The linear vs. backtracking reasoning analysis relies on observational study of distilled models rather than controlled training
- The paper does not propose a training-time solution (only data design guidance and inference-time intervention)

## Relevance

本文对推理模型训练中覆盖率下降提供了清晰的数据层面因果解释，揭示了多样性结构设计的关键作用，对关注后训练数据工程和推理模型可靠性评估的研究者具有直接参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the fork-in-the-road framework scale to larger models (7B, 70B+)? Do the same dynamics of overconfidence at decision points emerge?
- Can a quantitative "decision-point density" metric be developed to predict shrinkage severity for arbitrary training datasets before fine-tuning?
- How do other types of decision points (e.g., tool use selection, decomposition strategy choice, verification checkpoint placement) contribute to coverage shrinkage?
- Is there a training-time regularization loss that can prevent the overconfidence collapse at decision points, rather than relying on inference-time prefix perturbation?
- How does the interaction between decision-point structure and curriculum ordering affect shrinkage dynamics?
- Can the problem-level diversity principle be automated in data synthesis pipelines for reasoning training?
