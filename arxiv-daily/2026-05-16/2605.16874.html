Reasoning Can Be Restored by Correcting a Few Decision Tokens

Shen, Sheng, Chen, Zhang, Wang USTC & NUS 2605.16874 2026-05-16

The performance advantage of reasoning models over base models is extremely sparse: only ~8% of generated tokens account for the critical distributional gap (Gini = 0.936). These tokens cluster at early planning positions and are 17.6x enriched in planning decisions. Intervening on just ~4% of tokens recovers 91% of the Pass@8 gap; at 13%, a 0.6B base model surpasses the same-size Thinking model.

Why This Matters
A 0.6B base model with 13% guided intervention surpasses the same-size Thinking model (Pass@8: 80.0 vs 64.1). Reasoning gains come from few early planning decisions, not long CoT trajectories — reshaping how we allocate inference-time compute.
Critical Edge
Both models must run at every decoding step to compute disagreement scores. Actual compute cost far exceeds the reported intervention rate. The cross-model setup uses an 8B guide for 0.6B/1.7B bases — a significant asymmetry. Results are limited to math reasoning; generalization is untested.

Method: Disagreement-Guided Intervention

Step 1 · Score
Compute token-level disagreement DCE(t) between base pb and reasoning pr at each position.
Step 2 · Gate
Two-part gate: gt = [st > τ AND st > λ·s̄t,W]. Global threshold + sliding-window ratio test.
Step 3 · Intervene
When gate opens, reasoning model generates one token; base model resumes immediately. Introduces a "stop-and-check" pattern.
✓

Strengths

  • Gini 0.936 is a compelling quantitative summary of sparsity — one of the cleanest characterizations of the base-reasoning gap
  • Matched-budget baselines (random, early-only) isolate the contribution of position selection — 6x less budget needed than naive alternatives
  • Sample-level analysis: 152 examples flipped correct, only 3 flipped incorrect — the improvement is asymmetric and genuine
  • Generalizes beyond Qwen to LLaMA pairs (LLaMA-3.1-8B + DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B)
⚠

Limitations & Open Questions

  • Dual-model compute overhead: both models run at every step to compute disagreement — the authors frame this as "diagnostic sparse control, not optimized inference"
  • Math-only evaluation: no evidence that early-planning-sparsity holds for code, science, or logic domains
  • 8B → 0.6B asymmetry: the guide model is 13x larger than the base; whether same-size guides suffice is untested
  • Weak Thinking baseline: the 0.6B Thinking model may be under-trained, inflating the 157% recovery headline
  • No latency measurements in a real serving scenario
  • Can base-model entropy alone approximate the disagreement gate without significant performance loss?
  • Does the early-planning-sparsity pattern hold in non-math domains (code, multi-hop QA)?
  • What happens when the guide is same-size or smaller than the base model?
  • Can decision token locations be predicted from the prompt alone before generation?
  • Could sparsity insights focus RLVR training signal on planning-critical positions?
★

Key Findings at a Glance

  • Extreme sparsity: 8% of tokens drive the entire base-reasoning distributional gap
  • Early concentration: high-disagreement tokens peak at u ~ 0.05, inconsistent with long-CoT narratives
  • Sharp nonlinearity: most recovery arrives in the first few percent replacement; Pass@8 jumps ~20 points from ρ ~ 0.5% to 3%
  • Budget efficiency: guided intervention at 4% budget outperforms random/early baselines at 25% budget
  • Behavioral shift: intervention induces "stop-and-check" planning, resolving ambiguities upstream
  • Failure prediction: disagreement scores predict reasoning failures with high AUROC, outperforming entropy
