> **一句话总结：** 本文从傅里叶视角分析神经网络的学习动态，发现网络先学习幅值信息（像素对相关性）再学习相位信息（边缘和高阶相关性），并严格证明在各向同性输入下相位学习需要至少 $N^3 \log^2 N$ 步 SGD，而自然图像的幂律谱可将相位学习速度提升至多项式加速。

> **TL;DR:** This paper analyzes neural network learning dynamics through a Fourier lens, decomposing input features into amplitude (pair-wise pixel correlations) and phase (edges, higher-order correlations) components. The authors prove that for isotropic high-dimensional inputs, online SGD needs $n \gg N^3 \log^2 N$ steps to learn from phase alone -- an inherently hard task -- but power-law spectra (as in natural images) dramatically accelerate phase learning via a polynomial speedup, even when the spectra carry no class information. Experiments on CIFAR10, CIFAR100, ImageNet, and synthetic textures confirm that deep CNNs exhibit the same amplitude-before-phase learning order.

## 核心贡献

- 从傅里叶视角重新审视神经网络的简单性偏差，将自然图像的平移不变性和幂律谱特性纳入统一分析框架
- 实验发现神经网络在图像分类任务中先依赖幅值信息（二阶像素相关性），再利用相位信息（边缘和高阶相关性），且该现象在浅层和深层网络中均成立
- 提出了基于循环矩阵的合成数据模型（cyclic data model），可对平移不变输入的幅值和相位进行精确独立控制，同时保持理论可解性
- 严格证明了相位学习的样本复杂度上下界：各向同性输入下需要 $n \gg N^3 \log^2 N$ 步 SGD，而下界为 $n \gg N^3$
- 揭示了幂律谱可提供多项式级加速使相位信息更快被学习，即使谱本身不包含分类信息，这解释了为什么自然图像能被高效学习
- 在 CIFAR10/100、ImageNet 和纹理数据集上验证了幅值-相位学习顺序，证明该现象跨越不同网络深度和数据复杂度

## Key Contributions

- Introduces a Fourier perspective on neural network simplicity bias that naturally incorporates two key properties of natural images: translation-invariance and power-law spectra
- Demonstrates experimentally that both shallow and deep networks learn amplitude information before phase information across CIFAR10, CIFAR100, ImageNet, and synthetic texture datasets
- Proposes a cyclic synthetic data model based on circulant matrices that enables independent control of amplitude and phase while remaining theoretically tractable
- Proves tight sample complexity bounds for phase learning on isotropic inputs: upper bound of $n \gg N^3 \log^2 N$ and lower bound of $n \gg N^3$ SGD steps
- Shows that power-law spectra yield a polynomial speedup for phase learning even when spectra carry no label information, providing a mechanistic explanation for efficient learning on natural images
- Validates the amplitude-before-phase hypothesis on deep convolutional architectures (ResNet) trained on real-world datasets

## Methodology

The paper develops a comprehensive framework combining theoretical analysis and experiments:

**Fourier decomposition framework.** Any input image $x$ can be decomposed via discrete Fourier transform into amplitude $|\hat{x}_k|$ and phase $\phi_k$ components at each frequency $k$. Amplitudes encode pair-wise pixel correlations (second-order statistics), while phases encode edges, corners, and higher-order correlations. The key insight is that neural networks exhibit a learning order that mirrors the computational complexity hierarchy of these Fourier features.

**Cyclic data model.** The authors introduce a tractable synthetic data model for translation-invariant inputs. Inputs are parameterized as $x = F^{-1} \text{diag}(a) \Sigma \xi$, where $F$ is the DFT matrix, $a$ is the amplitude vector, $\Sigma$ is a diagonal phase matrix, and $\xi$ is a random phase vector. This construction ensures translation-invariance (circulant structure) while allowing independent control of amplitude spectrum and phase structure. Labels are determined purely by phase information, making amplitude information uninformative for classification.

**Two-layer network analysis.** The theoretical analysis focuses on a two-layer network $f(x) = \sum_{i=1}^P a_i \sigma(w_i^\top x)$ trained with online SGD. The key technical tool is the "stochastic process method": the SGD dynamics of order parameters (weights overlap with signal, norm) can be mapped to a system of stochastic differential equations driven by the data distribution. The authors track how the overlap between weights and the phase-encoded signal evolves over training.

**Phase complexity lower bound (Theorem 1).** Using an information-theoretic argument, the authors prove that for isotropic inputs (flat spectrum) with label information encoded only in phases, online SGD cannot learn anything useful within $n \ll N^3$ steps. The proof exploits the fact that the gradient signal from phase correlations is of order $O(1/N^{3/2})$ per sample, requiring cubic scaling in dimension to accumulate signal above noise.

**Phase complexity upper bound (Theorem 2).** The authors prove that with $n \gg N^3 \log^2 N$ samples, online SGD on a two-layer network with sufficient width ($P \geq 2$) can achieve non-trivial classification accuracy. The proof tracks the evolution of weight-signal overlaps through the online SGD dynamics and shows the signal accumulates constructively.

**Power-law acceleration (Theorem 3).** When the amplitude spectrum follows a power law $a_k \propto k^{-\alpha}$ with $\alpha > 1/2$, the sample complexity for phase learning drops to $n \gg N^{3-2\alpha} \log^2 N$. For $\alpha = 1$ (as in natural images), this gives $n \gg N \log^2 N$ -- a dramatic polynomial speedup. The mechanism is that power-law spectra concentrate energy in low frequencies, creating a structured scaffold that helps SGD navigate the loss landscape toward phase features.

**Experimental protocol.** The authors design experiments on four fronts: (1) two-layer networks on the cyclic model, (2) two-layer networks on realistic texture images from the Describable Textures Dataset (DTD), (3) ResNet-18 on CIFAR10/CIFAR100, and (4) ResNet-18 on ImageNet. They measure the "phase test accuracy" and "amplitude test accuracy" by swapping or randomizing the corresponding Fourier components of test images.

## Results & Highlights

**Amplitude-before-phase learning on CIFAR10/100 and ImageNet (Section 2).** On CIFAR10 with ResNet-18, amplitude test accuracy (measured on images with randomized phases) reaches ~80% within the first few epochs, while phase test accuracy (measured on images with randomized amplitudes) starts near chance and only begins improving after ~10 epochs, eventually reaching ~65%. The same pattern holds on CIFAR100 and ImageNet, though the gap narrows with increasing data complexity.

**Phase learning is hard on isotropic inputs (Section 3-4).** On the cyclic model with flat (isotropic) spectra, phase-based classification accuracy remains at chance level for $n \ll N^3$ samples. This confirms the theoretical lower bound: SGD literally cannot distinguish phase-structured inputs from pure noise until a cubic number of samples have been processed.

**Power-law spectra provide polynomial speedup (Section 5).** On the cyclic model with power-law spectra ($\alpha = 1$), phase learning begins after only $n \sim N$ samples -- matching the $N^{3-2\alpha} = N$ prediction. The speedup is observed both in the synthetic model and in texture experiments: when textures have natural power-law spectra, phase features are learned dramatically faster compared to whitened (isotropic) versions of the same textures.

**Mechanistic explanation: amplitude scaffolding.** The key finding is that power-law amplitudes do not help classification directly (amplitude test accuracy is at chance when labels depend on phases). Instead, the structured amplitude spectrum acts as a "scaffold" that biases the SGD trajectory toward regions of weight space where phase features can be extracted efficiently. This is a non-trivial interaction: the spectrum of the data distribution fundamentally shapes the optimization landscape even when it carries no label information.

**Texture experiments bridge theory and practice.** On the DTD texture dataset with two-layer networks, unwhitened textures (natural power-law spectra) show clear amplitude-before-phase learning, while whitened textures (flat spectra) show much slower phase learning. This directly confirms the theoretical prediction that power-law spectra accelerate phase learning.

**Appendix contributions.** The appendix provides full proofs of all three theorems, additional experiments on the effect of network width, learning rate, and power-law exponent $\alpha$, plus detailed derivations of the order parameter equations for the online SGD dynamics.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** High. The Fourier decomposition of simplicity bias into amplitude/phase learning is novel and goes beyond prior work that focused on quasi-isotropic or spherical inputs. The insight that power-law spectra accelerate learning of features they do not directly encode is genuinely surprising and non-trivial. The cyclic data model is a clean contribution that will likely be adopted by others.

- **Rigor:** Strong. All three main theorems have formal statements and complete proofs. The lower bound (Theorem 1) uses information-theoretic arguments; the upper bound (Theorem 2) carefully tracks SGD dynamics; the power-law speedup (Theorem 3) derives from the structure of the order parameter equations. The gap between lower ($N^3$) and upper ($N^3 \log^2 N$) bounds is only polylogarithmic, which is tight by theoretical standards.

- **Reproducibility:** Good. The cyclic data model is fully specified and should be straightforward to implement. Experiments on CIFAR/ImageNet use standard architectures (ResNet-18) and training procedures. However, the phase/amplitude accuracy metrics require careful implementation of Fourier swapping, and some experimental details (specific hyperparameters for texture experiments) could be more explicitly stated.

- **Significance:** High for the learning theory community. The paper provides a principled explanation for why natural images are "easy" for neural networks to learn despite the apparent curse of dimensionality. The power-law scaffolding mechanism has implications for understanding data efficiency and may inform data augmentation and curriculum design.

- **Red flags / concerns:**
  - The two-layer network assumption is a significant limitation. The authors show experiments on ResNets to address this, but the theory does not formally cover deep networks. Whether the amplitude-before-phase order persists in transformers or very deep networks remains an open question.
  - The cyclic data model assumes exact translation-invariance, which real images only satisfy approximately. The sensitivity of the results to violations of this assumption is not analyzed.
  - The online SGD analysis assumes fresh samples at each step, which differs from the epoch-based training used in practice. The connection between online and finite-sample SGD is mentioned but not rigorously established.
  - The phase test accuracy metric (randomize amplitudes, keep phases) may not perfectly isolate "phase information" because the Fourier transform is linear -- zeroing amplitudes changes the scale of the signal, which could interact with activation functions in unexpected ways.

## Strengths

- Elegant theoretical framework that connects Fourier analysis, statistical physics, and deep learning practice
- Both lower and upper bounds on sample complexity with near-matching dependence on dimension
- The power-law acceleration result is a genuine and surprising insight with practical implications
- Extensive experimental validation spanning synthetic data, textures, CIFAR, and ImageNet
- Clear exposition of the cyclic data model that will serve as a useful tool for future work
- The "amplitude scaffolding" mechanism provides a concrete, testable hypothesis about how data structure shapes learning dynamics

## Limitations

- Theoretical results are limited to two-layer networks with online SGD; extending to deep networks or mini-batch SGD with momentum is non-trivial
- The cyclic data model assumes exact translation-invariance, which is an idealization of natural image statistics
- The analysis is asymptotic (large $N$ limit); finite-size corrections are not characterized
- The phase accuracy metric used in experiments may conflate multiple effects when applied to non-linear networks
- The power-law exponent for natural images ($\alpha \approx 1$) is treated as fixed; the sensitivity of the speedup to the exact value of $\alpha$ could be explored further
- No comparison with alternative theoretical frameworks (e.g., neural tangent kernel, mean-field theory) that might predict similar or different learning orders

## Relevance

本文从傅里叶视角为神经网络的学习动态提供了严格的理论分析，揭示了幂律谱如何作为"脚手架"加速高阶特征的学习，这对于理解深度学习为何能高效处理自然图像具有根本性意义，也为数据增强和课程学习的设计提供了频率域的新视角。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the amplitude-before-phase learning order persist in vision transformers (ViTs), where the inductive bias for frequency-based features is weaker than in CNNs?
- Can the power-law scaffolding insight be exploited to design better data augmentation strategies (e.g., frequency-domain augmentations that preserve the scaffolding effect)?
- How does the cyclic data model extend to color images (multi-channel inputs) and what new interactions between channels emerge in the Fourier domain?
- Does pre-training on large datasets change the amplitude/phase learning dynamics during fine-tuning, or does the network retain the scaffolding from pre-training?
- Can the polynomial speedup from power-law spectra be combined with curriculum learning strategies for provably faster training?
- What happens to the phase learning complexity when the amplitude spectrum is adversarially chosen rather than following a natural power law?
