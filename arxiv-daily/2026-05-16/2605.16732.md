> **一句话总结：** DiRotQ提出了一种基于PCA子空间分解的旋转感知量化框架，通过将DiT激活旋转到PCA基并将高方差分量保留为16-bit、低方差分量量化到4-bit，在PixArt-Σ上W4A4设置下FID达到15.9（vs. SVDQuant的18.9），PSNR达19.1 dB（vs. 17.6），并在12B FLUX.1-dev上实现2.1倍内存压缩和2.3倍推理加速。

> **TL;DR:** DiRotQ is a W4A4 post-training quantization framework for Diffusion Transformers that rotates activations into a PCA-derived basis, preserves the top 10% high-variance components in FP16, and quantizes the remaining 90% to 4-bit after an additional random orthogonal rotation for variance equalization. The inverse rotation is fused into weights offline at zero cost. On PixArt-Sigma (MJHQ-30K), it achieves FID 15.9 and PSNR 19.1 dB vs. SVDQuant's 18.9 / 17.6 under identical W4A4 settings. A Triton kernel delivers 2.1x memory reduction and 2.3x speedup on FLUX.1-dev (12B) on a 24GB RTX 4090. It also introduces VLM-as-a-Judge for diffusion quantization evaluation.

## 核心贡献

- 提出DiRotQ，一种面向Diffusion Transformer的W4A4后训练量化框架，通过PCA将激活分解为高方差子空间（~10%通道，保留FP16）和低方差子空间（~90%通道，量化到4-bit）
- 对低方差残差子空间施加额外的随机正交旋转以均衡通道间方差，使max/median比值从83x降至2x，显著提升4-bit量化精度
- 逆旋转离线融合到权重矩阵中（因自适应LayerNorm等非线性层阻止向前融合），正向旋转仅在推理时在线执行，无需额外推理开销
- 结合GPTQ权重量化，在PixArt-Σ/MJHQ-30K上FID 15.9、PSNR 19.1 dB，显著优于SVDQuant（FID 18.9、PSNR 17.6），LPIPS 0.236 vs. 0.319
- 首次引入VLM-as-a-Judge评估协议（使用InternVL2-8B和Qwen2-VL-7B），从语义一致性和感知质量两个维度评估量化后生成质量
- 实现Triton自定义旋转内核，在24GB RTX 4090上将FLUX.1-dev内存降低2.1倍，推理加速2.3倍（batch=1），batch=2时加速9.2倍（避免CPU卸载）

## Key Contributions

- Proposes DiRotQ, a W4A4 PTQ framework that applies PCA-based rotation to decompose DiT activations into a high-variance subspace (~10% channels, kept in FP16) and a low-variance subspace (~90% channels, quantized to 4-bit)
- Applies an additional random orthogonal rotation to the residual low-variance subspace for variance equalization across channels, reducing max/median ratio from 83x to 2x
- Fuses the inverse rotation into weights offline (forward rotation must remain online due to AdaLN/softmax/GELU nonlinearities), enabling rotation-based W4A4 quantization at zero additional weight cost
- Achieves FID 15.9 and PSNR 19.1 dB on PixArt-Sigma (MJHQ-30K), substantially outperforming SVDQuant (FID 18.9, PSNR 17.6, LPIPS 0.319 vs. 0.236) under identical INT W4A4
- Introduces VLM-as-a-Judge evaluation protocol for diffusion model quantization using InternVL2-8B and Qwen2-VL-7B-Instruct, assessing Semantic Consistency and Perceptual Quality on a 1-10 scale
- Implements Triton-based rotation kernels achieving 2.1x memory reduction and 2.3x speedup on FLUX.1-dev (12B) at batch=1, and 9.2x speedup at batch=2 on a 24GB RTX 4090

## Methodology

DiRotQ addresses W4A4 quantization for Diffusion Transformers through rotation-aware activation quantization. The key insight is that raw DiT activations exhibit severe timestep-dependent and channel-wise outliers (max/median = 83x), making naive 4-bit quantization highly error-prone.

**Step 1: PCA-Based Activation Rotation.** For a linear layer Y = XW, PCA is applied to calibration activations (128 COCO samples, aggregated across all denoising timesteps) to compute the eigendecomposition of the second-moment matrix. The PCA basis U = [U_h | U_l] decomposes activations into high-variance components (top-k eigenvectors, k = r*m where r = 10%) and low-variance residuals.

**Step 2: Mixed-Precision Subspace.** The top-k components X*U_h are preserved in 16-bit precision. The residual components X*U_l are quantized to 4-bit. This isolates outlier energy into a small subspace.

**Step 3: Random Orthogonal Rotation of Residuals.** Within the low-variance residual subspace, a random orthogonal matrix R is applied to further equalize channel variance (max/median ratio drops from ~16x to ~2x), improving shared quantization scale efficiency. R is sampled once per distinct residual dimension using a fixed seed and reused across all layers.

**Step 4: Offline Weight Fusion.** The combined rotation V = [U_h | U_l*R] is applied: the forward rotation X*V runs online (cannot be fused due to AdaLN/softmax/GELU nonlinearities), while the inverse rotation V^T is fused into weights offline (W_h = U_h^T*W, W_l = (U_l*R)^T*W), adding zero inference overhead for the weight side.

**Step 5: GPTQ Weight Quantization.** Both weight branches are quantized using GPTQ (damping factor 0.01, block size 128).

**Key Design Choice:** The PCA basis U is computed per linear layer from time-averaged calibration activations and shared across timesteps. This is necessary because per-timestep bases would multiply weight memory by the number of denoising steps. Timestep-dependent magnitude shifts are absorbed by per-token activation scales and the high-precision tail.

## Results & Highlights

**Main Comparison (Table 1, PixArt-Sigma on MJHQ-30K, INT W4A4):**
- DiRotQ: FID 15.9, IR 0.941, LPIPS 0.236, PSNR 19.1
- SVDQuant: FID 18.9, IR 0.871, LPIPS 0.319, PSNR 17.6
- FP16 baseline: FID 16.6, IR 0.952
- DiRotQ at W4A4 actually achieves lower FID than FP16 (15.9 vs 16.6)

**Cross-Model Results (Table 1, INT W4A4):**
- FLUX.1-dev (MJHQ-30K): DiRotQ FID 8.0, IR 0.934 vs. SVDQuant FID 8.1, IR 0.928; DiRotQ LPIPS 0.084, PSNR 24.6 vs. SVDQuant LPIPS 0.113, PSNR 22.8
- SANA-1.6B (MJHQ-30K): DiRotQ FID 9.4, IR 0.937 vs. SVDQuant FID 10.8, IR 0.916; DiRotQ LPIPS 0.119, PSNR 22.5 vs. SVDQuant LPIPS 0.199, PSNR 18.9

**NVFP4 Results:** Consistent improvements across all models. On SANA, SSIM jumps from 0.744 (SVDQuant) to 0.846 (DiRotQ).

**Activation QSNR:** DiRotQ outperforms both RTN and SVDQuant by 5-10 dB across all 28 transformer blocks and 5 denoising timesteps, with the largest gains (>20 dB) in early cross-attention query projection blocks.

**Memory & Speedup (FLUX.1-dev, RTX 4090):**
- 2.1x memory reduction vs BF16 (nearly matches INT4 weight-only footprint)
- 2.3x per-step speedup at batch=1
- 9.2x speedup at batch=2 (BF16 requires CPU offloading; DiRotQ fits entirely in GPU)

**VLM-as-a-Judge (INT4):** InternVL2-8B prefers DiRotQ on 48.1% vs. SVDQuant on 39.9% of prompts (Overall score). Under NVFP4 the gap narrows as both approach FP16 quality.

**Ablation (Table 2, PixArt-Sigma, MJHQ-30K, INT W4A4):**
- Baseline RTN: FID 40.3, LPIPS 0.603, PSNR 13.0
- +PCA only: FID 19.7, LPIPS 0.452, PSNR 14.6
- +PCA +Rotation: FID 19.2, LPIPS 0.438, PSNR 14.8
- +PCA +Rotation +GPTQ: FID 15.9, LPIPS 0.236, PSNR 19.1 (full DiRotQ)
- The synergistic effect of GPTQ with rotation is key to closing the gap to FP16.

**Tail Fraction Ablation:** QSNR saturates at r=10%; even r=5% surpasses RTN by 10-20 dB. Beyond 10%, gains are <0.5 dB per additional 5%.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** The core idea (PCA-based rotation + random orthogonal rotation + offline fusion) is a principled combination of known techniques applied to a new domain. The contribution over ResQ (which uses PCA rotations for LLMs) is the timestep-aware design: a single time-averaged PCA basis per layer shared across timesteps, with magnitude shifts absorbed by per-token scales and a small FP16 tail. This is a meaningful engineering contribution rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic insight.
- **Rigor:** Strong evaluation across 3 models (PixArt-Sigma 600M, FLUX.1-dev 12B, SANA-1.6B), 2 datasets (MJHQ-30K, sDCI), 4 baselines (PTQ4DiT, Q-DiT, ViDiT-Q, SVDQuant), multiple metrics (FID, IR, LPIPS, PSNR, CLIP Score, CLIP IQA, SSIM, VLM-as-a-Judge). Ablations are thorough: component ablation, tail fraction sweep, per-layer-type QSNR analysis. However, PTQ4DiT could not be evaluated on FLUX.1-dev (>60 hours calibration on A100), slightly weakening the comparison on the largest model.
- **Reproducibility:** Good. Uses GPTQ (Apache 2.0), Nunchaku inference engine (Apache 2.0), Triton kernels, and standard PCA. 128 COCO calibration samples. Code is publicly available. All generation settings (seeds, CFG scales, step counts) are specified.
- **Significance:** The results are genuinely strong. DiRotQ at W4A4 achieves FID 15.9 on PixArt-Sigma, which is actually lower (better) than the FP16 baseline of 16.6. On FLUX.1-dev, the LPIPS improvement from 0.113 to 0.084 is substantial. The 9.2x speedup at batch=2 (eliminating CPU offloading) is a practical win. This could become a standard component in 4-bit DiT pipelines.
- **Red Flags / Concerns:**
  - The FID being *lower* than FP16 (15.9 vs 16.6) is suspicious -- it could indicate overfitting to the evaluation protocol rather than genuine improvement, though the PSNR/LPIPS gains corroborate real quality improvements.
  - VLM-as-a-Judge uses open-source models (Qwen2-VL-7B, InternVL2-8B) rather than GPT-4o as in VIEScore. The correlation with human judgment is not validated for these specific judges in the quantization setting.
  - The paper does not compare against rotation-based methods like QuaRot or SpinQuant adapted for DiTs, focusing instead on SVDQuant as the primary W4A4 baseline.
  - Memory overhead of storing FP16 rotation matrices and high-precision activation branches is not explicitly quantified in absolute terms (only relative to total model memory).

## Strengths

- Substantial and consistent improvements over SVDQuant across all models, datasets, and metrics (FID, PSNR, LPIPS, IR, SSIM, CLIP IQA)
- Comprehensive evaluation: 3 DiT architectures, 2 datasets, 4+ baselines, 7+ metrics, plus VLM-as-a-Judge
- Clean mathematical formulation with offline weight fusion ensuring zero extra inference overhead for the inverse rotation
- Thorough ablation study demonstrating each component's contribution and the synergistic effect of GPTQ with rotation
- Practical systems contribution: real speedup and memory numbers on consumer hardware (RTX 4090), with the 9.2x batch=2 result being particularly impactful
- Handles the key challenge of DiT quantization: joint timestep-, token-, and channel-wise variation through a single time-averaged PCA basis
- Tail fraction ablation shows robustness: performance saturates at r=10%, avoiding the need for per-layer tuning

## Limitations

- The forward rotation X*V must remain online (cannot be fused past AdaLN/softmax/GELU), adding some compute overhead at inference time; the paper does not explicitly quantify this forward rotation cost
- PCA basis is shared across timesteps to enable offline weight fusion; per-timestep-group rotations could further improve quality but are left to future work
- Not evaluated on video generation models, where temporal dependencies across frames introduce additional challenges
- The comparison with PTQ4DiT on FLUX.1-dev is missing (prohibitively expensive calibration), leaving a gap in the baseline coverage for the largest model
- VLM-as-a-Judge evaluation is limited to PixArt-Sigma (not FLUX.1-dev or SANA), and the open-source judges' correlation with human preference in this specific setting is not validated
- The method requires storing rotation matrices per layer and high-precision activation branches, which adds memory beyond pure INT4 weight-only quantization

## Relevance

该论文与扩散模型高效推理高度相关，特别是W4A4量化技术在大型DiT模型（PixArt-Sigma、FLUX、SANA）上的应用。对于关注模型压缩、推理加速和图像生成质量权衡的研究者具有重要参考价值，尤其是其中PCA子空间分解与离线旋转融合的设计思路可推广至其他生成模型的低比特量化场景。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- What is the computational overhead of the online forward rotation X*V during inference, and does it scale with activation dimension or sequence length?
- Can per-timestep-group rotations (e.g., clustering timesteps by activation statistics) improve quality further without negating memory savings?
- How does DiRotQ perform on video DiTs where temporal coherence across frames introduces correlated activation outliers?
- Could learned rotations (as in SpinQuant) outperform the data-independent random R for the residual subspace?
- What is the memory breakdown of the FP16 rotation matrices and high-precision branches relative to the 4-bit weight savings?
- Does the FID being lower than FP16 (15.9 vs 16.6) hold across other evaluation protocols (e.g., different inception features, larger sample sizes)?
- How does calibration set size and composition affect PCA basis quality -- is 128 COCO samples sufficient for all model scales?
