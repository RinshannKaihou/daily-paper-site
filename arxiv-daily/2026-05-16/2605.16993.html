Adversarial Fragility & Language Vulnerability in Clinical AI

Anthonio Oladimeji Gabriel Ahmad Rufai Yusuf clinical-ai adversarial-robustness cross-lingual-NLP low-resource-healthcare
Headline claim: Clinical AI diagnosis collapses under imperceptible image perturbations and cross-lingual drift. A DenseNet121 chest X-ray model drops from 89.3% to 62.0% accuracy with just 2.1% pixel change, while African-language models paradoxically degrade on African input -- revealing systemic safety gaps for low-resource healthcare deployment.
Key Metric
89.3→62.0%
DenseNet121 accuracy under FGM perturbation (epsilon = 0.021).
Normal-class detection: 78% → 42% (near random for a 3-class task).
Why This Matters
Standard defenses make things worse (Gaussian smoothing: 34.7%). African-context LLMs collapse on Pidgin English. Epsilon = 0.021 falls within routine clinical imaging noise -- this is not a hypothetical attack, it is a deployment hazard.
Critical Edge
Only 20 cross-lingual cases, single attack method (FGM), single architecture (DenseNet121), no confidence intervals. The NatLAS paradox is striking but unexplained -- no ablation isolates cause. Statistical robustness is weak.

Strengths

  • First dual-audit combining adversarial image fragility and cross-lingual diagnostic drift for clinical AI in low-resource settings
  • Per-class analysis reveals Normal-class vulnerability as the most clinically dangerous failure mode
  • Gaussian smoothing counter-productivity is a practically important negative result
  • Perturbation visualizations confirm adversarial noise concentrates in anatomically informative regions
  • Public code, fixed seeds, and consumer-hardware reproducibility
  • Clear, actionable policy proposals: mandatory adversarial robustness maps and cross-lingual drift profiles

Limitations

  • Only 20 cross-lingual clinical cases -- statistically underpowered and vulnerable to selection bias
  • Only FGM tested; stronger attacks (PGD, AutoAttack, C&W) not evaluated
  • Single imaging architecture (DenseNet121) -- no ConvNeXt, ViT, or EfficientNet comparison
  • Fine-tuned on only 600 images (200/class) -- may inflate adversarial vulnerability
  • No confidence intervals or statistical significance tests reported
  • Only two defenses tested; adversarial training and diffusion purification not evaluated
  • NatLAS paradox unexplained -- no ablation for cause isolation
  • No proposed solutions to the identified vulnerabilities
  • No comparison with human clinician performance under similar conditions

Open Questions

  • Would stronger attacks (PGD, AutoAttack) shift the clinically-relevant epsilon threshold?
  • Can adversarial training or certified defenses provide protection without sacrificing clean accuracy?
  • Why does NatLAS degrade more than Llama on Pidgin -- training data gap, model capacity, or prompt formatting?
  • Does fine-tuning on more data (beyond 600 images) improve robustness, or is the vulnerability architectural?
  • How do human clinicians perform on the same cases in different language registers?
