> **一句话总结：** 本文提出一种基于架构级功耗轨迹（architectural power traces）的早期PDN优化方法，通过HotSniper仿真器捕获微架构组件的时序功耗并映射为tile级空间功耗密度分布，再根据高/中/低功耗等级自适应分配PDN网格密度，在4核和16核系统上分别实现最高32.94%和32.57%的PDN金属面积缩减，同时满足IR drop和电迁移约束。

> **TL;DR:** This paper presents a workload-aware early-stage PDN optimization methodology that uses HotSniper architectural simulations to capture per-component temporal power traces, maps them to spatial power density distributions across the chip floorplan, classifies tiles into High/Medium/Low power categories, and adaptively constructs PDN grid density accordingly. Evaluated on 4-core and 16-core processors with PARSEC and SPLASH-2 benchmarks at 1GHz/2GHz, it achieves up to 32.94% PDN metal area reduction while satisfying all IR drop and electromigration constraints. Accepted at SMACD 2026.

## 核心贡献

- 提出了一种完整的基于架构级功耗轨迹的早期PDN优化工作流，从HotSniper仿真获取微架构组件功耗数据，到空间映射、tile分类，再到自适应PDN网格构建。
- 设计了三级功耗分类机制（High/Medium/Low），通过阈值参数 T_H=0.5 和 T_M=0.25 将tile按归一化功耗分类，分别分配全密度、k-th稀疏和2k-th稀疏的PDN网格线。
- 在4核和16核多处理器配置上，使用PARSEC和SPLASH-2基准测试套件进行验证，覆盖不同频率（1GHz/2GHz）和温度条件。
- 平均功耗模型下4核系统最高减32.94%、16核系统最高减32.57%的PDN金属面积；峰值功耗模型下分别减29.05%和30.31%，所有配置均满足IR drop和电迁移约束。

## Key Contributions

- Proposes an end-to-end workload-aware early-stage PDN optimization flow: from HotSniper architectural simulation for microarchitectural component-level power traces, through spatial mapping and tile classification, to adaptive PDN grid construction.
- Designs a three-tier power classification mechanism (High/Medium/Low) using normalized tile power with user-defined thresholds (T_H=0.5, T_M=0.25), allocating full-density, k-th sparse, and 2k-th sparse PDN grid wires respectively.
- Conducts evaluation on 4-core and 16-core multicore configurations using PARSEC and SPLASH-2 benchmark suites at 1GHz and 2GHz under varying temperature conditions.
- Achieves up to 32.94% (4-core) and 32.57% (16-core) PDN metal area reduction with average power modeling; 29.05% and 30.31% with peak power modeling, all while satisfying IR drop and electromigration constraints.

## Methodology

The methodology comprises four main stages:

1. **Architectural Power Trace Extraction (Section III-A):** Uses the HotSniper simulation framework (a Sniper-based many-core toolchain with integrated power and thermal estimation) to execute representative workloads and collect temporal power traces at microarchitectural component granularity. Components monitored include IALUs, FPUs, ROB, branch predictors, instruction caches, and other structures. Average power per component is computed as P_bar_k = (1/T) * sum(P_k(t)). For average-power experiments, temporally averaged traces are used; for peak-power experiments, maximum observed per-tile power is used.

2. **Spatial Power Density Mapping (Section III-B):** Maps architectural components onto the chip floorplan. The chip is partitioned into a set of tiles T. For each tile i, total power is the sum of component powers in that tile (Eq. 6), power density is P_i/A_i (Eq. 7), and current demand is I_i = P_i/V_dd (Eq. 8).

3. **Subcore Power Classification (Section III-C):** Normalizes each tile's power relative to the maximum tile power (p_i = P_i/P_max) and classifies tiles into three categories using thresholds T_H and T_M: High (p_i >= T_H), Medium (T_M <= p_i < T_H), Low (0 < p_i < T_M). Thresholds are user-tunable for design objectives.

4. **Workload-Aware PDN Grid Construction (Section III-D):** A uniform skeleton grid of candidate wire locations is defined. PDN wires are instantiated adaptively: all grid lines for High-power tiles, every k-th line for Medium-power tiles, and every 2k-th line for Low-power tiles. Current demand is distributed uniformly across grid nodes within each tile (I_{i,j} = I_i / N_i). Power pads are modeled as ideal voltage sources at boundary locations.

## Results & Highlights

- **PDN metal area reduction:** 4-core system achieves up to **32.94%** reduction (average power) and **29.05%** (peak power); 16-core system achieves up to **32.57%** (average) and **30.31%** (peak).
- Consistent ~30-33% area savings across all evaluated workloads and operating conditions.
- All configurations satisfy **IR drop and electromigration constraints** with zero violations.
- Average power model provides ~3.9% additional savings over peak power model, demonstrating the value of workload-specific temporal averaging.
- Evaluated at both **1GHz and 2GHz** operating frequencies, across different temperature conditions.
- Benchmarks: PARSEC (4-core) and SPLASH-2 (16-core) multithreaded workloads at maximum parallelism.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (Moderate):** The core idea of using workload-driven power traces for PDN optimization is sensible but builds incrementally on prior work in workload-aware power analysis and system-level PDN generation. The three-tier classification scheme (High/Medium/Low) with fixed thresholds is straightforward. The main novelty is the end-to-end integration from HotSniper traces to PDN grid construction.
- **Rigor (Moderate):** The paper evaluates two core counts (4 and 16) with established benchmark suites, which is reasonable for a conference paper. However, the experimental setup lacks detail on technology node, metal layer stack, floorplan specifics, and exact PDN grid dimensions. The paper does not provide absolute area numbers, only percentage reductions, making it hard to assess practical significance.
- **Reproducibility (Moderate):** HotSniper is publicly available, and the threshold parameters (T_H=0.5, T_M=0.25) are specified. However, floorplan details, grid resolution parameters, and the PDN analysis flow are not fully described. No code or artifact release is mentioned.
- **Significance (Moderate):** The problem is industrially relevant, but the 30-33% savings are against a worst-case uniform baseline that modern industrial flows have already moved beyond. The paper does not compare against more sophisticated baselines (e.g., existing adaptive PDN methods or ML-driven approaches cited in the introduction).
- **Red flags:**
  - No comparison against any alternative PDN optimization method -- only against a naive uniform worst-case baseline.
  - The fixed three-tier classification is coarse and may miss fine-grained spatial variations.
  - No discussion of how the method handles dynamic workload switching, multiple concurrent workloads, or worst-case transient events.
  - The thresholds (T_H=0.5, T_M=0.25) appear somewhat arbitrary with no sensitivity analysis.

## Strengths

- Addresses a genuinely important and practical problem in chip design: PDN over-provisioning.
- Provides a complete end-to-end methodology from simulation to PDN construction.
- Uses well-established simulation tools (HotSniper) and benchmark suites (PARSEC, SPLASH-2).
- Clear mathematical formulation with well-defined equations.
- Demonstrates consistent results across multiple configurations and operating conditions.
- All designs pass IR drop and EM checks, proving feasibility.

## Limitations

- Only compares against a naive uniform worst-case baseline; no comparison with existing adaptive or ML-based PDN optimization methods.
- The three-tier classification is coarse and threshold-dependent; no sensitivity analysis on T_H and T_M is provided.
- Does not address dynamic scenarios: what happens when workloads change at runtime, or when multiple diverse workloads run concurrently?
- Missing practical details: technology node, metal stack, absolute PDN area numbers, wire widths, and routing layer assignments.
- Only evaluated on two core counts (4 and 16) -- scalability to larger many-core designs (64+, 128+) is untested.
- The paper is a short conference paper (4 pages), which limits depth of analysis and ablation studies.

## Relevance

本文关注多处理器系统PDN的早期优化设计，属于计算机体系结构（cs.AR）和EDA交叉领域。对于关注芯片功耗管理、物理设计自动化和系统级设计方法学的研究者有参考价值，但与ML/AI核心研究方向关联较弱，主要适合硬件设计和EDA工具链研究方向的读者。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How sensitive are the results to the choice of T_H and T_M thresholds? Is there an optimal threshold selection strategy?
- How does the method perform against more sophisticated baselines (e.g., gradient-based wire sizing, ML-driven PDN synthesis)?
- Can the approach handle dynamic workload scenarios with runtime workload switching or mixed workloads?
- What is the scalability to larger many-core designs (64, 128, 256 cores)?
- How do the power trace extraction overhead and PDN analysis time compare to traditional flows?
- Could the three-tier classification be replaced with a continuous/gradient-based allocation for finer granularity?
