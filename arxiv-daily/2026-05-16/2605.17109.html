DynMuon: A Dynamic Spectral Shaping View of Muon

The Spectral Shaping Family

Muon's polar decomposition is just p = 0 in the family Dt(p) = Ut Σtp Vt⊤. Positive p emphasizes high-curvature directions (fast early progress); mildly negative p recovers signal from flat directions (fine late-stage refinement). DynMuon schedules p across training.

Key Metric
10.6–26.5%
Fewer training steps to reach the same target loss vs. Muon, across GPT-style (86M–688M) and Qwen-style architectures on FineWeb.
Why This Matters
A zero-overhead drop-in modification to Muon that directly translates to substantial compute savings for LLM training at scale.
Critical Edge
Only validated up to 688M parameters. Scaling to 7B+ is unverified, and the p-schedule hyperparameters may need task-specific tuning.
!
Core Insight

Residual signal migrates from high-curvature to low-curvature directions as training progresses. The residual-shift metric Πt turns negative after ~500 steps, and the flat-mode signal-to-noise advantage Ωt stays positive throughout—meaning flat directions still carry useful signal that a negative p can exploit.

Methodology

  • Spectral shaping parameterization — Reframes Muon's update as one member of the family UΣpV⊤ where p = −1 is inverse-spectrum, p = 0 is standard Muon, p = 1 recovers SGD-style updates.
  • Noise-aware local model — Derives mode-wise residual signal evolution via a locally linear gradient approximation and one-sided Kronecker-factored Hessian. Shows p controls the signal contraction rate through hi(p+1)/2 and noise amplification through hi(p-1)/2 for each curvature mode hi.
  • Empirical model validation — Validates predictions using a GPT-style model (768-dim, 12 layers) on FineWeb: estimates curvature via Hessian-vector products, noise from mini-batch variance, and residual signal from probe gradients. Metrics Πt and Ωt confirm signal migrates to flat modes.
  • Efficient computation — Extends Newton-Schulz iteration to approximate UΣpV⊤ for arbitrary p, avoiding full SVD. Per-step cost unchanged vs. Muon.

Results

Finding Detail
Step reduction vs. Muon 10.6–26.5% fewer steps to target loss
Model scales tested GPT-style 86M, 194M, 388M, 688M; Qwen-style various scales
Early positive p effect p = 1 for first 500 steps, then p = 0: lower validation loss than starting at p = 0
Late negative p effect Switching to p ∈ {−0.1, −0.25, −0.5, −0.75, −1} at step 500: p ≈ −0.1 optimal; overly negative values degrade from noise
Ablation: doubled LR Simply doubling early learning rate performs worse than Muon—benefit is not from larger updates
Ablation: reversed schedule Negative-to-positive schedule performs worse than Muon—direction matters
Per-step cost Unchanged (extended Newton-Schulz, no full SVD)

Strengths

  • Principled theory-to-algorithm arc: noise-aware model predictions are validated before the algorithm is proposed
  • The dynamic scheduling insight (positive early, mildly negative late) is novel and well-supported by metrics Πt and Ωt
  • Ablations cleanly isolate spectral shaping as the source of improvement, not increased learning rates
  • Zero per-step overhead via extended Newton-Schulz iteration
  • Consistent gains across architectures (GPT and Qwen) and multiple scales
  • Opens a new design axis (spectral exponent scheduling) for optimizer research

Limitations

  • Only tested up to 688M params; scaling to 7B+ or 70B+ is unverified
  • p-schedule introduces new hyperparameters (pstart, pend, transition point, schedule shape) with limited sensitivity analysis
  • Wall-clock overhead of extended Newton-Schulz iterations not reported
  • Primary comparison is vs. Muon only; broader baselines (AdamW, Shampoo) not emphasized
  • Simplifying assumptions (locally linear gradient, K-FAC-style Hessian) may not hold in all regimes
  • No code release mentioned as of publication

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does a single universal p schedule generalize across datasets, architectures, and training budgets, or is task-specific tuning required?
  2. What is the wall-clock speedup (not just step reduction) accounting for extended Newton-Schulz iterations?
  3. How does DynMuon interact with distributed training (tensor/pipeline parallelism) where gradient synchronization may interact differently with spectral shaping?
  4. Can the noise-aware model predict optimal schedules for anisotropic or mode-guided spectral transformations?
  5. Does the dynamic spectral shaping insight transfer to other matrix-aware optimizers like Shampoo or K-FAC?
  6. Do the signal-noise dynamics observed at 86M–688M persist at 7B+ and 70B+ scale?
  7. Could p be scheduled adaptively based on online estimates of Πt or Ωt rather than a pre-specified curve?
