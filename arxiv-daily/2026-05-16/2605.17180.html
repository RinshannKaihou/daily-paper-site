Methodology

1. Riemannian Framework

The projection head hφ induces a pullback metric G(z) = JhT ∇2L Jh on the backbone representation manifold. The effective Hessian decomposes into a PSD Gauss-Newton term and a curvature interaction term. Linear heads yield constant Jacobians (Mahalanobis metric); nonlinear heads produce state-dependent Jacobians enabling local metric adaptation.

2. Collapse Instability

At collapse (fθ(x) ≈ c), the effective Hessian Heff = G + M is analyzed. For nonlinear heads, M is a random symmetric matrix that almost surely has negative eigenvalues in the null space of G, making collapse a strict saddle. For linear/ReLU, ∇2h = 0 so no escape direction exists under continuous gradient flow.

3. Information-Invariance Trade-off

Enforcing augmentation invariance forces G(z) singular on augmentation tangent directions Vaug. Fisher information rank drops by dim(Vaug) after projection, formalizing the "guillotine effect" -- the head must be discarded to preserve backbone semantics.

Results
Finding Detail
Collapse escape dynamics Swish heads show a clear negative eigenvalue spike (λmin < 0) when variance increases from pseudo-collapsed init. Condition number spike: Spearman ρs = 0.609. ReLU heads show no such transitions.
ReLU gap Under continuous gradient flow without BatchNorm, ReLU/linear heads stay trapped (λmin > 0 always). Only discrete-time noise + BatchNorm enable escape. Swish/GELU escape natively.
Orbit compression (CIFAR-10) 21.85x mean orbit spread reduction. Intra-orbit shrinks 4.78x vs inter-class 3.89x, yielding 1.22x class/orbit separation improvement.
Information hierarchy Backbone linear probe 52.27% vs head 37.55% (−14.72%). Nonlinearity gap widens from 3.19% (backbone) to 6.01% (head), indicating entanglement.
ViT-Tiny amplification 4015x orbit compression, 2.59x curvature ratio -- but all heads trapped near collapse in 20 epochs.
Depth ablation Linear heads (L=1) create upstream rank bottleneck; deeper heads (L=2,3) isolate the metric singularity.
Foundation models DINO heads: geometric compressors (implicit whitening). VICReg/Barlow Twins: dimensional expanders (explicit whitening). All serve as geometric buffers.
Timescale separation Without BatchNorm: head updates 56–2544x larger relative to weights. BatchNorm suppresses to <3x, disrupting natural separation.
Strengths
  • Remarkably comprehensive single-author work with rigorous proofs (29 pages with appendices) and complementary empirical validation
  • First mechanistic explanation connecting projection head curvature to collapse avoidance, resolving a standing theoretical puzzle in non-contrastive SSL
  • The ReLU gap result has immediate practical implications and explains the empirical shift to GELU/Swish in modern frameworks
  • Honest about limitations: explicitly discusses the ViT stiffness issue and BatchNorm's disruption of timescale separation
  • The orbit compression and information hierarchy framework elegantly formalizes the "guillotine effect"
  • Universal across contrastive (SimCLR) and non-contrastive (SimSiam) methods, and across CNN/ViT architectures
Limitations
  • Experimental scale limited to CIFAR-10/100; no ImageNet-scale validation of Hessian tracking or collapse escape dynamics
  • ViT results partially contradict theoretical predictions (all heads trapped in 20 epochs), suggesting the theory is necessary but not sufficient
  • Timescale separation assumption (Assumption 7) appears violated when BatchNorm is used, which is standard practice
  • No explicit convergence rate bounds or predictions for optimal hyperparameters
  • No comparison of downstream task performance between smooth and piecewise-linear activations at scale
  • Framework assumes continuous augmentation parameters; discrete augmentations (e.g. horizontal flip) not covered
Questions for Further Exploration
  1. Can the theory predict optimal projection head depth as a function of backbone dimensionality d, loss intrinsic rank r, and augmentation tangent space dimension m?
  2. How does the framework extend to multi-modal SSL (e.g. CLIP), where the projection head maps between different modality spaces?
  3. Is it possible to design invertible or variance-preserving losses that enforce invariance without inducing the metric singularity, eliminating the need for a projection head?
  4. What is the quantitative relationship between head capacity (depth × width) and approximation error ε?
  5. How do geometric predictions change with LayerNorm or GroupNorm instead of BatchNorm in the projection head?
  6. Can Hessian tracking be scaled to ImageNet via sketching or randomized methods?
