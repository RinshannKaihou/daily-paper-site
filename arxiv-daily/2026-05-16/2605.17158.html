SPARK: A Near-Cache ILP Accelerator That Redefines the Bottleneck

By repurposing the CPU's own L1 SRAM cache into a compute substrate, SPARK proves that ILP's real enemy is data movement — not compute throughput. GPU generational upgrades barely move the needle.

Critical Edge
Not Apples-to-Apples
SPARK's Jacobi+B&B vs. Gurobi's full solver (simplex + presolving + cutting planes). Speedup conflates hardware acceleration with algorithmic choice. Fairer test: same algorithm on both platforms.

4 Four-Engine Architecture

SPARK augments a 16-bank 8T SRAM L1 cache (256x256 per bank) with four tightly-coupled near-memory engines, adding only 0.37mm² of logic.

FC
Fetch & Control. 32-bit counter detects sparsity. Classifies constraints, gates unused engines.
SA
Sparsity-Aware. MAC + subtraction + division. Exploits geometric structure of sparse constraints.
SLE
System of Linear Equations. In-memory dot-product via shift-and-add. Jacobi iterative solver.
B&B
Branch & Bound. Reuses SLE hardware. Near-memory queues for sparse constraints. +30% energy efficiency.

Cache Sensitivity

Workloads fitting in L1 benefit most. Halving L1 hurts large instances (NS, ST, BL) but prefetching recovers ~40% of the loss. Doubling throughput yields 2.4x for non-fitting workloads. L3 shows no sensitivity.

3C The 3C Criterion for ILP

ILP acceleration requires simultaneously satisfying three characteristics. Prior accelerators hit at most two:

C1 — Dot-product intensive SLE engine In-memory shift-and-add at 16 cols/bank
C2 — Sparse constraints SA engine Geometric interpretation of constraint planes
C3 — Control flow (L1 norm, B&B) FC + B&B Hardware sparsity detect + reuse-aware B&B
Prior art coverage ≤ 2 of 3 TPUs, Sparse-TPUs, Bison-e, CGRAs

⚙ Key Design Choices

  • Regularizing-divider — subtraction approximates division at 0.5ns / 0.15pJ, 0.2% avg error with 64B LUT correction
  • Reuse-aware B&B — shares SLE PIM compute, +30% energy efficiency, +20% compute density
  • SPARSE_DETECT — hardware counter classifies cardinality vs. general constraints, auto-gates engines
  • RISC-V ISA extensions — VMMAC, VSUB, VDIV, VSLC, VL1N, VBB custom vector instructions
  • 8T SRAM fill-path integration — near-memory logic on non-timing-critical fill datapath, 0.005mm²/core mux overhead

⚠ Critical Assessment

Strengths

  • Full-stack design: algorithm → microarchitecture → ISA → evaluation
  • Elegant hardware reuse (B&B shares SLE, regularizing-divider)
  • Thorough evaluation: 4 workload types, breakdown, cache sensitivity, A100
  • Validates the data-movement-bottleneck thesis with GPU generational data
  • Gem5-validated baseline simulator (±0.2%)

Limitations

  • Algorithm-level confound: Jacobi+B&B vs. Gurobi's full solver stack
  • Only 7 MIPLIB instances; selection may favor SPARK
  • 45nm synthesis vs. 7nm commercial chips (node-unfair energy comparison)
  • 1.4% area excludes 8T SRAM upgrade cost from 6T
  • Simulation-only; no FPGA or silicon. PIM timing not externally validated
The core question this paper raises but does not answer: How much of the 12-20x speedup is from near-memory hardware vs. from choosing Jacobi iteration over simplex? Running the same Jacobi+B&B solver on CPU/GPU without SPARK would isolate the hardware contribution.
