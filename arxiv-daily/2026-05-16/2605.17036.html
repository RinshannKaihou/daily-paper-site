Under identical demand paths across 30 runs, minor retailer volatility becomes extreme factory variance. Order variability decomposes into demand bullwhip (external) + decision bullwhip (agent's own stochastic policy). Repeated sampling (majority voting) fails to fix this.

Methodology
1
MIT Beer Game — 4-echelon serial supply chain (retailer → wholesaler → distributor → factory), deterministic 2-2-2 lead times, 20 weeks. Holding $0.50, backorder $1.00 per case/week.
2
Human baseline — 100+ Georgia Tech students across 12 cohorts (2024–2025). Average team cost: $3,207.
3
Models tested — Reasoning (GPT-5 mini, Llama 4 Maverick 17B, Qwen-3 4B) and non-reasoning (GPT-4o mini, GPT-4.1 mini, Llama 3.3 70B, Llama 4.3 70B). 30 repeated runs per config.
4
Bullwhip analysis — Formal Definition 1: cross-echelon amplification (σ²k,t > σ²k−1,t) and intertemporal accumulation (σ²k,t+1 > σ²k,t). Transfer-function decomposition in Appendix B.
5
GRPO post-training — Demand curriculum: Poisson(λ) with λ ~ U(5,20) and Truncated Normal(μ,σ²; [0,50]). System-level reward: −Csys. KL penalty constrains divergence from frozen reference model.
  • "Agent bullwhip" is a genuine conceptual contribution with formal mathematical definition and empirical backing
  • Comprehensive experimental design: multiple model families, four levers tested independently and in combination, 30-run reliability analysis
  • CTDE paradigm elegantly applied: shared backbone learns coordination, each agent acts on local info at deployment
  • Honest treatment of reliability risks alongside the headline 67% cost reduction
  • Theoretical appendix with transfer-function decomposition provides analytical grounding
  • Beer Game is a pedagogical simplification — no stochastic lead times, multi-product interactions, network topologies, or disruptions
  • No comparison to classical supply chain optimization (base-stock, MPC) — only vs. first-time students
  • GRPO demonstrated only on Qwen-3 4B; generalization to larger models untested
  • Computational cost of GRPO post-training not analyzed
  • Demand curriculum limited to Poisson and truncated normal; adversarial/seasonal demand not tested
  • Conclusion overreaches: "new paradigm for supply chain management" based on a 4-echelon game
