Workload-Aware Early-Stage PDN Optimization via Architectural Power Traces

Adapts PDN grid density to real workload power profiles instead of worst-case uniform provisioning. Uses HotSniper architectural simulation to extract per-component power traces, maps them spatially across a chip floorplan, classifies tiles into three power tiers, and constructs an adaptive PDN grid — reclaiming up to a third of PDN metal while passing every IR drop and electromigration check.

Key Finding
33%
PDN metal area reduction by adapting grid density to workload-derived power maps instead of uniform worst-case provisioning.

Why This Matters
Reclaim a third of PDN routing resources without violating signal integrity.
Directly frees metal layers for signal routing in area-constrained chip designs.
Critical Edge
No comparison against any adaptive baseline.
Savings measured only against a naive uniform grid; fixed 3-tier thresholds lack sensitivity analysis.

Tile Classification

Stage 4
Adaptive PDN Grid
Full-density wires for High; k-th sparse for Medium; 2k-th sparse for Low tiles

✓
Strengths
  • Addresses a genuinely important problem: PDN over-provisioning wastes scarce metal routing resources.
  • Complete end-to-end flow from architectural simulation to PDN grid construction.
  • Uses well-established tools: HotSniper simulation, PARSEC/SPLASH-2 benchmarks.
  • Clear mathematical formulation with well-defined equations at each pipeline stage.
  • 100% pass rate on IR drop and EM constraints across all configurations.
⚠
Limitations & Open Questions
  • No comparison against existing adaptive or ML-based PDN methods — only a naive uniform baseline.
  • Three-tier classification with fixed thresholds (TH=0.5, TM=0.25) — no sensitivity analysis provided.
  • Does not address dynamic workload switching or multiple concurrent workloads.
  • Missing practical details: technology node, metal stack, absolute area numbers, wire widths.
  • Evaluated only on 4-core and 16-core; scalability to 64+ cores is untested.
Key gap: The 30-33% savings are measured against a worst-case uniform baseline that modern industrial flows have already moved beyond. Without comparison to state-of-the-art adaptive methods, practical significance is unclear.
