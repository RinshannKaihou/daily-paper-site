> **一句话总结：** 本文发现在RLVR训练中，约16-30%的困难样本即使存在正确rollout也无法被学会，根本原因是模型对这些样本的表征存在缺陷（梯度相似度低、推理质量差），且现有优化、采样、数据增强和课程学习等方法均无法修复，只有预训练阶段的mid-training能有效改善梯度对齐。

> **TL;DR:** This paper reveals that 16-30% of hard examples in RLVR training remain unlearnable even when correct rollouts are present. Through systematic experiments across three models (Qwen2.5-0.5B, Qwen2.5-3B, Llama3.2-3B), the authors rule out optimization-side explanations (rollout scarcity, gradient clipping/interference) and demonstrate via cross-example gradient analysis that unlearnable examples are gradient outliers with low-quality, ungeneralizable reasoning traces. Data augmentation and curriculum learning fail to fix this, while mid-training on 20B tokens effectively improves gradient similarity -- suggesting the solution lies in pre-RL representation quality, not in RL tricks.

## 核心贡献

- **揭示并定义RLVR中的不可学习现象：** 在GRPO训练中，将初始成功率低于10%的困难样本分为可学习与不可学习两类，不可学习样本即使在整个训练过程中持续获得正确rollout也无法提升性能
- **系统性排除三大常见假设：** 通过受控实验证明正样本稀缺、梯度裁剪/KL惩罚、以及正确/错误rollout间的梯度干扰都无法解释不可学习性
- **发现不可学习样本是梯度空间中的离群点：** 不可学习样本与训练集其余样本的梯度相似度显著低于可学习和简单样本，且这种差异在训练全过程中持续存在
- **揭示"虚假推理"问题：** 即使最终答案正确，模型在不可学习样本上产生的中间推理步骤质量极低（包含矛盾和错误），说明模型在利用不可泛化的捷径而非真正推理
- **证明数据增强和课程学习均无效：** 生成相似问题和子问题的数据增强无法改善梯度相似度；先训练简单样本的课程学习也无法提升不可学习样本的推理质量
- **发现mid-training是可行解法：** 在RL前通过20B token的mid-training可以显著提升困难样本的梯度相似度，表明表征质量需要在预训练阶段解决

## Key Contributions

- **Define and characterize the unlearnability phenomenon:** Hard examples (initial pass@1 < 10%) are split into learnable and unlearnable groups; unlearnable examples receive correct rollouts throughout training but show no improvement at validation convergence (pass@1 < 0.1 with N=32 samples, tau=0.1 threshold)
- **Systematically rule out three optimization-side hypotheses:** (1) positive rollout scarcity -- controlling k_pos=1 and even SFT with correct responses fails; (2) gradient regularization from clipping/KL penalty -- ablating these has no effect; (3) gradient interference between correct/incorrect rollouts -- cosine similarity analysis shows no interference
- **Identify unlearnable examples as gradient outliers:** Cross-example gradient analysis shows unlearnable examples have substantially lower gradient similarity (cosine similarity) with the rest of the training data compared to learnable and easy examples, persisting throughout training
- **Expose "fake reasoning" on unlearnable examples:** GPT-5-mini scoring reveals that correct rollouts on unlearnable examples contain incoherent intermediate reasoning with contradictions and errors, indicating the model exploits ungeneralizable shortcuts rather than genuine reasoning
- **Show data augmentation fails:** Generating semantically similar problems and subproblems via GPT-5 produces augmented data with good learnability, but fails to transfer to original unlearnable examples; gradient similarity between augmented and original examples is also low
- **Demonstrate mid-training as a remedy:** OctoThinker models mid-trained from Llama-3B on 20B tokens show consistently higher gradient similarity for difficult examples, suggesting representation quality must be addressed before the RL stage

## Methodology

The authors study RLVR training using GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization) with dynamic sampling as the baseline algorithm. The experimental setup spans three model families:

- **Qwen2.5-0.5B** trained on MATH Easy (difficulty levels 1-4)
- **Llama3.2-3B-Instruct** trained on MATH Hard (difficulty levels 3-5)
- **Qwen2.5-3B** trained on DeepScaleR (40k math problems)

**Definition of unlearnability:** Examples with initial success rate < 10% are "hard." Among hard examples that observe correct rollouts during training, those with pass@1 < 0.1 (estimated via N=32 samples) under the final converged policy are "unlearnable." Three independent GRPO runs are conducted and intersections are taken to reduce noise.

**Analysis pipeline:**
1. **Hypothesis testing (Section 4):** Test three common explanations -- positive rollout scarcity (controlling k_pos, SFT with larger model responses, rollout group size 64), gradient regularization (ablating clipping and KL penalty), and gradient interference (cosine similarity between correct/incorrect rollout gradients)
2. **Gradient analysis (Section 5.1):** Compute per-example gradients from positive rollouts and measure cross-example cosine similarity
3. **Reasoning quality analysis (Section 5.2):** Use GPT-5-mini to score reasoning traces on a 0-5 scale
4. **Data augmentation (Section 5.3):** Generate similar problems and subproblems via GPT-5, validate with Gemini-2.5-pro
5. **Mid-training analysis (Section 5.4):** Compare Llama-3.2-3B-Base vs. OctoThinker-3B variants (mid-trained on 20B tokens)

## Results & Highlights

**Prevalence of unlearnable examples (Table 1):**
| Model | Unlearnable (%) | Learnable (%) | No Pos. Reward (%) |
|-------|----------------|---------------|-------------------|
| Qwen2.5-0.5B | 30.2 | 25.6 | 23.5 |
| Llama3.2-3B-Instruct | 21.9 | 31.6 | 37.7 |
| Qwen2.5-3B | 16.7 | 14.2 | 47.2 |

After excluding examples with zero positive reward, roughly half of hard examples are unlearnable across all settings.

**Key findings:**
- Controlling positive rollout count (k_pos=1) does not close the gap between unlearnable and learnable examples
- SFT with correct responses from Qwen2.5-7B: unlearnable examples remain resistant even under direct supervision
- GRPO with rollout group size k=64 on unlearnable-only data: no meaningful improvement despite massive rollout budget
- Ablating gradient clipping and KL penalty: no effect on unlearnable examples
- Gradient interference hypothesis rejected: correct and incorrect rollout gradients remain aligned (not opposing) for unlearnable examples
- Unlearnable examples show consistently lower gradient similarity across all training steps
- Reasoning quality (GPT-5-mini scores 0-5): unlearnable examples score lowest, and the gap widens during training rather than closing
- Data augmentation with similar problems: augmented data is itself learnable, but fails to transfer to original unlearnable examples
- Curriculum learning: training on easy/learnable data first does not improve reasoning quality on unlearnable examples
- Mid-training on 20B tokens: OctoThinker models show substantially higher gradient similarity than Llama-3.2-3B-Base
- Qwen models have more aligned gradients (cosine sim > 0.7 for easy examples) vs. Llama (< 0.6), which may explain Qwen's suitability for RL training

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: High.** The unlearnability phenomenon is genuinely counterintuitive -- one would expect that correct rollouts + reward signal = learning. The finding that this fails for a substantial fraction of data challenges core assumptions of RLVR training. The gradient analysis connecting representation quality to learnability is insightful.

- **Rigor: Strong.** The paper follows a principled hypothesis-testing structure, systematically ruling out explanations before arriving at the representation-deficiency conclusion. Using three independent runs and taking intersections for the unlearnable group is a sound methodological choice. The analysis spans multiple models and datasets.

- **Reproducibility: Good.** Public code, data, clear training details (learning rates, batch sizes, rollout group sizes), and specific thresholds (tau=0.1, N=32) are provided.

- **Significance: High.** This has practical implications for RLVR training pipelines -- it suggests that data curation and pre-training quality matter more than RL tricks for handling hard examples. The mid-training finding is actionable.

- **Red flags and concerns:**
  - The unlearnability definition (pass@1 < 0.1 with N=32) is somewhat arbitrary, though the authors acknowledge this and use intersection across 3 runs to mitigate noise
  - All experiments are on math reasoning -- generalization to code, logic, or agentic tasks is not tested
  - The models tested are relatively small (0.5B-3B); whether this phenomenon persists at 7B, 14B, or 70B+ scales is unknown
  - The reasoning quality analysis relies on GPT-5-mini scoring (0-5 scale), which introduces subjectivity
  - The mid-training result (Section 5.4) is observational rather than interventional -- they compare existing OctoThinker models to Llama base rather than conducting controlled mid-training experiments
  - The paper does not propose a concrete solution for the unlearnability problem beyond "improve pre-training representations," which is somewhat vague
  - Some unlearnable examples see sharp reward increases with validation drops (footnote 1), suggesting potential reward hacking -- this is mentioned but not deeply analyzed

## Strengths

- Systematic, hypothesis-driven analysis that rules out the most natural explanations one by one
- Clean experimental design with controlled interventions (k_pos, clipping ablation, SFT, large rollout groups)
- The gradient analysis framework provides a principled tool for understanding data learnability
- The finding that semantically similar augmented data has very different gradient profiles is surprising and important
- Practical finding that Qwen's more aligned gradients may explain its RL-friendliness vs. Llama
- Clear writing with good figures and a working definition that enables future research

## Limitations

- Only tested on math reasoning tasks with relatively small models (0.5B-3B); unclear if phenomenon generalizes
- The threshold for "unlearnable" (pass@1 < 0.1) is somewhat arbitrary; a continuous learnability metric might be more informative
- No proposed algorithmic solution beyond mid-training, which is already known to help
- The mid-training comparison is between different model families (OctoThinker vs. Llama), not a controlled ablation
- Reasoning quality assessment depends on GPT-5-mini judgment, which may have its own biases
- The paper does not explore whether process-based reward models (PRMs) or step-level supervision could address the "fake reasoning" issue on unlearnable examples
- No analysis of what makes specific examples unlearnable (e.g., problem structure, required skills, topic)

## Relevance

本文深入分析RLVR训练动态，揭示了"不可学习数据"这一反直觉现象，对于理解LLM推理训练的局限性具有重要价值。如果你关注RLVR/GRPO训练方法、训练数据质量分析或推理能力提升，这项工作提供了关于表征质量与RL效果关系的关键洞察，并暗示预训练阶段的数据和训练策略可能比RL阶段的技巧更为关键。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the unlearnability phenomenon persist at larger model scales (7B, 14B, 70B+), or do larger models have sufficiently good representations to avoid it?
- Can process reward models (PRMs) or step-level supervision address the "fake reasoning" problem on unlearnable examples, given that the paper identifies flawed intermediate reasoning as a key symptom?
- What specific linguistic, structural, or mathematical features characterize unlearnable examples? Is there a systematic way to predict which examples will be unlearnable before training?
- How does the proportion and nature of unlearnable examples change across different task domains (code generation, logical reasoning, scientific QA)?
- Could targeted representation fine-tuning (e.g., contrastive learning on hard example embeddings) before RL mitigate the issue, and how would this compare to the mid-training approach?
- The paper shows augmented similar problems are learnable but don't help original unlearnable examples -- could iterative augmentation with gradient-guided problem generation close this gap?
