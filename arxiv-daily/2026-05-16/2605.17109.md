> **一句话总结：** DynMuon将Muon优化器的固定极分解推广为谱塑形族 $U\Sigma^p V^\top$，通过噪声感知局部模型发现正值 $p$ 有利于训练早期加速高曲率方向收敛、轻微负值 $p$ 有利于训练后期强化低曲率方向的残余信号，据此提出动态调度 $p$ 的算法，在GPT和Qwen架构上以10.6-26.5%更少步数达到相同目标损失。

> **TL;DR:** DynMuon reframes Muon's polar decomposition ($p=0$ in $U\Sigma^p V^\top$) as a spectral shaping family parameterized by exponent $p$. Through a noise-aware local model analyzing mode-wise signal-noise tradeoffs, the authors show positive $p$ accelerates early training via high-curvature direction emphasis, while mildly negative $p$ helps late-stage training by refocusing on flat directions retaining useful residual signal. The proposed dynamic schedule consistently beats fixed Muon across GPT-style (86M-688M) and Qwen-style architectures on FineWeb, reaching target losses 10.6-26.5% faster, with per-step cost unchanged via an extended Newton-Schulz iteration.

## 核心贡献

- 提出了谱塑形（spectral shaping）的统一视角，将Muon类优化器重新框架化为 $U\Sigma^p V^\top$ 变换族，其中 $p=-1$ 为逆谱更新，$p=0$ 恢复Muon，$p=1$ 对应标准SGD风格更新
- 构建了噪声感知局部模型，通过对损失景观局部曲率方向上的残余信号和随机梯度噪声进行逐模态分析，揭示了 $p$ 控制信号-噪声权衡的机制：减小 $p$ 增加对平坦（低曲率）方向的更新集中度但同时也放大噪声
- 发现了一个此前被忽视的阶段依赖现象：训练早期残余信号集中在高曲率方向（正值 $p$ 有利），训练后期残余信号转移到低曲率方向（轻微负值 $p$ 有利），通过残差偏移指标 $\Pi_t$ 和信号-噪声优势 $\Omega_t$ 进行了实证验证
- 提出DynMuon算法，通过扩展Newton-Schulz迭代高效近似 $U\Sigma^p V^\top$，避免了完整SVD，保持了与Muon相同的每步计算成本

## Key Contributions

- Introduces a dynamic spectral-shaping perspective that reframes Muon-style optimization from a fixed spectral operation into the adaptive problem of choosing the spectral exponent $p$ as training dynamics evolve
- Develops a noise-aware local model (Section 2.1) using locally linear gradient approximation and one-sided Kronecker-factored Hessian approximation (K-FAC style), deriving the residual signal evolution equation $E_{t+1} = (I - \eta_t \kappa_t H^{(p+1)/2}) E_t - \eta_t H^{(p-1)/2} \Xi_t$ that reveals how $p$ controls the tradeoff between signal contraction rate and noise amplification per curvature mode
- Uncovers and empirically validates a stage-dependent regime: the residual-shift metric $\Pi_t$ steadily decreases and becomes negative after ~500 steps (signal shifts from strong to flat modes), while the flat-mode signal-noise advantage $\Omega_t$ remains positive (flat modes retain useful signal relative to noise), supporting the use of mildly negative $p$ in late training
- Proposes DynMuon (Section 3), an efficient algorithm that extends Newton-Schulz iterations to approximate $U\Sigma^p V^\top$ for any $p$, avoiding full SVD and maintaining the same per-step cost as Muon while scheduling $p$ from positive to mildly negative

## Methodology

**Spectral Shaping Framework.** The authors parameterize Muon-like updates as $D_t^{(p)} = U_t \Sigma_t^p V_t^\top$ for the stochastic gradient $G_t = U_t \Sigma_t V_t^\top$. The parameter update is $W_{t+1} = W_t - \eta D_t^{(p)}$. This family is highly expressive: $p=-1$ gives inverse-spectrum updates, $p=0$ recovers standard Muon (polar decomposition), and $p=1$ corresponds to standard SGD.

**Noise-Aware Local Model.** The theoretical analysis (Section 2.1) models the residual signal $E_t = W_t - W^*$ using: (1) a locally linear approximation of the population gradient $\nabla L(W_t) \approx \kappa_t H E_t$ where $H$ is a normalized effective local curvature matrix; (2) gradient noise decomposition $G_t \approx \kappa_t H E_t + \Xi_t$; (3) a curvature-aligned surrogate for the spectral shaping operator. This yields the mode-wise residual signal evolution showing that $p$ acts through $h_i^{(p+1)/2}$ on the signal contraction rate and $h_i^{(p-1)/2}$ on noise amplification for each curvature mode $h_i$.

**Key Insight on Signal-Noise Tradeoff.** For each curvature mode $i$ with curvature $h_i$: increasing $p$ amplifies updates along strong (high-curvature) directions while suppressing flat (low-curvature) directions; decreasing $p$ does the reverse. The model predicts that as training progresses, residual signal migrates from strong to flat modes, making mildly negative $p$ advantageous in late training.

**Empirical Validation of Model Predictions.** The authors validate the model using a GPT-style model (hidden dim 768, 12 layers, 6 heads) trained on FineWeb. They estimate curvature via Hessian-vector products, noise levels from mini-batch gradient variance (32 batches), and residual signal energy from fixed probe gradients. Key metrics: residual shift $\Pi_t$ (difference between strong and flat mode residual signal), noise-adjusted signal $u_{i,t}$, and flat-mode signal-noise advantage $\Omega_t$. Results confirm $\Pi_t$ decreases below zero after ~500 steps and $\Omega_t$ remains positive throughout training.

**DynMuon Algorithm.** The method schedules $p$ from a positive value ($p_{\text{start}}$) to a mildly negative value ($p_{\text{end}}$) over training. To compute $U\Sigma^p V^\top$ efficiently without full SVD, DynMuon extends the Newton-Schulz iteration (used in standard Muon for $p=0$) to handle arbitrary $p$ values, maintaining the same per-step computational cost. For the validation experiments, they use a two-stage schedule: $p=1$ for the first 500 steps, then switch to $p=0$ or $p=-0.1$.

**Validation Setup Details.** Experiments use last-layer-only training (6000 steps, optimizing one matrix in the final Transformer block) and full-model training (10000 steps, up to 3B training tokens). Learning rate 0.01 for matrix parameters, AdamW with lr 0.001 for non-matrix parameters, weight decay 0.01, linear warmup for 1% of steps followed by cosine decay with 0.2 final warmdown ratio. Exact SVD is used for validation experiments; Newton-Schulz for main experiments.

## Results & Highlights

- **Consistent validation loss improvement:** DynMuon achieves lower validation loss than Muon across model sizes (86M, 194M, 388M, 688M parameters for GPT-style; various Qwen-style scales), architectures, and training settings
- **Step reduction:** 10.6-26.5% fewer training steps to reach the same target loss compared to Muon
- **Positive-to-negative schedule beats alternatives:** Simply doubling the early learning rate or reversing the schedule (negative to positive) performs worse than Muon, confirming the benefit comes specifically from the dynamic spectral shaping rather than just increased early learning
- **Mildly negative $p$ helps late training:** Switching from $p=0$ to negative exponents ($p \in \{-0.1, -0.25, -0.5, -0.75, -1\}$) at step 500 shows that mildly negative values ($p \approx -0.1$) improve both last-layer and full-model training, while overly negative values degrade performance due to excessive noise amplification
- **Early positive $p$ helps full-model training:** Setting $p=1$ for the first 500 steps and then switching to $p=0$ or $p=-0.1$ achieves lower validation loss than starting with $p=0$
- **Per-step cost preserved:** Through extended Newton-Schulz iterations, DynMuon maintains the same computational cost per step as standard Muon, avoiding full SVD

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty (High):** The spectral shaping parameterization is a natural but genuinely insightful generalization. The key novelty is the *dynamic* scheduling insight: the observation that the optimal spectral bias should evolve across training stages (positive early, mildly negative late) is novel and well-supported. This goes beyond prior work that studied fixed spectral transformations.

**Rigor (Good):** The noise-aware local model is carefully derived with clearly stated simplifying approximations (locally linear gradient, one-sided Kronecker-factored Hessian, curvature-aligned spectral surrogate). Importantly, the authors empirically validate these approximations rather than relying on them purely theoretically. The use of metrics like $\Pi_t$ and $\Omega_t$ to verify model predictions is methodologically sound. However, the model is not an end-to-end convergence proof -- the authors are transparent about this limitation.

**Reproducibility (Moderate-High):** The validation setup is well-detailed (architecture, dataset, hyperparameters). The paper is 20 pages with appendices providing additional details. However, no code release is mentioned, and the exact $p$ schedule hyperparameters ($p_{\text{start}}$, $p_{\text{end}}$, schedule shape) for the main experiments are not fully specified in the extracted content. The extended Newton-Schulz iteration details need to be verified for implementation.

**Significance (High):** If the results hold at truly large scale, this is practically very significant. Muon is described as "the dominant method for training large language models" -- a 10.6-26.5% step reduction translates directly to substantial compute cost savings. The theoretical framework also opens a new design axis for optimizer research.

**Red flags:**
- The model sizes tested (up to 688M) are relatively small by modern standards. Scaling to 7B+ or 70B+ remains unverified.
- The claim that Muon is "the dominant method" is somewhat strong -- AdamW remains widely used and Muon adoption is still growing.
- The sensitivity of results to the $p$ schedule hyperparameters is not thoroughly explored. It is unclear whether a single universal schedule works or whether task-specific tuning is needed.
- The paper uses "mildly negative $p$" without precise guidance on how to choose the exact negative value.

## Strengths

- Principled theoretical framework that is predictive (not just post-hoc rationalization) -- model predictions are validated before the algorithm is proposed
- Clean narrative arc: theory -> validation -> algorithm -> experiments
- Empirically thorough: validates model predictions with multiple metrics ($\Pi_t$, $\Omega_t$, noise-adjusted signal), then shows training performance gains
- Practical efficiency: extends Newton-Schulz to avoid full SVD, maintaining per-step cost
- Ablations on alternative schedules (reverse schedule, doubled learning rate) clearly show the benefit comes from spectral shaping dynamics, not just from larger updates
- The insight that flat modes retain useful signal in late training is genuinely novel and opens research directions

## Limitations

- **Scale limitation:** Experiments only go up to ~688M parameters; whether benefits persist at billion-to-trillion parameter scales is unknown
- **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** The $p$ schedule introduces new hyperparameters ($p_{\text{start}}$, $p_{\text{end}}$, schedule shape, transition point) -- the sensitivity analysis appears limited
- **Wall-clock time not reported:** While per-step FLOPs are claimed to be the same, actual wall-clock overhead of the extended Newton-Schulz iterations is not measured
- **Interaction with other techniques:** Unclear how DynMuon interacts with distributed training (tensor/pipeline parallelism), mixed precision, or other optimizer tricks
- **Limited baseline comparison:** The primary comparison is against standard Muon ($p=0$); comparison against a broader set of optimizers (AdamW, Shampoo, etc.) is not emphasized
- **The model's simplifying assumptions** (locally linear gradient, Kronecker-factored Hessian, curvature-aligned spectral surrogate) may not hold in all training regimes

## Relevance

这项工作对大语言模型训练优化具有直接且重要的实际意义。DynMuon在Muon优化器基础上提出了无需额外每步计算开销的改进，10.6-26.5%的训练步数减少可直接转化为可观的计算资源节省。其噪声感知局部模型提供的"训练阶段决定最优谱指数"的洞察对理解优化器动力学具有更广泛的理论价值，值得任何关注LLM训练效率的研究者深入阅读。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How robust is the optimal $p$ schedule across different datasets, model architectures, and training budgets? Is there a universal schedule?
- What is the wall-clock speedup (not just step reduction) when accounting for the extended Newton-Schulz iterations?
- How does DynMuon interact with distributed training setups (data/model/tensor parallelism) where gradient synchronization overhead may interact differently with spectral shaping?
- Can the noise-aware local model predict optimal schedules for other spectral shaping variants (e.g., anisotropic or mode-guided transformations)?
- Does the dynamic spectral shaping insight transfer to other matrix-aware optimizers like Shampoo or K-FAC?
- What happens at truly large scale (7B+, 70B+)? Does the signal-noise tradeoff dynamics observed at 86M-688M persist?
- Could the schedule for $p$ be adaptive (e.g., based on online estimates of $\Pi_t$ or $\Omega_t$) rather than pre-specified?
