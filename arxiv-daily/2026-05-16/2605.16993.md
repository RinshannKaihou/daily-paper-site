> **一句话总结：** 本文系统审计了临床AI的双重安全漏洞——对抗性图像扰动导致DenseNet121胸片诊断准确率从89.3%骤降至62.0%（Normal类仅42%，接近随机），而跨语言（英语到尼日利亚洋泾浜/约鲁巴语）测试使Llama3.1:8b和NatLAS的诊断准确率分别降至60-65%和55%，且专为非洲语言设计的NatLAS反而在洋泾浜英语上退化更严重（30个百分点），揭示了在低资源医疗环境中部署AI的严重安全隐患。

> **TL;DR:** This paper presents the first systematic dual audit of clinical AI safety: (1) DenseNet121 (CheXNet backbone) fine-tuned on COVID-QU-Ex collapses from 89.3% to 62.0% accuracy under imperceptible FGM perturbations (epsilon=0.021), with Normal-class detection plummeting to 42% (near random chance) and Gaussian smoothing making things worse (34.7%); (2) Llama3.1:8b and NatLAS tested on 20 COVID-19 clinical cases across Standard English, Nigerian Pidgin, and Yoruba-inflected English show 15-30pp accuracy drops, with NatLAS paradoxically collapsing 30pp on Pidgin despite being an African-context model. The study establishes a quantitative failure envelope for clinical AI under Nigerian Primary Health Centre deployment conditions and provides publicly reproducible code.

## 核心贡献

- 首次对临床AI进行对抗性图像脆弱性与跨语言诊断漂移的双重系统审计，填补了低资源医疗环境AI安全评估的空白
- 证明了在不可感知的FGM对抗扰动（epsilon=0.021，仅2.1%像素强度变化）下，DenseNet121胸片COVID-19分类准确率从89.3%骤降至62.0%，Normal类仅剩42%（接近三分一分类的随机水平），且24名正常患者被误诊为非COVID肺炎
- 标准防御策略全部失效：高斯平滑（sigma=1.0）反而使准确率降至34.7%（比不防御更差），集成投票仅恢复至72.7%，仍低于临床安全阈值16.6个百分点
- 揭示了LLM在跨语言临床诊断中的脆弱性：Llama3.1:8b在洋泾浜和约鲁巴英语下分别降至65%和60%，而NatLAS在洋泾浜英语下暴跌30个百分点至55%，诊断一致性仅50%
- 提出临床AI部署前必须具备"对抗鲁棒性图"和"跨语言漂移档案"的审计要求

## Key Contributions

- First systematic dual audit combining adversarial image fragility and cross-lingual diagnostic drift in clinical AI for low-resource healthcare settings
- Demonstrates DenseNet121 (CheXNet backbone) accuracy collapse from 89.3% to 62.0% under FGM (epsilon=0.021) on COVID-QU-Ex, with per-class collapse: Normal 78%->42% (+36pp drop), COVID-19 100%->72%, Non-COVID Pneumonia 90%->70%
- Shows Gaussian smoothing (34.7%) performs *worse* than no defense (61.3%), destroying fine-grained textural features without eliminating adversarial signal; ensemble voting achieves only partial recovery (72.7%)
- Reveals paradoxical NatLAS fragility: despite African-context training on WAXAL dataset (11,000 hours, 21 Sub-Saharan African languages), it collapses 30pp on Nigerian Pidgin with diagnosis consistency dropping to 50%
- Establishes quantitative failure envelope and proposes mandatory Adversarial Robustness Map and Cross-Lingual Drift Profile for clinical AI deployment
- All experimental code and data publicly available on GitHub for reproducibility

## Methodology

**Experiment 1: Adversarial Robustness Decay**
- **Architecture:** DenseNet121 (6,956,931 params), pre-trained on ImageNet, final layer replaced for 3-class classification (COVID-19, Non-COVID Pneumonia, Normal)
- **Dataset:** COVID-QU-Ex (85,318 chest X-rays); sampled 50 images/class from test split (N=150), stratified, fixed seed=42
- **Fine-tuning:** 200 images/class from training split (N=600), 10 epochs, Adam (lr=1e-4), StepLR (step_size=3, gamma=0.5), cross-entropy loss, Apple Silicon MPS acceleration
- **Attack:** Fast Gradient Method (FGM) via Adversarial Robustness Toolbox (ART) v1.20, epsilon swept across 15 levels with key threshold at 0.021
- **Defenses tested:** Gaussian Smoothing (sigma=1.0), Ensemble Voting (5 augmentations)

**Experiment 2: Cross-Lingual Diagnostic Drift**
- **Models:** Llama3.1:8b (general-purpose) and NatLAS/N-ATLAS (African-context, supports Yoruba/Hausa/Igbo/Nigerian-accented English, trained on WAXAL 11,000 hours)
- **Test set:** 20 COVID-19 clinical cases manually translated into three language registers: Standard English, Nigerian Pidgin (Naija), Yoruba-inflected English
- **Metrics:** Per-language diagnostic accuracy, diagnosis consistency with Standard English predictions

## Results & Highlights

**Adversarial Image Audit:**
- Clean baseline: 89.3% overall; COVID-19=100%, Non-COVID=90%, Normal=78%
- At epsilon=0.021: overall drops to 62.0% (-27.3pp); COVID-19=72%, Non-COVID=70%, Normal=42% (+36pp drop, near random)
- Misclassification patterns: 24 Normal patients misclassified as Non-COVID Pneumonia; 13 COVID-19 patients misclassified as Non-COVID Pneumonia
- Perturbation visualizations confirm adversarial noise concentrates in anatomically informative regions (texture features most discriminative for COVID-19 classification)

**Defense Stress Test (epsilon=0.021):**

| Condition | Accuracy | vs. Clean |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Clean baseline | 89.3% | -- |
| Adversarial (no defense) | 61.3% | -28.0pp |
| Gaussian Smoothing | 34.7% | -54.6pp |
| Ensemble Voting | 72.7% | -16.6pp |

**Cross-Lingual Audit (Table 3):**

| Language | Llama3.1:8b Acc | NatLAS Acc | Llama Consistency | NatLAS Consistency |
|----------|-----------------|------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Standard English | 80.0% | 85.0% | 100% | 100% |
| Nigerian Pidgin | 65.0% | 55.0% | 85% | 50% |
| Yoruba-inflected English | 60.0% | 75.0% | 80% | 60% |

- Llama3.1:8b shows monotonic degradation across languages; NatLAS shows V-shaped profile with severe Pidgin-specific collapse followed by partial Yoruba-English recovery
- Case 19 (Normal patient) misdiagnosed as Non-COVID Pneumonia in both Pidgin and Yoruba-inflected English by Llama3.1:8b
- NatLAS paradox: 50% consistency on Pidgin means different diagnosis in 1 of every 2 cases compared to its own English prediction

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** High for the dual-audit framing. Adversarial attacks on medical imaging and multilingual LLM evaluation are individually well-explored, but their combination in a clinical AI safety audit targeting low-resource healthcare settings is genuinely novel. The NatLAS paradox finding is particularly interesting.
- **Rigor:** Moderate concerns. (1) Cross-lingual evaluation uses only 20 clinical cases -- too small for statistical robustness and vulnerable to case-selection bias. (2) Only FGM (a single-step first-order attack) is tested; PGD, C&W, or AutoAttack would strengthen adversarial claims. (3) Only DenseNet121 is evaluated; ConvNeXt, Vision Transformers, or modern architectures are not tested. (4) The model is fine-tuned on only 600 images (200/class), which is unusually small and may partially explain the adversarial vulnerability. (5) No confidence intervals, error bars, or statistical significance tests are reported for any experiment.
- **Reproducibility:** Strong -- publicly available code, standard dataset, fixed random seeds, detailed hyperparameter reporting (optimizer, LR, scheduling, image preprocessing), and the authors claim reproducibility on consumer hardware.
- **Significance:** High for the global health AI community. The epsilon=0.021 threshold is argued to fall within natural clinical artifacts (scanner calibration, JPEG compression, network transmission), making the findings practically relevant even absent deliberate adversarial attack. The language drift findings directly challenge deployment assumptions for African healthcare.
- **Red flags:** The NatLAS paradox (African-context model performing *worse* on African language variants) is striking but under-investigated. The paper hypothesizes insufficient medical annotations in local dialects, but does not test this with controlled experiments. The 20-case language sample is a serious limitation -- a single difficult or ambiguous case could shift results by 5pp. The Gaussian smoothing result (34.7%) is surprising enough to warrant verification with different sigma values.

## Strengths

- Addresses a critical and genuinely under-studied intersection of AI safety and global health equity
- Dual-audit design (imaging + language) provides holistic safety assessment
- Per-class analysis reveals Normal-class vulnerability is most dangerous (patients incorrectly told they have pneumonia)
- Perturbation visualizations effectively demonstrate imperceptibility of attacks
- The finding that Gaussian smoothing is counter-productive is practically important
- Public code and data ensure reproducibility
- Clear, actionable policy recommendations (mandatory adversarial robustness maps, cross-lingual drift profiles)

## Limitations

- Cross-lingual evaluation uses only 20 clinical cases -- statistically underpowered and vulnerable to selection bias
- Only FGM tested; stronger adversarial attacks (PGD, AutoAttack) not evaluated
- Single imaging architecture (DenseNet121) limits generalizability
- Model fine-tuned on only 600 images (200/class), which is unusually small and may inflate adversarial vulnerability
- No confidence intervals or statistical significance tests reported
- Only two defenses tested; adversarial training, certified defenses, and diffusion purification not evaluated
- NatLAS paradox is unexplained -- no ablation to determine if the issue is model architecture, training data, or prompt formatting
- The study does not propose or evaluate any solutions to the identified vulnerabilities
- No comparison with human clinician performance under similar perturbations or language switching

## Relevance

本文与关注临床AI安全性和多语言NLP在医疗领域应用的研究方向高度相关，特别是揭示了在非英语低资源环境中部署AI诊断系统的双重脆弱性（对抗性扰动+语言漂移）。epsilon=0.021处于常规临床图像处理噪声范围内的论点使发现具有实际意义。对面向全球健康公平的AI系统设计、对抗鲁棒性评估和跨语言临床NLP均具有重要参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How do stronger adversarial attacks (PGD, AutoAttack, C&W) affect diagnostic accuracy, and would the same epsilon thresholds remain clinically relevant?
- Would adversarial training or certified defenses (randomized smoothing, diffusion purification) provide meaningful protection without excessive clean-accuracy sacrifice?
- Why does NatLAS degrade more severely than Llama3.1:8b on Nigerian Pidgin? Is this a training data gap (insufficient medical Pidgin), model capacity issue, or prompt formatting artifact?
- How robust are findings across architectures (ConvNeXt, Vision Transformers, EfficientNet) and modalities (CT, MRI, pathology)?
- What minimum sample size is needed for statistically powered cross-lingual clinical AI benchmarks, and how should such benchmarks be constructed for reproducibility?
- Does fine-tuning on more training data (beyond 600 images) improve adversarial robustness, or is the vulnerability intrinsic to the architecture?
- How do human clinicians perform on the same cases presented in different language registers -- is the language drift AI-specific or a general clinical communication challenge?
