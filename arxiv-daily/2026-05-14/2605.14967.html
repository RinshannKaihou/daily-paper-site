InfoSFT: Learn More and Forget Less with Information-Aware Token Weighting

arXiv: 2605.14967

Date: 2026-05-14

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.14967

一句话总结： InfoSFT 通过在 SFT 中对 token 损失施加基于信息量的权重（重点加权模型中等置信度的 token），在数学、代码和推理任务上持续优于标准 SFT 和 DFT，同时更好地保持模型原有能力。
TL;DR: InfoSFT proposes a principled token-weighting scheme for SFT that concentrates learning on medium-confidence tokens (neither too easy nor too unlikely), consistently improving generalization on math/code/CoT benchmarks while mitigating catastrophic forgetting, requiring only a one-line code change.

核心贡献

  • 从近端更新（proximal update）框架出发，推导出在固定 KL 散度预算下最优的 token 加权规则：权重正比于 $q(\text{logit}(p) - \text{logit}(q))$，其中 $q$ 是模型对 token 的似然概率，$p$ 是校准常数。
  • 揭示了标准 SFT（均匀加权）和 DFT（按似然加权）的不足：SFT 过度拟合低似然样本，DFT 则过度压低低似然样本中包含的新行为信息。
  • InfoSFT 只需在标准 token-wise loss 中加一行权重修正，实现简单、即插即用。
  • 在数学、代码、推理和科学问答等多个任务上，InfoSFT 在 pass@1 和 pass@k 上均优于 SFT 和 DFT，同时在灾难性遗忘方面取得更好的新任务/旧能力权衡。

Key Contributions

  • Derive the optimal token-weighting rule for SFT under a fixed KL budget from a proximal update perspective, showing that medium-confidence tokens should receive the highest weight.
  • Propose InfoSFT: a one-line modification to the standard SFT loss that adds an information-aware correction term $[\text{logit}(p) - \text{logit}(\pi_\theta(y^*_t))]+$ to the token-level gradient.
  • Demonstrate that InfoSFT consistently outperforms SFT and DFT across math (MATH500, AMC), code (HumanEval, MultiPL-E), and reasoning (AIME) benchmarks with multiple model families (Qwen, Llama).
  • Show InfoSFT achieves a better new-task / prior-capability tradeoff in catastrophic forgetting experiments (Science Q&A, Tool Use).
  • Unify SFT, DFT, and InfoSFT under a general weighting framework $\Omega(q)$, providing clear theoretical motivation for each approach.

Methodology

The authors formulate SFT through a policy-gradient lens, showing that standard SFT implicitly assigns reward $\propto 1/q$ to expert samples while DFT assigns uniform reward 1. They unify both under a general weighting function $\Omega(q)$ where the effective gradient weight is $q\Omega(q)$.

Under a proximal update framework with a KL budget from the base model, they derive the oracle-optimal weighting rule: $\Omega^(q) \propto \text{logit}(p^(y^|x)) - \text{logit}(q)$, comparing model confidence against the unknown expert distribution. Since $p^$ is inaccessible, they replace it with its average value $\bar{p}$, estimated as the model's average next-token confidence on its own correct generations (stable around 0.9–0.95 across models; default $\bar{p} = 0.93$).

The final token-level gradient applies: $w_{\text{info}}(q) = q \cdot [\text{logit}(\bar{p}) - \text{logit}(q)]_+$, clipped to be non-negative to avoid penalizing high-confidence trivial tokens. This yields a "middle-confidence" weighting profile: low weight for very likely tokens (already learned), peak weight for medium-confidence tokens (most informative), and vanishing weight for extremely unlikely tokens (risk of instability). The authors show (Remark 2.1) InfoSFT is approximately equivalent to adding an entropy correction to DFT.

Results & Highlights

  • Math (NuminaMath-CoT, 100K samples): InfoSFT improves MATH500 acc@1 by 4.6–16.3 points over DFT and 0.8–3.8 over SFT across Qwen-1.5B, Qwen-7B, and Llama-3.1-8B. On AMC, InfoSFT gains 6–10 points over SFT/DFT for Qwen models.
  • Code (UltraFeedback): Comparable or better than DFT; +3 points on HumanEval for Llama-3.1-8B.
  • pass@k: InfoSFT achieves the best pass@k curves across all k (1–64), avoiding the entropy collapse that plagues DFT while maintaining diversity.
  • CoT reasoning (OpenR1-Math): SFT+InfoSFT two-stage training achieves best AIME pass@8 (50.0%), +6.7 over SFT-epoch2 and +16.6 over DFT.
  • Catastrophic forgetting: InfoSFT achieves the best new-task / prior-capability Pareto frontier on Science Q&A and Tool Use tasks, reaching higher new-task accuracy while preserving more prior capabilities.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. The proximal-update derivation of the optimal weighting rule is clean and theoretically motivated. The insight that medium-confidence tokens are most informative is intuitive but rigorously grounded here. Unifying SFT/DFT/InfoSFT under one framework adds clarity.
  • Rigor: Strong theoretical backing (Lemma 4.1, 4.2) with proofs in the appendix. The approximation of $p^*$ by $\bar{p}$ is well-justified empirically (Table 2 shows stability across models). However, the clipping heuristic for negative weights, while practical, breaks the theoretical optimality guarantee.
  • Reproducibility: Good. Single hyperparameter ($\bar{p} = 0.93$) works across all experiments. Code is public. Experimental settings are clearly described with learning rates, dataset sizes, and training durations.
  • Significance: High practical impact — a one-line change to SFT that consistently helps. The improved pass@k is particularly valuable for downstream RL/best-of-n pipelines.
  • Red flags: (1) The calibration constant $\bar{p}$ is estimated from the student model itself, creating a circular dependency that is not theoretically analyzed. (2) On CoT reasoning with very unlikely format tokens (e.g., ), InfoSFT alone underperforms SFT, requiring a two-stage workaround — this limits applicability when the fine-tuning data is far from the base distribution. (3) No experiments on very large models (70B+) or during RLHF-style multi-stage training.

Strengths

  • Principled theoretical derivation from proximal optimization, not just heuristic weighting.
  • Extremely simple implementation — one-line change to standard loss.
  • Consistent improvements across diverse tasks (math, code, reasoning), model families (Qwen, Llama), and sizes (1.5B–8B).
  • Strong pass@k gains preserve diversity, crucial for downstream alignment stages.
  • Better catastrophic forgetting tradeoff with no additional compute cost during training.
  • Thorough ablation on the calibration constant $\bar{p}$, showing robustness.

Limitations

  • Struggles when fine-tuning data contains very unlikely tokens (e.g., new reasoning format tags) — requires two-stage SFT→InfoSFT workaround.
  • The calibration constant $\bar{p}$, while empirically stable, is not adaptive during training; its optimality as a static value is not formally proven.
  • Only evaluated on models up to 8B parameters; scalability to 70B+ models is unknown.
  • No evaluation in RLHF or DPO-style multi-stage pipelines where SFT is just the first step.
  • The clipping of negative weights, while practical, is an unprincipled hack that deviates from the theoretically optimal rule.
