Position: Behavioural Assurance Cannot Verify the Safety Claims Governance Now Demands

arXiv: 2605.15164

Date: 2026-05-14

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.15164

一句话总结： 本文系统性论证了当前AI治理框架所依赖的行为评估方法（如红队测试、系统卡）在认知论上无法验证"无隐藏目标""无欺骗性对齐"等高风险安全性声明，提出了"审计鸿沟"和"脆弱保证"两个核心概念，并设计了一套基于线性探针、激活修补和训练前后对比的可复现验证试点协议。
TL;DR: This position paper argues that behavioural assurance methods (evals, red-teaming, system cards) are epistemically insufficient to verify the high-consequence safety claims that AI governance frameworks now demand (absence of hidden objectives, deception, loss-of-control precursors). The authors formalize this structural mismatch as the "audit gap," introduce "fragile assurance," analyze 21 governance instruments across 13 jurisdictions, and propose a concrete mechanistic-evidence pilot protocol (linear probes, activation patching, before/after-training comparisons) to extend voluntary pre-deployment access arrangements.

核心贡献

  • 定义"脆弱保证"（Fragile Assurance）：当安全声明无法被独立第三方在可比条件下复现检查，或证据结构与所支撑的声明之间的推理鸿沟不被证据本身支持时，该声明即为脆弱的。脆弱不等于虚假，但常被当作比其证据基础更有力来对待。
  • 系统性刻画"审计鸿沟"（Audit Gap）：对13个司法管辖区、21项治理工具进行了访问级别编码（行为级→白盒级），发现没有任何锚定案例能让独立验证者实际拥有的访问权限与其安全声明所隐含要求的访问级别相匹配。
  • 识别"激励梯度"（Incentive Gradient）：指出速度优势、主权竞争和操作可行性三股独立压力系统性地奖励表层行为代理指标，而非深层结构验证，形成自我强化的反馈循环。
  • 提出具体的机械可解释性验证试点协议：设计了P1–P6六步协议，包含线性探针、激活修补、训练前后对比三条证据线，在可信执行环境中由独立验证者复现，并设定预注册阈值。

Key Contributions

  • Formalizes the "audit gap" as the divergence between access levels governance instruments implicitly require ($A_i$) and what independent verifiers can actually supply ($V_i$), coded across a 21-instrument, 13-jurisdiction inventory on the five-level access taxonomy of Casper et al. (2024).
  • Introduces "fragile assurance" (Definition 1): safety claims that cannot be independently reproduced or where the inferential gap between evidence and claim exceeds the evidence's structural support.
  • Identifies the "incentive gradient": three independent resource-allocation pressures (speed-to-deployment, sovereign competitiveness, pragmatic tractability) systematically favour surface-level behavioural proxies over mechanistic verification, and governance ratification of those proxies creates a self-reinforcing loop.
  • Proposes a reproducible mechanistic-evidence pilot (P1–P6): three evidence lines (linear probes, activation patching, before/after-training comparisons) inside a TEE, with pre-registered floor thresholds, bounded compute, and mandatory failure-publishing.
  • Documents the verification retreat: institutional signals (UK AISI → AI Security Institute; Bletchley "Safety" → Delhi "Impact") and lab-level shifts (DeepMind's pragmatic turn away from ambitious circuit-level programs) narrowing the mechanistic and institutional axes even as governance expands.

Methodology

The paper combines legal-instrument analysis with technical AI-safety reasoning:

  1. Governance inventory construction: 21 governance instruments (binding law, soft law, recommendations, voluntary frameworks) spanning EU, US, Asia-Pacific, South Asia, Middle East, and international organizations, coded against the external-access taxonomy (behavioural < outside-the-box < grey-box < white-box < state-embedded).
  2. Access-gap matrix: For each instrument $i$, the authors compute $[V_i, A_i]$ — the interval between verifier-achievable access and implicitly required access. Cells are coded Green (verification feasible), Amber (closable under structured access), or Red (not closable with currently deployed tooling).
  3. Incentive-gradient analysis: Three independent pressures (speed, sovereignty, pragmatism) are shown to point in the same direction — toward behavioural proxies. A feedback loop is inferred from institutional trajectory.
  4. Agentic failure-mode coverage table: Four failure modes (insider threat, loss-of-control, goal drift, deceptive alignment, hidden objectives) rated on a 4-point scale across four evidence types (behavioural, circuit, probe, ablation).
  5. Pilot protocol specification (P1–P6): A contract-level specification for two independent labs to implement, including claim forms, three evidence lines, pre-registered thresholds (probe AUROC ≥ 0.95, ablation ≥ 1.5σ), TEE reproduction, bounded compute, and failure-publishing.
  6. Sensitivity analysis: Aggregate findings survive plausible re-codings of borderline Amber/Red cells (Section C of supplement).

Results & Highlights

  • No anchor case has $V_i \geq A_i$ uniformly across its claim set — the audit gap is structural, not incidental.
  • SB-53 retained catastrophic-risk claims from SB-1047 but dropped verification teeth (third-party safety determinations, kill switches, civil penalties).
  • Singapore AI Verify is the strongest positive analog (coded G/A), but still cannot close hidden-objectives and deception claims.
  • Behavioural evidence is sufficient for decomposable claims (bias, robustness, capability presence) but insufficient for latent absence claims (hidden objectives, deception absence).
  • Hidden objectives show the largest evidence gap in the failure-mode coverage table: behavioural evaluation scores weakest (•°°°) while mechanistic probes score strongest (••••).
  • Empirical support: Goldowsky-Dill et al. (2025) achieved AUROCs of 0.96–0.999 detecting strategic deception with linear probes; Marks et al. (2025) showed interpretability-equipped teams outperformed in hidden-objective audits — but both required access conditions (weights, activations) that independent verifiers lack under current regulation.
  • Falsifiability conditions stated: The position would be revised if (i) behavioural evals gain predictive validity on absence claims, (ii) structured-access pilots show mechanistic evidence adds nothing beyond behavioural evals, or (iii) Red-coded instruments routinely substantiate absence claims through process-accountable evidence.

Authors & Credibility

  • Pratinav Seth and Vinay Kumar Sankarapu are affiliated with Lexsi Labs. The paper is a well-structured position paper with 89 references spanning governance instruments, mechanistic interpretability literature, and empirical safety evaluations.
  • The analysis covers 13 jurisdictions with primary-text verification for 4 of 21 rows (EU Art. 55, SB-53, Singapore AI Verify, PRC Interim Measures) and secondary sources for the remainder — a transparency the authors explicitly flag as a limitation.
  • The work sits within the technical AI governance research program and engages seriously with counterpositions (Section 6), including the strongest objection that mechanistic evidence may itself be fragile.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty (Moderate-High): The "audit gap" framing and the 21-instrument matrix are novel contributions. The concept of "fragile assurance" is a useful formalization, though the underlying intuition (that testing outputs ≠ verifying internal properties) is not new. The pilot protocol (P1–P6) is the most operationally concrete proposal to date for bridging governance requirements with mechanistic verification.
  • Rigor (Moderate-High): The matrix coding mixes descriptive and normative claims, which the authors acknowledge. The sensitivity analysis (Section C) strengthens the main finding. The falsifiability conditions are commendable. However, the "widening" trajectory claim is explicitly qualitative — no time-series data on method maturity is provided.
  • Reproducibility (Moderate): The pilot protocol is specified at contract level, which is unusually concrete for a position paper. However, the matrix coding involves judgment calls on borderline cases, and only 4 of 21 rows were verified against primary statutory text.
  • Significance (High): If the argument gains traction, it could influence how regulators write AI governance requirements and how frontier labs structure pre-deployment access. The paper directly addresses an ongoing policy gap with an actionable proposal.
  • Red flags: (1) The authors are from a small lab (Lexsi Labs) with no prior track record of governance analysis at this scale, though the work itself is substantive. (2) The "incentive gradient" feedback loop is admitted to be inferred rather than causally proven — it serves as a "Bayesian prior on gap persistence." (3) Tier-2 evidence (full patching on 405B+ models, base-model access) remains aspirational; the near-term pilot only covers Tier-1.

Strengths

  • Exceptional breadth of governance coverage (21 instruments, 13 jurisdictions) with systematic coding.
  • Intellectually honest engagement with counterpositions — the authors identify their strongest objection (mechanistic fragility) and design around it with convergent validity.
  • The pilot protocol (P1–P6) is unusually concrete and implementable for a position paper.
  • Clear scope delineation: behavioural assurance is adequate for decomposable claims; the critique targets only the high-consequence tail.
  • Falsifiability conditions stated explicitly — rare and valuable in position papers.

Limitations

  • The matrix is partly normative ($V_i$ assignment requires judgment); the aggregate finding survives re-coding, but individual cell assignments are debatable.
  • Primary-text verification covers only 4 of 21 instruments.
  • The "widening" claim is directional/qualitative, not supported by longitudinal data.
  • The pilot protocol's Tier-2 feasibility (frontier-scale models) remains uncertain.
  • The verifier ecosystem (UK AISI, METR, Apollo, FAR.AI) is largely Anglophone; extending pilots through them risks concentrating verification capability.

Relevance

本文对AI治理与安全评估的交叉领域做出了重要贡献，系统性揭示了当前治理框架中行为评估与高风险安全声明之间的结构性错配。对于关注AI对齐、可解释性治理和前沿模型监管的研究者而言，本文提供了一个清晰的分析框架和可操作的验证协议提案。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How would the audit-gap matrix change if weighted by deployed-model-parameter count rather than by instrument count — are the highest-stakes deployments in the Red cells?
  2. Can the P1–P6 pilot be trialed on a 7B–70B model within 12 months, and what would the failure modes of the pilot itself reveal?
  3. How do open-weight models (where $V_i$ is effectively white-box) change the audit-gap picture, and should governance instruments distinguish open vs. closed access?
  4. What institutional mechanisms could prevent "probe gaming" — providers training models to produce probe-ready representations without the representation bearing safety-relevant work?
  5. How would the incentive gradient shift if a major jurisdiction adopted mechanistic-evidence requirements — would competitive pressure propagate the standard?
