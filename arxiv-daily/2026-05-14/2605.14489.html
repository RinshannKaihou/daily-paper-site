A Novel Schur-Decomposition-Based Weight Projection Method for Stable State-Space Neural-Network Architectures

arXiv: 2605.14489

Date: 2026-05-14

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.14489

一句话总结： 本文提出基于实 Schur 分解的状态矩阵稳定投影方案（直接投影 Schur (Proj.) 与预分解 Schur (Built.)），把准上三角因子投影到最近的 Schur 稳定等价形式，用 n_x²/2n_x² 个权重（远少于 SIMBa 的 5n_x²）保证线性离散状态空间层的渐近稳定性，在合成 LTI 系统和 Silverbox/CED/EMPS/Industrial Robot/Fine-Steering Mirror 等真实非线性辨识基准上达到与 SIMBa 同级的精度。
TL;DR: The paper proposes two backpropagation-compatible weight-projection methods — Schur (Proj.) (project the state matrix A via its real Schur decomposition each step) and Schur (Built.) (parameterize A directly through pre-factored Schur factors) — that project the quasi-triangular Schur factor onto its nearest Schur-stable peer using Algorithm 1 (Noferini–Poloni nearest Ω-stable block-diagonal projection). On the synthetic random-system benchmark (50 stable systems, nx=5, ny=nu=3, 50,000 epochs AdamW, η0=1e-3) and four real nonlinear-benchmarks (Silverbox, CED, EMPS, Industrial Robot, Fine-Steering Mirror), both Schur variants match SIMBa in NMSE while using 64 vs SIMBa's 164 parameters at nx=5; e.g., on EMPS the Regularized baseline diverges to NMSE = 2.50e1 while Schur (Proj.) achieves 3.21e-3.

核心贡献

  • Schur 稳定权重投影：基于 Noferini–Poloni 的最近 Ω-稳定矩阵理论，提出 Algorithm 1——对状态矩阵实 Schur 分解的准三角因子 T 做 2×2 块对角投影到稳定候选集 Ŝ(T_{i,i})（最多 15 个元素），保留正交因子 Z 不变，相当于在每次训练 batch 后执行一次 Noferini–Poloni 双层优化的单步迭代。
  • 两种参数化形式：Schur (Proj.) 直接对 A 做 Schur 分解再投影（仅需 n_x² 个权重参数化 A）；Schur (Built.) 预先以 Schur 因子形式参数化 A（Z 经 SVD 约束为正交，T 经 Algorithm 1 稳定化，需 2n_x² 个权重）——两者都远低于 SIMBa 的 5n_x²。
  • 全面基准：在合成 LTI 系统（Di Natale 等 [6] 的 50 个随机稳定系统，并扩展 Smaller/Small/Original/Extended/Large 配置）以及五个真实非线性辨识数据集上，与 SIMBa、Bemporad 的谱范数正则化方法对比 NMSE、NSSR、epoch time、epoch count。
  • 开源实现：在 https://codeberg.org/sergiovaneg/SchurSS 以 BSD-3-Clause 发布，Keras 3 + JAX 后端，附自定义 Schur 分解实现以支持 GPU。

Key Contributions

  • Introduce a backpropagation-ready Schur-stable weight-projection scheme (Algorithm 1) that exploits the Noferini–Poloni nearest Ω-stable matrix result: project each 1×1 / 2×2 block-diagonal element of the quasi-triangular Schur factor T of A onto its nearest stable peer while keeping the orthogonal factor Z fixed, equivalent to one iteration of the bi-level Riemannian optimization per training batch.
  • Provide two formulations: Schur (Proj.) projects A in-place (n_x² weights for A) and Schur (Built.) parameterizes A directly through pre-factored Schur factors (2n_x² weights), avoiding a per-step Schur factorization; both compare favourably to SIMBa's 5n_x²-weight LMI parameterization.
  • Prove the truncated projection is guaranteed to yield the nearest quasi-triangular Schur-stable matrix (a corollary of Noferini–Poloni), unlike regularization methods (which require careful ρ_r tuning and cannot strictly guarantee stability) and without the gradient fan-out of SIMBa.
  • Benchmark against SIMBa and the Bemporad spectral-norm regularizer on synthetic LTI systems and five real-world nonlinear system-identification datasets, with all code released as BSD-3-Clause on Codeberg.

Methodology

Problem. Identify a stable discrete LTI state-space layer x[k+1]=Ax[k]+Bu[k], y[k]=Cx[k]+Du[k] with Schur stability |λ_i| ≤ 1 ∀ λ_i ∈ Λ_A. Backpropagation loses the stability guarantees that classical subspace identification methods (SIMs) provide, so a constraint is needed.

Algorithm 1 (Quasi-triangular Schur factor projection). Input: Schur factor T∈R^{n×n} (quasi-triangular). For each diagonal block: if 1×1, set T̂_{i,i} = T_{i,i}/max{1,|T_{i,i}|}; if 2×2, search Ŝ(T_{i,i}) (≤15 candidate 2×2 matrices) for the closest Frobenius-norm stable block, filtering by |λ_1|≤1 ∧ |λ_2|≤1. The resulting Â = Z T̂ Z^⊺.

Two formulations.

  • Schur (Proj.): compute Schur decomposition A = ZTZ^⊺ at every step, apply Algorithm 1, reconstruct Â. Weight count for A: n_x².
  • Schur (Built.): parameterize A directly via (Z, T̂); Z constrained orthogonal via SVD-based retraction (Golub & Van Loan); T̂ constrained upper-quasi-triangular via Algorithm 1 (zero below block-diagonal). Weight count: 2n_x².

Comparison methods. SIMBa (5n_x² LMI parameterization, Di Natale et al., IEEE TCST 2024); Bemporad regularizer r(Θ)=max(‖A‖₂²−1+ε, 0)² with piece-wise penalty. S4/Mamba explicitly excluded for lacking stability guarantees on regularly-sampled continuous-valued systems.

Synthetic benchmark. 50 stable discrete-time systems (nx=5, ny=3, nu=3); one 300-sample trajectory per partition; GBN inputs (p=0.1); unbiased Gaussian output noise σ=0.25 (Original) or σ=0.01 (Extended/Small/Smaller/Large); AdamW η0=1e-3, 50,000 epochs. Configurations: Original, Extended (more samples + early stopping), Small (128 samples), Smaller (64 samples), Large (nx=10, ny=nu=6, multi-sequence partitions).

Real benchmark. Stacked Hammerstein-Wiener architecture (Fig. 1b); single-stage backprop, x[0]=0; AdamW η0=1e-3; up to 50,000 epochs with early stopping patience 10,000; optimal initial state on val/test via EKF + RTS smoother; five random initializations; NMSE on test. Datasets: Silverbox, CED (Coupled Electric Drives), EMPS (Electronic Motor Positioning System), Industrial Robot, Fine-Steering Mirror.

Implementation. Keras 3 with JAX backend on HPC CPU cores (CPU faster than GPU at these dataset sizes per [6]); JIT-compiled Algorithm 1; custom GPU-compatible Schur decomposition in Appendix C.

Results & Highlights

  • Matrix-approximation quality vs full bi-level optimization (Table 1). At n=100, the truncated Block-Diagonal Projection runs in 7.03e-5 s vs 7.05e4 s for the full bi-level Noferini–Poloni optimization — roughly 9 orders of magnitude faster — while achieving a better NSSR (0.628 vs 0.761, i.e., closer eigenvalue match) at the cost of a higher NSFE (0.373 vs 0.485). MSVR (stability violation) stays at 4.17e-1 vs 2.65e-2, but both are theoretically stable.
  • Parameter efficiency. For nx=5, ny=nu=3: Schur (Proj.) layer uses 64 parameters, Schur (Built.) 89, SIMBa 164 (more than half the training sample count of 300 in the Original setup).
  • Synthetic Original (Table 2). Test NMSE: Schur (Proj.) (5.91±5.31)e-4, Schur (Built.) (7.14±8.32)e-4, SIMBa (3.78±3.45)e-4 (best), Regularized (5.25±5.01)e-4 — all within each other's IQR. The Regularized method requires the most epochs: (2.13±0.22)e4 vs (1.79±0.20)e4 for SIMBa.
  • Synthetic Large (nx=10) (Table 2). Test NMSE: Schur (Proj.) (4.49±1.55)e-4, Schur (Built.) (4.09±1.93)e-4 (best), SIMBa (6.49±2.63)e-4, Regularized (5.19±1.22)e-4; Schur (Built.) also converges in (3.40±0.26)e4 epochs vs SIMBa's (3.11±0.28)e4.
  • Real-data EMPS (Table 3) — the discriminant case. Test NMSE: Schur (Proj.) (3.21±3.24)e-3, Schur (Built.) (3.07±4.51)e-3 (best), SIMBa (4.86±5.78)e-3, Regularized 2.50e1 (i.e., diverged). Schur (Proj.) epoch time 7.49e-3 s, epoch count 3.16e4 vs Regularized 4.78e4.
  • Real-data Silverbox (Table 3). Test NMSE: Schur (Proj.) (1.52±7.86)e-2, Schur (Built.) (1.51±7.87)e-2, SIMBa (1.50±7.97)e-2 (best), Regularized (1.53±7.49)e-2. SIMBa requires the most epochs (1.40e4) while Schur (Built.) the fewest (6.89e3).
  • Real-data Fine-Steering Mirror (Table 3) — largest configuration. Schur (Proj.) NMSE (6.63±12.3)e-1, Schur (Built.) (5.76±10.9)e-1 (best), SIMBa (7.25±17.5)e-1, Regularized (7.40±17.5)e-1. On this largest model, regularizer epoch time (1.86e-1 s) matches Schur (1.78e-1 s) because ‖A‖₂ computation at large nx dominates.

Critical Assessment

  • The proposed method does not beat the SOTA on accuracy — it ties. The authors are explicit (§4.1, §5) that all four methods fall within each other's IQR on every synthetic case and on Silverbox/CED/Fine-Steering Mirror; the only meaningful accuracy win is the EMPS divergence of the Regularized baseline, which is a failure of the baseline rather than a win for Schur. The contribution is therefore parameter efficiency + speed, not modelling power.
  • The truncated projection is no longer the global nearest Ω-stable matrix. Fixing the orthogonal factor Z breaks the Noferini–Poloni guarantee of global optimality; only the block-diagonal projection step retains its per-block nearest-stable guarantee. This is acknowledged indirectly (the paper says it is "equivalent to performing a single iteration of the bi-level optimization problem"), but the gap to the true nearest stable matrix is not measured empirically beyond Table 1's NSFE.
  • MSVR in Table 1 is concerning. At n=100 the truncated Block-Diagonal Projection has MSVR = 4.17e-1 (i.e., non-trivial eigenvalue-magnitude violation), versus 2.65e-2 for the bi-level optimizer. The paper hand-waves this as "both theoretically stable," but it directly contradicts the headline "stability guarantee up to numerical error" claim and is not analysed further.
  • GPU path is unverified. Experiments run on CPU "as CPU execution is faster for these dataset sizes [6]"; the GPU-compatible custom Schur decomposition (Appendix C) is described but never benchmarked, so the central scalability claim (usable inside deep NN training) rests on assertion.
  • S4/Mamba are strawmanned, not benchmarked. They are excluded "due to their hardware overhead and lack of stability guarantees," but no empirical comparison is provided even on the regular-sampled continuous-valued tasks where the authors claim they are unsuitable.

Strengths

  • Cleanly reduces a heavyweight bi-level Riemannian optimization (Noferini–Poloni, 7.05e4 s at n=100) to a JIT-compiled per-batch projection (7.03e-5 s) — a 9-order-of-magnitude speedup with a better NSSR.
  • Parameter efficiency at small nx (64 vs SIMBa's 164 parameters) matters for the low-dimensional real-time-control setting the authors target.
  • Honest, IQR-based reporting that does not overclaim accuracy wins.
  • BSD-3-Clause open-source release with both Keras 3 / JAX and a GPU-ready Schur decomposition.

Limitations

  • Accuracy ties (never beats) SIMBa; the only clear win is parameter count and per-step projection cost.
  • MSVR at large n suggests the truncated projection's numerical-stability guarantee degrades with matrix size; no remedy is offered.
  • No GPU runtime numbers, no large-nx (>10) experiments, no comparison to S4/Mamba even on a single task.
  • The nearest-stable guarantee of Algorithm 1 is per-block only; the global nearest-stable property is sacrificed by fixing Z, and the resulting gap is not characterised.
  • Future-work list (arbitrary eigenvalue-magnitude upper bounds, efficient GPU Schur, Najson-style LMI) is essentially an admission that the current method is incomplete relative to SIMBa's flexibility.

Relevance

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can the MSVR blow-up at n=100 be controlled by periodically re-running the full bi-level Noferini–Poloni optimization (e.g., every K batches) to refresh Z, while keeping per-batch cost low?
  2. Does the truncated projection interact well with modern selective SSMs (Mamba, S4) as a drop-in stability constraint, and does it recover HiPPO-style initialisation benefits?
  3. How does the method behave on stiff or oscillatory systems (complex-conjugate eigenvalue pairs) — the synthetic benchmark was extended to include them, but per-case results are not broken out.
  4. What is the actual wall-clock overhead on GPU at LLM-scale nx (≥256), and can the custom Schur decomposition (Appendix C) be fused with attention/Mamba kernels?
  5. Could the per-block nearest-stable projection be used as a forward-time stability check (a certificate) for any learned state-space layer, regardless of how it was trained?
