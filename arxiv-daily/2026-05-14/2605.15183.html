When Are Two Networks the Same? Tensor Similarity for Mechanistic Interpretability

arXiv: 2605.15183

Date: 2026-05-14

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.15183

一句话总结： 本文提出张量相似度（tensor similarity），一种基于权重空间且对排列对称性不变的度量方法，通过极化同构和Gram递归算法高效计算全局功能等价性，在追踪grokking、灾难性遗忘和后门注入等训练动态中显著优于行为相似性和矩阵相似度。
TL;DR: Introduces tensor similarity — a closed-form, data-free, symmetry-invariant weight-based metric for comparing multilinear (bilinear) neural networks. By projecting out weight-space symmetries via symmetrisation and the polarisation isomorphism, it provably captures global functional equivalence (including cross-layer mechanisms) through an efficient Gram-based recursive algorithm. Across four experiments (SVHN catastrophic forgetting, modular addition grokking, backdoor injection, and a 2-layer bilinear transformer on The Pile), it detects and localizes functional changes that behavioral similarity and matrix cosine miss entirely.

核心贡献

  • 提出了张量相似度（tensor similarity），将余弦相似度从向量推广到张量模型，通过在对称子空间上投影来消除权重空间的排列等对称性干扰。
  • 利用极化同构（polarisation isomorphism）建立了对称张量与多项式函数之间的一一对应，确保 sim(A,B)=1 当且仅当 A = λB (λ>0)。
  • 引入高斯度量（基于 Isserlis 定理推导），将行为相似度在 N(0,I) 输入上的期望值完全转化为权重空间的内积，无需任何数据采样。
  • 提出张量切片相似度和张量差异相似度，可将功能变化精确定位到特定输出维度或模型差异。
  • 通过Gram递归算法高效计算深层多线性模型间的内积，避免了指数级的全局张量实例化。

Key Contributions

  • Tensor similarity: generalises cosine similarity to multilinear models by computing the inner product of weight tensors projected onto the symmetric subspace via symmetrisation, with formal guarantee that sim_Pn(A,B) = 1 ⇔ ∃λ>0: A = λB (Eq. 4).
  • Polarisation isomorphism: establishes a bijection between symmetric tensors of order n and polynomials of degree n, ensuring the metric captures all and only functional equivalence.
  • Gaussian metric (Eq. 5): derives Λ = E_{x~N(0,I)}⊗^{2n}x using Isserlis' theorem, yielding a metric with S_{2n} symmetry that equals the expected behavioral inner product — entirely data-free.
  • Tensor slice and diff similarity: localises similarity to specific output dimensions or to the functional change captured by a difference tensor, enabling per-class attribution.
  • Gram-based recursion: computes inner products between deep multilinear models layer-by-layer without materialising the exponentially large global weight tensor, reducing memory from O(d^n) to O(log n).

Methodology

The paper builds on multilinear (bilinear) neural networks — variants of standard architectures that replace non-linear activations with multiplicative gating (Hadamard products), producing polynomial input-output maps. A bilinear layer maps x̃ = (1, x) via A(x) = D((Lx̃) ⊙ (Rx̃)), expressible as a tensor contraction. Bilinear attention replaces softmax with elementwise products of two linear attention patterns, yielding degree-5 polynomials. These compose into bilinear transformers competitive with standard transformers.

Tensor similarity (Eq. 3) is defined as sim_M(A, B) = ⟨A|M|B⟩ / (∥A∥_M ∥B∥_M) ∈ [-1, 1], where M = I ⊗ Λ is a metric tensor over the input-space indices. The key design choices:

  1. Symmetrisation: By projecting tensors onto the symmetric subspace (averaging over all permutations S_n), only the functionally relevant component survives (polarisation).
  2. Gaussian metric: Λ = E⊗^{2n}x computed via Isserlis' theorem, which has S_{2n} symmetry and equals the expected behavioral similarity under Gaussian inputs.
  3. Gram recursion: For deep models, inner products are computed layer-by-layer using Gram matrices that propagate from one layer to the next, avoiding global tensor materialisation.

The paper compares against: (1) behavioral similarity (CKA/logit correlation on data), (2) matrix cosine similarity (flattened weight Frobenius cosine), and (3) behavioral similarity on Gaussian inputs.

Results & Highlights

Experiment 1 — Catastrophic forgetting (SVHN): Incremental digit training {0-4} → {0-9} → remove 9 → re-add 9. Global tensor similarity reveals block structure separating stages with/without digit '9', while CKA and matrix cosine show weak/ambiguous signal. Per-class tensor slices precisely localise changes: the digit-'9' slice groups stages correctly by whether '9' was in training.

Experiment 2 — Grokking (modular addition): A single bilinear layer trained on modular addition (a+b mod 113). The K×K pairwise similarity matrix reveals three diagonal blocks (initialisation → memorisation → converged solution) that track continuous Fourier reorganisation beyond the accuracy jump. Tensor similarity makes this development legible without reverse-engineering the model.

Experiment 3 — Backdoor injection (SVHN): A black-diamond trigger relabels 10% of images as digit '9' (96% attack success, clean accuracy unchanged). Behavioral similarity on clean inputs: Δ = 0.09 (flat — attack invisible). Matrix cosine: Δ = 0.15 (weak scalar signal, no localisation). Tensor similarity on clean data alone: Δ = 0.24; digit-'9' slice sharpens to Δ = 0.43 — detects and localises the backdoor without seeing the trigger.

Experiment 4 — Language model (2-layer bilinear transformer, The Pile): 101 log-spaced checkpoints. Existing metrics (CKA on logits/activations, matrix cosine) blur all but the earliest checkpoints. Tensor similarity exhibits clear block structure with sharp transitions that align with n-gram development phases.

The theoretical contribution is rigorous and well-grounded in tensor algebra and representation theory.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: High. The combination of symmetrisation + polarisation + Gaussian metric to obtain a closed-form, data-free functional equivalence metric is novel. Prior work on weight-space similarity (SVCCA, CKA) either requires data or ignores symmetries. The framing of network similarity as a solved algebraic problem is a genuine conceptual advance.

Rigor: Strong. Theoretical guarantees are formal (Eq. 4 is a theorem, not a heuristic). Derivations connect tensor algebra (symmetrisation, polarisation, Isserlis) to the practical metric cleanly. However, all experiments use small-scale bilinear models (1–2 layers, low-dimensional tasks). The paper does not demonstrate applicability to standard (non-bilinear) architectures at any scale.

Reproducibility: Good. Code is public. The theoretical framework is self-contained with appendices providing full derivations. The experimental setups (SVHN, modular addition, The Pile) use standard benchmarks with clear descriptions.

Significance: The restriction to multilinear/bilinear models is the elephant in the room. The authors acknowledge this frankly: "This excludes most state-of-the-art architectures where non-polynomial non-linearities between components block the algebraic computation." While the authors argue bilinear models are competitive (citing Pearce et al., 2025; Riggs, 2026), this remains unproven at frontier scales. The metric is a foundational tool for the bilinear subfield but does not directly solve similarity for standard transformers with ReLU/GELU.

Red flags: (1) All baselines are somewhat straw-man — CKA and matrix cosine are known to be weak. No comparison to more recent representational similarity methods. (2) The backdoor experiment detects backdoors in bilinear models, but it is unclear whether this transfers to standard models. (3) The claim that "multilinear models can be in the same league as state-of-the-art architectures" is supported only by small-scale evidence. (4) No ablations on the choice of metric tensor beyond P_n and the Gaussian metric.

Strengths

  • Elegant algebraic foundation: reduces functional equivalence to a solved problem in tensor algebra
  • Data-free and symmetry-invariant by construction — the only metric satisfying all three desiderata (dataset independence, symmetry invariance, tractable scalability)
  • Slice and diff similarity enable per-output attribution, demonstrated convincingly on catastrophic forgetting and backdoor localisation
  • Clear exposition: the paper builds from bilinear layers → symmetrisation → polarisation → Gaussian metric in a logical progression
  • Honest about limitations: the authors explicitly state the multilinear model restriction and acknowledge incomplete attribution evidence

Limitations

  • Restricted to multilinear/bilinear models: non-polynomial non-linearities (ReLU, GELU, LayerNorm, Softmax) break the algebraic framework. Standard transformers are not covered.
  • Small-scale experiments only: SVHN classification, modular addition, a 2-layer bilinear transformer on The Pile. No experiments on models with >100M parameters.
  • Baselines are limited: comparison only against CKA and matrix cosine. No comparison to learned similarity metrics, representation engineering methods, or more recent mechanistic similarity approaches.
  • Attribution is under-demonstrated: the paper shows detection of changes more thoroughly than pinpointing which specific model components cause them.
  • The Gram recursion memory grows with hidden dimensions squared; scalability to very wide layers is unclear.

Relevance

该论文为机制可解释性提供了一个数学上严格的网络组件等价性度量，直接解决了电路分析中"两个子网络是否实现相同计算"这一基础问题。虽然目前限于双线性模型，但其理论框架对理解权重空间对称性、功能等价性验证方法具有普遍启发性，对可解释性研究和AI安全领域的研究者具有较高参考价值。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Can tensor similarity be approximated for standard (non-bilinear) transformers, e.g., by locally linearising activations or using polynomial approximations?
  • How does computational cost scale with hidden dimension and number of layers for the Gram recursion in practice?
  • Could the Gaussian metric be replaced with a task-specific distribution to focus similarity on functionally relevant input regions?
  • Does tensor similarity extend naturally to comparing circuits across different architectures (e.g., a bilinear model vs. a standard transformer)?
  • How does the metric behave under quantization, pruning, or distillation — can it detect when a compressed model functionally approximates the original?
