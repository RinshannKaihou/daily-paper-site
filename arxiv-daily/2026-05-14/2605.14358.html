Uncovering the Representation Geometry of Minimal Cores in Overcomplete Reasoning Traces

arXiv: 2605.14358

Date: 2026-05-14

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.14358

⚠️ Abstract-only summary

一句话总结： 本文提出"最小核心"概念，从过度完整的CoT推理链中提取最少的必要步骤，发现平均46%的步骤可被移除，且最小核心能显著改善推理轨迹的几何表示质量。
TL;DR: The paper introduces "minimal cores" — the smallest subsets of chain-of-thought reasoning steps that preserve a model's final answer or predictive distribution — and shows that 46% of steps in LLM reasoning traces are removable, while minimal cores reveal a cleaner representation geometry with better separability and lower intrinsic dimensionality.

核心贡献

  • 定义了最小核心（minimal core）：推理链中保持最终答案或预测分布不变的最小步骤子集，并提出了压缩比、冗余质量、步骤必要性和必要性集中度四个量化指标。
  • 在六个涵盖算术、竞赛数学、专家科学推理和常识多跳问答的基准测试上，发现LLM推理轨迹平均46%的步骤可被移除，且在86%的情况下保留原始答案。
  • 发现预测支持高度集中：排名前三的步骤平均贡献65%的必要性质量。
  • 证明最小核心能改善推理轨迹的几何性质：正确/错误轨迹分离度提升11个百分点，估计内在维度降低34%，且可跨模型家族迁移（85%的非对角线答案保留率）。
  • 从理论上建立了最小充分子集的存在性、贪心消除的局部不可约性保证以及过度完整性和稀疏必要性的证书。

Key Contributions

  • Define the minimal core — the smallest subset of reasoning steps preserving the final answer or predictive distribution — with four novel metrics: compression ratio, redundancy mass, step necessity, and necessity concentration.
  • Demonstrate substantial overcompleteness across six deliberative reasoning benchmarks: 46% of steps are removable on average while preserving the original answer in 86% of cases.
  • Show that predictive support is highly concentrated: the top three steps account for 65% of measured necessity mass on average.
  • Reveal that minimal cores expose a cleaner geometry of reasoning: +11 points correct-incorrect trace separation, -34% estimated intrinsic dimensionality, and 85% cross-model-family answer retention.
  • Provide theoretical foundations: existence of minimal sufficient subsets, local irreducibility guarantees for greedy elimination, and certificates of overcompleteness and sparse necessity.

Methodology

The authors study overcomplete reasoning traces — chain-of-thought outputs containing more intermediate steps than needed. Their approach involves:

  1. Minimal-core extraction: A greedy elimination procedure that iteratively removes the least necessary step while checking whether the model's answer (or predictive distribution) is preserved. This identifies the smallest subset of steps that still supports the final prediction.
  2. Metrics suite: Four complementary metrics quantify different aspects of trace overcompleteness — compression ratio (fraction of removable steps), redundancy mass, step necessity (per-step contribution), and necessity concentration (how top-heavy the necessity distribution is).
  3. Geometric analysis: Comparing the representation-space geometry of full traces versus minimal cores, including correct/incorrect trace separability, intrinsic dimensionality estimation, and cross-model transfer of cores.
  4. Theoretical framework: Formal guarantees for the existence of minimal sufficient subsets, local irreducibility of the greedy elimination procedure, and certificates that certify overcompleteness and sparse necessity.
  5. Benchmarks: Six deliberative reasoning benchmarks spanning arithmetic, competition mathematics, expert scientific reasoning, and commonsense multi-hop QA.

Results & Highlights

  • 46% average step removability: Nearly half of all reasoning steps can be removed without changing the final answer, indicating massive redundancy in LLM chain-of-thought outputs.
  • 86% answer preservation: After greedy minimal-core extraction, the original answer is preserved in the vast majority of cases.
  • 65% necessity concentration in top-3 steps: Predictive support is sharply concentrated in a small number of critical steps, suggesting most steps contribute little to the final prediction.
  • +11 points trace separability: Minimal cores separate correct from incorrect traces significantly better than full traces, suggesting they isolate the "signal" from the "noise."
  • -34% intrinsic dimensionality: The effective dimensionality of reasoning representations drops substantially when restricted to minimal cores.
  • 85% cross-model transfer: Minimal cores extracted from one model family retain answers when applied to another, suggesting the identified critical steps are model-agnostic.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. The concept of minimal cores in reasoning traces is novel and provides a principled framework for understanding redundancy in chain-of-thought. The geometric analysis and theoretical guarantees add depth beyond empirical observation.
  • Rigor: High. The paper combines empirical evaluation across six diverse benchmarks, a suite of complementary metrics, and theoretical foundations. The greedy elimination procedure is well-defined and the cross-model transfer experiments strengthen the claims.
  • Reproducibility: Moderate-High. The metrics and extraction procedure are clearly described. Full reproducibility would depend on access to the specific model checkpoints and benchmarks used.
  • Significance: High. If reasoning traces are indeed 46% redundant on average, this has direct implications for inference efficiency, training data curation, and our understanding of how LLMs actually "reason."
  • Red flags: (1) The abstract-only nature of this review limits assessment of experimental details, ablations, and edge cases. (2) The 86% answer preservation means 14% of answers change — the characterization of failure modes is important but not assessable from the abstract. (3) Greedy elimination may not find the true minimal core; the gap between greedy and optimal solutions needs scrutiny.

Strengths

  • Principled formalization of a widely observed but poorly understood phenomenon (verbose CoT).
  • Comprehensive metric suite that captures complementary aspects of overcompleteness.
  • Strong empirical results across diverse benchmarks.
  • Theoretical guarantees provide formal grounding.
  • The geometric analysis — showing minimal cores improve separability and reduce dimensionality — is a particularly elegant finding.

Limitations

  • Greedy elimination is a heuristic; optimal minimal cores may differ and the approximation gap is not characterized empirically.
  • 14% answer change rate after extraction raises questions about which types of reasoning tasks are most fragile.
  • The analysis is post-hoc (analyzing generated traces) rather than interventional (generating minimal cores directly).
  • The practical implications — can models be trained to generate minimal cores directly? — are not addressed.
  • Computational cost of the greedy elimination procedure at scale is unclear.

Questions for Further Exploration

  4. Could minimal cores serve as a training signal for more efficient reasoning — e.g., distilling minimal cores into shorter-chain training data?
