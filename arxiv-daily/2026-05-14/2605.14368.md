> **一句话总结：** DiHAL 利用曲率、单调性和有效秩三个几何代理指标定位预训练 Transformer 中对扩散最友好的浅层隐藏空间，用条件扩散桥替换该层以下的 Transformer 前缀，在隐藏状态层面（而非 token 层面）进行重建，在 8B 规模模型上几何评分与 bridgeability 的 Spearman 相关性超过 0.91。

> **TL;DR:** DiHAL proposes geometry-based scoring (curvature, monotonicity, effective rank) to identify diffusion-friendly layers in pretrained transformers, replaces the lower prefix with a conditional diffusion bridge that reconstructs hidden states instead of tokens, and achieves strong correlation (Spearman ρ > 0.91) between geometry scores and actual bridgeability on Llama-3.1-8B and Qwen3-8B.

## 核心贡献

- 首次将"扩散应插入 Transformer 哪一层"形式化为几何引导的接口选择问题，提出三个可计算的层间代理指标：局部曲率（m̂_curv）、全局单调性（m̂_mono）、有效秩（k̂）
- 设计固定几何评分 selection_score = z(log m̂_curv) + z(log m̂_mono) − z(log k̂)，无需逐层训练桥模块即可预测最优插入点
- 提出 Locate-and-Replace 策略：用 UNet 条件扩散桥替换选定层以下的 Transformer 前缀，保留上层和原始 LM head，将扩散目标从 token 恢复转为隐藏状态重建
- 在 Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct 和 Qwen3-8B 两个 8B 规模骨干上验证，几何评分与 bridgeability 的 Spearman 相关性分别达 0.914 和 0.927
- 在诊断性等预算比较中，DiHAL 的 Gen.PPL（136.02）和多样性（0.5913）均优于 Diffusion-LM、SED、LD4LG 和 CoDAR

## Key Contributions

- Formulates diffusion insertion in pretrained transformers as a geometry-guided interface-selection problem with three layer-wise proxies: local curvature (m̂_curv), global monotonicity (m̂_mono), and effective rank (k̂)
- Introduces a fixed geometry score combining z-score-normalized proxies that predicts bridgeability without exhaustive layer-wise bridge training (Spearman ρ = 0.914–0.927)
- Proposes the Locate-and-Replace strategy: replaces the lower transformer prefix with a conditional UNet-based diffusion bridge while retaining upper layers and the original LM head
- Reframes continuous diffusion for language as hidden-state reconstruction rather than direct token recovery, bypassing the continuous-to-discrete bottleneck
- Validates on Llama-3.1-8B and Qwen3-8B, showing geometry-selected shallow layers (layer 3 and 2 respectively) are competitive with validation-loss oracles after full training

## Methodology

DiHAL follows a three-step Locate-and-Replace pipeline grounded in theoretical motivation from Langevin dynamics and concentration theory.

**Geometric motivation.** Three theorems motivate diffusion-friendly properties: Theorem 1 shows larger curvature parameter m yields faster W₂ contraction under Langevin dynamics; Theorem 2 bounds distributional error under score perturbation by ε/m (stability); Theorem 3 connects low effective rank (via manifold approximation) with controlled representation complexity. These are not assumptions about real hidden states but qualitative desiderata.

**Locate step.** For each transformer layer, three empirical spectral proxies are computed from hidden-state activations over 100 repeated 3K-example subsamples: (1) m̂_curv from k-NN local covariance (1/λ_max of neighborhood covariance, median-pooled); (2) m̂_mono from global precision-based Mahalanobis stiffness on sampled pairs; (3) k̂ = reff(Σ) = tr(Σ)/∥Σ∥. The combined selection score uses z-score normalization: z(log m̂_curv) + z(log m̂_mono) − z(log k̂). The optimal layer ℓ* = argmax of this score.

**Replace step.** Given ℓ*, lower transformer layers (0 to ℓ*) are replaced with a UNet-based conditional diffusion bridge. The bridge is embedding-conditioned (using source model embeddings as condition), trained to reconstruct h_{ℓ*} with diffusion loss L_diff, reconstruction loss L_rec, next-token loss L_LM, and logit-distillation loss L_KD. At inference, the bridge denoises to produce ĥ_{ℓ*}, which is processed by retained upper layers F_{ℓ*+1:L} and the original LM head for token prediction. The architecture uses Stable-Diffusion-v1.5-style latent denoising components repurposed for hidden-state denoising (no image supervision).

## Results & Highlights

- **Geometry predicts bridgeability:** Fixed geometry score achieves Spearman ρ = 0.9143 (Llama) and 0.9267 (Qwen) with validation bridge loss, rank gaps of only 2 and 1 between predicted and observed best layers.
- **Shallow layers are optimal:** Geometry selects layer 3 for Llama-3.1-8B and layer 2 for Qwen3-8B, both embedding-adjacent. Deeper layers have much lower curvature and higher effective rank, making them poorly suited for diffusion.
- **Diagnostic comparison (Table 3):** Under matched 40 H100-hour budget on 300K examples, DiHAL achieves Gen.PPL 136.02 and diversity 0.5913, outperforming Diffusion-LM (683.43 / 0.4324), SED (778.82 / 0.4942), LD4LG (166.11 / 0.5797), and CoDAR (144.83 / 0.4777).
- **Full training (Table 4):** On Llama-3.1-8B, geometry-selected layer 3 achieves NLL 4.91 / PPL 135.64, outperforming the validation-loss oracle (5.11 / 165.67). On Qwen3-8B, geometry-selected layer 2 achieves 4.97 / 144.03, competitive with oracle (4.94 / 139.77). Both substantially outperform worst-layer controls (~186–176 PPL) and CoDAR (5.18 / 177.87).
- **Score stability:** Over 30 repeated 500-example estimation runs, geometry scores show low variance (Spearman std: 0.007–0.016).

## Authors & Credibility

The authors are Injin Kong and Yohan Jo from the Graduate School of Data Science at Seoul National University, and Hyoungjoon Lee from the Department of Biosystems & Biomaterials Science and Engineering at Seoul National University. The work is technically rigorous, grounding empirical proxies in formal theorems from optimal transport and concentration of measure theory. Experiments are at meaningful scale (8B parameters, 40 H100-hour budgets) with proper controls including validation-loss oracles and worst-layer baselines. The paper honestly frames results as diagnostic rather than claiming state-of-the-art language modeling performance.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** High. The framing of "where should diffusion enter" is original, and the geometry-guided layer selection with formal theoretical motivation is a fresh contribution. The Locate-and-Replace strategy is elegant.
- **Rigor:** Strong. Formal theorems motivate proxies, controlled experiments isolate the contribution of each component, correlation analyses include confidence intervals over 30 repetitions, and the paper clearly distinguishes diagnostic comparisons from pretraining-matched evaluations.
- **Reproducibility:** Moderate. Geometry scoring should be reproducible from the paper description. However, the 8B-scale bridge training requires significant compute (40 H100-hours). Code release is not mentioned in the extracted text.
- **Significance:** The insight that representation-space geometry—not just discreteness—explains diffusion language modeling difficulties is important. The geometry score as a cheap proxy for bridgeability could become a useful tool. However, the practical impact is currently limited: DiHAL's PPL (135–144) is far from the original autoregressive teacher, and the approach is not a standalone diffusion LM.
- **Red flags:** (1) The absolute PPL gap vs. autoregressive teachers is large—DiHAL is more of a proof-of-concept than a practical alternative. (2) The paper repurposes Stable-Diffusion-v1.5 UNet components for hidden-state denoising, which is an unusual engineering choice whose impact is not fully ablated. (3) Only embedding-adjacent layers work well, which limits how much of the transformer can be replaced. (4) The comparison against baselines is diagnostic (same budget for diffusion/recovery components), not a fair apples-to-apples comparison since DiHAL reuses a pretrained transformer suffix.

## Strengths

- Principled theoretical motivation connecting Langevin dynamics, concentration theory, and practical proxy design
- Clean experimental design with matched budgets, oracle controls, and stability analyses
- Elegant reframing: hidden-state recovery instead of token recovery bypasses the continuous-to-discrete bottleneck
- Strong correlation between geometry scores and actual bridgeability validates the core hypothesis
- Honest framing as diagnostic study rather than overclaiming practical performance

## Limitations

- DiHAL is not a standalone diffusion language model—it depends on retained transformer suffix and LM head for token prediction
- Only shallow (embedding-adjacent) layers are diffusion-friendly, limiting the extent of transformer replacement
- Large absolute PPL gap compared to autoregressive teachers (~135–144 vs. what the original models achieve)
- The 8B-scale experiments require significant compute resources
- Unclear whether the approach scales to replacing more transformer layers or to models with different architectures
- The UNet bridge architecture is borrowed from image generation without thorough justification for its suitability to hidden-state denoising

## Relevance

DiHAL 通过几何分析揭示了 Transformer 内部不同层隐藏空间的扩散友好性差异，这对理解大语言模型内部表示结构及探索扩散-自回归混合架构设计具有直接参考价值，尤其在扩散语言模型的接口选择问题上提供了理论启发性结论。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can the geometry proxies be used as regularization during bridge training to make deeper layers more bridgeable?
- How do the geometry profiles differ across model families (e.g., MoE architectures, encoder-decoder models)?
- Is the UNet the optimal architecture for hidden-state denoising, or could transformer-based denoisers perform better?
- Could progressive replacement (replacing multiple layers sequentially) improve the extent of diffusion-based replacement?
- How does the causal masking interface interact with the diffusion bridge at longer generation lengths?
- What is the minimum compute budget needed for the geometry scoring to remain reliable?
