Key Contributions

  • Employs policy-driven sampling to approximate the feasible set of hidden states reachable under a trained policy, reducing conservatism from verifying unreachable regions.

Methodology

RNN-ProVe addresses the core challenge of verifying RNN policies: the hidden state space grows unboundedly with sequence length and is difficult to characterize exactly. Rather than over-approximating the entire hidden state space (as deterministic verifiers do), RNN-ProVe takes a probabilistic approach:

  1. Policy-driven sampling: Execute the trained RNN policy in the environment to collect trajectories, recording the hidden states visited. This constructs an empirical approximation of the feasible hidden state set — states the policy actually reaches.
  1. Statistical estimation: Using the sampled trajectories, estimate the probability that the policy enters an undesired region of the state-action space. Statistical error bounds (likely based on concentration inequalities such as Hoeffding's or Clopper-Pearson) provide confidence intervals on these probability estimates.
  1. Multi-agent extension: The framework handles cooperative multi-agent settings where each agent may have its own RNN with independent hidden state dynamics, and the joint policy's behavior must be verified.

This approach trades the completeness guarantees of deterministic verification for quantitative, feasibility-aware probabilistic guarantees that scale to settings where deterministic methods are either inapplicable or overly conservative.

Strengths

  • Moves beyond binary (safe/unsafe) verification to quantitative probabilistic estimates, which are often more useful in practice.
  • The policy-driven sampling idea is elegant — verifying only reachable states is both principled and computationally efficient.
  • Handles both single-agent and multi-agent partially observable settings, broadening applicability.
  • Avoids the conservatism of over-approximation-based deterministic methods.

Limitations

  • Probabilistic guarantees are only as good as the sampling coverage — rare but catastrophic failures in unsampled regions may be missed.
  • The approach assumes the training environment adequately represents deployment conditions (distribution shift is not addressed in the abstract).
  • Sample complexity for tight confidence bounds may be high, especially in multi-agent settings with large joint hidden state spaces.
  • Does not provide deterministic safety certificates, which may be required in high-stakes domains (e.g., autonomous driving, medical).
