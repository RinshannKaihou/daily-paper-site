> **一句话总结：** 本文从理论上和实验上证明，学习率衰减末段的"冷却（cooldown）"是否有益并非由损失函数或梯度噪声大小决定，而是由噪声结构（各向同性 vs. 各向异性）与优化器归一化（如 Adam/AdamW 中逐参数的二阶矩归一化）共同决定；当归一化被去除时，冷却的收益会消失甚至反转。

> **TL;DR:** This paper shows that whether a learning-rate cooldown (linear decay to zero at the end of training) improves generalization is jointly determined by the *structure* of gradient noise and *optimizer normalization*: with SGD-style isotropic noise and unnormalized updates, cooldown helps; but for Adam/AdamW with per-parameter RMS normalization on anisotropic noise, cooldown can become neutral or harmful, with experiments on CIFAR-10/CIFAR-100 and Transformer language models showing cooldown reverses its benefit when the optimizer's normalization is removed or when noise becomes anisotropic.

## 核心贡献

- **揭示"冷却"收益的真正决定因素**：首次系统论证学习率冷却（linear cooldown to zero）是否有益不取决于损失函数本身，而是取决于梯度噪声的几何结构（各向同性 isotropic vs. 各向异性 anisotropic）以及优化器是否做逐参数归一化（optimizer normalization）。
- **统一解释 SGD 与 Adam 的差异**：在同一损失、同一噪声大小下，仅改变噪声结构和优化器归一化，即可使相同的冷却调度产生相反的效果——为 SGD 上"冷却有益"和 Adam 上"冷却中性/有害"的经典经验现象提供了统一理论解释。
- **给出可证的理论分析**：基于随机近似（stochastic approximation, SA）框架与冗余维（redundant dimension）分析，证明了冷却收益的存在条件与方向，并指出该收益等价于隐式地调整了有效步长/更新尺度。
- **跨任务、跨架构的实验验证**：在 CIFAR-10、CIFAR-100（ResNet）、Transformer 语言模型（LM）以及合成实验上验证结论，并提供了若干"干预"实验（去除归一化、改变噪声结构）来印证因果机制，而非仅相关性。

## Key Contributions

- **Decomposes the cooldown puzzle into two axes.** Rather than attributing the benefit of LR cooldown to loss sharpness or implicit regularization alone, the paper isolates two factors — (i) the *structure* of stochastic gradient noise (isotropic vs. anisotropic, with/without redundant/null directions) and (ii) *optimizer normalization* (whether updates are rescaled per-parameter, as Adam's second-moment RMS does) — and shows these jointly determine cooldown's effect.
- **Derives an analytical condition under SA theory.** Using stochastic-approximation arguments and a redundant-dimension decomposition, the authors prove when and why an end-of-training linear decay-to-zero schedule reduces final generalization error, and crucially that optimizer normalization can absorb/cancel this benefit.
- **Demonstrates "same loss, same noise magnitude, opposite schedule outcomes."** Through controlled experiments the paper shows that the *same* objective and noise level can yield opposite cooldown effects when the optimizer's normalization or the noise anisotropy is changed — turning the conventional wisdom ("cooldown always helps") into a conditional statement.
- **Reconciles the SGD-vs-Adam empirical discrepancy.** Provides a principled explanation for why cooldown is reliably beneficial with SGD on vision tasks but often neutral or harmful with Adam/AdamW, especially on Transformers / language models.
- **Validates across domains.** Empirical evidence is provided on image classification (CIFAR-10/100, ResNet), Transformer language modeling, and synthetic controlled setups with ablations that manipulate normalization and noise structure directly.

## Methodology

The paper combines a theoretical analysis with controlled empirical experiments.

**Theoretical setup (stochastic approximation view).** Training is cast as a stochastic approximation (SA) process on parameters θ with update direction `d_t` driven by a noisy gradient. The authors separate the parameter space into two subspaces: a *signal* subspace (directions along which the true gradient has nonzero projection) and a *redundant* / *null* subspace (directions along which the true gradient vanishes but stochastic noise persists). They show that the contribution of redundant-direction noise to final error scales with the *effective step size*, and that a linear cooldown to zero progressively shrinks this effective step size near convergence, suppressing the accumulation of noise-driven drift in redundant directions.

**The two governing factors.**
1. *Noise structure.* If the stochastic gradient noise is approximately **isotropic**, redundant directions receive noise comparable to signal directions, so reducing the step size via cooldown meaningfully reduces final redundant drift → cooldown helps. If the noise is strongly **anisotropic** (concentrated along a few principal directions, as is common in language models), the redundant subspace carries little noise, so the marginal benefit of shrinking the step size is small or negative.
2. *Optimizer normalization.* For Adam/AdamW, the per-coordinate division by the running second moment `v_t` (RMS) rescales each update independently, which changes how a global LR multiplier propagates into per-parameter steps. The paper shows this normalization effectively *absorbs* part of the cooldown-induced step-size reduction — and in anisotropic regimes can even invert it — so the schedule's theoretical benefit fails to materialize.

**Experimental design.** The authors construct controlled experiments that hold the loss and overall noise magnitude fixed while varying (a) noise isotropy (e.g., via data ordering, batch composition, or synthetic noise injection) and (b) optimizer normalization (Adam/AdamW vs. an Adam variant with normalization removed, and vs. SGD). They then compare final test performance for otherwise-identical schedules with and without a linear cooldown phase. They also run standard CIFAR-10/CIFAR-100 image classification with ResNets and Transformer language-model training to confirm that the predictions of the theory hold in realistic settings. The key diagnostic is whether the sign of the cooldown gap (test accuracy with cooldown minus without) flips when normalization or noise structure is changed.

## Results & Highlights

- The paper documents that **a linear LR cooldown reliably improves SGD on CIFAR-10/CIFAR-100 ResNet training** (consistent with prior work), but its benefit **shrinks or disappears for Adam/AdamW** — and the gap tracks the optimizer's per-parameter normalization rather than the loss itself.
- By **removing the RMS normalization from Adam** (so updates revert to a shared step scale), the cooldown effect reverts toward the SGD behavior: a schedule that was neutral/harmful under normalized Adam becomes beneficial once normalization is removed, confirming that normalization — not the loss — is what gates the benefit.
- When the authors deliberately make the **stochastic gradient noise anisotropic** (concentrated along principal directions, mirroring the LM regime), the benefit of cooldown weakens or reverses, while under isotropic noise cooldown's benefit is recovered — directly validating the "noise structure" axis of the theory.
- For **Transformer language models**, cooldown's generalization benefit is shown to be small-to-negative, consistent with the anisotropic-noise + Adam-normalization regime predicted by the analysis; this matches the common practical observation that LM schedules often prefer cosine decay or a plateau rather than an aggressive zero-cooldown.
- Synthetic controlled experiments confirm the **redundant-dimension mechanism**: final error reduction from cooldown is concentrated in the redundant/noise-carrying subspace and scales with the effective step size that cooldown suppresses — matching the SA-theoretic prediction.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (moderate-to-high).** The core claim — that noise structure *and* optimizer normalization jointly, not loss sharpness alone, determine whether LR cooldown helps — is a genuinely useful reframing. Prior work has separately studied LR schedules, SGD noise benefits, and Adam normalization, but the explicit *factorial* decomposition (same loss × same noise magnitude × varying structure/normalization → opposite outcomes) is a clean, testable thesis. The redundant-dimension / SA framing is consistent with known SGLD and implicit-bias analyses but is here tied specifically to the cooldown phase.

- **Rigor (mixed).** The strongest evidence is the controlled factorial experiments isolating noise isotropy and optimizer normalization — these are exactly the right interventions to support a causal claim rather than a correlational one, and the paper reports the *sign flips* that the theory predicts. However, several quantitative claims are described qualitatively in the abstract/intro (e.g., "benefit shrinks or reverses"); a reader will want the full tables (in the HTML body) to see exact deltas, confidence intervals, and seed variance. The theoretical result appears to be an asymptotic/SAGA-style argument rather than a non-vacuous finite-sample bound, so its predictive precision for realistic (short-horizon, non-convex) training should be treated as a qualitative guide.

- **Reproducibility (likely good).** The interventions are standard and implementable (toggle Adam's `v_t` division, change batch/data-order to alter noise isotropy, swap SGD↔Adam). Datasets are public (CIFAR-10/100; standard LM corpora). The main reproducibility risk is the exact definition/measurement of "noise structure / anisotropy," which is a known hard-to-pinch quantity; without a single agreed metric, independent groups may operationalize it differently.

- **Significance (medium).** The practical takeaway — "don't assume cooldown helps; it depends on your optimizer and your noise geometry, and for Adam on LMs it often won't" — is actionable and aligns with practitioner experience. It is unlikely to change fundamentals, but it sharpens schedule-selection heuristics and explains a long-standing SGD-vs-Adam discrepancy.

- **Red flags (minor).** (i) The paper's title is unusually strong ("Same Loss, Same Noise, Opposite Schedules") — it should be read as "same loss and *marginal* noise magnitude, with different noise *structure*," otherwise the title overclaims. (ii) As a 2026-07 preprint (HTML-only at time of review), the full numerical tables and appendix ablations were not available for independent verification here; claims should be checked against the complete results section. (iii) The "removing Adam normalization re-enables cooldown benefit" result is the linchpin of the mechanism — its robustness across architectures beyond the tested set is the key open question.

## Strengths

- Clean factorial experimental logic: holding loss and noise magnitude fixed while independently varying noise structure and optimizer normalization is exactly how a "what determines X" question should be attacked.
- Provides a *unified* explanation for the well-known but previously disconnected facts that cooldown helps SGD/vision but is often neutral/harmful for Adam/LMs.
- Theory (SA + redundant-dimension decomposition) and experiments are mutually reinforcing; the predicted sign flips are observed.
- Actionable guidance for practitioners choosing LR schedules per optimizer/task regime.

## Limitations

- The theoretical analysis is asymptotic / stochastic-approximation flavored; its quantitative predictive power for short-horizon non-convex deep training is not established.
- "Noise structure / anisotropy" lacks a single canonical measurement, making the key independent variable somewhat slippery to operationalize.
- Empirical scope, while spanning CIFAR and Transformer LMs, may not cover all optimizer/schedule/architecture combinations (e.g., Lion, Shampoo, Sophia, schedule-free variants), leaving generalization open.
- The headline framing ("same loss, same noise") elides that noise *structure* is being changed; readers could over-interpret the title.
- As a fresh preprint, full numerical tables and appendix ablations were not independently verifiable in this review.

## Relevance

对关注深度学习训练动力学、学习率调度选择与优化器设计的研究者而言，本文提供了一个实用且可操作的诊断框架：是否使用 cooldown 不再是"默认有益"的经验法则，而是取决于噪声结构与优化器归一化的组合，这对大规模 Transformer / 语言模型训练的调度策略选择尤其有指导意义。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the predicted cooldown benefit also **flip with normalization** for newer optimizers (Lion, Shampoo, Muon, Sophia, schedule-free Adam) whose update rescaling differs from classic Adam RMS?
- How should "noise anisotropy" be **measured at scale** during real LM pretraining, and is there a cheap online proxy that predicts whether cooldown will help before the full run completes?
- Can the redundant-dimension argument yield a **non-vacuous finite-sample** bound, or a principled *length* for the cooldown phase as a function of noise spectrum and `v_t` statistics?
- For **fine-tuning** (where the signal/redundant subspace is shaped by the pretrained weights), does the cooldown–normalization interaction reverse or amplify compared to pretraining?
- Does the mechanism extend to **non-monotonic schedules** (warm restarts, cosine, WSD) — i.e., is "cooldown benefit" specifically about the linear-to-zero tail, or about *any* terminal step-size collapse under anisotropic + normalized updates?
