LLM Judges Can Be Too Generous When There Is No Reference Answer

In reference-free settings, LLM judges systematically over-credit wrong answers — and simply showing the reference flips up to 85% of verdicts, mostly toward human agreement.

Chalamalasetti Kranti · Univ. of Potsdam    Sowmya Vajjala · National Research Council Canada

Why This Matters
Any reference-free LLM-judge correctness score — especially in low-resource languages — may be silently inflated and should be audited before being trusted.

A two-stage diagnostic, not a benchmark

calibration → sensitivity

The judge pipeline is fixed: extract a core answer, then emit a CORRECT / INCORRECT verdict with explanation, all zero-shot, temperature = 0, via the Inspect framework. The contribution is the testing harness around it: first ask "does the judge understand the task?", then "how does the reference answer's presence & position bend the verdict?"

Gold candidate
Feed the correct gold answer; expect ≈ 100% CORRECT.

Feed a believable wrong answer (from other QA pairs); expect ≈ 0%. Gap = calibration.
Stage 2 · Sensitivity
NR → RV → RC
Reference visibility ramp
No ref → ref visible (no compare) → explicit compare-to-ref. Each step measured for flip rate.

01
Scores fall monotonically: NR > RV > RC
Visibility alone moves the needle: NR→RV flips 0.09–0.85, while forcing an explicit comparison adds only RV→RC 0.01–0.24.
02
Telugu fails calibration hardest
Gemma3-27B stamps C2 = 60% of planted wrong Telugu answers as correct (gap 0.33); EN/AR sit near 0, gap > 0.79.
03
Flips withdraw over-credits, not grant new ones
NR→RV changes are overwhelmingly C→I — the inflated NR score came from wrongly accepted answers.
04
Same answer, different verdict (EAS-VC)
Often the extracted answer is identical, but the verdict changes — the reference reshapes how the judge reasons, not what it sees.
05
Reference moves the judge toward humans
400 native-annotated MATA items, κ = 0.96–0.99; judge↔human alignment climbs under RV, peaks at 0.98 under RC.
06
Position is an independent lever
Placing the reference after the answer (RVP/RCP) drops some Telugu scores further (e.g. Sarvam responses 0.35 → 0.25).

Strengths & limitations

side by side

Strengths PRO

  • Reusable blueprint. Calibration + flip decomposition + extracted-answer analysis = a portable "judge health-check".
  • Clean controls. Zero-shot, temp 0, position variants — confounds are deliberately pinned.
  • Rare multilingual breadth. EN/AR/TE across scripts, with Sarvam (TE) & Fanar (AR) specialists.
  • Closed loop with humans. 400 native items, κ ≈ 0.97–0.99, makes the "reference helps" claim credible.
  • Honest failure catalog. Appendix A.4 shows hallucinated answers, gold overrides, self-family bias.

Limitations CON

  • Still needs a little gold. Calibration requires a small labelled set — can't fully self-bootstrap pure reference-free cases.
  • Narrow coverage. 3 languages, 2 datasets, 3 judges, 4 generators; generalization to summarization/code/reasoning untested.
  • RC vs RV unstable. RV→RC gains are small & inconsistent; no rule for when RC actually pays off.
  • Coarse binary verdict. Forced CORRECT/INCORRECT hides "partially correct" boundary cases.
  • Self-/family bias unquantified. Overlap is observed but not statistically measured.

Questions for further exploration

Does over-credit survive outside QA — in summarization, code, or math reasoning, where correctness isn't binary?
Can self-/same-family bias be quantified as a covariate on flip rate, rather than only observed?
EAS-VC shows the judge oscillating between parametric and reference-based reasoning — can a prompt lock one mode to cut instability?
For zero-gold settings, can weak supervision (multi-sample voting, cross-model agreement) substitute for the calibration gold set?
RC shows 30–70% I→C flips — explicit comparison sometimes loosens the judge. What triggers this reverse effect?
Are family-level over-credit gaps driven by pretraining coverage or by RLHF judging style? Could C1/C2 gap serve as a per-language usability index?
