> **一句话总结：** 本文将 Pesme & Flammarion (2023) 关于两层对角线性网络在无穷小初始化下的鞍点到鞍点动力学，推广到深层对角线性网络和一般两层对角线性网络，证明这两类模型的隐式偏置是一个修正的 ℓ1 范数，并利用"结构不变流形 (SIM)"揭示其背后的几何机制。

> **TL;DR:** Extends Pesme & Flammarion (2023) by proving that, under infinitesimal initialization, both deep diagonal linear networks and a broader class of two-layer diagonal linear networks (Definition 4.1) follow saddle-to-saddle dynamics exactly characterized by a recursive Algorithm 1, and converge to a *modified* ℓ1-norm minimizer (R(k)=Σ(t_i^+ k_i^+ + t_i^- k_i^-)) rather than the standard ℓ1 solution; the mechanism is explained via the Structural Invariant Manifold (SIM).

## 核心贡献

1. **训练轨迹的推广 (Theorem 4.5):** 将 Pesme & Flammarion (2023) Theorem 1 中仅适用于标准两层对角线性网络、且深层网络仅在特定初始化方向下成立的结果，推广到(a) 一般两层对角线性网络 (Definition 4.1，允许任意实解析的 k_i(θ_i))，以及 (b) 深层网络在通用初始化方向下成立。证明梯度流轨迹可被 Algorithm 1 等价刻画。
2. **隐式偏置的刻画 (Corollary 4.6):** 推广 Woodworth et al. (2020)、Gunasekar et al. (2017) 的 ℓ1 隐式偏置结论。在无穷小初始化下，深层和一般两层对角线性网络的隐式偏置都是一个"修正的 ℓ1 范数" R(k)=Σ(t_i^+ k_i^+ + t_i^- k_i^-)，其中 t_i^± 由初始化方向决定。对深层网络，t_i^± = ±F_i(±∞)，F_i 由一个与初始化方向相关的积分定义；对一般两层网络，t_i^± = 1/λ_i^+ 与 -1/λ_i^-，即 Hessian 极端特征值的倒数。
3. **机制解释 (Section 6):** 首次用 Structural Invariant Manifold (SIM) 这一几何结构解释"为什么动力学等价于递归算法"。SIM 是对任意解析损失都保持不变的子流形 (Definition 6.1)，作者证明它正是向量场族 ℱ={∇_θ F(·)(x)} 的 orbit 之并 (Theorem 6.3, 6.5)。

## Key Contributions

- **Generalized trajectory theorem (Thm 4.5):** Extends saddle-to-saddle dynamics beyond the symmetric two-layer case to deep networks with generic initialization directions and to a broad class of two-layer networks where each k_i is any real-analytic function with a unique critical point at 0 and a Hessian with a unique max/min eigenvalue.
- **Modified ℓ1 implicit bias (Corollary 4.6):** Rather than the plain ℓ1 norm, the implicit bias is R(k)=Σ_i (t_i^+ k_i^+ + t_i^- k_i^-), a weighted/asymmetric ℓ1-type objective whose weights depend on initialization direction. This is a strict generalization of standard ℓ1 results.
- **A convergent recursive algorithm (Algorithm 1 + Theorem 4.2):** Provides a discrete "feature selection + sign-constrained least squares" algorithm and proves it terminates finitely and outputs the minimizer of the modified ℓ1 problem (verified via KKT conditions).
- **Mechanistic explanation via SIM (Section 6):** Identifies the Structural Invariant Manifold (orbit of ℱ) as the geometric object that (i) traps infinitesimally-initialized parameters near zero for O(1/s) time → induces the two-phase (feature-selection then learning) structure, and (ii) forces sign-locking of features during the learning phase.

## Methodology

The proof proceeds in two conceptual steps:

1. **Algorithm equivalence (Theorem 4.5).** The gradient flow trajectory Γ^s initialized at s·θ* is shown, in the limit s→0, to visit a sequence of points whose k-vectors are exactly {k^(p)} produced by Algorithm 1. The proof relies on:
   - **Distance-Speed Argument (§5.1):** During the feature-selection phase, v(t)≈v_0 is nearly constant; after time-rescaling, the conserved quantities a_j²-c²=μ² isolate a "distance" D⁺ (an integral depending only on the initialization *direction*) divided by a "speed" |v_0| (depending on feature-target correlation). The neuron minimizing D⁺/|v_0| activates first.
   - **Time-scale separation (§5.2):** Feature selection takes O(1/s); learning takes O(1). So learning is instantaneous relative to selection, and no new features grow away from zero during learning.
   - **Sign-locking (§5.2):** Because parameters are confined to SIM orbits (simple curves), flipping a feature's sign would require returning to the O(s) initialization, which costs O(1/s) time — impossible within the finite learning window.
2. **Modified ℓ1 convergence (Theorem 4.2).** Algorithm 1 is shown to terminate in finitely many iterations (loss strictly decreases and has finitely many feasible values) and to output the KKT point of min R(k) s.t. Xk=y.

For deep networks, the key technical step reparameterizes via the conserved quantities μ_{i,j}² = (a_{i,j}*)² − (a_{i,L}*)² and the integral F_i(z)=∫ dz / ∏√(x²+μ_{i,j}²), yielding t_i^±=±F_i(±∞). For general two-layer networks, Lemma A.2 (Li et al. 2021) on convergence to a "limit trajectory" under infinitesimal initialization gives t_i^±=±1/λ_i^∓.

## Results & Highlights

- **Modified-ℓ1 vs. standard-ℓ1 support shift (§8.2):** On the experimental data matrix X, standard ℓ1 minimization yields k3=1.43, k4=−0.20 (support {3,4}); applying a 0.5 penalty weight on |k1| (a modified ℓ1) shifts the optimum to k1=1.0, k4=−0.71 (support {1,4}). This concretely demonstrates that the modified ℓ1 bias changes *which* features are selected, not merely the sparsity level.
- **Algorithm 1 trajectory verified empirically (Fig. 1, Appendix C.1):** With initialization scale 10⁻⁶⁰, learning rate 0.1, and 200-digit mpmath precision, the empirical k-vectors at three saddle-transition epochs match Algorithm 1's predictions to high accuracy: k^(1)=(0.8,0,0,0), k^(2)=(0,0,1.4,0), k^(3)=(0,0,10/7,−10/49); the observed values were (0.800, 8.34e−70, 1.51e−28, −1.35e−100), (−1.01e−6, −6.23e−78, 1.400, −1.34e−17), and (−1.71e−6, −2.48e−57, 1.429, −0.204) respectively.
- **Deep-network growth-time predictions match emergence order (Fig. 2c, 3c):** For a 3-layer diagonal network (w=a⊙b⊙c) on M=3 samples, N=8 features, init scale ≈10⁻⁴, the algorithm's predicted per-stage "growth times" (integrated virtual distances) align with the empirical sequential emergence of neurons. Amplifying one neuron's initialization by 5× correctly flips it to be the first to emerge (Fig. 3), confirming the distance-speed model quantitatively.
- **ℓ1 solution recovered under "Zero-Balanced" init (Fig. 4):** With a=b, c=0 initialization, the trained parameters converge precisely to the minimum ℓ1-norm solution of the dataset rather than the sparse ℓ0 ground truth, empirically confirming the modified ℓ1 bias collapses to standard ℓ1 in this symmetric setting.

## Critical Assessment

The paper makes a genuine, clearly delimited theoretical contribution: it removes two restrictive assumptions in Pesme & Flammarion (2023) — (i) the restriction to two layers, and (ii) the restriction of deep networks to "specific initialization directions" — and replaces the standard ℓ1 conclusion with the strictly more general modified ℓ1 norm R(k)=Σ(t_i^+ k_i^+ + t_i^- k_i^-). The technical vehicle is the SIM framework from their own companion paper (Zhao et al. 2026), which is intellectually clean: SIM orbits are shown (Theorem 6.5) to factorize as products of per-neuron simple curves O_{ℱ_i}(θ_i), which simultaneously explains (a) the O(1/s) feature-selection timescale, (b) sign-locking, and (c) why only one neuron escapes per phase. The authors are unusually candid about the gap between theory and practice: they explicitly note (§8.2, Appendix C.1) that curved SIMs are *not* exactly invariant under discrete GD/SGD, and point to the empirical signature of this — in Fig. 1, k1 changes sign between epochs 3000–4000 without returning to its 10⁻¹²⁰ initialization scale, behavior impossible under exact GF. This intellectual honesty strengthens the paper. However, the contribution is incremental relative to Pesme & Flammarion (2023): Algorithm 1 and Theorem 4.2 are acknowledged (Remark 4.3) to be straightforward generalizations of the symmetric-growth-time (t_i^+=t_i^-=1) case, so the novelty is concentrated in (i) the modified-ℓ1 weighting and (ii) the SIM-based mechanism section. The paper is also purely a regression/MSE result; the classification (max-margin) regime is untouched.

## Strengths

- **Clean, correct generalization** of both the trajectory theorem and the implicit-bias characterization, with full proofs in the appendix.
- **Mechanistic depth:** Section 6 connects the algorithmic dynamics to a concrete geometric object (SIM = orbit of ℱ), giving an explanation rather than just a characterization. The decomposition O_ℱ(θ)=∏O_{ℱ_i}(θ_i) (Theorem 6.5) is elegant and reusable.
- **Sharp distinction between GF and GD:** The discussion of flat vs. curved SIMs (§8.2) gives a principled account of when the continuous-time theory survives discretization, and ties it to a concrete observed artifact in the experiments.
- **Quantitative experiments:** The match between Algorithm 1's closed-form k^(p) and 200-digit-precision GD trajectories (to ~6 significant figures on the active coordinates) is compelling evidence the theory captures real dynamics, not just an asymptotic fiction.

## Limitations

- **Infinitesimal initialization only (§8.1):** All results hold in the limit s→0. Real training uses finite-variance initialization; Woodworth et al. (2020) gave a single characterization valid across *all* init scales (interpolating between ℓ1 and L2), which this paper does not match. The 10⁻⁶⁰ experimental scale, while illustrative, is far from any practical regime.
- **Gradient flow only (§8.1):** No extension to GD, SGD, or Adam. As the authors themselves note, curved SIMs break exact invariance under discrete steps, so the sign-locking argument — central to the whole theorem — is only approximate in practice.
- **Regression/MSE only:** No treatment of classification losses, where the implicit bias (max-margin) and the relevant dynamics are qualitatively different.
- **Generic-position assumptions:** Theorem 4.2 requires the argmin index j and certain u_i to be uniquely defined/nonzero at each step; how robust the conclusions are to degenerate data geometries (e.g., duplicated features) is not discussed.
- **No matrix-factorization extension:** The authors flag this (§2) as future work but do not attempt it, despite diagonal networks being a special case of matrix factorization.

## Relevance

This paper is directly relevant to the theoretical deep-learning line on **implicit bias of overparameterized models** — the program aimed at explaining why GD generalizes despite having the capacity to fit random labels (Zhang et al. 2017). It advances the specific sub-question of *trajectory-level* (not just endpoint) understanding for diagonal linear networks, the standard toy model bridging linear regression and nonlinear networks. The SIM framework, if it extends to matrix sensing and deeper nonlinear networks as the authors speculate, could become a general tool for proving incremental-learning and saddle-to-saddle results. For practitioners, the modified-ℓ1 (rather than plain-ℓ1) conclusion is a useful caution: initialization direction, not just scale, shapes which sparse solution GD finds. The work is primarily of interest to researchers in optimization geometry, mirror flow theory, and the theory of feature learning.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can the modified-ℓ1 characterization be extended to **finite** initialization scales, recovering Woodworth et al.'s ℓ1↔L2 interpolation as a special case? The SIM framework currently relies on the s→0 limit.
- Does the SIM-based sign-locking argument survive under **SGD with noise** (Pesme et al. 2021) or **momentum** (Papazov et al. 2024)? The paper's GF/GD gap discussion suggests curved SIMs are fragile under discretization — is there a refined notion of "approximate SIM" that rescues the theorem?
- The modified ℓ1 weights t_i^± depend on the *initialization direction*. Can this be exploited **constructively** — i.e., can one choose θ* to steer GD toward a desired sparse support, turning implicit bias into a design tool?
- Theorem 6.5 shows SIM orbits factorize per-neuron for diagonal networks. Does a comparable **product structure** hold for matrix factorization (where the relevant symmetry group is larger), and could that yield an incremental low-rank learning theorem matching Li et al. (2021)?
- The paper assumes k_i has a unique critical point at 0 with a non-degenerate Hessian. How do the dynamics change for **non-analytic** or **degenerate-critical-point** activations (e.g., k_i with flat directions)?
