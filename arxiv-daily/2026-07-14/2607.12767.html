Why this matters

Applies directly to the default scoring path of MMLU, HellaSwag, ARC, WinoGrande — and eval frameworks like lm-eval-harness & lighteval. Compresses a systematic error that can reshape leaderboards.

Critical edge

τ ≈ 0 ≠ a more faithful eval. No evidence it makes rankings fairer or closer to true capability — and it removes only the linear length effect, leaving content priors untouched.

01Methodology

A length prior, subtracted — not divided

The insight is geometric: total log-likelihood is affine in answer length (positive intercept, negative slope). Dividing by length is the wrong transform; the correct one is to remove the slope, keep the intercept.

  • Formal metric. Length bias = mean within-question Kendall τ between candidate scores and byte-lengths — model- & dataset-agnostic.
  • Generative model. A hierarchical linear model per question yields a global trend E[ℓ|n] = a + b·n, with variance growing quadratically in length.
  • Bayesian correction. An exponential length prior P(n) ∝ exp(−b·n) gives a drop-in score that needs only the conditional log-likelihood already computed.
  • Robust estimator. Within-question centering estimates one b̂ per (model, dataset, prompt) — free of difficulty confounds, in O(M) instead of O(M²).
S_Bayes(c | x; b) ≡ ℓ_θ(c | x) − b · n // length-debiased score, no extra forward pass
02Results

Normalized accuracy is the worst offender

  • Standard accuracy → short-bias. Significantly negative τ on HellaSwag & ARC; near-zero on SciQ/WinoGrande; larger models shrink — but never erase — the bias.
  • Normalization → bigger positive bias. τ(S_byte, D) is positive almost everywhere, often larger in magnitude than the short-bias it "fixes" — instruction-tuned models over-shoot most.
  • Bayesian → near zero. Almost every Table 3 entry satisfies |τ| ≤ 0.1; best or near-best |τ̄| for every model, holding in zero- & few-shot.
  • Half the cost of PMI/ANPMI. Those need an extra unconditional forward pass per candidate; Bayesian reuses the conditional log-likelihood.

03Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Tight theory↔empirics loop: affine observation → hierarchical model → closed-form score.
  • Dead-simple & plug-in: one affine subtract, near-zero engineering cost.
  • Unified metric (Kendall τ) enables cross-benchmark, cross-model, cross-rule comparison.
  • Two crisp, usable "rule-of-thumb" boxes for when standard accuracy is safe.
  • Broad coverage: 7 benchmarks × 5 families × base/instruct × zero/few-shot.

Limitations

  • Only linear length bias removed — non-linear effects & non-length answer priors untouched.
  • No downstream validation: lower τ ≠ fairer rankings or closer-to-true capability.
  • b̂ fragility when candidate count M is small / within-question length variance is low; relies on held-out calibration & shrinkage — no systematic ablation.
  • Limited to likelihood-based MC scoring; instruction-following & generative evals out of scope.
  • Authorship unsigned in HTML — confirm against the camera-ready version.
04Open questions
After lowering τ, how do MC accuracy values & model rankings shift — are any benchmark/model pairs re-ranked materially?
Does Bayesian accuracy correlate better with generative evals / human judgment, i.e. is it actually more faithful to capability?
How much non-linear length effect (e.g. super-linear "fatigue" on long answers) survives — is a quadratic or piecewise term needed?
Can one b̂ calibrated on little data generalize to new benchmarks / prompt templates and stay low-bias?
Does it hold across more languages & tokenizers (beyond German ARC), e.g. non-Latin scripts?
Can the length prior be orthogonally combined with content-prior debiasers (PMI) into one unified score?
