Rethinking the Evaluation of Harness Evolution for Agents

arxiv.org/abs/2607.12227 ↗

Yike Wang, Huaisheng Zhu, Zhengyu Hu, Yige Yuan, Zhengyu Chen, Shakti Senthil, Hannaneh Hajishirzi, Yulia Tsvetkov, Pradeep Dasigi, Teng Xiao

Allen Institute for AI · University of Washington
Key finding

Harness evolution neither beats simple test-time baselines nor generalizes to held-out tasks — the reported gains are just extra search, not better design.

◆ Key metric
+0.6
points · held-out test (avg)
Evolved harnesses add +0.6 avg on a disjoint split — +0.0 on GPT-5.4 — versus large in-domain gains. The design does not transfer.
Why this matters

Benchmark gains overfit rather than reflect real harness design — a warning to anyone reusing the search-on-eval-benchmark protocol.

▲ Critical edge

All findings rest on a single benchmark, Terminal-Bench 2.1, that may itself not be harness-sensitive — so the conclusion's external validity is genuinely uncertain.

03

Methodology

The paper reframes automatic harness evolution as one of four methods that all spend the same compute budget K=5, then asks which one actually deserves the credit.

Given a fixed policy, task distribution, and budget K, every method consumes the same summarization map Φ to compress experience into feedback. The framework separates "did we improve the harness" from "did we just search more."

aParallel sampling — budget on K independent trajectories, pick the best.
bSequential refinement — budget on depth; each trajectory revises the last.
cHarness evolution — budget on a shared harness updated across tasks (instantiated as AHE, with its explore-agent disabled to rule out benchmark-fitted retrieval).
dHarness scaling — budget on per-instance harness edits (instance-guided adaptation).

All four start from the same minimal harness (one bash tool, no skills/middleware/memory), run on Terminal-Bench 2.1, across Claude Opus 4.6, GPT-5.4, GPT-5.4 mini — averaged over two independent runs in fresh E2B sandboxes.

04

Strengths & limitations

+ Strengths

  • Unified budget puts four methods on the same footing, dissolving the "spent more vs. improved more" confound.
  • Three escalating settings answer three questions: can it win? is the win from the harness? does it transfer?
  • Sober controls — disabling AHE's explore-agent and qualitatively separating "memory fixes" from "strategy distillation."
  • Reproducible — public code, full configs in Appendix A.

− Limitations

  • Single benchmark that may not be harness-sensitive — "shell + basic prompt" already solves most tasks.
  • Three frontier models only — no open/smaller models that might benefit more from harness edits.
  • One evolution instance (AHE) — Meta-Harness / AEVO not given the same matched-budget treatment.
  • Small disjoint split (45 train / 34 test) caps the statistical confidence of the generalization claim.
05

Open questions

Q1

On harness-dependent benchmarks (SWE, research workflows needing bespoke tools), can evolution finally beat test-time scaling?

Q2

Would stronger priors or structured search (edits confined to tool/middleware layers, difficulty-curriculum) yield transferable principles?

Q3

Can the "memory-fix vs strategy" split be quantified into a metric for edit transferability?

Q4

For weaker / open models, is evolution cheaper than sampling because the single-rollout ceiling is lower?

Q5

Real deployments couple retrieved + evolved harnesses — how to fairly separate their contributions in eval?

Q6

flat pass@1 but rising pass@5 — does evolution mainly expand the solvable set rather than raise single-shot success?
