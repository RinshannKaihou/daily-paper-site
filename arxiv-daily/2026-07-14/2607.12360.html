Cooldown helps or hurts depending on noise structure & optimizer normalization — not the loss.

2 Why This Matters

Cooldown is no longer “default-beneficial”: it depends on your optimizer & noise geometry — directly guiding schedule choice for large-scale Transformer / LM training.

3 Critical Edge

Theory is asymptotic; predictive power for short-horizon non-convex training is unproven.

“Noise anisotropy” lacks a canonical measurement, so the key independent variable is slippery to operationalize — independent groups may pinch it differently.

01 Methodology

A stochastic-approximation view, plus controlled factorial experiments that hold the loss and noise magnitude fixed.

  • SA framing. Split parameter space into a signal subspace (true gradient ≠ 0) and a redundant / null subspace (true gradient = 0 but noise persists). Redundant drift scales with the effective step size.
  • Why cooldown bites. Linear decay-to-zero shrinks the effective step size near convergence, suppressing noise-driven drift in redundant directions.
  • Axis A — noise structure. Isotropic noise → cooldown meaningfully reduces drift. Anisotropic noise (typical of LMs) → redundant subspace carries little noise, so benefit is small or negative.
  • Axis B — optimizer normalization. Adam/AdamW's per-coordinate division by v_t (RMS) absorbs part of the cooldown reduction — and in anisotropic regimes can invert it.
  • Factorial design. Fix loss + noise magnitude; vary (a) isotropy via data ordering / batch / synthetic injection and (b) normalization (Adam vs. Adam-without-RMS vs. SGD). Read out the sign of the cooldown gap.

02 Results & Highlights

  • SGD on CIFAR-10/100 (ResNet): linear cooldown reliably improves test accuracy — reproduces prior work.
  • Adam/AdamW: cooldown benefit shrinks or vanishes; the gap tracks the optimizer's normalization, not the loss.
  • Removing RMS from Adam reverts cooldown behavior toward SGD — a schedule that was neutral/harmful becomes beneficial. This is the causal linchpin.
  • Injecting anisotropic noise weakens or reverses cooldown's benefit; isotropic noise recovers it — validates the noise-structure axis directly.
  • Transformer LMs: cooldown shows small-to-negative generalization gain — matches the anisotropic + Adam-normalized regime (why LMs prefer cosine / plateau).
  • Synthetic runs confirm error reduction concentrates in the redundant subspace and scales with the suppressed step size.

03 Strengths / Limitations

Strengths

  • Clean factorial logic — isolates structure & normalization independently.
  • Unifies the SGD-helps / Adam-neutral discrepancy under one mechanism.
  • Theory & experiments mutually reinforce; predicted sign flips are observed.
  • Actionable schedule guidance per optimizer/task regime.

Limitations

  • Asymptotic SA theory; finite-sample predictive power unproven.
  • No canonical metric for “noise anisotropy” — slippery variable.
  • Scope misses Lion / Shampoo / Muon / Sophia / schedule-free.
  • Title overclaims: noise structure is being changed, not just magnitude.

04 Open Questions

  1. Does the verdict flip with normalization for newer optimizers (Lion, Shampoo, Muon, Sophia, schedule-free)?
  2. How to measure anisotropy at scale in LM pretraining — is there a cheap online proxy predicting cooldown gain?
  3. Can the redundant-dim argument yield a non-vacuous finite-sample bound or a principled cooldown length?
  4. In fine-tuning (subspace shaped by pretrained weights), does the interaction reverse or amplify?
  5. Does it extend to non-monotonic schedules (cosine, WSD, warm restarts) — is it about the linear-to-zero tail, or any terminal step-size collapse?
