SiMRA-based in-DRAM compute is silently corrupted by non-activated rows and peer columns — up to 10% and 48%.

PuDGhost: Experimental Analysis of Computation Result Corruption in Processing-using-DRAM Operations on Real DRAM Chips and Implications for Future Systems

Daichi Tokuda, İsmail Emir Yüksel, Tatsuya Kubo, Ataberk Olgun, Haocong Luo, Nisa Bostanci, Jikun Wang, A. Giray Yağlıkçı, Shinya Takamaeda-Yamazaki, Onur Mutlu et al. ·2026-06-17
processing-using-DRAM DRAM reliability SiMRA in-memory computing computer architecture
Headline metric
413×lower GEMV NMSE
With CS-2 column screening + isolation-row layout on real DDR4, worst-case NMSE on a 4096×N GEMV drops from 5.8·10⁻² (Base-worst, N=64) to <10⁻³ across all N — a 413× reduction at N=32 and 1.3·10³× BER cut.
Why this matters

Logically inert data — rows and columns the program never touches — can flip in-DRAM majority-vote results by up to 48%. That's a hidden reliability tax on every PuD energy/accuracy claim that hasn't measured neighbor state.

Changes the trade-off that justifies in-memory computing, and the threat model for multi-tenant DRAM.
Critical edge · read with care

The 48% number is a worst-case observation (16-row activation, pc=0.0 random neighbors). It is not "all PuD operations are 48% wrong" — average-case error under realistic ML/graph workloads is not reported.

Mitigation evaluation uses 3 of 12 modules → cross-module generalization of the fix is weaker than for the phenomenon.

Methodology

Target. SiMRA — a simultaneous multiple-row activation primitive (ACT–PRE–ACT issued at sub-3 ns intervals) that exploits SK Hynix DDR4's hierarchical row decoder to charge-share 2/4/8/16/32 rows at once, producing a MAJX (majority-of-X) bitwise result per bitline.

Platform. Custom FPGA DRAM Bender (PCIe Xilinx board) issuing precisely-timed ACT/PRE/WRITE/READ; thermocouple + heater pads hold dies at 50–80 °C. 96 chips across 12 modules, 3 die revisions (rev A 4Gb, rev M 4Gb, rev A 8Gb), on TimeTec, TeamGroup, and SK Hynix PCBs.

Controlled microbenchmarks. Fix the SiMRA operand data (128 random samples per condition); sweep (a) data in non-activated rows of the target / adjacent subarray and (b) SiMRA-row data in non-target columns. Yields 65 536 × 128 output bits per condition. Metric: Norm. po1 = po1(condition) / po1(baseline at pc=0.5).

End-to-end. 4096×N GEMV (N ∈ {4, 8, 16, 32, 64}) and a SiMRA-based TRNG kernel, re-measured under six column-screening configs and two compute-row layouts. A control experiment varies the pre-SiMRA refresh delay (0–120 ms) to rule out charge-leakage explanations.

Strengths  ·  Limitations

Strengths

  • First-of-its-kind real-chip study of an interference phenomenon the PuD community has largely ignored.
  • Large hardware sample — 96 chips / 12 modules / 3 die revisions / 2 densities with cross-module and 50–80 °C sweeps.
  • 15 distinct empirical observations decompose PuDGhost into orthogonal, testable sub-phenomena (adjacent-row vs. inter-column, parity, monotonic vs. non-monotonic, input-asymmetric).
  • Two concrete, hardware-validated countermeasures (CS-1 / CS-2 / isolation-row) with end-to-end GEMV and TRNG impact.
  • Forward-looking analysis of implications for PuD simulators, multi-tenant security, ECC, and reference-voltage schemes.
  • Clean causal hypothesis (electrical coupling during charge-sharing / sensing) that device-level follow-up can test.

Limitations

  • SK Hynix DDR4 only. DDR5 / LPDDR5 / HBM3 / 3D-stacked PIM are not characterized; vendor-agnosticism is argued, not proven.
  • 10% / 48% are worst-case. Average-case error under realistic ML / graph / database workloads is not reported.
  • CS-2 needs memory-controller changes to maintain fixed neighbor data — controller complexity, scheduling overhead, and runtime cost are unquantified.
  • Mitigation evaluated on 3 of 12 modules (§10, to bound test time) — cross-module generalization of the fix is weaker than of the phenomenon.
  • SiMRA-only. RowClone, near-DRAM PIM (UPMEM), and in-HBM analog compute may show different profiles and are not analyzed.
  • Column interference not fully decomposed into bitline- vs. SA-coupling; parity asymmetry is a hypothesis, not a circuit-level result.
  • No interaction with retention / VRT — all common in deployed DDR4 — is studied.
  • Security implications (§8.3.1) are raised but not demonstrated end-to-end; multi-tenant attack vectors remain hypothetical.

Questions for further exploration

  1. How does PuDGhost scale with DDR5 / LPDDR5X / HBM3 and 3D-stacking, where row/column and TSV coupling are expected to worsen?
  2. What is the average-case error rate under realistic ML, graph, or database workloads, rather than the worst-case random-input regime?
  3. Can CS-2 + isolation-row be combined with lightweight PuD ECC (e.g., Count2Multiply) to bound residual errors below 10⁻⁹ in production?
  4. How do these effects manifest in HBM-based PIM, where thermal and TSV coupling differ from planar DDR4?
  5. Can a programming model / ISA expose PuDGhost susceptibility to software, so compilers avoid ghost-prone columns or pair operands with appropriate isolation patterns?
  6. Can adversarial input patterns deliberately trigger PuDGhost as a fault-injection / side-channel attack on in-DRAM compute in multi-tenant clouds? End-to-end demo is missing.
  7. Can staged activation (smaller sequential groups) preserve MAJX correctness while reducing PuDGhost — and at what throughput cost?
  8. Can runtime reference-voltage adjustment compensate dynamically for the bias direction (e.g., lower SA Vref when all-0 neighbors expected), and at what power / circuit cost?
  9. How does PuDGhost interact with retention failures and variable refresh rates — and can the two be jointly modeled for PuD-aware refresh?
  10. Does the opposite- vs. same-parity asymmetry (Obsv. 13/14) point to a specific circuit-level coupling path that SPICE / TCAD could confirm — analogous to how device studies eventually explained RowHammer?
