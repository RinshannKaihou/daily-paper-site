> **一句话总结：** 基于开源大场景 RTS 游戏 Beyond All Reason(BAR),提出 RTSGameBench:通过 1v1/团队/自由对战等整局评估、五个针对单点战略能力的诊断性 mini-game,以及能将自然语言查询自动转译为新诊断任务的自演化多智能体生成框架,系统评测 11 个 SOTA VLM;同时提出结合 FSM 群体管理与 agentic memory 的 RTSGameAgent,实证表明当前 VLM 在紧密协调(尤其 3v4 劣势对局下 Gemini 仅 0.20 胜率)与地图/单位规模放大时性能显著退化,mini-game 上最强 VLM(Kimi-K2.5 在 MFD 上 DE 1.82)仍远逊于人类(DE 3.46)。

> **TL;DR:** RTSGameBench is a holistic, diagnostic, and extensible VLM benchmark built on the open-source RTS game Beyond All Reason. It evaluates 11 SOTA VLMs through (1) full-game matches across 1v1 / symmetric team / asymmetric team / free-for-all; (2) five diagnostic mini-games, each isolating a single RTS AI competency from Buro's taxonomy (Resource Management, Spatial & Temporal Reasoning, Opponent Modeling, Collaboration, Adversarial Planning); and (3) a self-evolving multi-agent pipeline that turns free-form user queries into new mini-games, improving playability and human preference over successive batches. To make large-scale RTS tractable for VLMs, the authors contribute RTSGameAgent, which replaces per-unit movement with FSM-based group management (4 states: move / move_force / stop / fight) and adds short-/long-term agentic memory. Main empirical findings: (a) Gemini-3-Flash leads full games (Duel 0.87 WR, FFA RS 0.66) but drops to 0.20 WR in 3v4 Asymmetric Team; (b) Diagnostic mini-games reveal a wide human–VLM gap on spatial/temporal reasoning (MFD) and opponent modeling (FS-F); (c) removing both FSM and memory drops Duel WR from 0.87 to 0.10, and removing vision drops it to 0.23; (d) all models degrade as map/unit scale grows; (e) the multi-agent self-evolving pipeline dominates E2E generation in playability and human preference.

## 核心贡献

- 提出 RTSGameBench:基于 BAR(超过 SC2 的单位种类、每玩家供应上限、地图尺寸与玩家上限)的大规模多智能体 RTS 评测平台,弥补现有 SC2 类基准在评估范围、诊断粒度与可扩展性上的不足。
- 三层互补的评测体系:(1) 整局对战(Duel / 对称团队 / 非对称团队 / FFA),(2) 5 个针对 Buro RTS 战略能力分类中单项能力的诊断 mini-game,(3) 自由文本查询驱动的自演化场景生成框架。
- 完整的 observe–decide–act 接口规范:同时接收全局 minimap + 局部相机视图(可置于自选位置)+ Python 包装器抽取的结构化文本观测;动作空间三型(建造/生产/移动,坐标 0–100 网格),固定推理间隔下冻结游戏以测战略质量而非反应速度。
- 自演化生成框架:Project Manager + Designer + Developer + Analyst 四角色,四阶段管线(Scenario Brief → GDD → Rule Set → Game Finalization),使用门控、rollback、共享知识数据库与 retrospective rubric 更新,可在循环中持续提升可玩性与人类偏好。
- 提出 RTSGameAgent:为应对 BAR 数百单位规模与长视野挑战,采用 FSM 群体管理(4 状态:move / move_force / stop / fight)+ 双记忆系统(短程事件日志 + LLM 摘要的长程记忆 + 检索的相关条目 m_t),首次让 VLM 能在 BAR 大场景中端到端运行。
- 11 个 SOTA VLM 的系统化评测(GPT-5.2、GPT-5-mini、Claude-4.5-Sonnet、Gemini-3-Flash、Kimi-K2.5、Grok-4.1-Fast、Qwen3.5-397B、Qwen3-VL-235B-{I,T}、LLaMA4-Maverick、Mistral-Large-3),并给出 mini-game 上的人类基线;代码与 BAR 子模块开源。

## Key Contributions

- **Benchmark substrate:** RTSGameBench on Beyond All Reason — quantitatively larger than StarCraft II in unit variety, supply cap, unit capacity, map size, and player limit (Tab. 1), with built-in per-unit automation that lets evaluation focus on strategic decisions rather than low-level micro.
- **Rigor desiderata (i)–(iii):** (i) *holistic* — full games across diverse matchup structures; (ii) *diagnostic* — five mini-games grounded in Buro's RTS AI taxonomy; (iii) *extensible* — self-evolving scenario generation from free-form queries.
- **Standardized interface:** observe–decide–act loop with multimodal observation o_t = (v_t, W(s_t)) (global minimap + placed local views + structured text), fixed-decision-interval game pause, action space = {build, produce, move} on a (0,0)–(100,100) grid.
- **RTSGameAgent:** FSM-based group management (4-state FSM per squad: `move`, `move_force`, `stop`, `fight`-auto-trigger) + agentic memory (short-term event log S_t ↔ long-term summary L_t via a memory-management LLM → relevant entries m_t).
- **Self-evolving generation pipeline:** Project Manager (orchestration/gating), Designer (scenario brief + GDD), Developer (Lua scripts + asset selection), Analyst (rubric checks + simulation-based visual validation). Two self-evolution mechanisms: (1) shared knowledge database (validated GDDs / rule sets) and (2) PM retrospective analysis that refines rubrics per cycle.
- **Empirical characterization of 11 SOTA VLMs** + a human baseline on mini-games, plus ablations (FSM × Memory, vision vs. text-only) and scale studies (map size, unit count).

## Methodology

### Benchmark substrate — Beyond All Reason (BAR)
- Open-source RTS with a larger battlefield and more units per match than SC2 (Tab. 1: 96 unit variety, 200 supply cap, 1,600 unit capacity per match, larger maps, 8-player limit). BAR's per-unit automation (target prioritization, energy management) keeps strategic depth while removing low-level micro overhead.

### Game interface (Eq. 1)
- Agent receives static game knowledge K (scenario, units, team config). At step t: visual channels v_t (minimap + local views at arbitrary positions) + structured text from Python wrapper W(s_t) → multimodal observation o_t. With fog-of-war, both channels are restricted to allied line-of-sight. Policy π (a VLM) emits action a_t; environment Env updates state.
- Action space: building construction, unit production, unit movement on a (0,0)–(100,100) grid. Engine pauses between steps so evaluation measures decision quality, not reaction speed.

### Full-game match-ups (Tab. 2 top)
- **Duel (1v1)** — individual decision-making; FoW on.
- **Symmetric Team (2v2, 3v3)** — allied coordination; FoW on.
- **Asymmetric Team (3v4)** — agent on the smaller side, tighter coordination under numerical disadvantage; FoW on.
- **Free-for-All (1v1v1v1)** — multi-polar threat prioritization; FoW on.
- Remaining slots filled by built-in BAR AI at Easy difficulty (next difficulty: all tested models near 0% WR per supplementary).

### Diagnostic mini-games (Tab. 2 bottom, Fig. 2)
Each mini-game isolates one competency from Buro's RTS AI taxonomy and reports a primary + auxiliary metric (Tab. 4).
- **TCP — Resource Management (Time-Constrained Production):** produce a target unit comp within a deadline while fending off raids; Build/Produce/Move; FoW on.
- **MFD — Spatial & Temporal Reasoning (Multi-Front Defense):** defend multiple objectives from staggered attacks; fixed forces, no production; Move; FoW off.
- **FS-F — Opponent Modeling (Fixed-Field Skirmish FFA):** 3+ agents, symmetric fixed forces, no production, last survivor wins; Move; FoW off.
- **FS-T — Collaboration (Fixed-Field Skirmish Team):** same skeleton as FS-F but team play, no explicit comms; Move; FoW off.
- **SP — Adversarial Planning (Siege Planning):** breach a static fortification within a time limit; Build/Produce/Move; FoW on.
- Decision Making under Uncertainty is selectively integrated via FoW (Tab. 2's FoW column).

### Self-evolving game generation framework (Fig. 3)
- **Four stages** with inter-stage gating and rollback:
  1. *Scenario Brief Generation* — Designer clarifies user intent via multi-turn dialogue → structured brief.
  2. *GDD Generation* — Designer expands brief into a Game Design Document specifying the targeted competency and rule components; Analyst validates by rubric.
  3. *Rule Set Generation* — Developer retrieves from a shared knowledge DB or writes Lua scripts; Analyst validates via rubric + simulation.
  4. *Game Finalization* — Developer composes the executable script (unit placement, end conditions, map, parameters); Analyst validates by rubric + full simulation with periodic VLM-judged screenshots.
- **Two self-evolution mechanisms:** (a) accumulating shared knowledge DB of validated GDDs/rule sets; (b) PM's retrospective analysis after each successful generation, updating Analyst rubrics based on verification discrepancies.

### RTSGameAgent (Fig. 4)
- **FSM-based group management:** retains Build + Produce at the per-unit level; replaces per-unit movement with two group-level actions — (i) *group assignment* (create named squads and allocate units) and (ii) *group movement* (target coordinate + command per squad). Group statuses live in W(s_t) and are part of o_t. Each group has a 4-state FSM (`move`, `move_force`, `stop`, `fight`); `fight` is auto-triggered on enemy contact and reverts when the engagement ends; `move_force` ignores auto-engagement. States persist across decision steps.
- **Inference with agentic memory (Eqs. 2–3):** short-term S_t (event logs: enemy sightings, battle events) and long-term L_t (concise experience summaries). Memory phase: a memory-management LLM consolidates the two stores and selects relevant entries m_t; short-term is flushed. Decision phase: the VLM receives (o_t, m_t | K) and outputs {build, produce, group assignment, group movement (with FSM command)}. Local views are positioned at the three largest groups and the home base.

### Experimental setup
- **Models:** 11 VLMs (proprietary + open) + a human baseline on mini-games. For full games, VLM occupies one slot; rest filled by built-in BAR AI at Easy difficulty. Same prompting templates for all.
- **Inference interval:** 1 min for full games; 15 s for combat mini-games (FS-F, FS-T, MFD); 60 s for planning mini-games (TCP, SP). Each result averaged over 30 runs.
- **Self-evolving generation agents:** all use GPT-5.2; 5 batches × 5 queries each.
- **Metrics:**
  - *Full game:* Win Rate (WR ↑), Game Time for wins/losses (GTW / GTL), Damage Efficiency (DE = dealt/received); FFA reports Rank Score (RS: 1st=1.0/2nd=0.67/3rd=0.33/4th=0.0).
  - *Mini-games:* primary + auxiliary per game (WR, AT min ↓, DE ↑, RS ↑).
  - *Self-evolving:* Playability (fraction generated that execute), Generation Time (min), Human Preference (pairwise A win / B win / tie, 4 RTS-experienced evaluators).

## Results & Highlights

### Full-game evaluation (Tab. 3)
- **Duel (1v1):** Gemini-3-Flash leads (WR 0.87) with shortest GTW; GPT-5.2 (0.53); Claude-4.5-Sonnet (0.27) delays defeat (GTL 43 min) but rarely wins. Open-source models largely fail except Qwen3-VL-235B-T (0.20). LLaMA4-Maverick, Mistral-Large-3, Qwen3-VL-235B-I score 0.00 WR.
- **Symmetric Team (2v2 / 3v3):** broad performance drops. Gemini 0.87→0.50 in 2v2; Qwen3.5-397B approaches GPT-5.2 in 3v3 (0.33 vs. 0.37) — larger allied contingent amplifies the role of team synergy.
- **Asymmetric Team (3v4):** hardest mode. Even Gemini reaches only WR 0.20; multiple models (Claude, Kimi, Qwen3.5, LLaMA4, Mistral, Qwen3-VL-235B-I) record 0.00 WR with no wins.
- **Free-for-All (1v1v1v1):** Gemini (RS 0.66) and Claude (0.57) lead; Qwen3-VL-235B-I collapses to RS 0.07 ("near-immediate elimination").

### Mini-game evaluation (Tab. 4) — human–VLM gap is large
- **TCP (Resource Mgmt):** four models reach WR 1.00 (near saturation), but none match human AT of 10 min.
- **MFD (Spatial & Temporal):** largest human gap — best VLM is Kimi-K2.5 with DE 1.82 vs. human 3.46; several models at 0.00 WR.
- **FS-F (Opponent Modeling):** Gemini-3-Flash leads (RS 0.64); Kimi-K2.5 drops to RS 0.20 despite excelling in MFD/SP — opponent modeling needs distinct capabilities.
- **FS-T (Collaboration):** Claude leads (WR 0.73); Mistral (which fails elsewhere) reaches 0.57 — structured coordination may compensate for individual weaknesses.
- **SP (Adversarial Planning):** Gemini 0.93, Claude & Kimi 0.80; Qwen3-VL-235B-T drops from TCP 1.00 to 0.20, suggesting production planning does not generalize to static-fortification breaches.

### Ablations — RTSGameAgent components and modalities (Tab. 5, Gemini-3-Flash)
- *w/o Both* (no FSM, no memory): Duel WR 0.10, TCP 0.47 — raw VLM is far from competitive.
- *w/o FSM Group Mgmt* (memory only): Duel 0.53 — big jump from "no both" but well below full.
- *w/o Agentic Memory* (FSM only): Duel 0.67 — FSM contributes more than memory in most settings.
- *Full:* Duel 0.87, MFD 0.60, FS-F 0.64, FS-T 0.50, SP 0.93 — best overall; FSM and memory play complementary roles.
- **Input modality:** removing vision drops Duel WR 0.87→0.23; MFD WR 0.60→0.27 (largest drop). TCP and FS-F are largely vision-independent (rely on text observations).

### Scaling analysis (Fig. 5)
- *Map size (Duel, FFA):* Qwen3.5 drops to 0.00 WR at the largest map; Gemini degrades more gradually; average game time rises with map size.
- *Unit count (FS-F, FS-T):* GPT-5.2 shows a sharp drop in FS-F but is stable in FS-T. **FS-T is more robust to unit scaling than FS-F** — team coordination is less sensitive to raw unit count than individual threat assessment.

### Self-evolving generation (Fig. 6, 5 batches × 5 queries)
- *Playability:* Both multi-agent variants (Agent w/ SE, Agent w/o SE) are high from batch 1; E2E baselines (with or without verify) stay far lower — structured stage-wise generation cannot be replaced by repeated verification.
- *Generation Time:* Self-evolving agent remains stable across batches; non-self-evolving shows no gain.
- *Human Preference:* Evaluators increasingly favor the self-evolving variant in later batches — corroborates that DB reuse + rubric updates lift quality over cycles.
- *Example (Fig. 7):* query "strategic foresight and phased preparation" → mini-game with massive ground assault at 10 min + heavy air assault at 30 min, executable with precise temporal triggers.

### Take-aways stated by the authors
- Performance degrades sharply as matchups demand tighter coordination.
- Multi-agent coordination is the weakest competency even for top models.
- Performance declines consistently as task scale (map size, unit count) increases.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (moderate):** The high-level "use an RTS game to test VLM strategic reasoning" is well-trodden (SC2 [1, 24, 28, 29], Diplomacy [17], Minecraft [54, 62], Civilization [39], Avalon [25], Balrog [36], Orak [37], V-s-bench [57], DSGBench [48], SC2Arena/StarEvolve [44], GameBot [26], lmgame-bench [19], Sketchtopia [23], Towermind [51]). Genuine novelty comes from (a) the BAR substrate (larger scale, lower micro overhead), (b) the *self-evolving* scenario generation loop (vs. static benchmarks — closest to [52] but with simulation-based verification in a game engine), and (c) the explicit mapping of mini-games to Buro's taxonomy. None of these is a conceptual breakthrough, but the combination is well-engineered.
- **Rigor (good, with caveats):**
  - *Positives:* 30 runs per cell; 11 models spanning proprietary and open; a human baseline on mini-games; per-component ablations (FSM × Memory, vision vs. text-only); scale sweeps (map size, unit count); standardized prompting templates; fixed map + Easy AI for comparability.
  - *Caveats:* (i) Only one map per matchup (one fixed map per cell) — the paper says "a fixed map" per matchup, so map-specific effects are not controlled. (ii) The "next-difficulty kills everyone" note is in the supplementary, not analyzed in depth. (iii) The VLM always shares parameters between memory and decision phases, so the contribution of larger memory LLMs is not isolated. (iv) No measure of per-game variance (std/CI) is shown in the main tables — 30 runs may still be noisy for low-WR cells.
- **Reproducibility (good):** Code, BAR engine, prompts, and rubrics are released; the GitHub is public. The "self-evolving" framework is nontrivial to reproduce (LLM-driven, simulation-validated), but the artifacts should help.
- **Significance:** Useful addition to the VLM-agent benchmark suite; the *taxonomy-grounded* mini-games are a real contribution for diagnostic studies. The headline finding — that VLM coordination collapses with team size and adversarial disadvantage — is informative if not surprising.
- **Red flags:**
  - *Built-in AI as the only opponent/ally:* No comparison to strong scripted bots, RL agents, or human opponents. "VLMs beat Easy AI but lose to Medium" is a thin story.
  - *Single fixed map per cell:* Generalization across maps is unverified.
  - *Mixed human and AI prompts:* The memory-phase LLM and the decision-phase VLM use the same model. The "agentic memory" ablation thus confounds memory architecture with model identity.
  - *Self-evolution metric:* "Playability" = "executes without error" is a weak quality bar; a generated game could be trivially playable yet strategically vacuous. Human Preference is more meaningful but is judged by only 4 evaluators on 25 generated games.
  - *VLM-vs-VLM not evaluated:* All full-game results are VLM + built-in AI; the paper does not pit models against each other (a much more revealing test of strategic reasoning).
  - *No cost / latency analysis:* The benchmark's applicability to closed-source frontier VLMs is constrained by per-step inference cost; this is not discussed.
  - *Survivorship in FFA:* The claim that Claude's "strong survival ability" transfers to FFA is post-hoc — it is consistent with the data but not a controlled comparison.
  - *Saturation on TCP:* Four models reach WR 1.00 on TCP, suggesting the diagnostic value of that game may be limited; this is not discussed.

## Strengths

- Largest-in-class RTS substrate (BAR) with an open engine and active community.
- Three complementary evaluation modes (full game + diagnostic mini-games + self-evolving generation) that together answer different questions.
- Explicit taxonomy-grounded mapping of mini-games to Buro's RTS AI competencies — much more principled than ad-hoc scenario suites.
- RTSGameAgent's FSM + memory is a clean, well-motivated architectural choice for VLM-on-RTS, with both components empirically necessary (full → 0.87 WR; w/o both → 0.10 WR).
- Systematic scale analysis (map size, unit count) showing graceful degradation patterns and that team play is more robust to unit scaling than FFA — a non-obvious empirical insight.
- Self-evolving generation is a step beyond static benchmarks and is shown to dominate end-to-end baselines in playability and human preference.

## Limitations

- Built-in Easy AI as the sole opponent; lack of strong scripted/RL/human baselines.
- One fixed map per evaluation cell; no cross-map generalization.
- Same VLM used for memory and decision phases — memory benefits are entangled with model choice.
- No cost / latency / token-budget analysis; the benchmark is impractical to run at scale with closed-source frontier models.
- Self-evolution quality is assessed via "playability" + a 4-evaluator pairwise study; deeper validity evidence is missing.
- No VLM-vs-VLM evaluation, which would directly probe the strategic reasoning gap.
- TCP near-saturation suggests the diagnostic value of some mini-games is bounded.
- BAR is a specific game; results may not transfer to other RTS, much less to non-game strategic settings (negotiation, auctions, etc.).
- The 1-minute full-game inference interval means VLM decisions are evaluated in a quasi-real-time regime but with substantial "thinking time" — a setting that may not reflect how these models would be deployed.

## Relevance

本工作直接对应 VLM/MLLM 的多智能体战略推理评测需求,对研究大模型在长视野、部分可观测、需要紧密协调的环境下的规划与博弈能力具有重要参考价值;其将 Buro RTS 能力分类与 mini-game 一一对应、以及自演化多智能体基准构建思路,均可迁移到其他多智能体/博弈评测场景的设计中。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How do these results change against (a) a strong scripted bot, (b) a learned RL agent, (c) human opponents, and (d) VLM-vs-VLM matchups? The Easy-AI opponent likely under-estimates the true coordination gap.
- The memory-phase LLM and the decision-phase VLM are forced to share parameters. Does using a larger / specialized memory LLM (e.g., GPT-5.2 for memory, smaller model for decisions) close the memory-ablation gap, or is it a structural limitation?
- TCP saturates for four models. Are the mini-games well-calibrated for the current generation of models, or do they need iterative difficulty scaling to remain diagnostic?
- The self-evolving generator's quality signal is "playability + 4-evaluator pairwise preference." Can we add behavioral validity (e.g., does the generated scenario actually distinguish strong from weak VLMs?).
- Map selection is fixed per cell. How much of the observed variance across matchups is map-specific vs. genuinely strategic?
- The team-vs-FFA robustness gap (FS-T > FS-F under unit scaling) is an interesting asymmetry. Does it generalize beyond the unit counts tested, and is it driven by observation/action-space structure or by model priors?
- Does fine-tuning on RTSGameBench traces transfer to other multi-agent / strategic benchmarks (Diplomacy, Hanabi, Overcooked), or does it overfit to BAR mechanics?
- The VLM always operates under heavy "thinking time" (1 min/step). How do these results degrade under deployment-realistic latency budgets, and does RTSGameAgent's FSM partially mask the underlying model's latency vulnerability?
