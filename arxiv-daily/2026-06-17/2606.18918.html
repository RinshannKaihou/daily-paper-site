BNN satisfiability is NP-complete — yet uniform-occlusion robustness is verifiable in polynomial time.

A clean dichotomy unavailable for ReLU networks: the worst-case satisfiability question is intractable, but a specific robustness question under uniform occlusion is tractable, because the first-layer pre-activation is affine in the occluded color and sign-induced breakpoints cannot propagate past layer one.

Why this matters

For binarized networks on edge hardware facing occlusion attacks, robustness is decidable by a finite test set per occluded region — no SMT solver, no MILP, no abstract interpretation needed. The threat model is narrow, but the algorithm is a real guarantee.

Critical edge

The "polynomial-time" claim is honest but weak: the complexity is O(N2 · ∏ Ni) — effectively exponential in depth L. On real deep BNNs the algorithm will not beat SMT-based verifiers.

Methodology

方法 · 纯理论 / 无实验

A purely complexity-theoretic study. Two structural results, one worked example, no empirical experiments.

  • BNN-SAT reduction from SAT. Encode Boolean variables as input neurons (True ↔ +1, False ↔ −1). Build NOT with 2 neurons, OR with 5, AND with m+2. Total gadget size is linear in the CNF.
  • Uniform-occlusion lemma chain. Lemma 3.1: first-layer pre-activation z²ₖ = mₖ·c + qₖ is affine in the occluded color c. Lemma 3.2: sign(z²ₖ) is piecewise-constant with at most one breakpoint cₖ = −qₖ/mₖ.
  • Induction on depth (Lemma 3.3). Linear combinations preserve piecewise- constantness; sign activation cannot introduce new breakpoints. By induction every hidden layer and the final logits are piecewise-constant in c.
  • Algorithm 1. For each occlusion position, evaluate the network on the finite set Ctest = B₂ ∪ {b₁ − 0.1} — exactly N₂+1 values.

Strengths

优点
  • Clean dichotomy. NP-complete satisfiability, poly-time uniform-occlusion robustness — a sharp contrast unavailable for general ReLU networks.
  • Self-contained in 8 pages. Definitions, gadgets, three lemmas, an algorithm, a complexity statement, and a worked example.
  • Reusable structural argument. "Affine at first layer → piecewise-constant after sign → preserved by linear combinations" is easy to extend to other threat models.
  • Explicit gadgets. The NOT/OR/AND reductions are concrete enough to serve as a teaching reference for SAT-hardness of neural verification.

Limitations

局限
  • No empirical evaluation. The single worked example is hand-computed on a 3×2 image — not a real BNN on MNIST or CIFAR.
  • Polynomial is exponential in depth. ∏Nᵢ grows multiplicatively with width, so practical scalability is unaddressed.
  • Narrow threat model. Uniform occlusion only — no bridge to L∞, patch, or general adversarial perturbations.
  • Binarized inputs assumed. Real-world BNNs typically quantize real-valued inputs at the front; the reduction does not cover that.
  • Notational inconsistency. The example writes {−1, −2/3} in one place and {−1.5, −1} in another — light proofreading.

Questions for further exploration

待探索的问题 · 7 questions
  • Does the piecewise-constant argument extend to L∞-bounded perturbations on binarized inputs, where each occluded pixel is independently constrained?
  • Can the multiplicative factor ∏Nᵢ be tightened (memoization, layerwise caching, vectorization) to be competitive with SMT verifiers on MNIST/CIFAR-scale BNNs?
  • Is uniform-occlusion tractability tight — does the problem become NP-hard under "almost-uniform" occlusion (two distinct colors in the region)?
  • How does this compare to Narodytska et al. (AAAI 2018)? Are the complexity results genuinely new, or do they follow from their framework?
  • Does BNN-SAT NP-completeness hold under restricted architectures (depth-2, no biases, bounded integer weights)?
  • Can the SAT-reduction gadgets be reused to encode richer properties (safety, liveness, fairness) on BNNs more efficiently than black-box SMT?
  • What is the analog of the piecewise-constant argument for ternary or low-bit networks (e.g. {−1, 0, 1}, INT4)?
