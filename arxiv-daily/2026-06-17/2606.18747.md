> **一句话总结：** 在 Pepper 人形机器人上以 GPT-4.1 直接生成由低层运动原语函数组成的协同手势代码，并通过 5 轮、每轮 50 人（累计 250 人、4000 条反馈）的众包用户研究同时做在线 few-shot 上下文累积与离线 DPO 微调，结果显示第 5 轮在表达性、相关性和流畅度上对基线均有 p<0.001 的显著提升，但同时暴露出对话历史一旦撤掉就出现"模式坍缩"到四五个手势的问题。

> **TL;DR:** This paper deploys GPT-4.1 as a runtime gesture-code generator for the SoftBank Pepper humanoid, where the LLM composes co-speech motions from a curated library of low-level NAOqi primitive functions. The generator is improved over **five user-study iterations** (50 new Prolific participants per round, 1000 feedback samples per round, 50 phrases across 5 social categories — Greeting, Encouragement, Apology, Explanation, Collaboration) using a **dual-track RLHF scheme**: (1) offline **Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)** on within-category and cross-iteration pairwise comparisons, and (2) online **few-shot prompting** via a growing conversation history. Welch's t-tests report significant Iteration-5-over-Iteration-1 improvements on expressiveness, relevance, and fluidity (p<0.001 across all five categories), with relevance/fluidity benefiting most from DPO and expressiveness driven primarily by few-shot context. Ablations also expose a **mode-collapse failure mode**: removing the conversation history causes the model to recycle only 4–5 gestures across all categories, indicating that DPO alone is insufficient to translate semantic preference into diverse low-level motion.

## 核心贡献

- **LLM 驱动的运行时手势代码生成器**：用 GPT-4.1 + 一套封装了 NAOqi 关节限制的 Python 原语函数库，从自然语言对话直接合成可由 Pepper 实时执行的协同手势代码。
- **在线 + 离线双轨迭代 RLHF**：在同一框架内同时使用 **DPO（基于成对偏好的离线微调）** 和 **对话历史累积的 few-shot 上下文学习**，每轮收集 1000 条众包反馈（50 名 Prolific 用户 × 20 段视频）。
- **5 轮、共 250 名参与者的真实机器人用户研究**：覆盖 5 个语义类别、50 条短语的协同手势，使用 5 点 Likert 量表评估表达性、相关性、流畅度，并通过消融实验拆解了 DPO 与 few-shot 的相对贡献。
- **诚实的失败模式分析**：报告了对话历史一旦撤除就出现 4–5 种手势的"模式坍缩"，并指出仅靠低层原语组合难以同时保持多样性与流畅性。

## Key Contributions

- **End-to-end LLM-to-robot pipeline** that maps spoken dialogue to executable co-speech gesture code on Pepper under NAOqi v2.5, with strict adherence to the official Aldebaran joint limits and angle ranges.
- **Dual-track iterative RLHF**: offline **DPO** on pairwise preference comparisons (1350 within-category + 150 between-iteration pairs) combined with **online few-shot prompting** by summarizing free-text comments and appending them to a persistent conversation history (c = 4 prior feedback cycles by Iteration 5).
- **Five-iteration within-subjects user study** on a physical Pepper robot (250 Prolific participants, 50 phrases × 5 categories — Greeting, Encouragement, Apology, Explanation, Collaboration) with three Likert dimensions (expressiveness, relevance, fluidity) and free-text feedback.
- **Ablation-driven attribution** of where gains come from: DPO most strongly improves relevance and fluidity, while few-shot context drives the largest expressiveness gains.
- **Transparent failure-mode reporting**, including mode collapse to 4–5 gesture templates when the conversation history is removed, and the observation that DPO alone produces robust but unnatural patterns.

## Methodology

**System architecture (Fig. 1, Fig. 2).** GPT-4.1 (`gpt-4.1-2025-04-14`) is prompted with (i) a system prompt fixing the Pepper persona, the joint limits, and the current joint angles, (ii) a structured **function library** of low-level Pepper primitives (e.g. `head_rotate_up(motion, speed, delay, radians)`, `left_elbow_extend`, `right_hand_open`, etc.) — each enforcing a per-joint angle limit and a post-motion delay, and (iii) a **conversation history** `Ch` of prior (input, response) pairs used for few-shot in-context learning. From a natural-language input `l_in` the model must emit a co-speech tuple τ = ⟨l_out, π⟩, where `l_out` is the spoken reply and π = [a₁, …, a_s] is a sequence of parameterized primitive calls with explicit timing intervals. `l_out` is sent to Pepper's native TTS, and π is executed via `execute_generated_movement(gesture_code, motion)` under the NAOqi runtime.

**Prompting choices.** Temperature is set to **1.5** (high) to encourage expressive variability in a non-deterministic generation task. Output is constrained via role-play, directional, and strict-schema instruction prompting, plus **meta-prompting** in which the system prompt is itself iteratively refined using LLM-assisted analysis.

**RLHF loop.** At iteration `i`, the model produces gestures π⁽ⁱ⁾ for a fixed set of n=50 inputs. Each gesture receives `p=20` Likert ratings on three dimensions, yielding n·p = 1000 feedback samples per round (4000 across 4 finetuning rounds). Two tracks of feedback are then applied:
1. **Offline (DPO).** For each of the 5 categories, ratings are aggregated per phrase and used to construct pairwise preferences. Within-category comparisons yield 1350 pairs; 150 additional pairs compare across iterations.
2. **Online (few-shot).** Free-text comments across all `p` raters are summarized by the LLM into a single feedback description `l_f,sum` and appended to `Ch` as a new in-context example. By the final iteration, `c = 4` prior feedback cycles are present.

The system is initialized with the base GPT-4.1 model in Iteration 1, then fine-tuned (cumulative) in Iterations 2–5.

**Evaluation.** Welch's unpaired t-test compares Iteration 5 to Iteration 1 per (category × metric) cell. Ablations compare the full Iteration-5 system against (a) DPO only, (b) base model + few-shot history only, and (c) base model alone.

## Results & Highlights

**Iteration 5 vs. Iteration 1 (Fig. 3).** All 15 cells (5 categories × 3 metrics) reach p<0.001. Largest absolute gain: Encouragement–Relevance climbs from 2.00 to 2.78. Largest expressiveness delta is 0.32 (Apology). Relevance and fluidity consistently improve more than expressiveness, which the authors attribute to the more abstract/subjective nature of expressiveness.

**Trajectory across iterations.** Iteration 2 yields the **largest single-step jump**, driven by the first round of few-shot context. Iterations 3–4 show partial regression, then Iteration 5 re-stabilizes higher than Iteration 2. This is interpreted as a shift from "rapid early adaptation" to "constrained incremental refinement."

**Category asymmetry.** Apology improves most, with Greeting and Encouragement also showing large gains. **Collaboration and Explanation** lag — the authors suggest these require more learning because they lack salient, easily reinforced expressive cues.

**Ablations (Fig. 5).** Few-shot prompting contributes the largest **expressiveness** gains (both with the base and combined model). DPO contributes the largest **relevance and fluidity** gains. The two are complementary.

**Qualitative behavior (Fig. 6, free-text analysis).** Iteration 1 produces repetitive arm-raises and open-hand motions. Iteration 2 strongly reflects the few-shot feedback ("you're not looking too energetic" → calmer gestures). Iteration 3 reduces gesture complexity (head nods over full-body), suggesting a conservative strategy under semantic ambiguity. Iteration 4 prunes redundant primitives and introduces novel motions (e.g. forward torso bends during apologies). Iteration 5 shows a slight decline in diversity under the combined model, attributed to few-shot over-reuse of earlier high-rated templates.

**Failure mode surfaced.** When the conversation history is ablated from the Iteration-5 model, gestures **collapse to 4–5 reused templates** across all five categories. With the base GPT model + full history, gestures are similar to the combined model and occasionally novel, but fluidity suffers. The authors conclude that **DPO alone is insufficient** to translate semantic preference into diverse motion; few-shot context carries the diversity load.

**RQ-level take-aways (as framed by the authors):**
- **RQ1 (yes):** Human feedback can finetune an LLM's code generation to improve perceived expressiveness, with statistically significant Iteration-5 gains on all metrics and categories.
- **RQ2 (partially):** LLMs *can* generate executable, human-aligned low-level gesture code, but sustaining diversity and fluidity over longer interactions requires complementary mechanisms such as pose interpolation, higher-level motion abstractions, or learned dynamical models.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty.** The architecture (LLM + motion-primitive library + RLHF) and the EMOTION-style iterative online/offline split are incremental relative to prior co-speech gesture papers (Mahadevan et al. 2024 "Generative Expressive Robot Behaviors using LLMs", Huang et al. 2025 EMOTION, Roy et al. 2025 "GPT-Driven Gestures"). The paper's specific contribution is the **Pepper-specific implementation** (NAOqi v2.5 angle limits, primitive docstring library), the **crowdsourced 5-iteration user study** with N=250, and the **ablation cleanly separating DPO and few-shot contributions**. The failure-mode analysis is genuinely honest and worth more than the headline numbers.

**Rigor.**
- *Positives.* Real physical robot, deployed in the loop. Pre-registered phrasing per category. 5-point Likert on three distinct dimensions, plus free text. Welch's t-test is reported per cell, not just overall. Pairwise preference construction is described with concrete counts (1350 within + 150 cross). The between-iteration design isolates the feedback effect because the verbal output is held fixed.
- *Weaknesses.* The use of **unpaired t-tests across iterations with different participants** is a mild statistical concern: a within-subjects design (the same participants re-rating the same prompts in each iteration) would be far more powerful and is standard in RLHF-from-human-ratings work. With between-subjects variance dominating the small absolute gains (often ≈0.3 on a 5-point scale), effect sizes — not just p-values — should be foregrounded and are not.
- The temperature 1.5 + strict-schema design risks **stochastic per-rater variation** masquerading as preference signal, and the paper does not report rater agreement (e.g. Krippendorff's α across the 20 raters per gesture), which would let the reader judge whether the preference pairs are reliable enough to drive DPO.
- "Welch's unpaired t-test" with N=50 per iteration is reasonable but is repeatedly stated without correction for multiple comparisons across the 15 (category × metric) cells.
- The qualitative figure (Fig. 6) shows iteration-level function-call counts, but **no statistical test** is applied to diversity. The "mode collapse" claim relies on qualitative inspection of the 4–5-template behavior, not a diversity metric.
- The 50 phrases are themselves **GPT-generated**, so the study tests generalization only within a distribution the LLM has effectively already seen the structure of during prompt construction.

**Reproducibility.**
- Strong: model name and version (`gpt-4.1-2025-04-14`) given, system prompt documented, function library partially shown in the paper and on the Qualtrics preview URL, and ethics approval cited.
- Weak: the DPO training data and hyperparameters (learning rate, epochs, regularization) are not reported, the full function library is not in the paper, the meta-prompt refinement is described but not shown, and the **base GPT-4.1 model is a closed OpenAI API endpoint** that may drift over time — the same study re-run 6 months later will not produce the same baseline.
- Free-text comments are summarized by the LLM, introducing a hidden stochastic step whose prompt is not published.

**Significance.** Modest. The paper is a useful **engineering case study** of how to put RLHF on a physical social robot and what to expect (mostly: large iteration-1 → iteration-2 jumps, then slow refinement, and a brittle mode collapse if you remove context). It does not advance RLHF theory, gesture synthesis, or LLM reasoning. The findings will mostly help practitioners building similar systems choose between DPO, few-shot, and a hybrid approach.

**Red flags / overclaiming.**
- The 0.3-point Likert gains (e.g. Apology expressiveness Δ=0.32 on a 5-point scale) are reported as "statistically significant improvements" with p<0.001, but with N=50 per iteration the practical effect is small. A reader skimming only the abstract will overestimate the magnitude.
- The claim "RLHF improved the LLM's co-speech generative capabilities" is a slight overreach: the comparison is always to **a base GPT-4.1 without RLHF**, not to other gesture baselines (rule-based, motion-capture-driven, or specialized gesture models such as Gesture2Vec). The paper does not position itself against any non-LLM baseline.
- The conclusion "human feedback effectively prunes inappropriate gestures and enables limited novelty" is a measured, fair summary; the abstract's "more expressive, relevant and fluid movements" is the puffier version. Both are present in the paper, and the gap between them is worth noting.
- The novelty score should also be docked for the limited use of free-text comments (summarized by the LLM rather than mined for content) and the lack of rater-agreement reporting.

## Strengths

- **Real robot deployment** (Pepper under NAOqi v2.5), not a simulation or video-rating study on a third-party robot.
- **Crowdsourced, large-N evaluation** (250 participants, 4000 ratings, 50 phrases × 5 categories × 5 iterations) — substantially bigger than typical HRI gesture studies.
- **Clean ablation** that decomposes the gains into few-shot and DPO contributions, which is rare in this subfield.
- **Honest reporting of failure modes**, especially the mode-collapse behavior when conversation history is removed, is genuinely useful for the community.
- **Practitioner-friendly details**: system prompt excerpt, function-library example, temperature 1.5, NAOqi-specific angle clamping.
- **Bridges two communities** (LLM/RLHF and social HRI) by recasting gesture generation as a code-generation problem with a motion-primitive DSL.

## Limitations

- **Between-subjects design** with different raters per iteration inflates noise and underestimates the magnitude of feedback effects relative to a within-subjects design.
- **No rater-agreement statistics** (Krippendorff's α, ICC) reported for the 20 raters per gesture.
- **No comparison to non-LLM baselines** (rule-based, motion-capture, dedicated gesture models like Gesture2Vec), so it is unclear how much of the "good" outcome is RLHF vs. just "an LLM with a primitive library."
- **Closed-API dependence** on OpenAI's GPT-4.1 couples reproducibility to model versioning and policy drift.
- **Self-generated stimuli**: the 50 phrases are produced by the same LLM family that scores them, raising concerns about evaluator-template co-adaptation.
- **Diversity not measured quantitatively** — the central failure mode (mode collapse to 4–5 gestures) is asserted rather than scored.
- **No long-horizon test**: all evaluations are 20-video micro-sessions, so the conclusion "improves long-term acceptance" in the abstract is not directly supported by the user study.
- **English-only stimuli** and AU/UK/US recruitment limit generalization to other cultures and languages where gesture norms differ substantially.
- **DPO hyperparameters, training data, and reward-model details are not disclosed**, making the offline track hard to reproduce.
- **Single platform (Pepper)**: low DOF, slow joints, fixed kinematic structure — generalization to higher-DOF humanoids is asserted in the conclusion but not demonstrated.

## Relevance

本文与本项目关注的研究兴趣**关联度较低**：核心在社交机器人 HRI 与协同手势生成，而非 LLM 训练/推理可靠性、机制可解释性、量化/算力可靠性或自演化智能体；唯一可借鉴之处是 **RLHF 在具身对话系统中的双轨（在线 few-shot + 离线 DPO）实施范式**，可作为"自演化智能体/自动奖励设计"主题下的人类反馈驱动策略优化参考案例。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- What are the rater-agreement statistics (Krippendorff's α, ICC) across the 20 raters per gesture, and how much of the DPO preference signal is noise vs. signal?
- Why does the iteration-2 → iteration-3 regression occur, and is it robust to re-running the study with a new cohort of raters?
- How does the system compare to a non-LLM baseline (e.g. a motion-capture-driven gesture library, or a dedicated co-speech gesture model like Gesture2Vec) on the same prompts and raters?
- What is the minimum feedback budget per iteration (N raters × M gestures) at which the DPO track stops overfitting and the few-shot track stops mode-collapsing?
- Can a hybrid system (high-level LLM policy + low-level motion-planning / pose-interpolation) recover diversity without sacrificing fluidity?
- How does the policy generalize to **new phrases outside the 50-stimulus set**, and how quickly does it drift back toward the training templates?
- Could the LLM summarization step in the online track be replaced with structured comment classification (thumbs-down category → targeted feedback snippet) to reduce hidden stochasticity?
- Is the DPO track actually needed at all, or would an offline preference dataset collected once and a much larger few-shot context window achieve the same Iteration-5 numbers at lower API cost?
- How does cultural variation in gesture norms (e.g. high- vs. low-contact cultures, East Asian vs. Anglophone gesture repertoires) affect the preference signal?
- Can the iterative RLHF loop be made continual — i.e. deployed with end users in the field, with online safety bounds — without the conservative-pruning behavior that emerged here?
