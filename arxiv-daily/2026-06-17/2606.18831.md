> **一句话总结：** 该工作从数据视角出发,把长上下文推理拆为检索、多证据合成、推理三类互补能力,据此构建 8 个数据集共 ~14K 样例的"数据配方",配合极简 outcome-based GRPO + 任务平衡采样与任务级优势归一化,在 Qwen3-4B/8B/30B-A3B 上获得 +7.2 / +3.2 / +6.4 平均提升,并迁移到 GAIA +4.8 / BrowseComp +7.0 的智能体任务。

> **TL;DR:** A data-centric long-context RL recipe that decomposes long-context reasoning into **retrieval, multi-evidence synthesis, and reasoning**. It curates **8 datasets (~14K examples)** across these categories, then trains Qwen3-4B/8B/30B-A3B-Thinking with a **minimal outcome-based GRPO** plus **task-balanced sampling** and **task-level advantage normalization**. It beats two strong baselines (DocQA-RL-1.6K, KeyChain-15K) on **seven long-context benchmarks**, with average gains of **+7.2 / +3.2 / +6.4**; the gains even transfer to **+4.8 on GAIA** and **+7.0 on BrowseComp** when continued on an agent-tuned model.

## 核心贡献

- **数据为中心的视角重构长上下文 RL**:认为长上下文 RL 当前的瓶颈在训练数据多样性与质量,而非 reward engineering;只要数据配方足够好,outcome-based GRPO 即可拿到强结果。
- **提出能力分解 + 数据配方**:把长上下文推理拆为 **retrieval / multi-evidence synthesis / reasoning** 三类互补能力,针对每类能力的典型失败模式(如 lexically-biased retrieval、surface-form shortcut、incomplete coverage、long-input 下的推理退化)分别设计新合成数据集。
- **8 个数据集 / ~14K 样例,全部开源承诺**:
  - *Retrieval*:FuzzyNeedle(1500, IS-A 改写去 lexical bias)、MultiNeedle(1500, 重复近义 needle 计数检索)
  - *Multi-evidence Synthesis*:CrossEntity(3521, 跨实体 derived-attribute 聚合)、WebSearch(684, 桥接链 + 同类实体干扰)、MultiQuery(226, 顺序必答多 query)、KeyChain(654, UUID 链式指针)
  - *Reasoning*:LongDocQA(3422, HotpotQA/MuSiQue/Qasper 扩展)、LongMath(2562, 把 DeepMath-103K / MATH-Hard 改写为长叙事场景)
- **稳定的极简 RL 方案**:使用 GRPO,省略 KL,采用 (i) 任务平衡采样 + (ii) 任务级优势归一化(按数据集 std 重缩放)以缓解异构任务间的方差失衡;reward 对多数任务用 word-level recall,对 CrossEntity / LongMath 改用 DeepSeek-V3.2 LLM-as-judge。
- **跨模型 + 跨长度泛化**:在 4B/8B/30B-A3B 三种规模上一致提升,且训练 cap 在 64K 但在 230K (LongBench v2) 与 128K (HELMET) 上仍保持增益。
- **智能体迁移**:在 AgentCPM-Explore 上继续 RL 50 步,GAIA Pass@3 +4.8、BrowseComp +7.0,且长上下文平均分从 45.57 升到 54.64。

## Key Contributions

- **Data-centric reframing** of long-context RL: argues the bottleneck is training-data diversity, not reward design, and shows that a minimal outcome-based GRPO is sufficient when fed a well-designed data mixture.
- **Three-ability decomposition** — retrieval, multi-evidence synthesis, and reasoning — each with targeted datasets that address specific failure modes (lexical shortcut, surface-form shortcut, incomplete coverage, distractor confusion, long-input reasoning degradation).
- **Curated, open-source recipe**: 8 datasets, 14,069 examples, with a two-stage post-processing pipeline (64K-token cap, then 4-rollout difficulty filtering with Qwen3-30B-A3B) that keeps problems of moderate difficulty.
- **Stable, minimal GRPO**: omits KL, adds task-balanced sampling and task-level advantage normalization; uses word-level recall reward for most tasks, DeepSeek-V3.2 LLM-as-judge for CrossEntity/LongMath.
- **Consistent gains across model scales**: +7.21 / +3.17 / +6.41 on Qwen3-4B-Thinking-2507 / Qwen3-8B-128K / Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking-2507 averaged over 7 long-context benchmarks; +2.66 / +1.02 / +3.66 over the strongest baseline (KeyChain-15K).
- **Length generalization beyond training cap**: 64K-trained models still improve at 64-256K (+6.9), 256-512K (+4.6), and 512K+ (+2.1) on LongBench v2.
- **Transfer to agentic tasks**: continuing RL on AgentCPM-Explore improves GAIA by +4.8 and BrowseComp by +7.0 (Pass@3), with the long-context average score rising 45.57 → 54.64.

## Methodology

**Three-ability taxonomy.** The authors argue that long-context reasoning naturally decomposes into three complementary core abilities, each addressing a distinct failure mode of current long-context LMs:

1. **Retrieval** — locates candidate evidence in a long input even when (a) the query and evidence share no keywords (lexically indirect) or (b) many near-duplicate needles are scattered through the context.
2. **Multi-evidence Synthesis** — integrates evidence from different positions, especially when no single span contains the answer, distractors are topically similar, or coverage must be exhaustive.
3. **Reasoning** — performs complex multi-step reasoning (e.g., math) over long inputs; the paper notes that long inputs *alone* degrade reasoning, motivating targeted long-form reasoning data.

**Datasets** (14,069 examples after post-processing; see paper Table 1):

- *FuzzyNeedle* (1,500, 0–32K): IS-A paraphrases so the query and the evidence share no keywords; sibling-entity statements act as distractors.
- *MultiNeedle* (1,500, 0–32K): multiple near-duplicate needles about the same topic, scattered and shuffled, queried by K-th occurrence.
- *CrossEntity* (3,521, 0–64K): groups of peer entities (e.g., NBA players); questions aggregate *derived* attributes that require within-document integration plus cross-document aggregation, defeating surface-form shortcuts and incomplete coverage.
- *WebSearch* (684, 0–64K): entity chains ("A directs B; B's lead is C; ...") where most entities are anonymized to letters; sibling-entity documents act as distractors.
- *MultiQuery* (226, 0–32K): documents sampled from a pre-training corpus, QA pairs generated by an LLM, queries embedded in document order, model must answer all in order (incomplete coverage = wrong answer).
- *KeyChain* (654, 0–32K): QA pair where the question is hidden behind a chain of UUID pointers, following the LoongRL pipeline.
- *LongDocQA* (3,422, 0–64K): training sets of HotpotQA, MuSiQue, Qasper, with extended context (full Wikipedia articles or padding) for length diversity.
- *LongMath* (2,562, 0–64K): difficult math problems from DeepMath-103K and MATH-Hard, rewritten as long narrative scenarios where original variables/conditions are scattered across ≥5 fragments; a second LLM verifies solvability.

**Curation.** Two-stage post-processing: (1) discard examples exceeding 64K tokens; (2) rollout each remaining prompt 4× with Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking-2507 and keep only those that are neither all-correct nor all-wrong (isolates moderate-difficulty problems for informative learning).

**RL training (GRPO).** Group Relative Policy Optimization (Shao et al., 2024), KL term omitted following DAPO (Yu et al., 2026). For each prompt x, sample G=8 rollouts from π_θ_old, score with rewards, compute group-relative advantage, and apply the clipped surrogate loss. Two stabilizers from QwenLong-L1.5 (Shen et al., 2025) are added because the mixture is heterogeneous: (i) **task-balanced sampling** (fixes per-dataset sample ratio in each batch) and (ii) **task-level advantage normalization** (rescales each rollout's advantage by its per-dataset reward std, so low-variance tasks aren't washed out by high-variance ones).

**Reward design.** Simple: word-level recall `r = |ŷ ∩ y⋆| / |y⋆|` for most tasks. For CrossEntity (where enumeration can hack recall) and LongMath (where numerical answers have multiple equivalent forms), an LLM-as-judge using DeepSeek-V3.2 is used instead.

**Training config.** Adam; LR 1e-6 for 4B/8B and 2e-6 for 30B-A3B; constant schedule; global batch 128 prompts, PPO batch 1024 (on-policy); temperature 1.0, top-p 0.95; max input 64K, max generation 16K; 100 steps (4B/8B) or 50 steps (30B-A3B). Backbone: Megatron-LM; rollout: SGLang; framework: Miles. Hardware: H100 GPUs. Wall-clock: ~22 h for 4B (16 GPUs), ~50 h for 8B (16 GPUs), ~14 h for 30B-A3B (32 GPUs).

**Evaluation.** Seven long-context benchmarks in three groups: (i) *Multi-hop QA on real-world documents* — LongBench v1 QA (5 doc-QA subsets), FRAMES; (ii) *Holistic Long-Context Reasoning* — LongBench v2, AA-LCR, DocFinQA; (iii) *Synthetic Long-Context Reasoning* — LongReason, GraphWalks. Unified decoding: temperature 0.6, top-p 0.95, max input 130K, max output 60K. LongBench v1-QA and FRAMES report max(EM, LLM-judge); LongBench v2 / AA-LCR report Avg@4. Two agentic benchmarks: GAIA (text-only validation, 127 examples) and BrowseComp (100-example subset), both Pass@3, with a real-time leakage filter that drops search results containing the verbatim question + gold answer. Agentic eval uses AgentCPM-Explore's AgentToLeaP framework with a 240K context window, max 200 interaction rounds.

## Results & Highlights

**Main results (Table 2).** The recipe beats both DocQA-RL-1.6K and KeyChain-15K on every base model's average score:

| Model | Avg. | vs. Base | vs. KeyChain-15K |
|---|---|---|---|
| Qwen3-4B-Thinking-2507 | 53.42 → 60.63 | **+7.21** | +2.66 |
| Qwen3-8B-128K | 49.93 → 53.10 | **+3.17** | +1.02 |
| Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking-2507 | 61.82 → 68.23 | **+6.41** | +3.66 |

All seven benchmarks improve on 4B and 30B-A3B; six of seven improve on 8B (GraphWalks slightly regresses from 42.27 → 40.96). The largest jumps are on holistic-reasoning benchmarks: +7.0 (LBv2), +10.5 (AA-LCR), +8.6 (DocFinQA) for 4B; +4.8 / +7.3 / +10.5 for 30B-A3B.

**Length generalization (Figure 2).** Even though training caps at 64K, on Qwen3-4B-Thinking-2507 the recipe improves LongBench v2 at every length bucket — +8.6 at 0–64K, +6.9 at 64–256K, +4.6 at 256–512K, +2.1 at 512K+. HELMET follows the same pattern (+1.2 at 32K, +2.0 at 64K, +2.1 at 128K).

**Category ablation (Table 3).** All three single-category subsets improve the average, with Reasoning being the strongest single category (+3.46 on Qwen3-4B). The largest pairwise union is Retrieval + Reasoning (57.48); the full mixture (57.58) is best, confirming the three categories are complementary. No single category or pair matches the full mixture.

**RL-design ablations (Table 4).** On Qwen3-4B: removing task balancing drops average from 57.58 → 56.05 (–1.53); adding a generic LLM-as-judge *process reward* (judging thinking-trajectory coherence) actually *hurts* from 57.58 → 56.29 (–1.29). The authors attribute this to (a) the judge scoring only its own priors without ground-truth evidence, and (b) the diverse data already providing informative outcome signal.

**Agentic transfer (Table 5 / Table 8).** Continuing the recipe on AgentCPM-Explore for 50 steps:
- GAIA Pass@3: 66.1 → 70.9 (**+4.8**)
- BrowseComp: 27.0 → 34.0 (**+7.0**)
- Avg. LC (7 long-context benchmarks): 45.57 → 54.64 (**+9.07**)
- GAIA L3 hardest tier: 42.1 → 52.6; BrowseComp "Hard" tier: 28.6 → 57.1.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty.** Modest but meaningful. The "data is the bottleneck, not reward design" argument is not new to RL, and the data recipe's three-ability taxonomy is largely a synthesis of prior observations (NoLiMa, MRCR, LongReason). The novelty is more in the *systematic curation and combination*: each dataset is built to attack a specific, named failure mode (lexical shortcut, surface-form shortcut, incomplete coverage, distractor confusion, long-input reasoning drop), and the post-processing difficulty filter is a useful practical contribution. The agentic transfer result, while preliminary, is genuinely suggestive.

**Rigor.** Mixed.
- *Positives*: three base models, seven long-context benchmarks across three difficulty tiers, a sensible length-stratified generalization analysis, ablations on both the data mixture and the RL design (task balancing, process reward), and a careful leakage filter for GAIA/BrowseComp.
- *Concerns*:
  - Only **two** baselines (DocQA-RL-1.6K, KeyChain-15K) — both are also data-centric long-context RL recipes, so the comparison is essentially "is the new data recipe better than two earlier data recipes?" without a head-to-head against algorithm-centric methods that supplement the outcome reward (e.g., LongRLVR, EPRO, PAS). The paper *does* ablate adding a process reward and finds it hurts, which indirectly speaks to that line, but a full comparison table against e.g. LongRLVR-8B/30B would strengthen the "reward engineering is unnecessary" claim considerably.
  - The 4-rollout pre-filtering is a **selection bias** — by design the training set contains only moderate-difficulty problems. This is rationalized as "informative learning signal," but it means the data is not the raw distribution of long-context problems; ablations on how the filter changes outcomes are not provided.
  - Evaluation on 8B is only 100 steps; the 4B/30B-A3B results suggest diminishing returns may not yet be reached, so the relatively modest +3.2 on 8B may be undertrained rather than a property of the recipe.
  - 8B's GraphWalks slightly regresses (42.27 → 40.96), and 8B also loses on DocFinQA relative to KeyChain-15K (39.59 vs 35.79 — actually fine, but FRAMES drops 74.76 → 77.67 — also fine). Cross-checking the table: 8B does regress on FRAMES (74.76 → 71.36 for DocQA-RL, 80.22 for KeyChain, 77.67 for ours) — meaning the recipe is not strictly Pareto-dominant on 8B, which the paper acknowledges.
  - Process-reward ablation is reported only for Qwen3-4B; whether the same conclusion holds at 30B-A3B (where the auxiliary signal might be more useful) is unknown.

**Reproducibility.** Strong on the surface: base models, hyperparameters, eval framework, judge prompts, and reward formulas are all in the paper or appendix. Datasets are promised (not yet linked from the abstract), and construction pipelines for all synthetic datasets are described in enough detail to replicate. Wall-clock and GPU counts are reported. The Miles framework and SGLang/Megatron are standard. Open questions: exact data balance ratios between datasets, full prompts for synthesis (FuzzyNeedle, WebSearch, CrossEntity, etc.), and whether the difficulty filter's retained items are released verbatim or only the prompts.

**Significance.** High for the long-context-RL community: it provides a concrete, well-described recipe that beats two leading baselines using *less* reward engineering, and the agentic transfer result (a +9.07 jump in long-context average and a +7.0 on BrowseComp just from data + GRPO) is the kind of result that could shift practice. For the broader LLM/agent community, the message "data > reward design" is pragmatic but not universally applicable — the paper only tests outcome-based GRPO and Qwen3 family up to 30B-A3B.

**Red flags.** None of the major "alarm-bell" kind. Minor points worth flagging:
- Authors are partly from OpenBMB, which releases AgentCPM-Explore; the agentic transfer experiment uses their own model, so there's a mild conflict-of-interest flavor (though disclosed via author affiliation).
- The 30B-A3B results run for only 50 steps vs. 100 for 4B/8B, and a 14-hour 32-GPU budget; whether additional steps would close or invert the 4B-vs-30B-A3B gap is unclear.
- Claims like "surpassing prior RL training sets" should be read as "surpassing the two RL training sets we compared against," not as a universal ranking.
- "Strengthening core long-context abilities effectively benefits long-horizon agentic capability" is a tempting causal claim, but with one model and two benchmarks it's correlational at best.

## Strengths

- A **clean, falsifiable thesis** ("diverse data > reward engineering") backed by direct head-to-head experiments and an ablation that *disconfirms* a common alternative (process reward).
- **Targeted dataset design**: each of the 8 datasets attacks a specific, named failure mode, not a generic "more long-context QA" pile.
- **Three-model, seven-benchmark evaluation** with proper length stratification and length-generalization analysis beyond the training cap.
- **Practical stabilizers** (task-balanced sampling, task-level advantage normalization) clearly motivated by the heterogeneity of the mixture.
- **Agentic transfer** is the headline practical result: +7.0 on BrowseComp and +4.8 on GAIA just from continuing long-context RL on an agent-tuned model.
- **Data release commitment**, sensible licensing, and explicit leakage filtering for web-based agentic benchmarks.

## Limitations

- Only Qwen3 family, only up to 30B-A3B (which is a MoE with only ~3B active params); recipe not validated on larger dense models or other families.
- RL training capped at 64K-token inputs; extension to native 128K+ training (rather than 64K-trained + 230K-tested) is left to future work.
- 8B gains are noticeably smaller (+3.17 vs. +7.21 / +6.41), and the paper offers no clear diagnosis.
- Synthetic data may not match real-world long-context distribution; the difficulty filter biases training toward moderate-difficulty problems.
- Agentic transfer is preliminary: one agent-tuned model (the authors' own AgentCPM-Explore), two benchmarks, no analysis of which skill transfers to which agent behavior.
- Comparison is limited to two data-centric baselines; algorithm-centric approaches (auxiliary rewards, modified policy optimization) are only touched via the process-reward ablation on 4B.
- No multilingual evaluation; all data is English.
- The pre-filtering step itself uses Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking-2507 rollouts, embedding a particular model's bias into which training examples survive.

## Relevance

该工作直接对应"**长上下文场景下,大模型如何通过强化学习获得稳健推理**"这一核心议题,把答案从"设计更精巧的 reward"转向"设计更系统、更多样的训练数据",对长上下文智能体的训练有直接借鉴价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the recipe transfer to non-Qwen3 dense families (e.g., Llama, Mistral) and to models ≥70B?
- Why does the 8B gain lag so much behind 4B and 30B-A3B — is it the YaRN extension, the 32K context, an undertrained run, or a fundamental scaling effect?
- Can the three-ability taxonomy be refined (e.g., adding "tool use over long contexts" or "multi-document temporal reasoning") and does the same "all three are needed" pattern hold?
- The process-reward ablation *hurts* on 4B — is this because the data is already sufficiently informative, or because generic LLM-as-judge process rewards are simply too noisy? Would an *evidence-anchored* process reward (e.g., from a stronger model that does see the gold evidence) flip the sign?
- How much of the agentic transfer is mediated by raw long-context reasoning ability vs. by exposure to a different action-distribution during continued RL? An ablation that continues RL with the same data but on a non-agent base model would help disentangle.
- The difficulty filter discards ~half the prompts; can the surviving subset be further stratified, and does training on the all-correct / all-wrong subsets in a curriculum (rather than discarding them) yield more sample-efficient RL?
- The recipe trains on synthetic data only — how much real long-context data (e.g., from AgentCPM-Explore's own trajectories, or from web crawls) would be needed to close the synthetic-to-real gap, and does adding it harm or help?
