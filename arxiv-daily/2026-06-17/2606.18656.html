Safety-aligned LLMs confidently override explicit evidence and answer wrong on stereotype-related questions — all 25 models tested misfire.

The Wrong Kind of Right: Quantifying and Localizing Misfired Alignment in LLMs

Naihao Deng · Yiming Feng · Chimaobi Okite · Kaijian Zou et al.

LLM Alignment Over-Refusal Stereotype Bias Mech. Interpretability Safety Evaluation
Key Metric · MAR range
4.7–18.9%

Misfired Alignment Rate across 25 LLMs. Worst: GPT-5.4-nano. Human baseline: 0.0%.

Why This Matters

Frontier models (GPT-5.4, Claude-4.7) misfire more than smaller ones — aggressive alignment training appears to install shallow safety heuristics that override evidence-based reasoning.

Critical Edge
Caveat

All mechanistic claims inferred from 3 open-weight models. The suppression circuit is not directly verified for GPT-5.4 or Claude-4.7.

Methodology

A four-stage attack on a single failure mode

  • VETO benchmark — 2,032 contrastive yes/no pairs derived from BBQ's disambiguated split across 8 demographic categories. Target and contrast instances share identical evidence; only the named individual differs.
  • MAR metric — P(target wrong | contrast right). Complementary Bias Rate (BR) captures the conventional direction. Refusals and "no" both count as wrong; in practice models almost always answer confidently.
  • Alignment-priming experiment — prepend "It is not okay to assume [GROUP] [stereotype]." to target instances. Isolates the causal effect of safety framing on already-aligned models.
  • Mechanistic localization — logit-lens traces per-layer "yes"/"no" preference in Llama-3.1-8B, Mistral-7B, Gemma-3-27B; attention-head ablation identifies specific late-layer veto heads responsible for the flip.

Priming causality. A single normative prefix raises MAR by +2.6 to +64.3 points — no model is fully immune. Suppression circuit. Logit-lens shows intermediate layers prefer the correct "yes"; the final 1–2 layers flip to "no" — handoff rate 50%–97% on failure pairs, 0% on controls. Peak logit gap: Llama L24/32 (+22.7), Mistral L31/32 (+14.2), Gemma L61/62 (+25.9). Provenance. Instruction tuning installs or amplifies the circuit: Llama 1.3% → 6.2%, Gemma 4.6% → 6.3% from base to instruct.

Assessment

Strengths and limitations, side by side

Strengths

  • Sharp, operationalizable failure mode (misfired alignment) distinct from bias and from over-refusal.
  • 25 models, 7 human annotators, 2,032 pairs across 8 categories — strong evaluation breadth.
  • Contrastive pair design (same evidence, different entity) is a clean experimental control.
  • Combines behavioral (priming) and mechanistic (logit-lens + ablation) evidence — causal and localized.
  • Causal ablation: top-10 head ablation flips 80–83% of failures back to correct (Llama, Mistral); controls preserved at 97–100%.
  • Code, data, and model outputs all publicly released; strong reproducibility signal.

Limitations

  • Binary yes/no format; free-form generation and multi-turn dialogue not tested.
  • VETO inherits BBQ's U.S./English scope; non-Western, non-English, and intersectional categories absent.
  • Mechanistic claims are inferences for closed models — only 3 open models measured.
  • Gemma ablation recovery is weak (7–17%), weakening the "small set of heads" generalization.
  • Base-vs-instruct provenance rests on 2 clean data points (Llama, Gemma); Mistral is inconclusive.
  • The +64.3 priming jump is an upper bound on cue sensitivity, not a deployment estimate.
Open Questions

What to test next

  • Does misfired alignment transfer to non-stereotype domains of over-refusal (medical, legal, coding, political-sensitive)?
  • Why do frontier models (GPT-5.4, Claude-4.7-Opus) often have higher MAR than smaller or open models — aggressive safety training, or a measurement artifact?
  • Can the late-layer suppression circuit be targeted during training (head-level regularization, circuit-aware RLHF) to preserve contextual grounding?
  • How does MAR correlate with other safety benchmarks (HarmBench, JailbreakBench, OR-Bench)? Complementary or redundant?
  • Would chain-of-thought elicitation make the suppression circuit self-correctable, or just push it to the final step?
  • Does the phenomenon appear in multilingual settings where safety training data is uneven across languages?
