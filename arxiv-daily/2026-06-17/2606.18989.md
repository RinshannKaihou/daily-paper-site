> **一句话总结：** 提出以 Wiktionary 英文释义为语义枢轴、覆盖 9 种核心语言 18,785 对习语的 G-IdiomAlign 基准，并通过"带类型化干扰项的多选题"和"No-gloss vs With-gloss 对比生成"两个协议系统揭示 LLM 在跨语言习语翻译中存在显著的"字面翻译陷阱"主导失败模式，机制分析进一步将 With-gloss 改进定位到 attention head 层面的"释义锚定"增强。

> **TL;DR:** G-IdiomAlign is a gloss-pivoted, precision-first benchmark of 18,785 idiom pairs across 9 core languages (De, En, Es, Fi, Fr, Ja, Pl, Zh, Pt) and 36 unordered language pairs, built by using Wiktionary English glosses as a shared semantic pivot, MNN-based bidirectional agreement, and distribution-aware filtering. Two evaluation protocols (a 4-way multiple-choice task with three typed distractors — Literal/LC/CA — for error attribution, and a contrastive generation task comparing No-gloss vs With-gloss) show that literal-translation bias is the dominant failure mode across DeepSeek-V3.2, Gemini-2.5-Pro, Claude-4.5-Haiku, Ministral-8B-Instruct, and Qwen3-8B, especially when the target is a low-resource language, and that adding an explicit gloss helps an embedding-based semantic proxy but only modestly. Attention-based diagnostics on Qwen3-8B show that cross-condition differences localize more to attention heads than to layers and that correct With-gloss generations coincide with stronger gloss anchoring.

## 核心贡献
- 发布 G-IdiomAlign：以 Wiktionary 英文释义为统一语义枢轴，覆盖 9 种核心语言、36 个无序语言对、18,785 对习语的高置信度跨语言习语对齐基准
- 设计"精度优先"的分阶段构建流程：(1) 从 Wiktionary 抽取 idiom 与核心 gloss（剔除多义条目）；(2) 用 OpenAI text-embedding-3-large 在共享 gloss 嵌入空间做 top-k 检索；(3) 用 MNN 双向最近邻约束保留一一对应；(4) 按语言对做分布感知过滤（pair-adaptive thresholding）剔除弱匹配
- 建立两种互补诊断协议：(1) 4-选 1 Multiple-Choice Idiom Equivalence，含三种类型化干扰项（字面翻译陷阱 LT、词面线索陷阱 LC、上下文关联陷阱 CA），支持按错误类型归因；(2) Gloss-Contrastive Generation，No-gloss vs With-gloss 对比，隔离显式语义枢轴的因果效应
- 系统量化"字面翻译偏好"作为 LLM 跨语言习语翻译的主导失败模式，且在低资源目标语言上加剧；Thinking 模式带来普遍提升但无法消除该偏好
- 引入 attention-based correlational diagnostics：以 Qwen3-8B 为对象，定义 IAR/GAR/DR/OtherTop1 等指标，量化显示 With-gloss 改进主要由 attention head 层面的"释义锚定"增强驱动，而非 layer 级重构

## Key Contributions
- A precision-first, gloss-pivoted benchmark G-IdiomAlign covering 9 core languages, 36 unordered language pairs, and 18,785 idiom pairs, anchored by Wiktionary English glosses
- A staged construction pipeline: gloss extraction → gloss-embedding top-k retrieval (k=10) → MNN bidirectional agreement → distribution-aware filtering with pair-adaptive thresholds
- A 4-way Multiple-Choice Idiom Equivalence protocol with three typed distractors (Literal Translation Trap, Lexical Cue Trap, Contextual Association Trap) for fine-grained error attribution
- A Gloss-Contrastive Generation protocol that contrasts No-gloss vs With-gloss inputs to isolate the causal effect of an explicit semantic pivot
- Large-scale empirical evidence that literal-translation bias is the dominant failure mode across 7 model variants (DeepSeek-V3.2 T/NT, Gemini-2.5-Pro, Claude-4.5-Haiku, Ministral-8B-Instruct, Qwen3-8B T/NT), with amplifications on low-resource targets
- Attention-based correlational evidence on Qwen3-8B that cross-condition (With-gloss vs No-gloss) differences localize to attention heads (Jaccard 0.32) rather than to layers (Jaccard 0.90), and that correct With-gloss generations coincide with stronger gloss anchoring (higher GAR, lower DR/OtherTop1)

## Methodology

### Construction pipeline
- **Language scope:** 9 core languages: De, En, Es, Fi, Fr, Ja, Pl, Zh, Pt; 4 additional underrepresented languages (Ar, Ko, Th, Vi) reported separately in Appendix A.
- **Source:** Wiktionary language-specific category pages; extract idiom entries and a cleaned core gloss from each sense definition (excluded usage notes and examples). Polysemous idioms are removed to preserve one-to-one interpretability ("single-sense filtering").
- **Gloss-based candidate retrieval:** Embed glosses with `text-embedding-3-large`; for a source idiom x with gloss g_x and a candidate y with gloss g_y, define `s(x, y) = cos(E(g_x), E(g_y))`. Retrieve top-k=10 candidates per direction.
- **MNN alignment:** Retain pair (x, y) iff x and y are rank-1 mutual nearest neighbors under both A→B and B→A, enforcing a one-to-one matching constraint.
- **Distribution-aware filtering:** For each language pair, discretize rank-1 similarity scores into 10 equal-width bins over the within-pair range, identify the modal bin, and retain pairs whose score falls at or above the lower edge of the modal bin. This handles encoder calibration drift and avoids spurious forced alignments.
- **Embedding consistency check:** Re-deriving similarities with `Qwen3-Embedding-8B` yields a systematic shift in absolute cosine values but preserves relative structure (Pearson r = 0.807, Spearman ρ = 0.784), supporting the use of similarity as a relative diagnostic signal.

### Task 1 — Multiple-Choice Idiom Equivalence
- 4-way multiple choice. The correct option is the canonical target idiom; distractors are three typed variants:
  - **LT — Literal Translation Trap:** word-for-word translation of the source idiom.
  - **LC — Lexical Cue Trap:** a target idiom sharing a partial lexical cue with the literal translation but conveying an unrelated meaning.
  - **CA — Contextual Association Trap:** a target idiom that is contextually plausible yet semantically opposite.
- Distractors generated with Qwen-Max under a unified prompt with explicit type constraints; an option-only control confirms no significant preference for the gold option over chance and 81.5% distractor-type validity.
- Greedy decoding (temperature=0, top-p=1); options shuffled per instance to reduce positional bias.
- 30 evaluation directions: 16 high-resource-target (8 Zh-target, 8 En-target) + 14 other-target.

### Task 2 — Gloss-Contrastive Generation
- Open-ended generation: given a source idiom, output a single target-language idiom (no explanation). No-gloss (idiom only) vs With-gloss (idiom + English gloss) on all 72 directions.
- Greedy decoding; embedding-based semantic proxy using Qwen3-Embedding-8B to compute `cos(E(output), E(canonical target))`. Report Acc@t at t=0.70 and t=0.80; t=0.80 ≈ median similarity of canonical pairs in G-IdiomAlign (≈0.78).
- Auxiliary metrics (EM, BLEU, ChrF) and a small human evaluation reported in Appendix J. Embedding-based scoring is treated as a "high-confidence semantic indicator" rather than definitive correctness; valid synonymous idioms may be under-counted.

### Models evaluated
- DeepSeek-V3.2 (Thinking / No-Thinking), Gemini-2.5-Pro, Claude-4.5-Haiku, Ministral-8B-Instruct, Qwen3-8B (T/NT). Open-source models run on a single NVIDIA A40 GPU; proprietary models via official APIs. Distractor generation uses Qwen-Max; embedding scoring uses Qwen3-Embedding-8B.

### Attention-based diagnostics (Qwen3-8B)
- Extract post-softmax self-attention with TransformerLens hooks under greedy decoding.
- 200 generations per condition; Type 0 invalid outputs excluded → 189 valid With-gloss, 189 valid No-gloss; two annotators label outcomes (Cohen's κ = 0.81).
- Error taxonomy: T2 (literal word-by-word missing figurative meaning), T3 (meaning-correct but non-idiomatic), T4 (meaning-incorrect and not literal).
- **Structural overlap:** compute Jaccard overlap of salient head sets vs salient layer sets between conditions (Table 6). J_heads=0.32 vs J_layers=0.90 ⇒ cross-condition differences are head-localized, not layer-wide.
- **Token-level metrics:** IAR = total attention mass on the idiom span; GAR = total attention on the gloss span (With-gloss only); DR = residual attention mass; OtherTop1 = max off-span share. Cliff's δ and BH-FDR-corrected Mann-Whitney U / Kruskal-Wallis tests used.

## Results & Highlights

### Dataset statistics
- 18,785 aligned idiom pairs across 36 unordered language pairs; pair sizes range 186–1,782, median 422 (IQR 323–574). Largest pairs: En–Zh (1,782), En–Pl (1,182), Es–Zh (1,028), En–Es (1,114). Smallest: Fr–Ja (186), Fr–Pt (209), Ja–Pt (206).
- **Alignment quality:** Mean strict accuracy 0.685 (95% CI [0.645, 0.724]) and lenient accuracy 0.923 (CI [0.907, 0.940]) over language pairs by LLM-based majority voting. En–De/En–Es/En–Pt/Pt–Es reach strict accuracy up to 0.96; De–Ja/Fr–Ja/Zh–Es are notably weaker.
- Construction-time similarity scores: mean 0.670, median 0.651, min 0.368.

### Task 1 — Multiple-Choice Idiom Equivalence
- Gemini-2.5-Pro is the strongest overall (macro 65.13%, micro 65.17%), particularly on Other targets.
- Other-target accuracy is consistently lower than Zh-/En-target across all models, confirming the low-resource challenge. Gap is most pronounced for weaker models.
- **Error decomposition (Figure 3):** **Literal Translation Trap (LT) is the dominant error type, especially for low-resource targets.** Zh-target shows more Lexical Cue (LC) errors (partial lexical overlap misleads when Chinese is the target). Lower-performing models also show higher Contextual Association (CA) rates on Other targets.
- Thinking mode: improves DeepSeek-V3.2 (reduces LT and LC) and Qwen3-8B (decreases LC and CA, LT still dominant). DeepSeek-V3.2: 52.70→61.45 (macro), 53.65→63.02 (micro); Qwen3-8B: 28.98→36.14 (macro), 30.56→37.55 (micro).
- Best per column: Gemini-2.5-Pro 65.13/65.17 (overall), DeepSeek-V3.2 (T) 69.37/69.18 (Zh-target), Gemini-2.5-Pro 66.53/64.95 (En-target), Gemini-2.5-Pro 62.82/62.91 (Other targets).

### Task 2 — Gloss-Contrastive Generation
- Glosses **consistently improve** every model: gains on Acc@0.80 range from +3.66 (DeepSeek-V3.2 NT) to +8.28 (Ministral-8B-Instruct) absolute percentage points.
- With-gloss ΔAcc@0.80 ordering: Ministral-8B-Instruct +8.28, Gemini-2.5-Pro +7.41, Qwen3-8B (NT) +7.22, Qwen3-8B (T) +6.82, Claude-4.5-Haiku +5.19, DeepSeek-V3.2 (T) +4.97, DeepSeek-V3.2 (NT) +3.66.
- **Best overall:** DeepSeek-V3.2 (T) With-gloss — MeanSim 74.24, Acc@0.70 54.90, Acc@0.80 29.63.
- Despite consistent gloss gains, **Acc@0.80 remains modest** (DeepSeek-V3.2 T best at 29.63%), indicating substantial headroom in the open output space.
- Smaller models (Ministral-8B, Qwen3-8B) benefit proportionally more from gloss injection, suggesting glosses partly compensate for weaker parametric knowledge.

### Attention-based diagnostics (Qwen3-8B)
- **Structural overlap (Table 6):** J_heads = 0.32 vs J_layers = 0.90 (All), and 0.32 vs 0.76 (Correct-only); cross-condition differences are expressed more through head reconfiguration than layer shifts.
- **Token-level (Table 7, With-gloss):** Correct vs Wrong — GAR (0.07 vs 0.06, δ=+0.24, q=0.026) and DR (0.90 vs 0.91, δ=-0.20, q=0.033) and OtherTop1 (0.57 vs 0.58, δ=-0.21, q=0.033) all reach significance; **IAR does not distinguish Correct from Wrong** (q=0.250). I.e., better With-gloss generations coincide with **stronger gloss anchoring**, not just more attention to the idiom span.
- **No-gloss:** IAR, DR, OtherTop1 differences do not survive correction (all q ≥ 0.087), and WrongType comparisons are strongly structured across error types (IAR/DR/OtherTop1 all q<0.001), indicating more heterogeneous failure modes in the absence of an explicit gloss anchor.

## Critical Assessment
- **Novelty:** Gloss-pivot idea is sensible and the MNN + distribution-aware filtering pipeline is a clean precision-first design. The typed-distractor taxonomy (LT/LC/CA) is a genuine contribution over prior idiom benchmarks that only report accuracy. The head-vs-layer mechanistic read is novel for this task family. However, the broader "use external definitions as pivot for low-resource idiom transfer" idea has prior art in lexicon/knowledge-base-augmented MT; the marginal novelty is in the controlled No/With-gloss contrast + attention diagnostics.
- **Rigor:** Methodological strengths: bidirectional MNN constraint; pair-adaptive thresholding instead of a global cosine cutoff; LLM-judge majority voting for alignment quality with confidence intervals; effect sizes (Cliff's δ) and BH-FDR correction for attention tests; inter-annotator κ=0.81. Weaknesses: the embedding-based semantic proxy is a known weak evaluator for open generation — the paper reports Acc@0.80 best at 29.63% for the strongest model, which is a low absolute number, and the authors honestly frame it as a "high-confidence indicator" rather than a definitive score. The attention analyses are correlational on a single model (Qwen3-8B) with 189 valid instances per condition; CIs are not reported.
- **Reproducibility:** Public dataset release (GitHub NLP2CT/G-IdiomAlign) is a strong plus. The 3-point human/LLM annotation rubric is clearly described, and LLM-judge prompts are referenced. Distractor generation uses Qwen-Max; if its API or model changes, exact replication is harder.
- **Significance:** Useful community benchmark for cross-lingual idiom alignment with diagnostic protocols. The LT-as-dominant-error finding is intuitive but now rigorously quantified across 7 model variants and 30 directions. The head-localized gloss-anchoring signal is interesting and worth following up.
- **Red flags / honest concerns:**
  1. **English-pivot bias is acknowledged but unresolved.** Wiktionary glosses can compress pragmatic/cultural nuance, vary in style/granularity across editions, and may not exist for culture-specific idioms. The four "underrepresented" languages (Ar, Ko, Th, Vi) are reported separately in Appendix A — the headline numbers (and mechanistic claims) are based on the 9 core languages, which are still relatively high-resource.
  2. **The headline "modest improvement with gloss" undercuts the framing.** Best Acc@0.80 is 29.63%; the glosses help everywhere but the absolute numbers are low, and the paper is honest about "substantial headroom." A practical takeaway would be that explicit glosses are not a sufficient mitigation.
  3. **Embedding-based scoring is the only aggregate Task 2 metric.** EM/BLEU/ChrF and a "small human evaluation" are deferred to Appendix J; without them in the main body, the central Task 2 claim rests on a noisy proxy.
  4. **Mechanistic claims are on a single model and a modest annotated sample (n=189/condition).** Head-vs-layer overlap is a structural metric, not a causal one; the paper correctly avoids causal language, but downstream readers may overinterpret.
  5. **Strict vs lenient accuracy gap (0.685 vs 0.923)** is large, suggesting a lot of "partial" pairs; this is honestly reported but means the benchmark has fuzzy edges.
  6. **Single-sense filtering and one-to-one MNN** improve interpretability but under-represent many-to-many synonym clusters and polysemous idioms — i.e., the benchmark is intentionally a slice of idiom alignment, not a complete map.

## Strengths
- Clean two-protocol design (MCQ with LT/LC/CA typed distractors + No/With-gloss contrastive generation) — supports both attribution and causal isolation
- Precision-first pipeline: MNN bidirectional agreement + distribution-aware filtering avoids spurious forced alignments
- Typed distractors enable systematic error attribution rather than only accuracy; LT-dominance finding is a clean, falsifiable result
- Head-level mechanistic analysis is a rare and welcome addition to a benchmark paper
- Honest framing of results — "modest improvement," "substantial headroom," and the embedding proxy is correctly called "high-confidence semantic indicator rather than a complete estimate"
- Public release of dataset and code (GitHub)
- Strong funding/affiliation backing; broad model coverage (7 variants including Thinking-mode ablations)

## Limitations
- Embedding-based semantic proxy is a noisy evaluator for open-ended generation; human eval and xCOMET-style metrics are absent from the main body
- Mechanistic findings are on a single model (Qwen3-8B) with 189 valid instances per condition; causal claims are out of scope
- English-pivot bias is acknowledged but not fully mitigated; coverage skews toward high-resource language pairs
- Single-sense filtering + MNN one-to-one constraint under-represent polysemous idioms and many-to-many synonym clusters
- Three of the four "underrepresented" languages (Ar, Ko, Th, Vi) are pushed to Appendix A; the main numbers are essentially high-resource
- Best Task 2 Acc@0.80 = 29.63% — even with gloss, the open-generation regime is far from solved
- 81.5% distractor-type validity leaves ~18.5% of distractors that may be subtly mis-typed, confounding LT/LC/CA attribution
- Distractor generation depends on a specific Qwen-Max API; replicability of distractors is sensitive to model version

## Relevance
- 与本仓库关注的"LLM 评测、跨语言迁移、低资源 NLP"高度相关：G-IdiomAlign 提供了可复现的习语翻译评测协议、定量失败模式分析（LT 主导、head-level 释义锚定）以及带类型化干扰项的错误归因方法，对研究多语言 LLM 的非组合性语义处理与机制可解释性具有直接参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration
- Does the head-level gloss-anchoring signal generalize beyond Qwen3-8B to other model families (Llama, Mistral, GPT-class) and to thinking-mode vs non-thinking mode?
- Can stronger semantic-quality metrics (xCOMET, LLM-as-judge with structured rubrics, large-scale human evaluation) corroborate the "modest improvement" finding from embedding-based proxies — or does the proxy systematically overstate gloss gains?
- How does the benchmark behave on truly low-resource targets (e.g., African, Indigenous, low-resource Asian languages) beyond the 4 in Appendix A?
- Is the literal-translation bias reducible by training (idiom-aware SFT, DPO on idiom pairs, retrieval-augmented decoding) rather than only by gloss prompting?
- How robust is the benchmark to noisy or ambiguous Wiktionary glosses, especially for culture-specific idioms, and can multilingual non-English glosses reduce English-pivot bias?
- Can the LT/LC/CA distractor taxonomy be extended to capture additional failure modes (register shift, connotation, sociolinguistic appropriateness)?
- Does the 81.5% distractor-type validity mask confounds — e.g., are some "literal" distractors actually valid paraphrases the model correctly rejects for the wrong reason?
- Could a causal intervention (e.g., attention-head ablation or steering) verify that the head-level gloss anchoring is **causally** responsible for the With-gloss improvement, or is it epiphenomenal?
- How does idiom alignment quality correlate with downstream MT quality on idiomatic-heavy sentences — is G-IdiomAlign predictive of real-world translation failures?
