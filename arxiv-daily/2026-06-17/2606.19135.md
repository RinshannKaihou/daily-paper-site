> **一句话总结：** 本文采用 Nickerson 迭代分类法，对九个主流开源 LLM Agent 通信协议（MCP、A2A、LAP、agents.json、Agora、ANP、LMOS、ACP、agntcy）进行五轮系统化分析，最终构建出涵盖"对手、负载、交互状态、发现机制、模式灵活性"五维度的技术分类法，并据此预测 LLM Agent 通信将走向"联邦化、分层协议栈"的演化路径。

> **TL;DR:** This paper builds a 5-dimension technical taxonomy of LLM agent communication protocols by iteratively applying Nickerson et al.'s method to nine actively maintained open-source protocols (MCP, A2A, LAP, agents.json, Agora, ANP, LMOS, ACP, agntcy). The dimensions are counterparty, payload, interaction state, discovery mechanism, and schema flexibility. Key findings: all 7 agent-to-agent protocols pair hybrid payloads with session-state persistence, 7/9 support multiple predefined schemas, only 2/9 (Agora, ANP) negotiate schemas at runtime, and only LMOS approaches decentralized discovery. The authors map the protocols to Marro's "communication trilemma" (versatility / efficiency / portability) and argue that, short-term, A2A-style protocols will likely extend MCP to unify A2A and A2C, but long-term the field will evolve toward a federated, layered stack analogous to the OSI model, because no single protocol can simultaneously maximize all three trilemma corners.

## 核心贡献

- **方法论严谨性**：显式声明分类法的目的（classify and distinguish LLM agent communication protocols）、元特征（按组件类型与通信特征分类）、终止条件，并经历三轮"实证→概念化"+两轮"概念→实证"迭代收敛；每次迭代覆盖 3 个新协议，确保维度饱和（最终迭代未发现新维度）。
- **五维分类法**：在协议层面（而非网络层/传输层）的应用层视角，提出五个相互正交、共同穷尽的维度——counterparty、payload、interaction state、discovery mechanism、schema flexibility。
- **协议层面的具体发现**：
  - **A2A 协议必有会话状态**（7/7 A2A 协议支持 session state），且**全部使用 hybrid payload**（7/7 支持结构化数据 + 对话文本）。
  - **多模式灵活性已成主流**（7/9 支持 multiple schemas），2/9（Agora + ANP）实现 runtime schema 演化。
  - **去中心化发现仍属罕见**：4/9 静态、4/9 中心化注册、1/9 混合（LMOS），无人实现纯去中心化发现。
- **拒绝维度的论证**：明确给出拒绝 Initiative Flow、Simplicity、Persistent State 三个候选维度的理由（违反互斥性 / 概念模糊 / 维度定位重叠）。
- **未来架构蓝图**：基于 Marro 的"通信三难"（versatility / efficiency / portability），提出一个五层候选协议栈（Identity & transport → Discovery → Execution → Interaction → Deliberation），类比 OSI 模型。
- **隐私 / 合规盲点**：指出所有被研究协议都缺乏隐私保护、合规检查与策略执行机制。

## Key Contributions

- **Methodological rigor**: Explicitly defines purpose, meta-characteristic, and ending conditions; runs three empirical-to-conceptual iterations and two conceptual-to-empirical iterations; saturation reached when the final iteration yields no new dimensions.
- **5-dimension taxonomy** at the application/protocol layer: counterparty, payload, interaction state, discovery mechanism, schema flexibility — mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive.
- **Empirical patterns across 9 protocols**:
  - A2A → session state is mandatory (7/7 A2A protocols).
  - A2A → payload is always hybrid (7/7), never conversation-focused-only or structured-only.
  - Schema flexibility is mainstream (7/9 multiple, 2/9 evolving).
  - Discovery is mostly static or centralized (8/9); LMOS is the only "hybrid" outlier; true P2P decentralized discovery is absent.
- **Rejected-dimension analysis** is unusually explicit — Initiative Flow, Simplicity, and Persistent State are excluded for mutual-exclusivity failure, conceptual vagueness, and overlap with the interaction-state dimension respectively.
- **Communication trilemma mapping**: Uses Marro's trilemma to show that rigid-schema / stateless protocols (e.g., MCP) maximize efficiency/portability at the cost of versatility, while evolving-schema protocols (Agora, ANP) maximize versatility at the cost of efficiency.
- **Layered-stack proposal**: 5-layer "OSI-like" stack (L1 Identity & transport, L2 Discovery, L3 Execution, L4 Interaction, L5 Deliberation) with candidate protocols assigned to each layer.
- **Open research gaps**: privacy, policy enforcement, decentralized discovery, authentication/security mechanisms, embedded policies/norms.

## Methodology

The authors follow Nickerson, Varshney & Muntermann's (2013) iterative taxonomy-development method, which prescribes the upfront definition of three artifacts (purpose, meta-characteristic, ending conditions) and alternating empirical-to-conceptual and conceptual-to-empirical passes.

**Sampling procedure.** Starting from a candidate pool of LLM-agent-targeted communication protocols, they apply four inclusion criteria:
1. Explicitly designed for LLM agents (excludes classical MAS protocols like FIPA ACL and the Contract Net Protocol, which are not LLM-targeted).
2. Open source with a runnable implementation (excludes Firecrawl, uAgents, and the LOKA proposal that has no implementation).
3. Active maintenance — last non-README commit after June 2024, with sufficient community traction (GitHub stars as a proxy); this excludes the Agent Protocol and AITP.
4. Demonstrable adoption, signaled by GitHub stars.

**Final sample (n = 9):** MCP, A2A, LAP, agents.json, Agora, ANP, LMOS, ACP, agntcy.

**Iterative procedure.** Three empirical-to-conceptual iterations analyze 3 protocols each; two conceptual-to-empirical iterations consolidate and test more abstract dimensions. The process stops when no new dimensions emerge (saturation criterion from the ending conditions).

**Quality criteria.** The authors self-audit on Nickerson et al.'s four qualitative-taxonomy requirements: conciseness, robustness (no new dimensions in the final iteration), comprehensiveness (all 9 protocols classifiable), and mutual-exclusivity & collective-exhaustiveness (enforced during dimension construction).

**Classification validation.** Two complementary tables (Table 1 — pattern detection view; Table 2 — per-protocol rationale view) are produced to cross-check that the taxonomy applies cleanly to all 9 protocols and to make patterns pop out (e.g., "all A2A protocols → session state").

## Results & Highlights

- **Counterparty**: agent = 7 (Agora, ANP, LAP, agntcy, A2A, ACP, A2A-related); context = 2 (MCP, agents.json); hybrid = 1 (LMOS, which explicitly advertises support for both).
- **Interaction State**: stateless = 2 (MCP, agents.json); session state = 7 (everything else). The 2 stateless protocols are exactly the 2 that target the context counterparty — a sharp pattern.
- **Payload**: structured data & artifacts = 2 (MCP, agents.json); conversation-focused = 0; hybrid = 7. Notable: even the A2A protocols do not restrict themselves to conversation payloads.
- **Discovery Mechanism**: static = 4 (MCP, LAP, agents.json, Agora), centralized = 4 (A2A, ANP, ACP, agntcy), partially centralized = 0, decentralized = 0, hybrid = 1 (LMOS). The authors highlight the absence of a true decentralized discovery mechanism as a major gap.
- **Schema Flexibility**: single = 2 (LAP, agents.json), multiple = 5 (MCP, A2A, LMOS, ACP, agntcy), evolving = 2 (Agora, ANP). The two evolving-schema protocols (Agora, ANP) are also the only ones to expose a meta-protocol negotiation layer.
- **The 5-layer stack proposal (Figure 3)**:
  - L1 Identity & transport — ANP, agntcy, LMOS
  - L2 Discovery — agents.json, LMOS
  - L3 Execution — MCP
  - L4 Interaction (tasks & streaming) — A2A, LAP, ACP, agntcy
  - L5 Deliberation (schema negotiation) — Agora, ANP
- **Trilemma mapping**: MCP is at the efficiency/portability extreme (rigid schema, stateless, structured payload); Agora/ANP are at the versatility extreme (evolving schema, session state, hybrid payload, with token overhead); the majority cluster (A2A, LMOS, ACP) sit in the middle by offering hybrid payloads + multiple pre-defined schemas.

## Authors & Credibility

All four authors are affiliated with **Technische Universität München (TUM), Munich, Germany**. Two authors — Habtom Kahsay Gidey and Alois Knoll — have a strong multi-agent / autonomous-agent research track record (prior work on cognitive bots, affordance representation, pattern languages for resilient agents, fundamentals of LLM agents, document-based knowledge discovery), which lends domain expertise to the survey. Alexander Lenz and Linus Sander are listed as contributing / corresponding authors. The paper is a structured qualitative taxonomy — not a benchmark or empirical study — so author expertise is judged primarily by their demonstrated familiarity with both classical MAS literature (FIPA, KQML) and the contemporary LLM-agent protocol stack. The taxonomy's explicit grounding in Nickerson et al.'s established methodology is a credibility plus.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty.** Moderate. As the authors themselves note, the closest prior work is Yang et al.'s (2025) "A survey of AI agent protocols," which already proposed an object-orientation dimension (context-oriented vs inter-agent) — this work extends that with a hybrid value, plus four additional dimensions (payload, interaction state, discovery mechanism, schema flexibility). The schema-flexibility dimension and the explicit mapping to Marro's communication trilemma are the more distinctive contributions.

**Rigor.** Strong on the qualitative-taxonomy side — the Nickerson method is correctly applied, iterations are transparent, and the rejected-dimension analysis is unusually explicit. Saturation is reported (no new dimensions in the final iteration), giving the 5-dimension claim some empirical grounding. However, the paper is short on validation: there is no inter-coder reliability check, no second rater independently classifying the 9 protocols, and no user study testing whether the taxonomy actually helps practitioners select protocols. A small-scale inter-rater agreement study (e.g., Cohen's κ on 9 protocols × 5 dimensions) would substantially strengthen the claims.

**Reproducibility.** High. The protocol selection criteria, the 5-dimension definitions, and the per-protocol classification rationale (Table 2) are all spelled out clearly enough that an independent researcher could re-derive the same assignments. The selection thresholds (e.g., 20 stars cut-off for AITP) are explicit, but a few of them are date-stamped (e.g., "accessed 7 Jul 2025") and will become stale quickly — the field moves faster than the snapshot.

**Significance.** Meaningful for practitioners choosing an LLM agent communication protocol and for researchers tracking the "Internet of Agents" literature. The layered-stack proposal is the most useful artifact for protocol selection. However, the paper does not propose a novel protocol, an evaluation, or any system — it is a survey/taxonomy, and should be cited as such.

**Red flags / weak points.**
- **Sample size of n = 9 is small**, and selection is biased toward Western big-tech / OSS-incubator protocols (Google A2A, Anthropic MCP, LangChain LAP, IBM/BeeAI ACP, agntcy/Linux Foundation, Eclipse LMOS, Oxford Agora). Chinese-origin agent-protocol efforts and any non-GitHub-distributed protocol are absent. The "global standard" claim therefore reads slightly provincial.
- **GitHub stars as an adoption proxy is noisy** — a single viral repository can dominate. The authors do acknowledge the cut-off (e.g., AITP with 20 stars) but a more rigorous adoption metric (e.g., number of dependent projects, PyPI/npm downloads, production deployments) would be more defensible.
- **The 5-dimension claim is not falsified rigorously.** Saturation across 9 protocols is weak evidence; a more careful analysis would argue why additional dimensions (e.g., security, transport) belong at a different abstraction layer rather than being "saturated out."
- **The "federated, layered protocol stack" prediction is intuitive but not novel** — it is essentially a direct analogy to the OSI model / DNS layering, applied to agents. The paper does not provide a mechanism (governance body, IETF-style working group, schema-registration policy) for how the field would actually arrive at such layering. The trilemma argument supports the *need* for layering but not the *coordination* problem.
- **No empirical performance evaluation.** The trilemma claims (e.g., "Agora's evolving schema introduces significant token overhead and latency") are repeated from Marro et al. and are not measured in this paper.
- **The privacy / policy-enforcement gap is acknowledged but not developed** — the paper could have pointed to specific standards (e.g., W3C DID, OAuth 2.0 extensions, OAuth-Scoped Tokens for MCP) that could plug the gap.

**Overall.** A solid qualitative taxonomy that is well-grounded methodologically, but its strongest contribution is the schematic / pedagogical (the 5-dimension matrix and the 5-layer stack) rather than predictive. Read it for the framework, not for evidence of convergence.

## Strengths

- Methodologically transparent: clearly states the Nickerson-method artifacts, iteration counts, and saturation criterion.
- Empirically grounded in 9 real, currently maintained, open-source protocols — not synthetic or hypothetical.
- Provides two complementary views (Table 1 for patterns, Table 2 for per-protocol rationale), which makes the classification easy to audit.
- Explicitly reports rejected dimensions and explains *why* — a sign of disciplined taxonomy work.
- Maps the taxonomy to Marro's communication trilemma, providing a principled argument for why a single monolithic protocol is unlikely to dominate.
- The 5-layer "OSI-like" stack proposal is a practical artifact for protocol selection (which protocol to use in which layer of a system).
- Cites both classical MAS literature (FIPA, KQML, Contract Net) and contemporary LLM-agent protocol work, situating the contribution well.

## Limitations

- n = 9 protocols is small; the "saturation" claim is correspondingly weak. Emerging or non-English-community protocols are not represented.
- GitHub stars is a noisy adoption proxy; production-deployed or enterprise-internal protocols may be missed.
- No inter-rater reliability check; the classifications are produced by the authors themselves.
- No empirical performance evaluation of the trilemma trade-offs — claims about token overhead and latency for evolving-schema protocols are inherited from prior work, not measured here.
- The layered-stack prediction is not paired with a governance or coordination mechanism for *how* the field would arrive at such a stack.
- The privacy / policy-enforcement gap is flagged but not addressed — no specific recommendations, no reference to existing standards (DID, OAuth, XACML) that could be plugged in.
- The paper is dated mid-2025; the field moves quickly, and several of the "actively maintained" protocols will have evolved by the time this taxonomy is read.
- The taxonomy operates at the application/protocol layer; lower-layer concerns (transport, encoding, security at the wire level) are explicitly out of scope and would need to be supplemented.

## Relevance

本论文与"自演化 Agent 与自动研究"方向高度相关——多 Agent 协同系统是 LLM 驱动的 AI 研究员/科学发现系统的核心架构之一，而通信协议的标准化与分层正是支撑可扩展、可互操作 Agent 网络的关键基础设施。同时，论文对协议层面的"通信三难"分析（versatility / efficiency / portability 不可同时最大化）也对自演化 Agent 系统的可靠性分析具有方法论参考价值。

## Relevance (English)

This paper is highly relevant to the "Self-Evolving Agents & Automated Research" thread — multi-agent collaboration is a core architecture for LLM-driven AI-researcher and scientific-discovery systems, and standardized, layered communication protocols are the missing infrastructure for scalable, interoperable agent networks. The communication-trilemma framing (versatility / efficiency / portability cannot be jointly maximized) is also methodologically useful for analyzing the reliability / efficiency trade-offs of self-improving agent stacks.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. **Layering governance**: If the field converges on a 5-layer stack, what governance body (IETF-style WG? Linux Foundation project? de-facto GitHub-org dominance?) would actually coordinate cross-layer schema and version compatibility? The paper notes the convergence pressure but not the coordination problem.
2. **Decentralized discovery**: With only LMOS even approaching peer-to-peer discovery, what concrete cryptographic / DHT / DID-based mechanisms could enable Internet-of-Agents-scale discovery without central registries? The paper flags the gap but offers no solution direction.
3. **Security & policy enforcement**: Can existing standards (OAuth 2.0, W3C DID, XACML, OPA) be retrofitted onto the 9 surveyed protocols? A systematic mapping of "protocol × security-standard" coverage would close the acknowledged gap.
4. **Empirical trilemma measurement**: The paper inherits Marro's claim that evolving-schema protocols incur token / latency overhead. A controlled benchmark (e.g., Agora vs. A2A on a fixed task suite) would quantify the cost and inform protocol selection.
5. **How does the taxonomy cope with protocols that are *under* active construction?** Several sampled protocols (Agora, ANP) had specification pages still in flux at the time of the snapshot. A "temporal-stability" sub-dimension or versioning of the classification over time would help.
6. **Cross-cultural / non-English-community protocol coverage**: A 2026 follow-up should include agent-protocol efforts from Chinese AI ecosystems (e.g., agent-protocol proposals tied to major Chinese LLM platforms), where open-source agent infrastructure is being developed in parallel and may diverge architecturally.
7. **The "schema flexibility" trade-off in practice**: Is the additional token cost of evolving schemas (Agora's natural-language→structured protocol negotiation) actually worth the versatility gain, or do pre-defined multiple schemas (A2A's approach) cover the practical need at lower cost? A user study with developers would be informative.
8. **Persistent state at the protocol layer**: The authors reject a "Persistent State" dimension because most protocols could in principle store long-term state. But long-term memory is central to LLM-agent capability. Should a follow-up taxonomy re-introduce persistent state as a first-class dimension, perhaps at a different layer (L0 substrate)?
