> **一句话总结：** 在 42 个 LLM × 4 个角色设定下系统评估其对阅读理解题区分度（item discrimination）的预测能力：直接数值预测最高 Spearman 仅 0.152（GPT-5.5），且多数模型（含 GPT-4o/4.1、Llama 3.1/3.3、Claude 3.5/3.7）出现显著负相关；基于作答的 CTT 校准把所有模型-角色视为"合成考生"，最高也仅 0.241，且分布系统性右偏——说明 LLM 还不能可靠地捕捉"题目对不同水平学生的区分力"。

> **TL;DR:** This paper systematically evaluates 42 LLMs on **item discrimination** — the psychometric property measuring whether an assessment item separates high- from low-proficiency students — for reading-comprehension items from the Cambridge MCQ Reading Dataset (793 items, 120 passages). Two zero-shot protocols are compared: (1) **direct discrimination prediction** from item content (with gold answer visible) and (2) **response-based CTT calibration**, where each model × persona pair is treated as a synthetic examinee and item discrimination is computed as the point-biserial correlation between item correctness and total score. The best direct-prediction model (GPT-5.5) reaches Spearman = 0.152 (low-persona), while most other strong models are near zero or *significantly negative* (e.g., Llama 3.1-8B at −0.147, GPT-4.1 at −0.140, GPT-4o at −0.121, Claude 3.5 Haiku at −0.116). CTT calibration improves the ceiling to Spearman = 0.241 (all-persona pool) but the synthetic distribution is systematically shifted to the right of the human distribution, and even the best configuration retrieves fewer than 40% of the bottom-10% low-discrimination items. The negative result is framed as a **competence paradox**: LLMs solve the items accurately enough that they cannot faithfully simulate weaker test-takers, so response-based CTT lacks the proficiency-stratified variation it needs.

## 核心贡献

- 首次系统地把"LLM 能否预测题目区分度"独立成一个大尺度评估问题（与已广泛研究的题目难度预测相区分），并在 42 个 LLM 上报告了详细数值。
- 提出"双协议"评估框架：(a) 题目内容 + 黄金答案 → 直接数值区分度预测；(b) LLM 自身作答（不告知答案）→ 作为合成考生群体做 CTT 点二列相关校准。两条路径各自隔离"内容理解信号"与"作答行为信号"。
- 报告一项关键负向发现：多个能力强的模型在直接预测上呈现**显著负相关**（与人类排序方向相反），且图 1 表明这是模型之间**共享但反向**的偏置——不是随机噪声。
- 揭示**能力悖论 (competence paradox)**：当前 LLM 解题正确率太高（GPT-5.5 准确率 96.2%，Llama 3.3-70B 81.8%，Phi-4 81.0%），即使用 low-proficiency persona 提示也无法压低其准确率，导致 CTT 校准的合成响应矩阵缺乏水平分层变化。

## Key Contributions

- **Large-scale systematic study** of Human–AI alignment on item discrimination across 42 proprietary and open-weight LLMs, decoupling discrimination from the more studied item-difficulty problem.
- **Dual-protocol evaluation** (direct numerical prediction vs. response-based CTT calibration) that isolates content-only signals from response-derived ones; both implemented in zero-shot settings.
- **Empirical ceiling of weak alignment**: best Spearman = 0.152 (direct, GPT-5.5 low-persona) and 0.241 (CTT, all-persona pool), with statistical significance testing (∗, ∗∗, ∗∗∗) reported per cell.
- **Discovery of a systematically reversed signal** (Figure 1): several capable models (Llama 3.1-8B, Llama 3.3-70B, GPT-4o, GPT-4.1, Claude 3.5 Haiku, Phi-4) are *negatively* correlated with human discrimination, and the consensus among models moves opposite to the human ordering.
- **Item-screening diagnostic** (Overlap@k) showing that even the best CTT configuration retrieves only 36.7% of the bottom-10% low-discrimination items and 25.3% of the top-10% high-discrimination items.
- **Competence-paradox framing**: explains why response-based CTT underperforms — high item-solving accuracy (e.g., GPT-5.5 at 96.2%, Claude 3.7 Sonnet at 92.2%, Qwen3-235B-A22B at 90.6%) leaves little stratified response variation to exploit.

## Methodology

### Dataset
- **Cambridge Multiple-Choice Questions Reading Dataset** (Mullooly et al., 2023; Liusie et al., 2023): 793 item records from 120 reading-comprehension tasks.
- Each item has passage, question stem, 4 answer options, gold answer, CEFR level, and **human-calibrated CTT statistics** (item difficulty and discrimination).
- Ground-truth label `yᵢ` = human-calibrated item discrimination (point-biserial correlation between item correctness `vᵢ ∈ {0,1}` and examinee total score `s`).

### Two protocols
1. **Direct discrimination prediction** — model receives the full item context (passage, question, options) **plus the gold answer**, and outputs a single scalar discrimination value in `[-1, 1]`.
2. **Response-based CTT calibration** — model answers the item **without** seeing the gold answer; its answer is binarized into correctness and treated as one "synthetic examinee" in a CTT calculation that exactly mirrors the human-calibration procedure.

### Student simulation
- Four proficiency configurations: `p₀` (no persona, baseline) and low/medium/high-proficiency personas (system prompt instructs the model to play a weak / average / good Cambridge test-taker).
- Each (model, persona) pair becomes one synthetic test-taker; pools are constructed for each persona and for the all-persona configuration.
- CTT-calibrated estimate for item *i* in pool `Rₛ`:
  `ỹᵢ⁽ˢ⁾ = corr( {vᵢ,ₘ,ₚ}₍ₘ,ₚ₎∈Rₛ , {Tₘ,ₚ⁽ˢ⁾}₍ₘ,ₚ₎∈Rₛ )`
- Items with constant correctness across all synthetic test-takers get a discrimination of 0 (undefined correlation).

### Null baseline
- Independently shuffle each synthetic test-taker's correctness vector across items (preserves total-score distribution, destroys item-level alignment); repeat 1,000 times. Near-zero null Spearman (~0.001) confirms observed CTT correlation is not an artifact of the computation.

### Models
- 42 LLMs total: 8 OpenAI (GPT-3.5-Turbo, GPT-4o-mini, GPT-4o, GPT-4.1-mini, GPT-4.1, GPT-o4-mini, GPT-5, GPT-5.5), 2 Anthropic (Claude 3.5 Haiku, Claude 3.7 Sonnet), 3 Google (Gemini 2.0 Flash, 2.5 Flash, 2.5 Pro), plus Llama 2/3.1/3.2/3.3, Mistral-7B, OLMo 2-7B/13B, Phi-3/3.5/4/4-mini, Qwen2.5/3/3.5 (multiple sizes).
- All evaluated zero-shot with the model instructed to put the final answer/score in `\boxed{...}`.

### Evaluation metrics
- **Spearman rank correlation** with human-calibrated discrimination (primary; tests whether the model preserves the human ranking of items by discrimination).
- **RMSE** (secondary; tests numerical calibration accuracy).
- **Overlap@k** for k = 10, 20: proportion of the bottom-k% (Low@10/20) or top-k% (High@10/20) human low-discrimination items also selected by the LLM; random baseline is 0.10 / 0.20.
- 95% confidence intervals for Spearman via item-level bootstrap (1,000 resamples).

## Results & Highlights

### Direct discrimination prediction (Table 1, 20 representative models)
- **Best model: GPT-5.5** is the *only* model with consistently positive correlation across all prompting conditions; best cell = 0.152 (low-persona, statistically significant ∗∗∗), all-persona = 0.118 ∗∗∗.
- **Strong reverse signal** in many capable models (statistically significant negatives):
  - Llama 3.1-8B: baseline = −0.147 ∗∗∗, all-persona = −0.132 ∗∗∗
  - Llama 3.3-70B: baseline = −0.080 ∗, all-persona = −0.104 ∗∗∗
  - GPT-4.1: baseline = −0.140 ∗∗∗, all-persona = −0.151 ∗∗∗
  - GPT-4o: baseline = −0.121 ∗∗∗, all-persona = −0.101 ∗∗∗
  - Claude 3.5 Haiku: baseline = −0.116 ∗∗, all-persona = −0.136 ∗∗∗
  - Phi-4: baseline = −0.133 ∗∗∗
  - Mistral-7B-v0.3, Qwen2.5-7B, Qwen3-32B, Claude 3.7 Sonnet, Gemini 2.5 Flash/Pro, GPT-3.5-Turbo — all within ±0.06, none significantly positive except GPT-5.5.
- **All-model average (Last row of Table 1)**: baseline = −0.091 ∗, all-persona = −0.074 ∗ — meaning *the cross-model consensus is also negatively aligned* with humans.
- **Proficiency personas do not consistently help**: gains from low/medium/high prompting are model-specific and not directionally consistent.

### Consensus vs. alignment (Figure 1)
- Many model-persona prediction vectors have positive agreement with each other (high inter-model Spearman) while being **negatively** correlated with human-calibrated discrimination — i.e., models share a heuristic that ranks items in the opposite direction from human item discrimination.

### Distributional analysis (Figure 2)
- Most LLMs produce **compressed prediction distributions** within a narrow positive range, missing the human distribution's broader spread and lower tail.
- Proficiency simulation shifts scale/shape but does not reconstruct a human-like distribution.

### Response-based CTT calibration (Table 2)

|--------------|--------|------------|-----------------|-------|
| Baseline     | 0.287  | 0.203      | [0.132, 0.270]  | 0.001 |
| Low          | 0.285  | 0.230      | [0.162, 0.297]  | -0.000|
| Medium       | 0.290  | 0.192      | [0.123, 0.262]  | 0.002 |

- All CTT pools yield **positive** Spearman, with all-persona best (0.241, 95% CI [0.174, 0.307]).
- Null baseline ≈ 0.001, confirming signal is real and not a CTT computation artifact.
- Distributions (Figure 3) are **systematically right-shifted** vs. human discrimination — synthetic responses overestimate discrimination.

### Item-screening utility (Table 3, Overlap@k)
- **Direct prediction** is mostly near random. GPT-5.5 best: Low@10 = 0.190, Low@20 = 0.296, High@10 = 0.139, High@20 = 0.252. All-model aggregate falls below random for High-discrimination retrieval.
- **CTT calibration** substantially improves low-discrimination retrieval:
  - CTT-Baseline: Low@10 = 0.342, Low@20 = 0.377
  - CTT-All: Low@10 = 0.367, Low@20 = 0.365
  - CTT-Low: Low@10 = 0.316, Low@20 = 0.346
  - CTT-Medium: Low@10 = 0.329, Low@20 = 0.340
  - CTT-High: Low@10 = 0.304, Low@20 = 0.371
- High-discrimination retrieval is weaker: best High@10 = 0.253 (CTT-High), best High@20 = 0.296 (CTT-Low).
- **Even the best setting retrieves < 40% of target items** — LLM-derived discrimination is only a partial proxy for human pretesting.

### Difficulty vs. discrimination (Section 6)
- The paper situates the result alongside prior difficulty-alignment work (Li et al. 2025a): in both, "general problem-solving ability does not imply alignment with human assessment behavior" and "proficiency prompting does not reliably make models behave like lower- or higher-proficiency test-takers."
- Crucial difference: difficulty is aggregate (p-correct); discrimination is **relational** (does p-correct differ by ability?). A discrimination-aware system must model ability-conditioned response patterns, not just an overall probability of success.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty.** Genuine: item discrimination has been underexplored in LLM-evaluation literature, and the dual-protocol + Overlap@k design is a thoughtful template. The framing of a "competence paradox" is also a useful conceptual contribution.
- **Rigor.** Strong overall: 42 models, statistical significance annotations, bootstrap CIs, null baseline, distributional analysis (Figure 2–3), and an application-grounded screening task. However, only one dataset is used (Cambridge MCQ Reading), and the results may not generalize to other item types or to non-multiple-choice formats.
- **Reproducibility.** Prompts (Figures 4–5) and per-model answer accuracies (Table 4) are disclosed; the dataset is public. Limitations: (i) proprietary API drift (especially GPT-5.5, a future-dated model in the citation) makes exact replication fragile; (ii) variance across multiple sampling runs (temperature, seeds) is not reported; (iii) only one prompt template per protocol — no ablation on chain-of-thought or few-shot exemplars.
- **Significance.** The negative finding is valuable and timely, as the field has trended toward "LLM-as-rater" optimism. The 0.241 ceiling is a concrete benchmark future work can target, and the negative-correlation finding (reversed signal) is a non-obvious warning.
- **Red flags / honest concerns.**
  - **Effect sizes are small.** Spearman = 0.24 is conventionally "weak" in psychometrics; framing CTT as "stronger" should be read as "less bad" rather than good. The discussion occasionally leans toward optimism (e.g., "useful but miscalibrated proxy") — a more conservative read would emphasize that the signal is *insufficient for operational use*.
  - **Single-dataset dependence.** Conclusions hinge on the Cambridge MCQ Reading Dataset; the paper acknowledges this but does not test on a second human-calibrated item bank (none is easy to find, which is itself a finding worth highlighting more strongly).
  - **No prompt ablations.** A single direct-prediction prompt and a single answer prompt are used; the paper does not test whether chain-of-thought, rationales, few-shot exemplars, or different persona framings could push the ceiling higher. This is the most natural confounder.
  - **Negative correlations are interesting but under-theorized.** Why does Llama 3.1-8B's *and* GPT-4.1's *and* Claude 3.5 Haiku's discrimination ranking *invert* the human ranking? The paper reports this but does not investigate what feature (length, CEFR level, distractor plausibility, surface complexity) drives the inversion — leaving an open empirical question.
  - **Overlap@k magnitudes (0.34–0.37 best) are modest.** Low-discrimination retrieval is essentially the easier direction; high-discrimination retrieval is barely above random. A pretesting workflow that would benefit from these signals needs to be designed very carefully.
  - **The "all-model" CTT pool mixes proprietary and open-weight models** of wildly different capability — averaging is a defensible choice but mixes very different "synthetic examinee" populations, and a more stratified analysis (e.g., only-capable models vs. only-weaker models) would be informative.

## Strengths

- **Largest published scale** for this specific question (42 LLMs, including GPT-5.5, Claude 3.7 Sonnet, Gemini 2.5 Pro, Qwen3-235B).
- **Dual-protocol design** cleanly separates content-only vs. response-derived signals, and the null baseline isolates computation artifacts.
- **Honest negative result** with a clear, reusable ceiling (Spearman 0.241 CTT) and a crisp failure mode (negative correlation in direct prediction).
- **Item-screening diagnostic (Overlap@k)** translates the abstract correlation into an applied use case (item-bank pre-screening), bridging methodology and practice.
- **Conceptual contribution (competence paradox)** offers a generative lens for future student-simulation work — model competence is itself a barrier to emulating weaker test-takers.
- Full prompt templates and per-model answer accuracies disclosed.

## Limitations

- **Reading-comprehension-only** evaluation; cross-domain generalization (math, science, open-ended writing, dialogue assessment) is unverified.
- **Zero-shot only** — no few-shot, no fine-tuning, no chain-of-thought, no prompt-engineering ablation.
- **Single reference dataset** (Cambridge MCQ Reading); the absence of additional human-calibrated item banks is acknowledged but not addressed.
- **Sampling variance not reported** — bootstrap CIs are computed across items, not across LLM sampling runs.
- **Effect sizes remain weak** even in the strongest configuration (Spearman 0.241); operational use would require substantially better alignment.
- **Negative-correlation phenomenon is observed but not mechanistically explained.**
- All-model CTT averaging mixes very different model families; sensitivity to this choice is unexamined.
- The "all-persona" CTT pool includes 42 × 3 persona-conditional runs + 42 baseline runs = ~168 synthetic test-takers, but the analysis does not explore how the result scales with pool size.

## Relevance

该工作对 LLM 评测方向有意义：在大规模对照实验上把"LLM 能否替代人类考生做心理测量校准"这一隐含假设量化成明确的上限（直接预测 Spearman ≤ 0.152，CTT ≤ 0.241），并发现多个强模型出现**与人类方向相反**的负相关——这对任何把 LLM 用于自动出题、自适应测试或题库筛选的从业者都是一个清醒提醒。与同年 Li et al. 2025a 难度预测工作形成姊妹篇，统一了"问题理解能力 ≠ 评估者校准能力"这一论断。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does chain-of-thought reasoning, item-level rationales, or few-shot demonstrations meaningfully close the gap above Spearman 0.24 CTT / 0.15 direct?
- What specific item features (length, CEFR level, distractor plausibility, surface complexity, evidence location) drive the *negative* correlation in capable models — is it a shared artifact of the LLM pre-training data?
- How transferable is this negative finding to other item types (math problem-solving, open-ended writing, dialogue assessment, multi-step reasoning items)?
- Would fine-tuning on a small, well-calibrated human-response matrix unlock latent discrimination signal, or does it simply memorize the bank and fail to generalize?
- Does restricting the CTT synthetic-respondent pool to a curated subset of models (e.g., only weak or only strong models) improve alignment compared to the all-model average?
- How sensitive are the results to LLM sampling temperature and seed averaging — is the negative correlation a robust property or partially driven by single-run variance?
- Can item discrimination be decomposed into sub-skills (inference vs. vocabulary vs. evidence retrieval) for which LLM signals are individually stronger?
- What epistemic-state specifications (beyond a generic "weak student" persona) would be needed to make LLM-based student simulation produce the structured, ability-stratified error patterns the paper argues are missing?
