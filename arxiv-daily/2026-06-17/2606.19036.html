Why this matters 02

A first-principles geometric & stochastic theory of Top-k MoE discontinuities — and a drop-in smoother that holds at 5.6 B parameters.

Initializes from a pretrained Top-k SMoE. No gating re-training. ≈½ an extra expert on average. Wins on language, vision, and 5.6 B VLM.

Critical edge 03

Diffusion is a proxy. Real inputs aren't Brownian, so empirical gains may just be regularising near-tie regions — not any diffusion-specific property. Headline language numbers also use a fractional-k = 2.5 configuration that isn't apples-to-apples with Top-2 baselines.

Methodology

§ 4–5 · measure theory + Itô diffusion
  • Order-n classification. A point on Γ is order-n iff n+1 experts tie at the Top-k threshold — geometry is a translated affine cone of codimension n.
  • Euclidean volume (Thm 4.2 / 4.4). Slicing gives λD(Tϵ(Γ(n)) ∩ BD) ∝ ϵn RD−n, so ratios across orders scale as (ϵ/R)n−m.
  • ℓ∞,ϵ test (Prop. A.20). Replace ϵ-balls with ℓ∞ strips — equivalent to "∃ non-top-k logit within ϵ of z[k](x)", cheap in logit space.
  • First hit (Thm 5.1). For d xt = σ d Bt, the exit set is a.s. Γ(1) (n-D BM misses fixed points for n≥2). Bound: P(τS ≤ t) ≥ 2(1 − Φ(rmin/√t)).
  • Occupation time (Thm 5.3). E[A(n)ϵ(T;Γ)] ≲ ϵn · (T1−n/2, log T, or √T) — high-order sets decay in the small-ϵ regime.
  • SmoothSMoE. Log-smoothstep mi(x) = h((zi − z[k] + ϵ)/ϵ) softly adds a margin to non-top-k logits; top-k untouched. Boundary loss Lboundary = α·(ϵ·K − k*), k* = k + 0.5. Anneal k* 2.5 → 2 over 80 epochs; hard Top-k at inference.

Strengths

+
  • Rare and well-executed theoretical treatment of a phenomenon usually discussed only empirically.
  • Clean order-n classification with matching slicing/projection arguments; the "n-D BM misses fixed points ⇒ high-order discontinuities invisible to isotropic diffusion" insight is elegant.
  • True plug-in: no retraining of the gating (unlike ReMoE), competitive at 5.6 B scale.
  • Annealing ablation cleanly separates training-time regularisation benefit from inference-time smoothing.
  • Code released · ICML 2026 · multi-modality evaluation exceeds the typical routing-stabilizer paper.

Limitations

−
  • Diffusion-based stochastic analysis is a model, not a property of natural data; structured perturbations are out of scope.
  • Empirical gains are modest (often within ~1 std); headline language numbers rely on a non-apples-to-apples fractional-k configuration at inference.
  • No wall-clock / FLOP overhead numbers; the "small overhead" claim is theoretical.
  • Limited ablations on tie-breaking, capacity factors, and expert-drop strategies.
  • Continuity shown on a single example rather than measured across the model.
  • Theoretical results assume linear scoring z(x) = Wx + b; non-linear scoring (cosine, temperature) is not covered.

Questions for further exploration

→ open
  • How sensitive are volume & first-hit bounds to tie-breaking (deterministic / random / learned)?
  • Does the theory extend to non-linear scoring (cosine routing, temperature-scaled gates)?
  • Can the smoothing compose with capacity-factor routing, expert dropout, or shared-expert schemes (e.g. DeepSeek-MoE) without retraining?
  • What wall-clock / FLOP overhead does the extra ≈0.5 expert on average translate to at billion-parameter scale?
  • Is there a more direct way to isolate the regularisation contribution (e.g. explicit auxiliary losses on near-tie logits)?
  • Would an adaptive metric (e.g. logit-whitened) yield a sharper test than axis-aligned ℓ∞,ϵ?
  • How does SmoothSMoE behave under KV-cache constraints in autoregressive decoding with batching?
  • Is the result robust under the standard auxiliary load-balancing loss? Does the boundary loss subsume or compose with it?
  • Can P(τS ≤ t) ≥ 2(1 − Φ(rmin/√t)) yield a data-dependent minimum perturbation budget for adversarial robustness?
  • Could "low-order dominates" inform a curriculum on routing difficulty (warm-up low-k → high-k)?
