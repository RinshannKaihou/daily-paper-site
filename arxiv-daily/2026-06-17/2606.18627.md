> **一句话总结：** PACT 通过"承重墙（LBW）"概念指出预训练权重本身就承担着关键任务知识，提出在合并前用 SVD 构建"全局正交护盾"剥离任务向量中会侵入其他任务 LBW 子空间的分量，可即插即用地提升 Task Arithmetic / TSV-M / Iso-C 等主流合并方法的精度，并能用随机化 SVD 高效近似。

> **TL;DR:** PACT challenges the unspoken premise of Task Arithmetic–style model merging — that the pre-trained base is just a passive initialization and all task-critical knowledge lives in the task vector. Through controlled destructive-perturbation experiments, the authors show that a small fraction of pre-trained directions (<2% of params; ~1.17% of subspace dimensions) are *load-bearing*: ablating them collapses task accuracy (e.g. GTSRB 99.92%→1.91%, EMNIST 99.78%→17.00%), while leaving the task vector mostly untouched. PACT formalizes this from both a scalar (Fisher-based crucial mask) and a subspace (top-K pre-trained singular vectors orthogonal to the task's active subspace) perspective, then defines a *global orthogonal shield* by aggregating the LBW subspaces of *all other* tasks via QR. Each task vector is filtered by projecting out its components along this shield before being handed to any existing merger (TA, TIES, TSV-M, Iso-C, KnOTS, …). On 8/14/20 vision tasks across ViT-B/32, B/16, L/14 (CLIP), PACT-Iso-C reaches a new SOTA on all 9 backbones×task-count settings (e.g. 20-task ViT-L/14: 90.3% vs. Iso-C 87.6% / Iso-CTS 90.1%). A randomized-SVD variant matches full SVD at a fraction of the cost, and the gains transfer to LoRA fine-tuning.

## 核心贡献

- **提出 LBW（Load-Bearing Wall）维度概念**：通过 Fisher 信息 + SVD 双视角，系统证明预训练模型中存在被 fine-tuning 几乎"忽略"但对任务性能至关重要的"承重"参数/子空间。
- **因果验证 LBW 存在并被破坏**：扰动 LBW 会让性能崩溃（Table 1：GTSRB 99.92→1.91）；合并后再单独"复原"LBW 即可恢复 0.7–2.3 个百分点（Table 2），直接证明现有合并方法确实在 LBW 上留下了伤痕。
- **PACT 算法**：为每个任务构建包含所有其他任务 LBW 子空间的"全局正交护盾" $V_{\text{protect}}^t$，再把任务向量在护盾上的投影分量去掉；模块化、可与 TA/TIES/TSV-M/Iso-C/KnOTS 等任意合并算法组合。
- **随机化 SVD 变体**：把计算复杂度从 $O(mn\min(m,n))$ 降到 $O(mnk)$，内存压力大幅下降，且性能与全 SVD 相当（甚至在 1/3 场景更优，作者解释为隐式去噪）。
- **理论与实验并重**：给出 Projected First-Order Performance Preservation 定理与 Active-Subspace Relaxation 推论，将 PACT 引入的损失上界与"任务梯度在护盾上的能量"绑定；在 8/14/20 视觉任务 + ViT-B/32, B/16, L/14 + 全微调与 LoRA 上取得一致 SOTA。

## Key Contributions

- **Conceptual reframing of Task Arithmetic.** Argues the field's implicit assumption — "task vector = full task knowledge, base model = inert initialization" — is empirically false; a small portion of *pre-trained* weights carry indispensable task-critical structure.
- **Two-view LBW characterization.** Scalar view: a "crucial mask" of <2% of parameters selected by low task-vector magnitude + high Fisher information + cross-task enrichment ($p<0.05$). Subspace view: $V_{t,\text{LBW}} = \mathrm{orth}\big((I - V_{t,k}V_{t,k}^\top)V_{0,K}\big)$, capturing pre-trained right-singular directions *not* updated by the task. Both views yield the same operational conclusion.
- **Causal validation.** A destructive-perturbation study shows that directly overwriting the crucial mask with another task's update collapses task accuracy, while a controlled *restoration* of just those <2% parameters in a merged model recovers 0.7–2.3 points — i.e., standard merging is genuinely breaking LBW.
- **Drop-in module PACT.** $V_{\text{protect}}^t = \mathrm{orth}\big(\{V_{s,\text{LBW}}\}_{s\neq t}\big)$, $\tilde\tau_t = \tau_t - \tau_t V_{\text{protect}}^t (V_{\text{protect}}^t)^\top$. Any downstream merger (TA / TIES / TSV-M / Iso-C / Iso-CTS / KnOTS) then runs on $\{\tilde\tau_t\}$. Algorithm 1 fits in ~5 lines per layer.
- **Scalable RSVD variant.** Replaces full SVD with randomized SVD: same $K, k, \alpha$ hyperparameters, $O(mnk)$ time, much smaller memory. In one-third of the 9×3 settings it actually beats full SVD (attributed to implicit low-pass denoising).
- **SOTA on all evaluated settings.** PACT-Iso-C is best in 9/9 backbone × task-count combinations on CLIP ViT-B/32/B/16/L/14, with the largest absolute gap at high task counts (20 tasks, ViT-L/14: 90.3% vs. Iso-C 87.6% normalized).
- **Ablations separating the moving parts.** A static "S-PACT" baseline that only filters against the global $V_{0,K}$ (ignoring per-task LBW) yields essentially no gain over Iso-C, demonstrating that the *dynamic, mutually orthogonal per-task shields* are the real driver, not the pre-trained core alone.
- **Layer-depth analysis.** Energy intrusion is concentrated in early-to-mid blocks; 75%-depth filtering often matches 100%, and for 20-task heavy loads the peak shifts back to 100% — a non-monotonic, capacity-dependent curve.
- **Hyperparameter robustness.** $(K, k) = (15, 8)$ is consistently best across scales; a broad neighborhood of $(K, k)$ all beats the no-PACT baseline.

## Methodology

**Notation.** Let $\theta_0$ be the pre-trained weights and $\theta_t = \theta_0 + \tau_t$ the fine-tuned weights for task $t \in \{1,\dots,T\}$. Model merging seeks a single function $f$ such that $\theta_M = f(\theta_0, \{\theta_t\})$ matches all tasks.

**Step 1 — Identify LBW in the parameter view (crucial mask).** For each layer, take the 30% of parameters with the smallest magnitudes in $\tau_A$, intersect with the 30% of those having the largest Fisher information for task $A$, then keep only coordinates that are statistically enriched ($p<0.05$) in a cross-task Fisher comparison. The resulting mask is <2% of the layer; control masks of equal size are "safe" (low Fisher) and "random". Perturbing the crucial mask with $\alpha \tau_B$ ($\alpha \in \{1,5,10,20,30\}$) drops task-A accuracy catastrophically, while equal-magnitude perturbations on safe/random masks cause only minor drops.

**Step 2 — Identify LBW in the subspace view (LBW subspace).** Compute right singular vectors $V_{0,K}$ of $\theta_0$ (top $K=15$) and $V_{t,k}$ of $\tau_t$ (top $k=8$). The LBW subspace of task $t$ is the component of $V_{0,K}$ that lies *outside* the active update subspace:
$$V_{t,\text{LBW}} = \mathrm{orth}\big((I - V_{t,k}V_{t,k}^\top)V_{0,K}\big).$$
This isolates pre-trained input directions that receive negligible updates during fine-tuning. A causal ablation constructs $\theta_{\text{abla}} = \theta_t(I - V_{t,\text{LBW}}V_{t,\text{LBW}}^\top)$, i.e. erases LBW; the resulting accuracy drops 82–98% across tasks, vs. ~0% for an equal-sized random subspace.

**Step 3 — Interference metrics.** Two geometric diagnostics on task pairs $(A,B)$:
- $\mathrm{Sim}(A,B) = \|V_{A,\text{LBW}}^\top V_{B,\text{LBW}}\|_F^2 / \min(r_A, r_B)$ (symmetric Grassmannian similarity).
- $\mathrm{Inf}(B \to A) = \|V_{B,k}^\top V_{A,\text{LBW}}\|_F^2 / k$ (asymmetric intrusion ratio).

Empirically (Block 0 mlp.fc layer, 8 tasks): $\mathrm{Sim}$ ranges 0.58–0.80 (showing that different tasks rely on heterogeneous LBW subspaces — no universal "safe zone"), while $\mathrm{Inf}$ reaches up to 0.48 (SUN397's active update direction obliterates nearly half of EuroSAT's LBW subspace).

**Step 4 — PACT filtering.** For each task $t$, build a *global* shield by stacking the LBW subspaces of all *other* tasks and orthonormalizing:
$$V_{\text{protect}}^t = \mathrm{orth}\!\Big(\{V_{s,\text{LBW}}\}_{s\neq t}\Big).$$
Then strip the projection of $\tau_t$ onto this shield:
$$\tilde\tau_t = \tau_t - \tau_t V_{\text{protect}}^t (V_{\text{protect}}^t)^\top.$$
Pass $\{\tilde\tau_t\}$ to any existing merger.

**Step 5 — Efficiency.** Full SVD is $O(mn\min(m,n))$ and stores $U, V$ fully; randomized SVD (Halko et al., 2011) replaces it with $O(mnk)$ and a narrow sketch $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times(k+p)}$, giving a "free lunch" that matches or occasionally beats full SVD.

**Step 6 — Theory.** Theorem 1 (Projected First-Order Performance Preservation) bounds the loss increase from filtering $\tau_j$ as
$$L_j(\theta_0 + \tilde\tau_j) - L_j(\theta_0 + \tau_j) \leq \|\nabla L_j(\theta_j) P_j\|_F \cdot \|\tau_j P_j\|_F + \tfrac{\beta_j}{2}\|\tau_j P_j\|_F^2,$$
i.e. the self-degradation is driven by the *projection of the task gradient and update onto the shield* $P_j$, not by their global norms. Corollary 1 (Active-Subspace Relaxation) shows that if the task gradient and update both concentrate in $V_{j,k}$, the first-order term shrinks to $\propto \cos^2\angle(V_{j,k}, P_j)$ — the very reason PACT works.

## Results & Highlights

**Vision, full fine-tuning (Table 4).** CLIP ViT-B/32 / B/16 / L/14 with 8/14/20 tasks. PACT-Iso-C is best in 9/9 (backbone × task-count) cells, normalized accuracy:

| Backbone | 8 tasks | 14 tasks | 20 tasks |
|---|---|---|---|
| ViT-B/32 | 94.1% (Iso-C 92.9) | 89.0% (Iso-C 88.1) | 86.0% (Iso-C 82.5) |
| ViT-B/16 | 97.2% (Iso-C 95.6) | 92.3% (Iso-C 91.1) | 89.3% (Iso-C 85.4) |
| ViT-L/14 | 98.3% (Iso-C 98.3) | 95.5% (Iso-C 94.5) | **95.0%** (Iso-CTS 94.9) |

PACT-TSV-M tops ViT-B/32 8-tasks (94.8% vs. TSV-M 92.3); PACT-TA on ViT-L/14 reaches 87.7% on 20 tasks (TA 78.1%) — i.e. +9.6 normalized points over TA at scale. The gap widens as the task count grows, supporting the paper's claim that LBW interference scales with $T$.

**LoRA fine-tuning (Table 5, 8 tasks).** PACT-Iso-C: ViT-B/32 75.1%, ViT-L/14 86.3% (vs. Iso-C 73.6/83.7, Iso-CTS 73.7/85.3, KnOTS-TIES 68.0/78.2). PACT-TA (66.9/77.7) approaches KnOTS-TIES even though KnOTS was designed for LoRA — evidence the LBW effect is *not* an artifact of full fine-tuning.

**Randomized SVD (Table 6).** Full SVD vs. RSVD on the same $(K, k, \alpha)$: 26/27 cells are within ±0.2 absolute, and 9/27 RSVD is *better* (e.g. ViT-L/14 8 tasks PACT-Iso-C: 99.3 vs. 98.3 normalized). Authors attribute this to the implicit low-pass denoising of random projection.

**Algorithmic ablation (Table 11, Appendix D.1).** S-PACT (filter only against the static $V_{0,K}$, ignore per-task LBW) gives essentially no gain on top of Iso-C (84.2 vs. 84.4 on ViT-B/16 14 tasks). Full PACT lifts the same setting to 86.1% — proving that the *mutual* per-task shields carry the value, not the global core.

**Layer-depth ablation (Table 12, D.2).** Performance is non-monotonic in filtering depth. For 8/14 tasks the optimum is 75% (e.g. 92.1% on ViT-B/16 8 tasks), confirming that deep layers route semantics in already-orthogonal directions. For 20 tasks on B/16 and L/14 the peak shifts to 100% — i.e. heavy multi-task load forces global shielding.

**Hyperparameter robustness (Table 13, D.3).** $(K, k) = (15, 8)$ is consistently optimal across all 9 cells; $(23, 16)$ is a close second. Even $(0, 0)$ (i.e. PACT turned off, plain Iso-C) underperforms any reasonable $(K,k)$, so LBW filtering is robust to a wide neighborhood of ranks.

**Causal ablations (Table 1, 2; Table 3, Appendix C.2).**
- Crucial-mask perturbation with $\alpha=30$ drops task A from 86.49→5.04% (Cars), 99.92→28.04% (GTSRB), 99.92→1.91% (GTSRB via subspace ablation), 99.78→17.00% (EMNIST). Safe/random controls drop < 10 points.
- Restoring just the crucial mask in a merged model recovers 0.7–2.3 points (Cars 70.97→71.78, GTSRB 91.55→93.47, PACM 74.69→77.03).

## Critical Assessment

**What's solid.** The diagnostic story is unusually thorough. The authors don't just propose PACT — they walk the reader through three independent pieces of evidence that LBW dimensions exist and are damaged by current mergers: (1) Fisher + magnitude crucial mask with three control masks, (2) subspace ablation with a matched random control, and (3) the *restoration* experiment (Table 2) that is the cleanest possible "the merged model is broken in exactly this way" signal. The plug-in design and the SVD→RSVD "free lunch" are practical contributions. Theory is not vacuous: Theorem 1 directly ties the PACT-induced loss to a measurable quantity (gradient × update energy inside the protection space), and Corollary 1 gives a clean geometric intuition ($\cos^2$ of principal angles). The cross-paradigm coverage — scalar, subspace, full FT, LoRA — is broader than typical merging papers.

**What to be cautious about.**
- **Benchmark set.** Experiments are entirely on the standard 8/14/20 CLIP visual classification benchmark of Wang et al. (2024) / Marczak et al. (2025). These are small-vocabulary classification tasks; whether LBW "load-bearing" translates to dense-prediction (detection/segmentation) or generative settings is untested. The authors acknowledge this as future work.
- **No LLM experiments.** Despite the discussion's LLM relevance, the paper does not evaluate PACT on language models, instruction tuning, or RLHF-merged models. Given how popular task-vector merging is for LLM adapters (TIES, DARE, Model Soups), the omission is conspicuous. The authors flag this as future work.
- **Fisher-information cost.** Crucial-mask selection uses Fisher information "in principle" (Remark 3.1 / Section 4), but the actual algorithm in §4 *does not use Fisher*; it relies on the cheaper SVD on $\theta_0$ and $\tau_t$. So the LBW *diagnostic* and the PACT *filter* are two different mechanisms that happen to align empirically. This is a mild conceptual muddle that the paper does not fully resolve.
- **Layer-wise / rank choices.** $(K, k) = (15, 8)$ is a global hyperparameter. The depth ablation (D.2) shows the optimal filtering depth shifts with backbone and task count, so $(K, k)$ is almost certainly also layer- and scale-dependent. A small per-layer search budget would likely improve gains further; the paper does not run this.
- **Comparison fairness.** Improvements are reported against *re-produced* Iso-C, Iso-CTS, TSV-M numbers (not the original paper numbers, although they are stated to match). The "SOTA" claim therefore holds on the authors' own evaluation, which is fine, but readers should be aware that some baselines (e.g. MAP, RegMean++) are missing from the comparison table.
- **Randomized SVD surprise.** The claim that RSVD matches or beats full SVD in 1/3 of cells is striking and is attributed to "implicit denoising." This is plausible (Halko et al. do discuss spectral filtering effects) but not formally proved; the paper should be read as empirical on this point.
- **Destructive-perturbation $\alpha$ values.** Perturbing with $\alpha=30 \times \tau_B$ is a *huge* magnitude, especially since $\tau_B$ is already in the same scale as $\theta_0$. The "vulnerability" claim would be more convincing if the curves were shown at smaller $\alpha$ (e.g. 0.3, 1, 3) to demonstrate that even moderate updates break LBW.

**Red flags.** None major. The work is honest about its limits (Sec. F is two short paragraphs) and the experiments are well-controlled. The most concerning thing is the gap between the theoretically-motivated Fisher crucial-mask view (§3.2.1) and the actually-deployed SVD view (§4) — a reader should not assume they are equivalent.

## Strengths

- Reframes a real conceptual gap in Task Arithmetic literature.
- Three independent causal validations of LBW existence and damage.
- Plug-in, data-free, training-free — minimal adoption cost.
- Theory explains *why* PACT helps (gradient × update energy in the protection space).
- RSVD variant is genuinely faster and occasionally better.
- Layer-depth ablation reveals a non-obvious non-monotonic structure.
- Per-task mutual shields (vs. static global core) are demonstrably the value driver.
- Works on LoRA even though LoRA leaves the base untouched — strong evidence the phenomenon is real.

## Limitations

- Limited to CLIP-classification vision tasks; no detection/segmentation/generation.
- No LLM or instruction-tuning experiments despite the obvious application.
- Crucial-mask view (Fisher) and the actually-deployed SVD view are not formally connected; PACT's success on the SVD view does not automatically justify the Fisher narrative.
- $(K, k)$ are global; per-layer / per-scale tuning is left to future work.
- Random-subspace control shows that the LBW direction matters, but does not control for *magnitude* of the projection — the perturbation experiment in §3.2.1 uses $\alpha$ as large as 30.
- Computational overhead remains non-trivial for very large (LLM-scale) base models, even with RSVD; the paper provides complexity formulas but no wall-clock benchmarks.
- Missing comparisons to MAP, RegMean++, and other loss-aware mergers.

## Relevance

与"无需训练的多任务模型合并 / 参数高效融合"方向高度相关，提出的"承重墙"视角对从事 LLM adapter 合并（LoRA / TIES / DARE 等）、持续预训练多任务整合、或关注预训练先验与任务向量交互的研究者具有方法论参考价值；同时 RSVD 变体为大规模模型的可扩展合并提供了实用路径。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does PACT's filter ever *hurt* when the task vector legitimately needs to modify directions already present in the base (e.g. style/format transfer, safety alignment, or tasks that intentionally erase base behavior)?
- How does PACT compose with sequential or iterative merging (merge A+B, then merge with C)? Is the shield recomputed at each step or reused?
- Is there a principled, per-layer rule for choosing $(K, k)$ — e.g. proportional to the spectral decay of $\theta_0$ at that layer?
- Can LBW subspaces be reused across base-model families (e.g. share protection masks across CLIP checkpoints), or are they fingerprint-specific?
- Does PACT interact well with quantization / low-precision merging, where the orthogonal projection may itself introduce rounding error?
- What happens on language models (decoder-only LLMs) where the task-vector geometry may differ substantially from CLIP's vision encoder?
- How sensitive are the gains to the choice of base model — a stronger or weaker pre-trained initialization could shift the LBW subspace significantly.
- Could a learned (instead of SVD) projection of the protection space close the remaining gap to individual-task fine-tuned performance?
