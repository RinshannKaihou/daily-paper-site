> **一句话总结：** 提出 SKCI——一种基于 testing-by-betting 与自适应优化 KCI 核统计量的序列条件独立性检验方法，借助自归一化 payoff 和 truncate-and-shift 高斯校准，在 Model-X 条件分布必须在线估计时仍能显著抑制 I 类错误膨胀，并在多个高维合成与公平性真实数据上同时优于现有序列 Model-X 基线。

> **TL;DR:** SKCI is a sequential kernel-based CI test that combines testing-by-betting with an adaptively optimized Kernel Conditional Independence (KCI) operator. A self-normalized cross-U-statistic plus a truncate-and-shift Gaussian calibration control Type-I error when the Model-X conditional P_{A|C} is estimated rather than known exactly. Theoretically the authors bound the one-step null drift (Berry–Esseen + sensitivity of the calibrating shift) and convert it to a finite-horizon Type-I inflation bound. Empirically, on linear Gaussian, He et al.'s CI-hardness 1D/3D benchmarks, RatInABox neural data, dSprites images, and the ProPublica car-insurance fairness audit, SKCI matches or beats e-CRT/DAVT/EC2ST in power while keeping Type-I error close to α, especially in the fully online regime.

## 核心贡献

- 提出 SKCI 序列条件独立检验框架，将 testing-by-betting 与 KCI（Zhang et al. 2011）的 RKHS 残差算子结合，构造自适应优化的核 payoff。
- 设计"自归一化 cross U-statistic"作为 betting score（除以历史 V-统计量+ε），无需预知 kernel 尺度即可获得近似常为 1 的备择信号。
- 引入 truncate-and-shift 校准：先用 Wasserstein/Berry–Esseen 近似 raw score 的条件零分布为 N(0,σ̂²)，再通过二分搜索找到满足零均值约束的最优平移 γ̂，确保 payoff 几乎处处 ≥ -1。
- 推导漂移上界 δt ≤ Ut（来自有限样本高斯近似 + 条件均值嵌入估计误差 + 方差失配），并据此给出 anytime-valid 的有限时域 Type-I 误差控制。
- 在 5 类基准（线性高斯、CI hardness 1D/3D、RatInABox、dSprites、汽车保险公平性审计）上系统优于 e-CRT / DAVT / EC2ST，且在 Online 模式（无离线辅助数据）下 Type-I 仍可控。

## Key Contributions

- A sequential CI testing framework (SKCI) built on testing-by-betting and the KCI operator, with payoff and betting fraction jointly adapted online via gradient steps on an empirical log-wealth criterion over leave-block-out historical proxies.
- A self-normalized cross U-statistic payoff that is approximately scale-free under H1 while retaining the conditional-mean-zero property needed for null validity.
- A truncate-and-shift calibration that solves the gap between the theoretically ideal supermartingale and a practical, bounded-below payoff by approximating the conditional null law of the raw score with N(0, σ̂²), with σ̂² estimated on a held-out validation buffer to avoid bias.
- A finite-sample Type-I inflation bound: one-step drift is controlled by a Berry–Esseen term, a conditional mean embedding (CME) regression-error term, and a variance-mismatch term; these combine into an explicit anytime-valid test through a corrected supermartingale.
- Comprehensive experiments under three regimes (Oracle / Pretrained / Online), showing SKCI is the only method that stays near α for Type I while matching or exceeding the highest power baseline.

## Methodology

**Setup.** A stream of i.i.d. triples (Aₜ, Bₜ, Cₜ) arrives in batches of size b. To keep the betting payoff Ft₋₁-measurable, SKCI partitions each round into three disjoint roles: a growing training set X^trₜ₋₁ (for fitting CME estimates and the kernel h⁽ᵗ⁾), a held-out validation set X^valₜ₋₁ (for estimating the null variance σ̂²), and a fresh test batch Yₜ that drives the wealth update. After each round, the previous test batch becomes validation, and the previous validation merges into training — a slow-aging scheme that preserves IID-style reasoning while growing the modeling data.

**KCI operator.** Following He et al. (2025), A, B, C are mapped into RKHSs H_A, H_B, H_C and the conditional mean embeddings μ_{A|C}, μ_{B|C} are estimated by kernel ridge regression on X^trₜ₋₁. The KCI residual operator is ψ(z) = (φ_A(a) − μ_{A|C}(c)) ⊗ (φ_B(b) − μ_{B|C}(c)) ⊗ φ_C(c), and the U-statistic kernel is h(Z, Z′) = ⟨ψ(Z), ψ(Z′)⟩. Under H0, E_H0[h(Z, Z′) | Z] = 0 for any Z; under H1 (with universal kernels), E[ψ(Z)] ≠ 0.

**Self-normalized raw payoff.** The raw score is a cross U-statistic U_{n,b}(X^tr, Y) divided by a historical V-statistic S_n(X^tr) + ε (eq. 3). The denominator is Ft₋₁-measurable, so the conditional mean under H0 stays at zero, while S_n → E[h(X,Y)] in large samples so the payoff ≈ 1 under H1 regardless of the kernel's intrinsic scale.

**Truncate-and-shift.** Ville's inequality requires V_t ≥ −1. The bounded-kernel raw score can still dip below −1 because of normalization noise, so SKCI defines V_t = max{V^raw_t − γ̂_t, −1} (eq. 5). The shift γ̂_t is chosen as the smallest γ ≥ 0 making E_H0[max{V^raw_t − γ, −1} | Ft₋₁] ≤ 0 (eq. 6). Since this conditional law is unknown, the paper approximates V^raw_t | Ft₋₁ by N(μ_t, σ_t²) (Berry–Esseen), plugs in μ̂_t = 0 and σ̂_t² estimated on the validation buffer (eq. 7), and solves the closed-form equation σ[φ(ξ) − ξΦ(−ξ)] − 1 = 0 with ξ = (γ − μ − 1)/σ via binary search.

**Strategy optimization.** At each round, kernel parameters (only k_C) and the betting fraction λ_t = σ(η_t) are jointly optimized on a leave-block-out proxy of historical log-wealth increments (eq. 9–10): the n training points are split into blocks of size b, each block is treated as a pseudo-test batch, and η_t, h⁽ᵗ⁾ are updated by S gradient steps before the round is played on Y_t. The block-out structure prevents self-interaction leakage.

**Theory.** Lemma 4.1 gives a Lipschitz-style sensitivity bound for the calibrating shift: |γ(μ,σ) − γ(μ̂,σ̂)| ≤ |μ − μ̂| + Λ(σ)|σ − σ̂|, where Λ(σ) = ϕ(ξ_σ)/Φ(−ξ_σ) and ξ_σ solves σ{ϕ(ξ) − ξΦ(−ξ)} = 1. Theorem 4.2 bounds the one-step null drift δ_t (eq. 11) by U_t = C₁ρ√(κ)/√(bε) + (κ/ε)·(|δ⁽ᵗ⁾_{A|C}| + |δ⁽ᵗ⁾_{B|C}|) + 2C₂κ²/(bε²), where δ_{A|C}, δ_{B|C} are the CME regression errors and ρ is the Berry–Esseen moment ratio. Proposition 4.3 then converts the per-step bound into a finite-horizon Type-I inflation: Pr_H0[∃t ≤ T : W_t ≥ 1/α] ≤ α·exp(Σ λ_t U_t), via a compensated supermartingale W̃_t = W_t / Π(1 + λ_i U_i). This is "anytime-valid" only on a fixed horizon T, not truly infinite-horizon — the authors acknowledge this explicitly.

## Results & Highlights

- **Linear Gaussian (d=20, Fig. 1 / Fig. 6):** All methods control Type I in Oracle; in Pretrained and especially Online mode SKCI is the only method whose Type-I stays near α=0.05 while reaching high power, while DAVT and e-CRT drift up.
- **CI hardness 1D (Fig. 2a):** SKCI matches/exceeds the strongest baseline in power with tight Type-I control.
- **CI hardness 3D — shared coordinate (Fig. 2b):** SKCI dominates on power; baselines either inflate Type-I or fail to detect dependence.
- **CI hardness 3D — separate (orthonormal) coordinate (Fig. 2c):** "Substantially harder": all baselines "fail to detect dependence and/or suffer severe Type I error inflation"; SKCI's online kernel optimization successfully "adapts to the relevant subspace, yielding reliable power and reasonable Type I control even when the dependence signal is decoupled from marginal structure."
- **RatInABox neural data (Fig. 3):** 100-dim head-direction cells vs. 100-dim conjunctive cells conditioned on 4-dim pose. SKCI has tight Type-I and strong power; EC2ST and DAVT-online have near-1 Type-I; DAVT-pretrained has near-zero power; e-CRT is conservative but slow.
- **dSprites (Fig. 4):** SKCI's Type-I stays low when C contains the full object (true null); baseline rejection rates approach 1. All methods achieve power in the dependent regime — the gap is in null calibration.
- **Car-insurance discrimination (Fig. 5):** Across California, Illinois, Missouri, Texas, SKCI is conservative on Type-I and competitive or superior on power; DAVT and EC2ST have Type-I near 1; e-CRT is controlled but underpowered.

Across all settings, SKCI's largest empirical win is the **Online regime** (no auxiliary side data), which is the most realistic and where prior sequential Model-X tests "fragile" (per the abstract).

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty.** Moderate-to-high. The individual pieces — testing-by-betting, KCI operator, self-normalized U-statistics, Berry–Esseen drift bounds — are well-established. The genuinely new engineering is (i) using leave-block-out historical proxies to make the betting fraction *and* the conditioning kernel data-adaptive while preserving null validity, (ii) the truncate-and-shift calibration tailored to the bounded-kernel setting, and (iii) a CME-regression-error term in the drift bound that prior sequential tests did not surface. The "principle for choosing test statistics that accumulate evidence quickly under weak signal" (Section 1) is described informally; a more formal treatment would strengthen the contribution.

**Rigor.** Strong on the math, more careful on assumptions than many neighbors. Specific concerns:

- **Finite-horizon validity, not anytime-valid.** Proposition 4.3 is stated for a fixed horizon T, and the test rejects when W_t ≥ (1/α)·Π(1 + λ_i U_i) — which is not a data-dependent threshold in the standard Ville sense. The paper does not claim infinite-horizon anytime validity for the *practical* Gaussian-plug-in version (only for the ideal γ_t in eq. 6). The title and intro occasionally suggest stronger guarantees than the finite-T bound delivers.
- **Gaussian approximation is load-bearing but only lightly audited.** The whole shift-and-truncate scheme rests on V^raw_t | Ft₋₁ ≈ N(0, σ_t²). The drift bound invokes Berry–Esseen with a "uniformly bounded" third central moment and a non-degenerate variance — strong assumptions that are not empirically validated in the paper (no goodness-of-fit tests, no diagnostic plots of the empirical null distribution of V^raw_t).
- **CME estimation error δ_{A|C}, δ_{B|C} enters as a *plug-in* in U_t**, but the paper does not give a procedure to estimate these terms from data; the practitioner must upper-bound them, which is non-trivial in the sequential setting. The bound is therefore not directly actionable as a stopping rule without extra assumptions.
- **Ablations are deferred to the appendix** (mentioned in passing near Fig. 12). The main paper would benefit from a visible study isolating the contribution of (a) the Gaussian shift, (b) the CME kernel choice, (c) the betting-fraction adaptation, (d) the self-normalization.

**Reproducibility.** Code is released, baselines are clearly named (e-CRT/DAVT/EC2ST), batch size b=20 and 100 runs are stated. The dSprites and RatInABox setups follow published protocols (Matthey 2017; George 2024). The car-insurance audit replicates Angwin et al. (2017) and Polo et al. (2023)'s protocol. Negative: hyperparameters (ε, kernel choices, S inner-loop steps, learning rates for the joint optimization) are not all surfaced in the main text.

**Significance.** SKCI moves the needle on a real fragility of sequential CI testing. Before this paper, the practitioner had to choose between (i) exact Model-X with a known P_{A|C} (unrealistic online) or (ii) heuristic approaches whose Type-I error quietly drifts up with sample size. SKCI is the first sequential test that explicitly accounts for and bounds the CME regression error, which is the dominant practical failure mode. The Car-Insurance Online numbers in Figure 5 (DAVT/EC2ST near-1 Type-I) are a sobering indictment of how badly the prior art fails outside Oracle.

**Red flags / things to watch.**
- The 3D-separate-coordinate claim is the strongest single claim in the paper and should be reproduced; it is also the regime where any implementation bug in the kernel-optimization loop would be hardest to detect.
- β = 0.05 plots at small sample sizes (n < 200) appear noisy; standard-error bands are shown but per-seed variance matters for a Bernstein-style test.
- No comparison against non-betting sequential methods (e.g., group-sequential or SPRT-style CI tests), which would clarify whether the gain is from betting or from the KCI + CME-error accounting.

## Strengths

- First sequential CI test that explicitly bounds and addresses CME estimation error under estimated Model-X.
- Joint adaptation of kernel hyperparameters *and* betting fraction, optimized on leave-block-out historical proxies — avoids the static-kernel weakness of prior e-CRT/DAVT.
- Principled, closed-form Gaussian shift via binary search on a 1-D monotone equation — simple to implement.
- Solid theory: drift decomposition isolates finite-sample, CME-error, and variance-mismatch contributions.
- Strong, broad empirical evaluation across synthetic (linear, hardness 1D/3D), neural (RatInABox), image (dSprites), and real-world fairness (ProPublica) benchmarks; consistent wins in the most realistic Online regime.
- Code released; venues (ICML 2026) and authorship (UBC + Amii) are credible.

## Limitations

- Only finite-horizon (fixed T) Type-I control is guaranteed for the practical Gaussian-plug-in shift; the paper does not promise infinite-horizon anytime validity of the implemented procedure.
- Still depends on the Model-X assumption (P_{A|C} being well-specified); SKCI does not address the harder "no assumption" impossibility (Shah & Peters 2020; Waudby-Smith & Ramdas 2023).
- The drift bound requires user-supplied bounds on the CME estimation error δ_{A|C}, δ_{B|C} — non-trivial to obtain in practice; no data-driven plug-in is provided.
- Gaussian plug-in may degrade in heavy-tailed or discrete settings; no goodness-of-fit diagnostics are reported.
- Joint optimization over kernel parameters and betting fraction adds non-trivial engineering complexity (S inner steps, learning rate schedule) that may be sensitive to seeds; ablations are deferred to the appendix.
- Compared only against e-CRT, DAVT, and EC2ST — all in the Model-X betting family — and not against classical group-sequential or SPRT-style sequential CI tests.
- Evaluation uses batch size b=20; behavior for very small b (1–5) or very large b is not studied.

## Relevance

与研究方向中"假设检验、可信机器学习、因果推断与公平性审计"高度相关。SKCI 直接解决了 Model-X 序列条件独立性检验中条件分布需精确已知这一关键脆弱性问题，并首次在漂移界中显式处理条件均值嵌入的回归误差，可直接用于在线公平性监控、在线特征选择、增量因果发现以及任何需要"数据依赖时刻"停止的 CI 检验场景。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can the Gaussian plug-in be replaced by a permutation- or bootstrap-based null calibration that still respects the Ft₋₁-measurability requirement?
- Does the drift bound extend gracefully to discrete or mixed-type A, B, C (where CME rates from Li et al. 2022/2024 may not apply)?
- Can the leave-block-out proxy be tightened (e.g., with cross-fitting à la DML) to give a sharp, data-driven upper bound on δ_{A|C} and δ_{B|C}?
- How does SKCI compare against group-sequential or mSPRT-style sequential CI tests in the non-Model-X regime?
- Is there a way to certify Type-I control on an unbounded horizon under verifiable, sequential assumptions on the CME estimator (e.g., online stochastic gradient descent with explicit step-size schedules)?
- Does the gain survive under distribution shift between training and test data — e.g., the anti-causal shift setting of Jiang & Veitch (2022) that motivates the pedestrian-detection example in the introduction?
