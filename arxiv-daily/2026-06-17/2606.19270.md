> **一句话总结：** 本文是一篇 IEEE 风格 Perspective,在医学影像 AI 中正式区分"算法创新"(在固定问题定义下优化实现)与"概念创新"(重新定义任务、评价标准与临床意义),诊断当前社区过度奖励算法新颖性的结构性成因(出版/审稿/激励/训练路径/数据可及性),并以重建保真度、分割重叠、诊断 vs. 预后三个案例具体说明"概念错位"如何被误读为"模型失败",最终面向导师、审稿人、期刊、机构与资助方提出可操作建议。

> **TL;DR:** A Perspective in medical imaging AI that draws a sharp, operational distinction between **algorithmic innovation** (better implementations of a fixed task) and **conceptual innovation** (redefining the task, evaluation, and clinical relevance). The author argues that publication formats, review criteria, shared benchmarks, training pipelines, labor-market incentives, and data-access asymmetries combine to systematically over-reward algorithmic novelty — with disproportionate professional risk for early-career researchers. Three worked examples (reconstruction fidelity vs. clinical validity, segmentation overlap vs. downstream consequence, diagnostic labeling vs. prognostic prediction) show how "model failure" is often upstream conceptual misalignment. The paper closes with concrete recommendations for mentors, reviewers, journals, institutions, funders, and data providers.

## 核心贡献

- **可操作的概念分离**:明确"算法创新"(架构/损失/训练机制,在固定任务定义下提升性能)与"概念创新"(问题重构、目标变量重定义、评价标准改写)的工作定义,并指出前者居于上游、决定后者优化的天花板。
- **可识别的特征清单(Box 1)**:给出判断一项工作是否构成"概念性贡献"的六条可操作特征——重构研究问题、暴露隐含假设、引入替代评价标准、跨方法/模态/场景的普适性、开启新研究方向、影响研究实践与话语。
- **结构性成因诊断**:系统梳理了算法创新主导的五个相互强化的因素——出版/审稿格式、共享基准与固定指标、引用动力学、训练路径偏优化、学术界之外的就业市场。
- **早期研究者风险分析**:论证"概念贡献"对初级研究者构成不成比例的职业风险,根源是评价模糊、发表规范不匹配、声誉杠杆不对称、概念成果成熟更慢。
- **三个落地案例**:重建保真度 vs. 临床有效性、分割几何重叠 vs. 下游后果、诊断分类 vs. 预后/风险分层——展示问题重构如何重塑"成功"的含义。
- **生态级建议矩阵**:面向导师/PI、审稿人、期刊编辑、机构人事与资助方、数据与验证体系,提出具体改革方向(明确的概念贡献合法性、概念性文章类型、跨职业阶段的风险分担、纵向/不确定数据共享、物理仿真数据接受度)。

## Key Contributions

- **Operational distinction.** Formalizes *algorithmic innovation* (architectures, losses, training mechanisms under a fixed task definition) vs. *conceptual innovation* (refining the target variable, assumptions about ground truth, and evaluation criteria). Conceptual choices are explicitly upstream — they govern what constitutes an error and which failure modes are even detectable.
- **Diagnostic Box 1.** Six reviewer-ready indicators that a study is conceptually innovative: reframes the research question, makes implicit assumptions explicit, introduces alternative evaluation criteria, exhibits method-agnostic generality, enables new lines of inquiry, or influences research practice/discourse.
- **Structural diagnosis.** Identifies five mutually reinforcing drivers of algorithm-centrism: (i) publication/review formats that reward benchmark gains, (ii) shared datasets and metrics that implicitly fix task definitions, (iii) citation dynamics that favor concrete artifacts, (iv) CS/engineering training pipelines emphasizing optimization over problem formulation, and (v) industry labor-market signals that valorize deployable model skills.
- **Early-career risk analysis.** Argues that conceptual innovation is structurally riskier for junior researchers because: it is harder to evaluate consistently, it does not fit method-centric templates, it matures more slowly, credit diffuses into follow-on algorithmic work, and challenging established benchmarks is reputationally costly.
- **Three illustrative examples** of how reframing changes what counts as progress:
  - *Reconstruction & denoising*: PSNR/SSIM are imperfect proxies for diagnostic utility; reframing asks whether the output supports a clinical decision, not whether it matches a reference image.
  - *Segmentation*: Dice overlap treats all pixels equivalently; reframing weights errors by downstream treatment-planning or biomarker consequences.
  - *Diagnosis vs. prognosis*: predicting an existing label is not always the task of greatest unmet need; reframing positions imaging as a longitudinal, uncertainty-aware, decision-support measurement.
- **Multi-stakeholder recommendations.** Concrete actions for mentors, reviewers, editors, institutions, funders, and data providers — including a call for dedicated article types (Perspectives/Frameworks/Methodological Analyses) with explicit conceptual-contribution criteria, and broader acceptance of longitudinal/uncertainty-annotated datasets and physics-based simulated data.

## Methodology

Perspective / position paper. Qualitative, argumentative synthesis grounded in the author's long experience with imaging physics, image reconstruction, task-based image quality assessment, and quantitative imaging. No new experiments, datasets, or algorithms; the "method" is conceptual framing, taxonomy, and recommendations. Diagrammatic support (Fig. 1) showing conceptual innovation as upstream control and algorithmic innovation as downstream execution. Box 1 supplies a six-item rubric for identifying conceptual contributions in review.

## Results & Highlights

- Frames the field's algorithm-centric bias as a **maturity problem**, not a performance problem: the gap to clinical translation is conceptual, not capacity-limited.
- Argues that "stress-test" behavior of modern deep models (e.g., diffusion-based or unrolled reconstruction methods producing visually plausible but diagnostically misleading outputs, per Antun et al. 2020; Bhadra et al. 2021) has *operationalized* a long-standing concern about PSNR/SSIM, converting an abstract worry into a measurable failure mode.
- Notes that the most durable advances in medical imaging (CT, MRI, task-based image quality, quantitative imaging biomarkers) were conceptual reframings of what was being measured and how performance was defined.
- Highlights that *algorithmic advances can also drive conceptual refinement*: pixel-based fidelity metrics are a paradigmatic case where a long-known conceptual weakness became sharply visible only with deep learning reconstruction.
- Provides reviewer-ready criteria (Box 1) usable today for distinguishing "absence of algorithmic novelty" from "absence of substantive contribution."

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty.** The *terminology* (conceptual vs. algorithmic innovation in medical imaging AI) is the contribution; the underlying critique echoes broader ML concerns (Lipton & Steinhardt 2019 on troubling trends; Maier-Hein et al. 2024 "Metrics Reloaded"; Roberts et al. 2021 on common pitfalls in COVID-19 imaging). Anastasio packages these threads into a coherent taxonomy anchored in medical imaging, which is genuinely useful but should be cited as consolidating prior critiques rather than introducing them.
- **Rigor.** As a Perspective, claims are argued rather than empirically tested. The diagnosis is plausible and well-motivated by cited examples (Antun et al. 2020; Bhadra et al. 2021; Zech et al. 2018; Oakden-Rayner 2020; Kelly et al. 2019), but there is no quantitative evidence that the proposed interventions would actually shift review, publication, or training outcomes. The Box 1 rubric is a useful starting point, but its reliability and inter-rater agreement have not been validated.
- **Reproducibility.** N/A — opinion/position piece; no experimental artifacts to reproduce. The "review criteria" in Box 1 could in principle be evaluated for inter-rater reliability, which would strengthen the paper.
- **Significance.** High for the medical imaging AI community. The translation gap from benchmark to clinic is real, persistent, and widely acknowledged; a clean vocabulary plus stakeholder-targeted recommendations are actionable now, and a few journals already have the article types the paper recommends.
- **Red flags / things to push back on:**
  - **Selection bias in examples.** The three illustrative cases (reconstruction, segmentation, diagnosis-vs-prognosis) are chosen to fit the argument; counter-examples where algorithmic novelty did drive translation (e.g., the McKinney et al. 2020 breast screening paper, which the paper cites as a positive case) are acknowledged but not deeply reconciled with the broader "conceptual weakness" thesis.
  - **Mechanistic claim about data access.** The argument that limited access to raw k-space / projection / list-mode data "restricts how inverse problems are studied" is plausible but uncalibrated; fastMRI and LoDoPaB-CT are cited as enablers, and one could argue the field is *already* responding to this gap.
  - **Risk of over-correction.** The recommendation to legitimate "diverse data representations and validation strategies" could be read as lowering evidentiary standards if not paired with explicit guardrails; the paper gestures at this ("Broadening contribution types should not relax evidentiary standards") but does not operationalize the guardrails.
  - **Generalization beyond medical imaging.** The argument is anchored in medical imaging AI but the structural critique (publication incentives, training pipelines, citation dynamics) is generic. A reader outside the field should view the framework as a case study, not a discovery.
  - **Single-author voice.** A community-position paper authored by one senior researcher inevitably reflects one institutional perspective; multi-author or community-endorsed statements (e.g., the Maier-Hein et al. "Metrics Reloaded" consortium) carry broader legitimacy.

## Strengths

- **Clean operational taxonomy.** The conceptual-vs-algorithmic split is genuinely usable: reviewers, authors, and program committees can apply Box 1 today.
- **Concrete recommendations across stakeholder groups.** Rare for a Perspective to address mentors, reviewers, journals, institutions, funders, *and* data providers in one go.
- **Honest about where the line is blurry.** The paper explicitly acknowledges that "conceptual and algorithmic innovation are often intertwined" and that algorithmic advances can act as "stress tests" exposing conceptual gaps — this nuance is a strength.
- **Anchored in concrete failure modes** (instabilities of deep reconstruction, hallucinations, segmentation errors with non-uniform clinical consequence, diagnosis-only models missing prognostic value) rather than abstract hand-waving.
- **Timely.** Aligns with and consolidates parallel calls in the field (Metrics Reloaded, common-pitfalls reviews, reporting guidelines CONSORT-AI/DECIDE-AI).

## Limitations

- **No empirical validation** of the diagnostic rubric (Box 1) or the proposed interventions; effects on review outcomes, career trajectories, or translation success are hypothesized.
- **Recommendations under-specified on cost and feasibility.** Calls for new article types, broader acceptance of simulated data, and revised promotion criteria are easier stated than implemented; a pilot study (e.g., at one journal or one funding agency) would be more persuasive.
- **Limited engagement with counter-arguments.** Cases where benchmark-driven optimization *did* produce clinical value (screening/triage, where the author concedes the point) could be analyzed more deeply rather than acknowledged in passing.
- **The "conceptual innovation" category remains hard to define mechanistically.** The paper concedes that a network redesign can be either algorithmic or conceptual depending on intent, and that the boundary is often fuzzy in practice; this is honest but means the rubric requires expert judgment, not mechanical application.
- **Single-discipline anchor.** Most illustrative examples come from CT/MRI physics-style imaging; ultrasound, pathology, and ophthalmology are not explicitly addressed.

## Relevance

高度相关。该 Perspective 触及当前医学影像 AI 研究的方法论与生态问题,与"如何定义任务、如何衡量成功、如何让模型真正服务于临床"这一长期议题一致。对关注可解释性、评价方法、临床转化路径的研究者尤其有用,可作为研究社区自我反思与改革议程的参考。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- **Operationalization of Box 1.** Could a multi-rater study quantify inter-rater agreement when applying the six conceptual-contribution indicators? Would they correlate with downstream citation or translation impact?
- **Empirical link between problem-framing quality and generalization.** Roberts et al. 2021, Oakden-Rayner 2020, and Zech et al. 2018 document failures; is there a systematic study of how upstream conceptual choices (label construction, evaluation design) predict cross-site generalization?
- **Mechanism design for incentives.** Which specific changes — dedicated review rubrics, separate conceptual-contribution tracks, citation of conceptual precursors in algorithmic papers, "credit diffusion" mitigation — are tractable to pilot, and what is the cost / political feasibility at journals like IEEE TMI, Medical Image Analysis, or MICCAI?
- **Training-pipeline interventions.** What curricular or mentorship interventions (problem-framing coursework, clinician immersion, cross-disciplinary co-supervision) measurably shift early-career researchers toward more conceptually grounded work?
- **Generalization beyond medical imaging.** Does the conceptual-vs-algorithmic framework transfer usefully to other safety-critical AI domains (clinical NLP, autonomous driving, scientific discovery)? Where does it break?
- **Reconciliation with successful benchmark-driven work.** What distinguishes cases like McKinney et al. 2020 (where benchmark gains tracked clinical value) from cases where they did not? Is the difference purely about task-clinical alignment, or are there other moderating factors?
