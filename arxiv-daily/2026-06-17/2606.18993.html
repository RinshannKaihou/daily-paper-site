Key Metric · Type-I Held
0.05≈ α
In the Online regime (no side data), SKCI stays near α. Baselines DAVT & EC2ST inflate toward 1.0.
Why This Matters

Sequential CI testing was previously fragile whenever the Model-X conditional isn't known exactly — the realistic case for online fairness monitoring and causal discovery.

⚠ Critical Edge
FINITE-HORIZON ONLY

Type-I control is finite-horizon (fixed T) for the practical Gaussian plug-in — not infinite-horizon anytime-valid. The drift bound also needs user-supplied CME error bounds with no data-driven plug-in.

01 Methodology · how it works

betting × KCI SKCI fuses testing-by-betting with the KCI operator (Zhang et al. 2011). Each round, A, B, C are mapped into RKHSs and the residual operator ψ captures A ⊥ B | C.

Self-normalized payoff. A cross U-statistic — the raw cross score divided by a historical V-statistic + ε — is scale-free under H₁ while keeping conditional mean zero under H₀, regardless of the kernel's intrinsic scale.

truncate-and-shift Because Ville's inequality needs the wealth process ≥ −1, SKCI fits the null score as N(0, σ̂²) (Berry–Esseen, σ̂² from a held-out buffer) and binary-searches the smallest shift γ̂ making the truncated mean ≤ 0.

Joint adaptation. Kernel hyperparameters and the betting fraction λₜ are optimized together on leave-block-out historical proxies, preventing self-interaction leakage.

theory A one-step drift bound combines a Berry–Esseen term, a CME regression-error term, and a variance-mismatch term — the first sequential test to surface CME error explicitly — yielding finite-horizon anytime-valid Type-I control.

03 Strengths
  • First sequential CI test to explicitly bound and address CME estimation error under estimated Model-X.
  • Joint adaptation of kernel + betting fraction via leave-block-out proxies — beats static-kernel e-CRT/DAVT.
  • Closed-form Gaussian shift via 1-D binary search — simple to implement.
  • Drift bound cleanly separates finite-sample, CME-error, and variance-mismatch terms.
  • Broad eval (synthetic + neural + image + real fairness); code released; ICML 2026, UBC + Amii.
04 Limitations
  • Finite-horizon (fixed T) only for the practical Gaussian plug-in — no infinite-horizon validity.
  • Still relies on the Model-X assumption; doesn't break the Shah–Peters impossibility.
  • Drift bound needs user-supplied CME error bounds — no data-driven plug-in.
  • Gaussian plug-in may degrade on heavy-tailed/discrete data; no goodness-of-fit diagnostics.
  • Joint optimization adds tuning sensitivity; ablations deferred to the appendix.
  • Compared only to betting-family baselines — no group-sequential / SPRT contrast.
05 Questions for Further Exploration
  1. Can the Gaussian plug-in be replaced by a permutation- or bootstrap-based null calibration that still respects the Ft−1-measurability requirement?
  2. Does the drift bound extend to discrete or mixed-type A, B, C, where standard CME rates may not apply?
  3. Can the leave-block-out proxy be tightened (cross-fitting à la DML) into a sharp, data-driven bound on the CME errors?
  4. How does SKCI compare against group-sequential or mSPRT-style tests in the non-Model-X regime?
  5. Is unbounded-horizon Type-I control certifiable under verifiable sequential assumptions on the CME estimator (e.g., online SGD step-size schedules)?
  6. Does the gain survive distribution shift between training and test data — e.g., the anti-causal shift motivating the intro's pedestrian-detection example?
