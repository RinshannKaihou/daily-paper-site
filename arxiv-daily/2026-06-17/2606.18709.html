Key Finding

Even the strongest LLMs cannot reliably rank which reading items separate strong from weak students — and several models rank items backwards.

LLMs Struggle to Measure What Distinguishes Students of Different Proficiency Levels: Item Discrimination in Reading-Comprehension Assessment

Han Chen, Ming Li, Chenguang Wang, Yijun Liang, Dawei Zhou, Hong Jiao, Tianyi Zhou — MBZUAI · University of Maryland · Virginia Tech

LLM evaluation educational assessment psychometrics item discrimination student simulation 42 LLMs
Key Metric — Alignment Ceiling
0.241
Spearman ρ
Best CTT calibration (all-persona). Direct prediction tops out at just 0.152 (GPT-5.5); most strong models are negative.
Why This Matters

Anyone using LLMs to auto-screen test items, build adaptive assessments, or simulate test-takers gets a sober ceiling — plus a competence paradox: high accuracy prevents models from emulating weaker students.

Critical Edge

ρ = 0.24 is conventionally "weak" in psychometrics. One dataset, zero-shot only, no prompt ablation, and the striking negative correlation is observed but never mechanistically explained.

Methodology

Dataset. Cambridge MCQ Reading Dataset — 793 items from 120 passages, each with passage, stem, 4 options, gold answer, CEFR level, and human-calibrated CTT item discrimination.

Two zero-shot protocols.

  • Direct prediction — model sees full item + gold answer, outputs a scalar discrimination in [−1, 1].
  • Response-based CTT — model answers blind; each (model × persona) pair is one "synthetic examinee"; discrimination = point-biserial(item-correct, total-score).

Personas. Four proficiency settings (none / low / medium / high student) tested across 42 LLMs (GPT-3.5→5.5, Claude 3.5/3.7, Gemini 2.x, Llama 2/3.x, Mistral, OLMo, Phi, Qwen). Null baseline via 1,000 shuffles per examinee.

Strengths

  • Largest published scale for this question — 42 LLMs incl. GPT-5.5, Claude 3.7, Gemini 2.5 Pro, Qwen3-235B.
  • Dual-protocol design cleanly separates content-only vs. response-derived signals; null baseline isolates computation artifacts.
  • Honest negative result with a reusable ceiling (ρ 0.241) and a crisp failure mode (negative correlation).
  • Overlap@k diagnostic bridges correlation to an applied item-pre-screening use case.
  • "Competence paradox" framing — high accuracy is itself a barrier to emulating weak test-takers.

Limitations

  • Reading-comprehension only — no math, science, or open-ended writing domains.
  • Zero-shot only; no few-shot, fine-tuning, chain-of-thought, or prompt-engineering ablation.
  • Single reference dataset; no second human-calibrated item bank tested.
  • Bootstrap CIs are across items, not across LLM sampling runs (temperature/seed).
  • Effect sizes remain weak even at the ceiling; negative correlation observed but not mechanistically explained.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Do chain-of-thought, item rationales, or few-shot exemplars close the gap above ρ 0.24 CTT / 0.15 direct?
  2. Which item features (length, CEFR level, distractor plausibility, surface complexity) drive the negative correlation — is it a shared pretraining artifact?
  3. Does the finding transfer to math problem-solving, open-ended writing, dialogue assessment, or multi-step reasoning items?
  4. Would fine-tuning on a small human-response matrix unlock latent signal, or just memorize the item bank?
  5. Does restricting the synthetic-respondent pool (only weak vs. only strong models) beat the all-model average?
  6. What epistemic-state specification beyond a generic "weak student" persona is needed to yield ability-stratified error patterns?
