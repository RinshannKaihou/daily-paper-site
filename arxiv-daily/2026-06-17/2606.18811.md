> **一句话总结：** 这篇论文发现把 ModernBERT、RoBERTa、Ettin 等大 MLM-head 范数骨干用于 SPLADE 时,因 MLM 输出尺度与无归一化点积打分耦合,会出现训练不稳定甚至崩溃;只需在训练前用 W ← W/k 对 MLM-head 投影做一次常数缩放,就能让 ModernBERT 在 BEIR-13 上相对提升 215%,Ettin 提升 77%,并在多数设定下达到甚至超过经典 BERT-SPLADE 基线。

> **TL;DR:** This paper shows that under the standard SPLADE recipe, swapping BERT for stronger modern encoders (RoBERTa, ModernBERT, Ettin) can actually *hurt* retrieval — the failure is caused by the unnormalized sparse dot product inheriting the large L2 norm of the MLM head, which inflates sparse activations and destabilizes contrastive training. A single initialization-time constant rescale `W_head ← W_head / k` (no architecture, loss, or inference changes) fixes it: ModernBERT gains +215% relative nDCG@10 on BEIR-13 at k=16, Ettin +77%, and rescaled models match or surpass BERT-SPLADE on most in-domain (MS MARCO Dev, TREC-DL 2019) and out-of-domain (BEIR-13) benchmarks.

## 核心贡献
- 揭示 SPLADE 类学习式稀疏检索的"尺度耦合"机制:sparse 表示由 MLM-head 输出直接构造,query–document 相关性是无归一化点积,因此 MLM-head 的 L2 范数直接决定 sparse 激活、匹配分数与训练动力学的尺度。
- 系统化地将七种 BERT 系/现代编码器(BERT、DistilBERT、ALBERT、GTE-MLM、RoBERTa、ModernBERT、Ettin)按 MLM-head 范数分类,发现小范数(||W||<2)骨干在标准 SPLADE 训练下表现稳定(BEIR-13 nDCG@10 0.361–0.403),大范数(||W||>2)骨干则出现严重退化甚至崩溃。
- 提出初始化期常数缩放 `W_head ← W_head/k`,不引入额外参数,不修改架构、训练目标与推理流程,只对 tied MLM head 同步处理以保持权重共享。
- 在 MS MARCO Dev、TREC-DL 2019、BEIR-13 上系统验证:ModernBERT 在 k=16 下 BEIR-13 nDCG@10 由 0.127→0.400、MS MARCO MRR@10 由 0.045→0.250;TREC-DL 2019 nDCG@10 由 0.266→0.609;Ettin 在 k=16 下 BEIR-13 由 0.221→0.391。RoBERTa 在 k=2 处取得最佳 BEIR-13 0.386。
- 在 NanoBEIR 上重做 FLOPS–effectiveness 曲线,显示缩放后把大范数骨干从"高 FLOPs、低效果"区域推向"低 FLOPs、高效果"的成功 SPLADE 区域,说明收益源于更好的标定而非更密的表示。
- 给出反证:对已经小范数的骨干再做激进缩放会破坏性能(如 ALBERT),证明"无限缩小"并非普适正则化技巧;SPLADE 训练对 MLM-head 尺度有一个适当区间。

## Key Contributions
- Diagnoses a previously under-reported failure mode in learned sparse retrieval (LSR): under the standard SPLADE recipe, modern encoders with **large MLM-head L2 norms** (RoBERTa, ModernBERT, Ettin) suffer degraded effectiveness and even training collapse, despite being stronger encoders.
- Formalizes the **scale-coupling mechanism** unique to SPLADE-style LSR: sparse lexical vectors are built directly from `H W_head^T` and matched by an **un-normalized dot product**, so the L2 norm of `W_head` directly scales sparse activations, matching scores, and contrastive gradient magnitudes.
- Categorizes seven encoder backbones by MLM-head norm: small-norm (||W||<2) — BERT, DistilBERT, ALBERT, GTE-MLM — yield competitive sparse retrievers (BEIR-13 nDCG@10 0.361–0.403); large-norm (||W||>2) — RoBERTa, ModernBERT, Ettin — collapse. Notably, **anisotropy does not predict failure** (RoBERTa, ModernBERT, Ettin have low anisotropy), so the paper isolates **scale** as the operative factor.
- Proposes a **single-line, initialization-time rescale** `W_head ← W_head / k` (with tied weights handled correctly). Zero new parameters, no architecture/loss/inference change.
- Demonstrates large empirical gains:
  - ModernBERT (k=16): MS MARCO MRR@10 0.045 → 0.250; TREC-DL 2019 nDCG@10 0.266 → 0.609; BEIR-13 nDCG@10 0.127 → 0.400 (+215% relative).
  - Ettin (k=16): BEIR-13 nDCG@10 0.221 → 0.391 (+77%); MS MARCO MRR@10 0.099 → 0.238.
  - RoBERTa peaks at k=2 (BEIR-13 0.331 → 0.386); k=16 collapses it to 0.004.
  - Rescaled ModernBERT/Ettin match or surpass the classic BERT-SPLADE baseline (BEIR-13 0.370) on most settings.
- Shows on **NanoBEIR** that rescaling moves large-norm backbones into the "high-effectiveness, low-FLOPs" region of the sparsity–effectiveness frontier, indicating the benefit is **better calibration** rather than denser representations.
- Reframes the bottleneck of adapting pretrained encoders to LSR as a **calibration / scale issue** at the MLM head, not a capacity issue at the encoder.

## Methodology
- **Setting:** SPLADE-style learned sparse retrieval, trained on MS MARCO triplets with a standard recipe (in-batch contrastive ranking loss + FLOPS regularization).
- **Formalization:**
  - Sparse activation: `A = log(1 + ReLU(H W_head^T))` (bias omitted).
  - Sequence-level sparse vector: `v = max_i A_i`.
  - Retrieval score: `s(q, d) = v_q^T v_d` (**unnormalized**).
  - Loss: `L = L_rank + λ_q L_FLOPS(v_q) + λ_d L_FLOPS(v_d)`, with in-batch negatives and temperature τ=1.0.
- **Diagnosis:** Because the score is an unnormalized dot product of MLM-head outputs, the **L2 norm of `W_head` controls** (i) the magnitude of sparse activations, (ii) the magnitude of matching scores, and (iii) the gradient scale of the contrastive objective. When the head is too large, the ranking objective and FLOPS regularizer sit in sharper tension, destabilizing training.
- **Backbone survey (Table 1):** Mean / max row-wise L2 norm of `W_head` plus anisotropy:
  - BERT-base: mean 1.401, max 2.045, aniso 0.445 — works.
  - DistilBERT: 1.660 / 2.611 / 0.532 — works.
  - ALBERT: 0.407 / 0.737 / 0.108 — works (very small norm).
  - GTE-MLM: 1.278 / 1.814 / 0.085 — works.
  - **RoBERTa: 3.645 / 4.480 / 0.024 — fails.**
  - **ModernBERT: 2.553 / 4.273 / 0.044 — fails.**
  - **Ettin-encoder-150m: 2.628 / 4.506 / 0.036 — fails.**
  - Failure correlates with norm, not anisotropy.
- **Fix:** `W_head ← W_head / k` applied once at initialization (k=1 is the standard recipe; for tied MLM heads the operation is applied to the shared matrix so weight tying is preserved).
- **Training:** AdamW, peak LR 1e-4, linear schedule with 0.1 warmup, 1 epoch on MS MARCO triplets, per-GPU batch 64 × 8 NVIDIA RTX A6000 (DDP), max seq 256, bfloat16. λ_q=5e-2, λ_d=3e-3, ramped from zero with a quadratic schedule over the first third of training.
- **Evaluation:** MS MARCO Dev (MRR@10, Recall@1000), TREC-DL 2019 (nDCG@10), BEIR-13 (mean nDCG@10). NanoBEIR used for the sparsity–effectiveness trade-off.

## Results & Highlights
- **Table 2 highlights** (BERT-style small-norm vs. large-norm modern, with various k):
  - BERT (k=1, ||W||=1.40): MS MARCO MRR@10 .254, Recall@1000 .931, TREC .594, BEIR-13 .370.
  - GTE-MLM (k=1, ||W||=1.28): .273 / .953 / .635 / .403 — best small-norm result.
  - RoBERTa (k=1, ||W||=3.65): .194 / .900 / .554 / .331. RoBERTa (k=2, ||W||=1.82): .226 / .934 / .590 / .386. RoBERTa (k=16): total collapse to .000 / .008 / .000 / .004.
  - ModernBERT (k=1, ||W||=2.55): .045 / .554 / .266 / .127. ModernBERT (k=8, ||W||=0.32): .248 / .948 / .588 / .405. ModernBERT (k=16, ||W||=0.16): .250 / .948 / .609 / .400.
  - Ettin (k=1, ||W||=2.63): .099 / .752 / .354 / .221. Ettin (k=16, ||W||=0.16): .238 / .940 / .587 / .391.
- **Headline gains:** ModernBERT +215% relative BEIR-13 nDCG@10; Ettin +77%.
- **Sparsity frontier (Figure 2, NanoBEIR):** Rescaled ModernBERT/Ettin move into the high-effectiveness, low-FLOPs region; BERT/DistilBERT are already there.
- **Scale window (Figure 3):** Rescaling is not "shrink-as-much-as-possible" — for already-small-norm heads, aggressive scaling (e.g., ALBERT) collapses performance. There is an appropriate scale band; k must be backbone-dependent.
- **Training dynamics (Figure 4):** ModernBERT/Ettin start from extremely large losses under k=1 and converge to substantially higher final losses. As k increases, the loss scale drops and curves smooth out — direct evidence of optimization stabilization.

## Critical Assessment
- **Novelty:** The intervention is one line of code. The conceptual contribution is sharper: the paper isolates *why* a standard recipe breaks (un-normalized dot product × MLM-head scale) and shows anisotropy is not the right variable. This is a useful reframing for the LSR community, but the technical novelty is modest — the community may have already noticed this implicitly.
- **Rigor:** The diagnosis is well-supported by (i) backbone statistics, (ii) BEIR-13 nDCG@10 correlation with ||W||, (iii) per-k ablation across k∈{1,2,4,8,16}, (iv) training-loss curves showing stabilization, and (v) the sparsity–effectiveness frontier. Missing pieces: no theoretical justification of *which* k is optimal for a given backbone; no closed-form mapping from ||W||_init to k. The choice is essentially a per-backbone hyperparameter search.
- **Reproducibility:** Low-friction change (initialization only) and standard SPLADE recipe mean the recipe is easy to reproduce. Per-backbone k-search is small (5 values). However, the paper does not provide a heuristic to pick k without search, so practical adoption still requires a small sweep per backbone.
- **Generalization concerns:** Tested on MS MARCO → BEIR-13 transfer. No experiments on multilingual, long-document, or code-retrieval benchmarks. The authors note a related line of work (Milco) on multilingual LSR, but do not test it.
- **Significance:** Useful engineering contribution for practitioners who want to drop ModernBERT/Ettin into a SPLADE pipeline. The conceptual insight ("MLM-head scale, not encoder capacity") is durable. However, LSR has been increasingly outperformed by dense and hybrid retrievers, so the practical ceiling of even a fixed SPLADE may be limited — the paper does not compare to dense retrievers on the same backbones.
- **Red flags:**
  - The optimal k is per-backbone (RoBERTa→2, ModernBERT→8/16, Ettin→16), and the paper offers no principled way to set it. This makes the "zero-cost" framing slightly optimistic: there is a small but non-zero per-backbone tuning cost.
  - Excessive down-scaling (RoBERTa at k=16) catastrophically collapses training — the "appropriate scale band" claim is consistent with the data but not explained.
  - No head-to-head with dense retrievers on the same backbones; it is unclear whether the corrected LSR models are still SOTA on BEIR-13 (a 0.400 BEIR-13 is competitive but below modern dense retrievers such as Qwen3-Embedding or mE5).
  - 5-page ACM short paper format limits space for ablations; the claim of matching/surpassing BERT-SPLADE holds on most metrics but is silent on statistical significance.

## Strengths
- **Clean, well-motivated diagnosis** that connects an abstract symptom (unstable training) to a concrete, testable mechanism (un-normalized dot product × MLM-head scale).
- **Minimal-intervention fix** — one line, no architecture, loss, or inference change — easy to adopt and reason about.
- **Comprehensive backbone survey** covering both classical (BERT, DistilBERT, ALBERT, RoBERTa) and modern (GTE-MLM, ModernBERT, Ettin) encoders, with explicit MLM-head statistics and anisotropy.
- **Multi-metric evaluation** (MRR@10, Recall@1000, nDCG@10) across in-domain (MS MARCO Dev, TREC-DL 2019) and out-of-domain (BEIR-13, NanoBEIR) benchmarks.
- **Training-dynamics visualization** (loss curves) gives direct evidence of optimization stabilization, not just end-task performance.
- **Sparsity–effectiveness frontier analysis** clarifies that the gain comes from better calibration, not denser representations.

## Limitations
- The rescale factor k is a **per-backbone hyperparameter**; the paper provides no closed-form rule (e.g., k = ||W||_init / target_norm) to set it without search.
- The optimal band is narrow: **excessive down-scaling collapses training** (RoBERTa at k=16 → 0.004 BEIR-13), so practitioners must be careful.
- Tested only on **English MS MARCO → BEIR-13** transfer; no multilingual, long-document, or domain-specific (biomedical, legal, code) experiments.
- The analysis focuses on the **vanilla SPLADE** formulation; the transferability to **SPLADE-v3**, **uniCOIL**, or **TILDE-v2** is not verified.
- **No head-to-head with dense retrievers** on the same backbones, which would position the corrected LSR systems in the modern retrieval landscape.
- **No theoretical derivation** of the appropriate scale band; the explanation is empirical.
- The "**zero-cost**" claim is true for inference and per-step compute but **omits the small per-backbone k-search** required in practice.
- The **5-page short paper** format limits ablations: no learned-rescale variants, no gradient-norm analysis, no statistical significance tests.

## Relevance
该工作直接对应"用更强预训练编码器改进检索模型"这条主线,尤其是将 ModernBERT / RoBERTa / Ettin 等大范数骨干适配到 SPLADE 类学习式稀疏检索时的工程痛点;其"无归一化点积 + 输出头尺度耦合"这一诊断对其他"logit-as-feature"的下游任务(token 级分类、稀疏检索、神经匹配)亦有借鉴价值,提示我们在做骨干替换时应额外关注输出头尺度的标定。

## Questions for Further Exploration
- Can the optimal k be derived **a priori from `||W_head||_init`** (e.g., a closed-form mapping that targets a fixed `||W_head||_post` such as ~0.3–0.6), removing the per-backbone search?
- Does the same scale-mismatch phenomenon arise in **other sparse retrieval variants** (SPLADE-v3, uniCOIL, TILDE-v2, Milco) that also derive sparse term weights from a model head? If so, do they need different rescale factors?
- How do rescaled LSR models compare **head-to-head with dense retrievers** (e.g., GTE, Qwen3-Embedding, mE5) built on the same ModernBERT/Ettin backbones? Is LSR still competitive at the upper end?
- Can the principle of **output-head scale calibration** improve stability when adapting **LLMs to token-level "logit-as-feature" tasks** such as sparse retrieval, slot filling, or neural ranking?
- What is the **interaction between MLM-head rescaling and the FLOPS regularizer** (λ_q, λ_d)? Does rescaling allow weaker sparsity regularization to be used, or does it require re-tuning?
- Could a **learned per-row rescale** (instead of a scalar) further improve calibration by correcting anisotropic heads more precisely?
- Does rescaling help when fine-tuning the **MLM head** (rather than freezing it at initialization) — i.e., does the issue persist if the head is allowed to adapt during SPLADE training?
