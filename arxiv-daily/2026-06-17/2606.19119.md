> **一句话总结：** 本文在 96 颗真实 DDR4 芯片上首次系统性地揭示了 PuD（Processing-using-DRAM）操作中由非激活行数据（最高 10%）和并发计算列数据（最高 48%）引发的新型干扰现象 "PuDGhost"，并提出两类 PuDGhost 感知的列筛选（CS-1/CS-2）和带隔离行的计算行布局缓解方案，使 GEMV 任务的归一化均方误差降低 413×、TRNG 任务防止 93% 熵损失。

> **TL;DR:** This paper experimentally reveals "PuDGhost," a previously unstudied interference phenomenon in Processing-using-DRAM (PuD) where SiMRA-based in-DRAM computations are corrupted by (1) data in non-activated rows (up to 10% error) and (2) data in concurrently computing columns (up to 48% error), characterized across 96 real DDR4 chips from 12 modules. The authors propose two hardware-validated stack-spanning mitigations—robust column screening (CS-1/CS-2) and a compute-row layout with isolation rows—that reduce GEMV NMSE by 413× and prevent 93% of entropy loss in SiMRA-based TRNG, providing a foundation for robust future PuD systems.

## 核心贡献
- 首次在 96 颗真实 DDR4 芯片（覆盖 12 个模组、3 种 die revision、4Gb/8Gb 两种容量）上系统性地表征 PuD 操作中两类被忽略的干扰源：非激活行数据（adjacent-row interference）与并发计算列数据（inter-column interference），并将其命名为 "PuDGhost"。
- 提出 **15 项新的实证观察**，定量给出错误率上界（adjacent-row 10%、inter-column 48%），并分析其对数据模式、列奇偶性、温度等因素的依赖关系。
- 设计两层 PuDGhost 感知缓解方案并在真实芯片上验证：1) **CS-1/CS-2 列筛选**（在筛选阶段改变或固定相邻行数据）；2) **带隔离行的计算行布局**（在计算行之间插入固定数据模式的隔离行，仅 0.68% 行数开销）。
- 在两类真实应用上量化端到端收益：GEMV 任务 NMSE 降低 413×（N=32，最差场景），SiMRA-based TRNG 防止 93% 熵损失，并指出 PuDGhost 对 PuD 仿真器、TRNG/PUF 等安全敏感场景的广泛影响。

## Key Contributions
- First extensive experimental characterization of **PuDGhost**, an interference phenomenon in PuD caused by data in (1) non-activated DRAM rows (adjacent to SiMRA rows) and (2) concurrently computing columns under the same SiMRA activation, on **96 real DDR4 chips across 12 modules**.
- **15 new empirical observations** that quantify error rates (up to 10% from rows, up to 48% from columns for random inputs) and their dependence on data patterns, even/odd column parity, input-output correlations, and temperature.
- **Two stack-spanning countermeasures validated on real hardware:** PuDGhost-aware column screening (CS-1 varies adjacent-row patterns during screening; CS-2 fixes adjacent-row data consistently between screening and execution) and an **isolation-row compute layout** that reserves fixed-pattern rows between compute rows.
- **End-to-end impact quantification** on real PuD applications: 413× lower NMSE in GEMV and prevention of 93% entropy loss in TRNG.
- Discussion of **broader implications** for PuD simulation frameworks (column errors are not independent), multi-tenant security (adjacent-row attacks / side channels), and the need to redesign PuD-aware ECC and reference-voltage schemes.

## Methodology
- **Target operations.** PuD operations based on **simultaneous multiple-row activation (SiMRA)**. By issuing an APA (ACT-PRE-ACT) sequence with sub-3ns intervals, the hierarchical row decoder of SK Hynix DDR4 chips can simultaneously activate 2, 4, 8, 16, or 32 rows, producing a **MAJX (majority-of-X)** analog computation on each bitline through charge sharing and sense amplification.
- **Experimental platform.** Custom **FPGA-based DRAM Bender** infrastructure (PCIe-attached Xilinx board) that issues precisely-timed ACT/PRE/WRITE/READ commands to commodity DDR4 modules, with **thermocouple + heater pads** maintaining temperatures of 50–80°C.
- **Chip sample.** 96 chips from 12 modules: SK Hynix dies (rev A 4Gb, rev M 4Gb, rev A 8Gb) mounted on TimeTec, TeamGroup, and SK Hynix PCBs. (Note: PuD/SiMRA has only been demonstrated on SK Hynix DDR4 chips in prior work.)
- **Pre-characterization.** Reverse-engineer logical-to-physical row mapping (via RowHammer bitflip patterns), identify subarray boundaries (via RowCopy), and classify true/anti cells and even/odd column parity.
- **Interference isolation.** Controlled microbenchmarks that **fix the SiMRA operand data** (128 random samples per condition) while sweeping (a) data in non-activated rows in the target/adjacent subarrays and (b) SiMRA-row data in non-target columns, producing **65536×128 output bits per condition**.
- **Metric.** `Norm. po1` = ratio of the fraction of logic-1 outputs (`po1`) under a controlled condition to a baseline `po1` (pc=0.5 random neighbors). A value of 1.0 = no interference; <1.0 = bias toward 0; >1.0 = bias toward 1.
- **Mitigation evaluation.** Real-chip end-to-end measurement of (i) column screening configurations (Base / CS-1-01 / CS-1-sweep / CS-2-0 / CS-2-1 / CS-2-check), (ii) compute row layouts (contiguous vs. isolation-row), and (iii) downstream GEMV (4096×N for N∈{4,8,16,32,64}) and SiMRA-based TRNG kernels.
- **Control experiments.** Show that **refreshing data** before SiMRA (0–120ms delays) does **not** mitigate PuDGhost, demonstrating it is a transient in-operation effect rather than a charge-leakage effect.

## Results & Highlights
- **Obsv. 1–3 (adjacent rows).** Non-activated adjacent rows bias SiMRA outputs by **up to 10%** (Norm. po1 as low as 0.90 for all-zeros neighbors, as high as 1.10 for all-ones neighbors); interference is highly localized to physically adjacent rows (R-adj ≈ S-tar ≈ S-all, but R-far/S-adj ≤ 0.7% deviation); magnitude grows with the number of SiMRA rows (e.g., 2-row ≈ 5% max, 8-row ≈ 10% max).
- **Obsv. 4–7 (adjacent rows continued).** Bias is **monotonic in pc** (fraction of logic-1 in adjacent rows) but **independent of structured pattern** (0xF0, 0x55, 0xAA all give Norm. po1 ≈ 1.0 at pc=0.5); **column-local** (each column is biased by its own adjacent-row cells, with Hi-Cols/Lo-Cols diverging in opposite directions); **cross-column** effects also exist (other columns' adjacent-row cells shift the target by ~1–2%).
- **Obsv. 8 (input-asymmetry).** Cells storing logic-1 in SiMRA rows are **substantially more vulnerable** to adjacent-row interference than cells storing logic-0 (Norm. po1 stays ≈1.0 when bitin=0, but shifts to 0.97–1.03 when bitin=1).
- **Obsv. 9 (temperature).** Adjacent-row interference shows **no significant temperature dependence** between 50–80°C.
- **Obsv. 10–12 (inter-column).** Data in concurrently computing columns biases SiMRA outputs by **up to 48%** (Norm. po1 0.86–1.11 for 32-row activation; max deviation at 16-row activation, pc=0.0). The trend is **monotonic for 2–4 rows but non-monotonic for 8+ rows** (peaks at intermediate pc).
- **Obsv. 13–14 (parity).** Opposite-parity columns (sharing a sense amplifier) bias target columns monotonically; same-parity columns show a **non-monotonic bias that reverses direction at high pc for 8+ rows** — pointing to distinct coupling mechanisms (bitline vs. SA).
- **Obsv. 15 (temperature).** Inter-column interference varies **monotonically** with temperature (e.g., 16-row @ pc=0.0: Norm. po1 0.87→0.89 from 50→80°C).
- **Column screening comparison (Fig. 20).** PuDGhost-unaware (Base) screening has a **column break rate of 2.1%** and **BER of 9.2·10⁻⁵** on the supposedly reliable columns — a *silent* reliability gap. **CS-2-1 (fixed all-ones neighbors)** improves Column Passing Rate by **1.06× over Base** while reducing CBR by **125×** and BER by **91×**; estimated **~8.3·10³× lower ECC correction failure rate** under a prior PuD ECC scheme. **CS-1-01** (screening with both all-0 and all-1 neighbors) matches CS-1-sweep, indicating a 2-pattern screen is sufficient.
- **GEMV end-to-end (Fig. 21).** Without mitigations, Base-worst NMSE reaches **2.2·10⁻² at N=16 and 5.8·10⁻² at N=64**. CS-1/CS-2 reduce NMSE **<10⁻³** across all N. Compared to Base-rand: **36× reduction at N=32, 14× at N=64**; compared to Base-worst: **413× at N=32, 114× at N=64**, and **1.3·10³× BER reduction**.
- **TRNG end-to-end (Fig. 22).** Worst-case inter-column interference (Col-1) reduces normalized entropy to **0.07 on average** (≈**93% entropy loss**); Col-0 and Col-rand also destroy entropy to 0.12 and 0.15. Adjacent-row interference (Row-1) reduces entropy to 0.35.
- **Compute-row layout overhead.** Replacing a contiguous 4-compute-row subarray layout with an isolation-row layout adds **only 0.68% row-count overhead** per 1024-row subarray (7 isolation rows for 6 compute rows following Ambit's 6-row MAJ3 design).

The group has a long, well-cited track record of rigorous experimental DRAM studies (RowHammer, RowPress, DRAM Bender, PiDRAM, SIMDRAM, MIMDRAM, Proteus, Count2Multiply, EDEN, ComputeDRAM, FracDRAM, Clutch, MVDRAM, PUDTune). ## Critical Assessment
- **Novelty.** High. While PuD has been studied via simulation and small-scale prototypes, this is the first work to systematically expose and quantify interference from *non-participating* rows and *concurrently computing* columns on real commodity DDR4. The "ghost" framing — logically inert data that physically interferes — is a clear conceptual contribution that future PuD papers will have to cite.
- **Rigor.** Strong. 96 chips across 12 modules is a substantial sample for a real-chip DRAM study. The methodology of fixing SiMRA operands while sweeping neighborhood state is the right controlled experiment. Reporting up to 48% error on a primitive that is *supposed* to be deterministic is a striking, hard-to-cherry-pick result. The control experiment showing refresh-before-SiMRA does *not* help is well-designed and rules out an obvious confound.
- **Reproducibility.** Moderate-to-good. The DRAM Bender infrastructure is described in prior work, and the screening / layout designs are described at a level that allows re-implementation. The exact DDR4 modules, temperatures, refresh configurations, and timing parameters for the worst-case numbers live in tables not fully visible from the abstract; readers need the paper body and (presumably) code to reproduce worst-case claims. The work is SK Hynix DDR4 only — generalizing to DDR5/LPDDR5/HBM is left to future work, which is honestly acknowledged.
- **Significance.** High for the PuD and in-memory-computing community. If a SiMRA-based primitive can be off by up to 48% purely from peer-column state, this materially changes the energy/accuracy tradeoffs that have justified PuD as a data-movement-saving paradigm. The two mitigations are immediately actionable: CS-2 + isolation-row layout preserves ~94% of columns and cuts error rates by 1–2 orders of magnitude with <1% capacity overhead.
- **Red flags.** Few.
  - The 48% upper bound is a *worst-case* observation under random inputs and specific configurations (e.g., 16-row activation with pc=0.0 neighbors); readers should be careful not to over-generalize to "all PuD operations are 48% wrong."
  - CS-2 requires *runtime support* in the memory controller to maintain fixed neighbor data — practical deployment depends on hardware modifications that the paper does not (and cannot) fully evaluate at the system level.
  - The work only covers SiMRA-based PuD on DDR4; other PuD mechanisms (charge-domain analog compute on HBM/PIM devices, copy-based RowClone, near-DRAM PIM like UPMEM) may exhibit different interference profiles.
  - The paper does not (from the abstract) quantify the *average-case* PuDGhost error rate on realistic ML/graph workloads — only worst-case and on synthetic kernels. The 36×/14× NMSE reductions for Base-rand are striking but apply to a randomized adjacent-row distribution, not a worst-case adversarial one.
  - Mitigation evaluation runs on **only 3 of the 12 modules** in §10 (to limit testing time), so the cross-module generalization of the *mitigation* effectiveness is weaker than the cross-module generalization of the *phenomenon* characterization.

## Strengths
- First-of-its-kind, real-chip experimental study of an interference phenomenon that the PuD community has largely ignored.
- Large hardware sample (96 chips, 12 modules) with explicit cross-module variation analysis and temperature sweep (50–80°C).
- 15 distinct empirical observations decompose PuDGhost into orthogonal, testable sub-phenomena (adjacent-row vs. inter-column, same-parity vs. opposite-parity, monotonic vs. non-monotonic, input-asymmetric).
- Two concrete, hardware-validated countermeasures (CS-1 / CS-2 / isolation-row layout) with end-to-end application impact on GEMV and TRNG.
- Strong forward-looking analysis of implications for PuD simulation frameworks, multi-tenant security, ECC, and reference-voltage adjustment.
- Clear conceptual naming ("PuDGhost") and a clean causal hypothesis (electrical coupling during charge sharing / sensing) that future device-level work can test.

## Limitations
- Results are bounded to **SK Hynix DDR4**; how PuDGhost scales to DDR5 / LPDDR5 / HBM3 / 3D-stacked PIM or to more advanced process nodes is open. The paper argues PuDGhost should be vendor-agnostic because it stems from shared DRAM fundamentals, but does not prove this.
- 10% / 48% error numbers are worst-case; **average-case error rates under realistic ML / graph workloads are not reported** in the abstract, and how often worst-case patterns arise in practice is unclear.
- **CS-2 requires memory-controller modifications** to maintain fixed neighbor data; the resulting controller complexity, scheduling overhead, and performance impact are not quantified.
- Mitigation evaluation (§10) uses **only 3 of 12 modules** to bound testing time, weakening the cross-module generalization of mitigation effectiveness.
- The work focuses on SiMRA-based primitives; other PuD mechanisms (RowClone-based data movement, near-DRAM PIM like UPMEM, in-HBM analog compute) may exhibit different interference profiles and are not analyzed.
- The **column-wise interference is not fully decomposed** into bitline-coupling vs. sense-amplifier-coupling components; the paper offers only a hypothesis (Obsv. 13/14 parity difference) without circuit-level validation.
- The paper does not address **how PuDGhost interacts with retention failures, variable refresh rates, or variable retention time** — all common in real DDR4 deployments.
- Security implications (§8.3.1) are raised as a concern but **not demonstrated end-to-end**; the multi-tenant attack vectors remain hypothetical.

## Relevance
该工作与计算机体系结构、内存系统及存内计算研究高度相关。Onur Mutlu 课题组在 DRAM 可靠性方面一贯严谨的实验方法学、96 颗真实芯片的大规模表征、以及对 PuD 安全与仿真建模的延伸讨论，对评估存内计算可行性、设计鲁棒 PuD 系统、以及跟踪 DRAM 干扰研究前沿的研究人员都有直接参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration
- How do PuDGhost error rates scale with **DRAM process node** (DDR5, LPDDR5X, HBM3) and with **3D-stacking**, where row/column and TSV coupling are expected to be more severe?
- What is the **average-case** PuDGhost error rate under realistic ML, graph, or database workloads, rather than the worst-case random-input regime reported here?
- Can CS-2 / isolation-row layout be combined with **lightweight PuD ECC** (e.g., Count2Multiply) to bound residual error rates to <10⁻⁹ in production?
- How do these interference effects manifest in **HBM-based** PIM architectures, where thermal and TSV coupling differ from planar DDR4?
- Is it possible to design a **programming model / ISA** that exposes PuDGhost susceptibility to software, so compilers can avoid placing sensitive operands in ghost-prone columns or pair them with appropriate isolation patterns?
- Could **adversarial input patterns** be constructed to deliberately trigger PuDGhost as a **fault-injection / side-channel** attack on in-DRAM computations, especially in multi-tenant cloud environments? The paper raises this as a concern; an end-to-end demonstration is missing.
- Can **staged activation** (activating rows in smaller sequential groups) preserve MAJX correctness while reducing PuDGhost — and at what throughput cost?
- Can **runtime reference-voltage adjustment** (lowering SA reference when all-0 neighbors are expected) compensate for the bias direction dynamically, and at what power / circuit cost?
- How does PuDGhost interact with **retention failures and variable refresh rates**, and can the two be jointly modeled for PuD-aware refresh?
- Does the opposite-parity vs. same-parity asymmetry (Obsv. 13/14) point to a specific circuit-level coupling path that device-level simulations (SPICE, TCAD) could confirm — analogous to how device studies eventually explained RowHammer?
