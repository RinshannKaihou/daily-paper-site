> **一句话总结：** 本文通过SASS指令级分析揭示LLM推理不可复现性的根本原因是内核边界处高精度寄存器向低精度存储的截断误差（而非算术本身），并提出HEAL方法——通过INT16量化Q/K/V张量和代数误差补偿GEMM——在保持16位内存占用和Tensor Core吞吐的同时，实现与FP32基线相同的可复现性，性能开销降低最高7.1倍。

> **TL;DR:** LLM inference non-reproducibility under greedy decoding stems not from low-precision arithmetic but from truncation when high-precision registers are downcast at CUDA kernel boundaries. HEAL fixes this with two surgical interventions -- INT16 quantization of Q/K/V tensors (which is actually more precise than BF16 for typical value distributions) and algebraic error compensation for GEMM via dual-FP16 decomposition -- achieving FP32-level reproducibility on mission-critical benchmarks with only 40-109% TPOT overhead versus native BF16, compared to the 4.2-13.1x slowdown of a full FP32 pipeline.

## 核心贡献

- 通过SASS指令级剖析，证明LLM推理中大多数关键内核内部实际使用FP32累加器运算，非可复现性的根源是内核边界处高精度寄存器截断为低精度存储格式（boundary truncation），而非浮点算术本身
- 发现误差积累呈非线性特征，存在明确的"结果翻转阈值"（divergence threshold）：低于阈值的数值噪声被模型自然吸收，超过阈值则引发灾难性输出分歧
- 提出HEAL方法：(1) 对Q/K/V张量采用INT16量化，利用浮点格式对位宽的浪费实现更低MSE且不扩大KV cache；(2) 对GEMM采用FP16提升+代数误差补偿（dual-FP16分解），在Tensor Core上实现近FP32精度
- 引入MCR-Bench（Mission-Critical Reproducibility Benchmark），覆盖医疗、法律、金融领域的高风险推理任务
- 在5个主流开源模型、3种GPU硬件上验证HEAL达到FP32级可复现性，性能开销相比FP32基线降低最高7.1倍

## Key Contributions

- Demonstrates via SASS-level profiling that most critical LLM inference kernels internally compute in FP32; the root cause of non-reproducibility is boundary truncation (downcasting high-precision registers to lower-precision storage at kernel boundaries), not the arithmetic itself
- Identifies a non-linear divergence threshold: accumulated numerical error below the threshold is safely absorbed by the model's argmax margin, but above it causes catastrophic result flipping
- Proposes HEAL with two mechanisms: (1) INT16 quantization for Q/K/V tensors that achieves lower MSE than BF16/FP16 without expanding KV cache memory, and (2) algebraic error compensation for GEMM via dual-FP16 decomposition that approximates FP32 precision while running entirely on 16-bit Tensor Cores
- Introduces MCR-Bench, a benchmark targeting reproducibility in medical, legal, and financial reasoning tasks
- Validates across 5 open-source models (Qwen3-8B/14B/32B, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-14B) and 3 GPU types (A100, H100, MI300X)

## Methodology

**Diagnosis (Section 2):** The authors trace non-reproducibility in reverse: from the macroscopic failure (result flipping at argmax) back to its microscopic origin. Key findings:

1. **Result Flipping Threshold:** The argmax operation creates a highly non-linear relationship between accumulated numerical noise and output divergence. There exists a distinct divergence threshold in Final Hidden MSE -- below it, errors are absorbed; above it, flip rates surge dramatically (e.g., from ~0% to ~12% on MedQA).

2. **Error Propagation:** Using a formal decomposition (Equations 1-4), error is attributed to three sources: newly injected error from the current layer's rounding (epsilon_new), horizontal error inherited from past tokens via KV cache (epsilon_kv), and vertical error propagated from prior layers via the residual stream (epsilon_old). Layer-wise tracking shows non-linear (exponential) MSE accumulation with structural imbalance across layers.

3. **Boundary Truncation as Root Cause:** SASS profiling of NVIDIA A100 kernels reveals that critical kernels (FlashAttention, GEMM) internally use FP32 accumulators (HMMA.16816.F32.BF16 instructions). The instability comes exclusively from truncating these FP32 results back to BF16/FP16 at kernel output boundaries. The arithmetic error contribution (epsilon_arith) is negligible compared to truncation error (epsilon_trunc) for all kernels except Attention (which uses TF32 in Triton).

**HEAL Method (Section 3):** Two targeted interventions:

1. **INT16 Quantization for Attention Q/K/V:** Standard 16-bit floating-point formats waste bits on the exponent for Q/K/V tensors, whose values are concentrated in a narrow range. INT16 uniform quantization (Q_INT16(X) = Round((2^15 - 1)X / max|X|)) achieves lower MSE than FP16 by a factor of at least 256 * sigma^2/M^2 in the worst case. For 70% of Q/K/V tensors empirically, MSE reduction is >= 50%. The INT16 values are algebraically unpacked into dual-FP16 pairs (X_hi + X_lo/2^10) at compute time, yielding 20 bits of effective fractional precision while keeping the 16-bit memory footprint.

2. **FP16 Promotion + Algebraic Error Compensation for GEMM:** All GEMM operations are promoted from BF16 to FP16 (gaining 10 significand bits matching TF32 precision). For tasks requiring even higher precision, a dual-FP16 pairing decomposes the input tensor h into (h_hi + h_lo/2^10), executing two parallel FP16 GEMMs on Tensor Cores to approximate FP32 precision: GEMM(h, W^T) ~ GEMM(h_hi, W^T) + GEMM(h_lo/2^10, W^T).

**Progressive Configurations:** HEAL-Base (GEMM only) -> HEAL+ (adds INT16 for Attention) -> HEAL++ (skips early layers whose error is naturally absorbed) -> HEAL-Full (complete hybrid with dual-FP16 for both Attention and GEMM).

## Results & Highlights

**Reproducibility (Table 2, MCR-Bench):**
- Native BF16 flip rates: 6.2% (Qwen3-8B) to 12.8% (DeepSeek-14B)
- HEAL-Full flip rates: 0.1-0.3% across all models, matching FP32 (+LayerCast) baseline (0.0%)
- HEAL-Full Final Hidden MSE: ~10^-7, closely aligned with FP32 baseline (~10^-10)
- HEAL-Base alone already reduces flip rates dramatically (e.g., Qwen3-8B: 6.2% -> 0.7%)

**Performance (Table 3, Qwen3-14B on ShareGPT):**
- FP32 (+LayerCast) TPOT: 1332ms (A100), 547ms (H100), 243ms (MI300X)
- HEAL-Full TPOT: 202ms (A100), 76ms (H100), 110ms (MI300X)
- BF16 baseline TPOT: 108ms (A100), 42ms (H100), 55ms (MI300X)
- HEAL-Full incurs ~89.5% average overhead vs. BF16, but is up to 7.1x faster than FP32
- Memory: HEAL maintains 28.3-28.5 GiB vs. BF16's 28.1 GiB (negligible increase), while FP32 requires 29.6 GiB

**Cross-hardware generalization:** Results are consistent across NVIDIA A100, H100, and AMD MI300X, confirming the approach is hardware-agnostic.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** High. The SASS-level root cause analysis distinguishing boundary truncation from arithmetic error is a genuinely novel diagnostic insight. The INT16 quantization for Q/K/V exploiting the observation that floating-point formats waste exponent bits for these tensors is a clever and non-obvious design choice. The dual-FP16 algebraic decomposition is conceptually simple but practically effective.

**Rigor:** Strong. The error decomposition formalism (Equations 1-4) properly separates error sources. The SASS instruction-level profiling provides hardware-ground-truth evidence rather than relying on software-level reasoning. The divergence threshold analysis (Figure 1) provides a clear conceptual framework. The evaluation spans 5 models and 3 GPU architectures.

**Reproducibility of the work itself:** Moderate concern. The paper does not mention code release. The approach requires custom CUDA/Triton kernel implementations (INT16 unpacking, dual-FP16 decomposition), which would be non-trivial to reproduce from the paper alone. The MCR-Bench benchmark composition is not fully detailed in the main paper.

**Significance:** High for production LLM deployments. The 89.5% overhead vs. BF16 is meaningful but far more practical than the 4.2-13.1x FP32 penalty. The problem is real and growing as LLMs are deployed in regulated domains (healthcare, law, finance).

**Red flags:**
- The residual 0.1-0.3% flip rate in HEAL-Full (vs. 0.0% for FP32) is acknowledged but not deeply analyzed -- are these residual flips from model knowledge boundaries or remaining numerical issues?
- The FP16 overflow risk is dismissed as affecting "less than 1/64 of the representable range" and "0.1-1% of fixed channels," but the paper does not provide a formal guarantee against overflow; targeted rescaling is mentioned but not detailed in the main text
- Performance numbers use ShareGPT workloads which may not reflect worst-case scenarios for the algebraic decomposition overhead

## Strengths

- Exceptionally thorough root cause analysis at the hardware instruction level (SASS profiling), going beyond the typical software-level reasoning in the literature
- The insight that INT16 quantization can be more precise than BF16/FP16 for Q/K/V tensors is counterintuitive and well-supported mathematically
- Practical and deployable: maintains 16-bit memory footprint, runs on standard Tensor Cores, works across NVIDIA and AMD hardware
- Clean progressive evaluation (HEAL-Base through HEAL-Full) that isolates the contribution of each component
- The divergence threshold framework provides a useful conceptual tool for the community -- you don't need bit-exact FP32 parity, just need to stay below the threshold

## Limitations

- ~89.5% TPOT overhead vs. BF16 is non-trivial for latency-sensitive applications; the paper frames this as a win vs. FP32, but many practitioners will compare against BF16
- No code release mentioned; custom kernel implementations would be needed to adopt this
- MCR-Bench details are largely deferred to appendices; the benchmark's composition and difficulty calibration are not thoroughly examined in the main paper
- The dual-FP16 decomposition roughly triples the FLOPs for Attention and doubles GEMM FLOPs; the paper argues this is absorbed under the roofline model (memory-bound for Attention, Tensor Core-bound for GEMM), but this may not hold for all batch sizes and sequence lengths
- Only evaluated on models up to 32B parameters; scaling behavior to 70B+ models is unknown
- The approach requires per-channel rescaling for FP16 overflow edge cases, adding implementation complexity not fully detailed

## Relevance

本文对LLM推理系统的数值稳定性进行了深入的硬件级分析，提出的HEAL方法在保持推理效率的同时实现跨硬件的可复现性，对将LLM部署到医疗/法律/金融等高可靠性场景具有直接工程价值。其INT16量化Q/K/V的思路也为KV cache压缩研究提供了新视角。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How does HEAL scale to models larger than 32B (e.g., 70B, 405B), where the error accumulation across more layers and the memory pressure on KV cache are both more severe?
2. Can the divergence threshold be predicted or estimated cheaply at runtime, enabling adaptive precision switching on a per-token or per-layer basis?
3. How does HEAL interact with existing KV cache compression techniques (e.g., GQA, sliding window attention, token pruning) that already modify the Q/K/V representation?
4. What is the residual 0.1-0.3% flip rate in HEAL-Full attributable to -- remaining numerical noise or inherent model sensitivity at decision boundaries?
5. Could the INT16 quantization insight for Q/K/V be applied during training to improve training stability or enable lower-precision training regimes?
