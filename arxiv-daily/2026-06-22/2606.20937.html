Key Finding

Learning through Internalization

Tsilivis, Joshi, Medvedev, Kempe, Srebro · arXiv 2606.20937 · 22 Jun 2026
Networks absorb chain-of-thought into weights -- wider models internalize better than deeper ones, but OOD generalization degrades.
Key Metric
Depth-1
Single-layer transformers succeed at full internalization. Prime-order tasks require O(n) MLP width; smooth numbers compress exponentially.
Why This Matters
Internalization could slash LLM inference costs by replacing multi-step CoT with single forward passes -- if OOD degradation is solved.
Critical Edge
OOD performance degrades fundamentally as CoT tokens are removed. Internalized models learn shortcut solutions that generalize worse.

Semiautomata Testbed

  • State machines with state space, input space, and transition function; predict final state after T steps.
  • Primary testbed: modular counters C_n (q_{t+1} = (q_t + sigma_t) mod n) and permutation group S_3.
  • Three regimes compared: end-to-end, full CoT supervision, and curriculum-based internalization.

Curriculum Strategies

  • Left curriculum: remove CoT tokens from the beginning of the sequence.
  • Right curriculum: remove tokens from the end of the sequence.
  • Inductive curriculum (novel): train on progressively larger leaps between states.
  • Mixture distribution: single distribution over all CoT lengths replaces multi-phase curriculum.

Theoretical Framework

Internalization is formalized (Definition 1) as: given h_theta and a procedure P using it multiple times, internalization yields h_{theta'} from the same hypothesis class reproducing P(h_theta) in fewer steps.

Theorem 1 -- Compact Representation

A transformer with O(log T) layers, O(k) embedding dim, and MLP width O(sum of prime-power factors of n) simulates any counter semiautomaton C_n over T steps. Smooth numbers compress exponentially; primes need width proportional to n.

Theorem 2 -- Provable Internalization for Parity

A one-layer linear-attention transformer learns hidden sparse parity through internalization in poly(d, log(1/delta)) time. The resulting representation is computationally hard to find via direct learning (hard in the SQ model).

E2E vs. CoT vs. Internalization

  • End-to-end training completely fails even on "simple" local operations like mod-n addition due to long-range dependencies.
  • CoT training succeeds with good in-distribution and OOD generalization, but at high inference cost.
  • Internalization succeeds even with depth-1 transformers -- intermediate reasoning steps need not be explicit.

Width > Depth

  • At a fixed parameter budget, wider-shallower architectures consistently outperform deeper ones for internalization.
  • Computation appears to be internalized "horizontally" across width, not "vertically" across layers.
  • Contradicts the intuition that removed tokens unfold into additional depth.

Prime vs. Composite Order

  • Prime n (C_13, C_17) show markedly lower internalization success than composite n (C_10, C_12, C_16).
  • Explained by Theorem 1: primes need O(n) width; smooth numbers compress to O(sum of small prime powers).

OOD Degradation Tradeoff

  • OOD performance degrades progressively as more CoT tokens are removed.
  • Internalized models learn shortcut solutions that generalize worse than CoT-trained representations.
  • Parallels human automatization: automated skills become more error-prone under novel conditions.

Positive Distribution Shift Connection

The internalization curriculum constitutes a "positive distribution shift" relative to the hard end-to-end distribution. The curriculum can often be replaced by a single mixture distribution over all CoT lengths, allowing the same model to both answer directly and generate full CoT when needed.

Strengths
  • Rigorous theory: formal definitions, representation theorems (Thm 1), and the first provable internalization guarantee (Thm 2).
  • Well-chosen testbed: semiautomata provide clean, controllable experiments with systematically variable structure.
  • Intellectually honest: OOD degradation is characterized rather than hidden.
  • Actionable: "width over depth" directly informs model configuration for internalization workloads.
Limitations
  • Theory-practice gap: Theorem 2 relies on linear attention, fixed activations, and weight rounding -- far from practical softmax transformers.
  • Narrow task scope: only semiautomata and parity; transfer to arithmetic, multi-step logic, or natural-language CoT is unverified.
  • OOD unsolved: the fundamental degradation tradeoff is identified but not resolved.
  • Curriculum sensitivity: success depends on task-specific curriculum choices (left/right/inductive), limiting generality.
  • Limited engagement with concurrent work: does not deeply address when internalization fails vs. succeeds beyond the semiautomata setting.
Q1
Can width-over-depth be exploited in production LLMs? Would wider MLP layers improve reasoning internalization in scaled models?
Q2
How does the OOD degradation scale with task complexity? Is there a principled way to preserve generalization during internalization?
Q3
Does the mixture-distribution approach extend to natural-language CoT, where token semantics vary far more than in semiautomata?
Q4
Can Theorem 2's guarantees be extended to softmax attention and learned nonlinearities, closing the theory-practice gap?
Q5
What distinguishes tasks where internalization produces robust representations from those where it produces brittle shortcuts?
Q6
How does internalization interact with model scale? Does the prime-vs-composite difficulty gap vanish at sufficient width?
