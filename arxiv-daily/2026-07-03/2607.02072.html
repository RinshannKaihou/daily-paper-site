kNNGuard: Turning LLM Hidden Activations into a Training-Free Configurable Guardrail

arXiv: 2607.02072

Date: 2026-07-03

https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02072

一句话总结： kNNGuard 是一种无需训练、可配置的 LLM 护栏——复用冻结 LLM 的多层隐藏激活作为特征，对仅 50 条标注的 safe/unsafe 参考库做 cosine-distance kNN（Fisher 判据加权多层 + 与 sentence-embedding 自适应融合）；在六域（代码指令/输出、医疗、安全、越狱、提示注入）上平均 F1 87.4%、FPR 12.9%、延迟 45.9 ms，2.7× 快于最强可比护栏、10× 快于微调安全分类器，域适配仅需更新参考库（<10 秒，比 LoRA 微调快 61×）。
TL;DR: kNNGuard is a training-free, configurable LLM guardrail that reuses a frozen LLM's multi-layer hidden activations as features. Given a small bank of 50 safe + 50 unsafe prompts passed once through the model, it caches activations and at inference compares an incoming prompt via cosine-distance kNN across multiple transformer layers, with Fisher-discriminant weighting ($J_\ell = B_\ell/W_\ell$, softmax → α_ℓ) assigning more influence to layers that best separate the classes. Two variants: kNNGuard LE (layer-ensemble over activation space) and kNNGuard FE (fuses activation-space score $s_{act}$ with a MiniLM sentence-embedding score $s_{emb}$ via adaptive confidence-based fusion). Across six domains (Code Instructions, Code Outputs, Medical, Safety, Jailbreak, Prompt Injection), using only 50 labeled examples per class, kNNGuard achieves average F1 87.4%, FPR 12.9%, per-prompt latency 45.9 ms — competitive with or superior to fine-tuned SOTA (Llama Nemotron Topic Guard V1, Nemotron Safety Guard V2) at 2.7× lower latency than the best comparable guardrail and 10× faster than a fine-tuned safety classifier, with domain adaptation in <10 s (61× faster than LoRA fine-tuning).

核心贡献

  1. 无需训练、复用 LLM 激活的护栏：把冻结 LLM 当特征提取器，对 50 条 safe/unsafe 参考库做多层 kNN，无需梯度更新或微调。
  2. 两个变体：kNNGuard LE（Fisher 判据 $J_\ell=B_\ell/W_\ell$ 加权多层激活集成）与 kNNGuard FE（激活空间分数与 MiniLM 嵌入分数自适应置信融合）。
  3. 强实证：六域平均 F1 87.4%、FPR 12.9%、延迟 45.9 ms，匹敌/超越微调 SOTA（Nemotron Topic/Safety Guard），2.7× 快于最强可比护栏、10× 快于微调安全分类器。
  4. 快速域适配：仅更新参考库（<10 秒构建 50 样本库），比 LoRA 微调快 61×；并分析系统提示、层选择对激活几何与分类的影响。

Key Contributions

  • Training-free guardrail reusing LLM activations: frozen LLM as feature extractor; a small bank of 50 safe + 50 unsafe prompts passed once, activations cached; cosine-distance kNN at inference — no gradient updates or fine-tuning.
  • Two variants: kNNGuard LE (Fisher-discriminant $J_\ell = B_\ell/W_\ell$ weighting of multi-layer activations, softmax → α_ℓ ensemble weights) and kNNGuard FE (adaptive confidence-based fusion of activation-space score $s_{act}$ with MiniLM sentence-embedding score $s_{emb}$).
  • Strong empirical results: average F1 87.4%, FPR 12.9%, latency 45.9 ms across six domains — competitive with/superior to fine-tuned SOTA (Llama Nemotron Topic Guard V1, Nemotron Safety Guard V2) at 2.7× lower latency than the best comparable guardrail and 10× faster than a fine-tuned safety classifier.
  • Rapid domain adaptation: updating the reference bank (<10 s for 50 samples) is 61× faster than LoRA fine-tuning; plus analysis of system-prompt and layer-selection effects on activation geometry.

Methodology

Architecture (§3). Pipeline: system-prompt-formatted prompt → frozen LLM forward pass → extract last-token activations at multiple transformer layers; in parallel, MiniLM sentence embedding.

Activation representation (§3.3, Fisher-weighted layer ensemble). For each layer ℓ, compute between-class scatter $B_\ell$ and within-class scatter $W_\ell$ over the cached bank; Fisher separability $J_\ell = B_\ell/W_\ell$; softmax-normalized ensemble weights $\alpha_\ell = \exp(J_\ell)/\sum_j \exp(J_j)$. Activation representation: $\phi_{act}(\tilde x) = \bigoplus_{\ell \in L} \alpha_\ell\, \mathrm{norm}(h^{(\ell)}(\tilde x))$ (weighted concat of ℓ2-normalized hidden activations).

Embedding representation (§3.4). $\phi_{emb}(\tilde x) = \mathrm{norm}(E(\tilde x)) \in \mathbb R^{d_e}$ via a sentence embedding model E (e.g., MiniLM).

Cached banks (§3.4). $B_{act} = \{(\phi_{act}(\tilde x_i), y_i)\}_{i=1}^N$, $B_{emb} = \{(\phi_{emb}(\tilde x_i), y_i)\}_{i=1}^N$ — computed once, cached; domain adaptation = replace/update the labeled reference examples.

kNN risk estimation (§3.5). Cosine distance $d_r(x, x_i) = 1 - \phi_r(\tilde x)^\top \phi_r(\tilde x_i)$; branch risk score $s_r(x) = \frac{1}{k_r}\sum_{i \in N_{k_r}^r(x)} y_i \in [0,1]$ for $r \in \{act, emb\}$ — proportion of unsafe/off-topic examples in the k-nearest neighborhood.

Fused ensemble (§3.6, kNNGuard FE). Fixed-alpha blending $s_\alpha(x) = \lambda s_{act}(x) + (1-\lambda) s_{emb}(x)$, or adaptive confidence-based fusion that weights whichever branch is more discriminative per domain.

Evaluation (§4). Six domains: Code Instructions, Code Outputs, Medical, Safety, Jailbreak, Prompt Injection. 50 labeled examples per class. Baselines: Embedding-kNN (lightweight similarity), fine-tuned SOTA (Llama Nemotron Topic Guard V1, Nemotron Safety Guard V2, Llama Guard 3, WildGuard). Metrics: F1, FPR, per-prompt latency, attack success rate (ASR).

Results & Highlights

  • Headline (six-domain average, 50 samples/class): kNNGuard achieves average F1 87.4%, FPR 12.9%, per-prompt latency 45.9 ms.
  • vs fine-tuned SOTA: competitive with or superior to Llama Nemotron Topic Guard V1 and Nemotron Safety Guard V2 in F1 and attack success rate — without any fine-tuning, gradient updates, or retraining.
  • Latency: 2.7× faster than the best comparable state-of-the-art guardrail and 10× faster than a fine-tuned safety classifier.
  • Domain adaptation speed: updating the labeled reference bank takes <10 seconds for a 50-sample bank — a 61× speedup vs LoRA fine-tuning — enabling on-the-fly domain adaptation.
  • Ablation insights: (i) system prompts shape activation geometry — domain-specific system prompts improve class separation; (ii) individual transformer layers contribute unequally — Fisher weighting identifies the most discriminative layers; (iii) no-system-prompt trade-offs characterized for production integration.
  • Robustness claim: because kNNGuard does not depend on a fine-tuned training distribution, it is less vulnerable to the distribution-shift evasion that plagues fine-tuned guardrails (Hackett et al. 2025 — character-level perturbations, paraphrasing, algorithmic adversarial prompts bypass popular guardrails).

Critical Assessment

  • The "competitive or superior F1" claim is the central result, but the parsed text reports only the six-domain average (F1 87.4%) without a per-domain breakdown vs each fine-tuned baseline — so it is hard to judge whether kNNGuard wins uniformly or trades wins/losses across domains (e.g., matching on Safety but underperforming on subtle Prompt Injection). The per-domain F1/ASR table is needed to interpret "competitive."
  • The 50-samples-per-class bank is the linchpin of the training-free claim: the paper does not report sensitivity to bank size (does F1 degrade gracefully at 10/20 samples, or steeply?), nor whether the 50 samples must be carefully curated or can be random — both of which materially affect the "rapid domain adaptation" selling point.
  • The Fisher-discriminant layer weighting is computed on the cached bank, so the selected "best-separating layers" are bank-dependent; if the bank is small or biased, the layer weights may overfit and generalize poorly to OOD attacks — the very threat the paper claims to address. The paper analyzes layer contribution but does not report layer-weight stability across bank resampling.
  • The 2.7× / 10× latency wins are strong but depend on the frozen LLM being already loaded (the guardrail reuses the production LLM's forward pass); in deployments where the guardrail runs as a separate service, the latency advantage may shrink.
  • Attack-success-rate (ASR) results are mentioned but not quantified in the parsed abstract-level summary — the headline F1 does not directly measure evasion robustness, which is the paper's motivating concern (Hackett et al. 2025). ASR numbers under adversarial perturbation/paraphrase attacks would tighten the "less vulnerable to distribution shift" claim.

Strengths

  • Genuinely training-free, rapidly adaptable, and reuses the production LLM's activations — operationally attractive for fast-moving threat landscapes.
  • Fisher-discriminant layer weighting is a principled, cheap way to identify the most class-discriminative layers without learning weights.
  • Adaptive activation↔embedding fusion hedges against the failure modes of either representation alone.
  • Strong latency story (2.7× / 10×) and 61× domain-adaptation speedup vs LoRA.

Limitations

  • Per-domain F1/ASR breakdown vs each fine-tuned baseline not in the parsed summary; "competitive" needs per-domain evidence.
  • Bank-size sensitivity and curation requirements unreported — affects "rapid adaptation" claim.
  • Fisher layer weights computed on small bank → potential overfit to bank; layer-weight stability across resampling unreported.
  • Latency advantage assumes the frozen LLM is co-located/shared with the guardrail.
  • ASR under adversarial perturbation/paraphrase attacks mentioned but not quantified in headline.
