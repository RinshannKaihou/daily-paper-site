Conditional Co-Ablation: Recovering Self-Repair Backups in Transformer Circuits

arXiv: 2607.01940

Date: 2026-07-03

https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01940

一句话总结： CoAx 把组件重要性从"孤立 ablation 的效应"重新定义为"primary 电路被移除后，剩余单元 ablation 效应的增长"，用这种二阶条件分数揭示 self-repair 备份头——在 GPT-2-small IOI 上把备份头恢复的 ROC-AUC 从 0.33 提到 0.91，并把同一套方法用于 attribution、capability knockout 与跨规模结构化剪枝。
TL;DR: First-order component-importance scores systematically miss transformer self-repair backups (the Hydra effect); CoAx fixes this by asking how much each unit's ablation effect grows once a primary set has been removed — a label-free, Fisher-weighted, output-grounded second-order score that lifts backup-head recovery from 0.33 to 0.91 ROC-AUC on GPT-2 IOI and transfers to attribution, capability knockout, and structured pruning at 124M-7B scale.

核心贡献

  • 把 self-repair 形式化为组件重要性的"可加性失效"：当 primary 组件被移除时，沉默的备份会接管，导致一阶分数同时低估 primary 与 backup（IOI name-mover 模块的超加性达 1.9×）。
  • 提出 conditional co-ablation (CoAx) 分数：给定 primary 集 S，度量每个剩余单元的 ablation 效应在"已移除 S"条件下相对单次 ablation 基线的增长。这是无需标签、输出接地（Fisher 加权 ablation 能量）的二阶分数。
  • 在 GPT-2-small IOI 标注电路把备份头识别 ROC-AUC 从 0.33 提到 0.91，超过包括 self-repair-aware AtP* GradDrop（0.82）在内的所有基线；反事实 patching 证明恢复出的头确实承担修复。
  • 同一无标签流程在 6 个架构家族、8 个模型上迁移到 induction 任务。
  • 下游应用闭环：(i) 修正被 self-repair 掩盖的 attribution（logit-diff 下降从 0.22 提到 1.76）；(ii) 识别 capability knockout 所需组件（0.70 准确率，匹敌 0.72 的 oracle）；(iii) 结构化头剪枝在 124M–7B 全规模上超越最佳基线。

Key Contributions

  • Formalisation: Reframes self-repair as an additivity failure of component importance; introduces conditional circuit completion — given a primary circuit, recover the components that become causal only when that circuit is removed.
  • CoAx score: A label-free, Fisher-weighted, output-grounded second-order score capturing the interaction that node-additive scores (EAP-IG, AtP*, Wanda, sparse feature circuits) discard.
  • Empirical validation: 0.33 → 0.91 ROC-AUC on the labeled GPT-2-small IOI backup heads, beating self-repair-aware gradient scores; causal patching confirms the recovered heads carry the repair.
  • Cross-architecture transfer: The same label-free procedure recovers induction-circuit backups across 8 models in 6 architecture families.
  • Downstream closure: CoAx-recovered backups fix self-repair-masked attribution, identify capability-knockout components, and improve structured head pruning from 124M to 7B.

Methodology

  • Setup: Component importance in mechanistic interpretability is usually first-order — ablate unit i alone, measure Δoutput. This is correct when importance is additive, but self-repair ("Hydra effect": McGrath 2023, Rushing & Nanda 2024) breaks additivity.
  • Definition: For a primary set S and a candidate unit u ∉ S, define CoAx(u | S) as the growth in u's ablation effect measured on the "S-ablated" model relative to u's effect on the intact model. A dormant backup has small first-order effect but large conditional effect; an irrelevant unit is small on both.
  • Energy metric: Uses Fisher-weighted ablation energy, with a single-ablation baseline plus one conditional pass per primary set — so the cost is one extra forward/ablation sweep given a known primary circuit.
  • Complement, not replacement: CoAx assumes a primary set S is already known (from manual analysis or a first-order method); it recovers the backups, it does not discover the primary circuit from scratch.
  • Validation methodology: Labeled IOI circuit for ground truth; counterfactual patching to causally confirm recovered heads; controlled-redundancy benchmark; faithfulness/completeness checks; label-free transfer to induction.

Results & Highlights

  • Backup recovery (GPT-2-small IOI): ROC-AUC 0.33 (first-order) → 0.91 (CoAx), vs 0.82 for the best self-repair-aware gradient baseline (AtP* GradDrop).
  • Attribution correction: Recovering the backups exposes a 1.76 logit-difference drop vs the 0.22 measured under first-order ablation — i.e. ~8× more of the true causal effect becomes visible.
  • Capability knockout: Adding CoAx-recovered backups to the ablated primary set drives IOI accuracy down to match the documented oracle (0.70 vs 0.72); first-order top-up overshoots, random top-up fails.
  • Structured pruning (124M–7B): At 50% sparsity CoAx beats the best competing baseline at every scale tested (full sweep in Fig. 5).
  • Cross-model transfer: Induction-circuit backup recovery works across 8 models in 6 architecture families using the same label-free score.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: Substantive. Reframing component importance as a conditional quantity is a real conceptual move, not just a new score; the connection to second-order interactions and to the additivity-failure view of self-repair is clean. Several prior lines (Hydra effect, AtP* GradDrop) gesture at this; CoAx operationalises it directly.
  • Rigor: Strong. Uses labeled ground truth (IOI backup heads), counterfactual patching for causal validation, controlled-redundancy benchmarks, and reports scaling across 6 architecture families and 124M-7B parameter counts. The headline 0.33→0.91 ROC-AUC is the right metric in the right place.
  • Reproducibility: Good — code + project page + tutorial linked. The method is one extra conditional ablation sweep on top of standard first-order pipelines, so the engineering bar is low.
  • Significance: High for mechanistic interpretability. Self-repair is increasingly recognised as a confound for circuit discovery, attribution, and pruning; a label-free, output-grounded score that exposes it cleanly is the kind of tool the field has been missing. The pruning payoff at scale (124M-7B) is a useful bridge from interp to capability-relevant applications.
  • Red flags:
  • Requires a known primary set S. CoAx recovers backups conditional on a primary circuit; if S is wrong, the conditional score is meaningless. The paper is honest about this but it limits the method's out-of-the-box applicability.
  • The IOI circuit is small, well-studied, and GPT-2-specific. The headline 0.33→0.91 is on this one circuit; the cross-architecture induction transfer is label-free and harder to validate as cleanly.
  • Cost scales with |S|. A conditional pass per primary set is cheap for IOI-scale circuits but could become expensive for large, distributed primary sets in frontier models.
  • The 1.9× super-additivity number is cited for IOI name-movers specifically; how super-additive other circuits are is not surveyed — so it is unclear whether CoAx matters equally outside self-repair-prone circuits.
  • Pruning results at 50% sparsity are encouraging but the comparison is to a single best baseline per scale; whether the gain persists at higher sparsity or under different cost models is unverified.

Strengths

  • Clean conceptual reframing: importance is conditional, not intrinsic — captures exactly what self-repair breaks.
  • Label-free and output-grounded — no extra supervision needed, transfers across architectures.
  • Strong empirical validation: labeled ground truth + causal patching + cross-model transfer + downstream applications.
  • Genuinely useful downstream payoffs (attribution correction, capability knockout, pruning), not just a benchmark improvement.
  • Code/project page/tutorial provided.

Limitations

  • Requires a known primary set; cannot discover the full circuit from scratch.
  • Headline numbers anchored on IOI / GPT-2-small; cross-architecture transfer is label-free and so harder to grade.
  • Conditional pass cost scales with the number and size of primary sets — unclear how it behaves on large frontier-model circuits.
  • Self-repair is not universal — some circuits are nearly additive, where CoAx adds overhead with little benefit. The paper does not characterise when the gain is large.
  • Pruning comparison is at one sparsity level per model; a full sparsity sweep would be more convincing.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Can CoAx be made to discover the primary set S automatically (e.g. by iterating conditional recovery), or is it fundamentally bound to a first-order primary-finder?
  • How does the cost of the conditional pass scale on frontier-model circuits where the primary set may have hundreds of components?
  • Is there a clean predictor of which circuits are super-additive and therefore benefit most from CoAx, so practitioners know when to apply it?
  • The 0.33→0.91 IOI number is striking — what is the equivalent recovery quality on the label-free induction transfer? How much should we trust a score we cannot grade?
  • Does CoAx compose with attribution patching methods (EAP-IG, AtP) at the edge* level, not just the node level, to expose self-repair in edge circuits?
  • For pruning: does the CoAx-aware importance persist at higher sparsity (70/90%) and under different pruning cost models (latency, memory)?
