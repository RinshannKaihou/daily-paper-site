> **一句话总结：** DSGNAR（Doubly-Sketched Gauss–Newton with Adaptive Ratio）用双重 sketch 的 Gauss–Newton 模型 + 自适应正则化/步长策略正面攻击 PINN 的病态损失景观，在 Burgers 方程上把相对 ℓ2 误差推到 ~10⁻¹⁴（比 SOTA 改善 5 个数量级），在高维 Poisson 上改善 8 个数量级，且单精度下 10 秒内解到 4.75×10⁻⁷。

> **TL;DR:** PINNs' accuracy bottleneck is ill-conditioning of the loss landscape; DSGNAR — a scalable doubly-sketched Gauss–Newton second-order optimiser with adaptive regularisation and step-length control — drives relative ℓ2 errors as low as 3×10⁻¹⁶ in double precision, beats the SOTA by 5 orders of magnitude on Burgers and 8 on high-dimensional Poisson, and reaches 4.75×10⁻⁷ in under 10 seconds in single precision on one H100.

## 核心贡献

- 提出 **DSGNAR**：为 PINN 的非线性最小二乘目标设计的二阶优化框架，核心是双重 sketch 的 Gauss–Newton 模型 + 一种新的"同时控制正则化与步长"的策略。
- 把 PINN 精度瓶颈明确归因于优化（损失景观严重病态，[Rathore 2024]），并正面处理之，而非依赖架构或采样技巧。
- 在非线性、混沌、多尺度、高维、Navier–Stokes 一整套 PDE 上系统超越 SOTA：
  - Burgers：相对 ℓ2 误差 ~10⁻¹⁴，比 [Kiy+25] 改善 5 个数量级。
  - 高维 Poisson：改善 8 个数量级。
  - 双精度下可达 3×10⁻¹⁶（接近机器精度）。
  - 单精度下 10 秒内 Burgers 解到 4.75×10⁻⁷。
- 对架构、算术精度、初始超参数都鲁棒。
- 提供开源代码：github.com/wephy/physics-informed-neural-networks。

## Key Contributions

- A scalable second-order optimisation framework (DSGNAR) that directly targets the ill-conditioned PINN loss landscape, instead of treating PINN accuracy as an architecture or sampling problem.
- A doubly-sketched Gauss–Newton model (randomised linear algebra) that avoids ever forming the full Gauss–Newton matrix, plus a novel joint regularisation + step-length control strategy ("Adaptive Ratio").
- Across a comprehensive PDE suite (nonlinear, chaotic, multi-scale, high-dimensional, Navier–Stokes): relative ℓ2 errors as low as 3×10⁻¹⁶ in double precision; 5 orders of magnitude improvement on Burgers; 8 orders on high-dimensional Poisson.
- Single-precision mode: Burgers to 4.75×10⁻⁷ in <10 s on a single H100.
- Robustness to architecture choice, arithmetic precision, and initial hyperparameters — an unusual and important property for PINN solvers.
- Open-source code provided.

## Methodology

- **Problem framing:** PINNs solve PDEs by minimising the residual of the governing equations sampled at collocation points (using automatic differentiation). The bottleneck identified by [Rathore 2024] and a growing consensus is the *optimiser*: standard first-order methods (Adam) are insufficient because the PINN loss landscape is severely ill-conditioned.
- **DSGNAR:** Combines:
  1. A **Gauss–Newton** approximation of the Hessian (the natural choice for nonlinear least-squares — for the PINN least-squares objective, the energy natural gradient coincides with a Gauss–Newton step).
  2. **Double sketching** (randomised linear algebra) to avoid forming the full Gauss–Newton matrix — same family of techniques as [Rathore 2024, Bes+25, Yan+22] but pushed further.
  3. **Adaptive Ratio:** a novel strategy that *jointly* controls both regularisation and step length, rather than picking one globalisation scheme (line-search / trust-region / Levenberg–Marquardt) a priori.
- The framework is matrix-free in its heavy computations and GPU-friendly; reported runs use a single NVIDIA H100.

## Results & Highlights

- **Burgers' equation:** ℓ²_rel driven to ~10⁻¹⁴ (331 iterations, 346 s on one H100), improving on the strongest prior baseline [Kiy+25] (4.04×10⁻⁵) by roughly 5 orders of magnitude.
- **High-dimensional Poisson:** ~8 orders of magnitude improvement over prior art.
- **Double-precision ceiling:** ℓ²_rel as low as 3×10⁻¹⁶ — i.e. essentially at the limit of double-precision round-off.
- **Single-precision speed:** Burgers to 4.75×10⁻⁷ in under 10 seconds.
- **Robustness:** Same framework works across architectures (SIREN, etc.), arithmetic precisions, and initial hyperparameters without bespoke tuning.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Substantial on the optimisation side. Doubly-sketched Gauss–Newton is a meaningful extension of prior sketched-GN work for PINNs, and the joint regularisation/step-length control ("Adaptive Ratio") is a real methodological contribution beyond picking line-search vs trust-region vs LM. The framing — that PINN accuracy is fundamentally an optimisation-conditioning problem — is the right one and aligns with current consensus.
- **Rigor:** Strong. Comprehensive PDE benchmark suite (nonlinear, chaotic, multi-scale, high-dimensional, Navier–Stokes), explicit comparison to prior SOTA (Table 1) with orders-of-magnitude improvements, machine-precision-level error floors that are themselves evidence the optimiser is well-behaved, and an Oxford optimisation group behind the theory.
- **Reproducibility:** Good. Open-source code provided; runs on a single H100, so the engineering bar to replicate is realistic.
- **Significance:** High if the gains hold up. PINNs have struggled to rival classical solvers on precision; this paper claims to close that gap by 5–8 orders of magnitude on canonical benchmarks — exactly the kind of result that could move PINNs from "promising" to "competitive" for production scientific computing.
- **Red flags:**
  - **Order-of-magnitude gains invite scrutiny.** A 5-order improvement on Burgers and 8 on Poisson over a 2025 SOTA is extraordinary; the comparison baseline ([Kiy+25], Adam→quasi-Newton hybrid) is strong but readers should verify the metric definitions and reference solutions match exactly.
  - **Ill-conditioning is not the only PINN failure mode.** Spectral bias, collocation-point pathologies, and stiff dynamics all matter; DSGNAR addresses the optimisation-conditioning axis but does not claim to solve the others.
  - **All single-H100.** Scaling behaviour on larger clusters or for very high-dimensional problems is not characterised in the front matter.
  - **Second-order cost.** Gauss–Newton with sketching is still more expensive per iteration than Adam; the wall-clock wins are driven by reaching machine precision in few iterations, but for *lower-accuracy* regimes (say 10⁻³) first-order methods may remain competitive and the paper does not characterise the crossover.
  - **Robustness claim is qualitative** in the front matter — the actual breadth of architectures / precisions / hyperparameters tested is in the appendix.

## Strengths

- Addresses the right bottleneck (ill-conditioning) with the right tool (second-order, Gauss–Newton) rather than architectural tweaks.
- Orders-of-magnitude improvements on canonical PDE benchmarks, including machine-precision-level error floors in double precision.
- Single-precision 10-second Burgers solve is a striking practical result.
- Robustness across architecture / precision / hyperparameters — genuinely useful for practitioners.
- Oxford optimisation group + open-source code = strong credibility and reproducibility signals.

## Limitations

- The headline gains are so large that they warrant independent replication, especially the 8-order Poisson improvement.
- Per-iteration cost of second-order + sketching is higher than Adam; the regime where DSGNAR dominates may be moderate-to-high accuracy, not low-accuracy screening.
- PINN failure modes beyond ill-conditioning (spectral bias, sampling pathologies, stiff dynamics) are not addressed.
- Larger-scale / distributed behaviour not characterised in the front matter.
- "Adaptive Ratio" is the methodological core but its precise rule and convergence theory (if any) live in the body — readers should verify it is not just a well-tuned heuristic.

## Relevance

高度匹配用户对「数值稳定性 / 病态条件 / 数学基础」的兴趣——本文正面处理优化层面的病态条件（ill-conditioning），并追求接近机器精度的解，是"误差可控 + 数学基础扎实"范式的代表；其双重 sketch 与自适应正则化思路对理解大规模非线性最小二乘的数值可靠性也具有借鉴意义。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Are the 5- and 8-order-of-magnitude gains robust to independent reimplementations, or do they depend on details of the reference solution / metric computation?
- At what accuracy threshold does DSGNAR's wall-clock advantage over first-order methods actually appear? Where is the Adam regime still competitive?
- Can the "Adaptive Ratio" rule be given a convergence-rate guarantee, or is it an empirically tuned heuristic?
- How does DSGNAR interact with PINN failure modes it does not target — spectral bias, collocation sampling, stiff dynamics — when combined with the relevant countermeasures?
- Does the framework extend to inverse / data-assimilation PINN problems where the loss includes data-fit terms, not just residuals?
- What is the scaling behaviour on distributed multi-GPU clusters for very high-dimensional PDEs, where the sketch size and Jacobian communication could become the bottleneck?
