> **一句话总结：** 本文构建一个医生撰写的高难度临床推理评测（5 个跨四专科场景、184 条原子化加权 MECE 评分标准，每任务 25–62 条），在统一保守单轮 harness 下评测 GPT 5.4 / Claude Opus 4.7 / Gemini 3.1 Pro；平均通过率 Claude 0.47 / GPT 0.39 / Gemini 0.37，核心发现是"临床优先级倒置"——critical 权重-5 标准仅 32.4–41.7% 通过、低风险权重-1 标准 80–90% 通过，108 条 critical 标准中 56 条（52%）无任何模型满足。

> **TL;DR:** A deliberately hard, clinician-authored clinical reasoning evaluation (5 scenarios across anaesthesia / internal-family medicine / emergency medicine / obstetrics; 184 atomic weighted MECE rubric criteria, 25–62 per task) evaluated three frontier models (GPT 5.4, Claude Opus 4.7, Gemini 3.1 Pro) under an identical conservative single-turn harness. Mean rubric pass rates were **0.47 (Claude), 0.39 (GPT), 0.37 (Gemini)**. The central finding is an **inversion of clinical priority**: the highest-weighted (weight-5, critical) criteria passed at only **32.4–41.7%**, while low-stakes weight-1 criteria passed at **80–90%**. **56 of 108 critical (weight-5) criteria (52%) were satisfied by no model**. Three LLM autoraters reproduced expert met/not-met labels on **92.8–94.7% of 552 graded criteria**. Positioned as a methods-and-preliminary-findings contribution toward a scalable large-scale clinical benchmark.

## 核心贡献

1. **医生撰写的评测方法学**：执业临床医生撰写有机叙事场景（HIPAA 合成患者数据）+ 起草金标准答案 → 构造原子化、加权（1–5）、MECE 的 rubric 标准。
2. **受控多模型对比**：GPT 5.4 / Claude Opus 4.7 / Gemini 3.1 Pro 在统一保守单轮 harness 下评测，按 OpenAI 第三方评测框架术语报告（claim type、harness、contamination、refusals、reward hacking）。
3. **权重×类别失败分析**：揭示"临床优先级倒置"——模型通过琐碎标准、失败关键标准；识别 108 条 critical 标准中 56 条（52%）无任何前沿模型满足。
4. **autorater 校准**：三个 LLM 评分员在 552 条评分标准上复现专家 met/not-met 标签 92.8–94.7%。
5. **可扩展流水线**：5 任务演示了一个可辩护、可规模化的大规模临床基准雏形。

## Key Contributions

- **Clinician-authored evaluation methodology**: practising clinicians write organic narrative scenarios with HIPAA-compliant synthetic patient data and draft a golden answer, from which atomic, weighted (1–5), MECE rubric criteria are constructed.
- **Controlled multi-model comparison**: GPT 5.4, Claude Opus 4.7, Gemini 3.1 Pro under an identical conservative single-turn harness, reported in OpenAI third-party-evaluation framework terminology (claim type, harness, contamination, refusals, reward hacking).
- **Weight- and category-resolved failure analysis**: surfaces an inversion of clinical priority — models pass trivial criteria and fail critical ones; 56 of 108 weight-5 critical criteria (52%) satisfied by no frontier model.
- **Autorater calibration**: three LLM graders reproduce expert met/not-met labels on 92.8–94.7% of 552 graded criteria.
- **Scalable pipeline**: five tasks demonstrate a defensible pipeline ready to develop into a large-scale benchmark.

## Methodology

**Dataset construction (§3.1).** Five clinician-authored scenarios (Table 1), each by a senior front-line decision-maker in their specialty, using HIPAA-compliant synthetic patient data. Tasks span four specialties: (1) Anaesthetist — intraoperative cardiac arrest during prone neurosurgery (62 criteria); (2) Family/Internal Medicine — overnight digoxin toxicity on decompensated heart-failure admission (25); (3) Family/Emergency Medicine — rural ED "triple whammy" (NSAID+diuretic+ARB) AKI with salicylate toxicity masquerading as pneumonia/sepsis (25); (4) Emergency Physician — ruptured abdominal aortic aneurysm at a district hospital with no vascular surgery; time-critical transfer decision (30); (5) Obstetric Registrar — pre-eclampsia with severe asthma and twin pregnancy; labetalol relative-contraindication decision (42). **184 criteria total.** Each prompt demands multi-step chained reasoning under contradictory anchors (e.g., admission diagnosis contradicted by later biomarker trend) and a clinically meaningful output (ranked differential, structured table, SOAP plan, time-bound commitments).

**Rubric schema (§3.2).** Each criterion = atomic, MECE assertion about the ideal response, carrying: criterion text; guideline source(s); justification; **weight 1 (trivial) → 5 (critical, clinical-impact-weighted)**; category tag(s) — Reasoning, Safety, Extraction, Instruction Following, Style. Derived from the clinician-drafted golden answer.

**Controlled harness (§3.3).** Identical conservative single-turn harness for all three frontier models (GPT 5.4, Claude Opus 4.7, Gemini 3.1 Pro); reported in OpenAI third-party-evaluation framework terminology (claim type, harness, contamination, refusals, reward hacking) for validity accounting.

**Autorater calibration.** Three LLM graders scored the 552 graded criteria; agreement with expert met/not-met labels reported as reproduction rate.

## Results & Highlights

- **Mean rubric pass rates:** **Claude Opus 4.7 = 0.47**, **GPT 5.4 = 0.39**, **Gemini 3.1 Pro = 0.37** — all far below ceiling, confirming open-ended clinical reasoning is unsolved even for frontier models.
- **Inversion of clinical priority (central finding):** highest-weighted **weight-5 (critical) criteria passed at only 32.4–41.7%**, while low-stakes **weight-1 criteria passed at 80–90%** — models optimize for completeness on trivial points while missing decisive high-stakes inferences.
- **Critical-criteria failure mass:** **56 of 108 weight-5 critical criteria (52%) were satisfied by no frontier model** — a concentrated, model-agnostic failure set.
- **Autorater calibration:** three LLM graders reproduced expert met/not-met labels on **92.8–94.7% of 552 graded criteria** — supporting scalable automated grading (comparable to MedHELM's LLM-jury ICC 0.47 vs clinician–clinician 0.43, and AMIE's auto-rater alignment).
- **Context (HealthBench Hard):** the result is consistent with HealthBench Hard's top score of 0.32 — open-ended clinical performance remains far from solved.

## Critical Assessment

- **The "inversion of clinical priority" is the strongest and most actionable finding**, and the 56-of-108 critical-criteria failure mass is a concrete, model-agnostic diagnostic. However, the study is **5 tasks / 184 criteria** — explicitly a preliminary methods contribution — so the inversion could be an artifact of how weight-5 criteria were drafted (clinicians may write critical criteria that are inherently harder to satisfy from a single-turn response, e.g., requiring time-bound commitments under resource constraints). The paper does not report whether the inversion persists when tasks are stratified by category (Reasoning vs Safety vs Extraction).
- **The autorater calibration (92.8–94.7%) is on met/not-met labels, not on weight-weighted scoring** — a model could reproduce binary labels but still mis-rank models if it systematically errs on high-weight criteria. The paper reports label agreement but not weight-stratified agreement, which is the more relevant metric for a weighted rubric.
- **Single-turn conservative harness is a deliberate choice** (validity accounting), but real clinical reasoning is often multi-turn (clarifying questions, accumulating evidence). ER-Reason's finding that "accuracy degrades as evidence accumulates" suggests the single-turn setting may *understate* the failure — the paper acknowledges this but does not test it.
- **Three frontier models only** (GPT 5.4, Opus 4.7, Gemini 3.1 Pro); no open-weight comparison, so the critical-criteria failure could partly reflect alignment/refusal behavior rather than pure capability (the paper mentions refusals and reward hacking in its framework but the parsed summary does not break out refusal-driven failures).
- **Inter-physician baseline is the right yardstick but not reported here**: ER-Reason's 52.6% exact-agreement and MedHELM's 0.43 clinician–clinician ICC set the relevant ceiling; without a clinician–clinician agreement baseline on these 5 tasks, the 32.4–41.7% critical pass rate is hard to interpret (is it below clinician agreement, or at it?).

## Strengths

- Clinician-authored, golden-answer-derived atomic weighted MECE rubrics — methodologically rigorous and the right expertise.
- The "inversion of clinical priority" finding is novel, concrete, and actionable — a sharper diagnostic than aggregate pass rates.
- Controlled multi-model comparison under a fixed harness with OpenAI-style validity accounting.
- Autorater calibration (92.8–94.7%) supports scalable grading; honest scoping as preliminary.

## Limitations

- 5 tasks / 184 criteria — preliminary; inversion could be an artifact of weight-5 drafting.
- Autorater calibration on binary labels, not weight-stratified; the relevant metric for weighted scoring.
- Single-turn harness may understate multi-turn failure (ER-Reason evidence-accumulation degradation).
- Three closed frontier models only; no open-weight or refusal-stratified breakdown.
- No clinician–clinician agreement baseline on these tasks (the relevant yardstick per ER-Reason/MedHELM).

## Relevance

与 **LLM Inference Evaluation**（开放式、rubric-based、权重解析的临床推理评估，autorater 校准）和 **Model System Cards & Technical Reports**（前沿模型在安全攸关临床任务上的能力边界、refusal/reward-hacking 有效性核算）两个方向直接相关；对关注医疗 AI 安全评估、rubric 基准设计、autorater 可靠性、以及临床决策优先级建模的研究者与医生具有一手参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. "临床优先级倒置"在按类别（Reasoning/Safety/Extraction）分层后是否仍然成立？还是主要由某一类（如 Safety）的 weight-5 标准驱动？
2. autorater 校准（92.8–94.7%）是二分类标签——权重分层的一致性如何？高权重标准的评分一致性是否更低？
3. 单轮 harness 是否低估了失败？多轮（澄清提问、证据累积）下 critical 通过率如何退化（参考 ER-Reason 的证据累积降解）？
4. 56 条无模型满足的 critical 标准——其中多少是 refusal/reward-hacking 驱动（模型拒答或取巧）vs 纯能力缺失？开权重模型是否不同？
5. 临床医生间一致基线（参考 ER-Reason 52.6%、MedHELM ICC 0.43）在这 5 任务上是多少？32.4–41.7% 是低于还是接近该基线？
6. 把 weight-5 标准的"难以满足性"控制后（如改为多轮可澄清），倒置是否消失？即倒置是能力问题还是任务设置问题？
7. 该 rubric 流水线扩展到大规模（数百任务、多专科）时，autorater 的权重分层一致性是否会下降？是否需要 LLM-jury + 人工审核混合？
