Kernel Renormalization in Bayesian Deep Neural Networks: the Equivalent Wishart Ansatz in the Proportional Regime

Paolo Baglioni, Christian Keup, Vincenzo Zimbardo, Rosalba Pacelli, Alessandro Vezzani, Raffaella Burioni, Pietro Rotondo — U. Modena, RWTH Aachen, U. Parma, INFN

The Equivalent Wishart Ansatz (EWA) reduces intractable Bayesian deep networks to just L scalar order parameters, yielding the first tractable non-perturbative theory for deep nonlinear networks in the proportional-width regime — quantitatively predicting training loss and generalization error where both the infinite-width NNGP kernel and first-order corrections fail.

Key Metric
α < 2.5
Quantitative predictions valid across α = P/N up to ~2.5, network depths L = 1–10, validated on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and synthetic data with 4 independent MCMC samplers (LMC, NUTS HMC, MALA, pCN). Systematic deviations of 5–8% emerge above α = 2.5 at large depth.
Why This Matters
First low-dimensional description of finite-width effects in deep nonlinear networks. Goes beyond both the infinite-width kernel limit (which fails qualitatively even at small depths) and shallow single-hidden-layer results. The reduction to L self-consistent scalar order parameters via a physically interpretable effective action opens a new analytical handle on deep Bayesian inference.
Critical Edge
The EWA is an uncontrolled approximation — no rigorous error bounds exist, and the Wishart ansatz has no proof of becoming exact in any limit. Breaks down at α > 2.5 with increasing depth due to O(L/√N) error accumulation. Restricted to Bayesian inference with squared-error loss; no established connection to SGD-trained networks.

Methodology

  1. Setup. Bayesian MLPs with L hidden layers, Gaussian priors, squared-error likelihood. Partition function via characteristic function of the output prior.
  2. EWA. Replace each layer's intractable empirical-kernel conditional with a central Wishart distribution, decoupling layers into independent Q_l ~ chi^2(N_l) variables.
  3. Large Deviation. Gartner-Ellis theorem yields LDP with rate I_q(x) = (x - 1 - ln x)/2. Varadhan's lemma converts to asymptotic free energy.
  4. Effective Action. S(q) = ∑ [q_l - ln q_l] + U(q), where U(q) is GP log-likelihood with renormalized kernel K_Q^(R) = Q · Θ^L(C).
  5. CNN Extension. Stacked EWA with omega-mapping for patch structure; matrix-valued order parameters of size N_PL × N_PL.
  6. Validation. Langevin MC, HMC NUTS, MALA, pCN on N = 100–4000, L = 1–10, α = 0.25–2.5.

Results & Highlights

Quantitative Agreement at Moderate Load

EWA tracks experimental learning curves for α < 2.5 across Erf and ReLU activations, significantly outperforming the infinite-width NNGP theory.

Non-Perturbative Capture

At α ≥ 1, EWA and NNGP curves cross as functions of depth, proving the theory captures strong finite-width effects invisible to first-order corrections.

Metastable Transient Phase

For L > 5 and α = 2.5, Bayesian sampling enters an intermediate phase persisting for millions of updates. Transient averages match EWA predictions; true equilibrium shows additional corrections.

CNN & muP Validation

Stacked EWA predicts single-hidden-layer CNN losses on MNIST. EWA also captures muP-parametrization generalization, where improvements stem mainly from variance reduction.

Strengths

  • First tractable effective theory for deep (L > 1) nonlinear Bayesian networks in the proportional regime
  • Elegant LDP + Varadhan framework yields a physically interpretable action as hierarchical GP regression
  • Extensive validation: MLP & CNN, Erf & ReLU, MNIST & CIFAR-10 & synthetic, 4 independent MCMC samplers
  • Handles both standard and mean-field (muP) parametrizations
  • Transient phase discovery is scientifically interesting and honestly reported
  • Clean separation of entropic (prior) and energetic (likelihood) contributions

Limitations

  • Uncontrolled approximation: no error bounds, O(L/√N) error accumulation, breakdown at α > 2.5
  • Restricted to Bayesian inference with squared-error loss; no classification, SGD, or other optimizers
  • Proportional regime excludes both heavily overparameterized (P << N) and underparameterized (P >> N) settings
  • CNN extension demonstrated only for shallow (single hidden layer) convolutional architectures
  • Transient phase complicates experimental validation and raises MCMC reliability concerns
  • No feature learning beyond global kernel rescaling; renormalized kernel retains prior structure
  • No treatment of residual connections, normalization layers, or attention mechanisms

Open Questions

  1. Can the EWA be refined with higher-order corrections to extend validity beyond α = 2.5 and very deep networks?
  2. Is the transient phase driven by a phase transition in the Bayesian posterior landscape?
  3. How does the O(L/√N) error accumulation relate to layer-wise integration errors in student-teacher settings?
  4. Can the framework extend to attention/transformer architectures with a well-defined large-width limit?
  5. Is there a principled bridge from Bayesian renormalized kernels to the feature learning observed under SGD?
  6. Do transient phase phenomena undermine the reliability of posterior sampling in practical Bayesian deep learning?
