Key Finding

Batch-induced token flips are extremely sparse and predictable from logit margins — enabling selective verification that restores 100% determinism at ~15-19% trigger rate.

MarginGate exploits the observation that only 0.3-1.3% of synchronous decode steps diverge in batched BF16 LLM inference, and that these divergences are reliably signaled by near-tie top-1/top-2 logit margins. By verifying only low-margin steps and repairing a single K/V column on disagreement, it cuts LLM-42's latency overhead by roughly 2x.

Chu, Zhou, Zhang — UConn / UC Davis 2026-05-28 arXiv:2605.30218
Key Metric
2.23x

Latency reduction over LLM-42 on Llama-3.1-8B. MarginGate triggers verification at only 18.56% of decode steps while restoring 100% sequence-level determinism. On Qwen2.5-14B: 1.99x reduction at 15.05% trigger rate.

Why This Matters
Production reproducibility at half the cost

Evaluation pipelines, regression test suites, and LLM-as-judge workflows all require deterministic outputs. MarginGate makes deterministic greedy decoding practical for serving stacks without requiring kernel-level changes or full-sequence recomputation.

Critical Edge
Greedy only

Does not address non-determinism under temperature > 0 or nucleus sampling — the dominant deployment regime for chat applications. DSR1-Distill-Qwen-7B needs 49.50% triggers, nearing always-on verification. Prototype uses restart-from-rollback, not native K/V overwrite.

Methodology — Phase 1: Observations

The authors compare a protected request decoded inside a batch against a deterministic batch_invariant_ops reference trajectory. Three observations emerge:

  • Sparse flips: Token divergence rates stay at 0.3-1.3% across five models and three benchmarks. Over 98.7% of synchronous decode steps are token-stable.
  • No K/V drift before flips: Cache deviations remain at a flat noise floor before the first divergence (median regression slope < 0.007/token), then jump discretely at the flip point (12-44x for K, 17-44x for V). Branch points are instantaneous, not gradual.
  • Near-tie margin exposes flips: Logit neighborhood count is ~1 at stable steps but ~2 at divergent steps for small delta, yielding 1.7-3.0x density ratios. The top-1/top-2 margin serves as a step-local, reference-free risk signal.
Methodology — Phase 2: MarginGate Design

The margin trigger computes g_t = l_(1) - l_(2) from BF16 forward-pass logits at zero overhead and fires when g_t < tau.

  • On trigger: A deterministic verifier recomputes the current token and K/V column.
  • On disagreement: MarginGate emits the verifier token and copies the verifier-produced K/V column into the cache (single-column repair).
  • Threshold calibration: tau is set via a sweep on MATH500, selecting the smallest value achieving 100% sequence-level determinism. This transfers to GSM8K, SharedGPT, and HumanEval without re-calibration.

The policy is per-request: a serving system attaches it only to requests requiring reproducibility, leaving other requests on the fast path.

Strengths

  • Clean problem framing: asks a precise question ("which steps need verification?") and answers with careful measurement.
  • Three well-supported observations (sparsity, no drift, margin signal) that are independently useful for the community.
  • Transparent Pareto frontier: full trigger-rate vs. determinism curve shown rather than cherry-picking one operating point.
  • Cross-model evaluation spanning five architectures and four datasets strengthens generality claims.
  • Per-request policy design integrates into existing serving stacks without requiring global kernel changes.

Limitations

  • Greedy decoding only: Does not address non-determinism under temperature > 0 or nucleus sampling, the dominant regime for chat/creative applications.
  • Calibration dependency: Each model requires a threshold sweep on a calibration dataset; initial calibration is non-trivial.
  • Boundary case models: DSR1-Distill-Qwen-7B needs 49.50% verification, offering limited savings over always-on.
  • Implementation gap: Prototype uses restart-from-rollback rather than in-place K/V overwrite; real serving overhead may differ.
  • Single-GPU evaluation: All measurements on one A6000. Multi-GPU/tensor-parallel scenarios not addressed.
Open Questions
  1. How does MarginGate interact with tensor parallelism, where numerical non-determinism has additional reduction-order sensitivity across devices?
  2. Can the margin threshold be adapted online (per-request or per-domain) rather than requiring an offline calibration sweep?
  3. What is the interaction between MarginGate and speculative decoding, where draft tokens introduce additional verification steps?
  4. Does the sparsity observation hold for larger models (70B+, 405B) where batch scheduling is more complex and memory pressure is higher?
  5. Could a learned predictor (rather than a fixed margin threshold) improve trigger precision and further reduce verifier invocations?
