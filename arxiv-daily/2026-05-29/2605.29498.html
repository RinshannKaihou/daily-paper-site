Mask the Target: A Plug-and-Play Regularizer Against LoRA Forgetting

Removing the ground-truth token from both distributions, renormalizing, and applying KL divergence only over non-target tokens preserves the base model's relative preferences — without opposing the cross-entropy adaptation signal.

Runze Xu · Arpit Garg · Hemanth Saratchandran · Simon Lucey 2026-05-28 cs.CL · cs.CV
Key Metric
0 inference overhead

The regularizer operates purely at the loss level during training. At inference time there is no additional cost — no extra parameters, no architectural modifications, no adapter redesign.

Why This Matters
No replay data. No architecture changes. Drop-in compatible.

LoRA catastrophic forgetting is a real production bottleneck. This method slots into any existing LoRA training pipeline, works across LoRA variants and model families, and needs zero replay buffers or auxiliary models.

Critical Edge
Best under large distribution shifts; does not eliminate forgetting.

The approach improves the learning-forgetting frontier but does not erase forgetting. Gains are most pronounced when adaptation data diverges substantially from pretraining distributions — benefit for small shifts remains unclear.

Methodology

Three-step procedure applied at the loss level during LoRA fine-tuning:

  1. Mask the target token — remove the ground-truth token from both the base model's and adapted model's output distributions.
  2. Renormalize — renormalize the remaining probabilities over the non-target vocabulary so both distributions sum to 1.
  3. KL regularization — apply KL divergence regularization only over non-target tokens, preserving the base model's relative preferences among alternatives.

This avoids directly opposing the cross-entropy signal needed for adaptation while preventing the model from forgetting prior knowledge.

Results & Highlights

  • Across all LoRA variants tested and various backbones, the method improves the frontier between new learning and forgetting.
  • Particularly effective when the adaptation distribution differs substantially from the base model's original training or alignment distributions.
  • Theoretical motivation: preserves base model preferences without opposing the adaptation signal.
  • Highly reproducible — operates purely at the loss level with no special infrastructure requirements.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Extremely simple and practical: no replay data, no architecture changes, no inference overhead.
  • Theoretically motivated: preserves base model preferences without opposing adaptation signal.
  • Broadly applicable across LoRA variants and model architectures.
  • Addresses a genuinely important practical problem in LLM deployment.

Limitations

  • Benefits most pronounced under large distribution shifts — unclear benefit for small shifts.
  • Does not eliminate forgetting entirely; only improves the learning-forgetting frontier.
  • Potential negative interactions with other regularization techniques are not discussed.
  • Full experimental details pending (abstract-only summary).
