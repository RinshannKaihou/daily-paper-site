Demystifying Data Organization for Enhanced LLM Training

The first systematic study of data ordering as a first-class lever for LLM training quality. Four orthogonal guidelines, composed into two strategies (STR and SAW), reuse pre-computed sample scores at near-zero additional cost.

02

Four Guidelines

G1 Boundary Sharpening

Start with low-score (easy) data to stabilize early optimization; end with high-score (complex) data to maximize final performance. Implemented as Segment Ordering (SEG): partition sorted data into L segments by percentile, shuffle within each, then concatenate.

G2 Cyclic Scheduling

Periodically revisit the full score spectrum during one-pass training to prevent catastrophic forgetting. Folding Ordering (FO) distributes samples into L cycles via strided partitioning. Each cycle maintains low-to-high order but spans the full score range.

G3 Curriculum Continuity

Maintain smooth transitions to prevent optimizer shocks. Zig-zag Ordering (ZIG) reverses every odd-indexed cycle from FO, creating a triangle-wave pattern where adjacent cycles connect at similar score levels.

G4 Local Diversity

Preserve heterogeneity within local sections to prevent gradient collapse. Jittering Ordering (JIT) partitions sorted data into buckets of size w and shuffles within each, maintaining global trend while restoring gradient noise.

03

Cross-Guidance Strategies

STR — Stair Ordering

G1 G2 G4

Partitions sorted data into K sections with stable and transition regions. Stable regions retain monotonic order (G1); transition regions apply FO for knowledge review (G2); optional JIT for local diversity (G4). Creates a staircase score-index pattern.

SAW — Saw Ordering

G1 G2 G3 G4

Extends STR by replacing FO with ZIG in transition regions, ensuring smoother attribute transitions via triangle-wave cycling. Integrates all four guidelines into a unified sawtooth pattern.

05

Assessment

Strengths

  • Systematic decomposition into four orthogonal, well-motivated guidelines
  • Near-zero additional cost by reusing pre-computed scores from selection
  • Comprehensive coverage: pre-training + SFT, multiple domains, multiple scales
  • Strong mechanistic analysis: PPL trajectories, gradient norms, weight perturbation
  • Clean separation of data organization vs. data selection as distinct problems
  • Open-sourced under Microsoft's official GitHub organization

Limitations

  • Pre-training experiments limited to 1.7B params / 50B tokens; larger claims are extrapolated
  • STR and SAW perform comparably, questioning SAW's added complexity
  • Effectiveness depends heavily on quality and relevance of pre-computed scores
  • Performance sensitive to hyperparameters (L, w, rho, K); best configs reported
  • Not tested on multimodal, non-English, or multi-epoch regimes
  • Interactions with data mixing ratios, LR schedules, and batch sizes unexplored
06

Open Questions

  1. How do data organization strategies interact with online data mixing and dynamic batch composition?
  2. Can the guidelines adapt for multimodal training where scoring dimensions are more complex?
  3. How does ordering interact with distributed training (data/pipeline parallelism) where sharding may disrupt order?
  4. Would adaptive methods adjusting hyperparameters during training outperform static configurations?
  5. How do these methods perform with scoring functions beyond educational quality (perplexity-based, diversity-based)?
  6. Can data organization principles combine with data augmentation or synthetic data generation for further gains?
