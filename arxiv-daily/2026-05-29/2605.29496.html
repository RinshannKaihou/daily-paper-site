Research Paper

On Asymmetric Optimization of Reasoning and Perception in Vision-Language Model Post-Training

Xueqing Wu, Yu-Chi Lin, Kai-Wei Chang, Nanyun Peng — UCLA
Post-training (SFT and RL) improves reasoning far more than perception in VLMs. In SFT, chain-of-thought supervision contains too few perception tokens. In RL, outcome rewards correlate more with reasoning than perception. Targeted fixes close the gap with up to +18.2 points end-to-end.
Key Metric
+18.2 pts
End-to-end performance gain from dynamic loss reweighting in SFT, balancing perception and reasoning token contributions.
Why This Matters
Perception-reasoning asymmetry is a critical bottleneck for end-to-end visual reasoning. Fixing it could reshape how VLM post-training is done across the field.
Critical Edge
Generalization risk
Synthetic tasks may overestimate real-world gains. Generalization to frontier models (GPT-4V, Gemini) is not established.
Methodology

Diagnostic Framework

Two synthetic tasks cleanly separate perception (identifying visual attributes) from reasoning (logical operations on those attributes), enabling precise measurement of how post-training affects each component independently and jointly.

SFT & RL Interventions

SFT: Token-level loss analysis reveals perception tokens are underrepresented in CoT. Dynamic reweighting increases their gradient signal.

RL: Outcome-based rewards couple more strongly with reasoning. Perception-aware auxiliary rewards decouple the signal. Even surrogate rewards (no ground truth) provide gains.

✓ Strengths

Clean experimental design with controlled synthetic tasks that isolate perception from reasoning.
Identifies distinct mechanisms for SFT and RL rather than a one-size-fits-all explanation.
Practical, lightweight interventions (loss reweighting, surrogate rewards) that are easy to adopt.
Surrogate reward result (+3.2 pts without ground truth) is immediately practically useful.

✗ Limitations

Synthetic tasks may not reflect the complexity of real-world visual reasoning scenarios.
Unclear how findings scale to frontier models (likely uses smaller open-source VLMs).
Perception and reasoning are measured as separable modules, but in practice they are deeply intertwined.
Reweighting and reward interventions introduce hyperparameters that may require tuning per task.
Open Questions
  1. How do the interventions perform on established real-world VLM benchmarks like MMMU or MathVista?
  2. Can the diagnostic framework extend to study asymmetries in other capability pairs (e.g., instruction following vs. factual knowledge)?
  3. What is the interaction between perception-reasoning balance and model scale — does the asymmetry worsen or improve with larger models?
  4. How sensitive are results to the specific synthetic task design?
  5. Can surrogate rewards combine with self-supervised or self-critique methods to further reduce ground-truth dependence?
