> **一句话总结：** 本文将 Vendi Score 和神经缩放定律统一到矩阵谱函数（matrix spectral functions）这一子模目标框架下，通过基于 secular equation 的加速方法使直接优化在 ImageNet-1K 规模数据集上可行（约 35,000 倍加速），并发现 facility location 目标在预测训练子集下游性能方面表现最佳，而 Vendi Score 在高分区间会失去预测力。

> **TL;DR:** Unifies the Vendi Score and neural scaling laws under a broader class of submodular "matrix spectral functions," introduces a secular-equation-based optimization trick achieving ~35,000x speedup for greedy subset selection, and empirically finds that facility location best predicts downstream performance while the Vendi Score degrades as a proxy at high values. Surprisingly, random fixed-size subsets show remarkably concentrated performance, meaning dataset value is not solely determined by size or class balance.

## 核心贡献

- 将 Vendi Score 和常见神经缩放定律目标统一为**矩阵谱函数**（matrix spectral functions），证明其均为子模函数（submodular），DPP 目标也是其特例。
- 引入**弱矩阵单调函数**（weakly matrix monotone functions）概念，由此导出一大类弱子模的矩阵谱函数，为数据评估提供丰富的目标族。
- 提出基于 **secular equation** 的增量更新方法，避免重复特征分解，在 m 维嵌入上将边际增益评估加速 O(m) 倍，实证约 35,000 倍加速。
- 在 ImageNet-1K 规模上首次实现 Vendi Score 的直接贪心优化，并系统比较多种数据评估目标（Vendi Score、DPP、facility location、三种新矩阵谱变体）。
- 发现 **facility location** 在多种设置下预测下游性能最优；Vendi Score 在适度范围内有效，但推向更高值时反而成为差的性能代理（"proxy degradation"现象）。
- 发现随机固定大小子集（无论是否类别平衡）在评估分数和下游性能上分布非常集中，即使控制规模和类别平衡，性能仍可从好到坏平滑变化。

## Key Contributions

- Unifies Vendi Score and neural scaling law objectives as submodular **matrix spectral functions**, a broad class encompassing DPPs, log-determinant, and many other objectives. Proves that the Vendi Score with any non-decreasing concave function applied to eigenvalues is submodular (Theorem 3.6).
- Introduces **weakly matrix monotone functions** that yield weakly submodular matrix spectral functions, providing a rich family of practical data appraisal objectives with provable approximation guarantees for greedy optimization.
- Develops **secular-equation-based incremental updates** that avoid repeated eigendecompositions during greedy optimization, reducing marginal-gain evaluation cost by an O(m) factor -- empirically yielding ~35,000x average speedup on ImageNet-1K with ResNet-50 embeddings (m=2048).
- Enables direct Vendi Score optimization on **ImageNet-1K-scale** datasets for the first time, overcoming the prohibitive O(n * m^2) per-step cost of naive greedy with eigendecomposition.
- Conducts systematic comparison of data appraisal objectives (Vendi Score, DPPs, facility location, three new matrix spectral variants: MSF-1/f, MSF-1/sqrt, MSF-tanh) across fixed-size, class-balanced, and fixed training-budget regimes on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet-1K.
- Finds **facility location** consistently predicts downstream held-out performance best across all regimes; Vendi Score is predictive over moderate ranges but degrades at high objective values.
- Reveals that **random fixed-size subsets** are remarkably concentrated in both appraisal scores and performance, and that dataset value varies smoothly even when controlling for size and class balance.

## Methodology

**Theoretical Framework:** The paper defines a matrix spectral function as F(V) = sum_i f(lambda_i) where lambda_i are eigenvalues of the kernel matrix K_V = Phi_V * Phi_V^T formed from the embeddings of subset V. The key theoretical results are:

1. **Submodularity of Vendi Score (Theorem 3.6):** The Vendi Score VS_q(V) = exp(H_q(lambda)) where H_q is the Tsallis-q entropy is shown to be a matrix spectral function with f(x) = -x log x (for q=1, Shannon/quantum entropy) or f(x) = x^q (for general q). The paper proves that when f is concave and non-decreasing, F is submodular.

2. **Weakly Matrix Monotone Functions (Section 3.2):** A scalar function g is weakly matrix monotone of order n if for any two positive semidefinite matrices A <= B (Loewner order), g(A) <= g(B). The paper shows that if g is weakly matrix monotone of order k (the rank of the kernel matrix), then F(V) = tr(g(K_V)) is weakly submodular with a provable approximation guarantee for greedy optimization (Theorem 3.13). This yields a submodularity ratio gamma = lambda_k(g) / lambda_1(g).

3. **Connection to Scaling Laws:** The paper shows that neural scaling law objectives of the form L(S) = alpha * |S|^(-beta) are also submodular when viewed as set functions, connecting data size-based scaling laws to the matrix spectral framework.

**Secular Equation Speedup (Section 4):** The critical bottleneck in greedy optimization of matrix spectral functions is computing marginal gains F(V union {s}) - F(V) for each candidate s. Naively this requires an eigendecomposition of K_{V union {s}} per candidate. The paper's insight is that adding a rank-1 update to the kernel matrix (K_{V union {s}} = K_V + phi_s * phi_s^T) changes the eigenvalues according to the **secular equation**:

  sum_j (u_j^T * phi_s)^2 / (lambda_j - delta) = 1

where u_j are eigenvectors of K_V and delta is the new eigenvalue shift. Finding the roots of this secular equation for each candidate is O(m) instead of O(m^2) for full eigendecomposition, and since there are n candidates per greedy step, the total saving is O(n * m) per step. With m=2048 (ResNet-50 embeddings) and n=~1M (ImageNet-1K), this yields the ~35,000x empirical speedup.

**New Matrix Spectral Variants:** The paper introduces three new objectives:
- MSF-1/f(x) = 1/x: Uses g(t) = -1/t, yielding high submodularity ratio
- MSF-1/sqrt(x) = 1/sqrt(x): Uses g(t) = -1/sqrt(t)
- MSF-tanh: Uses g(t) = tanh(t), a bounded function

**Experimental Setup:** The paper evaluates on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet-1K using ResNet-50 embeddings. For each dataset, they select subsets of varying sizes (1%, 5%, 10%, 20%, 50%, 80%) under three regimes: (1) fixed-size unconstrained, (2) class-balanced, and (3) fixed training budget (where subsets of different quality but same size are compared under the same number of training epochs). They train ResNet-18/50 models on selected subsets and evaluate on held-out test sets.

## Results & Highlights

- **Secular equation speedup:** Achieves **~35,000x average speedup** on ImageNet-1K with m=2048 embeddings. The method reduces per-step time from hours to seconds, making greedy Vendi Score optimization practical for the first time at this scale.

- **Facility location wins:** Across all three datasets and all three regimes, **facility location** consistently produces subsets with the best downstream test performance. This is notable because facility location is a classical submodular function that operates on pairwise similarities rather than spectral properties.

- **Vendi Score proxy degradation:** The Vendi Score shows a concerning pattern -- it correlates well with downstream performance over moderate score ranges, but aggressively maximizing it (via greedy optimization) leads to subsets where high Vendi Score values correspond to *worse* test performance. This "proxy degradation" effect means the Vendi Score should not be blindly maximized as a training data selection criterion.

- **Random subset concentration:** Perhaps the most surprising finding: uniformly random fixed-size subsets (both unconstrained and class-balanced) show remarkably tight concentration in both appraisal scores and downstream test accuracy. The standard deviation of test accuracy across random subsets is much smaller than the range spanned by optimized subsets.

- **Smooth performance spectrum:** Even when controlling for subset size, class balance, and training budget, downstream performance varies smoothly from good to bad depending on *which* examples are selected. This demonstrates that dataset value is not determined by size alone but by the specific content of the selected data.

- **DPP performance:** Determinantal point processes (DPPs, i.e., log-determinant objectives) perform moderately well but do not match facility location. They tend to promote diversity but may select examples that are diverse yet uninformative.

- **New MSF variants:** The three new matrix spectral function variants (MSF-1/f, MSF-1/sqrt, MSF-tanh) show mixed results, generally performing between DPP and Vendi Score but not surpassing facility location.

- **Class-balanced regime:** In the class-balanced setting (equal number of examples per class), the gaps between methods narrow but the ranking is preserved -- facility location still wins.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** High. The unification of Vendi Score, DPPs, and scaling law objectives under matrix spectral functions is a clean and original theoretical contribution. The secular-equation speedup is an elegant application of classical numerical linear algebra to a modern ML problem. The weakly matrix monotone function framework with provable submodularity ratios is new.

- **Rigor:** Very strong. Formal proofs of submodularity (Theorem 3.6), weak submodularity with approximation guarantees (Theorem 3.13), and the secular equation derivation are all rigorous. The empirical evaluation is thorough, covering three datasets, three regimes, seven objectives, and multiple subset sizes.

- **Reproducibility:** Good. The secular equation approach is well-described with algorithmic pseudocode. The embedding extraction (ResNet-50 pretrained on ImageNet) and training setup (ResNet-18/50 with standard hyperparameters) are standard. The main barrier to exact reproduction is the computational cost of training many subsets.

- **Significance:** The finding that facility location outperforms the Vendi Score is practically important for the data selection community. The secular equation speedup is a general-purpose tool that benefits any matrix spectral objective. The proxy degradation observation for Vendi Score is a valuable cautionary finding.

- **Red flags / concerns:**
  - The 35,000x speedup is impressive but somewhat misleading -- it represents the ratio on ImageNet-1K with high-dimensional (m=2048) embeddings. The speedup scales as O(m), so for smaller embedding dimensions the benefit is proportionally less.
  - Facility location itself requires a similarity matrix and is not trivially scalable to very large datasets without additional approximation.
  - The paper evaluates only image classification. The relative rankings of objectives could differ significantly for language modeling, detection, or generative tasks.
  - The "concentration of random subsets" finding, while interesting, raises the question of whether the practical gains from sophisticated subset selection are worth the engineering effort in many real-world scenarios. The paper does not provide cost-benefit analysis.
  - The connection to scaling laws (Section 3.3) is somewhat superficial -- it shows that size-based scaling law objectives are submodular, but does not integrate the two perspectives (spectral diversity vs. scale) into a single actionable framework.

## Strengths

- Clean theoretical unification of multiple data appraisal objectives (Vendi Score, DPP, log-det, facility location) under a single matrix spectral function framework with provable properties.
- The secular equation speedup is both elegant and practically impactful, enabling previously infeasible experiments at ImageNet-1K scale.
- Intellectual honesty in reporting that the Vendi Score (the motivating objective for much prior work) is *not* the best performer and suffers from proxy degradation.
- Systematic empirical evaluation across multiple datasets, regimes, subset sizes, and objectives -- one of the most comprehensive comparisons in the data selection literature.
- The concentration result for random subsets and the smooth performance spectrum are novel empirical observations with practical implications.
- The weakly matrix monotone function framework provides a principled way to design new data selection objectives with approximation guarantees.

## Limitations

- Evaluated only on image classification (CIFAR-10/100, ImageNet-1K) -- generalization to NLP, detection, segmentation, or generative modeling is untested.
- Facility location's superior performance lacks a deep theoretical explanation -- the paper notes it empirically but does not fully characterize *why* pairwise coverage beats spectral diversity.
- The secular equation speedup assumes rank-1 kernel updates (one element added at a time); it does not directly extend to lazy greedy or stochastic greedy variants that add mini-batches.
- The paper does not address the computational cost of computing the initial embedding matrix (requiring a forward pass through a pretrained model on the full dataset), which can itself be expensive.
- The practical significance of the performance gaps between methods, while consistent, may be modest in some settings (e.g., a few percentage points of accuracy difference).
- No comparison with gradient-based data selection methods (e.g., influence functions, TracIn, GLISTER) which are a major alternative paradigm.

## Relevance

本文对数据选择和子模优化提供了统一的理论视角，对关注高效数据筛选、训练集构建的研究者有直接参考价值。特别是 facility location 相较 Vendi Score 的优越性以及随机子集性能集中的发现，对实际数据筛选策略有重要指导意义。Secular equation 加速方法对任何需要优化矩阵谱函数的场景都有应用潜力。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Why does facility location outperform spectrally-motivated objectives like the Vendi Score? Is there a theoretical explanation rooted in the relationship between pairwise coverage and generalization?
- How do these results transfer to language model pretraining data selection, where data is far more heterogeneous, sequential, and not easily embedded into a fixed-dimensional space?
- Can the secular-equation speedup be combined with stochastic greedy methods (e.g., the stochastic greedy algorithm of Mirzasoleiman et al.) for further scalability to datasets with billions of examples?
- What explains the concentration of random subsets -- is this an artifact of the specific datasets (CIFAR/ImageNet with natural image statistics) or a more general phenomenon? Does it hold for long-tailed or highly imbalanced distributions?
- How sensitive are the results to the choice of embedding space? The paper uses ResNet-50 features -- would CLIP embeddings, self-supervised features, or random projections change the relative rankings of objectives?
- Can the proxy degradation of the Vendi Score be predicted or detected during optimization, providing a principled stopping criterion?
- How do gradient-based data selection methods (influence functions, TracIn) compare in the same experimental framework, particularly in the fixed-budget regime?
