Nine Judges, Two Effective Votes: Correlated Errors Undermine LLM Evaluation Panels

Guneet Kohli — arXiv 2605.29800

A panel of 9 frontier LLMs from 7 model families yields only ~2 effective independent votes because the models make correlated errors on the same items. The best single judge matches or outperforms the full panel. Neither adding judges nor smarter aggregation closes the gap.

Key Metric
2.0–2.5
effective votes out of 9 judges (neff)
~75% of nominal independence is lost to correlated errors. Condorcet gap: 8–22 pp below independent-voting predictions (p < 10-4).
Why This Matters
Adding more LLM judges or using smarter aggregation (Dawid-Skene, weighted voting) cannot fix fundamentally correlated evaluation. The scaling curve asymptotes at neff ≈ 2.56. The bottleneck is structural — shared errors — not algorithmic. This directly challenges the "more judges = better" assumption underlying most multi-model evaluation pipelines.
Critical Edge
Results are restricted to NLI and pairwise preference tasks; generalizability to open-ended generation or code evaluation is untested. The "best single judge beats the panel" finding is post-hoc — you need ground truth to know which judge is best, which is exactly what evaluation panels are meant to replace. NLI's structured 3-class label space may inflate correlation.
⚙

Methodology

  • Datasets: ChaosNLI (100 annotator labels/item) — MNLI (1,599 items), SNLI (1,514 items), AlphaNLI (1,532 items). 1,000 items per dataset via entropy-stratified sampling.
  • Judge panel: 9 frontier LLMs from 7 model families (Anthropic, Google, OpenAI, Meta, DeepSeek, Mistral, Alibaba). Temperature 0.0 with standardized NLI prompt.
  • Kish neff: Binary error vectors per judge → 9×9 pairwise φ correlation matrix → neff = k² / Σφij. Measures truly independent votes in the panel.
  • Condorcet null model: Per-judge confusion matrices simulate majority-vote accuracy if judges voted independently (preserving individual accuracy, removing correlation).
  • Aggregation tested: Majority vote, Dawid-Skene EM, accuracy-weighted voting, φ-optimal (Markowitz portfolio-style) weighting.
  • Robustness: 3 prompt variants, temperature 0.0/1.0, chain-of-thought, RewardBench pairwise preference. Split-half validation, bootstrap CIs, permutation tests.

  • Best single judge (Claude Sonnet 4.5) matches or beats the full panel across all conditions.
  • Removing 6 of 9 judges actually improves accuracy in leave-one-out analysis.
  • 51 all-wrong items on MNLI: 70.6% are high-entropy; over-prediction of contradiction accounts for 51% of failures.
  • Distributional misalignment (TV distance) correlates strongly with human entropy (ρs = 0.434, p < 10-46).
✓

Strengths

  • Principled framework: Two complementary metrics — Kish neff for information content, Condorcet gap for accuracy impact.
  • Exhaustive robustness: 3 datasets, 3 prompt variants, 2 temperatures, CoT, RewardBench, split-half validation (overfitting ratios near 1.0).
  • Scaling curve analysis quantifies exactly how neff saturates, providing actionable guidance for panel design.
  • Definitive negative result on aggregation with oracle access, ruling out the most obvious objection cleanly.
  • Excellent positioning: Clearly distinguishes from PoLL, connects to Condorcet theory, acknowledges complementary theoretical work.
✗

Limitations

  • Task scope: Restricted to NLI and pairwise preference; generalizability to open-ended generation, code, or dialogue is untested.
  • Post-hoc benchmark: "Best single judge" finding requires ground truth for selection, which is what panels aim to replace.
  • No interventions tested: Does not propose methods for achieving genuine independence (adversarial training, structured disagreement).
  • Label space effect: NLI's 3-class structure may inflate correlation vs. open-ended evaluation where disagreement has more degrees of freedom.
  • Single author: Independent replication by a separate team would strengthen confidence.

Open Questions

  1. Can adversarial training or structured disagreement protocols (e.g., requiring judges to argue opposing positions before voting) produce genuinely independent LLM judges?
  2. Is correlation driven by shared pre-training data, similar RLHF objectives, or fundamental task-structure properties — and does the answer differ by evaluation domain?
  3. How does neff change when judges are drawn from fundamentally different architectures (LLMs + reward models + human annotators + rule-based systems)?
  4. Could item-level correlation prediction enable adaptive evaluation — panels on low-correlation items, fallback to humans on high-correlation items?
  5. What is the theoretical minimum φ achievable with current LLM architectures, and what would it take to reach neff ≥ 5?
  6. Does this generalize to generative evaluation where the label space is not discrete?
