Gradient Perturbation: Learning to Perturb Gradients for Adaptive Training

SAM, gradient clipping, and noise injection are not separate tricks — they are all specific instances of gradient perturbation. Unifying them reveals a new lever: adaptively perturbing class-level logit gradients to boost or suppress learning per category.

Author Hua Li Date 2026-05-29 arXiv 2605.29494 Aliases LPG · Learning to Perturb Gradients
Key Metric
Consistent gains

LPG outperforms existing methods across balanced classification, long-tail classification, and noisy-label learning benchmarks. Also composable as a plug-in module on top of other optimizers.

Why This Matters

Category-aware gradient perturbation lets you differentially amplify or suppress learning per class — a direct handle on the hardest problems in real-world data: class imbalance and label noise.

Critical Edge

Single-author paper with very broad claims spanning theory, method, and multi-task evaluation. The conjecture equating gradient norm amplification to positive augmentation lacks rigorous empirical proof. Computational overhead of LPG is unreported.

Methodology

The paper builds a unified gradient perturbation framework reinterpreting existing techniques: Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) perturbs gradients toward flatter minima, gradient clipping constrains magnitude, and noise injection adds stochastic perturbations. Drawing on the Logit Perturbation Learning (LPL) analogy, the authors propose that amplifying gradient norms for a class enhances learning (positive augmentation) while dampening gradient norms suppresses overfitting (negative augmentation). LPG adaptively learns to perturb logit-level gradients at the class level, enabling category-aware optimization. PAC-Bayesian analysis connects the perturbation magnitude to generalization bounds.

Results & Highlights

  • LPG consistently outperforms baselines on balanced, long-tail, and noisy-label benchmarks
  • Functions as a plug-in module — composable with SAM and other existing methods for additive gains
  • PAC-Bayesian generalization bounds provide theoretical grounding for the perturbation framework

Strengths

  • Elegant unification of disparate optimization techniques under one lens
  • Category-aware training via gradient perturbation is a genuinely novel angle
  • Theoretical grounding via PAC-Bayesian bounds
  • Broad empirical evaluation across three distinct task types
  • Plug-in compatibility with existing methods

Limitations

  • Single authorship may limit depth of evaluation and analysis
  • Gradient norm ↔ augmentation conjecture needs more formal justification
  • Relationship to forward-chain perturbation (feature, logit, label) underexplored
  • Computational overhead of LPG is not discussed
