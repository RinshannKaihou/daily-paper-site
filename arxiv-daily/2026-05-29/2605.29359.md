> **一句话总结：** 本文通过构建分布式训练模拟器，量化证明分布式训练可在低于所有现行或拟议监管阈值的硬件条件下训练出超越GPT-4、GPT-5规模的前沿模型（分别仅需$91M和$2.3B硬件），并提出芯片追踪、举报机制、内存阈值等组合应对措施。

> **TL;DR:** This paper builds a simulator to show that distributed training (using DiLoCo algorithms over consumer-grade internet) can produce models exceeding every existing or proposed compute governance threshold while staying below every hardware monitoring threshold. Training equivalent to GPT-4 costs ~$91M and GPT-5 costs ~$2.3B in distributed hardware. The author proposes a layered defense combining chip registries with location verification, whistleblower programs, memory-based cluster thresholds (1,280 GB HBM), and challenge inspections to close this governance loophole.

## 核心贡献

- 构建并开源了分布式训练模拟器，可量化评估不同硬件、网络和DiLoCo配置下的逃逸可行性，区分原始本地等价算力(C_local)和质量调整算力(C_quality)
- 证明在100 Mbps消费级互联网、每节点仅16 H100等价算力的严格限制下，分布式训练仍可突破所有现行/拟议监管阈值：10^24 FLOP仅需$1.6M，EU AI Act的10^25 FLOP仅需$31M，SB 53的10^26 FLOP仅需$3.8B
- 提出内存阈值（1,280 GB HBM）作为对现有纯算力阈值的关键补充，使逃逸成本增加约50%
- 系统评估了6类对策的有效性，推荐芯片追踪+举报机制+内存阈值+挑战性检查的组合方案

## Key Contributions

- Builds and open-sources a distributed training simulator that quantifies evasion feasibility under configurable hardware, network, and DiLoCo settings, distinguishing raw local-equivalent compute (C_local) from quality-adjusted compute (C_quality)
- Demonstrates that under strict constraints (100 Mbps consumer internet, 16 H100-equivalent per node limit from Scher et al. 2025), distributed training can breach all existing/proposed governance thresholds: 10^24 FLOP for $1.6M (Scher threshold), 10^25 FLOP for $31M (EU AI Act), 10^26 FLOP for $3.8B (SB 53)
- Proposes adding a memory threshold of 1,280 GB HBM to cluster definitions (matching 16 H100s), which forces evaders into inefficient regimes and raises costs by ~50%
- Systematically evaluates 6 countermeasures, recommending a combination of chip registries with location verification, whistleblower programs, memory-based thresholds, and challenge inspections
- Calibrated against real distributed training results including Covenant-72B (150x compression with negligible quality loss)

## Methodology

The paper develops a parameterized simulator modeling distributed training efficiency. The core efficiency model decomposes the distributed training inefficiency factor as:

**eta = eta_H x eta_comp x eta_rep x eta_act**

where:
- **eta_H** (sync inefficiency): penalty from inner steps between synchronizations (~2-10% loss)
- **eta_comp** (compression quality): accounts for gradient compression artifacts (150x compression yields eta_comp = 0.99, calibrated to Covenant-72B)
- **eta_rep** (replica divergence): the dominant factor, ranging 0.15-0.90, as gradients from different replicas diverge; scales inversely with model size
- **eta_act** (activation quality): penalty from compressing activations in pipeline-parallel mode

Quality-adjusted compute C_quality = C_local x chi, where chi penalizes models that are over- or under-trained relative to Chinchilla optimality.

The adversarial model assumes: (1) consumer-grade 100 Mbps/100ms latency internet, (2) max 16 H100-equivalents per node (the Scher et al. 2025 threshold), (3) a 740-day training time limit (based on Sevilla et al. 2022's model of growth rates under restrictive governance), (4) an active governing body monitoring traffic, power, satellite imagery, and conducting inspections. The simulator is calibrated against published ML experiments and validated against Covenant-72B results.

## Results & Highlights

**Table 1 (key results) -- minimum-cost distributed configurations:**

| Target | Nodes | Mode | Model | eta | C_quality | Cost |
|--------|-------|------|-------|-----|-----------|------|
| 10^24 FLOP (Scher) | 2 | Flat | 91B | 0.80 | 1.3x10^24 | $1.6M |
| DeepSeek-V3 (3.3x10^24) | 7 | Flat | 160B | 0.60 | 3.3x10^24 | $6.3M |
| 10^25 FLOP (EU AI Act) | 34 | Flat | 160B | 0.37 | 6.4x10^24 | $31M |
| GPT-4 (2.1x10^25) | 101 | Hierarchical | 160B | 0.26 | 7.8x10^24 | $91M |
| Llama 3.1-405B (3.8x10^25) | 625 | Hierarchical | 250B | 0.15 | 1.2x10^25 | $441M |
| GPT-5 (6.6x10^25) | 2,880 | Pipeline-Parallel | 180B | 0.42 | 1.9x10^25 | $2.3B |
| 10^26 FLOP (SB 53) | 4,706 | Pipeline-Parallel | 180B | 0.39 | 2.1x10^25 | $3.8B |

- At 100 Mbps, distributed training costs ~3x more than centralized, but this is not a substantial feasibility barrier
- The A100 80GB is the optimal evader hardware due to its high memory-to-compute ratio (4,000 GB HBM per sub-threshold node vs. 1,280 GB for 16 H100s)
- Adding a 1,280 GB HBM memory threshold reduces max per-node model from 240B to ~91B parameters, forces ~5x more nodes, and raises cost by ~50%
- Bandwidth caps and ISP traffic monitoring are assessed as ineffective countermeasures
- Chip tracking with location verification and whistleblower programs are the most effective measures

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: High. While the general concern about distributed training evading governance has been raised informally, this is the first paper to systematically quantify the feasibility with a calibrated simulator and evaluate specific countermeasures against specific regulatory proposals.
- **Rigor**: Strong. The simulator is calibrated against real distributed training experiments (Covenant-72B achieving 150x compression validated after initial development). The author explicitly identifies three modeling choices that are "generous to the evader" and discusses their impact. The efficiency model is well-decomposed with interpretable factors.
- **Reproducibility**: Excellent. The simulator is open-source with an interactive web interface. All parameter values and scaling law calibrations are documented.
- **Significance**: Very high. The paper directly challenges the practical foundations of major governance proposals (EU AI Act, California SB 53, Scher et al. 2025). If its conclusions hold, compute governance needs fundamental redesign.
- **Red flags**: Single-author paper, though with feedback from key governance researchers. The quality-adjusted compute metric (C_quality) is sometimes well below C_local (e.g., 0.21 chi for 10^26 FLOP), meaning the "equivalent" model quality is substantially lower than a centralized run at the same raw FLOP. The paper could be clearer about whether these models would actually match frontier capabilities or merely exceed FLOP thresholds. Some results involve very high overtraining ratios (51x for 10^26 FLOP), whose practical implications for model quality need further investigation. The assumption of sufficient high-quality training data (tens of trillions of unique tokens) is acknowledged as potentially generous to the evader.

## Strengths

- First systematic quantitative analysis of distributed training evasion with a calibrated, open-source simulator
- Validates against real-world distributed training results (Covenant-72B)
- Concrete, actionable policy recommendations with cost estimates for each countermeasure
- Transparent about assumptions and their direction of bias (three pro-evader assumptions explicitly noted)
- Directly engages with specific regulatory proposals rather than abstract concerns

## Limitations

- Very high overtraining ratios (up to 51x) at large scales mean the quality-adjusted compute is 4-5x lower than raw FLOP, raising questions about whether these models would actually achieve frontier-level capabilities
- Assumes evaders have access to tens of trillions of unique high-quality tokens, which may not be realistic
- Countermeasures like chip location verification require hardware that does not exist on current chips and cannot be retrofitted
- Single-author paper; benefit from broader peer review from both ML systems and policy communities
- Does not fully address the operational complexity of coordinating thousands of geographically distributed nodes reliably over months/years

本文与AI治理和前沿AI安全研究高度相关，首次定量揭示了分布式训练技术对算力治理框架的根本性挑战，并提出了可操作的应对方案。对关注AI监管政策、算力治理和前沿模型安全的研究者和政策制定者具有重要参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How would the economics change if frontier-scale distributed training were attempted today with current (2026) bandwidth and hardware?
- Can advanced traffic analysis or ML-based anomaly detection identify DiLoCo gradient synchronization patterns even through VPNs?
- What is the minimum viable governance framework that remains robust to both centralized and distributed training evasion?
- How quickly are distributed training efficiency improvements (e.g., better compression, heterogeneous hardware support) closing the gap with centralized training?
- Would a memory threshold of 1,280 GB HBM remain effective as chip memory capacities increase in future hardware generations?
