> **一句话总结：** MiraBench提出了一个分层基准，用于评估机器人世界模型的动作条件可靠性，揭示了当前模型虽然视觉上逼真但普遍存在乐观偏差——即在失败动作条件下仍预测成功结果，且扩大模型规模并不能解决这一问题。

> **TL;DR:** MiraBench is a hierarchical benchmark that evaluates whether robotic world models faithfully predict outcomes conditioned on actions, rather than just looking visually plausible. It decomposes reliability into three levels (Physics Adherence, Action-Following Fidelity, Optimism Bias Detection) across 12 model configurations. Key finding: current models systematically hallucinate successful outcomes under failure-inducing actions, and neither scaling up nor success-only post-training fixes this problem.

## 核心贡献

- **提出"动作条件可靠性"（action-conditioned reliability）作为评估目标**，将世界模型的仿真器保真度与通用视觉质量明确区分开来。
- **定义并量化了"乐观偏差"（optimism bias）**——世界模型在接收本应导致失败的动作时，仍然预测出成功结果的系统性失败模式。
- **构建了分层评估框架MiraBench**，包含三个递进层级：物理一致性（Physics Adherence）、动作跟随保真度（Action-Following Fidelity）和乐观偏差检测（Optimism Bias Detection）。
- **发布了包含906个生成视频和16,704条结构化人工标注决策的标注语料库**，为评估器提供了人类基准的监督信号。
- **对12种代表性世界模型配置进行了广泛评估**，涵盖向量条件和文本条件模型、开源和闭源系统（NVIDIA、阿里巴巴、快手等），揭示了视觉保真度与动作保真度之间的系统性脱节。

## Key Contributions

- Introduces **action-conditioned reliability** as a distinct evaluation target for robotic world models, separating simulator faithfulness from generic visual fidelity.
- **Formalizes Optimism Bias** as a measurable failure mode where world models hallucinate successful outcomes under failure-inducing actions, a problem caused by success-dominated training data.
- Presents **MiraBench**, a hierarchical benchmark with three progressively demanding levels: Physics Adherence (reference-free physical consistency), Action-Following Fidelity (whether predictions respect action inputs), and Optimism Bias Detection (whether models preserve failure outcomes).
- Releases a **human annotation corpus with 906 videos and 16,704 structured decisions** across four evaluation modules, providing per-indicator supervision for VLM-based evaluators that achieve over 85% agreement with human judgments.
- Conducts a **broad evaluation of 12 model configurations** spanning vector-conditioned and text-conditioned world models, revealing that visual fidelity is a poor proxy for action fidelity, scaling does not reliably improve failure preservation, and optimism bias is pervasive.

## Methodology

MiraBench is built from manipulation episodes in the GR-1 humanoid corpus and the Lingchu bimanual dataset, covering contact-rich tasks such as pick-and-place, pouring, folding, bimanual handover, sliding, and free-fall interactions.

**Three-level evaluation hierarchy:**

1. **Level 1 -- Physics Adherence**: Evaluates whether generated rollouts are physically meaningful. Comprises two sub-modules: (a) *Physical Consistency* measures whether manipulated objects maintain coherent shape, material, size, and identity through occlusion using 16 indicators; (b) *Physics Law Compliance* uses SAM2.1 trajectory extraction and kinematic checks (uniformly-accelerated motion, impact behavior, post-contact drift, bounce decay) plus a VLM quality term. This level is entirely reference-free.

2. **Level 2 -- Action-Following Fidelity**: Tests whether physically plausible rollouts reflect the conditioning action. Evaluates three dimensions: task completion, target-object grounding, and motion quality. Includes cross-embodiment settings spanning GR-1, Unitree G1, DROID, and bimanual manipulation.

3. **Level 3 -- Optimism Bias Detection**: Constructs paired counterfactual inputs by perturbing nominal successful actions into six failure-inducing perturbation families (insufficient grip force, premature release, carry slip, contact oscillation, wrist tilt, approach overshoot) while keeping the visual scene fixed. Measures whether the model predicts the expected failure or overwrites it with a hallucinated success.

**Evaluator design**: Human annotations on representative model outputs serve as supervision for frontier VLM judges (multi-frame voting for stability). Evaluators achieve >85% agreement with human annotations and outperform Gemini-2.5-pro by 30%.

## Results & Highlights

- **Visual fidelity does not predict action fidelity**: Models producing visually plausible videos (e.g., DreamDojo-14B with high visual quality scores of 2.83) still exhibit substantial optimism bias (15%) and poor failure preservation.
- **Scaling up does not fix the problem**: Within the DreamDojo family, scaling from 2B to 14B improved some physical consistency metrics but failure preservation *dropped* from 48.7% to 12.8%. Cosmos-GR1 showed similar non-monotonic behavior.
- **Success-only post-training is counterproductive**: GR1 post-training dramatically improved nominal action-following (task completion from 14.0 to 92.0) while failure preservation *fell* from 48.7 to 23.1.
- **Optimism bias is pervasive**: Even the best-performing models show significant optimism bias. Happy Horse achieves 93.3% physical consistency but still shows 35.7% optimism bias. DreamDojo-14B shows 93.1% physical plausibility yet 15% optimism bias.
- **No model dominates all levels**: WAN 2.1 achieves the strongest failure-preservation score (87.2%) but is not uniformly strongest in physics or nominal action-following. Happy Horse excels at physics and action-following but leaves a gap in failure preservation (56.4%).
- **Closed-source vs. open-weight**: Kling 3.0 Omni achieves 100% occlusion consistency and task completion but only 35.9% bias resistance. Text-conditioned models sometimes show stronger failure preservation than vector-conditioned ones.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: High. The formalization of "optimism bias" as a measurable failure mode in robotic world models is novel and important. The hierarchical decomposition of action-conditioned reliability into three levels is well-motivated and goes beyond existing benchmarks (WorldSimBench, WorldArena) that primarily measure visual quality. The finding that success-only post-training *worsens* failure preservation is striking and counterintuitive.

- **Rigor**: Strong. The benchmark includes proper mathematical formalization (Equations 1-3), a motivating pilot study with controlled human annotation, cross-embodiment evaluation, and validated VLM evaluators with >85% human agreement. The perturbation taxonomy is grounded in common manipulation failures.

- **Reproducibility**: Moderate-to-good. The benchmark uses publicly available models (DreamDojo, Cosmos, Wan series) and describes the evaluation pipeline in detail. However, closed-source models (Happy Horse, WanX, Kling) cannot be independently reproduced, and the human annotation protocol details are in appendices. The VLM evaluator prompts are described but not explicitly released yet.

- **Significance**: High. This work challenges the prevailing assumption that improving visual fidelity in world models leads to better simulators for robot learning. The optimism bias finding has direct implications for anyone using world models as data generators for policy training -- if the model always predicts success, the downstream policy cannot learn from failure.

- **Potential concerns**: (1) The benchmark currently focuses on tabletop manipulation with short-horizon dynamics; generalization to navigation, locomotion, or long-horizon tasks is untested. (2) The perturbation taxonomy, while well-motivated, covers six families and may not be exhaustive. (3) The reliance on VLM judges introduces its own potential biases, though the human validation partially mitigates this.

## Strengths

- Clearly identifies and formalizes a real, previously underappreciated problem (optimism bias in world models).
- Hierarchical evaluation design is diagnostic -- it can pinpoint whether a model fails at physical coherence, action sensitivity, or success-biased prediction.
- Strong empirical evidence across 12 diverse model configurations, covering multiple scales, conditioning paradigms, and access types.
- Human-grounded evaluator construction with substantial annotation corpus.
- The finding that scaling up and success-only post-training can *worsen* failure preservation has important practical implications for the field.

## Limitations

- Restricted to tabletop manipulation with short-horizon contact dynamics; does not cover navigation, locomotion, deformable objects, or long-horizon planning.
- The perturbation taxonomy targets representative failures but is not exhaustive; it may miss failure modes in more complex or unstructured environments.
- VLM-based evaluators, while validated against human annotations, may inherit biases from their training data and may not generalize to new failure modes.
- The benchmark evaluates model outputs but does not directly measure the downstream impact on policy learning (i.e., how much optimism bias in a world model degrades a policy trained on its rollouts).
- Closed-source models cannot be independently evaluated by other researchers.

## Relevance

本文对机器人世界模型的评估提出了系统性方法，对从事具身智能和世界模型研究的学者具有直接参考价值。特别是乐观偏差的发现对于使用世界模型作为仿真器生成训练数据的研究者具有重要警示意义——模型可能在高保真视觉生成的同时，系统性地忽略失败场景。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does optimism bias in world models quantitatively affect downstream policy performance when policies are trained on generated rollouts?
- Can contrastive training objectives that explicitly pair nominal and failure-inducing actions mitigate optimism bias?
- How does the benchmark generalize to non-manipulation domains (navigation, locomotion, deformable-object interaction)?
- Would incorporating failure demonstrations into training data at different ratios produce a reliable improvement in failure preservation?
- Are the VLM evaluators robust to adversarial or distribution-shifted inputs, and could they be exploited to inflate scores?
- What is the relationship between training data diversity (beyond success/failure ratio) and action-conditioned reliability?
