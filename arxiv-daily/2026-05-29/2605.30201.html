HPO: Hysteretic Policy Optimization for Stable and Efficient Training under Sparse-Reward Regime

GRPO's negative-advantage samples dominate updates under sparse binary rewards, suppressing rare successes. HPO fixes this with a single advantage-estimator change: down-weight negative updates by alpha and normalize by batch-mean length.

Mohamed Sana, Nicola Piovesan, Antonio De Domenico, Fadhel Ayed (Huawei Paris Research Center) · Haozhe Zhang (Huawei Technologies, China) · 2026-05-28

Key Metric

A-HPO also surpasses GSPO (0.75) by 11% and SAPO (0.79) by 5%, with comparable response length (686 vs 729 tokens). Gains are largest in early, hard, and small-model regimes.

Why This Matters

A single-line change to GRPO's advantage estimator consistently improves LLM reasoning training wherever rewards are sparse and binary.

GRPO is the standard RL method for training reasoning LLMs (DeepSeek-Math, etc.). Sparse binary rewards are the default for math and code tasks. A drop-in fix with negligible overhead has high adoption potential.

Critical Edge

TeleLogs is proprietary. Independent verification on this benchmark is impossible. Benefits vanish on saturated/easy regimes where GRPO matches or slightly exceeds A-HPO.

No evaluation on standard reasoning benchmarks (MATH-500, LiveCodeBench, GPQA). Countdown is a simple arithmetic task. The 32B scale test starts from a strong SFT checkpoint (49% pass@1), not truly sparse.

Methodology

Failure Mode Analysis

Under sparse binary rewards with success probability p << 1/2, GRPO's group-centered advantages produce far more negative than positive samples. The ratio p-/p+ grows as success probability decreases, skewing updates toward suppressing the rare successes. Per-response length normalization compounds this: short positive responses dominate, long negative ones are weakly penalized.

HPO (3 modifications to GRPO)

  • Remove standard deviation from advantage: Â_i = R_i - mean(R)
  • Replace per-response length normalization with batch-mean length normalization using |τ̄|
  • Asymmetric hysteretic weighting: w = 1 for positive advantage, w = α for negative

A-HPO Adaptive Rule

Sets α = clip(p̂+ / (p̂- + ε), α_min, 1) from batch-level sign frequencies. Strong hysteresis when rewards are sparse, automatic relaxation as the policy improves. Acknowledged as a lightweight heuristic, not an unbiased gradient-norm estimator.

Key Observations

  • Positive-only limit (α=0) degenerates to 16-token outputs at 0.71 reward -- gains are not from trivial shortening
  • Batch-size ablation: even at batch size 1 (8 rollouts), all HPO variants outperform GRPO
  • 32B feasibility: HPO lifts pass@1 from 49.45% to 95.86% on TeleLogs (SFT+RL)
  • Contribution balance diagnostic shows A-HPO progressively corrects negative-dominance

Assessment

Strengths

  • Precisely characterizes a mathematical failure mode rather than proposing a generic fix
  • Minimal modification: one parameter to existing GRPO pipelines, negligible overhead
  • A-HPO removes hyperparameter tuning entirely via the adaptive rule
  • Thorough ablation design: N-HPO isolates normalization, V-HPO tests alternative adaptive schemes
  • Honest framing: "a narrow but common failure mode," transparent about heuristic limitations
  • Strong empirical gains exactly where claimed (sparse, early, hard, small-model regimes)

Limitations

  • Scope limited to sparse binary verifiable rewards; no dense-reward (RLHF) evaluation
  • TeleLogs is proprietary Huawei data; independent verification not possible
  • Countdown is simple arithmetic; no MATH-500, LiveCodeBench, or GPQA evaluation
  • On saturated/easy settings, A-HPO provides no benefit or slightly underperforms GRPO
  • No comparison to other sparse-reward strategies (reward shaping, curriculum learning)
  • 32B evaluation starts from strong SFT checkpoint (49%); does not test sparse-regime claim at scale

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does A-HPO compose with other GRPO improvements like DAPO or Dr. GRPO?
  2. Does the sign-frequency approximation hold for dense reward settings, or break down when gradient magnitudes vary across signs?
  3. How does A-HPO perform on standard benchmarks (MATH-500, LiveCodeBench, GPQA Diamond) where tasks are more complex?
  4. Could the adaptive rule be improved by incorporating gradient magnitude info (EMA of per-sign loss magnitudes) rather than pure sign counts?
  5. Does A-HPO's benefit diminish as rollout count N increases, since more rollouts naturally reduce sign imbalance?
  6. Is the optimal alpha (~0.6 on TeleLogs) transferable across domains, or does it vary significantly with task structure?
