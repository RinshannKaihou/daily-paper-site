The Complexity of Verifying Feedforward Neural Networks in Quantised Settings

Eric Alsmann, Martin Lange, Marco Sälzer — Univ. Kassel & RPTU Kaiserslautern-Landau

Quantising neural networks does not increase the asymptotic complexity of verification under linear-program specifications — it remains NP-complete, matching idealized real-valued networks. The jump to PSPACE (and beyond) occurs only when dynamic quantisation is combined with bit-vector specifications.

Key Metric
PSPACE-complete
Dynamically quantised FNNs verified against bit-vector specs under fixed-point arithmetic (Theorem 9). NP-complete for all other combinations with LP specs.
Why This Matters
No asymptotic overhead for practical verification
Existing NP-based solvers can be adapted to quantised settings without complexity penalties — quantisation is not a verification barrier for standard LP constraints.
Critical Edge
Purely theoretical — no solver, no experiments
Results limited to feedforward ReLU networks. The NEXPTIME upper bound for general floating-point BV verification may be loose (only PSPACE-hardness is known below it).

Methodology

Automata-Theoretic Proof Strategy

  • Three FNN classes formalized with precise arithmetic semantics: rational, quantised (fixed finite-width), dynamically quantised (rational network evaluated under finite-width arithmetic).
  • Two specification formalisms: LP (linear constraints) and BV (bit-vector logic with non-linear expressiveness).
  • NP-membership via witness guessing (polynomial-size input justified by LP solution bounds) with polynomial-time verification.
  • NP-hardness via 3SAT reduction using small ReLU gadgets with parameters exactly representable in any non-trivial arithmetic.
  • PSPACE upper bounds via succinct NFA construction: encode FNN behavior and BV formula as succinctly represented automata, reduce to non-emptiness of intersection.
Results

Complete Complexity Taxonomy

  • LP specs never push complexity beyond NP, regardless of quantisation regime — all three FNN classes yield NP-complete verification.
  • BV specs on already-quantised networks: still NP-complete (Thm 7).
  • BV specs with dynamic quantisation: PSPACE-complete for fixed-point and bounded-exponent floating-point (Thm 9), NEXPTIME for general floating-point (Prop 14).
  • Corrections: Henzinger et al. (2021) showed only PSPACE-hardness (not completeness) and used a richer specification language, not standard LP.
  • Open gap: whether REACH(F, BV) under general floating-point hits the NEXPTIME upper bound or collapses to PSPACE.
Strengths
  • Clean separation of arithmetic model from specification language yields an interpretable complexity landscape.
  • Succinct NFA technique is elegant and potentially reusable beyond this verification setting.
  • Practical take-away for tool developers: no asymptotic penalty for quantised LP verification.
  • Honest, thorough prior-work discussion including correction of community misconceptions.
  • Identifies concrete open problems and clear directions for future work.
Limitations
  • No empirical validation, prototype solver, or discussion of constant factors.
  • NEXPTIME upper bound for general floating-point may be loose (PSPACE-hard below it).
  • Restricted to feedforward ReLU networks; CNNs, RNNs, and transformers not addressed.
  • Hardness gadgets assume parameters exactly representable (1, 0, 1/2) — may not cover all practical activations.
  • No discussion of approximate or incomplete verification methods used in practice.

Open Questions for Further Exploration

Q1 Can the NEXPTIME upper bound for REACH(F, BV) under general floating-point be matched with a lower bound, or does the problem collapse to PSPACE?
Q2 Does the succinct NFA construction scale well enough to serve as the basis for a practical prototype verification tool?
Q3 Do the complexity results extend to quantised CNNs, where weight-sharing structure might alter the automata construction?
Q4 For LP specifications, do quantised FNNs admit more efficient average-case algorithms despite matching worst-case NP-completeness?
Q5 Can the BV specification framework combine with incomplete methods (e.g., abstract interpretation) for practical sound-but-incomplete quantised-network tools?
