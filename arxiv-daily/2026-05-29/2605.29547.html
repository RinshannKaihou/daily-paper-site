Singularity-aware Optimization via Randomized Geometric Probing

S-Adam detects non-smooth singularities in loss landscapes by measuring variance of randomized directional derivatives, then adaptively damps its step size — eliminating gradient chattering while converging to Clarke stationary points at optimal rate.

Ruoran Xu, Borong She, Xiaobo Jin, Qiufeng Wang — Xi'an Jiaotong-Liverpool University   arXiv:2605.29547
Key Metric

Why This Matters
Eliminates training instability in quantized and small-batch deep learning without Hessian computation. The LGI metric probes local geometry cheaply and the optimizer degrades gracefully to standard Adam in smooth regions — zero overhead when not needed.
Critical Edge
k extra forward passes per step add real cost at scale. Gains narrow sharply at larger batch sizes (BS=64), and evaluation is limited to image classification with ResNet-18 — no LLM, ViT, or NLP experiments yet.
Methodology

From Gradient Chattering to Geometric Braking

Momentum-based optimizers oscillate near non-smooth singularities where the Clarke subdifferential has large diameter, causing conflicting signals to accumulate in the momentum buffer. S-Adam addresses this at the geometry level.

eta_eff = eta · exp(-λ · ρt)
ρt = Var[∇f(xt + εui)]   i=1..k
1
Sample k random perturbation directions u1...uk
2
Compute directional derivatives via k additional forward passes
3
Estimate LGI as variance of these directional derivatives
4
Damp step size: high LGI → slow down; ρ→0 → standard Adam

Convergence Proof

  1. Cast S-Adam as stochastic approximation of a differential inclusion with drift F(x) ∈ -α(x)H(x)
  2. Show affine interpolation is an asymptotic pseudotrajectory via Benaim (2006) conditions
  3. Establish f as strict Lyapunov function: df/dt ≤ -αmin · c1 · dist(0, ∂Cf(x))2
  4. Apply invariance principle → limit sets contained in Clarke stationary set → O(1/ε2) optimal rate
Results & Highlights

QAT: Quantization-Aware Training

S-Adam (k=8) maintains stable loss ~3.4–3.6 on CIFAR-100 while Prox-SGD rebounds to ~4.6 with large oscillations. AdamW degrades after epoch 3.

Learning Rate Sensitivity

S-Adam wins at every tested learning rate (1e-4, 5e-4, 1e-3), improving by +4.02, +8.28, and +4.99 points respectively. Best S-Adam run (70.87% at lr=1e-4) exceeds best AdamW run (66.85%).

Key Observation

S-Adam's advantage is largest in small-batch, high-instability regimes and narrows as geometry smooths with larger batches — consistent with damping driven by local geometry, not gradient noise magnitude.

Strengths
  • Clean theoretical framework connecting Clarke subdifferential geometry to practical optimizer design
  • Optimal O(1/√T) convergence rate under clearly stated assumptions
  • LGI metric requires no Hessian — scalable by design
  • Graceful degradation to standard Adam in smooth regions (ρ→0 → α→1)
  • Comprehensive ablations: batch size, learning rate, k-probe tradeoff
  • Consistent improvements across all tested configurations
  • Paradigm shift: randomized smoothing as diagnostic probe, not gradient surrogate
Limitations
  • Computational overhead: k additional forward passes per step (k=2 fast/low-fidelity; k=8 accurate but ~2× forward cost)
  • Evaluated only on image classification with ResNet-18 and a custom CNN — no ViT, LLM, or NLP experiments
  • QAT CIFAR-100 absolute accuracy ~18.6% suggests an extreme regime where all optimizers struggle
  • Advantage narrows significantly at larger batch sizes (BS=64), limiting standard-regime applicability
  • No comparison with recent optimizers (Adafactor, Lion, Sophia) beyond AdamW and Prox-SGD
  • Additional hyperparameters (k, λ) add tuning complexity
  • No wall-clock comparison normalized to equivalent compute budget
Open Questions
How does k-forward-pass overhead scale with billion-parameter transformers? Can LGI be computed every N steps to amortize cost?
Can the LGI metric use cheap gradient approximations or parameter subsets instead of full forward passes?
Does S-Adam benefit LLM training where ReLU/GELU activations and quantization create non-smooth landscapes?
Could LGI serve as a generalization predictor, analogous to sharpness-based measures like SAM?
How does S-Adam interact with gradient accumulation, mixed precision, and distributed data parallel training?
Is the advantage over AdamW from the damping mechanism itself, or does LGI-guided step size exceed what simple gradient clipping or LR warmup could achieve?
