> **一句话总结：** 本文系统研究了数据组织（排序策略）对LLM训练效率的影响，提出了四条关键指导原则（边界锐化、循环调度、课程连续性、局部多样性），并设计了STR和SAW两种跨指导策略，复用已有评分实现几乎零额外开销，在160M-1.7B模型、1B-50B token、预训练和SFT阶段均有效提升训练稳定性和下游性能，已发表于ACL 2026主会。

> **TL;DR:** This paper presents the first systematic study of data organization (ordering strategy) for LLM training, formalizing four guidelines: Boundary Sharpening (start easy, end hard), Cyclic Scheduling (periodically revisit easy data), Curriculum Continuity (smooth score transitions), and Local Diversity (shuffle within windows). Two cross-guidance methods, STR (G1+G2+G4) and SAW (all four), reuse pre-computed sample-level scores from data selection at near-zero cost. On Mistral 160M-1.7B trained with 1B-50B tokens from FineWeb-Edu, both methods consistently outperform random ordering and vanilla curriculum learning across 8+ benchmarks. SFT experiments on DeepMath-103K and OpenCodeInstruct with Qwen3-1.7B confirm gains in math and code generation. Chinchilla scaling law extrapolation suggests benefits persist to GPT-3 175B / Llama 3.1 405B scale. Accepted to ACL 2026 Main Conference.

## Key Contributions

- First systematic study of data organization for LLM training, clearly separating it from data selection and formalizing the problem as a permutation-based ordering task
- Four formalized guidelines with concrete algorithmic implementations: SEG (G1), FO (G2), ZIG (G3), JIT (G4), each independently validated through ablation
- Two cross-guidance methods: STR (combines Boundary Sharpening + Cyclic Scheduling + Local Diversity) and SAW (adds Curriculum Continuity via zig-zag replacement of folding in transition regions)
- Reuses pre-computed sample-level scores from data efficiency pipelines (e.g., FineWeb-Edu educational quality scores) at near-zero additional computational cost
- Comprehensive experiments spanning model scales from 160M to 1.7B parameters, data sizes from 1B to 50B tokens, both pre-training and SFT stages
- Chinchilla scaling law extrapolation showing persistent gains up to 405B parameter scale
- Accepted to ACL 2026 Main Conference; code open-sourced at github.com/microsoft/data-efficacy

## Methodology

### Problem Formulation

The paper formalizes the LLM data pipeline in three stages:
1. **Data Scoring**: A scoring function g maps dataset D to a score vector gamma (quality, difficulty, etc.)
2. **Data Selection**: A selection function f_s filters D to a subset D_sub (changes scale, not order)
3. **Data Organization**: An ordering function f_o applies a permutation pi derived from gamma to reorder data (changes order, not scale)

The key insight: use the same score vector gamma for both selection and organization, extracting maximum value from pre-computed scores.

### Four Guidelines and Implementations

**G1: Boundary Sharpening** -- Controls data characteristics at training start and end. Start with low-score (easy) data to stabilize early optimization; end with high-score (complex) data to maximize final performance. Implementation: Segment Ordering (SEG) partitions sorted data into L segments by percentile intervals, shuffles within each segment, then concatenates.

**G2: Cyclic Scheduling** -- Periodically revisits the full score spectrum during one-pass training to prevent catastrophic forgetting of early-learned fundamentals. Implementation: Folding Ordering (FO) distributes sorted samples into L cycles via strided partitioning (sample at rank i goes to cycle i mod L). Each cycle maintains low-to-high internal order but covers the full score range.

**G3: Curriculum Continuity** -- Maintains smooth transitions in data attributes to prevent optimizer shocks. Implementation: Zig-zag Ordering (ZIG) extends FO by reversing every odd-indexed cycle, creating a triangle-wave pattern where adjacent cycles connect at similar score levels, eliminating sharp attribute drops at cycle boundaries.

**G4: Local Diversity** -- Preserves heterogeneity within local sections to prevent gradient collapse and overfitting. Implementation: Jittering Ordering (JIT) partitions sorted data into contiguous buckets of size w and shuffles within each bucket, maintaining the global trend while restoring essential gradient noise.

### Cross-Guidance Strategies

**STR (Stair Ordering)**: Combines G1+G2+G4. Partitions D_sort into K sections with transition regions (radius rho) around split points and stable regions between them. Stable regions retain monotonic order (G1); transition regions apply FO for knowledge review (G2); optional JIT for local diversity (G4). Creates a "staircase" score-index pattern.

**SAW (Saw Ordering)**: Extends STR by adding G3. Replaces FO with ZIG in transition regions, ensuring smoother attribute transitions. Integrates all four guidelines into a unified "sawtooth" pattern.

## Results & Highlights

### Guidance Analysis (Mistral-160M, 1B tokens, FineWeb-Edu)

**G1 (Boundary Sharpening)**: Ending with high-score data consistently yields gains (e.g., SEG(l10-h10) > Random). Starting with only high-score data gives negligible benefit. For SFT, both starting and ending with high-score data is optimal (SEG(h10-h10)). Dynamic accuracy trajectories confirm a sharp final performance boost when ending with hard data.

**G2 (Cyclic Scheduling)**: FO-2 consistently outperforms vanilla CL. PPL analysis on the easiest 10% data reveals that CL causes forgetting (PPL rebounds in later training), while FO-3 maintains low PPL throughout via periodic review. Performance is sensitive to L (folding layers).

**G3 (Curriculum Continuity)**: ZIG consistently matches or outperforms FO counterparts. Gradient norm analysis shows FO-3 exhibits sharp spikes at cycle boundaries, while ZIG maintains stable gradient magnitude.

**G4 (Local Diversity)**: JIT applied to any strictly sorted ordering (CL, FO, ZIG) yields consistent gains. Weight perturbation analysis shows JIT-enhanced models find flatter minima with better robustness to noise.

| Method | Pre-training Avg (8 benchmarks) | Math SFT Avg (AIME24/25) | Code SFT Avg (HE/MBPP) |
|--------|--------------------------------|--------------------------|------------------------|
| Random | baseline | baseline | baseline |
| CL | 37.61 | 1.78 | 58.30 |
| DELT | 37.35 | 2.42 | 59.70 |

STR and SAW significantly outperform CL and DELT across both pre-training and SFT stages. SAW achieves the best pre-training average (38.78%) and math SFT average (2.53%).

### Scaling Results (50B tokens, Mistral 160M-1.7B)

- Both STR and SAW maintain gains as model and data scale increases
- Test loss on DCLM corpus shows ordered data consistently achieves lower loss across all model sizes

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: Moderate-to-high. While curriculum learning is well-established (Bengio et al., 2009), the systematic decomposition into four orthogonal guidelines and their principled combination is novel. The distinction between data selection and data organization is clearly articulated. The cross-guidance strategies (STR, SAW) provide a clean framework for composing these principles. The key insight of reusing pre-computed scores is elegant and practical.
- **Rigor**: Strong. Each guideline is independently validated with ablation studies. The paper includes PPL trajectory analysis, gradient norm visualization, and weight perturbation analysis to provide mechanistic understanding. Random baselines are averaged over 3 seeds. The Chinchilla scaling law extrapolation, while not a substitute for actual large-scale experiments, provides a principled estimate of scaling behavior.
- **Reproducibility**: High. Open-sourced code, publicly available datasets (FineWeb-Edu, QuRatedPajama, DeepMath-103K, OpenCodeInstruct), standard architectures (Mistral, Qwen3), and detailed hyperparameters in appendices.
- **Significance**: Practically significant. The methods require only reordering data that is already scored for selection purposes -- a near-zero-cost intervention that yields measurable improvements. If the scaling extrapolation holds, this could become a standard practice in LLM training pipelines.
- **Red flags**: (1) Pre-training experiments use relatively small models (160M-1.7B), and the 405B-scale claims rely entirely on extrapolation. (2) STR and SAW perform comparably, suggesting the additional complexity of SAW (G3 integration) may not always justify the overhead. (3) Performance is sensitive to hyperparameters (folding layers L, JIT window size w), and the paper reports best configurations rather than average performance. (4) The reliance on pre-existing scores means effectiveness depends heavily on score quality and relevance.

## Strengths

- Systematic, principled framework that decomposes data ordering into four orthogonal, well-motivated guidelines
- Near-zero additional computational cost by reusing pre-computed scores -- a genuinely practical contribution
- Comprehensive experimental coverage: pre-training + SFT, multiple data domains (general, math, code), multiple model scales
- Strong mechanistic analysis: PPL trajectories, gradient norms, weight perturbation, and scaling law extrapolation
- Clean problem formulation clearly distinguishing data organization from data selection
- Open-sourced implementation at a reputable institutional repository

## Limitations

- The authors explicitly acknowledge that efficacy depends on the quality and relevance of pre-computed scores; poor scores could lead to suboptimal ordering
- Pre-training experiments are limited to 1.7B parameters and 50B tokens; claims at larger scales are based on extrapolation, not direct evidence
- STR and SAW perform comparably, raising questions about whether the additional complexity of SAW (G3) is necessary in practice
- The methods have not been tested on multimodal data, non-English corpora, or multi-epoch training regimes
- Performance sensitivity to hyperparameters (L, w, rho, K) adds configuration complexity that practitioners must navigate
- The paper does not explore how data organization interacts with other training choices like data mixing ratios, learning rate schedules, or batch sizes in depth

## Relevance

这篇论文对于关注LLM训练效率、数据工程和课程学习的研究者具有直接实用价值。它提供了一套可即插即用的数据排序框架（STR/SAW），复用已有评分无需额外计算开销，可直接集成到现有预训练和SFT流程中。如果你在研究大模型训练优化或数据管理，这些指导原则提供了一种低成本提升模型性能的新维度。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How do these data organization strategies interact with online data mixing and dynamic batch composition methods?
- Can the four guidelines be adapted for multimodal training (vision-language, audio-language) where scoring dimensions may be more complex?
- What is the interaction between data ordering and distributed training (data parallelism, pipeline parallelism) where ordering may be disrupted by sharding?
- Would adaptive methods that adjust ordering hyperparameters (L, w) during training based on loss dynamics outperform static configurations?
- How do these methods perform with different scoring functions beyond educational quality (e.g., perplexity-based, diversity-based, or multi-dimensional scores)?
- Can data organization principles be combined with data augmentation or synthetic data generation for further gains?
