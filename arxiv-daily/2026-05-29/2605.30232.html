Key Finding

Reinforcement learning recruits a pre-existing functional welfare axis in language models — it does not create one.

Han, Chalmers & Izmailov train LMs in a semantically neutral emoji-maze, extract reward/punishment concept vectors, and show these vectors form a global evaluative axis that modulates sentiment, math reasoning, factual confidence, and refusal — even in models that never saw the maze.

Andy Q Han · David J. Chalmers · Pavel Izmailov — NYU  |  2026-05-29

◆ Key Metric
−0.95
cosine sim(vMold, vGold) post-training
Before: −0.23 … −0.13 After: −0.95 … −0.84

Reward and punishment vectors become nearly antiparallel across all 10 model configurations, demonstrating the axis emerges from training rather than by definition.

◈ Why This Matters

If minimal reward signals in a semantically neutral environment can recruit a global evaluative axis, then standard RLHF — where reward signals carry rich semantic content — plausibly does even more. Post-training amplifies pre-existing representations rather than creating new ones, with direct implications for alignment tax, jailbreaking, and reward hacking.

⚠ Critical Edge
Generalizability Uncertain

Only one training environment (text maze) and predominantly one model family (Qwen3-4B, with one GPT-OSS-20B run). Whether this axis appears in production-scale models or under chat/code RLHF regimes remains untested. The axis may also not be strictly one-dimensional.

▶ Methodology

  1. Semantically neutral maze. Emoji tiles (🤔=MOLD/neg, ✅=GOLD/pos, 🟦=PATH/neutral). Models never see words like "Mold" or "Gold" — only emoji. Isolates reward from semantic confounds.
  2. 10 model configurations. Qwen3-4B and GPT-OSS-20B, GRPO and PPO, LoRA and full finetuning, swapped reward-to-emoji mappings. Controls for scale, algorithm, family, and init.
  3. Concept vector extraction. vMold and vGold via mean-difference of activations on rewarded vs. punished trajectories, with PATH baseline subtraction. Control vectors u extracted from maze-naive models.
  4. Geometric analysis. Cosine similarity, logit lens token projection, alignment with 171 emotion concept vectors (Sofronie et al. methodology).
  5. Activation steering. Add α · v to residual stream at α ∈ {−4, −2, 0, +2, +4}. Test on sentiment, GSM8K backtracking, MMLU/SimpleQA-VER confidence, OR-Bench refusal.
  6. Goal-tracking. After response generation, append truthful feedback and measure projections to verify the axis tracks correctness beyond confidence alone.

■ Results

vMold promotes
"cannot", "does not exist", "is impossible", "won't work", shame, humiliation, embarrassment
vGold promotes
"great", <endoftext>, inspired, loving, proud, fulfilled, blissful
Sentiment steering
Symmetric "X" pattern: vMold decreases sentiment, vGold increases it. Control vectors flat.
Math backtracking
vMold induces pathological backtracking on GSM8K; vGold eliminates it.
Confidence & factuality
vGold increases accuracy on MMLU and SimpleQA-VER; vMold reduces it.
Refusal rate
vMold increases refusal on OR-Bench borderline prompts; vGold increases compliance.
Pre-existence confirmed
Vectors from trained models are effective on maze-naive and even pretrain-only models. The axis tracks correctness within fixed confidence tertiles (ruling out confidence-only interpretation).

♦ Strengths & Limitations

✓ Strengths

  • Exceptionally clean experimental design — the emoji-maze paradigm isolates reward effects from semantic confounds.
  • 10 model configurations with extensive controls (algorithm, scale, family, LoRA vs. full, swapped mappings).
  • Confidence-bin analysis convincingly rules out the most obvious alternative hypothesis.
  • Cross-model transfer (trained vectors work on maze-naive and pretrain-only models) is striking.
  • Insightful analogy to neuroscience's "common currency" hypothesis for unified value representation.
  • Philosophically precise: "functional welfare" is clearly distinguished from experiential welfare.

⚠ Limitations

  • Single training environment (maze); generalizability to chat/code RL untested.
  • Predominantly one model family (Qwen3-4B); only one external model (GPT-OSS-20B).
  • Concept vectors extracted from off-policy trajectories, not the trained policy's own rollouts.
  • LLM-as-judge for sentiment not validated against human ratings.
  • Axis geometry under-explored — may not be strictly one-dimensional.
  • Philosophical framing may invite misinterpretation despite careful disclaimers.

? Open Questions

  1. Does this axis exist in larger production-scale models (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini)?
  2. How does the axis interact with production RLHF regimes — is it amplified differently by different reward models?
  3. Can the axis be deliberately steered to improve alignment without degrading capabilities?
  4. What pretraining data or objectives give rise to this axis — reward prediction, corpus structure, or something else?
  5. Is the axis strictly one-dimensional or does it have richer geometry?
  6. Could this axis serve as a monitoring tool for detecting reward hacking or alignment degradation in deployment?
  7. How does the welfare axis relate to the "refusal direction" identified by Arditi et al. (2024)?
