Can AI Weather Models Predict Beyond Two Weeks? A Quantitative Benchmark and Analysis of Long Rollouts

Fanny Lehmann, Firat Ozdemir, Yun Cheng, Torsten Hoefler, Sebastian Schemm, Benedikt Soja, Siddhartha Mishra — ETH Zurich • EPFL • University of Cambridge

Key Finding Stable AI weather models act as implicit denoisers—progressively filtering high-frequency noise to converge toward a seasonal attractor. Aurora recovers realistic weather fields from pure Gaussian noise or even a cat image within ~50 rollout steps, generating unique, non-memorized trajectories.

★
Why This Matters
Sub-seasonal AI forecasting requires models that survive months of autoregressive rollouts. This is the first formal failure-mode taxonomy and the mechanistic insight—denoising—to fix unstable ones.
Directly relevant to Weather Quest (S2S) and AI4MIP climate emulation efforts.
⚠
Critical Edge
Ablation studies are restricted to Aurora's ViT architecture only.
Results may not generalize to GNN (GraphCast), FNO (FourCastNet/SFNO), or diffusion (GenCast) families. Ablating the most stable model reveals what preserves stability—not what causes instability.

▶ Methodology

  • Models: 9 AI weather models, 3.5M to 4.7B params, spanning ViT/Swin, GNN, FNO, and ConvNeXt+GRU architectures.
  • Rollout: 2-year autoregressive rollouts (2,920 steps at 6h intervals), initialized Jan 1 2021 (out-of-training). Validated across 5 init dates.
  • Blow-up metric: Log-linear correlation R² > 0.9 on spatial extremes over 30-day sliding window.
  • Seasonality metric: Large-scale energy spectra (>5,000 km wavelengths) deviating >2× climatological range for 45+ consecutive days.
  • Small-scale ratio: Predicted vs. ERA5 energy at <250 km wavelengths in final 30 days.
  • Noise experiments: 1σ and 10σ Gaussian noise, pure white noise, cat image, structured noise.
  • Memorization test: Persistence ratio (distance to 1st vs. 2nd nearest training neighbor within 10 calendar days).
  • Ablation: Aurora_S (113M), one-variable-at-a-time: window/patch size, normalization, atmospheric levels, static vars, time embedding, capacity, resolution, lead time.

✓ Strengths

  • First formal quantitative taxonomy of AI weather model failure modes with reproducible metrics.
  • Coverage of 9 models, 4 architecture families, 9 variables—unprecedented scale.
  • Clean mechanistic link: stability correlates with denoising behavior, not specific inductive biases.
  • Vivid demonstrations (cat image → realistic weather in ~50 steps) that make the insight intuitive.
  • Formal memorization test (persistence ratio ~1 throughout) eliminates "stochastic parrot" concern.
  • Public code: github.com/lehmannfa/ai-weather-stability

✗ Limitations

  • Ablation restricted to Aurora's ViT only—may not transfer to GNN, FNO, or diffusion families.
  • Does not address climate change / distribution shift—historical training data limits climate emulation applicability.
  • Extreme events in long rollouts are less extreme and less frequent than ERA5 (ratio 0.7-1.1 at 90th percentile).
  • No physics-based baselines (IFS, GFS) included in long-rollout comparison.
  • DLESyM's extreme smoothing (0.03) raises the question: is "stable" always desirable if spatial detail is lost?
  • Distinction between "drift" and "loss of seasonality" is underexplored relative to blow-up.

? Open Questions

Denoising as training signal? Can an explicit denoising auxiliary loss stabilize currently unstable models like GraphCast or FourCastNet?
Capacity threshold. What is the minimum model capacity for seasonality, and is it architecture-dependent or a universal scaling law?
Spectral bias link. Does the denoising behavior connect to spectral bias in neural networks—stable models implicitly suppressing high-frequency errors?
Stability vs. sharpness tradeoff. Is there a fundamental tension between preserving small-scale spatial detail and maintaining long-range stability?
Climate non-stationarity. How would these models perform under climate change scenarios with out-of-distribution forcings?
Blow-up as early stopping. Can the blow-up metric serve as a training signal for checkpoint selection that maximizes long-range stability?
