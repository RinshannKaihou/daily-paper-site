Resolution Diagnostics for Paired LLM Evaluation

Anany Kotawala · Princeton University · ICML 2026 Workshop on Hypothesis Testing

28% of Open LLM Leaderboard v1 pairwise rankings and 44–67% of MMLU-Pro top-10 adjacent pairs are statistically unresolved — the displayed rankings that decide model selection are, for a large fraction of leaderboards, indistinguishable from noise.

Key Metric
2×
The unpaired Cohen-h shortcut underestimates the required paired sample size by a factor of exactly 2 in the close-comparison regime (median nh/N* = 0.5002). Cohen 1988, G*Power, and R pwr all silently inherit this deficit.
Why This Matters
Three of the most widely-used statistical power calculators carry an invisible factor-of-two deficit. Most LLM evaluation studies are dramatically underpowered, yet their results are treated as conclusive.
Critical Edge
Restricted to binary accuracy only. Does not cover graded scores or pairwise-preference leaderboards such as Chatbot Arena. Only two leaderboards validated; single-author work.
Methodology

Formal Framework

Paired LLM evaluation (two models on identical benchmark items) is formalized as a hypothesis-testing problem. Binary accuracy is assessed via McNemar's test on discordant pairs. The required sample size N* is obtained by inverting level-α, power-(1-β) tests:

N* = (z1-α/2 + z1-β)² · σD² / (pA − pB)²

The resolution ratio q = N / N* is the primary diagnostic: q ≥ 1 means the comparison is unresolved with the available data.

Lemma 1 — The Factor-of-Two Result

The widely-used shortcut of computing unpaired Cohen's h and post-multiplying by (1−ρ) yields:

  • nh / N* → 1/2 as δ → 0
  • Explicit second-order bound: |nh/N* − 1/2| ≤ C(p,ρ) · δ² + O(δ4)
  • Corollary 1: for any ε ∈ (0, 1/2), there exists δ* such that the shortcut underestimates N* by at least (1/2 − ε)N*

Empirical Analysis

  • Open LLM Leaderboard v1 — 40 pairwise comparisons across 4 tasks
  • MMLU-Pro top-10 — 9 adjacent-rank pairs (N = 12,032 items)
  • N* computed from observed (p̂A, p̂B, ρ̂) for each pair

Stress Tests

  • Bonferroni / Holm multiplicity correction (N* inflated ~2.11×)
  • Cluster-aware sensitivity: MMLU-Pro 14 subject categories with empirical ICC and design effects up to 31.5×
  • Anytime-valid sequential e-process (N* inflated 2.15×)
  • Category-bootstrap (B=1000), leave-one-subject-out
  • Multi-arbiter agreement across 4 statistical tests

Shortcut Validation

On 40 OLL v1 pairs: shortcut ratio nh/N* has median 0.5002, IQR [0.4999, 0.5035]. The 7 close-comparison pairs (|δ| ≤ 2%) hit 1/2 to four decimal places.

Prospective Design Check

Three OLL v1 pairs at framework-prescribed N* achieve empirical power 0.796–0.827, within ±2.7pp of the 0.80 target. At 0.8×N* power drops to 0.69–0.72.

Resolution by Effect-Size Band

  • |δ| ≤ 2%: 7/7 unresolved
  • 2% < |δ| ≤ 5%: 4/10 unresolved (mixed)
  • |δ| > 5%: all resolved

External Replication

Closed-source frontier panel (4 models, MMLU-Pro N=1,350) confirms Lemma 1: shortcut/N* ratio at [0.496, 0.500], consistent with the factor-of-two underestimate.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Addresses a critical gap: the statistical meaningfulness of leaderboard rankings
  • Lemma 1 is a genuine theoretical contribution with explicit second-order constant and practical corollary
  • Exceptional stress-testing breadth: multiplicity, clustering, sequential testing, bootstrap, LOSO
  • The resolution ratio q = N/N* is simple and intuitive for practitioners
  • Honest about scope: restricts claims to binary accuracy and close-comparison regime
  • Delta-binned breakdown provides a clear rule of thumb: resolution boundary near |δ| ~ 5%
  • Prospective validation confirms N* calibration on real data, not just asymptotics
  • Multi-arbiter agreement analysis proves the HellaSwag boundary case is a genuine split verdict

Limitations

  • Restricted to binary accuracy metrics; graded scores and preference leaderboards not covered
  • Only two leaderboards analyzed; broader validation needed
  • MMLU-Pro cluster analysis uses K=14 categories, relatively coarse
  • No ready-to-use software tool (code available but not packaged)
  • Single author; dense statistical content would benefit from broader peer review
  • Focuses on statistical resolution, not construct validity — a benchmark can be statistically resolvable yet measure the wrong thing
Questions for Further Exploration
  1. 01How should the resolution diagnostic be adapted for pairwise-preference leaderboards (e.g., Chatbot Arena) using Bradley-Terry e-processes?
  2. 02Can the corrected paired sample-size formulas be integrated into lm-eval-harness or HELM as automatic post-hoc diagnostics?
  3. 03What is the cost-benefit tradeoff of extending benchmarks (e.g., 12K → 40K items) to resolve specific adjacent pairs?
  4. 04How sensitive are findings to benchmark contamination — does inflated ρ from memorized items distort N*?
  5. 05Would a Bayesian posterior-credible-interval approach provide more actionable information than the frequentist framework for boundary cases?
  6. 06Which benchmarks are both statistically resolvable and construct-valid?
