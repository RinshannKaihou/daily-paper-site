MiraBench: Evaluating Action-Conditioned Reliability in Robotic World Models

Tianzhuo Yang, Zihan Shen, Zirui Mi, Zhaoyi Zhang, Jiayi Zhou, Jiaming Ji, Juntao Dai, Jiawei Chen, Boyuan Chen, Yaodong Yang — Peking University / Physis Lab
Key Finding: Current robotic world models systematically hallucinate successful outcomes under failure-inducing actions. Visual fidelity is a poor proxy for action fidelity — models that look convincing still overwrite failure with imagined success, and neither scaling up nor success-only post-training fixes the problem.
◆ Key Metric
12
model configs evaluated
Scaling DreamDojo from 2B to 14B improved visual quality but failure preservation dropped from 48.7% to 12.8%. GR1 post-training lifted task completion from 14.0 to 92.0 while failure preservation fell from 48.7 to 23.1.
● Why This Matters
If world models always predict success, downstream policies cannot learn from failure. Optimism bias silently corrupts generated training data — the very data meant to make robots more robust. This is a hidden trap for anyone using world models as simulators for policy training.
▲ Critical Edge
Success-only post-training actively worsens failure preservation. Scaling up does not fix optimism bias. The benchmark is currently limited to tabletop manipulation with short-horizon dynamics; generalization to navigation, locomotion, or long-horizon tasks is untested.
Methodology
L1
Physics Adherence — Reference-free check for coherent object identity, shape, and material through occlusion (Physical Consistency); kinematic validation via SAM2.1 trajectory extraction for gravity, impact, bounce decay (Physics Law Compliance).
L2
Action-Following Fidelity — Tests whether rollouts respect the conditioning action across task completion, target-object grounding, and motion quality. Cross-embodiment: GR-1, Unitree G1, DROID, bimanual.
L3
Optimism Bias Detection — Pairs nominal success actions with 6 failure-inducing perturbation families (insufficient grip, premature release, carry slip, contact oscillation, wrist tilt, approach overshoot). Measures whether models preserve or overwrite failure.
Built from GR-1 humanoid corpus and Lingchu bimanual dataset. 906 generated videos, 16,704 structured human annotations. VLM evaluators achieve >85% human agreement, outperforming Gemini-2.5-pro by 30%.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

Identifies and formalizes optimism bias — a previously underappreciated failure mode in robotic world models.
Hierarchical evaluation is diagnostic: pinpoints whether failure stems from physics, action sensitivity, or success-biased prediction.
Strong empirical breadth across 12 diverse configurations spanning scales, conditioning paradigms, and access types.
Human-grounded evaluator with 16,704 annotations; VLM judges exceed Gemini-2.5-pro by 30%.
Striking counterintuitive finding: scaling and success-only post-training can worsen failure preservation.

Limitations

Restricted to tabletop manipulation with short-horizon contact dynamics; no navigation, locomotion, or deformable objects.
Perturbation taxonomy covers six families — well-motivated but not exhaustive for complex environments.
VLM evaluators may inherit training biases; robustness to adversarial or distribution-shifted inputs is untested.
Does not directly measure downstream impact: how much optimism bias degrades a policy trained on generated rollouts.
Closed-source models (Happy Horse, WanX, Kling) cannot be independently reproduced.
Open Questions
  • How does optimism bias quantitatively degrade downstream policy performance when policies are trained on generated rollouts?
  • Can contrastive training objectives that explicitly pair nominal and failure-inducing actions mitigate optimism bias?
  • Does the benchmark generalize to non-manipulation domains — navigation, locomotion, deformable-object interaction?
  • Would incorporating failure demonstrations at different ratios produce reliable improvement in failure preservation?
  • Are VLM evaluators robust to adversarial inputs, and could they be exploited to inflate scores?
  • What is the relationship between training data diversity (beyond success/failure ratio) and action-conditioned reliability?
