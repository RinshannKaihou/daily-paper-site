Does Distributed Training Undermine Compute Governance?

A calibrated simulator proves that distributed training over consumer-grade internet can breach every existing and proposed compute governance threshold — training GPT-4-equivalent models for $91M and GPT-5-equivalent models for $2.3B in hardware, using fewer than 16 H100s per node.

Robi Rahman MIRI 2026-05-29 cs.CY / cs.AI
Key Metric
$91M
101 nodes on consumer internet to match GPT-4 training. GPT-5 scale: $2.3B across 2,880 nodes. Every major regulatory threshold is breachable.
Why This Matters
Every major compute governance framework — EU AI Act, California SB 53, Scher et al. — is vulnerable to distributed training evasion and needs fundamental redesign.
Critical Edge
Quality-adjusted compute at large scales is 4-5x lower than raw FLOP due to extreme overtraining (up to 51x). Whether distributed-trained models actually reach frontier capabilities remains uncertain.

pipeline-parallel penalty
Adversarial Model
Constraints assumed:
100 Mbps / 100ms latency consumer internet
Max 16 H100-equivalents per node (Scher threshold)
740-day training time limit
Active governing body monitoring traffic, power, satellite imagery

The simulator is calibrated against Covenant-72B (150x compression, negligible quality loss) and validated with real distributed training experiments. Three modeling choices are explicitly "generous to the evader."

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • First systematic quantitative analysis with a calibrated, open-source simulator
  • Validated against real-world results (Covenant-72B, DeepSeek-V3)
  • Concrete policy recommendations with cost estimates per countermeasure
  • Transparent about assumptions and direction of bias
  • Directly engages specific regulatory proposals (EU AI Act, SB 53, Scher)

Limitations

  • Quality-adjusted compute 4-5x lower than raw FLOP at large scales due to extreme overtraining
  • Assumes access to tens of trillions of unique high-quality tokens
  • Chip location verification requires hardware not yet on current chips
  • Single-author paper; needs broader peer review from ML and policy communities
  • Does not fully address operational complexity of coordinating thousands of nodes over months
Open Questions
  1. How would the economics change with 2026 bandwidth and hardware?
  2. Can advanced traffic analysis or ML-based anomaly detection identify DiLoCo gradient synchronization patterns through VPNs?
  3. What is the minimum viable governance framework robust to both centralized and distributed training evasion?
  4. How quickly are efficiency improvements (better compression, heterogeneous hardware) closing the gap with centralized training?
  5. Would a 1,280 GB HBM memory threshold remain effective as chip memory capacities increase?
