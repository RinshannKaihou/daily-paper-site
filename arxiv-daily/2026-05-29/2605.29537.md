> **一句话总结：** 本文系统研究了量化设置下前馈神经网络验证问题的计算复杂度，将FNN分为有理数、量化和动态量化三类，证明了固定精度量化FNN在LP和BV规范下验证均为NP完全，并为动态量化FNN的BV验证建立了PSPACE完全（定点/受限浮点）和NEXPTIME（一般浮点）上界，补全了复杂度全景图。

> **TL;DR:** This paper provides a complete complexity landscape for verifying feedforward neural networks (FNNs) under quantised arithmetic. Three FNN classes are formalized — rational, quantised (fixed finite-width arithmetic), and dynamically quantised — alongside two specification types (LP: linear constraints, BV: bit-vector with non-linear expressiveness). Key findings: (1) verification of quantised FNNs remains NP-complete under both LP and BV specs, matching the rational case; (2) for dynamically quantised FNNs with BV specs, PSPACE-completeness is established for fixed-point and bounded-exponent floating-point arithmetic, while NEXPTIME membership is shown for general floating-point. These results complement the previously known PSPACE-hardness lower bound from Henzinger et al. (2021) and clarify that developing sound verification tools for quantised networks does not incur asymptotic overhead over existing real-valued solvers.

## 核心贡献

- 系统性地定义了三类前馈神经网络：有理数FNN（精确有理权重）、量化FNN（有限宽度算术权重）、动态量化FNN（有理网络在给定有限宽度算术下求值），并形式化了对应的五种可达性问题。
- 证明了REACH_F(LP)和REACH_F(BV)——即固定精度量化FNN在LP和BV规范下的验证——均为NP完全（Theorem 5, 7），与有理数情况复杂度一致。
- 证明了REACH(F, LP)——动态量化FNN在LP规范下的验证——也是NP完全（Theorem 6），表明即使算术精度作为输入的一部分，LP规范下复杂度不升高。
- 为动态量化FNN在BV规范下的验证REACH(F, BV)建立了精确上界：定点和有界指数浮点算术下为PSPACE完全（Theorem 9），一般浮点算术下属于NEXPTIME（Proposition 14），补全了Henzinger et al. (2021)仅给出PSPACE下界的缺口。
- 采用简洁表示的非确定性有限自动机（succinct NFA）技术路线，将FNN和BV规范的验证问题编码为自动机非空性问题，从而利用已知的图可达性复杂度结果获得上界。

## Key Contributions

- Formalizes three distinct FNN classes (rational, quantised, dynamically quantised) and five corresponding reachability/verification problems, providing a clean framework that distinguishes the arithmetic model from the specification type.
- Proves NP-completeness for REACH_F(LP) and REACH_F(BV) — verification of already-quantised FNNs under both linear program and bit-vector specifications — matching the complexity of the rational/arbitrary-precision case (Theorems 5, 7).
- Proves NP-completeness for REACH(F, LP) — dynamically quantised FNNs with LP specs where the finite-width arithmetic is part of the input — showing that LP specifications never push complexity beyond NP regardless of quantisation regime (Theorem 6).
- Establishes PSPACE-completeness for REACH(F, BV) under fixed-point and bounded-exponent floating-point arithmetic (Theorem 9), and NEXPTIME membership for general floating-point arithmetic (Proposition 14), complementing the PSPACE-hardness lower bound from Henzinger et al. (2021).
- Develops a succinct NFA-based proof technique that encodes both FNN input-output behavior and BV formula satisfaction as succinctly represented automata, then reduces verification to non-emptiness of the intersection automaton — leveraging the known PSPACE complexity of succinct graph reachability (Proposition 10).
- Corrects a widespread misunderstanding in the community about the Henzinger et al. (2021) result: that paper showed only PSPACE-hardness (not PSPACE-completeness) and considered a richer specification language, not standard LP constraints.

## Methodology

The paper takes a rigorous theoretical computer science approach combining computational complexity theory, automata theory, and formal verification. The methodology proceeds as follows:

1. **Formal definitions:** Three FNN classes are defined with precise arithmetic semantics. Finite-width arithmetics F_{n1,n2} are formalized as fixed-point or floating-point representations with configurable integer and fractional bit-widths. Two specification formalisms are defined: LP specifications (conjunctions of linear constraints over rationals or quantised values) and BV specifications (bit-vector logic formulas following Kovasznai et al., 2016, enabling bit-level reasoning and non-linear constraints).

2. **Five verification problems:** REACH_Q(LP), REACH_F(LP), REACH(F, LP), REACH_F(BV), REACH(F, BV) — distinguished by whether the network is rational/quantised/dynamically quantised and whether the specification is LP or BV.

3. **NP-completeness proofs:** Membership in NP is shown by guessing an input (polynomial size, justified via Theorem 1 from Korte and Vygen on polynomial solution bounds for LPs) and polynomial-time verification. Hardness is established via reduction from 3SAT, constructing small FNN gadgets that simulate boolean variable assignments and clause satisfaction using ReLU-like step functions with parameters exactly representable in any non-trivial finite-width arithmetic.

4. **PSPACE upper bounds via succinct automata:** The key technical innovation for REACH(F, BV) involves constructing succinctly represented NFAs for both the FNN input-output relation (extending Salzer et al., 2024) and the BV specification (Lemma 13). The intersection automaton's non-emptiness is then checked using the PSPACE algorithm for succinct graph reachability. For fixed-point and bounded-exponent floating-point, the state space remains polynomially describable; for general floating-point, the binary encoding of bit-width leads to exponentially large witnesses, yielding NEXPTIME membership.

## Results & Highlights

- **Complexity landscape table:** The paper provides a complete complexity table across all combinations of FNN class and specification type. The main results are:
  - REACH_Q(LP): NP-complete (known result)
  - REACH_F(LP): NP-complete (Theorem 5)
  - REACH(F, LP): NP-complete (Theorem 6)
  - REACH_F(BV): NP-complete (Theorem 7)
  - REACH(F, BV): PSPACE-complete for fixed-point and bounded-exponent floating-point (Theorem 9); NEXPTIME for general floating-point (Proposition 14); PSPACE-hard in all cases (Theorem 8, from Henzinger et al.)

- **Quantisation does not increase complexity for LP specs:** A practically important finding — standard LP-based verification of quantised networks stays NP-complete, the same complexity as idealized real-valued verification. This means existing NP-based solvers can in principle be adapted to quantised settings without asymptotic overhead.

- **BV specs reveal a complexity gap only for dynamic quantisation:** The jump to PSPACE (or higher) occurs only when combining dynamic quantisation (arithmetic as input parameter) with bit-vector specifications. For already-quantised networks, BV verification remains NP-complete.

- **Open problem identified:** Whether REACH(F, BV) for general floating-point arithmetic achieves the NEXPTIME upper bound as a lower bound remains open — the alignment buffer needed for floating-point normalization appears to be the source of additional complexity beyond PSPACE.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** The systematic taxonomy of five verification problems across three FNN classes and two specification types is a useful organizational contribution. The NP-completeness results for quantised FNNs (REACH_F(LP), REACH_F(BV), REACH(F, LP)) are technically correct but arguably unsurprising — the 3SAT reductions use gadgets with small integer parameters that naturally embed in any reasonable arithmetic. The genuinely novel contributions are the PSPACE-completeness result (Theorem 9) using the succinct NFA technique and the NEXPTIME upper bound for general floating-point.
- **Rigor:** The proofs are detailed and use standard complexity-theoretic techniques (reductions from 3SAT, membership via witness guessing). The succinct NFA construction for BV formulas (Lemma 13) and the automata-based encoding of FNN behavior are technically solid. The paper carefully handles the subtleties of different floating-point representations.
- **Reproducibility:** High — theoretical results with complete proof sketches. The formal definitions are precise enough to allow independent verification.
- **Significance:** Moderately high for the formal verification community. The key practical takeaway — that quantisation does not make verification harder under LP specs — is important for tool developers. The correction of misunderstandings about Henzinger et al. (2021) is also valuable. However, the paper is purely theoretical with no algorithmic or empirical contributions, limiting its direct practical impact.
- **Red flags:** None. The paper is honest about its scope (theoretical complexity analysis), clearly states what is new versus what builds on prior work, and explicitly identifies open problems. The discussion of the community misunderstanding about Henzinger et al. is handled academically.

## Strengths

- Clean, systematic formalization that disentangles the role of the arithmetic model from the specification language, resulting in a clear complexity landscape.
- The succinct NFA technique for obtaining PSPACE upper bounds is elegant and may have broader applicability beyond this specific verification setting.
- Practically important conclusion: LP-based verification of quantised networks remains NP, justifying the adaptation of existing solvers to quantised settings.
- Honest and thorough discussion of prior work, including correction of community misconceptions about Henzinger et al. (2021).
- Identifies concrete open problems (NEXPTIME lower bound for general floating-point, practical implementation of succinct automata solvers).

## Limitations

- Purely theoretical — no empirical validation, no prototype solver implementation, and no discussion of constant factors or practical algorithm design implications.
- The NEXPTIME upper bound for general floating-point REACH(F, BV) is loose (only known to be PSPACE-hard), leaving a gap that the authors acknowledge but do not resolve.
- Restricted to feedforward networks with standard activation functions. The complexity of verifying quantised CNNs, RNNs, or transformers is not addressed, though the automata-based technique may extend.
- The assumption of ReLU-like step functions with exactly representable parameters (1, 0, 1/2) in the NP-hardness constructions may not cover all practical activation functions.
- No discussion of approximate or incomplete verification methods, which are what practitioners actually use for large networks.

## Relevance

本文对量化神经网络验证问题建立了完整的计算复杂度图谱，其核心结论——LP规范下量化网络验证复杂度不高于理想情况——对实际部署中量化模型的形式化验证工具开发具有直接指导意义。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can the NEXPTIME upper bound for REACH(F, BV) under general floating-point arithmetic be matched with a NEXPTIME lower bound, or can the PSPACE upper bound be extended to this case?
- How does the succinct NFA construction scale practically — could it serve as the basis for a prototype verification tool that handles quantised networks soundly?
- Do the complexity results extend to convolutional architectures (quantised CNNs), where the weight-sharing structure might affect the automata construction?
- For LP specifications, do quantised FNNs admit more efficient average-case verification algorithms than their rational counterparts, even though worst-case complexity is the same?
- Could the bit-vector specification framework be combined with incomplete verification methods (e.g., abstract interpretation) to obtain practical sound-but-incomplete tools for quantised networks?
