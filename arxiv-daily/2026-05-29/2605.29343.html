Draft-OPD: On-Policy Distillation for Speculative Draft Models

Standard SFT trains draft models on fixed target-model trajectories, but during inference they are evaluated on their own draft-induced states. Draft-OPD closes this gap with error-position anchor replay and an acceptance-aware KL objective — breaking through the SFT plateau to deliver over 5x lossless speedup on thinking-mode LLMs.

Why This Matters
Speculative decoding is the workhorse technique for LLM deployment efficiency. Better draft model training directly translates to faster, cheaper inference at scale — especially for the thinking-mode reasoning models now dominating production workloads.
Critical Edge
Qwen3-only eval; OPD training cost not quantified; no code release
Generalization to Llama, Mistral, DeepSeek is untested. The OPD stage requires online target-model interaction — significantly more expensive than offline SFT, but the paper does not report this cost. Gains on non-thinking tasks (MT-Bench) are more modest.

Methodology — Draft-OPD Pipeline

  1. 01
    Rollout with error-position collection. Run speculative decoding normally. The draft model proposes blocks of K tokens. Record the starting position of each block as an anchor. The target model verifies and produces a high-quality rollout. Anchors capture where the draft model actually acted.
  2. 02
    Replay from anchors. From each anchor, replay the draft-generated block. Compute both draft and target log-probabilities on the same draft-induced prefix. This includes rejected tokens — the most informative training signals that standard SFT discards entirely.
  3. 03
    Acceptance-aware KL objective. Accepted tokens: forward KL (encourage coverage of target distribution). Rejected tokens: reverse KL with exponentially decaying weights by position (penalize high-probability draft modes where the target disagrees).
Core Insight

SFT plateaus because it trains on target-induced states but evaluates on draft-induced states. Standard on-policy distillation fails for drafters because self-rollouts degenerate into repetitions, while target-assisted rollouts erase the on-policy signal by discarding rejected tokens. Draft-OPD's anchor-based replay preserves both.

Ablation Highlights
  • Training data: Continued SFT on the same prompt pool underperforms Draft-OPD — gains are not from extra data.
  • KL design: Mixed forward/reverse KL outperforms both all-forward and all-reverse variants.
  • Anchors: Error-position replay outperforms random anchors — concentrating on verification-exposed errors is critical.

Strengths

  • Clear diagnosis of the SFT plateau with empirical evidence (Figure 1 shows saturation)
  • Elegant anchor-based replay that preserves both stable rollouts and on-policy signal
  • Acceptance-aware KL objective is theoretically well-justified: forward KL for coverage, reverse KL for error suppression
  • Comprehensive ablations isolating each design choice
  • Real deployment validation on SGLang with consistent throughput gains across concurrency levels 1–32
  • Particularly effective for thinking-mode models — increasingly dominant in practice

Limitations

  • Evaluated exclusively on Qwen3 family; cross-architecture generalization is unknown
  • OPD training requires online target-model inference — cost is not quantified
  • Non-thinking task gains are more modest (MT-Bench: 3.18x vs 2.70x)
  • No code or model release announced, limiting community adoption
  • Designed for lossless verification only; extension to approximate speculative decoding is future work
  • Thinking-mode drafting gap is only partially addressed, not solved

Open Questions

  1. How does the computational cost of the OPD training stage (including online target-model inference) compare quantitatively to SFT? What is the total training cost increase?
  2. Would Draft-OPD transfer to other model families (Llama 4, Mistral, DeepSeek) with different draft architectures?
  3. Can the anchor-based replay mechanism be combined with RL objectives to directly optimize acceptance length rather than distributional matching?
  4. How does Draft-OPD interact with dynamic draft block sizes or adaptive speculation strategies?
  5. Is there a theoretical upper bound on acceptance length improvement that on-policy methods can achieve over offline SFT, given architectural constraints?
