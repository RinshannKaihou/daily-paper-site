Key Finding

AsymVLM prunes vision and text tokens with separate strategies — and beats the full-token baseline on document tasks while cutting FLOPs in half.

Vision tokens are spatially redundant and dominate prefill; text tokens accumulate during decoding. Treating them the same is a mistake. AsymVLM learns what matters per modality and prunes accordingly.

Feng, Gulhan & Kandemir — Penn State cs.LG · May 2026 Phi-3.5-Vision · Gemma 3
Key Metric
54%
FLOPs savings at 50% token retention
Peak memory drops to 25% of baseline. Outperforms FastV and SparseVLM on DocVQA (+2–3%) and matches or exceeds the full-token baseline on ChartQA. Highest efficiency among all SOTA methods.
Why This Matters
VLMs are compute-hungry at deployment. AsymVLM makes them practical by halving inference cost without accuracy loss. The scorer trains in 1 hour on 1,500 samples on a single A100 and generalizes across 5 benchmarks with no task-specific tuning. The asymmetric design principle applies beyond the two tested architectures.
Critical Edge
Tested on only 2 architectures (4B and 12B); behavior at larger scales is unknown. No wall-clock latency reported — only FLOPs. Text eviction thresholds require manual per-benchmark tuning. Very long multi-turn dialogues (50+ turns) are untested. No public code repository.
Methodology — Three Asymmetric Components

1. Learned Vision Token Scorer

Computes importance as S = (V̂ · w) @ T̂ᵀ where w is a learnable per-dimension weight re-scaling vision embeddings before dot-product similarity with text embeddings. Trained by minimizing MSE between pruned and unpruned output hidden states. Only w updates; the VLM stays frozen. Trains in 1 hour on 1 A100.

2. Per-Sample Adaptive Budget

Computes an importance gap (75th minus 25th percentile scores) per input. Samples with larger gaps (easier pruning decisions) get more aggressive retention reduction. Two variants: threshold-sweep tiers or a learned linear mapping r(g) = clamp(r_target + a·(g − ė)).

3. Threshold-Based Text Eviction

Protects all prefill tokens (vision + prompt) unconditionally. Only generated tokens are scored by final-layer attention and evicted when the token count exceeds a fixed budget. In multi-turn dialogue, entire previous answers are evicted at turn level using cross-modal similarity.

Strengths
  • Well-motivated asymmetry analysis: every design choice is grounded in empirical observation of how vision vs. text tokens behave differently.
  • Learned output-aware scorer dramatically outperforms input-side heuristics (cosine similarity, raw attention).
  • Per-sample adaptive budget is a principled solution to variable pruning difficulty across inputs.
  • Achieves genuine FLOPs and memory reductions, unlike attention-masking approaches (e.g., FastV).
  • Scorer trained on DocVQA transfers cleanly to ChartQA, TextVQA, MME, and OCRVQA.
  • Evaluated on two contrasting architectures: vision-dominant (2,500 tokens) and text-dominant (256 tokens).
Limitations
  • Only 2 VLM architectures tested; generalization to LLaVA, Qwen-VL, InternVL is unverified.
  • Adaptive budget thresholds need grid search on a calibration set — not fully automatic.
  • Text eviction uses fixed per-benchmark thresholds rather than adaptive ones.
  • No wall-clock latency measurements; FLOPs savings may not translate linearly to speedup.
  • Scorer training requires two forward passes per sample through the frozen VLM, which may be costly for very large models.
  • Video-language models and multi-image settings (beyond MMDU) are not evaluated.
Questions for Further Exploration

Q4Does the method extend to video-language models where temporal redundancy adds another compression axis?
Q5Is the scorer learning transferable importance patterns, or implicit dataset features?
Q6What happens when both vision and text pruning are active simultaneously in long multi-turn dialogue?
