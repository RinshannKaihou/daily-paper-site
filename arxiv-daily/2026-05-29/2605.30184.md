> **一句话总结：** 本文对九个前沿AI天气模型（Aurora、SFNO、Pangu、DLESyM、AIFS、GraphCast、FourCastNet、FuXi、GenCast）进行长达2年的自回归滚动预测，首次将失败模式形式化分类为爆炸（blow-up）、漂移（drift）和季节性丢失（loss of seasonality）三种机制，发现稳定性取决于模型对小尺度时空特征的处理方式——不稳定模型放大高频能量而稳定模型充当隐式去噪器，并通过Aurora架构消融实验证明稳定性对大多数设计选择（窗口大小、归一化方式、大气层数等）具有鲁棒性，但受模型容量和时间嵌入影响。

> **TL;DR:** This paper runs 2-year autoregressive rollouts of nine AI weather models (Aurora, SFNO, Pangu, DLESyM, AIFS, GraphCast, FourCastNet, FuXi, GenCast) and provides the first formal quantitative taxonomy of failure modes: blow-up (exponential growth of spatial extremes), drift (systematic bias accumulation), and loss of seasonality. Only Aurora, SFNO, Pangu, and DLESyM survive 730+ days without blow-up. The key mechanistic insight is that stable models (especially Aurora and SFNO) act as implicit denoisers — progressively filtering high-frequency noise to converge toward a seasonal attractor — while unstable models amplify small-scale energy. Crucially, stable models generate unique, non-memorized trajectories. Ablation studies on Aurora's Swin3D ViT architecture show stability is remarkably robust to most design choices but requires minimum model capacity and absolute time embedding for seasonality.

## 核心贡献

- 首次对AI天气模型长期自回归预测的失败模式进行形式化分类和定量度量：爆炸（blow-up，空间极值的指数增长）、漂移（drift，系统性偏差累积）和季节性丢失（loss of seasonality）
- 对九个最先进的AI天气模型（参数量从3.5M到4.7B，涵盖ViT、GNN、FNO、UNet四种架构族）进行了完整的2年滚动预测评估
- 揭示稳定性与小尺度时空特征处理之间的核心联系：不稳定模型放大高频能量（FourCastNet的T2m小尺度能量比为10^5），稳定模型的小尺度能量比低于1（Aurora为0.8）
- 证明稳定的AI天气模型（Aurora、SFNO）具有隐式去噪能力——从纯高斯噪声甚至猫的图片初始化都能恢复真实天气轨迹，且生成的是独特的、非记忆化的轨迹
- 通过Aurora（113M参数）的系统性消融实验，发现稳定性对窗口大小、patch大小、归一化方式、大气层数、静态变量等设计选择均具有鲁棒性，但去除时间嵌入会丧失季节性，过度缩减参数量（从113M到21M）也会消除真实季节循环

## Key Contributions

- First formal, quantitative taxonomy of AI weather model failure modes in long autoregressive rollouts: blow-up (exponential growth of spatial extremes, detected via log-linear correlation R^2 > 0.9 over 30-day windows), drift (systematic bias), and loss of seasonality (large-scale energy spectra deviation beyond 2x climatological range for 45+ consecutive days)
- Comprehensive 2-year rollout benchmark of nine SOTA models: Aurora (1.3B, 3D Swin Transformer), SFNO (289M), Pangu (256M, Swin+Earth-specific), DLESyM (3.5M, ConvNeXt+GRU), AIFS (253M, Graph Transformer), GraphCast (36.7M, GNN), FourCastNet (75M, Adaptive FNO), FuXi (4.7B, 3D Swin), GenCast (57M, Diffusion)
- Only Aurora, SFNO, Pangu, and DLESyM survive 730+ days without blow-up across all 9 variables (T2m, U10m, MSLP, Z500, T500, Q500, U300, T100, Q100); FourCastNet blows up in just 9 days for T2m
- Demonstrated that stable models act as implicit denoisers: Aurora and SFNO converge to realistic fields even from pure Gaussian noise, spatially structured noise, or a cat image, within ~50 rollout steps
- Proved absence of memorization via persistence ratio analysis (distance ratio to nearest training neighbor stays ~1 throughout rollout)
- Ablation studies on Aurora_S (113M params) showing stability is robust to window size, patch size, normalization type, number of atmospheric levels (13 vs 3), and static variable removal, but requires time embedding for seasonality and minimum model capacity

## Methodology

**Models evaluated:** Nine AI weather models spanning 3.5M to 4.7B parameters and four architecture families (ViT/Swin Transformer, Graph Neural Network, Fourier Neural Operator, ConvNeXt+GRU). All models trained on ERA5 reanalysis data.

**Rollout protocol:** 2-year autoregressive rollouts (2,920 steps at 6-hour lead time), initialized January 1, 2021 (outside training period for most models). ERA5 serves as reference. Metrics validated across 5 different initialization dates.

**Quantitative metrics:**
- **Blow-up time:** First 30-day window where global spatial extremes show log-linear correlation R^2 > 0.9 (exponential divergence). For fast-blowing models like FourCastNet, window reduced to 10 days.
- **Seasonality:** Large-scale energy spectra (wavelengths > 5000 km) deviating from ERA5 by more than 2x climatological range for 45+ consecutive days.
- **Small-scale energy ratio:** Ratio of predicted vs. ERA5 energy spectra at wavelengths < 250 km during last 30 days of rollout. Values < 1 indicate smoothing (blurriness), values > 1 indicate amplification.

**Noise experiments:** Additive Gaussian noise (1-sigma and 10-sigma), pure white noise initialization, structured noise, cat image initialization, and decoupled static vs. dynamic variable perturbation.

**Memorization test:** Persistence ratio comparing distance to first vs. second nearest neighbor in training set (within 10 calendar days). Ratio ~1 indicates no memorization.

**Ablation protocol:** Aurora_S (113M params) trained from scratch on ERA5 1979-2019, varying one component at a time: Swin3D window sizes, patch sizes (1/2/4), LayerNorm vs. RMSNorm, atmospheric levels (13 vs. 3), static variable inclusion, time embedding removal, parameter count reduction (113M to 21M), spatial resolution (0.25 vs. 1.5 degrees), and lead time (6h vs. 24h).

## Results & Highlights

**Blow-up results (Table 1):** Aurora, SFNO, Pangu, and DLESyM survive >730 days for all variables. FourCastNet blows up fastest (9 days for T2m and U10m). AIFS blows up at 8 days for MSLP and 41 days for T2m. GraphCast blows up at 38 days for T2m. GenCast (probabilistic diffusion model) blows up at 71-77 days for most variables.

**Small-scale energy (Table 3):** Stable models have ratios < 1 (Aurora 0.8, SFNO 0.8, Pangu 0.7 for T2m) indicating smoothing/denoising. Unstable models show catastrophic amplification: FourCastNet reaches 10^5, FuXi reaches 10^3 for T2m. DLESyM shows extreme smoothing (ratio 0.03) — stable but blurry.

**Denoising behavior (Figure 2):** Aurora, SFNO, and Pangu progressively denoise noisy inputs, with error converging to a stable value. Aurora handles even 10-sigma noise. Aurora successfully recovers from cat image initialization within ~50 steps.

**Seasonal shift experiment (Figure 3):** When initialized with January 1 physical conditions but given June 1 time embedding, Aurora and AIFS rapidly (within days) adjust to the June seasonal cycle, confirming time embedding drives long-term temporal structure.

**Ablation findings:**
- Changing Swin3D window sizes, removing window shifting, varying patch sizes: all stable with seasonality
- Replacing LayerNorm with RMSNorm: stable (Figure 4, row 2)
- Reducing atmospheric levels from 13 to 3: stable despite less data (Figure 4, row 1)
- Removing static variables: stable (model learns not to rely on them)
- Removing time embedding: stable but no seasonality (converges to time-invariant state)
- Reducing to 21M params (encoder (2,2,2), embedding dim 128): eliminates realistic seasonal cycle — minimum capacity threshold exists
- Both 0.25 and 1.5 resolution models are stable; 24h lead time models preserve more accurate seasonality; 6h models drift after ~244-381 days
- Seasonal cycle RMSE decreases with training, and small Aurora models match large Aurora's seasonality within 60k steps

**Extreme events:** The three stable models (Aurora, SFNO, DLESyM) produce realistic extreme events in 10-year rollouts but with less extreme and less frequent events than ERA5 (ratio 0.7-1.1 at 90th percentile).

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** High. While individual failure modes have been anecdotally observed, this is the first systematic, quantitative taxonomy with formal metrics. The denoising interpretation of stable models is a genuinely new theoretical insight. The finding that stability is robust to most architectural choices but requires minimum capacity is non-obvious.
- **Rigor:** Very strong. 2-year rollouts across 9 models, metrics validated across 5 initialization dates, noise perturbation experiments at multiple levels (1-sigma, 10-sigma, pure noise, structured noise, cat image), formal memorization tests, and systematic ablation studies. The blow-up detection via log-linear correlation is a clean, reproducible metric.
- **Reproducibility:** High. Metrics are precisely defined (Appendix B), code is publicly available on GitHub, and all models are either publicly available or accessible via Nvidia Earth2Studio.
- **Significance:** Very significant for the AI weather/climate modeling community. Directly relevant to ongoing efforts like the Weather Quest (subseasonal-to-seasonal) and AI4MIP (climate scales). The finding that denoising behavior underpins stability gives a concrete target for model improvement.
- **Red flags:** The ablation studies are restricted to ViT architectures only — the authors acknowledge this limitation. Aurora is used as the primary ablation subject, and since Aurora is already the most stable model, the ablation results may not reveal what makes inherently unstable models unstable. The extreme event analysis (10-year rollouts) is somewhat underdeveloped.

## Strengths

- First comprehensive, quantitative benchmark with formal metrics for AI weather model long-rollout stability
- Large-scale evaluation spanning 9 models, 4 architecture families, and 9 variables
- Clean mechanistic insight: stability correlates with denoising behavior, not with specific inductive biases
- Remarkably thorough ablation study systematically varying architecture, resolution, lead time, and training
- Compelling demonstrations (cat image recovery, pure noise initialization) that make the denoising property vividly clear
- Formal memorization test with persistence ratio puts the "stochastic parrot" concern to rest for stable models
- Public code release enables community adoption of the benchmark

## Limitations

- Ablation studies are restricted to Aurora's Swin3D ViT architecture; results may not generalize to GNN (GraphCast), FNO (FourCastNet/SFNO), or other architectures. Authors acknowledge this.
- Aurora is already the most stable model, so ablating it reveals what preserves stability rather than what causes instability in other models.
- The paper does not address climate change / distribution shift — models trained on historical data cannot be expected to handle non-stationary climates, which limits applicability to climate emulation.
- Extreme events in long rollouts are biased (less extreme, less frequent than ERA5), indicating the models' climate statistics are not yet fully realistic.
- DLESyM's extreme smoothing (small-scale energy ratio 0.03) raises the question of whether "stable" is always desirable if it comes at the cost of lost spatial detail.
- The distinction between "drift" and "loss of seasonality" is somewhat underexplored compared to blow-up.
- No physics-based baselines (e.g., IFS, GFS) are included in the long-rollout comparison.

## Relevance

本文对AI天气模型的长期自回归稳定性提供了首个系统性定量基准，对于关注AI在地球科学和物理系统模拟中应用的研究者具有直接参考价值。其关于隐式去噪行为与稳定性之间关系的发现，对理解深度学习模型在长时间物理模拟中的误差累积机制具有普遍启示意义，也为跨领域的自回归模型稳定性研究提供了方法论参考。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can the denoising property be explicitly optimized for during training (e.g., via a denoising auxiliary loss) to improve stability of currently unstable models like GraphCast or FourCastNet?
- What is the minimum model capacity threshold for seasonality, and does it depend on the architecture family or is it a universal scaling law?
- How does the denoising behavior relate to spectral bias in neural networks — are stable models implicitly learning to suppress high-frequency errors?
- Can the blow-up metric be used as a training signal (early stopping criterion) to select checkpoints that maximize long-range stability?
- How would these models perform under climate change scenarios (non-stationary forcings, out-of-distribution boundary conditions)?
- Is there a fundamental tradeoff between spatial sharpness (preserving small-scale details) and long-range stability, or can both be achieved simultaneously?
