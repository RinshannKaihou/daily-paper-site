> **一句话总结：** 提出S-Adam优化器，通过基于随机方向导数方差的局部几何不稳定性(LGI)指标动态阻尼步长，在非光滑损失景观（含ReLU和量化的网络）中消除梯度振荡，证明以最优O(1/sqrt(T))率收敛到Clarke驻点，并在QAT和小批量场景中CIFAR-100提升最高+4.99点、Imagewoof2-160提升+24.69点。

> **TL;DR:** Published at ICML 2026, this paper introduces Singularity-aware Adam (S-Adam), which estimates the Clarke subdifferential diameter via randomized directional derivative variance (LGI metric) and uses exp(-lambda*rho) adaptive damping to suppress gradient chattering in non-smooth loss landscapes. It provably converges to Clarke stationary points at optimal O(1/sqrt(T)) rate. On batch-size-2 training with ResNet-18, S-Adam outperforms AdamW by +4.99 on CIFAR-100 and +24.69 on Imagewoof2-160, and consistently wins across all tested learning rates and batch sizes in QAT benchmarks.

## 核心贡献

- 提出局部几何不稳定性(LGI)指标：通过采样k个随机扰动方向的梯度方差高效估计Clarke次微分直径，无需求Hessian，作为非光滑区域的实时检测器
- 设计S-Adam优化器：将Adam更新中的学习率乘以自适应阻尼因子 exp(-lambda*rho_t)，在高LGI区域自动减速，在光滑区域退化为标准Adam
- 基于微分包含(differential inclusions)框架给出严格收敛证明：在局部Lipschitz连续、有界迭代和步长Robbins-Monro条件下，S-Adam几乎必然收敛到Clarke驻点，迭代复杂度O(1/epsilon^2)为最优
- 核心范式转变：不使用随机平滑替代原始梯度，而是将随机平滑作为"几何探针"来估计局部不稳定性，保留原始梯度方向
- 在QAT（2/4/8-bit量化）和极端小批量(BS=2)场景中全面超越AdamW和Prox-SGD，最大增益达+24.69点

## Key Contributions

- Introduces the Local Geometric Instability (LGI) metric: a variance-based estimator of the Clarke subdifferential diameter computed from k randomized directional derivative probes, requiring no Hessian computation
- Proposes Singularity-aware Adam (S-Adam): modifies Adam's effective learning rate by exp(-lambda*rho_t), where rho_t is the LGI metric, providing adaptive damping near singularities while recovering standard Adam in smooth regions (rho -> 0 implies alpha -> 1)
- Provides rigorous convergence analysis via differential inclusions (Benaim 1996 framework): under local Lipschitz continuity, bounded iterates, Robbins-Monro step sizes, and moment estimator consistency, S-Adam converges a.s. to Clarke stationary points with O(1/epsilon^2) iteration complexity (optimal rate)
- Paradigm shift: uses randomized smoothing as a diagnostic geometric probe rather than a gradient surrogate, preserving the original gradient direction
- Demonstrates comprehensive empirical superiority: in QAT (2/4/8-bit), S-Adam with k=8 achieves best accuracy on CIFAR-100, TinyImageNet, and ImageNet; under BS=2, improves over AdamW by +4.99 on CIFAR-100 and +24.69 on Imagewoof2-160

## Methodology

The paper addresses gradient chattering — pathological oscillations that arise when momentum-based optimizers encounter non-smooth singularities where the Clarke subdifferential has large diameter, causing conflicting directional signals to accumulate in the momentum buffer.

**Local Geometric Instability (LGI) metric:** At each iteration, S-Adam samples k random perturbation directions, computes directional derivatives along each, and measures their variance. Formally, the LGI metric rho_t is defined as the variance of these randomized directional derivatives, serving as a proxy for the Clarke subdifferential diameter. High LGI indicates the optimizer is in a region where gradient signals conflict — a signature of non-smoothness from ReLU kinks or quantization discontinuities.

**Adaptive damping (Geometric Brake):** The standard Adam update is modified with a damping coefficient. The effective step size becomes eta_t proportional to eta * exp(-lambda * rho_t), where lambda controls damping sensitivity. A key theoretical result (Corollary A.2) shows that the effective damping alpha_t >= alpha_min > 0, ensuring updates never stall completely. In smooth regions where rho -> 0, alpha -> 1, recovering standard Adam.

**S-Adam algorithm:** The full algorithm adds a probing step to the standard Adam loop: (1) sample k random directions, (2) compute directional derivatives via additional forward passes, (3) estimate LGI as their variance, (4) compute the damped step size, (5) perform the standard Adam update with the modified learning rate. The parameter k controls the trade-off between LGI estimation fidelity and computational cost (k=2 for speed, k=8 for accuracy).

**Convergence proof structure:**
1. Cast S-Adam as a stochastic approximation of a differential inclusion, where the drift map F(x) is in -alpha(x)*H(x) with H(x) the Clarke-valued mean Adam direction
2. Show the affine interpolation is an asymptotic pseudotrajectory (APT) of the mean-field DI using Benaim et al. (2006) conditions
3. Establish f as a strict Lyapunov function using the chain rule for path-differentiable functions, yielding df/dt <= -alpha_min * c1 * dist(0, partial_C f(x))^2
4. Apply the Lyapunov invariance principle to show limit sets are contained in the Clarke stationary set

## Results & Highlights

**Experiment 1: Quantization-Aware Training (QAT)**
- Customized CNN with 2/4/8-bit quantization of weights and activations
- Simulated Quantization Mapping creates discrete steps that induce gradient chattering
- CIFAR-100: S-Adam (k=8) achieves 18.6% accuracy vs. Prox-SGD collapsing to 5% and AdamW degrading after epoch 3
- TinyImageNet: S-Adam reaches 23.2% through steady improvement while baselines stagnate/decline
- QAT loss trajectories: S-Adam maintains stable loss ~3.4-3.6 on CIFAR-100, while Prox-SGD rebounds to ~4.6 with large oscillations

**Experiment 2: High-Noise Small-Batch Learning (ResNet-18, BS=2)**
- Maximum gains on Imagewoof2-160: S-Adam (k=8) at 75.87% vs. AdamW at 51.18% (+24.69 points)
- CIFAR-100: 56.11% vs. 51.12% (+4.99); CIFAR-10: 86.13% vs. 80.48% (+5.65)
- At k=2, S-Adam is faster than AdamW on every dataset (e.g., 2136s vs. 2317s on CIFAR-100)

**Batch size scaling (Table 4):**
- BS=4: Imagewoof2-160 S-Adam 81.22% vs. AdamW 56.25% (+25 points)
- BS=16: CIFAR-100 S-Adam 74.43% vs. Prox-SGD 68.09% (+6.34)
- BS=64: advantage narrows as landscape smooths, but S-Adam still leads

**Learning rate sensitivity (Table 5):**
- S-Adam wins at every tested LR (1e-4, 5e-4, 1e-3), improving over AdamW by +4.02, +8.28, and +4.99 points respectively
- Best S-Adam run (70.87% at lr=1e-4) exceeds best AdamW run (66.85%)

**Key observation:** S-Adam's advantage is largest in small-batch, high-instability regimes and narrows as geometry smooths (larger batch sizes), consistent with damping driven by local geometric instability rather than gradient noise magnitude alone.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (High):** The core insight — using randomized directional derivative variance as a diagnostic probe for Clarke subdifferential diameter rather than as a gradient surrogate — is genuinely novel. This paradigm shift from smoothing the objective to probing the geometry is a clean conceptual contribution. The specific mechanism (LGI + exp(-lambda*rho) damping) is straightforward but well-motivated.
- **Rigor (High):** The convergence analysis is thorough and uses the correct mathematical framework (differential inclusions for non-smooth optimization). Four explicit assumptions are stated: local Lipschitz continuity, bounded iterates, Robbins-Monro step sizes, and moment estimator consistency. The three-step proof (APT construction, strict Lyapunov descent, invariance principle) follows established methodology from Benaim (1996). The guarantee that alpha_t >= alpha_min > 0 prevents pathological stalling.
- **Reproducibility (Medium-High):** Algorithm details are clearly specified with k (number of probes) as the main hyperparameter. Standard benchmarks used. However, the full pseudocode (Algorithm box) was on a page that could not be extracted from the PDF, and the exact computational overhead per step is discussed but not precisely quantified as a fraction of training time.
- **Significance (High):** If results generalize, this has strong practical impact for quantization-aware training and any non-smooth optimization setting. The +24.69 improvement on Imagewoof2-160 at BS=2 is striking. However, the advantage diminishes with larger batch sizes, suggesting the method is most valuable in resource-constrained settings.
- **Red flags (Minor):** (1) The headline "+6%" and "+3%" claims from the abstract understate the actual gains in some settings but overstate them in others — the actual improvements vary significantly by configuration. (2) QAT results on CIFAR-100 show very low absolute accuracy (18.6%), suggesting extreme quantization difficulty rather than practical utility. (3) The computational overhead of k additional forward passes per step is acknowledged but could be prohibitive for very large models. (4) Evaluation is limited to image classification; no NLP or generative model experiments.

## Strengths

- Clean theoretical framework connecting Clarke subdifferential geometry to practical optimizer design
- Optimal O(1/sqrt(T)) convergence rate established under clearly stated assumptions
- The LGI metric requires no Hessian computation, making it scalable
- S-Adam degrades gracefully to standard Adam in smooth regions (rho -> 0 implies alpha -> 1)
- Comprehensive ablation studies: batch size sensitivity, learning rate sensitivity, k-probe tradeoff
- Consistent improvements across all tested configurations, not just cherry-picked settings
- The paradigm of using randomized smoothing as a diagnostic probe rather than a gradient surrogate is elegant and potentially generalizable

## Limitations

- Computational overhead: k additional forward passes per training step (k=2 is fast but lower fidelity; k=8 gives best accuracy but doubles the forward-pass cost)
- Evaluation limited to image classification with ResNet-18 and a custom CNN; no experiments on large-scale models (Vision Transformers, LLMs) or other tasks
- QAT CIFAR-100 results show very low absolute accuracy (~18.6%), suggesting the benchmark is in an extreme regime where all optimizers struggle
- The advantage narrows significantly at larger batch sizes (BS=64), limiting applicability in standard training regimes
- The paper does not compare against more recent adaptive optimizers (e.g., Adafactor, Lion, Sophia) beyond AdamW and Prox-SGD
- The number of hyperparameters (k, lambda) adds tuning complexity; while ablations show robustness, optimal settings may vary across tasks
- No wall-clock time comparison normalized to equivalent compute budget (i.e., comparing S-Adam k=8 with its extra forward passes against AdamW allowed more iterations)

## Relevance

本文针对深度学习中的非光滑优化问题提出了原理清晰、理论严格且实证有效的S-Adam优化器，与量化感知训练、高效训练和优化理论设计直接相关。其LGI指标作为非光滑性诊断工具的思路具有更广泛的适用价值，对于关注模型压缩或训练稳定性的研究者值得深入阅读。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does the computational overhead of k forward passes scale with model size (e.g., billion-parameter transformers), and is there a way to amortize this cost (e.g., computing LGI every N steps)?
- Can the LGI metric be computed more efficiently using cheap gradient approximations or subset of parameters rather than full forward passes?
- Does S-Adam provide benefits for training large language models where ReLU/GELU activations and potential quantization create non-smooth landscapes?
- Could the LGI metric serve as a generalization predictor, analogous to sharpness-based measures like SAM?
- How does S-Adam interact with modern training techniques like gradient accumulation, mixed precision, and distributed data parallel training?
- Is the advantage over AdamW primarily due to the damping mechanism, or does the LGI-guided step size provide benefits beyond what simple gradient clipping or LR warmup could achieve?
