Why Larger Models Learn More

Smaller models fail on rare tasks because high-frequency tasks monopolize neurons via gradient interference, starving low-frequency and complex tasks. Larger models overcome this through a residual-controlled mechanism: once enough capacity is allocated to common tasks, their gradient signal weakens, allowing rare-task features to accumulate without being overwritten.

Why This Matters

Model scaling is not merely about fitting more data — it is an irreducible requirement for learning rare capabilities. When α > γ, no amount of additional data lets a small model match a large one. This reframes compute budgets: capacity determines which tasks are learnable at all, not just how fast they are learned.

Critical Edge

Only two algorithmic tasks (comparison, modular addition) were injected into OLMo pretraining. The 4B parameter ceiling is modest, and the theoretical framework assumes orthogonal task blocks — real tasks share features. Whether these dynamics hold at frontier scale (70B+) for complex natural language capabilities remains unverified.

Utility ordering
Width-N encoder spans the top-N eigenspace. Features learned in order of utility = freq × eigenvalue. High-freq, low-complexity tasks win.

Interference bottleneck
Common-task gradient norm bounded by residual signal. Small model: residual stays large → rare features overwritten. Large model: residual → 0.

Rare-feature stability
Rare feature stable iff its utility exceeds the weakest occupied common-feature utility. Below critical width: update-and-forget dynamics.
Theorem 3 — Utility-Ordered Learning
Optimal encoder spans top-N eigenspace of weighted covariance. Features acquired in strict utility order.
Theorem 4 — Residual-Controlled Interference
Common-task gradient norm bounded by unresolved residual. Larger model → weaker residual → less interference.
Proposition 6 — Critical Width
Rare feature stable iff pi_r · lambda_r > mu_N^F. Derives the minimum width for rare-task learning.

01
Phenomenological Argument
From L(N,D) = L_0 + A/Nα + B/Dβ, compute-optimal scaling gives LC ∝ N-γ while asymptotic gives L∞ ∝ N-α. Since α > γ, a genuine capacity gap exists.
02
Synthetic Multi-Task Regression
K=32 linear tasks with power-law frequencies and spectra. Shared width-N encoder with task-specific decoders. Closed-form analysis; trained with AdamW for 100K steps.
03
OLMo Pretraining Validation
4M to 4B params on Dolma v1.7 (210B tokens). Injected comparison (T_CMP) and modular addition (T_ADD) at controlled frequencies. Behavioral, representational (DAS, Fourier), and gradient-level analysis.

✓ Strengths

  • Exceptionally well-structured: theory → synthetic → large-scale, each layer building on the previous.
  • Formal theorems with clean proofs yielding quantitative, testable predictions (critical widths, residual thresholds).
  • Multi-level OLMo evidence: behavioral, representational (DAS, Fourier), and gradient-level.
  • Matched-frequency injection protocol isolates retention from frequency confounds — clever experimental design.
  • Extensive appendices with sweeps over complexity, frequency, and training horizons up to 1M steps.
  • Open-source models and clear descriptions enhance reproducibility.

✗ Limitations

  • Only two algorithmic injected tasks; generality to diverse natural language capabilities is unverified.
  • 4B parameter ceiling limits conclusions about frontier-scale dynamics (70B+).
  • Linear regression model assumes orthogonal task blocks; real networks have shared, nonlinear representations.
  • No exploration of interventions: data reweighting, curriculum learning, MoE architectures.
  • Task complexity operationalized only in synthetic setting (spectral decay rate).
  • Injected tasks are artificial and may not reflect statistical properties of natural rare tasks.

? Open Questions

  1. Can data reweighting or curriculum learning fully substitute for model scaling, or is there an irreducible capacity requirement for rare tasks?
  2. Do gradient interference dynamics and critical-width thresholds hold at 70B+ scales where emergent capabilities appear?
  3. Does shared task structure in real data mitigate or exacerbate interference vs. the orthogonal assumption?
  4. Could MoE architectures naturally allocate separate experts to rare vs. common tasks, bypassing the interference bottleneck?
  5. What is the crossover point where frequent tasks transition from memorization to generalization, and does scaling affect this transition?
  6. How do these findings relate to continual learning and catastrophic forgetting? Are the same mitigation strategies applicable?
  7. For natural language tasks with Zipf-distributed frequencies, what model sizes does Proposition 6 predict for specific capabilities?
