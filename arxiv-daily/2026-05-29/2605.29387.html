On the Optimizer Dependence of Neural Scaling Laws

Vansh Ramani & Shourya Vir Jain — IIT Delhi

The scaling exponent α in neural scaling laws is not a fixed constant — it depends systematically on the optimizer. Preconditioned optimizers (natural gradient, Muon proxy) boost α from 0.12 to 0.31 under natural-language spectral conditions, a 2.6× improvement that compounds with every model-size doubling.

Why This Matters
Scaling law predictions that ignore optimizer choice are unreliable.
The paper provides a spectral diagnostic: measure the eigenvalue decay exponent s from your data's embedding covariance. If s < 0.3 (flat spectrum), advanced optimizers bring negligible scaling gains. If s > 0.8 (steep, as in NLP), preconditioners like Muon can meaningfully shift your scaling curve.
Critical Edge
Random-feature regression ≠ real LLM training. The advantage may shrink at scale.
Results are in the “lazy” regime: no feature learning, no depth, no attention. Recent 1.2B-parameter evidence (Wen et al., 2025) shows Muon's advantage attenuating from ~1.4× to ~1.1×. Whether the α-shift survives in real transformer training at scale remains unproven.

Methodology

Framework

Random-feature regression (Maloney 2022; Bordelon 2024) — the canonical solvable model for neural scaling laws. Inputs x ∈ R1000 drawn from N(0, Σ) with eigenvalues λi = i-(1+s). Teacher: 100 random ReLU features (source exponent b = 1.0). Student: N random features with trainable top-layer weights only — making the problem linear regression where optimizers differ only as preconditioners.

Protocol

8 model sizes (N = 25–5000), 6 spectral conditions (s = 0.25–2.0), 10 seeds each, T = 2000 steps. Full NG uses one-step Newton (Cholesky + Tikhonov). α fit by OLS on (log N, log L) for N ≥ 200. 2,400 main runs + 180 robustness runs, CPU-only, under $20 total.

Results & Highlights

  • Curves fan out at s = 1.0: preconditioned optimizers achieve lower loss at every N with visibly steeper slopes; at s = 0.5, curves are nearly parallel.
  • Consistent optimizer ordering: αFullNG ≈ αMatSign > αDiag > αSign ≈ αGD across all spectral conditions.
  • Power-law fits: R2 > 0.95 for all (optimizer, s) pairs except Sign-GD at large s (R2 < 0.5).
  • Full NG (direct solve) ≈ Matrix-Sign (2000 iterative steps): same α, confirming spectral preconditioning — not convergence speed — drives the ordering.
  • Sign-GD negative control: destroys gradient magnitude information, produces noisy trajectories, no α improvement. Key mechanism is spectral equalization, not generic adaptivity.

Strengths

  • Exceptionally clean experimental design: controlled comparisons, shared data realizations, Sign-GD negative control.
  • Clear mechanistic story: spectral equalization, not generic adaptivity, drives the α improvement.
  • Honest discussion of limitations, especially scale-dependent decay (Appendix J).
  • Provides falsifiable prediction and practical spectral diagnostic tool.
  • Low compute cost, high reproducibility (~600 CPU-hours, <$20).

Limitations

  • Random-feature model omits feature learning, depth, attention, and tokenization — all central to real LLM training.
  • Width-only scaling; depth scaling involves qualitatively different phenomena.
  • Recent evidence (Wen et al., 2025): Muon advantage attenuates from ~1.4× at 100M to ~1.1× at 1.2B parameters.
  • Proposed GPT-2 validation has not been conducted (exceeds workshop budget).
  • Absolute α values differ from real LLM scaling; only relative ordering is claimed as robust.

Open Questions

Can a closed-form derivation of α(s, b, P) be obtained via resolvent-trace analysis in the proportional-asymptotics limit?
How does feature learning in the “rich” regime interact with preconditioning — does it amplify or dampen the α-shift?
Can the spectral diagnostic predict Muon vs. AdamW ROI on real training data before committing compute?
What drives the scale-dependent decay of the optimizer advantage? Does feature learning substitute for explicit preconditioning at scale?
How do results change with depth scaling and residual connections in deep networks?
Would the proposed GPT-2 validation (standard vs. spectrum-flattened data with Muon/AdamW) confirm the falsifiable prediction?
