NuclearQAv2: A Structured Benchmark for Evaluating Domain-Science Competence in Large Language Models

Henry Shaowu Yuchi, Michal Kucer, Benjamin H. Sims, Selma Peterson, et al. · Los Alamos National Laboratory · 2026-06-25
Key Finding

Frontier LLMs ace factual recall but collapse on quantitative reasoning — exposing single-metric benchmarks as dangerously flattering in technical domains.

Why This Matters

Aggregate accuracy hides where models truly fail; in high-stakes engineering, numeric reasoning and conceptual clarity must be measured separately — not averaged away.

Critical Edge
SHARPEST CRITIQUE

The same Llama 3 70B that built the questions also judges them — a circularity the benchmark's strongest claims quietly depend on.

Methodology

NuclearQAv2 is defined as a dataset D = {(qi, ai, ti)} where each item carries a question, reference answer, and type t ∈ {boolean, numeric, verbal}. Five construction principles enforce single-type assignment, domain relevance, unambiguous context-independent answers, concise expected answers, and answerability without the source text.

All content derives from a single nuclear engineering textbook, parsed into structured text (including math) with Nougat. Boolean items partly come from domain experts and textbook facts; numeric items are drawn from textbook exercises spanning a difficulty range; verbal and remaining boolean items are generated automatically by Llama 3 70B Instruct under structured prompts encoding the design principles, with a manual filtering pass to remove invalid or ambiguous items.

Evaluation is type-specific and deterministic: normalized exact match for boolean, relative-tolerance accuracy (|â−a|/|a| ≤ 0.15) for numeric, and a fixed single-prompt LLM-judge returning correct/incorrect for verbal answers based on semantic similarity. Each model is run three times to estimate mean accuracy and standard error.

Frontier models cluster near 0.8 on boolean (factual recall is comparatively mature), but numeric tasks show the widest spread and verbal articulation clearly benefits from scale. Model scale alone does not close the boolean→numeric gap — pointing toward domain fine-tuning or tool integration rather than raw size.

Strengths

  • Clear three-axis competence decomposition (factual / quantitative / conceptual) that surfaces differences invisible to single-format benchmarks.
  • Genuinely hybrid, scalable construction: expert quality plus automated coverage, with a mostly-automated reproducible evaluation protocol.
  • Strong domain credibility from Los Alamos authors and real expert involvement in authoring and review.
  • Honest multi-model results table spanning nine current proprietary and open-weight systems.

Limitations

  • Built from a single textbook — narrow breadth and possible train/test contamination from a public source.
  • 60% boolean composition inflates aggregate accuracy and underrepresents the harder numeric/verbal skills the paper cares about.
  • Numeric tolerance (ε=0.15) is generous; verbal grading rests on one LLM judge with no reported human-agreement calibration.
  • Text-only: no multimodal items despite schematics and decay curves being central to nuclear engineering.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Would stricter numeric tolerances reorder the leaderboard — and do wrong answers stem from formula, unit, or arithmetic errors?
  2. What is the human-vs-model agreement of the verbal judge, and how sensitive are scores to judge-model and prompt wording?
  3. Is there contamination from the public textbook, and how do results shift with held-out or paraphrased questions?
  4. Can the benchmark extend to multimodal content (reactor schematics, decay curves) and tool-/retrieval-augmented evaluation?
  5. How does equal per-type weighting (vs. count-weighting) change aggregate rankings given the boolean-heavy mix?
