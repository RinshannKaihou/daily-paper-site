Auditing Framing-Sensitive Behavioral Instability in Large Language Models for Mental Health Interactions

Abla Bedoui · Ashley L. Greene · Mohammed Cherkaoui · Long Island University · 2026-06-25
LLM mental-health-AI mechanistic-interpretability activation-steering behavioral-calibration trustworthy-AI probing
Key Finding

Non-adversarial contextual framing systematically shifts LLM mental-health interpretive routing — decodable across transformer layers and partially steerable.

Key Metric

Why This Matters
A full audit paradigm linking prompt-framing sensitivity from behavior → representation → causal steering — a direct template for LLM consistency and robustness evaluation.
Critical Edge
The TF-IDF lexical baseline (0.89–0.94) far exceeds the held-out probe (0.73–0.83) — most decodable signal is surface lexical, not abstract framing — and no inter-annotator κ is reported.

Methodology

A four-module pipeline runs over a shared matched-prompt set. Matched Prompt Construction yields 653 psychologically realistic but semantically stable ambiguous-support scenarios, each rewritten into 6 framing variants (Base, Documentation, Epistemic, Institutional, Liability, Role) with communicative intent held constant — deliberately avoiding crisis, self-harm, diagnosis, or jailbreak language.

Behavioral Annotation uses a 4-category scheme (Weak/Disengaged, Restrained-Supportive, Interpretive-Supportive, Escalated Interpretation) collapsed to a binary interpretive-routing label, with a psychology co-author reviewing a subset and logistic regression testing framing effects (Wald tests, p<0.05).

Hidden-State Extraction pulls the final residual-stream activation of the last pre-EOS response token from every layer. Layer-wise Probing uses balanced logistic regression with grouped 5-fold CV (preventing semantic leakage) plus two controls — random-label permutation and a TF-IDF lexical baseline — and a held-out framing generalization test.

Activation Steering derives CAA contrastive directions and applies h' = h − αd at high-decoding layers with α ∈ {0, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0} across 50 balanced prompts per model.

Strengths

  • Clean matched-prompt design with grouped CV that genuinely isolates framing from semantics.
  • Multiple rigorous probe controls (random-label, lexical, held-out) that honestly bound the abstraction claim.
  • Pragmatic framing taxonomy (documentation / institutional / liability) drawn from real healthcare workflows.
  • Cautious, non-overclaimed interpretation — steering positioned as preliminary, not a complete mechanism.
  • Cross-architecture breadth (7 models, 4 families) with relative-depth normalization.

Limitations

  • Lexical confound dominates: TF-IDF baseline (0.89–0.94) far exceeds held-out probe.
  • Small steering sample (50/model), no CI / multiple-comparison correction across 7×5 tests.
  • No inter-annotator agreement (κ) for the 4-category scheme; only a subset reviewed.
  • Circularity: models generate the labels decoded from their own hidden states.
  • No validated "correct" calibration — "instability" is relative, not a safety verdict.
  • Scale ceiling at 9B; no frontier/proprietary models.

Open Questions

  1. Would the held-out vs. TF-IDF probe gap shrink — or reverse — if prompts were paraphrased to equalize lexical overlap across framings?
  2. Does the institutional-framing "suppression" effect hold at frontier scale (GPT-4 / Claude-class), or is it an artifact of small open-weight alignment?
  3. Can steering directions transfer across architectures, or are they family-specific, as the paper hints?
  4. What is the causal link between the decodable latent direction and user-facing outcomes like trust calibration, measured in a real human study?
  5. Could a sparse-autoencoder decomposition find cleaner single-direction framings (cf. Arditi's refusal direction)?
