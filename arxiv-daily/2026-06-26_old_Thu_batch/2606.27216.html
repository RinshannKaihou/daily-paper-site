Hierarchical Muon: Tiled Newton-Schulz Updates for Efficient Muon Optimization

Ziyuan Tang · Tianshi Xu · Yousef Saad · Yuanzhe Xi

muon-optimizer newton-schulz llm-training gpu-kernels matrix-function

Per-tile Newton-Schulz maps cut Muon's leading optimizer cost from O(HW·min{H,W}·K) to O(HW·T·K), with Qwen3 loss staying close to full-matrix Muon.

2026-06-25 · University of Minnesota & Emory University
Key Metric
1.4–2.9×
Per-layer Newton-Schulz speedup on real Qwen3 block-0 weight shapes at T=512 (0.6B / 1.7B / 4B).
Why This Matters
Directly relieves the compute & communication bottleneck of Muon-class optimizers in LLM pretraining, with fp8/bf16 robustness for accelerator deployment.
⚠ Critical Edge
End-to-end comparisons are single-seed, 1000-step runs — too short to prove quality parity — and headline speedups come from the small-T regime where the capacity diagnostic shows the steepest degradation.

Methodology

For a momentum-gradient matrix G ∈ ℝ^{H×W}, HiMuon pads it to a grid of R×C T×T tiles, applies the same quintic Newton-Schulz recurrence (coefficients (3.4445, −4.7750, 2.0315), K=5 steps, Frobenius-normalized X₀) independently to each tile, then reassembles. The tile-size-dependent RMS scalar is τ(T)=c√T (c=0.2). Momentum (β=0.95), Nesterov lookahead, and decoupled weight decay are inherited unchanged from Muon.

Because tiling does not commute with the polar factor, the tiled map ΦK,T is a distinct local matrix-function update — not a convergent approximation to full-matrix Muon — so spectral coupling survives within tiles and is severed across tile boundaries. Cost drops from O(HW·min{H,W}·K) to O(HW·T·K).

GPU plan: (i) tile-size-dependent dispatch — large tiles use a multi-kernel Triton path with epilogue fusion; small tiles (≈T=128 on A40) use an SRAM-resident single kernel keeping all K NS iterations on-chip; (ii) cross-layer batching of compatible tiles (under FSDP, tiled NS is rank-local — no optimizer-internal all-gather); (iii) memory-bounded chunking; (iv) a runtime reconfigure() routine that changes T mid-training (e.g. ∞→512→128) without resetting momentum buffers.

Strengths

  • Rigorous framing of tile size as a local matrix-function map — explicit non-commutativity, no hand-waving about approximation.
  • SRAM-resident small-tile kernel is a genuine engineering win, turning arithmetic savings into wall-clock savings.
  • Associative-memory capacity diagnostic cleanly isolates the update map from LR/stochasticity confounds.
  • Restrained claims; single-seed & short-run caveats stated openly; public code & scripts.
  • Timely fp8e4m3 robustness study for accelerator deployment.

Limitations

  • Only up to 4B params, 1000 steps, single seed — quality-parity claim is under-powered.
  • No theory for the tiled map: no convergence or approximation-error guarantee.
  • Pareto frontier unresolved: biggest speedups come from small T where quality degrades most.
  • Hardware coverage limited to A40/L40S; no H100/B200, no TP/PP at scale.
  • GQA blind spot: k/v-proj gain little; schedule treats all layers uniformly.
  • Cross-layer batching adds +22–25% memory in the regimes where Muon cost matters most.

Questions for Further Exploration

Does training-loss parity survive multi-seed, >50k-step pretraining at 7B–70B scale, or does the T=128 gap surface downstream?
Can the tile schedule (switch points and T values) be chosen adaptively from gradient statistics rather than hand-tuned?
What is the formal approximation theory of ΦK,T vs ΦK — spectral-norm error as a function of T and off-tile mass?
How does HiMuon interact with tensor/pipeline parallelism beyond DDP/FSDP?
Could per-layer non-uniform tile sizes exploit GQA structure (large T for q/o/MLP, full-matrix for narrow k/v-proj)?
Is the fp8e4m3 robustness specific to the K=5 quintic map, and does it hold for higher-K or alternative coefficients?
