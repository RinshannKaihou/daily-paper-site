LMs as Task-Specific Knowledge Bases: An Interpretability Analysis

The same fact is stored in task-specific, disjoint parameter subsets — not as one task-invariant source of truth.

Amit Elhelo · Amir Globerson · Mor Geva / Tel Aviv University & Google Research / 2026-06-26
llm_interpretability mechanistic_interpretability knowledge_representation factual_knowledge chain_of_thought
Key Metric
47.9%
of (fact, task) pairs fail to co-emerge as a true knowledge base would predict — tracked across 105 OLMo-3-7B checkpoints.
Why This Matters
Undermines the “LM-as-knowledge-base” analogy — explaining why knowledge editing, unlearning, and single-format evaluation can be quietly unreliable.
Critical Edge
Mask disjointness is partly forced. Masks are trained sequentially with earlier components excluded from later ones, so the “disjoint storage” result is not fully independent evidence.

Methodology

The paper triangulates four analyses to test whether a language model satisfies the task-invariance property — the knowledge-base expectation that one fact draws on one source of truth regardless of query format. It formalizes task-invariance as a fact × task conditional-independence condition and tests it from complementary angles.

01 · Behavioral

Co-emergence across checkpoints

Defines the first reliable-retrieval step E(f,t) (correct-probability > θ=0.6) and tests whether a fact emerges consistently across all task formats. Run on OLMo-3-7B IT over 105 checkpoints, 5 relational datasets × 6 tasks × 10 paraphrases, down-sampled to 230 facts. Independence is tested via two-way ANOVA at every checkpoint.

02 · Mechanistic

Localized binary masks

Extends Bayazit et al. (2024): learns a straight-through-estimator mask over MLP neurons & attention heads, minimizing necessity + sufficiency + specificity + β·sparsity (β=10). Sufficiency uses subject→placeholder activation patching (Yona et al. 2026). Tested on OLMo-2-7B/13B & Gemma-2-9B IT, 5 datasets, 5 train / 2 held-out paraphrases.

Two further analyses quantify cross-task entanglement (a score combining target drop and collateral change on both fact and task axes) and run a CoT ablation study on Gemma-2-9B IT comparing direct vs. chain-of-thought accuracy under zero-ablation of a task’s own encoding versus other tasks’ encodings.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Conceptually clean framing — task-invariance as the testable KB property, with formal definitions.
  • Triangulation across behavioral, mechanistic, and reasoning-based evidence — rare and convincing.
  • Strong reproducibility — public OLMo checkpoints, released code/data, fixed hyperparameters.
  • Actionable implications for knowledge editing, unlearning, and multi-format evaluation.
  • Honest limitations section engaging with the redundancy-vs-organization distinction.

Limitations

  • Only relational, well-known facts; high baseline threshold filters out the long tail.
  • Sequential mask training forces disjointness — somewhat circular evidence.
  • Redundancy uncharacterized; sufficiency patching is not a “full encoding” proof.
  • Limited scale (7B–13B); scaling behavior unknown.
  • CoT main-text results rest on one dataset/model pair.
  • Causal role of training-data format is hypothesized, not tested.

Questions for Further Exploration

Does task-invariance violation increase or decrease at 70B+ / frontier scale? The authors hypothesize worse — is this confirmed?
Does task-specificity originate from pretraining format? Can multi-format augmentation induce more invariant storage?
Can the entanglement metric predict edit robustness, enabling more reliable editing / unlearning?
Is the CoT-routing finding specific to relational facts, or does it extend to procedural / commonsense / long-tail knowledge?
How does task-specific storage interact with cross-lingual storage — a unified “format-specific” principle?
Can a consistency training objective across paraphrased formats improve editing reliability without hurting capability?
