# DMuon: Efficient Distributed Muon Training with Near-Adam Overhead

> **一句话总结：** DMuon 是一个分布式 Muon 优化器实现，通过"owner-centric"执行、形状自适应 Gram Newton-Schulz 内核与测量驱动的负载均衡，把分片训练中 Muon 的每步开销压缩到接近 AdamW 水平（平均仅高 ~2%），同时严格保留 Muon 的收敛语义。

> **TL;DR:** DMuon is an open-source, drop-in distributed Muon optimizer that, by eliminating redundant Newton-Schulz (NS) computation via per-matrix owner assignment and hiding the rest behind hierarchical, pipelined communication plus shape-specialized symmetric kernels, brings Muon's per-step wall-clock within ~2% of AdamW on average (1.48x-3.01x faster end-to-end than vanilla gather-then-compute Muon) on real embodied-foundation-model and LLM training workloads.

## 核心贡献

- **细粒度通信优化：** 提出 owner-centric 执行模型——每个权重矩阵只在一个 owner rank 上做一次 NS 正交化，配合基于 XOR 规则的 fine-grained 权重布局，把 owner-to-all 广播与 all-to-owner 归约组织成 intra-node / inter-node 两级、并与前向/反向计算重叠。
- **形状自适应执行栈：** 采用 Gram Newton-Schulz 公式（把迭代留在 m×m Gram 空间）、SYRK 风格对称内核（只算下三角，主积算术量减半）、对小矩阵做 batched NS、并用 TileLang / CUTE DSL 做按形状的自动调优并持久化缓存。
- **计算感知的负载均衡：** 把 owner 分配形式化为 measured-cost 的混合整数线性规划（MILP），用运行期 profiling 直接测量每种 (shape, batch) 的真实执行时间，最小化预测 makespan；变量超阈值时回退贪心。
- **即插即用模块：** 三行代码（`dedicate_params` + `Muon` + state-dict 访问器）即可替换 AdamW，保持标准 PyTorch 优化器协议，与 stock FSDP2 / HSDP / 张量并行组合无需源码改动，且保持精确 Muon 语义。
- **生产规模验证：** 在 WALL-OSS-0.5、Pi0、Wall-WM、Qwen2.5-7B 四个负载上，跨 8–256 张 A800 验证，端到端 step 平均比 AdamW 仅慢 ~2%，相对 vanilla gather-then-compute Muon 端到端提速 1.48x–3.01x、optimizer-step 提速 6.85x–163x。

## Key Contributions

- **Fine-grained communication optimization:** An owner-centric execution model in which each weight matrix is orthogonalized exactly once on a designated owner rank, plus a fine-grained (bitwise-XOR) weight layout that spreads consecutive matrix publications across inter-node columns to reduce collective contention and enable two-stage, pipelined forward/backward overlap.
- **Shape-adaptive execution stack:** A high-performance kernel engine combining (a) the Gram Newton-Schulz formulation (iteration stays in the m×m Gram space, dropping dominant cost from O(m²n) to O(m³)), (b) a SYRK-style symmetric kernel that computes only the lower triangle, (c) batched NS for under-occupying small matrices, and (d) DSL-driven (TileLang/CUTE) per-shape autotuning with a persistent kernel cache.
- **Computation-aware load balancing:** Owner assignment is cast as a MILP minimizing predicted makespan over a *measured* (not analytical) execution-cost model `c_{s,b}` benchmarked once at init; falls back to greedy when the decision-space exceeds a threshold.
- **Drop-in distributed module:** A standalone Python module (~10K LoC) exposing three calls that preserves the PyTorch optimizer protocol, nests a TP-owner level under the DP-owner level for tensor parallelism, and composes with stock FSDP2 without source-level disruption.
- **Production-scale validation:** Validated end-to-end on robotics (WALL-OSS-0.5, Pi0) and LLM (Wall-WM, Qwen2.5-7B) training across 8–256 A800-80GB GPUs, achieving average step-time within +2% of AdamW and up to 3.01x / 163x speedups over vanilla distributed Muon.

## Methodology

DMuon attacks the core "granularity mismatch": sharded stacks (ZeRO / FSDP / TP) expose parameters in pieces, but Muon's Newton-Schulz (NS) iteration needs the full reduced-matrix gradient on a single device to compute X X^T. A naive gather-then-compute baseline forces every rank to materialize every full matrix and redundantly run the same NS — at scale the optimizer step can rival or exceed the combined forward+backward cost.

DMuon's four-phase per-step design:
1. **Parameter materialization** — owner-held weights are materialized into packed buffers right before the layer that consumes them (lookahead-issued inter-node, then local intra-node), preserving FSDP-style transient peak memory.
2. **Gradient routing** — backward gradients are reduced *only to owners*, producing the same averaged full-matrix gradient a synchronous Muon reference would use, but materialized only at the owner.
3. **Owner-side Muon update** — each owner runs the Gram-space NS for its assigned matrices in fp16 (3 more mantissa bits than bf16 at equal tensor-core cost, validated by wgrad dynamic-range analysis), updates fp32 master weights, casts back to bf16. Non-matrix params fall through to the host stack's AdamW.
4. **Asynchronous publication** — updated weights are published back from owners, scheduled asynchronously and waited on only at the consuming layer's pre-forward hook.

The owner-side NS is the dominant residual cost and is optimized three ways: (i) **Gram NS** reduces arithmetic to symmetric G = XX^T plus a polynomial in G; (ii) a **SYRK-style symmetric kernel** computes only the lower triangle with the elementwise follow-up fused into the epilogue; (iii) **batching + autotuning** groups sub-occupying small matrices and picks per-shape optimal tile/pipeline schedules via TileLang/CUTE DSLs, cached by problem shape.

Owner assignment is a MILP (solved once with SciPy) minimizing `max_r Σ c_{s,b} x_{s,b,r}` subject to all matrices of each shape being assigned exactly once; the cost coefficients come from one-time hardware benchmarks rather than FLOP proxies, because batching/autotuning make analytical cost models inaccurate.

## Results & Highlights

- **End-to-end step time within ~2% of AdamW** on average across all four workloads (ΔA column); on Pi0 it is occasionally *faster* than AdamW at small GPU counts (Muon allows larger batch under the same memory budget).
- **1.48x–3.01x end-to-end / 6.85x–163.00x optimizer-step speedup** over vanilla gather-then-compute distributed Muon (Muon-AG). Speedups grow with GPU count and matrix scale.
- **Wall-OSS-0.5 at 256 GPUs:** DMuon 1519ms vs AdamW 1496ms (+1.5%) vs Muon-AG 2857ms (1.88x).
- **Qwen2.5-7B at 256 GPUs:** DMuon 2850ms vs AdamW 2844ms (+0.2%) vs Muon-AG 6219ms (2.18x); optimizer step 163.82x faster.
- **Component attribution (Wall-OSS-0.5 @ 128 GPUs):** symmetric Gram kernel = 48% of the optimizer-time reduction, owner scheduling + load balancing = 32%, autotuning + NS batching = 16%.
- Small near-square matrices (e.g. 1024×1024) run up to ~3x faster per-matrix at batch 16 vs batch 1 via occupancy recovery; large rectangular weights already saturate the GPU and gain little.
- TP support: a second-level TP owner is nested inside the DP owner slot, detected by inspecting DTensor Shard placements at hook-registration time (re-read, not cached) so PyTorch DTensor internals changes do not break it.

## Authors & Credibility

- Authored by the **X Square Robot team** (Vincent Chen as project lead, Hao Wang corresponding). The team concurrently produces WALL-OSS-0.5 and WALL-WM (both cited as the production workloads validated here), indicating genuine production-scale use rather than synthetic benchmarks.
- Positioning is carefully scoped: the paper *explicitly* states DMuon "does not propose a new optimizer" and "inherits Muon's convergence exactly" — it removes only systems overhead. This honesty about scope is a positive signal.
- Builds on and credits recent prior art (Gram Newton-Schulz by Zhang et al./Dao Lab, Polar Express coefficients, Distributed Shampoo's owner-compute paradigm, Canzona) rather than overclaiming novelty.
- Open-sourced at https://github.com/X-Square-Robot/dmuon, with clear three-call interface, improving reproducibility.
- The publication itself is an arXiv tech report (cs.DC) dated June 2026, not (yet) peer-reviewed; numbers are vendor-internal workloads on an A800 cluster.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty — Moderate.** The individual techniques (owner-compute, Gram NS, SYRK symmetric GEMM, tile-DSL autotuning, MILP load balancing) are each known. The contribution is the *integration* and the fine-grained communication-overlap runtime specifically engineered for PyTorch FSDP2/HSDP. Compared to the most directly related concurrent work, Canzona (Megatron-style, 1.57x on Qwen3-32B @ 256 GPUs), DMuon targets FSDP2/HSDP and claims higher speedups — but on *different* models and baselines, so head-to-head is not possible from the text alone.
- **Rigor — Good.** Preserves exact Muon semantics by construction (owner receives the same averaged full-matrix gradient); ablations isolate each component (Table 2); scaling swept 8–256 GPUs on four real workloads; precision choice (fp16 NS) justified empirically via wgrad dynamic-range analysis.
- **Reproducibility — Strong intent, partial execution.** Open-source code, precise interface, named coefficient set (Polar Express, k=5). However: no public configs/hyperparameters for the four production runs, no measurement methodology for timing (warm-up iterations, variance/error bars), and the hardware (A800-SXM4-80GB, 8/node, NVLink + 200 Gb/s IB) is specific. MILP solver fallback threshold `S_thr` and occupancy threshold are given qualitatively but not numerically.
- **Significance — Practically high, scientifically moderate.** If the ~2%-overhead claim holds generally, it materially lowers the barrier to adopting Muon-style optimizers in production FSDP2 stacks, which is a real systems contribution. It does not advance optimization theory.
- **Red flags / concerns:**
  - The headline "+2% vs AdamW" is the *favorable* comparison; the Muon-AG baseline is a deliberately naive implementation, so the 1.48x–3.01x headline partly measures against a strawman. The AdamW numbers are the more meaningful target and are presented honestly.
  - Single-vendor, self-reported benchmarks on internal models (WALL-OSS-0.5, Wall-WM) with no external replication.
  - No statistical reporting of variance; ns-level step times are reported to the millisecond with no confidence intervals.
  - The 163x optimizer-step speedup (Qwen2.5 @ 256) is a peak achieved at the largest scale and on the workload with the smallest matrices — representative of a regime, not a universal figure.
  - "Three lines of code" drop-in claim elides that `dedicate_params` requires a device mesh and TP/DP placement metadata, and that tied-embedding aliasing needs special handling.

## Strengths

- Genuinely production-validated rather than toy benchmarks; four diverse workloads spanning VLA (robotics) and LLM pretraining.
- Exact-semantics guarantee is architecturally enforced — no approximations to the Muon update.
- Clean separation of concerns (communication runtime / kernel engine / scheduling) with a small, composable user surface.
- Pragmatic engineering details that practitioners will value: fp16-vs-bf16 rationale, transient materialization for peak memory, non-owner zero-size placeholders for module-graph compatibility, TP nesting detected from live DTensor placements.
- Honest scoping and limitation disclosure (less benefit on single-GPU; cannot eliminate the irreducible largest-matrix NS cost without changing the optimizer).

## Limitations

- Benefit shrinks on single-GPU training (~2x optimizer-time reduction, but no distributed-overlap gains).
- Cannot remove the irreducible critical-path NS of the largest owner-side matrix — that requires a different optimizer.
- All results on one cluster type (A800); behavior on H100/B200, RoCE fabrics, or different NVLink topologies is untested.
- MILP assignment is solved once at init, so it cannot adapt if shapes or batch sizes change mid-training (e.g., sequence-length schedules) without re-profiling.
- No peer review at time of writing; numbers are vendor-reported.
- Cost model is hardware/batch/kernel specific and was benchmarked once — kernel-cache staleness on driver/firmware updates is not discussed.

## Relevance

与大规模 LLM / VLA 预训练的工程实践高度相关：若 Muon 类矩阵优化器在未来生产训练中持续普及（Kimi-K2、DeepSeek-V4、Moonlight 已先行采用），DMuon 这类把分布式开销压到接近 AdamW 的"系统侧补丁"正是落地必需的基础设施，对预训练团队和训练框架开发者都有直接参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does DMuon behave on Hopper/Blackwell (H100/B200) where tensor-core throughput and interconnect differ — does the symmetric-kernel and autotuning advantage transfer, and is fp16 NS still precision-safe at larger matrix scales?
- Can the MILP owner assignment be made *adaptive* mid-training (e.g., when sequence length, batch, or context schedules change) without prohibitive re-profiling cost?
- What is the wall-clock cost of the one-time autotuning and MILP solve, and how does it amortize for short vs. long runs?
- Could the irreducible largest-matrix NS be reduced by a hybrid that orthogonalizes the largest matrices in a lower-rank / block-diagonal fashion *without* breaking Muon semantics — i.e., can systems + algorithm co-design close the last 2%?
- Independent reproduction on a different cluster and model family is needed to confirm the "+2% vs AdamW" claim outside the authors' own workloads.
- How does DMuon interact with asynchronous / 1F1B pipeline-parallel schedules, where the forward/backward overlap structure is different from pure FSDP2?
