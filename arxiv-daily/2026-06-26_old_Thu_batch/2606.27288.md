> **一句话总结：** 本文证明多模型 LLM 编排（路由、投票、级联、融合、MoA）的准确率上限由"所有模型同时答错"的共失败率 β 决定，而非业界惯用的成对错误相关性 ρ——ρ 在数学上无法识别 β；在 67 个前沿模型上的实测表明，开放性任务中 ρ 系统性地低估共失败尾部约 2.5 倍，且低估随池子增大而加剧。

> **TL;DR:** This paper proves that the accuracy ceiling of any LLM selection policy (router, vote, cascade) is 1 − β, where β is the rate at which ALL models fail on the same query. The field's favored diagnostic, mean pairwise error correlation ρ, provably cannot identify β for m ≥ 3 models. Across 67 frontier models (GPT-5.5, Claude Opus 4.8, Gemini 3.1 Pro, etc.), a correctly tetrachoric-calibrated single-factor model still underprices the all-wrong tail by ~2.5× on open-ended math (and the gap grows with pool size). The author converts β into a $0 pre-deployment Clopper–Pearson "certificate" and shows a deployable learned router captures ~0% of the oracle gain on the 2026 frontier.

## 核心贡献

- **共失败上限定理（Prop. 1）**：任何输出为成员模型答案的选择策略（路由/投票/级联），其准确率严格不超过 1 − β；并给出增益的精确局部化 G = Pr[单最佳模型答错] − β，以及把 β 转换为部署前"零成本证书"（Clopper–Pearson 置信下界）。
- **ρ 无法识别 β（Prop. 2 & 3）**：在尾依赖下，由 ρ 标定的单因子 Gaussian copula 对共失败尾部存在系统性向下偏差，偏差随池规模 m 发散；驱动因子是"共模原子"（common-mode atom）而非尾依赖本身。证明了 m ≥ 3 时 β 不是成对错误律的函数（Fréchet 类经典事实的特化）。
- **市场级实测**：在 67 个模型 / 21 个供应商家族的活跃 OpenRouter 池上，经验 β 在开放性数学（MATH-500, β=0.052, k=17/330）上比正确标定的单因子模型高约 2.5×，比全 Σ Gaussian copula 还高 2.25×，比 Clayton copula（带真实下尾依赖）仍高 ~1.96×——说明残差是 Gaussian/copula 无法表达的共模原子。
- **两种对立的"封顶机制"**：开放性任务（数学/代码/自由作答）属于 ceiling-bound（β>0 封顶）；多选题（GPQA-Diamond MC、MMLU-Pro）属于 realizability-bound（β≈0，天花板敞开但可学习的路由器仍抓不到增益）。
- **格式而非内容决定机制**：同样的 79 道 GPQA-Diamond 题，仅把多选题改成自由作答（去选项 + 5 评判 LLM 评审组，κ 0.73–0.92），β 从 ≈0 跳到 0.127——共失败追踪的是"开放性"本身，而非学科。
- **经济脚手架（App. A）**：预算约束路由为带单一影子价格 λB 的定价分配；成本感知融合有多样化极限 k*(ρ,c)；校准级联在验证器 AUC→1/2 时退化为随机混合。

## Key Contributions

- **The orchestration ceiling (Prop. 1)**: For any selection policy whose output is a.s. one of the members' answers, accuracy ≤ 1 − β, attained by the per-query oracle. The oracle gain G = Pr[single-best wrong] − β lives entirely on the "resolvable" mass; β contributes nothing. A Clopper–Pearson lower bound on β yields a pre-deployment $0 certificate on the maximum gain any such policy could deliver.
- **ρ is blind to β (Props. 2, 3)**: Under tail dependence the single-factor estimate of β from ρ is downward-biased, with the bias diverging in pool size and driven by a common-mode atom (a positive-probability joint-failure event). For m ≥ 3, β is provably not a function of the pairwise error law (specialization of a classical Fréchet-class fact).
- **Market-scale measurement**: On a 67-model / 21-family live OpenRouter pool, the empirical all-wrong rate β = 0.052 on MATH-500 exceeds the tetrachoric-calibrated single-factor prediction by ~2.5× (90% CI 1.7–3.4), exceeds the full 67×67 pairwise-tetrachoric Gaussian copula by 2.25×, and exceeds a Clayton copula (with genuine lower-tail dependence) by ~1.96× — so the residual is a common-mode atom no exchangeable pairwise copula can represent.
- **Two opposite foreclosure regimes**: Ceiling-bound domains (open-ended math, execution-graded code, free-response GPQA) have β > 0 capping every policy; realizability-bound domains (multiple-choice GPQA, MMLU-Pro) have β ≈ 0 with a large oracle gain that a deployable router still cannot capture.
- **Format, not content, sets the regime**: Re-asking the same 79 GPQA-Diamond questions in free-response vs. multiple-choice flips β from ≈0 to 0.127 (5-judge LLM panel, κ 0.73–0.92), isolating open-endedness as the driver of co-failure.
- **Economics (App. A)**: Budget-constrained routing as a priced assignment with a single shadow price λB (Prop. 4); a cost-aware diversification limit k*(ρ, c) (Props. 6, 7); and a cascade calibration boundary where advantage collapses to random mixing as verifier AUC → 1/2 (Prop. 8, Cor. 1).

## Methodology

- **Theory layer**: Defines β = Pr[all m models wrong] as the binding quantity. Prop. 1 gives the ceiling 1 − β and the gain-localization identity; Prop. 2 (common-shock mixture) shows pairwise ρ underprices β with bias growing in m; Prop. 3 establishes non-identification of β from pairwise statistics for m ≥ 3. Tools (LP duality, Clopper–Pearson intervals, Gaussian copula, single-factor probit) are explicitly conceded as standard.
- **Pillar experiments** (≈$47): 15 current models across 9 families (frontier Claude Opus 4.8 / GPT-5.1 / Gemini 3.1 Pro / Kimi K2.7; mid; cheap), 5 benchmarks (GSM8K, MMLU, ARC-Challenge, MATH-500, MMLU-Pro), 100–200 queries each. Programmatic grading only (exact-match, boxed-letter/integer extraction) — no LLM judge here.
- **Market-scale realizability** (≈$111): 67 models / 21 families from live OpenRouter (GPT-5.5, Grok-4.3, GLM-5.2, Qwen3.7-Max, DeepSeek V4, MiniMax M3, plus small open-weights), with hard co-failure domains: MATH-500, MATH-Hard (Level-5), AIME-2024/25, GPQA-Diamond.
- **Calibration discipline**: The single-factor copula is calibrated with the **tetrachoric** (latent) correlation, not the naive Pearson of 0/1 indicators — an earlier Pearson calibration produced a spurious 32× that the author flags and discards. Underpricing ratios are reported with bootstrap and Clopper–Pearson intervals.
- **Third independent domain** (≈$110): execution-graded competitive programming (63 hard problems, rating 1900–3500, Python-fair time limit, stress-test grading), and free-response GPQA-Diamond graded by a 5-judge LLM panel.
- **Router evaluation**: Four routers tested on the 15-model saturated mix (held-out TF-IDF+domain logistic; gradient-boosted per-model predictor; multiclass best-model predictor; LLM-as-router). The market-scale and GPQA matrices store outcomes but not prompts, so no router was trained there — stated plainly as a scope limit.

## Results & Highlights

- **β vs. ρ across domains** (Table 3 / Fig. 4): MATH-500 β=0.052 (k=17, ρ-underpricing 2.5×); MATH-Hard β=0.044 (8.3× point ratio, 3.3× at matched ρ); code_contests β=0.079 (k=5, 3.1×, CI [1.5, 6.2] excludes 1); GPQA-Diamond MC β≈0; MMLU-Pro β=0.008 (1 event).
- **Format flip (Fig. 3)**: Same 79 GPQA questions, MC → free-response, β 0 → 0.127 (10/79 all-wrong, CP[0.062, 0.220]); mean accuracy 0.66 → 0.51; best 0.91 → 0.77.
- **Pool-size effect (Fig. 2 right)**: Tetrachoric underpricing ratio rises monotonically from 1.0 at k=2 to median 2.5 (5–95% band [2.1, 2.7]) at k=67 over random k-subsets — size, not model composition, is the driver, exactly as Prop. 2 predicts.
- **Realizable routing gain ≈ 0** (Pillar A, Fig. 9): Learned router captures 9% of G on the mix (CI [−0.67, 0.50] on the fraction of G, spanning zero); GBDT per-model predictor captures −0.09; LLM-as-router routes to single-best 100% of the time.
- **Naive diversity is a liability** (Pillar B, Fig. 7): Across all 455 three-model triplets, mean majority-vote gain over best member is negative (−0.10 hard, −0.02 saturated, robust under leave-one-model-out jackknife).
- **Matched-quality diversification works in one regime** (Fig. 8): At matched member quality, low-ρ heterogeneous ensemble (ρ=0.42) beats high-ρ Self-MoA (ρ=0.80) from k=3 onward (+0.027 partition-averaged, positive in all 60 resamplings), supporting the diversification-limit prediction but flagged as "supported in one regime, not established."
- **Cascade collapse confirmed** (Pillar C, Fig. 6): Cascade advantage over random mixing falls monotonically 0.121 → 0.012 as verifier AUC degrades 0.899 → 0.510; volume ceiling 1 − aL/aH = 0.188; dominance survives 5-fold held-out evaluation (+0.114 held-out, sd 0.010).
- **Cost**: Core pillar ≈$47, market-scale ≈$111, third-domain ≈$110; reported-experiment total ≈$270 of ≈$560 total account usage.

## Authors & Credibility

- Single author, Josef Chen, affiliated with KAIKAKU (an independent/niche entity, not a major lab). No co-authors, no institutional IRB/peer-review signal at submission; this is an arXiv v1 preprint.
- The paper is unusually self-critical and methodologically careful: it explicitly concedes the classical ingredients (equicorrelated variance floor, Kuncheva's Oracle combiner, Gaussian copula), flags its own earlier Pearson-calibration error (spurious 32×), reports Clopper–Pearson intervals on β because all-wrong counts are small (k=5–17), and uses model-clustered (jackknife) inference rather than naive i.i.d. bootstraps.
- Credibility rests on the theoretical claims (which reduce to elementary identities and classical Fréchet-class facts with one-line proofs in App. B) plus the transparent, low-cost, reproducible measurement design. The honest scope-stating (e.g., "we did not train a router on the market-scale matrix; the routing statement rests on the certificate") is a credibility plus.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty — moderate**: The ceiling 1 − β itself is not new (it is Kuncheva's Oracle combiner, explicitly conceded). What is novel: (a) the *specialization to priced LLM orchestration*, (b) the conversion to a labels-free $0 finite-sample certificate (Prop. 1(iii)), (c) the proof that ρ is provably non-identifying for β for m ≥ 3, and (d) the transfer of the Gaussian-copula "base-correlation smile" / common-mode-atom argument to LLM ensembles with a market-scale empirical confirmation. The author is scrupulous about marking which pieces are conceded vs. owned.
- **Rigor — high**: Proofs are elementary and checked; statistical machinery (Clopper–Pearson, tetrachoric vs. Pearson calibration, bootstrap with joint uncertainty propagation, model-clustering jackknife) is correctly deployed. The "trap" of naive Pearson-of-indicators calibration is diagnosed and corrected rather than hidden.
- **Reproducibility — high-to-moderate**: Cheap ($270 reported), programmatic grading, named model snapshots and prices frozen in a registry, explicit per-script names (realizability.py, residual_decomp.py, clayton_real.py, cascade_heldout.py). Caveats: depends on a paid OpenRouter account at a specific date; market-scale and GPQA matrices store outcomes but not prompts, blocking router reproduction there; small event counts (k=5–17) make β estimates noisy.
- **Significance — high if the diagnosis holds broadly**: The β/ρ distinction and the $0 certificate are immediately actionable for any buyer deciding whether to build a routing layer. The "format flips the regime" finding is a sharp, generalizable experimental result. The "realizable routing gain ≈ 0 on the 2026 frontier" claim, if it generalizes, materially reduces the expected value of routing investments.
- **Red flags**:
  - **Single author / no peer review**: The entire empirical edifice rests on one person's pipeline; a replication by an independent group is needed before the headline ratios are load-bearing.
  - **Small all-wrong counts**: β on MATH-500 rests on k=17, code on k=5, GPQA-free on k=10. The point ratios (2.5×–8.3×) carry wide CIs; the author admits the magnitude is uncertain even if the sign (underpricing) and monotone-growth shape are robust.
  - **Code result is the weakest link**: 18 models, k=5 events, "strict-but-not-official" judge — the author concedes a "tight-ratio code replication" with an official judge is the one remaining sharpening for a top-venue submission.
  - **Router claim is indirect**: The market-scale "routing gain ≈ 0" rests on the Prop. 1 certificate, not an end-to-end 67-model routing run (no prompts were logged). The negative-routing result is shown only on the 15-model saturated mix.
  - **Churn study (App. E)** is stylized and observational; the cross-epoch "option value of breadth" framing is acknowledged as a decomposition, not a solved Bellman system.
  - **Potential conflict / framing**: The author's own workflow is described as "tiered but single-provider with unmetered cost/latency" — disclosed transparently but worth noting given the paper's commercial-relevance claims.

## Strengths

- A genuinely useful conceptual reframing: replace the single diagnostic ρ with the right object β, and a $0 pre-deployment test to decide whether orchestration can pay for itself.
- Honest, calibrated statistical reporting throughout (CP intervals on β, bootstrap CIs on ratios, model-clustered inference, discarded spurious Pearson numbers).
- The format-flip experiment (MC vs. free-response on identical GPQA content) is a clean content-controlled test that pins co-failure to open-endedness, not subject matter.
- Cheap, reproducible, and the theory reduces to verifiable elementary identities with one-line proofs.
- Concedes prior art precisely (Turmen et al.'s equicorrelated Gaussian floor, Li et al.'s Self-MoA result, Dekoninck et al.'s routing/cascade optimality) and positions the contribution against each.

## Limitations

- β estimates rest on very few all-wrong events; point underpricing ratios are uncertain in magnitude.
- Programmatic grading covers only verifiable tasks; open-ended generative quality would reintroduce judge bias (the GPQA-free panel is the only LLM-judged result).
- Saturated benchmarks inflate ρ; the hard regime mitigates but does not remove this.
- Static-price economic assumptions (App. A) conflict with the churn analysis (App. E); claims restricted to within-release-epoch validity.
- The diversification mechanism is "supported in one regime, not established"; the sensitivity λ is derived theoretically but not empirically fit across ρ levels for out-of-sample k* prediction.
- No router trained on the 67-model market pool — the flagship routing-gain claim is certificate-based, not end-to-end.
- Single-author, arXiv-only, awaiting independent replication.

## Relevance

对做多模型 LLM 编排、路由、级联或 MoA 的工程与研究者高度相关：它给出一个零成本的部署前测试（β 的 Clopper–Pearson 证书）来判断"组合模型到底能不能超过单最佳模型"，并指明真正值得优化的杠杆是失败模式的异质性（dispersion）与市场更新（churn），而非模型数量或峰值能力。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Does the common-mode-atom signature (β exceeding any exchangeable pairwise copula) hold on truly open-ended generative tasks (creative writing, long-form reasoning, agentic tool use) beyond programmatically-gradable benchmarks?
2. Can a router with strong query-level signal (e.g., process-reward model confidence, embeddings of the reasoning trace) actually capture the large oracle gain in realizability-bound domains like multiple-choice GPQA, where the ceiling is open?
3. What is the structure of the common-mode atom — is it shared training data, shared RLHF alignment, shared algorithmic paradigm (e.g., chain-of-thought), or shared benchmark contamination? Ablating model family / training corpus would help.
4. How does β evolve across model generations (e.g., GPT-4 → GPT-5.5)? Does the underpricing ratio grow with capability convergence even within a single provider family?
5. Can the $0 certificate be made adaptive (sequential Clopper–Pearson stopping) so a buyer pays only for enough queries to decide orchestration ROI at a target confidence?
