Key Finding

Emotion vectors generalize beyond Claude in two open-weight LLMs — but valence emerges along model-specific layer paths, and arousal encoding is corpus-dependent.

Where Do Models Find Happiness? Emotion Vectors in Open-Source LLMs

Key Metric
0.83 r
Peak PC1–valence correlation (Gemma-4-E4B, layer 16) — exceeding the Claude Sonnet 4.5 reference of r = 0.81 (p < 10⁻³).
Why This Matters
Validates emotion vectors transfer across model families, enabling cross-model alignment monitoring via internal valence-axis probes — a reproducible foundation for interpretability & activation steering.
⚠ Critical Edge
Despite framing as "replication," the central causal claim — that emotion vectors actually steer behavior — is untested. Everything is correlational, and "generalization" rests on just n = 2 model families.

Methodology

The authors replicate the emotion-contrast-vector protocol of Sofroniew et al. (2026) — the Anthropic Transformer Circuits study that released no code, so every step is here reconstructed and open-sourced. The decisive methodological move is treating story corpus as an independent variable.

  1. Two synthetic corpora. 9 stories per emotion × 171 emotions + 40 neutral stories = 1,539 stories each. One written by Apertus-8B, one by Gemma-4-E4B, both prompted to convey emotion implicitly without naming it.
  2. Two models. Instruction-tuned open-weight transformers: Apertus-8B (32 layers) and Gemma-4-E4B (42 layers); vectors drawn from every residual stream.
  3. Contrast-vector extraction. Per emotion, average 9 story activations to u_e; build a neutral subspace via PCA on the 40 neutral stories (top-K at 50% variance); subtract the projection → emotion contrast vector v_e, stacked into V^(l) per layer.
  4. Three analyses. PCA axes correlated with NRC Valence–Arousal–Dominance ratings (Pearson r); cross-layer CKA for geometry evolution; cosine similarity of the valence direction across depths.

Strengths

  • Clean, well-motivated replication directly answering the original's three generality questions.
  • Two-corpus design is a real confound control prior work lacked.
  • Open weights, open code, open data — a materially higher reproducibility bar than the Anthropic original.
  • Conceptual payoff: peak correlation alone can hide very different internal computation; CKA stability ≠ stable feature axis.

Limitations

  • Only two model families; "different paths" claims rest on n = 2.
  • Model-generated corpora conflate corpus and model properties.
  • No causal steering experiments — recovered directions may not drive behavior.
  • Method reconstructed without reference code → possible numerical drift; valence-axis cosines are largely noisy.

Open Questions

  1. Do recovered valence directions causally steer outputs (reward hacking, sycophancy, refusal) as in the original Claude work — and at which layers is steering most effective?
  2. Do steering vectors transfer across layers given the observed axis rotation inside otherwise CKA-stable regions?
  3. What drives the divergent emergence trajectories — architecture, pre-training, or post-training? Can base-vs-instruct ablations attribute it?
  4. Would sparse autoencoders show the same interpretable features carry emotion across layers, or different feature sets recomposing it?
  5. Is the valence axis preserved in multi-modal models across text, image, and audio inputs?
