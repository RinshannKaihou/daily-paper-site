RolloutPipe: Overlapping Pipelined Rollout and Training in Disaggregated On-Policy LLM Reinforcement Learning

Rongjian Chen, Jianmin Hu, Kejiang Ye, Minxian Xu · SIAT CAS / UCAS / SUSTech · 2026-06-25
Pipelining rollout-to-training at the complete-group boundary cuts rollout-to-train-end time by up to 42% with zero stale data.
LLM-RL GRPO RLVR Pipeline Optimization Disaggregated Systems
Key Metric
42.3%
Reduction in rollout-to-train-end time over Slime (R=96). Trainer waiting ratio falls from 47–52% to 14–33%.
Why This Matters
Disaggregated on-policy GRPO leaves rollout GPUs idle while training waits — RolloutPipe overlaps them with no stale data, directly relevant to RLVR training-system engineers.
Critical Edge
Validated on a single 1.7B model with frontier width frozen at Fw=2 and no end-to-end accuracy check — the scheduling gains may not survive at 7B+ scale.

Methodology

RolloutPipe sits atop Slime (rollout/training control), Megatron-LM (pipelined trainer), SGLang (serving), and Ray (execution), coordinating three event-driven nodes. The Rollout node runs Frontier-Group Dispatch (FGD): an admission controller holds a frontier set F of width Fw and admits only requests whose group is in F, concentrating serving capacity on the earliest groups that will form the next batch; a group leaves F once all K of its requests complete. The Control node runs Complete-Group Pipelining (CGP): it materializes each group (rewards, verifier, GRPO advantage A = (r−μ)/(σ+ε)) and, as soon as U = B/K complete groups accumulate, hands them to the trainer rather than waiting for the full R-group rollout. The Training node consumes U groups per optimizer step — matching Slime's logical-update boundary — then republishes weights. Crucially, all R groups share the same fixed rollout weights, so CGP/FGD change only dispatch timing and order, never samples, advantages, or gradients. Overhead is just O(R·K) control flow; per-rollout trainer compute O(R·K·L²·d) is unchanged.

Setup: Qwen3-1.7B on 8×RTX 4090 (TP=4, DP=2) training + 2×A100 40GB (TP=2) rollout; K=8, B=16 ⇒ U=2, Fw=2; four reasoning/science workloads at R‐{32,64,96} over four rounds; exact-match accuracy reward.

Strengths

  • Genuinely on-policy: no stale data, no extra weight snapshots, optimizer-step boundary preserved.
  • Consistent 30.7–42.3% gains that scale favorably with R; clean CGP-vs-FGD decomposition.
  • Strong confound control: verifies response length and trainer compute are unchanged.
  • Simple O(R·K) admission + handoff policy; implementable on real frameworks without architectural changes.

Limitations

  • Only Qwen3-1.7B tested; 7B–70B+ generalization unverified, and KV-cache/scheduling dynamics differ at scale.
  • Frontier width frozen at Fw=U=2 with no sensitivity sweep — a central hyperparameter left unexplored.
  • No end-to-end convergence or final-accuracy comparison to confirm on-policy-equivalent outcomes empirically.
  • No quantitative comparison to asynchronous baselines (AReaL, AsyncFlow); only Slime benchmarked.
  • Single modest hardware setup; absolute timings are hardware-specific.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Do the speedups survive at 7B/14B/32B scale, where KV-cache pressure and SGLang scheduling differ — does FGD's admission throttling hurt serving throughput?
  2. How sensitive is FGD to frontier width (Fw ∈ {1,2,4,8})? Is Fw=U always optimal, or does a wider frontier cut tail latency at small overlap cost?
  3. Does CGP's group-boundary logic generalize to PPO-style sample-level RLVR, or become redundant there?
  4. How does RolloutPipe place on the throughput-vs-staleness Pareto against AReaL/AsyncFlow, accuracy held fixed?
  5. Does early dispatch change gradient noise across rounds enough to affect final converged accuracy, even with identical per-update compute?
  6. What is the effect on total round latency and on the tail of the last group under FGD deferral?
