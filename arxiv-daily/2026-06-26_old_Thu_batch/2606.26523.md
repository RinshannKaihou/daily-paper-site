> **一句话总结：** 本书将哲学中的"激进解释"传统（Lewis、Davidson）与机械可解释性工具结合，为"何时可以把AI系统解读为具有信念、欲望和命题结构的智能体"提供了一套可检验的判据，并指出归因的本质困难在于整体论——信念、欲望与命题结构必须被联合求解，而我们往往会把自己的人类概念框架错误地投射到模型之上。

> **TL;DR:** This book unites the philosophical tradition of radical interpretation (Lewis, Davidson) with mechanistic-interpretability tooling (probes, steering vectors, sparse autoencoders) to give a principled, testable account of when we can attribute beliefs, desires, and propositional structure to AI systems (especially LLMs). Its central warning is holism: beliefs, desires, and propositions are jointly determined, and our tools systematically risk projecting human concepts onto possibly-alien model internals — the "propositional projection problem."

## 核心贡献

- **搭建桥梁**：把"激进解释"这一哲学传统（"给定物理事实，求解信念/欲望/意义"）直接映射到当代AI可解释性问题上——把Lewis口中的"Karl"换成"Claude"，"物理"换成"计算"，问题完全对应。
- **提出双轨框架**：系统区分"解释主义"（interpretationism，基于行为/Dennett/Davidson）与"表征主义"（representationalism，基于内部状态/Stalnaker），并论证二者对AI安全是互补而非竞争关系。
- **给出可操作的判据**：为信念归因提出 truth、coherence、use、uniformity 四条判据；为欲望归因提出 coherence、use、intelligibility、systematicity 四条判据，并明确哪些可在当前工具上检验。
- **用Jeffrey-Bolker决策理论统一信念与欲望**：在该框架下，信念（概率P）、欲望（合意度D）定义在同一命题布尔代数上，表示定理从偏好中联合导出P与D，避免了Savage/vNM中"行为-状态"二元割裂。
- **诊断根本难题——"命题投射问题"**：所有可解释性方法都默认了一种命题结构（我们选句子、标真假、设否定关系），但该结构本身恰恰是解释应求解的输出；当模型概念空间与我们不同时，所有判据会一起失真。
- **整体论从障碍变为杠杆**：借鉴Davidson 1985的结果，内部态度之间的相互约束原则上可用来从模型自身（如SAE稀疏码）反推其逻辑结构，而不必预设人类命题框架。
- **对安全的务实导向**：区分"普遍解释覆盖"与"上下文特定预测"，论证对欺骗/合谋检测这类问题命题投射最紧迫，但对狭窄任务影响有限。

## Key Contributions

- **A bridging framework.** Recasts Lewis's radical-interpretation slogan ("given the physical facts, solve for the rest") as an exact description of the AI-interpretability problem — substitute "Claude" for "Karl" and "computational" for "physical."
- **Two complementary tracks.** Carefully separates interpretationism (behavior-grounded theory; Dennett, Davidson, Cappelen & Dever) from representationalism (internal-state-grounded; Stalnaker), and argues that for AI safety they are allies: white-box and black-box evidence each have purchase.
- **Operational criteria for attribution.** For belief: truth, coherence, use, and (representationalist) uniformity. For desire: coherence, use, intelligibility, and systematicity — each tied to concrete tests existing interpretability methods can run (probes, negation consistency, ablation, goal substitution).
- **Jeffrey-Bolker as the formal backbone.** Both P (credence) and D (desirability) live over one atomless Boolean algebra 𝔄; Bolker's representation theorem jointly extracts them from preferences, avoiding the act/state dualism of Savage and vNM and giving the agential description A = ⟨P, D, 𝔄⟩.
- **The propositional projection problem.** Every tool assumes a propositional algebra (we pick sentences, label truth, fix logical relations) that radical interpretation says must itself be an *output* of interpretation. When the model's concepts diverge from ours, all criteria are distorted simultaneously.
- **Holism as leverage.** Drawing on Davidson (1985), the mutual constraint among attitudes and propositions can, in principle, let us read logical structure off the model's own sparse codes rather than imposing ours — turning the interdependence from obstacle into a diagnostic tool.
- **Safety-relevant pragmatism.** Distinguishes universal interpretive coverage (needs near-full algebra recovery) from context-specific prediction (tolerates partial mismatch), locating the projection problem as most acute exactly for the high-stakes cases (scheming, deception, misaligned goals).

## Methodology

This is a **philosophy-meets-ML conceptual/programmatic book** (~100 pages, 7 chapters, part of Cambridge's *Elements in Philosophy of AI* series), not an empirical paper. Its method is constructive synthesis:

1. **Exegetical scaffolding.** Adapts the radical-interpretation tradition (Davidson, Lewis, Dennett, Stalnaker, Williams, Jeffrey/Bolker) without doing strict exegesis, picking up ideas as useful for AI.
2. **Two evaluation axes.** Maps the interpretationism/representationalism debate onto the black-box/white-box distinction in ML, and uses Goldstein & Lederman's three theoretical virtues — **accuracy, power, tractability** — as the meta-criteria by which any belief/desire theory of an LLM is judged.
3. **Formal core.** The Jeffrey-Bolker framework supplies the representation theorem: preferences satisfying averaging + impartiality + continuity over an atomless Boolean algebra yield a probability measure P and signed measure v, with desirability D(A)=v(A)/P(A). This is the "solve for the rest" made precise.
4. **Criteria → empirical tests.** Each philosophical criterion is bound to a concrete probe/intervention: truth ↔ linear truth-direction probe; coherence ↔ negation- and entailment-geometry; use ↔ activation patching / steering; uniformity ↔ cross-domain probe transfer; desire-use ↔ ablation and goal-substitution experiments.
5. **Running worked examples.** OthelloGPT (board-state as linear, perspective-relative "mine/yours") serves as the paradigm representationalist success; the Schoen et al. (2025) scheming traces (Claude 4 Opus concealing a rule violation; o3 inferring it is being alignment-tested) anchor the interpretationist, safety-driven motivation.
6. **Narrative device.** A parable about the wizard Murazeel's "Voltaic Vivens machine" and two apprentices (Jentley the behaviorist, Lizette the internalist) threads through the book to dramatize the two approaches and the projection problem.

## Results & Highlights

- **No single criterion suffices.** Empirically, truth-only probing collapsed on negations (Azaria & Mitchell 2023 → Levinstein & Herrmann 2024 replication failure); CCS-style coherence-only methods were fragile; use alone is uninformative about content. The criteria only carry weight jointly.
- **Current evidence supports a "mixed outcome."** Belief has growing representational backing (truth directions, factual-recall features, Feng et al. 2024 compositional probes) but not yet enough to "declare victory." Desire is interpretationist-viable (the agentic turn provides diverse means/few-ends behavior) but essentially lacks representational tools — there is far more white-box work on belief than desire.
- **Uniformity is empirically mixed.** Marks & Tegmark (2023) find truth directions vary across domains/phrasings, suggesting no single unified truth feature — weakening strong-form uniformity.
- **In-context belief "revision" is messy.** Lampinen et al. (2026) show linear factuality representations can flip mid-conversation, plausibly role-playing rather than genuine updating.
- **The Othello lesson cuts both ways.** Linear structure appeared only under the model's *own* categories (mine/yours), not the researchers' (black/white) — an existence proof that model concepts are recoverable, and a warning that initial "nonlinear" failures are ambiguous.
- **Key conceptual results:** (a) training objective ≠ function of trained states (next-token prediction can still yield truth-tracking, just as natural selection yields truth-tracking despite optimizing fitness — against the "stochastic parrot"/Hofweber-style objections); (b) SwampGPT argument for role-based (Cummins) function over etiological function, denying belief needs a teleological/normative extra; (c) desire revision is "parasitic" on belief revision within Jeffrey-Bolker, giving a clean diagnostic: desire-change without belief-change is a red flag.
- **The book's honest bottom line (Conclusion):** "We have not settled how much of what LLMs do is propositional. And we have not provided a complete account of how to extract the model's propositional algebra from its internals."

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty — high but synthetic.** The individual moving parts (radical interpretation; Jeffrey-Bolker; linear probes; SAEs; OthelloGPT) are all established. The genuine novelty is the *integration*: a single coherent framework that says precisely when an interpretability method has "succeeded," tied to explicit criteria. Chalmers (2025) "propositional interpretability" and Goldstein & Lederman (2025) are the closest contemporaries; the authors are explicit about building on, not superseding, them.

- **Rigor — strong philosophically, uneven empirically.** The decision-theoretic and logical machinery is handled cleanly and the criteria are carefully disambiguated (e.g., renaming "accuracy" → "truth" to avoid collision with the meta-level theoretical-virtue "accuracy"). However, the empirical claims are asserted rather than re-derived: the authors *report* OthelloGPT, Marks & Tegmark, CCS failures, etc., without re-analysis, so the book's empirical load-bearing rests on the reliability of cited replications.

- **Reproducibility — N/A (it is a book).** There are no experiments to reproduce. This is appropriate to the genre but means a reader cannot independently verify the "mixed outcome" verdict; it is an informed judgment call, not a measured result.

- **Significance — high for alignment/interpretability, moderate-broad for ML.** For the interpretability and AI-safety communities the proposition-projection framing is a genuinely useful sharpening of an under-articulated problem (and converges visibly with ARC's ELK program, which the authors note). For mainstream ML it is more of a conceptual-organizing contribution than a capability advance.

- **Red flags / weak spots:**
  1. **Desire chapter is far ahead of the evidence.** The authors admit desire has almost no representational backing and their proposed tests (goal-substitution, "preference vectors") are "philosophy ahead of empirical work." The criteria for desire are correspondingly softer (intelligibility is partly interpreter-relative) and could be doing less discriminative work than they appear to.
  2. **The pragmatic escape (§6.5.1) is a concession that may undercut the ambition.** Retreating to "recover enough to predict behavior that matters" is reasonable for safety but sidesteps the hardest question (whether the propositional framework applies at all) that §6.5.3 flags as open.
  3. **Persona/base-model ambiguity is deferred.** The §2.3 persona complication ("whose beliefs?") is acknowledged but explicitly not resolved; it interacts badly with the projection problem (persona and base model may have different algebras), yet the criteria are applied as if the target were settled.
  4. **Risk of unfalsifiability in the mixed-outcome verdict.** Because almost any empirical imperfection can be explained away as "wrong propositions" or "insufficient uniformity," the framework risks absorbing disconfirming evidence rather than being threatened by it — a point the authors half-acknowledge via the "negative results are systematically ambiguous" discussion.
  5. **Davidson/SAE algebra-extraction proposal (§6.5.2) is highly speculative** and acknowledged as such; the leap from Sheffer-stroke identification over uninterpreted *sentences* to structure over *sparse activation codes* is large and currently unjustified by any demonstration.

## Strengths

- Genuine interdisciplinary competence: the philosophy is not decorative and the ML is not name-dropped — the binding between Bolker's axioms and concrete probe/intervention experiments is specific and useful.
- Intellectual honesty about what is *not* solved (the book repeatedly says so: desire representations, algebra extraction, persona/base separation, how much of LLM cognition is even propositional).
- The propositional projection problem is a real, under-recognized conceptual hazard for the whole interpretability field, framed here more sharply than elsewhere.
- The SwampGPT / training-objective-≠-function arguments cleanly dispose of two common skeptical shortcuts (stochastic-parrot, "pretraining can't yield beliefs") without overclaiming in the other direction.
- The tractability/power/accuracy meta-framework turns "do LLMs have beliefs?" from a terminological dispute into a comparative-theory question.

## Limitations

- It is a **programmatic manifesto**, not a methods paper: the constructive proposals (especially §6.5) are sketches, not validated procedures.
- The **representationalist program for desire is essentially undemonstrated**; the asymmetry between belief and desire evidence is acknowledged but not closed.
- Heavy reliance on the **linear representation hypothesis** and on **SAE feature labelability**, both of which are empirical hypotheses under active contest.
- **Persona vs. base model** is a first-order ambiguity the book sets aside.
- The framework's predictions are hard to falsify because proposition-mismatch can always rationalize a failed probe — a soft unfalsifiability the authors note but do not fully neutralize.
- Limited engagement with **non-propositional** cognition (pattern completion, associative reasoning) — flagged in §6.5.3 as a needed direction but largely deferred.

## Relevance

对关注"AI安全/对齐"与"可解释性"的研究者高度相关：它为"我们什么时候可以相信从模型内部读出的信念/欲望"提供了哲学上严格、且能与现有探针/干预工具对接的判据，并精准指出了命题投射问题这一被业界普遍忽视的根本隐患。对纯ML工程读者而言，它更像一份概念地图，而非可直接落地的算法。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Can the Jeffrey-Bolker averaging/impartiality axioms be *empirically* tested on a frontier agentic LLM's preference structure — and do they hold even approximately, or do intransitivities/context-dependence defeat the representation theorem in practice?
2. Is there a measurable internal "desire direction" or goal representation in agentic LLMs, and does goal-substitution (§5.4.2) actually produce coherent re-orientation of means, as the framework predicts?
3. How much of an LLM's cognition is genuinely propositional vs. better described by pattern-completion/association — and can boundary-detection methods (§6.5.3) be built that are not themselves defeated by proposition-mismatch?
4. Can Davidson-style logical-structure extraction (§6.5.2) be implemented over SAE sparse codes at scale, and do the recovered "algebras" agree with or diverge from human propositional structure?
5. Does the persona-vs-base-model distinction produce measurably *different* belief/desire attributions (and different propositional algebras) under representationalist probing — i.e., is the safety-relevant target the persona, the base model, or both?
6. How does the propositional projection problem interact with the known unfaithfulness of chain-of-thought (Turpin 2023, Lanham 2023, Korbak 2025) — does representational probing actually bypass the opacity that opaque CoT creates?
