Key Finding

Any multi-model LLM orchestration's accuracy ceiling is 1 − β (the all-models-wrong rate) — which the field's favored diagnostic ρ provably cannot identify for three or more models.

When Does Combining Language Models Help? A Co-Failure Ceiling on Routing, Voting, and Mixture-of-Agents Across 67 Frontier Models ↗
AuthorsJosef Chen (KAIKAKU) Date2026-06-26
llm-orchestration model-routing ensemble-methods copula-modeling inference-economics co-failure-analysis

Key Metric

2.5×

Mean pairwise error correlation ρ underprices the true all-wrong rate β=0.052 on MATH-500 (90% CI 1.7–3.4); gap grows monotonically with pool size.

Why This Matters

A $0 pre-deployment Clopper–Pearson certificate tells any buyer whether multi-model routing can beat the single-best model — and the real lever is failure-mode dispersion, not model count or peak capability.

⚠ Critical Edge

The flagship “realizable routing gain ≈ 0” is certificate-based, not end-to-end — no router was trained on the 67-model pool, since prompts were never logged. The claim rests on theory, not a run.

Methodology

The binding quantity is β = Pr[all m models wrong]. Prop. 1 gives the ceiling 1 − β (attained by the per-query oracle) and localizes the gain to the “resolvable” mass; Prop. 2 shows pairwise ρ underprices β with bias that diverges in pool size, driven by a common-mode atom; Prop. 3 proves β is non-identifiable from pairwise statistics for m ≥ 3. Tools (LP duality, Clopper–Pearson intervals, Gaussian copula, single-factor probit) are conceded as standard.

Empirically: a 15-model pillar (~$47) on 5 benchmarks, a 67-model / 21-family live OpenRouter pool (~$111) on MATH-500/MATH-Hard/AIME/GPQA, and a third domain (~$110) of execution-graded code plus a 5-judge free-response GPQA panel. The copula is calibrated with tetrachoric correlation (not naive Pearson, which produced a spurious 32× that was discarded), and ratios carry bootstrap + Clopper–Pearson intervals.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Useful reframing: replace ρ with β, plus a $0 pre-deployment ROI test.
  • Honest statistical reporting — CP intervals, bootstrap CIs, model-clustered jackknife; discards a spurious 32×.
  • Clean content-controlled format-flip experiment pinning co-failure to open-endedness.
  • Theory reduces to verifiable elementary identities; prior art conceded precisely.

Limitations

  • β rests on only 5–17 all-wrong events; point ratios (2.5×–8.3×) are magnitude-uncertain.
  • Programmatic grading only; open-ended quality would reintroduce judge bias.
  • No router trained on the 67-model pool — flagship claim is certificate-based.
  • Single-author, arXiv-only; awaiting independent replication.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does the common-mode-atom signature persist on truly open-ended generative tasks (creative writing, agentic tool use) beyond programmatically-gradable benchmarks?
  2. Can a router with strong query-level signal (process-reward confidence, trace embeddings) capture the large oracle gain in realizability-bound domains where the ceiling is open?
  3. What structures the common-mode atom — shared training data, shared RLHF, shared CoT paradigm, or benchmark contamination?
  4. How does β evolve across model generations? Does underpricing grow with capability convergence within a single provider family?
  5. Can the $0 certificate be made adaptive (sequential Clopper–Pearson stopping) so a buyer pays only for enough queries to decide orchestration ROI?
