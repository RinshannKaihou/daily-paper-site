Why This Matters

First MHC-aware speculative decoder for DeepSeek-V4 — a deploy-ready speedup recipe for a major new multi-path architecture family, with a transferable "reuse the target's gating" principle.

⚠ Critical Edge

Every result is on one non-reproducible internal model, and the headline KL distillation contribution is never ablated in isolation — so its real share of the gains is unquantified.

Methodology

HyperDFlash keeps DFlash's block-parallel interface (drafting a whole block from an accepted anchor token + cached target hidden state) but re-engineers the three points where a naive port breaks multi-hyper-connection (MHC) alignment in DeepSeek-V4.

1. Pre-collapse residual conditioning. Instead of aggregated intermediate-layer features, the drafter is conditioned on the target's final pre_hc_head MHC residual state — the tensor already cached for native MTP. This sits one step before the LM head, preserving full multi-path semantics and the target's native prediction pathway.

2. Inherited HC-Gate Reducer. The target's hc_head collapses paths via per-path sigmoid gating, not a static linear map. The drafter reuses this exact form and initializes gate weights from the pretrained hc_head, cutting reducer params from ~67M to ~65K while being inherently target-aligned.

3. Position-restricted KL distillation. An auxiliary KL term distills the target LM head over cached hidden states, but only on the first P=2 block positions with conservative weight α≈0.1–0.2. Later positions see masks while the teacher sees ground truth, so matching there would inject high-variance conflicting gradients.

Training: 2 stages on 8× NVIDIA H20 (batch 4/GPU, 5 epochs each) — ~300K general examples (LR 8e-4) then ~150K code/instruction examples (LR 1e-4); checkpoint chosen after Stage 2.

✓ Strengths

  • Concrete, measurable problem: native MTP acceptance collapses at Pos1–5, which HyperDFlash demonstrably flattens (Figure 1).
  • Elegant reducer: reusing the target's own functional form + weights yields alignment and a ~1000× param cut simultaneously — no trade-off.
  • Honest limitations: explicitly flags missing latency study, un-ablated KL term, mean-pool teacher bias, single-target scope.
  • Comprehensive grid: 2 modes × 2 temps × 7 benchmarks × 4 drafting settings on one target + vLLM stack.
  • Conservative, well-reasoned defaults (KL on first 2 positions; small α; fallback to generic fc when assumptions fail).

✗ Limitations

  • Single target model (DeepSeek-V4-Flash); generalization to other MHC designs is untested and deferred.
  • KL distillation — one of three headline contributions — has no isolated ablation.
  • KL teacher mean-pools paths instead of gated hc_head collapse, a known bias merely "mitigated" by small α.
  • No production latency study separating drafter cost, verification cost, and batching effects.
  • No released code/checkpoints; target model and part of training data are internal, blocking reproduction.
  • Reducer is specialized (needs pre_hc_head + matching widths); EAGLE-2/3, Medusa, REST not benchmarked head-to-head.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How much of the gain comes from each component in isolation? A full 3-way ablation (pre-collapse conditioning / HC-Gate reducer / KL) would tighten the claims.
  2. Does it generalize beyond DeepSeek-V4-Flash — to other MHC models, or future V4 variants with different path counts / hidden widths?
  3. Would using the gated hc_head collapse (not mean-pool) as the KL teacher remove the need for the small-α mitigation?
  4. What are end-to-end production latency numbers under realistic batching, and how does the drafter's own forward cost scale with batch size?
  5. Can the smoother acceptance profile be pushed further by supervising later positions (k≥3) instead of only the first two?
