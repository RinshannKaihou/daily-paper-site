Key Finding

Under strict one-hot supervision, transformer representations first organize by latent semantics, then collapse to Neural Collapse's symmetric ETF geometry — a transient phase, not a contradiction.

Methodology

A controlled study: when semantic geometry is recovered from data, how does it evolve under strictly one-hot supervision where Neural Collapse predicts terminal ETF symmetry?

  • Three synthetic languages of increasing linguistic realism, where inputs share known latent factors but map to mutually exclusive one-hot labels.
  • Exp 1 — Color-Shape: triplets [nuisance, color, shape]; 2×4 latent factors → K=8 labels; fine-grained variants hide coarse factors, nuisance IDs block memorization.
  • Exp 2 — Zipfian template language; Exp 3 — hierarchical grammar + Dyck brackets, class set by the c-commanding node.
  • Training: standard transformer decoders swept over depth/width; single prediction per example guarantees strictly one-hot supervision.

RSA diagnostic pipeline. Three Representational Similarity Matrices over n samples:

  • Sem-RSM — shared latent attributes.
  • ETF-RSM — 1 if same label (SELI for imbalance).
  • Emp-RSM — Gram matrix of last-layer context embeddings.
  • Track Pearson ρ(Emp, Sem) & ρ(Emp, ETF) over epochs; supplement with NC1/NC2, attribute-clustering, cross-layer & different-label controls.
  • Spherical BoW proxy: Theorem 1 — one GD step biases Emp toward Sem; Theorem 2 — global minimizer attains ETF only with spherical normalization.

Results & Highlights — beyond the headline

Early-semantic, late-collapse transient
At epoch ~30 the Emp-RSM is block-diagonal matching Sem-RSM; by epoch ~10k it converges to the binary same/different-label ETF pattern.
Phase transition tracks capacity
ρsem rises early then declines while ρETF rises later — most pronounced for larger/deeper models. Narrow models (d=8,16) hold semantic alignment longer.
Generalizes across language complexity
Same transient in Zipfian-template (Exp 2) and hierarchical-grammar (Exp 3); in Exp 3, c-command-licensed contexts form faint category blocks even at terminal phase for narrow models.
Graded signal, not mere same-label collapse
Different-label pairs sharing a factor are more similar than pairs sharing none — even after same-label pairs are removed.

Theory reproduces the transient: spherical normalization is what forces terminal ETF convergence — its absence yields pure semantic geometry.

Strengths

  • Sharp theoretical tension (NC vs. observed LM semantics) turned into a precise, answerable question.
  • Three language-like settings give genuine generality, not one contrived example.
  • Diagnostics disentangle "same-label collapse" from "graded semantic structure" — a common NC confound.
  • Spherical BoW yields an interpretable mechanism (input-bias vs. normalization-collapse) with matching theorems.
  • Intellectual honesty: multi-epoch and fresh-data limits stated openly.

Limitations

  • One-epoch / fresh-data gap: multi-epoch training; transfer to real LM pretraining is an open question.
  • Synthetic-only: hand-designed, perfectly separable factors — real linguistic semantics is messier.
  • Scale: small transformers, small vocabularies; phase-transition timing not shown predictable.
  • Theory is preliminary: restrictive assumptions; BoW proxy omits attention & depth.
  • No downstream evaluation: functional importance of the transient phase untested.

Open Questions

  1. Does the transient persist in a single-epoch, fresh-data regime at realistic scale?
  2. What determines the timing/sharpness of the phase transition — can it be predicted a priori?
  3. Is the transient phase functionally important (early-stopping → better OOD / probing / ICL)?
  4. Can the Spherical BoW mechanism extend to attention-based models?
  5. How do soft labels interpolate between the semantic-bridge and transient one-hot geometries?
  6. Do real linguistic "semantics" factorize cleanly enough to build a Sem-RSM?
