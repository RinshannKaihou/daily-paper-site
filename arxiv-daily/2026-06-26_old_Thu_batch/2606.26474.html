Localizing RL-Induced Tool Use to a Single Crosscoder Feature

RL-instilled tool use localizes to one steerable neuron — adding 65 points of correctness with zero retraining.

Andrii Shportko, Shubham Bhokare et al. · Northwestern University · 2026-06-25 · arXiv 2606.26474

Why This Matters
A runtime side-channel for capability

First systematic evidence that RL-instilled agentic behavior sits in a handful of steerable features — and that crosscoders can spill +6.8 pp of it into a frozen base model, a real safety risk.

Critical Edge
DFC isolates nothing

The exclusive partition behaves as a filter, not a sink: penalizing it pushes tool signal back into the shared sub-dictionary — so DFC partially refutes its own design intent.

Self-undermining result

Methodology

The authors diff two Qwen2.5-3B models — base (Model B) vs. ToolRL-fine-tuned (Model A, d=2048, 36 layers) — using Dedicated Feature Crosscoders (DFC), which split a joint dictionary into A-exclusive, B-exclusive, and shared sub-dictionaries enforced by gradient masking. Training mixes 40k FineWeb general samples with 40k ToolRL instruction pairs; the loss blends MSE reconstruction with L1 on shared (λsh) and on exclusive+shared (λexcl) slices, plus top-k sparsity.

A 48-variant sweep varies five axes — architecture (CrossCoder vs DFC), dictionary size {8192, 16384}, top-k {45, 90, 160}, exclusive share {3, 5, 10%}, λexcl {0, 10⁻³} — yielding 36 DFC + 12 CrossCoder runs (9k steps, batch 1024, Adam lr=10⁻⁴). Behavioral scoring uses format_accuracy, tool_correctness (fuzzy-match to a numbered tool), and an overall score, on 100 held-out prompts per variant (40 per steering cell, greedy decoding).

Neurons are ranked by Cohen's d between tool-use and general-text activations, filtered by firing rate. Targeted steering applies an additive correction to Model A's residual stream; decoder geometry is probed with mass-ratios, UMAP + HDBSCAN, k-NN purity, and ARI against a matched-size CrossCoder proxy to rule out UMAP artifacts.

Strengths

  • Pre-registered hypothesis table (8 hypotheses) with explicit confirm/reject status — including the rejected H7.
  • Matched-size UMAP control isolates the architectural cause of DFC separation from label imbalance.
  • Genuinely surprising, safety-relevant spillover finding (inference-time side channel).
  • Strong statistical hygiene: CIs, multiple test types, effect sizes, explicit "n.s." labels.
  • Practical, gradient-free, inspectable runtime control complementing RLHF/DPO/SFT.

Limitations

  • Single model pair (Qwen2.5-3B) and single task — generalization untested.
  • Underpowered architecture comparison (12 CC vs 36 DFC); DFC>CC claim is directional only.
  • Steering evaluated on n=40 prompts per cell; best-cell not replicated on a fresh sample.
  • Shared partition — which carries most spilled capability — is unexamined by autointerp.
  • "Capability" means structured tool-call propensity under a fixed prompt, not tool-use writ large.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does single-neuron steering saturation generalize beyond Qwen2.5-3B / ToolRL — to larger models, RLHF/DPO, or other agentic capabilities?
  2. Can the capability-spillover side channel be suppressed by objective design, or is it inherent to joint sparse decomposition of non-orthogonal capabilities?
  3. What does autointerp of the shared partition reveal, given it carries most of the spilled tool capability?
  4. How do Delta-Crosscoders (Kassem et al., 2026) — which model asymmetric fine-tuning — change the filter-vs-sink finding and spillover magnitude?
  5. Is the +65 pp effect robust to prompt distribution shift, or overfit to the 40-prompt evaluation cell?
  6. Can single-neuron steering be inverted (clamped to zero) to suppress unwanted RL behaviors under adversarial prompting?
