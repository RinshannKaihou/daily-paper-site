Why This Matters
Held-out accuracy cannot expose fine-tuning-introduced evasion vulnerabilities; a cheap canonical-input monitoring workflow (linear probe + indicator-token sign test) flags them pre-deployment for any security-classification team.
⚠ Critical Edge
Headline evasion signals rest on 4-pair groups and a single model pair with only r = 0.41 cross-model probe transfer — so generalization to other security fine-tunes remains unproven.

Methodology

The authors study a natural base/fine-tuned pair — Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct and Foundation-Sec-8B-Instruct — sharing the same 32-layer, 32-head architecture, so any behavioral difference is attributable to fine-tuning rather than architecture. The task is binary malicious-code classification for PowerShell scripts, evaluated on a 293-pair within-family matched cohort (456 unique scripts, seven indicator families), where benign and malicious scripts share suspicious indicators so token presence alone cannot solve it.

The four-stage pipeline:

  1. Discovery. Head-level attention over suspicious spans, coarse layer-band ablations, a defined "malicious direction" residual-stream vector, and per-head contribution decomposition.
  2. Validation. Two grouped causal interventions on the 293-pair cohort: path patching (sufficiency) copies candidate route activations from malicious into benign scripts; head ablation (necessity) disables candidate heads. Reported as mean decision-score shift and flip rate.
  3. Evasion benchmark. Variants generated only from baseline-correct seeds across four transformations (keyword_hiding, string_construction, execution_indirection, case_mutation) in three tiers, validated via PowerShell parser / tree-sitter plus behavioral invariants (URL-set preservation, literal paths, -EncodedCommand equivalence).
  4. Drift monitoring. A 5-fold Ridge linear probe from resid_pre13 predicts per-head contributions of the four core Layer-12 heads; an indicator-token sign test mean-ablates canonical indicator tokens and compares confidence-delta signs between base and fine-tuned models to flag role-reversed families.

The central mechanistic question — whether evasion deletes the malicious-evidence route or preserves it while later computation reverses its use — is answered via the same circuit measurements on seed vs. variant scripts.

Key mechanistic finding: in successful evasion (e.g., iwr alias), the late attention heads still fire — the malicious evidence is still present. MLP computation before Layer 13 reverses the signal. Evasion = signal reversal, not detection-route deletion.

Monitoring: linear probe r = 0.80–0.87 within-model; indicator-token sign test flags Invoke-WebRequest (+1.13 vs Llama −1.60) as the red-team priority before any variants are tested.

Strengths

  • Clean natural experiment: shared architecture isolates fine-tuning effects.
  • Dual causal tests (sufficiency + necessity) with family-level granularity and an honest claim ladder.
  • Cheap operational output: family-level red-team prioritization from canonical inputs only.
  • Reusable benchmark with explicit behavioral invariants enabling failure attribution.
  • Signal-reversal (vs deletion) reframes evasion in fine-tuned models.
  • Transparent about limitations: small counts, single pair, filtered cohort.

Limitations

  • Small sample sizes (4/4 headline groups); not stable prevalence estimates.
  • Single base/fine-tuned model pair — generalization unproven.
  • Cohort restricted to baseline-correct matched pairs; narrow external validity.
  • Reversal mechanism distributed across MLP layers 0–12, not localized.
  • Cross-model probe transfer modest (r = 0.41); reliable only at family level.
  • No comparison vs adversarial training, canonicalization, or ensembles.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does the "inherited circuit + specialization → brittle indicator rules" pattern replicate across other security fine-tunes (different bases, phishing/URL classifiers, non-PowerShell)?
  2. Can the Layer-13-boundary MLP reversal be localized to specific feed-forward neurons, and would targeted editing remove the evasion surface without hurting baseline accuracy?
  3. Does adversarial training on flagged families close the gaps, or merely shift the brittle-indicator problem elsewhere (as prompt-remediation hints)?
  4. Is the driver-to-suppressor sign flip a reliable signal across model scale?
  5. Could a canonicalization front-end neutralize the evasion surface more cheaply, and how do the two compose?
  6. What is evasion prevalence on the full unfiltered distribution, and does the inherited-circuit mechanism persist there?
