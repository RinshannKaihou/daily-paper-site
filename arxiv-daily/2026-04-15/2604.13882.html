Evaluating Supervised Machine Learning Models: Principles, Pitfalls, and Metric Selection

一句话总结： 通过15个真实数据集上的7个受控实验场景，系统揭示监督学习评估中指标不一致现象（如不平衡数据下准确率虚高、R2掩盖残差结构等），提出以任务目标和错误代价为核心的指标选择与验证协议设计框架。
TL;DR: Through 7 controlled experimental scenarios on 15 real-world datasets spanning classification and regression, this paper systematically demonstrates how metric disagreement arises under class imbalance, asymmetric costs, multiclass skew, and outlier-sensitive regression, and argues that metric selection must be aligned with the operational objective and error cost structure of the deployment context.

核心贡献

  • 提出了"指标不一致性"（metric disagreement）的系统分析框架，证明同一模型在不同指标下可呈现截然相反的结论，且这种分歧是数据结构特性的必然结果而非统计异常。
  • 在分类任务中揭示了四种典型评估陷阱：不平衡数据下的准确率悖论、医疗诊断中假阴性与假阳性代价不对称问题、多类场景下micro/macro平均策略的差异、以及概率校准不足导致的Log Loss与Accuracy排名反转。
  • 在回归任务中展示了三种关键误判风险：MAE与RMSE对离群值的敏感度差异、R2作为独立指标对残差结构的掩盖效应、以及MAPE在低基数值上的不稳定性。
  • 将模型评估定位为"面向决策、依赖上下文"的方法论问题，提出指标选择应基于预测任务类型、数据集结构属性和应用特定的错误代价三个维度进行对齐。

Key Contributions

  • Introduces a structured "metric disagreement" analysis framework, demonstrating across 15 datasets that divergent metric conclusions are a systematic and interpretable phenomenon rather than a statistical curiosity.
  • Identifies four classification evaluation pitfalls through controlled scenarios: the accuracy paradox under class imbalance (e.g., 81% Accuracy vs. 0.37 MCC on the Credit Card dataset), asymmetric misclassification costs in medical settings, micro/macro averaging divergence in multiclass problems (e.g., Covertype: 0.89 Micro F1 vs. 0.84 Macro F1), and probability calibration failures where Log Loss reverses model rankings.
  • Reveals three regression evaluation risks: MAE-RMSE divergence on heavy-tailed targets (California Housing: MAE 0.33 vs. RMSE 0.51), the illusion of R2 without residual analysis, and MAPE instability near zero baselines (Seoul Bike: MAE 14.6 but MAPE 16.6%).
  • Positions model evaluation as a decision-oriented, context-dependent methodological problem, advocating that metric selection must be justified in relation to data characteristics and operational error costs rather than default reporting conventions.

Methodology

The study employs a scenario-driven experimental design across 15 publicly available benchmark datasets (10 classification, 5 regression) from UCI and OpenML repositories. Classification datasets span binary and multiclass tasks across finance, medicine, agriculture, ecology, text, image recognition, demographics, and automotive domains, with class counts ranging from 2 to 26 and instance counts from 400 to 581,012. Regression datasets cover real estate, energy, medical, transportation, and clinical domains, with sizes from 442 to over 2 million instances.

All experiments use 5-fold stratified cross-validation (for classification) to ensure stable generalization estimates and reduce partition-sensitivity. Models include Random Forest and Decision Tree classifiers/regressors as the primary algorithms. Classification metrics evaluated include Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1, MCC, ROC AUC, PR AUC, F2 Score, and Log Loss. Regression metrics include MAE, RMSE, R2, and MAPE. Seven controlled scenarios isolate specific evaluation challenges: (1) accuracy trap in imbalanced binary classification, (2) asymmetric misclassification costs in medical diagnostics, (3) micro vs. macro averaging in multiclass settings, (4) probability calibration via Log Loss, (5) MAE-RMSE divergence under outliers, (6) R2 limitations with residual analysis, and (7) MAPE instability near zero baselines.

Results & Highlights

  • Imbalanced binary classification: On the Default of Credit Card dataset, Random Forest achieved 81.69% Accuracy but only 0.3851 MCC and 0.5230 PR AUC, demonstrating that high accuracy is largely driven by true negatives on the majority class. On Bank Marketing, 90.31% Accuracy coincided with only 0.5855 PR AUC and 0.4460 MCC.
  • Medical diagnostics: On the Breast Cancer dataset, a model with 96.48% Accuracy and 95.80% Precision yielded an F2 Score of only 94.98%, highlighting that F1 (which equally weights Precision and Recall) underrepresents the clinical cost of false negatives.
  • Multiclass averaging: Micro F1 matched global Accuracy across all four multiclass datasets (Dry Bean 0.9226, Covertype 0.8916, 20 Newsgroups 0.6879, Letter Recognition 0.9615), while Macro F1 diverged substantially for imbalanced datasets like Covertype (0.8425), but converged with Micro F1 for balanced datasets like Letter Recognition (0.9614).
  • Probability calibration: On the Car Evaluation dataset, Decision Tree outperformed Random Forest in Accuracy (0.9167 vs. 0.9039) but was dramatically worse in Log Loss (3.0035 vs. 0.3350), revealing severe overconfidence in incorrect predictions.
  • Regression outlier sensitivity: On California Housing, Random Forest achieved MAE of 0.3308 but RMSE of 0.5085, reflecting disproportionate penalties on luxury property outliers. On Power Consumption, MAE was 0.0216 but RMSE was 0.0401.
  • R2 illusion: On the Parkinson dataset, R2 reached 0.9673 with MAE of 0.8398, potentially creating false confidence without residual analysis to detect structured prediction bias at higher symptom levels.
  • MAPE instability: On the Power Consumption dataset, MAE was only 0.0216 but MAPE reached 3.49%, and on Seoul Bike Sharing, MAE was 14.57 but MAPE was 16.62%, driven by near-zero baselines inflating percentage errors.

Critical Assessment

This paper makes a valuable pedagogical and methodological contribution by systematizing well-known but frequently disregarded evaluation pitfalls into a coherent experimental framework. The scenario-driven approach effectively demonstrates that metric disagreement is not a marginal phenomenon but a structural feature of non-ideal data settings. The seven scenarios cover the most practically relevant failure modes. However, the study relies exclusively on Random Forest and Decision Tree models, leaving open the question of whether the same patterns hold for gradient-boosted trees, neural networks, or linear models. The paper also does not introduce new metrics or a formal decision procedure for metric selection; its contribution is primarily analytical and cautionary. The experimental design, while thorough, stops short of proposing an automated or semi-automated framework to help practitioners actually select metrics given their task constraints.

Strengths

  • Comprehensive coverage of both classification and regression evaluation with 15 diverse datasets and 7 well-structured experimental scenarios.
  • Clear, reproducible experimental protocol using 5-fold stratified cross-validation with both fold-level and aggregate reporting.
  • Strong pedagogical value: each scenario isolates a specific pitfall with concrete numerical evidence (e.g., 81% Accuracy vs. 0.37 MCC).
  • Practical recommendations grounded in empirical evidence, not just theory.
  • Includes both threshold-dependent and calibration-sensitive metrics (Log Loss), an often-overlooked dimension.

Limitations

  • Only two model families tested (Random Forest and Decision Tree); generalization to deep learning, SVMs, or gradient boosting is not established.
  • No novel metrics or formal metric-selection algorithm proposed; the paper remains descriptive rather than prescriptive.
  • The study does not address temporal or spatial data leakage scenarios experimentally, despite discussing them in the related work.
  • Limited statistical testing (e.g., no significance tests comparing metrics across folds or datasets).
  • The regression experiments use only three metrics (MAE, RMSE, R2) plus MAPE; other important metrics like quantile loss or calibration in regression are not explored.

Relevance

该论文与"机器学习可靠性与评估方法论"高度相关，为从业者提供了系统性的指标选择指南。对于任何涉及模型评估、基准测试或AutoML中指标自动选择的研究，本文的实验结论和警示具有重要的参考价值。七个场景中揭示的指标不一致现象，特别是准确率悖论和R2的局限性，对所有依赖单一指标进行模型选择的实践都有直接启发。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How do the observed metric disagreement patterns generalize to deep learning models (e.g., transformers, CNNs) and gradient-boosted trees (XGBoost, LightGBM)?
  • Can an automated decision-support tool be built that recommends primary metrics based on dataset characteristics (class distribution, outlier ratio, target scale)?
  • What role should cost-sensitive learning or threshold optimization play as a complement to metric selection in addressing the asymmetric error cost problem?
  • How would the findings change under distribution shift or concept drift, where the evaluation data diverges from training data?
  • Are the observed metric disagreement patterns stable under different cross-validation schemes (e.g., 10-fold, leave-one-out, nested CV)?
