# Stochastic Trust-Region Methods for Over-parameterized Models

> **一句话总结：** 本文提出统一的随机信赖域(STR)优化框架，在强增长条件下对无约束问题达到O(eps^{-2} log(1/eps))复杂度，通过二次罚函数扩展到等式约束问题达到O(eps^{-4} log(1/eps))复杂度，无需手动调节学习率即可在深度网络训练和正交约束子空间拟合中获得与精调基线相当的性能。

> **TL;DR:** Proposes a unified stochastic trust-region (STR) framework for over-parameterized models that eliminates manual step-size tuning, achieves O(eps^{-2} log(1/eps)) oracle complexity for unconstrained problems under the strong growth condition, and O(eps^{-4} log(1/eps)) for equality-constrained problems via quadratic penalty, with competitive empirical performance on deep learning and orthogonal subspace fitting tasks.

## 核心贡献

- 提出首个基于插值类假设（强增长条件）的统一随机信赖域复杂度分析框架，填补了信赖域方法在过参数化模型理论分析中的空白。
- 针对无约束优化设计一阶随机信赖域算法，在强增长条件下证明迭代复杂度和随机一阶预言机复杂度均为O(eps^{-2} log(1/eps))，与确定性全梯度方法可比。
- 通过二次罚函数将框架自然扩展至等式约束问题，选取罚参数 mu=1/eps，证明复杂度为O(eps^{-4} log(1/eps))，对应原问题O(eps)-近似KKT点。
- 在CIFAR-10深度网络训练和正交约束子空间拟合实验中验证了方法的有效性，无需手动学习率调度即可获得与精调SGD/Adam相当的性能，并能稳定处理硬约束。

## Key Contributions

- Proposes the first unified complexity analysis of stochastic trust-region methods under interpolation-type assumptions (strong growth condition), bridging a gap between classical TR theory and modern over-parameterized learning.
- Develops a first-order STR algorithm for unconstrained nonconvex optimization that achieves O(eps^{-2} log(1/eps)) iteration and stochastic first-order oracle complexity for finding an eps-stationary point.
- Introduces a quadratic-penalty-based STR method for equality-constrained problems, proving O(eps^{-4} log(1/eps)) complexity to reach an O(eps)-approximate KKT point of the original problem.
- Validates the methods empirically on ResNet-20 / CIFAR-10 classification and orthogonal subspace fitting, demonstrating competitive performance with well-tuned baselines while eliminating the need for manual learning-rate scheduling.

## Methodology

The paper develops a unified stochastic trust-region framework in two stages:

**Unconstrained setting (Algorithm 1):** At each iteration, the method samples a single data index i, computes the stochastic gradient g_k = nabla f_i(x_k), and solves a first-order trust-region subproblem m_k(p) = g_k^T p + (1/2)||p||^2 subject to ||p|| <= Delta_k. The solution has a closed-form Cauchy-type expression p_k = -a_k g_k, where a_k is adaptively determined by the trust-region radius. The acceptance ratio r_k compares actual function reduction (evaluated on the same sample) to model-predicted reduction. Based on r_k, the algorithm decides whether to accept the step and adjusts Delta_k via a standard multiplicative update rule (shrink on poor ratios, expand on good ones). The step size a_k is bounded below by 2(1-c_0)/(L_max - c_0), ensuring sufficient descent at successful iterations.

**Constrained setting (Algorithm 2):** For equality-constrained problems min f(x) s.t. c(x)=0, the method transforms the problem via quadratic penalty: phi(x) = f(x) + (mu/2)||c(x)||^2 with mu = 1/eps. The local quadratic model incorporates the constraint-induced Hessian H_k = I + mu nabla c(x_k)^T nabla c(x_k) at negligible additional cost. The Cauchy-type step again admits a closed-form solution. Under the extended strong growth condition on the stochastic Lagrangian and the LICQ assumption, the method finds an O(eps)-approximate KKT point.

**Theoretical analysis:** The convergence proofs leverage three key lemmas: (1) a lower bound on the step-size parameter a_k ensuring sufficient decrease, (2) a lower bound on the trust-region radius preventing it from collapsing, and (3) a bound on the ratio of unsuccessful to successful iterations. These are combined with the strong growth condition E[||g_k||^2] <= rho ||nabla f(x_k)||^2 to establish the convergence rates. For the constrained case, an additional result (Lemma 4.12) shows that stationarity of the penalized objective implies proximity to a KKT point of the original problem, with constraint violation bounded by C*eps.

## Results & Highlights

- **Unconstrained (CIFAR-10 / ResNet-20):** STR matches the final test accuracy of SGD (lr=0.2), Adam, and SLS. While STR shows slightly higher training loss in later epochs, this reflects the conservative stability of trust-region updates rather than a deficiency -- generalization remains on par with all baselines.
- **Constrained -- moderate scale (d=100, k=5, n=500):** STR-P (penalty-based STR) achieves faster objective reduction than projected SGD, Riemannian GD, and stochastic augmented Lagrangian in early and intermediate phases. Constraint violation ||W^TW - I||_F is driven to near machine precision.
- **Constrained -- larger scale (d=500, k=10, n=1000):** The favorable behavior of STR-P persists at larger scale, consistently outperforming AugLag and matching projection-based methods in final objective value while not requiring explicit QR retraction at every step.
- **Key practical advantage:** STR eliminates the need for learning-rate scheduling entirely. The trust-region radius adapts automatically based on local model quality, providing a principled alternative to manual hyperparameter tuning.

## Critical Assessment

The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution by providing the first unified complexity analysis of stochastic trust-region methods under interpolation-type assumptions. The extension to equality-constrained problems via quadratic penalty is natural and well-executed. The O(eps^{-2} log(1/eps)) complexity for the unconstrained case is nearly optimal (up to a log factor) compared to the O(eps^{-2}) lower bound for first-order methods. The constrained result O(eps^{-4} log(1/eps)) is expected given the penalty parameter scaling mu = 1/eps. However, the practical experiments are somewhat limited in scope: only one unconstrained deep learning benchmark (CIFAR-10 with ResNet-20) and one constrained synthetic problem are tested. The paper would benefit from larger-scale experiments (e.g., ImageNet, real-world constrained optimization) and comparison with more recent adaptive methods (e.g., AdaGrad, LAMB). The first-order algorithm ignores curvature information (H_k = I), which may limit its effectiveness on ill-conditioned problems.

## Strengths

- Clean, unified theoretical framework covering both unconstrained and constrained settings under a common set of assumptions.
- Eliminates the need for manual learning-rate tuning -- a significant practical advantage.
- Closed-form Cauchy-type solutions for the trust-region subproblem keep per-iteration cost low.
- The penalty-based constrained approach is flexible and avoids the need for explicit projection or manifold retraction at each step.
- Rigorous convergence proofs with explicit complexity bounds.

## Limitations

- The strong growth condition (SGC) is a strong assumption that may not hold in practice for all over-parameterized models, especially with regularization or early stopping.
- The first-order variant (H_k = I) ignores curvature, which could slow convergence on ill-conditioned problems compared to second-order or adaptive methods.
- Experimental evaluation is limited to one deep learning benchmark and one synthetic constrained problem; broader empirical validation is needed.
- The constrained complexity O(eps^{-4} log(1/eps)) is polynomially worse than the unconstrained case, which is inherent to the penalty approach but may limit scalability.
- The assumption that iterates remain in a compact set (Assumption 6) is standard but not verified algorithmically.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can second-order information (e.g., Hessian approximations via subsampling or quasi-Newton updates) be incorporated to improve performance on ill-conditioned problems while maintaining the complexity guarantees?
- How does the STR framework compare with adaptive gradient methods (AdaGrad, Adam, LAMB) on larger-scale benchmarks such as ImageNet or language model fine-tuning?
- Is it possible to develop an adaptive penalty update mechanism (rather than fixing mu = 1/eps) that improves the constrained complexity bound?
- Can the framework be extended to handle inequality constraints (e.g., via barrier or augmented Lagrangian methods)?
- How sensitive are the results to the choice of trust-region hyperparameters (c_0, c_1, c_2, nu_1, nu_2), and can these be set adaptively?
- Does the strong growth condition hold in practice for modern architectures with techniques like dropout, weight decay, or batch normalization?
