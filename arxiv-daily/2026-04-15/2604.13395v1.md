# Quantifying and Understanding Uncertainty in Large Reasoning Models

> **一句话总结：** 针对大型推理模型（LRMs），提出CoRAP框架，利用共形预测对推理链-答案结构进行联合不确定性量化并提供有限样本统计保证，同时基于Shapley值构建分层示例到步骤的解释框架，在CLEVR-Math和ScienceQA上生成更紧凑的不确定性集并准确识别关键训练样本和推理步骤。

> **TL;DR:** Proposes CoRAP, a conformal prediction framework that jointly quantifies uncertainty over reasoning-answer pairs in Large Reasoning Models with finite-sample coverage guarantees, and a hierarchical Shapley value-based explanation method that identifies provably sufficient subsets of training examples and reasoning steps preserving those guarantees, validated on CLEVR-Math and ScienceQA benchmarks.

## 核心贡献

- 提出CoRAP（Conformal Reasoning-Answer Prediction）方法，通过定义序列质量函数Q、集合置信函数F和条件答案质量函数A三个质量指标，将推理链与最终答案的逻辑依赖关系纳入不确定性量化，利用FWER校准构建具有有限样本保证的不确定性预测集
- 开发基于Shapley值的分层"示例到步骤"解释框架：先识别对不确定性覆盖贡献最大的训练样本子集，再在其内部定位关键推理步骤，两层均通过蒙特卡洛近似和Hoeffding置信下界提供可证明的充分性保证（Theorem 3.2）
- 提供完整理论分析：Theorem 3.1保证预测集的风险E[LRA] <= alpha（概率至少1-epsilon），Theorem 3.2保证所提取的训练子集足以维持该风险控制，并设计分组预热选择策略将计算复杂度从O(n^2)降至与训练规模解耦
- 在CLEVR-Math和ScienceQA上，CoRAP在多个alpha水平下均保持经验损失低于目标阈值，预测集大小显著小于基线CP-Router，Shapley解释的成功率（0.833-1.000）均超过理论下界1-delta

## Key Contributions

- Introduces CoRAP, which defines three distinct quality functions (sequence quality Q, set confidence F, conditional answer quality A) to disentangle reasoning trace quality from answer correctness, applying FWER-controlled binomial-tail p-values over a discrete threshold grid to certify that prediction sets cover a valid reasoning-answer pair with probability at least 1-alpha
- Develops a unified hierarchical Shapley value explanation framework operating at two levels: example-level attribution identifies pivotal training samples via Monte Carlo Shapley estimation with lower confidence bounds, and step-level attribution further isolates critical reasoning steps within selected examples, both with provable sufficiency guarantees (Theorem 3.2)
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis including Theorem 3.1 (risk control of CoRAP under FWER correction) and Theorem 3.2 (sufficiency of extracted subsets for preserving risk guarantees), with a group-based warm-start preselection strategy that decouples inference cost from quadratic scaling in training data size
- Demonstrates empirically on CLEVR-Math and ScienceQA using LMM-R1 (3B), R1-Onevision (7B), and LLaVA-CoT (11B) that CoRAP produces smaller prediction sets than CP-Router while maintaining coverage, and that Shapley-based explanations achieve success rates exceeding theoretical lower bounds

## Methodology

The paper tackles two coupled problems for Large Reasoning Models (LRMs): (1) constructing statistically valid uncertainty sets for the reasoning-answer structure, and (2) explaining which training factors drive the uncertainty coverage.

**CoRAP (Conformal Reasoning-Answer Prediction).** Given a calibration set, the method samples multiple candidate reasoning-answer sequences from the LRM. Three quality functions filter candidates: Q measures sequence-level log-probability, F evaluates the best candidate in the accumulating set, and A checks the model's confidence in the answer conditioned on the generated reasoning trace. A three-dimensional threshold vector is calibrated on a held-out calibration set using binomial-tail p-values with family-wise error rate (FWER) control at level epsilon. Theorem 3.1 guarantees that the selected threshold yields expected loss below alpha with probability at least 1-epsilon over the calibration data.

**Shapley Value Explanations.** At the example level, each training example is treated as a player in a cooperative game where the value function measures whether CoRAP successfully covers the test answer when trained on a coalition of examples. Monte Carlo permutations estimate Shapley values, and Hoeffding-based lower confidence bounds identify the smallest subset whose cumulative certified contribution exceeds 1-alpha. At the step level, the same procedure is applied to individual reasoning steps within the selected examples. Theorem 3.2 guarantees that both the example subset and step subset suffice to maintain the CoRAP risk control.

**Computational Optimization.** Training examples are partitioned into G disjoint groups. A warm-start preselection with a small number of permutations identifies the active group, restricting intensive estimation to that group alone. This reduces the overall complexity to be dominated by group-level operations rather than individual training samples, enabling practical application to large-scale models.

## Results & Highlights

- **Validity:** On both CLEVR-Math and ScienceQA, CoRAP keeps empirical loss strictly below the target alpha across all tested levels (alpha = 0.3, 0.4, 0.5), while CP-Router sometimes violates the target. On R1-Onevision with CLEVR-Math at higher alpha levels (0.6-0.8), CoRAP similarly outperforms.
- **Efficiency:** CoRAP generates significantly smaller prediction sets than CP-Router. On CLEVR-Math with LMM-R1, CoRAP produces sets of size ~2-3 versus CP-Router's ~4-6. On ScienceQA, CoRAP achieves ~5-7 versus CP-Router's ~10-15.
- **Example-level explanations:** Shapley-based selection achieves top-1 Shapley values of 0.433 (LMM-R1) and 0.356 (R1-Onevision) versus 0.042 and 0.021 for random baselines. Success rates for maintaining risk control reach 0.833 (LMM-R1) and 1.000 (R1-Onevision), exceeding the 1-delta_ex = 0.75 threshold.
- **Step-level explanations:** Top-1 Shapley values reach 0.739 (LMM-R1) and 0.589 (R1-Onevision) versus near-zero for random selection. Success rates of 0.875 and 0.750 again exceed theoretical lower bounds.
- **Calibration sensitivity:** Varying epsilon from 0.2 to 0.8 shows the expected trade-off: higher epsilon (looser FWER) yields modestly smaller prediction sets while maintaining validity. Increasing calibration set size from 300 to 1500 reduces loss variance and improves stability.
- **Computational cost:** The influence function approximation is substantially faster than both full fine-tuning and LoRA across varying training data sizes. Group-based partitioning further reduces running time significantly as group count increases.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** The combination of conformal prediction with reasoning-answer structure is a meaningful extension beyond standard CP for label prediction. The hierarchical Shapley explanation with provable sufficiency guarantees is the strongest contribution, as prior data valuation work lacks statistical certification of extracted subsets.
- **Theoretical soundness:** The proofs are clean and rely on well-established tools (FWER control, Hoeffding bounds, Shapley efficiency). The assumptions are reasonable for i.i.d. settings. However, the condition v(P\U) >= v(P) - sum(phi_u) - xi in Theorem 3.2 is a structural assumption on the value function that may not always hold in practice.
- **Experimental scope:** Only two benchmarks (CLEVR-Math and ScienceQA) are tested, both in multimodal settings. The models are relatively small (3B-11B parameters). The paper acknowledges this limitation but does not discuss how the framework would scale to frontier LRMs like o1 or DeepSeek-R1-671B.
- **Practical gap:** The FWER calibration over a grid of thresholds adds computational overhead. The admission function V relies on ROUGE-L >= 0.2, which is a weak proxy for reasoning validity. The influence function approximation assumes small parameter perturbations, which may not capture complex training dynamics.

## Strengths

- Principled statistical framework with rigorous finite-sample guarantees (Theorems 3.1 and 3.2)
- Novel disentanglement of reasoning quality from answer correctness through three quality functions (Q, F, A)
- Hierarchical explanation framework that provides both coarse-grained (example) and fine-grained (step) attribution with certified sufficiency
- Comprehensive ablation studies on calibration set size, sample count, epsilon levels, and computational cost
- Clear visualization of CoRAP vs. CP-Router behavior on individual examples (Figure 2)

## Limitations

- Evaluated only on two multimodal reasoning benchmarks; generalization to text-only LLMs, longer reasoning chains, and open-ended generation is untested
- Models tested are 3B-11B parameters; scalability to state-of-the-art LRMs (o1, DeepSeek-R1-671B) is unclear
- The admission function relies on ROUGE-L, a surface-level metric that may not reliably assess reasoning validity
- The structural assumption in Theorem 3.2 (bounded value function deviation) is not empirically validated
- Computational cost, while reduced via grouping, still involves multiple forward/backward passes per permutation for influence functions

## Relevance

本文与"LLM可靠性"和"推理模型评估"方向高度相关。CoRAP为推理模型输出提供带有统计保证的不确定性量化，填补了传统方法无法处理推理链-答案结构不确定性的空白。分层Shapley解释框架为理解训练数据对推理质量的影响提供了新视角，对数据策展和模型调试具有实际意义。研究可应用于需要可信推理的高风险场景，如医疗诊断辅助和法律推理。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does CoRAP scale to models with much longer reasoning traces (e.g., o1-style models producing thousands of tokens of chain-of-thought)?
- Can the admission function V be replaced with a learned verifier to improve filtering of invalid reasoning traces?
- Does the Shapley explanation framework reveal systematic failure patterns or data quality issues in training sets?
- How sensitive are the guarantees to violations of the i.i.d. assumption between calibration and test data?
- Can the framework be extended to open-ended generation tasks where the answer space is not discrete?
