Dataset-Level Metrics Attenuate Non-Determinism: A Fine-Grained Non-Determinism Evaluation in Diffusion Language Models

一句话总结： 揭示数据集级评估指标通过聚合效应系统性掩盖扩散语言模型（LLaDA/LLaDA-1.5）中样本级的非确定性预测翻转，提出Factor Variance Attribution (FVA)方差分解方法量化非确定性来源，发现代码生成任务的非确定性（FVA~0.80）远高于问答任务（FVA~0.50-0.60），且约81%的评估变异来自因素间差异而非因素内设置差异。
TL;DR: Demonstrates that dataset-level metrics systematically mask sample-level non-determinism in diffusion language models (LLaDA/LLaDA-1.5 8B), proposes Factor Variance Attribution (FVA) to decompose non-determinism into between-factor and within-factor variance, and reveals that code generation (FVA ~0.80) is far more sensitive to inference configuration choices than question answering (FVA ~0.50-0.60), with ~81% of variability driven by which factor is varied rather than specific setting choices.

核心贡献

  • 揭示数据集级指标（如整体准确率）通过跨配置聚合样本级预测质量，系统性掩盖了扩散语言模型中普遍存在的非确定性——不同推理配置在数据集层面表现相同，但对相同样本产生完全不同的预测结果和错误模式
  • 提出细粒度评估框架，系统性地研究模型相关因素（引导尺度CFG、扩散步数、蒙特卡洛采样次数）和系统相关因素（批大小、GPU类型、数值精度FP16/BF16/INT8）对样本级非确定性的影响
  • 引入Factor Variance Attribution (FVA)方差分解方法，将观测到的非确定性分解为因素间方差（between-factor）和因素内方差（within-factor），实现任务依赖的非确定性归因分析
  • 发现非确定性的结构性规律：代码生成对推理配置的敏感度远高于问答任务，且两个DLM主干网络（LLaDA和LLaDA-1.5）表现出一致的任务依赖FVA模式

Key Contributions

  • Identifies systematic attenuation of non-determinism by dataset-level metrics in diffusion language models: configurations yielding identical aggregate accuracy can produce divergent per-sample predictions and distinct error modes on the same inputs
  • Conducts a fine-grained evaluation framework spanning model-related factors (CFG scale, diffusion steps, MC sampling) and system-related factors (batch size, GPU type, numerical precision FP16/BF16/INT8) without any performance-driven tuning
  • Introduces Factor Variance Attribution (FVA), a variance-based decomposition metric that separates between-factor effects (which factor is varied) from within-factor sensitivity (specific setting choices), enabling task-dependent non-determinism attribution
  • Reveals that non-determinism in DLMs is pervasive and structured: code generation tasks exhibit markedly higher sensitivity (FVA ~0.80) than question answering (FVA ~0.50-0.60), with consistent patterns across both LLaDA and LLaDA-1.5 backbones

Methodology

The paper uses LLaDA and LLaDA-1.5 (both 8B parameter models) as diffusion language model backbones. LLaDA generates text through an iterative masked-token diffusion process, predicting all masked tokens in parallel at each step. The evaluation spans five benchmarks: PIQA, WinoGrande, and ARC-Challenge for question answering (multiple-choice, binary correctness); HumanEval and MBPP for code generation (execution-based unit tests).

The methodology operates at three levels: (1) Sample-level evaluation defines binary correctness indicators per (input, configuration) pair, measuring prediction flip rates across configurations while holding all other factors fixed. (2) Factor-vs-setting distinction separates evaluation variability into factor-level effects (e.g., CFG vs. steps vs. MC) and setting-level effects (e.g., FP16 vs. BF16 within precision). (3) Factor Variance Attribution (FVA) decomposes observed variance into between-factor variance and within-factor variance: FVA = sigma_squared_between / (sigma_squared_between + sigma_squared_within). FVA near 1.0 indicates non-determinism is dominated by which factor is varied; FVA near 0.5 indicates comparable between- and within-factor contributions.

The paper also extends the analysis to autoregressive models (LLaMA-2-7B, Qwen2.5-7B) in the appendix, finding that autoregressive models exhibit a different pattern where variability is attenuated under aggregation while remaining visible at the sample level. A sensitivity analysis confirms FVA is robust to changes in factor ranges and value selections (absolute FVA change < 0.03).

Results & Highlights

  • Dataset-level stability masks sample-level chaos: Across all factors, dataset-level standard deviation and standard error remain extremely small (e.g., accuracy Std < 0.002 for precision on PIQA), while sample-level standard deviations range from 0.30 to 0.50, indicating massive per-sample prediction variability hidden by aggregation.
  • Code generation is highly sensitive: For HumanEval and MBPP, dataset-level accuracy varies substantially across diffusion step and CFG settings (e.g., LLaDA HumanEval accuracy drops from 0.31 to 0.15 across step settings), with sample-level Std reaching 0.25-0.47. FVA for code generation reaches 0.797-0.800 (LLaDA), indicating that choosing which factor to vary is the primary driver of non-determinism.
  • Monte Carlo sampling does not monotonically reduce variability: Increasing MC samples reduces standard error but sample-level standard deviation remains large, suggesting variability is driven by systematic differences in how inputs are resolved, not merely stochastic noise.
  • Overall FVA ~0.81: Roughly 81% of observed variability is attributable to differences across factors (which dimension is varied), while ~19% arises from variation across settings within the same factor. This pattern is consistent across both LLaDA and LLaDA-1.5.
  • Autoregressive models show attenuated but present non-determinism: LLaMA-2-7B and Qwen2.5-7B exhibit much smaller absolute variance but similar sample-level patterns, confirming that non-determinism is not specific to diffusion models but manifests differently across generative paradigms.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The core statistical insight -- that aggregation attenuates variance -- is well-known in statistics, but its systematic demonstration for DLM evaluation is valuable and timely given the rapid growth of diffusion-based language models. The FVA decomposition is a principled and useful contribution.
  • Experimental rigor: The paper evaluates 6 factors across 2 model backbones and 5 datasets, with controlled single-factor variation and appendix-level comparisons to autoregressive models and sensitivity analysis. This is commendable breadth. However, interaction effects between factors are acknowledged but not studied.
  • Significance: The work is primarily diagnostic rather than prescriptive. It identifies and quantifies a problem but does not propose concrete solutions for mitigating non-determinism beyond suggesting that fine-grained evaluation should be adopted.
  • Scope limitation: The study is scoped to inference-time variability and does not address training-time randomness. The DLM backbones studied (LLaDA/LLaDA-1.5) represent only masked discrete diffusion models; continuous-space DLMs and other diffusion paradigms are not covered.

Strengths

  • Well-structured evaluation framework with clear factor-vs-setting hierarchy and formal mathematical definitions
  • Compelling qualitative examples (Table 1, Table 6) showing identical dataset-level scores with contradictory per-sample predictions
  • FVA decomposition provides an interpretable and robust metric for cross-factor non-determinism attribution
  • Sensitivity analysis (Appendix F) confirms FVA stability under changes to factor ranges and value selections
  • Inclusion of autoregressive model comparison (Appendix E) strengthens the generality of findings

Limitations

  • Primarily observational/diagnostic -- limited actionable recommendations for practitioners beyond "use fine-grained evaluation"
  • Does not model interaction effects between factors (e.g., CFG scale combined with diffusion steps)
  • Limited to LLaDA/LLaDA-1.5 as DLM backbones; results may not generalize to continuous-space DLMs or other discrete diffusion architectures
  • Does not study training-time sources of non-determinism or propose methods to mitigate inference-time non-determinism
  • The paper acknowledges that its factor choices are not exhaustive and that broader task coverage (long-horizon reasoning, structured prediction) is needed

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How do interaction effects between factors (e.g., CFG scale x diffusion steps) contribute to non-determinism, and can multi-factor FVA extensions capture these?
  • Can FVA guide the design of more robust inference configurations that minimize sample-level non-determinism while preserving generation quality?
  • Do the findings generalize to continuous-space diffusion language models and to larger-scale DLMs beyond 8B parameters?
  • How should evaluation benchmarks and reporting standards be redesigned to incorporate sample-level non-determinism metrics alongside traditional aggregate scores?
  • What is the relationship between training-time randomness and inference-time non-determinism, and can joint attribution improve model reliability assessment?
