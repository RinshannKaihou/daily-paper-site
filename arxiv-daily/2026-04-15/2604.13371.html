Empirical Evidence of Complexity-Induced Limits in Large Language Models on Finite Discrete State-Space Problems with Explicit Validity Constraints

一句话总结： 本文提出"推理崩塌"(Reasoning Collapse)概念，通过9个参数化离散推理任务（汉诺塔、SAT、数独、魔方等）构建确定性验证框架，系统揭示LLM在复杂度超过临界阈值后出现断崖式性能衰退，并归纳出四种核心失败机制。
TL;DR: This paper formalizes "Reasoning Collapse" -- a sharp, non-linear degradation in LLM success probability beyond a complexity threshold -- using 9 parameterized puzzle environments with deterministic validators. Across 6 frontier models (GPT-5 Pro, Gemini 3 Pro, Claude 3.7 Sonnet, etc.), the steepest drop occurs between complexity levels L3 and L4. Four failure mechanisms are identified: long-horizon state degradation, constraint propagation failure, unstable feasibility planning, and non-scaling of reasoning with token length.

核心贡献

  • 提出"推理崩塌"的形式化定义：将LLM在离散推理任务上的性能退化建模为成功率关于复杂度的函数，临界阈值 lambda* 定义为 dAcc(lambda)/dlambda 的最大值点，标志着从"逐步退化"到"崩塌"的相变
  • 构建9个参数化推理任务的确定性评估框架：Tower of Hanoi（递归规划）、Checker Jumping（序列规划）、River Crossing（安全约束规划）、Boolean SAT（全局约束满足）、Cryptarithmetic（符号CSP）、Graph Coloring（组合CSP）、Water Jug（算术规划）、Sudoku（稠密全局CSP）、Rubik's Cube（空间搜索），每个任务配有形式化验证器
  • 揭示L3-L4复杂度悬崖：在6个前沿模型上发现最剧烈的性能衰减发生在中等复杂度（L3）到高复杂度（L4）的过渡区间
  • 归纳四种失败机制：长时域状态退化、约束传播失败、不稳定可行性规划、推理不随token长度缩放
  • 引入确定性验证范式：每个任务均有独立的算法验证器（不依赖模型自述），对输出进行严格合法性检查，杜绝了"看似合理但实际错误"的解答通过评估

Key Contributions

  • Formalizes "Reasoning Collapse" as a phase-transition phenomenon where LLM success probability drops sharply beyond a complexity threshold lambda* = argmax of dAcc(lambda)/dlambda, distinguishing it from gradual linear degradation
  • 9 parameterized puzzle environments with deterministic validators: Each task spans multiple complexity levels (L1-L6+) and has an independent algorithmic validator that checks solution correctness and constraint satisfaction -- no reliance on model-generated explanations or self-assessment
  • Identifies the L3-L4 complexity cliff: The most severe performance degradation across all 6 frontier models occurs at the transition from moderate (L3) to high (L4) complexity, not at the extremes
  • Four failure mechanism taxonomy: (1) Long-horizon state degradation -- errors accumulate over multi-step state transitions; (2) Constraint propagation failure -- local consistency checks not propagated globally; (3) Unstable feasibility planning -- inability to determine solvability before committing; (4) Non-scaling of reasoning with token length -- longer chains of thought do not proportionally improve accuracy
  • Cross-model comparison covering Qwen Plus 0728 Thinking, Kimi K2 Thinking, Claude 3.7 Sonnet Thinking, Gemini 3 Pro Preview, GPT-5 Pro, and DeepSeek V3.2

Methodology

The paper designs 9 classical discrete reasoning tasks, each parameterized to control problem complexity along a well-defined axis:

| Task | Complexity Parameter | Planning Type |

|------|---------------------|---------------|

| Tower of Hanoi | Number of disks (3-8) | Recursive planning |

| Checker Jumping | Board size / peg count | Sequential planning |

| River Crossing | Number of items/constraints | Safety-constrained planning |

| Boolean SAT | Number of variables/clauses | Global constraint satisfaction |

| Cryptarithmetic | Number of unique digits/letters | Symbolic CSP |

| Graph Coloring | Graph size / chromatic number | Combinatorial CSP |

| Water Jug | Jug capacities / target | Arithmetic planning |

| Sudoku | Grid size / clue density | Dense global CSP |

| Rubik's Cube | Number of scramble moves | Spatial search |

Each task is instantiated at complexity levels L1 (minimal) through L6+ (extreme), with 30 instances per level. Crucially, each task has a deterministic validator (9 validator algorithms provided in pseudocode in the paper) that independently verifies: (1) all constraints are satisfied, (2) the solution is complete and correct, (3) no illegal moves or state transitions are made. This eliminates the "plausible but wrong" problem that plagues LLM evaluation.

Six frontier models are evaluated in their thinking/reasoning modes. The evaluation protocol records success rate, constraint violation rate, and partial solution rate per complexity level. Global analysis is performed by normalizing complexity across tasks and computing the success probability curve Acc(lambda).

Results & Highlights

  • GPT-5 Pro achieves highest overall success rate (~80% at L1-L2), followed by Gemini 3 Pro (~70%), Claude 3.7 Sonnet (~65%), and DeepSeek V3.2 (~55%)
  • Reasoning collapse is universal: All 6 models exhibit the same qualitative pattern -- near-ceiling performance at L1-L2, gradual decline at L3, and dramatic collapse at L4-L5
  • The L3-L4 transition is the critical cliff: Success rates drop by 30-50 percentage points between L3 and L4 across all models, far exceeding the L2-L3 decline (~10-15 pp)
  • Sudoku is the most fragile task: Models fail earliest on Sudoku because it requires maintaining dense global constraints -- every cell depends on every other cell in the same row/column/block
  • Tower of Hanoi shows recursive planning limits: Models that solve 4-disk variants fail catastrophically on 6+ disks, suggesting depth-limited recursive reasoning
  • Rubik's Cube is near-impossible for all models at any meaningful scramble depth (5+ moves), confirming that pure search-space exploration remains beyond current LLM capability
  • Token budget does not rescue performance: Longer thinking traces correlate only weakly with success at high complexity, supporting the "non-scaling of reasoning with token length" failure mechanism
  • Constraint violation patterns are task-specific: SAT/Cryptarithmetic show isolated clause violations; Sudoku/River Crossing show cascading constraint failures; Tower of Hanoi shows illegal move sequences

Critical Assessment

The paper makes a genuine contribution through its evaluation methodology rather than through novel findings. The core empirical observation -- "LLMs get worse as tasks get harder" -- is not surprising in itself. What makes this paper valuable is:

  1. The formalization rigor: Defining reasoning collapse as a phase transition with a precise threshold (lambda*) elevates the discussion from anecdotal to measurable.
  2. The deterministic validator paradigm: By checking solutions algorithmically rather than relying on LLM-as-judge or human evaluation, the authors sidestep a major source of evaluation noise.
  3. The cross-task, cross-model scope: Evaluating 9 tasks x 6 models x 6+ complexity levels produces a rich dataset that reveals consistent patterns.

However, several weaknesses deserve attention:

  • Theoretical depth is thin: The paper identifies what happens (performance collapse at L3-L4) but offers limited insight into why this specific threshold emerges. Is it related to attention head capacity, context window limitations, or something inherent to autoregressive generation? The discussion section raises these questions but does not answer them.
  • Task selection bias: All 9 tasks are classical puzzles with well-defined state spaces. The "reasoning collapse" finding may not generalize to open-ended reasoning, creative problem-solving, or tasks where the state space is not explicitly enumerable.
  • No comparison to symbolic solvers: Showing that a SAT solver handles all complexity levels trivially would strengthen the framing, but the paper focuses exclusively on LLM-vs-LLM comparison.
  • Limited novelty of individual findings: Each individual observation (models fail on hard Sudoku, models can't solve scrambled Rubik's Cubes) has been informally known. The contribution is the systematic aggregation.
  • The "thinking" mode evaluation may not be optimal: Some models (e.g., Claude 3.7, GPT-5) may perform differently when their thinking budget or temperature is varied, but the paper uses fixed settings.

Strengths

  • Deterministic validation eliminates evaluator bias -- this is the paper's strongest methodological contribution
  • The complexity parametrization is clean and well-defined for each task
  • Cross-model comparison reveals that reasoning collapse is model-agnostic, suggesting a fundamental limitation rather than a model-specific weakness
  • The 4-failure-mechanism taxonomy provides a useful conceptual framework for future work
  • Full pseudocode for all 9 validators enables exact reproduction

Limitations

  • No theoretical explanation for why L3-L4 is the critical transition point
  • Classical puzzle tasks may not represent real-world reasoning demands
  • Single-pass evaluation without prompt optimization or chain-of-thought variation limits the scope of claims
  • The paper does not investigate whether fine-tuning or specialized training can push the collapse threshold higher
  • Model access is limited to API-level -- no probing of internal representations to understand where reasoning breaks down
  • The complexity levels, while parameterized, are not normalized across tasks in a principled way (e.g., computational complexity classes are not used to define levels)

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. 能否通过训练数据中增加特定复杂度级别的样本来推迟或消除推理崩塌？还是这种崩塌是架构层面的根本限制？
  2. L3-L4临界点是否与模型的注意力头数量或层数存在定量关系？如果是，是否存在一个"推理深度常数"来预测给定模型的崩溃阈值？
  3. 将符号求解器（如SAT solver）作为工具调用集成到LLM推理流程中，能否在保持LLM灵活性的同时绕过推理崩塌？
  4. 在非离散、开放状态的推理任务中（如科学推理、法律论证），是否也存在类似的"复杂度悬崖"现象？
  5. 确定性验证器范式能否扩展到评估LLM在代码生成、数学证明等任务中的正确性？验证器本身的复杂度是否会成为瓶颈？
