MathDuels: A Self-Play Benchmark for Evaluating Mathematical Reasoning in Large Language Models

Zhiqiu Xu, Shibo Jin, Shreya Arya, Mayur Naik

arXiv: 2604.21916v1

Date: 2026-04-23

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.21916

一句话总结： MathDuels 让大模型在"作者-解题者"双角色对抗中互相博弈，用 Rasch IRT 模型分离出创题能力与解题能力的独立排名，发现 Gemini-3.1-Pro-high 综合第一，而 GPT-5.4-high 虽解题最强但创题能力显著落后。
TL;DR: MathDuels is a self-play benchmark where LLMs compete as both problem authors and solvers; IRT-based Rasch scoring reveals that authoring and solving abilities are largely decoupled, with Gemini-3.1-Pro-high leading on composite score while GPT-5.4-high tops solver-only rankings.

核心贡献

  • 提出双角色自博弈评测框架：同一模型既负责出题（Author）又负责解题（Solver），通过模型间配对对局计算 Elo 分数。
  • 引入三阶段题目生成流程：元提示驱动生成 → 符号验证 → 难度放大（Difficulty Amplification），保证题目可验证且难度可调。
  • 使用 Rasch IRT 模型联合估计模型能力与题目难度，绕开传统基准的"题目固定"缺陷，支持持续扩充。
  • 公开排行榜 mathduels.ai 及完整数据集，共评测 19 个前沿模型，揭示解题/创题能力解耦现象。

Key Contributions

  • Dual-role self-play framework: each model acts as both problem author (generating novel problems) and solver; head-to-head matchups produce separate Author-Elo and Solver-Elo via Bradley-Terry.
  • Three-stage generation pipeline: meta-prompting generates candidates → symbolic verifier filters solvable/correct problems → difficulty amplification loop iteratively hardens problems until a reference model fails.
  • Rasch IRT ranking: jointly estimates per-model ability and per-problem difficulty; composite score balances authoring and solving; avoids static benchmark saturation.
  • Open leaderboard at mathduels.ai covering 19 frontier models with reproducible protocols.

Methodology

Problems are seeded by meta-prompts that specify topic, format, and difficulty level, then passed through a symbolic verifier to confirm correctness. A difficulty amplification step repeatedly mutates accepted problems until a reference LLM fails, enforcing a difficulty ceiling. During evaluation, each model pair plays as author vs. solver in round-robin matchups; correctness is checked symbolically. Bradley-Terry pairwise comparisons yield Solver-Elo and Author-Elo; a composite score (weighted average) determines final ranking. The Rasch IRT model simultaneously infers per-model ability parameters and per-problem difficulty parameters via maximum likelihood, providing calibrated difficulty estimates that generalize across model cohorts.

  • GPT-5.4-high is the strongest solver but ranks substantially lower on authoring, suggesting strong mathematical reasoning does not automatically transfer to problem generation.
  • Authoring and solving abilities are poorly correlated across models, validating the need for dual-role evaluation.
  • Several open-weight models achieve competitive solver scores but lag severely on authoring.
  • The dynamic difficulty amplification step prevents ceiling effects observed on fixed benchmarks.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: High — combining self-play, dual-role evaluation, and IRT scoring in one framework is genuinely novel for math benchmarks.

Rigor: Moderate-to-high — symbolic verification provides ground-truth correctness, and Rasch estimation is statistically principled. However, the meta-prompting pipeline's sensitivity to prompt wording is not fully ablated.

Reproducibility: Good — public leaderboard and dataset; API-based models require access that may vary by reader.

Significance: High for the LLM evaluation community; directly addresses saturation on static benchmarks.

Red flags:

  • The 19-model sample, while broad, clusters around top commercial APIs; weaker open-weight models may expose evaluation instability at low Elo ranges.
  • Difficulty amplification loops could inadvertently generate adversarial or ill-posed problems that pass symbolic verification but are semantically flawed.
  • Author-Elo variance may be high when a model generates very few solvable problems, but confidence intervals are not prominently reported.

Strengths

  • Clean separation of authoring and solving enables new insight into model capabilities.
  • Rasch IRT mitigates the problem of different models seeing different problem sets.
  • Public infrastructure lowers barrier to community participation.
  • Avoids benchmark contamination by generating new problems dynamically.

Limitations

  • Restricted to symbolic-verifiable math; complex proof-based or open-ended problems are excluded.
  • Difficulty amplification biases the problem distribution toward a specific reference model's weakness, which may not generalize.
  • Closed-source models cannot be reproduced externally; leaderboard relies on API access.
  • No human baseline provided; it is unclear how "hard" the hardest problems are in absolute terms.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How stable are Author-Elo scores when the model pool changes — does adding one very strong author significantly re-rank all others?
  2. Could the difficulty amplification loop be used as a data generation engine for fine-tuning, and would that improve solver performance in a self-improvement loop?
  3. How does the symbolic verifier handle problems where multiple solution paths exist but only one is numerically verified?
  4. Is there evidence that models that score high on authoring also produce more "educational" problems (e.g., covering diverse mathematical concepts)?
  5. What is the distribution of problem topics across the generated set — does meta-prompting produce balanced coverage or cluster around certain areas?
