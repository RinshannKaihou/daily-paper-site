How VLAs (Really) Work In Open-World Environments

Amir Rasouli, Yangzheng Wu, Zhiyuan Li, Rui Heng Yang, Xuan Zhao, Charles Eret, Sajjad Pakdamansavoji

arXiv: 2604.21192v1

Date: 2026-04-23

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.21192

一句话总结： 通过对 BEHAVIOR-1K 基准上最优 VLA 策略的深度分析，揭示现有"成功率"指标严重高估实际性能，并提出 sQ 与 seQ 两项安全感知评估指标，在 20 个任务上将报告分数降低最高 35%，同时记录了 10 类系统性失效模式。
TL;DR: State-of-the-art VLA policies (RLC, Comet) on the BEHAVIOR-1K challenge are analyzed in depth, revealing that reported success rates are non-reproducible (>27% gap between published and reproduced scores), highly inconsistent across trials, and mask frequent safety violations. Two new safety-aware metrics (sQ and seQ) are proposed that penalize object mishandling and environment disruption, reducing scores by up to 35% on 20 selected tasks.

核心贡献

  • 可重现性危机：使用官方检查点重现 RLC 模型结果，发现多数任务存在超过 27% 的分数差异，表明模拟器的非确定性对 VLA 性能评估产生根本性影响。
  • 一致性分析：约 50% 的任务场景呈现极端二元化（完全成功或完全失败），表明策略表现高度依赖场景初始化随机性。
  • 10 类失效模式分类：通过 8 名机器人专家对 500 段视频的定性分析，将失效归纳为抓取失败、碰撞、导航失败、语义混淆、任务混淆等 10 类，其中抓取失败是最常见的单一失效原因。
  • sQ 与 seQ 安全指标：在 B1K BDDL 子目标框架上扩展，引入放置准则（目标物体保持直立 ±30°）和处理准则（关键物体未被丢弃）构成 sQ，以及引入非目标支撑物体的附加子目标构成 seQ；在 20 个任务上，安全违规使两模型平均得分各下降约 7–10%。

Key Contributions

  • Reproducibility failure documented: Official checkpoints evaluated on local hardware reproduce results with >27% disparity on tasks 24, 26, and 27. Some tasks flip from 0 to non-zero and vice versa. The simulator's non-deterministic lighting/rendering is identified as a key instability source.
  • Consistency analysis: ~50% of scenarios show bimodal Q-score distributions (near 0 or near 1), with high within-task variance indicating policy behavior is highly contingent on scene initialization.
  • Expert failure analysis: 8 robotics experts annotate 500 video recordings across 50 tasks×10 trials. Grasp failure is the most frequent single-task failure; when total occurrences are counted, execution order confusion rivals collision in frequency.
  • Safety Q-score (sQ): Extends B1K's Q-score by multiplying each sub-goal by placement indicator $p_i$ (object upright within ±30°) and handling indicator $h_i$ (critical object not dropped). Critical objects include electrical devices, fragile objects, non-empty containers, and cooked food.
  • Safety-Enhanced Q-score (seQ): Further adds M sub-goals for non-target support objects (e.g., chopping board, table), where $s_j = 0$ if the object is displaced >10 cm or falls. seQ-Oracle (all support sub-goals set to 1) isolates non-target contribution.
  • Practical remedies proposed: Safety-aware data curation, structured curriculum design, and deployment-oriented validation are recommended as architecture-agnostic future directions.

Methodology

The analysis targets BEHAVIOR-1K (B1K), a benchmark with 1,000 household tasks in realistic simulation (NVIDIA Omniverse-based). The study uses the 50-task B1K 2025 Challenge setup, with 10 trials (randomized initial conditions) per task. The two evaluated models are RLC (1st place) and OpenPI Comet (2nd place), both based on the π0.5 architecture.

Reproducibility is assessed by running RLC's official checkpoints and seeds on independent hardware with the official installation scripts. Consistency is assessed from posted leaderboard results by examining within-task Q-score variance across trials. Failure analysis is conducted by 8 human experts who annotate 500 video recordings, categorizing failures into 10 mutually non-exclusive categories per task.

The safety metrics sQ and seQ are formalized mathematically on top of the BDDL predicate structure. For sQ: $sQ = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} g_i \cdot p_i \cdot h_i$. For seQ: $seQ = \frac{\sum g_i \cdot p_i \cdot h_i + \sum s_j}{N+M}$. The authors rerun both models on 20 selected tasks (moderate difficulty, involving non-target object interactions) and compute all four metrics (Q, sQ, seQ-Oracle, seQ) along with average target violations (TV) and non-target violations (nTV).

Results & Highlights

  • Reproducibility: RLC posted vs. reproduced scores diverge by >27% on tasks 24, 26, 27. Task 37: reproduced 0.1 vs. posted 0.0.
  • Overall success: Maximum Q-score across 20 analyzed tasks is ~26% (RLC average 0.256, Comet 0.192), indicating both models fail the large majority of tasks.
  • Safety penalty: RLC average sQ = 0.239 vs. Q = 0.256 (−7%); Comet sQ = 0.173 vs. Q = 0.192 (−10%). In 85% of tasks for both models, target violations occurred.
  • seQ non-target penalty: In up to 40% of analyzed tasks, non-target violations contributed additional score reduction. "Cook cabbage" is notable: Q=0 for RLC (no sub-goals satisfied), yet the robot also dropped the chopping board (nTV=1.5).
  • Qualitative failures: Shoe placed in fridge instead of shoe rack; robot halts indefinitely in front of fridge; picks up plant instead of spray bottle — illustrating semantic confusion and task confusion.
  • Failure frequency ranking: Grasp failure > placement failure > collision > navigation failure (per-task); when total occurrences counted, order confusion rises to rival collision.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The safety metric extensions (sQ, seQ) are incremental over B1K's Q-score and relatively straightforward to compute. The main value is in the systematic empirical analysis and the reproducibility documentation, which are genuinely useful to the community.

Rigor: The failure analysis relies on 8 expert annotators, but inter-annotator agreement statistics are not reported. The 20-task selection for safety metric evaluation (out of 50) introduces a selection bias toward tasks with non-target object interactions — the reported safety penalty rates may not generalize to the full benchmark. Reproducibility claims are well-documented with specific task-level numbers.

Reproducibility: The paper itself is highly reproducible — it uses official checkpoints and public benchmarks, and provides task-level numbers. However, the finding that results are not reproducible is the main message.

Significance: The reproducibility crisis finding is significant for the field. The safety metric framework is practically relevant for robot deployment evaluation, though the absolute performance levels (~26% Q-score) suggest the models are not yet near deployment-ready regardless of safety scoring.

Red flags: Only 20 of 50 tasks analyzed with safety metrics — cherry-picking of moderate-difficulty tasks. No ablation on the 30° orientation threshold or 10 cm displacement threshold, which are arbitrary. The paper does not analyze whether the two models' failures are correlated (sharing the same architecture π0.5 base).

Strengths

  • Rare, honest reproducibility analysis with official checkpoints demonstrating a major gap in benchmark reliability
  • Expert failure analysis provides actionable diagnostic categories beyond success rate
  • Safety metrics are implementable and build directly on the existing BDDL evaluation framework
  • seQ-Oracle provides a useful diagnostic decomposition isolating target vs. non-target violations
  • Discusses practical remedies and future research directions rather than only criticizing

Limitations

  • Safety metric evaluation covers only 20 of 50 tasks (moderate difficulty selection)
  • Metric thresholds (30° orientation, 10 cm displacement) are not ablated or validated against human judgment
  • Inter-annotator agreement for failure categorization is not reported
  • Both evaluated models share the π0.5 architecture base, limiting generalizability of conclusions
  • Simulation environment limitations (rigid-body physics, limited deformable objects) constrain realism
  • No positive proposal for how to fix VLA training — recommendations are high-level

Relevance

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Do the reproducibility gaps (>27% score variance) persist across different simulator versions and hardware configurations, or are they specific to NVIDIA Omniverse rendering non-determinism?
  2. How do sQ and seQ scores change if the models are retrained with safety-aware demonstrations that penalize object mishandling?
  3. Can the proposed safety metrics be extended to real-world robot evaluation, or do the BDDL predicate structures remain simulation-specific?
  4. Is there a correlation between the failure categories (e.g., grasp failure) and the reproducibility gaps, or do they arise from different sources?
  5. Would a simple task decomposition (subtasking) approach substantially reduce execution order confusion failures without architectural changes?
