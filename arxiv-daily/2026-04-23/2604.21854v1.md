> **一句话总结：** 本文提出一个两阶段统计认证框架，用 RoMA/gRoMA 工具将"可接受风险"量化为可核查的失败概率上界，弥补 EU AI Act 等法规缺乏技术度量工具的根本缺口。

> **TL;DR:** This paper proposes a two-stage pre-deployment certification framework that translates abstract AI risk regulation requirements into an auditable, falsifiable safety certificate: a regulator fixes acceptable failure probability δ and perturbation domain ε, then a developer uses statistical robustness tools (RoMA/gRoMA) to compute a guaranteed upper bound on failure rate—without needing model internals.

## 核心贡献

- 识别当前 AI 监管（EU AI Act、NIST RMF、欧洲委员会公约）的根本缺口：均要求"可接受风险"但无人定义其量化含义或验证方法。
- 提出两阶段认证架构：阶段一由监管机构规范性确定失败概率阈值 δ 和扰动域 ε；阶段二开发者使用 RoMA/gRoMA 统计工具计算可证明的合规上界。
- 证明该框架可映射至 EU AI Act 第 9、15 条和 NIST RMF 的现有合规义务，并生成可由第三方独立验证的合规证书。
- 通过自动紧急制动（AEB）视觉系统的案例研究演示框架的实际部署可行性。

## Key Contributions

- Identifies a structural gap common to all major AI governance frameworks (EU AI Act, NIST RMF, Council of Europe CETS 225): risk-based regulation without a quantitative definition of "acceptable risk" or a technical method to verify compliance.
- Proposes a two-stage certification architecture decoupling normative determination (Stage 1, regulators set δ and ε) from technical verification (Stage 2, developers apply RoMA/gRoMA).
- Demonstrates that the RoMA statistical certificate is third-party auditable, falsifiable (re-testable against the same δ/ε), and structurally analogous to DO-178C qualification evidence in aviation.
- Operationalizes the framework on an Autonomous Emergency Braking system using aerospace-grade δ = 10⁻⁹ per operational hour as a proof-of-concept.

## Methodology

The paper adopts a two-stage pre-deployment certification procedure:

**Stage 1 — Normative Parameterization:** A competent authority publicly fixes two parameters: (1) δ, the maximum acceptable failure probability—treated as analogous to aviation's 10⁻⁹ catastrophic failure rate per flight hour, which was arrived at through expert deliberation not technical optimization; and (2) ε, the perturbation domain specifying the set of input variations within which the system must remain stable. Both parameters must be publicly stated before any technical verification begins.

**Stage 2 — Statistical Verification via RoMA/gRoMA:** RoMA (Robustness Measurement and Assessment) evaluates local robustness of a neural network by sampling perturbed inputs around a fixed point, extracting runner-up confidence scores, fitting a normal distribution (verified via Anderson-Darling goodness-of-fit test, with Box-Cox transformation for skewed cases), and using Z-score/Gaussian CDF to compute the probability that a perturbation within ε causes a misclassification exceeding confidence threshold δ. gRoMA extends this to global categorical robustness by aggregating local scores weighted by an operational profile, using Hoeffding's inequality to compute a formal error bound. The system is certified if and only if the computed upper bound P ≤ δ.

The AEB case study decomposes failure modes via FMEA into three sub-domains (transient glare ω_high, optical degradation ω_low, stochastic thermal noise ω=1), defines per-mode risk budgets δᵢ such that Σωᵢ·P(failure|εᵢ) ≤ δ, and runs gRoMA on each sub-domain.

Fallback pathways for non-normal distributions include: (i) domain narrowing to find sub-domains where normality holds; (ii) brute-force exact counting at exponential cost.

## Results & Highlights

- Empirical validation of RoMA against the Exact Count algorithm (exhaustive formal verification) on small ACAS Xu networks: statistical probabilities deviated from ground truth by less than 1% (94.44% vs. 94.61% and 93.94% vs. 93.84%), while formal methods required >24 hours vs. 16 minutes for RoMA.
- The AEB case study produces a concrete, auditable certificate with a well-defined re-certification trigger.
- The framework integrates with EU AI Act Articles 9 and 15 (risk management, accuracy/robustness/cybersecurity documentation) and NIST RMF's Measure function, supplying quantitative outputs where those instruments currently require process documentation without specifying its form.
- Re-certification requires only re-sampling against the same δ and ε, enabling CI/CD pipeline integration without access to model internals.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** Moderate. RoMA and gRoMA are pre-existing tools developed by the same authors. The paper's contribution is primarily the regulatory mapping and the two-stage framing, not new algorithms. The aviation analogy (DO-178, ARP 4754) is drawn repeatedly but the translation is not always tight—DO-178 governs deterministic code, not statistical inference systems.

**Rigor:** The empirical claim that RoMA deviates <1% from ground truth is real but limited to small (300-neuron ACAS Xu) networks where exact counting is tractable—the paper honestly acknowledges this. Hoeffding bounds are famously conservative, so certified failure rates may overestimate true rates, forcing unnecessary retraining.

**Reproducibility:** The case study is explicitly described as a "structured proof-of-concept" without an actual deployed AEB system. A full empirical validation would require a real deployed system, an independently established δ, and longitudinal re-certification—all identified as future work. The procedure itself is well-specified and reproducible in principle.

**Significance:** High conceptually. The paper correctly diagnoses a genuinely important problem: AI regulation mandates safety without providing measurement instruments. The framework provides a concrete template.

**Red flags:** (1) Normality assumption for runner-up confidence scores fails for LLMs under orthographic perturbations—the paper acknowledges this but the proposed fix (domain narrowing to recover near-Gaussian sub-domains) is heuristic. (2) δ-setting (Stage 1) is described as a "governance question, not an engineering one," but no institutional pathway for this deliberation exists today in any jurisdiction—the framework's most critical precondition is also the least operationalized. (3) The framework explicitly does not address out-of-distribution inputs, explainability, fairness, or systemic concentration risk—significant gaps for real-world deployment assurance.

## Strengths

- Correctly identifies a structural gap that legal scholars and computer scientists have independently converged on.
- The certificate is falsifiable and re-testable—a property explicitly absent from most current AI compliance documentation.
- Black-box operation: no access to model internals required, making it applicable to proprietary commercial models.
- Honest and detailed threats-to-validity section using standard validity categories (construct, internal, external, statistical).
- Strong interdisciplinary framing calling for joint working groups between computer science and law.

## Limitations

- Normality assumption breaks for LLMs under natural text perturbations; fallback (brute force) is computationally prohibitive for large models.
- No empirical validation on a real deployed system—the AEB case study is a proof-of-concept.
- Hoeffding bounds may be too conservative, leading to over-certification requirements.
- δ-setting (Stage 1) requires public deliberation that has not occurred in any jurisdiction; framework cannot be deployed without it.
- Does not address OOD inputs, structural data drift, or black-swan failure modes.
- Framework scope is explicitly limited to distributional robustness; explainability and fairness obligations require separate instruments.

## Relevance

本文直接关联用户对**ML 可靠性**和**系统卡（system cards）**的兴趣：它提供了一种将模型鲁棒性量化为可审计上界的技术框架，并探讨如何将统计验证嵌入监管合规义务，与 ML 部署可靠性保障的核心问题高度契合。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Can conformal prediction or distribution-free concentration inequalities replace Hoeffding bounds to reduce conservatism while maintaining coverage guarantees?
2. What institutional process analogous to aviation's expert consensus could establish societal δ values for high-stakes AI domains (medical diagnosis, credit scoring)?
3. How does the framework interact with model updates: if LoRA fine-tuning changes only adapter weights, does the certificate remain valid or require full re-certification?
4. Can gRoMA be extended to handle semantic perturbations in text (paraphrase, synonym substitution) where Gaussian noise models are inappropriate?
5. How should δ be decomposed for multi-modal systems where failure modes span both visual and linguistic inputs?
