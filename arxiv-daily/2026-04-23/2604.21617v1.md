> **一句话总结：** 本文提出三项量化指标（均值位移、位移偏差、最近锚点分配误差）与三种可视化手段，系统评估MLP参数投影在高斯噪声扰动下的局部邻域稳定性，并证明Jacobian正则化可将均值位移降低高达85%，且标准邻域保留指标（Trustworthiness/Continuity）对此类不稳定性完全失明。

> **TL;DR:** Standard quality metrics for neural parametric projections (Trustworthiness, Continuity) are blind to input-perturbation instability. This paper introduces three displacement-based stability metrics and matching visualizations, showing that Jacobian regularization reduces projection instability by up to 85% with negligible cost to reconstruction quality.

## 核心贡献

- 提出三项专门用于参数投影稳定性评估的定量指标：均值位移 (D_dev)、位移偏差 (D_bias)、最近锚点分配误差 (E_NA)。
- 提出三种对应的可视化方法：锚线图（Anchor Lines）、局部主成分分析椭球（Local PCA）、Voronoi分配图，分别从不同角度揭示投影的局部不稳定性。
- 通过实验证明，标准的Trustworthiness和Continuity指标无法区分稳定与不稳定投影，存在根本性评估盲点。
- 验证Jacobian正则化在提升投影稳定性上远优于单纯增大网络容量。

## Key Contributions

- Three quantitative stability metrics: mean displacement (D_dev), displacement bias (D_bias), and nearest-anchor assignment error (E_NA), all computed via Gaussian perturbations around class-centroid anchors.
- Three paired visual diagnostics — Anchor Lines, Local PCA ellipsoids, and Voronoi Assignment — that expose instability geometry and directionality.
- Empirical proof that Trustworthiness and Continuity are insensitive to stability differences exceeding 80% in displacement magnitude.
- Demonstration that Jacobian regularization (+J) is a more effective stabilizer than increasing MLP depth or width.
- Code and results shared on OSF and GitHub for reproducibility.

## Methodology

The framework targets MLP-based parametric projections (learned mappings from high-dimensional data to 2D). Given a baseline projection (UMAP or t-SNE), class-centroid anchors are selected in 2D projection space. Isotropic Gaussian noise is added to each anchor's input-space representation at a calibrated scale (0.25th percentile of pairwise distances). N=2000 perturbed copies per anchor are projected through the trained MLP. Three scalar metrics are computed: D_dev (average Euclidean displacement from anchor), D_bias (displacement of the centroid of projected cloud from anchor), and E_NA (fraction of perturbed points falling outside their anchor's Voronoi cell). Visualizations encode these geometrically.

Two MLP sizes are tested: small (3 layers, 512 units) and large (6 layers, 1024 units), with and without Jacobian regularization (penalizing Frobenius norm of the input-output Jacobian). Experiments run on MNIST and Fashion-MNIST, with UMAP and t-SNE as base methods, averaged over 10 seeds with 80/10/10 splits.

## Results & Highlights

- Jacobian regularization is the dominant stabilizer: MLP-small+J reduces mean displacement from 1.80 to 0.265 (−85%) and E_NA from 0.294 to 0.006 on MNIST+UMAP.
- The benefit holds across both projection methods and both datasets.
- Increasing network size alone provides only moderate stability gains; +J is substantially more effective.
- Standard Trustworthiness (T: 0.850–0.931) and Continuity (C: 0.864–0.944) remain nearly flat across all conditions, confirming they cannot detect the instabilities revealed by displacement metrics.
- On MNIST+UMAP, unregularized MLP-small shows D_dev ≈ D_bias (1.80 vs. 1.76), indicating almost pure systematic drift rather than isotropic noise amplification.
- Stability metrics are computationally cheap: ~135 ms wall-clock per model; E_NA is O(NA²) and sub-millisecond.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** The three metrics are conceptually simple (they are essentially first and second moments of a projected perturbation cloud). The individual ideas (Jacobian regularization, Voronoi partitioning, local PCA) are all established; the contribution is their systematic combination into an evaluation framework for parametric DR. This is incremental but practically useful.

**Rigor:** Ten-seed averaging, separate train/val/test splits, two datasets, two projection methods, two architectures — the experimental protocol is solid. The Jacobian regularization result is an expected but quantified consequence; the authors are honest about this.

**Reproducibility:** Code shared on GitHub and OSF with full parameter descriptions. High.

**Significance:** The paper identifies a real gap: practitioners deploying parametric projections in streaming/interactive scenarios may be misled by stable T/C scores. The takeaway — pair parametric DR with Jacobian regularization and validate stability before deployment — is actionable.

**Red flags:** (1) Only isotropic Gaussian noise is tested; real-world perturbations (sensor drift, domain shift) may be highly anisotropic. (2) Only class-centroid anchors are used, which are the easiest case; boundary or minority-class regions may behave differently. (3) Only MLP architectures are evaluated; autoencoders or CNNs for DR are untested. (4) The paper evaluates a single noise level per dataset; a sweep across noise scales would provide a more complete picture. (5) The stability improvement from Jacobian regularization is mathematically expected (it directly penalizes the Jacobian), so the finding, while important to quantify, is not surprising.

## Strengths

- Identifies and fills a genuine blind spot in existing projection quality assessment.
- Concise, well-structured, six-page workshop paper with full experimental rigor.
- Practical, actionable recommendation for deployment.
- Visual diagnostics are intuitive and informative.

## Limitations

- Limited to isotropic Gaussian perturbations, which may not reflect realistic noise profiles.
- Only MLP architectures and class-centroid anchor selection strategies are studied.
- Only MNIST and Fashion-MNIST are tested; performance on more complex datasets (e.g., molecular embeddings, genomic data) is unknown.
- Jacobian regularization may harm generalization in settings where the decision boundary is genuinely non-smooth.
- Single noise level per experiment; a multi-scale stability profile would be more informative.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How do these stability metrics behave for autoencoder-based parametric projections compared to direct MLPs?
2. Do the instabilities correlate with adversarial vulnerability — are the same anchors that are displacement-unstable also the most adversarially attackable?
3. Can Jacobian regularization be applied selectively (e.g., only near decision boundaries) to reduce its impact on expressiveness?
4. How do the metrics scale when anchors are placed at class boundary regions rather than centroids?
5. For LLM-derived embeddings (e.g., token-level representations), would these metrics reveal instability that neighborhood-preservation metrics miss?
