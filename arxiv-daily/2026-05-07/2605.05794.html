Revealing Modular Gradient Noise Imbalance in LLMs: Calibrating Adam via Signal-to-Noise Ratio

Ziqing Wen, Zhouyang Liu, Jiahuan Wang, Ping Luo, Li Shen, Dongsheng Li, Tao Sun

arXiv: 2605.05794

Date: 2026-05-07

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.05794

一句话总结： 本文揭示了 LLM 各模块间梯度信噪比（SNR）的系统性失衡，提出 MoLS 方法在预热阶段一次性估计各模块 SNR，并以相对缩放因子 $\alpha_m = \sqrt{S_\text{base}/S_m}$ 自动调整模块级学习率，在 LLaMA 60M–7B 预训练和多任务微调中均优于手动调参的基线，最大降低验证困惑度 1.82 PPL。
TL;DR: Transformer modules (Embedding, QK, VO, MLP, Head) have wildly different gradient signal-to-noise ratios throughout LLM training, yet Adam uses a single global learning rate with no structural awareness. MoLS fixes this by estimating each module's SNR once during the warm-up phase and applying a square-root-proportional scaling factor to its learning rate — no new hyperparameters, under 2% extra training time, no GPU memory overhead. On pre-training LLaMA models from 60M to 7B on C4/OpenWebText, Adam+MoLS consistently beats Adam, NADAM, LAMB, MUON, Adam-mini, GaLore, and APOLLO in final validation perplexity, with gains up to 1.82 PPL on 1.3B. It is plug-and-play on top of any Adam variant and compatible with LoRA fine-tuning. The method is worth reading if you train LLMs and are frustrated by the tedium of per-module learning rate grid search.

  • 与内存高效优化器的兼容性： 与 Adam-mini 结合后在 1.3B 模型上 PPL 再降 1.53，同时训练时间仅增加 1.71%，展示了在资源受限场景下的实用价值。

Key Contributions

  • Systematic SNR analysis across LLM modules: Empirically demonstrates that gradient SNR varies by orders of magnitude across Embedding, QK, VO, MLP, and Head modules throughout training. VO/QK modules consistently hold the highest SNR; Embedding and Head layers remain below 0.1 even at initialization, causing Adam's updates to degenerate toward $\sqrt{S_m}$-damped noise.
  • MoLS — a principled, parameter-free calibration method: Derives the scaling factor $\alpha_m = \sqrt{S_\text{base}/S_m}$ from first principles (the inverse square-root relationship between Adam's damping and SNR), estimated once during the warm-up phase via 16,384 gradient samples (~32 iterations). No new hyperparameters are introduced.
  • Broad empirical validation: Pre-training across LLaMA 60M–7B, GPT-2 125M/355M, and Qwen2.5-350M; fine-tuning with LoRA on Gemma3-1B, LLaMA3.2-3B, Qwen2.5-7B covering 8 commonsense benchmarks and MMLU. MoLS consistently achieves best-in-class perplexity with negligible overhead.
  • Practical plug-and-play design: Operates without modifying Adam's internal update rule; compatible with 8-bit Adam, Adam-mini, and LoRA; introduces less than 2% additional wall-clock training time and zero GPU memory overhead.

Methodology

Problem formulation. Adam's second-moment estimate $\mathbf{v}^t \approx \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{g}^{\odot 2}] = \boldsymbol{\mu}^{\odot 2} + \boldsymbol{\sigma}^{\odot 2}$ conflates true gradient signal ($\boldsymbol{\mu}$) and stochastic noise ($\boldsymbol{\sigma}$). The Effective Signal Step $\mathcal{D}_{m,i}$ for parameter $i$ in module $m$ is:

$$\mathcal{D}_{m,i} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + \sigma^2_{m,i}/\mu^2_{m,i}}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + 1/S_{m,i}}}$$

where $S_{m,i} = \|\mu_{m,i}\|^2 / \|\sigma_{m,i}\|^2$ is the per-parameter SNR. In low-SNR modules, $\mathcal{D}_{m,i} \approx \sqrt{S_{m,i}}$, so Adam's effective update is heavily suppressed by noise.

Module-level SNR estimation. Parameters are grouped by functional module (Emb, Head, QK, VO, MLP). The module-level SNR $S_m$ is the average of per-parameter SNRs within the module. During the first 1% of training steps (warm-up), 16,384 gradient samples are collected to estimate $\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{g}]$ and $\text{Var}(\mathbf{g})$ per element; these are averaged within each module to yield $S_m$.

Relative Signal-wise Equilibrium. The reference module $m_\text{base}$ is chosen as the one with the highest estimated SNR (typically VO). The scaling factor for module $m$ is:

$$\alpha_m = \sqrt{S_\text{base}/S_m}$$

This is anchored at 1.0 for $m_\text{base}$; noise-dominated modules receive a boost proportional to the square root of their SNR deficit.

Practical application. The scaling factors are fixed after warm-up and applied multiplicatively to the global learning rate per module. To prevent loss spikes from abrupt learning rate changes, the factors are gradually ramped to their target values over the remaining warm-up steps, ensuring training stability. The entire procedure adds under 2% to total training time and runs SNR estimation on CPU, introducing no GPU memory overhead.

Results & Highlights

Pre-training (C4, full-rank). On LLaMA 60M–1.3B, Adam+MoLS achieves final validation PPL of 28.48 / 21.94 / 16.41 / 13.45, compared to vanilla Adam's 29.55 / 22.82 / 17.25 / 14.51 — consistent reductions of 0.84–1.07 PPL across all sizes.

Pre-training (C4, memory-efficient). Adam-mini+MoLS achieves 28.49 / 21.91 / 16.86 / 13.57, reducing final PPL by 1.14–1.82 PPL over Adam-mini. Against GaLore and APOLLO, Adam-mini+MoLS is the best performing memory-efficient optimizer at all scales.

Large-scale scaling (3B and 7B). Using 8-bit Adam with gradient checkpointing on LLaMA-3B and 7B, MoLS reduces final PPL by 1.12 (3B) and 1.69 (7B) respectively.

Training efficiency. On LLaMA-1.3B, Adam+MoLS achieves a 7.30% PPL gain at only 1.66% additional wall-clock time (~55 hours vs. 54 hours). MoLS delivers 1.2–1.4x speedup over Adam with tuned hyperparameters in early-stage convergence.

Fine-tuning. LoRA+MoLS achieves average accuracy of 55.62 on commonsense reasoning (8 tasks) for LLaMA3.2-3B, versus 54.86 for vanilla LoRA. On MMLU, LoRA+MoLS yields 73.01 for Qwen2.5-7B versus 72.41 for LoRA alone.

Cross-architecture generalization. Pre-training GPT2-125M/355M and Qwen2.5-350M on C4 shows consistent PPL improvements, confirming that SNR imbalance is not specific to the LLaMA family.

Ablation: finer grouping is better. Grouping as {QK, VO, MLP, Emb, Head} achieves loss 3.088 vs. coarser {Attn, MLP, Emb} at 3.103 and vanilla Adam at 3.127. Finer-grained module boundaries lead to measurably better performance.

Comparison to hand-tuned module LRs. MoLS closely matches the best exhaustive grid-search result (3.074) for LLaMA-130M with only automated one-shot estimation, at a tiny fraction of the computational cost.

Critical Assessment

Novelty. The core insight — that Adam's noise damping is SNR-dependent and that this creates systematic module-wise imbalances — is not entirely new. Prior work (Wang et al. 2025a on sharpness heterogeneity, Li et al. 2025 on gradient grouping) observed module-wise differences but lacked a principled calibration formula. MoLS is novel in deriving the specific $\sqrt{S_\text{base}/S_m}$ scaling from Adam's update mechanics and implementing it as a lightweight, one-shot warm-up calibration. The contribution is incremental but well-grounded and practically impactful.

Rigor. The theoretical framing is solid: the decomposition of $\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{g}^{\odot 2}]$ into signal and noise, and the derivation of effective signal step, are standard statistical arguments applied cleanly. Empirical evaluation covers a broad range of model sizes, architectures, datasets, and optimization settings, with 3-seed averages reported. Ablations on grouping strategy, SNR sample size, and extended token budgets are thorough.

Reproducibility. Appendix A provides full hyperparameters, architecture specifications, random seeds, and training configurations. Algorithm 1 is unambiguous. The SNR estimation uses only 16,384 gradient samples, which is easy to reproduce. Pseudo-code is promised ("we present the pseudo-code in Algorithm 1") and provided. Reproducibility appears high for an academic group with sufficient GPU resources.

Significance. Module-wise learning rate tuning is a real pain point in LLM training. MoLS effectively automates this with negligible overhead, which is practically useful. The consistent 0.8–1.8 PPL improvements at scale, combined with compatibility with LoRA and memory-efficient optimizers, make this a deployment-ready technique rather than a toy result.

Red flags.

  • The reference module choice (VO by default) is a design decision not fully ablated. The paper argues it is stable across architectures, but its interaction with non-Transformer architectures (MoEs, SSMs) is unexamined.
  • SNR estimation is static (one-shot at warm-up). The paper acknowledges that late-stage SNR dynamics (e.g., gradual noise increase in MLP modules) may not be captured, potentially leaving performance on the table.
  • Experiments rely on relatively short training runs (up to 150K iterations for 7B). Whether MoLS's advantage persists for full Chinchilla-optimal training runs at production scale (e.g., trillions of tokens) is unknown.
  • The 7.30% PPL improvement framing is somewhat misleading — the absolute difference is 1.06 PPL (14.51 vs. 13.45), which is meaningful but not transformative.

Strengths

  • Theoretically grounded derivation connecting Adam's update mechanics to per-module SNR, going beyond empirical heuristics.
  • Zero new hyperparameters: the only user-visible parameter is the grouping strategy, which has sensible defaults.
  • Genuinely negligible overhead: under 2% extra wall-clock time, zero GPU memory, CPU-side SNR computation.
  • Comprehensive evaluation spanning model families (LLaMA, GPT-2, Qwen), scales (60M–7B), tasks (pre-training, SFT, PEFT), and optimizer variants (full-rank, 8-bit, memory-efficient).
  • Ablation confirming that MoLS-estimated scaling factors closely match manually optimized ones, validating that the method recovers the right signal.
  • Compatible with LoRA fine-tuning, making it relevant for the dominant paradigm of LLM adaptation.

---

Limitations

  • Static scaling: SNR is estimated once during warm-up and held fixed. Late-training SNR shifts (particularly in MLP layers as gradient magnitudes shrink) are not captured, potentially limiting gains in the fine-grained convergence phase.
  • Predefined module boundaries: MoLS requires specifying module groups a priori. For non-standard architectures (MoE, SSM-based, multimodal) or architectures with tied weights (GPT-2 ties Head to Emb), the grouping must be manually adapted.
  • Sensitivity to batch size and data distribution: SNR estimation from stochastic gradients is inherently noisy. Very small batch sizes or highly domain-specific data distributions could degrade SNR estimates, though module-level averaging partially mitigates this.
  • Limited large-scale validation: The largest reported experiment is LLaMA-7B, which is still relatively small by modern standards. No experiments on models above 10B parameters or on instruction-tuning/RLHF pipelines are presented.
  • Reference module dependency: Normalization relative to a chosen reference module ($m_\text{base}$) introduces a mild design choice. While VO is empirically stable, an automatic or reference-free scheme would be cleaner.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does MoLS interact with learning rate warmup schedules of different lengths? The paper uses 10% warmup — does SNR estimation quality degrade meaningfully with shorter warmup (e.g., 1%)?
  2. Can dynamic SNR re-estimation (e.g., every 10K iterations) further improve final performance, and at what computational cost does the benefit plateau?
  3. The paper shows that MoLS-estimated $\alpha$ values are consistent across model sizes (Table 13). Is this consistency predictable enough to transfer $\alpha$ values directly from a small model to a larger one, eliminating re-estimation?
  4. How does MoLS perform in Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures, where different experts may have heterogeneous gradient statistics even within the same functional module type?
  5. Does combining MoLS with layer-wise adaptive rate scaling (LARS/LAMB-style) provide additive benefits, or do the two approaches address overlapping phenomena?
  6. The paper focuses on the Transformer decoder family. How would SNR imbalance manifest in encoder-decoder models (T5-style) or diffusion transformer architectures, and would the same scaling formula apply?
