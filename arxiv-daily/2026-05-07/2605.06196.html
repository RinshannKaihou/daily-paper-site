The Granularity Axis: A Micro-to-Macro Latent Direction for Social Roles in Language Models

Chonghan Qin, Xiachong Feng, Ziyun Song, Xiaocheng Feng, Jing Xiong, Lingpeng Kong

arXiv: 2605.06196

Date: 2026-05-07

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06196

一句话总结： 本文在大语言模型（Qwen3-8B 和 Llama-3.1-8B）的隐层激活空间中发现了一条"粒度轴"——该方向以余弦相似度 0.972 对齐角色表示空间的第一主成分，能够解释 52.6% 的方差，并且通过激活引导可将模型输出的社会视角从个体微观层面连续地偏移至国家宏观层面。
TL;DR: This paper discovers and validates a single linear direction—the Granularity Axis—in the hidden-state activations of instruction-tuned LLMs that encodes the micro-to-macro social scale of role-conditioned prompts. Constructed by contrasting mean representations of macro-level roles (institutions, nations) vs. micro-level roles (individuals, communities) over 91,200 responses across 75 ordered social roles, the axis aligns with the dominant PC1 of the role representation space (cosine 0.972, 52.6% variance in Qwen3-8B) and is causally manipulable: steering along it shifts output granularity on a 1–5 scale from ~2.0 to ~3.17 in Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct on micro-targeted prompts. The finding repositions social role granularity from a surface stylistic variable to a representational primitive, with direct implications for auditing multi-agent simulations for "granularity confusion."

核心贡献

  • 发现粒度轴（Granularity Axis）：在 Qwen3-8B 和 Llama-3.1-8B 的隐层激活空间中，通过对比宏观角色与微观角色的均值表示，得到一条有序的潜在方向，与第一主成分（PC1）高度对齐（余弦 0.972），在 Qwen3-8B 中解释 52.6% 的角色表示空间方差。
  • 构建有序社会角色数据集：涵盖 5 个粒度层级（个体→群体/社区→组织→机构→国家/超主体）共 75 个社会角色，生成 91,200 条角色条件响应。
  • 验证单调性和跨模型可迁移性：五个粒度层级在轴上的投影严格单调递增，在层级、提示变体、问题集和角色子集等多项消融实验中保持稳健，并跨 Qwen3-8B 和 Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct 两个模型家族可迁移。
  • 激活引导的因果验证：沿轴进行激活引导可在预测方向上改变输出的社会视角粒度；两个模型的表现因各自的基准饱和度而存在差异，但效果经人工标注者和 LLM 裁判双重验证。
  • 理论框架：将"粒度混淆"（granularity confusion）定义为多智能体模拟中角色的表示尺度与其名义社会尺度不匹配的失效模式，并提供可审计的工具。

Key Contributions

  • Discovery of the Granularity Axis: A contrast-defined latent direction in LLM hidden states that encodes social-scale granularity from micro (individual) to macro (national/supranational), aligning with PC1 at cosine 0.972 and accounting for 52.6% of role-space variance in Qwen3-8B.
  • Ordered Social Role Taxonomy: 75 social roles spanning five granularity levels (Individual, Group/Community, Organization/Meso, Institution/Systemic, Nation/Super-Actor) with 91,200 role-conditioned responses collected using five prompt variants and 240 shared extraction questions.
  • Robust Representation Results: Monotonic level ordering is stable across layers (8–35 in Qwen3-8B, 6–31 in Llama), endpoint definitions, prompt templates, score-filtering thresholds, and held-out splits; transfers across model families.
  • Causal Activation Steering: Intervening along the axis shifts output granularity in the predicted direction; Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct shows strong behavioral shifts on micro-targeted prompts (2.00→3.17 on a 5-point scale at α=+4); human annotators confirm the macro-leaning direction at above-chance pairwise rates (≥0.639 across conditions).
  • Conceptual Contribution: Reframes social role granularity as a representational primitive rather than a stylistic surface variable, opening pathways to audit multi-agent LLM simulations for granularity confusion and to control social scale at deployment time.

Methodology

1. Social Role Construction

The authors manually construct an ordered taxonomy of 75 social roles spanning five granularity levels (L1–L5): Individual (Worried Parent, Anxious Patient…), Group/Community (Community Organizer, PTA President…), Organization/Meso (Hospital Administrator, NGO Director…), Institution/Systemic (Education Minister, Military General…), and Nation/Super-Actor (Central Bank Governor, UN Ambassador…). Roles are annotated with domain, role type, and short description capturing perspective scale, constraints, and agency.

2. Response Collection

Each role is combined with 5 prompt variants (direct identity assignment, explicit role-play instruction, worldview/priority emphasis, first-person scale/time-horizon emphasis, authenticity/practical-constraints emphasis) and 240 shared general extraction questions from the Assistant Axis study, yielding 75×5×240 = 90,000 role-conditioned responses from Qwen3-8B (primary) and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (replication), plus 1,200 default-assistant reference responses (91,200 total).

3. Role-Level Representation

For each response, token-level hidden states are extracted at each layer. Response-level vectors are computed by mean-pooling over assistant-turn tokens; role-level vectors are computed by averaging over all (prompt variant, question) pairs per role. Layer 18 is selected as the primary analysis layer based on layer-wise stability analysis.

4. Granularity Axis Definition

The axis is defined as the contrast vector:

g = mean(v_macro) − mean(v_micro)

where macro roles are L4–L5 and micro roles are L1–L2. The axis is validated by (i) computing cosine similarity with PC1 of the centered role-vector matrix, and (ii) checking that all five level means project monotonically onto the axis.

5. Activation Steering

The causal role of the axis is tested by adding α·g to each generated-token hidden state at the intervention layer during greedy decoding:

h_t(new) = h_t + α·g

Positive α pushes toward macro reasoning; negative α pushes toward micro. Coefficients {−4, 0, +4} are used conservatively. Output granularity is evaluated by gpt-5.4-mini as judge on a 1–5 granularity_overall scale, with gemini-3.1-flash-lite-preview as robustness check, and three graduate-level human annotators providing pairwise direction validation.

Robustness Checks

Layer-wise stability, endpoint ablations, held-out prompt/question/role splits, prompt-template sensitivity, score filtering, role-type controls, and domain/family subgroup analyses are all reported in the appendix.

Results & Highlights

Representation:

  • In Qwen3-8B, the Granularity Axis aligns with PC1 at cosine 0.9720, explaining 52.57% of role-space variance (Layer 18). Spearman = 0.9472, Pearson = 0.9414 against the five-level ordering.
  • In Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, PC1 cosine = 0.9596, variance = 42.46%, Spearman = 0.9459, Pearson = 0.9373.
  • Mean projections increase monotonically L1→L5 in both models (Qwen: 0.54 → 23.26; Llama: −0.88 → 3.39), with saturation between L4 and L5.
  • The default assistant persona sits near L3 in Qwen3-8B and L4 in Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, indicating a meso-to-macro default.

Steering:

  • Qwen3-8B on generic prompts: baseline already near ceiling (4.90/5), limiting observable shift. On micro-targeted prompts, positive steering raises score 2.00→2.67 (+0.50 delta); no degeneration observed.
  • Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct shows larger shifts: micro-targeted prompts move 2.00→3.167 (Δ=+1.167 at α=+4). However, negative steering at α=−4 on generic prompts produces 42.5% degeneration, indicating instability.
  • Human pairwise direction rates: Llama exceeds 0.85 on both prompt sets (micro: 0.861, generic: 0.903); Qwen generic cell is near ceiling (0.639), micro cell is 0.750.
  • Random and Assistant Axis directions do not reproduce the micro-macro movement, confirming specificity.

Robustness:

  • Ordering is stable across layers 8–35 (Qwen) and 6–31 (Llama); cosine alignment remains ≥0.93 under all endpoint variant definitions; all five prompt-template variants remain monotonic; signal survives score-filtering (Spearman ≥0.9459 under score≥3).
  • Role-holdout generalization is slightly weaker but still high (Spearman 0.9335–0.9404), the sole softer point in the robustness profile.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: High within its niche. The direct application of the contrast-and-project pipeline (borrowed from the Assistant Axis work) to social-scale granularity specifically is well-motivated. The conceptualization of "granularity confusion" as a diagnostic for multi-agent simulations is a useful framing. However, the core methodological toolkit (contrast vectors, PCA alignment, activation steering) is largely adapted from prior representation-engineering literature; the novelty is primarily in the application domain and the careful sociology-informed role taxonomy.

Rigor: Strong on the representation side. The monotonicity results are compelling, and the appendix covers an unusually thorough set of robustness checks. The steering results are more cautious and appropriately hedged — the authors acknowledge that steering is a "partial causal probe" rather than uniform control. The degeneration problem in Llama at negative α is disclosed and analyzed rather than buried. The paper correctly distinguishes directional consistency from stable control.

Reproducibility: High. The 75-role taxonomy is fully published (Table 11 in appendix), the pipeline is given as a formal algorithm (Algorithm 1), the question sets are from a prior published study, and code/data availability is announced. Replication would require significant compute (91,200 inference calls) but is straightforwardly executable.

Significance: Medium-to-high for the LLM interpretability and social simulation communities. The finding that social-scale granularity is a dominant representational axis (not merely scattered variation) is actionable: it enables auditing and, partially, controlling social scale in role-conditioned generation. The L4–L5 saturation finding and the "headroom gate" analysis are practically important caveats for deployment.

Red flags / weaknesses:

  • The study is limited to two 8B-scale instruction-tuned models; generalizability to larger models, base models, or RLHF-heavy models (e.g., GPT-4 class) is not established.
  • The role taxonomy is manually constructed and inherently reflects the authors' operationalization of "social scale," which conflates multiple correlated dimensions (authority, temporal horizon, constraint type). The paper acknowledges this but does not formally decompose it.
  • Role-holdout generalization is slightly fragile in Qwen, meaning the axis captures level averages well but individual novel roles are somewhat less predictable.
  • LLM-judge evaluation (gpt-5.4-mini) is the primary quantitative measure of output granularity; the rubric is an instrument developed by another study and not independently validated here for the granularity concept specifically.
  • The health and economy domains show reduced monotonicity, indicating domain-specific confounds that the current single-axis framework cannot fully capture.

---

Strengths

  • Unusually rigorous robustness testing across layers, endpoints, prompt variants, question splits, score thresholds, and semantic domains.
  • The claim is falsifiable and falsification attempts (random directions, Assistant Axis baseline, domain controls) are explicitly reported.
  • The paper is honest about asymmetric steering behavior and degeneration, and frames steering as a diagnostic tool rather than an alignment solution.
  • The sociological grounding (Coleman, Schelling, Granovetter, Giddens, Bronfenbrenner) is thoughtfully used to motivate rather than rationalize the hypothesis.
  • Multi-judge (LLM + human) validation strengthens the steering evaluation.
  • Algorithm 1 and Figure 1 make the pipeline transparent and replicable.

---

Limitations

  • Only two models tested, both ~8B parameters; results may not generalize to larger or differently trained models.
  • The Granularity Axis is a first cut along one dimension; the paper acknowledges that authority, time horizon, and formality likely collapse onto the same axis and should be disentangled in future work.
  • Behavioral control is model-dependent and prompt-dependent; Qwen3-8B already saturates near the macro end on generic prompts, making steering effects visible only with specially crafted micro-targeted prompts.
  • The role taxonomy is manually constructed, potentially introducing subjective ordering choices and limiting scale.
  • LLM-as-judge evaluation introduces judge-specific biases; Krippendorff's α between human annotators and LLM is only in the range 0.49–0.78, indicating moderate agreement, not near-perfect alignment.
  • The L4–L5 saturation region (institutional vs. national) suggests that the representation may not cleanly separate the two highest granularity levels, limiting fine-grained control at the top of the scale.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does the Granularity Axis persist in larger models (e.g., 70B+ parameter models, GPT-4 class)? Does stronger RLHF training push the default assistant placement further toward the macro end?
  2. Can the contrast-and-project pipeline be applied to other graded social dimensions (formality, temporal horizon, risk aversion) and do those axes remain orthogonal to the Granularity Axis?
  3. What is the causal mechanism within the model? Are the granularity-encoding neurons identifiable via sparse autoencoders, and do they correspond to interpretable attention heads or MLP features?
  4. The L4–L5 saturation suggests the top two levels collapse representationally — does this reflect a genuine limit of current 8B models in distinguishing institutional from national reasoning, or is it an artifact of the training data distribution?
  5. In actual multi-agent simulations (e.g., policy debate with a Worried Parent vs. a UN Ambassador), how often do agents "collapse to the same region" of the axis, and does steering during simulation reduce this granularity confusion measurably?
  6. How does the Granularity Axis interact with persona vectors and other known latent directions (Assistant Axis, refusal direction)? Is steering along multiple axes simultaneously stable or do interference effects dominate?
