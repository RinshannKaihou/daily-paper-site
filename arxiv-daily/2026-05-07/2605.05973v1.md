> **一句话总结：** 本文提出 SIREN 协议，通过冻结候选集合、重复拆分评估和高斯乘数 Bootstrap 置信区间，将自适应提示/程序搜索后的 LLM 基准测试从报告"样本内冠军分数"纠正为无偏估计完整"调优-部署流程"的真实泛化性能。

> **TL;DR:** When LLM benchmarks are reused during adaptive prompt or program search (e.g., DSPy, random search), the winner's reported score is inflated by selection bias — potentially by 1–4 percentage points on MMLU-Pro. SIREN is a post-search reporting protocol that freezes the candidate shortlist, repeatedly splits a disjoint evaluation pool into scoring and held-out subsets, uses stabilized softmax-type weights rather than hard argmax, and applies a Gaussian multiplier bootstrap to produce asymptotically valid simultaneous confidence intervals over a budget grid. On MMLU-Pro Math and Law experiments across 11 open-weight models and two tuner families (random search, DSPy), SIREN's signed bias stays within ±0.21 percentage points of a Monte Carlo ground truth, while naive winner reporting inflates scores by up to +4.35 points. SIREN can reverse tuned-versus-default deployment conclusions that naive winner reporting gets wrong.

## 核心贡献

- **识别"赢家诅咒"问题**：指出自适应提示/程序搜索中，benchmark 样本被用于调优循环，导致最终报告的"冠军分数"高估了完整调优-部署流程在新数据上的真实泛化性能；两者是不同的统计目标量。
- **重新定义评估目标**：将评估目标从"ex-post 冠军工件的分数"转变为"预算-B 调优-部署流程的过程级性能曲线 θ_j(B)"，即对程序而非对特定样本选出的工件做推断。
- **提出 SIREN 协议**：一种事后报告协议——冻结搜索后的候选集、用独立评估池进行重复拆分评分（分离选择证据与泛化评估）、用稳定化选择器（softmax）取代硬 argmax，并以高斯乘数 Bootstrap 构造置信区间。
- **理论保证**：在固定候选集 + 平滑选择器条件下，证明估计量存在逐项一阶线性化展开（Selection-aware CLT），Bootstrap 在预算网格上同时覆盖有效，支持等预算比较、跨预算比较等预设对比。
- **实验验证**：在 MMLU-Pro Math/Law 上对 11 个开源模型、两类调优器（随机搜索、DSPy）、四个 token 预算水平进行验证；证明 SIREN 偏差极小（均值偏差 -0.20 ~ +0.08 pp），而朴素 winner 报告系统性偏高（最大 +4.35 pp），且 SIREN 可纠正"调优系统是否优于默认系统"的错误结论。

## Key Contributions

- **Formalizes the winner's curse in adaptive LLM benchmarking**: Benchmark items reused inside the tuning loop make the reported winner's score an upward-biased estimate of fresh-data performance; the paper explicitly distinguishes the "ex-post winning artifact" from the "procedure-level target" (the full tune-then-deploy rule).
- **Redefines the estimand**: The target is a budget-indexed procedure-performance curve θ_j(B) — the expected fresh-data accuracy of deploying whatever artifact the budget-B tuner returns — not the score of any particular selected artifact.
- **Proposes SIREN** (Selection-aware Inference for Repeated-split EvaluatioN): A post-search reporting protocol with three key ingredients: (1) freeze the post-search candidate shortlist, (2) repeatedly split a disjoint evaluation pool into a scoring subset (for stabilized selection weights) and a held-out subset (for evaluation), and (3) aggregate with a Gaussian multiplier bootstrap that reweights benchmark items rather than re-running the tuner.
- **Theoretical guarantees**: Under a fixed-shortlist smooth-selector regime, proves a first-order item-level CLT for the repeated-split estimator and shows the multiplier bootstrap simultaneously covers the full system-budget grid, supporting pre-specified equal-budget and cross-budget comparisons without rerunning the tuner.
- **Empirical validation on real LLM tuning**: MMLU-Pro experiments across 11 open-weight models, two tuner families, and four token budgets show SIREN achieves mean signed bias between −0.20 and +0.08 percentage points versus a 10,000-redraw Monte Carlo reference, while naive winner reporting inflates by up to +4.35 points and can reverse deployment conclusions.

## Methodology

**Problem Setup.** Each "system" j consists of a base LLM plus fixed pipeline components. A "tuner" with token budget B searches over an artifact space (prompts, few-shot sets, program configurations) on a development sample D and returns a deployable artifact â_j(B; D). The procedure-level target θ_j(B) is the expected fresh-data accuracy of running this full procedure — not the accuracy of the specific artifact that happened to win on D.

**SIREN Protocol.**
1. *Freeze shortlist*: After the upstream tuning stage ends, retain the top-K candidate artifacts. These are fixed and not regenerated.
2. *Independent evaluation pool*: Collect M i.i.d. benchmark instances W_1, ..., W_M that were not used during tuning.
3. *Repeated-split scoring*: For each of R rounds, partition the pool into a scoring subset D^score_r (fraction ρ_score ≈ 0.5) and a held-out subset E_r. The scoring subset is used to compute stabilized selection weights g_{j,B}(Ŝ_{r,j}(B)) for the candidate artifacts via a smooth selector (e.g., softmax with temperature τ). The held-out subset yields per-artifact scores T̂_{r,j,k}(B) that are combined with those weights to produce the split-level estimate Ŷ_{r,j}(B).
4. *Aggregate*: The SIREN point estimate θ̃_{j,R}(B) = Σ_r ω_r Ŷ_{r,j}(B) averages across repeated splits with natural weights.
5. *Gaussian multiplier bootstrap*: For each benchmark item W_i, compute its item-level contribution ψ̂_{i,j,B,M} (combining centered held-out scores and the first-order derivative of the selector applied to scoring contributions). Draw i.i.d. Gaussian multipliers ζ_1,...,ζ_M ~ N(0,1) and form the bootstrap process G̃*_{j,B} = (1/√M) Σ_i ζ_i(ψ̂_{i,j,B,M} − ψ̄_{j,B,M}). Bootstrap quantiles of max_{j,B}|G̃*_{j,B}| give simultaneous confidence bands.

**Key design choice.** Hard argmax selection (used by baselines M1–M4) ignores the first-order effect of the scoring subset on which artifact gets selected. SIREN's smooth selector g_{j,B} is differentiable, so its Jacobian Dg_{j,B} appears explicitly in the item-level contribution formula. This derivative-weighted selection term is the mechanism by which SIREN accounts for selection bias without rerunning the tuner.

**Comparison baselines.** M1 (naive max), M2 (M1 + Wald interval), M3 (single-split holdout), M4 (R-split argmax with Student-t CI), and PromptEval (IRT-based budget-aware prompt scoring). All share the same shortlist and evaluation pool as SIREN; they differ only in the reporting rule.

## Results & Highlights

- **Procedure-level bias (RQ1)**: On MMLU-Pro Math and Law, naive winner reporting (M1) shows consistent positive bias: mean +1.62 to +4.35 percentage points depending on tuner and budget. M2 inherits M1's bias (the Wald interval only changes the CI, not the point estimate). M3 and M4 reduce but do not eliminate bias. SIREN's mean signed bias ranges from −0.20 to +0.08 pp across the four aggregate rows in Tables 1–2, the only method consistently near zero.
- **Directional accuracy**: SIREN achieves 10–11/11 correct sign agreements with the Monte Carlo reference across nearly all subject-tuner-budget cells. Competing methods frequently flip the sign (show A>B when truth is A<B or vice versa), especially for near-tie model pairs.
- **Deployment conclusion changes (RQ2)**: Table 3 shows representative cases where M1/M2 incorrectly conclude that a tuned system beats the default, while SIREN's estimate matches the Monte Carlo truth. SIREN preserves gains attributable to the tuning procedure while removing gains due to same-data winner selection.
- **Comparison vs. PromptEval (RQ3)**: PromptEval faces a fundamental bias trade-off controlled by the cell-observation fraction f. Sparse PromptEval (small f) is too pessimistic; dense PromptEval (f→1) degenerates to M1 and inherits the winner's-curse bias. SIREN avoids this trade-off: its signed errors stay between −0.09 and −0.03 pp across all budgets on MMLU-Pro Math with DSPy, while every PromptEval fraction yields substantially larger absolute bias.
- **Calibration**: Appendix controlled simulations (Study A) confirm multiplier-bootstrap coverage with M^{-1/2} width scaling. Study B demonstrates near-tie instability of hard argmax. Study C shows same-data winner optimism grows with the number of searched artifacts.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty.** The paper makes a genuine conceptual contribution by precisely distinguishing the "ex-post artifact" from the "procedure-level" estimand in adaptive LLM benchmarking. The application of post-selection inference tools (specifically the Gaussian multiplier bootstrap for simultaneous inference over a budget grid) to this setting is non-trivial and not previously done. The work is more than incremental — it reframes what the evaluation number in leaderboard comparisons actually means.

**Rigor.** The theoretical development (Theorems 1–4, Lemmas 1–4 with proofs in Appendix B) is careful. The key smoothness assumption on the selector g_{j,B} is stated explicitly (Assumption 1) and its scope (softmax-type selectors; hard argmax requires additional smoothing) is acknowledged. The paper is intellectually honest that the guarantees are conditional on the completed tuning stage, so they do not cover open-ended iterative resampling.

**Reproducibility.** Experiments use publicly available MMLU-Pro benchmarks and 11 named open-weight models served via vLLM on a single GPU (NVIDIA RTX 4090, 24 GB VRAM). Hyperparameters (R=10, ρ_score=0.5, τ=1, 2,000 bootstrap draws) are stated. The 10,000-redraw Monte Carlo ground truth provides a transparent reference. Code is released. Reproduction requires substantial compute (running 11 models × 2 tuners × 4 budgets × many splits) but is feasible.

**Significance.** The finding that naive winner reporting can flip deployment conclusions (tuned vs. default) is practically important, especially as adaptive prompt and program optimization tools (DSPy, OPRO, etc.) become standard. Any practitioner using benchmark scores to decide whether to deploy a tuned prompt should care.

**Red flags / limitations.** (1) The smooth-selector regime is required for the main theoretical guarantees; the paper acknowledges hard argmax requires additional conditions but does not fully resolve it. (2) The method assumes the shortlist and evaluation pool are both fixed before reporting begins — this may not hold in practice when iterative human-in-the-loop refinement continues after initial search. (3) Experiments are limited to MMLU-Pro (classification-style accuracy). It is unclear how well the approach generalizes to generation tasks with LLM-judge scoring, where Z_j(W, a, ξ) itself has higher variance and the scorer may also be adaptive. (4) The paper does not discuss computational cost of SIREN versus baselines in wall-clock terms, though bootstrapping 2,000 resamples of item contributions is fast once scores are computed.

## Strengths

- Precisely identifies and formalizes a bias mechanism that was previously only informally noted in the adaptive benchmarking literature.
- Provides both theoretical (CLT + bootstrap validity theorems) and empirical (Monte Carlo calibration + real LLM tuning experiments) validation.
- SIREN does not require rerunning the tuner, making it computationally practical.
- The single bootstrap procedure simultaneously supports point-wise confidence intervals, simultaneous bands over the budget grid, equal-budget comparisons, and cross-budget comparisons — a flexible and unified inference toolkit.
- Well-positioned relative to related work (PromptEval, ReliableEval, Ladder, post-selection inference literature); the paper clearly articulates what SIREN targets that those methods do not.
- Code is publicly released.

---

## Limitations

- Theoretical guarantees require the selector to be a smooth (differentiable) function of selection scores. Hard argmax, the most common practical selector, requires an additional smoothing step or margin condition not fully resolved.
- The framework is conditional on the completed tuning stage; fully adaptive pipelines where search and evaluation interleave remain outside scope.
- Experiments focus on MMLU-Pro (accuracy on multiple-choice tasks). Extension to generation-task evaluation with LLM-as-judge (where the scoring function itself may be stochastic and potentially adaptive) is left as future work.
- The method assumes a fixed, disjoint evaluation pool. In many real deployments, the test set is small or shared across multiple reporting cycles, raising adaptive data analysis concerns not addressed here.
- Only two tuner families (random search, DSPy) are tested; behavior with other optimizers (OPRO, EvoPrompt, APE) may differ.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How does SIREN behave when the tuner is highly adaptive (e.g., 100+ candidate artifacts searched), where the winner's curse bias is largest but the smooth-selector assumption may be harder to satisfy near the optimum?
2. Can SIREN be extended to generation tasks scored by an LLM judge, where the scoring function Z_j(W, a, ξ) is itself stochastic and potentially correlated across items?
3. What is the minimum evaluation pool size M needed for SIREN's bootstrap CIs to achieve nominal coverage in practice? Is there a sample-size recommendation relative to the shortlist size K?
4. How does SIREN interact with multi-objective or multi-metric evaluation settings, where the artifact selected depends on a composite score across several criteria?
5. The paper conditions on the completed tuning stage. Is there a principled way to extend the framework to an online or iterative setting where tuning and reporting interleave?
6. For practitioners without access to a large disjoint evaluation pool (e.g., low-resource languages), what are the practical alternatives or approximations to the SIREN protocol?
