Beyond Accuracy: Policy Invariance as a Reliability Test for LLM Safety Judges

Shihao Weng, Yang Feng, Xiaofei Xie

arXiv: 2605.06161v1

Date: 2026-05-07

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06161

一句话总结： 本文提出"策略不变性"框架，通过三项压力测试原则（语义等价改写不变性、阈值定向变化性、歧义感知校准）揭示GPT-5.4-mini、Claude-Haiku-4.5、DeepSeek-V3.2、Gemini-3-Flash等四种主流安全裁判模型存在严重可靠性缺陷——内容保留的改写可翻转高达9.1%的裁定，并引入策略不变性分数（PIS）和Judge Card报告规范，暴露出准确率排行榜完全看不见的20倍可靠性差距。
TL;DR: Current LLM safety judges (used to automatically label agent behavior as "safe" or "unsafe") are unreliable in a specific way: they respond to meaningless cosmetic rewrites of the evaluation policy with nearly the same sensitivity as they do to genuine normative shifts. This paper defines "policy invariance" as a minimum bar for any safety judge, operationalizes it as three testable principles, stress-tests four widely-used small judge models (GPT-5.4-mini, Claude-Haiku-4.5, DeepSeek-V3.2, Gemini-3-Flash) on trajectories from ASSEBench and R-Judge, and finds that content-preserving policy rewrites flip up to 9.1% of verdicts above baseline jitter, while 18–43% of all observed flips occur on unambiguous cases. The authors introduce the Policy Invariance Score (PIS) and a "Judge Card" reporting protocol that reveal an order-of-magnitude reliability spread (PIS 0.03–0.70) invisible to accuracy-only leaderboards, and release the stress-test protocol and code publicly.

核心贡献

  • 策略不变性定义：首次将"策略不变性"形式化为LLM安全裁判的最低可靠性要求，并将其分解为三个可操作的可测试原则。
  • 三原则压力测试框架：
  • 原则1（语义不变性）：内容保留的等价改写不应翻转裁定；
  • 原则2（阈值定向变化性）：策略从严到宽的有意变更应产生大且方向一致的裁定变化；
  • 原则3（歧义感知校准）：裁定翻转应集中于真正模糊的案例，而非明确案例。
  • 系统性实证发现：在四个主流小型安全裁判上发现共同失效模式——仅将例外条款从行内移至段首（T4转换）即可翻转DeepSeek的9.1%裁定；18–43%的翻转发生于无歧义的明确案例。
  • 策略不变性分数（PIS）与Judge Card：提出复合评分指标PIS和标准化报告卡，揭示四模型间20倍可靠性差距（0.03–0.70），而该差距在准确率排行榜上完全不可见。
  • 公开协议与代码：发布可在任意新裁判上以少量API调用运行的压力测试协议。

Key Contributions

  • Formalization of policy invariance: The paper is the first to define policy invariance as a minimum reliability requirement for LLM safety judges and operationalize it through three testable principles with formal statistical grounding.
  • Three-principle stress-test protocol: A systematic framework testing (1) rubric-semantics invariance under certified-equivalent rewrites, (2) rubric-threshold invariance under intentional strict-to-lenient normative shifts, and (3) ambiguity-aware calibration to separate legitimate instability on genuinely ambiguous items from unreasonable flips on clear ones.
  • Empirical discovery of a shared failure mode: Four widely-deployed small judge models all react to meaningless structural rewrites with comparable strength to meaningful normative shifts, and cannot distinguish the two. Exception placement alone (T4) flips up to 9.1% of verdicts above jitter.
  • Policy Invariance Score (PIS) and Judge Card: A composite metric bounded in [0,1] and a standardized reporting template that expose an order-of-magnitude reliability spread invisible to accuracy-only benchmarks.
  • Open stress-test toolkit: Protocol and code released so future agent-safety benchmarks can audit their own evaluators rather than trusting them by default.

Methodology

Problem formulation. A safety judge J maps a (policy π, trajectory τ) pair to a binary verdict in {SAFE, UNSAFE}. Policy invariance requires the verdict to be a function of the trajectory's normative content, not of how the policy happens to be worded.

Transformation taxonomy. Five rewrite types are defined:

  • Certified-equivalent (T1 syntax, T2 lexicon, T4 exception placement): preserve all six semantic dimensions of the policy (deontic force, scope, exception set, burden of proof, default rule, implied threshold), certified by three independent annotators with 97% first-pass acceptance.
  • Near-equivalent (T3 deontic strength, T5 framing inversion): intentionally shift normative emphasis in one dimension.
  • Supplementary (T6 irrelevant context injection): add metadata irrelevant to policy content.

Three principles operationalized:

  1. Rubric-semantics invariance: excess flip rate Δ_flip = P_flip(T) − P_jitter ≤ ε for all certified-equivalent T. P_jitter is measured by re-running identical prompts three times at temperature 0.
  2. Rubric-threshold invariance: strict-to-lenient switching should produce large, directional flips (R_dir ≈ 1.0, predominantly UNSAFE→SAFE).
  3. Ambiguity-aware calibration: decompose flips into explainable (ambiguous items or near-equivalent transforms) vs. unreasonable (clear items under certified-equivalent transforms). Unreasonable flip rate U_rate should be minimal.

Policy Invariance Score:

PIS = max(0, 1 − (w₁·Δ_flip^cert + w₂·(1 − R_dir) + w₃·U_rate)·S)

with weights (0.4, 0.3, 0.3) and scale S=5.

Experimental setup. Four small, cost-efficient judge models (GPT-5.4-mini, Claude-Haiku-4.5, DeepSeek-V3.2, Gemini-3-Flash) are tested on 500 items sampled from ASSEBench (1,476 trajectories with paired strict/lenient human labels) and R-Judge (571 records across 27 risk scenarios). GPT-5.4-mini is the anchor model (n=500); others use stratified subsets of 200–245 items. Each item receives 3 identical reruns (jitter estimation) plus 5 rewrite conditions, yielding 8 judge calls per item per model. Statistical inference uses item-clustered bootstrap BCa confidence intervals and GEE logistic regression.

Rewrite generation. A non-evaluated generator model (GPT-5.4-thinking) produces rewrites; each is automatically validated for length ratio, non-identity, and verdict keywords. Three annotators certify equivalence on six deontic dimensions; pairs that fail certification are regenerated until they pass.

Results & Highlights

Principle 1 (Rubric-semantics invariance) — violated by three of four models:

  • Claude-Haiku-4.5: pooled certified Δ_flip = 3.6% (p < 0.05)
  • DeepSeek-V3.2: pooled certified Δ_flip = 3.5% (p < 0.05)
  • Gemini-3-Flash: pooled certified Δ_flip = 7.6% (p < 0.05); 29% of cert pairs return parse-failed JSON, worst-case Δ_flip = 26.6%
  • GPT-5.4-mini: Δ_flip = 1.1% (not significant after jitter correction, despite highest raw flip rates due to high baseline jitter of 6.8%)
  • T4 (exception placement) is the universal weakness: 6.4% (Claude), 9.1% (DeepSeek), 6.6% (Gemini)

Principle 2 (Rubric-threshold invariance) — all models pass directionality, confirming they read policies:

  • Strict-to-lenient switching flips 32–71% of verdicts; directional ratios R_dir = 0.992–1.000 (nearly all flips go UNSAFE→SAFE as expected)
  • This confirms that the failure is not random noise but a specific inability to distinguish meaningful from meaningless wording variation

Principle 3 (Ambiguity-aware calibration) — unreasonable flips are common:

  • 18–43% of all flips are unreasonable (clear items under certified-equivalent transforms)
  • DeepSeek has the highest unreasonable rate (43%), driven largely by T4 sensitivity on unambiguous items
  • GPT-5.4-mini: 18% unreasonable; Claude-Haiku: 31%; Gemini-Flash: 29%

Judge Cards (PIS):

| Model | PIS | Interpretation |

|---|---|---|

| GPT-5.4-mini | 0.70 | Moderate |

| Claude-Haiku-4.5 | 0.47 | Fragile |

| DeepSeek-V3.2 | 0.28 | Unreliable |

| Gemini-3-Flash | [0.03, 0.41] | Unreliable/Fragile |

Irrelevant context injection (T6): Adding procedurally irrelevant metadata flips 2.5–10.5% of verdicts; GPT-5.4-mini is most susceptible (10.5%), primarily flipping SAFE→UNSAFE (metadata read as increasing strictness).

Ranking stability impact: 2.9–7.4% of items cross a binary pass/fail threshold when evaluated on rewritten vs. original policies — sufficient to alter benchmark conclusions.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: High. While prompt sensitivity of LLM judges has been studied, no prior work isolates the evaluation policy (rubric) as the variable under perturbation in the agent-safety domain, or proposes a composite invariance metric for judges. The distinction between certified-equivalent and near-equivalent transforms, certified by human annotators, is a genuine methodological advance over heuristic perturbation studies.

Rigor: Strong. The statistical framework is carefully constructed: jitter correction prevents conflating nondeterminism with rewrite sensitivity; item-clustered bootstrap and GEE logistic regression provide convergent inference; formal proofs are given for the unbiasedness of the estimator and for PIS bounds and monotonicity. Pre-registration of the 5% practical significance threshold is commendable.

Reproducibility: Good in principle. The protocol is described in sufficient detail, item IDs are released, and the rewrite generator and annotation codebook are documented. However, the rewrite generator uses GPT-5.4-thinking (a non-public model variant at submission time), which may limit exact reproducibility for researchers without access. Annotation costs (three annotators, paid at market rates) are non-trivial.

Significance: High for the agent-safety evaluation community. The finding that 18–43% of flips occur on unambiguous items under content-preserving rewrites is a concrete indictment of current judge trustworthiness. The order-of-magnitude PIS spread (0.03–0.70) among models that appear comparable on accuracy leaderboards is a striking finding with immediate practical implications for benchmark design.

Red flags / limitations acknowledged by authors:

  • Only a single standardized six-criterion base policy is tested; real-world deployment rubrics may have hundreds of clauses, jurisdictional qualifications, and domain-specific carve-outs. Per-pair human certification scales linearly with policy length, making the approach expensive at scale.
  • All four judges are small, cost-optimized models (deployment-profile agentic models); whether frontier-scale models (GPT-4o, Claude Opus, etc.) show similar failures is untested and explicitly acknowledged as a limitation.
  • PIS weights (0.4, 0.3, 0.3) encode a prior on failure mode costs without downstream validation.
  • The framework operates at trajectory level with binary verdicts; step-level judging, final-answer-only judging, multilingual rubrics, and abstention regimes are outside scope.
  • Gemini-3-Flash's 153/582 cert pairs producing malformed JSON is itself a reliability failure that inflates uncertainty in its PIS bracket and limits comparability.

Strengths

  • Identifies a previously unmeasured, practically consequential failure mode in LLM-as-a-Judge pipelines for agent safety.
  • Methodological rigor: formal estimators with proofs, pre-registered thresholds, convergent statistical methods, and human-certified equivalence labels.
  • The Judge Card is a practical and deployable artifact — any new benchmark can run the stress-test and report PIS alongside accuracy at modest API cost.
  • The diagnostic is actionable: the per-principle breakdown tells practitioners exactly what kind of invariance fails for each model, pointing toward targeted mitigations (e.g., ensemble judging, rubric locking, invariance-aware fine-tuning).
  • Cross-benchmark replication (ASSEBench and R-Judge) and cross-provider replication (four model families) strengthen the generality of findings.

Limitations

  • The single standardized policy may not capture real deployment rubric complexity; scaling the annotation process to long, multi-clause rubrics is expensive and not yet demonstrated.
  • Frontier-scale judge models (GPT-4o, Claude Opus 4, etc.) are excluded; the PIS spread observed may narrow significantly at scale, and the paper cannot speak to this.
  • The PIS weighting scheme is not empirically validated against downstream harms; different stakeholders may weight the three principles differently.
  • Gemini-3-Flash parsing failures (~26% of cert pairs) limit the reliability of its PIS estimate and introduce substantial uncertainty.
  • The framework is binary (SAFE/UNSAFE) and whole-trajectory; it does not yet cover the increasingly common paradigm of step-level or abstention-capable judges.
  • Irrelevant context injection (T6) uses only two metadata types (version tags, timestamps); the breadth of the over-conditioning failure mode is not fully characterized.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Do frontier-scale judge models (e.g., GPT-4o, Claude Opus 4, Llama-3-70B with specialized judge fine-tuning) exhibit similar policy invariance failures, or does scale and RLHF mitigate rubric-wording sensitivity?
  2. Can invariance-aware training (explicitly optimizing for low Δ_flip on certified-equivalent rewrites) improve PIS without sacrificing accuracy on the primary safety classification task?
  3. Would ensemble judging across semantically equivalent rubric variants reduce unreasonable flip rates below any individual judge, and what is the cost-benefit tradeoff?
  4. How does policy invariance degrade as rubric length and specificity increase — at what clause count does the per-pair human certification protocol become impractical?
  5. Does the T4 (exception placement) weakness reflect a general discourse-structure sensitivity in transformer attention, and can prompt engineering (e.g., front-loading all exceptions by default) serve as a low-cost mitigation?
  6. How should the PIS weights be calibrated against downstream safety outcomes — are there natural tasks (red-team evaluations, deployment decisions) that provide an external validation signal for the weight choices?
