> **一句话总结：** 本文提出 LMO-IGT，通过隐式梯度传输（IGT）将随机梯度在"前瞻点"而非当前参数处求值，在不增加梯度计算次数的前提下将 LMO 类优化器（Lion、Muon 等）的迭代复杂度从 O(ε⁻⁴) 提升至 O(ε⁻³·⁵)，且 Muon-IGT 在 CIFAR-10、语言建模等任务上一致优于基线。

> **TL;DR:** LMO-IGT is a new class of stochastic optimizers for methods like Lion and Muon (which normalize gradient momentum via linear minimization oracles). By evaluating the stochastic gradient at a "transported" lookahead point rather than the current iterate—borrowing the implicit gradient transport (IGT) trick from normalized SGD—the method improves iteration complexity from O(ε⁻⁴) to O(ε⁻³·⁵) while using only a single gradient evaluation per step (matching plain stochastic LMO, not variance reduction which costs two). The paper also introduces a unified analysis framework with a new stationarity measure (regularized support function, RSF) that bridges unconstrained gradient-norm and constrained Frank-Wolfe-gap criteria. Empirically, Muon-IGT consistently outperforms Muon with negligible wall-clock overhead across image classification and language modeling.

## 核心贡献

- **LMO-IGT 算法族**：将隐式梯度传输（IGT）从欧氏归一化更新推广至一般线性最小化预言机（LMO）更新，涵盖 Lion（ℓ∞ 范数球）和 Muon（谱范数球）等现代优化器，得到 Lion-IGT 和 Muon-IGT 两个具体实例。
- **统一分析框架**：基于一个新的稳定性度量——正则化支撑函数（RSF），将随机 LMO、方差缩减 LMO（LMO-VR）和 LMO-IGT 三类方法纳入同一理论框架，同时处理无约束与有约束两种优化设置。
- **理论改进**：在单次梯度评估的条件下，LMO-IGT 的迭代复杂度达到 O(ε⁻³·⁵)，优于普通随机 LMO 的 O(ε⁻⁴)，且仅比需要双倍梯度代价的方差缩减方法（O(ε⁻³)）略逊。
- **实证验证**：在 CIFAR-10/ResNet-18 图像分类、Shakespeare 语言建模（nanoGPT 10M）和 OpenWebText 大规模语言建模（nanoGPT 124M）三个任务上，Muon-IGT 均以接近原版 Muon 的速度取得最佳或靠前的准确率/损失，优于方差缩减方法（后者因额外梯度计算导致墙钟时间大幅增加）。

## Key Contributions

- **LMO-IGT algorithm family**: Extends IGT—previously limited to ℓ₂-normalized SGD—to any LMO-based optimizer. By evaluating the stochastic gradient at a transported point x_t (a lookahead along the same LMO direction), momentum lag is reduced without extra gradient calls. Instantiations include Lion-IGT (ℓ∞-ball) and Muon-IGT (operator-norm ball).
- **Unified framework with RSF**: Introduces the *regularized support function* Ψ_{C,λ}(w) = sup_{v∈C}⟨-∇F(w), v - λw⟩ as a stationarity measure that reduces to the scaled Frank-Wolfe gap when λ > 0 and to the dual-norm gradient measure when λ = 0. This enables a single convergence analysis across constrained and unconstrained LMO settings.
- **Complexity hierarchy**: Establishes O(ε⁻⁴) for stochastic LMO, O(ε⁻³) for LMO-VR (two gradient evals), and O(ε⁻³·⁵) for LMO-IGT (one gradient eval), all measured under the unified RSF.
- **Practical efficiency**: Muon-IGT achieves better final accuracy than Muon-VR while running at nearly the same wall-clock speed as plain Muon, addressing the practical gap between variance-reduction theory and compute reality.

## Methodology

**Core mechanism — IGT for LMO updates.** In standard stochastic LMO (e.g., Muon), the momentum buffer m_t accumulates exponential moving averages of gradients at the *current* iterate w_t, creating a "lagging" effect where m_t can be misaligned with ∇F(w_t). LMO-IGT mitigates this by evaluating ∇f at a transported point

    x_{t+1} = (1 - λη_{1,t})w_t + η_{1,t} v_t

which lies along the same LMO direction v_t = LMO_C(g_t), but uses a larger step size η_{1,t} = η_{2,t}/(1-β₂). The actual iterate w_{t+1} uses the smaller step η_{2,t}. This produces a double-momentum structure (β₁ ≤ β₂) where the fast momentum g_t drives the LMO query and the slower buffer m_t carries trajectory information.

**Unified framework.** Algorithm 2 captures all three method classes via parameters α_{1,t}, α_{2,t} (variance-reduction corrections) and η_{1,t} ≠ η_{2,t} (IGT split step sizes). Setting α = 0, η₁ = η₂ recovers stochastic LMO; nonzero α with η₁ = η₂ gives LMO-VR; α = 0 with η₁ ≠ η₂ gives LMO-IGT.

**Analysis.** The proofs track the gradient query error ε̂_t = g_t - ∇F(w_t) by decomposing it into a martingale noise term N_t and a deterministic bias D_t. Under second-order smoothness (Lipschitz Hessian, Assumption 4), the transported point x_t introduces a second-order remainder Z(x_t, w_t) with ∥Z∥ ≤ ρ∥x_t - w_t∥², which the analysis bounds using the step-size ratio. Optimal parameter tuning (η = T^{-5/7}, β₂ = 1 - T^{-4/7}) yields the O(T^{-2/7}) = O(ε^{-3.5}) rate.

**Experiments.** Three tasks: (1) ResNet-18 on CIFAR-10, 200 epochs, 5 seeds; (2) nanoGPT (10M) on Shakespeare, 5000 steps; (3) nanoGPT (124M) on OpenWebText. Hyperparameters are separately tuned per method via grid search. LMO-VR methods serve as the theoretical upper-bound comparison; AdamW and NIGT serve as practical baselines.

## Results & Highlights

- **CIFAR-10 / ResNet-18**: Muon-IGT achieves the highest test accuracy among all methods. Muon-VR, despite better theoretical rate, is substantially slower in wall-clock time due to double gradient evaluations and only matches or underperforms Muon-IGT. Lion-VR actually performs *worse* than plain Lion, suggesting sign-based LMO is not uniformly helped by variance reduction corrections.
- **Shakespeare nanoGPT (10M)**: Muon-IGT leads in both training and test loss from early in optimization, outperforming AdamW, Muon, and Lion.
- **OpenWebText nanoGPT (124M)**: The same qualitative trend persists at 124M scale, with Muon-IGT achieving lower training and test loss than baselines.
- **Ablation**: A "Muon*" variant (double momentum only, no IGT) improves over plain Muon; adding IGT on top gives further accuracy and loss gains, confirming both components contribute independently.
- **Robustness**: Muon-IGT shows broad high-accuracy regions across a 9×10 grid of learning rate × weight decay values, indicating the gains are not from narrow hyperparameter tuning.
- **Complexity table**: Stochastic LMO O(ε⁻⁴), LMO-VR O(ε⁻³) [2 grad evals], LMO-IGT O(ε⁻³·⁵) [1 grad eval].

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** The core algorithmic idea—applying IGT to LMO-based methods—is a natural and well-motivated extension. IGT for normalized SGD already existed (Cutkosky and Mehta, 2020). The non-trivial contributions are: (a) showing IGT transfers to *general* LMO geometries (sign, operator-norm), (b) the RSF stationarity measure unifying constrained and unconstrained cases, and (c) placing all three method classes on a common complexity axis. The novelty is incremental-but-solid rather than transformative.

**Rigor:** The theoretical analysis appears technically sound. Proofs follow established patterns from stochastic non-convex optimization (STORM-style momentum bounds, IGT second-order remainder bounds). The RSF unification is a genuine conceptual contribution. The required second-order smoothness assumption (Lipschitz Hessian) for the improved LMO-IGT rate is stronger than what plain stochastic LMO requires—this trade-off is acknowledged.

**Reproducibility:** Experimental details are thorough (Appendix F): exact hardware, hyperparameter grids, all final hyperparameter values tabulated per method, batch sizes, architectures. Code is not released but implementation from the paper description appears straightforward. Experiments are run on a single RTX 3090, which is accessible. 5-seed averaging with standard deviation reported for main results.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a genuine practical concern: variance reduction for Lion/Muon theoretically improves rates but doubles gradient cost, hurting practitioners. The O(ε⁻³·⁵) at single gradient cost is a meaningful contribution. However, the improvement over plain Muon in absolute accuracy (e.g., CIFAR-10 ~94.5% vs ~93.5%) is modest, and experiments are relatively small-scale (ResNet-18, 124M GPT). Whether gains persist for billion-parameter models is unverified.

**Red flags:** (1) The theory requires Lipschitz Hessian (ρ), an assumption that is standard but not easily verified in practice. (2) Lion-VR performing *worse* than plain Lion in the main experiment is not fully explained—this inconsistency between theory and practice for sign-based methods deserves deeper analysis. (3) Wall-clock comparison figures show curves terminating at different times, which is correct but can make visual comparison subtle. (4) Large-scale experiments (OpenWebText, 124M) show only training/test loss curves without quantitative tables, making precise comparison harder.

## Strengths

- Clean theoretical contribution: a new stationarity measure (RSF) that genuinely unifies two previously disconnected convergence analyses.
- Practical efficiency advantage is clearly demonstrated: Muon-IGT matches or beats variance-reduction baselines at the cost of plain Muon in wall-clock time.
- Ablation study isolates the double-momentum and IGT components separately, providing interpretable evidence for the design choices.
- Thorough hyperparameter robustness analysis over a wide grid strengthens empirical credibility.
- Tight integration between theoretical framework (Algorithm 2 as the umbrella) and all algorithmic variants, making the paper coherent.

## Limitations

- The O(ε⁻³·⁵) guarantee requires second-order smoothness (Lipschitz Hessian), a stronger assumption than the O(ε⁻⁴) baseline which only needs L-smoothness and bounded variance. This assumption is not verified empirically.
- No high-probability convergence bounds or last-iterate guarantees; only expected RSF averaged over iterates.
- Experiments are limited to ResNet-18 and sub-200M-parameter language models. It is unclear whether the Muon-IGT advantage is maintained at the billion-parameter frontier where Muon is increasingly being adopted.
- The failure of Lion-VR (variance reduction for sign-based LMO) is not theoretically explained, leaving a gap between theory and observed behavior.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Muon-IGT requires a different learning rate (5e-4) than Muon (5e-2) in the language modeling experiments, suggesting the optimal regime shifts with IGT and may require re-tuning.
- No study of interaction with learning rate schedules (explicitly omitted to "isolate optimizer effects"), which is a common practical component.

## Relevance

LMO-IGT 直接改进了 Muon 和 Lion 这两类近期在大模型训练中获得关注的优化器，通过隐式梯度传输实现了理论加速与实际效率的统一，对关注深度学习优化理论或希望替换 AdamW 的研究者具有较强的参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Does Muon-IGT's advantage persist at billion-parameter scale, where Muon is increasingly used as an alternative to AdamW in LLM pretraining pipelines?
2. Can the second-order smoothness assumption be relaxed or replaced with a weaker condition that still permits the O(ε⁻³·⁵) rate?
3. Why does Lion-VR (variance reduction for sign-based LMO) hurt performance while Muon-VR is neutral-to-helpful? Is this a property of the ℓ∞ geometry, the correction term magnitude, or gradient noise structure in the sign direction?
4. How does LMO-IGT interact with learning rate schedules (cosine decay, warmup) that are standard in practice but excluded from this study?
5. Can the RSF stationarity measure be used to compare LMO-based methods against adaptive methods (AdamW, Adafactor) in a theoretically principled way, given that adaptive preconditioners do not naturally fit the LMO framework?
6. The transported point x_t requires computing and storing an auxiliary iterate—how does this interact with memory-efficient training techniques like gradient checkpointing or ZeRO?
