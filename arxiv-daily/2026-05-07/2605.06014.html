Quantizing With Randomized Hadamard Transforms: Efficient Heuristic Now Proven

Ran Ben-Basat, William Kuszmaul, Michael Mitzenmacher, Amit Portnoy, Shay Vargaftik

arXiv: 2605.06014

Date: 2026-05-07

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06014

一句话总结： 本文从理论上严格证明了将两个随机哈达玛变换（2-RHT）组合使用，可在 Kolmogorov 距离和 1-Wasserstein 距离上实现与均匀随机旋转（URR）等价的 $\mathcal{O}(d^{-1/2})$ 高斯逼近保证，并将此结论应用于 DRIVE 和 QUIC-FL 两个实际标量量化方案，消除了单 RHT 所带来的理论性能损失；进一步证明三个 RHT（3-RHT）可为向量量化（VQ）提供充分的坐标去相关性保证。
TL;DR: Replacing a Uniform Random Rotation (URR) with a single Randomized Hadamard Transform (RHT) is fast but theoretically lossy for worst-case inputs. This paper proves that composing two RHTs suffices to recover all URR-level guarantees for scalar quantization schemes (DRIVE, QUIC-FL) up to a $\mathcal{O}(d^{-1/2})$ additive term, and that three RHTs additionally provide the coordinate decorrelation needed for Vector Quantization. A linear-time $\mathcal{O}(d)$ adaptive check on the input's $\ell_3$ and $\ell_\infty$ norms lets practitioners skip unnecessary RHT layers at runtime. The results convert a widely-used practical heuristic into a fully proven theory.

核心贡献

  • 2-RHT 标量边际保证：证明对任意输入向量，两次 RHT 组合后每个坐标的归一化分布在 Kolmogorov 距离和 1-Wasserstein 距离上均与标准正态 $\mathcal{O}(d^{-1/2})$ 接近，彻底消除单 RHT 的最坏情况失效问题。
  • 应用于 DRIVE：将 2-RHT 的 $W_1$ 界代入 DRIVE 的分析框架，为有偏和无偏估计器分别恢复了与 URR 完全一致的 vNMSE 渐近界（误差衰减至 $\mathcal{O}(d^{-1/2})$）。
  • 应用于 QUIC-FL：利用 2-RHT 的 Kolmogorov 界恢复了 QUIC-FL 的 URR 级别量化误差界，消除了单 RHT 带来的 $3.2\times$ 乘法惩罚。
  • 3-RHT 向量量化去相关：证明两个 RHT 不足以为向量量化提供坐标去相关（存在稀疏输入反例），三个 RHT 的均方根条件协方差衰减为 $\mathcal{O}(\sqrt{\log d / d})$，从而使任意固定的 URR 或高斯码书在期望误差上与三 RHT 等价。
  • 自适应线性时间验证：提出基于输入立方 $\ell_3$ 范数（标量检验）和平方 $\ell_\infty$ 范数（向量检验）的 $\mathcal{O}(d)$ 自适应判定方法，动态决定所需 RHT 层数（1、2 或 3 层），兼顾理论严谨与实际效率。

Key Contributions

  • Universal 2-RHT scalar marginal guarantee: For any arbitrary input vector and any fixed output coordinate, two composed RHTs yield a marginal distribution within $\mathcal{O}(d^{-1/2})$ of $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ in both Kolmogorov ($d_K$) and 1-Wasserstein ($W_1$) distance. The explicit bound is $d_K \leq 1.28/\sqrt{d}$.
  • Formal guarantees for DRIVE: Plugging 2-RHT $W_1$ bounds into the DRIVE analysis recovers URR-matching vNMSE for both biased ($1 - 2/\pi + \mathcal{O}(d^{-1/2})$) and unbiased ($\pi/2 - 1 + \mathcal{O}(d^{-1/2})$) configurations.
  • Formal guarantees for QUIC-FL: The 2-RHT Kolmogorov bound restores the exact URR-based Bounded Support Quantization error integrals, removing the 3.2x multiplicative penalty of single-RHT and producing vNMSE bounds 2.77x–3.17x tighter across bit budgets 1–4.
  • 3-RHT decorrelation for VQ: Two RHTs provably fail to decorrelate sparse inputs (Lemma 3 shows perfect conditional correlation can arise). Three RHTs bound the RMS conditional covariance by $\mathcal{O}(\sqrt{\log d / d})$, guaranteeing codebook universality via multivariate Stein's Method (Theorem 7).
  • Adaptive $\mathcal{O}(d)$ verification: A single-pass computation of the cubed $\ell_3$ mass and squared $\ell_\infty$ norm determines whether 1, 2, or 3 RHTs are required, enabling GPU-friendly dynamic adaptation without sacrificing worst-case theoretical guarantees.

Methodology

The paper's argument proceeds in a modular, three-stage structure:

Stage 1 — Two-RHT marginal normality (Sections 2–3).

The key insight is that after one RHT, the intermediate vector's cubed $\ell_3$ norm $\rho_3(a) = \sum_i |a_i|^3$ has small expected value $\mathcal{O}(d^{-1/2})$ regardless of input (Lemma 1, proved via Hölder's inequality on a fourth-moment calculation). The second RHT then applies the Berry–Esseen theorem for non-i.i.d. variables: each output coordinate becomes a sum of independent terms whose $\ell_3$ mass is bounded by the already-small $\rho_3(a)$, yielding Kolmogorov distance $\leq 0.5606 \cdot C_3 / \sqrt{d} \leq 1.28/\sqrt{d}$ (Theorem 1) and 1-Wasserstein distance $\leq C_W C_3 / \sqrt{d}$ (Theorem 2).

Stage 2 — Application to scalar quantizers (Sections 4–5).

For DRIVE, the biased vNMSE is expressed purely in terms of the expected $L_1$ norm of a single rotated coordinate; the $W_1$ bound translates this directly to the Gaussian limit. The unbiased variant requires a careful decomposition into parallel and orthogonal components of the expected direction vector $\mu$, bounding both via the proxy Gaussian scale $c = \sqrt{2/\pi}$ (Theorem 3). For QUIC-FL, whose Bounded Support Quantization error function has jump discontinuities (making $W_1$ inapplicable), the Kolmogorov distance is used instead, controlling the difference in expected quantization error through a total-variation argument (Theorem 5).

Stage 3 — Three-RHT decorrelation for Vector Quantization (Section 6).

Lemma 3 exhibits a concrete sparse counterexample showing 2-RHT conditional covariance between coordinate pairs can be exactly $\pm 1$. With a third RHT, the first RHT acts as a "pre-smoother": by sub-Gaussian union bounds on $\|R_1\tilde{x}\|_\infty^2 = \mathcal{O}(\log d / d)$, the subsequent 2-RHT analysis bounds the RMS conditional covariance (Theorem 6). Codebook universality (Theorem 7) is then established using multivariate Stein's Method via a Wasserstein bound on the deviation of the empirical covariance from the identity, decomposing the VQ error into a quadratic (canceling) part and a 2B-Lipschitz remainder term.

Adaptive verification (Section 7).

Both the scalar ($\rho_3$) and vector ($\|\tilde{x}\|_\infty^2$) diagnostic quantities can be computed in one pass as simple moments ($S_2 = \sum x_r^2$, $S_3 = \sum |x_r|^3$, $M_2 = \max x_r^2$), enabling a GPU-friendly $\mathcal{O}(d)$, $\mathcal{O}(1)$-space adaptive decision at runtime.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: High. The paper resolves a long-standing open question: prior work identified RHT as a practical heuristic for URR but could not prove it achieves the same asymptotic performance. The 2-RHT composition argument is non-trivial — it requires showing the first RHT smooths the $\ell_3$ mass, then applying Berry–Esseen to the second. The 3-RHT VQ result and its multivariate Stein's Method proof are likewise genuinely new.

Rigor: High. All major theorems are fully proved (main body + appendices A–C covering deferred proofs). The use of Berry–Esseen for non-i.i.d. variables (Tyurin's 2009 bound) and the multivariate Stein's Method lemma are correctly cited and carefully applied. The counterexample (Lemma 3) is concrete and unambiguous.

Reproducibility: Good. The adaptive verification algorithm is fully specified and computable in $\mathcal{O}(d)$. No experiments are run (this is a purely theoretical paper), so there are no implementation artifacts to reproduce. The bounds contain explicit constants (e.g., $0.5606 C_3 \leq 1.28$), making numerical verification straightforward.

Significance: High for the quantization-for-ML community. DRIVE and QUIC-FL are actively deployed in federated learning and gradient compression; removing the theoretical gap between RHT and URR has direct implications for correctness guarantees in these systems. The VQ result is also timely given growing use of codebook-based quantization in KV-cache compression and inference acceleration.

Potential red flags:

  • The paper is almost entirely theoretical with no empirical validation of whether the improvement in theoretical bounds translates to measurable gains in practice for finite $d$.
  • The 3-RHT VQ bound decays as $\mathcal{O}(\sqrt{\log d / d})$ rather than the $\mathcal{O}(d^{-1/2})$ of the scalar case — the extra $\sqrt{\log d}$ factor may be a proof artifact rather than tight.
  • The adaptive check thresholds ($\eta_3 = \mathcal{O}(d^{-1/2})$, $\eta_\infty = \mathcal{O}(\log d / d)$) are stated asymptotically; specific finite-$d$ cutoff values are not given, leaving some ambiguity for practitioners.
  • All guarantees are for the Sylvester–Hadamard construction; generalization to non-power-of-2 dimensions via padding is standard but not discussed.

Strengths

  • Closes a genuine theoretical gap that the community had left open for years, turning a widely-used heuristic into a proven method.
  • Modular proof structure: the core probabilistic statements (2-RHT normality, 3-RHT decorrelation) are proved once and then cleanly plugged into each application, making the framework reusable for future quantization schemes.
  • Explicit, non-asymptotic constants throughout (e.g., $d_K \leq 1.28/\sqrt{d}$), enabling direct numerical comparison.
  • The Lemma 3 counterexample is an important negative result: it warns practitioners that single-pass decorrelation checks (unconditional covariance) are insufficient for VQ.
  • The adaptive $\mathcal{O}(d)$ check is a practical contribution that makes the theory actionable without overhead.

---

Limitations

  • No empirical experiments: the paper does not validate whether the theoretically tighter bounds translate to measurable improvement in real systems (federated learning, LLM quantization) for practical values of $d$.
  • The VQ result (Theorem 7) gives a bound on the difference in expected quantization error, not an end-to-end bound on absolute VQ error; users still need the URR-optimal codebook as a starting point.
  • The adaptive check operates on worst-case norms, so for data with heterogeneous structure across the dimension, it may conservatively require more RHT layers than necessary.
  • The paper focuses on Sylvester–Hadamard matrices; the analysis for other structured orthogonal transforms (e.g., DCT-based) would require separate treatment.
  • The 4-RHT extension (for non-fixed-size VQ blocks) is mentioned as future work without any formal result.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Do the 2-RHT guarantees hold with tighter (dimension-free) constants if the input distribution satisfies a log-Sobolev inequality, or is the $\mathcal{O}(d^{-1/2})$ rate fundamentally tight for all structured orthogonal transforms?
  2. Can the $\sqrt{\log d}$ gap between the 3-RHT VQ bound and the 2-RHT scalar bound be closed, or does a matching lower bound exist for coordinate decorrelation under structured rotations?
  3. How does the 2-RHT theory interact with post-training quantization frameworks (e.g., HIGGS, QuIP#, QTIP) that use Hadamard incoherence preprocessing — do their RHT-based guarantees inherit from this work directly?
  4. The adaptive check uses $\ell_3$ and $\ell_\infty$ norms as proxies for "flatness." Is there a minimax-optimal choice of norm for this adaptive decision, or are there inputs where these statistics are misleading?
  5. For non-power-of-2 dimensions, padding introduces zeros that trivially satisfy the flatness conditions — does this mean 1-RHT always suffices for padded inputs, and does that padding cost outweigh the RHT savings?
  6. Can a similar composing argument prove URR-matching guarantees for other fast structured transforms (e.g., sparse random projections, butterfly networks) used in modern LLM quantization?
