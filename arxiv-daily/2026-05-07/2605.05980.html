TACT: Mitigating Overthinking and Overacting in Coding Agents via Activation Steering

arXiv: 2605.05980

Date: 2026-05-07

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.05980

一句话总结： TACT 通过在 Transformer 残差流中提取"过度思考"与"过度行动"两条对比激活方向轴，并在推理时将偏离的隐藏状态拉回校准区域，在不需要微调或修改提示的前提下，将 Qwen3.5-27B 在 SWE-bench Verified 上的解决率提升 5.8 个百分点，并将步骤数减少最多 26%。
TL;DR: Coding agents operating over long trajectories tend to degrade through two failure modes — "overthinking" (redundant reasoning over already-known information) and "overacting" (redundant tool calls that retrieve already-observed observations). TACT formalizes these failures, labels them with an LLM-as-judge pipeline that maintains a rolling compressed state, extracts two orthogonal contrastive directions (drift axes) in the residual stream at the token, and at test time steers out-of-band hidden states back toward the calibrated region via a lightweight forward hook — no fine-tuning, no extra LLM calls, no prompt changes. On SWE-bench Verified and two additional coding benchmarks the method lifts resolve rate by up to +5.8 pp on Qwen3.5-27B and +4.8 pp on Gemma-4-26B, while reducing steps-to-resolve by up to 26%. Worth reading if you work on long-horizon agent reliability, activation/representation engineering, or want a training-free alternative to prompt-based self-reflection.

核心贡献

  • 定义并量化了两类智能体漂移： 将"过度思考"（Overthinking, OT）和"过度行动"（Overacting, OA）形式化为轨迹步骤级别的标签，并证明两者的比率与 SWE-bench 解决率呈显著负相关，为下游干预提供了明确动机。
  • LLM-as-Judge 标注流水线： 设计了三阶段（State Maintainer → State Verifier → Annotator）滚动状态标注方案，以 10 步分块方式高效管理长轨迹上下文，并通过人工验证达到 Cohen's κ = 0.78 的实质性一致度。
  • 对比激活轴提取： 在 token 处提取每层隐藏状态，用逐问题均值差法构造 OT-vs-CAL 和 OA-vs-CAL 两条方向向量，并通过 Gram-Schmidt 正交化保证两轴独立；OT-vs-CAL 在早期-中期层（第 3 层）的 AUC 达 0.91，三类可线性分离。
  • 测试时双轴引导（三种策略）： 提出 TACT-Cap（双向限幅）、TACT-Add（加性偏移）、TACT-Gate（投影门控）三种仅需向前钩子的干预方式，最优方案 TACT-Gate 在 SWE-bench Verified 上将解决率从 67.0% 提升至 73.3%，步骤数从 75.2 降至 65.3。
  • 无需微调、无需额外 LLM 调用： 与需要多次 rollout 的 AgentDebug 基线相比，TACT 以常数时间读写残差流，在同等或更低计算开销下取得更高收益。

Key Contributions

  • Formal taxonomy of agent drift: Defines overthinking (OT) — re-deriving or re-reasoning from information already in the agent's knowledge state — and overacting (OA) — firing tool calls that return already-observed observations — as measurable step-level labels, and shows both drift ratios anti-correlate with task resolve rate.
  • Scalable LLM-as-judge annotation with rolling state: A three-agent pipeline (State Maintainer, State Verifier, Annotator) processes trajectories in chunks of 10 steps, forwarding a compressed verified state to avoid context blow-up. Achieves Cohen's κ = 0.78 vs. human consensus and is robust to judge model substitution (GPT-5.4 vs. Claude Opus 4.6 yields <1 pp resolve-rate difference).
  • Drift axis extraction via representation engineering: Per-layer contrastive directions are computed as per-problem balanced mean differences in the residual stream at tokens. Orthogonalization via Gram-Schmidt ensures the two failure-mode axes do not interfere during steering. OT-vs-CAL AUC peaks at 0.91 (layer 3); 3-class LDA confirms linear separability.
  • Three training-free test-time steering operators: TACT-Cap (bidirectional projection clamping), TACT-Add (constant additive shift), and TACT-Gate (conditional projection-gated correction) are all implemented as residual-stream forward hooks at the top-K layers (K=10 by default). No retraining, no prompt engineering, no extra inference passes.
  • Consistent gains across models and benchmarks: Evaluated on SWE-bench Verified (500 real GitHub issues), Terminal-Bench 2.0 (89 hard terminal tasks), and CLAW-Eval (autonomous agent trustworthiness), with two open-weight reasoning models (Qwen3.5-27B, Gemma-4-26B-A4B-it).

Methodology

TACT is a three-stage pipeline operating entirely at inference time.

Stage 1 — Trajectory Annotation. Given completed agent rollouts, every step s_t = (r_t, a_t, o_t) (reasoning, action, observation) is labeled as OVERTHINKING, OVERACTING, or CALIBRATED by an LLM-as-judge system. To handle the long-range context dependency (label at step 50 requires knowing what the agent knew at step 1), the pipeline maintains a rolling compressed state with two fields: KnownFacts (concrete files read, hypotheses, edits, test results) and Progress (stagnation signals, hypothesis cycling, re-read counts). A State Maintainer updates this state per chunk; a State Verifier audits for hallucination, omission, and contradiction (up to 3 correction iterations); and an Annotator labels each candidate step given the verified rolling state plus the raw steps in the current chunk. Only high- and medium-confidence labels are retained for axis training.

Stage 2 — Drift Axis Extraction. For each transformer layer ℓ, TACT reads the hidden state at the token (the reasoning-action boundary). Three contrastive axes are computed via per-problem balanced mean differences: OT-vs-CAL (primary overthinking axis), OA-vs-CAL (overacting axis), and OT-vs-OA (diagnostic only). To decouple the two steering directions, OA-vs-CAL is orthogonalized against OT-vs-CAL via Gram-Schmidt, yielding the pair (v̂_OT, v̂_OA⊥). The calibrated centroid μ_CAL and per-axis projection standard deviation σ_CAL are estimated on calibrated training steps and serve as the reference band. Layers are ranked by per-problem AUC of OT-vs-CAL on a held-out validation split; the top K=10 layers are selected as the steering set.

Stage 3 — Dual-Axis Test-Time Steering. A forward hook is registered at each of the K selected layers. At every step, after the token is generated, the hook reads h_t^(ℓ), computes its centered projections onto the two drift axes, and conditionally modifies h_t^(ℓ) before the forward pass continues:

  • TACT-Cap: Clamps both projections into [-τ, +τ] (τ = k·σ_CAL); only fires when activations leave the band. Conservative and precise.
  • TACT-Add: Applies a fixed shift s·(v̂_OT + v̂_OA⊥) at every step regardless of current projection; simpler but less selective.
  • TACT-Gate: Fires when |p| > τ and subtracts λ·excess(p, τ)·v̂_a; combines selectivity with a tunable correction strength λ. Best overall performance.

The entire axis-extraction procedure is closed-form linear algebra and completes in seconds on a single GPU after a one-time forward pass to cache hidden states.

Results & Highlights

Main Results (Table 1):

| Model | Method | SWE-V Res. | Steps↓ | TB2 Res. | CLAW Pass@3 | Avg. Res. |

|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

| Qwen3.5-27B | Base | 67.0 | 75.2 | 29.8 | 55.9 | 51.1 |

| Qwen3.5-27B | AgentDebug† | 70.5 | 116.4 | 32.8 | 58.2 | 53.8 |

| Qwen3.5-27B | TACT-Gate | 73.3 | 65.3 | 36.0 | 61.5 | 56.9 |

| Gemma-4-26B | Base | 59.7 | 52.1 | 15.7 | 56.3 | 43.9 |

| Gemma-4-26B | AgentDebug† | 62.0 | 78.9 | 17.2 | 58.0 | 45.7 |

| Gemma-4-26B | TACT-Gate | 65.5 | 44.7 | 19.1 | 61.4 | 48.7 |

(†AgentDebug requires at least two rollouts per task; all other methods use a single rollout.)

Key findings:

  • TACT-Gate achieves +6.3 pp over baseline on Qwen3.5-27B SWE-V and +5.8 pp on Gemma, exceeding AgentDebug at roughly half the inference cost.
  • TACT-Cap, despite firing conservatively, still cuts steps-to-resolve by 26% on SWE-V for both models — indicating that even targeted interventions on a small fraction of steps produce trajectory-level efficiency gains.
  • Ablations (Table 2) confirm both axes contribute: removing OT axis drops gain to +2.6 pp, removing OA axis drops to +4.2 pp, using all 64 layers instead of top-10 drops to +1.4 pp.
  • Drift axes are stable across early-to-middle transformer layers (AUC > 0.86 through layer ~20), demonstrating that agent drift is a multi-layer distributed representation, not a single-layer artifact.
  • Judge label robustness: replacing GPT-5.4 with Claude Opus 4.6 changes end-to-end resolve rate by <1 pp across all three TACT variants (Table 3).

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The core idea — treating agent behavioral drift as a steerable direction in activation space — is a meaningful conceptual contribution that bridges representation engineering (previously applied to single-turn behaviors like refusal or truthfulness) with long-horizon multi-step agents. The LLM-as-judge annotation pipeline with rolling state compression is also a non-trivial engineering contribution for handling trajectory-length context. The connection between drift ratios and task success provides motivation beyond anecdote. That said, the mechanical components (contrastive mean difference axes, Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization, residual stream hooks) are all prior art; novelty is primarily in their combination and application to coding agents.

Rigor: Experimental rigor is above average for the area. The use of three independent benchmarks, two model families, and multiple baseline categories is commendable. Ablations are well-targeted and the hyperparameter sweeps are reported rather than cherry-picked. The human annotation reliability study with per-class precision/recall and Cohen's κ is appropriate and the numbers (κ = 0.78, F1 ≥ 0.83 per class) are genuinely substantial rather than marginal.

Reproducibility: Potential concerns exist. The method requires access to model internals (residual stream activations), so it is limited to open-weight models. The axis extraction requires a corpus of labeled agent trajectories on the target benchmark — the paper does not clearly specify the size of the training split used for axis fitting, which is a practical reproducibility gap. Full system prompts and rolling state schemas are deferred to appendix G (not reviewed here), but their quality will critically affect the quality of the OT/OA labels and therefore the axes. The choice of GPT-5.4 as the judge (a cutting-edge, potentially expensive model) may limit reproducibility for researchers with constrained budgets, though the cross-judge robustness check partially mitigates this concern.

Significance: Practically significant for the coding agent community: the +5–6 pp gains on SWE-bench Verified are meaningful on a benchmark that is competitive and extensively studied, and the 26% step reduction has real inference cost implications. The training-free nature makes deployment straightforward for practitioners with open-weight model access.

Red flags:

  • The method is inherently benchmark-coupled: axes are extracted from trajectories on the same distribution as the test set (with a train/val/test split, but all from SWE-bench Verified). Generalization to genuinely out-of-distribution tasks is not demonstrated.
  • The paper evaluates only two model families. Both are open-weight reasoning models; it is unclear whether the drift axes transfer across architectures or whether they must be re-extracted per model.
  • The claim that TACT works with "no prompt changes" is accurate, but the axis-extraction phase still requires a batch of labeled trajectories, which involves running the agent, labeling with GPT-5.4, and extracting axes — a non-trivial offline cost that is not fully accounted for in the efficiency comparison.
  • Overacting is harder to detect than overthinking (OA-vs-CAL AUC peaks at 0.77 vs. 0.91 for OT), and the orthogonalization places OT as primary; this asymmetry may limit OA correction more than the results suggest.

Strengths

  • Clean formalization of two distinct failure modes with measurable drift ratios and empirical correlation to task outcome.
  • Lightweight test-time intervention: constant-time hook with no LLM calls, no extra tokens, no rollout retries.
  • Comprehensive ablations that convincingly isolate the contribution of each design choice.
  • Human reliability study is thorough and reported transparently, including per-class confusion and OT↔OA cross-labeling rates.
  • Cross-judge consistency check (GPT-5.4 vs. Claude Opus 4.6 vs. Kimi K2.6 vs. Grok-4.1-fast) significantly strengthens the claim that the axis is a model property, not a judge artifact.
  • Dual-axis orthogonalization is a principled design choice — not just a heuristic — with the ablation confirming it contributes +0.8 pp vs. non-orthogonalized dual-axis steering.

---

Limitations

  • Open-weight only: Requires direct access to transformer hidden states; closed-source models (GPT-5, Claude Opus) are out of scope.
  • Domain specificity: Axes are fitted on coding trajectories; transfer to non-coding agent domains (web navigation, tool-using assistants) is explicitly noted as future work and not demonstrated.
  • Linear representation assumption: The entire approach relies on drift being linearly separable in the residual stream. More complex or compositional drift patterns may not be well-captured by a single contrastive direction per layer, and the paper acknowledges sparse autoencoders as a natural next step.
  • Offline axis extraction cost: Requires a batch of completed agent rollouts, LLM-as-judge labeling (with a strong judge like GPT-5.4), and a forward pass over labeled data to compute axes. This is a one-time cost but is non-trivial and may need to be repeated for significantly different task distributions.
  • Limited model diversity: Only tested on two model families (Qwen 3.5 and Gemma 4); transferability across architectures with different hidden dimensions, layer counts, or training regimes is unknown.
  • Calibrated class imbalance: The calibrated class accounts for ~58% of labeled steps vs. ~10% OT and ~26% OA; class imbalance in label distribution may affect axis quality, though the per-problem balanced mean difference partially addresses this.

Relevance

对于关注 LLM 智能体可靠性和推理效率的研究者而言，本文提供了一种无需微调、可在开源推理模型上即插即用的干预框架，尤其适合研究如何通过表示工程（representation engineering）提升代码智能体在长轨迹任务中的鲁棒性，其双轴激活引导思路也可启发对其他类型智能体漂移的探索。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Axis transfer across models and tasks: Do drift axes extracted from Qwen3.5-27B coding trajectories transfer to Qwen3.5-7B or to a different model family like Llama-4? What is the minimum number of labeled trajectories needed to extract axes with sufficient AUC for effective steering?
  1. Beyond linear representation: Sparse autoencoders have been shown to extract more interpretable features than mean-difference vectors. Would SAE-based drift features yield higher AUC and stronger steering gains, or does the per-problem balanced mean difference already saturate the available linear signal?
  1. Non-coding agent domains: The paper explicitly limits evaluation to coding benchmarks. How do overthinking and overacting manifest in web agent tasks (e.g., WebArena) or tool-using chat assistants, where the action space and trajectory structure are quite different?
  1. Online axis adaptation: Axes are currently fixed offline. Could an online variant update the calibrated centroid and drift axes during a trajectory using the agent's own recent steps, improving calibration for novel task types encountered at test time?
  1. Interaction with thinking budget and test-time scaling: Reasoning models are often deployed with extended thinking budgets. How does TACT interact with varying thinking lengths — does reducing overthinking in early layers conflict with or complement inference-time scaling strategies like majority voting or best-of-N?
  1. Failure mode specificity of the judge: The OT↔OA confusion rate is 5–6% in the human study. For tasks where both failure modes co-occur within a single step, how should the label be assigned, and does mixed labeling degrade axis purity in practice?
