Towards Annotation-Free Validation of MLLMs: A Vision-Language Logical Consistency Metric

Ying Gu, Mei Chee Leong, Hui Li Tan, Shangbo Mao, Liyuan Li, Nancy Chen

arXiv: 2605.06201

Date: 2026-05-07

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06201

一句话总结： 本文提出 VL-LCM（视觉语言逻辑一致性度量），通过在多选 VQA 与逐项是/否 VQA 之间的充分-必要逻辑关系上联合测试，无需真值标注即可评估 MLLM 的可靠性，实验揭示当前前沿模型准确率虽高但逻辑一致性普遍落后 20-40 个百分点。
TL;DR: This paper introduces VL-LCM, a reference-free metric that probes multimodal LLMs for logical consistency by jointly testing each VQA instance under both sufficient and necessary cause-effect conditions (MC-format vs. per-choice Yes/No). Applied to 11 open-source MLLMs (7–8B) across five challenging benchmarks (MMMU, NegBench, ConBench, NaturalBench, and the newly constructed NatConBench), the metric reveals a systematic gap: top models achieve 80–95% accuracy yet score only 40–70% on VL-LCM, strongly correlating with ground-truth F1 (Pearson r > 0.92). VL-LCM can be used without any ground-truth annotation for model ranking, selection, and per-sample answer justification. If you care about whether high benchmark accuracy truly reflects reliable vision-language understanding, this paper is worth reading.

核心贡献

  • 提出 VL-LCM 框架：基于形式逻辑充分-必要因果关系，定义了无需真值标注的视觉语言逻辑一致性度量，同时适用于传统 MC-VQA（MMMU/ConBench）和 NaturalBench 格式。
  • 构建新基准 NatConBench：将 ConBench 的19类多样能力测评与 NaturalBench 的视觉对抗性格式结合，自动生成1850个测试样本，提供更严格的逻辑一致性评估。
  • 系统性实验：在5个基准上对4个主流开源家族（InternVL、Qwen-VL、LLaVA、Gemma）的11个7–8B 模型进行全面评估，揭示当前 MLLM 逻辑一致性与准确率之间的显著鸿沟。
  • 实用化验证：证明 VL-LCM 与带真值的 F1 指标强相关（Pearson r > 0.92），并展示其在无标注场景下的模型排名、答案可靠性筛选等实际应用价值。

Key Contributions

  • VL-LCM metric: A principled, annotation-free evaluation framework grounded in classical cause-effect logic. For MC-VQA, the metric combines the MC prediction probability with joint Yes/No (sufficient + necessary) probabilities via a geometric mean. For NaturalBench, four MC sub-tests are derived from alternating image-question pairs and averaged to a per-sample score.
  • NatConBench dataset: A new 1,850-sample benchmark automatically assembled from ConBench samples reorganized into the NaturalBench adversarial pairing protocol, covering 19 categories across Sensation, Cognition, and Knowledge.
  • Empirical gap finding: Across all five benchmarks, VL-LCM scores are consistently 20–40 percentage points below the corresponding accuracy rates even for state-of-the-art models, exposing a hidden reliability deficit invisible to accuracy-only metrics.
  • Annotation-free model selection: Per-sample VL-LCM > 0.3 identifies reliable answers with > 80% precision, enabling practical deployment of MLLMs in novel tasks without ground-truth labels.
  • Response distribution insight: High VL-LCM correlates with low "Overconfidence" (multiple Yes/No choices confirmed simultaneously) and high "Confidence" (exactly one choice confirmed), linking the metric to hallucination risk.

Methodology

The paper builds on the logic of cause-effect relations: if a VQA instance (V, T) → a holds, a reliable model should also satisfy the necessary conditions (¬V, T) → ¬a and (V, ¬T) → ¬a.

VL-LCM on MC-VQA (e.g., MMMU, ConBench, NegBench):

For a K-choice MC sample, the model is run twice per choice: once in the standard MC format (producing P_MC(a_k)), and once for each choice independently as a Yes/No question (producing P_YN(a_k) and P_YN(¬a_k) for necessary conditions). The joint Yes/No probability is:

P_JYN(a_k) = [P_suf(a_k) × P_nec(a_k)]^{1/2}

where P_suf is the Yes probability for the correct choice and P_nec is the minimum "No" probability across all other choices. The final per-sample VL-LCM is:

P_LC = max_k [P_MC(a_k) × P_JYN(a_k)]^{1/2}

This geometric mean rewards models that are simultaneously confident on the MC task and consistent on the YN decompositions.

VL-LCM on NaturalBench format:

Each test unit has two visually similar images (V1, V2) and two semantically similar questions (T1, T2) with alternating correct answers. Four binary MC sub-tests are constructed: (a) V1 + {T1, T2}, (b) V2 + {T1, T2}, (c) T1 + {V1, V2}, (d) T2 + {V1, V2}. Each sub-test combines the MC prediction with the aligned YN tests via a fourth-root geometric mean, and the four sub-scores are averaged for the final P_LC.

Benchmarks used: NegBench (COCO/VOC2007), ConBench, MMMU (val set), NaturalBench, NatConBench.

Models evaluated: InternVL-2.0/2.5/3.0/3.5-8B, Qwen-VL-7B-Chat, Qwen2.0/2.5-VL-7B, Qwen3.0-VL-8B, LLaVA-1.5/1.6-13B, Gemma-3.0-12B.

Computational cost: Adding Yes/No tests increases inference time roughly 4–7× for simple tasks and up to 60× for complex ones (MMMU with Gemma), which is a non-trivial overhead.

Results & Highlights

  • Accuracy vs. consistency gap: On NegBench-COCO, InternVL-3.0 achieves 93.2% accuracy but only 56.4% VL-LCM. On MMMU, InternVL-3.5 leads accuracy at 57.7% but VL-LCM is only 34.0%, while Qwen3.0-VL tops VL-LCM at 40.1%.
  • Strong correlation with ground-truth metrics: VL-LCM correlates with F1 (MC + joint YN accuracy) at Pearson r = 0.96 (NegBench-VOC), 0.92 (ConBench), 0.92 (MMMU), and 0.93 (NaturalBench), validating its use as a proxy for accuracy+reliability without labels.
  • Ranking stability: Spearman's ρ between VL-LCM ranking and F1 ranking ranges from 0.73 to 0.95, indicating robust model ordering.
  • Per-sample reliability: Among samples with VL-LCM > 0.3, over 80% match the ground-truth answer, enabling reliable answer selection in zero-annotation settings.
  • NatConBench pattern: Qwen2.0-VL-7B is the top model (LCM 0.504) despite ranking only 5th on NaturalBench (LCM 0.387), showing that benchmark format significantly affects perceived model ranking.
  • MMMU test-set generalization (Appendix): LCM scores on the hidden MMMU test set closely track validation set scores, suggesting that annotation-free ranking generalizes beyond seen splits.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The idea of testing models on both sufficient and necessary conditions is conceptually elegant and well-grounded in classical logic. However, the basic insight — that a reliable model should answer "no" for a variant when it answers "yes" for the original — is similar in spirit to prior self-consistency and adversarial-pairing work (ConBench, NaturalBench, Khan & Fu 2024). The key advance is the formal unification into a single probability-based score applicable without ground truth, which is genuinely useful. The claim of annotation-free validation is the strongest novel element.

Rigor: The mathematical formulation is clean and internally consistent. The use of three correlation coefficients (Pearson, Spearman, Kendall) is appropriate. The authors honestly report that VL-LCM is weakly correlated with raw accuracy (by design) and strongly correlated with F1, which is the correct framing. Limitations are acknowledged clearly (binary logic may not capture natural language nuance, consistent answers may still be wrong if the model has systematic biases, additional compute cost, open-ended answers are not handled).

Reproducibility: All benchmarks are public except NatConBench, whose construction procedure is described in detail in Appendix B. Prompting strategies are documented in Appendix C. Model identifiers are specified. However, no code repository is linked, which makes exact replication somewhat dependent on re-implementing the YN prompt adaptations.

Significance: The finding that high-accuracy MLLMs have substantially lower logical consistency is practically important for deployment decisions. The annotation-free framing is particularly valuable for evaluating models on new domain-specific tasks where labels are unavailable. However, the focus on 7–8B open-source models means conclusions may not directly transfer to proprietary frontier models (GPT-4o, Gemini 1.5 Pro, Claude 3).

Red flags:

  • The paper cites "Kalai et al. (2026)" (a Nature paper) and "Asadi et al. (2026)" — both are future-dated relative to the nominal submission year of 2025, suggesting the paper was written or revised in 2026. The internal consistency of dates is questionable.
  • Geometric mean as the combination rule for P_LC is motivated by analogy to prior work (Malinin & Gales 2020) but is not formally justified as optimal; it could attenuate or amplify inconsistency signals non-uniformly.
  • The comparison baseline is accuracy-only; the paper does not compare against other annotation-free consistency metrics (e.g., LLM-as-judge approaches) to establish relative merit.

Strengths

  • Principled, self-contained metric with a clear logical foundation and no external judge models required.
  • Thorough evaluation: 11 models across 4 families, 5 benchmarks, 3 correlation statistics, per-category ablations, and a timing appendix.
  • The annotation-free property is genuinely useful for real-world deployment in novel tasks.
  • Introduces NatConBench, a reusable resource that combines the difficulty of ConBench with the adversarial structure of NaturalBench.
  • The per-sample nature of VL-LCM (rather than a dataset-level statistic) enables online validation and answer selection, expanding practical utility.

---

Limitations

  • Binary logic assumption: Natural language VQA answers are not strictly binary; modeling them as yes/no may oversimplify complex reasoning.
  • Systematic bias risk: If a model has a consistent but wrong bias (e.g., always selecting the same visual cue), VL-LCM can be high even when the model is factually wrong.
  • Computational overhead: YN test inference adds 4–60× wall-clock time depending on model and benchmark, which is prohibitive for large-scale evaluation.
  • Open-ended answers not supported: The metric is defined only for selective (MC or Yes/No) VQA, excluding generation-based tasks.
  • Only open-source models evaluated: No closed-source frontier models (GPT-4o, Gemini, Claude) are included, leaving open the question of whether the accuracy-consistency gap persists for the strongest current models.
  • No code release mentioned: Reproducibility depends on the reader implementing the YN prompt adaptations from Appendix C.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does the accuracy-vs-consistency gap persist for proprietary frontier models (GPT-4o, Gemini 1.5 Pro, Claude 3.5 Sonnet)? The paper focuses exclusively on open-source 7–8B models.
  2. Can VL-LCM be extended to open-ended generation tasks (e.g., captioning or VQA with free-form answers) through a rephrasing strategy, and would it maintain its correlation with ground-truth quality metrics?
  3. The paper shows that high VL-LCM correlates with low Overconfidence. Could VL-LCM be used as a training signal (reward or regularization) to improve MLLM reliability, and would that improve downstream task performance?
  4. How sensitive is VL-LCM to the specific prompt template used for the Yes/No tests? The authors adapt official MC prompts, but different YN prompt designs might yield different consistency scores.
  5. The geometric mean combination rule is adopted from uncertainty estimation literature; would other aggregation strategies (e.g., harmonic mean, minimum, or learned combination) yield better correlation with ground-truth reliability?
