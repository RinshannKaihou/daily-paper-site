> **一句话总结：** 本文揭示LLM摘要系统导出的向量表示（最终token隐藏状态和均值池化表示）可泄露输入中的敏感属性（如患者种族），并提出SurfaceLoRA——一种基于梯度反转的LoRA微调方法，能将目标向量中的种族可恢复性降至接近随机水平同时保持摘要质量，但未被针对性处理的其他向量仍存在显著隐私泄露风险。

> **TL;DR:** This paper shows that vector representations exported from LLM summarization systems (final-token hidden states and mean-pooled prompt representations) can leak sensitive attributes like patient race from clinical discharge summaries. Using Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct on MIMIC-IV-Ext-BHC (270K clinical notes), the authors demonstrate that reducing race recoverability from one exported artifact (lasttok) does NOT reduce it from another (meanpool). Their proposed SurfaceLoRA uses a gradient-reversal discriminator on a designated exported vector during LoRA fine-tuning, driving race probing accuracy on the targeted lasttok artifact to near chance (0.203, chance=0.20) while preserving ROUGE-L of 14.54, yet meanpool accuracy remains ~0.32. The key takeaway: privacy auditing must target the exact vector artifact exposed downstream.

## 核心贡献

- 首次将LLM摘要系统导出的向量表示（而非生成文本）定义为敏感信息泄露的具体审计面
- 发现一种关键的不对称性：降低最终token隐藏状态（lasttok）中敏感属性的可恢复性，不意味着均值池化表示（meanpool）中的可恢复性也会降低——两种伪影的泄露是独立的
- 提出SurfaceLoRA：将梯度反转层（GRL）判别器附加到指定导出向量上的LoRA微调方法，实现针对性隐私擦除
- 在五类种族标签的平衡探测协议下，将目标lasttok伪影的种族可恢复性降至接近随机水平（Acc=0.203, 95% CI包含0.20），同时保持摘要质量（ROUGE-L=14.54）
- 揭示隐私-效用权衡的非单调性：训练过程中泄露可能先降后升，因此必须使用验证集选择检查点而非默认取最终步
- 强调提示工程（如"demographic-neutral"指令）和事后线性变换（PCA removal）均不足以消除敏感信息泄露

## Key Contributions

- Frames exported LLM summarization vectors (not generated text) as concrete, auditable privacy surfaces for sensitive-information inference
- Demonstrates artifact-specific leakage asymmetry: near-chance recovery from lasttok coexists with substantial recovery from meanpool (~0.32 accuracy vs. 0.20 chance)
- Introduces SurfaceLoRA: an exported-vector-targeted PEFT method combining LoRA adapters with a gradient-reversal discriminator on a designated prompt representation
- Achieves chance-level race probing on targeted lasttok_L-1 (LR Acc=0.203, MLP Acc=0.206, both 95% CIs include 0.20) while preserving summarization utility (ROUGE-L=14.54, above prompt-only baselines at 12.47–12.87)
- Reveals non-monotonic leakage dynamics: for λ=0.02, MLP probe accuracy drops to 0.206 at step 1200 but rebounds to 0.228 at step 2000, motivating validation-based checkpoint selection
- Shows that prompt-level neutrality instructions (NEUTRAL baseline) and post-hoc linear transforms (PCA removal) are insufficient — PCA reduces LR gap to 0.005 but MLP remains above chance (0.212)

## Methodology

**Task & Data:** Brief Hospital Course (BHC) generation from MIMIC-IV-Ext-BHC v1.2.0, a clinical summarization corpus with 270,033 note-BHC pairs from deidentified MIMIC-IV EHR data. EHR-recorded race labels mapped to five groups (WHITE, BLACK, HISPANIC, ASIAN, OTHER). Race-balanced subsets: bal_train (20K), bal_val (2.5K), test_balanced (2.5K). Chance accuracy = 0.20.

**Base Model:** Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, fine-tuned with LoRA adapters. Input prompt truncated to 1,024 tokens; full training sequence to 1,536 tokens. Greedy decoding with max_new_tokens=256.

**Audited Artifacts:**
- **lasttok_L-1**: Final prompt-token hidden state from the last transformer block (compact prompt-level vector at generation boundary)
- **meanpool_L-1**: Mean of all non-padding prompt-token hidden states from the last block (resembles retrieval/analytics embeddings)

**SurfaceLoRA Training:** At each step, combines (A) an SFT batch for summary generation (cross-entropy on BHC targets) with (B) a prompt-only balanced batch for adversarial sensitive-label prediction. A gradient reversal layer (GRL) reverses gradients from the adversarial loss flowing into LoRA adapters. The training objective is: min_θ L_util(θ) − λ·L_adv(θ, φ). λ swept over {0.0, 0.02, 0.05, 0.10, 0.20, 0.50}, trained for 2,000 steps with AdamW (lr=2×10⁻⁴).

**Evaluation:** Post-hoc probes (logistic regression + 2-layer MLP) trained on frozen exported vectors, separate from the training-time discriminator. Checkpoint selection uses a pre-specified validation rule: maximize validation ROUGE-L subject to LeakageGap_LR ≤ 0.025 on the same exported artifact.

**Baselines:** BASE prompt (standard summarization instruction), NEUTRAL prompt (adds demographic-neutrality directive), plus post-hoc transforms (PCA removal, XCov decorrelation).

## Results & Highlights

- **SurfaceLoRA (λ=0.02, step 1200, validation-selected):** LR probe Acc=0.203 [0.188, 0.219], MLP Acc=0.206 [0.191, 0.222] — both 95% CIs include chance (0.20). ROUGE-L=14.54 [14.19, 14.86], substantially above both prompt-only baselines (12.47–12.87)
- **Artifact asymmetry is stark:** While lasttok variants are near chance (0.203–0.208), meanpool remains highly predictive (LR 0.316–0.323, MLP 0.270–0.326) — a 12+ percentage point gap above chance
- **Meanpool-targeted stress test:** Even with an MLP discriminator targeting meanpool, the lowest-leakage validation checkpoint remains far above chance (LR=0.308, Gap=0.108)
- **Non-monotonic trade-off:** At λ=0.02, MLP probe accuracy on lasttok drops to 0.206 at step 1200 but rebounds to 0.228 at step 2000 — adversarial pressure temporarily suppresses signal, but later optimization recovers it
- **Prompt engineering fails:** Both BASE and NEUTRAL baselines remain above chance (LR Acc 0.234–0.236); NEUTRAL additionally causes 46.44% meta-term contamination (generating terms like "race" or "racial identity")
- **Post-hoc transforms are insufficient:** PCA removal achieves smallest LR gap (Acc=0.2048, Gap=0.0048) but MLP probe remains above chance (0.212); XCov decorrelation actually *increases* recoverability
- **Output-level leakage persists:** Even with SurfaceLoRA, generated summaries remain above chance under Bio_ClinicalBERT output attacker (Acc=0.299, Macro-AUROC=0.616), though modestly improved over baselines (0.306/0.631)
- **Cross-model validation:** Results confirmed on Qwen-2.5-7B-Instruct (Appendix P)
- **Binary gender audit:** Additional stress test with EHR-recorded gender shows analogous patterns (Appendix M)

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** The GRL+LoRA combination is not novel in isolation — gradient reversal for adversarial fairness dates to Ganin et al. (2016), and LoRA is standard PEFT. The paper's novelty lies in the *artifact-specific deployment framing*: the insight that different exported vectors from the same model have independent leakage profiles, and that mitigation must match the exact vector exposed. This is a genuine systems-level contribution. The non-monotonic trade-off finding (signal rebounds with continued training) is also practically important and somewhat surprising.
- **Rigor:** Strong experimental design: balanced five-way probing with patient-level stratified bootstrap CIs, separate validation/test splits with pre-registered checkpoint selection rules, multiple probe classes (linear + MLP), output-level attackers as complements, and cross-model validation (Qwen-2.5-7B). The statistical reporting with 95% CIs on all metrics is commendable.
- **Reproducibility:** Code promised upon publication. MIMIC data requires credentialed access but is widely available. Deterministic split seeds and config files will be released. Clinical text cannot be shared due to DUA, which is standard. Overall reproducibility should be good.
- **Significance:** High practical relevance for healthcare AI deployments where LLM-generated summaries feed into RAG, monitoring, or analytics pipelines. The finding that "auditing one vector ≠ auditing all vectors" challenges common assumptions in the privacy literature. Relevant to GDPR and HIPAA compliance discussions.
- **Red flags:** (1) Single sensitive attribute (race) as primary audit, with gender only in appendix — multi-attribute auditing remains untested. (2) Meanpool remains stubbornly leaky (~0.32), and the paper offers no solution beyond "target that artifact too." (3) Output-level race recoverability is still 0.299 (vs. 0.20 chance), suggesting the clinical text itself contains racial correlates that representation-level mitigation cannot address. (4) The paper does not compare against differential privacy baselines. (5) Probe-based evaluation does not guarantee protection against all possible attackers or information-theoretic leakage.

## Strengths

- Clean, well-controlled experimental design with balanced subsets, patient-level splits, and pre-registered selection rules
- The artifact-specific asymmetry finding is counterintuitive, important, and well-documented
- Comprehensive evaluation: multiple artifacts, multiple probe classes, output attackers, cross-model validation, gender stress test
- Honest about limitations — explicitly states SurfaceLoRA is not a universal sanitizer
- Non-monotonic trade-off analysis provides actionable guidance for deployment
- Strong statistical reporting with bootstrap CIs throughout

## Limitations

- Only one primary sensitive attribute (race) systematically tested; multi-attribute auditing is an open problem
- Meanpool artifacts remain substantially leaky with no demonstrated solution
- Output-level sensitive-attribute invariance is not achieved (Acc=0.299 vs. chance=0.20)
- Limited to one domain (clinical BHC summarization) and two model families (Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen-2.5-7B)
- Probe-based evaluation does not provide information-theoretic guarantees against all adversaries
- EHR-recorded race is an administrative category, not a biological variable — generalization to other sensitive attributes requires separate audits
- No comparison with differential privacy mechanisms
- The "deploy the exact artifact you audited" recommendation, while sound, may be difficult to enforce in complex real-world systems with multiple downstream consumers

## Relevance

本文与"LLM Hidden States & Interpretability"方向高度相关——直接研究隐藏状态和池化表示中编码的敏感属性信息，通过探测方法量化信息泄露。同时触及ML系统可靠性和安全性问题，以及训练稳定性和检查点选择的重要性。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can SurfaceLoRA be extended to simultaneously target multiple exported artifacts (lasttok + meanpool) without degrading summarization utility?
- How does the non-monotonic leakage dynamic interact with different LoRA ranks and adapter configurations?
- Would differential privacy (DP-SGD) during fine-tuning provide stronger formal guarantees than adversarial training for this problem?
- How do findings generalize to non-clinical summarization domains where sensitive attributes are less correlated with input text?
- Can information-theoretic measures (e.g., mutual information between representations and sensitive attributes) complement probe-based auditing for stronger privacy guarantees?
- What happens when multiple sensitive attributes (race, age, gender, insurance) must be protected simultaneously — does adversarial pressure on one attribute increase leakage on another?
