Dense2MoE: Pushing the Pareto Frontier of On-Device LLMs via Unified Pruning & Upcycling

Fengfa Li, Hongjin Ji, Yifeng Ding, Lei Ren, Chen Wei Li Auto · CUHK-Shenzhen 2026-05-26
Key Finding

Dense2MoE unifies layer pruning with MoE upcycling, achieving +9.78 pp accuracy gain while cutting latency 10.6% on-device.

Key Metric
+9.78 pp
47.31 vs 37.53 avg · 174.95ms vs 195.87ms · Qwen2.5-0.5B
Why This Matters

Enables capable LLMs on edge and automotive hardware without sacrificing accuracy—a practical path to on-device AI.

Critical Edge

Primary results on 0.5B; gains shrink at 7B scale. Top-1 routing limits MoE expressiveness.

Methodology

1. Inter-layer Similarity Identifies redundant adjacent layers via dual-constraint metric: cosine similarity of decoder outputs + MLP inputs (δ), plus feature norm matching (ε). Greedy algorithm partitions into retained / pruned sets.
2. Layer-Fusion UpCycling Prunes attention modules from redundant layers (bandwidth bottleneck). Preserves MLPs as heterogeneous MoE experts in the retained base block. Dynamic Top-1 router dispatches tokens; residual paths allow expert bypass.
3. Continual Pre-Training 225B tokens optimizes router and aligns fused experts. Less than 1% of from-scratch training cost. Depth chosen via hardware-aware reward grounded in Roofline theory (NVIDIA Jetson Thor-U).

Optimal pruning depth: Rewardi = Si × (LaTi / LaTbase)w — balancing benchmark scores against Roofline-derived theoretical latency.

Strengths

  • Principled integration of pruning and upcycling avoids destructive pure-pruning side effects.
  • Heterogeneous expert init is a simple, effective solution to Winner-Takes-All routing collapse.
  • Hardware-aware depth selection grounded in Roofline theory gives practical deployment guidance.
  • Strong empirical gains across multiple benchmarks and model families.
  • Thorough ablations on expert count, similarity constraints, and training dynamics.

Limitations

  • Primary validation on 0.5B; larger models show more modest relative improvements.
  • Theoretical latency may not capture real-world kernel optimization overhead.
  • 225B-token continual pre-training is still a non-trivial computational cost.
  • Top-1 routing limits MoE expressive power; higher Top-k values unexplored.
  • Assumes inter-layer redundancy exists, which may not hold for all architectures.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does Dense2MoE perform with Top-k > 1 routing—would multi-expert activation improve the capacity-efficiency trade-off?
  2. Can LF-UC be combined with quantization (INT4/INT8) for further memory reduction on edge devices?
  3. How sensitive is the inter-layer similarity analysis to calibration dataset choice, and does it generalize across domains?
  4. What happens when applying Dense2MoE to models already designed with efficiency in mind (e.g., MoE-to-MoE conversion)?
  5. How do theoretical Roofline latency predictions compare with actual measured latency once optimized MoE kernels are available?
