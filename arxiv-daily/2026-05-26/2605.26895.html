Negligible in Size, Significant in Effect: On Scale Vectors in Large Language Models

Wang, Zhu, Fang, Li, Shen, Zhong 2026-05-26 arXiv 2605.26895
Scale vectors are negligible in parameters but critical for LLM pre-training via a self-amplifying preconditioning effect.
0.12B–2B
Parameters tested with consistent loss reduction across dense & MoE models.
Lightweight improvements to an overlooked component can improve every LLM's pre-training with near-zero overhead.
Gains verified only up to 2B params; frontier-scale (70B+) remains unproven.

⚙ Methodology

The paper combines theoretical analysis with large-scale empirical validation. It analyzes scale vectors through expressivity and optimization in Pre-Norm transformers, deriving conditions under which weight decay helps or hurts. Three lightweight improvements are proposed — branch-specific heterogeneity, improved placement around linear mappings, and magnitude-direction reparameterization — each motivated by theoretical insights. Experiments span dense and MoE models from 0.12B to 2B parameters, multiple optimizers, varied learning rate schedules, and industrial-scale token budgets.

★ Results & Highlights

  • Removing scale vectors substantially degrades LLM pre-training despite their negligible parameter fraction
  • Each of the three proposed improvements yields consistent individual gains
  • Unified strategy achieves lower terminal loss than well-tuned baselines across all tested configurations
  • More favorable scaling behavior with negligible parameter and computational overhead
  • Results hold across both dense and mixture-of-experts architectures

◆ Key Contributions

  • First systematic study of scale vectors from expressivity, optimization, and architecture perspectives
  • Theoretical proof: scale vectors improve optimization via self-amplifying preconditioning, not expressivity
  • Weight decay is beneficial for Input-Norm but harmful for Output-Norm layers
  • Three complementary improvements unified into a single scale-vector strategy
  • Validated on dense and MoE models from 0.12B to 2B under industrial-scale token budgets

✓ Strengths

  • First systematic study of an overlooked but ubiquitous architectural component
  • Clean theoretical framework explaining why scale vectors matter
  • Practical, lightweight improvements with minimal overhead
  • Extensive validation across architectures and training setups
  • Clear distinction between Input-Norm and Output-Norm with actionable training recommendations

✗ Limitations

  • Maximum model size is 2B parameters; scaling to frontier-size models is unverified
  • Theoretical analysis focuses on Pre-Norm; Post-Norm or other normalization variants not deeply studied
  • Interaction with other recent training techniques (new optimizers, data mixtures) is unexplored
  • Industrial-scale token budgets may mask differences at smaller data regimes

? Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Do the proposed scale-vector improvements transfer to models at the 7B–70B+ scale with similar relative gains?
  2. How do the improvements interact with normalization-free or alternative normalization architectures?
  3. Can the magnitude-direction reparameterization insight be applied to other learnable parameters (e.g., attention head scaling)?
  4. What is the interaction between the proposed strategy and quantization/pruning at inference time?
