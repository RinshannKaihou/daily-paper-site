> **一句话总结：** MONA在Muon优化器的正交化流程前加入基于梯度差EMA的Nesterov加速项（$A_k = \beta_a A_{k-1} + (1-\beta_a)(G_k - G_{k-1})$），使更新方向具备曲率感知能力，从而逃离尖锐极小值；在1B到68B参数的MoE预训练中收敛速度和下游性能均优于Muon和AdamW，68B模型SFT后在BigCode等基准上达到SOTA，且额外开销仅约1个梯度的内存（MONA-Lite可削减75%）。

> **TL;DR:** MONA augments Muon's gradient with an EMA-of-gradient-differences acceleration term *before* orthogonalization, giving the optimizer curvature awareness that helps escape sharp minima while preserving Muon's spectral-norm regularization. Across three MoE scales (1B–68B, up to 1T tokens), MONA consistently achieves lower validation loss than Muon and AdamW on code, math, and general text. On the 68B-A3B model, MONA averages 0.4557 on general benchmarks vs. Muon's 0.4478 and AdamW's 0.4382. After SFT, MONA wins 6/7 BigCode tasks over Muon. Memory overhead is ~1 extra gradient buffer (MONA-Lite cuts this by 75% via BF16 + streaming), and wall-clock time is essentially identical to Muon.

## 核心贡献

- **将Nesterov型加速融入Muon正交化流程**：在momentum累积之前，将梯度替换为 $\tilde{G}_k = G_k + \alpha A_k$（$A_k$为梯度差的EMA），使正交化处理的输入携带曲率信息，从而逃离尖锐极小值、收敛到更平坦解。
- **完整的非凸收敛分析**：在L-平滑、有界方差、有界梯度和方向对齐假设下，证明MONA的收敛率为 $O(K^{-1/2})$，与SGD/AdamW一致；Proposition 1形式化证明了加速项通过Hessian依赖的缩放选择性抵抗尖锐极小值。
- **三规模MoE实证验证**：MOE-1B-A0.2B（400B tokens）、MOE-6B-A0.5B（1.2T tokens）、MOE-68B-A3B（700B tokens）上，MONA在代码、数学推理和通用文本的验证损失均低于Muon和AdamW。
- **SFT达到SOTA**：68B模型经代码SFT后，MONA在BigCode 7项任务中6项优于Muon（HumanEval+ 88.41% vs 87.20%, MBPP+ 78.57% vs 77.78%）。
- **MONA-Lite高效部署**：BF16量化 + 流式梯度计算将额外内存开销削减约75%，训练质量无损失，端到端迭代时间与Muon基本一致。

## Key Contributions

- Integrates a Nesterov-style acceleration term ($A_k = \beta_a A_{k-1} + (1-\beta_a)(G_k - G_{k-1})$) into Muon's pipeline *before* momentum accumulation and orthogonalization, preserving Muon's geometric structure while adding curvature awareness.
- Proves non-convex convergence at $O(K^{-1/2})$ rate under standard assumptions (L-smoothness, bounded variance, bounded gradient, directional alignment), and formalizes sharp-minimum escape via Hessian-dependent scaling of the acceleration term.
- Demonstrates consistent validation loss improvements over Muon and AdamW across three MoE scales (1B/6B/68B parameters), with the largest model trained on 1T tokens.
- After supervised fine-tuning on the 68B-A3B model with code data, MONA achieves SOTA on BigCode benchmarks (HumanEval+ 88.41%, MBPP+ 78.57%), winning 6/7 tasks vs. Muon.
- Introduces MONA-Lite (BF16 quantization + streaming gradient computation) that reduces extra memory overhead by ~75% with no training quality degradation and essentially zero wall-clock overhead.

## Methodology

MONA modifies the Muon optimizer by injecting curvature-aware acceleration before the orthogonalization step. The core algorithm:

1. Compute stochastic gradient $G_k = \nabla_W \ell(W_k, \zeta_k)$.
2. Compute gradient difference $D_k = G_k - G_{k-1}$.
3. Update acceleration buffer: $A_k = \beta_a A_{k-1} + (1-\beta_a) D_k$.
4. Form curvature-aware gradient: $\tilde{G}_k = G_k + \alpha A_k$.
5. Update momentum: $M_k = \mu M_{k-1} + \tilde{G}_k$.
6. Apply Newton-Schulz orthogonalization: $O_k = \text{NS}(M_k, T)$.
7. Update weights: $W_{k+1} = W_k - \eta(\gamma O_k + \lambda W_k)$.

The key design choice is applying acceleration *before* momentum and orthogonalization. Applying it after orthogonalization would destroy the orthogonal structure. The theoretical insight is that $g_k - g_{k-1} \approx H_k(\theta_k - \theta_{k-1})$, so the acceleration term implicitly encodes Hessian information, pointing away from sharp minima (since $-\nabla\|\nabla f\|^2 \approx H(\theta - \theta^*)$). The two new hyperparameters $\beta_a$ (acceleration EMA decay) and $\alpha$ (acceleration coefficient) follow a consistent relationship: $\alpha = -1/(2(1-\beta_a))$ across all experiments, with $\beta_a$ ranging from 0.975 (68B) to 0.99 (1B). Vector-valued parameters (embeddings, biases) fall back to AdamW. For memory efficiency, MONA-Lite stores $G_{k-1}$ and $A_k$ in BF16 and uses streaming computation to eliminate the $G_{k-1}$ buffer entirely.

## Results & Highlights

**Pretraining (MOE-68B-A3B, 700B tokens):**
- General capability average: MONA 0.4557 > Muon 0.4478 > AdamW 0.4382.
- Improvements on MMLU (0.6373 vs 0.6281), MMLU-Pro (0.3375 vs 0.3250), CMMLU (0.6756 vs 0.6723), CEVAL (0.6717 vs 0.6586), DROP (0.4868 vs 0.4757).
- Code/Math average: MONA 0.3495 > Muon 0.3397 > AdamW 0.3353.
- Strong gains on BigCodeBench (0.3377 vs 0.3061), LiveCodeBench (0.0502 vs 0.0430), Multiple (0.2848 vs 0.2239).

**Smaller scales:** MONA consistently achieves lower validation loss than Muon across code, math, general English, and Chinese academic text for both 1B and 6B models.

**Supervised Fine-Tuning (68B, code data):**
- MONA-pretrained checkpoint achieves lower SFT training loss than Muon throughout, with the gap widening in later epochs.
- BigCode evaluation: MONA wins 6/7 tasks (HumanEval+ 88.41% vs 87.20%, MBPP+ 78.57% vs 77.78%, DS1000 37.03% vs 33.95%).

**Ablation (AdamW-Acc):** Adding acceleration to AdamW alone also improves over vanilla AdamW, confirming the acceleration term's standalone value. The ordering is MONA > Muon > AdamW-Acc > AdamW.

**Overhead:** Optimizer inner step ~1% slower; end-to-end iteration time indistinguishable from Muon. MONA-Lite (BF16 + streaming) reduces extra memory by ~75% with no quality loss.

Authors are from **Meituan** (Beijing, China). The work builds on the LongCat/ScMoE architecture from the same group. Training a 68B MoE model on 700B+ tokens requires substantial industrial-scale compute, lending strong credibility to the empirical claims. ## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Moderate-to-good. The acceleration idea is adapted from ALTO (Zhao et al., 2026), but the specific integration with Muon's orthogonalization pipeline is non-trivial — applying before vs. after orthogonalization matters. The convergence proof and sharp-minimum escape analysis (Proposition 1) are genuine theoretical contributions. However, the core mechanism (EMA of gradient differences) is not new.
- **Rigor:** Strong. Multi-scale validation (1B/6B/68B), comprehensive benchmarks (9 general + 11 code/math), convergence proof, overhead analysis, and ablation against AdamW-Acc. The consistent hyperparameter rule $\alpha = -1/(2(1-\beta_a))$ across scales is practically useful. One weakness: the paper does not evaluate on dense transformer models, only MoE.
- **Reproducibility:** Moderate. The optimizer itself should be implementable from the algorithm description and pseudocode. However, reproducing the 68B-scale experiments requires industrial compute. The specific model architecture (LongCat/ScMoE) is referenced but not fully described in this paper.
- **Significance:** High if claims generalize. An optimizer that beats both Muon and AdamW at 68B scale with minimal overhead is impactful. The fact that Kimi K2 and DeepSeek V4 already use Muon in production makes MONA a natural upgrade path.
- **Red flags:** (1) The 68B model has 3B active parameters — results may not transfer to dense 68B training. (2) GPQA scores slightly decrease under MONA vs. AdamW (0.2555 vs 0.2743), suggesting the improvement is not uniform. (3) Math benchmark (Hendrycks MATH) underperforms Muon at 700B tokens (0.1594 vs 0.1684), though this reverses after SFT. (4) The consistent $\alpha = -1/(2(1-\beta_a))$ rule is empirically observed but not theoretically motivated — it could be fragile for other architectures or scales.

## Strengths

- Clean, minimal modification to Muon that preserves all its benefits while adding curvature awareness.
- Strong empirical results across three model scales with consistent improvements.
- MONA-Lite makes the approach practical for resource-constrained settings with negligible quality loss.
- Convergence proof provides theoretical grounding; Proposition 1 offers clear geometric intuition.
- Negligible wall-clock overhead (optimizer step ~1% slower, end-to-end identical).

## Limitations

- Evaluated exclusively on MoE architectures; generalization to dense transformers is unverified.
- Two new hyperparameters ($\beta_a$, $\alpha$) add tuning cost, though the empirical rule mitigates this.
- The 68B model's 3B active parameters mean the effective compute is much smaller than 68B dense training.
- GPQA and MATH scores show inconsistent improvements at the 700B-token pretraining checkpoint.
- The acceleration buffer adds ~1 extra gradient's worth of memory even with MONA-Lite.
- The theoretical rate $O(K^{-1/2})$ matches SGD/AdamW but does not show an asymptotic speedup — the gains are in constants and solution quality, not convergence rate.

## Relevance

MONA直接改进了大语言模型的训练优化器，在MoE架构的多个规模上验证了效果。如果你关注LLM训练效率、优化器设计或Muon系列优化器的演进，这篇论文非常值得深入阅读，尤其是在工业级规模训练的背景下。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does MONA perform on dense transformer architectures (e.g., standard LLaMA-style models) compared to MoE?
- Is the empirical rule $\alpha = -1/(2(1-\beta_a))$ robust across different learning rate schedules, batch sizes, or architectures?
- Can MONA's acceleration term be combined with other Muon variants like AdaMuon or ROOT for further gains?
- What happens to the acceleration buffer's magnitude and direction during different phases of training (early vs. late)?
- How does MONA interact with distributed training optimizations like ZeRO or tensor parallelism at truly massive scale (e.g., trillion-parameter models)?
