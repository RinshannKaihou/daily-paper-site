The Labyrinth and the Thread: Rethinking Regularizations in Sequential Knowledge Editing for Large Language Models

Wang, Zhang, Chen, Zhang & Lu ICML 2026 arXiv 2605.26670
knowledge-editing sequential-editing optimization-theory locate-and-edit
Key Finding
Sequential editing stability comes from OTE-SE mathematical equivalence, not from regularization or null-space projection.
Key Metric
2,000sequential edits
SST restored: 0.846 vs 0.000 without alignment
Why This Matters
Common regularization strategies are provably unnecessary, simplifying future LLM editing method design.
Critical Edge
Only FFN locate-and-edit methods; untested beyond 8B params; no multi-hop or compositional edits.
Methodology

A theory-driven empirical approach that reframes sequential knowledge editing as an ordinary least-squares problem, proving that the distinction between one-time and sequential editing vanishes when constraints are properly accumulated.

  1. Failure analysis of null-space projection — a "Memorize-the-Latest" task shows AlphaEdit's null-space projection catastrophically fails (degenerate generation), proving it is not the stabilizing mechanism.
  2. Optimization-theoretic analysis — reveals AlphaEdit implicitly drops K₀K₀ᵀP terms. Reintroducing them restores stability (SST 0.846 vs 0.000).
  3. Unified OLS formulation — all locate-and-edit methods become closed-form least-squares solutions; sequential vs. one-time distinction disappears.
  4. Constructive SE design — the difference between consecutive OTE optima is itself the per-step solution to a well-defined optimization problem.
  5. Experimental validation — GPT-2 XL (1.5B), GPT-J (6B), LLaMA-3 (8B), Qwen2.5 (7B) on CounterFact & ZsRE benchmarks.

Results & Highlights
  • OTE-SE equivalence verified: AlphaEdit (aligned) and MEMIT (aligned) produce nearly identical results under both SE and OTE across all models
  • Null-space projection is non-essential: restoring "neglected" terms fully recovers model capability
  • Post-processing regularization is non-essential: PRUNE and RECT match performance once OTE-aligned
  • GLUE performance preserved across 2,000 edit steps; misaligned methods collapse
  • Ablation hierarchy: Fully Aligned > No Error Correction > Not OTE Aligned
  • Hidden representation shifts caused by OTE misalignment, not regularization
Strengths & Limitations
Strengths
  • Clean, unifying theoretical framework explaining why existing methods work or fail
  • Resolves key misconception: AlphaEdit succeeds via implicit OTE-SE equivalence, not null-space projection
  • Constructive design principle (Remark 3.3) offers a practical recipe for future methods
  • Thorough validation: 4 models, 2 benchmarks, 6 GLUE tasks
Limitations
  • Only FFN-based locate-and-edit; excludes SERAC, GRACE, IKE, MEND
  • Tested up to 8B parameters only — 70B+ scaling unknown
  • FFN key-value assumption may not capture all knowledge storage
  • Conflict resolution formalized but empirically thin
  • No multi-hop or compositional editing scenarios
Questions for Further Exploration
Does OTE-SE equivalence hold for non-FFN targets — attention layers, adapter modules?
Can the framework extend to compositional or multi-hop edits where facts interact?
How does error-correction scale to 70B+ models with thousands of edits?
Can the constructive design principle apply to continual skill learning or preference updating?
