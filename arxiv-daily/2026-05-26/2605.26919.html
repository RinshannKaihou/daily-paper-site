Agile Online Model Selection: Resolving Adaptation Lag via Safeguarded Large Learning Rates

Kei Takemura · Ryuta Matsuno · Keita Sakuma

Post-hoc penalty mechanism enables safeguarded large learning rates, reducing adaptation lag from hundreds of rounds to just a few.

O(log T)
cumulative penalty · near-optimal worst-case dynamic regret

Eliminates the robustness-agility trade-off in online model selection with principled theoretical guarantees.

Per-round overhead from tracking multiple learning-rate candidates; sensitivity to penalty threshold design.

online-learning model-selection non-stationary dynamic-regret learning-rates

Methodology

Builds on optimistic online mirror descent (OMD), but permits learning rates as large as Θ(T) instead of constraining them to O(1). A post-hoc penalty mechanism monitors each round for excessive regret accumulation and dynamically excludes learning rates that misbehave. The algorithm starts aggressive and reins in only what provably fails — rather than being conservative from the start. Theoretical analysis establishes dynamic regret bounds matching near-optimal worst-case guarantees while improving in benign (slowly varying) scenarios.

Results & Highlights

  • Evaluated on synthetic data and 11 real-world datasets
  • Adaptation lag reduced from hundreds of rounds to a few
  • Consistently outperforms all tuning-free baselines
  • Near-optimal worst-case dynamic regret preserved
  • O(log T) cumulative penalty is theoretically tight

Strengths

  • Principled resolution of robustness-agility trade-off
  • Clean theoretical framework with O(log T) penalty bound
  • Comprehensive evaluation across 11 real-world datasets
  • Adaptation lag reduced by orders of magnitude

Limitations

  • Computational overhead per round from tracking multiple candidate learning rates
  • Performance under highly adversarial distribution shifts remains open
  • Sensitivity to penalty threshold hyperparameter design

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does the computational cost of the post-hoc penalty mechanism scale with T and the number of candidate learning rates?
  2. Can the safeguarded large learning rate approach extend to bandit or partial-information feedback settings?
  3. What are the optimal penalty threshold choices, and how sensitive are results to this hyperparameter?
  4. Does the approach extend to non-convex loss landscapes (e.g., neural network models)?
