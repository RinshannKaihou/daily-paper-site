Beyond Trajectory-Level Attribution: Graph-Based Credit Assignment for Agentic RL

GraphGPO · arXiv 2605.26684
Cheng et al., NTU & Alibaba
ICML 2026 RL LLM Agents
GraphGPO unifies rollout trajectories into a state-transition graph for step-level credit, beating GRPO by up to 20 points.
+19.88pp
Sokoban success: 86.98% vs GRPO 67.1%. Overhead: 0.04% of training time.
Critic-free step-level credit assignment at negligible cost — a fundamental improvement for training LLM agents.
Relies on exact state matching; may degrade in continuous or open-ended environments where identical states are rare.

Methodology

1. Graph BuildMerge M rollout trajectories into a directed state-transition graph G=(S,E). Identical states become one node.
2. DistanceRun Dijkstra from goal state. d(s) = min cost to reach ssucc. Unreachable states get d = +∞.
3. AdvantageStep reward RG = rsucc · ωd(s')+c(s,a). Group edges per state; compute relative advantage. Final: A = βGAG + βEAE.

Key insight: ~22% of steps in failed trajectories actually contribute to task progress, while ~65% of steps in successful trajectories are non-progress. GraphGPO separates signal from noise.

Assessment

Strengths

  • Elegant formulation separating step-level credit from trajectory outcomes
  • Negligible overhead (0.04% of training time) — no critic needed
  • Consistent gains across all benchmarks and model sizes
  • Faster convergence, especially when rollout success rate is low
  • Theoretically grounded (monotonicity + variance reduction propositions)
  • Credits good actions in failed trajectories — critical for sparse rewards

Limitations

  • Requires exact state matching; struggles with continuous observations
  • Formal proofs assume deterministic environments
  • Only tested on structured benchmarks; unclear for open-ended tasks
  • Three new hyperparameters: ω, βG, βE
  • Graph scales as M × T — potentially large for very long horizons

Open Questions

  1. How does GraphGPO behave under stochastic transitions where the same (s, a) leads to different next states?
  2. Can approximate state matching (embedding similarity) extend the approach to continuous observation spaces like real web browsing?
  3. How does the method scale to 100+ step horizons where the state-transition graph becomes very large?
  4. What is the interplay between graph-based advantages and reward shaping or dense reward signals?
  5. Can the graph structure be leveraged for planning or data augmentation beyond credit assignment?
