What Makes Chain-of-Thought Work at Probe Time? Local Co-Occurrence Rather Than Global Derivation

Xiang Wang & Wei Wei · Huazhong University of Science and Technology · 2026-05-26

CoT gains arise from short-range local token co-occurrence (n* = 2–3), not sentence-level logical derivation order—even word-shuffled rationales outperform no-rationale baselines by wide margins.

Key Metric
+15.6 pp Word-bag rationale over no-rationale on MMLU-Pro; sentence shuffle costs only 0.8 pp
Why This Matters
If CoT benefits are locally driven, it challenges faithfulness assumptions and opens paths for prompt compression and efficient rationale design.
Critical Edge
Generator–probe setting is artificial—real usage couples generation with reading. LogiQA requires n* ≈ 5–8, suggesting task-dependent limits.

Methodology

A generator–probe framework: a generator model produces a CoT rationale, injected into a (potentially different) probe model's context. Interventions include:

  1. Sentence Shuffle (SS) — reorder rationale sentences; tests whether global logical order matters.
  2. Word Shuffle (WS) — globally shuffle all tokens; destroys compositional structure but preserves token inventory.
  3. N-gram perturbation — partition into contiguous n-token blocks, shuffle blocks, preserve internals. Introduces the gap-recovery statistic GR(n).
  4. Concept compression — reduce each sentence to a 5–15 word phrase retaining core domain terms (~85–90% token removal).
  5. Controls — answer-stripping, answer-value removal, option-letter removal, BM25-retrieved Wikipedia passages, question-stem perturbation, and a tail-sweep experiment.

Evaluated on MMLU-Pro, LogiQA, MedQA-USMLE, GSM8K, MATH500 across Qwen2.5, Qwen3.5, and GPT-4o-mini (0.8B–35B). Significance via two-sided McNemar tests (α = 0.05, n = 1500 per condition).

Results

  • Sentence shuffle ≈ full CoT: destroying derivation order causes only ~0.8 pp accuracy drop on MMLU-Pro (83.1% vs 83.9%).
  • Word bag >> IO baseline: globally shuffled rationales outperform no-rationale by ~15.6 pp on MMLU-Pro (66.5% vs 50.9%).
  • Sharp transition at small n: GR(n) curves show steep rise from n=1 to n=3 with early saturation; n*=2 recovers most WS→CoT gap on MMLU-Pro.
  • Answer stripping negligible: removing answer-declaration sentences produces no measurable degradation.
  • Concept compression ≈ full CoT: reducing rationales to ~10–15% of original length recovers GR ≈ 0.91–0.99 on MMLU-Pro and MedQA.
  • Wikipedia control fails: topic-matched passages produce near-zero or slightly negative gains.
  • Pattern stable across scales: n-gram recovery curve shape consistent from 0.8B to 35B parameters.
  • Open-ended generation consistent: locality effect persists on GSM8K and MATH500 with free-form numerical answers.

Strengths

  • Clean experimental paradigm isolating probe-time effects from generation-time confounds.
  • Striking, counterintuitive finding with strong empirical support across models, scales, and datasets.
  • Comprehensive control experiments systematically ruling out alternative explanations.
  • Well-written with clear motivation; cross-model generalization (Qwen2.5, Qwen3.5, GPT-4o-mini).

Limitations

  • Generator–probe setting is artificial; real CoT usage involves the same model generating and reading rationales.
  • May not extend to tasks requiring genuine multi-step symbolic reasoning (mathematical proof, program synthesis).
  • LogiQA results show larger critical windows (n* ≈ 5–8), hinting at task-dependent variation.
  • LCA account is descriptive—identifies what matters but doesn't fully explain why models are sensitive to these patterns.
  • Limited to English-language rationales and predominantly knowledge-intensive MCQ tasks.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does the LCA account hold for multi-turn reasoning tasks or agentic settings where rationales are generated and consumed iteratively?
  2. Can the n*=2–3 finding be leveraged for ultra-efficient prompt compression that preserves CoT benefits while drastically reducing token usage?
  3. What training-data statistics give rise to local co-occurrence sensitivity—do specific pre-training corpora produce different critical window sizes?
  4. How does the pattern change with human-authored rationales vs. model-generated ones?
  5. Could explicitly designing rationales to maximize locally informative co-occurrence (rather than logical coherence) further improve probe-time performance?
