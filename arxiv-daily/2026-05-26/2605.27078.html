Two Speeds of Learning: A Representation-Readout Decomposition of Grokking and Double Descent

Chi-Ning Chou· Oscar Uzdelewicz· Neng-Chun Chiu· Yao-Yuan Yang· SueYeon Chung
Key Finding
Grokking and double descent arise from two competing processes — representation learning and readout calibration — running at different speeds.
Key Metric
2 competing speeds at odds
Why This Matters
Unifies grokking & double descent under one diagnostic framework and distinguishes genuine from spurious generalization.
Critical Edge
Representation-readout boundary is ambiguous for complex architectures like transformers.
Methodology
  • Representational geometry — tracks how encoder feature spaces evolve structurally during training.
  • Neural Tangent Kernels (NTK) — characterizes readout layer and encoder dynamics separately.
  • Linear probing — isolates feature quality from readout alignment by training probes on intermediate representations.
  • Decomposition principle — splits generalization into representation learning (encoder) + readout calibration (classifier), tracking each independently.
Core insight: grokking is not a phase transition. Representation learning is gradual all along — the readout simply gets ahead of it early.
Results & Highlights
  • Grokking explained — Readout becomes train-biased early while representation learning progresses gradually; generalization emerges only when representation catches up.
  • Lazy-to-rich rebutted — Representation learning is not absent before grokking, only slower — contradicting the dominant account.
  • Spurious vs. genuine — Diagnostic criteria reveal some reported grokking/double descent is artifact from non-standard training setups.
  • Epoch-wise double descent — Stems from representation degradation and readout misalignment, not a fundamental algorithm property.
Strengths
  • Unified, task-agnostic framework for two previously disconnected phenomena.
  • Empirical validation across a wide range of tasks and architectures.
  • Challenges the dominant lazy-to-rich account with concrete evidence.
  • Practical diagnostic tools for the community.
  • Bridges theory and practice — accessible to both communities.
Limitations
  • Representation-readout boundary ambiguous for very deep/complex models.
  • Framework is diagnostic, not prescriptive — explains why, not how to fix.
  • Theoretical depth (proofs vs. observations) unclear without full paper.
  • Spurious-generalization claims depend on debatable training recipe choices.
Questions for Further Exploration
  1. How does the decomposition apply to LLMs during pre-training and fine-tuning?
  2. Can the framework predict grokking onset, beyond post-hoc explanation?
  3. What role do hyperparameters (LR, weight decay, batch size) play in modulating relative speeds?
  4. Does it extend to multi-modal or multi-task settings with shared encoders?
  5. How does the decomposition interact with curriculum learning and data ordering?
Relevance
This paper is significant for understanding deep learning training dynamics. The representation-readout decomposition offers a new lens to diagnose grokking, double descent, and related phenomena — directly relevant to research on implicit regularization and generalization in neural networks.
