arXiv 2605.27028 · 2026-05-26

Less is More: Early Stopping Rollout for On-Policy Distillation

Zhou Ziheng, Jiaqi Li, Huacong Tang, Ying Nian Wu, Demetri Terzopoulos UCLA

Truncating rollout to early tokens in on-policy distillation consistently outperforms full rollout across all tested settings.

ESR-trained students exceed teacher performance

A trivially simple intervention — just truncate rollouts — consistently improves LLM distillation while cutting GPU cost.

Students surpassing teachers may reflect evaluation confounds or implicit regularization, not genuine capability gains.

Methodology

On-policy distillation trains a student by scoring the student's own rollouts with a teacher. The authors identify Off-policy Teacher Decay: as rollout length grows, the student-generated prefix becomes increasingly off-policy for the teacher, corrupting its scoring signal. Early Stopping Rollout (ESR) truncates rollout generation after the first k tokens — the teacher scores only these early partial sequences. Evaluated across model sizes, families, tasks, training regimes, and cross-architecture scenarios.

Results

  • ESR surpasses full-rollout distillation across all tested dimensions
  • Significantly higher GPU efficiency and training stability
  • ESR-trained students exceed teacher performance in some settings via Cascading Alignment & Sub-mode Commitment
  • Position-based truncation is not fully explained by KL divergence or entropy — suggesting novel dynamics

Core Contributions

  • Identifies Off-policy Teacher Decay — teacher scoring degrades for later tokens, falling back to pre-training completion
  • Proposes ESR — a minimalist strategy restricting rollout to first tokens only
  • Discovers Cascading Alignment and Sub-mode Commitment mechanisms
  • Position selection is not explained by standard information-theoretic measures

Strengths

  • Extremely simple and practical — just truncate rollouts
  • Consistent improvements across diverse settings
  • Mechanistic explanations beyond surface-level gains
  • Addresses a previously unrecognized problem
  • Energy-efficient: fewer tokens, lower compute cost

Limitations

  • Full mechanistic details require the complete paper
  • Sensitivity to truncation length k is unclear
  • Abstract does not discuss approach limitations
  • Cross-model-family gains not quantified
  • Generalization beyond tested architectures unknown

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How should optimal truncation length k be determined — universal heuristic or task-dependent?
  2. Can ESR combine with progressive distillation or mixture-of-teachers for further gains?
  3. What is the relationship between Off-policy Teacher Decay and distribution shift in RLHF?
  4. Does Sub-mode Commitment mean ESR students are less diverse but more precise?
  5. How does ESR interact with different teacher-student capacity ratios?
