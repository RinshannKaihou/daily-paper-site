Tracing Computation Density in LLMs

Corentin Kervadec, Iuliia Lysova, Iuri Macocco, Marco Baroni, Gemma Boleda UPF / ICREA 2026-05-26

Just 0.1% of an LLM’s computational graph suffices for top-1 prediction, revealing a universal two-phase computation structure.

Key Metric
0.1%

of the full computational graph recovers the top-1 prediction across all 10 models tested — a stable “minimal core” that is architecture-invariant.

Why This Matters

s-Trace systematically quantifies LLM computation density, with direct implications for dynamic compute allocation and early-exit strategies.

Critical Edge

No downstream application demonstrated — the paper characterizes sparsity but never exploits it. Entropy–density correlation is only moderate (r = 0.22).

Methodology

Graph formulation. Each LLM is a DAG: nodes are intermediate token representations (per-layer, per-token); edges are transformer operations (attention heads, MLPs, residual connections). A 100-token input with 32 heads × 32 layers yields ~6,300 nodes and ~5M edges.

s-Trace extraction. Greedy best-first search starting from the last-token node in the last layer, iteratively adding the highest-importance edge (scored by L1-norm, node-normalized). Only one forward pass needed; no training.

Evaluation. Reconstruction fidelity via Total Variation distance (bounded, mode-focused). Computational Density C defined as AUC of error vs. log(trace-size).

Scale. 5,000 Wikitext sequences × 10 LLMs (7B–14B, 7 families) × 26 trace-size levels → ~1.3M total traces.

Results & Highlights

  • Pervasive sparsity: 0.1% of the full model suffices for top-1 prediction across all 10 models.
  • Construction phase (s ∈ [10−4, 10−2]): early-layer edges dominate (~80% at s = 10−3); balanced component mix (~30% residual, ~20% MLP, ~50% attention).
  • Refinement phase (s > 10−2): late-layer attention heads increasingly contribute; log-linear scaling suggests additive accumulation of relatively independent paths.
  • Stable minimal core: top 20% most frequent components account for 80% of edges at s = 10−3 (Qwen3-14B).
  • Entropy–density link: high-entropy outputs require extended refinement; low-entropy contexts reconstruct near-perfectly with minimal traces.
  • Frequency–density link: frequent tokens use sparser computation (r ∈ [−0.45, −0.22]), confirming sparse traces encode superficial statistics.

Strengths

  • Elegant formulation of computation density as a per-input, measurable quantity with clear mathematical definition.
  • Broad validation across 10 models from 7 families, lending credibility to the generality of the two-phase finding.
  • The minimal-core concept is intuitive and well-supported by multiple analyses (stability, layer makeup, component balance).
  • Connects mechanistic interpretability to cognitive science questions about symbolic vs. distributed processing.

Limitations

  • Single-domain evaluation: only Wikitext (English Wikipedia). Generalization to code, multilingual, or specialized domains is unverified.
  • Heuristic importance scoring: L1-norm may miss important low-magnitude contributions; comparison with gradient-based or attribution-patching methods is absent.
  • Zero-ablation can push activations off-manifold; mean-ablation experiments are deferred to future work.
  • Moderate entropy correlation (r = 0.22 mean) suggests other unmeasured factors drive most variance in computation density.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does s-Trace compare to gradient-based edge pruning or attribution patching in reconstruction fidelity at equivalent budgets?
  • Can the minimal core enable adaptive computation — routing high-confidence inputs through 0.1% of the model while allocating full capacity to uncertain inputs?
  • How does computation density vary across domains (code generation, math reasoning, multilingual text) and longer contexts?
  • What linguistic properties beyond entropy and unigram frequency drive computation density? Could syntax complexity, semantic ambiguity, or discourse structure be predictive?
  • Does the two-phase structure hold for encoder-decoder models, mixture-of-experts architectures, or multimodal models?
