Guiding LLM Post-training Data Engineering with Model Internals from Sparse Autoencoders

Yi Jing, Zao Dai, Jinwu Hu, Zijun Yao, Lei Hou, Juanzi Li, Xiaozhi Wang — Tsinghua University
sparse-autoencoder post-training reinforcement-learning data-engineering mechanistic-interpretability
SAERL uses Sparse Autoencoder internals to guide post-training data selection, boosting Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B accuracy by 3% while cutting training steps 20%.

Why This Matters
First framework bridging mechanistic interpretability and practical data engineering—making RL post-training more efficient and principled.
Critical Edge
Evaluated only on Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B—generalization to larger models and non-math domains remains unproven.

Methodology

SAERL extracts model-internal signals via Sparse Autoencoders and operationalizes them into three data engineering levers applied on top of GRPO reinforcement learning.

01
Diversity Clusters data in SAE latent space to ensure moderate batch mixing during RL training.
02
Difficulty Derives a difficulty proxy from activation patterns to construct an easy-to-hard curriculum.
03
Quality Trains a quality probe on SAE features to filter low-quality training samples.

Strengths

  • Novel bridge between mechanistic interpretability and practical data engineering
  • Three well-motivated data properties with concrete operationalizations
  • Demonstrated efficiency gains (20% fewer steps)
  • Cross-model transferability of SAE features is practically valuable

Limitations

  • Evaluation focused on math reasoning with relatively small models
  • SAE quality and training cost not discussed in the abstract
  • Unclear how the framework scales to larger models or non-math domains
  • Dependency on SAE quality—noisy SAEs could degrade performance

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does SAERL perform on larger models (7B, 70B) and non-math domains?
  • What is the computational overhead of SAE feature extraction vs. the training savings?
  • How robust is the framework to SAE quality—does a poorly trained SAE hurt or merely fail to help?
  • Can the difficulty proxy and quality probe combine with external signals (e.g., reward models)?
  • How do the three data properties interact—does optimizing one come at the cost of another?
