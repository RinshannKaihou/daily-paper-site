> **一句话总结：** 本文用 Data Kernel Perspective Space（DKPS）刻画黑盒生成式模型群体的"行为几何"，在 79 个跨 20 家提供商的模型与 100 个 Gemma-3-4b-it 系统提示配置上，仅用约 2% 的探测预算（m=50/2622）即可对越狱易感性检测达到 AUPRC 0.94，并用 k-medoids 选出 3 个代表模型即可覆盖整个群体的防御迁移收益。

> **TL;DR:** A black-box "behavioral geometry" framework (DKPS) that embeds each model by the mean nomic-embed-text-v1.5 embeddings of its responses to a probe set, then runs k-NN/Ensemble prediction of Attack Success Rate (ASR). On 79 models (20 providers, from Llama-3.2-1B to GPT-4o/Claude-3.7/Gemini-2.5) attacked with 2,622 MultiBreak probes across 11 harmful categories, the Ensemble (DKPS + sample score) reaches **AUPRC 0.94** for top-quartile susceptibility detection at m=50 probes (~**98% probe reduction** vs full 2,622-probe evaluation), beating the family-mean baseline; semantic-vs-behavioral distance Mantel ρ=0.649 (p<10⁻⁶). For defense transfer, nearest-neighbor DKPS transfer gives mean ASR reduction 0.307, **+2% over same-provider assignment (p=0.03)**, and k-medoids coverage saturates at **K≈3 development models**.

## 核心贡献

- 形式化"行为几何"框架：用 (T, δ) 刻画黑盒模型群体 F={f_1,...,f_n}，并以 Data Kernel Perspective Space（DKPS）作为可估计实例，理论上有群体级一致性保证（Acharyya et al.；Helm et al.）。
- 验证行为几何编码安全相关结构：语义相近的攻击类别产生行为相近的 DKPS（Mantel ρ=0.649, p<10⁻⁶），且 DKPS 投影与整体 ASR 相关——即便用无害探针也有效，意味着安全画像无需暴露模型于有害内容。
- 高效易感性预测：在 79 模型（50 训练/29 测试，200 次随机划分）上，Ensemble(DKPS+Sample) 在各探测预算下 MAE 最低、AUPRC 在 m=50 时达 0.94，仅用约 2% 探测预算。
- 有效防御迁移：行为上更近的模型防御迁移效果更好（Spearman ρ=−0.68, p=0.03），DKPS 最近邻迁移比同提供商迁移平均提升 +2%（p=0.03），k-medoids 选 3 个开发模型即覆盖大部分收益。

## Key Contributions

- **Behavioral geometry formalization (Sec 2.1):** Defines a population F of n black-box generative systems, a probe set Q, embedding g:X→R^p, summary statistic T_i, and dissimilarity δ; (T,δ) is a behavioral geometry. DKPS instantiates T_i=µ_i (matrix of mean embedded responses) and δ as rescaled Frobenius norm, embedded into R^d via metric MDS — proven consistent as m,r→∞ (Acharyya et al. [1]; Helm et al. [14]).
- **Geometry validation (Sec 3.1):** Per-category DKPS distances mirror semantic distances (Mantel ρ=0.649, p<10⁻⁶); PLS projections from *every* source category (incl. non-harmful probes) correlate with overall ASR under both keyword and LLM judges, suggesting DKPS captures a general behavioral posture rather than category-specific vulnerability.
- **Efficient susceptibility prediction (Sec 3.2):** Three methods (DKPS k-NN h^(n), Sample Score, Ensemble α-weighted) over 200 random 50/29 train/test splits and probe budgets m∈{1,2,5,10,20,50,100}; Ensemble dominates at every budget.
- **Defense-transfer via behavioral nearest-neighbor (Sec 3.3):** In-context defenses (single attack/refusal example) optimized per development model via Eq.(1); transferred to 30 target models × 550 attacks; DKPS nearest-neighbor beats random/size/family baselines, and k-medoids coverage saturates at K≈3.

## Methodology

**Population.** Two collections: (1) 79 models across 20 providers via OpenRouter (Llama-3.2-1B up to GPT-4o, Claude-3.7 Sonnet, Gemini-2.5 Flash); (2) 100 system-prompt configurations of google/gemma-3-4b-it generated by o3-mini.

**Attack probes.** Final turn of 2,666 MultiBreak [36] multi-turn sequences used as single-turn attacks; intents labeled by GPT-4o into 12 fine-grained safety categories, 2 dropped (<50 intents), 5 merged; plus non-harmful prompts → **M=|Q|=2,622 probes across 11 harmful + 1 non-harmful categories**.

**ASR judges.** (i) Keyword judge [47]: success if response contains none of 30 refusal phrases and is ≥10 chars; (ii) LLM-as-judge with GPT-4o-mini on a 493-query subsample across all models (39,440 judgments). Provider blocks (HTTP 403) and empty responses treated as refusals. Keyword- and LLM-judge ASRs highly correlated (Pearson r=0.86, Spearman ρ=0.87). Transfer results use keyword judge only (LLM judge would need ~1.5M extra calls, ~$1,000).

**DKPS construction.** Responses embedded with nomic-embed-text-v1.5 [27]; one DKPS per source category + an overall DKPS; default dimensionality d=8; robustness to embedding and d studied in §3.5.

**Susceptibility prediction protocol.** 200 random splits of 50 train / 29 test models; report MAE vs probe budget m∈{1,2,5,10,20,50,100}; susceptibility detection = top-quartile ASR (75th percentile threshold); precision-recall AUPRC averaged. Methods: DKPS k-NN (k=5); Sample Score (mean ASR on the m-probe subset); Ensemble α·Sample+(1−α)·DKPS with α CV-learned per split (α=11% at m=1, 73% at m=50, keyword judge). Baselines: population-mean ASR; family-mean (same provider).

**Defense transfer protocol.** 220 candidate in-context refusal examples (20 per harmful category) extracted from existing model responses; for each development model, the candidate minimizing defended ASR on a 99-attack subsample is selected via Eq.(1). 23 development models: 13 via k-medoids (union of K=1..10) + 10 random. Each defense applied to 30 random target models × 550 attacks (50/category). Four transfer conditions: xfer(rand.), xfer(size), xfer(family), xfer(near. = DKPS nearest); plus a random-example baseline. Full transfer experiment ≈1.5M API calls, ~$1,000.

## Results & Highlights

- **Behavioral geometry encodes safety structure (Fig 1):** semantic distance vs behavioral-geometry distance Mantel **ρ = 0.649, p < 10⁻⁶**; per-category PLS projections (including **non-harmful probes**) correlate with overall ASR under both judges, suggesting safety profiling may not require harmful probes.
- **Efficient susceptibility detection (Fig 2, keyword judge, AUPRC at m=5/10/50):** DKPS **(0.67, 0.77, 0.87)**; Sample **(0.59, 0.75, 0.93)**; **Ensemble (0.73, 0.84, 0.94)** — i.e. Ensemble reaches **AUPRC 0.94 at m=50**, vs random-baseline AUPRC 0.25 and family-baseline 0.64. m=50/2622 ≈ **98% probe reduction** vs full evaluation.
- **MAE beats baselines by m=2:** Ensemble matches or beats the family-mean MAE baseline (0.169 keyword / 0.106 LLM) at m=2 onward under both judges; all methods beat population-mean (0.191 / 0.141) by m=5.
- **Defense transferability decays with DKPS distance (Fig 3, left):** mean ASR reduction vs DKPS distance Spearman **ρ = −0.68, p = 0.03, n=10 bins** — closer models transfer defenses better.
- **DKPS transfer beats same-provider (Fig 3, center):** mean ASR reduction **0.307 (DKPS nearest)** vs **0.287 (same-family)** — a **+2% gain, p=0.03**; random/size/family conditions are statistically indistinguishable (p=0.13/0.42). On head-to-head wins, DKPS wins 18/44 targets by a median of **7.5% (mean 11.1%)** vs same-family's 15/44 by median **1.4% (mean 3.4%)**.
- **Coverage saturates at K≈3 (Fig 3, right):** k-medoids selection of development models saturates at **K≈3**, matching the agglomerative-clustering silhouette peak (Appendix E) and capturing most of the benefit of 23 candidate developers.
- **Robustness:** results are stable to DKPS dimensionality d and to embedding choice (§3.5) and hold under both the keyword and LLM judges.

## Critical Assessment

- **ASR measurement noise:** the two judges, though correlated (Pearson r=0.86), differ enough that *transfer* results are reported under the keyword judge only for cost reasons — and the keyword judge is a crude 30-phrase / ≥10-char rule, so the headline +2% transfer gain (p=0.03) is not confirmed under the stronger LLM judge.
- **Statistical power for transfer is thin:** the Spearman ρ=−0.68 (p=0.03) is computed over only **n=10 bins**, and the +2% DKPS-vs-family effect, while significant, rests on a single benchmark's keyword-judge ASR.
- **Black-box only:** the framework deliberately ignores model internals/weights, so it cannot exploit white-box signals (activations, weights) that prior low-dim-representation work [2,10,18,22,33] uses — potentially leaving accuracy on the table.
- **Development-model coverage claim (K≈3) is benchmark-specific:** "3 models suffice" is measured only against MultiBreak's 11 categories on 79 OpenRouter models; whether 3 still covers a 1000-model fleet or a different attack suite is untested.
- **Non-harmful-probe ASR correlation is intriguing but unexplained:** the paper notes non-harmful probes predict ASR nearly as well as harmful ones, but offers no mechanism for *why* a benign probe set reveals jailbreak vulnerability.

## Strengths

- Genuinely practical: turns cached model responses into a reusable asset for both prediction and defense deployment, with clear cost savings (~98% fewer probes).
- Broad, realistic model population (79 production models, 20 providers) rather than a toy suite.
- The non-harmful-probe finding has real deployment value (safety profiling without exposing models to harmful content).
- Honest about cost (~1.5M calls / $1,000) and about judge discrepancies.

## Limitations

- Transfer results rest on the keyword judge only.
- Black-box only — ignores internals/weights.
- Coverage "K≈3" is specific to MultiBreak / 79 models.
- No mechanism given for why non-harmful probes correlate with ASR.
- Defense set is restricted to single-example in-context refusals; richer defenses untested.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- The transfer gain (+2%, p=0.03) is reported only under the keyword judge — does it survive under the LLM-as-judge ASR, which the authors themselves treat as more reliable?
- The Mantel/Spearman statistics rest on n=10–11 bins/categories; would the significance hold under a bootstrap over probe subsets rather than over categories?
- Non-harmful probes predict ASR nearly as well as harmful ones — is this because benign probes still elicit provider-level refusal styles, and could a *minimal* benign probe set be designed deliberately?
- The paper cites Acharyya et al. [1] and Helm et al. [14] for consistency of DKPS — what sample size (models × probes) is needed for the k-NN predictor h^(n) to beat the sample-score baseline with high probability, per the theory?
- Coverage saturates at K≈3 on MultiBreak/79 models — how does this scale to a 1000-model fleet or to a different attack suite like HarmBench [8]?
