> **一句话总结：** Tournament-GRPO 提出用组内锦标赛式的相对比较替代逐点绝对评分，为开放长文本生成的 RL 训练提供更可靠的奖励信号，在 Deep Research Bench 上比最强基线提升 4.52 分。

> **TL;DR:** Tournament-GRPO replaces pointwise absolute rubric scoring with repeated multi-round tournament comparisons among same-query rollouts, yielding better-calibrated group-wise rewards for GRPO and achieving a 4.52-point improvement over the strongest baseline on Deep Research Bench.

## 核心贡献

- 识别了逐点绝对评分在开放长文本 RL 中的三大局限：绝对分数校准困难、同组 rollout 区分度弱、优化过程中奖励饱和
- 提出 Tournament-GRPO，通过对同 query 的多个 rollout 进行重复多轮锦标赛比较，将 rubric 引导的 LLM 判断转化为相对奖励
- 在 Deep Research Bench 上系统验证了锦标赛奖励在效果和效率上的优势，并分析了锦标赛超参数对训练动态的影响

## Key Contributions

- Identifies three key limitations of pointwise rubric-based reward design for RL in open-ended long-form generation: poor absolute-score calibration, limited discriminative ability among same-query rollouts, and optimization-induced reward saturation
- Proposes Tournament-GRPO, a group-wise tournament reward framework that conducts repeated multi-round rubric-guided comparisons among same-query rollouts and normalizes accumulated tournament scores into GRPO rewards
- Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on Deep Research Bench with a 4.52-point overall-score improvement over the strongest baseline, while maintaining O(MK) judge-call complexity versus O(K^2) for exhaustive pairwise comparison

## Methodology

The method operates within the GRPO framework, which estimates advantages by comparing multiple sampled rollouts from the same query. Tournament-GRPO modifies the reward computation:

1. **Rollout Sampling:** For each query q, the policy samples K rollouts.
2. **Tournament Process:** Repeated M times: rollouts are shuffled (to reduce position bias), partitioned into groups of size G, and an LLM judge selects Kwin winners from each group based on query-specific rubrics. Winners advance and accumulate points (omega_t = 1 per round).
3. **Normalization:** Accumulated tournament scores are min-max normalized within each query group to produce relative rewards r_i^tour.
4. **GRPO Integration:** The final reward r_i = r_i^tour + r_i^fmt (binary format reward) feeds into GRPO's group-relative advantage estimation.

Two tournament structures are studied: binary (G=2, Kwin=1, Kfinal=1) and larger-group (G=4, Kwin=2, Kfinal=2). The total judge-call complexity is O(MK), compared to O(K^2) for exhaustive pairwise comparison.

## Results & Highlights

- **Best overall score:** 55.09 achieved by (G=4, Kwin=2, Kfinal=2) with M=8 after 2 epochs, versus 50.57 for pairwise comparison and 41.20 for the best absolute-scoring baseline (GDPO explicit).
- **Early-stage advantage:** Binary tournament (G=2, Kwin=1, Kfinal=1) with M=3 achieves 52.28 after epoch 1, outperforming pairwise by 6.03 points, providing a strong early learning signal.
- **Effectiveness-efficiency trade-off:** Tournament-GRPO achieves the best performance with O(MK) complexity, substantially cheaper than O(K^2) pairwise comparison.
- **Training dynamics:** Binary tournaments are most effective early (sharp elimination of weak rollouts), while larger-group tournaments with multiple retained winners become superior after longer training (softer signal for finer-grained discrimination).
- **Backbone:** Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct policy with Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct as judge, trained on DR Tulu RL-stage data with ReAct-style tool use (google_search, browse_webpage).

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Moderate. The core idea—adapting tournament-style ranking (from TourRank) to reward construction for GRPO—is natural and well-motivated but not fundamentally new as an algorithmic paradigm. The insight that group-relative rewards better match GRPO's advantage estimation is valuable.
- **Rigor:** Strong. Multiple baselines (implicit/explicit scoring, GDPO, pairwise), controlled comparisons (same backbone, data, judge, training steps), and detailed ablations on tournament hyperparameters. Single-run results reported (no error bars), which is a minor concern.
- **Reproducibility:** Good. Code is public, hyperparameters are documented in Appendix C, and the training setup is clearly described. Uses publicly available models and data.
- **Significance:** High for the RL-from-LLM-judge subfield. Addresses a real and growing problem as more work applies RL to open-ended generation tasks.
- **Red flags:** Evaluation relies entirely on Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct as judge on Deep Research Bench—a single benchmark with a single evaluator. The overall scores are in the 32–55 range on a 0–100 scale, leaving room to question whether absolute improvements translate to practical quality gains. No human evaluation is provided.

## Strengths

- Directly addresses a practical limitation of rubric-based absolute scoring for GRPO
- Simple and general design that can be applied to any group-based RL method
- Favorable O(MK) efficiency compared to O(K^2) pairwise while achieving better results
- Careful analysis of how tournament structure interacts with training stage dynamics
- Public code and clear documentation

## Limitations

- Still relies on LLM judges, inheriting their biases and inconsistencies; tournament comparison mitigates but does not eliminate judge-side errors
- Introduces additional judge-call overhead compared to pointwise scoring
- Tournament hyperparameters (G, Kwin, Kfinal, M) interact with the training stage and must be tuned jointly—no clear single best configuration
- Evaluated on only one benchmark (Deep Research Bench) with one judge model; generalization is uncertain
- Tournament rewards provide relative preference signals only, not fine-grained feedback on factual errors, missing evidence, or reasoning flaws

## Relevance

与当前 LLM 强化学习训练中 rubric-based reward design 的核心问题高度相关。Tournament-GRPO 提供了一种将绝对评分转化为相对比较的通用框架，对于任何需要在开放生成任务中用 LLM-as-judge 构建 RL 奖励信号的工作都有直接参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does Tournament-GRPO perform on other open-ended generation benchmarks beyond Deep Research Bench (e.g., report generation, creative writing)?
- Can tournament rewards be combined with fine-grained factuality verification to address the limitation of providing only relative preference signals?
- Is there a principled way to adaptively select tournament hyperparameters (G, Kwin, M) during training as the policy evolves?
- What happens when the LLM judge has systematic biases—does tournament comparison amplify or mitigate such biases compared to pointwise scoring?
- Could curriculum-style tournament designs (e.g., starting with binary and switching to larger groups mid-training) combine the benefits of both structures?
