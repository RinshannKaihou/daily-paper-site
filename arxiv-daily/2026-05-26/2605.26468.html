Diffuse to Detect: Generative Diffusion Models for Unsupervised IC Anomaly Detection

Yuxuan YinChen HeTodd JacobsJialei HeBoxun XuRobert JinPeng Li
Key Finding
First unsupervised IC anomaly detection via Diffusion Transformer on industrial 16nm test data.
Key Metric
State-of-the-art under extreme class imbalance (16nm IC)
Why This Matters
Latent defect screening is costly and difficult; unsupervised approach eliminates expensive labeling.
Critical Edge
Proprietary data limits reproducibility; wafer-scale inference cost unaddressed.
Methodology
1
Autoencoder Compression
→
2
Tokenization + Wafer-Position Embeddings
→
3
Diffusion Transformer Denoising

Raw high-dimensional IC test measurements are compressed via autoencoder into latent vectors, reshaped into structured token sequences with sinusoidal and per-device wafer-position embeddings, then processed by a Diffusion Transformer trained to denoise. At inference, noise-prediction error at mid-range diffusion timesteps serves as the anomaly score — higher error flags anomalous devices. Entirely unsupervised: no labeled defects required.

Results & Highlights
  • State-of-the-art anomaly detection on real industrial 16nm IC test data
  • Effective under extreme class imbalance characteristic of latent defect screening
  • Fast wafer-scale screening without manual feature engineering
  • Interpretable failure localization via latent-space reconstruction residuals
Critical Assessment
  • Novelty: High — first Diffusion Transformer applied to IC test anomaly detection with domain-specific embeddings
  • Rigor: Solid — real industrial 16nm data; full evaluation details pending paper access
  • Reproducibility: Unclear — proprietary industrial data may limit external validation
  • Significance: High — latent defect screening is a genuine and economically important challenge
Strengths
  • Addresses a genuinely important industrial problem
  • Fully unsupervised — no labeled anomalies needed
  • Combines modern generative modeling with domain-specific inductive biases
  • Interpretable outputs, not a black-box score
  • Evaluated on real industrial data, not synthetic benchmarks
Limitations
  • Autoencoder compression introduces an extra training stage and potential information loss
  • Mid-range timestep selection may require per-dataset tuning
  • Performance on out-of-distribution defect types is unknown
  • Generalization beyond IC test data is not discussed
  • Computational cost of diffusion inference at wafer scale is unaddressed
Questions for Further Exploration
How does the framework compare to simpler autoencoder-reconstruction baselines (without diffusion) on the same data?
What is the computational overhead at wafer scale, and can it meet production throughput?
How sensitive is performance to the mid-range timestep choice, and is there a principled selection method?
Can wafer-position embeddings capture spatial cluster defects that purely parametric approaches miss?
How does the framework generalize to other process nodes (7nm, 5nm) or non-IC manufacturing?
