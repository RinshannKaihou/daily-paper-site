Spend Your Rollouts Where It Counts: Rollout Allocation for Group-Based RL Post-Training

Woojeong Kim · Ziyi Yang · Jing Nathan Yan · Jialu Liu  —  Cornell / Databricks  ·  2026-05-26
Key Finding
Pilot-Commit concentrates rollouts on high-variance prompts, reaching target accuracy up to 4× faster than DAPO.
Key Metric
1.5 – 4.0×
fewer cumulative rollouts to target accuracy
Why This Matters
Rollout generation is the dominant cost in RL post-training — smarter allocation cuts compute without sacrificing accuracy.
Critical Edge
Only validated on math reasoning with binary rewards; generalization to continuous rewards or RLHF is untested.

Methodology

In group-based policy optimization (GRPO), prompts where all rollouts succeed or all fail produce zero advantage — wasting expensive inference. Pilot-Commit treats rollout allocation as a first-class optimization problem.

Theoretical Basis

The GRPO gradient magnitude is maximized when within-group reward dispersion is highest. For binary rewards, this peaks at success rate p = 0.5, where variance p(1−p) is largest. This makes reward variance a principled proxy for prompt informativeness.

Two-Stage Algorithm

  1. Pilot stage: Sample npilot rollouts per prompt (3× oversampled batch). Estimate empirical success rate p̂(q) and reward variance p̂(1−p̂).
  2. Filter: Retain prompts with 0.125 ≤ p̂ ≤ 0.75. Asymmetric thresholds favor hard prompts approaching the high-variance regime while evicting near-solved ones.
  3. Commit stage: Allocate ncommit additional rollouts only to retained prompts (capped at training batch size).
  4. Train: Policy update on the union of pilot + commit rollouts for selected prompts.

Practical Optimizations

  • One-step binding: Overlap pilot at step t−1 with commit at step t, trading 1-step off-policy delay to eliminate sequential inference overhead.
  • Replay buffer: Store excess pilot survivors; sample when current pilot yields too few prompts. Evict entries older than 4 steps.
  • Solved-prompt eviction: Permanently remove prompts from the pool when all pilot rollouts are correct.

Results & Highlights

  • Rollout efficiency: Under ample budgets (n=64/128), reaches target accuracy first in all 4 settings. Uses 1.5–1.9× fewer rollouts than GRPO, 2.3–4.0× fewer than DAPO. DAPO never reaches target at 4B scale within budget.
  • Per-step quality: Maintains highest per-prompt reward std. dev. throughout training. Higher peak accuracy than GRPO and DAPO despite same training step count.
  • Wall-clock: On Qwen3-14B with 64× H200: PC has 1.21× total slowdown vs GRPO, while DAPO has 1.83×. Rollout savings translate to practical cost reductions.
  • Peak accuracy: Highest or comparable in 7 of 8 settings while consuming fewer total rollouts than DAPO in every case.
  • Ablations: Pilot/commit split ratio is robust — all configs converge to similar accuracy under equal sampling cost. Thresholds are robust across a wide range.

Strengths

  • Clear theoretical link between reward variance and gradient signal strength.
  • Consistent rollout savings across 4 model scales (1.5B–14B) and 6 benchmarks.
  • Minimal overhead: one-step binding eliminates sequential inference cost.
  • No auxiliary models required — uses online pilot feedback from the current policy.
  • Higher peak accuracy than GRPO in most settings.
  • Robust to hyperparameter choices across a wide range.

Limitations

  • Evaluated only on math reasoning with verifiable binary rewards.
  • 3× oversampling adds per-step overhead vs vanilla GRPO; savings are cumulative, not per-step.
  • Requires pilot/commit split and thresholds (though robust to choices).
  • 1-step off-policy delay (+ replay buffer up to d=4) could matter in non-stationary settings.
  • Limited budget (n=16) shows marginal savings at 1.5B and 14B scales.
Critical gap: Generalization to continuous rewards, RLHF with human preferences, and open-ended tasks is entirely untested. The method is specifically designed for binary rewards with group-based methods (GRPO).

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does Pilot-Commit interact with reward shaping or multi-turn RL settings where rewards are not simply binary?
  2. Can the pilot-stage variance estimate be combined with learned difficulty predictors (e.g., MoPPS, GPS) for even better allocation?
  3. What happens when the oversampling factor s is reduced below 3 — is there a sweet spot between pilot coverage and commit budget?
  4. Does the one-step off-policy delay degrade performance in settings with faster policy drift (e.g., higher learning rates, smaller models)?
  5. How does eviction interact with dataset diversity — could aggressive eviction lead to mode collapse on remaining prompts?
