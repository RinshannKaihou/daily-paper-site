From Static Context to Calibrated Interactive RL: Mitigating Distribution Shift in Multi-turn Dialogue with Aligned Simulator

Xiaohua Wang, Jiakang Yuan, Zisu Huang, Muzhao Tian, Changze Lv, Kaitao Song, Tao Chen, Xiaoqing Zheng — Fudan University

Key Finding

Distribution shift in multi-turn dialogue RL compounds quadratically over turns; a calibrated simulator via SFT paired with interactive GRPO surpasses even the oracle upper bound.

Key Metric
91.5%
MATH-Chat accuracy
base 82.3 · static RL 85.0 · naive interactive 89.3 · oracle 89.7
Why This Matters

Uncalibrated simulators exhibit sycophancy that causes reward hacking—interactive RL can be worse than static training without proper simulator alignment.

Critical Edge

Naive interactive RL drops document-editing BLEU from 33.8 → 26.1. The framework's gains are uneven across tasks; simulator calibration is a necessity, not a luxury.

Methodology

Phase I — Simulator Calibration (SFT)

Collect multi-turn traces from an oracle simulator (Qwen3-235B) interacting with the base policy (Gemma-3-4B-IT). Fine-tune Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct via causal LM loss on user turns only. The simulator receives oracle context with ground-truth access, enabling grounded verification and feedback. Training: 1,860 trajectories (MATH) and 16,028 trajectories (DocEdit).

Phase II — Interactive Policy Optimization (GRPO)

Freeze the calibrated simulator; train policy via GRPO on self-generated multi-turn trajectories. Up to 7 rounds per episode, G=32 samples per prompt, sparse outcome reward at episode end. Binary accuracy (MATH) or BLEU (DocEdit). Asymmetric clipping (εlow=0.2, εhigh=0.28), constant LR 5×10−6, no KL penalty. Distributed across 32×A800-80GB GPUs.

Naive interactive RL is destructive on DocEdit—sycophantic simulators enable reward hacking, making uncalibrated interactive training worse than static.

Qualitative analysis of 29 improved cases: direct solving (62%), structural understanding (24%), algebraic strategy (14%), with up to 67% fewer turns.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths
  • Clean theoretical decomposition with provable O(H²) bounds
  • Well-designed ablations isolating each error source
  • SFT calibration is practical and scalable vs adversarial alternatives
  • Counterintuitive finding: uncalibrated interactive RL can be worse than static training
  • Comprehensive qualitative case studies with behavioral evidence
Limitations
  • Only two task domains tested, both with ground-truth outcomes
  • 32×A800 GPU requirement for Phase II rollouts
  • SFT calibration may not handle OOD dialogue states from exploring policies
  • All experiments on a 4B policy; scaling to frontier models is unverified
  • Phase I requires strong oracle (235B) traces; may not exist for new domains

Questions for Further Exploration

How does the framework perform with larger policy models (7B, 13B, 70B)? Does the quadratic bound change with scale?
Can SFT calibration be replaced with online RL or adversarial training for OOD robustness?
What is the minimum oracle data volume for effective simulator calibration?
How sensitive are results to reward function choice (binary accuracy vs BLEU vs LLM-judge)?
Does the framework extend to multi-party dialogue or evolving user goals?
