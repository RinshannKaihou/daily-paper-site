> **一句话总结：** 该研究揭示了多领域RL后训练中跨领域干扰的局部化机制——即使全局梯度近乎正交，后续领域训练仍可通过共享计算路径上的二阶曲率效应选择性损害先前领域，而短时领域刷新能在低维冲突子空间内几何收缩有害分量，实现选择性恢复。

> **TL;DR:** This paper shows that cross-domain interference in sequential multi-domain RL is not caused by global gradient conflict, but by localized second-order damage concentrated in a low-dimensional shared "conflict subspace" along active computation routes. Sparse domain updates with weak neuron-level overlap can still harm earlier domains through shared routes where directional misalignment matters. A short domain refresh geometrically contracts this harmful component, enabling selective recovery with limited collateral damage—empirically recovering Math performance from 57.66 to 66.04 while preserving other domains.

## 核心贡献

- **揭示全局梯度正交性无法解释跨领域干扰**：单领域RL产生的全局梯度可以近乎正交，但后续领域训练仍会对先前领域造成显著性能下降，说明传统的“全局梯度冲突”或“灾难性遗忘”解释是不完整的。
- **提出基于局部计算路径的干扰机制**：虽然不同领域的参数编辑稀疏且顶层修改的神经元重叠度低（Jaccard系数<0.19），但推理领域在推理时高度共享活跃的MLP计算路径（Jaccard系数0.44–0.52）；在这些共享路径上，更新方向决定是协同还是冲突。
- **建立局部扰动理论**：证明后续领域对先前领域的损害主要由二阶曲率项主导，且该损害集中在低维共享冲突子空间中；短时领域刷新能在该子空间内几何收缩有害分量，实现快速选择性恢复。
- **提供双重实验验证**：(1) 在Code→Math→QA→CW序列后对Math进行短时刷新，Math从57.66恢复至66.04，且其他领域基本不变，获得最佳平均分66.39；(2) 在Math-QA对上对仅2%的MLP神经元进行无训练回滚，可恢复20.4%的Math损失，直接验证了损害的局部化。

## Key Contributions

- **Global gradient orthogonality does not prevent interference**: Full-model gradient cosines between domains can stay near zero, yet later-domain training still selectively degrades earlier domains, showing that catastrophic forgetting and global gradient conflict are incomplete explanations.
- **A route-level mechanistic account**: Domain RL induces sparse, small-magnitude edits with weak top-changed-neuron overlap (<0.19 Jaccard), but reasoning domains share highly overlapping active computation routes at inference time (0.44–0.52 Jaccard). Directional alignment of updates on these shared routes determines synergy vs. conflict.
- **Local perturbation theory**: Proves that damage to an earlier domain is driven by a second-order term concentrated in a low-dimensional shared active conflict subspace. A short refresh on the damaged domain geometrically contracts this harmful component, enabling selective recovery with bounded collateral damage.
- **Dual empirical validation**: (1) A brief Re-Math refresh after Code→Math→QA→CW recovers Math from 57.66 to 66.04 while preserving other domains, yielding the best average score of 66.39. (2) A training-free rollback on only 2% of MLP neurons selected by a conflict-subspace proxy recovers 20.4% of the QA-induced Math loss, providing direct evidence for localized damage.

## Methodology

The authors construct a controlled multi-domain RL setting using **Qwen3-4B-Thinking-2507** as the base model and four domains: **Math** (OpenR1-math), **Code** (KlearReasoner-CodeSub-15K), **QA** (SuperGPQA), and **Creative Writing (CW)**. All training uses **GRPO** (Group Relative Policy Optimization) with identical hyperparameters across runs. A fixed sequential curriculum **Code → Math → QA → CW** is used, with checkpoints selected by best validation performance on the current domain.

The analysis proceeds in three layers:

1. **Global gradient analysis**: Compute domain-specific gradient cosines during joint training to test whether full-model gradient conflict explains interference.
2. **Structural localization**: Measure (a) element-wise parameter changes between single-domain experts and the base model; (b) Jaccard overlap of top-10% changed MLP neurons; (c) Jaccard overlap of top-5% active neurons at inference; (d) directional cosine of updates on shared changed neurons.
3. **Theory & validation**: Formulate a local perturbation model with propositions on second-order damage and low-dimensional conflict subspace, plus a theorem on geometric contraction via refresh. Validate with task-level refresh and a training-free proxy rollback.

The proxy rollback for the Math→QA pair selects neurons by a composite score **A × M × C**, where **A** = shared activation under Math and QA, **M** = magnitude of QA update, **C** = directional conflict between Math and QA task vectors. Only the QA-induced increment on selected neurons is reverted: θ_rev = QA_o − P_Ŝ δ_Q|M.

## Results & Highlights

**Selective interference is asymmetric.** After Code→Math→QA→CW sequential training, Math drops from 66.49 (after its own stage) to 57.66, while Code stays stable (~50.5), QA improves to 62.34, and CW improves to 86.52. This shows interference is not uniform forgetting.

**Domain updates are sparse and mild.** 77%–89% of parameters have absolute changes below 1e-7 relative to the base model, and relative changes below 1e-3 for 77%–89% of parameters. Top-changed neuron overlap across domains is low (Jaccard < 0.19), but active-neuron overlap among reasoning domains is high (Math-Code: 0.449; Math-QA: 0.515).

**Short refresh achieves selective recovery.** Re-Math from CW_o raises Math to 66.04 (near the Math expert 66.84 and Math_o 66.49), while Code slightly rises to 51.05, QA stays at 62.49, and CW stays at 85.96. This yields the **best average score of 66.39**, outperforming naive joint training (JT: 65.62) and CGPO (65.30).

**Proxy rollback directly validates localization.** Reverting only 2% of MLP neurons by A×M×C raises Math from 59.90 (QA_o) to 61.25, recovering 20.4% of the QA-induced Math loss, while QA changes by only −0.06. Random rollback at the same budget *hurts* Math (drops to 59.49). Extending to joint MLP+Attention at 32% budget recovers 73.6% of the loss, showing additional recoverable damage outside MLP coordinates.

- **Siyu Ding** (project leader) is from Baidu Inc., bringing industry-scale RL training experience.

- The theoretical claims are accompanied by formal propositions and a theorem with proofs in appendices. Empirical claims are supported by both task-level metrics and a direct weight-space intervention.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty**: High. The paper makes a genuine conceptual advance by showing that global gradient conflict and neuron-level edit overlap are *insufficient* to explain cross-domain interference, and by shifting the analysis to shared active routes and second-order curvature effects. The local perturbation theory and the connection between sparse edits, shared routes, and selective damage is a novel mechanistic framing.

**Rigor**: Generally strong. The theory is formally stated with explicit assumptions (approximate stationarity, sparsity, low-dimensional conflict subspace). The empirical analysis is multi-level (global → module → neuron → direction). However, the theory relies on local smoothness assumptions that may not fully capture the non-convex, discrete nature of RL training with large language models. The "low-dimensional shared conflict subspace" is operationally defined through a proxy rather than directly identified.

**Reproducibility**: Moderate to good. Training uses open-source models (Qwen3-4B-Thinking-2507) and standard frameworks (VeRL/GRPO). Hyperparameters and data sources are specified, though some datasets involve custom sampling (e.g., Creative-Writing series mixing) and LLM-as-a-judge rewards that depend on a specific judge model (Qwen3-235B). The proxy rollback methodology is precisely described and should be replicable. The full training cost is non-trivial but feasible for well-resourced labs.

**Significance**: High for the LLM post-training community. Multi-domain RL is a pressing practical problem (reasoning, coding, QA, creative tasks). The finding that a simple sequential curriculum plus targeted refresh can outperform sophisticated methods like CGPO is practically valuable. The mechanistic insight suggests new algorithmic directions (route-aware regularization, projection-based constrained updates).

**Red flags / concerns**:
- **Limited domain diversity**: Only four domains are tested, and three are reasoning-heavy (Math, Code, QA) with high active-neuron overlap. CW is relatively independent. It is unclear whether the same structure holds for more heterogeneous domains (e.g., math vs. translation vs. safety alignment).
- **Curriculum sensitivity**: The results use a single fixed order (Code→Math→QA→CW). The theory does not clearly predict which domain pairs will conflict vs. synergize, nor whether the refresh order matters.
- **Baseline strength**: CGPO and naive JT are the only mixed-training baselines. Comparison against stronger methods (e.g., data mixture tuning, replay-based approaches, or more recent multi-task RL methods) is absent.
- **Proxy interpretation**: The 32% MLP+Attn budget for 73.6% recovery is relatively large. The authors acknowledge this is a "causal sufficiency test" rather than a tight estimate of the conflict subspace dimension. The redundancy among proxy signals (Appendix D.4) suggests the true minimal subspace may be smaller but the current method cannot isolate it.
- **Refresh as algorithm**: The paper does not propose a fully automatic training algorithm. Domain selection, refresh timing, and budget are hand-designed.

## Strengths

- Clear, well-motivated shift from global to local analysis of interference.
- Theory tightly integrated with empirical observations (sparse edits → shared routes → second-order damage → refresh recovery).
- The training-free proxy rollback is a particularly elegant validation: it directly intervenes on model weights to test a theoretical prediction without retraining.
- Strong writing clarity; figures effectively communicate the multi-scale gradient and neuron analyses.
- Honest limitation section that directly addresses proxy coarseness, algorithmic incompleteness, and scope boundaries.

## Limitations

- **Narrow domain scope**: Four domains on a single model size (4B). Generalization to other model scales, architectures, or domain types is unproven.
- **Fixed curriculum**: No ablation on curriculum order or on which domains most severely interfere with which others.
- **Coarse proxy for conflict subspace**: The A×M×C score is basis-aligned and likely redundant; a more principled subspace estimation (e.g., via Hessian-vector products or SVD on task vectors) could yield sharper interventions.
- **No automatic algorithm**: The practical takeaway—"train sequentially, then refresh damaged domains"—requires manual inspection and is not integrated into a continuous training loop.
- **Missing normalization/residual-stream analysis**: The proxy rollback covers MLP and attention coordinates but does not address normalization parameters or higher-order cross-module interactions, which the authors note may contain residual interference.

## Relevance

该论文对多领域RL后训练中的跨领域干扰提供了深刻的局部化机理解释，提出的“低维冲突子空间”与“短时刷新恢复”框架与关注LLM推理能力持续学习和多任务优化的研究方向高度相关，其实证发现（稀疏编辑、共享路径、方向性冲突）为设计更稳定的多领域RL算法提供了新的理论基础。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Can the conflict subspace be estimated more precisely using Hessian-vector products or second-order information, enabling smaller and more targeted interventions than the 2%–32% neuron budgets used here?
2. Does the local perturbation framework generalize to other post-training paradigms, such as on-policy distillation or DPO-style alignment, where training distributions also evolve dynamically?
3. Can an automatic algorithm be built that detects degraded domains, selects refresh order and budget, and iterates toward a Pareto-stationary compromise without human curriculum design?
4. How does the conflict subspace structure change with model scale—do larger models exhibit more route specialization (reducing interference) or more route sharing (amplifying it)?
5. Would replay-based or constrained-update methods (e.g., projecting gradients orthogonal to the conflict subspace during training) prevent degradation altogether, rather than recovering after the fact?
