Does Compression Preserve Uncertainty? A Unified Benchmark for Quantized and Sparse LLMs via Conformal Prediction

arXiv: 2606.01850

Date: 2026-06-01

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.01850

一句话总结： 本文首次利用共形预测对量化与剪枝后的大语言模型进行系统性的不确定性基准测试，发现压缩常常使准确率与不确定性脱钩，大模型能更好地吸收压缩带来的不确定性膨胀，且这种膨胀往往呈阈值式突变而非渐进变化。
TL;DR: This paper presents the first comprehensive benchmark evaluating the uncertainty of quantized and pruned LLMs through conformal prediction. It reveals that compression frequently decouples accuracy from uncertainty, larger models absorb compression-induced uncertainty more effectively, and uncertainty inflation is often threshold-like rather than gradual.

核心贡献

  • 全新评估视角： 首次将研究焦点从“压缩后准确率是否保持”转向“压缩后不确定性是否保持”，填补了模型压缩与可信AI交叉领域的空白。
  • 严谨的统计框架： 采用共形预测（Conformal Prediction） 作为核心评估工具。该框架无需对数据分布做任何假设，即可为预测集提供有限样本覆盖保证（$P[Y \in C(X)] \geq 1 - \alpha$）。
  • 大规模统一基准： 覆盖 12 个大语言模型（参数量 1B–70B，横跨 Llama-2/3、Qwen-3、DeepSeek、Falcon 四个家族），在五种 NLP 任务上对比了四种压缩范式：
  • 权重量化 W4A16（RTN、AWQ、GPTQ）
  • 权重-激活量化 W4A4（QuaRot、SpinQuant、FlatQuant）
  • 非结构化剪枝 50%（Magnitude、SparseGPT、Wanda）
  • 结构化剪枝 20%（LLM-Pruner、SliceGPT）
  • 三大核心发现：
  1. 压缩常导致准确率与不确定性脱钩；
  2. 模型规模显著影响对压缩诱导不确定性的吸收能力（70B 模型的不确定性膨胀幅度可比 7B 模型小近 7 倍）；
  3. 不确定性的膨胀往往呈阈值式突变——在达到某一压缩强度临界点之前表现平稳，一旦越过临界点则急剧恶化。
  • 实践指南： 明确提出在模型压缩流程中应将不确定性评估作为标准步骤，并给出了面向安全关键场景的部署建议。

Key Contributions

  • Novel Evaluation Perspective: For the first time, systematically shifts the focus from accuracy preservation to uncertainty preservation under compression, filling a critical gap at the intersection of model compression and trustworthy AI.
  • Statistically Principled Framework: Employs Conformal Prediction as the core evaluation tool. This distribution-free framework provides finite-sample coverage guarantees ($P[Y \in C(X)] \geq 1 - \alpha$) without parametric assumptions on the model or data.
  • Large-Scale Unified Benchmark: Evaluates 12 LLMs (1B–70B parameters, spanning Llama-2/3, Qwen-3, DeepSeek, and Falcon families) on five NLP tasks across four compression paradigms:
  • Weight-only quantization W4A16 (RTN, AWQ, GPTQ)
  • Weight-activation quantization W4A4 (QuaRot, SpinQuant, FlatQuant)
  • Unstructured pruning at 50% sparsity (Magnitude, SparseGPT, Wanda)
  • Structured pruning at 20% sparsity (LLM-Pruner, SliceGPT)
  • Three Key Empirical Findings:
  1. Compression frequently decouples accuracy from uncertainty.
  2. Model scale strongly modulates uncertainty absorption (70B models exhibit ~7× less uncertainty inflation than 7B models under the same W4A4 regime).
  3. Uncertainty inflation is often threshold-like rather than gradual—stable up to a critical compression intensity, then deteriorating abruptly.
  • Actionable Recommendations: Advocates for integrating explicit uncertainty evaluation into standard compression pipelines and provides deployment guidance for safety-critical settings.

Methodology

Conformal Prediction Setup. The authors adopt split conformal prediction with a nonconformity score function $s(X, Y)$. Given a calibration set $\mathcal{D}_{\text{cal}}$ and a user-specified error rate $\alpha = 0.1$, a threshold $\hat{q}$ is computed as the $\lceil(1-\alpha)(n+1)\rceil/n$ quantile of calibration scores. At test time, the prediction set is $C(X_{\text{test}}) = \{y \in \mathcal{Y} : s(X_{\text{test}}, y) \leq \hat{q}\}$. Two score functions are averaged to ensure robustness:

  • LAC (Least Ambiguous set-valued Classifiers): $s_{\text{LAC}}(X, Y) = 1 - \hat{\pi}(Y|X)$.
  • APS (Adaptive Prediction Sets): accumulates sorted class probabilities until the cumulative mass exceeds $\hat{q}$.

Tasks & Datasets. Five tasks from Ye et al. (2024) are used, all formulated as six-option multiple-choice questions to enable conformal prediction:

  • Question Answering: MMLU
  • Reading Comprehension: CosmosQA
  • Commonsense Inference: HellaSwag
  • Dialogue Response Selection: HaluDial
  • Document Summarization: HaluSum

For HaluDial and HaluSum (originally binary), two random distractors are added, plus "I don't know" and "None of the above" for all datasets. Due to computational cost, 2,000 instances per dataset are randomly sampled (50% calibration, 50% test).

Models. 12 models across four families: Llama-2 (7B, 13B, 70B), Llama-3 (1B, 8B, 70B), Qwen-3 (8B, 14B, 32B, 30B-A3B MoE), DeepSeek-7B, Falcon-7B.

Compression Configurations.

  • Quantization: W4A16 (weight-only to 4-bit: RTN, AWQ, GPTQ) and W4A4 (weight+activation to 4-bit: QuaRot, SpinQuant, FlatQuant).
  • Pruning: Unstructured at 50% (Magnitude, SparseGPT, Wanda) and structured at 20% (LLM-Pruner, SliceGPT).

Evaluation Metrics.

  • Prediction Accuracy (Acc): proportion of correct top-1 predictions.
  • Coverage Rate (CR): sanity check that $CR \geq 1 - \alpha$ (target 90%).
  • Set Size (SS): average number of labels in the prediction set—the primary uncertainty metric. Smaller SS = higher confidence; larger SS = greater uncertainty.

Results are averaged over LAC and APS, as well as three prompting strategies (base, shared instruction, task-specific instruction). Experiments run on 8× NVIDIA H100 GPUs.

Results & Highlights

Overall Results (Table 1)

  • W4A16 weight-only quantization is the most uncertainty-preserving regime. For example, Llama2-70B under AWQ achieves 73.20% Acc and 2.18 SS, nearly identical to the FP16 baseline (72.48% Acc, 2.17 SS). Qwen3-8B under AWQ also stays close to its dense baseline.
  • W4A4 weight-activation quantization is substantially more disruptive. On Llama2-7B, QuaRot drops Acc from 47.09% to 22.04% while inflating SS from 3.09 to 5.65. However, Llama2-70B under the same setting retains 66.49% Acc with SS 2.54, underscoring the scale effect.
  • Pruning is more heterogeneous. Under 50% unstructured pruning, Magnitude pruning is consistently the weakest. SparseGPT and Wanda better preserve metrics but remain scale-sensitive: Wanda yields 66.63% Acc and 2.42 SS on Llama2-70B, but drops to 31.13% Acc and 3.58 SS on Llama2-7B. Structured pruning at 20% is volatile—LLM-Pruner works well on larger models (e.g., Llama3.1-70B), while SliceGPT is unstable on Qwen3-8B (degenerate output with CR ≈ 12% and SS = 1.00).

Finding 1: Accuracy–Uncertainty Decoupling (§5.2)

Compression does not degrade accuracy and uncertainty in tandem. The paper plots $\Delta\text{Acc}$ vs. $\Delta\text{SS}$ across all methods and tasks and finds wide dispersion rather than a compact negative-slope trend. Two canonical patterns emerge:

  1. Accuracy preserved, uncertainty inflated: Llama2-13B on Commonsense Inference with W4A16 AWQ slightly improves accuracy (59.63% → 61.17%) yet SS increases (2.83 → 3.13).
  2. Accuracy drops sharply, uncertainty barely changes: Llama3.1-8B under 50% Wanda pruning sees QA accuracy plummet from 62.93% to 45.97%, while SS changes only from 2.98 to 3.09.

This confirms that accuracy alone is insufficient for uncertainty-sensitive deployment.

Finding 2: Scale Absorbs Compression (§5.3)

Model scale strongly buffers uncertainty inflation. Under W4A4 QuaRot, the average SS increase across five tasks is approximately +2.56 for Llama2-7B, +0.72 for Llama2-13B, and +0.37 for Llama2-70B—a roughly 6.9× reduction in uncertainty inflation from 7B to 70B. The same trend holds under pruning: with 50% Wanda on Reading Comprehension, SS increases are +1.02 (7B), +0.23 (13B), and +0.15 (70B). Notably, Llama3.1-70B on RC under Wanda even shows a slight SS decrease (1.61 → 1.56). However, scale is not absolute immunity: Llama3.1-70B under Magnitude pruning still shows a large CI SS increase (1.89 → 3.25).

Finding 3: Threshold-Like Inflation (§5.4)

Uncertainty trajectories under increasing Wanda pruning sparsity (0%–50%) are often non-linear. For Llama3.1-8B on Reading Comprehension, SS stays within 1.89–2.06 from 0% to 40%, then jumps to 2.53 at 50%. On Commonsense Inference, it remains near 2.75–2.80 through 20%, then rises to 3.04 at 40% and 3.42 at 50%. For Llama2-7B on RC, the step from 40% to 50% alone increases SS by +0.59 (2.89 → 3.48), accounting for more than half of the total 0%–50% inflation. Larger models exhibit flatter trajectories (Llama2-70B RC changes only 1.79 → 1.94 across the full range), but even 70B models are not immune (Llama3.1-70B CI rises from 1.89 to 3.16 at 50%). This implies that a configuration stable at 30% sparsity may be dangerously close to a tipping point at 40% or 50%.

MoE Results (§5.5)

Qwen3-30B-A3B (Mixture-of-Experts) shows highly method-dependent behavior. W4A16 RTN is stable (Acc 45.64, SS 3.09 vs. dense 46.52/3.32), while AWQ degrades Acc to 37.17 and inflates SS to 3.59. GPTQ appears strong on average (Acc 48.40, SS 2.75) but has a degenerate entry on Document Summarization (CR 12.68%, SS 1.00). Structured pruning is the most polarized: LLM-Pruner at 20% improves both Acc and SS (74.33, 2.01), whereas SliceGPT collapses to 16.05 Acc and 5.57 SS. This shows that sparsity or bit-width alone is insufficient to predict uncertainty reliability in MoE architectures.

Precision Ablation (§5.6)

On Llama2-7B, W8A8 and W6A6 are relatively tolerable (W8A8 QuaRot: Acc 47.20, SS 3.33; W6A6 QuaRot: Acc 45.36, SS 3.35), but W4A4 SpinQuant causes sharp degradation (Acc 26.26, SS 4.52). This reinforces that uncertainty degradation is not gradual with precision—there is a damaging regime transition around 4-bit.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The reframing of compression evaluation around uncertainty preservation is genuinely novel and timely. While prior work has studied calibration under quantization (e.g., Zhong et al., ACL 2025), this is the first paper to apply conformal prediction systematically across both quantization and pruning paradigms for LLMs. The three empirical findings are memorable and potentially impactful for deployment pipelines.
  • Rigor: The methodology is sound. Split conformal prediction with two score functions (LAC and APS) averaged across three prompting strategies is a robust design. The 50/50 calibration-test split and fixed $\alpha=0.1$ are clearly stated. The authors also report Coverage Rate as a sanity check, which is good practice. However, split conformal prediction only guarantees marginal coverage (averaged over the distribution), not conditional coverage. The paper does not discuss whether certain subgroups or difficult instances experience systematically worse conditional coverage under compression—this could be an important gap for fairness-critical deployments.
  • Reproducibility: The benchmark is well-documented: exact models, compression methods, task datasets, prompting strategies, and evaluation protocol are all specified. The authors follow the established protocol of Ye et al. (2024), which aids comparability. Reproducibility would be further strengthened by release of code and calibration/test splits. The use of 2,000 sampled instances (rather than full datasets) due to computational cost is reasonable but introduces some sampling variance that is not quantified.
  • Significance: The findings have high practical relevance. The threshold-like nature of uncertainty inflation (Finding 3) is particularly surprising and actionable—it means that small increases in compression ratio can push a model from "safe" to "unreliable" without warning. The scale effect (Finding 2) provides a concrete justification for deploying larger compressed models over smaller dense ones in high-stakes settings, even when the smaller model meets accuracy targets.
  • Potential Weaknesses:
  • Task formulation limitation: All tasks are cast as multiple-choice classification. While this enables clean conformal prediction, it does not capture open-ended text generation where uncertainty manifests differently (e.g., semantic equivalence, hallucination). The authors acknowledge this as a limitation.
  • No open-ended generation: The "I don't know" and "None of the above" options, while useful for conformalization, are artificial constructs that do not exist in real-world generative deployment.
  • Fixed hyperparameters: The error rate $\alpha=0.1$ and 50/50 split are fixed; it is unclear how sensitive the findings are to these choices. For example, stricter coverage (e.g., $\alpha=0.05$) might reveal even stronger uncertainty inflation.
  • No comparison to heuristic UQ methods: The paper does not compare conformal prediction findings against simpler alternatives like temperature scaling or verbalized confidence, making it harder to argue that conformal prediction is uniquely necessary (as opposed to complementary).
  • Calibration overhead: The computational and memory cost of conformal prediction calibration at deployment time is not discussed. In resource-constrained edge settings where compression is most needed, maintaining a calibration set and computing quantiles may introduce non-trivial overhead.
  • MoE anomalies: The SliceGPT degenerate result on Qwen3-8B (CR ≈ 12%) and the GPTQ degenerate DS entry on Qwen3-30B-A3B suggest that some compression methods can break conformal validity entirely; the paper does not deeply analyze the mechanism behind these failures.

Strengths

  • Addresses a critical and genuinely underexplored dimension (uncertainty) in a very popular area (LLM compression).
  • Uses a statistically principled, model-agnostic tool (conformal prediction) with finite-sample guarantees rather than heuristic confidence scores.
  • Large empirical scope with specific, quantified examples supporting each claim.
  • Strong practical relevance for safety-critical and high-stakes LLM deployments.
  • Clear, actionable recommendations distilled from findings (e.g., evaluate across compression sweeps, prefer larger base models for aggressive compression).
  • Honest and explicit discussion of limitations, including the multiple-choice-only restriction and fixed hyperparameters.

Limitations

  • Multiple-choice only: All tasks are formulated as six-option multiple-choice questions. This enables conformal prediction but does not extend to open-ended text generation, where uncertainty manifests through semantic drift, hallucination, or sequence-level entropy.
  • Fixed parameters: The conformal error rate is fixed at $\alpha = 0.1$ and the calibration-test split at 50/50. Varying these may reveal additional sensitivity patterns.
  • Sampling variance: Due to computational constraints, only 2,000 instances per dataset are evaluated. The variance induced by this subsampling is not reported.
  • No heuristic UQ baseline: The paper does not compare against simpler uncertainty quantification methods (e.g., temperature scaling, maximum softmax probability, verbalized confidence), making it harder to assess whether conformal prediction is uniquely informative.
  • Calibration overhead not quantified: The runtime and memory cost of maintaining a calibration set and computing conformal quantiles at deployment time is not discussed, which is relevant for edge deployment scenarios.
  • Conditional coverage not examined: The paper reports marginal coverage rates but does not investigate whether compression disproportionately degrades coverage for difficult subgroups or rare classes.
  • Degenerate cases underexplained: Some configurations (e.g., SliceGPT on Qwen3-8B, GPTQ on Qwen3-30B-A3B DS) produce degenerate outputs with near-invalid coverage. The mechanism behind these failures is not deeply analyzed.
  • Limited to post-training compression: The benchmark focuses on post-training quantization (PTQ) and one-shot pruning. The impact of quantization-aware training (QAT) or fine-tuning after pruning on uncertainty preservation is not studied.

Relevance

本文直接击中了当前大模型压缩部署中最危险的盲区：一个量化或剪枝后的模型可能在准确率测试中表现“合格”，却在不确定性层面悄然失控。对于医疗诊断、法律推理、自动驾驶等安全关键场景，本文的实证发现和方法论框架具有极高的工程指导价值——它证明了仅凭准确率无法判断压缩模型的部署就绪状态，并给出了具体的评估协议和风险预警指标（Set Size 的阈值突变）。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Conditional coverage under compression: Do compressed models maintain valid conditional coverage across subgroups (e.g., rare classes, difficult instances), or does marginal coverage mask systematic undercoverage for certain inputs?
  2. Extension to open-ended generation: How can conformal prediction (or its extensions, e.g., conformal language modeling) be applied to free-form text generation under compression? Would the same scale effects and threshold behaviors hold?
  3. Quantization-aware training (QAT): Does fine-tuning or QAT mitigate the observed uncertainty degradation, or is post-training compression universally harmful to uncertainty calibration?
  4. Mechanism of degenerate failures: What causes SliceGPT and GPTQ to produce degenerate conformal outputs (invalid coverage, SS = 1.00) on certain models/tasks? Can this be predicted from weight/activation statistics prior to compression?
  5. Calibration overhead vs. compression savings: What is the runtime and memory cost of maintaining a calibration set and computing conformal quantiles at inference time? Is the overhead negligible compared to the gains from W4A16 quantization?
  6. Sensitivity to $\alpha$ and split ratio: How do the threshold-like uncertainty inflation patterns change under stricter error rates (e.g., $\alpha = 0.05$) or smaller calibration sets (e.g., 10% or 20%)?
  7. Comparison to heuristic UQ methods: Do simpler methods like temperature scaling or verbalized confidence exhibit the same decoupling and threshold phenomena, or is conformal prediction uniquely sensitive to compression artifacts?
  8. Dynamic compression policies: Can the observed threshold behaviors inform adaptive compression policies that dynamically reduce precision or sparsity when the model encounters inputs predicted to trigger high uncertainty?
