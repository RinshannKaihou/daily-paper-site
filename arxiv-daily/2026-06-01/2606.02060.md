> **一句话总结：** 本文构建了首个深度研究智能体跨度级错误定位基准TELBench（1,000实例），并提出基于声明追踪的审计框架DRIFT，通过构建声明账本、检查证据支持度和追踪依赖关系来定位有害错误跨度，在多个模型家族上将错误定位F1和首次错误准确率提升高达30个百分点。

> **TL;DR:** This paper introduces TELBench, a 1,000-instance benchmark for span-level error localization in deep-research agents built from 2,790 real trajectories, and proposes DRIFT, a claim-centric auditing framework that tracks agent claims, verifies their evidential support, and traces dependencies to localize harmful error spans, improving macro-F1 and first-error accuracy by up to 30 percentage points across multiple model families.

## 核心贡献

- **提出跨度级错误定位问题**：超越基于最终答案的评估范式，首次系统性地研究深度研究智能体长轨迹中具体哪个片段导致答案不可靠。
- **构建大规模轨迹语料库与TELBench基准**：收集并标注来自2个智能体框架（MiroFlow、OAgent）、3个基础模型（GPT-5、Gemini-2.5-Pro、Claude-Sonnet-4.5）和3个基准测试（GAIA-val、XBench、BrowseComp-test）的**2,790条真实轨迹**，通过LLM辅助的双人专家审核，筛选出**1,000个验证实例**（600易/400难），平均每轨迹11.95个语义跨度。
- **提出DRIFT声明中心审计框架**：包含**Claim Keeper**（构建声明账本）、**Support Seeker**（检查证据支持度：直接/弱/缺失/冲突）、**Dependency Tracer**（回溯依赖关系定位有害跨度）三个核心模块。
- **系统性机制分析**：归纳出6大错误家族、18种主要错误类型和8个操作阶段标签，揭示36.9%的成功轨迹也包含过程级错误，证明最终答案正确性与过程可靠性不等价。
- **显著实验提升**：DRIFT在5个模型家族上均取得最佳整体F1，相比裸LLM提示和通用智能体审计框架（Codex、Claude Code）提升高达**30个百分点**，且首次错误准确率亦有大幅提升。

## Key Contributions

- **Formulates span-level error localization**: Moves beyond final-answer evaluation to systematically study which specific segments in long deep-research trajectories make the answer unreliable.
- **Builds large-scale corpus and TELBench benchmark**: Collects and annotates **2,790 real trajectories** from 2 agent frameworks (MiroFlow, OAgent), 3 backbone models (GPT-5, Gemini-2.5-Pro, Claude-Sonnet-4.5), and 3 benchmarks (GAIA-val, XBench, BrowseComp-test). Through LLM-assisted dual expert review, constructs **TELBench with 1,000 verified instances** (600 easy / 400 hard), averaging 11.95 semantic spans per trajectory.
- **Proposes DRIFT claim-centric auditing framework**: Comprises three core modules — **Claim Keeper** (builds claim ledger tracking when claims are introduced, become consequential, and are reused), **Support Seeker** (assigns support statuses: direct, weak, missing, or conflicting), and **Dependency Tracer** (backtraces unsupported/conflicting claims to localize harmful spans).
- **Systematic mechanism analysis**: Induces 6 fault families, 18 primary fault types, and 8 operation-stage labels. Reveals that **36.9% of successful trajectories also contain process errors**, demonstrating that final-answer correctness is not equivalent to process reliability.
- **Strong empirical gains**: DRIFT achieves the best overall F1 across 5 model families, improving over bare LLM prompting and general agentic auditors (Codex, Claude Code) by up to **30 percentage points**, with substantial gains in first-error accuracy as well.

## Methodology

**Trajectory Collection and Span Segmentation.** The authors collect 2,790 long-form agent trajectories by running three frontier models (GPT-5, Gemini-2.5-Pro, Claude-Sonnet-4.5) under two representative frameworks (MiroFlow and OAgent) on three benchmarks (GAIA-val, XBench, and BrowseComp-test, with BrowseComp downsampled to 200 tasks). Raw logs are normalized and converted into **semantic spans** — contiguous segments around locally coherent objectives such as planning, retrieval, verification, comparison, or finalization. Boundaries are determined by changes in search target, candidate set, time scope, verification criterion, or reasoning objective.

**Error Annotation.** Each span receives a binary error/non-error label through a rigorous **LLM-assisted dual expert annotation** pipeline: two independent LLM annotators from different model families propose high-recall candidate errors with rationales, then two expert annotators (from a pool of seven, each spending 300+ hours) verify, revise, and adjudicate labels. An error span is defined as one that introduces, relies on, amplifies, or finalizes a mistaken, unsupported, contradicted, or prematurely committed judgment that affects the answer path. Normal exploration, failed searches, tentative hypotheses, recovered mistakes, and tool noise are **not** labeled as errors.

**Mechanism Labels.** After binary labels, each span receives an operation-stage label from an 8-stage schema (planning, retrieval, source verification, extraction, computation, decision-making, recovery, finalization). Error spans additionally receive one primary-fault label from an induced taxonomy of 18 faults grouped into 6 families. These labels are used only for analysis, not for evaluation inputs.

**TELBench Construction.** From 1,890 error-bearing trajectories (67.7% of the corpus), the authors filter for clear boundaries, trajectory-internal evidence, stable segmentation, and sufficient non-error distractors. This yields 1,000 verified instances split into 600 easy and 400 hard cases based on trajectory complexity and error subtlety.

**DRIFT Framework.** DRIFT audits trajectories through three claim-centric stages:
1. **Claim Keeper**: Reads the full trajectory and maintains a claim ledger recording each claim's introduction span, first consequential span, reusing spans, claim type (entity, constraint, evidence, retrieval, compute, process), and commitment status (exploratory, tentative, consequential, finalized).
2. **Support Seeker**: Checks whether each consequential claim is supported by trajectory evidence, assigning one of four statuses: **direct** (decisive link established), **weak** (partial/implicit/snippet-based), **missing** (no support shown), or **conflicting** (evidence contradicts the claim).
3. **Dependency Tracer**: Determines which risky claims correspond to harmful error spans by checking whether unsupported/conflicting claims are committed, propagated, computed from, or finalized in the answer. Marks spans that commit to, reuse, amplify, or finalize harmful claims as errors.

**Evaluation Setup.** The benchmark is evaluated across five model families (Qwen-series, GPT-5.4, DeepSeek-V3.2, Claude-Sonnet-4.6, Gemini-2.5-Pro) and four diagnostic frameworks (Bare LLM, Codex, Claude Code, DRIFT). Metrics include macro precision, recall, F1, and **first-error accuracy** (detecting the earliest predicted error).

## Results & Highlights

**DRIFT dominates across all models.** Table 2 and Figure 4 show DRIFT achieves the best overall macro-F1 across all backbone models. For example, with DeepSeek-V3.2, DRIFT achieves 50.51% F1 vs. Bare LLM's 22.46% — a **+28.05 pp** improvement. With Claude-Sonnet-4.6, DRIFT reaches 54.91% F1 vs. 21.89% Bare — a **+33.02 pp** gain. First-error accuracy similarly improves dramatically (e.g., Claude + DRIFT: 24.10% vs. Bare: 11.30%).

**Generic agentic frameworks are insufficient.** Surprisingly, Codex and Claude Code (adapted as general agentic auditors) bring **inconsistent gains** and can even **degrade** performance compared to bare LLM prompting. For DeepSeek-V3.2, Codex drops F1 from 22.46% to 12.05%. This demonstrates that simply wrapping an LLM in a more complex agentic workflow does not solve trajectory diagnosis — the claim-centric structure of DRIFT is essential.

**First-error localization remains challenging.** Despite strong F1 gains, first-error accuracy stays relatively low, especially on the hard split. This reveals that detecting erroneous regions and pinpointing the **earliest** error are distinct abilities. Current auditors can often tell a trajectory is unreliable, but struggle to identify where it first goes wrong.

**Scaling alone is insufficient.** Experiments across Qwen variants show that larger models do not consistently improve trajectory diagnosis, and the hard split remains challenging at all scales. The bottleneck is not just backbone capacity, but the **absence of diagnostic structure** tailored to long, noisy agent trajectories.

**Module ablation validates design.** Figure 5(c) shows performance improves steadily as DRIFT modules are added: Claim Keeper provides the largest gain, Support Seeker and Dependency Tracer further improve evidence grounding and span localization.

**Error-type coverage.** DRIFT improves recall consistently across nearly all 12 most frequent error types, with the **largest gains on evidence- and constraint-related failures** — exactly aligning with its claim-centric design.

**Efficiency.** DRIFT achieves a favorable efficiency-performance trade-off and mostly lies on the Pareto frontier, improving F1 without disproportionate token overhead.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** High. The paper addresses a genuinely underexplored and timely problem. While process-level evaluation has gained traction in math reasoning (ProcessBench) and coding traces (CodeTracer), span-level error localization specifically for deep-research agents — with their unique mixture of exploration, weak evidence, failed searches, and harmful mistakes — is novel. The claim-centric perspective (tracking when claims become consequential) is a conceptual advance over simple step-level or section-level error identification.

**Rigor:** Strong. The annotation methodology is rigorous: dual LLM pre-annotation followed by dual expert review and adjudication, with references to established inter-annotator agreement practices (Dligach & Palmer, 2011; Artstein & Poesio, 2008). The error taxonomy is data-induced rather than imposed. The controlled experimental setup (unified Serper search and Jina reading interfaces across frameworks) reduces confounding variables. The Verified-1K filtering criteria (clear boundaries, internal evidence, stable segmentation, sufficient distractors) are well-justified.

**Reproducibility:** Good. The benchmark dataset and DRIFT code are publicly released. The evaluation protocol is clearly specified: same input (question + ordered spans), same output format (error span indices), three repetitions per setting. However, the exact prompt templates for DRIFT are lengthy and complex (shown in Appendix), which may make exact reproduction challenging without the released code.

**Significance:** High. As deep-research agents are deployed in increasingly high-stakes settings, understanding *where* they fail is critical for improvement. The finding that 36.9% of successful trajectories contain process errors is particularly striking — it implies that current outcome-based evaluations may be systematically overestimating agent reliability.

**Limitations and red flags:**
1. **First-error accuracy remains low** even with DRIFT (e.g., 24.10% for Claude on overall), suggesting the problem is far from solved.
2. **Hard split performance** shows there is substantial room for improvement on subtle, long-horizon errors.
3. The **expert annotation cost** (2,100+ person-hours) may limit scalability to new domains or languages.
4. The **six claim types** (entity, constraint, evidence, retrieval, compute, process) are comprehensive but may not cover all failure modes in multi-modal or tool-rich settings.
5. The evaluation is **offline** — real-time auditing latency and cost are not deeply analyzed (though efficiency analysis suggests reasonable token costs).
6. The paper does not explore whether DRIFT's error localization insights can be **fed back to improve agent training** — it is currently an evaluation/diagnostic tool only.

## Strengths

- Addresses a critical, underexplored gap in agent evaluation with strong practical motivation
- Large-scale, high-quality data collection with rigorous dual-annotation and expert review
- Novel claim-centric conceptual framework that distinguishes exploration from harmful commitment
- Strong empirical results with comprehensive cross-model and cross-framework evaluation
- Honest reporting of limitations (first-error accuracy remains low, hard split is challenging)
- Controlled experimental setup reducing confounding from retrieval/reading tools
- Public release of both benchmark and code
- Rich mechanism analysis revealing stage-dependent fault patterns and setting-specific fingerprints

## Limitations

- **First-error localization is still poor**: Even DRIFT's best first-error accuracy is only ~24% overall, meaning auditors usually miss where the trajectory first goes wrong.
- **Hard cases remain unsolved**: The 400 hard instances show only modest gains over baselines, suggesting the most subtle errors are still beyond current methods.
- **Annotation scalability**: The heavy reliance on expert annotators (300+ hours each) makes it difficult to scale TELBench to new domains or languages.
- **No training-time application**: DRIFT is purely diagnostic; the paper does not show how to use its outputs to train better agents.
- **Limited real-time analysis**: While token efficiency is analyzed, latency and streaming applicability for live agent monitoring are not deeply explored.
- **Claim taxonomy scope**: The six claim types may not fully capture failures in highly multi-modal or specialized tool-use scenarios.
- **Gemini efficiency outlier**: Gemini's DRIFT run incurs unusually high token costs due to extensive "thinking" tokens, suggesting framework-specific tuning may be needed.

## Relevance

本文对深度研究智能体的可靠性评估提供了开创性的过程级视角，揭示了最终答案正确并不等于过程可靠这一关键事实。TELBench和DRIFT为智能体开发者提供了系统化的诊断工具，对当前AI智能体在高风险场景下的部署与改进具有重要的实践指导意义，也为过程级奖励模型和智能体自我修正研究奠定了基础。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can DRIFT's error localization outputs be used as reward signals or fine-tuning data to train more reliable deep-research agents?
- How well would DRIFT generalize to other agent paradigms such as embodied agents, software engineering agents, or multi-agent debate systems?
- What is the real-time latency of running DRIFT auditing during live agent execution, and can it be made fast enough for interactive use?
- Can the claim-centric framework be adapted to detect errors *before* they become consequential, enabling proactive intervention rather than post-hoc diagnosis?
- How does human performance on TELBench compare to DRIFT? The paper does not report human baseline results.
- Can the annotation pipeline be automated further to scale TELBench to new languages, domains, or agent frameworks without 300+ hours of expert time?
- What happens when DRIFT audits trajectories generated by the *same* model family used for its own components? Are there systematic blind spots?
