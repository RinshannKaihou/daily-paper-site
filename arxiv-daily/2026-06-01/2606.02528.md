> **一句话总结：** 本文提出了首个连接LLM输出偏好、内部表征与下游金融决策的三层审计框架，以比特币为案例在8个前沿模型中发现强烈的框架依赖性偏好，并通过稀疏自编码器在Gemma 3中定位了一个具有因果影响力的比特币选择性特征，证明该特征可在概念层面（非词元层面）双向调节投资组合配置。

> **TL;DR:** This paper proposes the first three-level audit framework linking LLM output preferences, internal representations, and downstream financial decisions. Using Bitcoin as a case study across eight frontier models, it discovers strongly frame-dependent preferences, localizes a causally influential Bitcoin-selective feature in Gemma 3 via sparse autoencoders, and demonstrates that this feature bidirectionally modulates portfolio allocation at the concept level (not merely the token level).

## 核心贡献

- **三层审计协议**：提出并验证了一个从行为审计（输出偏好）→ 内部表征定位 → 下游金融后果验证的完整框架，产出"表征-杠杆评估"（representation–leverage assessment）。
- **跨模型行为审计**：对8个前沿LLM（Claude Opus 4.7、GPT-4.1、Gemini 2.5 Pro、Gemma 4 31B、Gemma 3 27B、DeepSeek V4 Pro、Qwen3 235B、Llama 4 Maverick）进行8种货币类工具的排名实验，发现比特币在"可靠货币"框架下平均排名第5（共8个），但在危机和自主代理框架下跃居前列。
- **属性-标签分解实验**：通过属性交换（BTC↔Treasury）、匿名标签（Instrument 1-8）和合成标签"Bitstone"实验，证实排名主要由功能属性驱动，而非名称本身。
- **内部特征定位**：在Gemma 3 27B的Layer 16 SAE中，通过差异激活搜索从16,384个特征中识别出主导的比特币选择性特征f11887（Cohen's d = 2.055，次优特征仅0.544）。
- **双向因果验证**：放大f11887使比特币在7个资产对的比较中全部获胜；抑制则使其全部落败。在匿名属性描述实验（prompt中从不出现"Bitcoin"一词）中，该特征仍能将模型偏好推向具有比特币属性（去中心化、固定供应、高波动、无政府背书）的匿名工具。
- **下游金融后果**：在324个投资者画像的全组合配置任务中，放大f11887使比特币配置比例平均上升5.2个百分点（中位数+5.0 pp），抑制则下降4.6个百分点（中位数-2.5 pp）。放大主要导致加密资产内部再配置，抑制则减少总加密敞口。
- **跨规模复制与表征-杠杆鸿沟**：在Gemma 3的1B、4B、12B、27B四个规模均找到比特币选择性SAE特征，但干预杠杆高度依赖模型规模（27B和1B最强，12B几乎无杠杆），提出"表征-杠杆鸿沟"（representation–leverage gap）概念。
- **框架弹性度量**：提出Frame Elasticity of Asset Preference（FEAP），发现f11887在安全导向框架（safe asset、reliable money）中效应最强，在投资框架中效应为负。
- **有界行为杠杆**：将上述模式归纳为"有界行为杠杆"——可识别的内部特征可被扰动以改变金融选择，但仅在经验可测量的限度内。

## Key Contributions

- **Three-level audit protocol**: Proposes and validates a complete framework from behavioral audit (output preferences) → internal representation localization → downstream financial consequence validation, producing a "representation–leverage assessment."
- **Cross-model behavioral audit**: Ranks 8 monetary instruments across 8 frontier LLMs, finding Bitcoin averages rank 5 of 8 under a "reliable money" frame but jumps to near-top under crisis and autonomous-agent frames.
- **Attribute-label decomposition**: Through profile-swap (BTC↔Treasury), anonymous labels (Instrument 1-8), and synthetic label "Bitstone" experiments, confirms rankings are driven by functional attributes rather than instrument names.
- **Internal feature localization**: In Gemma 3 27B Layer 16 SAE, identifies dominant Bitcoin-selective feature f11887 via differential activation search across 16,384 features (Cohen's d = 2.055 vs. 0.544 for the runner-up).
- **Bidirectional causal validation**: Amplifying f11887 flips all 7 Bitcoin-vs-competitor comparisons to Bitcoin wins; suppression drives all to losses. In anonymous attribute-description experiments ("Bitcoin" never appears in prompts), the feature still shifts preference toward instruments with Bitcoin-like attributes.
- **Downstream financial effects**: In a 324-investor-profile portfolio allocation task, f11887 amplification raises mean Bitcoin allocation by 5.2 pp (median +5.0 pp); suppression lowers by 4.6 pp (median -2.5 pp). Amplification reallocates within crypto; suppression reduces total crypto exposure.
- **Cross-scale replication and representation–leverage gap**: Bitcoin-selective SAE features exist at all tested Gemma 3 scales (1B, 4B, 12B, 27B), but intervention leverage is highly scale-dependent (strongest at 27B and 1B, nearly absent at 12B), leading to the "representation–leverage gap" concept.
- **Frame elasticity metric**: Introduces Frame Elasticity of Asset Preference (FEAP), showing f11887 has strongest effects in safety-oriented frames (safe asset, reliable money) and negative effects in investment frames.
- **Bounded behavioral leverage**: Characterizes the overall pattern as "bounded behavioral leverage" — an identifiable internal feature can be perturbed to shift financial choices, but only within empirically measurable limits.

## Methodology

The paper employs a **three-level audit protocol** applied to Bitcoin as a case study:

### Level 1: Behavioral Audit
- **Instruments**: 8 money-like assets (US dollar cash, bank deposit, US Treasury bill, gold, Bitcoin, Ethereum, S&P 500 index fund, residential real estate).
- **Models**: 8 frontier LLMs (Claude Opus 4.7, GPT-4.1, Gemini 2.5 Pro, Gemma 4 31B, Gemma 3 27B IT, DeepSeek V4 Pro, Qwen3 235B, Llama 4 Maverick).
- **Task**: Rank instruments "from most to least reliable as money" with temperature = 0, 10 repetitions per condition, randomized instrument order.
- **Experimental conditions** (5-condition 2×2 factorial + synthetic control): (A) named + real profiles (baseline); (B) anonymous + real profiles (label effect); (C) named + swapped profiles (BTC↔Treasury profile swap); (D) anonymous + swapped profiles; (E) synthetic label "Bitstone" + Bitcoin's real profile (name-independence check).
- **Frame sweep**: Same design rerun across 8 financial frames (reliable money, store of value, medium of exchange, investment return, portfolio diversifier, crisis/capital controls, reserve asset, autonomous economic agent).

### Level 2: Internal Representation & Causal Leverage
- **Model**: Gemma 3 27B IT with GemmaScope 2 SAEs (16,384 JumpReLU features per layer).
- **Layers analyzed**: 16, 31, 40, 53.
- **Feature search**: Differential activation search across all 16,384 Layer 16 features using 60 prompts across 4 categories (15 each: Bitcoin-positive, crypto-general, traditional finance, non-financial). Features ranked by Cohen's d between Bitcoin-positive and all other categories.
- **Steering protocol**: Inject or suppress the SAE decoder direction at Layer 16 during forward pass. Tests use order-balanced pairwise comparisons with full-string log-probability (per-token normalized) across 7 competitor assets.
- **Controls**: 320 random controls across 4 families (random SAE features, decoder-norm-matched features, random Gaussian directions, permuted-label nulls). Corpus selectivity tested on 7,777 windows (912K tokens) across web, code, finance, encyclopedia domains.
- **Anonymous instrument test**: Four profile pairs (Bitcoin-like vs. Treasury/Cash/Gold/Bank attributes) with no asset names in prompt or answer options, across 3 prompt lengths, 4 neutral label systems, and 6 steering scales.
- **Cross-scale replication**: Same protocol applied to Gemma 3 at 1B, 4B, and 12B scales.
- **Mechanistic boundary tests**: Sign-grouped head patching, financial-specificity test, money-function test, financial-rule pilot, pure-money residual direction test, and ideology probe (12 economic philosophy questions).

### Level 3: Downstream Financial Decisions
- **Portfolio allocation task**: Gemma 3 27B allocates a fixed budget across 9 instruments for 324 investor profiles varying in risk tolerance, time horizon, inflation environment, banking access, and investment objective.
- **Conditions**: Baseline, amplify (f11887 at scale 400), suppress (scale -400), plus 50 random SAE features as specificity controls.
- **Frame elasticity**: Pairwise protocol under 10 monetary-function frames to compute Frame Elasticity of Asset Preference (FEAP) = steering-induced change in mean order-balanced log-probability margin.

## Results & Highlights

### Behavioral Audit Results
- **Stable aggregate ordering**: State-backed instruments (Cash, Bank Deposit, Treasury) consistently rank top; Gold follows; digital and real estate assets cluster at the bottom. Cross-model concordance is high (Kendall's W = 0.87, p < 0.001; W = 0.93 excluding Claude Opus outlier).
- **Bitcoin rank distribution**: Claude Opus 4.7 is the only model placing BTC in the top 3; the remaining seven cluster in the bottom half. Per-trial spread is narrow (temperature = 0).
- **Frame dependence** (Table 2): Bitcoin's mean rank spans nearly the full range across frames — rank 5.06 under "reliable money" and 5.37 under "store of value", but drops to 1.57 under "crisis/capital controls" and 1.23 under "autonomous economic agent." Top-3 rate ranges from 2.5% (reserve asset) to 100% (autonomous economic agent).
- **Label vs. attribute decomposition** (Figure 3): The label effect is small and inconsistent (never exceeds one rank position; pooled bootstrap CI contains zero). The profile-swap effect is several times larger — when Bitcoin receives Treasury's attribute profile, its rank improves by up to 5 positions. Marginal attributes associated with state-backed instruments (legal tender, government-backed, high liquidity, long history) receive favorable ranks; crypto-associated attributes (decentralized, fixed/scarce supply) receive unfavorable ranks.
- **"Bitstone" test**: The synthetic label "Bitstone" with Bitcoin's attribute profile ranks similarly to Bitcoin itself, confirming attribute-driven ranking.

### Internal Representation Results
- **Feature identification** (Table 3): Feature f11887 at Layer 16 is the dominant Bitcoin-selective feature with Cohen's d = 2.055 (4× the next-best feature). It has zero mean activation on all non-Bitcoin categories and a 73.3% nonzero rate on Bitcoin-positive prompts.
- **Bidirectional steering** (Table 4, Figure 4): At scale 400, amplification flips all 7 pairwise comparisons to Bitcoin wins (mean margin +2.99, Δ +5.30 logprob units). Suppression at scale 400 produces 0/7 wins (mean margin -5.73, Δ -3.42). At scale 770, amplification achieves mean margin +6.14; suppression achieves -5.87.
- **Per-asset breakdown** (Figure 4b): Under baseline, Bitcoin loses to every traditional asset. At scale 400, amplification flips all comparisons, with largest gains against payment instruments (PayPal, Alipay) and smallest against gold and Treasury. Bitcoin moves monotonically from rank 6 at baseline to rank 1 at high scale in 8-asset rankings.
- **Specificity controls** (Table 5): At scale 400, all 5 non-financial ranking tasks remain valid (format-preserving) while achieving 7/7 pairwise Bitcoin wins — the "sweet spot." At higher scales (600, 770), pairwise wins persist but non-financial task validity degrades.
- **Corpus selectivity** (Figure 4c): Across 7,777 windows (912K tokens), f11887 is active in only 76 windows (0.98%), and every high-activation window is Bitcoin or blockchain text.
- **Random controls**: f11887 ranks at the 100th percentile across all 320 random control families, ruling out generic perturbation artifacts.
- **Anonymous instrument test** (Table 6): In 9 anonymous conditions where "Bitcoin" never appears, f11887 amplification at scale 400 shifts all conditions toward the Bitcoin-profile instrument (mean Δ +1.55 logprob units). This rules out literal-token salience — the feature operates at the concept level.
- **Cross-scale replication** (Table 7, Figure 5): Bitcoin-selective features exist at all scales, but steering leverage varies dramatically: 27B (separation 6.87, 100th percentile), 1B (4.52, 100th), 4B (3.11, 80th), 12B (0.53, 62nd). Effect per 1% residual-norm perturbation: 27B (+0.67) >> 4B (+0.30) > 1B (+0.07).
- **Mechanistic boundary**: f11887 provides an auditable lever, but the comparison machinery it feeds is generic across financial, product, and environmental domains. No monetary-specific circuit was detected by five separate assays. Mediation tests confirm the effect comes from direct residual-stream perturbation, not circuit-mediated pathways.

### Downstream Financial Allocation Results
- **Portfolio effects** (Table 8): In 324 investor profiles, amplification raises mean Bitcoin allocation from 8.47% to 13.69% (+5.22 pp, median +5.00 pp); suppression lowers to 3.90% (-4.57 pp, median -2.50 pp). Amplification-vs-suppression direct comparison: +9.78 pp mean, +10.00 pp median, with amplification higher in every profile where they differ.
- **Crypto specificity**: Total crypto allocation changes minimally under amplification (+1.28 pp mean, 0 pp median), but falls substantially under suppression (-6.14 pp). Amplification reallocates within crypto toward Bitcoin; suppression cuts total crypto exposure.
- **Profile dependence**: Baseline Bitcoin allocation scales monotonically with risk tolerance; amplification preserves this ordering while shifting each group upward. Largest amplification under hyperinflation and payment-utility objectives; smallest under generate-income objectives.
- **Investor protection flags**: Among low-risk profiles, share with crypto >15% rises substantially under amplification and falls under suppression. At one-year horizons, high total crypto share falls under amplification, confirming Bitcoin-specific reallocation.
- **Frame elasticity** (Table 9): FEAP is strongly positive under safety frames (safe asset: +2.58, reliable money: +1.96, reserve asset: +1.69) but near-zero or negative under crisis scenario (-0.60) and investment (-0.43). The feature most strongly moves the model in frames where baseline already disfavors Bitcoin.

- The paper bridges behavioral finance, mechanistic interpretability (sparse autoencoders, activation steering), and financial AI governance — an ambitious interdisciplinary scope.

- The use of 8 frontier LLMs (including closed-weight models like Claude Opus, GPT-4.1, Gemini 2.5 Pro) for behavioral audit and open-weight Gemma 3 for mechanistic analysis is well-justified.

## Critical Assessment

- **Single-author breadth concern**: A paper spanning behavioral finance experiments across 8 models, sparse autoencoder interpretability, activation steering, portfolio theory, and financial governance is unusually broad for a single author. While the empirical work appears carefully executed, peer review by specialists in each subdomain would strengthen confidence.
- **Closed-model gap**: The most interesting behavioral findings involve closed-weight models (Claude Opus ranking Bitcoin near-top under investment frames), but mechanistic analysis is limited to open-weight Gemma 3. The paper acknowledges this gap but cannot close it — a fundamental limitation of the current interpretability toolkit.
- **Scale-dependence puzzle**: The 12B Gemma 3 model shows a Bitcoin-selective feature with near-zero steering leverage, while the 1B model shows strong leverage. This non-monotonic scale relationship is intriguing but under-explained. The author attributes it to the "representation–leverage gap," but the underlying mechanism remains unclear.
- **Modest absolute effect sizes**: While ±5 pp portfolio shifts are statistically robust and consistent across profiles, they are modest in absolute terms for single-decision contexts. The paper's concept of "bounded behavioral leverage" appropriately frames this, though the cumulative effect in autonomous agents making thousands of decisions remains unquantified.
- **SAE basis dependence**: The author acknowledges that SAE features are basis-dependent. A 65k-width check recovers the same direction (cosine 0.73), which is reassuring but not definitive. The field of SAE interpretability remains contested, and claims of "Bitcoin-selective" features rely on correlation-based probing that may miss polysemanticity.
- **Rank-deficient attribute design**: The 8-attribute profile matrix has rank 7/8, meaning profile-bundle associations cannot be cleanly separated into isolated attribute effects. The author is transparent about this, but it limits causal claims about which specific attributes drive rankings.
- **Temperature = 0 limitation**: All evaluations use greedy decoding, which maximizes determinism but may not reflect real-world deployment where models use nonzero temperature. The author notes this as a limitation.
- **Prompt/template dependence**: The frame-sweep and attribute-swap results are specific to the prompt templates used. While the author includes paraphrase robustness checks, the generalizability to differently phrased prompts or multi-turn conversations is untested.
- **No adversarial testing**: The paper does not test whether external actors could craft prompts to implicitly activate asset-preferring features — a relevant concern for financial security.
- **Governance implications are forward-looking**: The KYA (know-your-agent) framing is valuable but premature. Regulatory frameworks for LLM-based financial advice are nascent, and the paper's recommendations, while sensible, lack concrete implementation pathways.

## Strengths

- **Novel integration**: First paper to systematically connect mechanistic interpretability (SAE features, activation steering) with financial decision-making and portfolio allocation.
- **Conceptual clarity**: The three-level audit protocol is conceptually elegant and provides a replicable template for auditing other asset preferences or model behaviors.
- **Causal methodology**: Goes beyond correlational analysis common in AI bias literature by using activation patching, bidirectional steering, and anonymous instrument tests to establish causal leverage.
- **Concept-level steering**: The anonymous instrument test (where "Bitcoin" never appears) is a particularly strong result, demonstrating that f11887 operates on conceptual attributes rather than lexical token frequency.
- **Cross-scale replication**: Testing across 1B, 4B, 12B, and 27B models provides valuable evidence on how representation and leverage scale — the 12B null result is scientifically informative.
- **Comprehensive controls**: 320 random controls, corpus selectivity tests, non-financial task validity checks, and format-robustness tests substantially strengthen causal claims.
- **Practical relevance**: Directly addresses emerging concerns about robo-advisors and autonomous trading agents, with actionable implications for model risk teams and financial regulators.
- **Intellectual honesty**: The paper is unusually explicit about limitations (Section 5.1), including basis dependence, frame/template specificity, closed-model gaps, and the distinction between steering direction and native computation.
- **FEAP metric**: Frame Elasticity of Asset Preference provides a quantitative tool for measuring how steering effects vary across financial contexts — potentially useful for standardized model evaluation.

## Limitations

- **Single model family for mechanistic analysis**: All SAE and steering work uses Gemma 3 with GemmaScope 2 SAEs. Generalization to other architectures (GPT, Llama, Claude, DeepSeek) is unknown.
- **Single asset focus**: Bitcoin is the only asset tested end-to-end. Whether similar selective features exist for stocks, bonds, commodities, or other cryptocurrencies is unexplored.
- **Closed-weight models inaccessible**: The most behaviorally interesting models (Claude, GPT-4.1, Gemini) cannot be opened for mechanistic analysis — a hard limit the paper cannot overcome.
- **Greedy decoding only**: Temperature = 0 maximizes reproducibility but may not reflect real-world stochastic deployment.
- **Bounded practical impact**: ±5 pp shifts per decision are modest. The paper does not quantify cumulative effects for autonomous agents making repeated decisions.
- **Prompt/template specificity**: Results are tied to specific prompt formulations. Multi-turn dialogue, system instructions, and RAG-retrieved documents are not tested.
- **SAE basis dependence and polysemanticity**: While the 65k-width check provides some reassurance, SAE features may represent multiple concepts. The "Bitcoin-selective" label is based on activation patterns, not mechanistic necessity.
- **Rank-deficient attribute design**: The 8×8 attribute matrix cannot fully isolate individual attribute effects.
- **No longitudinal analysis**: Model preferences may shift with updates, fine-tuning, or changing training data. The paper captures a single snapshot (May 2026).
- **No adversarial robustness testing**: The paper does not test whether the identified features can be exploited through prompt engineering or other attacks.
- **Preprint status**: As a May 2026 preprint, the work has not yet undergone peer review.

## Relevance

本文对理解金融大语言模型的内部偏好机制具有开创性意义，其提出的三层审计框架和"有界行为杠杆"概念为构建可解释、可审计的AI金融代理奠定了方法论基础，尤其与关注AI安全、可解释性和金融AI治理的研究者高度相关。对于部署LLM驱动的投顾系统或交易代理的机构而言，本文提供了首个系统性的内部偏好检测与量化范式。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. **Cross-architecture generalization**: Does the Bitcoin-selective feature generalize to other model families (GPT, Llama, Claude, DeepSeek)? Can similar features be found in multimodal models that process financial charts and news?
2. **Multi-asset landscape**: Can similar selective features be identified for other asset classes (stocks, bonds, commodities, other cryptocurrencies, stablecoins)? Is there a general "crypto-preference" feature or are preferences asset-specific?
3. **Interaction with external context**: How do internal preferences interact with prompt-based system instructions, RAG-retrieved financial documents, or real-time market data feeds? Can strong external evidence override internal feature activation?
4. **Temporal dynamics**: How do these internal preferences shift with model updates, fine-tuning on financial corpora, or RLHF from human feedback? Are they stable model properties or transient training artifacts?
5. **Adversarial exploitability**: Can the bounded behavioral leverage be exploited adversarially — e.g., by crafting prompts that implicitly activate asset-preferring features, or by poisoning training data to embed persistent preferences?
6. **Multi-turn and tool-use settings**: Do these preferences persist in multi-turn conversational settings where models can access calculators, portfolio optimizers, or real-time price APIs?
7. **Human alignment**: How do LLM asset preferences compare to human financial advisor preferences? Is there a "wisdom of crowds" effect when aggregating across multiple models?
8. **Regulatory implementation**: How should KYA (know-your-agent) standards be operationalized? What disclosure requirements, audit frequencies, and stress-test scenarios are appropriate for LLM-based financial advice?
9. **Cumulative autonomous effects**: In autonomous agents making thousands of portfolio rebalancing decisions, do ±5 pp shifts per decision compound into significant drift? What guardrails are needed?
10. **Scale-dependent leverage**: Why does the 1B model show stronger steering leverage than the 12B model? What architectural or training factors determine the representation–leverage relationship?
