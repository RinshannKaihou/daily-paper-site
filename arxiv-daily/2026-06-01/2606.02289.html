DECK: A Consistency × Confidence Taxonomy of LLM Hallucinations

Mohit Singh Chauhan

arXiv: 2606.02289

Date: 2026-06-01

Tags: ["cs.CL", "hallucination", "uncertainty-quantification", "taxonomy"]

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.02289

一句话总结： 本文提出了一种新的LLM幻觉分类法 DECK，根据跨样本一致性和token级置信度将幻觉分为四种可检测类型（漂移、固化、臆造、纠结），并发现当模型对知识缺口输入产生自信且可重复的虚构内容时，所有基于输出的不确定性量化方法都从根本上失效。
TL;DR: This paper proposes the DECK taxonomy, a 2×2 framework that classifies LLM hallucinations by their detectability signature along inter-sample consistency and token-level confidence, mapping each cell to specific uncertainty scorer families and revealing a universal blind spot when models emit confident, repeatable fabrications on knowledge-gap inputs.

核心贡献

  • 互补性分类框架：现有分类法按"输出出了什么问题"来分类（记忆错误、推理失败、流畅虚构），而 DECK 按"哪种不确定性评分器能检测到此错误"来分类，填补了诊断与检测之间的空白。
  • DECK 四象限模型：以跨样本一致性（consistency）和 token 级置信度（confidence）为轴，将幻觉行为划分为四类：
  • Drift（漂移，D）：不一致且高置信度——每次采样产生不同的自信错误答案，黑盒一致性评分器和 Judge 可检测
  • Entrenched（固化，E）：一致且高置信度——模型锁定在记忆错误或预训练共识错误上，每次采样重复同样的自信错误答案，只有具备独立预训练的 LLM-as-a-Judge 能检测
  • Confabulation（臆造，C）：不一致且低置信度——模型确实不知道，不同采样产生不同的低概率错误答案，三种评分器家族均可检测
  • Knotted（纠结，K）：一致且低置信度——模型持续不确定，每次都给出同样的低概率答案，白盒 token 概率评分器和 Judge 可检测
  • 操作化定义：通过 Youden's J 最优切分在每个评分器轴上确定象限归属，使分类具有可计算性和可复现性。
  • 实验验证：在三个模型（Llama-3-8B、GPT-4o、Gemini-2.5-Flash）和四个数据集（TriviaQA、HaluEval、SelfAware、PopQA）上，通过评分器对分歧分析以及外部标签验证分类法的预测准确性。
  • 发现普遍盲区：当生成器对知识缺口输入（如 SelfAware 的不可回答问题）发出自信、可重复的虚构内容时，所有基于输出层的不确定性量化方法（黑盒一致性、白盒 token 概率、LLM-as-a-Judge）同时失效。
  • 激活层探索：对 Llama-3-8B 最后一层隐藏状态训练的逻辑回归探测（in-distribution AUROC 0.72）在 SelfAware 上崩溃至随机水平（AUROC 0.44，95% CI [0.35, 0.53]），暗示该盲区可能延伸到内部表示层面。

Key Contributions

  • Complementary Taxonomy: Existing taxonomies classify by what is wrong with the output; DECK classifies by which uncertainty scorer would catch it, bridging the gap between diagnosis and detection.
  • DECK 2×2 Framework: Partitions hallucination behaviors along inter-sample consistency and token-level confidence into four regimes:
  • Drift (D): Inconsistent and high-confidence — different confident wrong answer each sample; detectable by black-box consistency scorers and Judge
  • Entrenched (E): Consistent and high-confidence — model locked onto a memorised misconception or shared-pretraining error, reproducing it without variance; detectable only by LLM-as-a-Judge with independent pretraining
  • Confabulation (C): Inconsistent and low-confidence — classic uncertainty where the model genuinely does not know; detectable by all three scorer families
  • Knotted (K): Consistent and low-confidence — model consistently unsure, settling on the same hedged answer each time; detectable by white-box token-probability scorers and Judge
  • Operationalised Cell Membership: Uses Youden's J optimal split on each scorer axis to make cell assignment computable and reproducible, base-rate robust.
  • Empirical Validation: Validates the taxonomy across three models and four datasets via scorer-pair disagreement analysis (hallucination-restricted complementarity CH) and by checking that external labels (SelfAware unanswerable, HaluEval adversarial, PopQA entity popularity) land in predicted DECK cells.
  • Universal Blind Spot Discovery: On knowledge-gap inputs where the generator emits confident, repeatable fabrications, every output-level uncertainty quantification family collapses by construction — a property of the (generator, task) pair, not any specific scorer.
  • Activation-Level Exploration: A logistic probe on Llama-3-8B's last-layer hidden states (calibrated in-distribution at AUROC 0.72) collapses to chance on SelfAware (AUROC 0.44, CI-overlapping chance), suggesting the failure may persist even at the internal representation level.

Methodology

Taxonomy Construction. The DECK framework is built on two observationally independent axes: (1) inter-sample consistency — whether the model produces the same incorrect answer across multiple independent samples; and (2) token-level confidence — whether the model assigns high probability to the tokens of its generated response. Each sample is assigned to one of four quadrants by a Youden's J optimal split on the black-box (consistency) and white-box (confidence) axes. Youden's J maximises balanced accuracy (TPR − FPR) and is base-rate robust.

Scorer Families. The authors evaluate 15 scorer configurations per model-dataset pair using the UQLM toolkit:

  • Black-box (6 scorers): Semantic Negentropy, Non-Contradiction, Entailment, Cosine Similarity, Exact Match, Semantic Sets Confidence — all requiring only sampling access, using five sampled responses at temperature 1.0.
  • White-box (5 scorers): Sequence Probability, Min Token Probability, Mean Token Negentropy, Probability Margin, P(True) — requiring per-token log-probability access (k = 15 for top-k).
  • LLM-as-a-Judge (4 judges): Self-judge, cross-LLM judges (the other two generators), and Claude Sonnet 4.6 as an independent-provider judge. All use Likert scoring.

Datasets. Four datasets with distinct hallucination profiles:

  • TriviaQA: Factual trivia where larger models are prone to memorising systematic misconceptions (targets Entrenched).
  • HaluEval: Curated hallucinated answer pairs with binary labels, skewing toward Entrenched and Knotted.
  • SelfAware (500 unanswerable questions): Probes model abstention behaviour on genuinely unanswerable questions (targets the universal blind spot).
  • PopQA: Entity-centric questions stratified by Wikipedia page-view popularity; popular-entity slices target Entrenched, rare-entity slices target Confabulation.

Validation Strategy. Two independent validation approaches: (1) scorer-pair disagreement analysis using CH (the fraction of hallucinated samples on which two scorers disagree at their Youden's J thresholds); and (2) external-signal validation checking whether samples with known mechanism labels land in the predicted DECK cells via χ² tests.

Results & Highlights

Individual Scorer Performance. No scorer family Pareto-dominates across the 12 model-dataset combinations. On Llama-3-8B TriviaQA, Judge leads (AUROC 0.844); on GPT-4o TriviaQA, white-box P(True) leads (0.675); on PopQA, black-box Cosine Similarity and white-box Mean Token Negentropy reach 0.77–0.80 for GPT-4o and Gemini-2.5-Flash. Absolute AUROC compresses with model scale, consistent with an Entrenched-shift predicted by DECK. SelfAware is the lowest-performing dataset for all methods.

Disagreement Analysis (Primary Validation). Judge-involving pairs serve as the primary validation because the Judge is a third axis not used to define the four quadrants. On TriviaQA and HaluEval, judge-involving disagreements spread across all four quadrants as predicted. On Llama-3-8B PopQA, BB–Judge disagreements concentrate in K (49%) and E (23%) — placing 72% of disagreements inside BB's predicted high-consistency blind spots. CH values for judge-involving pairs range from 0.044 to 0.571 across combinations, tracking the predicted taxonomy profile.

External-Signal Validation. Seven of twelve (model, dataset, external-label) rows match the predicted concentration cell exactly; the remaining five differ in a single direction (four extend with a secondary cell, one is narrower). SelfAware lands in Entrenched across all three models (62.0–71.8%, all p < 0.001). HaluEval adversarial inputs show model-dependent patterns: GPT-4o concentrates sharply in E alone (64.6%, p < 0.001), while Llama-3-8B spreads across E/C/K. PopQA popular-entity samples concentrate in E (53.3–86.7%); rare-entity samples lead in C (38.3–50.4%) with model-dependent secondary cells.

Universal Blind Spot on SelfAware. GPT-4o (90.6%), Llama-3-8B (92.8%), and Gemini-2.5-Flash (88.0%) of SelfAware responses are graded as confident fabrications. Every scorer family collapses toward chance; 13 of 15 GPT-4o scorers fall below AUROC 0.5, with P(True) inverting from 0.675 on TriviaQA to 0.331. A linear probe on Llama-3-8B's last-layer hidden states (NF4 4-bit quantisation) also collapses (AUROC 0.44, 95% CI [0.35, 0.53]). No ensemble weight configuration moves point-estimate AUROC out of [0.41, 0.61] on SelfAware.

The empirical claims are supported by multi-model (Llama-3-8B, GPT-4o, Gemini-2.5-Flash), multi-dataset validation and a principled statistical method (Youden's J). The paper uses the UQLM toolkit (Bouchard et al., 2026) for scorer computation, which improves reproducibility. However, the paper does not explicitly mention code release, and as a single-author arXiv preprint, it has not undergone peer review. The reproducibility of the taxonomy depends on the availability of scorer implementations, threshold values, and the exact grading procedures (which are documented in Appendix B).

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The reframing of hallucination taxonomies from "what is wrong" to "what can detect it" is conceptually elegant and addresses a real gap in the literature. The 2×2 consistency-confidence partition is not entirely new — similar axes appear in prior UQ work (e.g., Farquhar et al.'s confabulation framing) — but the systematic operationalisation via Youden's J and the explicit mapping to scorer-family blind spots is a genuine advance. The naming of the four cells (DECK) is memorable and pedagogically useful.
  • Rigor: The use of Youden's J for optimal thresholding is methodologically sound and base-rate robust. The authors correctly distinguish primary validation (judge-involving pairs, which test against an independent signal) from secondary validation (BB–WB pairs, which are structurally constrained by construction). The external-signal validation using χ² tests is a strong independent check. However, the thresholds are data-dependent and within-condition; the authors acknowledge this limitation but do not quantify how sensitive the cell assignments are to threshold choice.
  • Reproducibility: The paper provides detailed appendices including scoring procedures, grading code, AUROC tables with 95% bootstrap CIs, and references to the UQLM toolkit. This is above-average for an arXiv preprint. That said, without an explicit code release statement, independent replication is not guaranteed. The linear probe experiment is described at a high level; specifics about layer selection, training hyperparameters, and data splits are sparse.
  • Significance: If the taxonomy proves robust across more models and tasks, it could serve as a valuable diagnostic tool for practitioners choosing UQ methods. The universal blind spot finding is particularly important for safety-critical applications — it provides a mechanistic explanation for why ensemble UQ can still fail catastrophically. The recommended "abstention envelope" engineering response is practical but shifts the problem to out-of-scope detection, which is itself unsolved.
  • Red Flags: The claim of a "universal blind spot" is strong and potentially overstated. The authors carefully define "universal" as "across the three output-level families studied here," which is appropriately scoped, but the term risks being misinterpreted. The SelfAware results are compelling but depend on a regex-based grading of "appropriate uncertainty" that may not generalise to other refusal phrasings. The linear probe on a single model (Llama-3-8B) under 4-bit quantisation is a very limited test of internal-state methods; the authors are appropriately cautious in calling it preliminary. The single-author nature is not a weakness per se, but the lack of institutional affiliation and peer review means the empirical scope has not been independently validated.

Strengths

  • Conceptual clarity of the 2×2 framework and its direct mapping to scorer-family strengths and blind spots
  • Methodological rigour in operationalising cell membership via Youden's J and distinguishing primary from secondary validation
  • Multi-model, multi-dataset empirical validation with independent external-label confirmation
  • Honest and precise limitation statements, including careful scoping of the "universal" blind spot claim
  • Identification of a genuinely important failure mode (confident repeatable fabrications on knowledge-gap inputs) with both empirical and theoretical explanation
  • Practical engineering recommendation (abstention envelope) rather than simply advocating for more complex ensembles
  • Comprehensive appendices with scorer details, grading code, and full AUROC tables

Limitations

  • Threshold operationalisation is data-dependent: within-condition Youden's J splits shift cell boundaries under alternative threshold choices (e.g., F1-optimal, precision-at-k, or calibrated absolute cutoffs)
  • Judge bias is not fully eliminated: all judges share systematic misconceptions from common web-scale pretraining, especially pronounced on knowledge-gap inputs
  • Internal-state scope is very limited: only a single logistic probe on Llama-3-8B's last-layer hidden states under NF4 4-bit quantisation was tested; richer methods (UQ heads, information-theoretic estimators, multi-layer probes) remain untested
  • GPT-4o and Gemini-2.5-Flash do not expose hidden states, so the linear probe test cannot be replicated on closed-weight models
  • Analysis is restricted to short-form QA; long-form generation involves claim-level dynamics that may require a different framework
  • The universal blind spot claim is scoped to the three output-level families studied, but the framing could be misinterpreted as broader than intended
  • No explicit mention of code or data release for full reproducibility

Relevance

本文对LLM幻觉检测的不确定性量化方法进行了系统性分类，并揭示了现有方法在知识缺口场景下的根本性局限，对构建更可靠的LLM评估和监控体系具有重要的实践指导意义，特别是在高安全要求的应用场景中。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How do the optimal Youden's J thresholds vary across model families (e.g., GPT, Llama, Gemini) and scales? Are they stable enough to be pre-computed and deployed without per-domain calibration?
  2. Can richer internal-state methods such as dedicated UQ heads (Shelmanov et al., 2025), information-theoretic estimators (Yadkori et al., 2024), or multi-layer probes overcome the blind spot identified for output-level and single-layer internal scorers?
  3. How does the DECK taxonomy perform on multilingual or code-generation tasks, where hallucination patterns may differ from English short-form QA?
  4. Can the DECK framework be extended to include scorers that use retrieval-augmented signals or external knowledge bases, potentially covering the knowledge-gap blind spot?
  5. What is the practical cost-benefit tradeoff of deploying multiple scorer families (consistency + token-probability + LLM-as-a-Judge) versus a single best-performing scorer, and does this calculus change as model scale increases?
  6. How does the cell distribution shift as models scale — do larger models concentrate more in the Entrenched cell, and what does this imply for future UQ method design?
  7. Can an abstention envelope be designed that reliably identifies knowledge-gap inputs before generation, or is the problem fundamentally as hard as the original hallucination detection task?
  8. Would a human evaluation of the SelfAware grading regex reveal false positives or false negatives in the "appropriate uncertainty" classification, and how would that affect the blind spot conclusions?
