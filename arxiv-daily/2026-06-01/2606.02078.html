Beyond $\ell_2$-norm and $\ell_\infty$-norm: A Curvature-Inspired $\ell_p$-Norm Scheme for Deep Neural Networks

arXiv: 2606.02078

Date: 2026-06-01

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.02078

一句话总结： 本文提出了一种基于余弦退火的动态 $\ell_p$-范数优化方案，通过让 $p$ 值从大于 2 的初始值逐渐退火到 2，使优化器在早期训练阶段抑制高曲率方向的支配，在后期阶段实现更稳定的精细更新，并据此设计了 LPSGD 和 LPSGDM 两种新优化器，在 CIFAR 和 ImageNet 上取得了优于 SGD、AdamW 和 Lion 的泛化性能。
TL;DR: This paper proposes a cosine-annealed dynamic $\ell_p$-norm scheme for DNN optimization where $p$ decays from an initial $p_{\max} > 2$ down to 2 during training. This allows the optimizer to suppress high-curvature dominance early on (via larger-$p$ behavior resembling $\ell_\infty$) while transitioning to stable Euclidean updates later. The resulting LPSGD and LPSGDM optimizers achieve state-of-the-art generalization on CIFAR-10/100 and ImageNet-1K, outperforming SGD with Momentum, AdamW, and Lion.

核心贡献

  • 揭示了曲率各向异性的演化规律：通过可视化 ResNet-18/CIFAR-10 和 ResNet-50/ImageNet-1K 训练过程中 Hessian 特征值的分布变化，证明了深度神经网络训练早期存在强烈的曲率各向异性（Hessian 特征值分散且存在极端离群值），而后期逐渐过渡到弱各向异性的近似凸区域。
  • 提出余弦 $\ell_p$-范数调度方案：设计了一种随训练过程动态调整 $p$ 值的策略，$p_s = 2 + (p_{\max} - 2) \cdot \frac{1 + \cos(\pi \frac{s-1}{S-1})}{2}$，使得优化器从较大的 $p$ 值（接近 $\ell_\infty$ 行为）平滑退火到 $p=2$（标准欧几里得几何）。
  • 导出两种新优化器：将动态 $\ell_p$-范数方案分别嵌入 SGD 和带动量的 SGD，得到 LPSGD 和 LPSGDM。两种方法仅引入 $O(d)$ 的额外元素级缩放计算，计算开销与原始 SGD 几乎相同。
  • 建立非凸收敛理论：在 $\ell_p$-光滑性假设和变换方向的条件对齐假设下，严格证明了 LPSGD 和 LPSGDM 在非凸随机优化中均能达到 $O(T^{-1/2})$ 的收敛速率。
  • 在标准基准上验证有效性：在 CIFAR-10、CIFAR-100 和 ImageNet-1K 上，使用 VGG-11、ResNet-18 和 ResNet-50 进行对比实验，LPSGDM 在所有设置下均优于 SGD w/Momentum、AdamW 和 Lion。例如在 ImageNet-1K/ResNet-50 上，LPSGDM 达到 74.04% 的 top-1 准确率，超过 AdamW 2.25 个百分点。

Key Contributions

  • Characterizes curvature anisotropy evolution: By visualizing Hessian eigenvalue distributions during training of ResNet-18/CIFAR-10 and ResNet-50/ImageNet-1K, the authors demonstrate that DNN training exhibits strong curvature anisotropy early on (dispersed eigenvalues with extreme outliers) and transitions to weakly anisotropic, approximately convex regions later.
  • Proposes cosine $\ell_p$-norm scheduling: A dynamic strategy that adjusts $p$ during training via $p_s = 2 + (p_{\max} - 2) \cdot \frac{1 + \cos(\pi \frac{s-1}{S-1})}{2}$, smoothly annealing from a larger $p$ (behaving more like $\ell_\infty$) to $p=2$ (standard Euclidean geometry).
  • Derives two novel optimizers: LPSGD and LPSGDM are obtained by embedding the dynamic $\ell_p$-norm scheme into SGD and SGD with momentum. Both introduce only $O(d)$ additional element-wise rescaling computation, with computational cost almost identical to vanilla SGD.
  • Establishes nonconvex convergence theory: Under $\ell_p$-smoothness and conditional alignment assumptions for the transformed direction, the authors rigorously prove that both LPSGD and LPSGDM achieve $O(T^{-1/2})$ convergence rates for nonconvex stochastic optimization.
  • Validates on standard benchmarks: On CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet-1K using VGG-11, ResNet-18, and ResNet-50, LPSGDM outperforms SGD w/Momentum, AdamW, and Lion in all settings. On ImageNet-1K/ResNet-50, LPSGDM achieves 74.04% top-1 accuracy, exceeding AdamW by 2.25 percentage points.

Methodology

Motivation from Curvature Anisotropy. The authors analyze the training dynamics of DNNs through the lens of Hessian eigenvalue distributions. Figure 1 in the paper shows that early in training, the Hessian exhibits widely dispersed eigenvalues with pronounced outliers (e.g., 17.5 and 1180 for the two setups) and even negative eigenvalues, indicating highly anisotropic and non-convex geometry. As training progresses, eigenvalues concentrate near zero, outliers become rare, and negative eigenvalues disappear, signaling a transition to flatter, approximately convex regions.

Limitations of Fixed Norms. Under $\ell_2$-norm (Euclidean) geometry, gradient descent preserves directional imbalance linearly: $|d^{(i)}| / |d^{(j)}| = \lambda^{(i)} |\theta^{(i)}| / (\lambda^{(j)} |\theta^{(j)}|)$. High-curvature directions dominate, requiring small learning rates and slowing progress along flat directions. Under $\ell_\infty$-norm (sign-based) geometry, updates become coordinate-wise sign operations that maximally suppress directional imbalance but discard useful scale information, causing oscillations in flatter regions (demonstrated in toy examples in Figure 3).

Dynamic $\ell_p$-Norm Scheme. To interpolate between these extremes, the authors propose a cosine $\ell_p$-norm schedule:

$$p_s = 2 + (p_{\max} - 2) \cdot \frac{1 + \cos(\pi \frac{s-1}{S-1})}{2}$$

where $p_{\max} \geq 2$ is the maximum norm parameter and $S$ is the total number of epochs. This schedule starts at $p_{\max}$ and smoothly decays to 2 at the final epoch.

The coordinate-wise scaling operator is defined as:

$$u_t = \frac{g_t}{(|g_t| + \varepsilon)^{\rho_s}}$$

where $\rho_s = \frac{p_s - 2}{p_s - 1}$ and $\varepsilon > 0$ is a small stability constant.

LPSGD (Algorithm 1). SGD equipped with the dynamic $\ell_p$-norm scheme. The update is $\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta u_t$, where $u_t$ applies the scheduled coordinate-wise rescaling to the stochastic gradient.

LPSGDM (Algorithm 2). Extends LPSGD with momentum and decoupled weight decay. The momentum buffer is updated as $m_t = \beta m_{t-1} + (1-\beta) g_t$, then the scheduled rescaling is applied to the momentum-smoothed gradient: $v_t = m_t / (|m_t| + \varepsilon)^{\rho_s}$. The parameter update includes decoupled weight decay: $\theta_{t+1} = (1 - \eta\lambda) \theta_t - \eta v_t$.

Key distinction from GSGD: Unlike prior work (GSGD) that transitions from signSGD to SGD with a fixed implicit schedule $p = t/T$, LPSGD introduces a tunable $p_{\max}$, providing greater flexibility to match different models' curvature characteristics.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The core idea—interpolating between $\ell_2$ and $\ell_\infty$ via a scheduled $\ell_p$-norm—is conceptually clean and well-motivated by the observed evolution of Hessian eigenvalue distributions. However, the paper needs to be viewed in the context of a rich literature on adaptive optimization. While the authors distinguish their work from GSGD, the broader relationship to methods like Adam (which also performs coordinate-wise scaling, albeit through second-moment estimation) is not deeply explored. The novelty lies more in the dynamic scheduling of the norm geometry rather than the use of $\ell_p$-norm itself.

Rigor: The convergence analysis (Theorems 1 and 2) follows a standard Lyapunov-style argument and achieves the expected $O(T^{-1/2})$ rate for nonconvex stochastic optimization. However, several assumptions merit scrutiny:

  • Assumption 5 (conditional alignment and bounded transformed second moment) is quite strong and non-standard. The paper does not provide empirical verification that these assumptions hold in practice for DNN training.
  • The $\ell_p$-smoothness assumption (Assumption 1) replaces the standard Euclidean norm with the $\ell_p$-norm in the smoothness bound. While mathematically consistent within the paper's framework, this is a non-standard condition whose relationship to classical $L$-smoothness is not fully clarified.
  • The decoupled weight decay is discussed algorithmically but excluded from the formal convergence analysis, with the authors noting this would require additional assumptions.

Reproducibility: The experiments use standard datasets and architectures, and hyperparameter settings are provided in Appendix A. The grid search strategy is clearly described. However, the results show some concerning variance patterns (e.g., ResNet-18/SGD w/Momentum on CIFAR-100 shows 44.12% at epoch 50 but 76.86% at epoch 200 with a large standard deviation), suggesting potential training instability that should be investigated further.

Significance: If the improvements are consistently reproducible, this work offers a lightweight alternative to second-order or complex adaptive methods. The $O(d)$ overhead is negligible, making it practical for large-scale deployment. However, the introduction of $p_{\max}$ as an additional hyperparameter adds tuning burden, and the observation that optimal $p_{\max}$ varies by architecture complicates universal adoption.

Red flags:

  • The paper claims to "outperform state-of-the-art baselines by 0.68%, 0.31%, and 0.27% on CIFAR-10" but these margins seem to refer to specific architecture-optimizer combinations rather than universal improvements. The abstract phrasing could be misleading.
  • The CIFAR-100 results for SGD w/Momentum on ResNet-18 show anomalously poor early performance (44.12% at epoch 50), which may indicate suboptimal hyperparameters for the baseline rather than a fair comparison.
  • The relationship between the proposed method and existing sharpness-aware minimization (SAM) or flat-minima-seeking methods is not discussed, despite the shared goal of improving generalization.

Strengths

  • Strong empirical motivation: The Hessian eigenvalue visualization (Figure 1) provides compelling evidence for the training-stage-dependent curvature anisotropy hypothesis.
  • Conceptual clarity: The interpolation between $\ell_2$ and $\ell_\infty$ via a single tunable parameter $p$ is elegant and easy to understand.
  • Minimal computational overhead: The method requires only element-wise rescaling ($O(d)$) and no additional gradient computations, making it highly practical.
  • Comprehensive experimental evaluation: Testing across three datasets and three architectures with multiple checkpoints provides robust validation.
  • Theoretical grounding: The convergence proofs, while relying on strong assumptions, lend credibility and follow a clear logical structure.
  • Flexible design: The tunable $p_{\max}$ allows practitioners to adapt the method to different models, unlike fixed-schedule alternatives.

Limitations

  • Strong theoretical assumptions: Assumptions 5 and 6 (conditional alignment of transformed directions) are non-standard and their practical validity for DNNs is not established.
  • Additional hyperparameter: $p_{\max}$ must be tuned per architecture, adding to the optimization burden. The paper does not provide a principled method for selecting $p_{\max}$ without grid search.
  • Limited architecture scope: Experiments are conducted only on CNNs (VGG, ResNet). Performance on modern architectures like Vision Transformers or large language models is unknown.
  • No wall-clock comparison: While the theoretical computational cost is minimal, actual training time comparisons are not reported.
  • Weight decay not analyzed: Decoupled weight decay is included in the algorithm but excluded from the formal convergence analysis.
  • Missing ablations: The paper does not systematically ablate the contribution of the cosine schedule shape versus other annealing strategies (e.g., linear, exponential).
  • Baseline fairness concerns: Some baseline results (particularly SGD w/Momentum on ResNet-18/CIFAR-100) appear suboptimal, which may inflate the reported improvements.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Assumption validation: Can Assumptions 5 and 6 be empirically verified for real DNN training? Under what conditions do they hold or fail?
  2. Automatic $p_{\max}$ selection: Is it possible to design an adaptive mechanism that automatically sets $p_{\max}$ based on online curvature estimates, eliminating the need for grid search?
  3. Transformer and LLM scaling: How does LPSGDM perform on Vision Transformers, BERT, GPT-style models, or modern large language models where optimization dynamics may differ significantly from CNNs?
  4. Comparison with SAM and related methods: What is the relationship between the $\ell_p$-norm scheme and sharpness-aware minimization? Do they synergize or address different aspects of generalization?
  5. Schedule shape ablation: How does cosine annealing compare to linear, exponential, or step-wise $p$-schedules? Is the smooth transition critical, or would simpler schedules suffice?
  6. Wall-clock efficiency: What is the actual training time overhead of LPSGDM compared to vanilla SGD with Momentum, especially at large scale?
  7. Theoretical relaxation: Can the convergence analysis be extended to weaker assumptions that more closely match practical DNN training, such as non-uniform smoothness or generalized smoothness conditions?
  8. Combination with other techniques: Can the $\ell_p$-norm scheme be combined with learning rate warm-up, gradient clipping, or other modern training techniques in a principled way?
