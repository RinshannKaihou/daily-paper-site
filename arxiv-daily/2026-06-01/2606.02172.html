Closing the Alignment-Maturity Gap in Federated Prototype Learning

arXiv: 2606.02172

Date: 2026-06-01

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.02172

一句话总结： 本文提出 FedSAP 框架，通过确定性对齐课程和几何驱动的代理分离损失，解决联邦原型学习中早期全局对齐压力压制局部判别结构的问题，在不增加通信开销的情况下显著提升非独立同分布场景下的表示学习效果。
TL;DR: This paper proposes FedSAP, a framework that stabilizes federated prototype learning by delaying global alignment until local representations mature and enforcing inter-class separation on the unit hypersphere, substantially improving recognition performance under non-IID conditions without extra communication overhead.

核心贡献

  • 识别并形式化联邦原型学习中的"对齐-成熟度差距"（alignment-maturity gap）：早期训练轮次中，由不成熟局部表示聚合而成的全局原型作为对齐锚点，产生与距离成正比的过大梯度，压制了局部判别结构的形成。
  • 提出 FedSAP（Federated Scheduled and Adaptive Prototypes）框架，包含两个互补机制：
  1. 渐进式对齐课程（progressive alignment curriculum）：通过对齐权重 λ_t 的线性预热，在前 tw 轮逐步增加全局原型对齐压力，使局部表示先稳定再对齐；
  2. 几何驱动代理正则化（geometry-driven proxy regularisation）：将服务器分发的全局原型库直接复用为代理银行，在超球面上通过余弦 softmax 目标强制类间分离，不引入任何额外参数或通信开销。
  • 在 FEMNIST、CIFAR-10、CIFAR-100 三个基准上，以多种异质性配置（n-way k-shot）进行系统评估，相比 FedProto 和 FedMPS 等原型基线提升最多约 4 个百分点，且在高异质性下增益最显著。
  • 框架可自然扩展至半监督联邦学习：在监督阶段稳定嵌入空间后，通过基于原型最近邻的伪标签和弱-强一致性损失，利用无标签数据进一步提炼表示。

Key Contributions

  • Identifies and formalizes the alignment-maturity gap in prototype-based FL: in early rounds, global prototypes aggregated from immature local embeddings act as unreliable anchors, producing distance-dependent gradients that dominate the cross-entropy signal and suppress the emergence of local discriminative structure.
  • Proposes FedSAP with two complementary mechanisms:
  1. A progressive alignment curriculum that linearly warms up the prototype alignment weight λ_t over tw rounds, allowing local representations to consolidate before being pulled toward global consensus.
  2. A geometry-driven proxy regularisation that repurposes the existing global prototype bank as a shared proxy set on the unit hypersphere, enforcing inter-class separation via a cosine-softmax objective without additional parameters or communication.
  • Evaluates systematically across FEMNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 under multiple heterogeneity levels (n-way k-shot), achieving gains of up to ~4 percentage points over FedProto and FedMPS, with the largest improvements under high heterogeneity.
  • Extends naturally to semi-supervised FL through a two-phase protocol: after the supervised phase stabilizes the embedding space, unlabelled data is incorporated via prototype-based pseudo-labelling and weak-strong consistency regularisation.

Methodology

Problem Setup. Standard FL with Q clients coordinated by a central server over T rounds. Each client q holds a local dataset D_q drawn from a client-specific distribution P_q, observing only a subset C_q ⊂ C of the global classes. Each client maintains a feature extractor f_θ and classifier head g_φ. At the end of each round, local prototypes are computed by averaging embeddings per class: p^t_{q,c} = (1/|D_{q,c}|) Σ f_θ(x_i). The server aggregates them class-wise: p̄^t_c = (1/|Q_c|) Σ p^t_{q,c}. Communication cost per round is Q·μ·d (μ = avg classes per client), independent of model size.

The Alignment-Maturity Gap. The gradient of the prototype MSE loss L_Proto = ||f_θ(x) − p̄_c||² scales with the embedding-prototype distance. In early rounds, this distance is large for two compounding reasons: (1) local feature extractors are unrefined, and (2) global prototypes are aggregated from these same noisy embeddings. Large alignment gradients dominate the cross-entropy signal precisely when local feature learning is most fragile, creating a self-reinforcing instability.

Progressive Alignment Curriculum. FedSAP schedules the alignment weight as λ_t = M · min(t/t_w, 1), where t_w is the curriculum length and M is the maximum coefficient. For t < t_w, the embedding space is shaped primarily by local classification; alignment pressure increases linearly as representations stabilize. The linear schedule is chosen for simplicity; the authors note that cosine, sigmoid, and step variants perform comparably.

Geometry-Driven Proxy Regularisation. Rather than introducing separate learnable proxies, FedSAP repurposes the global prototype bank as a shared proxy set. Embeddings and prototypes are ℓ2-normalized onto the unit hypersphere: ĥ_i = f_θ(x_i)/||f_θ(x_i)||, p̂_c = p_c/||p_c||. Scaled cosine-similarity logits z_{i,c} = s · ĥ_i^T p̂_c are fed into a softmax cross-entropy: L_Proxy = −(1/|B|) Σ log(exp(z_{i,y_i}) / Σ_c exp(z_{i,c})). This enforces inter-class separation using the existing prototype bank with zero extra parameters or communication.

Semi-Supervised Extension. A two-phase protocol: (1) supervised phase of T_sup rounds establishes a stable prototype bank; (2) semi-supervised phase uses unlabelled data. For each unlabelled sample x_u, a pseudo-label is assigned by nearest cosine distance to the global prototype bank. Samples are accepted only if the margin between nearest and second-nearest prototype distances exceeds adaptive thresholds M and D_max, calibrated from labelled data at the end of the supervised phase. A consistency loss penalizes the cosine distance between a strongly-augmented view and the target prototype. Mixed prototypes incorporating both labelled and accepted pseudo-labelled embeddings are returned to the server.

Results & Highlights

Datasets & Protocol. Evaluated on FEMNIST (CNN), CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 (ResNet-18 with ImageNet pre-training). Heterogeneity simulated via n-way k-shot partitioning: each client receives n ~ N(μ_n, σ²) classes with k ~ N(μ_k, σ²) samples per class. Three heterogeneity levels per dataset. 20 clients, full participation, 100 rounds, 1 local epoch per round, batch size 8, SGD with lr=0.01 and momentum=0.5.

Main Results (Table 1).

  • FEMNIST: FedSAP achieves 93.01% (n=3), outperforming FedProto (92.22%) and FedMPS (92.65%) under classifier-head inference, with notably lower variance than FedAvg (33.60±15.12).
  • CIFAR-10: FedSAP reaches 92.68% (n=3) vs FedProto 89.04% and FedMPS 87.63%. Gains persist across heterogeneity levels.
  • CIFAR-100: Largest improvements observed. FedSAP achieves 87.59% (n=10) vs FedProto 79.91% and FedMPS 77.75%—a gain of ~7-10 percentage points under classifier-head inference. Under prototype-based (nearest-centroid) inference, FedSAP reaches 84.68% vs FedProto 80.98% and FedMPS 79.70%.

Communication Efficiency (Table 2). FedSAP matches FedProto's minimal communication cost (Q·μ·d), exchanging only prototype vectors. FedAvg communicates full model parameters (~350× more on CIFAR-10/100). FedMPS incurs additional cost due to shared sub-model components.

Convergence & Robustness (Figure 3, 5). FedSAP shows stable convergence across all datasets. On CIFAR-10, it maintains a consistent margin over baselines across all k-shot sizes, including severely data-scarce regimes (k=10).

Latent Space Quality (Figure 4, 6). t-SNE visualizations show FedSAP produces tighter intra-class clusters with cleaner inter-class boundaries compared to FedAvg (diffuse), FedProto, and FedMPS (moderate). Silhouette scores confirm progressive organization of class structure during training, with further refinement in the semi-supervised phase.

Semi-Supervised Extension (Table 3). Adding 50 rounds of unlabelled refinement improves both silhouette score and accuracy. On CIFAR-10 with k=25 (data scarcity), accuracy improves from 69.18% to 73.98%. FEMNIST shows gains from 87.34% to ~89.8%, with minimal sensitivity to unlabelled pool size.

Ablation Study (Table 4). On CIFAR-100 (n=10):

  • Curriculum alone improves classifier-head accuracy by ~1pp (79.70% → 82.52%), validating the alignment-maturity gap hypothesis.
  • Proxy regularisation alone accounts for the majority of gains, lifting head accuracy by over 7pp (79.70% → 87.45%), confirming geometry-driven separation as the primary driver.
  • Combined: 84.68% (prototype inference) / 87.59% (head inference).
  • Schedule shape sensitivity (Figure 7): linear, cosine, and sigmoid ramps perform comparably; step schedules show greater variance.
  • Start round sensitivity (Figure 8): intermediate start rounds (r_start ≈ 20) work best; too early over-constrains, too late leaves insufficient consolidation time.

Authors & Credibility

The authors (Mario Casado-Diez, Alejandro Dopico-Castro, Verónica Bolón-Canedo, Bertha Guijarro-Berdiñas) are from CITIC at Universidade da Coruña, Spain, a research center with established work in machine learning and distributed systems. The paper is well-grounded in the federated learning literature, building directly upon FedProto, FedNH, FedMPS, and ProxyAnchor/ProxyNCA from deep metric learning. The experimental protocol follows standard community practice (n-way k-shot partitioning, multiple heterogeneity levels, ResNet-18 with pre-training). The code is noted as "to be released upon acceptance." Funding from Spanish and EU research programs is disclosed.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The core conceptual contribution—identifying the alignment-maturity gap and addressing it through scheduling—is well-motivated and clearly articulated. However, the idea of curriculum/warm-up scheduling is not new in deep learning broadly; the novelty lies in its application to the federated prototype setting and the coupling with proxy-based separation. The proxy separation mechanism is essentially an application of ProxyNCA/ProxyAnchor loss to the existing prototype bank, which is elegant but not fundamentally new.

Rigor: The theoretical analysis is limited to a gradient scaling argument (Eq. 6) showing distance-dependent gradient magnitude. There is no formal convergence proof or generalization bound. The ablation study is thorough and isolates component contributions effectively. However, the paper reports results over only 3 independent runs with a small seed set {1234, 1235, 1236}; while standard deviations are reported, statistical significance testing (e.g., paired t-tests) is not mentioned.

Reproducibility: The experimental setup is described in detail, and the n-way k-shot protocol follows FedProto's established formulation. However, the code is not yet available, which limits immediate reproducibility. The supplementary material (referenced for additional sensitivity analyses) is not accessible from the main PDF.

Significance: The improvements on CIFAR-100 are substantial (~7-10pp), but on FEMNIST and CIFAR-10 they are more modest (~0.4-3.6pp). The claim that FedSAP "does not alter the underlying communication protocol" is accurate but somewhat expected since it uses the same prototype exchange as FedProto. The semi-supervised extension is presented as preliminary, and the closed-set assumption (unlabelled data from known classes) is a notable limitation.

Red flags: The paper positions the semi-supervised extension as "evidence that scheduled alignment is a general design principle" but does not compare against dedicated SSFL baselines like FedMatch or ProtoFSSL, making it difficult to assess whether the gains come from scheduled alignment or simply from adding unlabelled data. The claim that improvements are "most pronounced under high heterogeneity" is supported by the CIFAR-100 results but less clearly on the other datasets.

Strengths

  • Clear, well-motivated identification of the alignment-maturity gap with intuitive theoretical grounding.
  • Elegant and lightweight design: no extra parameters, no extra communication, no protocol modification.
  • Strong empirical gains on CIFAR-100, the most challenging benchmark, with comprehensive ablations.
  • The proxy separation mechanism is a principled adaptation of deep metric learning to the federated setting.
  • Natural extensibility to semi-supervised settings demonstrates generality of the design principle.
  • Thorough sensitivity analyses (schedule shape, start round, proxy temperature) support robustness claims.

Limitations

  • Only 3 independent runs reported; lack of statistical significance testing weakens confidence in small gains.
  • The core mechanisms (curriculum scheduling, proxy-based metric learning) are borrowed from other domains rather than invented anew.
  • Semi-supervised evaluation is preliminary: closed-set assumption, no comparison against dedicated SSFL baselines, and limited datasets.
  • Prototype-based inference underperforms classifier-head inference for FedSAP, suggesting the cosine-softmax objective aligns better with classifier boundaries than with Euclidean centroid proximity.
  • No evaluation on more realistic large-scale datasets or heterogeneous architectures across clients.

Relevance

本文对联邦学习中的表示学习与非独立同分布挑战提出了深入见解，FedSAP 的轻量级设计使其在实际部署中具有吸引力，对关注分布式机器学习、视觉表示学习及联邦优化算法的读者具有重要参考价值。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How would FedSAP perform under adaptive or learned scheduling strategies (e.g., data-dependent curriculum based on local validation loss) compared to the fixed linear schedule?
  2. Can the proxy separation loss be extended to open-set semi-supervised settings where unlabelled data may contain unseen classes, and how would out-of-distribution detection integrate?
  3. What is the theoretical relationship between the alignment-maturity gap and convergence properties in non-convex federated optimization? Can a formal bound be derived?
  4. How does FedSAP scale to much larger client populations (e.g., thousands of edge devices) with partial participation, where prototype aggregation may become sparser and noisier?
  5. Would incorporating local self-supervised pre-training (as in FedX) before the supervised phase reduce or eliminate the need for explicit alignment scheduling?
