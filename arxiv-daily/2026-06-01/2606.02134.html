Rethinking Evaluation Paradigms in IBP-based Certified Training

arXiv: 2606.02134

Date: 2026-06-01

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.02134

一句话总结： 本文指出基于IBP的认证训练领域长期以来用单一超参数配置比较方法具有严重误导性，提出以帕累托前沿为核心的多目标评估范式，通过自动化贝叶斯超参数优化发现此前文献大量欠调参，并首次对现有方法进行了全面的多目标比较，揭示SABR在自然准确率上占优而MTL-IBP在认证准确率上更强，且老牌方法IBP/CROWN-IBP的潜力被严重低估。
TL;DR: This paper argues that comparing IBP-based certified training methods via a single hyperparameter configuration is misleading due to the inherent natural–certified accuracy trade-off. It proposes a Pareto-front-based multi-objective evaluation paradigm using automated Bayesian hyperparameter optimization, uncovers substantial undertuning in prior literature, and presents the first comprehensive multi-objective comparison—revealing that SABR excels at natural accuracy, MTL-IBP at certified accuracy, and that older methods (IBP, CROWN-IBP) were significantly underestimated.

核心贡献

  • 指出评估范式的根本缺陷：认证训练中存在自然准确率与认证准确率的固有冲突，此前文献普遍报告单一配置或隐式优化单一目标，导致结论高度依赖所选权衡点，可能产生严重偏差。
  • 提出帕累托前沿评估范式：主张通过比较各方法在整个自然–认证准确率权衡空间上的帕累托前沿来进行公平、无偏的方法评估，使研究者和实践者能根据自身需求选择合适方法。
  • 设计高效的多目标超参数优化流程：基于多目标贝叶斯优化（高斯过程代理 + 约束EHVI采集函数），在方法特定的搜索空间内联合优化所有相关超参数，包括学习率、正则化权重、预热/ ramp-up轮数、ϵ缩放因子、方法特有权衡参数（κ, β, τ, α）等。
  • 揭示大量欠调参与性能提升：发现此前文献中许多报告的配置存在显著欠调参；重新优化后，SABR在CIFAR-10 (ε=2/255) 上同时提升自然和认证准确率超1%；CROWN-IBP提升自然准确率近6%；MTL-IBP和SABR在Tiny ImageNet上提升约2%。
  • 首次全面多目标比较：发现大扰动半径下各方法性能趋于接近且进步停滞；小扰动半径下SABR在自然准确率上占优，MTL-IBP在认证准确率上更强；IBP在大半径高自然准确率区域仍为SOTA。
  • 提供可操作的社区洞见：通过fANOVA分析关键超参数的重要性，发现κ过渡、ϵ缩放、预热/ ramp-up长度等曾被忽视的参数对性能有关键影响。

Key Contributions

  • Exposes fundamental flaws in current evaluation paradigms: The inherent conflict between natural and certified accuracy makes single-configuration reporting highly dependent on the chosen trade-off point and potentially biased.
  • Proposes Pareto-front-based evaluation: Advocates comparing certified training methods over their entire Pareto fronts across the natural–certified accuracy trade-off space, enabling nuanced, requirement-driven method selection.
  • Designs efficient multi-objective hyperparameter optimization: Uses multi-objective Bayesian optimization (Gaussian process surrogate with constrained EHVI acquisition) to jointly optimize all relevant hyperparameters—including learning rate, regularization, warm-up/ramp-up epochs, ϵ scaling, and method-specific trade-off parameters (κ, β, τ, α)—within method-specific search spaces.
  • Reveals substantial undertuning and performance gains: Re-optimization uncovers that prior literature significantly undertuned methods. Notable improvements include >1% clean and certified accuracy for SABR on CIFAR-10 (ε=2/255), ~6% clean accuracy gain for CROWN-IBP, and ~2% gains for MTL-IBP and SABR on Tiny ImageNet.
  • First comprehensive multi-objective comparison: Shows that at larger perturbation radii, performance differences between methods are modest and progress has stalled; at smaller radii, SABR excels at natural accuracy while MTL-IBP delivers the strongest certified guarantees; IBP remains state-of-the-art for high natural accuracy at larger radii.
  • Provides actionable insights for the community: fANOVA analysis reveals that previously overlooked hyperparameters—such as κ transitions, ϵ scaling, and warm-up/ramp-up lengths—are crucial for achieving optimal trade-offs.

Methodology

The authors frame certified training evaluation as a multi-objective optimization problem over the natural–certified accuracy trade-off. To discover Pareto-optimal configuration sets, they employ multi-objective Bayesian optimization using BoTorch within Optuna, with Gaussian process surrogates and a constrained Expected Hypervolume Improvement (EHVI) acquisition function (Daulton et al., 2020). Three pseudo-random seeds are used per benchmark to avoid local optima; the resulting fronts are merged into a single Pareto front.

The search space is designed to be comprehensive and method-agnostic, including general deep learning hyperparameters (learning rate, learning rate decay schedule, optimizer—Adam or RAdam, ℓ1 regularization weight), certified training specific parameters (warm-up epochs, ramp-up epochs, ϵ scaling factor for training, Shi et al. regularization weight), and method-specific trade-off parameters (κ_start ≥ κ_end and β for IBP/CROWN-IBP; τ and PGD attack parameters for SABR; α and PGD attack parameters for MTL-IBP). Notably, they include κ and β even though recent work had deemed them unnecessary, allowing the optimizer to empirically determine their importance.

Since complete verification is computationally infeasible for every trial, the optimization uses incomplete verification (IBP, CROWN-IBP, CROWN) as a proxy for certified accuracy. After optimization, the Pareto-optimal configurations are evaluated with the state-of-the-art complete verifier αβ-CROWN (timeout: 1000s). To reduce verification costs, single-linkage clustering (d_min = 0.05) is applied to merge configurations with negligible performance differences before complete verification.

Experiments focus on CNN7 (Shi et al., 2021) on CIFAR-10 (ε = 2/255 and 8/255) and Tiny ImageNet (ε = 1/255). The evaluation budget is 100 trials per seed (300 total per benchmark). The authors also investigate tuning on a validation set (80/20 split) to quantify test-set tuning inflation, and analyze hyperparameter importance via fANOVA.

Results & Highlights

Pareto fronts dominate prior literature. In nearly all scenarios, the configurations discovered by the authors' evaluation procedure Pareto-dominate results reported in original publications and in the recent CTBench benchmark (Mao et al., 2025). Figure 3 illustrates this dominance across all methods and benchmarks.

CIFAR-10, ε = 2/255. The combined Pareto front consists of MTL-IBP and SABR. SABR achieves the highest natural accuracies while maintaining strong certified robustness; MTL-IBP achieves the strongest certified guarantees overall, though by a small margin. SABR gains >1% in both clean and certified accuracy over prior best results.

CIFAR-10, ε = 8/255. All four investigated methods (IBP, CROWN-IBP, SABR, MTL-IBP) contribute to the combined Pareto front. IBP and CROWN-IBP exhibit the strongest certified guarantees at higher natural accuracies, while SABR and MTL-IBP achieve better trade-offs at higher certified accuracies. Performance differences are relatively modest, confirming that progress at larger radii has stalled. Mao et al.'s claim that methods converge to similar certified accuracy is validated, but the authors show clean accuracy differences are much less pronounced than previously assumed.

Tiny ImageNet, ε = 1/255. New state-of-the-art results substantially surpass prior work. MTL-IBP improves by ~2% in both clean and certified accuracy; SABR achieves comparable results with even stronger clean accuracy. The Pareto front shows clear complementarity: SABR excels at high natural accuracy, MTL-IBP at high certified accuracy.

Test-set vs. validation-set tuning. Validation-set-tuned Pareto fronts are strictly dominated by test-set-tuned fronts, confirming that prior evaluations substantially overestimate generalization performance. However, the shapes of the fronts are visually similar, suggesting relative performance characteristics may remain unchanged.

Computational cost reduction. The authors demonstrate that reducing complete verification timeout from 1000s to 100s on CIFAR-10 preserves the final Pareto fronts while reducing compute by more than an order of magnitude (e.g., MTL-IBP verification drops from 1311 to 208 hours). This suggests future evaluations can be substantially cheaper without sacrificing discriminative power.

Hyperparameter importance (fANOVA). Key findings include: (1) For IBP, κ_start and κ_end are highly important across all scenarios, often taking larger values than in prior work, with longer ramp-up and warm-up phases beneficial. (2) For CROWN-IBP, κ parameters are similarly crucial; β_end is a key driver of certified accuracy with optimal values depending on ϵ. (3) For SABR, the subselection ratio τ is extremely effective at governing the trade-off, and training ϵ scaling >1 is influential. (4) For MTL-IBP, α is the most important parameter; on CIFAR-10 (ε=2/255), more warm-up epochs than previously used unlock better trade-offs; on Tiny ImageNet, scaled ϵ and smaller α drive the largest improvements.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The conceptual shift from single-point to Pareto-front evaluation is methodologically important for the certified training community. While multi-objective optimization and Pareto analysis are well-established in other fields (notably automated ML, where Hoos has made seminal contributions), their systematic application to certified training evaluation is novel and overdue. The paper does not propose a new certified training algorithm per se, but rather a new evaluation standard—this is a meta-contribution that may have broader impact than yet another training method.

Rigor: The methodology is generally sound. The use of automated multi-objective Bayesian optimization directly addresses the ad-hoc tuning problem. However, several caveats exist: (1) The authors follow the certified training community's seemingly common but problematic practice of tuning hyperparameters on the test set; while they acknowledge this and provide validation-set experiments, the main results still use test-set tuning, which inflates reported numbers. (2) The fairness of comparing methods with different training costs under a fixed optimization budget (100 trials per seed) could be debated—methods with faster training may get more effective search per unit time. (3) The choice of d_min = 0.05 for clustering and the minimum accuracy constraints (to exclude trivial regions) introduce some subjectivity, though these choices are clearly reported.

Reproducibility: The approach is inherently more reproducible than ad-hoc configuration choices. The open-source CTRAIN implementation, the use of standard optimization tools (Optuna/BoTorch), and the detailed search space descriptions in the appendices are strengths. However, reproducing the full results requires substantial computational resources (hundreds to thousands of GPU hours plus verification time), which may limit replication for smaller research groups.

Significance: If adopted by the community, this work could fundamentally reshape how certified training papers are evaluated, reviewed, and compared. The demonstration that older methods (IBP, CROWN-IBP) can match or exceed newer methods when properly tuned is a particularly important finding that challenges recent publication incentives favoring incremental algorithmic modifications over proper empirical methodology.

Red Flags: (1) Test-set tuning inflates the absolute numbers, though the relative Pareto dominance claims are likely robust. (2) The computational cost is high, and the authors' suggestion that verification timeouts can be reduced to 100s may not hold for more complex architectures or datasets. (3) The analysis is limited to ℓ∞ perturbations and CNN7; whether the findings generalize to other architectures (e.g., ResNet, Vision Transformers), datasets, or threat models (ℓ2, semantic perturbations) remains open. (4) The paper focuses on deterministic certified training; randomized smoothing methods, which follow a different paradigm, are not addressed.

Strengths

  • Provides a principled, method-agnostic solution to a widely recognized but under-addressed evaluation problem in certified training.
  • Backed by extensive experiments across multiple benchmarks, perturbation radii, and methods, with clear visualization of Pareto fronts.
  • Challenges existing state-of-the-art claims with empirical evidence of undertuning, potentially redirecting research effort toward more meaningful progress.
  • Introduces multi-objective thinking and rigorous hyperparameter optimization to a community that has relied on manual tuning and single-metric leaderboards.
  • Offers concrete, actionable insights about which hyperparameters matter and how they interact, derived from systematic fANOVA analysis.
  • Demonstrates that verification costs can be reduced by an order of magnitude without affecting Pareto front quality, making the protocol more accessible.
  • Open-source implementation in CTRAIN facilitates community adoption.

Limitations

  • Primary results rely on test-set hyperparameter tuning, which inflates absolute performance figures; validation-set experiments show strictly worse (but similarly shaped) fronts.
  • High computational requirements (hundreds of GPU hours + complete verification) may still be prohibitive for some research groups, despite cost-reduction strategies.
  • Does not propose new certified training algorithms; it re-evaluates existing ones, which may limit perceived novelty.
  • Limited to CNN7 architecture, CIFAR-10, and Tiny ImageNet under ℓ∞ perturbations; generalization to other architectures, larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet), or other threat models is not demonstrated.
  • The optimization budget (100 trials × 3 seeds) may be insufficient for very high-dimensional search spaces; the authors note that optimization continued to improve throughout the full budget.
  • Randomized smoothing and complete-verification-based training methods are outside the scope.
  • The minimum accuracy constraints (d_min) used to filter the Pareto front introduce some subjectivity in excluding "uninteresting" regions.

Relevance

本文对认证训练领域的评估范式提出了根本性反思，与关注神经网络鲁棒性、可信机器学习以及自动化机器学习的读者高度相关；其提出的帕累托前沿比较方法和发现的超参数交互规律，有望显著提升该领域实证研究的严谨性并重塑未来论文的评估标准。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How sensitive are the discovered Pareto fronts to the choice of hyperparameter search space boundaries and optimization budget? Would 500 trials per seed uncover substantially better configurations?
  • Do the performance complementarities between SABR (natural accuracy) and MTL-IBP (certified accuracy) suggest that ensemble or hybrid approaches could yield better trade-offs than any single method?
  • How does the Pareto-front evaluation paradigm extend to more complex architectures (ResNet, Vision Transformers) and larger-scale datasets such as full ImageNet?
  • Can this multi-objective framework be adapted for randomized smoothing methods, where the trade-off involves natural accuracy versus certified radius rather than certified accuracy?
  • What would community-wide adoption of this protocol look like? Should benchmark organizers (e.g., CTBench) mandate Pareto-front reporting with standardized search spaces and budgets?
  • How much of the apparent progress in certified training over the past few years is attributable to better hyperparameter tuning rather than algorithmic innovation? Can a historical re-evaluation of prior methods using this protocol quantify this?
  • Given that test-set tuning inflates results, what is the true generalization gap for these Pareto-optimal configurations? Are there regularization strategies that can close the gap between validation-set and test-set tuned fronts?
