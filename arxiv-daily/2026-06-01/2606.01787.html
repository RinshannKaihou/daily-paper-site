Stochastic convergence of parallel asynchronous adaptive first-order methods

arXiv: 2606.01787

Date: 2026-06-01

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.01787

Key Contributions

  • Introduces ASADPREC, the first unified framework for asynchronous adaptive first-order optimization, extending the synchronous ADPREC framework to the async regime and covering async variants of AdaNorm, full/diagonal AdaGrad, Shampoo, and Muon.
  • Establishes global convergence theory in a fully stochastic, bounded-delay, nonconvex setting, proving an expected gradient-norm convergence rate of $O(1/\sqrt{t})$ (up to logarithmic factors) without requiring the commonly assumed bounded-gradient condition.
  • Proposes and analyzes two momentum variants (MM and MG): the MM momentum (applied to the preconditioner update) preserves the same convergence rate as the momentum-less variant without extra assumptions; the MG momentum (applied to the gradient) requires a sufficiently small stepsize, but its rate degradation in high-variance regimes can be mitigated by decaying the momentum parameter.
  • Extends the theory to allow inexact step normalization, which is practically important for matrix-preconditioned methods like Muon where exact normalization via Newton–Schultz iterations can be expensive.
  • Conducts systematic experiments on six benchmarks (MLPs, Mixture-of-Experts, recommender systems, and CTR prediction), showing that asynchronous block-parallel adaptive optimization preserves convergence quality while delivering significant throughput gains in heterogeneous compute environments.

Methodology

The paper builds on the synchronous ADPREC framework [Gratton & Toint, 2026] and extends it to asynchronous block-parallel execution. The key methodological steps are:

Problem geometry. The parameter vector is decomposed into $L$ disjoint blocks, each living in its own normed space $\mathbb{R}^{n_\ell \times m_\ell}$ with a block-specific norm $\|\cdot\|_\ell$ and dual norm $\|\cdot\|_{\ell,*}$. The overall space is the Cartesian product equipped with the norm $\|V\|^2 = \sum_\ell \|V_\ell\|_\ell^2$. This totally separable geometry avoids inter-block overlap and is natural for layer-wise neural network training.

Algorithmic framework (ASADPREC). Each processor repeatedly: (1) reads the shared parameter vector (block-atomically, allowing inconsistent reads); (2) selects an available block and an associated method $(U_\ell, N_\ell)$; (3) draws a stochastic sample and computes the block gradient; (4) updates the block preconditioner $\Pi_\ell$ via $U_\ell$; (5) computes the preconditioned direction $Z_\ell = \Pi_\ell^{-1/2} G_\ell$; and (6) applies a normalized step using $N_\ell$. The framework is lock-free at the block level, with a flag mechanism preventing two processors from working on the same block simultaneously.

Delayed equivalent (DADPREC). For analysis, the asynchronous sequence is reinterpreted as a synchronous delayed algorithm: each update to block $\ell$ at global time $t$ is viewed as having been computed from a delayed iterate $X_{t-\tau_{t,\ell}}$ with bounded delay $\tau_{t,\ell} \leq \tau$. This equivalence allows the authors to leverage the existing ADPREC convergence machinery with appropriate modifications for delays.

Momentum variants. Two momentum mechanisms are introduced:

  • ASADPREC.MM (momentum on the preconditioner update): $M_\ell = \mu_k M_\ell^- + (1-\mu_k) G_\ell$, then $\Pi_\ell = \Pi_\ell^- + U_\ell(M_\ell^2)$. This only requires the nuclear norm of the momentum term, which is cheap for Muon.
  • ASADPREC.MG (momentum on the gradient): $\Pi_\ell = \Pi_\ell^- + U_\ell(G_\ell^2)$, then $M_\ell = \mu_k M_\ell^- + (1-\mu_k) G_\ell$ is used as the step direction. This requires the nuclear norm of the raw gradient, which is more expensive.

Inexact normalization. The theory is extended to allow approximate normalization operators satisfying $\kappa_{1,N} \leq \|N(Z)\| \leq \kappa_{2,N}$, which covers inexact Newton–Schultz iterations for computing polar factors in Muon.

Experimental design. Six benchmarks are used: FashionMNIST (1-hidden-layer MLP), MoE-FMNIST (Mixture-of-Experts), CovType (2-hidden-layer MLP), MovieLens 100K (NeuralMF), Criteo (hash-embed + MLP), and SVHN (2-hidden-layer MLP). Each model is decomposed into blocks where large weight matrices use Muon preconditioning and remaining parameters use diagonal AdaGrad. Three algorithmic modes are compared: SyncSingle (one gradient shared across all blocks), SyncMulti (each worker computes the full gradient with barrier synchronization), and ASADPREC (async block-owner with per-block locks and a bounded-delay throttle of $\tau_{\max}=512$). A warm-started power iteration (WSPOWER) is introduced to cheaply approximate nuclear norms for the MG momentum variant.

Results & Highlights

Theoretical results.

  • For the momentum-less DADPREC/ASADPREC, Theorem 3.1 shows that under standard smoothness, unbiased oracle, cumulative variance, and bounded-delay assumptions, the expected average gradient norm satisfies:

$$\frac{1}{t+1} \sum_{j=0}^t \mathbb{E}[\|G_j\|_*] \leq \frac{\kappa_a + \kappa_b + \kappa_c \sqrt{N \log(\Theta_t)} + \kappa_c \Theta_t}{\sqrt{t+1}}$$

where $\Theta_t$ grows slowly (logarithmically in the cumulative variance bound $\nu_t$). Under an iteration-wise variance bound decaying as $|J_{t,\ell}|^{-\alpha}$, Corollary 3.2 gives rates of $O(\psi_t^{(1-\alpha)/2}/\sqrt{t+1})$ for $\alpha<1$, $O(\sqrt{\log(\psi_t)/(t+1)})$ for $\alpha=1$, and $O(1/\sqrt{t+1})$ for $\alpha>1$, where $\psi_t$ is the maximum number of updates to any block.

  • Theorem 4.1 shows that the MM momentum variant preserves exactly the same convergence rate without requiring small stepsizes.
  • Theorem 4.2 and Corollary 4.3 show that the MG momentum variant achieves the same rate order only if the stepsize is sufficiently small (or a uniform bound on preconditioned gradient norms holds). Decaying the momentum parameter as $\mu_{t,\ell} \sim |J_{t,\ell}|^{-\beta}$ shifts the effective variance exponent from $\alpha$ to $\alpha+2\beta$, allowing recovery of the momentum-less rate even in high-variance regimes.

Experimental results.

  • Convergence quality: ASADPREC achieves the best final training loss on 4/6 benchmarks and the best gradient norm on 3/6 benchmarks. On generalization metrics (test accuracy/RMSE/AUC), ASADPREC is best on 5/6 benchmarks.
  • Speedup: Throughput speedups of ASADPREC over SyncMulti range from 1.14$\times$ (MovieLens) to 1.43$\times$ (FashionMNIST), closely tracking the effective heterogeneity ratio $(t_{\text{grad}} + t_{\max})/(t_{\text{grad}} + t_{\min})$ rather than the raw block-cost ratio.
  • Momentum: No single momentum variant dominates across all problems, but momentum variants consistently outperform the no-momentum baseline. The MM approach is attractive because it avoids a second matrix decomposition, yielding accuracy gains of +1.8 to +3.6 points over MG on classification tasks. Decaying momentum ($\beta=0.5$) shows problem-dependent benefits.
  • WSPOWER approximation: The warm-started power iteration for approximating nuclear norms restores MG throughput to be comparable with MM, and in some cases produces the best results (e.g., MoE-FMNIST).

Critical Assessment

Novelty and significance: The paper makes a genuinely novel contribution by being (to the authors' knowledge) the first to provide a unified convergence theory for asynchronous adaptive first-order methods, including momentum and inexact normalization. Prior work on asynchronous SGD (Hogwild!, ASAGA, etc.) either did not cover adaptive preconditioning or required stronger assumptions such as bounded gradients or convexity. The extension to methods like Shampoo and Muon is particularly timely given the growing interest in these optimizers for large-model training.

Rigor: The analysis is technically sound and carefully built on the authors' prior ADPREC framework. The use of the DADPREC equivalence to analyze ASADPREC is a standard and valid technique in asynchronous optimization. The assumptions (smoothness, unbiased oracle, cumulative variance, bounded delays) are reasonable and well-motivated. However, the "cumulative variance" condition (Assumption 6) is somewhat non-standard and weaker than per-iteration variance bounds; while this is a strength in generality, it may make the assumptions harder to verify in practice.

Reproducibility: The theoretical results are fully derived in the paper with detailed proofs in the appendices. The experimental setup is described in reasonable detail, including architectures, block decompositions, and hardware (Apple M3 Pro, 12 cores, single-threaded PyTorch). However, the paper does not provide a public code repository, which limits immediate reproducibility. The experiments use relatively small-scale models (the largest being SVHN with 14.7M parameters), so it remains unclear whether the observed benefits will transfer to billion-parameter-scale training.

Red flags:

  1. The totally separable geometry assumption (disjoint blocks with no overlap) is theoretically restrictive. The authors acknowledge this but argue it covers the important case of layer-wise preconditioning. In practice, many modern architectures have more complex parameter coupling.
  2. The experiments assume a "full gradient evaluation model" where each worker computes the entire gradient before extracting its block component. This means the observed heterogeneity and speedups are muted compared to a true block-local gradient setting. The authors explicitly note this as a limitation and suggest that real block-local gradients would make the asynchronous advantage even larger.
  3. The MG momentum variant requires either a small stepsize or a uniform bound on preconditioned gradient norms (condition 4.4), which is hard to verify in practice. This makes the MM variant theoretically cleaner and practically preferred.
  4. The experimental comparisons do not include standard non-adaptive baselines like async SGD or Adam, making it harder to isolate the benefit of adaptive preconditioning in the asynchronous setting versus asynchrony itself.

Strengths

  • First unified theory for asynchronous adaptive first-order methods with momentum and inexact normalization.
  • Weak assumptions: No bounded-gradient requirement, covers nonconvex objectives, and allows cumulative variance rather than strict per-iteration bounds.
  • Practical relevance: Covers currently popular optimizers (Muon, Shampoo, AdaGrad) and addresses real implementation concerns like inexact normalization.
  • Clean momentum theory: The MM variant achieves the same rate without small-stepsize assumptions, a notable improvement over prior async momentum analyses.
  • Well-designed experiments: Six diverse benchmarks with controlled heterogeneity levels, explicit measurement of effective block-cost ratios, and careful comparison of sync vs. async variants.
  • Useful algorithmic insight: The warm-started WSPOWER approximation for nuclear-norm estimation is a practical contribution that makes the MG variant feasible.

---

Limitations

  • Totally separable geometry restricts the applicability to block-disjoint parameter partitions; overlapping blocks or more general sparsity patterns are not covered.
  • Small-scale experiments: The largest model has only ~14.7M parameters. The practical impact on modern LLM-scale training (hundreds of billions of parameters) is not demonstrated.
  • Full-gradient evaluation model: Each worker computes the complete gradient, which limits the observed heterogeneity and speedups. The true potential of ASADPREC may only be realized with cheaper block-local gradients.
  • No comparison to non-adaptive async baselines: The experiments compare sync vs. async variants of the same adaptive method, but do not show whether adaptivity itself is crucial in the async regime.
  • MG momentum requires impractical assumptions: The small-stepsize condition for MG momentum (Theorem 4.2) depends on the unknown Lipschitz constant $L_G$, making the theory less actionable for that variant.
  • Limited momentum clarity: No single momentum variant dominates, and the interaction between momentum decay and problem variance is problem-dependent, leaving practitioners without a clear default recommendation.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can the totally separable geometry assumption be relaxed to allow partially overlapping blocks or general sparsity patterns, as in Hogwild! or ARock?
  2. How do the theoretical guarantees and practical performance translate to billion-parameter models trained on GPU clusters with high communication latency?
  3. What is the optimal block decomposition strategy for a given architecture? Should blocks correspond to layers, to expert groups in MoE, or to finer-grained tensor partitions?
  4. Can the analysis be extended to handle adaptive learning-rate schedules (e.g., cosine decay, warmup) combined with the adaptive preconditioning updates?
  5. Is it possible to design block-local gradient approximations that avoid the full-gradient computation, and how would this change the heterogeneity/speedup trade-off observed in the experiments?
  6. Can the MM vs. MG momentum trade-off be characterized more sharply, perhaps via a problem-dependent criterion that tells practitioners which to use?
  7. How does ASADPREC interact with other distributed-training techniques such as gradient compression, model parallelism, or pipeline parallelism?
