> **一句话总结：** 本文首次系统评估了开放集测试时适应（Open-Set TTA）方法在已知类（InD）准确率与未知类（OOD）拒识率之间的权衡，发现现有方法虽能提升InD准确率却严重牺牲OOD性能，并提出用sigmoid替代softmax输出作为一种新的平衡基线。

> **TL;DR:** This paper provides the first systematic benchmarking of open-set test-time adaptation methods on both in-distribution (InD) accuracy and out-of-distribution (OOD) rejection performance. It reveals that current methods improve InD accuracy at a severe cost to OOD accuracy, and proposes replacing softmax with sigmoid output as a simple new baseline that trades off InD and OOD performance differently.

## 核心贡献

- 首次对现有的开放集TTA方法（SAR、OSTTA、UniEnt、SoTTA）在InD和OOD准确率上进行了系统且标准化的联合评估，填补了以往研究只关注InD指标的空白。
- 提出了一个简单而有效的新基线：将分类头的softmax输出替换为sigmoid输出，使模型能够对所有类别同时保持低置信度，从而实现对OOD样本的显式拒识。
- 设计了更贴近真实部署场景的实验设置，包括OOD样本在batch中比例变化（25%/50%/75%）和OOD batch间隔设置（1/2/3个连续OOD batch），发现现有方法对OOD比例高度敏感。
- 系统比较了不同归一化层（BN、GN、LN）在开放集TTA中的稳定性，发现Layer Normalization（LN）表现最稳定，而Batch Normalization（BN）波动最大。
- 深入分析了各方法自身OOD过滤机制的可靠性，发现这些方法自带的过滤策略远非完美，部分情况下甚至不如简单的置信度阈值。

## Key Contributions

- First systematic and standardized joint evaluation of existing open-set TTA methods (SAR, OSTTA, UniEnt, SoTTA) on both InD and OOD accuracy, filling the gap where prior work focused almost exclusively on InD metrics.
- A simple yet effective new baseline: replacing the softmax output of the classification head with sigmoid output, allowing the model to keep all class probabilities simultaneously low and enabling explicit rejection of OOD samples.
- More realistic evaluation settings closer to real-world deployment, including varying OOD proportions within batches (25%/50%/75%) and OOD batch intervals (1/2/3 consecutive OOD batches), revealing that existing methods are highly sensitive to OOD ratio.
- Systematic comparison of different normalization layers (BN, GN, LN) for open-set adaptation, finding that Layer Normalization (LN) is the most stable while Batch Normalization (BN) exhibits the largest fluctuations.
- In-depth analysis of the reliability of each method's own OOD filtering mechanism, showing that these built-in filters are far from perfect and in some cases perform worse than simple confidence thresholding.

## Methodology

The authors benchmark four established TTA methods on standard corruption benchmarks: **CIFAR-10-C** (small scale, InD) paired with **SVHN-C** and **CIFAR-100-C** (OOD), and **ImageNet-C** (large scale, InD) paired with **ImageNet-O-C** (near OOD) and **Textures-C** (far OOD). The evaluated methods include:

- **Tent**: A closed-set baseline that minimizes prediction entropy.
- **SAR**: A robust TTA method that filters high-entropy and low-entropy (unstable gradient) inputs.
- **UniEnt / UniEnt+**: Open-set methods that minimize entropy on filtered InD samples and maximize entropy on filtered OOD samples.
- **OSTTA**: Filters inputs based on whether confidence drops after an update.
- **SoTTA**: Filters inputs by thresholding the softmax response.

The core methodological contribution is the **sigmoid output baseline**: the final softmax layer is replaced with independent sigmoid activations per class, fine-tuned on source data to match original accuracy. This allows all outputs to remain low simultaneously, enabling explicit rejection when confidence is insufficient. The authors evaluate using multiple metrics: InD accuracy, OOD accuracy (correct rejection rate), mixed accuracy (average), AUROC, FPR@TPR95, and OSCR.

The study goes beyond the standard 50% InD/50% OOD fixed-proportion setting by testing varying OOD proportions (25%, 50%, 75%) and OOD batch intervals (1, 2, 3 consecutive OOD batches between InD batches). The authors also compare normalization layers (BN, GN, LN) across ResNet-50 and ViT-B/16 architectures.

## Results & Highlights

- **InD-OOD trade-off is severe**: Current open-set TTA methods achieve strong InD accuracy (e.g., SoTTA reaches 86.28% on CIFAR-10-C), but when their own OOD filtering is applied, OOD accuracy is often near zero (Tent: 0%, SAR(BN): 8.96%). Tuning thresholds for InD accuracy essentially destroys OOD rejection.

- **Sigmoid baseline improves OOD metrics**: Tent(Sigmoid) achieves dramatically better OOD detection than Tent(Softmax) — AUROC improves from 70.02 to 83.01 on CIFAR-10-C/SVHN-C, with minimal InD accuracy loss (<1% in most cases). On ImageNet-C with varying OOD proportions, Tent(Sigmoid) even surpasses softmax Tent in accuracy.

- **UniEnt achieves best mixed accuracy**: UniEnt(Tent) achieves the highest mixed accuracy (77.44% on CIFAR-10-C/SVHN-C) and best OOD separation via entropy confidence, likely due to its opposing updates on InD vs. OOD data.

- **OOD proximity matters**: When OOD data is more similar to InD (CIFAR-100-C vs. CIFAR-10-C, or ImageNet-O-C vs. ImageNet-C), InD accuracy drops less and can even improve compared to closed-set settings. However, OOD detection becomes harder (lower OOD accuracy).

- **Methods are highly sensitive to OOD proportion**: As OOD proportion increases from 25% to 75%, all metrics consistently degrade. Performance is much more sensitive to mixed InD-OOD batches than to OOD-only batch intervals.

- **Normalization layer effects**: On ImageNet-C with varying OOD proportions, BN shows large accuracy fluctuations; GN is relatively stable; and LN (on ViT-B/16) demonstrates the most stable performance overall, achieving ~54% accuracy and ~69% AUROC across all proportions.

- **Method-specific filtering is imperfect**: Each method's built-in OOD filter (confidence thresholding for SAR/SoTTA, GMM clustering for UniEnt, learned scores for UniEnt+) performs worse than oracle thresholding in many cases, indicating room for improvement in filtering mechanisms.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty**: The paper's primary contribution is empirical rather than algorithmic. The observation that current open-set TTA methods sacrifice OOD accuracy for InD accuracy is important but perhaps unsurprising — methods optimized for InD accuracy would naturally struggle with OOD rejection. The sigmoid output baseline is conceptually simple (multi-label vs. multi-class formulation), though the authors frame it as a "new baseline that strikes a different InD/OOD trade-off." The more realistic evaluation settings (varying OOD proportions and intervals) are a meaningful contribution to benchmark design.

**Rigor**: The experiments are thorough and well-controlled. The authors use standard benchmarks, compare multiple metrics, and carefully tune hyperparameters using both optimistic (test-shift) and realistic (held-out shift) schemes. However, several limitations should be noted:

- **Sigmoid trade-off is not a strict improvement**: The authors are intellectually honest about this — sigmoid improves OOD metrics at a cost to InD accuracy. It is not a Pareto improvement.
- **SAR evaluated out of its intended configuration**: SAR is designed for Group Normalization (GN) or Layer Normalization (LN), but the main experiments use Batch Normalization (BN) for fair comparison. The authors acknowledge this and provide supplementary results with GN, but the main tables may understate SAR's performance.
- **Limited architectural diversity**: Most experiments use ResNet variants. The ViT-B/16 results are confined to the normalization-layer comparison section.
- **Corruption-only shifts**: The evaluation focuses on synthetic corruptions (Hendrycks & Dietterich benchmarks). The single additional shift benchmark (ImageNet-R renditions) suggests results may not generalize to natural distribution shifts.
- **No theoretical analysis**: The paper is entirely empirical. While the empirical findings are valuable, there is no theoretical justification for why sigmoid output should improve OOD separation beyond the intuitive argument about independent probabilities.

**Reproducibility**: The paper uses standard public benchmarks and established model checkpoints (RobustBench). The methodology is described in sufficient detail for reproduction. However, code is not provided in the paper, and some hyperparameter tuning details (exact learning rates, threshold ranges) are abbreviated.

**Significance**: The paper makes a valuable contribution by highlighting a critical blind spot in open-set TTA research. The finding that current methods' own OOD filters are imperfect is particularly actionable. The sigmoid baseline, while simple, opens a useful direction for rethinking output distributions in open-set settings.

## Strengths

- Comprehensive benchmarking across multiple methods, datasets, scales, and metrics.
- Honest and clear presentation of trade-offs; the authors do not oversell the sigmoid baseline.
- Realistic evaluation settings (varying OOD proportions and intervals) that better reflect deployment conditions.
- Systematic investigation of normalization layers for open-set adaptation, complementing prior closed-set analyses.
- Well-written and well-organized; the motivation and experimental design are clearly explained.
- Peer-reviewed in TMLR with open review, enhancing trustworthiness.

## Limitations

- The sigmoid baseline trades InD accuracy for OOD metrics — it does not achieve strict improvement on both fronts.
- Main SAR experiments use BN instead of its intended GN/LN configuration.
- Evaluation is limited to synthetic corruptions with only one natural shift benchmark (ImageNet-R).
- No theoretical analysis or ablation studies on why sigmoid entropy distributions become bimodal.
- Method-specific filtering is only evaluated on CIFAR-10-C, not ImageNet-C.
- The paper does not investigate other output distributions beyond softmax and sigmoid, nor does it explore calibration effects of sigmoid output.
- Computational cost analysis is minimal; only a brief timing comparison on ImageNet-C is provided.

## Relevance

本文对开放集测试时适应领域具有重要的警示意义：现有方法在追求已知类准确率的同时，往往严重忽视了未知类的正确拒识。对于从事模型部署、自动驾驶、内容审核等需要同时应对分布偏移和未知类别的应用研究者来说，本文提出的评估框架和sigmoid基线值得深入参考。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Can the sigmoid output formulation be combined with existing open-set filtering techniques (e.g., UniEnt's opposing updates) to achieve better Pareto fronts on both InD and OOD metrics?
2. How does the sigmoid baseline perform on natural distribution shifts (e.g., domain adaptation benchmarks like Office-Home, VisDA) rather than synthetic corruptions?
3. What is the theoretical mechanism behind the bimodal sigmoid entropy distribution observed for InD and OOD data, and can this be exploited for better filtering?
4. Would calibration techniques (temperature scaling, label smoothing during fine-tuning) improve the InD accuracy of sigmoid models without sacrificing OOD performance?
5. How do the findings extend to other architectures (ConvNeXt, Swin Transformer) and other normalization strategies (RMSNorm, Filter Response Normalization)?
6. Can the OOD proportion be estimated online during adaptation to dynamically adjust thresholds or update rules, mitigating the sensitivity to OOD ratio?
7. The paper finds that near-OOD data can actually help InD adaptation in some cases — can this phenomenon be exploited as a feature rather than a bug, perhaps via OOD-aware self-training?
