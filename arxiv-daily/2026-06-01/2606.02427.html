Key Contributions

  • Introduces a Jacobian-based spectral audit framework for in-context operator learning (ICON), addressing a gap in existing evaluation methods.
  • Treats the derivative of network output with respect to the query function as a learned tangent operator, projected onto a real Fourier basis to obtain local spectral characterization (frequency-dependent gains, phase structure, cross-mode coupling).
  • Designs a set of complementary diagnostics: Fourier-mode gains, accumulated relative spectral error, Fourier feature maps, and off-diagonal ratio.
  • Validates the audit on three classical PDE benchmarks (advection, Burgers, Allen–Cahn), revealing operator-level failure modes invisible to standard prediction-error metrics.
  • Through corrupted-prompt negative controls and training-bandwidth ablations, demonstrates that prediction accuracy and local operator fidelity are distinct and decoupled properties.

Methodology

The audit examines a trained ICON after a prompt is fixed. For a given context $C_\eta$, the ICON induces a deterministic map $G^{\text{ICON}}_{C_\eta}: \mathcal{A} \to \mathcal{U}$ from query function to predicted output. The authors compute the query Jacobian $J^{\text{ICON}}_{C_\eta}(u_q) = D_{u_q} G^{\text{ICON}}_{C_\eta}(u_q)$ via automatic differentiation and interpret it as the learned tangent operator. The reference object is the true PDE tangent map $J^\dagger_\eta(u_q) = D_{u_q} G^\dagger_\eta(u_q)$.

The Jacobian is probed with real Fourier basis perturbations $\phi^{\cos}_k(x) = \sqrt{2}\cos(2\pi kx)$ and $\phi^{\sin}_k(x) = \sqrt{2}\sin(2\pi kx)$. The Fourier-mode gains are defined as $g^\alpha(k; \eta) = \|J \phi^\alpha_k\|_2 / \|\phi^\alpha_k\|_2$. The accumulated relative spectral error compares the learned and true gains cumulatively up to mode $K$. To inspect mode mixing, the Jacobian is projected onto finite Fourier bases yielding matrices $A^{\text{ICON}}_C$ and $A^\dagger_\eta$, from which the relative Frobenius error and off-diagonal ratio $r_{\text{off}} = \|A - \text{diag}(A)\|_F / \|A\|_F$ are computed.

All main experiments use the same Transformer ICON architecture (6 layers, 8 heads, model dimension 256, head dimension 32), trained with AdamW for 200,000 iterations on an offline pool of 2000 hidden operators. The three PDE families tested are: (1) advection $u_t + cu_x = 0$ with hidden speed $c \in [0.5, 1.0]$; (2) viscous Burgers $u_t + uu_x = cu_{xx}$ with hidden viscosity $c \in [0.005, 0.05]$; and (3) Allen–Cahn $u_t = \varepsilon^2 u_{xx} + u - u^3$ with hidden interface width $\varepsilon \in [0.02, 0.08]$.

Results & Highlights

Advection. The exact operator is a pure shift with unit gain for every Fourier mode and block-diagonal 2×2 rotation structure. The audit confirms that ICON preserves amplitudes, rotates sine–cosine pairs by the correct phase, and avoids cross-frequency mixing within the training range. Outside the training range, spectral bias causes rapid high-frequency decay.

Burgers. The audit successfully separates two distinct mechanisms: viscosity-dependent damping (visible in the ordered decay of gain curves) and nonlinear mode coupling (visible in off-diagonal Fourier structure). Sharper base states (near-shock-like profiles) demand more from the model, producing stronger mode coupling and larger accumulated spectral errors.

Corrupted-prompt negative control (Burgers). A model was trained on prompts where context and query were generated with different viscosities. When evaluated on clean prompts, this model still produced partially accurate pointwise predictions, but its learned tangent operator showed a severely degraded viscosity-dependent damping profile and incorrect Fourier mode coupling. This demonstrates that the spectral audit detects operator-level inconsistency that prediction error alone cannot.

Allen–Cahn. The audit captures the competition between reaction-driven low-frequency growth (near $u \approx 0$) and diffusion-driven high-frequency damping. ICON reproduces the dominant diagonal Fourier structure and avoids spurious global mode mixing, indicating it learns the correct local stability mechanism rather than mere smoothing.

Bandwidth ablation (Advection). A model trained only on 8 Fourier modes fails to extrapolate the correct phase rotation beyond $k=8$, even when pointwise errors on 16-mode test functions appear modest. The audit identifies this as a structural generalization failure, not just larger prediction error.

Critical Assessment

The paper makes a clear and important conceptual contribution: it distinguishes between prediction accuracy and operator fidelity, and provides a concrete, theoretically grounded protocol for auditing the latter. The diagnostics are well-chosen—gain curves capture amplitude response, phase/block errors capture transport structure, and feature maps capture mode coupling—and the experiments systematically show what each diagnostic reveals.

The three PDE examples are well-calibrated to test different operator properties (linear shift, nonlinear diffusion-coupling, reaction-diffusion stability). The corrupted-prompt negative control is particularly strong: it shows that a model can pass the prediction-error test while failing the operator-fidelity test, which is the paper's central claim.

However, there are limitations. The experiments are restricted to 1D periodic domains with simple geometries, where Fourier analysis is maximally transparent. The computational cost of computing full Jacobians or even Jacobian–vector products for high-dimensional problems (2D/3D, irregular domains) is not quantified, though the authors acknowledge this as a natural extension. The ICON architecture is fixed (6-layer Transformer); it is unclear whether the observed phenomena generalize to other architectures such as FNO or DeepONet. The paper also focuses entirely on a posteriori auditing and does not explore whether spectral diagnostics could be incorporated as training objectives to improve operator fidelity directly.

A subtle concern is that the audit assumes differentiability of the network with respect to the query input, which holds for standard neural operators but may be restrictive for certain discrete or hybrid architectures. Additionally, the metric design (cumulative relative error to avoid division-by-tiny-denominator issues) is sensible but means high-frequency errors are weighted by their contribution to the cumulative sum rather than inspected mode-by-mode.

Overall, the work is rigorous, well-motivated, and provides a valuable new evaluation paradigm. The caveats are acknowledged by the authors and are largely directions for future work rather than fundamental flaws.

Strengths

  • Conceptually novel and timely: shifts evaluation from output matching to operator-level fidelity, a distinction increasingly important for rollout, inverse problems, and design tasks.
  • Theoretically grounded in variational calculus and Fourier analysis, with clean mathematical definitions for all diagnostics.
  • Reveals hidden failure modes (high-frequency degradation, incorrect phase recovery, prompt–operator inconsistency) that standard $L^2$ prediction metrics miss.
  • Strong experimental design: three distinct PDE families, deterministic base-state probes, corrupted-prompt negative controls, and bandwidth ablations.
  • The spectral audit is architecture-agnostic in principle and can be extended to other bases (wavelets, Laplacian eigenfunctions, randomized directions).
  • Companion code and detailed appendices (true tangent derivations, prediction checks) support reproducibility.

Limitations

  • Experimental scope is limited to 1D periodic domains; extension to higher dimensions and irregular geometries requires replacing the Fourier basis and is not demonstrated.
  • Computational cost of Jacobian-based auditing for large-scale problems is not quantified or discussed in detail.
  • Only a single architecture (Transformer ICON) is tested; generalization to FNO, DeepONet, or other conditional operators is mentioned as future work but not validated.
  • The audit is a posteriori and diagnostic; the paper does not explore using spectral feedback to improve training (e.g., as a regularization or loss term).
  • The experiments use a fixed prompt size ($N_{\text{demo}} = 5$); sensitivity to prompt length and quality is not systematically studied.
  • All examples involve relatively smooth solutions; behavior near shocks or discontinuities is not explicitly tested.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does the computational cost of the full Jacobian or Jacobian–vector product audit scale with spatial resolution, problem dimension, and model size? Can randomized sketching methods reduce this cost?
  • Can the spectral audit be extended to 2D/3D problems on irregular domains using wavelets, Laplacian eigenfunctions, or graph Fourier bases?
  • Does the audit generalize to other neural operator architectures (FNO, DeepONet, GNN-based operators) and to non-differentiable or hybrid models?
  • Can spectral diagnostics be incorporated into the training objective (e.g., as a regularization term on the query Jacobian) to improve operator fidelity directly?
  • How sensitive are the audit results to prompt size, prompt quality, and the presence of noisy or partially inconsistent context pairs?
  • How does the audit behave for PDEs with discontinuities, shocks, or sharp interfaces, where Fourier analysis is less natural?
  • Could the tangent-operator structure be used to certify stability or sensitivity bounds for learned operators in safety-critical applications?
  • What is the relationship between the spectral audit and physics-informed training? Would PINN-style models exhibit better operator fidelity, and can the audit quantify this?
