> **一句话总结：** 本文将经典的Polyak自适应步长思想推广到Sharpness-Aware Minimization（SAM）及其非归一化变体USAM，提出了无需精细调参即能达到与手工调优基线相当或更优泛化性能的确定性及随机自适应调度器，并在凸/强凸光滑设定下建立了线性及次线性收敛理论保证。

> **TL;DR:** This paper extends the classical Polyak adaptive step-size principle to Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) and its unnormalized variant (USAM), proposing deterministic and stochastic adaptive schedulers that match or outperform hand-tuned SAM baselines without extensive hyperparameter tuning, with proven linear and sublinear convergence guarantees under smooth convex/strongly convex assumptions.

## 核心贡献

- **Polyak型SAM自适应步长**：首次将Polyak步长思想与SAM型更新相结合，针对USAM推导了确定性与随机情形下的闭式自适应学习率调度器；当扰动半径$\rho_t = 0$时，所提方法退化为经典Polyak步长及随机Polyak步长SPS$_{\max}$，实现了标准梯度方法与锐度感知方法的统一框架。
- **确定性收敛理论**：在光滑且（强）凸的设定下，证明了USAM搭配Polyak Scheduler在强凸目标上的线性收敛率$\|x^t - x^*\|^2 \leq (1 - \frac{\mu(1-L\rho)^2}{4L})^t \|x^0 - x^*\|^2$，以及凸目标上的$\mathcal{O}(1/T)$次线性收敛率；同时允许非增的半径调度$\rho_t \to 0$，并保证$\|\nabla f(x^t)\| \to 0$。
- **随机收敛理论**：在有限和（finite-sum）随机设定下，证明了USAM搭配Stochastic Polyak Scheduler在强凸目标上的期望线性收敛至一个由方差量$\sigma^2 = \mathbb{E}[f_{S_t}(x^*) - \ell^*_{S_t}]$刻画的邻域内，以及凸目标上的次线性收敛界$\mathbb{E}[f(x^T) - f^*] \leq \mathcal{O}(1/T) + \mathcal{O}(\sigma^2)$。该理论无需此前自适应SAM分析中常见的有界方差、有界梯度或增长条件等额外假设。
- **插值情形与附带结果**：证明了在插值（interpolation）条件下（$\sigma^2 = 0$），随机方法的邻域项消失，可收敛至最优解；作为副产品，还首次给出了常数步长USAM在随机情形下的收敛分析，同样无需额外假设。
- **深度学习的实验验证**：在CIFAR-10/100上使用ResNet-20/32进行系统评估，所提Stochastic Polyak Scheduler在无需调参的情况下，达到了与精心调优的常数学习率SAM/USAM相当或更高的测试精度，且对较大的锐度半径$\rho$表现出显著优于Cosine Annealing的鲁棒性。

## Key Contributions

- **Polyak-type SAM Adaptive Step Sizes**: For the first time, connects Polyak step sizes with SAM-style updates. Derives closed-form adaptive learning-rate schedulers for USAM in both deterministic and stochastic settings. When the sharpness radius $\rho_t = 0$, the schedulers reduce to the classical Polyak step size (GD-PS) and its stochastic analogue SPS$_{\max}$, unifying standard and sharpness-aware gradient methods within a single framework.
- **Deterministic Convergence Theory**: Under smooth and (strongly) convex assumptions, proves linear convergence for USAM with Polyak Scheduler on strongly convex objectives with contraction factor $1 - \frac{\mu(1-L\rho)^2}{4L}$, and an $\mathcal{O}(1/T)$ sublinear rate for convex objectives. Also allows a non-increasing radius schedule $\rho_t \to 0$ and guarantees $\|\nabla f(x^t)\| \to 0$.
- **Stochastic Convergence Theory**: In the finite-sum stochastic setting, proves expected linear convergence to a neighborhood whose size is governed by the variance measure $\sigma^2 = \mathbb{E}[f_{S_t}(x^*) - \ell^*_{S_t}]$ for strongly convex objectives, and sublinear bounds $\mathbb{E}[f(x^T) - f^*] \leq \mathcal{O}(1/T) + \mathcal{O}(\sigma^2)$ for convex objectives. Crucially, this analysis does **not** require extra assumptions such as bounded variance, bounded gradients, or growth conditions commonly imposed in prior adaptive SAM analyses.
- **Interpolation and Side Results**: Shows that the neighborhood term vanishes under interpolation ($\sigma^2 = 0$), enabling convergence to the optimum. As a side result, provides a novel convergence analysis for constant-step-size USAM in the stochastic regime, again without extra assumptions.
- **Deep-Learning Empirical Validation**: Conducts systematic experiments on CIFAR-10/100 with ResNet-20/32, demonstrating that the Stochastic Polyak Scheduler matches or exceeds the test accuracy of carefully tuned constant-learning-rate SAM/USAM baselines, while exhibiting significantly better robustness to larger sharpness radii $\rho$ compared to Cosine Annealing.

## Methodology

The paper targets the finite-sum optimization problem $f(x) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n f_i(x)$, where each $f_i$ is differentiable, lower bounded, convex, and $L_i$-smooth. The authors focus on the **Unnormalized Sharpness-Aware Minimization (USAM)** update:

$$e_t = x_t + \rho_t \nabla f_{S_t}(x_t), \quad x_{t+1} = x_t - \gamma_t \nabla f_{S_t}(e_t)$$

where $S_t$ is a mini-batch of size $\tau$, and $\rho_t$ is the sharpness radius. The goal is to derive an adaptive $\gamma_t$ that eliminates manual learning-rate tuning.

**Polyak Scheduler for deterministic USAM** is derived by bounding $\|x_{t+1} - x^*\|^2$ and minimizing this bound over $\gamma_t \geq 0$:

$$\gamma_t = \frac{[f(e_t) - f^* - \rho_t \langle \nabla f(e_t), \nabla f(x_t) \rangle]_+}{\|\nabla f(e_t)\|^2}$$

where $[z]_+ = \max\{z, 0\}$. When $\rho_t \leq 1/L$, the numerator is automatically non-negative, so the ReLU safeguard is redundant. The step size is automatically non-negative and satisfies $\gamma_t \geq \frac{1 - L\rho_t}{2L(1 + L\rho_t)}$.

**Stochastic Polyak Scheduler** extends this to mini-batch settings by analogy with SPS$_{\max}$:

$$\gamma_t = \min\left\{ \frac{[f_{S_t}(e_t) - \ell^*_{S_t} - \rho_t \langle \nabla f_{S_t}(e_t), \nabla f_{S_t}(x_t) \rangle]_+}{\|\nabla f_{S_t}(e_t)\|^2}, \; \gamma_b \right\}$$

where $\ell^*_{S_t}$ is any lower bound on the mini-batch objective (typically $\ell^*_{S_t} = 0$ for non-negative losses), and $\gamma_b > 0$ is a cap preventing excessively large steps. When $\rho_t \leq 1/L_{\max}$, a similar lower bound $\gamma_t \geq \frac{1 - L_{\max}\rho_t}{2L_{\max}(1 + L_{\max}\rho_t)}$ holds.

The paper also extends the same Polyak-style principle to normalized SAM, yielding:

$$\gamma_t = \min\left\{ \frac{[f_{S_t}(e_t) - \ell^*_{S_t} - \langle \nabla f_{S_t}(e_t), e_t - x_t \rangle]_+}{\|\nabla f_{S_t}(e_t)\|^2}, \; \gamma_b \right\}$$

In the SAM case, the non-negativity argument does not directly apply, so the ReLU safeguard $[\cdot]_+$ is retained, though it is observed to be rarely active in deep learning experiments.

## Results & Highlights

**Synthetic verification (Section 4.1)**:
- On strongly convex ridge regression problems, deterministic USAM with Polyak Scheduler exhibits the predicted linear convergence and reaches the optimum faster than constant-step-size USAM baselines.
- In the stochastic mini-batch setting, Stochastic Polyak Scheduler converges to a neighborhood of the solution, consistent with the theoretical predictions.
- Compared to other adaptive SAM variants (including AdaGrad-based LightSAM rules), the Polyak Scheduler is the only adaptive method that both converges fastest in the deterministic regime and carries formal convergence guarantees without extra assumptions.

**Deep learning benchmarks (Sections 4.2–4.3)**:
- **CIFAR-100 / ResNet-32 (USAM)**: Stochastic Polyak Scheduler achieves **92.24%** test accuracy at $\rho = 0.2$, compared to **90.45%** for constant USAM and **88.77%** for Cosine Annealing. At larger radii ($\rho = 0.4$), the gap widens: Polyak Scheduler maintains **92.01%** while Cosine Annealing drops to **86.52%**.
- **CIFAR-100 / ResNet-32 (SAM)**: Similar trends hold for normalized SAM. Stochastic Polyak Scheduler achieves **92.24%** at $\rho = 0.2$, outperforming constant SAM (90.53%) and Cosine Annealing (89.03%). At $\rho = 0.4$, Polyak Scheduler scores **91.61%** versus Cosine Annealing's **84.61%**.
- **Robustness to $\rho$**: Cosine Annealing suffers a severe accuracy fall-off as $\rho$ increases, whereas Stochastic Polyak Scheduler remains stable and high-performing across the entire tested range $\rho \in \{0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.4\}$.
- **No hyperparameter tuning**: The Polyak Scheduler uses only $\ell^*_{S_t} = 0$ and $\gamma_b = 1.0$, requiring **no learning-rate grid search**, in contrast to the baselines which tune $\gamma \in \{10^{-3}, 10^{-2}, 10^{-1}\}$.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty**: The core intellectual contribution is the synthesis of two well-established ideas—Polyak adaptive step sizes and SAM/USAM updates—into a unified framework. While neither idea is new, their combination is novel and non-trivial: the derivation must carefully handle the two-step USAM/SAM structure (perturbation + gradient step) and the interaction between the perturbation radius $\rho_t$ and the adaptive step size. The paper successfully navigates this and provides the first convergence theory for Polyak-type adaptive USAM/SAM.

**Rigor**: The proofs are technically sound and follow classical optimization analysis patterns. The deterministic results (Theorems 3.1–3.2) are clean and tight: when $\rho = 0$, the rates exactly match those of GD with the Polyak step size. The stochastic results (Theorems 3.5–3.8) are more involved but avoid the extra assumptions (bounded variance, bounded gradients, growth conditions) that plague prior adaptive SAM analyses. This is a meaningful theoretical advance. The variance measure $\sigma^2$ is standard in the Polyak literature and well-motivated.

**Reproducibility**: The paper provides extensive experimental detail in Appendix E, including full hyperparameter grids, random seeds, hardware specifications, and pseudocode. The code is publicly available. The synthetic experiments directly verify the theoretical predictions. The deep learning experiments are standard (CIFAR-10/100, ResNet-20/32) and reproducible.

**Limitations and red flags**:
1. **Convexity gap**: The primary weakness is that the theoretical guarantees assume convexity/strong convexity and smoothness. Deep neural network objectives are neither convex nor smooth in general. The authors acknowledge this openly and suggest extending to PL conditions or non-convex settings as future work. The empirical success on CIFAR suggests the method is promising beyond the theory, but the theoretical guarantees do not formally cover the deep learning regime.
2. **Neighborhood convergence in stochastic setting**: The stochastic guarantees only hold up to a $\sigma^2$-neighborhood. While the paper shows this vanishes under interpolation (a realistic condition for over-parameterized models), the non-interpolated case remains approximate.
3. **Dependence on known lower bound**: Both deterministic and stochastic schedulers require knowledge of $f^*$ (or $\ell^*_{S_t}$). The paper uses $\ell^*_{S_t} = 0$ for non-negative losses (e.g., cross-entropy), which is valid but may be loose. A tighter lower bound could improve performance.
4. **Sharpness radius still requires tuning**: While the learning rate is now adaptive, the sharpness radius $\rho$ remains a hyperparameter. The paper demonstrates robustness across a range of $\rho$ values, but $\rho$ is not eliminated as a tuning knob.
5. **Limited scale**: The deep learning experiments are limited to ResNet-20/32 on CIFAR-10/100. Performance on larger architectures (e.g., Vision Transformers) or larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) and on language models remains unverified.

## Strengths

- **Eliminates learning-rate tuning for SAM**: The primary practical benefit. SAM users typically perform expensive grid searches over learning rates; this scheduler removes that burden while maintaining or improving accuracy.
- **Strong theoretical grounding**: First convergence theory for adaptive SAM/USAM based on Polyak step sizes, with clean rates and minimal assumptions.
- **Robustness to sharpness radius**: Unlike Cosine Annealing, which degrades severely at larger $\rho$, the Polyak Scheduler maintains stable high performance, making it more reliable in practice.
- **Unifying framework**: The step size formulas naturally reduce to classical Polyak/SPS$_{\max}$ when $\rho = 0$, providing conceptual clarity and backward compatibility.
- **Interpolation insight**: The theoretical observation that the neighborhood term vanishes under interpolation is practically relevant for modern over-parameterized deep learning.
- **Extends to normalized SAM**: The same principle applies to both USAM and SAM, demonstrating generality.

## Limitations

- **Theory assumes convexity/smoothness**: The convergence guarantees do not formally cover the non-convex, non-smooth objectives encountered in deep neural network training. Empirical success on CIFAR is encouraging but not theoretically guaranteed.
- **Stochastic convergence is approximate**: Without interpolation, convergence is only guaranteed to a neighborhood of the optimum, not the optimum itself.
- **Requires a lower bound $f^*$ or $\ell^*_{S_t}$**: While $\ell^*_{S_t} = 0$ works for common non-negative losses, this may not always be the case, and a poor lower bound could degrade performance.
- **Sharpness radius $\rho$ is still a hyperparameter**: The scheduler adapts $\gamma_t$ but not $\rho_t$. The paper uses a constant $\rho$, leaving open the question of how to adaptively schedule $\rho$.
- **Limited empirical scale**: Experiments are restricted to ResNet-20/32 on CIFAR-10/100. Generalization to larger models, datasets (e.g., ImageNet), and domains (e.g., NLP) is unverified.
- **Computational overhead**: Like standard SAM, each iteration requires two gradient evaluations (at $x_t$ and $e_t$). The adaptive scheduler does not reduce this overhead, though it does not increase it either.

## Relevance

本文将Polyak自适应步长引入SAM优化器，在保持甚至提升模型泛化性能的同时，从根本上缓解了SAM对学习率精细调参的依赖，对深度学习实践者尤其具有吸引力；其严格的收敛理论也为后续在非凸设定下的推广奠定了坚实基础。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Can the convexity-based analysis be extended to Polyak-Lojasiewicz (PL) or general non-convex objectives while retaining Polyak-style adaptivity and the minimal-assumption flavor?
2. How does the Stochastic Polyak Scheduler perform on large-scale benchmarks such as ImageNet or modern architectures like Vision Transformers and LLMs, where training dynamics differ substantially from CIFAR-ResNet?
3. Is it possible to derive an adaptive rule for the sharpness radius $\rho_t$ itself, potentially eliminating the last remaining hyperparameter in SAM-style training?
4. How sensitive is the method to the choice of lower bound $\ell^*_{S_t}$ in losses that are not naturally non-negative (e.g., certain regression or RL objectives)?
5. Can the Polyak SAM scheduler be combined with other SAM variants (e.g., mSAM, Fisher SAM, LookSAM) or second-order approximations to further improve efficiency or generalization?
6. What is the interplay between the Polyak step size and common deep learning techniques such as learning-rate warmup, weight decay, and mixed-precision training? Does the adaptive rule remain effective without warmup?
