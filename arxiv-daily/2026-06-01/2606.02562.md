> **一句话总结：** 本文首次提出了无需假设完美推理或滤波器有效性的信念空间安全滤波器（BeliefSF）的概率安全认证框架，通过将共形预测验证聚焦于"可信推理区域"，在保持形式化安全保证的同时显著提升了滤波器的许可性（permissiveness）。

> **TL;DR:** This paper introduces the first verification framework for belief-space safety filters (BeliefSF) that provides probabilistic safety guarantees without assuming perfect inference or filter validity, achieving substantially more permissive filtering by focusing conformal prediction verification on "trusted inference regions" where the robot's runtime inference is expected to be reliable.

## 核心贡献

- **首次为 BeliefSF 提供形式化安全保证**：建立了首个无需假设完美推理或滤波器有效性的交互式信念空间运动规划的闭环安全保证框架。
- **提出 JIST 验证算法**：设计了联合推理-安全测试（Joint Inference–Safety Test, JIST），一种显式考虑推理质量的共形预测安全验证算法。
- **引入"可信推理区域"概念**：通过定义推理评分函数 $h_L(z)$，将验证和部署聚焦于推理算法预期可靠的区域，从而显著降低安全滤波器的保守性。
- **在 18 维对抗性驾驶场景中验证**：在具有策略性欺骗性对手的智能车辆-行人交互基准上，展示了 JIST 相比标准共形预测基线能获得大得多的认证安全集和显著更低的拒绝率。

## Key Contributions

- **First formal safety guarantees for BeliefSF without perfect inference assumptions**: Establishes the first framework for closed-loop safety certification of interactive motion planning in belief spaces that does not require perfect inference or filter validity.
- **Proposes the JIST verification algorithm**: Designs the Joint Inference–Safety Test (JIST), a conformal prediction-based safety verification algorithm that explicitly accounts for inference quality.
- **Introduces the "trusted inference region" concept**: Defines an inference score function $h_L(z)$ to focus verification and deployment on regions where the robot's inference algorithm is expected to be reliable, substantially reducing conservativeness.
- **Validates on an 18D adversarial driving scenario**: Demonstrates on a vehicle-pedestrian interaction benchmark with a strategically deceptive opponent that JIST achieves a significantly larger certified safe set and much lower rejection rates compared to standard conformal prediction baselines.

## Methodology

The paper builds on three key technical pillars: **belief-space safety filtering (BeliefSF)**, **Hamilton-Jacobi (HJ) reachability analysis**, and **conformal prediction (CP)**.

**Belief-Space Safety Filtering (BeliefSF).** Standard safety filters operate only in physical state space, treating uncertainty over other agents as static and leading to excessive conservatism. BeliefSF [22] reduces conservativeness by conditioning the opponent's control bound on the robot's runtime inference. The joint physical-belief dynamics are defined as $z_{t+1} = F(z_t, u_t^e, u_t^o)$, where $z_t = (x_t, b_t)$ is the information state combining physical state $x_t$ and belief $b_t$ over the opponent's hidden type $\theta$. The opponent's inferred control bound is:
$$\hat{U}_b^o(z_t) := \bigcup_{\theta \in \Theta} \hat{U}_\theta^o(z_t), \quad \hat{U}_\theta^o(z_t) := \begin{cases} U_\theta^o(x_t), & \text{if } b_t(\theta) \geq \epsilon_\theta \\ \emptyset, & \text{if } b_t(\theta) < \epsilon_\theta \end{cases}$$

The safety value function is extended to information space via a game-theoretic Isaacs equation:
$$V(z) = \min\{g(x), \max_{b^o(z)} \min_{u^e \in U^e} \max_{u^o \in \hat{U}_b^o(z)} V(F(z, u^e, u^o))\}$$

**Direct CP Baseline.** Following Lin and Bansal [27], the authors first consider a direct extension of split conformal prediction to belief-space safety. Given a learned safe set $\Omega_\delta = \{z \mid V(z) \geq \delta\}$, they sample $N$ i.i.d. initial states from $\Omega_\delta$, forward-simulate trajectories under the BeliefSF and opponent policy, and compute empirical safety scores $J_\phi(\mathbf{z}_0^i) = \min_{t \in [0,T]} g(x_t)$. Counting $k$ unsafe trajectories where $J_\phi < 0$, Proposition 1 provides a probabilistic guarantee: if $(\epsilon, \beta)$ satisfy $\sum_{i=0}^{k} \binom{N}{i} \epsilon^i (1-\epsilon)^{N-i} \leq \beta$, then with probability at least $1-\beta$, $P_{z \in \Omega_\delta}(J_\phi(\mathbf{z}) \leq 0) \leq \epsilon$.

**JIST: Joint Inference–Safety Test.** The key insight is that standard CP conflates safety failures caused by inaccurate inference with those caused by the neural safety filter itself. When inference is generally accurate but occasionally fails in specific regions, standard CP must shrink the safe set uniformly. JIST decouples these failure modes by:

1. **Defining an inference score function** $h_L: \mathcal{Z} \to \mathbb{R}$ that maps an information state to a score assessing inference quality. In practice, this can be a binary classifier predicting whether $u_t^o \in \hat{U}_b^o(z_t)$ for all $t \in [0,T]$, or a confidence score from modern trajectory predictors.

2. **Defining the trusted inference region**: $\Omega_{\delta|h} := \{z \mid V(z) \geq \delta, h_L(z) \geq 0\}$, a subset of the learned safe set where inference is expected to be accurate.

3. **Performing CP only within $\Omega_{\delta|h}$**: Sampling $N$ i.i.d. initial states from $\Omega_{\delta|h}$ (rather than all of $\Omega_\delta$) and applying the same split CP procedure.

**Theorem 1** establishes that with probability at least $1-\beta$, $P_{z \in \Omega_{\delta|h}}(J_\phi(\mathbf{z}) \leq 0) \leq \epsilon$ when $(\epsilon, \beta)$ satisfy the same binomial tail bound. The proof follows from standard split CP, with the key difference that sampling is restricted to the trusted inference region.

**Online Deployment (Algorithm 2).** At the start of interaction, the system checks if $z_0 \in \Omega_{\delta|h}$. If yes, standard BeliefSF is applied with provable safety guarantees. If not, the system executes **REJECT()** (handing control back to human) or **FALLBACK()** (switching to a more conservative backup filter). This can also operate in receding horizon fashion by recursively checking $z_t \in \Omega_{\delta|h}$.

## Results & Highlights

The evaluation uses an **18-dimensional vehicle-pedestrian interaction system** where the ego autonomous vehicle is uncertain about the opponent's semantic class (pedestrian vs. Segway rider) and intended destination among 9 goal positions. The opponent applies an adversarial policy synthesized via game-theoretic RL that learns to exploit the ego robot by generating deceptive motions keeping the robot's belief close to uniform.

**BeliefSF Empirical Performance (1000 trials, δ = 0):**
- BeliefSF: **94.3% safe rate**, **99.8% task completion**
- Physical-space baseline (same RL algorithm): **77.7% safe rate**, **37.6% task completion**

This demonstrates BeliefSF is dramatically safer and more performant than physical-space filters. Notably, the adversarial opponent actively exploits the ego in belief space through deceptive behaviors.

**Verification Results (N = 20,000, β = 10⁻⁵, δ = 0.1):**
| Method | Failure Cases (k) | Certified Safety (1−ϵ) |
|--------|------------------|------------------------|
| DIRECT CP | 438 | 97.34% |
| JIST | 107 | **99.21%** |

JIST achieves **1.87% higher certified safety coverage** than DIRECT CP. Among JIST's 107 failures, inference was incorrect in 101 cases (94.4%), confirming that inference errors dominate safety failures.

**Rejection Rates (Figure 3):** For the same safety coverage, JIST achieves dramatically lower rejection rates:
- At δ = 0.1: JIST rejection ≈ **4.22%** vs. DIRECT CP ≈ **71.92%**
- At δ = 0.3: JIST rejection ≈ **5.55%** vs. DIRECT CP ≈ **41.88%**

The JIST rejection rate is close to the false inference rate (4.67%), since BeliefSF generally enforces safety when inference correctly predicts the opponent's control bound. DIRECT CP must use a substantially larger δ_baseline to achieve the same safety coverage, leading to much higher rejection.

**Visualizations (Figure 5):** Planar slices of the certified safe set show that JIST preserves a much larger operational envelope than DIRECT CP for equivalent safety coverage.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: High. The core novelty lies in decoupling inference failures from filter failures during safety verification, achieved through the trusted inference region concept. While CP for neural safety filters was introduced by Lin and Bansal [27], extending it to belief spaces and explicitly accounting for inference reliability is a genuine advancement. The JIST algorithm is elegant in its simplicity—it requires no additional sample or computational complexity beyond training $h_L$.

- **Rigor**: Strong. The theoretical framework is sound. Theorem 1's proof correctly applies standard split CP machinery. The paper is careful about assumptions: it explicitly notes that CP provides distributional (not worst-case) guarantees, acknowledges the exchangeability assumption (Assumption 1), and discusses when it may fail. The empirical evaluation uses a large sample size (N = 20,000) and a challenging adversarial opponent, lending credibility to the results.

- **Reproducibility**: Good. The running example is described in sufficient detail (18D system, bicycle model, Bayesian inference with Gaussian likelihood, safety specifications) that an expert could reproduce it. The reliance on prior work [22] for BeliefSF synthesis means some implementation details are external. Code availability is not mentioned.

- **Significance**: High for the robotics safety community. Interactive robotics under uncertainty is a critical application domain, and the tension between safety and permissiveness is a central challenge. The paper provides a principled path toward deploying BeliefSF with formal guarantees, which could accelerate adoption of belief-space reasoning in safety-critical applications.

- **Potential Concerns**: 
  1. The inference score function $h_L$ must be trained on a large dataset ($10^6$ samples in the running example), which may be burdensome in some domains.
  2. The guarantees are conditional on the trusted inference region; outside this region, safety is not certified, and the system must reject or fall back.
  3. The evaluation uses a single benchmark scenario; generalization to other interaction types (e.g., multi-agent, non-vehicular) is not demonstrated.
  4. The opponent policy is fixed during verification; while Remark 3 argues this is practically useful, it is not a worst-case guarantee over all possible opponents.

## Strengths

- **Elegant and principled**: The JIST algorithm is remarkably simple—merely restricting CP sampling to the trusted inference region—yet yields substantial practical benefits.
- **Theoretically grounded**: Builds on established foundations (HJ reachability, split CP, adversarial RL) and extends them rigorously.
- **Practically motivated**: Directly addresses a real deployment bottleneck for BeliefSF: how to certify safety when inference is imperfect.
- **Strong empirical results**: The 1.87% improvement in safety coverage and order-of-magnitude reduction in rejection rate are practically meaningful.
- **Honest about limitations**: The paper clearly discusses distributional vs. worst-case guarantees, distribution shift concerns, and the need for accurate inference modules.
- **Well-written and accessible**: Complex material is presented clearly with a running example that grounds abstract concepts.

## Limitations

- **Dependence on inference score quality**: The benefit hinges on access to a reasonable $h_L(\cdot)$ and sufficiently accurate inference. If inference is poor everywhere, JIST offers no advantage.
- **Distributional guarantees, not worst-case**: CP provides probabilistic safety with respect to a trajectory distribution, not against all possible opponent behaviors. The verification opponent is a fixed (albeit adversarial) policy.
- **Exchangeability assumptions**: Assumption 1 requires that applying the safety filter does not alter the underlying interaction distribution. In settings where the robot's filtered actions substantially influence opponent behavior, guarantees may break down.
- **Single benchmark scenario**: Evaluation is limited to one 18D vehicle-pedestrian scenario. Broader validation across diverse interaction types would strengthen claims.
- **Computational cost of $h_L$ training**: Training the inference classifier required $10^6$ samples in the running example, which may be expensive in complex domains.
- **No physical experiments**: All results are in simulation; real-world validation on physical robots remains future work.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How does JIST perform when the inference module itself is a deep neural network with poorly calibrated uncertainty estimates, rather than Bayesian inference with well-defined likelihoods?
2. Can the framework be extended to handle continuous (rather than discrete) hidden opponent types, and how would that affect the design of $h_L$ and the trusted inference region?
3. What is the sample complexity trade-off between training $h_L$ and the CP calibration samples? Is there an optimal allocation strategy?
4. How would JIST behave under adaptive conformal prediction methods that explicitly account for interaction-induced distribution shift [30,10]?
5. Can the verification-aware feedback loop suggested in Section 6—using CP outcomes to tune BeliefSF parameters such as $\epsilon_\theta$—be formalized and demonstrated empirically?
6. What happens when both the inference module and the neural safety filter fail simultaneously? Does the current framework provide any graceful degradation guarantees?
