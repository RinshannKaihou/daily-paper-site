Provable Data Scaling Law for Meta Learning via Complexity Minimization

arXiv: 2606.02008

Date: 2026-06-01

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.02008

一句话总结： 本文提出了一种名为"复杂度最小化"的元表征学习框架，首次从端到端理论上证明了预训练数据量（m）的增加可以持续提升下游小样本任务的收敛速率，从而在理论上解释了经验性的数据缩放定律；并通过在多种元学习算法中加入基于范数的复杂度正则化验证了这一效应。
TL;DR: This paper introduces "complexity minimization," a meta-representation learning framework that provably achieves the data scaling law: as meta-training data size $m$ grows, the downstream few-shot error rate decays faster with the downstream sample size $n$. The authors combine Lepski's adaptive model selection with a worst-case meta-learner, provide the first end-to-end theoretical guarantee, and empirically validate the effect via spectral-norm regularization across four meta-learning baselines.

核心贡献

  • 提出复杂度最小化框架：一种新颖的元表征学习范式，元学习者通过最小化所有观测源域上的最坏情况"最佳模型复杂度"来选择特征提取器，将下游收敛速率作为优化目标。
  • 引入 Lepski 方法估计复杂度：利用 Lepski 自适应模型选择方法，每个基学习器可以从预训练样本中无先验地估计其域内的最佳模型复杂度，将理论分析与实际算法桥接。
  • 端到端理论保证数据缩放定律：证明了下游超额误差满足 $\tilde{O}((n/\ln n)^{-\beta^ + O(\ln^\gamma m / \ln n)})$，其中指数随 $m \to \infty$ 趋近理想值 $\beta^$，即预训练数据越多，下游任务收敛越快。
  • 跨算法、跨数据集实验验证：在 MAML（一阶/二阶）、原型网络、R2-D2 四种元学习算法上，加入谱范数（及 $\ell_1$ 范数）复杂度正则化后，下游样本效率在 CIFAR-10、CIFAR-100、SVHN 上均获得一致提升。

Key Contributions

  • Complexity Minimization Framework: A novel meta-representation learning paradigm in which the meta-learner selects a feature extractor by minimizing the worst-case best model complexity across observed source domains, using downstream convergence rate as the optimization objective.
  • Lepski's Method for Complexity Estimation: Each base-learner employs Lepski's adaptive model selection to estimate the best model complexity from pre-training samples without prior knowledge of underlying smoothness parameters, bridging theory and practice.
  • End-to-end Theoretical Guarantee of the Data Scaling Law: The downstream excess error satisfies $\tilde{O}((n/\ln n)^{-\beta^ + O(\ln^\gamma m / \ln n)})$, where the exponent approaches the ideal $\beta^$ as $m \to \infty$—the first end-to-end result showing that more meta-training data provably improves downstream convergence.
  • Cross-algorithm, Cross-dataset Empirical Validation: Adding spectral-norm (and $\ell_1$-norm) complexity regularizers consistently improves downstream sample efficiency across MAML (FO/SO), Prototypical Networks, and R2-D2 on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and SVHN.

Methodology

The paper addresses a meta-representation learning problem: given $D$ domains with $m$ i.i.d. samples each, learn a feature extractor $\phi$ that minimizes the sample complexity of downstream regression on a new domain with $n$ samples.

Complexity Minimization Framework: The key departure from prior meta-learning is replacing the meta-objective (typically downstream regression error) with the best model complexity of the head function. For a sequence of increasing model classes $\mathcal{F}_J$ indexed by complexity $J$, the best complexity for domain $d$ under $\phi$ is:

$$J_n^{\dagger(d)}(\phi) = \arg\min_{J \in \mathbb{N}} \bar{\mathcal{E}}_\phi(f_{n,J}; X^{(d)}, Y^{(d)})$$

The meta-learner then solves:

$$\min_{\phi \in \Phi} \max_{d=1,\dots,D} J_n^{\dagger(d)}(\phi)$$

Since $J_n^{\dagger(d)}(\phi)$ depends on the unknown downstream distribution, it must be estimated from data.

Base-Learner — Lepski's Method: Each base-learner estimates the best complexity using Lepski's method, a classical adaptive model selection procedure from nonparametric statistics. Given a sieved least-squares estimator $\hat{f}_{m,J}$ for each $J$, the estimated complexity is:

$$\hat{J}^{(d)}(\phi) = \min\left\{ J \in [m] : \forall J \leq J' \leq \frac{m}{\ln m}, \; \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m \left(\hat{f}_{m,J}^{(d)} \circ \phi - \hat{f}_{m,J'}^{(d)} \circ \phi\right)^2 \leq \rho V_{m,J'} \right\}$$

where $V_{m,J} = J\ln(m)/m$ is the variance term and $\rho > 0$ is a constant.

Meta-Learner: The meta-learner selects $\phi$ to minimize the worst-case estimated complexity:

$$\hat{\phi} = \arg\min_{\phi \in \Phi} \max_{d \in [D]} \hat{J}^{(d)}(\phi)$$

Downstream Algorithm: At test time, Lepski's method is applied again with the frozen $\hat{\phi}$ and $n$ downstream samples to select $\hat{J}_n$ and produce the final regressor $\hat{f}_n = \hat{f}_{n,\hat{J}_n}$.

Theoretical Assumptions:

  • Assumption 1 (Polynomial approximation): The approximation error $A_J(\phi; X, Y) \asymp J^{-2\alpha(\phi;X,Y)}$ for some smoothness parameter $\alpha$.
  • Assumption 2 (Complexity of $\Phi$): Metric entropy conditions on the feature extractor class.
  • Assumption 3 (Uniform domain sampling): The domain distribution is sufficiently uniform so observed domains cover the population in terms of $\beta$.
  • Assumption 4 (Sub-gaussian noise): Standard noise condition.

Under these assumptions, Theorem 2 establishes that with probability at least $1 - O(m^{-1}) - O(n^{-1})$:

$$\bar{\mathcal{E}}_{\hat{\phi}}(\hat{f}_n) \lesssim \left(\frac{n}{\ln n}\right)^{-\beta^*_P + O(\ln^\gamma m / \ln n)}$$

where $\beta^*_P = \sup_{\phi \in \Phi} \inf_{(X,Y) \in P} \beta(\phi; X, Y)$ is the ideal downstream convergence exponent.

Empirical Validation: To test the theory in practice, the authors augment the meta-losses of four algorithms (first-order MAML, second-order MAML, Prototypical Networks, R2-D2) with a spectral-norm regularizer on model parameters: $R(\theta) = \sum_l \sigma_{\max}(W_l)$. They meta-train on Mini-ImageNet (5-way 1-shot) or Omniglot (5-way and 20-way 1-shot), then fine-tune on subsets of CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, or SVHN. The regularization coefficient $\lambda$ is grid-searched over $\{10^{-2}, 10^{-1}, 1, 10, 10^2, 10^3\}$.

Results & Highlights

Theoretical:

  • First end-to-end data scaling law for pre-training: Prior theoretical results on meta-learning established downstream sample complexity reductions but the derived error rates were independent of the pre-training data size $m$. This paper is the first to show that the rate itself improves with $m$.
  • The exponent $-\beta^*_P + O(\ln^\gamma m / \ln n)$ explicitly interpolates between a "small-$m$" regime (where the correction term is non-negligible) and the ideal oracle rate as $m \to \infty$.
  • The construction relies on deep nonparametric regression theory (Hayakawa & Suzuki 2020; Schmidt-Hieber 2020) and covers modern deep neural network estimators.

Empirical:

  • Spectral-norm regularization improves downstream scaling: Across all four meta-learning algorithms and multiple transfer pairs (Mini-ImageNet $\to$ CIFAR-10/100, Omniglot $\to$ SVHN), models trained with spectral-norm regularization achieve lower test error rates for any given fine-tuning size $n$, and the gap widens as pre-training size $m$ increases (Figure 2, Figure 3).
  • Algorithm-agnostic: The improvement is consistent across gradient-based (MAML), metric-based (ProtoNet), and closed-form-solver (R2-D2) meta-learners.
  • Other norms also work: $\ell_1$-norm regularization also improves downstream efficiency, though less vividly than spectral norm (Figure 4).
  • Compute: Full experimental sweep consumed approximately 1,000 NVIDIA H100-hours.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The paper makes a genuinely novel theoretical contribution. Prior meta-learning theory proved that pre-training reduces downstream sample complexity, but none showed that the rate improves with more pre-training data—a gap that this paper closes. The use of Lepski's method as a bridge between complexity estimation and the meta-learner is elegant and well-motivated.

Rigor: The proof strategy is sound and rooted in established nonparametric statistics. However, several caveats apply:

  • Assumption 2 requires that the feature extractor class $\Phi$ satisfies certain metric entropy conditions simultaneously for two different metrics. The authors candidly state that "constructing concrete families $\Phi$ satisfying Assumption 2 is an important open problem." This means the theorem is currently more of an existence result than a recipe practitioners can directly instantiate.
  • The analysis is restricted to regression with squared error. Extension to classification or structured prediction is left open.
  • The empirical validation uses a proxy for the theoretical framework—spectral-norm regularization is a practical surrogate for the ideal complexity minimization objective, not a direct implementation of Eq. (2). While the results are supportive, they do not constitute a formal proof that the exact algorithm behaves as predicted in the deep learning setting.

Reproducibility: The experimental setup is well-documented (Appendix C): JAX + Equinox + Optax, Conv-64/128 backbones, Adam optimizer, grid-searched $\lambda$, three random seeds. The code is not provided in the paper but the hyperparameters and architectures are standard enough that reproduction is feasible. The ~1,000 H100-hour compute budget is substantial but reasonable for the sweep described.

Significance: This paper is important for the theoretical understanding of foundation models. It provides a principled mechanism (complexity minimization) that explains why and how more pre-training data leads to better downstream performance beyond mere representation sharing. It also suggests a practical design principle: meta-learning objectives should explicitly penalize model complexity, not just task loss.

Red flags: None major. The gap between theory (idealized regression, abstract $\Phi$) and practice (deep CNNs, classification) is acknowledged by the authors.

Strengths

  • First end-to-end theoretical proof of the data scaling law in meta-learning, a phenomenon observed empirically for years but unexplained by prior theory.
  • Elegant connection between classical nonparametric statistics (Lepski's method) and modern meta-representation learning.
  • Well-motivated framework: Complexity minimization is intuitively appealing—the meta-learner should seek representations on which downstream tasks are "simplest," not just representations on which a particular loss is lowest.
  • Broad empirical validation across four distinct meta-learning families and three downstream datasets.
  • Honest and thorough discussion of limitations and related work.

Limitations

  • Open construction of $\Phi$: No concrete feature extractor class is shown to satisfy Assumption 2. The theorem proves achievability but does not provide a directly implementable algorithm with provable guarantees for deep networks.
  • Regression-only theory: The analysis covers squared-error regression; classification and other modalities are not addressed.
  • Empirical-proxy gap: The experiments use norm-based regularization as a practical surrogate rather than implementing the full Lepski-based complexity minimization pipeline.
  • Domain distribution assumptions: Assumption 3 requires near-uniform domain sampling, which may not hold in real-world settings with heavy-tailed task distributions.
  • Computational cost: Lepski's method requires fitting multiple models (one per complexity level $J$) and pairwise comparisons, which could be expensive at scale.

Relevance

对于关注元学习、表征学习与基础模型缩放定律的研究者而言，本文提供了首个端到端的数据缩放定律理论保证，并从"复杂度最小化"的视角为预训练算法设计提供了新的原则。实验上，在现有元学习方法中加入谱范数正则即可提升下游效率，具有较强的实践参考价值。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can Assumption 2 be satisfied by practical deep neural network families (e.g., ReLU networks with spectral-norm constraints, or transformer architectures)? What concrete constructions of $\Phi$ would work?
  2. How can the framework be extended to classification, especially cross-entropy loss and structured prediction tasks?
  3. Does the complexity minimization principle hold in the feature-learning regime (beyond the NTK/lazy-training limit)? Can we prove that gradient-based meta-learning implicitly minimizes complexity?
  4. Can Lepski's method be approximated efficiently in high dimensions, or can cheaper proxies (e.g., spectral norm, sharpness-based measures) be theoretically justified as valid complexity estimators?
  5. How does the framework behave under task distribution shift or when domains are sampled from a heavy-tailed rather than uniform distribution?
  6. Can the same principle explain scaling laws in self-supervised and in-context learning settings, where the meta-training and downstream tasks are not explicitly separated?
