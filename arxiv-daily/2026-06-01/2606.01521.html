Fast Generalization after Interpolation via Critically Damped Momentum Optimization

arXiv: 2606.01521

Date: 2026-06-01

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.01521

一句话总结： 本文提出 GROKtimizer——一种双阶段优化器，在插值前快速拟合训练数据，插值后通过临界阻尼动量（CDM）加速寻找低范数插值解，从而在理论和实验上显著加速模型的泛化过程。
TL;DR: This paper introduces GROKtimizer, a biphasic optimizer that rapidly fits training data before interpolation, then switches to Critically Damped Momentum (CDM) to accelerate convergence toward low-norm interpolating solutions, yielding both theoretical quadratic speedups and strong empirical gains on synthetic and real-world benchmarks.

Key Contributions

  • Biphasic optimization framework: Explicitly separates training into (1) a rapid interpolation phase (no weight decay) and (2) a post-interpolation complexity-minimization phase (CDM + weight decay), mirroring the memorization-to-generalization two-stage structure observed in grokking.
  • Theoretical acceleration via critically damped momentum: Under a local quadratic approximation of the post-interpolation landscape, proves that CDM achieves quadratic speedup over classical gradient descent along zero-eigenvalue directions of the Hessian, and is information-theoretically optimal among first-order methods.
  • Optimal momentum and learning-rate formulas: Derives the critical-damping momentum parameter β = 1 − 2√(λη) and the maximal stable learning rate η_max ≈ 4/λ_max, providing computable hyperparameters for the post-interpolation phase.
  • The GROKtimizer algorithm: Proposes a practical two-phase schedule that first reaches interpolation without weight decay, then switches to weight-decayed CDM to efficiently find low-norm solutions on the interpolation manifold.
  • Broad empirical validation: Achieves best or near-best mean performance on 6 synthetic grokking benchmarks, 3 real-world high-dimensional datasets (Leukemia, TCGA, QM9), low-data MNIST, and WikiText2 / BabyLM language-model pretraining.

Methodology

The paper builds on the observation that optimization in overparameterized regimes naturally separates into two phases: empirical risk minimization (fitting the training data) and complexity minimization (moving among interpolating solutions toward lower-norm predictors). This two-phase structure has been recently identified as the dynamical mechanism underlying grokking.

Theoretical modeling. After interpolation, the authors approximate the regularized loss around an interpolating solution w* via a local quadratic Taylor expansion (Assumption 1):

L_reg(w) ≈ L_unreg(w) + ½(w − w)ᵀH(w − w*) + ½λ‖w‖²₂

where H is the Hessian at w*. Projecting onto the eigenbasis of H decouples the dynamics into independent harmonic oscillators. Components associated with zero eigenvalues (the flat directions of the loss landscape) dominate post-interpolation behavior. The authors show that tuning momentum to critical damping — the boundary between oscillatory and overdamped decay — yields the fastest non-oscillatory convergence along these flat directions.

Key theoretical results.

  • Theorem 1 (Quadratic Speedup): For a model trained to interpolation, CDM with β = 1 − 2√(λη) converges to the minimum-norm solution quadratically faster than gradient descent: E_CDM ~ √E_GD.
  • Theorem 2 (Maximal Learning Rate): The maximal stable learning rate under the CDM schedule is η_max = 4(√λ_max + 2√λ − √λ)² / (λ_max + λ)² ≈ 4/λ_max.

Practical algorithm (GROKtimizer).

  1. Phase 1 (Interpolation): Train without weight decay using any standard optimizer (e.g., SGD) to rapidly reach near-zero training loss.
  2. Phase 2 (Norm Minimization): Switch to CDM with weight decay. The momentum parameter β and learning rate η are set according to the theoretical formulas above. An Aitken-corrected power iteration on the Hessian-vector product (via Pearlmutter's trick) can estimate λ_max online in ~10–20 steps.

The method is architecture-agnostic and requires only the ability to detect interpolation (e.g., training loss below a threshold) and standard first-order gradient access.

Results & Highlights

Synthetic grokking benchmarks (Table 1). GROKtimizer achieves the best mean performance across all six classical grokking tasks: Gaussian, Binary Addition, Modular Addition, RF Teacher Linear, Sparse Parity, and Two-Subspace Linear. On the Gaussian task, it reduces validation loss by several orders of magnitude relative to the strongest baseline (Muon / SGD at 94.2 vs. GROKtimizer at effectively zero). On Modular Addition, it achieves 95.1% test accuracy versus 78.6% for the best baseline.

Real-world high-dimensional datasets (Table 2). On biomedical classification tasks (Leukemia, TCGA) and molecular property prediction (QM9), GROKtimizer again achieves the best mean performance:

  • Leukemia: 80.3% accuracy vs. ~57% for Adam/AdamW/SGD
  • TCGA: 97.5% accuracy vs. ~90% for baselines
  • QM9: 0.16 validation loss vs. 0.28–0.38 for baselines

The largest gains occur on biomedical datasets where features vastly outnumber samples, consistent with the hypothesis that explicit norm minimization is most beneficial when the solution space is underdetermined.

Sample efficiency on MNIST (Figure 4). In a low-data regime (10–50 images per class), GROKtimizer provides the largest advantage at the most data-scarce setting (10 images/class), with the gap narrowing as more data becomes available. This confirms that the method selectively improves generalization when the training set underdetermines the model.

Language-model pretraining (Figure 5). On WikiText2 (14M params) and BabyLMStrictSmall (38M params), a biphasic pattern is still observable: after reaching low training loss, switching to norm minimization alters the parameter norm trajectory and yields small but consistent improvements in validation loss. However, the relationship between norm and validation loss is less direct than in tabular settings, suggesting that scale symmetries and adaptive updates in transformers complicate the simple norm-generalization picture.

Ablation: learning-rate comparison (Figure 8). When Adam is given the same elevated post-interpolation learning rate as GROKtimizer's Phase 2, it becomes highly unstable. This confirms that the gains are not merely due to a larger learning rate, but specifically arise from the critically damped momentum dynamics.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The core insight — treating post-interpolation dynamics as a damped oscillator and tuning momentum to critical damping — is elegant and novel. While the connection between grokking's two-phase structure and optimization has been explored before (e.g., Boursier et al., 2026; Musat, 2025), the explicit derivation of optimal momentum and learning-rate formulas for norm minimization along flat directions, and the resulting practical algorithm, represent a distinct contribution.

Rigor: The theoretical analysis is rigorous within its stated assumptions (local quadratic approximation, slow evolution of the eigenbasis). The authors acknowledge that the eigenbasis may drift and suggest a corrective coefficient α ∈ (0,1) as a practical safeguard. The proofs in the appendix appear complete, and the main results (Theorems 1 and 2) are cleanly stated with explicit dependence on problem parameters (λ, λ_max, η).

Reproducibility: The algorithm is simple to implement — it requires only a phase switch, a momentum parameter formula, and optionally a power-iteration estimate of λ_max. No proprietary data or exotic hardware is needed. The synthetic benchmarks are standard in the grokking literature. However, the paper does not release code, which slightly hampers immediate reproducibility.

Significance: The work is significant for data-constrained scientific and biomedical applications where high-dimensional, low-sample regimes are common. The language-model experiments, while showing smaller gains, suggest the principle may extend to larger-scale settings. The reconciliation with the flat-minima hypothesis (using null-space dimensionality as a reparameterization-invariant flatness measure) is a promising theoretical direction.

Red flags / concerns:

  1. Assumption of interpolation: The method is only meaningful if the model can actually interpolate the training data. In regimes where this is impossible (e.g., high label noise, insufficient capacity), the biphasic schedule collapses to standard training.
  2. Norm as complexity proxy: The paper treats parameter norm as an operational proxy for complexity, but acknowledges this breaks down in settings with scale symmetries (e.g., transformers with LayerNorm). The language-model results already show this limitation.
  3. Detection of interpolation threshold: The method requires detecting when training loss has reached interpolation. In practice, this threshold may be noisy or ill-defined, especially with non-zero label noise or mini-batch stochasticity.
  4. Comparison scope: The baselines are standard (Adam, AdamW, SGD, Muon) but do not include more recent phase-specific methods like Grokfast or NeuralGrok in the main tables, though these are discussed in related work.

Strengths

  • Strong theoretical foundation with explicit, closed-form formulas for optimal hyperparameters.
  • Simple and practical — the algorithm requires no architectural changes, learned components, or auxiliary networks.
  • Consistent empirical gains across a diverse set of tasks spanning synthetic algorithmic problems, biomedical genomics, molecular property prediction, and language modeling.
  • Clear mechanistic insight linking post-interpolation dynamics to classical physics (damped harmonic oscillators) and control theory.
  • Information-theoretic optimality claim for first-order methods is well-supported by referencing Nesterov's lower bounds.
  • Honest discussion of limitations including the restricted applicability to data-constrained regimes and the breakdown of norm-generalization correlation in transformers.

---

Limitations

  • Data-constrained focus: The method is designed for and most effective in high-dimensional, low-sample regimes. It is less clearly beneficial in large-scale vision or language pretraining where data abundance dominates.
  • Interpolation prerequisite: GROKtimizer's second phase is only triggered after interpolation. Models that cannot reach zero training loss (due to capacity constraints or high noise) receive no benefit from the schedule.
  • Local quadratic approximation: The theoretical analysis assumes the post-interpolation basin is locally quadratic. While the authors argue this is approximately valid for slow eigenbasis evolution, the approximation may fail in highly non-convex or rapidly changing landscapes.
  • Norm-complexity correspondence: The method assumes lower parameter norm implies lower complexity and better generalization. This correspondence is empirically supported in the studied settings but can be invalidated by architectural symmetries (e.g., rescaling invariance in networks with normalization layers).
  • Interpolation detection: Practical deployment requires a robust criterion for detecting the interpolation threshold. In stochastic mini-batch training with noisy labels, this threshold may be fuzzy.
  • Limited ablation on phase-switch timing: The paper does not extensively study how sensitive the method is to the exact timing of the phase switch or to the choice of first-phase optimizer.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How sensitive is GROKtimizer's performance to the exact interpolation-detection threshold, and can an adaptive criterion (e.g., based on gradient norm plateau) replace a fixed loss threshold?
  2. Can the CDM phase be combined with adaptive optimizers (AdamW) rather than SGD-style momentum, and would the adaptive preconditioning interact favorably or unfavorably with the critical-damping condition?
  3. In transformer-scale language models, can a more invariant complexity proxy (e.g., function-space norm or NTK-based measure) replace parameter norm to restore a stronger generalization correlation?
  4. Does the method retain its advantages in the presence of significant label noise, where exact interpolation may be undesirable?
  5. Can the Hessian-nullspace dimensionality be used as a practical, computable measure of flatness for optimizer comparison and neural architecture search?
