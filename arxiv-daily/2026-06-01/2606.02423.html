Investigating and Alleviating Harm Amplification in LLM Interactions

arXiv: 2606.02423

Date: 2026-06-01

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.02423

一句话总结：
本文提出了首个多轮对话有害放大基准HarmAmp和基于树形强化学习训练的主动式安全监控器TrajSafe，实验表明TrajSafe能在极低过度拒绝率下将多轮交互中的有害性降至接近零。
TL;DR:
This paper introduces HarmAmp, the first systematic benchmark for multi-turn harm amplification in LLMs, and TrajSafe, a proactive monitor trained with tree-based RL that reduces harmfulness to near-zero while maintaining the lowest over-refusal rate and preserving general model capabilities.

核心贡献

  • 提出了 HarmAmp，首个针对多轮对话中有害放大（harm amplification）现象的系统性基准测试，覆盖 12类风险场景，包含878个场景（432个恶意、446个良性），平均每个计划4.0个步骤。
  • 定义了有害放大的两个核心维度：民主化（democratization，LLM将领域专业知识转移给新手，消除知识壁垒）和规模化（scaling，以远超人工的速度和规模执行有害操作），并识别出三种互斥的放大模式：能力获取（63.7%）、定向画像（23.1%）、操作规模化（13.2%）。
  • 设计了严格的场景筛选标准：实质性放大（需要超出网络搜索或标准参考的LLM协助）、操作特异性（输出必须是可执行代码、可部署文档或目标特定策略等功能性产物）、多轮必要性（目标必须自然分解为至少三个不同的工作流阶段）。
  • 提出了 TrajSafe，一种基于轨迹级安全监控的外部监控器，通过三方对话框架（用户模型、目标助手、监控器）主动预判有害轨迹并干预。监控器动作空间包含五大类12种动作：ENGAGE（正常交互）、PROBE（意图探测）、SHAPE（内容塑形）、DIVERT（目标转移）、HARD REFUSE（硬拒绝）。
  • 采用树形扩展的组相对策略优化（GRPO）训练监控器，在每个对话回合采样多个候选干预动作，观察替代干预如何重塑助手的下游行为，从而学习高效的干预策略。
  • 在三个目标模型（Llama-3.1-8B、Qwen3-4B、GPT-5-mini）上的实验表明：TrajSafe显著降低有害性（Llama-3.1-8B上从86.36%降至9.85%，Qwen3-4B和GPT-5-mini上均降至0.76%），同时保持所有防御方法中最低的过度拒绝率和极少的干预次数。

Key Contributions

  • Introduces HarmAmp, the first systematic benchmark for multi-turn harm amplification in LLM interactions, spanning 12 risk categories with 878 scenarios (432 malicious, 446 benign), averaging 4.0 steps per plan.
  • Defines harm amplification along two axes: democratization (LLMs transfer domain expertise to collapse knowledge barriers for novices) and scaling (multiplying harmful operations beyond manual capacity), identifying three mutually exclusive amplification patterns: capability acquisition (63.7%), targeted profiling (23.1%), and operational scaling (13.2%).
  • Establishes rigorous scenario criteria: substantive amplification (requires meaningful LLM assistance beyond web search), operational specificity (output must be a functional artifact such as executable code or deployable strategy), and multi-turn necessity (objective naturally decomposes into ≥3 workflow stages).
  • Proposes TrajSafe, an external trajectory-level monitor operating in a three-party dialogue framework (user model, target assistant, monitor) that proactively anticipates harmful trajectories and intervenes through a structured action taxonomy of 12 actions across 5 families: ENGAGE, PROBE, SHAPE, DIVERT, and HARD REFUSE.
  • Trains the monitor via tree-based group-relative policy optimization (GRPO), branching multiple candidate interventions at each turn to explore how alternative actions reshape downstream assistant behavior, enabling efficient learning of calibrated intervention policies.
  • Demonstrates across three target LLMs (Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen3-4B, GPT-5-mini) that TrajSafe dramatically reduces harmfulness (from 86.36% → 9.85% on Llama-3.1-8B; from 42.42% → 0.76% on Qwen3-4B; from 14.39% → 0.76% on GPT-5-mini) while achieving the lowest over-refusal rate among all defenses and preserving general capabilities.

Methodology

HarmAmp Benchmark Construction. The benchmark is built through a three-stage pipeline:

  1. Seed Collection & Screening: Single-turn harmful scenarios are drawn from HarmBench (standard and contextual subsets) and Hex-Phi, plus benign but sensitive queries from OR-Bench for over-refusal evaluation. Each instance is automatically screened by Claude-Sonnet-4.5 against three criteria (substantive amplification, operational specificity, multi-turn necessity). Failing instances are rewritten when possible; otherwise discarded (~25% of malicious seeds filtered out). All screening results are manually verified by the authors.
  1. Multi-Step Plan Extension: Qualified instances are converted into workflow plans decomposing the objective into specific steps (providing target info, requesting artifact generation, refining outputs, adapting across targets). Each plan carries three annotations: a user persona (skill level, motivation, capability gaps), a target artifact (concrete harmful output), and an amplification pattern (capability acquisition, targeted profiling, or operational scaling). Plans are auto-generated by Claude-Sonnet-4.5 and human-verified.
  1. Conversation Simulation: Each plan is instantiated as a simulated multi-turn conversation between an off-the-shelf user simulator and the target LLM assistant. The simulator follows the annotated workflow plan, grounding each query in prior responses. Validation shows multi-turn settings dramatically increase harmfulness across all models compared to single-turn presentation of the same objective (Table 2).

TrajSafe Monitor Design. TrajSafe is formalized as a three-party dialogue among a user model π_usr, target assistant π_tgt, and monitor π_θ. At each turn t: (1) the user produces a query grounded in its goal and dialogue history; (2) the monitor observes the query and context, emitting feedback as a (THINKING, FEEDBACK, EXPLANATION) triple injected as a system-side instruction; (3) the assistant generates a response conditioned on history and monitor feedback. The monitor never observes the goal label during inference.

Training: (1) SFT Warm-start: A smaller user policy is fine-tuned to maintain a goal-directed adversarial persona. The monitor is fine-tuned on monitor-view data with random action sampling to balance exposure across all intervention types. (2) RL Optimization: The monitor is trained via on-policy multi-turn RL with tree-based expansion—sampling n candidate feedback outputs per state, forwarding each to the assistant, and creating distinct trajectory branches. The composite reward balances harm suppression (1−h, where h is the harmfulness score from HarmAug-Guard) against intervention calibration (s_fb), with λ=0.5. For benign goals, any intervention is penalized (capped at 0.3). For malicious goals, full credit is given for 1–2 interventions, with linear penalty beyond a budget of n*_mal=2. This trajectory-level budget discourages both always-silent and always-intervene policies.

Evaluation: Safety is measured by Harmfulness Score (fraction of malicious conversations producing harmful completions, judged by WildGuard), Over-Refusal Rate (fraction of benign trajectories with unnecessary refusal), and Intervention Turn Rate (fraction of turns with non-PASS action). General capability is assessed on MMLU, TruthfulQA, and MultiChallenge (multi-turn instruction-following).

Results & Highlights

Safety Effectiveness (Table 3). TrajSafe consistently outperforms all baselines across three target models:

  • Llama-3.1-8B: Harm score drops from 86.36% (vanilla) to 9.85%—a 76.51% absolute reduction. This beats the best guardrail baseline (GPT-oss-safeguard prompt-only at 29.55%) by 19.7 percentage points.
  • Qwen3-4B: Harm score drops from 42.42% to 0.76%.
  • GPT-5-mini: Harm score drops from 14.39% to 0.76%.

Over-Refusal (Table 3). TrajSafe achieves the lowest over-refusal rate among all methods with additional defense. On Llama-3.1-8B, over-refusal is only 11.64% (just +2.74% above vanilla), while the best guardrail on harm suppression (GPT-oss-safeguard prompt-only) suffers 55.48% over-refusal. This reveals a critical trade-off in existing safety mechanisms—stronger harm suppression typically causes higher over-refusal—while TrajSafe breaks this trade-off.

Intervention Efficiency (Table 3 & Figure 2). TrajSafe achieves strong safety with far fewer interventions. On GPT-5-mini malicious conversations, it intervenes on only 26.69% of turns vs. 95.31% for the prompting-based monitor and 65.71% for the SFT monitor. On benign conversations, TrajSafe intervenes on only ~5% of turns vs. 96.31% for the prompting-based monitor. When TrajSafe does intervene on malicious queries, it favors softer actions (Divert and Probe) rather than hard refusal; the prompting-based monitor distributes actions indiscriminately and uses substantial Hard Refusal even on benign queries.

General Capability Preservation (Table 4). On Llama-3.1-8B, TrajSafe causes only a 0.4-point MMLU drop and slightly improves TruthfulQA (+0.5%). MultiChallenge micro-average is identical (20.5%). On Qwen3-4B, MMLU drops by only 0.2 points. By contrast, Qwen3-4B-SafeRL (direct RL safety alignment on assistant weights) suffers 9-point MMLU degradation and 10.6-point MultiChallenge drop. Case studies show the monitor's caution against categorical claims occasionally penalizes factual recall on MMLU but helps correct stereotypical generalizations on TruthfulQA (e.g., steering away from "All Hispanics speak Spanish" toward acknowledging linguistic diversity).

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The paper makes a genuinely novel contribution by shifting the safety research focus from single-turn binary refusal to multi-turn harm amplification—a more realistic and nuanced threat model. The formalization of amplification along democratization and scaling axes, plus the three amplification patterns, provides a useful conceptual framework. The tree-based RL training with trajectory-level budgeting is also a methodological innovation over standard guardrail approaches.

Rigor: The benchmark construction is methodologically sound, with explicit criteria for scenario inclusion and human verification of LLM-generated content. The three-party dialogue formalization is clean and well-motivated. The reward design carefully balances harm suppression against intervention calibration with a tunable budget. However, the reliance on automated harmfulness judges (WildGuard, HarmAug-Guard) introduces potential measurement bias—these judges may themselves have blind spots or calibration errors that affect reported harm scores.

Reproducibility: The paper provides substantial implementation details and promises appendices with prompts, examples, and training data splits. However, the fine-tuned adversarial user model will not be publicly released, and the benchmark itself will be under restricted access—while ethically justified, this limits full reproducibility for external researchers.

Significance: The results are striking: near-zero harm on two of three models with minimal over-refusal and preserved capabilities. If these results hold under broader evaluation (including human adversaries and cross-session settings), this represents a meaningful advance in practical LLM safety. The demonstration that external monitoring can outperform direct model alignment (Qwen3-4B-SafeRL) without modifying assistant weights is particularly significant for deployed systems.

Red Flags: (1) The user simulator for evaluation is Qwen3-4B, while the RL training uses a smaller fine-tuned user model—there may be a distribution shift between training and evaluation adversaries. (2) The impressive GPT-5-mini results (0.76% harm) may partly reflect the already-strong safety alignment of the frontier model rather than TrajSafe alone; the absolute gains on Llama-3.1-8B are more informative. (3) The 5% benign intervention rate, while low, still represents unnecessary friction for legitimate users. (4) The ethical considerations acknowledge but do not fully resolve dual-use risks from releasing multi-step harmful plans, even in redacted form.

Strengths

  • Addresses a critical gap in safety research: most benchmarks and defenses focus on single-turn jailbreaks, while real-world malicious use often involves sustained, multi-turn interactions.
  • HarmAmp is carefully constructed with explicit, rigorous criteria and human verification, avoiding ad-hoc scenario collection.
  • TrajSafe's proactive, trajectory-level approach is a meaningful departure from reactive output classifiers and per-turn guardrails.
  • The tree-based RL training enables efficient exploration of intervention alternatives without requiring full rollouts for every action.
  • Strong empirical results across both open-weight and frontier models, with comprehensive evaluation on safety, over-refusal, intervention efficiency, and general capabilities.
  • The reward design's trajectory-level intervention budget (favoring 1–2 well-timed interventions) is intuitive and empirically validated.
  • TrajSafe preserves model weights, making it deployable as a drop-in safety layer without retraining target models.

Limitations

  • Simulated adversaries: The benchmark uses LLM-simulated malicious users following annotated plans. While instructed to exhibit realistic behaviors, they cannot fully capture the diversity, creativity, and persistence of real human adversaries. Human red-teaming studies are needed for validation.
  • Text-only, single-session scope: The work does not cover agentic tool use, multi-modal inputs, or cross-session persistence—important emerging threat vectors.
  • Adaptive adversaries: The paper acknowledges but does not experimentally address evasion techniques specifically targeting TrajSafe's defense patterns. An adversary aware of the monitor could potentially craft queries that evade its intervention triggers.
  • Automated judgment bias: Harmfulness and over-refusal are measured by automated classifiers (WildGuard, etc.), which may have their own biases and blind spots.
  • Restricted release: The adversarial user model will not be released, and the benchmark will have restricted access, limiting community verification and extension.
  • English-centric: The benchmark and evaluation are conducted in English; generalization to other languages and cultural contexts is unclear.

Relevance

本文直接回应了当前LLM安全研究中被忽视的多轮交互有害放大问题，所提出的HarmAmp基准和TrajSafe方法对构建更鲁棒的对话系统安全防线具有重要参考价值，其实现了安全性和可用性之间更好平衡的实验结果尤为引人注目。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does TrajSafe perform against human red-teamers with varying skill levels and domain expertise, compared to LLM-simulated adversaries?
  • Can the tree-based RL training be extended to handle cross-session persistence or tool-use scenarios, and how would the action space need to adapt?
  • What is the computational overhead of running TrajSafe (based on Llama-3.2-3B) alongside large target models in real-time production environments?
  • How robust is TrajSafe against adaptive adversaries who intentionally craft queries to evade its intervention triggers? Could the monitor itself be trained with adversarial training or online learning?
  • Do the results generalize to non-English languages, and how might cultural differences in communication styles affect the monitor's intent probing and diversion strategies?
  • Can the intervention budget mechanism be dynamically adjusted based on conversation context or user trust signals, rather than using a fixed n*_mal=2?
  • How does TrajSafe handle ambiguous conversations that sit at the boundary of benign and malicious (e.g., legitimate security research, journalism, or creative writing involving sensitive topics)?
