> **一句话总结：** 本文提出了一种面向下游决策可靠性校准的条件多变量预测集合方法，将其嵌入电力系统鲁棒优化以替代传统的基于预测覆盖率的校准方式，从而在保障约束满足的同时显著降低运行成本。

> **TL;DR:** This paper introduces decision-calibrated prediction sets—conditional multivariate prediction sets calibrated on downstream decision reliability rather than predictive coverage—and embeds them as uncertainty sets in robust power system optimization, achieving target constraint satisfaction with much smaller sets and lower operating costs than standard coverage-based approaches.

## 核心贡献

- **决策校准的预测集合**：提出了一种全新的预测集合校准范式，校准目标不再是传统的预测覆盖率，而是下游鲁棒优化决策的可靠性（即运行约束的满足程度）。
- **基于部分输入凸神经网络（PICNN）的评分函数**：学习以范数为基础的评分函数的条件子水平集，既能够捕捉上下文信息和多变量依赖关系，又能保证下游鲁棒优化问题的凸性和计算可追踪性。
- **受共形风险控制（CRC）启发的阈值校准算法**：校准评分阈值参数以控制不确定性集合的体积，从而直接控制下游运行约束的期望违反率。
- **混合范数评分函数**：提出了1-范数与∞-范数之和的混合评分函数，构造了更灵活的多面体预测集合，在保持计算可追踪性的同时实现了更优的成本-可靠性权衡。
- **在真实电力系统场景下的全面验证**：将方法应用于含仿射矫正策略的鲁棒直流最优潮流（DC-OPF）问题，在修改的5节点系统和IEEE RTS-GMLC 73节点系统上进行了数值实验验证。
- **显著的性能优势**：数值实验表明，决策校准集合能在约3.1–3.2个百分点内达到预设的约束满足目标，而标准基于覆盖率的校准系统性地超出目标约11.5–13.5个百分点，导致更大的集合和更高的运行成本。

## Key Contributions

- **Decision-calibrated prediction sets**: A novel calibration paradigm where prediction sets are calibrated on downstream decision reliability (operational constraint satisfaction) rather than traditional predictive coverage.
- **PICNN-based score functions**: Conditional sub-level sets of norm-based score functions learned via partially input-convex neural networks (PICNNs), capturing contextual information and multivariate dependence while preserving convexity and tractability in downstream robust formulations.
- **Conformal risk control-inspired threshold calibration**: A practical algorithm to calibrate the score-threshold parameter that controls the volume of the uncertainty set, directly regulating the expected violations of downstream operational constraints.
- **Hybrid norm-based scores**: A sum-of-norms score function (1-norm + ∞-norm) that constructs more flexible polyhedral prediction sets, achieving favorable cost-reliability trade-offs while retaining tractable robust reformulations.
- **Comprehensive validation on realistic power systems**: Application to a robust DC optimal power flow problem with affine recourse on a modified 5-bus system and the IEEE RTS-GMLC 73-bus system with 120 transmission lines, 73 conventional generators, and 4 wind power plants.
- **Significant performance gains**: Numerical experiments show decision-calibrated sets achieve prescribed constraint-satisfaction targets within ~3.1–3.2 percentage points, whereas standard coverage-based calibration exceeds targets by ~11.5–13.5 percentage points, leading to larger sets and higher operating costs.

## Methodology

The authors propose a two-stage methodology for constructing decision-calibrated prediction sets:

**Stage 1 — Learning conditional prediction sets (Section 3):** The prediction sets are constructed as sub-level sets of norm-based score functions, given by $U_\theta(x; \rho) = \{y \in \mathcal{Y} : s_\theta(x, y) \leq \rho\}$, where $x$ represents contextual features (weather forecasts, historical production) and $y$ represents the uncertain variables (renewable power production). The score function $s_\theta(x, y)$ is parameterized by a partially input-convex neural network (PICNN), ensuring the prediction set remains convex with respect to $y$. This convexity is critical for embedding the set directly as a tractable uncertainty set in robust optimization.

The score function is defined as a $p$-norm: $s_\theta(x, y) = \|L_\phi(x)^{-1}(y - f_\eta(x))\|_p$, where $f_\eta(x)$ is a conditional location (point predictor) and $L_\phi(x)$ is a lower-triangular Cholesky factor yielding a positive semidefinite conditional covariance. The authors focus on $p \in \{1, 2, \infty\}$ because they yield tractable RO formulations: $p=1$ gives rotated/scaled diamond sets; $p=2$ gives ellipsoids; $p=\infty$ gives boxes. To increase flexibility, they propose a sum-of-norms score: $s^{\text{sum}}_\theta(x, y) = \|L^{-1}(y - f)\|_1 + \|L^{-1}(y - f)\|_\infty$, which creates polyhedral sets that accommodate both concentrated and distributed error patterns.

Training minimizes a negative log-likelihood (NLL) objective induced by the score-based model, plus an optional MSE term for the point predictor. A three-stage training procedure is used: (i) pre-train $f_\eta$ with MSE, (ii) freeze $f_\eta$ and train $L_\phi$ with NLL, (iii) fine-tune jointly.

**Stage 2 — Decision-aware calibration (Section 5):** Rather than calibrating $\rho$ to achieve target coverage probability (standard conformal prediction), the authors calibrate $\rho$ to achieve target downstream decision reliability. Inspired by conformal risk control (CRC), they define a constraint violation loss $\ell^{\text{viol}}_i(\rho) = \mathbb{1}\{g_{\text{DC}}(z^*(x_i; \rho), \xi_i) > 0\}$, where $g_{\text{DC}}$ measures the maximum constraint residual across all robust constraints in the DC-OPF problem. Algorithm 1 implements a bisection-like search over $[0, \hat{\rho}_{\text{mis}}]$ with a cache-based monotonicity correction, solving at most $K \cdot n_{\text{cal}}$ offline robust optimization problems. The corrected empirical risk is compared against $\varepsilon - (1-\varepsilon)/n_{\text{cal}}$ to select the threshold.

**Downstream robust optimization (Section 4):** The motivating application is 15-minute-ahead reserve scheduling with network-constrained deliverability, formulated as a robust DC-OPF with affine recourse. An affine recourse matrix $A$ adjusts generator outputs as $p(\xi) = p - A\xi$, with $A^\top \mathbf{1} = \mathbf{1}$ preserving power balance. The robust constraints ensure feasibility of recourse actions for all forecast errors within the prediction set. Tractable reformulations are derived using support functions for $p$-norm sets, box sets, and their intersections.

## Results & Highlights

- **Calibrated thresholds (Tables 1 & 2):** Coverage-based calibration produces substantially larger $\rho$ values. Averaged across models and $\varepsilon$, $\hat{\rho}_{\text{mis}}$ is ~1.76× larger than $\hat{\rho}$ for the 5-bus system and ~1.92× for the RTS system. The gap decreases as $\varepsilon$ gets smaller.

- **Constraint satisfaction vs. predictive coverage (Figures 3 & 5):**
  - *5-bus system:* Coverage-based calibration exceeds target constraint satisfaction by ~11.5 percentage points on average, while decision-based calibration exceeds by only ~3.2 percentage points. Predictive coverage for decision-calibrated models is ~16.3 percentage points below nominal targets.
  - *RTS system:* Coverage-based calibration exceeds target by ~13.5 percentage points, while decision-based calibration exceeds by ~3.1 percentage points. Predictive coverage for decision-calibrated models is ~23.0 percentage points below nominal targets.

- **Operating cost (Figures 4 & 6):**
  - *5-bus system:* S-(1+∞) yields the lowest average cost across all reliability levels. The cost gap between S-(1+∞) and the second-best model increases as reliability increases, from ~$0.11 at $\varepsilon=0.30$ to ~$0.36 at $\varepsilon=0.05$. Coverage-calibrated models are always more expensive, with additional cost ranging from ~$0.93 to $1.89.
  - *RTS system:* S-2 yields the lowest cost but falls short of reliability targets for $\varepsilon \leq 0.15$. S-(1+∞) meets reliability targets in all cases with only a small cost increase. Coverage-calibrated models cost substantially more, with additional cost ranging from ~$2,880 to $4,960.

- **Score function comparison:** S-(1+∞) constructs more flexible score functions with polyhedral sets visually resembling ellipsoids, achieving target reliability at smaller volumes. S-1 is closest to target constraint satisfaction on the 5-bus system (2.7 pp exceedance), while S-2 achieves smallest mean absolute deviation on the RTS system.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** The core conceptual advance is shifting the calibration objective from predictive coverage to downstream decision reliability. While conformal prediction and robust optimization are mature fields, their integration through decision-calibrated sets is a meaningful and timely contribution. The hybrid sum-of-norms score function is an interesting architectural innovation that goes beyond prior work on norm-based conformal sets.
- **Rigor:** The use of partially input-convex neural networks is mathematically well-motivated—convexity of uncertainty sets is essential for tractable robust optimization. The calibration procedure is grounded in conformal risk control theory. The tractable reformulations of robust constraints using support functions are carefully derived for all score function variants ($p$-norm, box, intersection, sum-of-norms). The paper acknowledges that the constraint violation loss is not theoretically monotone (breaking CRC guarantees) but provides empirical evidence of near-monotonicity and a practical correction heuristic.
- **Reproducibility:** The methodology is described clearly and in detail, including hyperparameters (learning rate 1e-3, $\gamma=0.1$, 3 layers × 50 nodes, batch size 512), training procedure, and calibration algorithm. The experimental setup specifies exact systems (modified 5-bus, IEEE RTS-GMLC), data sources (first 5 months of 2020), feature construction (last 3 measurements + hourly forecast), and data splits (5,000 train / 1,500 calibration / 4,500 test). However, no code repository is mentioned.
- **Significance:** The results demonstrate practically meaningful cost reductions while maintaining reliability—critical for power system operators facing high renewable penetration. The 11.5–13.5 percentage point gap between coverage-based and decision-based calibration represents substantial operational savings.
- **Potential limitations / red flags:**
  1. The theoretical CRC guarantee (Eq. 18) does not strictly apply because the constraint violation loss is not monotone in $\rho$ due to the optimizer $z^*(x; \rho)$ changing with $\rho$. The authors acknowledge this and rely on empirical near-monotonicity.
  2. The affine recourse assumption limits applicability to more complex operational scenarios with non-linear or multi-stage recourse.
  3. Computational cost of training PICNNs and solving $K \cdot n_{\text{cal}}$ robust optimization problems during calibration is non-trivial, though the paper notes the algorithm is trivially parallelizable.
  4. The exchangeability assumption underlying CRC is approximately applied to time-series data; the authors note block/bootstrap variants could be used but do not implement them.
  5. Real-world validation is on standard test systems with simulated wind data, not actual grid operations with full complexity (e.g., unit commitment, AC power flow, market considerations).
  6. The method requires re-calibration when the downstream optimization problem structure changes.

## Strengths

- Conceptually elegant and well-motivated shift from coverage-based to decision-based calibration.
- Preserves convexity and computational tractability through careful PICNN architecture design.
- Demonstrates significant practical improvements in operating costs on realistic test systems.
- Builds on solid theoretical foundations (energy-based learning, conformal risk control, robust optimization).
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across two test systems, multiple score functions, and a grid of reliability targets.
- Relevant to critical infrastructure challenges (high renewable penetration power grids).
- Extensive and well-organized related work situating the contribution within multiple literatures.
- Bilingual structure with clear methodology, reproducible experimental setup, and detailed algorithmic description.

## Limitations

- The constraint violation loss lacks strict theoretical monotonicity, so CRC finite-sample guarantees do not directly apply (though empirical performance is strong).
- Limited to DC-OPF with affine recourse; extension to AC-OPF or more general recourse structures is not addressed.
- Computational burden of calibration (solving many robust optimization problems) may be significant for large-scale systems, though parallelization helps.
- Exchangeability assumption for CRC is only approximately valid for time-series wind forecasting data.
- Experiments use standard test systems with simulated data rather than real-world grid operations.
- The method requires re-calibration when the optimization problem formulation or cost structure changes.
- Training stability issues (loss spikes when learning $L_\phi$) required gradient clipping, suggesting some sensitivity in the learning process.
- Heavily penalized slack variables were needed for feasibility in some instances, particularly on the RTS system with large $\rho$.

## Relevance

本文提出的决策校准预测集合方法对高比例可再生能源电力系统的经济运行具有重要意义，特别是在需要在不确定性下精确平衡成本与可靠性的场景。该方法将共形预测、能量基学习与鲁棒优化有机结合，为电力系统调度提供了更锋利的概率不确定性描述工具，对关注机器学习与优化在能源系统交叉领域应用的研究者和工程师具有重要参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does the method perform on nonlinear AC optimal power flow (AC-OPF) problems where convexity assumptions may not hold, and what approximations would be needed?
- What is the computational scaling of the calibration procedure (Algorithm 1) to very large power systems with thousands of buses and renewable generators?
- Can the decision-calibration framework be extended to multi-stage stochastic programming or distributionally robust optimization settings?
- How sensitive are the results to the choice of affine recourse policies, and what happens with piecewise-affine or general nonlinear recourse structures?
- Is there a way to enforce or prove approximate monotonicity of the constraint violation loss, recovering finite-sample theoretical guarantees?
- How would the method perform under non-stationary wind conditions or regime shifts, and would online/adaptive recalibration be necessary?
- Can the hybrid score function $S$-(1+∞) be extended to sums of more than two norms, and would this provide additional modeling benefits?
- What is the sample complexity of the calibration procedure—how does performance degrade with smaller calibration sets?
- Could this framework be applied to other domains with robust optimization under uncertainty, such as supply chain management, portfolio optimization, or autonomous systems?
