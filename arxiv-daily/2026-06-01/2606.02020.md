> **一句话总结：** 本文系统揭示了思维链推理中的熵动态规律——存在从不确定性探索到确定性收敛的两阶段结构，并基于此提出了基于CUSUM变点检测的无训练实时推理控制框架，在高效早期退出和测试时扩展上均取得了显著优势。

> **TL;DR:** This paper systematically uncovers a two-phase entropy dynamics structure in Chain-of-Thought reasoning—an Uncertainty Region of stochastic exploration followed by an abrupt transition to a Confidence Region of deterministic convergence—and proposes a training-free, CUSUM-based change-point detection framework for real-time inference control, achieving superior performance in both early exit and test-time scaling.

## 核心贡献

- **发现思维链推理的熵动态两阶段结构**：通过预测熵（predictive entropy）量化每一步对最终答案的不确定性，揭示了从不确定性区域（Uncertainty Region，高熵探索）到置信区域（Confidence Region，低熵收敛）的急剧转变。这一结构在不同模型架构和规模上保持一致。
- **揭示置信区域的两个关键特性**：**高可靠性**（High Reliability）——进入该区域后答案准确率从不足20%跃升至60%以上并保持稳定；**高冗余性**（High Redundancy）——模型在得出正确答案后仍继续生成大量冗余token，通常超过总轨迹长度的30%。
- **提出基于统计变点检测的无训练推理控制框架**：首次将经典序列变点检测问题应用于CoT推理监控，采用CUSUM（累积和）算法实时检测从不确定性到置信区域的过渡，无需任何模型训练或微调。
- **设计两种高效推理策略**：**早期退出**（Early Exit）利用置信区域的可靠性和冗余性在安全时终止计算；**CUSUM加权测试时扩展**（CUSUM-Weighted Test-Time Scaling）利用最终CUSUM分数作为轨迹质量指标，优先选择已收敛的高质量推理路径。
- **全面的理论保证与实验验证**：证明了CUSUM的误报可控性和渐近最小最大最优性；在三个模型（DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B、Qwen3-4B-Thinking-2507、Qwen3-14B）和三个基准（AIME24、AIME25、GPQA-Diamond）上验证，早期退出达到更优的帕累托前沿，测试时扩展一致优于自一致性方法。

## Key Contributions

- **Discovered a consistent two-phase entropy dynamics structure in CoT reasoning**: By tracking predictive entropy over intermediate answers at each reasoning step, the paper reveals a sharp transition from an Uncertainty Region (high-entropy stochastic exploration) to a Confidence Region (low-entropy deterministic convergence). This structure holds consistently across different model architectures and scales.
- **Revealed two critical properties of the Confidence Region**: **High Reliability** — upon entering this region, answer accuracy surges sharply from below 20% to a high plateau above 60% and remains stable; **High Redundancy** — models continue generating substantial redundant tokens, often exceeding 30% of total trajectory length, well beyond solution discovery.
- **Proposed a training-free inference control framework via statistical change-point detection**: The first work to frame CoT reasoning monitoring as a classical sequential change-point detection problem, adopting the CUSUM (Cumulative Sum) algorithm to detect the transition from Uncertainty to Confidence Region in real-time without any model training or fine-tuning.
- **Designed two efficient inference strategies**: **Early Exit** leverages the reliability and redundancy of the Confidence Region to safely terminate computation; **CUSUM-Weighted Test-Time Scaling** uses the final CUSUM statistic as a trajectory quality indicator to prioritize converged, high-quality reasoning paths over unconverged ones.
- **Comprehensive theoretical guarantees and empirical validation**: Proved false-alarm controllability and asymptotic minimax optimality of CUSUM; evaluated on three models (DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B, Qwen3-4B-Thinking-2507, Qwen3-14B) and three benchmarks (AIME24, AIME25, GPQA-Diamond), achieving superior Pareto frontiers for early exit and consistently outperforming self-consistency for test-time scaling.

## Methodology

The paper views LLM reasoning as a process of uncertainty reduction, where each CoT step serves as an incremental information gain. To quantify this, the authors track the model's confidence in its final answer at each reasoning step through **predictive entropy**.

**Entropy Computation.** For an input X, the model generates reasoning steps {T_i}. At each step i, an intermediate answer A_i is obtained via an answer-inducing prompt (e.g., `</think> The answer is \boxed{}`). The predictive entropy H_i is computed as the average next-token entropy over the m_i tokens of A_i:

H_i = - (1/m_i) Σ_{j=1}^{m_i} Σ_{u∈V} P_j(u|·) log P_j(u|·)

High entropy indicates active exploration among competing hypotheses; low entropy indicates deterministic convergence.

**Two-Phase Regime-Shift Model.** The entropy sequence {H_i} is modeled as switching between two stochastic regimes at an unknown change-point ν:
- f_0 (Uncertainty Region): high-entropy exploration
- f_1 (Confidence Region): low-entropy convergence

**CUSUM Change-Point Detection.** The detection problem is formulated as minimizing worst-case average detection delay (WADD) subject to a false-alarm constraint. The per-step log-likelihood ratio is:

Z_i = log(f_1(H_i) / f_0(H_i))

The CUSUM statistic S_i is defined recursively:
S_0 = 0, S_i = max(0, S_{i-1} + Z_i)

Detection occurs when S_i ≥ h for a predefined threshold h. The distributions f_0 and f_1 are estimated via histogram density estimation on 100 calibration trajectories from the Bespoke-Stratos-17k dataset.

**Early Exit (Algorithm 1).** CUSUM runs online along the CoT trajectory. Generation terminates as soon as S_i ≥ h, signaling sufficient statistical evidence that the model has entered the Confidence Region.

**Test-Time Scaling (Algorithm 2).** For N sampled trajectories, each trajectory is scored by its final CUSUM statistic S_final. Answers are weighted by this score, prioritizing trajectories with strong evidence of convergence over those stalled in the Uncertainty Region.

**Theoretical Guarantees.** Under the statistical separability assumption (positive KL divergences between f_0 and f_1), the paper proves: (1) False-alarm control — setting h = log γ guarantees E_∞[τ_h] ≥ γ; (2) Asymptotic optimality — as h → ∞, the detection delay approaches the theoretical minimum.

## Results & Highlights

**Characterizing the Transition.** Analysis across 100 calibration instances reveals:
- **Problem difficulty governs transition timing**: On easy problems, models enter the Confidence Region within the first third of the trajectory (30–34%); on hard problems, the transition is delayed to the middle-to-late stage (52–56%).
- **Stronger models converge earlier**: Qwen3-14B transitions at 30%/52% (easy/hard) versus DeepSeek-7B at 33.9%/56.4%, suggesting model capacity directly accelerates the exploration-to-convergence transition.
- **Earlier convergence correlates with correctness**: The negative correlation between normalized change-point and accuracy is strongest for the distilled 7B model (r = -0.45) and weakest for the natively trained 14B model (r = -0.26).

**Early Exit.**
- CUSUM achieves **63.06% average accuracy with 11.1% token reduction** across all models and benchmarks.
- Outperforms DEER (59.78%) by **3.28 percentage points** and Dynasor (58.70%) by **4.36 percentage points** in accuracy.
- Particularly pronounced on challenging scenarios: on GPQA with DeepSeek-7B, CUSUM achieves 40.40% accuracy versus DEER (35.73%) and Dynasor (19.32%).
- Achieves a **superior Pareto frontier** across all three models on AIME25 (Figure 3).
- **Computational overhead is minimal**: On AIME25 with Qwen3-4B, CUSUM achieves 39.17% accuracy with 11,740 tokens in 419s latency, compared to Vanilla (41.04%, 14,556 tokens, 504s), DEER (37.5%, 12,148 tokens, 460s), and Dynasor (37.11%, 12,822 tokens, 492s). The efficiency gains from reduced token usage significantly outweigh the marginal increase in latency.

**Test-Time Scaling.**
- CUSUM-weighted voting **consistently outperforms self-consistency** across all three models on AIME25.
- The performance gap **widens as the number of sampled trajectories increases**: for Qwen3-4B, the improvement grows from a slim margin at N=2 to a 3.33% lead at N=64.
- **S_final serves as a reliable quality indicator**: correct trajectories exhibit higher S_final values than incorrect ones, with clear statistical separation (Figure 5). Both correct and incorrect trajectories show a small peak near S_final = 0, attributed to persistent exploration without clear evidence accumulation (incorrect) and early truncation by token limit (some correct cases).

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty (8/10):** The core insight—viewing CoT reasoning as an uncertainty reduction process with a detectable regime shift—is conceptually elegant and genuinely novel in its systematic treatment. While prior work has explored confidence-based early exit (DEER, Dynasor) and entropy heuristics (HALT-CoT, EAT), this paper is the first to formally bridge CoT dynamics with classical sequential change-point detection theory. The application of CUSUM, a method from statistical process control (1950s–1970s), to monitor LLM reasoning is a refreshing cross-disciplinary contribution.

**Rigor (7.5/10):** The paper provides solid theoretical foundations: formal optimization objectives, statistical separability assumptions, false-alarm control proofs, and asymptotic optimality guarantees. The methodology for computing predictive entropy is clearly defined. However, several concerns remain: (1) The "training-free" claim relies on 100 calibration trajectories for histogram density estimation—while not gradient-based training, this is still a data-dependent calibration step whose sensitivity is not fully explored. (2) The KL divergence values (Table 4 in appendix) are positive but modest; the practical detection performance depends heavily on the empirical separation between regimes, which may vary across tasks. (3) The i.i.d. assumption within regimes is acknowledged as violated in practice due to temporal dependence across reasoning steps; while the authors cite Kokoszka & Leipus (1998) for robustness under dependence, no empirical validation of this robustness is provided.

**Reproducibility (7/10):** The method is largely reproducible: it uses open-source models, standard benchmarks, and a simple algorithm with one main hyperparameter (threshold h). However, reproducibility is hampered by: (1) reliance on the Bespoke-Stratos-17k dataset for calibration, which may not be universally accessible; (2) lack of released code or exact hyperparameter settings (Appendix B.1 is referenced but not available in the extracted text); (3) small evaluation sample sizes (especially GPQA-Diamond with only 4 random runs), which may produce noisy estimates.

**Significance (8/10):** The implications for efficient LLM inference are substantial. If the two-phase entropy structure is indeed universal across reasoning models, this work provides a principled, theoretically grounded alternative to heuristic early-exit methods. The dual application to both early exit (saving compute) and test-time scaling (improving quality) demonstrates versatility. The 11% token reduction with maintained or improved accuracy is practically meaningful. The finding that stronger models converge earlier also has implications for model scaling laws and reasoning efficiency.

**Potential Red Flags:**
- **Modest accuracy improvements**: The 3–4% accuracy gains over DEER/Dynasor are consistent but not dramatic. On some benchmarks (e.g., AIME25 with Qwen3-14B), CUSUM actually underperforms Vanilla in absolute accuracy while saving tokens.
- **Threshold sensitivity**: While the paper claims the single hyperparameter h provides simplicity, the Pareto frontier analysis suggests practitioners must still navigate a trade-off space.
- **Limited task diversity**: All benchmarks are mathematical/scientific reasoning (AIME, GPQA). It is unclear whether the two-phase entropy structure holds for other reasoning types (e.g., commonsense reasoning, symbolic manipulation, multi-hop QA) where the answer may not converge monotonically.
- **"Statistically optimal" claim**: CUSUM's optimality is asymptotic and under specific assumptions (known distributions, i.i.d. within regimes). The paper does not quantify how close the practical setting is to these assumptions.
- **Distilled vs. native models**: The correlation between change-point timing and accuracy is much weaker for natively trained models (r = -0.26 for Qwen3-14B) than distilled ones (r = -0.45 for DeepSeek-7B), suggesting the method may be more reliable for certain model types.

## Strengths

- **Conceptually clean and interpretable**: The entropy dynamics framework provides an intuitive, information-theoretic lens for understanding CoT reasoning.
- **Strong theoretical grounding**: Formal connection to sequential analysis with proven guarantees (false-alarm control, asymptotic minimax optimality) distinguishes this work from purely heuristic approaches.
- **Training-free and lightweight**: No model fine-tuning or gradient computation required; minimal computational overhead during inference.
- **Dual utility**: Simultaneously addresses two major inference challenges—efficiency (early exit) and quality (test-time scaling)—with a single unified signal.
- **Consistent empirical gains**: Superior Pareto frontiers and consistent outperformance of strong baselines across multiple models and benchmarks.
- **Well-motivated cross-disciplinary approach**: Successfully imports classical statistical process control into modern LLM inference.

## Limitations

- **Limited task scope**: Evaluated only on mathematical and scientific reasoning benchmarks (AIME24, AIME25, GPQA-Diamond). Generalization to other reasoning domains (commonsense, symbolic, legal, medical) is unverified.
- **Calibration dependency**: Requires 100 calibration trajectories per model for histogram density estimation. The quality of detection depends on the representativeness of this calibration set.
- **Single-hyperparameter simplification may be overstated**: While only threshold h is explicitly tuned, the binning strategy for histogram estimation implicitly introduces additional choices.
- **Temporal dependence not fully addressed**: Although cited, the robustness of CUSUM under the sequential dependence inherent to autoregressive generation is not empirically validated.
- **Small sample sizes**: 100 calibration instances and 4–16 evaluation runs may produce noisy estimates, particularly for the correlation analysis and Pareto frontier characterization.
- **Redundancy is task-dependent**: The high redundancy property (>30% wasted tokens) is predominantly observed on easy instances; hard problems naturally have later convergence, reducing the scope for early-exit savings.
- **No comparison with learned methods**: The paper compares only against training-free baselines (DEER, Dynasor, self-consistency). Comparison with learned early-exit or trajectory-ranking methods could better contextualize the gains.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How does the two-phase entropy structure generalize to non-mathematical reasoning tasks (e.g., commonsense QA, code generation, creative writing) where convergence may not be monotonic?
2. Can adaptive or online methods replace the offline histogram calibration, enabling zero-shot deployment on new models without any calibration trajectories?
3. How does the method perform on extremely long reasoning chains (e.g., 100k+ tokens) where the relative proportion of redundancy may differ?
4. Can the CUSUM score be fused with other quality signals (e.g., semantic entropy, verifier confidence, process reward models) for more robust trajectory selection?
5. What is the impact of answer-extraction prompt design on predictive entropy computation? Would different prompting strategies change the detected change-point?
6. How does the framework extend to multi-agent or iterative refinement settings where reasoning involves backtracking or revision rather than monotonic convergence?
