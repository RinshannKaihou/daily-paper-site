When Do Attention Circuits Form? Developmental Trajectories of Capability and Attention-Sink Emergence Across Three 1B-Class Architectures

["Yongzhong Xu"]

arXiv: 2606.02378

Date: 2026-06-01

Tags: ["mechanistic interpretability", "attention circuits", "attention sink", "induction heads", "training dynamics", "circuit emergence"]

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.02378

一句话总结： 本文通过参与比（PR）频谱信号与能力特异性筛选，在三个1B级语言模型（Pythia 1B、OLMo 1B、OLMoE 1B-7B）的30个对数间隔检查点上系统追踪了induction head、previous-token head和BOS-attractor head的形成轨迹，发现induction电路与attention-sink的形成是两个在DCLM训练中相隔10–20倍token量的独立过渡阶段，且BOS-attractor的涌现形状受数据与架构的交互影响呈现三种截然不同的模式。
TL;DR: This paper tracks how attention-head circuits (induction, previous-token, BOS-attractor) emerge during pretraining across three 1B-class models—Pythia 1B (dense, Pile), OLMo 1B (dense, DCLM), and OLMoE 1B-7B (MoE, DCLM)—using participation-ratio spectral analysis and capability-specific selectivity screens at 10 log-spaced checkpoints per model (30 runs total). It finds that induction-circuit formation and attention-sink emergence are distinct transitions separated by 10–20× in tokens on DCLM, that the L0/L1 zero-BOS floor is an invariant architectural property, and that BOS-attractor dynamics follow three qualitatively different shapes (gradual ramp, sharp phase transition, gradual ramp) depending on data–architecture interactions. The capability screen converges to the core induction circuit within 0.3–2% of training, and per-head PR consistently rises at or before capability-selectivity threshold crossings.

核心贡献

  • 在三个1B级模型、两个架构族（dense transformer与mixture-of-experts）和两个预训练语料库（The Pile、DCLM）上，系统追踪了注意力头电路的完整发育轨迹。
  • 发现L0/L1层在所有30个运行检查点中均不产生BOS-attractor头——这是一个架构属性而非学习结果，且在所有模型中成立。
  • 揭示了三种不同的BOS-attractor整体比例涌现形状：Pythia 1B的渐进式 ramp、OLMo 1B的 sharp phase transition（相邻检查点间从7%跃升至70%）、以及OLMoE 1B-7B的渐进式 ramp。
  • 在DCLM模型中，induction电路的形成（20–23B tokens）比BOS-attractor形成（264B–419B tokens）早10–20倍；能力电路形成与attention-sink形成是两个独立过渡，而非单一的"induction phase transition"。
  • 能力特异性筛选在仅0.3–2%的总训练token内即可收敛到最终induction电路核心（67% recall）——电路识别无需完整训练后的模型。
  • 所有最终检查点的induction头，在其首次超过能力选择性阈值时或之前，PR值已经升高，表明PR可作为电路形成的早期预测信号。
  • 发现BOS-attractor并非从底层向上涌现，而是从中层向外扩散——最先在中层（L4–L10）出现，靠近地板的L2/L3反而最晚或永不参与。

Key Contributions

  • Systematically tracks attention-head circuit formation trajectories across three 1B-class models spanning two architecture families (dense transformer, mixture-of-experts) and two pretraining corpora (The Pile, DCLM).
  • Discovers that Layers 0 and 1 produce zero BOS-classified heads at every checkpoint across all 30 runs — the L0/L1 zero-BOS floor is an architectural property, not learned, and is invariant across data and architecture.
  • Reveals three distinct whole-model BOS-attractor fraction emergence shapes: a gradual monotonic ramp in Pythia 1B, a sharp phase transition in OLMo 1B (7% → 70% between adjacent checkpoints), and a gradual ramp in OLMoE 1B-7B.
  • Shows that in DCLM-trained models, induction-circuit formation (20–23B tokens) precedes BOS-attractor formation (264B–419B tokens) by 10–20×; capability-circuit formation and attention-sink formation are two separate transitions, not one unified "induction phase transition."
  • Demonstrates that the capability-specific screen converges to the core induction circuit (67% recall) within 0.3–2% of total training tokens — most of the circuit is identifiable from checkpoints two orders of magnitude smaller than the final model.
  • Finds that for every final-checkpoint induction head sampled across all three models, per-head PR is already elevated at or before the first revision where that head crosses its capability-selectivity threshold, validating PR as an early predictor of circuit formation.
  • Discovers that attention sinks emerge mid-layer-outward rather than bottom-up: sinks crystallize first in middle layers and propagate outward, with near-floor layers (L2, L3) trailing or never participating.

Methodology

The study examines three publicly available 1B-class language models: Pythia 1B (GPT-NeoX dense transformer trained on The Pile), OLMo 1B-0724-hf (Llama-style dense transformer trained on DCLM), and OLMoE 1B-7B-0924 (Llama-style MoE with 1B active / 7B total parameters, 64 experts, top-8 routing, trained on DCLM). For each model, 10 log-spaced training checkpoints are analyzed (30 total mechanistic-interpretability runs).

The analytical pipeline follows a three-step recipe from a companion methodology paper:

  1. Synthetic induction batch: 2000 sequences of length 256 (RNG seed 42), each structured as [filler] A B [filler] A, where A and B are random vocabulary tokens. The induction prediction is B at the position immediately following the second occurrence of A.
  1. Per-head participation ratio (PR): For each (layer, head, revision), the per-head attention output at the second-A position is extracted over the batch (matrix M ∈ ℝ^(N×d_head)). The singular spectrum {σ_i} of M is computed, and PR = exp(H(p)) where p_i = σ_i² / Σ_j σ_j². The trajectory feature is the integral I(L,H) = Σ_t max(PR(L,H,t) − 1, 0) · Δ log(tokens_t).
  1. Capability-pattern screen: For each head, attention from the query position to canonical target positions is computed for six classes: induction, previous-token, duplicate-token, first-token/BOS, self, and local. Selectivity = (target attention) / (uniform-other baseline). A head is classified into the class with maximum selectivity if that maximum exceeds 30× (assignment threshold); it is admitted to a class circuit if selectivity exceeds 50× (induction, BOS) or 100× (previous-token).

All-head capability-specific screen: In the attention-sink-dominated regime (≥70% of heads classify as first-token), best-class ranking surfaces BOS heads ahead of capability heads. For identifying a specific target capability X, the screen instead takes all heads with sel_X ≥ τ_X regardless of best class.

Causal verification: Group ablation of screen-identified circuits by zeroing the per-head slice of the residual contribution at the attention output projection, with matched-random controls sharing layers and head count but not identities. Top-1 accuracy on the synthetic induction batch is the primary metric.

Checkpoint availability caveats: Earliest published checkpoints differ by an order of magnitude across models (Pythia step1 at ~2M tokens, OLMo step1000 at 2B tokens, OLMoE step5000 at 20B tokens). OLMoE was sampled at 8 rather than 10 revisions due to higher forward-pass cost (top-8 routing across 64 experts). Pythia early checkpoints require fp32 precision because baseline attention values at random-init scale fall below fp16 representable range.

Results & Highlights

Finding 1 — L0/L1 Zero-BOS Floor: In all three models at every sampled revision, layers 0 and 1 produce zero heads classified as first-token/BOS at the ≥30× assignment threshold. This holds from random initialization through trillions of tokens. The floor is architectural, not learned. A mechanistic reading is that L0's inputs are raw token embeddings (not yet contextualized), so attending to position 0 would discard content; L1's inputs are L0 outputs, which by the L0 floor do not yet contain cross-token integration. Sinks emerge mid-layer-outward: in Pythia, first sinks appear at L6–L8 and L10–L14 simultaneously (~6B tokens); in OLMo, first at L4 (~10B tokens) while L2 only acquires sinks at 264B tokens (~26× later); in OLMoE, first at L7–L10 and L12–L13 (~20B tokens) while L2 only acquires sinks at ~2.5T tokens (>100× later).

Finding 2 — Three Distinct BOS Emergence Shapes: (1) Pythia 1B (Pile, dense): gradual monotonic ramp from 0% → 10.9% (~6B) → 46.1% (~80B) → 57.8% (300B). (2) OLMo 1B (DCLM, dense): sharp phase transition — 0% through 52B, 7.4% at 117B, then jumps to 70.3% at 264B between two adjacent checkpoints, saturating at ~80.9%. (3) OLMoE 1B-7B (DCLM, MoE): gradual monotonic ramp from 3.5% (20B) → 42.2% (104B) → 50.8% (419B) → 75.8% (5117B). The architectural change (dense → MoE) on the same data both reduces the final BOS-class fraction (~10pp lower) and smooths the dynamics from sharp transition to gradual ramp.

Finding 3 — Induction Precedes BOS-Attractor in DCLM: Induction reaches its final 3–6 head size within the first 6–25B tokens in all three models, then stays flat. The BOS-attractor's 50% milestone is reached an order of magnitude later: OLMo 1B induction at 23B vs BOS-50% at 264B (11.5× gap); OLMoE 1B-7B induction at 20B vs BOS-50% at 419B (21× gap). In Pythia 1B, induction and BOS-10% co-emerge at ~6B, but finer checkpoints would be needed to resolve whether this is truly co-emergence or a resolution limit. Previous-token and self-attention milestones also precede BOS-attractor saturation in all three models.

Finding 4 — Capability Screen Converges Within ~1% of Training: 67% recall of the final-checkpoint induction circuit is reached at 6B tokens for Pythia (~2.0% of training), 10B for OLMo (~0.3%), and 41B for OLMoE (~0.8%). The core circuit converges early; the last 1/3 of circuit membership shifts slowly through threshold crossings of marginal heads. The null-selectivity 99th-percentile noise floor is stable across training (1.45–2.08× in Pythia), so a single per-model threshold applies throughout. The nullmax is unstable (7.3 to 767× across revisions), confirming that threshold rules based on nullp99 are checkpoint-stable.

Finding 5 — PR Rises at or Before Capability Selectivity: For every final-checkpoint induction head sampled (3 in Pythia, 3 in OLMo, 4 in OLMoE), per-head PR rises one or more checkpoints before induction selectivity crosses 50×. Lead times vary: 1–2 checkpoints for induction heads, co-emergence (0 lead) for previous-token heads, and 2–4 checkpoints for BOS-attractor heads. For example, Pythia L4·H4's PR rises sharply between 0.13B and 0.54B tokens (2.58 → 27.35) and peaks at 6.3B, while induction selectivity does not cross 50× until 6.3B — a ~12× lead in token count. Important scope limit: this holds for heads that end up capability-selective; PR-integral ranking alone does not identify capability-specific heads in the 1B attention-sink regime — the two-step pipeline (PR-integral + class-specific screen) is necessary.

Parameter-space bridge: At the final checkpoint, activation PR shows modest positive association with weight stable rank (Spearman ρ = +0.32 pooled over 640 heads; +0.16 Pythia, +0.28 OLMo, +0.38 OLMoE). High-PR heads tend to have lower top singular values (ρ(PR, σ₁) = −0.55 in Pythia). Developmentally, heads that gain more activation PR also gain more weight stable rank (growth co-evolution ρ = +0.30 Pythia, +0.39 OLMo, +0.14 OLMoE). However, weight spectra do not predict induction selectivity (|ρ| ≤ 0.29), consistent with PR being a general specialization indicator rather than capability-specific.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. The decoupling of induction-circuit formation from attention-sink emergence challenges the prevailing narrative that conflates them under a single "induction phase transition." The L0/L1 zero-BOS architectural floor, the mid-layer-outward emergence pattern, and the early-convergence finding (0.3–2% of training) are all genuinely surprising and theory-relevant. The three-shape taxonomy of BOS-attractor dynamics and its dependence on data–architecture interactions is a novel empirical contribution.
  • Rigor: Strong. The multi-model, multi-dataset, multi-architecture design provides robust comparative evidence. The use of two independent signals (PR + selectivity screen) with causal verification via group ablation provides methodological triangulation. The null-selectivity calibration and its stability analysis (nullp99 vs nullmax) demonstrate careful statistical thinking. However, the OLMo 1B sharp transition (7% → 70%) occurs between adjacent log-spaced checkpoints — the within-gap dynamics remain unresolved, and the claim of "sharp phase transition" is inference-laden given this resolution limit.
  • Reproducibility: Very high. All models, datasets, and checkpoints are public. The code and data are released on GitHub. The methodology is described in sufficient detail for replication, including exact thresholds, batch construction (RNG seed 42, vocabulary IDs [100, 10,000]), and revision schedules.
  • Significance: Important for the mechanistic interpretability community and for training-dynamics research. If attention-sink and induction-circuit formation are indeed separable by an order of magnitude in tokens, this has direct implications for when training can be monitored or intervened upon. The early-convergence result suggests that interpretability-driven training stopping or lightweight safety monitors could operate on very early checkpoints.
  • Red flags: (1) Single author — unusual for this scale of empirical work; the companion papers suggest a coherent research program, but peer review at the time of writing is unknown. (2) Sparse checkpoint sampling — 10 log-spaced checkpoints per model miss fine-grained dynamics, especially around the OLMo 1B sharp transition. The dramatic jump could be smoother or sharper than observed. (3) Single seed per model — no within-architecture re-pretraining; findings represent specific training runs rather than expected behavior. The author acknowledges this explicitly. (4) Synthetic batch evaluation — capability selectivity is computed on controlled random-token sequences; whether natural-text induction targets follow the same trajectories is an open question (though final-checkpoint cross-validation exists in the methodology paper). (5) Capability class limitations — only six canonical classes are tracked; composed tasks (e.g., IOI) are not covered. (6) Scope overstatement risk — the PR-leads-selectivity claim is carefully scoped to heads that end up capability-selective, but readers may misread it as PR-integral alone being sufficient for circuit identification, which the author explicitly warns against.

Strengths

  • Exceptionally clean factorial design isolating corpus (Pile vs DCLM) and architecture (dense vs MoE) effects at fixed ~1B scale.
  • Methodological triangulation: PR spectral signal, capability-specific selectivity screen, and causal group ablation converge on consistent conclusions.
  • Surprising and theoretically consequential findings: the L0/L1 architectural floor, mid-layer-outward sink emergence, and the 10–20× token gap between induction and sink formation.
  • Practical implication that circuit identification converges at <2% of training — directly relevant to early-training monitoring and interpretability budgets.
  • Explicit, honest scope statements and limitation discussions (e.g., PR-integral is not capability-specific; checkpoint resolution bounds; single-seed caveat).
  • Strong reproducibility posture with public code, data, and detailed methodological documentation.

Limitations

  • Only 1B-class models are studied; generalization to 7B, 70B, or larger scales is unverified and would be valuable given the compute cost of checkpoint releases at scale.
  • Sparse checkpoint sampling (10 log-spaced points, 8 for OLMoE) may miss fine-grained dynamics, especially the OLMo 1B sharp transition between 117B and 264B tokens.
  • Single seed per architecture; no within-configuration re-pretraining to assess trajectory variability.
  • Evaluation on synthetic random-token sequences; natural-text induction-target dynamics may differ.
  • MoE analysis at 8 rather than 10 revisions due to forward-pass cost, leaving the very early OLMoE trajectory (<20B tokens) unobserved.
  • Only six canonical capability classes; composed-task circuits (e.g., IOI) are not tracked.
  • The parameter-space bridge measurements are exploratory and modest in strength (ρ ~ +0.32); the promised integration with spectral-edge dynamics remains future work.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Do these developmental trajectories scale consistently to 7B, 70B, or larger models, or do new dynamics (e.g., earlier or later sink formation, different layer-wise patterns) emerge at scale?
  2. What happens in the unobserved gap between OLMo 1B's adjacent checkpoints (117B to 264B tokens) where BOS-attractors jump from 7.4% to 70.3%? Is the transition truly discontinuous, or would finer-grained checkpoints reveal a steep but continuous ramp?
  3. Can the early-convergence property (0.3–2% of training) be exploited to build lightweight early-training safety monitors or anomaly detection systems that flag circuit-formation deviations without training to completion?
  4. Does the L0/L1 zero-BOS floor hold across non-transformer architectures (e.g., state-space models like Mamba, linear attention variants, or hybrid architectures)?
  5. How do these circuit formation timelines vary with different data mixtures, curricula, or pretraining objectives (e.g., multilingual data, code-heavy mixtures, or continual pretraining)?
  6. Can the PR-integral + selectivity-screen pipeline be extended to composed tasks (e.g., IOI, factual recall) to track when multi-head composed circuits form during training?
  7. What is the precise mechanistic explanation for the MoE smoothing effect — why does sparse expert routing reduce both the asymptotic BOS-class fraction and the transition sharpness?
