CANARY: Zero-Label Detection of Fine-Tuning Contamination in Language Models

arXiv: 2606.01695

Date: 2026-06-01

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.01695

一句话总结： CANARY 是一种无需标签的检查点审计工具，通过将两次前向传播得到的隐藏状态差异投影到在基模型上训练的稀疏自动编码器（SAE）中，过滤风格噪声并隔离语义漂移，从而在仅 1% 的训练数据被污染时以近乎完美的 AUROC 检测出语言模型微调过程中的隐蔽后门攻击，比任何输出级防御方法早 7.5 倍触发，并构建起从检测到修复的完整治理流程。
TL;DR: CANARY is a zero-label checkpoint auditor that detects fine-tuning contamination by projecting hidden-state differences through a Sparse Autoencoder trained on base-model activations, achieving AUROC = 1.000 at just 1% contamination — 7.5× below where output-level defenses trigger — and enables a complete governance pipeline for detection, verification, prioritization, and remediation without any labeled harmful examples.

Key Contributions

  • Introduces CANARY, the first zero-label framework to detect, verify, prioritize, and remediate supply-chain contamination from hidden states alone
  • Achieves AUROC = 1.000 at only 1% data contamination (95% CI = [0.997, 1.000]; Cohen's d = 3.28), far below the 7.5% threshold where output-level methods trigger
  • Uses a Sparse Autoencoder (SAE) trained on base-model activations to filter style noise and isolate safety-relevant semantic drift; fraction of variance explained (FVE) ranges from 0.74–0.88
  • Validated across four model architectures (Qwen2.5-0.5B, Llama-3.2-1B, SmolLM2-1.7B, Gemma-2-2B) and two training paradigms (SFT, RLHF) with zero false positives on benign fine-tuning
  • Fully robust against style-matching and gradient-noise adaptive attacks; derives a closed-form detection-limit formula $r^ = \Phi^{-1}(\text{AUROC}^) \cdot \sigma\sqrt{2}/c$ that quantitatively predicts performance across architectures
  • Builds a complete governance pipeline on the same SAE feature basis: SAE-filtered hidden-layer LDA surfaces latent harm at 5× higher rate while remaining coherent (PPL = 58 vs. 1.8 M); score-ranked prompts yield 4.2× red-teaming lift; inference-time suppression of 16 contamination-specific features reduces harm from 70% to 10%, flips the Harmful Logit Score (HLS) from +1.78 to −1.96, with zero perplexity penalty

Methodology

CANARY operates as a checkpoint auditor that inspects a fine-tuned language model without requiring any labeled examples of harmful behavior. The core methodology involves running two forward passes over an unlabeled prompt set — one through a trusted base checkpoint $\theta_{\text{base}}$ and one through the suspect checkpoint $\theta_{\text{tuned}}$ — and analyzing the differences in their hidden-state representations at a mid-network layer $L$.

SAE Training. A $k$-sparse autoencoder is trained on activations sampled from the base model at layer $L$. The reconstruction quality is measured by fraction of variance explained (FVE), ranging from 0.74 to 0.88 across model pairs.

Noise Feature Identification. The SAE dimensions most suppressed by amplification — those with the most negative $\Delta$-activation — correspond to dense, high-activation style and formatting directions. These "noise features" are zeroed before computing the detection score.

CANARY Score. Let $h_{\text{base}}^{(L)}(x)$ and $h_{\text{tuned}}^{(L)}(x) \in \mathbb{R}^d$ be the last-token hidden states at layer $L$, and define $\Delta h(x) = h_{\text{tuned}}^{(L)}(x) - h_{\text{base}}^{(L)}(x)$. The CANARY score is:

$$\widehat{\Delta h} = \text{Dec}\left(\text{mask}\left(\text{Enc}(\Delta h)\right)\right), \quad S(x) = \|\widehat{\Delta h}(x)\|_2^2$$

where Enc/Dec are the SAE encoder/decoder and mask zeros the $K$ features with the most negative $\Delta$-activation. A checkpoint is flagged if $\bar{S}(\mathcal{X}) \gg \bar{S}_{\text{clean}}$.

SAE-Filtered Hidden-Layer LDA. For verification, the SAE-filtered difference is injected at layer $L$ instead of logit space: $h_{\text{amp}}^{(L)} = h_{\text{tuned}}^{(L)} + \alpha \cdot \widehat{\Delta h}$, letting the remaining transformer layers re-normalize into coherent vocabulary. This avoids the incoherence collapse that plagues original logit-space LDA at large $\alpha$.

Surgery. Contamination-specific features are identified by mean $\Delta$-activation and suppressed at inference time via a hook at layer $L$.

Results & Highlights

  • Sub-Threshold Early Detection (§5.1): AUROC = 1.000 at every tested rate from 1% to 20%. At 1%, the bootstrapped 95% CI is [0.997, 1.000]; at 5%+ it collapses to [1.000, 1.000]. Cohen's d ranges from 2.99 to 3.67 (all "very large"). At the $\mu_{\text{clean}} + 2\sigma$ threshold, false positive rate on clean fine-tunes is below 2.5% by construction, while true positive rate at 1% is 1.000.
  • Baseline Comparison (§5.2): CANARY (0.96) matches Logit KL divergence (0.96) without needing labels. Raw L2 norm achieves 0.88; Top-K Magnitude (SAE-free) reaches 0.88. Supervised methods (cosine refusal direction, linear probe) reach 1.00 but require labeled contrastive data unavailable at deployment.
  • Harm Specificity (§5.3): Benign persona shifts and random-token noise produce AUROC ≈ 0.5 across all rates, confirming the signal is harm-specific, not merely any distribution shift.
  • SAE-Filtered Amplification (§5.4): Peak harm rate of 33.3% at PPL = 58, versus original LDA's 6.7% at PPL = 1.8 M (5× higher harm at 31,000× lower perplexity). Two independent gains: hidden-layer injection reduces PPL from 890 to 51 at equivalent harm; noise masking alone surfaces 33.3% harm at $\alpha = 0$.
  • Cross-Architecture Generalization (§5.6): M1 (Qwen 0.5B): AUROC = 1.000; M2 (Llama 1B): 0.987; M3 (SmolLM 1.7B): 0.847; M4 (Gemma 2B): 0.862. Three of four architectures reach AUROC ≥ 0.95. M3's lower performance is predicted by the detection-limit formula ($r^* \approx 4.5\%$ due to high variance $\sigma = 578$).
  • Adaptive Attack Robustness (§5.7): Style-matching and gradient-noise attacks leave AUROC at 1.000 and ≥ 0.95. LoRA rank-4 reduces AUROC to 0.82–0.86, but the low-rank constraint is self-defeating: it proportionally limits the attacker's capacity to implant persistent harm.
  • Layer Robustness (§5.8): AUROC = 1.000 from layer 4 onward across nine layers; only earliest layers show slight degradation (0.953–0.955). Effect size peaks in mid-to-late layers (Cohen's d up to 5.68).
  • Red-Teaming Prioritization (§5.9): Top-ranked prompts reach 97% harm vs. 23% for mid-ranked (4.2× lift). Score distribution separates fine-tuning contamination (high scores) from jailbreak prompts (low scores), confirming complementary coverage.
  • SAE-Guided Surgery (§5.10): On M1 at 5% contamination, suppressing 16 SAE features reduces harm from 70% to 10% (86% reduction); HLS flips from +1.78 (harm-steering) to −1.96 (refusal-steering); perplexity unchanged.
  • Continuous Monitoring (§5.9): The alarm threshold fires at the first available training checkpoint for contaminated runs, while clean fine-tuning remains below threshold throughout.

Authors & Credibility

Swapnil Parekh (Intuit Inc.) is the sole author of this work. The paper presents comprehensive empirical validation across multiple model architectures, training paradigms, and attack types, with rigorous statistical reporting including bootstrap confidence intervals and effect sizes (Cohen's d). The author derives a closed-form detection-limit formula and validates its predictive power across architectures. The experiments span three model families (Qwen, Llama, Gemma) plus SmolLM, suggesting substantial computational resources. However, the single-authorship, while not disqualifying, means the work has not yet benefited from multi-reviewer scrutiny. The author commits to releasing code, experiment scripts, and trained SAE weights upon publication, which will be critical for independent verification.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The combination of hidden-state analysis with Sparse Autoencoders for zero-label contamination detection represents a genuinely novel contribution. Prior work has focused on output-level monitoring (LDA), weight-space methods (SVD, task vectors), or probing/steering that requires labeled contrastive pairs. CANARY uniquely requires no labels, no generation, and no per-pair retraining, while the SAE basis enables downstream interpretability and surgical remediation.

Rigor: The statistical reporting is commendable, with bootstrapped 95% CIs and Cohen's d effect sizes reported alongside point estimates. The evaluation across four architectures and two paradigms strengthens external validity. The detection-limit formula provides an analytic framework for predicting performance, and the ablation studies cleanly isolate the contributions of hidden-layer injection and noise masking.

Reproducibility: The methodology is described in sufficient detail for replication, and the author has committed to releasing code and trained SAE weights. However, until these materials are publicly available, some hyperparameters (exact SAE training settings, layer selection criteria) remain undisclosed. The use of synthetically generated medical advice examples (no real patient data) is responsible but limits domain diversity.

Significance: If the results hold under broader evaluation, this work addresses one of the most critical vulnerabilities in deployed language models — supply-chain attacks via fine-tuning APIs. The 7.5× earlier detection threshold and the complete detect-to-fix pipeline represent a major practical advance over existing defenses.

Red Flags: The AUROC of exactly 1.000, even with a confidence interval, is an exceptionally strong claim that warrants independent verification. The single-authorship without clear peer review status should be noted. While the paper mentions releasing code "upon publication," the preprint format means these results have not yet undergone formal peer review. The near-perfect detection on some architectures could reflect evaluation artifacts if the probe prompt set overlaps with the contamination distribution; broader prompt diversity testing would strengthen confidence.

Strengths

  • Addresses a critical and timely problem in AI safety (supply-chain attacks via fine-tuning) with a practical, deployable solution
  • Achieves dramatically earlier detection (1%) than any existing output-level method (7.5% threshold)
  • Requires no labeled data, no text generation, and no per-checkpoint retraining, making it practical for real-world deployment
  • Provides not just detection but a complete governance pipeline: detect → verify (via SAE-filtered LDA) → prioritize (score-ranked red-teaming) → remediate (inference-time feature suppression)
  • Strong empirical validation with statistical rigor across multiple architectures and paradigms
  • The SAE-based approach is inherently interpretable, enabling human oversight and surgical remediation
  • Derives and validates a closed-form detection-limit formula that quantitatively predicts performance
  • Harm-specific: does not flag benign distribution shifts or random noise, reducing false alarm rates

Limitations

  • Requires access to a trusted base checkpoint, which rules out settings where the clean baseline is unknown or contested
  • Near-perfect AUROC claims (especially exact 1.000) require independent verification on diverse datasets and prompt distributions
  • Single-author preprint without formal peer review; some implementation details (exact SAE hyperparameters) are not fully disclosed pending code release
  • M3 (SmolLM2-1.7B) shows lower performance (AUROC ~0.85), suggesting architectural variance matters; the detection-limit formula explains but does not eliminate this gap
  • LoRA rank-4 fine-tuning reduces AUROC to ~0.83–0.86; while the author argues this is "self-defeating" for attackers, it remains a partial evasion pathway
  • Computational cost of SAE training and inference-time suppression at scale (≥70B parameters) is unexplored
  • Evaluation uses only synthetic medical advice harmful examples; generalization to other harm domains (e.g., cyberattack instructions, bioweapons) is not tested
  • The SAE-filtered LDA amplification component is intended for safety researchers and model providers, not end-user deployment, as it intentionally surfaces latent harmful behavior

Relevance

这项工作对 AI 安全领域具有直接而深远的意义：随着微调 API 的普及，供应链后门攻击已成为部署语言模型的核心风险之一，而 CANARY 首次在无需任何标签数据、无需生成文本的条件下实现了对隐蔽污染的早期检测和精准修复，为模型提供者和安全审计人员提供了一套可实际部署的治理工具链。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does CANARY's performance scale to frontier-class models (≥70B parameters), and can the SAE surgery be extended to multi-layer circuits?
  2. Can adversaries design contamination attacks that specifically target and evade SAE-based detection by constraining perturbations to non-SAE directions?
  3. How well does the method generalize to other harm domains beyond synthetic medical advice (e.g., cyberattack instructions, disinformation, bioweapons)?
  4. What is the minimum size and diversity of the unlabeled prompt set required for reliable detection across domains?
  5. Can the SAE feature basis be made transferable across model families, or must it be retrained for each architecture?
  6. How does the method perform when the reference checkpoint itself has minor, unknown contamination or when multiple successive fine-tuning steps have been applied?
  7. Can CANARY be adapted to detect multi-objective fine-tuning where both capability and alignment shift simultaneously?
  8. What are the computational overheads of SAE training and inference-time suppression at production scale, and can they be amortized across multiple audits?
