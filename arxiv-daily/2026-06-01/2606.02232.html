A Doeblin-Anchored Contrastive Chart for Learning Markov Transition Kernels

arXiv: 2606.02232

Date: 2026-06-01

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.02232

TL;DR: This paper introduces a Doeblin-anchored contrastive chart that mixes a target Markov transition kernel with a restart law to create an invertible statistical coordinate, enabling contrastive learning to produce a valid Doeblin-minorized kernel while establishing a complete theoretical chain from contrastive excess risk to finite-horizon dynamical error.

核心贡献

  • 提出了 Doeblin-锚定对比图（Doeblin-anchored contrastive chart），一种将统计学习坐标与动态系统性质桥接的框架。
  • 通过将目标转移核 $K$ 与重启律 $\nu$ 以锚定强度 $\varepsilon$ 混合，构造出锚定核 $A_{\varepsilon,\nu}K = (1-\varepsilon)K + \varepsilon\nu$。该锚定核同时满足三种身份：(1) Doeblin 小化的合法马尔可夫核；(2) 二元对比实验中的正条件分布；(3) 原始转移律的显式可逆坐标。
  • 证明了锚定对比风险（anchored contrastive risk）以锚定转移密度 $a_0$ 为唯一总体最小化点，并建立了超额风险与密度误差之间的 $L^2$ 校准不等式 $c_-\|a-a_0\|_{2,\mu\lambda}^2 \leq E(a) \leq c_+\|a-a_0\|_{2,\mu\lambda}^2$。
  • 引入了 可测马尔可夫化算子（measurable Markovization operator），将反锚定后可能带符号或未归一化的得分恢复为合法转移核，同时保持积分 $L^1$ 精度至多损失一个常数因子 2。
  • 利用 Oracle 不等式与 Hölder–ReLU 逼近界，在独立转移对设定下获得了非参数收敛速率 $n^{-2\beta/(2\beta+2d)}$（至多相差对数因子），并证明了该速率在极小极大意义下近乎最优。
  • 对于平稳几何 $\beta$-混合轨迹，通过保守的稀疏化-耦合扩展（thinning-and-coupling extension），在有效样本量 $N_{\text{eff}} \asymp N/\log(N/\eta)$ 下保持了相同的重建接口与速率。
  • 建立了占用加权扰动界（occupancy-weighted perturbation bounds），将一步核误差传递到有限时域的边缘分布、路径律和占用测度误差，并证明了覆盖条件（occupancy coverage）是不可或缺的（通过两状态反例）。

Key Contributions

  • Introduces the Doeblin-anchored contrastive chart, a framework bridging statistical learning coordinates with dynamical system properties.
  • Constructs an anchored kernel by mixing the target transition $K$ with a restart law $\nu$ at strength $\varepsilon$: $A_{\varepsilon,\nu}K = (1-\varepsilon)K + \varepsilon\nu$. This anchored kernel simultaneously satisfies three roles: (1) a Doeblin-minorized valid Markov kernel; (2) the positive conditional law in a binary contrastive experiment; (3) an explicitly invertible coordinate for the original transition law.
  • Proves that the anchored contrastive risk has the anchored transition density $a_0$ as its unique population minimizer, and establishes an $L^2$ calibration inequality $c_-\|a-a_0\|_{2,\mu\lambda}^2 \leq E(a) \leq c_+\|a-a_0\|_{2,\mu\lambda}^2$.
  • Introduces a measurable Markovization operator that restores kernel validity from a potentially signed or unnormalized de-anchored score while preserving integrated $L^1$ accuracy up to a constant factor of 2.
  • Derives nonparametric convergence rates $n^{-2\beta/(2\beta+2d)}$ (up to log factors) for independent transition pairs via oracle inequalities and Hölder–ReLU approximation bounds, and proves minimax near-optimality.
  • For stationary geometrically $\beta$-mixing trajectories, provides a conservative thinning-and-coupling extension preserving the same reconstruction interface at effective sample size $N_{\text{eff}} \asymp N/\log(N/\eta)$.
  • Establishes occupancy-weighted perturbation bounds transferring one-step kernel error to finite-horizon marginal, path-law, and occupation-measure errors, and proves that the occupancy coverage condition is indispensable (via a two-state counterexample).
  • Designs diagnostic experiments verifying calibration, valid-kernel reconstruction, anchor-strength tradeoffs, trajectory stress tests, and coverage failure.

Methodology

The paper's core methodology is a statistical-to-dynamical coordinate framework organized around a three-step pipeline:

1. Anchoring (Statistical Coordinate). Given a target transition kernel $K_0$ with density $k_0(y|x)$, a simulable restart law $\nu$ with density $r(y)$, and an anchor strength $\varepsilon \in (0,1)$, define the anchored kernel:

$$A_{\varepsilon,\nu}K_0(x, B) = (1-\varepsilon)K_0(x,B) + \varepsilon\nu(B)$$

with anchored density $a_0(y|x) = (1-\varepsilon)k_0(y|x) + \varepsilon r(y)$. This construction guarantees $a_0(y|x) \geq \varepsilon r(y)$, supplying a uniform lower envelope.

2. Contrastive Estimation. The anchored density $a_0$ is learned via a binary contrastive experiment: positive samples are drawn from the anchored transition (implemented as a weighted double-positive risk with weight $1-\varepsilon$ on observed transitions and weight $\varepsilon$ on restart draws), while negative samples are drawn from $\nu$. The contrastive posterior coordinate is $\eta_a(x,y) = a(y|x)/(a(y|x) + \tau r(y))$, and the population risk is:

$$R(a) = \mathbb{E}\left[a_0 \log\frac{a_0}{a_0+\tau r} + \tau r \log\frac{\tau r}{a_0+\tau r}\right]$$

3. De-anchoring and Markovization. The learned score $\hat{a}_n$ is de-anchored via the affine inverse: $\tilde{k}_n = (\hat{a}_n - \varepsilon r)/(1-\varepsilon)$. Since this may produce a signed or unnormalized object, a deterministic Markovization operator $M$ is applied: clip negative entries to zero and renormalize each row, falling back to $r(y)$ on degenerate rows. Theorem 9 proves $\|Mg - k\|_{1,\mu\lambda} \leq 2\|g - k\|_{1,\mu\lambda}$.

4. Dynamical Transfer. Under an explicit occupancy coverage condition ($\xi K_0^s \ll \mu$ with bounded Radon-Nikodym derivative $C_s$), one-step kernel error is propagated to finite-horizon marginal error:

$$\text{TV}(\xi K_0^m, \xi \hat{K}^m) \leq \frac{m\sqrt{C_{\xi,\mu,m}}}{(1-\varepsilon)\sqrt{c_-}} \sqrt{E(\hat{a})}$$

and similarly for path laws and normalized occupation measures.

5. Dependent Data Extension. For geometrically $\beta$-mixing trajectories, a thinning parameter $q$ is chosen so that $(M-1)\beta_X(q-1) \leq \eta/2$, yielding effective sample size $N_{\text{eff}} = \lfloor N/q \rfloor$. The same reconstruction interface applies with $N_{\text{eff}}$ replacing $n$.

The experiments train a clipped ReLU network $a_\theta(x,y) = \gamma + (\Gamma-\gamma)\sigma(f_\theta(x,y))$ by binary contrastive logistic loss, then de-anchor, Markovize, and evaluate on grid quadrature.

Results & Highlights

  • Identification & Calibration (Theorem 6): The anchored target density $a_0$ uniquely minimizes the contrastive risk. Excess risk is tightly calibrated to $L^2$ density error with constants depending on $(\varepsilon, \tau, r_-, r_+, M_0, \gamma, \Gamma)$. An exact Bernoulli-KL representation connects the excess risk to posterior coordinate mismatch.
  • Oracle Inequalities (Theorem 7): Global and localized fast-rate oracle bounds are established using sup-norm covering numbers and Bernstein geometry. With probability $1-\eta$:

$$E(\hat{a}_n) \leq \frac{5}{3}\inf_{a \in \mathcal{A}_n} E(a) + C_{\text{fast}}\left(L_\ell \delta + \delta_{\text{opt}} + \frac{\log(2N_\infty^{\text{int}}(\delta, \mathcal{A}_n)/\eta)}{n}\right)$$

  • Hölder–ReLU Rate (Theorem 11): For transition densities in a Hölder class $H_d^\beta(R)$ on $[0,1]^d \times [0,1]^d$, using a clipped ReLU sieve tuned to $S_n = n^{d/(2\beta+d)}$, the estimator achieves with probability $1-\eta$:

$$\|\hat{k}_n - k_0\|_{2,\mu\lambda}^2 \vee d_{\mu,\text{TV}}(\hat{K}_n, K_0)^2 \leq C \cdot n^{-2\beta/(2\beta+2d)}(\log n)^\kappa + \delta_{\text{opt}} + \frac{\log(1/\eta)}{n}$$

This rate is minimax near-optimal up to logarithmic factors.

  • Dynamical Transfer (Theorem 12): Under occupancy coverage, one-step kernel TV error controls finite-horizon rollout error with factor $m\sqrt{C_{\xi,\mu,m}}$. The same bound applies to path-law and occupation-measure distances.
  • Diagnostic Experiments:
  • Calibration: Validation excess risk correlates with anchored $L^2$ error at $\sim$0.94 log-log correlation.
  • Markovization: Pre-Markovization scores show nonzero negative mass and row-sum error; post-Markovization both are exactly zero. The empirical repair ratio is $\sim$0.96, far below the theoretical bound of 2.
  • Rate diagnostic: Fitted log-log slopes for TV reconstruction are approximately 0.48 (smooth), 0.32 (rough), and 0.38 (multimodal).
  • Anchor strength: Reconstruction TV shows an interior optimum in $\varepsilon$, confirming the conditioning-inversion tradeoff.
  • Trajectory stress test: Fast-mixing trajectories behave like i.i.d. pairs; slow-mixing trajectories benefit from thinning.
  • Coverage failure: A rare-state design mass of $\delta = 0.01$ with integrated TV error 0.01 produces rollout TV of 1.0—an amplification factor of $100\times$, confirming Proposition 17.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The core conceptual contribution is elegant and novel: using Doeblin minorization not merely as an analytical tool in Markov chain theory, but as an explicit statistical coordinate for learning. The unification of contrastive estimation, kernel validity, and dynamical perturbation theory in one framework is technically ambitious and well-executed.
  • Rigor: The paper is densely theoretical with multiple layers of proofs (Theorems 2, 6, 7, 9, 11, 12, 14). The calibration constants $c_-, c_+$ are explicit in their dependencies, and the Markovization bound is clean and tight (factor of 2). The two-state counterexample in Proposition 17 effectively demonstrates that coverage assumptions are not merely technical conveniences.
  • Limitations honestly disclosed: The author explicitly notes several limitations in Section 9: (1) the main oracle theory is for independent pairs, with trajectory extension analyzing thinned ERM rather than full-trajectory ERM; (2) restart law selection remains a design problem; (3) bounded-density assumptions exclude heavy-tailed settings; (4) dynamical guarantees are finite-horizon and occupancy-weighted, not general stationary-distribution perturbation theorems.
  • Experimental scope: The experiments are explicitly framed as "interface diagnostics" rather than benchmarks. They are synthetic (necessary because the target kernel must be known to evaluate TV exactly), limited to low-dimensional settings, and do not compare against state-of-the-art model-based RL or video prediction systems. This is appropriate for the paper's theoretical focus but limits immediate practical assessment.
  • Reproducibility: The public repository and detailed protocol descriptions are positive signs. However, the paper does not report compute budgets or wall-clock times at scale.
  • Red flags: None significant. The paper does not overclaim—experiments are carefully labeled as diagnostics, limitations are explicitly listed, and the author avoids making broad claims about beating empirical benchmarks.

Strengths

  • Provides a principled, theoretically grounded solution to the "kernel validity" problem in transition model learning, going beyond standard conditional density estimation.
  • Unifies three historically separate areas—contrastive learning, Doeblin minorization/Markov perturbation theory, and nonparametric statistics—through an elegant coordinate transform.
  • The Markovization operator is simple, deterministic, and comes with a clean constant-factor guarantee.
  • Handles both independent and dependent (mixing) data within a consistent framework.
  • Error propagation bounds are explicit, occupancy-weighted, and interpretable.
  • The ablation study carefully tests each interface component and confirms the theoretical division of labor.
  • Writing is exceptionally clear for a paper at this theoretical depth, with helpful diagrams and explicit statement of limitations.

Limitations

  • No real-world empirical validation: All experiments are synthetic, low-dimensional diagnostics. The framework's scalability to high-dimensional state spaces (e.g., images in video prediction, latent states in world models) is untested.
  • Trajectory theory is conservative: The thinning-and-coupling extension for dependent data is a conservative proof technique; full-trajectory ERM rates are not established.
  • Restart law selection: The choice of $\nu$ and $\varepsilon$ involves a nontrivial tradeoff between statistical conditioning and reconstruction stability. The paper does not provide an automatic, data-driven selection procedure with theoretical guarantees.
  • Boundedness assumptions: Global upper and lower bounds on densities ($r_-, r_+, M_0, \gamma, \Gamma$) are required for the calibration and concentration results. These exclude unbounded or heavy-tailed transition densities common in some applications.
  • Computational cost: While Markovization is cheap, the contrastive score network training dominates runtime. The paper does not establish computational complexity guarantees or compare training cost to simpler baselines.
  • Finite-horizon focus: The dynamical transfer is finite-horizon and requires explicit occupancy coverage. Stationary-distribution guarantees require additional uniform ergodicity assumptions (Dobrushin coefficient $< 1$).

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does the framework scale to high-dimensional state spaces such as images or latent representations in world models? Can the Markovization operator be implemented efficiently when $\lambda$ is high-dimensional?
  2. Can the restart law $\nu$ and anchor strength $\varepsilon$ be selected adaptively from data with theoretical guarantees, rather than by validation heuristics?
  3. How do the nonparametric rates compare numerically to modern neural network-based transition learners (e.g., diffusion world models, JEPA architectures) on standard RL benchmarks?
  4. Can the framework be extended to continuous-time Markov processes, partially observed settings (POMDPs), or non-stationary environments?
  5. Is there a path to full-trajectory ERM rates without thinning, perhaps via martingale concentration or mixingale techniques?
  6. Can the boundedness assumptions be relaxed via tail-localized arguments or drift-minorization techniques, as suggested in the conclusion?
