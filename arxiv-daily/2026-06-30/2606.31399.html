Phase transition · Long-horizon agents

Long-horizon agent failure is a phase transition: world-state fidelity collapses before action validity — and stronger models only shift the cliff, never erase it.

World-Model Collapse as a Phase Transition

Xinyuan Song · Emory    Zekun Cai · The University of Tokyo / LocationMind

world-models phase-transition long-horizon-agents LLM-agents agent-evaluation
Key metric · precursor lead 2 median steps

World-state fidelity fails before action validity. Success collapses 100% → 0% between SC=10 and SC=20 (DD=1).

Why this matters

For long-horizon-agent researchers it offers a step-level “world-model collapse” bottleneck — directly probing whether scaling search can rescue failing agents.

Critical edge

Confirmed only in StatefulPuzzle via one fixed Planner / Updater / Self-Diag loop. Alternative memory regimes are untested — the cliff may be a harness artifact, not a universal bottleneck.

01

Methodology

  • Physics vocabulary, no thermodynamic limit. Control params SC (state cardinality) & DD (dependency density); order param = task success p; precursor = world-state fidelity ρ.
  • Def. 1. Abrupt transition if an adjacent SC pair drops ≥ δ; crossing s⋆ = inf{s : p ≤ ½}.
  • 3 deterministic simulators (seed-only randomness); a pilot rule locked before evaluation selects StatefulPuzzle — GraphNav & ToolDAG fail shape / drop criteria.
  • Confirmatory grid: SC∈{5,10,20,40} × DD∈{1,2,4,6}, n=100/cell → 1,600 sha256-seeded episodes, zero collisions.
  • Agent: fixed 3-call loop (Planner → Updater → Self-Diag), claude-haiku-4-5, temp 0. Code released.
  • Mechanism test: τW (world onset) < τA (first invalid action) ⇒ representational collapse.
02

Results & highlights

  • Confirmatory cliff (haiku). SC=5 solves all (1.00); SC=10 degrades 1.00→0.20 with DD; SC=20 & 40 → 0.00. The decisive axis is SC.
  • World-state fails first. Median lead ≈ 2 steps; no paired episode shows plan-first collapse. Final success alone cannot diagnose it.
  • SC-Fine brackets crossover SC=13 (0.78)→14 (0.52)→15 (0.013); 4-PL fit SC⋆≈13.9. T-Fine stays flat → horizon is a modulator, not a driver.
  • Ablations: horizon=enabler, branching=null, observation=gate, mutation=modulator.

03

Strengths

what holds up
  • Clean, memorable framing with a precise operational definition (Def. 1), not a vague analogy.
  • Falsifiable step-level claim (τW<τA); transparently self-corrects its initial “symmetric synchrony” hypothesis.
  • Strong hygiene: locked pre-evaluation selection rule, exact gold state, seed control, released code.
  • Negative control (SC-Fine resolves a boundary, T-Fine flat) cleanly rules out a horizon reparametrization.
  • Actionable: evaluate scaffolds by whether they delay τW, not just lift final success.
04

Limitations

where it frays
  • Single-environment confirmatory claim; no WebArena / SWE-Bench / TravelPlanner validation.
  • Fixed agent architecture — the cliff may be harness-dependent; raw/summarized/external memory untested.
  • Discretized axes; no critical exponents or scaling laws; boundaries interpolated from integer grids.
  • Cross-platform serving differences limit the cross-model table to “translation,” not a capability leaderboard.
  • Mutation-rate axis is exploratory/descriptive, not equally established as a driver.
05

Questions for further exploration

  1. Does the (SC,DD) cliff survive a harness swap — raw vs. summarized vs. external symbolic memory — or does the boundary move or change shape?
  2. How does SC⋆ scale with model size and training data (a power law?), and does it differ between base and instruction-tuned models?
  3. Can state-externalizing interventions push SC⋆ outward, or only translate the boundary like larger models do?
  4. Is the τW<τA ordering stable across all six stress dimensions, or does it invert under partial observation?
  5. Does the plateau / transition / floor geometry appear in naturalistic benchmarks once SC and DD are estimated and controlled?
