> **一句话总结：** 本文提出相对惊奇指数 (RSI = 1 + log π_θ(o_t) / H_t)，将 token 熵与所选 token 概率耦合，并基于 Theorem 1 证明 RSI 是 logit 梯度范数对预测熵的局部一阶变化率之倒数；据此设计的 RSI-S 区间过滤方法（保留 RSI∈[a,b]，a<0<b）统一了"高熵优先"与"低概率抑制"两类矛盾的 RLVR 范式，在 Qwen2.5-1.5B/3B/7B 上 AIME 与 AMC 的 avg@32 较 GRPO 提升 2.1–3.3 个百分点，同时显著缩短响应长度。

> **TL;DR:** The authors introduce the Relative Surprisal Index (RSI = 1 + log π_θ(o_t|q,o_<t) / H_t), prove (Theorem 1) that RSI equals the reciprocal of the local first-order variation rate of the logit-gradient norm w.r.t. predictive entropy, and use it to build RSI-S — an asymmetric interval token filter [a,b] (a<0<b) that reconciles the contradictory high-entropy-vs-low-probability RLVR paradigms. RSI-S improves avg@32 by 2.1–3.3 points over GRPO on Qwen2.5-1.5B/3B/7B (AIME, AMC) while producing shorter responses.

## 核心贡献

- **提出 RSI（相对惊奇指数）**：定义为 `RSI_t = δ_t / H_t = 1 + log π_θ(o_t|q,o_<t) / H_t`，其中 δ_t 是所选 token 对数概率相对分布均值的偏离，H_t 为 token 熵。它将"分布不确定性"与"所选 token 的实现概率"耦合，而非孤立看待任一者。
- **定理 1 给出梯度解释**：证明了在 H_t>0、对所选 logit 施加小扰动条件下，`d log T_t / d log H_t = 1 / RSI_t`（T_t 为 log 概率关于 logit 的 ℓ2 梯度范数）。这说明 RSI 刻画了"token 熵变化"与"梯度范数变化"之间的局部敏感性关系，为 token 选择提供了非启发式的理论基础。
- **提出 RSI-S 自适应 token 过滤**：保留 RSI∈[a, b]（a<0<b，|a|>b 的非对称区间）的 token，下界 a 剔除极端高惊奇尾部 token，上界 b 剔除过度可预测的低惊奇 token，且筛选范围随熵自适应变化（`e^(a-1)H_t ≤ π_θ(o_t) ≤ e^(b-1)H_t`），而非静态概率截断。
- **统一了两类矛盾范式**：RSI 同时整合了 Wang et al. 的"高熵 80/20 规则"与 Yang et al. 的"抑制低概率 token"两种思路，并在实验中均超越这两个基线。
- **跨尺度一致收益**：1.5B/3B/7B 上 avg@32 平均提升 +2.10 / +3.30 / +2.19 个百分点，且响应长度大幅缩短（−108 至 −266 tokens）。

## Key Contributions

- **Introduces the Relative Surprisal Index (RSI):** Defined as `RSI_t = δ_t / H_t = 1 + log π_θ(o_t|q,o_<t) / H_t`, where δ_t is the selected token's log-probability deviation from the distribution-level mean and H_t is the token entropy. It jointly couples predictive uncertainty with the realized token's likelihood, overcoming the insufficiency of evaluating probability or entropy in isolation.
- **Theorem 1 gives a gradient-based interpretation:** Under H_t>0 and a small perturbation of the selected token's logit, the authors prove `d log T_t / d log H_t = 1/RSI_t`, where T_t is the ℓ2-norm of the gradient of log π_θ(o_t|q,o_<t) w.r.t. logits. This grounds RSI in how gradient magnitude co-varies with predictive entropy, not as a heuristic.
- **Proposes RSI-S adaptive token filtering:** Keeps tokens with RSI∈[a,b] (a<0<b, |a|>b). The lower bound removes extreme high-surprisal tail tokens; the upper bound removes overly predictable low-surprisal tokens. The admissible probability range is entropy-adaptive (`e^(a-1)H_t ≤ π_θ(o_t) ≤ e^(b-1)H_t`), unlike static probability truncation.
- **Reconciles the two contradictory paradigms:** RSI-S simultaneously integrates Wang et al.'s high-entropy "80/20 rule" and Yang et al.'s low-probability suppression, and empirically beats both baselines.
- **Consistent cross-scale gains:** avg@32 improvements of +2.10 / +3.30 / +2.19 points on 1.5B/3B/7B over GRPO, with markedly shorter responses (−108 to −266 tokens).

## Methodology

The paper targets the tension in RLVR between two empirical-but-contradictory findings: (1) Wang et al. [24] advocate training on the top-20% highest-entropy token positions (the "80/20 rule"), and (2) Yang et al. [29] caution that low-probability tokens can induce unstable gradient magnitudes and should not dominate updates. The authors empirically observe (Fig. 2) that high-entropy positions are usually correlated with low sampled-token probability, and that most generated tokens concentrate in the high-probability regime. They argue probability or entropy alone cannot characterize a token's contribution to policy optimization.

**RSI definition.** For a sampled token o_t ∼ π_θ(·|q,o_<t) = [p_t,1,…,p_t,V] with entropy H_t = −Σ_s p_t,s log p_t,s, they define the deviation δ_t = log(p_t,n) − E_{p_t,s}[log p_t,s] and normalize it by H_t:

  RSI_t = δ_t / H_t = 1 + log π_θ(o_t|q,o_<t) / H_t.

RSI is defined for non-degenerate distributions (H_t>0); for H_t→0, RSI→1 by continuity (proven in Appendix C). RSI_t>0 means the selected token is more likely than average (low surprisal); RSI_t<0 means it is less likely (high surprisal). The expected RSI over the policy distribution is 0.

**Theorem 1 (gradient grounding).** Let T_t be the ℓ2-norm of ∇_{l_t} log π_θ(o_t|q,o_<t) w.r.t. the final-layer logits. Under a small perturbation l_t,n → l_t,n+Δl_t,n with H_t>0 and RSI_t≠0, `d log T_t / d log H_t = 1/RSI_t`. The proof (Appendix C) derives ∂H_t/∂l_t,i = −p_t,i H_t (1 + log p_t,i/H_t) and the analogous ∂T_t/∂l_t,i terms via softmax derivatives, then takes the ratio for a single-logit perturbation. This means |RSI_t| near 0 implies strong coupling between entropy change and gradient-norm change; extreme RSI values decouple them.

**RSI-S filtering.** Because the empirical RSI distribution is heavily concentrated near 1 (most tokens are low-surprisal) and asymmetric around 0, the authors use an asymmetric interval [a,b] with a<0<b and |a|>b. A token is retained iff RSI_t∈[a,b], equivalently e^(a−1)H_t ≤ π_θ(o_t) ≤ e^(b−1)H_t. This is an entropy-adaptive gate (probability bounds scale with H_t), not a static threshold. The RSI-S GRPO objective reweights only retained tokens: J^RSI(θ) = E[… (1/G)Σ (1/|κ^{a,b}_i|) Σ γ_{i,t}(θ)], where γ_{i,t}(θ) = I[RSI_{i,t}∈[a,b]]·η_{i,t}(θ), |κ^{a,b}_i| is the count of retained tokens, and trajectories with zero retained tokens are excluded.

**Training setup.** Built on EasyR1 (on top of verl). Base models only (Qwen2.5-1.5B/3B/7B) to avoid SFT confounds. Dataset: DAPO-MATH-17K. Group size G=16, batch 1024, mini-batch 128 (8 mini-batch updates/outer step), lr 1e-6, 10 epochs = 160 outer steps / 1280 mini-batch updates, seed 0, sampling temp 1.0/top-p 1.0, eval temp 0.6/top-p 0.95, avg@32 every 4 steps. RSI intervals: [−6,0.95]/[−6,0.9]/[−6,0.8] for 1.5B/3B/7B. Baselines: GRPO, EB (80/20 entropy rule ported into the same framework), PB (Yang et al.'s released implementation).

## Results & Highlights

**Main results (avg@32 average over AIME 2024–2026 + AMC 2022–2024):**

| Model | GRPO | +EB | +PB | +RSI-S | ΔAcc vs GRPO | ΔLen vs GRPO |
|-------|------|-----|-----|--------|--------------|--------------|
| 1.5B | 12.15 | 11.64 | 11.83 | 14.25 | +2.10 | −108.70 |
| 3B | 18.53 | 17.86 | 18.32 | 21.83 | +3.30 | −182.86 |
| 7B | 29.05 | 29.88 | 28.87 | 31.24 | +2.19 | −265.95 |

- RSI-S **beats both EB and PB** at every scale; notably EB/PB barely help (or even hurt) on smaller models, whereas RSI-S is uniformly positive — supporting the claim it synthesizes both signals.
- RSI-S produces **substantially shorter responses** (up to −345 tokens on 7B AIME), suggesting efficiency gains beyond accuracy.
- Largest gains are on **AMC** (e.g., +5.95 on 3B AMC); AIME gains are smaller (the 1.5B AIME absolute scores are near floor at ~2.4%).

**Ablations:**
- **Static probability thresholds fail:** P1=[0.1,0.9], P2=[0.2,0.8], P3=[0.3,0.7] all *degrade* accuracy (−2.37 to −4.89) at 3B/7B, proving RSI-S is not equivalent to fixed probability truncation — the benefit comes from entropy-adaptive gating.
- **Both bounds are necessary:** RSI-SU (drop upper bound → [a,1]) and RSI-SL (drop lower bound → [−∞,b]) both underperform the balanced [a,b] (e.g., 3B: RSI-SU +0.61, RSI-SL −0.08 vs +3.30 full).
- **Scale shifts the upper bound:** best b decreases with model size (1.5B→0.95, 3B→0.9, 7B→0.8). Smaller models need more low-surprisal tokens (exploitation/knowledge consolidation); larger models benefit from more exploration, so predictable tokens become less critical.
- **RSI more stably captures RL-critical tokens:** Using per-token Jensen–Shannon divergence between pre-/post-RL outputs (following Meng et al. [15]) as a proxy for RL-affected tokens, RSI's recall of high-JS tokens exceeds entropy-based selection across scales and JS thresholds, with threshold sensitivity only 0.50×/0.11×/0.22× that of entropy for 1.5B/3B/7B.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (Moderate-High):** The single-metric framing that provably couples entropy and selected-token probability is genuinely appealing, and Theorem 1's `d log T_t / d log H_t = 1/RSI_t` is a clean, non-trivial result. However, RSI itself is structurally a normalized pointwise-mutual-information / surprisal ratio — a known information-theoretic primitive; the novelty is principally in (a) deriving the gradient coupling and (b) the asymmetric interval filtering recipe.
- **Rigor (Moderate):** Theorem 1's proof is detailed and checked in Appendix C, but it holds under specific conditions — H_t>0, RSI_t≠0, and crucially a *single-logit* perturbation (Δl_t,n only). The authors state the "only consider a small amount on Δl_t,n" assumption explicitly at the end of the proof, which limits the result's generality to one coordinate at a time. Whether the relationship holds under joint multi-coordinate perturbations is unaddressed.
- **Reproducibility (Good):** Open base models (Qwen2.5), public datasets (DAPO-MATH-17K), open frameworks (EasyR1, verl), fixed seed (0), and full hyperparameters (lr, batch, group size, intervals per scale) are disclosed. PB uses the authors' released code; EB is ported into the same framework for fair comparison. This is well above average for the subfield.
- **Significance (Moderate):** A reliable +2–3 avg@32 with shorter outputs is a useful, drop-in improvement to GRPO. The unifying conceptual story is the larger contribution. Gains are not transformative and are concentrated on easier benchmarks (AMC >> AIME), where smaller models are near floor on AIME.
- **Red flags / concerns:**
  - No variance, confidence intervals, or multiple-seed runs are reported despite tiny absolute numbers on AIME (1.5B AIME ≈ 2.4%); a +0.11 point gain there is likely within noise.
  - Only GRPO as the base RL algorithm and only math reasoning (AIME/AMC). Transfer to code, tool-use, other RLVR variants (DAPO/GSPO), or larger scales (>7B) is untested.
  - The "expected RSI over the policy distribution is 0" property is asserted as motivating the zero-anchored interval, but the asymmetric [−6, b] interval with a very negative lower bound effectively barely filters the negative tail while the asymmetric b does most of the work — the "balancing" framing may be stronger than the actual mechanism.
  - Self-citation overlap: an author co-authored the gradient-magnitude token-selection baseline (GMTS [13]); GMTS is discussed in related work but not run as a head-to-head baseline.

## Strengths

- Provides a *principled, theoretically grounded* metric (with a real theorem and proof) rather than another heuristic token-weighting scheme.
- The asymmetric interval design elegantly fuses two contradictory prior paradigms and is ablated to show both bounds matter.
- Convincing ablation that static probability truncation fails (P1/P2/P3 all negative), isolating the value of entropy-adaptive gating.
- Strong reproducibility profile: open models/data/frameworks, full hyperparameters, fair baselines ported into one pipeline.
- Bonus efficiency win: shorter generations at higher accuracy.

## Limitations

- Theorem 1 is restricted to single-coordinate (selected-logit) perturbations; the general multi-coordinate case is not proven.
- No statistical significance / multi-seed results; some headline gains (1.5B AIME +0.11) sit in the noise floor.
- Evaluation is narrow: only math (AIME/AMC), only GRPO base, only up to 7B. Generalization claims to other domains/algorithms/scales are untested.
- RSI intervals are scale-specific ([−6,0.95]/[−6,0.9]/[−6,0.8]) — some per-scale tuning is involved, though Table 6 shows the method stays positive across tested b values.
- The very negative lower bound a=−6 likely filters almost nothing on the negative side, so the practical "asymmetry" mostly reduces to choosing b, slightly undercutting the symmetric-balancing narrative.

## Relevance

对于关注大模型推理训练与 RLVR 算法改进的研究者，本文提供的 RSI 指标（含梯度层面的理论解释）与 RSI-S 自适应 token 过滤方法具有直接的方法论价值，可作为理解 token 级梯度动态、改进 GRPO 类算法以及缩短推理输出的实用参考。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does Theorem 1's `d log T_t / d log H_t = 1/RSI_t` generalize beyond a single-coordinate logit perturbation to joint multi-coordinate perturbations?
- How does RSI-S behave with multi-seed runs and confidence intervals, especially on near-floor AIME scores for small models?
- Does RSI-based selection transfer to non-math domains (code, tool use, general QA) and to other RLVR variants (DAPO, GSPO, GSPO) and larger models (>7B)?
- How sensitive is performance to the lower bound a — given a=−6 filters almost nothing, is RSI-S effectively just an entropy-adaptive upper-bound (probability) cap?
- Can RSI serve as a *diagnostic* (not just a filter) to analyze token-level learning dynamics in PPO/RLOO/other RL algorithms?
- Would combining RSI-S with sequence-level methods (e.g., GSPO) or with reweighting (rather than hard masking) yield compounding gains?
- Is the "expected RSI = 0" zero-mean property robust under non-uniform temperature/top-p sampling, and does it justify the zero-anchored interval empirically vs. alternatives?
