> **一句话总结：** FARS 是一个由 Analemma 团队构建的全流程"AI 做 AI 研究"系统，用 Ideation/Planning/Experiment/Writing 四阶段专用 agent 通过共享可审计工作空间协作，首次公开部署在 417 小时、约 18.6 万美元成本下产出覆盖 67 个 AI/ML 细分主题的 166 篇完整论文；282 份专家评审（均分 3.23/10）显示它能稳定产出可送审、偶有较强水平的成果，但贡献与严谨性偏低、27.9% 的论文存在完整性问题，主要瓶颈是实验充分性而非写作质量。

> **TL;DR:** FARS is a fully autonomous AI-for-AI research system (from Analemma) whose stage-specific agents — Ideation, Planning, Experiment, Writing — coordinate via a shared auditable workspace; its first public deployment ran 417 hours for ~$186K and produced 166 complete papers across 67 fine-grained AI/ML topics, evaluated by 282 structured expert reviews (mean 3.23/10). It reliably yields review-worthy and occasionally strong artifacts, but contribution/soundness scores are low, 27.9% of papers have integrity issues, and the binding bottleneck is experimental sufficiency rather than writing.

## 核心贡献

- **端到端全自动研究系统：** 提出 FARS，将开放式研究方向在没有执行期人工干预的情况下转化为完整论文，定位为"AI 做 AI 研究"的部署型系统。
- **阶段化 Agent + 共享可审计工作空间架构：** 用面向 Ideation/Planning/Experiment/Writing 四个阶段的专用 agent，通过同时作为项目记忆与制品存储的共享工作空间协作，使成功与失败的轨迹都可被外部审计。
- **大规模公开部署的真实证据：** 首次公开部署产出 166 篇完整论文、覆盖 67 个细粒度 AI/ML 主题，运行 417 小时、消耗 21.6B tokens、成本约 18.6 万美元（约 $1,120/篇），并将全部中间制品（提案、代码、日志、结果、稿件）保留为可审计语料而非精选成功集。
- **以人工专家评审为核心评估：** 从 583 名申请者中筛选出 88 名志愿者评审，最终 55 人对 140 篇论文给出 282 份结构化评审，采用 ICLR 量规并新增 8 类"AI 完整性审计"，评审者可访问每篇论文的代码/日志/原始输出进行交叉核验。
- **与现有系统的统一对比：** 用统一的 Stanford Agentic Reviewer 对 7 个系统评分，FARS 以 5.00 分领先于 DeepScientist(4.13)、ScientistOne(4.00) 等所有基线，是唯一有论文达到自动接受阈值的系统。
- **诚实暴露失败模式：** 公开指出实验范围狭窄、方法局限与完整性问题（27.9% 论文被标记完整性问题，且高度集中于低分论文）是反复出现的弱点。

## Key Contributions

- **End-to-end fully autonomous research system:** Introduces FARS, a deployed AI-for-AI research system that turns open-ended research directions into complete hypothesis-and-validation papers with no human intervention during execution.
- **Stage-specific agents coordinated via a shared auditable workspace:** Designs four stages (Ideation, Planning, Experiment, Writing) whose specialized agents exchange outputs through a persistent shared workspace serving as both project memory and artifact store, leaving both successful and failed trajectories inspectable.
- **At-scale public deployment with full artifact retention:** First public deployment produced 166 complete papers across 67 fine-grained AI/ML topics (417 hours, 21.6B tokens, ~$186K, ~$1,120/paper), with all intermediate artifacts (proposals, plans, code, logs, results, manuscripts) preserved as an auditable corpus rather than a curated success set.
- **Human expert review as the primary evaluation:** Recruited 88 reviewers from 583 applicants (82% had reviewed >20 papers); 55 reviewers produced 282 structured reviews across 140 papers, using an ICLR-style rubric augmented with an 8-category AI Integrity Audit and giving reviewers access to each paper's code, logs, and raw outputs.
- **Uniform cross-system comparison:** Using a single Stanford Agentic Reviewer across 7 systems, FARS achieves the highest mean rating (5.00 over 165 papers), ahead of DeepScientist (4.13) and ScientistOne (4.00), and is the only system with any paper reaching the automated acceptance threshold.
- **Honest reporting of failure modes:** Transparently documents recurring issues — narrow experimental scope, methodological limitations, and integrity violations concentrated in the weakest outputs — rather than only showcasing wins.

## Methodology

**System design (FARS).** FARS is a multi-agent system organized as four sequential stages that share a workspace acting as both project memory and an auditable artifact store. Three design principles guide it: (1) the unit of production is a *focused* contribution (a clear hypothesis plus a validation attempt), with negative results treated as first-class when limitations are explicit; (2) the system is *artifact-centered*; (3) stages have clear inputs, outputs, and boundary checks so many projects run independently with common structure.

- **Ideation stage.** Seeded with 9 suggested research directions (RLVR, SLM post-training, frontier-LLM evaluation, architectures beyond Transformers, continual learning, diffusion LMs, agent memory, test-time scaling, world models) but allowed to explore freely. It is internally a collaboration of four agents — a **Lead Agent** (literature search, three-tier knowledge base, gap identification, proposal authoring), a **Summarization Agent**, an adversarial **Peer Discussion Agent** that prunes weak ideas, and an **Evaluation Agent** (accept/revise/reject with revision feedback) — driving a six-step pipeline. Every accepted proposal proceeds downstream; a circuit breaker selects the best draft after repeated failures. Proposals are constrained to the AI/ML domain, require no human annotation, and stay within a fixed compute budget.
- **Planning stage.** Converts an accepted proposal into a machine-readable *experiment contract*: a two-level hierarchy where each item belongs to one of five task categories (**environment configuration, baselines, main experiments, effectiveness evaluation, analysis**) and is decomposed into ordered, executable steps. This counters two model failure modes on long-horizon tasks: single-pass shallow attempts and premature completion claims. Automated validation checks JSON format, required fields, category names, and ordering.
- **Experiment stage.** Separates orchestration from execution: a harness decomposes the plan into sequential items executed one at a time, maintaining a progress record between items. An *effectiveness-evaluation gate* transitions from main experiments to analysis only if the main claim is supported (preserving negative outcomes). Within each item, an experiment agent produces an execution plan reviewed by an independent review agent for fidelity; execution uses full tool permissions; **dual verification** combines the agent's semantic self-assessment with deterministic harness checks. Key infrastructure: a **layered environment** (locked base deps like PyTorch/vLLM/flash-attention; agent works in an isolated overlay), a **curated skill library** of 19 ML-practice categories retrieved on demand, **managed compute** (a 160-GPU NVIDIA cluster) with ownership isolation and plan-item attribution, a **unified model proxy** for chat/embedding/rerank/image endpoints, and **checkpoint-based fault tolerance** (immediate version-control commits, resume-from-last-item, credential-leak scanning, termination handlers).
- **Writing stage.** Separates **Analysis Agent** (determines what can be claimed from upstream artifacts and builds a *paper blueprint* linking each central claim to supporting experiments, source artifacts, figures/tables, and literature) from the **Writing Agent** (drafts LaTeX section by section). Analytical plots are generated by running plotting scripts over saved outputs; methodological diagrams are synthesized from figure descriptions and refined with vision-language feedback (instantiated with NanoBanana-Pro). Refinement includes automatic compilation feedback, citation/reference/figure/table checks, numerical verification against source artifacts, and a paper-level review for unsupported/overstated claims.

**Public deployment.** Ran 417 hours, consumed 21.6B tokens, cost ~$186K (~$1,120/paper; ~2.51 deployment-hours/paper; ~130M tokens/paper). Produced 166 papers across 67 fine-grained topics: 72 within the 9 seed topics and 94 in emergent topics. Largest seed clusters: RLVR (13), Automated LLM Evaluation (10), Agent Memory (10), Continual Learning (9). Largest emergent: AI Safety/Alignment (13), Vision-Language Models (7), Code Generation (6). The run was streamed publicly; artifacts were committed to a GitLab namespace (gitlab.com/fars-a) operated by the system itself. A small arXiv-bound subset received a *minimal human integrity review* (factual accuracy, citation validity, artifact consistency, safety claims, AI-generation disclosure) with research content left unchanged.

**Human review protocol.** Open call yielded 583 applications (503 with reviewing experience); 88 reviewers selected on topic match, academic standing, and review experience (82% had reviewed >20 papers), spanning PhD students to faculty/industry researchers across countries (largely China and US). Review cycle: March 21 – April 12, 2026. Reviews follow an ICLR-modeled form (overall rating {0,2,4,6,8,10}, sub-scores for soundness/presentation/contribution on 1–4, confidence 1–5) adapted for short, focused papers (length not penalized; a well-motivated hypothesis rigorously falsified is treated as a first-class contribution). Crucially, reviewers received each paper's full artifact trace and were required to perform an **AI Integrity Audit** over eight failure modes (hallucinated citations, fabricated/unverifiable results, spurious novelty, hallucinated methods/baselines, math/logic errors, internal inconsistencies, experimental-design pathologies, language pathologies). Papers were self-claimed by reviewers (max 3 per paper). After auditing, 284 submissions yielded 282 valid reviews (3 mismatches corrected, 2 excluded); 55 reviewers participated; 140 of 166 papers received ≥1 review. Free-text strengths/weaknesses were categorized using the Kargaran et al. (2025) taxonomy via Claude Opus 4.6 (temperature 0).

**Cross-system comparison.** A single Stanford Agentic Reviewer (SAR) was applied uniformly to public papers from 7 systems (AI Scientist, AI Scientist-v2, CycleResearcher, AutoResearchClaw, ScientistOne, DeepScientist, FARS) under an ICLR-style 0–10 rubric.

## Results & Highlights

- **Headline scale:** 166 papers / 67 topics from one continuous run; ~$1,120 and 2.5 hrs per paper.
- **Overall human rating (review level):** mean **3.17**, median 2.0. Distribution: 8.2% rated 0, 42.9% rated 2, 31.2% rated 4, **17.7% rated 6**; **no review gave 8 or 10** (6 was the max observed).
- **Paper-level overall rating:** mean **3.23**, median 3.0; 39.3% have mean ≥4; **only 11.4% (16 papers) reach the acceptance threshold (mean ≥6)** — and 14 of those 16 were single-reviewer papers; only 2 of 95 multi-review papers reached mean 6.
- **Sub-scores:** soundness mean 2.15, contribution mean 2.00 (lowest), presentation mean 2.46 (strongest), confidence mean 3.78 (clusters at 4). Top sub-scores are rare (contribution never reaches 4).
- **Predictors of overall rating:** contribution r=0.743 (strongest), soundness r=0.652, presentation r=0.451 — all highly significant, indicating the bottleneck is substantive contribution, not prose. Reviewer confidence is *inversely* related to rating (4.30 at rating 0 vs 3.46 at 6) — reviewers are more certain when rejecting.
- **Integrity audit:** **16.7% of reviews (47/282) flagged a violation**, affecting 27.9% of papers (39/140), with a steep quality gradient: 43.5% of rating-0 reviews vs 2.0% of rating-6 reviews. In free text, 44.0% of reviews mention ≥1 failure mode; most common are experimental-design pathologies (22.7%) and internal-consistency failures (13.1%). Severe, code-verifiable failures are almost always escalated: hallucinated methods/baselines (20/22 flagged), hallucinated citations (13/16), fabricated results (16/21).
- **Reviewer agreement:** mean per-paper spread 2.00 (one rating level); 28.4% identical, 4.2% maximum spread — comparable to NeurIPS 2021 (r≈0.58) and ICLR 2024/2025 (~1.5 points), i.e., within normal peer-review variability.
- **LLM-assisted reviewing:** 74.5% of reviews (210/282) disclosed LLM use (GPT 81, Claude 40, Gemini 30), mostly for polishing/summarization.
- **What reviewers reward:** execution over originality — methodological soundness (63.5%), experimental design (61.0%), clear motivation (57.4%), results (48.9%); novelty rewarded least (31.9%). Recognition of novelty rises sharply with quality (21.5% low-rated → 54.0% high-rated).
- **Recurring weakness:** experimental weaknesses appear in **92.9%** of reviews and are *universal* (100%) even among the highest-rated papers — narrow evaluation (46.8%), poor generalization (46.8%), missing ablations (31.9%), weak baselines (30.1%).
- **Cross-system comparison (SAR):** FARS mean **5.00** (165 papers) > DeepScientist 4.13 > ScientistOne 4.00 > AutoResearchClaw 3.19 > CycleResearcher 2.78 > AI Scientist-v2 2.40 > AI Scientist 2.29; pooled non-FARS mean 2.74. FARS is the only system with papers reaching the automated ≥6 threshold (1.8%). Note SAR is more lenient than the human panel (FARS human mean 3.23 vs SAR 5.00).

## Authors & Credibility

- **Authors:** Qiong Tang, Xiangkun Hu, Xiangyang Liu, Yiran Chen, Yunfan Shao — all from **Analemma** ({name}@analemma.ai), equal contribution, listed alphabetically by first name. The paper also formally lists 41 named reviewers (plus 2 anonymous) from institutions including NUS, Fudan, CMU, Stanford, Tsinghua, Oxford, Vector Institute, HKUST, ByteDance, Tencent, etc.
- **Evidence standard:** The work goes well beyond the cherry-picked demos common in this subfield. It grounds its central claims in a large corpus of preserved artifacts, a substantial recruited reviewer panel with disclosed expertise, an explicit AI-integrity audit, and LLM-use disclosure. Reviewer agreement is benchmarked against published NeurIPS/ICLR consistency figures, which is a credibility plus.
- **Openness:** Reviewed papers and reviews are released on HuggingFace (`analemma-ai/fars-a-reviews`); intermediate artifacts are on a public GitLab namespace; a public deployment page exists (analemma.ai/fars/). Six reviews were withheld at reviewer request.
- **Caveats on credibility:** Reviewers are recruited volunteers, not an independent venue program committee, so results are explicitly framed as "structured expert analysis" rather than conference peer review. The arXiv-submitted subset received a human integrity pass, but the research content itself was not independently verified by the authors before archival submission.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (Moderate–High):** Fully automated AI-for-AI pipelines exist (AI Scientist, AI Scientist v2, CycleResearcher, DeepScientist, ScientistOne), but FARS's combination of (a) genuine at-scale public deployment with artifact retention, (b) a recruited expert panel with an artifact-grounded integrity audit, and (c) a uniform cross-system automated comparison is a step beyond the typical few-paper demo. The systems-engineering contributions (experiment contract, dual verification, layered environments, curated skill library) are credible and transferable.
- **Rigor (Good, with honest gaps):** The evaluation is unusually rigorous for this area — 282 reviews, eight-mode integrity audit, reviewer-confidence reporting, and benchmarking against NeurIPS/ICLR reviewer-disagreement norms. The authors are forthcoming that the absolute SAR scores are lenient and that single-reviewer papers inflate the acceptance-threshold rate. However, several risks remain: reviewer self-selection/self-claim assignment may bias which papers got reviewed; 26 papers were never reviewed; and 74.5% of reviews used an LLM, which the authors themselves flag as a potential influence on phrasing and attention.
- **Reproducibility (Mixed):** Strong on *artifact* reproducibility (full GitLab trace, public reviews, deployment page) and on reporting operational costs. Weaker on *system* reproducibility — the paper does not clearly state that the agent prompts, skill-library contents, or exact configurations are open-sourced, so re-running FARS independently may not be possible. Compute scale (160 GPUs, $186K) also limits replication.
- **Significance (High):** The most valuable contribution may be the *characterization of failure modes* at deployment scale — especially the finding that integrity violations concentrate sharply in the weakest outputs and that the binding constraint is experimental sufficiency, not writing. This is actionable evidence for the whole AI-for-AI-research subfield.
- **Red flags / things to scrutinize:**
  - The headline "17.7% of reviews reach the acceptance threshold" shrinks to ~2% of multi-review papers when single-reviewer inflation is removed — a material nuance the paper deserves credit for disclosing but that readers should weight heavily.
  - "Occasionally strong" is carefully hedged; no paper received an 8 or 10 from any human reviewer.
  - 27.9% of reviewed papers had flagged integrity issues; 44% of reviews describe at least one integrity failure mode in free text. These are non-trivial rates for a system whose outputs are being arXiv-archived.
  - The cross-system SAR comparison uses small, unbalanced baseline corpora (3–71 papers) and an LLM proxy reviewer; the authors flag this as "for reference only," but the prominent Table 6 may still be over-read.
  - Author affiliation "Analemma" is a relatively unknown entity; the credibility load is carried by the recruited external reviewer panel rather than the authors' prior track record.

## Strengths

- Genuine end-to-end autonomy across a wide topic space, not a few hand-picked tasks.
- Engineering design that directly targets known agent failure modes (shallow completion, single-pass execution) via the experiment contract, dual verification, and effectiveness-gating.
- Auditable intermediate artifacts retained for *every* project, enabling external verification rather than trust.
- Evaluation via structured external human reviews with an artifact-grounded integrity audit and LLM-use disclosure — a high bar for this subfield.
- Transparent, quantitative disclosure of failure modes, cost, and the gap between scalable generation and reliable contribution.
- Public release of papers, reviews, and the artifact trace.

## Limitations

- Quality is inconsistent: mean human rating 3.23/10 and only ~2% of multi-review papers reach the acceptance threshold.
- 27.9% of reviewed papers had flagged integrity violations; fabrication/hallucination failures are present even if usually caught.
- The dominant weakness — experimental sufficiency — affects even the best papers (100% of high-rated papers still drew experimental criticism).
- Reviewers are self-selected volunteers with self-claimed assignments, not an independent PC; 26 papers were unreviewed; 74.5% of reviews used LLMs.
- Demonstrated only in AI/ML; generalization to domains needing physical experiments, proprietary data, or long theoretical proofs is unproven.
- Cost ($186K, 160 GPUs) and unclear release of prompts/skill-library contents limit independent replication.
- The cross-system comparison rests on an LLM proxy reviewer and small, unbalanced baseline corpora.

## Relevance

对于关注自动化科研、LLM 智能体与"AI 做 AI 研究"方向的工作而言，FARS 提供了少有的大规模公开部署证据与可审计制品语料，并用人工专家评审加完整性审计刻画了当前全自动研究系统的真实能力边界与典型失败模式，对评估该方向成熟度、设计可验证的自动研究流程具有直接参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How were the 9 seed topics chosen, and how sensitive is the topic distribution (72 seed vs 94 emergent) to that seeding? Are the "emergent" topics genuinely novel or recombinations of the seeds?
- Given that only ~2% of multi-review papers reach the acceptance threshold, is FARS's *per-paper* scientific yield high enough to justify the $1,120/paper cost, or is the value primarily in the auditable corpus of failures?
- Are the agent prompts, the 19-category curated skill library, and exact configurations publicly released to enable independent reproduction?
- How much of the integrity failure rate (27.9%) is attributable to specific stages (e.g., Experiment self-reporting vs. Writing), and could a stronger cross-stage claim-verification loop reduce it?
- Since 74.5% of reviews used LLMs, what is the sensitivity of the score distribution and integrity-flag rate to LLM-assisted reviewing? Is there a non-LLM-assisted control subset?
- Can the experiment-contract + dual-verification design be ported to non-AI domains (e.g., wet-lab biology, where execution cannot be fully automated), and would the effectiveness-evaluation gate still be meaningful?
- Does the inverse confidence–rating relationship (reviewers more certain when rejecting) generalize, and what does it imply for using reviewer confidence as a quality signal in automated research evaluation?
