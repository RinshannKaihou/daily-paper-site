Revisiting the Volume Hypothesis ↗

Gradient learning's generalization edge over random sampling diminishes as training data grows — recasting the volume debate as a data-size crossover.

Ari Pakman · Lior Kreimer · Yakir Berchenko  — Ben-Gurion University · ICML 2026 (PMLR 306)

wang-landau-sampling loss-landscape implicit-bias overparameterization binary networks

Key Metric · Optimization Gap

30.2→13.5pp

Random-vs-GD test-accuracy gap, MNIST · Base, shrinking as training set grows from D=30 to D=600. Trend holds across all 3 architectures × 2 datasets.

Gradient (GD) Random (REWL peak)

Why This Matters

Applies Wang-Landau flat-histogram sampling to quantify loss-landscape geometry vs. generalization — directly relevant to implicit-bias, overparameterization, and simplicity-bias theory, and a methodological starting point for continuous-weight, large-scale density estimation.

Critical Edge

Binary ±1 weights only — no real-valued nets. Tiny scale (D ≤ 600, ~152k params). No code released. The reconciling insight is conceptually modest; the genuine novelty is methodological, not theoretical.

01

Methodology

Binary-weight networks (w ∈ {±1}^N) give a finite 2^N state space, making flat-histogram sampling tractable. The target is the joint density of states g(A, Q) over training accuracy A and test accuracy Q — and critically g(A=D, Q), the volume of perfect-interpolation solutions.

  • Wang-Landau core. Random walk in weight space, reweighted by 1/g(A,Q) so the visited histogram goes flat; modification factor f ← √f on each flatness check, converged at log f ≤ 2⁻¹⁷.
  • Replica Exchange (REWL). (A,Q) space split into overlapping sub-regions, each with an independent walker; configs swapped via Metropolis-Hastings — essential to cover wide accuracy ranges.
  • Execution. 4–6 walkers (one per RTX 600 ADA), ~3–9 weight flips/proposal targeting ~40% acceptance; test-accuracy axis restricted to a 10%-wide window around the density peak.
  • Baseline. BinaryConnect + Adam (lr 1e-4); gap = GD/SGD test accuracy − Q* = argmax g(A=D,Q).

Results & Highlights

  • Gap shrinks with data. MNIST Base GD-gap: 30.2 pp @ D=30 (28.5% vs 58.7%) → 13.5 pp @ D=600 (75.0% vs 88.5%). Fashion-MNIST Wider: 27.8 pp → 11.5 pp.
  • SGD gaps smaller & noisier than full-batch GD — mini-batch noise already injects implicit regularization.
  • Density curves sharpen (curvature ↑) with D — volume of well-generalizing interpolants concentrates and shrinks, matching Fan et al. (2025).
  • Width helps, depth is mixed. Wider nets generalize best; Deeper effects are noisy and attributed to design choices, not the volume question.
  • Reconciled picture. Small data → interpolants abundant & heterogeneous, SGD lands atypically well (implicit bias dominates). Large data → density concentrates, random interpolants approach SGD (volume effects emerge). Crossover is governed by data size.
03

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Directly reconciles two genuinely conflicting results (Peleg & Hein 2024 vs. Yang et al. 2026).
  • Principled REWL tool quantifies basin volumes rather than arguing qualitatively.
  • Multi-arch × multi-dataset sweep separates robust trends from architecture noise.
  • Honest, nuanced framing; concrete comparable numbers in Table 2.

Limitations

  • Binary weights — may not transfer to real-valued nets (sampling far harder there).
  • Tiny scale: D ≤ 600, ~152k params — no ImageNet / transformer evidence.
  • "Generally diminishes" — Deeper/SGD cells are noisy, not universally monotonic.
  • Truncated 10% Q-window; tail behavior & multimodality unmeasured.
  • Observational across regimes — no causal manipulation of simplicity bias.
04

Open Questions

Does the same crossover regime appear for real-valued (continuous-weight) networks, and how could REWL-style sampling be adapted there?
What sets the exact data size where the volume hypothesis "takes over" — sample complexity, log|H_eff|, or task structure?
Within a well-generalizing basin, does SGD impose a finer-grained bias that random sampling misses — is the gap ever truly zero?
Can the joint density-of-states predict generalization a priori for a new arch/dataset pair, rather than explaining it post hoc?
How do BatchNorm, residuals, attention shift relative basin volumes and the crossover point?
