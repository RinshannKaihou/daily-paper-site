> **一句话总结：** 本文提出将贝叶斯工作流（后验预测检查、模拟校准、采样器几何诊断、留出预测密度）聚合为"校准预言机"，用于检测并修复 LLM 生成的概率程序中的统计错误指定——其检测 AUC 达 0.97（参考上界），并在 15 个模型的修复循环中显著优于单元测试反馈（后者因"通过即自信"反而比无反馈更差）。

> **TL;DR:** A probabilistic program that compiles and passes every unit test can still be *statistically* wrong. This paper aggregates the Bayesian workflow (posterior predictive checks, simulation-based calibration, sampler diagnostics, held-out predictive density) into a "calibration oracle" that detects misspecification with AUC 0.97 and, used as feedback in an LLM repair loop across 15 models, beats unit-test feedback — which is itself *significantly worse than no feedback* because passing tests induce false confidence.

## 核心贡献

- **提出"校准预言机"（Calibration Oracle）：** 用完整的贝叶斯工作流——后验预测检查（PPC）、模拟校准（SBC）、采样器几何诊断（split-R̂、发散、ESS）、留出预测密度（lppd）——替代单元测试，作为判断概率程序统计正确性的真正验证器。形式化为 `O(M,D) = 1[ran] ∧ 1[R̂<1.05] ∧ 1[#div≤15] ∧ 1[min pT ≥ α] ∧ 1[lppd ≥ lppd*−δ]`（α=0.01, δ=0.15 nats）。
- **系统化错误指定基准：** 覆盖 10 个模型族、14 种错误指定类型、共 200 个实例（全部可编译运行）；校准预言机检测率达 88%（代码不可见 bug 93%），FPR 2%，AUC 0.97，而单元测试检测率为 0%。
- **揭示单元测试的"有害性"：** 证明通过的单元测试会制造虚假信心、抑制修复——在修复循环中，单元测试反馈显著**差于无反馈**（Claude: 25% vs 75%）。
- **校准反馈的修复优势：** 在 15 个模型上，校准反馈对每一个能利用它的模型都是最强信号；强但未饱和模型提升巨大（GPT-5.1 33→92%，Claude 75→100%；配对 McNemar 检验显著）。
- **真实 LLM 程序验证：** LLM 从零编写的程序中，15–47% 存在统计错误指定（单元测试一个也检测不到）；校准修复以 84% 显著优于 LLM-as-judge（71%）、贝叶斯检查清单（63%）、数据摘要自调试（46%）和无反馈（46%）。

## Key Contributions

- **Introduces a calibration oracle** — the Bayesian workflow aggregated into a single verdict `O(M,D)` — as the proper verifier for probabilistic programs, replacing unit tests. Correct programs are a fixpoint of the oracle's feedback map (Remark 1: do-no-harm).
- **A controlled misspecification benchmark:** 14 misspecification types × 10 model families × 10 instances (200 total), all compiling and running. Achieves 88% detection (93% on code-invisible bugs) at 2% FPR (AUC 0.97), versus **0% for the unit-test oracle**.
- **Shows unit tests are actively harmful** in repair loops: passing tests induce false confidence and suppress repair, making test feedback significantly *worse than no feedback* (Claude 25% vs 75%; McNemar p=3.4×10⁻³).
- **Calibration-guided repair** is the best signal at every capability level able to use it, with the largest gains for strong-but-unsaturated models (GPT-5.1 33→92%, Claude 75→100%; pooled paired McNemar, n=228).
- **Reveals real-world misspecification:** 15–47% of runnable LLM-written programs are statistically wrong (unit tests catch none); calibration repair (84%) significantly beats LLM-as-judge, a Bayesian-workflow checklist, and data-summary self-debug (all p<0.05).
- **Component ablations & bug localization:** held-out density (59%) and PPC (56%) are the strongest single signals; SBC alone is nearly useless for misspecification (5%). The oracle localizes bug *type* with 61% top-1 / 77% top-2 accuracy.

## Methodology

The paper formalizes a task as `(b, D_tr, D_te)` — a natural-language brief plus data from an unknown process `p⋆`. A candidate program `M` defines a joint density; it is *statistically correct* iff its posterior predictive is indistinguishable from `p⋆` under chosen discrepancies, else *misspecified*. Two programs can be syntactically near-identical yet encode different `p_M` (e.g., Poisson vs. NegativeBinomial differ by one token).

**The calibration oracle `O(M, D)`** aggregates four families of Bayesian-workflow diagnostics:
- *Posterior predictive checks (PPC):* tail probabilities `pT` over test statistics {mean, sd, max, #zeros}; values near 0/1 flag aspects the model cannot reproduce.
- *Simulation-based calibration (SBC):* rank-uniformity of prior draws among posterior draws; flags inference/specification error.
- *Sampler geometry:* split-R̂ (<1.05), bulk-ESS, divergences (≤15) — detect pathological posterior geometry (e.g., hierarchical funnel).
- *Held-out predictive density (lppd):* a strictly proper scoring rule; misspecified models have strictly lower expected lppd (Gibbs' inequality).

**Bug taxonomy — visible vs. invisible.** *Code-visible* bugs are diagnosable from source under a neutral brief (hard-coded tiny noise, absurd prior, missing link); *code-invisible* bugs are faithful standard implementations that are wrong *for this data* (Gaussian for heavy tails, Poisson for over-dispersed counts, centered parameterization for a funnel). The invisible class is exactly where tests are powerless (Proposition 1) and calibration retains power (Proposition 2).

**Repair loop (Algorithm 1):** the LLM drafts a program, NUTS fits it, the oracle is queried; if not calibrated, a structured diagnostic `ϕ` (naming which statistic is misfit, in plain language, without prescribing the fix) is fed back, up to budget K=3. Three feedback maps compared: `none` (self-refine), `test` (unit-test verdict), `diag` (calibration diagnostics).

**Setup:** 15 writers (DeepSeek, Qwen-Coder, Llama, Phi, GPT, Claude, Gemini); NUTS 2 chains, 600+600 draws (or 800+800); jax x64; fixed PRNG seeds; temp 0.7; ≤1300 output tokens; API cost <US$60; pinned model snapshots; system prompts/templates released. All within-model comparisons (feedback A vs B on identical programs) are invariant to absolute capability.

## Results & Highlights

- **Detection (Table 1, three-tier oracle):** reference-free (PPC+sampler+SBC) catches **62%** (57% invisible) vs **0%** for unit tests; adding a trivial GLM baseline → 68%; an automated model-search (LOO over a small model library) → **78%** (79% invisible); the reference oracle (upper bound, given correct program) → **88%** (93% invisible), 2% FPR, AUC **0.97**. A threshold chosen on one half generalizes to 80% at 1% FPR on a disjoint half.
- **Detection by type (Figure 2):** essentially perfect for likelihood, dispersion, zero-inflation, support, parameterization, transformation, missing-state, missing-covariate errors; hard cases are stochastic volatility (56%) and the visible identity/clamped link error (26%).
- **Component ablation (Table 3):** held-out lppd 59%, PPC 56%, sampler diagnostics 6%, **SBC alone only 5%** (SBC tests self-consistency with the model's own prior, so an internally-coherent-but-wrong model passes it). Full oracle 88%.
- **Bug localization (Figure 4):** 61% top-1, 77% top-2 bug-type prediction; errors concentrate in the shared "under-dispersed predictive" symptom (over-dispersion / invalid support / prior–data conflict mutually confused).
- **Repair across 15 models (Table 2, Figure 3):** calibration (`diag`) is the best signal at every usable capability level. Strong-but-unsaturated gains: GPT-5.1 33→92% (+59), Claude 75→100% (+25), Gemini-3.1-pro +25. Pooled paired McNemar (n=228): diag vs none p=4.4×10⁻³; diag vs test p=6.5×10⁻⁷.
- **Unit-test feedback is harmful:** test ≤ none for capable models (Claude 25% vs 75%; GPT-5.5 75% vs 83%; GPT-4.1 58% vs 67%); none vs test McNemar p=3.4×10⁻³.
- **Non-monotone capability effect:** diagnostic advantage ≈0 for models too weak to act on diagnostics (DeepSeek-6.7B, Qwen-7B, GPT-4o-mini) and for models that already repair blindly (GPT-5.5, DeepSeek-V4-flash).
- **Real LLM programs (Table 4):** 79–89% run; of those, 15–47% are statistically misspecified; unit tests flag **none**.
- **Real-program repair (Table 5, Figure 5):** pooled over 7 capable writers (n=98), calibration repairs **84%** vs LLM-as-judge 71%, checklist 63%, data-summary self-debug 46%, no feedback 46%, unit-test 36%. Significant against every baseline (McNemar; vs test p=2×10⁻¹¹, vs none p=9.3×10⁻⁹).
- **Feedback design ablation:** replacing raw numbers with a plain-language reading lifts Qwen2.5-Coder-32B from 33%→50%, but cannot substitute for the modeling knowledge weak models lack (7B coder stays at 0%).

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: High.** The conceptual reframing — "correctness is calibration, not compilation" for LLM-written probabilistic programs — and the finding that unit tests are not merely insufficient but *actively counterproductive* are genuinely novel and well-motivated. The three-tier oracle (reference-free → GLM baseline → model-search → reference upper bound) is a thoughtful practical contribution.
- **Rigor: Strong.** Controlled benchmark with clean labels; honest separation of upper-bound (reference) and deployable (reference-free) results; paired McNemar tests with explicit n and p-values; multiple baselines of escalating strength (LLM-as-judge, checklist, self-debug); threshold generalization across disjoint data halves. The authors are unusually forthright about the 88% figure being an upper bound.
- **Reproducibility: Good.** Concrete settings (NUTS config, thresholds, seeds, temperature, token budget), pinned model snapshots, <$60 API cost, and a stated commitment to release prompts/contract/feedback templates/code. Caveat: full reproducibility still hinges on the benchmark catalog and calibration-pipeline code being public.
- **Significance: Potentially high** for probabilistic-programming and LLM-coding communities; reframes how statistical-code correctness should be judged and gives a deployable recipe.
- **Red flags / caveats:**
  - The headline AUC 0.97 / 88% is an *upper bound* requiring the correct reference program; the realistic reference-free figure is 62–78%.
  - Repair experiments use an 8-bug subset with 3 seeds/cell — per-cell rates are noisy; the authors rightly lean on pooled tests and the larger real-program study.
  - Correctness is judged against a known reference with *fixed thresholds*, not full SBC inside the repair loop (SBC used for detection only).
  - The real-world "15–47%" range is broad and depends on the chosen neutral briefs.
  - Heavy reliance on frontier snapshots (GPT-5.1/5.5, Claude Sonnet 4.6, Gemini-3.1) whose behavior may drift.
  - Scope limited to classical low-dimensional models; NUTS fails to mix on the correct mixture/BNN (excluded from headline FPR), so the reported 2% FPR is a *lower bound* on harder model classes.

## Strengths

- Memorable, well-argued thesis backed by both theory (two propositions on test-blindness vs. calibration-consistency) and extensive empirics.
- Honest three-tier framing of the oracle (reference-free → practical → upper bound) rather than reporting only the best number.
- Surprising, actionable finding that unit-test feedback is worse than no feedback — important for agentic-coding system design.
- Strong, escalating baselines (LLM-as-judge, checklist, data-summary self-debug) on *real* LLM-written programs, not just injected bugs.
- Non-monotone capability analysis gives a clear, honest deployment guideline (pair strong-but-unsaturated writers with calibration feedback).
- PPL-agnostic claim is credible (R̂, divergences, ESS, PPC, SBC, lppd are first-class in Stan/PyMC/Pyro/NumPyro).

## Limitations

- Reference-free detection (the deployable setting) tops out at 62–78% and the upper figure needs an automated model search.
- SBC is impractical in high dimensions (requires many refits); PPC/held-out density become the workhorses, with limited power against statistics one doesn't track (e.g., the clamped/identity link error at 26%).
- Predictive checks test *distributional* fit, not causal/generative structure — two programs with identical posteriors but different causal mechanisms are indistinguishable.
- Inference failure (poor NUTS mixing) masquerades as misspecification, producing false alarms — a fundamental confound for hard-to-fit families.
- Diagnosis without remedy: weak writers receive a correct diagnostic but cannot act on it (Qwen-7B stays at 0% even with interpretive feedback).
- Tasks are classical low-dimensional models; extension to high-dimensional/structured (spatial, deep) models is future work.

## Relevance

本文对"用 LLM 自动生成/审查统计建模代码"方向高度相关，揭示了传统代码评测（编译+单元测试）在概率程序上的系统性失效，并提供了一套可直接落地的"贝叶斯工作流即验证器"的检测与修复范式。对于关注 LLM 代码生成可靠性、自动化科学计算验证以及智能体（agentic）反馈设计的研究者有直接参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How robust is the reference-free detector to model families outside the 10 studied — e.g., hierarchical with many groups, non-conjugate, discrete-latent-variable, or spatial models?
- Can SBC be scaled into the repair loop for high-dimensional models (e.g., via cheaper SBC approximations or SMC), and does it add value beyond PPC + held-out density?
- Does the "test feedback is harmful" phenomenon generalize to other statistical/scientific code (e.g., causal-inference pipelines, simulation code), or is it specific to probabilistic programs?
- How stable are the strong-but-unsaturated gains (GPT-5.1 +59, Claude +25) across prompt paraphrases and newer model versions?
- Can passing unit tests be *recombined* with calibration signals (rather than replaced) to recover their cheap signal without the false-confidence trap?
- What statistics beyond {mean, sd, max, #zeros} would close the gap on the clamped/identity link and stochastic-volatility blind spots?
