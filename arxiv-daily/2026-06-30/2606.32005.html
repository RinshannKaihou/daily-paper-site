Random Reshuffling Dominates Stochastic Gradient Descent

Zijian Liu · Stern School of Business, New York University

02 Why This Matters

Theoretically clarifies why the default RR strategy strictly beats classical SGD in smooth convex optimization — directly guiding stepsize choices and providing the foundation for extensions to nonconvex / deep-learning settings.

03 Critical Edge

“Dominates” is an upper-bound comparison only — no matching lower bound exists, and leading constants (6, 51) are explicitly unoptimized. The result is convex-only, in-expectation, averaged iterate; the nonconvex deep-learning regime remains open.

Strongest caveat · not yet tight

Mechanism & Results

Methodology

Problem. Minimize finite-sum convex objective f(x) = (1/n)Σᵢ fᵢ(x). Assumptions are minimal — convexity, per-component Li-smoothness, and existence of a minimizer. No bounded-variance, no Lipschitz-gradient assumptions.

RR: each epoch draw πk ~ Sₙ uniformly;
run n gradient steps in that order;
output weighted average x̄K.

The intellectual core. Theorem 1 fuses two independent bounds:

  • Bound I — “never worse than SGD.” First multi-epoch RR rate converging for any η ≲ 1/L̂ without extra assumptions.
  • Bound II — “strictly faster when stepsize is small.” Provably beats SGD when η ≲ 1/(nL̄).

Lemma 5 (the novelty): a new inequality bounding E[f(xi)] − E[fπᵢ(xi)] by the second moment of stochastic gradients — using a virtual-sequence + co-coercivity argument (Sherman et al. 2021) that replaces the Wasserstein machinery and drops the Lipschitz assumption.

  • Any reasonable stepsize. Valid for η ≲ 1/L̂ — the first analysis to permit the practical regime η ≳ 1/n.
  • Never worse; strictly better. Dominates SGD throughout; strictly faster when η ≲ 1/(nL̄).
  • Both bottlenecks removed. No stepsize ceiling O(1/n), and no “epoch count K > Θ(n)” prerequisite.
  • Shared minimizer (σ⋆² = 0): L̂D²/(nK) — an n-fold gain over prior RR bounds.
  • Order comparison: OrderNewRR(K) ≤ OrderSGD(K) for all K, with strict dominance in the relevant regimes.
§ 03

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

what lands

  • Cleanly resolves a named, longstanding open question with a precise affirmative answer.
  • Removes two restrictive conditions simultaneously — stepsize ceiling and epoch-count threshold.
  • Drops the Lipschitz assumption and Wasserstein machinery; broadens applicability (e.g., to quadratics).
  • More general setup: nonuniform smoothness, no bounded-variance assumption.
  • Clear two-bound proof architecture with core lemmas stated up front.
  • Vetted at COLT 2026 by learning-theory experts.

Limitations

what to watch

  • Convex-only. The nonconvex deep-learning regime is not covered — remains open.
  • In-expectation, averaged iterate. No last-iterate or high-probability guarantee.
  • Upper-bound dominance. No matching lower bound under η ≲ 1/L̂, arbitrary K.
  • Loose constants; split proof. Author states constants are unoptimized; proof not unified.
  • No experiments. Pure theory; no empirical confirmation of predicted regimes.
  • Confirmatory in practice. RR is already standard; the work justifies rather than changes deployment.
§ 04

Open Questions

  1. Can dominance extend to nonconvex smooth objectives (the deep-learning regime), e.g. under PL / KL / gradient-domination conditions?
  2. Is there a matching lower bound for RR under η ≲ 1/L̂ and arbitrary finite K, confirming the new rate is information-theoretically tight?
  3. Can the two-part proof be unified into a single analysis (an open direction the author explicitly raises)?
  4. How do the loose constants (6, 51) tighten under sharper analysis, and do predicted regime boundaries match empirical RR-vs-SGD behavior?
  5. Does the virtual-sequence + co-coercivity technique (Lemma 5) transfer to last-iterate or high-probability guarantees, or to variance-reduced shuffling methods?
