01

Methodology — how it actually works

A single Orchestrator (a Claude Code session) takes PDF + LaTeX + a natural-language prompt and returns Lean code. It manages two pipelines and can reorder stages, open feedback bridges, and roll back on failure — deliberately blending structure with non-linear flexibility. Backing model: Claude Opus 4.7 (PutnamBench) / 4.8 (STOC).

Core novelty — Auxiliary Lemma ("unit test" for definitions): for every new type, generate general property lemmas and try to prove them. If they fail, the definition is wrong — retry before any theorem work. This guards foundational correctness at the source.
02

Results & the numbers

What was achieved

PutnamBench: 32/32 on a random sample (internet disabled, no prepared proofs) → ≥91.3% lower bound over all 672 problems at 95% CI.

5 STOC papers fully formalized and machine-checked — Pham; Mackenzie & Saffidine; Gravin & Jia; Rivkin et al. (2025); Kalai et al. (2010) — spanning combinatorics, communication complexity, mechanism design, info-theoretic lower bounds, learning theory.

Scientific finding: while formalizing Pham (STOC 2025), the pipeline uncovered a computer-verified gap — Lemma 3.6 is false as stated (explicit counterexample on a legitimate non-p-small family), so the surjection Ψᵤ is not surjective. The theorem may still hold; only this proof step fails, admitted as a single scoped axiom.

Total cost: ~$1,000 over 5 months on a $200/mo plan (≈$10,762 at API list rates). No local GPUs.

04

Strengths vs. Limitations

Strengths

  • Tackles genuinely hard research-level math (STOC theorems), not just competition/textbook problems.
  • Auxiliary Lemma technique is an elegant, transferable idea — software unit-testing discipline applied to mathematical definitions.
  • Orchestrator with dynamic backtracking + structured-failure feedback is a principled advance over rigid fixed pipelines.
  • Publicly released, machine-checked formalizations + the Pham gap-finding give concrete verifiable scientific value.
  • Two fully axiom-free STOC formalizations signal strong faithfulness discipline.
  • Cross-domain evaluation (Putnam + 5 theory areas) + a compelling cost/accessibility story.

Limitations

  • Algorithmic results not formalized (correctness + complexity) — deferred to future work.
  • Closed-source backing model (Claude) — no control, subject to change, hampers exact reproduction.
  • Small-scale benchmarking (n=32) yields a lower bound, not a full PutnamBench solve rate.
  • "Human-expert validation" asserted without a documented review protocol.
  • Cost advantage hinges on a flat-rate subscription that may not persist; metered equivalent ~$10,762.
  • Only main theorems of curated papers are targeted, not entire papers.
05

Open questions for further work

  1. How does success vary across PutnamBench sub-domains (algebra, analysis, combinatorics, number theory)?
  2. What is the true full-benchmark (all 672) solve rate — does the 91.3% lower bound hold?
  3. How sensitive are results to backbone (Claude vs. GPT-5.x vs. open-weight), temperature, and repeated runs?
  4. Does Auxiliary Lemma scale as definitions grow complex (measure/sheaf-theoretic), or does cost explode?
  5. What are the dominant failure modes, and what fraction of nodes need human intervention?
  6. Can it extend to entire papers (all theorems + algorithmic correctness/complexity)?
  7. Can the cost advantage survive the move off a flat-rate subscription — sustainable price at metered rates?
  8. For the Pham gap: has the theorem since been re-proved, and does the counterexample generalize to related fractional-cover arguments?
