> **一句话总结：** 该论文提出了"替代保真度"（surrogate fidelity）框架，用于衡量用开源LLM的内部信号能否解释闭源模型；通过对11个模型（Llama/Qwen/GPT/Gemini）在二分类任务上的预测、归因、表示三层评估，发现预测一致性（Fpred）远高于归因一致性（Fattr），白盒信号（注意力、扰动幅度）虽跨模型稳定却几乎无法预测因果归因，存在"访问有效性倒置"——可用API测得的归因信号反而比白盒信号更具因果相关性。

> **TL;DR:** This paper introduces "surrogate fidelity"—a framework asking whether mechanistic interpretability (MI) measurements on open LLMs can validly explain closed models accessible only via log-probability APIs. Across 11 models (Llama, Qwen, GPT, Gemini) on binary classification tasks, prediction fidelity consistently overstates attribution fidelity: models agree on *what* the answer is but disagree on *why*. White-box signals (attention, perturbation magnitude) are stable across models yet uncorrelated with causal attributions—an "access-validity inversion" where the API-compatible black-box ablations are the most causally relevant signals.

## 核心贡献

- **提出"替代保真度"（Surrogate Fidelity）概念框架**：将"用开源模型解释闭源模型"这一隐含假设正式化为可测量问题，并要求遵循"最小权限原则"（principle of least privilege）——衡量信号所需的模型访问权限应与所做声明要求的访问权限匹配。
- **构建分层保真度指标体系**：预测保真度（Fpred）、归因保真度（Fattr）、表示保真度（Frepr：含 Fmag 扰动幅度、Falign 对齐方向、Fattn 注意力）、跨层保真度（Fcross）。其中 Fpred 与 Fattr 仅需 top-K 对数概率，可对闭源API计算。
- **揭示"预测保真度夸大归因保真度"现象**：例如 BoolQ 词级消融中 Fpred=0.708 而 Fattr 仅 0.175，二者差距巨大。
- **发现并解释"访问有效性倒置"（access-validity inversion）**：注意力模式（Fmean_attn 中位 0.905）与扰动幅度（Fmag 中位 0.855）跨模型高度稳定，但对因果归因的预测力极弱（Fmean_attn→attr 中位仅 0.003）；真正能预测因果归因的是黑盒输入消融，而这些恰恰不需要内部访问。
- **给出根因的几何解释**：归因 ℓ 分解为扰动幅度 ‖Δz‖ 与对齐 cos(Δz,v)，模型在幅度上趋同但在方向（Falign 中位仅 0.200）上分歧严重；约15–23%归因来自 LayerNorm 重缩放，且其方向与归因几乎不相关。
- **跨4家族11模型、5基准验证**（BoolQ/ANLI/WinoGrande/LAMBADA/RACE），结论具有跨家族与跨任务普适性。
- **面向实践者的落地建议**：替代有效性必须测量而非假设，应在与拟做声明相对应的层级上验证（Fpred/Fattr 仅需log-probs，Fcross 需开源权重）。

## Key Contributions

- **Formalizes the surrogate fidelity problem for mechanistic interpretability**: reframes the pervasive but under-examined assumption that MI findings transfer across models, governed by a "principle of least privilege"—the access required to measure a signal should match the access the claim being evaluated demands.
- **Defines a hierarchy of fidelity metrics**: prediction (Fpred), attribution (Fattr), representation (Frepr: Fmag, Falign, Fattn), and cross-level (Fcross). Fpred and Fattr require only top-K log-probabilities and are thus computable for closed API models; Frepr/Fcross additionally require open-weight access.
- **Demonstrates prediction fidelity overstates attribution fidelity**: e.g., on BoolQ word-level ablations, Fpred = 0.708 while Fattr = 0.175—a striking dissociation where models agree on outputs but disagree on causal drivers.
- **Identifies and explains the "access-validity inversion"**: highly stable white-box signals (mean attention r² 0.762–0.984; perturbation magnitude Fmag median 0.855) are nearly uncorrelated with causal attributions (Fmean_attn→attr median 0.003). The API-compatible black-box ablations are paradoxically the most causally relevant signals.
- **Provides a geometric root-cause analysis**: attribution decomposes into perturbation magnitude ‖Δz‖ and alignment cos(Δz, v); models converge on magnitude but diverge sharply on direction (Falign median only 0.200). ~15–23% of attribution is attributable to LayerNorm rescaling, whose direction is uncorrelated with attribution.
- **Validates across 4 families / 11 models / 5 benchmarks** (BoolQ, ANLI R1/R2/R3, WinoGrande, LAMBADA, RACE), establishing cross-family and cross-task generality.
- **Delivers concrete practitioner guidance**: surrogate validity must be measured, not assumed; validate at the level of the claim (Fpred/Fattr need only log-probs; Fcross needs surrogate open-weights). No surrogate-side representation signal in their evaluation clears the cross-level bar.

## Methodology

**Problem framing.** The authors observe a bifurcation in MI: classic tools (circuit analysis, sparse autoencoders, activation patching) require full internals, yet frontier models (GPT, Gemini) expose at most top-K log-probabilities. They ask when measurements on an open *surrogate* model MS license claims about a closed *target* model MT.

**Key geometric insight.** For binary classification {y+, y−}, the log-odds ℓ(x) = z·v are *exactly linear* in the residual-stream representation z, where v = u+ − u− is the difference of unembedding columns. Critically, log-odds are recoverable from top-K log-probabilities (a difference cancels the softmax normalizer C that is otherwise unrecoverable), making them both API-accessible and geometrically interpretable.

**Attribution via ablation.** Under an input intervention that perturbs z → z′, the attribution Δℓ = ℓ(z) − ℓ(z′) = Δz·v decomposes into three factors: Δℓ = ‖Δz‖·‖v‖·cos(Δz, v). Attribution is governed by perturbation magnitude ‖Δz‖ and alignment with the readout direction. The framework is intervention-agnostic; experiments use prompt-level leave-one-out ablations (sentence- and word-level) because they are black-box/API-compatible. The residual-stream decomposition (logit lens, direct logit attribution) and the role of LayerNorm/RMSNorm (direct vs. indirect rescaling terms) are worked out formally.

**Fidelity metrics** (all measured as Pearson r² with bootstrapped 95% CIs):
- Fpred = r²(ℓ_MS, ℓ_MT) — output agreement.
- Fattr = r²(Δℓ_MS, Δℓ_MT) — causal-importance agreement under leave-one-out ablation.
- Frepr: Fmag = r²(‖Δz‖), Falign = r²(cos(Δz,v)), Fattn = r²(attention scores, with mean/max/rollout aggregation).
- Fcross = r²(α_MS, Δℓ_MT) — whether surrogate internals predict target attributions.

The choice of r² is justified via "readout-compatible linear transfer": if MS, MT are related by a linear transform z_T ≈ A·z_S with Aᵀv_T ≈ c·v_S, then ℓ_T ≈ c·ℓ_S, so r² measures variance explained under the simplest such calibration. Connections to RV coefficient and CKA are noted.

**Models (11, 4 families):** Llama 3.1-8B/70B, 3.3-70B, 4 Maverick; Qwen 2.5-0.5B/3B/7B/14B; GPT-4o, GPT-4.1; Gemini 2.5 Flash Lite. (Claude/GPT-5 excluded—no logprob access.)

**Benchmarks:** BoolQ (n=3,270), ANLI R1/R2/R3, WinoGrande (n=1,267), plus LAMBADA (last-token log-prob) and RACE (4-choice, RV coefficient).

## Results & Highlights

- **Prediction fidelity is high within family, lower across.** Within-family pairs are high: Llama 3.1-70B vs 3.3-70B Fpred = 0.910; Qwen-7B vs 14B = 0.796; GPT-4o vs 4.1 = 0.678. Highest cross-family Fpred = 0.684 (Llama 3.1-70B vs Qwen 14B). Qwen-0.5B is an outlier with low fidelity to everything.
- **Attribution fidelity is strikingly low everywhere.** Median within-family Fattr is only 0.432 (BoolQ sentence-level); even the closest pair (Qwen-7B vs 14B) reaches only 0.560. Word-level ablation *collapses* Fattr (0.460 → 0.175) driven by Falign collapse (0.200 → 0.012), while representational signals (attention) stay flat.
- **The access-validity inversion is stark.** From Table 1: Fmean_attn (0.762–0.984) and Fmag (0.641–0.903) are high, yet their cross-level predictive power for attribution is near zero (Fmean_attn→attr median 0.003; Frollout_attn→attr median 0.002). Models share attention patterns while diverging in causal structure.
- **Root cause: directional divergence.** Models agree on perturbation magnitude (high Fmag) but disagree on direction (low Falign, median 0.200). Max cos(Δz, v) tops out ~0.4, mostly near zero. LayerNorm rescaling accounts for ~15–23% of attribution but is directionally uncorrelated with it.
- **Depth dissociation.** Per-layer analysis shows high representation-level agreement across most depths, but low attribution-level agreement for the first ~55% of depth with a sharp late rise. A random-direction control also rises late, so the v-specific premium at the readout layer is only ~0.29 r²—partial qualification of the "disagree on what drives predictions" claim.
- **Cross-benchmark robustness.** The qualitative ordering (Fpred > Fattr; attention stable; Fmag > Falign) holds across BoolQ, ANLI, WinoGrande, LAMBADA, RACE. Notably, ANLI difficulty (R1<R2<R3) shows no monotonic relationship with fidelity. RACE Fattr (0.524) modestly exceeds Fpred (0.462).
- **Practical takeaway:** Fpred is a useful but systematically over-optimistic screen; report both Fpred and Fattr when making surrogate claims; Fcross is the right test for open→closed mechanism transfer, and no surrogate signal in their evaluation passes it.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: High.** The "surrogate fidelity" framing, the four-level metric hierarchy, and the "access-validity inversion" are genuinely new conceptual contributions. The counterintuitive finding that *more* model access yields *less* causally valid signal is sharp and memorable. The geometric decomposition of attribution (magnitude vs. alignment vs. LayerNorm) is a clean explanatory lens.
- **Rigor: Strong, with honest caveats.** The log-odds-as-linear-projection trick is elegant and well-justified; r² is motivated via readout-compatible linear transfer and connected to RV/CKA. The authors include a *random-direction control* that partially qualifies their own headline claim (the late-depth Fattr rise is partly generic), which is commendable intellectual honesty. Bootstrapped CIs are reported throughout.
- **Reproducibility: Likely strong.** Public code + results; open-weight models used are standard. The main external dependency is closed-model logprob APIs, which may drift/change over time (a real but unavoidable limitation).
- **Significance: Potentially field-shaping.** The paper directly challenges the implicit, pervasive assumption that open-model MI transfers to frontier closed models—a foundational assumption for academic MI work aimed at GPT/Gemini. It also adds quantitative fuel to the long-standing "attention is not explanation" debate by showing attention is stable-but-non-causal.
- **Red flags / caveats:**
  1. *Scope.* Binary classification is a deliberately narrow setting (chosen for clean log-odds readouts). The authors flag that extension to open-ended generation—where most LLM use happens—is future work requiring new scoring functions. The headline "models disagree on why" is established only for saliency/feature-attribution claims, not circuit-level claims.
  2. *Attribution-method dependence.* All causal claims hinge on leave-one-out input ablation. The authors acknowledge this trades mechanistic depth for API compatibility; activation patching, steering vectors, or SAE features could tell a different story (and they note SAE cross-seed inconsistency might imply similarly low representation fidelity).
  3. *Workshop-paper posture.* It is explicitly a "first proposal" inviting refinement—some metrics/choices (attention aggregation scheme sensitivity, r² vs. NRMSE) are acknowledged as unsettled.
  4. *The negative result dominates.* The paper is strong on diagnosing *failure* of transfer but offers little on *conditions for success*. Practitioners are told to "measure rather than assume," but not given predictors of when transfer will work.

## Strengths

- Sharp, well-named conceptual contributions ("surrogate fidelity," "access-validity inversion," "principle of least privilege") that are easy to communicate and act upon.
- The log-odds-as-linear-projection insight elegantly unifies API-accessibility with geometric interpretability.
- Broad empirical coverage (11 models, 4 families, 5 benchmarks) and the inclusion of genuinely closed targets (GPT-4o/4.1, Gemini) via logprob APIs—not just open models.
- Intellectual honesty: random-direction controls, LayerNorm confound analysis, and explicit acknowledgment of scope limitations.
- Practitioner-oriented: concrete guidance on which metrics to report and at what access level.
- Openly released code and results (facebookresearch/surrogate).

## Limitations

- **Narrow task scope.** Binary classification is clean but unrepresentative of generative LLM use; the authors flag open-ended generation as a hard, unsolved extension (no obvious scalar summary).
- **Attribution-method dependence.** Conclusions are tied to leave-one-out input ablation; sensitivity to activation patching, steering vectors, or SAE features is untested. Ablations may also shift inputs off-distribution with unintended effects.
- **Closed-model API dependence.** Requires logprob access, which excludes Claude and GPT-5 entirely and may change as providers update APIs.
- **Predominantly diagnostic.** Documents the failure of surrogate transfer thoroughly but offers little on *when* transfer succeeds or how to construct valid surrogate explanations—leaving practitioners with a measuring stick but no cure.
- **Attention-aggregation sensitivity.** Mean/max attention correlate moderately, but rollout attention is nearly uncorrelated with them—so "attention is stable" depends on the aggregation choice, complicating the headline.
- **Representational vs. causal framing.** The low Falign could reflect genuine divergence *or* freedom to shuffle operations within polytope-lens consistency regions; the paper discusses this but cannot fully disentangle it.

## Relevance

该论文对关注大模型可解释性与可信度评估的研究者高度相关，它系统性地揭示了"用开源模型解释闭源模型"这一常见实践的有效性边界，并提出了API兼容、可直接计算的评估指标。对于依赖GPT/Gemini等闭源模型做归因分析或安全审计的实际场景，本文提供了关键的方法论警示与可操作的验证框架——在做任何跨模型解释性声明前，应至少同时报告 Fpred 与 Fattr。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Under what conditions (task type, model family/similarity, attribution method) does surrogate fidelity recover? Are there reliable *predictors* of when transfer will work, beyond measuring Fattr directly?
- Do the findings generalize beyond binary classification to open-ended generation, multi-step reasoning chains, and safety-relevant behaviors where no scalar log-odds summary exists?
- How sensitive are the conclusions to the attribution method? Would activation patching, steering vectors, or SAE features change the access-validity inversion, or reinforce it?
- Could richer API-compatible interventions (meaning-preserving perturbations, ContextCite-style attribution) yield *partially* causally valid mechanistic accounts of closed models?
- Does alignment/distillation of an open model toward a closed model's behavior improve *attribution* fidelity, or only *prediction* fidelity (mimicry without mechanistic alignment)?
- Is the access-validity inversion specific to instruction fine-tuning? The authors hint log-prob divergence between true/false prompts appears only post-instruction-tuning—does the inversion vanish for base models?
- Can the geometric decomposition (magnitude vs. alignment vs. LayerNorm) predict, ahead of time, which model pairs will exhibit low Fattr without running expensive ablations?
