A rare large-scale public deployment with auditable artifacts and expert human review — mapping the real capability ceiling and typical failure modes of fully autonomous research systems.
For anyone tracking automated science, LLM agents, and “AI doing AI research,” this is direct evidence on where the field actually stands.

03 Methodology

FARS is a multi-agent system run as four sequential stages sharing one workspace that doubles as project memory and an auditable artifact store. The unit of production is a focused contribution — a clear hypothesis plus a validation attempt, with negative results treated as first-class when limits are explicit.

01Ideation
Four sub-agents (Lead, Summarization, adversarial Peer-Discussion, Evaluation) drive a six-step pipeline over a three-tier knowledge base. Seeded with 9 directions but free to explore; circuit-breaker picks best draft on repeated failure.
02Planning
Turns a proposal into a machine-readable experiment contract: a two-level hierarchy across 5 task categories, decomposed into ordered executable steps — countering shallow single-pass attempts and premature completion claims.
03Experiment
Orchestration vs. execution split; an effectiveness-evaluation gate advances only if the main claim is supported (preserving negative outcomes). Dual verification + a 160-GPU layered environment, 19-category skill library, checkpoint fault-tolerance.
04Writing
Analysis Agent builds a paper blueprint linking each claim to supporting experiments and artifacts; Writing Agent drafts LaTeX section by section. Plots run over saved outputs; diagrams refined with vision-language feedback (NanoBanana-Pro).

Human review protocol. Open call → 583 applicants → 88 selected reviewers (82% had reviewed >20 papers). 55 reviewers produced 282 structured reviews on 140 papers using an ICLR-style rubric plus an 8-category AI Integrity Audit, with full access to each paper's code, logs, and raw outputs.

05 Strengths & limitations

◆ Strengths
  • Genuine end-to-end autonomy across a wide topic space — not a few hand-picked tasks.
  • Engineering that targets known agent failure modes: experiment contract, dual verification, effectiveness-gating.
  • Auditable intermediate artifacts retained for every project, enabling external verification over trust.
  • Structured external human review with an artifact-grounded integrity audit and LLM-use disclosure — a high bar for this subfield.
  • Transparent, quantitative disclosure of failure modes, cost, and the gap between scalable generation and reliable contribution.
  • Public release of papers, reviews, and the full artifact trace.
◆ Limitations
  • Inconsistent quality: mean 3.23/10; only ~2% of multi-review papers reach acceptance.
  • 27.9% of reviewed papers flagged for integrity violations; fabrication/hallucination present though usually caught.
  • Experimental sufficiency is the dominant weakness — affecting even the best papers (100%).
  • Self-selected volunteer reviewers with self-claimed assignments; 26 papers unreviewed; 74.5% of reviews used LLMs.
  • Demonstrated only in AI/ML; physical-experiment, proprietary-data, or long-proof domains unproven.
  • $186K / 160-GPU cost and unclear release of prompts & skill-library contents limit replication.

06 Questions for further exploration

Q1

How were the 9 seed topics chosen, and is the 72-seed / 94-emergent split sensitive to that seeding — or are “emergent” topics just recombinations?

Q2

At only ~2% acceptance for multi-review papers, is per-paper yield enough to justify $1,120/paper — or is the real value the auditable corpus of failures?

Q3

Are agent prompts, the 19-category skill library, and exact configurations publicly released to enable independent reproduction?

Q4

How much of the 27.9% integrity-failure rate is stage-specific (Experiment self-reporting vs. Writing), and would a cross-stage claim-verification loop cut it?

Q5

With 74.5% of reviews LLM-assisted, how sensitive are the score distribution and integrity-flag rate to LLM use — is there a non-LLM control subset?

Q6

Can the experiment-contract + dual-verification design port to non-AI domains (e.g. wet-lab biology), and would the effectiveness-evaluation gate still be meaningful?
