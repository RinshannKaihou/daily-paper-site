> **一句话总结：** Cohere 与 LG CNS 联合推出 LuckyStar 111B，一个基于已完成后训练的 Command A 模型、面向韩英企业级工具调用智能体的 111B 混合推理模型；通过"混合 SFT → RLVR → DPO"三阶段适配、英文推理/韩文作答的混合语言策略、语言一致性奖励以及 4-bit 量化，在单张 H100 上即可部署，并在数学推理、函数调用与 NL2SQL 上取得大幅提升。

> **TL;DR:** LuckyStar 111B is a 111B-parameter bilingual (Korean–English) hybrid-reasoning agent model jointly built by Cohere and LG CNS. Adapted from the fully post-trained Command A via a three-stage pipeline (hybrid SFT → RLVR → DPO), it uses preamble conditioning to switch between concise and reasoning modes, a "think in English, answer in Korean" multilingual strategy, a language-consistency reward penalty, and 4-bit quantization to deploy on a single 80GB H100 — posting large gains in math reasoning, function calling, and agentic NL2SQL over Command A.

## 核心贡献

- 提出 **LuckyStar 111B**，面向韩英企业级工具调用智能体的 111B 混合推理模型；从 Cohere 已完成全部后训练的 **Command A** 继续训练，而非从头预训练，从而低成本地保留通用指令遵循与多语言能力。
- 设计了一套**三阶段适配流水线**：(1) 混合监督微调（Hybrid SFT，约 80% 推理数据 / 20% 非推理数据）；(2) 带可验证奖励的强化学习（RLVR）；(3) 离线偏好对齐（DPO，低学习率）。三阶段分别负责加推理能力、优化工具使用、纠正 RLVR 引入的冗长。
- 通过**前置条件（preamble conditioning）**实现"一模型双模式"：同一套权重既可输出简短非推理回复，也可输出面向工具的逐步推理，由推理时选择，无需为不同工作负载维护多个模型。
- 提出**"英文推理、韩文作答"的混合语言策略**：实验证明英文推理轨迹对可验证任务提供更强的学习信号（韩文 prompt 下英文推理 vs 韩文推理：AIME 2024 69.3 vs 50.9；MATH 500 93.6 vs 85.6），并通过**语言一致性惩罚奖励**（`r = r_correctness − r_penalty`，错配扣 0.5）将韩文 prompt 漂移到英文作答的比例从 12.2% 降至 0.8%。
- 构建了 **10 万条可验证的 NL2SQL 训练对**（源自 Spider、BIRD、SynSQL），并用 **best-of-N 拒绝采样 SFT** 解决基座模型在 NL2SQL 上 <1% 正确率的冷启动难题。
- 在**单张 80GB H100** 上以 **4-bit 量化**部署 111B 模型（显存约减半），并在所报告的学术基准上与 FP8 基本持平。
- 提供了一套实用的训练配方与**失败模式分析**（NL2SQL 冷启动失败、RLVR 期间韩→英语言漂移、推理优化后的冗长），对可扩展智能体系统具有普适参考价值。

## Key Contributions

- Introduces **LuckyStar 111B**, a 111B-parameter bilingual Korean–English hybrid-reasoning agent model, adapted from Cohere's fully post-trained **Command A** rather than a new pretraining run — cheaply preserving general instruction-following and multilingual ability.
- Designs a **three-stage adaptation pipeline**: (1) Hybrid SFT (~80% reasoning / 20% non-reasoning); (2) Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR); (3) offline Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) at a low learning rate — respectively adding reasoning, optimizing tool use, and curing RLVR-induced verbosity.
- Implements **preamble conditioning** so one weight set serves two inference-time modes: concise non-reasoning replies vs. longer step-by-step tool-oriented reasoning — eliminating the need for separate per-workload models.
- Proposes a **"think in English, answer in Korean"** multilingual strategy: English reasoning traces yield stronger verifiable-reasoning signal (for Korean prompts: AIME 2024 69.3 vs 50.9, MATH 500 93.6 vs 85.6 for English vs Korean reasoning), and a **language-consistency reward penalty** (`r = r_correctness − r_penalty`, −0.5 on mismatch) cuts Korean→English answer drift from 12.2% → 0.8% during RLVR.
- Curates **100K verifiable NL2SQL prompt–query pairs** (from Spider, BIRD, SynSQL) and uses **best-of-N rejection-sampled SFT** to solve a severe cold-start problem (base policy <1% accuracy on NL2SQL).
- Deploys the 111B model on a **single 80GB H100** via **4-bit quantization** (~50% memory reduction), with near-parity to FP8 on the reported academic benchmarks.
- Provides a practical training **recipe and failure-mode analysis** (NL2SQL cold-start, language drift, post-RLVR verbosity) broadly relevant to scalable agentic systems.

## Methodology

LuckyStar 111B is initialized from **Command A** (Cohere's fully post-trained 111B enterprise model) and adapted through three stages.

**Stage 1 — Hybrid SFT.** Teaches the two operating modes. Roughly **80% reasoning** examples, **20% non-reasoning** instruction-following (the mix is reasoning-heavy because the base model already has strong multilingual chat ability). Reasoning examples use a preamble that encourages intermediate reasoning before the answer; non-reasoning examples use a preamble encouraging concise responses. For math/code, internal data is combined with public reasoning datasets (OpenThoughts, DeepMath-103K). For prompts that have ground-truth answers but no solution traces, candidate step-by-step completions are generated with strong internal/open models (Qwen3, DeepSeek-R1) and only those whose extracted final answer exactly matches the ground truth are retained (LLM-judge fallback when extraction is unreliable). Multiple correct traces per prompt are kept to teach reasoning-path diversity.

**Multilingual reasoning strategy.** Early experiments showed English reasoning outperformed Korean reasoning even for Korean prompts (Table 1). Machine-translating 30% of English traces into Korean (Command A, temp 0.3, p=0.95, JSON output, reviewed by 30 Korean annotators) gave little improvement because translation degraded trace naturalness. The authors therefore adopt a **mixed-language strategy**: reason in English, answer in the user's language. To teach this, they sampled 20,000 mid-difficulty Korean prompts, generated English-reasoning / Korean-answer traces via rejection sampling, and added the verified examples to SFT.

**Agentic NL2SQL data.** Curated from Spider, BIRD, and SynSQL. Raw examples were often unverifiable (failing gold queries, empty outputs, very long results), so only gold queries that executed and returned 1–500 tokens were kept → **100,000 verifiable pairs**. The base policy scored <1%, creating a cold-start problem: RLVR alone had insufficient reward signal. They bootstrapped with best-of-N rejection-sampled SFT traces (8 candidates/prompt from internal + open models given a read-only SQL execution tool; verified by a two-stage heuristic + LLM-judge pipeline), holding out a prompt subset from RLVR to preserve starting entropy.

**Stage 2 — RLVR.** Uses **REINFORCE Leave-One-Out (RLOO)** with binary rewards over **>50,000 agentic NL2SQL prompts** and **4,000 math prompts**. Final answers are checked by rule-based verifiers where possible, else by a Command-A-based LLM judge. **Prompt selection:** only prompts whose pre-RLVR pass rate is in **12.5%–87.5%** are used (avoiding trivially-solved and impossible examples). **Language-consistency penalty:** for Korean prompts, an LLM judge checks the final response language while reasoning stays English; mismatches incur a 0.5 penalty (eq. 1). Rollouts **>32k tokens are filtered from the batch** rather than zero-rewarded, to avoid teaching premature termination. RLVR improved reasoning/NL2SQL and reduced repetition failures but **increased verbosity** (e.g., over-elaborating on multiple-choice).

**Stage 3 — Offline Preference Alignment (DPO).** Low-learning-rate DPO over preference pairs combines the base model's alignment data with newly curated Korean-specific preferences plus **targeted conciseness pairs** (direct answers = chosen; verbose post-RLVR outputs = rejected). The low LR refines chatbot behavior without eroding the reasoning/tool-use gains.

**Deployment.** The 111B model is quantized from FP8 to 4-bit weights (~50% memory reduction), enabling single-80GB-H100 serving. Preamble conditioning lets downstream systems select reasoning depth at inference time without weight changes.

## Results & Highlights

**Reasoning & tool use (Table 3, Pass@1, 32k context):**

| Model | AIME'24 KO | MATH500 KO | AIME'24 EN | MATH500 EN | Ent. NL2SQL | BFCL v3 | LG Agentic |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| LuckyStar 111B | **69.3** | 93.6 | **73.7** | 94.0 | 38.0 | 70.6 | 4.85 |
| LuckyStar 4-bit | 66.0 | 95.6 | 72.3 | **96.0** | – | – | – |
| Command A | 10.0 | 76.2 | 13.3 | 79.6 | 7.3 | 64.4 | 2.67 |
| GPT-4o (11/20) | 16.7 | 76.4 | 13.3 | 78.6 | 37.3 | 71.7 | 2.56 |
| Claude 3.7 Sonnet | 46.7 | 92.8 | 50.0 | 92.5 | 38.0 | 58.6 | **5.04** |
| Qwen3 235B A22B | **80.0** | 92.4 | **80.0** | 91.2 | 22.0 | 67.9 | 3.29 |

- Massive gains over Command A: AIME'24 Korean **10.0 → 69.3**, Enterprise NL2SQL **7.3 → 38.0**, LG Agentic Eval **2.67 → 4.85**, BFCL v3 **64.4 → 70.6**.
- LuckyStar is competitive with / ahead of GPT-4o and Claude 3.7 Sonnet on most enterprise-tool and Korean-math metrics, though Qwen3 235B leads raw English/Korean math (it is a dedicated reasoning model). The authors stress absolute scores show realistic enterprise tool use remains hard.
- **4-bit LuckyStar closely matches FP8** on academic benchmarks (e.g., MATH500 even rises to 96.0); tool-use numbers for 4-bit are missing because eval runs weren't finished before the freeze.

**Language-consistency effect (Tables 1 & 2):** English reasoning beats Korean reasoning for Korean prompts; the consistency penalty drives Korean→English answer mismatch from 12.2% (SFT) → 3.3% (step 100) → 0.8% (step 200).

**General multilingual quality (Table 4):** Quality is largely preserved. LuckyStar: KMMLU 68.6, KO ARC-C 89.2, KO IFEval 77.8, KO MT-Bench 8.09; MMLU 82.7, EN ARC-C 93.8, EN IFEval 89.4, EN MT-Bench 8.45. Minor regressions vs Command A on some English metrics (e.g., MMLU 82.7 vs 83.9; EN IFEval 89.4 vs 90.9) but strong Korean gains — the reasoning/tool-use improvements do not come at the cost of broad bilingual instruction-following.

## Authors & Credibility

- A genuine **Cohere × LG CNS** production collaboration. Cohere built (and provides) the Command A base model; LG CNS is a major Korean IT-services/enterprise-AI provider supplying domain and business workflows. The author list spans both organizations, with equal-contribution first authors and correspondence routed through Cohere.
- The framing — memory/serving budgets, verifiable tool use, explicit failure modes, LLM/agent usage disclosure, and acknowledgments of multiple internal labs (GenAI Product Lab, Agentic AI Lab at LG CNS AI Lab) — is consistent with a credible industry research report aimed at real deployment.
- Honest about limitations (internal/proprietary eval sets, limited reproducibility, reliance on English reasoning) rather than overselling.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: Moderate.** The components (multilingual SFT, RLVR/RLOO, DPO, 4-bit quantization, preamble/switchable reasoning, rejection-sampled cold-start SFT) are all established. The contribution is their *combination* into a 111B Korean–English enterprise-agent stack, the explicit "think in English, answer in Korean" empirical justification (Table 1 quantifying the English-reasoning advantage), and the language-consistency reward penalty. It is a high-quality *systems/recipe* paper, not a new method.
- **Rigor: Good but with caveats.** Public benchmarks (AIME, MATH500, BFCL v3, MMLU/KMMLU, ARC-C, IFEval, MT-Bench) are used alongside internal ones, baselines are named (Command A, GPT-4o, Claude 3.7 Sonnet, Qwen3 235B), and the failure-mode analysis and prompt-selection (12.5–87.5% pass-rate band) are thoughtful. **However, the paper presents no per-stage ablations** isolating SFT vs RLVR vs DPO, nor a clean ablation of the language-consistency penalty's downstream effect beyond the mismatch-rate table. Baselines are frozen at an earlier snapshot (newer releases not re-run).
- **Reproducibility: Low-to-moderate.** Named base model and techniques help conceptually, but the training data is proprietary/legally restricted, the two headline enterprise benchmarks (Enterprise NL2SQL, LG Agentic Eval) are internal and not released, and a 111B model is expensive to reproduce independently. The 4-bit single-H100 claim is concrete and checkable in principle but no latency/throughput numbers are given.
- **Significance: Practically high, fundamentally modest.** Directly useful for the Korean enterprise-agent market and for the broader "adapt a post-trained frontier model to verifiable tool workflows under memory constraints" question. Less impactful as foundational research.
- **Red flags:** (a) Headline enterprise gains rest on **internal, unreleased** benchmarks (Enterprise NL2SQL, LG Agentic Eval) — external validation impossible. (b) **No ablations** to attribute gains to individual stages. (c) 4-bit tool-use results are **missing** (eval incomplete at freeze), so the deployment story is partial. (d) Qwen3 235B — a smaller, open, dedicated reasoning model — **beats LuckyStar on every math column**, undercutting the "111B" framing for pure reasoning. (e) "Preserves quality" holds broadly but with small English regressions vs Command A. (f) No serving-efficiency numbers despite the deployment emphasis (explicitly deferred to future work).

## Strengths

- Industrially grounded: real Cohere × LG CNS collaboration targeting real enterprise constraints and workflows.
- End-to-end recipe spanning training (Hybrid SFT + RLVR + DPO) **and** deployment (4-bit, single-H100), with preamble conditioning for inference-time mode switching.
- Honest, useful **failure-mode analysis** (cold-start, language drift, verbosity) with concrete mitigations — uncommon and valuable.
- Quantitatively justified design choices (e.g., Table 1 showing English-reasoning advantage; Table 2 showing the penalty working).
- Avoids costly pretraining by adapting an already post-trained model; pragmatic "deployment-scoped" scaling philosophy.
- Transparent LLM-usage disclosure.

## Limitations

- **No ablations** isolating each of the four key techniques or the three training stages.
- Headline enterprise gains rely on **internal, unreleased benchmarks**; public tool-use benchmarks don't fully capture the workflows, so external validation is impossible.
- Proprietary data and eval sets (legal/privacy/confidentiality) **limit reproducibility**.
- **4-bit tool-use results missing**; no latency/throughput/energy measurements despite the deployment emphasis.
- Still **relies on English reasoning** for many Korean prompts — native-Korean reasoning (and culturally/domain-specific knowledge) is deferred to future work.
- A **smaller open model (Qwen3 235B) beats it on pure math**, raising questions about the cost/value of the 111B scale for reasoning specifically.
- Minor English regressions vs the Command A base on some knowledge/instruction metrics.
- Korean-enterprise focus may limit transferability to other language pairs/domains.

## Relevance

与智能体工具调用、多语言大模型适配及可验证强化学习方向高度相关：其"在后训练模型上做高效适配 + 单 GPU 4-bit 量化部署"的实用配方，以及对 NL2SQL 冷启动、语言漂移、冗长三类失败模式的处理，对构建低成本、可落地的企业级 Agent 具有直接参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- What does a **per-stage ablation** (SFT vs +RLVR vs +DPO) show on each benchmark — how much does each stage contribute, and is DPO's conciseness fix erasing any RLVR gains?
- How does the **language-consistency penalty** affect *answer correctness* (not just mismatch rate) — does forcing Korean answers ever hurt accuracy on culturally/linguistically Korean content?
- On pure reasoning, is the **111B scale justified** given Qwen3 235B (smaller, open) beats it on every math column — what does LuckyStar gain that justifies the size?
- How robust is the **"think in English, answer in Korean"** approach to non-math/non-SQL tasks where reasoning is culturally or domain-specifically Korean?
- What are the actual **serving numbers** (latency, throughput, tokens/s, energy) for the 4-bit single-H100 config, and how does it compare to a 2× FP8 setup?
- Could the **rejection-sampled cold-start SFT** recipe generalize to other tool domains (e.g., retrieval, API orchestration) where base accuracy is near zero?
- Does preamble conditioning introduce any **reliability gap** (e.g., reasoning leaking into concise mode) in production traffic not captured by benchmarks?
