> **一句话总结：** 本文系统性地揭示了LLM作为评估器时，对话历史中评价的极性会显著偏移后续判断（Cohen's d = -0.17），且负面历史的影响比正面强62%，这种偏置在5轮对话后即饱和、不随上下文长度增长，对模型不确定的判断项影响最大。

> **TL;DR:** Across 75,898 API calls to 11 models from 4 providers, conversation history with predominantly positive or negative evaluations systematically biases subsequent LLM judgments toward the prevailing polarity (d = -0.17). The effect is strongest on items where the model is uncertain at baseline (d = -0.34), saturates after just 5 turns, and exhibits a negativity asymmetry where negative history induces 1.62x more bias than positive. Scaling up models reduces but does not eliminate the problem.

## 核心贡献

- **首次大规模系统研究LLM评估中的对话历史偏置效应（AMEL）**：涵盖75,898次API调用、11个模型、4个提供商（OpenAI、Anthropic、Google、开源模型），横跨代码审查、内容审核、营养评估三个领域
- **揭示负面不对称性**：负面对话历史比正面历史产生62%更强的偏置（配对检验 t = 13.46, p < 10^-39），且即使是平衡（50/50）的历史也会将模型推向"否"方向
- **发现偏置快速饱和现象**：仅5轮偏置历史即可产生与50轮相同的效果（Spearman |r| < 0.01），模型不会随更多上下文进一步偏移
- **证明模型不确定性是最关键的调节因素**：模型在基线时不确实的判断项承受的偏置是确定项的两倍（d = -0.34 vs d = -0.15）
- **提供实用的缓解建议**：每个评估项使用独立上下文是最简单有效的解决方案；必须批处理时平衡历史有帮助

## Key Contributions

- **Large-scale empirical study of conversation-history bias in LLM-as-judge settings**: 75,898 API calls across 11 models from OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and local OSS models, spanning 3 evaluation domains (code review, content moderation, nutritional assessment)
- **Quantifies the accumulated message effect (AMEL)**: models shift toward the conversation's prevailing polarity with overall d = -0.17 (p < 10^-46); 9 of 11 models show significant bias after Bonferroni correction
- **Documents negativity asymmetry**: negative history induces 1.62x stronger bias than positive history in paired per-item comparison (t = 13.46, p < 10^-39, n = 2,481); even balanced 50/50 history pushes models toward "no"
- **Shows rapid saturation**: 5 prior biased turns produce the same shift as 50 (Spearman |r| < 0.01, p > 0.94; OLS slope p = 0.80) — the model detects the pattern quickly and additional context adds no further bias
- **Identifies baseline uncertainty as the key moderator**: items where the model is genuinely uncertain at baseline absorb ~2x more bias than items where baseline is deterministic (d = -0.34 vs d = -0.15)
- **Characterizes the mechanism**: the effect is a continuous shift in the token probability distribution (not a threshold artifact), has both token-level and semantic components for the negativity asymmetry, and positional placement within the conversation does not matter

## Methodology

The study uses a between-subjects experimental design. Identical test items are presented under four conditions:

1. **Baseline**: item follows only the system prompt, no conversation history
2. **No-saturated**: item follows N prior turns where 90% of evaluations were "no" and 10% "yes"
3. **Yes-saturated**: same structure but 90% "yes" and 10% "no"
4. **Neutral**: N prior turns with 50/50 split

Context length N varies over {5, 10, 20, 50} turns. Each condition is repeated 10 times at temperature T = 1.0. The **bias score** is defined as BS = P(r* | treatment) − P(r* | baseline), where r* is the target response for the polarity condition. A positive BS means the model conformed to the saturated polarity.

Three binary evaluation domains are tested:
- **Code review**: "Is this code production-ready?" (55 positive + 55 negative context items, 21 test items)
- **Content moderation**: "Is this comment appropriate?"
- **Nutritional assessment**: "Is this a healthy choice?"

Test items span three categories per domain: clear positive (7), ambiguous (7), and clear negative (7). Eleven models from 4 providers are tested, including GPT-4.1 Nano, GPT-5.2, Claude Haiku/Sonnet/Opus 4.6, Gemini 2.5 Flash/Pro, and 4 local OSS models (Llama 3.2 3B, Qwen3/3.5 4B, Qwen3 30B).

Statistical analysis includes one-sample t-tests with Bonferroni correction (factor of 21), Cohen's d effect sizes, paired t-tests for the negativity asymmetry, Spearman correlation for accumulation, and a linear mixed-effects model with random intercepts for models. Three follow-up experiments probe mechanism: logprob analysis (1,050 additional calls to GPT-4.1 Nano), semantic vs. token-level decomposition, and positional manipulation.

## Results & Highlights

- **Overall effect**: Mean BS = -0.052 (d = -0.17, p < 10^-46). Models systematically shift toward the conversation's prevailing polarity. Even balanced history shifts toward "no."
- **Scaling trends**: Within Anthropic, bias decreases from Haiku (d = -0.22) to Opus (d = -0.17). Within OpenAI, Nano (d = -0.34) to GPT-5.2 (d = -0.17). Gemini Pro paradoxically shows more bias than Flash (d = -0.27 vs -0.18), possibly due to mandatory thinking tokens.
- **Qwen3 4B exhibits contrarian bias** (d = +0.19): it shifts opposite to the conversation polarity, likely from instruction tuning penalizing repetitive patterns.
- **Negativity asymmetry**: |BS_no| = 0.166 vs |BS_yes| = 0.102, paired ratio 1.62x (p < 10^-39).
- **Ambiguity modulation**: Author-coded ambiguous items show d = -0.28; empirically uncertain items (high baseline entropy) show d = -0.34; deterministically answered items show only d = -0.15.
- **Domain variation**: Code review (d = -0.24) > meals (d = -0.13) > content moderation (d = -0.12).
- **No accumulation**: Spearman r < 0.01 between context length and bias score across all polarities.
- **Assimilation vs. contrast**: Congruent items (context polarity matches ground truth) show assimilation (BS = +0.051); incongruent items show contrast/resistance (BS = -0.110).
- **Mixed-effects model** confirms findings with random intercepts for models; between-model ICC = 0.031, indicating AMEL is universal across models.

## Authors & Credibility

Single-author paper by Temkit Sid-Ali. The scale of the study is impressive: 75,898 API calls across 11 models from 4 providers. The experimental design is rigorous with between-subjects controls, Bonferroni corrections, mixed-effects modeling, and extensive appendices covering robustness checks (unparseable response handling, deduplication strategies, MAR/MNAR analysis). The author provides a public repository with data and code. Item categorization was verified with intra-rater agreement (95% after one-week interval). The paper is transparent about limitations, including partial Gemini data due to API quotas and a disk-full crash affecting Qwen3 30B. The work is positioned within relevant literature on sycophancy, in-context learning bias, and conversational inertia.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: The AMEL phenomenon itself has been hinted at in prior work (conversational inertia, threshold priming), but this is the first systematic, multi-model, multi-domain quantification with rigorous statistical analysis. The distinction from sycophancy and few-shot label bias is clearly articulated. The saturation finding and negativity asymmetry are genuinely new results.
- **Rigor**: Strong experimental design with controlled conditions, large sample sizes, multiple statistical tests with appropriate corrections, and a mixed-effects model. The use of bias scores constructed from 10 repetitions per cell is methodologically sound. However, the single-author study raises questions about independent validation of the item categorization (intra-rater agreement is weaker than inter-rater), and the Gemini data is incomplete.
- **Reproducibility**: The author provides a public repository. The experimental design (test items, prompts, model identifiers) is described in sufficient detail. The use of temperature 1.0 and 10 repetitions enables stochastic verification. Some concerns about API versioning and model drift over time.
- **Significance**: High practical relevance for anyone using LLMs as evaluators in production. The recommendation of fresh context per item is actionable. The finding that bias saturates quickly is important for understanding the mechanism.
- **Red flags**: Single author; Gemini models have partial data due to API quotas (1,470 and 990 calls vs 8,190 for others); 8.3% unparseable response rate concentrated in Claude Opus; Qwen3 30B results are sensitive to deduplication strategy; the neutral condition's negative shift is noted but not fully explained.

## Strengths

- Massive scale: 75,898 API calls across 11 models from 4 providers provides strong statistical power
- Clean experimental design: between-subjects with four conditions and systematic variation of context length and polarity
- Well-differentiated from related phenomena (sycophancy, few-shot label bias, anchoring)
- The saturation finding is practically important and theoretically interesting — bias does not accumulate
- Mechanism follow-up experiments (logprobs, semantic vs. token-level, positional) narrow the explanation
- Transparent about limitations and data quality issues
- The entropy-based stratification (Section 4.9) is more informative than author labels alone

## Limitations

- Single-author study: no inter-rater agreement for item categorization, limited independent verification
- Only binary (yes/no) evaluation tasks; the effect may differ for continuous or multi-class judgments
- Three domains may not generalize to all LLM-as-judge applications (e.g., creative writing, legal analysis)
- Gemini data is incomplete; Qwen3 30B had a disk-full crash and deduplication ambiguity
- The paper does not propose or test mitigation strategies beyond the "fresh context" recommendation
- The mechanistic experiments are exploratory at reported sample sizes — they narrow but do not isolate a single cause
- All models tested are from early-to-mid 2026; the effect may change with future training techniques
- The neutral condition producing a negative shift is intriguing but lacks a definitive explanation

## Relevance

本文与LLM推理评估和输出一致性的研究兴趣高度相关。AMEL揭示了LLM作为评估器时的系统性偏置，直接影响了推理结果的可复现性和可靠性——当同一批项目在不同对话上下文中被评估时，结果会因历史极性而系统性偏移。这对理解LLM评估输出的稳定性具有重要意义。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does AMEL interact with chain-of-thought prompting? Does requiring explicit reasoning before a judgment reduce or amplify the accumulated message effect?
- Would the saturation finding hold for longer contexts (e.g., 200+ turns), or is there a second accumulation regime?
- Can logprobs-based correction methods reliably compensate for AMEL in production evaluation pipelines?
- Is the contrarian bias in Qwen3 4B a generalizable effect of certain instruction-tuning strategies, or specific to this model?
- How does AMEL manifest in non-binary evaluation settings (Likert scales, ranking, open-ended scoring)?
- Does fine-tuning on balanced evaluation data reduce AMEL susceptibility?
