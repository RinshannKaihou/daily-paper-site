How Many Different Outputs Can a Transformer Generate?
Maxime Meyer, Mario Michelessa, Caroline Chaux, Vincent Y. F. Tan · 2026-05-21
transformer-theory expressivity-bounds finite-precision representation-geometry sequence-accessibility
Key Metric
Upper bound: (1 + 2r/ε)dm — theoretical slope within 5–20× of empirical measurements across Pythia, Qwen-2.5, Llama-3.2, and Gemma-3 (10 model sizes).
Why This Matters
Explains why transformers fail on seemingly trivial tasks like copying long sequences — an architectural limitation, not a resource constraint. Direct implications for context compression and in-context learning.
Critical Edge
The Wasserstein precision assumption is plausible but not empirically verified; the 5–20× tightness gap limits engineering predictiveness. Cramming experiments are limited to short prompts (m = 1–5).

Methodology

The analysis proceeds in three stages. Embedding space partitioning: The decoder matrix partitions the embedding space into regions Et per token based on logit argmax. Sequence regions shrink exponentially with length n. Packing number argument: Under finite precision ε, the embedding space is partitioned into ε-cubes. The number of distinguishable inputs for prompt length m is bounded by the packing number of the ball Bd×m(0, r), yielding the (1 + 2r/ε)dm bound (Theorem 4.5). Mean-field generalization: For arbitrary prompt lengths, transformers are reinterpreted as maps over probability measures under Wasserstein distance, giving a prompt-length-independent upper bound (Theorem 4.9). Refinements replace the isotropic ball with ellipsoid/cone enclosures and non-uniform cell volumes. Experiments use cramming (optimize soft prompts to generate target sequences) and copying-length generalization.

Strengths

  • Clean theoretical framework translating directly into testable quantitative predictions
  • Comprehensive experiments across 4 architecture families and 10 model sizes
  • Progressive refinement (ball → cone → ellipsoid → non-uniform cells)
  • Copying experiment elegantly demonstrates the practical consequence
  • Honest discussion of assumptions and limitations throughout

Limitations

  • Wasserstein precision assumption not directly verified empirically
  • Precision ε treated as a single scalar; varies across layers in practice
  • Cramming experiments limited to short prompts (m = 1–5) due to compute cost
  • Analysis assumes greedy decoding; no formal treatment of beam/sampling
  • Connection to SSMs (Mamba) left entirely for future work

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does the bound change with quantized models (4-bit, 8-bit)? Does reduced ε proportionally tighten it?
  2. Can the packing-number framework analyze accessibility of specific capabilities (e.g., arithmetic) rather than raw sequences?
  3. Do MoE architectures (Mixtral) have fundamentally different bounds due to sparse activation?
  4. What is the empirical Wasserstein precision of real transformers, and how does it vary across layers?
  5. Can the threshold n* be used as a diagnostic for comparing context-compression methods?
