Why SGD is not Brownian Motion: A New Perspective on Stochastic Dynamics

Igor Ignashin, Anna Radovskaya, Andrew Semenov, Egor Lopatin, Stanislav Potapov, Aleksandr Kovalenko, Andrey Veprikov, Aleksandr Shestakov, Andrey Leonidov, Aleksandr Beznosikov 2026-05-21 sgd-dynamics optimization-theory fokker-planck stochastic-optimization training-dynamics
Key Finding

The standard Langevin approximation of SGD omits O(η²) correction terms of the same order as its own diffusion term, producing qualitatively wrong predictions — like stationary states where SGD actually exhibits exponential variance growth.

Key Metric

Along flat Hessian eigendirections, SGD variance grows linearly in iteration count (∝ γη²n), while sharp directions stay bounded at ηγ/2 — a dichotomy the Langevin approximation entirely misses.

Why This Matters

Rigorous mathematical analysis revealing systematic bias in standard SGD theory at finite learning rates — directly impacts how we understand training stability and optimization behavior.

Critical Edge

The gradient-Hessian proportionality assumption (EL[GGT] ≈ γEL[H] + ε) is central to all analytical results but only empirically verified on small models; analysis also assumes sampling with replacement, unlike real training.

Methodology

Master equation → discrete Fokker–Planck (§3–4). Starting from the exact SGD update wn+1 = wn − η∇Ln(wn) with independent minibatch sampling, the authors write the master equation for pn(w) and expand to second order in η. This yields the discrete Fokker–Planck equation (Eq. 4), containing a second-order term EL[∇kL · ∇lL] absent from the standard Langevin form.
Local analysis near critical points (§4). Under a quadratic approximation of the minibatch loss near a critical point v, covariance dynamics simplify in the mean-Hessian eigenbasis. With assumptions on Hessian fluctuation independence and gradient-Hessian proportionality, the authors derive closed-form variance evolution along each eigendirection (Proposition 3, Eq. 19).
Empirical validation (§5). NanoGPT on Shakespeare computes exact Hessians to validate structural assumptions. A 6.6M-parameter NanoGPT on WikiText-2 tests variance predictions at scale using stochastic Lanczos for top-20 sharp eigendirections and 50 independent SGD ensembles at three learning rates (0.001, 0.005, 0.010).

Results & Highlights

  • The discrete Fokker–Planck equation differs from the Langevin form at O(η²), containing an additional term proportional to (∇L̄)² that the Langevin approximation omits.
  • In a toy 1D setting, standard Langevin predicts a stationary state (finite variance) where exact discrete SGD exhibits exponential variance growth — a qualitative discrepancy.
  • Near critical points, sharp Hessian directions (large λi) exhibit bounded variance ≈ ηγ/2, while nearly-flat directions (λi ≈ 0) show variance growing linearly as γη²n.
  • A "modified Langevin" SDE (Eq. 10) recovers the discrete predictions at second order; the standard Langevin does not.
  • Experiments confirm structural agreement between theoretical and empirical covariance profiles, with Langevin predictions increasingly underestimating the plateau at larger η.

Strengths

  • Clean conceptual reframing: SGD as deterministic dynamics in a fluctuating landscape vs. Brownian motion in a fixed potential.
  • Rigorous derivation of the discrete Fokker–Planck equation from the exact master equation.
  • Clear identification of the specific O(η²) term missing from the Langevin approximation, with a concrete example showing qualitative failure.
  • Confined/diffusive decomposition along Hessian eigendirections provides a new lens for understanding SGD dynamics.
  • Honest about limitations and assumptions; does not overclaim.
  • Single fitted constant γ across learning rates provides non-trivial validation.

Limitations

  • Local quadratic approximation restricts applicability to the vicinity of critical points; does not address dynamics during the bulk of training.
  • Key assumptions (Hessian fluctuation independence, gradient-Hessian proportionality) are empirical, not proven.
  • Sampling with replacement differs from standard epoch-based without-replacement training used in practice.
  • Proportionality constant γ is not determined by theory; must be estimated from data.
  • Large-scale experiments only probe top-20 sharp eigendirections; flat directions are harder to validate.
  • No comparison with other non-Langevin frameworks (e.g., heavy-tailed noise models, stochastic modified equations).

Open Questions

  1. How does the discrete Fokker–Planck framework extend to momentum-based methods (SGD with momentum, Adam) where the update rule involves additional state variables?
  2. Can the confined/diffusive decomposition be used to design adaptive learning rate schedules that exploit the different dynamical regimes?
  3. What happens to the analysis under sampling without replacement, where consecutive minibatches are correlated?
  4. Does the gradient-Hessian proportionality hold across architectures (Transformers, ResNets, MLPs) and training stages, or only near critical points?
  5. Can the framework extend beyond the quadratic regime to describe SGD dynamics during active training?
