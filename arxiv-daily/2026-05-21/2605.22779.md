> **一句话总结：** FAME 提出一种基于失败感知混合专家架构的消息级日志异常检测框架，仅离线调用一次 LLM 进行语义分组，训练轻量级路由器和领域专家实现本地推理，在 BGL 数据集上以 76 倍标注缩减达到 F1=98.16，并对未见过的日志模板保持 86.3% 的异常检测率。

> **TL;DR:** FAME is a label-efficient message-level log anomaly detection framework using a failure-aware Mixture-of-Experts architecture. It invokes an LLM only once offline to semantically partition event templates into failure domains, then trains lightweight on-premise experts. On BGL, it achieves F1=98.16 with 76x less labeling effort and detects 86.3% of anomalies from unseen event types.

## 核心贡献

- 提出消息级（单行日志）异常检测框架 FAME，每条日志输出二元异常判定 + 具名失败域标签，实现精确定位故障行而非粗粒度窗口告警
- 利用 LLM 一次性离线将日志模板按故障机制分组为语义连贯的失败域，之后不再调用 LLM，所有推理在本地轻量模型上完成
- 基于稀有异常先验下 K-shot 采样的非对称置信度特性，设计非对称 MoE：纯异常域仅靠路由即可判定，混合域使用独立校准的 BERT 分类器
- 在 BGL 数据集上以 K=100 实现 F1=98.16，标注量减少 76 倍；在 Thunderbird 上达到 F1=99.95
- 单次 LLM 调用 + 训练总成本 $10.23，而直接使用前沿 LLM 推理每次需 $6,698–$10,047

## Key Contributions

- Message-level log anomaly detection with both binary anomaly decisions and named failure-domain labels for direct fault localization
- Failure-aware asymmetric MoE exploiting the statistical asymmetry of K-shot sampling under rare-anomaly prior: pure-anomaly domains resolved by routing alone, mixed domains via per-message BERT classifiers
- One-time offline LLM invocation for semantic template partitioning, followed by fully on-premise lightweight inference (DistilBERT router + BERT experts)
- F1 = 98.16 on BGL at K = 100 with 76x annotation reduction; F1 = 99.95 on Thunderbird with perfect recall
- 86.3% detection of anomalies from unseen EventIDs, demonstrating generalization beyond training templates

## Methodology

FAME operates in two stages:

**Offline Setup (run once):**
1. **Log Parsing & K-Shot Sampling:** Raw logs are parsed by Drain3 into event templates (EventIDs). At most K labeled lines per EventID are selected (most recent), split 80/20 into training and calibration subsets. Binary signals (HAS_NORMAL_SIGNAL, HAS_ANOMALY_SIGNAL) and representative examples are extracted.
2. **Failure-Domain Partitioning:** An LLM receives per-EventID statistics and proposes a partition of templates into semantically coherent failure domains. A deterministic certification step validates the proposal against K-shot evidence: pure-anomaly domains (all K labels anomalous), mixed domains (at least one normal), and domains with no anomaly signal are dissolved into UNIVERSAL_NORMAL.
3. **Router Training:** A DistilBERT gate (binary: expert vs. UNIVERSAL_NORMAL) and selector (multiclass over expert domains) are trained with Focal Loss. Gate threshold fixed at 0.5; training stops when validation recall reaches 0.95.
4. **Two-Phase Expert Training:**
   - *Phase 1:* Domain-adaptive MLM pre-training on shared normal-only corpus (capped at 200K lines).
   - *Phase 2:* Supervised fine-tuning with frozen bottom layers; only top 2 transformer layers + classification head are trained using Focal Loss with class weighting.
5. **Threshold Calibration:** Per-expert thresholds optimized for F1 on the held-out calibration split with a recall floor of 0.90. UNIVERSAL_NORMAL uses score fusion combining expert and gate outputs.

**Online Inference:**
- Gate scores each line; below 0.5 routes to UNIVERSAL_NORMAL (fused scoring).
- Above 0.5: selector assigns to a specific expert domain. Pure-anomaly domains flag immediately; mixed domains are scored by the domain's BERT expert against its calibrated threshold.
- Throughput: ~1.2M lines/hour on a single 4×T4 GPU node (~3 ms/line).

## Results & Highlights

- **BGL (primary benchmark, heterogeneous templates):** F1 = 98.16, Precision = 98.18, Recall = 98.14 at K = 100. This surpasses all baselines including direct LLM inference (best LLM: Claude Haiku 4.5 at F1 = 96.92).
- **Thunderbird (structurally separable templates):** F1 = 99.95, Recall = 100.00, confirming near-perfect detection on easier datasets.
- **Annotation efficiency:** At K = 100, only 53,287 labeled lines needed on BGL vs. 4.7M full annotation (76x reduction). Even K = 25 achieves F1 = 97.57 with 178x reduction.
- **Generalization to unseen templates:** 86.3% of anomalies from EventIDs absent during setup are successfully detected via semantic routing.
- **Cost advantage:** One-time FAME cost $10.23 vs. $6,698–$10,047 per full run for frontier LLM inference.
- **Ablation findings:** Single global BERT (no routing) achieves only F1 = 29.65 on BGL, confirming that expert decomposition is the dominant source of improvement. Removing the gate drops F1 to 90.99. Phase 1 MLM acts as a precision booster (+2.17 precision). FAME remains effective with TF-IDF grouping (F1 = 93.01) showing the architecture is robust to grouping mechanism choice.
- **LLM grouping sensitivity:** Tested with GPT-5.4, Claude Sonnet 4.6, Gemini 3.1 Pro, and Kimi 2.5. Claude Sonnet 4.6 gives best BGL result (98.16); all produce semantically similar failure categories with different granularity.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** The asymmetric MoE design grounded in the K-shot statistical asymmetry is a genuine conceptual contribution. Using LLM for one-time semantic partitioning with deterministic certification is practical and novel for log analysis. However, individual components (Drain3 parsing, BERT fine-tuning, MoE routing) are well-established; the contribution is in their integration.
- **Rigor:** Evaluation is thorough with 6 research questions, multiple baselines (statistical, neural, LLM), ablations, cost analysis, K-sensitivity, LLM sensitivity, and run-to-run variance reporting. Chronological train/test splits prevent data leakage.
- **Reproducibility:** Pipeline details are well-specified (Drain3 params: threshold 0.5, depth 4; Focal Loss γ=2, α=0.75; gate threshold 0.5; recall floor 0.90). Datasets are public. LLM grouping is non-deterministic but the certification step and TF-IDF fallback provide safeguards.
- **Significance:** High practical impact for production log monitoring. The cost comparison ($10.23 vs. thousands per LLM run) is compelling. Message-level granularity with failure-domain labels addresses a real operational pain point.
- **Red flags:** (1) Certification step never actually dissolved any expert group across all experiments, raising questions about whether it provides meaningful protection or if the LLM proposals are consistently well-formed for these specific datasets. (2) BGL and Thunderbird are supercomputer logs from the mid-2000s — generalization to modern cloud-native microservice logs with different log structures remains untested. (3) The non-monotone K-sensitivity (performance drops slightly at K=400) is acknowledged but not fully explained. (4) Comparison with direct LLM inference uses a constrained prompt setup (anomaly-only demonstrations capped at 50-96 examples), which may not represent optimal LLM usage.

## Strengths

- Principled statistical motivation for the asymmetric MoE design
- Dramatic cost reduction: one-time $10.23 vs. recurring thousands for LLM inference
- Strong generalization to unseen log templates (86.3%)
- Fully on-premise inference after setup, eliminating data egress and API dependency
- Comprehensive evaluation with ablations, multiple LLM backends, and cost analysis
- Practical failure-domain labels for downstream triage

## Limitations

- Evaluated only on two supercomputer log datasets (BGL, Thunderbird) from the mid-2000s; applicability to modern cloud/microservice logs is unverified
- Partition certification step was never triggered in experiments, so its robustness as a safeguard is untested
- Relies on Drain3 parsing quality; poorly structured or unstructured logs may degrade performance
- K-shot calibration instability at low K (F1 std up to 3.50 at K=50) may require careful K selection in practice
- UNIVERSAL_NORMAL expert acts as a fallback but its fused scoring is calibrated on a small subset, which may be fragile

## Relevance

本文与 ML 可靠性和系统可观测性方向高度相关：FAME 通过混合专家架构实现日志异常的细粒度检测，涉及模型路由可靠性、标注效率、以及 LLM 在生产系统中的成本效益问题，与训练稳定性监控和故障检测的研究兴趣有直接交集。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does FAME perform on modern cloud-native application logs (e.g., Kubernetes, microservices) where log structure is less regular and failure modes differ from supercomputer environments?
- Can the failure-domain partitioning be incrementally updated as new log templates emerge during deployment, without re-running the full offline pipeline?
- What is the sensitivity of FAME to Drain3 parsing errors, and could learned parsers improve end-to-end robustness?
- How would FAME's asymmetric routing principle transfer to other rare-event detection domains (e.g., network intrusion, fraud detection)?
- Is the non-monotone K-sensitivity behavior (slight degradation at K=400 on BGL) a fundamental property of the K-shot regime or an artifact of temporal drift in the most-recent sampling strategy?
