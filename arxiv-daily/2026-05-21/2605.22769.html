Chronologically ordered pre-training makes a 6B model outperform Qwen3-8B, Gemma-3-12B, and Olmo3-7B on recent factual knowledge — data ordering, not scale, determines temporal freshness.
Understanding Data Temporality Impact on Large Language Models Pre-training
Pilchen Hippolyte, Fabre Romain, Signe Talla Franck, Perez Patrick et al. 2026-05-21
temporal-knowledge pre-training-dynamics data-ordering continual-learning benchmark
Key Metric
OLMES parity at ~71.7% while the sequential 6B model surpasses models 2–4x larger on 2023–2024 temporal facts; shuffled baselines peak on 2015/2020 data and degrade sharply toward 2024.
Why This Matters
Proves that data ordering — not just data volume — is a first-order factor in LLM knowledge freshness. A smaller, chronologically trained model can be more up-to-date than a much larger shuffled one, with direct implications for continual LLM deployment.
Critical Edge
Introduces KairosQA (7,000+ temporally grounded QA pairs) and identifies "temporal alignment inertia" — shuffled models internalize older facts more strongly due to repetition bias across Common Crawl dumps, a previously undocumented systematic bias.
Methodology

A 6B-parameter Transformer decoder (32 layers, 32 heads, 4096 hidden dim, GQA, RoPE, SwiGLU) is pre-trained on Common Crawl snapshots using the dactory pipeline (HTML extraction, 24-language filtering, Bloom dedup, domain-weighted quality scoring). The sequential model processes 2018–2025 snapshots in strict chronological order (2.5T tokens); the shuffled baseline uses aggregated 2020–2024 snapshots globally shuffled. AdamW with Warmup-Stable-Decay (peak LR 1e-3, batch 4.2M tokens, context 4096). Branching cooldown (30k steps cosine decay) produces converged yearly checkpoints. KairosQA is built from 17M Wikidata triplets, filtered to 7,167 subject–relation pairs with temporal variation ≥ 2 changes across 2018–2025; questions generated via GPT-4o mini, evaluated in both cloze (log-prob ranking) and generative (F1) settings.

Strengths & Limitations
Strengths
  • Rigorous controlled experiment isolating data ordering effects at production scale.
  • KairosQA benchmark with careful construction, quality control, and dual evaluation protocols.
  • Strong evidence that a small sequential model beats much larger shuffled models on recent facts.
  • Excellent reproducibility — full code, checkpoints (Sequential Helium 6B), and dataset released.
  • Honest reporting of failed forgetting mitigation (model soup, replay).
Limitations
  • KairosQA dominated by sports (~55%) and award facts; temporal dynamics may differ for other domains.
  • Forgetting mitigation (model soup, replay) fails — core recency-vs-retention trade-off unresolved.
  • Sequential training lags shuffled during mid-training, potentially costly in practice.
  • Experiments only at 6B scale; transfer to 70B+ models unverified.
  • Covers only 24 European languages; temporal dynamics may differ elsewhere.
Questions for Further Exploration
  1. How do findings transfer to 70B+ scale models with different architectures (e.g., MoE)?
  2. Can time-aware parameter architectures (e.g., TiMoE) break the forgetting-relevance zero-sum trade-off?
  3. Would combining sequential pre-training with domain upsampling yield complementary benefits?
  4. How do temporal dynamics differ for scientific vs. sports/award facts?
  5. Can yearly checkpoints enable efficient model updating without full retraining?
