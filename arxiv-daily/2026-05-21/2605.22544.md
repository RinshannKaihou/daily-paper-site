> **一句话总结：** 本文通过在6个指令微调嵌入模型、11个数据集、每个数据集15条提示词（共990组实验）上系统性地研究提示词敏感性，发现当前单提示词的基准评估方式会系统性高估或低估模型性能，且通过对抗性选择提示词可以让任意模型登顶排行榜。

> **TL;DR:** This paper demonstrates that instruction-tuned embedding models are highly sensitive to prompt phrasing — single-prompt benchmarks systematically misrepresent model performance, and adversarial prompt selection can make any model rank first on leaderboards.

## 核心贡献

- 揭示了当前指令嵌入模型基准评估的核心缺陷：单提示词评估无法反映模型在合理提示词变化下的真实性能分布。
- 定义并量化了两种系统性偏差：**提示词膨胀**（prompt inflation，默认提示词高估性能）和**提示词缩水**（prompt deflation，默认提示词低估性能）。
- 证明排行榜排名对提示词选择不具鲁棒性：在对抗性提示词选择下，研究中任意模型都可以被提升至第一名，包括默认排名最后的 bge-small。
- 提出了"提示词黑客行为"（prompt hacking）的概念：在不违反提交规则的前提下，通过选择性报告有利提示词来操纵基准分数，类似于 p-hacking。
- 建议基准测试应采用多提示词评估或报告敏感性指标。

## Key Contributions

- Systematically quantifies prompt sensitivity across 6 embedding models, 11 datasets, and 15 task-specific prompts per dataset (990 total configurations).
- Identifies two systematic patterns: **prompt deflation** (default prompt understates performance) and **prompt inflation** (default prompt overstates performance).
- Demonstrates that leaderboard rankings are not robust to prompt selection — any model can be promoted to rank 1 under adversarial prompt configurations.
- Introduces the concept of **prompt hacking**: selective reporting of favorable prompts while remaining compliant with benchmark submission policies.
- Recommends benchmarks adopt multi-prompt evaluation or report sensitivity metrics alongside point estimates.

## Methodology

The authors evaluate 6 instruction-tuned embedding models: Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B, multilingual-e5-large-instruct, KaLM-embedding-multilingual-mini-instruct-v2.5, and three BGE variants (small/base/large-en-v1.5). They select 11 datasets from MTEB/MMTEB spanning retrieval (MIRACL, Touche2020, FEVER), classification (TweetSentiment, IMDB, AmazonCounterfactual), clustering (MedrxivP2P, StackExchange), and semantic similarity (STS15, STS14, STS22.v2).

For each task, 15 task-specific prompts are generated using gpt-oss-120b with structured outputs. Prompts are short (mean 5.24 words) and validated against human-written prompts. Models are evaluated across all prompt–task combinations, and results are compared against reported MTEB scores. The authors analyze coefficient of variation (CV) to measure prompt sensitivity, construct adversarial leaderboard configurations using Borda ranking, and compute empirical probabilities of prompt inflation.

## Results & Highlights

- **Prompt sensitivity varies widely**: Median CVs are moderate (2–6%), but BGE models show individual task CVs reaching 30–45%, indicating extreme sensitivity in specific model–task combinations.
- **Prompt deflation is common**: BGE models show reported scores in the lower tail of score distributions on several tasks, meaning default prompts systematically understate their capability.
- **Prompt inflation also occurs**: Qwen3's reported score on MIRACL aligns with a secondary upper-range mode, overstating expected performance.
- **Any model can rank first**: Under adversarial configurations (target model gets best prompts, others get worst), even bge-small (last place by default) rises to rank 1. Even in milder scenarios (best prompt for target, default for others), meaningful rank shifts occur (e.g., bge-large rises from rank 5 to 3).
- **Systematic prompt inflation in reported scores**: Most models consistently report performance above their median scores across prompts, suggesting favorable prompt selection is common practice.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: High. While prompt sensitivity has been studied for generative LLMs, this is the first systematic study isolating prompt effects in instruction-tuned embedding model evaluation. The prompt hacking framing is a valuable conceptual contribution.
- **Rigor**: Strong experimental design with 990 configurations, validated synthetic prompts, and multiple analysis angles (CV, adversarial ranking, inflation probability). However, the study uses only 15 prompts per task, which carries non-trivial uncertainty for variance estimates.
- **Reproducibility**: Excellent. Code is publicly available, all model versions and revisions are specified, datasets are from standard MTEB/MMTEB, and prompt generation methodology is fully described.
- **Significance**: High. The finding that any model can be made to rank first fundamentally undermines current embedding model evaluation practices and has immediate implications for MTEB/MMTEB benchmark design.
- **Red flags**: The authors evaluate only 6 open-weight models (max 0.6B parameters) and only English subsets. Whether findings generalize to larger models (e.g., OpenAI text-embedding-3) or multilingual settings remains unclear. The synthetic prompt generation (by a single LLM) may not capture the full diversity of practitioner-written prompts.

## Strengths

- Clean, focused research question with well-controlled experimental design.
- Compelling adversarial analysis that vividly illustrates the severity of the problem.
- Practical recommendations that benchmark maintainers can act on immediately.
- Honest discussion of limitations (model scope, prompt sample size, language coverage).
- The dual framing — prompt sensitivity as both a validity threat and a practical asset — adds nuance.

## Limitations

- Only 6 models evaluated, all open-weight and relatively small (up to 0.6B parameters).
- 15 prompts per task is a relatively small sample for robust variance estimation.
- English-only evaluation; multilingual settings may exhibit different or amplified sensitivity.
- Does not evaluate prompt search/ensembling strategies that practitioners could use.
- The adversarial scenario (assigning worst prompts to competitors) is somewhat extreme and may overstate real-world risk.

## Relevance

该研究直接揭示了当前嵌入模型基准评估的系统性缺陷，对理解MTEB/MMTEB排行榜的可靠性至关重要，也为开发更鲁棒的评估方法论提供了实证依据。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How do findings generalize to larger (1B+) or closed-source embedding models?
- What is the optimal number of prompts to evaluate for reliable robustness estimates?
- Can prompt robustness be improved through training-time interventions (e.g., prompt augmentation during fine-tuning)?
- How does prompt sensitivity interact with multilingual evaluation — does translation of prompts introduce additional variance?
- Would ensembling over multiple prompts provide both better point estimates and improved downstream performance?
