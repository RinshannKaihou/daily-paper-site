Deep ensembles of message-passing GNNs suffer “epistemic collapse” — independently trained models converge to functionally identical predictions, neutralizing the uncertainty mechanism ensembles rely on.

Do Deep Ensembles Actually Capture Uncertainty in Graph Neural Networks?

Pedro C. Vieira, Pedro Ribeiro, Viacheslav Borovitskiy

2026-05-21 graph-neural-networks uncertainty-quantification deep-ensembles epistemic-collapse

Key Metric

On PEMS, the deep ensemble achieves NLL of 2.39 — worse than the Trivial baseline (1.37). Classification likelihood gains are 0.1–1% vs. ~20% in computer vision. Epistemic-to-aleatoric ratios well below 1 (vs. ~5 in CV).

Why This Matters

Training multiple GNNs for uncertainty quantification is wasteful. The widely assumed transferability of deep ensembles to graph learning does not hold. Practitioners should use the best single model or Bayesian alternatives instead.

Critical Edge

NLL decomposition (DE-R, DE-A baselines) proves gains come entirely from averaged point predictions, not uncertainty calibration. Model soups degrade to Trivial baseline, confirming functional-space convergence driven by message-passing bias.

Methodology

Standard deep ensembles (5 independently initialized models) of message-passing GNNs evaluated across 7 datasets: node classification (Cora, Citeseer, Tolokers2), node regression (Artnetviews, Chameleon, PEMS), and graph regression (QM9-5%). Architectures: GCNs for 4 datasets, GATv2 for 2. Regression models include dedicated variance heads. All results averaged over 10 independent runs. Analysis layers: (1) absolute metrics (NLL, ECE, RMSE/accuracy) vs. Trivial baseline and single GNN; (2) likelihood ratios compared to CV benchmarks; (3) NLL deconstruction via DE-R/DE-A for regression and Jensen’s gap for classification; (4) explicit aleatoric/epistemic decomposition; (5) model soups for weight-space convexity analysis.

Strengths

  • Comprehensive evaluation across classification, regression, and graph-level tasks.
  • Multiple independent lines of evidence converging on the same conclusion.
  • Proper comparison against CV baselines to contextualize the gap magnitude.
  • Honest framing of convexity as hypothesis rather than proven fact.
  • Practical recommendation (use best single model) immediately actionable.

Limitations

  • Only message-passing architectures (GCN, GATv2); graph transformers not covered.
  • Functional-space convexity remains hypothesis without formal proof.
  • Connection to oversmoothing suggested but not empirically validated.
  • No proposed remedies for GNN uncertainty quantification.
  • Smaller datasets (Cora, Citeseer) may not represent modern graph learning.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Does epistemic collapse extend to graph transformers (GraphGPS, Graphormer) with global attention?
  • Can diversity-promoting regularization (negative correlation learning, repulsion terms) mitigate collapse?
  • Is functional-space convexity specific to message passing, or does it generalize to other architectures with strong structural biases?
  • Can the smoothing–convergence connection be formalized via spectral graph theory?
  • How do Bayesian GNN methods compare in epistemic uncertainty quality given Bayesian GCN’s success on PEMS?
