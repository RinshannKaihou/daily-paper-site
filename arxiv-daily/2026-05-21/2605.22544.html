Any embedding model can be made to rank first on leaderboards via adversarial prompt selection
One prompt is not enough: Instruction Sensitivity Undermines Embedding Model Evaluation
Yevhen Kostiuk, Kenneth Enevoldsen · 2026-05-21
embedding-models benchmark-evaluation prompt-sensitivity instruction-tuning reproducibility

Key Metric

990 configurations tested (6 models × 11 datasets × 15 prompts); coefficient of variation reaches 30–45% on individual tasks for BGE models.

Why This Matters

Single-prompt benchmarks systematically misrepresent embedding model performance, enabling “prompt hacking” — selective reporting that can boost any model to rank 1 without violating submission rules.

Critical Edge

Even bge-small (last place by default) rises to rank 1 under adversarial prompt assignment. Most models consistently report scores above their median across prompts, suggesting favorable selection is already common practice.

Methodology

The authors evaluate 6 instruction-tuned embedding models (Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B, multilingual-e5-large-instruct, KaLM-embedding-multilingual-mini-instruct-v2.5, and three BGE variants) across 11 MTEB/MMTEB datasets spanning retrieval, classification, clustering, and semantic similarity. For each task, 15 prompts are generated using gpt-oss-120b. Models are evaluated across all 990 prompt–task combinations, and results are compared against reported MTEB scores. The authors analyze coefficient of variation, construct adversarial leaderboard configurations using Borda ranking, and compute empirical prompt inflation probabilities.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Clean, focused research question with well-controlled design (990 configurations)
  • Compelling adversarial analysis vividly illustrating the severity of the problem
  • Practical recommendations that benchmark maintainers can act on immediately
  • Honest discussion of limitations and dual framing of prompt sensitivity

Limitations

  • Only 6 open-weight models evaluated, all ≤0.6B parameters
  • 15 prompts per task is a small sample for robust variance estimation
  • English-only evaluation; multilingual settings may amplify sensitivity
  • Adversarial scenario (worst prompts to competitors) may overstate real-world risk

Open Questions

  1. How do findings generalize to larger (1B+) or closed-source embedding models?
  2. What is the optimal number of prompts for reliable robustness estimates?
  3. Can prompt robustness be improved through training-time interventions (e.g., prompt augmentation during fine-tuning)?
  4. Would ensembling over multiple prompts provide both better point estimates and improved downstream performance?
