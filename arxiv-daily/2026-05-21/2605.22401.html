Cross-Species RSA Reveals Conserved Early Visual Alignment but Divergent Higher-Area Rankings Across Human fMRI and Macaque Electrophysiology
Nils Leutenegger · 2026-05-21
representational-similarity-analysis cross-species-comparison learning-rules visual-cortex brain-score
Local learning rules (STDP, PC) consistently outperform backpropagation at early visual cortex (V1/V2) in both human fMRI and macaque electrophysiology — but IT rankings show zero cross-species correspondence.

Key Metric

STDP achieves ρ ≈ 0.30 at macaque V1/V2. IT rankings: Kendall's τ = 0.00 (p = 1.00) — no cross-species correspondence. ResNet-50 boosts IT to ρ ≈ 0.25.

Why This Matters

Early visual representations are conserved across species, suggesting fundamental computational principles. IT alignment is capacity-limited, not learning-rule-determined — the field needs larger models to draw IT-level conclusions.

Critical Edge

n = 5 learning rules makes ranking comparisons severely underpowered (only significant at τ = ±1.0). Stimulus domains differ across species (THINGS vs. HVM), confounding IT-level interpretations.

Methodology

Five learning rule conditions (BP, FA, PC, STDP, Random Weights) evaluated on a small 3-layer CNN trained on CIFAR-10 (40 epochs, 5 seeds). Identical trained weights tested against two macaque electrophysiology datasets from Brain-Score: MajajHong2015 (V4/IT, HVM objects) and FreemanZiemba2013 (V1/V2, texture stimuli). RSA uses Spearman ρ between model and neural RDMs (correlation distance) with bootstrap 95% CIs (10,000 resamples). A pretrained ResNet-50 serves as capacity control. Cross-species ranking correspondence assessed via Kendall's τ with exhaustive permutation tests.

Strengths

  • Identical model weights across species — clean comparison
  • Transparent about limitations and confounds
  • ResNet-50 capacity control reframes IT convergence finding
  • Comprehensive supplementary analyses (noise ceilings, stimulus control, seed variability)

Limitations

  • n = 5 rules: ranking comparisons severely underpowered
  • Stimulus domain differs across species and brain regions
  • Small CNN (3 layers, CIFAR-10) limits generalizability
  • Human data from only 3 subjects

Open Questions

  • Would a shared stimulus set across species eliminate IT ranking divergence?
  • How do results change with Vision Transformers trained on larger datasets?
  • Does the STDP/PC advantage reflect genuine locality or implementation artifacts?
  • What is the minimum n for adequate statistical power in cross-species ranking comparisons?
