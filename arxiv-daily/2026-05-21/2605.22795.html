Finite-Particle Convergence Rates for Conservative and Non-Conservative Drifting Models

Krishnakumar Balasubramanian · 2026-05-21

The first finite-particle convergence rates for drifting generative models: a conservative KDE-gradient velocity achieves root residual rate N−1/(d+4), while the original Laplace-kernel method is proven to converge only to an irreducible scale-mismatch residual neighborhood.

Key Metric

Root residual velocity rate N−1/(d+4) under h-uniform quadrature regularity for the conservative method. Optimal bandwidth h☆, N = ((d+2)A / ((2−β)C))1/(d+4−β).

Why This Matters

Provides the first theoretical grounding for one-step drifting generative models. The irreducible Laplace scale-mismatch residual explains why non-conservative variants may have fundamental performance ceilings regardless of particle count.

Critical Edge

The sharp-companion-kernel decomposition reveals that for Laplace kernels, displacement-based velocities carry an unavoidable residual ex = h(r̄ν,h − r̄x,h)σν,h that cannot be eliminated by increasing N — a structural, not proof, artifact.

Methodology

The paper develops two parallel finite-particle convergence analyses. For the conservative method, the displacement-based velocity is replaced with the KDE-score gradient b̂ = sν,h − sμ,h, which is always a gradient field. For N particles following the center-evaluation ODE, a joint-entropy identity (Theorem 3.1) relative to the product smoothed target decomposes entropy dissipation into a Stein drift term and a reciprocal-KDE self-interaction correction. Bandwidth optimization balances the self-interaction term A/(Nhd+2) against quadrature term C(h)h2.

For the non-conservative Laplace method, a sharp companion kernel Lh(u) = h(‖u‖ + h)Kh(u) decomposes the mean-shift into a scaled conservative term plus an irreducible scale-mismatch residual. The leave-one-out formulation removes self-interaction but introduces approximation errors not explicitly bounded in terms of N and h.

Strengths

  • Extremely rigorous with explicit tracking of all constants and assumptions.
  • Complete treatment of both conservative and non-conservative methods in one framework.
  • Sharp identification of Laplace scale-mismatch as a structural feature.
  • Useful sufficient conditions for reciprocal-KDE control (deterministic + probabilistic).

Limitations

  • No empirical evaluation at all — rates are purely theoretical.
  • Main rates require β < 2; kernel-scale derivative scaling gives β = 4 in practice.
  • Continuous-time idealization; discrete-time training not analyzed.
  • Non-conservative Laplace theorem depends on uncontrolled leave-one-out errors.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Can the quadrature exponent β be bounded empirically for real data distributions?
  • Is N−1/(d+4) tight, or could improved analysis yield faster rates?
  • How do continuous-time rates degrade under discrete-time stop-gradient training?
  • Can the scale-mismatch residual Δh be controlled dynamically during training?
  • What is the practical performance of conservative vs. displacement velocity on image generation?
