Full covariance matching breaks the Ω(1/T) diffusion path-KL barrier to O(1/T²) — achievable with a training-free Lanczos sampler using only 3 Jacobian-vector products per step.
The Value of Covariance Matching in Gaussian DDPMs and the Lanczos Sampler
Md Sahil Akhtar, Aymane El Gadarri, Vivek F. Farias, Adam D. Jozefiak 2026-05-21
diffusion-models sampling-theory lanczos-methods covariance-matching information-theory
Key Metric
FID 5.06 on CIFAR-10 (T=50) vs OCM-DDPM's 5.83; FID 5.21 on CelebA 64×64 (T=100) vs 7.09 — with only 3 JVPs per denoising step and zero auxiliary training.
Why This Matters
First rigorous proof that diagonal covariance approximations are fundamentally bottlenecked at Ω(1/T), and that full covariance matching is practically feasible without auxiliary models — raising the ceiling for fast diffusion sampling quality.
Critical Edge
Novel I-MMSE-based proof technique combining Gaussian-channel mutual information with higher-order derivative formulas, plus the Lanczos sampler's exponential error decay O(3-m) that makes m=3 steps sufficient — a clean bridge between theory and algorithm.
Methodology

The authors express path KL as L(θ) - L*, then compute a second-order Taylor expansion of the per-step loss. For the optimal reverse kernel L*_t, they derive a novel representation as Gaussian-channel mutual information I(X₀|ₜ; √γ_t X₀|ₜ + Z) and use the I-MMSE identity plus higher-order derivative formulas to bound its expansion. The key result: first and second-order terms of L_t(θ*) and L*_t are identical, so their difference is O(γ_t³) = O(1/T³), summing to O(1/T²). The Lanczos Gaussian Sampler (LGS) builds a Krylov subspace {Z, ΣZ, Σ²Z, …} to approximate Σ^(1/2)Z without storing the dense covariance matrix. Each Lanczos step requires one JVP of the score model. The tridiagonal projected matrix (m×m, m=3) is efficiently diagonalized. Ritz value clamping and a Hutchinson diagonal guard stabilize the final step.

Strengths & Limitations
Strengths
  • Elegant theoretical contribution cleanly separating mean, covariance, and higher-order moment value.
  • LGS requires no auxiliary training — uses only the pretrained score model.
  • Strong empirical gains on CIFAR-10 and CelebA with only m=3 Lanczos steps.
  • 20+ pages of complete proofs in appendices.
  • Connection to classifier guidance broadens impact beyond unconditional sampling.
Limitations
  • ImageNet 64×64 gains marginal or negative in some configurations vs OCM-DDPM.
  • Adds 3–5 backward passes per step; batching workaround introduces stale approximations.
  • Only evaluated at 64×64 resolution; scalability to 256×256+ untested.
  • Bounded data assumption ∥x₀∥ ≤ D may be restrictive for some distributions.
  • Best wall-clock variant (LGSb) applies LGS to only last 25% of steps.
Questions for Further Exploration
  1. How does LGS perform on higher-resolution benchmarks (ImageNet 256×256, LSUN)?
  2. Can the O(3⁻ᵐ) decay bound be tightened, and does m=3 suffice for distributions with large condition numbers?
  3. Does full covariance matching combine favorably with DDIM, DPM-Solver, or consistency models?
  4. Can higher-order information beyond covariance achieve even faster than O(1/T²) rates?
  5. How does LGS behave under classifier-free guidance, more common in practice?
