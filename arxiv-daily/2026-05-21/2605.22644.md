> **一句话总结：** 本文从离散SGD更新出发推导出离散Fokker–Planck方程，证明标准Langevin近似在有限学习率下会遗漏与扩散项同阶的校正项，并揭示SGD在损失景观临界点附近沿Hessian特征方向分解为"受限"和"扩散"两种截然不同的动力学模式。

> **TL;DR:** The paper derives a discrete Fokker–Planck equation directly from the SGD update rule, showing that the standard Langevin/Brownian-motion approximation omits O(η²) correction terms — including one of the same order as the retained diffusion term — leading to qualitatively wrong predictions at finite learning rates (e.g., predicting a stationary state where SGD actually exhibits exponential variance growth). Near critical points, SGD decomposes along the Hessian eigenbasis into confined directions (bounded variance ∝ η) and diffusive directions (variance grows linearly in time). Experiments on NanoGPT confirm the predictions and show increasing deviation from the Langevin approximation as learning rate grows.

## 核心贡献

- 从离散SGD更新出发，严格推导了参数分布演化的离散Fokker–Planck方程，无需连续时间近似。
- 证明标准Langevin近似在O(η²)阶遗漏了额外的(∇L̄)²项，该遗漏可导致定性错误的预测（如假稳定态）。
- 提出SGD应理解为"在波动损失景观中的确定性动力学"，而非固定势场中的布朗运动。
- 在临界点附近，揭示SGD沿Hessian特征方向分解为"受限"（方差有界）和"扩散"（方差随时间线性增长）两种模式。
- 在NanoGPT/Shakespeare和6.6M参数NanoGPT/WikiText-2上验证了理论预测，观察到受限模式与扩散模式的清晰分离。

## Key Contributions

- Derive a discrete Fokker–Planck equation for SGD from first principles (master equation), bypassing continuous-time Langevin approximations entirely.
- Identify a systematic O(η²) mismatch between the standard Langevin Fokker–Planck equation and the exact discrete SGD dynamics — the Langevin form omits a (∇L̄)² term that is the same formal order as the retained diffusion.
- Reframe SGD as deterministic dynamics in a fluctuating loss landscape (due to minibatch resampling), rather than Brownian motion in a fixed potential.
- Show that near critical points, the Hessian eigenbasis decomposes SGD into confined eigendirections (bounded stationary variance ∝ ηγ) and diffusive eigendirections (variance growing linearly with iteration count, ∝ γη²n).
- Demonstrate that a "modified Langevin" SDE (Eq. 10) recovers the discrete result at second order but the standard Langevin does not — and that in certain parameter regimes, standard Langevin predicts stationarity while exact SGD exhibits exponential variance growth.
- Empirically validate on NanoGPT (small-scale Shakespeare and 6.6M-param WikiText-2), confirming structural agreement with theoretical variance profiles and increasing Langevin deviation at larger learning rates.

## Methodology

The paper proceeds in three stages:

1. **Master equation and discrete Fokker–Planck derivation (Section 3–4):** Starting from the exact SGD update w_{n+1} = w_n − η∇L_n(w_n) with independent minibatch sampling (with replacement), the authors write the master equation for the parameter density p_n(w) and expand it to second order in η. This yields Eq. (4) — the discrete Fokker–Planck equation — which contains a second-order term involving EL[∇k L · ∇l L] that is absent from the standard Langevin form. A 1D comparison (Proposition 2) explicitly shows the missing (∇L̄)² contribution.

2. **Local analysis near critical points (Section 4):** Under a quadratic approximation of the minibatch loss near a critical point v, the covariance dynamics simplify in the mean-Hessian eigenbasis. With assumptions (independence of Hessian fluctuation components, approximate diagonality, proportionality between gradient covariance and Hessian EL[GG^T] ≈ γEL[H] + ε), the authors derive a closed-form expression for the variance evolution along each eigendirection (Proposition 3, Eq. 19). This yields the confined/diffusive dichotomy (Corollary 1).

3. **Empirical validation (Section 5):** Two experimental settings are used. First, NanoGPT on Shakespeare (small enough to compute exact Hessians) validates the structural assumptions. Second, a 6.6M-parameter NanoGPT on WikiText-2 tests the variance predictions at scale using stochastic Lanczos for the top-20 sharp eigendirections and 50 independent SGD ensembles at three learning rates (0.001, 0.005, 0.010). A single γ is estimated at η=0.001 and used without refitting for the other rates.

## Results & Highlights

- The discrete Fokker–Planck equation (Eq. 4) differs from the Langevin form at O(η²), containing an additional term proportional to (∇L̄)² that the Langevin approximation omits.
- In the toy 1D setting, the standard Langevin approximation predicts a stationary state (finite variance) in regimes where exact discrete SGD exhibits exponential variance growth — a qualitative, not just quantitative, discrepancy.
- Near critical points, sharp Hessian directions (large λ_i) exhibit bounded variance ≈ ηγ/(2), while nearly-flat directions (λ_i ≈ 0) show variance growing linearly as γη²n.
- The modified Langevin SDE dθ = −∇L̄dt − η√(D + (∇L̄)²)dW_t recovers the discrete predictions at second order.
- Experiments on NanoGPT confirm: (i) structural agreement between theoretical and empirical covariance profiles, (ii) the plateau variance is approximately independent of λ_i and scales linearly with η, (iii) Langevin predictions increasingly underestimate the plateau at larger η.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (High):** The key insight — that SGD should be viewed as deterministic dynamics in a fluctuating landscape rather than Brownian motion in a fixed potential — is conceptually clean and leads to concrete O(η²) corrections. The identification of confined vs. diffusive eigendirections is a new and useful characterization. The observation that Langevin can give qualitatively wrong predictions (stationarity vs. explosion) is important.
- **Rigor (Moderate-High):** The master equation derivation is exact; the expansion to O(η²) is careful. However, the local analysis (Proposition 3) relies on several assumptions (independence, approximate diagonality, gradient-Hessian proportionality) that are empirically motivated but not proven. The paper is transparent about these limitations.
- **Reproducibility (Moderate):** Experimental details are in Appendix C. The use of NanoGPT (open-source) and standard datasets (Shakespeare, WikiText-2) helps. However, the proportionality constant γ is not determined by theory and must be estimated from data, and the "structural comparison" (shape matching rather than absolute prediction) weakens the empirical claim somewhat.
- **Significance (Moderate-High):** If the discrete Fokker–Planck framework proves generalizable, it could change how the community thinks about SGD dynamics. The immediate practical impact is limited since the paper primarily analyzes behavior near critical points rather than during active training.
- **Red flags:** The proportionality assumption EL[GG^T] ≈ γEL[H] + ε is central to the analytical results but only empirically verified on small models. The analysis assumes sampling with replacement, which is not how most practitioners train. The gap between the theoretical quadratic-local regime and real training dynamics is acknowledged but not addressed.

## Strengths

- Clean conceptual reframing: SGD as deterministic dynamics in a fluctuating landscape vs. Brownian motion in a fixed potential.
- Rigorous derivation of the discrete Fokker–Planck equation from the exact master equation.
- Clear identification of the specific O(η²) term missing from the Langevin approximation, with a concrete example showing qualitative failure.
- The confined/diffusive decomposition along Hessian eigendirections provides a new lens for understanding SGD dynamics.
- Honest about limitations and assumptions; does not overclaim.
- Experiments use a single fitted constant γ across learning rates, providing a non-trivial validation.

## Limitations

- Local quadratic approximation restricts applicability to the vicinity of critical points; does not address SGD dynamics during the bulk of training.
- Key assumptions (Hessian fluctuation independence/diagonality, gradient-Hessian proportionality) are empirical, not proven.
- Sampling with replacement differs from the standard epoch-based without-replacement training used in practice.
- The proportionality constant γ is not determined by the theory; must be estimated from data.
- Large-scale experiments only probe the top-20 sharp eigendirections (via Lanczos); flat directions are harder to validate at scale.
- No comparison with other non-Langevin frameworks (e.g., heavy-tailed noise models, stochastic modified equations).

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does the discrete Fokker–Planck framework extend to momentum-based methods (SGD with momentum, Adam) where the update rule involves additional state variables?
- Can the confined/diffusive decomposition be used to design adaptive learning rate schedules that exploit the different dynamical regimes?
- What happens to the analysis under sampling without replacement, where consecutive minibatches are correlated?
- Does the gradient-Hessian proportionality (EL[GG^T] ≈ γEL[H] + ε) hold across architectures (e.g., Transformers, ResNets, MLPs) and training stages, or is it specific to near-critical-point regimes?
- Can the framework be extended beyond the quadratic regime to describe SGD dynamics during the active training phase, not just near critical points?
