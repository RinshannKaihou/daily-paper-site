> **一句话总结：** 本文从有限精度和嵌入空间几何出发，严格证明了 Transformer 可输出的不同序列数量存在紧致上界，且可访问序列的最大长度随 prompt 长度线性增长、超过阈值后比例指数衰减——即使上下文和计算资源无上限。

> **TL;DR:** By analyzing the geometry of a transformer's embedding space under finite numerical precision, this paper proves that the maximal length of sequences a transformer can generate scales linearly with prompt length, and that the proportion of accessible sequences decays exponentially beyond a model-dependent threshold. These bounds are tight within a factor of 10 across architectures and model sizes, and hold even with unbounded context and computation.

## 核心贡献

- 证明任何 Transformer（无论规模、训练数据或计算资源）只能输出有限数量的不同序列；大多数序列在架构层面不可访问。
- 给出了可访问序列长度随 prompt 长度线性增长的上界公式，并在多个模型家族（Pythia、Qwen-2.5、Llama-3.2、Gemma-3）上验证该上界与实际值差距小于 10 倍。
- 证明超过临界阈值后，可访问序列比例随序列长度指数衰减，解释了 Transformer 在复制、压缩等任务上的"断崖式"失败现象。
- 通过 mean-field 框架将分析推广到任意 prompt 长度，证明了独立于 prompt 长度的绝对可访问性上限。
- 从嵌入空间几何（椭球、锥体近似和非均匀单元格体积）出发，逐步精化理论上界，使其更接近实验观测值。

## Key Contributions

- Prove that every transformer can only output a finite set of distinct sequences — most sequences are fundamentally inaccessible regardless of prompt, context length, or inference strategy.
- Derive an explicit upper bound on the number of accessible sequences as a function of prompt length m: `(1 + 2r/ε)^{dm}`, where d is the embedding dimension, r the embedding radius, and ε the numerical precision.
- Establish three formal results: (i) maximal accessible sequence length grows linearly with prompt length, (ii) proportion of accessible sequences decays exponentially beyond a critical threshold n*, and (iii) the linear coefficient admits a theoretical upper bound.
- Extend the finite-prompt analysis to arbitrary prompt lengths via the mean-field formulation of transformers (mapping over probability measures under Wasserstein distance), yielding a prompt-length-independent accessibility ceiling.
- Empirically validate predictions through cramming experiments across Pythia (160M–2.8B), Qwen-2.5 (0.5B, 1.5B), Llama-3.2 (1B, 3B), and Gemma-3 (270M, 1B), showing the theoretical slope is tight within a factor of 5–20.

## Methodology

The analysis proceeds in three stages:

1. **Embedding space partitioning.** The decoder matrix F partitions the embedding space into regions E_t, one per token t, based on the argmax of the logit output. A sequence of n tokens corresponds to a region formed by intersecting n such partitions across autoregressive steps. The volume of these regions shrinks exponentially with n.

2. **Packing number argument.** Under finite precision ε (the model cannot distinguish inputs closer than ε), the embedding space can be partitioned into a finite grid of cubes of side length ε. The number of distinguishable inputs for prompt length m is bounded by the packing number of the ball B^{d×m}(0, r), yielding the `(1 + 2r/ε)^{dm}` bound (Theorem 4.5).

3. **Mean-field generalization.** For arbitrary prompt lengths, transformers are reinterpreted as maps over probability measures under the Wasserstein distance. Under a finite Wasserstein precision assumption, the same packing argument gives a prompt-length-independent upper bound on total accessible sequences (Theorem 4.9).

**Refinements** include: (a) replacing the isotropic ball with anisotropic enclosures (ellipsoid, cone) to better approximate the actual embedding support; (b) estimating the empirical distribution of decoder cell volumes |E_t| to replace the uniform-volume assumption; (c) allowing variable precision ε across the embedding space.

**Experiments** use two protocols:
- **Cramming** (Kuratov et al., 2025): optimize soft prompts of length m to make a frozen model generate a target sequence, measuring accessibility as the success rate over 20 targets.
- **Copying-length generalization**: fine-tune models on exact string copying up to length 50, then evaluate on longer strings to observe the predicted sharp accuracy drop.

## Results & Highlights

- The theoretical slope (upper bound on C in n* = Cm) is within a factor of 5–10 of the empirically measured slope for PG19 targets, and within 5–20 for random targets, across all tested architectures and sizes.
- The isotropic ball bound is the loosest; replacing it with an ellipsoid or cone enclosure tightens the bound consistently. Further incorporating non-uniform cell volumes (common tokens get larger regions) tightens it further (ratios drop to 4.5–11).
- Cramming experiments confirm: for each fixed m, accessibility stays high up to a critical length n_50 then drops sharply (sigmoid transition, min R² = 0.88). The critical length scales linearly with m (R² ≥ 0.995).
- Copying experiments show near-100% accuracy up to a model-specific threshold, then abrupt failure — consistent with the predicted exponential decay of accessible sequences (median sigmoid R² = 0.95).
- Natural text (PG19) remains accessible longer than random strings at the same m, because its structure reduces effective information content.
- Results hold even with unbounded context windows and unlimited computation time — the limitation is architectural, not resource-driven.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: High.** This is the first work to provide tight quantitative bounds on the number of sequences a transformer can output, connecting finite precision to expressivity in a principled way. Prior work (Huang et al., 2025) proved impossibility of copying under idealized assumptions (absolute positional encodings); this paper removes those assumptions and provides quantitative predictions.
- **Rigor: Strong.** The proofs are clean and rely on standard packing number arguments. The progression from finite prompt (Theorem 4.5) to arbitrary prompt via mean-field (Theorem 4.9) is well-motivated. The Wasserstein precision assumption (Assumption 4.8) is discussed honestly, with Appendix E showing it can be replaced by a weaker elementary-operations assumption.
- **Reproducibility: Good.** Code is publicly available. The experimental setup (cramming with frozen models, PG19/random targets) is clearly described and uses standard open-source models (Pythia, Qwen, Llama, Gemma).
- **Significance: High.** The results explain a fundamental architectural limitation of transformers — why they fail on seemingly trivial tasks like copying long sequences. This has direct implications for context compression, in-context learning, and the design of future architectures.
- **Red flags:** The Wasserstein precision assumption (Assumption 4.8) is non-trivial and the authors acknowledge this. The tightness factor of 5–20 between theory and practice, while impressive for a worst-case bound, means the bound is not yet predictive enough for engineering use. The mean-field framework assumes permutation equivariance, which may be affected by positional encodings (addressed briefly in Appendix B but not fully explored empirically).

## Strengths

- Clean theoretical framework that translates directly into testable quantitative predictions.
- Comprehensive experiments across 4 architecture families and 10 model sizes.
- Progressive refinement of the bound (ball → cone → ellipsoid → non-uniform cells) shows the theory can be tightened with better geometric information.
- The copying-length generalization experiment elegantly demonstrates the practical consequence: transformers cannot generalize exact copying beyond a threshold, no matter how well trained.
- Honest discussion of assumptions and their limitations throughout.

## Limitations

- The Wasserstein precision assumption, while plausible and supported by Appendix E, is not directly verified empirically — there is no experiment measuring the actual Wasserstein precision ε of a real transformer.
- The theoretical bound treats precision ε as a single scalar; in practice, precision varies across layers and positions. Appendix G.2 attempts variable ε but reports looser bounds.
- The cramming experiments are limited to short prompt lengths (m = 1–5) due to computational cost; the linear regime is extrapolated rather than directly observed at large m.
- The analysis assumes greedy decoding; while Remark 3.2 argues the results extend to stochastic decoding (via the 50% probability argument), a formal treatment of beam search or sampling-based decoding is absent.
- The connection to state-space models (Mamba) mentioned in Remark 1.1 is left entirely for future work.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How does the accessibility bound change with quantized models (e.g., 4-bit, 8-bit)? Does reduced precision ε proportionally tighten the bound?
2. Can the packing-number framework be adapted to analyze the accessibility of specific functional capabilities (e.g., arithmetic reasoning) rather than raw sequence generation?
3. Do mixture-of-experts architectures (e.g., Mixtral) have fundamentally different accessibility bounds due to their sparse activation patterns?
4. What is the empirical Wasserstein precision ε of real transformers, and how does it vary across layers, attention heads, and model sizes?
5. Can the theoretical threshold n* be used as a diagnostic for comparing context-compression methods (e.g., prompt compression, context distillation)?
