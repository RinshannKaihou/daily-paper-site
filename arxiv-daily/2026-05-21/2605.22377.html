⚠️ Abstract-only summary
Key Metric
L2 norm of Layer-8 hidden representations partitions tokens into HIGH/LOW activation buckets via an upper-quartile threshold — no retraining required.
Why This Matters
Attention-based interpretability often highlights structural tokens (punctuation, function words); AFN provides a direct measure of representational salience grounded in semantics.
Critical Edge
Title claims “SLMs” (plural) but experiments are limited to BERT at a single layer — the “model-agnostic” and “glass box” claims overreach relative to the narrow experimental scope.

Methodology

The Activation Flow Network (AFN) computes the L2 norm of hidden-state representations at Layer 8 of BERT for each input token, yielding a Token Activation Strength score. Tokens are partitioned into HIGH-activation and LOW-activation buckets using an empirical upper-quartile threshold as the boundary. This shifts away from attention-based interpretability — which often highlights structurally important but semantically weak tokens — toward a direct measure of representational salience. The framework is lightweight, requires no model retraining or architectural modification, and is designed to be model-agnostic in principle.

Strengths

  • Lightweight, model-agnostic design requiring no retraining
  • Directly addresses a real limitation of attention-based interpretability
  • Clear and intuitive methodology using L2 norms
  • Practical potential for token-level explainability in downstream tasks

Limitations

  • Focused on a single model (BERT) and a single layer (Layer 8)
  • Upper-quartile threshold choice lacks theoretical justification
  • Unclear generalization to other architectures or larger models
  • Does not correlate activation importance with downstream task performance

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How do activation patterns differ across all 12 layers of BERT, and is Layer 8 truly unique?
  2. Does AFN generalize to larger models (LLaMA, Mistral) or encoder-decoder architectures?
  3. How does token activation strength correlate with downstream task performance?
  4. Can the bucket formulation be replaced with a principled statistical or information-theoretic threshold?
  5. How stable are activation rankings across different input domains (code, scientific text)?
