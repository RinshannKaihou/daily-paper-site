> **一句话总结：** AMUSE通过河-谷损失景观分析揭示了Muon正交化的双面性（加速平坦方向但放大振荡），并利用时变插值系数β_t将Muon的快速收敛与Schedule-Free的轨迹平均稳定性结合，实现无需学习率调度的随时训练，在720M LLM预训练上以1.51倍更少步数达到Muon的最终性能。

> **TL;DR:** AMUSE is a schedule-free optimizer that combines Muon's orthogonalized momentum with Schedule-Free averaging via a time-varying interpolation coefficient β_t. Through river-valley landscape analysis, the authors show Muon's orthogonalization accelerates bulk-subspace progress (the "river") but amplifies dominant-direction oscillations (valley walls). AMUSE starts with small β_t (evaluating gradients near the fast Muon sequence for rapid early adaptation), then gradually increases it toward the averaged sequence to suppress oscillations. It requires no learning rate schedule and supports anytime checkpoint extraction. AMUSE reaches Muon's final performance with 1.51x fewer steps on 720M LLM pretraining, 1.12x on ImageNet ResNet-50, and 3.08x on ImageNet ViT fine-tuning, while maintaining only one extra state copy (same memory as AdamW).

## 核心贡献

- **通过河-谷（river-valley）损失景观系统分析Muon优化器的行为机制**：首次从Hessian特征空间分解角度实证证明，Muon的正交化操作将更新从高曲率的主导方向（谷壁）转移到低曲率的体量方向（河流），加速训练但也放大了主导方向的噪声振荡。
- **提出AMUSE优化器**（Anytime MUon with Stable gradient Evaluation）：采用时变插值系数 β_t = 1 - ((T₀-1)/(t-1))^ρ · (1-β₁)，训练初期在快速Muon序列附近评估梯度以获取快速适应能力，随后逐渐向平均序列偏移以抑制谷壁振荡。
- **无需学习率调度，支持随时（anytime）训练**：β_t 趋近1的调度与总训练步数无关，模型可在任意时刻提取有效检查点，无需预定义训练总长度或衰减阶段。
- **显著加速收敛**：在720M LLM预训练上以1.51倍更少步数达到Muon最终性能；ImageNet ResNet-50上1.12倍；ImageNet ViT微调上3.08倍。仅需一个额外状态副本（与AdamW内存开销相同）。
- **广泛的实验验证**：覆盖CIFAR-10/100、SVHN、ImageNet分类、ISIC 2018分割、MAE ViT微调以及124M/720M/1B三个规模的LLM预训练，AMUSE在所有任务上持续优于AdamW、Schedule-Free AdamW和Muon。

## Key Contributions

- Provides the first systematic river-valley landscape analysis of Muon, demonstrating that orthogonalization makes updates more bulk-oriented (accelerating river progress) while preventing small dominant components from decaying (causing valley-wall oscillations). This is verified via Hessian eigenspace decomposition on MNIST-5k, CIFAR-10-5k, and SST-2-1k.
- Shows that Schedule-Free averaging stabilizes Muon trajectories by evaluating gradients at points closer to the river, reducing dominant-subspace components before orthogonalization.
- Proposes AMUSE with a time-varying β_t schedule (controlled by ρ ∈ [0,1]) that interpolates between fixed-β SF-Muon and a constant-window upper bound. The schedule is independent of total training steps.
- Achieves consistent improvements on the performance-iteration Pareto frontier across all evaluated tasks, reaching Muon's final performance with 1.51x, 1.12x, and 3.08x fewer steps on 720M LLM, ImageNet ResNet-50, and ImageNet ViT fine-tuning respectively.
- Adds only one extra state copy (z_t) compared to vanilla Muon, matching AdamW/SF-AdamW memory footprint.

## Methodology

AMUSE builds on two pillars:

**1. River-valley landscape analysis of Muon.** The authors decompose the loss Hessian into a small number of high-curvature "dominant" directions (k = number of classes, forming steep valley walls) and a much larger "bulk" subspace (the flat river where useful progress occurs). By projecting Muon updates onto these subspaces, they show: (a) orthogonalization via Newton-Schulz iteration increases the bulk ratio relative to raw momentum, explaining Muon's speed; (b) but polar normalization removes magnitude information, so even tiny dominant components receive constant-magnitude updates, causing bouncing (formally demonstrated on a quadratic example in Appendix A). Scaling experiments confirm that amplifying bulk components accelerates training while amplifying dominant components destabilizes it.

**2. Time-varying Schedule-Free Muon.** The standard SF framework maintains three sequences: base iterate z_t (updated by Muon), gradient-evaluation point y_t = (1-β)z_t + βx_t, and averaged sequence x_t (used for inference). The key insight is that evaluating gradients closer to x_t (larger β) suppresses dominant components, but using a large fixed β early in training is harmful because x_t has not yet reached the river. AMUSE solves this with:

β_t = 1 - ((T₀ - 1)/(t - 1))^ρ · (1 - β₁)  for t ≥ T₀

where β₁ is the initial coefficient, T₀ is the warmup length, and ρ controls the rate of increase. When ρ = 0, this reduces to fixed-β SF-Muon; when ρ = 1, the effective averaging window is strictly constant after warmup.

The algorithm applies Muon (Newton-Schulz orthogonalization) to hidden matrix-valued layers and SF-AdamW/SF-SGD to non-matrix parameters. Linear warmup is used but no learning rate decay. Post-hoc tests (EWA and LR decay) confirm AMUSE already follows the river, unlike constant-LR Muon.

## Results & Highlights

**Image domain:**
- CIFAR-10 (WideResNet-16-8): AMUSE achieves highest accuracy throughout training, clearly outperforming Muon which requires the full 300 epochs to become competitive.
- CIFAR-100 (DenseNet), SVHN (ResNet-3-96): Same pattern — AMUSE dominates the entire training curve.
- ImageNet-1k (ResNet-50): AMUSE reaches Muon's final performance with **1.12x fewer steps**.
- ISIC 2018 segmentation (U-Net): AMUSE outperforms AdamW, SF-AdamW, and Muon.
- MAE ViT-B/16 fine-tuning: AMUSE works effectively even when pretrained checkpoint was trained with AdamW (Muon struggles here). **3.08x fewer steps** to reach Muon's final performance.

**LLM pretraining (FineWeb-100B, Llama-style):**
- 124M model: AMUSE achieves lowest validation perplexity throughout training; extended training confirms sustained advantage.
- 720M model: AMUSE reaches Muon's final performance with **1.51x fewer steps**.
- 1B model: AMUSE continues to outperform all baselines at scale, including SF-AdamW (which struggles with large batches per Morwani et al., 2025).
- Fixed-β SF-Muon experiments show small β gives better early performance but degrades later; large β helps late-stage but hurts early. AMUSE's time-varying β_t achieves the Pareto-optimal balance.

**Efficiency:** Wall-clock time comparison shows AMUSE has negligible overhead vs. vanilla Muon. Memory footprint equals AdamW/SF-AdamW (one extra state copy z_t).

**Hyperparameter sensitivity:** AMUSE introduces only two additional hyperparameters (β₁ and ρ) beyond standard Muon tuning. Sensitivity analysis on 124M Llama shows reasonable robustness to both.

## Authors & Credibility

The authors are Jueun Kim, Baekrok Shin, Jihun Yun, Beomhan Baek, Minhak Song, and Chulhee Yun, affiliated with KAIST (South Korea). Chulhee Yun's group has produced multiple related works on optimization and the river-valley landscape (Song et al., 2025a,b at ICLR/NeurIPS), and co-author Minhak Song is a key contributor to the river-valley analysis of Schedule-Free methods. The work directly extends their prior NeurIPS 2025 paper "Through the River: Understanding the Benefit of Schedule-Free Methods for Language Model Training."

The paper is thoroughly empirical with controlled analysis (Hessian eigenspectrum decomposition, dominant/bulk subspace scaling), plus large-scale experiments across diverse domains. The river-valley framework provides principled motivation rather than ad hoc tricks. The code is not explicitly mentioned as released, but the algorithm is clearly specified (Algorithm 1 in Appendix C.1).

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: High.** The river-valley analysis of Muon's dual behavior (bulk acceleration + dominant oscillation) is a genuine and insightful contribution. The time-varying β_t schedule is well-motivated by the analysis of why fixed β fails. The combination of Muon + SF is natural in hindsight but the specific design (ρ-controlled window, derivation from averaging dynamics) is non-trivial.
- **Rigor: High.** The paper provides: (1) controlled Hessian decomposition experiments across 3 datasets and 3 architectures; (2) a formal quadratic example of bouncing dynamics (Appendix A); (3) derivation of the β_t schedule from averaging window constraints (Appendix C.3); (4) comprehensive baselines including SGD, AdamW, SF-SGD, SF-AdamW, Muon (all with cosine schedules), plus additional optimizers in Appendix F.1 (AdEMAMix, D-AdamW, C-AdamW, etc.). The Ablation studies (Appendix F.6) verify each component's contribution.
- **Reproducibility: High.** All hyperparameters are reported in tables. Algorithm 1 is complete. No proprietary data — FineWeb-100B and standard image benchmarks are public. The method uses only standard components (Newton-Schulz, SF averaging).
- **Significance: Very high.** Eliminating learning rate schedules for LLM pretraining is a major practical benefit. The anytime training property is valuable for production ML workflows. If the results hold at frontier scale (>10B parameters), this could become a standard optimizer.
- **Red flags / concerns:**
  - No experiments beyond 1B parameters. Scaling to 70B+ remains unverified.
  - The 1B experiment uses hyperparameters directly from the 720M tuning (no separate tuning), so it's unclear if AMUSE's advantage at 1B is fully realized.
  - AMUSE still uses a linear warmup phase, so it's not fully "schedule-free" — just decay-free.
  - The paper claims AMUSE requires "no learning rate schedule" but still tunes the base learning rate, weight decay, β₁, and ρ. The practical tuning burden is reduced but not eliminated.
  - Non-Muon parameters (embeddings, biases, norms) use separate SF-AdamW or SF-SGD, adding implementation complexity.

## Strengths

- Principled analysis connecting Muon's empirical behavior to loss landscape geometry.
- Elegant solution: time-varying β_t naturally transitions from exploration to stabilization.
- Eliminates learning rate decay — a significant practical simplification.
- Anytime training: valid checkpoints at every step without performance penalty.
- Memory-efficient: same footprint as AdamW (one extra state copy vs. Muon).
- Comprehensive evaluation: 6 image tasks + 3 LLM scales + multiple baseline optimizers.
- Strong empirical results with consistent Pareto improvements across all settings.

## Limitations

- No experiments at frontier scale (>10B parameters). The 1.51x speedup on 720M is promising but may not transfer.
- 1B experiments reuse 720M hyperparameters due to compute constraints — suboptimal tuning may underestimate AMUSE's potential.
- AMUSE is not fully schedule-free: linear warmup is still required (though this is trivial compared to cosine/decay schedules).
- The hybrid setup (Muon for matrix layers + SF-AdamW/SF-SGD for others) adds implementation complexity compared to a single unified optimizer.
- Additional memory vs. vanilla Muon (one extra state copy z_t), though the same as AdamW.
- The paper does not compare with some concurrent Muon variants (e.g., MOUSSE with curvature-aware preconditioning, Adam+Muon) in the main text — these appear only in Appendix F.1.
- BatchNorm requires special handling (PreciseBN-style recalibration for evaluation), though this is irrelevant for LLMs using RMSNorm.

## Relevance

AMUSE直接解决了大语言模型预训练中学习率调度和优化器不稳定两大痛点。其河-谷景观分析框架为理解优化器行为提供了新视角，1.51倍的收敛加速对训练成本极高的LLM预训练有重大实际价值。无需调度的特性与Muon优化器的改进均属于当前ML系统优化的核心研究方向。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does AMUSE perform at frontier scale (e.g., 7B, 70B, 405B parameters)? Does the 1.51x speedup hold or diminish?
- Can the river-valley analysis framework explain the behavior of other recent optimizers (AdEMAMix, C-AdamW, FOCUS) beyond Muon?
- Is the time-varying β_t schedule optimal, or could an adaptive schedule (e.g., based on dominant/bulk ratio monitoring) perform better?
- What happens when AMUSE is applied to fine-tuning or instruction-tuning of pretrained models (as opposed to pretraining from scratch)?
- Could the ρ parameter be eliminated entirely by deriving an optimal schedule from convergence theory?
- Does AMUSE interact favorably with distributed training techniques like tensor parallelism or pipeline parallelism?
- How does AMUSE compare to Muon + EMA in terms of final converged performance (not just speed-to-threshold)?
