More capable LLMs produce worse distributional forecasts on time series with superlinear growth and regime-change tail risk — and standard metrics hide this failure entirely.

Is Capability a Liability? More Capable Language Models Make Worse Forecasts When It Matters Most

Nick Merrill, Jaeho Lee, Ezra Karger

2026-05-21 LLM-forecasting inverse-scaling tail-risk evaluation-methodology

Key Metric

CRPS–ECI correlation flips from ρ=+0.67 at short horizons to ρ=−0.42 at long horizons (FBSim). On the same outputs, Brier shows ρ=+0.45 — opposite sign, identical data.

Why This Matters

Standard binary/threshold metrics systematically mask a dangerous failure mode. Models deployed for pandemic response, financial risk, or monetary policy may be worst at precisely the forecasts with the highest stakes.

Critical Edge

Failure isolates to p90 upper tail via per-quantile decomposition. SIR causal experiment: exponential growth triggers inverse scaling (ρ=−0.62) while matched linear control shows positive scaling (ρ=+0.61), CIs non-overlapping.

Methodology

Seven-layered experimental design: (1) FBSim benchmark built on FreeCiv AI-vs-AI game rollouts with 7 forecast horizons and 6 question templates; (2) Synthetic SIR epidemic series (50 realizations, 60-step history, forecast to 210 steps) with matched linear controls; (3) Within-family Llama-3.1 2×2 ablation ({70B, 405B}×{base, instruct}) across 100 SIR and 100 linear series; (4) Real-world replication across four domains (COVID-19 60 countries, housing bubble 19 metros, hyperinflation 12 episodes, US measles 1,339 state-seasons); (5) Prompt interventions (generic uncertainty, domain naming, full identity); (6) Metric comparison via paired binary/continuous questions on identical FBSim worlds; (7) Capability measurement via Epoch Capabilities Index (ECI) across 28–30 models with provider fixed effects and within-lineage replication.

Strengths

  • Counterintuitive finding with immediate high-stakes deployment implications.
  • Exceptional experimental design: causal isolation, negative controls, ex-ante unselected test, within-family ablation.
  • Metric-reversal on identical outputs is elegant and devastating for current evaluation practices.
  • Per-quantile decomposition provides mechanistic clarity: overcommitment, not random error.
  • Public benchmark (FBSim) released for community follow-up.

Limitations

  • Scope bounded to superlinear-growth + regime-change time series.
  • Three real-world domains selected on known regime change; only measles is unselected ex-ante.
  • Small per-domain sample sizes (N=12 hyperinflation, N=19 housing).
  • No mitigation or calibration strategy proposed beyond metric recommendations.
  • Knowledge–calibration gap mechanism deferred to future work.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • What internal mechanisms prevent recoverable domain knowledge (46/48 correct identification) from translating into calibrated tails?
  • Can targeted fine-tuning on tail-heavy distributions or regime-change scenarios mitigate overcommitment?
  • Does tool use (calling statistical software) affect the upper-tail bias?
  • Does inverse scaling appear in other structured prediction tasks with asymmetric loss?
  • Does RLHF reward modeling on threshold-based accuracy directly incentivize upper-tail overcommitment?
  • Could conformal prediction bound the upper-tail miscalibration?
