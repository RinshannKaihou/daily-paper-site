In self-play RL, data gating is the binding constraint on training stability — strict filtering keeps all reward variants stable, while removing the gate causes universal collapse regardless of reward design.
Survive or Collapse: The Asymmetric Roles of Data Gating and Reward Grounding in Self-Play RL
Sophia Xiao Pu, Zhaotian Weng, Chengzhi Liu, Jayanth Srinivasa et al. · 2026-05-21
self-play-RL reward-hacking data-gating training-stability LLM-reasoning
Key Metric
Gate-on: 0.71 in-domain accuracy (baseline 0.14). Gate-off: 0.002–0.007 across all reward variants. Training decouples at ε ≈ 0.05; validation collapses at ε ≈ 0.40.
Why This Matters
Challenges the dominant paradigm of reward engineering in self-play RL — prioritizing data pipeline quality over reward signal design reframes how these systems should be built.
Critical Edge
The “Grounded Proposer Paradox”: a proposer with oracle access paradoxically accelerates collapse with self-consistency rewards by generating clean tasks that form the fastest path to spurious consensus.

Methodology

The paper formalizes self-play RL as an asymmetric two-role system: Proposer (generates tasks) and Solver (answers), both updated via GRPO on Qwen3-4B-Base. Two task domains are tested: Python output-prediction and a synthetic 15-operator prefix-expression DSL designed to eliminate pretraining priors, output ambiguity, and executor noise. The experimental matrix spans 7 configurations across two axes: reward grounding (grounded vs. intrinsic/self-consistency) and data gating (exec-only vs. all-parseable). A continuous gate relaxation parameter ε ∈ [0, 1] controls the probability of admitting execution-failed tasks, swept at 7 values. Training runs up to 700 steps on 8× A100 40GB, with a 16,384-entry FIFO pool using recent+uniform sampling.

Strengths

  • Clean separation of two confounded axes with rigorous controlled experiments
  • DSL twin task elegantly isolates optimization dynamics from environment confounders
  • Grounded Proposer Paradox is a striking, memorable finding
  • Phase transition analysis provides practical guidance for practitioners
  • Well-written with clear formalization and thorough empirical coverage

Limitations

  • Only tested on Qwen3-4B-Base; scaling to larger models unexplored
  • Narrow task scope (output prediction); generalization to math/QA not shown
  • Proposer-capacity ceiling: <1% of outputs admitted late in training under strict gate
  • No formal theory for why validation holds despite training-side decoupling
  • Adaptive gate scheduling explored only briefly

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does the finding generalize to math reasoning (MATH, GSM8K) where executability checks are harder?
  2. Can the proposer-capacity ceiling be overcome through curriculum scheduling or external seeding?
  3. How does the asymmetry manifest in multi-agent systems beyond two-role self-play?
  4. What explains the two-stage phase transition theoretically?
  5. Does the Grounded Proposer Paradox apply whenever an oracle-access generator feeds self-consistency training?
