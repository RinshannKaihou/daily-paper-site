Hallucination as Commitment Failure: Larger LLMs Misfire Despite Knowing the Answer

Jewon Yeom, Jaewon Sok, Heejun Kim, Seonghyeon Park, Jeongjae Park, Taesup Kim | 2026-05-21

hallucination instruction-tuning scaling-laws interpretability LLM-reliability

16–47% of instruction-tuned LLM hallucinations occur when the correct answer concept already has substantial probability mass — the model "knows" the answer but disperses that mass across token variants instead of committing to one surface form, and this failure rate rises monotonically with scale.

Key Metric

16–47% of Instruct hallucinations have correct concept present; rate increases monotonically with model scale (0.5B–72B). Correct generations concentrate probability; hallucinations disperse it.

Why This Matters

Instruction tuning creates a scale-dependent sharpening mechanism where helpfulness and confident hallucination are two products of the same process. The commitment failure pattern is detectable in pre-generation hidden states, opening a path to internal monitoring.

Critical Edge

The failure rate increases with scale — larger models are more susceptible despite stronger representations. This is counterintuitive: more capable models hallucinate not because they lack knowledge, but because instruction tuning amplifies a commitment sharpening that can misfire.

Methodology

Abstract-only summary

The authors propose a semantic answer availability framework. Rather than checking whether a single correct token appears in top predictions, they aggregate probability mass across all token-level variants expressing the same answer concept (e.g., "Paris," " paris," "PARIS"). This determines whether the correct concept was already represented when the model committed to an answer.

Evaluation spans Qwen and Llama families from 0.5B to 72B parameters, in both Base and Instruct variants. For each hallucinated response, they check whether the correct concept had substantial probability mass. They then compare hallucinations against correctly answered questions with matched semantic support, analyzing how probability mass distributes across surface forms. The analysis extends to multi-token answers and examines pre-generation hidden states.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Novel conceptual framing that challenges the dominant "hallucination = knowledge gap" assumption.
  • Systematic evaluation across multiple model families and scales.
  • Practical implication: detection via probability dispersion rather than external verification.
  • Connects behavioral analysis (output distributions) to internal representations (hidden states).

Limitations

  • Semantic aggregation may not generalize to free-form or multi-faceted answers.
  • Restricted to Qwen and Llama; generalization to other architectures is untested.
  • Does not explain the remaining 53–84% of hallucinations lacking correct concept availability.
  • Scale correlation could confound with instruction-tuning data differences across sizes.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does the commitment failure mechanism apply to open-ended generation tasks or is it specific to factual QA?
  2. Can the probability dispersion signal serve as a real-time hallucination detector during inference?
  3. How does the mechanism interact with different alignment methods (DPO, RLHF variants, constitutional AI)?
  4. Is the scale-dependent increase a fundamental property of instruction tuning or an artifact of current training recipes?
  5. Can targeted fine-tuning on probability sharpening reduce the commitment failure rate?
