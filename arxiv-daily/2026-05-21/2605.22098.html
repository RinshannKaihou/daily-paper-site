TextTeacher: What Can Language Teach About Images?

Tobias Christian Nauen, Stanislav Frolov, Brian Bernhard Moser, Federico Raue, Ahmed Anwar, Andreas Dengel | 2026-05-21

vision-language knowledge-distillation image-classification representation-learning training-techniques

Frozen text-encoder embeddings of image captions act as feature-space preconditioners during early vision training, improving ImageNet accuracy by up to +2.7 p.p. with zero inference overhead — the text head is simply discarded at deployment.

Key Metric

+2.7 p.p. on ImageNet (DeiT-L), +8.4 p.p. at 50% label noise, +7.7 p.p. at 5% data. Outperforms CLIP/DINOv2 distillation at equal compute or matches accuracy with 33% less training time.

Why This Matters

The text guidance is concentrated in the first 10–50 epochs, acting as a feature-space preconditioner that accelerates semantic representation formation. Inference remains purely visual — no text components, no latency cost.

Critical Edge

Consistent gains across 15 architectures (ViT, DeiT, Swin, XCiT, ResNet), yet the most dramatic improvements come in practically important but under-studied settings: noisy labels and data scarcity, where gains reach +7.7 to +8.4 p.p.

Methodology

TextTeacher adds a dual-head training scheme on top of a standard vision classifier. Each training image is paired with a caption (generated by off-the-shelf captioners like CoCa or BLIP-L). A frozen text encoder (BERT-Large by default) produces embeddings that are corpus-level whitened to mitigate anisotropy.

Alongside the classification head, a text head projects the image representation into the text embedding space. Both heads share the same backbone. The total loss combines standard cross-entropy with a CLIP-style contrastive alignment loss, using adaptive weighting to equalize gradient magnitudes.

At inference, the text head and all text components are discarded, yielding a standard pure-vision model with no added latency or parameters. The most effective caption strategy uses CoCa captions processed by Qwen3-32B to extract keyword-style tags, though the method is robust to caption source and length.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Simple drop-in modification — no architecture changes, no inference overhead.
  • Consistent improvements across 15 architectures and multiple training regimes.
  • Strong results in label noise, data scarcity, and limited compute budgets.
  • Insightful analysis of why it works (early-phase preconditioning, CKA similarity).
  • Robust to caption source and length — works even with simple label-based captions.

Limitations

  • Only beneficial from scratch; harmful when finetuning pretrained weights not aligned with text space.
  • ResNet backbones show smaller or occasionally negative transfer gains.
  • Most effective caption pipeline (CoCa + Qwen3 tag extraction) requires significant upfront compute.
  • Modest gains (+0.6 to +1.7 p.p.) in standard full-data training.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can the early-phase preconditioning insight combine with other training enhancements (mixup, augmentations) for compounding gains?
  2. How does the method scale to larger models (ViT-H, ViT-G) and larger datasets (ImageNet-21k, JFT)?
  3. Could adaptive scheduling that detects preconditioning saturation replace the fixed schedule?
  4. What happens when applying TextTeacher to dense prediction tasks with region-level captions?
  5. Is the method effective for domain-specific datasets where general-purpose captioners may struggle?
