Conversation history systematically biases LLM-as-judge decisions toward the prevailing polarity (d = -0.17) — negative history is 62% stronger than positive, and just 5 turns is enough to fully saturate the effect.
AMEL: Accumulated Message Effects on LLM Judgments
Temkit Sid-Ali 2026-05-21
llm-evaluation cognitive-bias llm-as-judge conversation-history reproducibility
Key Metric
Cohen's d = -0.17 overall; d = -0.34 for uncertain items; negativity asymmetry 1.62x; effect saturates at 5 turns — confirmed across 75,898 API calls to 11 models from 4 providers.
Why This Matters
Directly undermines LLM-as-judge reliability in production — the same items evaluated in different conversation contexts yield systematically different results, threatening reproducibility of any LLM-based evaluation pipeline.
Critical Edge
First systematic multi-model quantification revealing rapid saturation (5 turns = 50 turns, no further accumulation) and a negativity asymmetry, with entropy-based stratification proving model uncertainty is the key moderator — not domain, not model size.
Methodology

A between-subjects design presents identical test items under four conditions: Baseline (system prompt only), No-saturated (90% prior "no" evaluations), Yes-saturated (90% prior "yes"), and Neutral (50/50). Context length N varies over {5, 10, 20, 50} turns with 10 repetitions per cell at temperature 1.0. Bias score is defined as BS = P(r* | treatment) − P(r* | baseline). Three binary evaluation domains are tested: code review ("Is this production-ready?"), content moderation ("Is this appropriate?"), and nutritional assessment ("Is this healthy?"). Test items span clear positive, ambiguous, and clear negative categories (7 each per domain). Eleven models are tested across OpenAI (GPT-4.1 Nano, GPT-5.2), Anthropic (Haiku, Sonnet, Opus 4.6), Google (Gemini 2.5 Flash/Pro), and 4 local OSS models. Statistical analysis includes Bonferroni-corrected t-tests, Cohen's d, paired negativity-asymmetry tests, Spearman correlation for accumulation, and a linear mixed-effects model with random intercepts.

Strengths & Limitations
Strengths
  • Massive scale: 75,898 API calls across 11 models from 4 providers.
  • Clean between-subjects design with four conditions and systematic variation.
  • Saturation finding (5 turns = 50 turns) is practically important and theoretically interesting.
  • Mechanism follow-ups (logprobs, semantic vs. token-level, positional) narrow explanations.
  • Entropy-based stratification is more informative than author labels alone.
Limitations
  • Single-author study: no inter-rater agreement for item categorization.
  • Only binary (yes/no) tasks; effect may differ for continuous or multi-class judgments.
  • Gemini data incomplete; Qwen3 30B had disk-full crash.
  • No mitigation strategies tested beyond "fresh context" recommendation.
  • Mechanistic experiments are exploratory — they narrow but do not isolate a single cause.
Questions for Further Exploration
  1. How does AMEL interact with chain-of-thought prompting — does explicit reasoning reduce or amplify the effect?
  2. Would saturation hold for 200+ turns, or is there a second accumulation regime?
  3. Can logprob-based correction methods reliably compensate for AMEL in production pipelines?
  4. Is the Qwen3 4B contrarian bias generalizable to other instruction-tuned models?
  5. How does AMEL manifest in non-binary settings (Likert scales, ranking, open-ended scoring)?
