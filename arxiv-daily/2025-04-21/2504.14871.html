Natural Fingerprints of Large Language Models

arXiv: 2504.14871

Date: 2025-04-21

https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.14871

一句话总结： 本文通过系统性控制实验表明，即使在完全相同的训练数据和架构下，仅因参数规模、优化器超参数、随机种子（初始化权重和训练数据顺序）等微小差异，LLM 也会在生成文本中留下可被分类器识别的"自然指纹"，其中训练数据顺序对词频分布的影响最为显著。
TL;DR: This paper shows that LLMs trained on the exact same dataset with the same architecture still produce distinguishable outputs -- a phenomenon the authors call "natural fingerprints." By systematically isolating training factors (parameter size, learning rate, weight decay, random seeds), they demonstrate that even different random seeds alone (affecting initialization or data ordering) create detectable model-specific patterns. Training data order has a stronger effect on word-frequency-level fingerprints than initialization does. Natural fingerprints diminish over longer training but never fully disappear, and persist through supervised fine-tuning. This finding implies that training dynamics, not just data, systematically shape LLM behavior.

核心贡献

  • 提出"自然指纹"(natural fingerprints)概念：LLM 在训练过程中自发产生的、可被分类器识别的模型特有输出模式，区别于人为设计的水印或指纹方案。
  • 证明即使在相同训练数据、相同架构下，仅因参数规模(0.5B/1B/3B)、学习率、权重衰减、随机种子等训练配置差异，模型输出仍可被区分。
  • 深入分析随机性的两个来源：训练数据顺序对词频分布的影响远大于初始化权重的差异，但两者均会产生可检测的自然指纹。
  • 发现自然指纹随训练进行逐渐减弱（模型趋向收敛），但在实际训练条件下无法完全消除。
  • 证明自然指纹在监督微调(SFT)阶段同样存在，微调过程可放大或保留预训练中的偏差。

Key Contributions

  • Introduce the concept of natural fingerprints -- unintended, distinctive characteristics in LLM outputs that emerge spontaneously during training without explicit design, as opposed to deliberate watermarking or fingerprinting methods.
  • Demonstrate that even with identical training data and architecture, factors like parameter size, learning rate, weight decay, and random seeds produce distinguishable model outputs (e.g., Transformer-based classifier achieves 55.6% accuracy on same-data 0.5B/1B/3B models vs. 33.3% chance rate).
  • Isolate two sources of randomness: training data order has a larger impact on word-frequency-level fingerprints than initialization weights, though both contribute to detectable patterns.
  • Show that natural fingerprints diminish over training as models converge on the same data distribution, but never fully disappear in practical training regimes.
  • Demonstrate that natural fingerprints persist through supervised fine-tuning (SFT), even when starting from the same pretrained weights and fine-tuning on the same dataset.

Methodology

The paper adapts the classical dataset classification paradigm (Torralba and Efros, 2011) to an LLM classification task. The core idea is straightforward: if a classifier can predict which model generated a given text at above-chance accuracy, the models embed identifiable characteristics.

Experimental pipeline:

  1. Prompt sampling: First 50 characters from 1M web pages (CommonCrawl subset of SlimPajama) serve as prompts.
  2. Text generation: Each model generates 512-token continuations via pure sampling.
  3. Deduplication: Semantic deduplication removes near-duplicate samples.
  4. Classification: Two classifiers are trained -- (a) a unigram-based logistic regression (bag-of-subwords features) probing word-frequency-level patterns, and (b) a DeBERTaV3 transformer probing higher-order linguistic patterns.

Progressive control experiments:

  • Section 4.1 (Baseline): Classify outputs from 7 public LLM families (Llama2, Mistral, Qwen2, Falcon, Pythia, Sarashina2) -- all ~7B parameters but trained on different data. Transformer classifier achieves 85.0% accuracy (vs. 16.7% chance).
  • Section 4.2 (Same data, different sizes): Train 0.5B, 1B, 3B models on 100B tokens from FineWeb-Edu. Accuracy drops but remains well above chance (55.6% transformer, 39.0% unigram).
  • Section 4.3 (Same data, same size, different hyperparameters): Train seven 1B models varying learning rate, weight decay, and random seeds one at a time. Classification remains above chance for all factors.
  • Section 4.4 (Isolating randomness): Decompose random seed into initialization vs. data order effects. Data order has stronger unigram-level impact; both factors produce detectable fingerprints. Extended training (1T tokens) shows fingerprints diminish but persist.
  • Section 4.5 (SFT): Fine-tune 1B pretrained models on Alpaca dataset with varying data order or initialization. Fingerprints persist and remain detectable (~40% accuracy vs. 33.3% chance).

Results & Highlights

  • Public LLMs are highly distinguishable: Transformer-based classifier reaches 85.0% across 6 model families, and even unigram features achieve 49.2% (chance: 16.7%).
  • Same-data models remain distinguishable: With identical training corpora, 0.5B/1B/3B models classified at 55.6% by transformer (chance: 33.3%).
  • Random seed alone is sufficient: Models differing only in random seed (affecting both init and data order) produce outputs distinguishable at above-chance rates.
  • Data order > initialization for word-frequency patterns: Unigram accuracy is higher when data order varies (46.6%) than when initialization varies (38.9%).
  • Training duration reduces fingerprints: Over 1T tokens of training, classification accuracy trends downward as models converge, but does not reach chance level.
  • SFT preserves and introduces fingerprints: Even fine-tuning the same base model on the same data in different orders yields ~40% classification accuracy.

Critical Assessment

Novelty (Moderate-High): The concept of "natural fingerprints" extending beyond training data to training dynamics is genuinely novel. While prior work (Antoun et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2025) established that different LLMs produce distinguishable outputs, this paper isolates the specific training factors responsible -- a meaningful advance. The decomposition of random seed into initialization vs. data order is a clean experimental design choice.

Rigor (High): The progressive experimental design -- starting from public models, then controlling data, then size, then hyperparameters, then isolating randomness -- is methodologically strong. Using two classifier types (unigram vs. transformer) to probe different levels of pattern complexity is informative. Multiple random splits (3 runs) with standard deviations reported. Downstream task scores confirm comparable model quality.

Reproducibility (High): Training details are provided in appendices. Use of publicly available datasets (FineWeb-Edu, SlimPajama, Alpaca) and standard tools (Megatron-LM, LIBLINEAR, DeBERTaV3). The experimental setup is relatively straightforward to replicate, though training multiple 1B-3B models requires significant compute (800 GPU-hours per 1B model on H100).

Significance (Moderate): The finding that training dynamics alone create identifiable patterns has practical implications for model provenance, accountability, and understanding unintended biases. However, the paper primarily establishes the phenomenon and does not propose methods to mitigate or exploit it. The practical impact depends on whether these insights lead to actionable recommendations for training or deployment.

Potential concerns:

  • The paper uses pretrained base models (not instruction-tuned), which have simpler generation patterns. Whether natural fingerprints manifest similarly in instruction-tuned or RLHF-aligned models remains unexplored.
  • The 1M-sample-per-class classification setup is generous; real-world detection scenarios may have far fewer samples.
  • The paper does not analyze why certain tokens become fingerprints or whether these patterns affect downstream task performance beyond distinguishability.

Strengths

  • Exceptional experimental control: The progressive elimination of confounding factors (data, size, hyperparameters, randomness sources) is methodologically exemplary and provides clear causal attribution.
  • Dual-classifier design: Using both unigram and transformer classifiers reveals the level at which fingerprints operate -- word frequency vs. higher-order patterns -- which is analytically insightful.
  • Practical relevance: The findings connect to important real-world concerns about model identification, bias, and reproducibility.
  • Clear writing and structure: The paper progresses logically from known phenomena to increasingly controlled experiments.
  • Honest about limitations: The authors acknowledge that natural fingerprints diminish with longer training and are subtle when data/architecture are controlled.

Limitations

  • Scope limited to pretrained models: The study focuses on base pretrained models. Instruction-tuned, RLHF-aligned, or reasoning models may exhibit different fingerprinting behavior.
  • No mitigation strategies proposed: The paper identifies the problem but does not explore how to reduce or control natural fingerprints.
  • Classification as proxy: Using classification accuracy as the sole metric of distinguishability does not reveal the practical severity of the fingerprinting -- e.g., how many samples are needed for reliable attribution in adversarial settings.
  • Model scale: Experiments use up to 3B parameters. Whether findings scale to 70B+ models is unknown (though the public model experiments suggest they do).
  • English-centric: All experiments use English data. Multilingual settings may yield different results.
  • No analysis of downstream impact: It remains unclear whether natural fingerprints actually harm model performance or fairness in practical applications.

Relevance

本文对 LLM 可解释性研究具有重要参考价值：揭示了训练动态本身（而非仅训练数据）会导致模型输出中产生可识别的系统性偏差模式，这对理解模型行为的来源、评估模型输出的可靠性以及训练复现性问题都有直接启示，也为未来研究 LLM 训练稳定性与推理评估提供了新的分析维度。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Do natural fingerprints survive paraphrasing, translation, or adversarial text perturbations? If so, how robust are they?
  2. How do natural fingerprints interact with RLHF alignment -- does alignment amplify, suppress, or reshape these patterns?
  3. Can natural fingerprints be intentionally minimized through specific training strategies (e.g., ensembled initialization, deterministic data ordering, regularization)?
  4. What is the minimum number of generated samples needed to reliably attribute text to a specific model in an adversarial setting?
  5. Do natural fingerprints correlate with measurable differences in downstream task performance, fairness metrics, or safety evaluations?
  6. How do natural fingerprints manifest in multimodal models or models with significantly different architectures (e.g., Mamba, hybrid architectures)?
