## 一句话总结

本文系统对比了欧盟《人工智能法案》通用AI行为准则（第三草案）中的安全与安保措施与12家以上领先AI公司现有自愿承诺，发现72项措施中52项已有充分行业先例，表明该准则主要对齐而非超越现有行业实践。

## TL;DR

This 166-page report from the Oxford Martin AI Governance Initiative performs a systematic comparison between the EU AI Act's General-Purpose AI (GPAI) Code of Practice (Third Draft) Safety and Security measures (Commitments II.1-II.16) and the current voluntary commitments of 12+ leading AI companies (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Microsoft, Meta, Amazon, NVIDIA, xAI, Cohere, G42, Magic, Naver). The key finding: out of 72 measures analyzed, 52 had at least 3 separate company documents providing relevant supporting quotes, and the majority reached saturation of 5 or more relevant quotes. This suggests the Code of Practice largely codifies existing industry precedent rather than imposing novel regulatory burdens. However, several measures showed thinner coverage, particularly around systemic risk assessment methodology (II.1.3), AI self-improvement monitoring (II.5.1), red-teaming specificity (II.8.3), and incident response protocols (II.11, II.12, II.13). The paper is essential reading for AI policy professionals, compliance teams at GPAI providers, and anyone tracking the practical implications of the EU AI Act's implementation.

## Key Contributions

1. **First systematic measure-by-measure mapping**: Provides the first detailed comparison of all 72 Safety and Security measures in the GPAI Code of Practice (Third Draft) against current industry commitments, establishing a comprehensive baseline for regulatory alignment assessment
2. **Quantitative precedent analysis**: Demonstrates that 52 out of 72 Commitments and Measures have at least 3 separate company documents with relevant quotes, with the majority reaching saturation (5+ quotes), suggesting the Code largely reflects rather than extends existing practice
3. **Gap identification for policymakers**: Identifies specific measures with thinner industry coverage (Table 1), including systemic risk assessment methodology, AI self-improvement monitoring, detailed red-teaming protocols, and incident response mechanisms, providing actionable priorities for regulatory refinement
4. **Cross-company comparative methodology**: Develops and applies a systematic review methodology across 12+ companies' public-facing documents (frontier safety frameworks, model cards, acceptable use policies, system cards), offering a replicable approach for future regulatory alignment studies
5. **Practical compliance roadmap**: Translates abstract regulatory obligations into concrete industry benchmarks, enabling GPAI providers to assess their readiness for EU AI Act enforcement

## Methodology

The authors conducted a systematic document review across multiple categories of public-facing materials from 12+ leading AI companies:

**Document Types Reviewed:**
- Frontier Safety Frameworks (e.g., Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy, OpenAI's Preparedness Framework)
- Model Cards and System Cards
- Acceptable Use Policies
- Corporate blog posts and policy announcements
- Transparency and safety reports

**Companies Covered:**
Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Meta, Amazon, Microsoft, G42, xAI, NVIDIA, Cohere, Magic, Naver, and others.

**Analysis Framework:**
- Each of the 72 measures in Commitments II.1-II.16 was treated as an individual unit of analysis
- For each measure, the authors collected relevant excerpts from company documents
- Coverage was quantified by counting the number of distinct company documents with relevant quotes per measure
- A threshold of 3+ companies was used to indicate "strong precedent," while measures with fewer quotes were flagged as having thinner coverage
- The analysis focused on the Safety and Security section specifically (Part II of the Code), covering areas including: safety frameworks, risk assessment, CBRN risks, cybersecurity, red-teaming, model evaluation, incident response, and systemic risk governance

**Key Risk Categories Addressed:**
- CBRN (Chemical, Biological, Radiological, Nuclear) threats
- Cyber offense capabilities
- Loss of control and AI autonomy risks
- AI self-improvement and recursive improvement scenarios
- Capability thresholds (High vs. Critical)

## Results & Highlights

- **52 of 72 measures (72%)** had at least 3 separate company documents with relevant quotes, indicating strong industry precedent for the majority of the Code's requirements
- **Majority of measures reached saturation** (5+ relevant quotes), suggesting these measures are well-aligned with existing voluntary commitments
- **Measures with thinner coverage** include: systemic risk assessment methodology (II.1.3), AI self-improvement monitoring (II.5.1), specific red-teaming protocols (II.8.3 with multiple sub-measures flagged), incident disclosure and response (II.11.1, II.12, II.13), and some capability evaluation measures (II.4.9-II.4.14)
- **Company-specific findings**: Leading frontier AI companies (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind) tend to have the most comprehensive documentation relevant to the Code's measures, while smaller or newer entrants show less alignment
- **Framework alignment**: The Code's structure maps well to existing frontier safety frameworks, suggesting regulatory drafters drew substantially from industry precedents
- **Notable gap areas**: The measures with least coverage tend to involve more speculative or emerging concerns (AI self-improvement, detailed incident response chains) rather than established safety practices

## Critical Assessment

**Overall Quality**: This is a well-executed systematic mapping exercise that provides genuine value for both policymakers and industry practitioners. The measure-by-measure approach is methodologically sound and the inclusion of direct company quotes lends transparency and verifiability to the analysis.

**Key Strengths of the Analysis:**
- The quantitative coverage metric (counting relevant quotes per measure) provides a clear, reproducible measure of industry alignment
- The paper avoids normative claims about whether the Code should be stronger or weaker, instead presenting empirical evidence about alignment
- Including lesser-known companies alongside major players provides breadth

**Key Weaknesses:**
- **Relies on public-facing documents only**: Company internal practices may differ significantly from their published commitments. A safety framework's existence does not guarantee faithful implementation
- **Binary coverage metric**: Counting the number of relevant quotes does not capture the depth, quality, or specificity of alignment. A brief mention in a blog post is weighted equivalently to a detailed framework commitment
- **Snapshot in time**: Company policies evolve rapidly; the analysis reflects a specific point in time and may not capture subsequent updates
- **Third Draft focus**: The Code of Practice was still being refined at the time of analysis; the final version may differ significantly
- **No outcome-based assessment**: The paper maps procedural commitments but does not evaluate whether these commitments have led to meaningful safety outcomes
- **Survivorship bias in coverage**: Companies with more published documents appear more "aligned," potentially conflating transparency with actual safety practice quality

## Strengths

- **Comprehensive scope**: Covers all 72 Safety and Security measures across 12+ companies, providing unmatched breadth
- **Systematic and reproducible methodology**: Clear framework for measuring industry precedent makes the analysis auditable and updatable
- **Policy-relevant timing**: Published during the active development of the Code of Practice, maximizing potential impact on regulatory outcomes
- **Direct quote inclusion**: Enables readers to independently assess the quality and relevance of company commitments
- **Gap identification**: Table 1 provides a concise summary of measures needing further attention, directly useful for policymakers
- **Balanced framing**: Positions findings as alignment assessment rather than compliance judgment

## Limitations

- **Public documentation bias**: Only public-facing documents were reviewed; internal practices, unpublished evaluations, and classified safety assessments were not accessible
- **Equating mention with commitment**: A company mentioning a concept in a blog post is treated similarly to a detailed binding framework, overstating alignment in some cases
- **Rapidly evolving landscape**: Company policies and the Code itself are both moving targets; findings may become outdated within months
- **Geographic scope limitation**: Focuses primarily on US and EU companies; AI safety practices in other jurisdictions (China, etc.) are not covered
- **No implementation verification**: Documents commitments without assessing whether companies follow through in practice
- **Sample selection**: The 12+ companies were likely selected based on document availability rather than systematic sampling criteria

2. What would a similar analysis look like for the Transparency (Part III) and Copyright (Part IV) sections of the Code of Practice?
3. How will the final version of the Code of Practice differ from the Third Draft analyzed here, and will the industry alignment patterns hold?
4. For the 20+ measures with thinner coverage, what specific policy interventions would be most effective in building industry capacity?
5. How do the safety commitments of Chinese AI companies (e.g., Baidu, Alibaba, Zhipu) compare to the Code of Practice measures, and what does this imply for international regulatory harmonization?
6. What outcome-based metrics could supplement the document-based coverage analysis to assess whether procedural commitments translate into real safety improvements?
7. How should regulators handle the inherent lag between rapidly evolving AI capabilities and the slower pace of policy document updates?
