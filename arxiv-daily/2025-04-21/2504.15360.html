Reliable Classification with Conformal Learning and Interval-Type 2 Fuzzy Sets

arXiv: 2504.15360

Date: 2025-04-21

https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.15360

一句话总结： 本文将保形学习（conformal learning）与区间二型模糊规则分类器相结合，利用区间运算和 K_alpha 算子定义非一致性分数的序关系，并通过规则级别的保形预测和自定义适应度函数来减小预测集大小，在保证覆盖率的条件下提升了分类器的可靠性和可解释性。
TL;DR: This paper integrates conformal prediction with fuzzy rule-based classifiers (FRBCs), extending the framework to Interval-Type 2 (IV-T2) fuzzy sets via admissible interval ordering. It introduces rule-wise conformal analysis to diagnose unreliable rules and a custom fitness function that penalizes rule support on incorrect classes. Experiments on 10 KEEL datasets show that IV-T2 systems yield higher non-empty prediction rates and better rule-level F1 scores than Type-1 counterparts, though at the cost of slightly larger prediction sets and more tuning effort. The work is a solid but incremental contribution at the intersection of explainable AI and distribution-free uncertainty quantification.

  • 将保形预测（conformal prediction）框架与模糊规则分类器（FRBC）结合，使分类器输出不再是单一类别标签，而是一个保证覆盖率（如 95%）的预测集。
  • 将区间二型模糊集（Interval-Type 2, IV-T2）引入保形学习，通过 K_alpha 算子定义区间的全序关系，从而在区间值预测上计算非一致性分数分位数。
  • 提出规则级保形分析（rule-wise conformal learning），可以逐条规则评估其可靠性，帮助诊断和修正导致错误预测的特定规则。
  • 设计了一种自定义适应度函数，在遗传算法优化过程中惩罚对错误类别有过高支持的规则，从而减小保形预测集的平均大小。
  • 实验证明 IV-T2 系统在非空预测比例和规则级 F1 分数上优于 Type-1 系统，尤其在配合自定义适应度函数时效果更显著。

Key Contributions

  • Integrates conformal prediction with fuzzy rule-based classifiers (FRBCs), replacing point predictions with guaranteed-coverage prediction sets.
  • Extends conformal learning to Interval-Type 2 (IV-T2) fuzzy sets by using the K_alpha admissible ordering operator to handle interval-valued nonconformity scores and quantiles.
  • Proposes rule-wise conformal analysis: per-rule nonconformity histograms that identify which rules consistently fire on incorrect classes, enabling targeted rule refinement.
  • Designs a conformal-aware fitness function for the genetic algorithm that adds a Laplacian penalty term (Eq. 12) reducing the firing strength of rules on non-target classes, thereby shrinking average prediction set size.
  • Demonstrates experimentally on 10 KEEL datasets that IV-T2 FRBCs achieve higher non-empty prediction rates and better per-rule F1 scores than Type-1 FRBCs, especially when combined with the custom fitness function.

Methodology

The paper builds on three pillars: conformal prediction, fuzzy rule-based classification, and Interval-Type 2 fuzzy sets.

  1. Baseline conformal FRBC: A standard Type-1 fuzzy rule-based classifier is trained using a genetic algorithm optimizing the Matthew Correlation Coefficient (MCC). Nonconformity scores are computed via cross-conformal prediction (K-fold cross-validation aggregated scores). Given a significance level alpha, prediction sets are constructed as all classes whose predicted score exceeds the (1 - q_hat) threshold.
  1. Interval-Type 2 extension: When the FRBC uses IV-T2 fuzzy sets (with lower membership capped at 0.8), predictions become intervals rather than scalars. The authors adapt conformal prediction by:
  • Computing interval-valued nonconformity scores: S_i = (1,1) - f_hat_IV(X_i)_{Y_i}.
  • Using the K_alpha operator (a convex combination of interval endpoints) to define a total admissible order on intervals.
  • Computing quantiles under this order and constructing prediction sets accordingly.
  1. Rule-wise conformal analysis: Each fuzzy rule is treated as an independent sub-classifier. Nonconformity histograms are built at the classifier level but applied per-rule, allowing identification of "spurious" rules that fire on incorrect classes. This is computationally cheaper than building per-rule histograms from scratch.
  1. Conformal-aware fitness function: An additional penalty term L_2 (Eq. 12) is added to the genetic algorithm's fitness function. This term penalizes rules whose firing strength on incorrect classes is high. Combined with MCC via a 0.01 multiplier, it encourages the GA to learn rules that produce sharper class separations in conformal prediction space.
  1. Experimental setup: 10 classification datasets from KEEL, 80/20 train-test split, 5-fold cross-validation for conformal calibration, max 15 rules per classifier, max 3 antecedents per rule, 3 linguistic labels (low/medium/high). Results compare T1 vs. IV-T2, standard vs. custom fitness, across multiple significance levels.

Results & Highlights

  • Accuracy: Both T1 and IV-T2 FRBCs achieve comparable classification accuracy across 10 datasets (detailed in Table II). IV-T2 occasionally underperforms T1 due to requiring more parameters to tune.
  • Prediction set size: IV-T2 produces marginally larger prediction sets on average compared to T1, but with a higher proportion of non-empty predictions at the same significance level -- meaning IV-T2 is less likely to output an empty (uninformative) prediction set.
  • Custom fitness function impact: The conformal-aware fitness function significantly reduces prediction set size and standard deviation for IV-T2 systems without reducing non-empty prediction rates. T1 systems show little improvement from this modification.
  • Rule-level F1 scores: Both T1 and IV-T2 show increasing per-rule F1 scores at higher significance levels, primarily due to improved recall. IV-T2 rules consistently outperform T1 rules on F1.
  • Trade-off insight: The results reveal a practical trade-off: IV-T2 requires more computational effort (more generations in GA) but delivers more reliable rules and better uncertainty quantification when properly tuned.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: Moderate. The combination of conformal prediction with fuzzy classifiers is natural but underexplored. The IV-T2 extension using admissible interval orders is the most technically novel contribution. The rule-wise conformal analysis and custom fitness function are straightforward extensions rather than deep methodological innovations.
  • Rigor: Acceptable but limited. The paper uses 10 relatively small KEEL datasets (e.g., Iris, Wine, Ecoli). No comparison with other uncertainty quantification baselines such as Bayesian neural networks, Monte Carlo dropout, deep ensembles, or even standard conformal predictors applied to other classifiers (e.g., random forests). Statistical significance tests (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank) are absent.
  • Reproducibility: Reasonably good. The authors use the publicly available Ex-Fuzzy library, describe the fuzzy partition setup, and specify hyperparameters (15 rules, 3 antecedents, 3 labels, 0.01 fitness multiplier). However, exact GA parameters (population size, number of generations, mutation rate) are not specified, and the code for the experiments is not linked.
  • Significance: The work is relevant for practitioners who need explainable classifiers with distribution-free uncertainty guarantees, particularly in regulated domains. However, the scalability to higher-dimensional or modern deep learning problems remains untested.
  • Red flags: (1) The paper is only 6 pages, including references, limiting depth of analysis and discussion. (2) IV-T2 underperforms T1 in some metrics, which the authors attribute to insufficient GA generations but do not empirically validate. (3) No baseline comparison with other conformal classifiers makes it hard to assess the practical value added by the fuzzy component. (4) The paper mentions Section V for results but the numbering skips Section IV in the text (labeled "Methods"), suggesting a hasty submission.

Strengths

  • Clean formulation of the interval-valued conformal prediction problem using admissible orders, which is mathematically sound.
  • The rule-wise conformal analysis is a practical diagnostic tool that leverages the inherent interpretability of fuzzy rule-based systems.
  • The conformal-aware fitness function is a simple but effective idea that exploits the structure of conformal prediction during training.
  • Good use of cross-conformal prediction for data-efficient calibration.
  • The paper addresses a genuinely useful intersection: explainable classifiers with rigorous uncertainty quantification.

Limitations

  • Experiments are limited to small, low-dimensional datasets (max ~18 features). Generalization to real-world high-dimensional problems is unknown.
  • No comparison with other conformal prediction baselines (e.g., conformal applied to random forests, gradient boosting, or neural networks).
  • IV-T2 results are not conclusively better than T1 on accuracy or prediction set size; the main advantage is higher non-empty prediction rates, which is a narrow metric.
  • GA optimization details are underspecified, making exact reproduction difficult.
  • The paper is very short (6 pages) and would benefit from more thorough ablation studies and statistical testing.
  • The claim that IV-T2 rules are "more reliable" is not rigorously substantiated beyond F1 score trends.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does the conformal FRBC framework scale to datasets with hundreds or thousands of features (e.g., text or image domains)?
  • Can the rule-wise conformal analysis be extended to deep learning models with rule extraction (e.g., neural-fuzzy systems)?
  • What is the sensitivity of the IV-T2 results to the choice of K_alpha parameters (alpha, beta) for the admissible interval order?
  • Would a deeper comparison with Bayesian fuzzy systems or credal set-based approaches reveal clearer advantages for the conformal approach?
  • Can the conformal-aware fitness function idea be generalized to other classifier families (e.g., decision trees, SVMs) where conformal prediction is applied post-hoc?
  • How does the framework perform under distribution shift, where the exchangeability assumption of conformal prediction is violated?
