Values in the Wild: Discovering and Analyzing Values in Real-World Language Model Interactions

arXiv: 2504.15236

Date: 2025-04-21

https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.15236

一句话总结： Anthropic团队通过隐私保护的大规模分析方法，从30万条Claude真实对话中自下而上地发现了3307种AI价值观，构建了首个大规模实证AI价值观分类体系（实用、认知、社会、保护、个人五大类），并揭示了AI价值观如何随任务类型和用户价值观动态变化——Claude在多数情况下支持用户价值观，但在面对"道德虚无主义"等有害价值观时表现出强烈的伦理抵抗。
TL;DR: This paper presents the first large-scale empirical taxonomy of AI values by analyzing 308K+ real-world Claude conversations using privacy-preserving methods. The authors discover 3,307 unique AI values and organize them into a 4-level hierarchy with 5 top-level categories (Practical 31.4%, Epistemic 22.2%, Social 21.4%, Protective 13.9%, Personal 11.1%). Key findings: (1) a handful of service-oriented values dominate (helpfulness 23.4%, professionalism 22.9%, transparency 17.4%), (2) values are highly context-dependent (e.g., "healthy boundaries" in relationship advice, "human agency" in AI ethics), (3) Claude mirrors prosocial human values but resists harmful ones like "moral nihilism" with opposing ethical values, and (4) values become more explicitly stated during moments of resistance. The taxonomy and dataset are released on HuggingFace. This is a significant empirical contribution to understanding how high-level alignment principles (helpful, harmless, honest) manifest as specific contextual expressions in deployment.

核心贡献

  • 首个大规模实证AI价值观分类体系：从30万条真实Claude对话中自下而上发现3307种AI价值观，构建四层分级分类法（266个一级聚类、26个二级聚类、5个顶级类别：实用、认知、社会、保护、个人），突破了以往依赖人类心理测量框架（如Schwartz价值观理论）评估AI的局限。
  • 揭示了AI价值观的上下文依赖性：通过卡方检验量化分析，证明许多价值观高度依赖于具体任务（如关系建议中的"健康边界"、AI伦理讨论中的"人类能动性"），而非静态不变。
  • 发现了AI-人类价值观互动的动态模式：Claude在面对亲社会价值观时倾向于镜像反映（如用户表达"authenticity"时回应"authenticity"），但在面对有害价值观时使用对立价值观进行抵抗（如用"伦理诚信"回应"欺骗"）。
  • 提供了对HHH训练原则的实证检验：验证了"helpful, harmless, honest"高层训练原则如何转化为具体的上下文表达（如"accessibility"映射到helpfulness，"historical accuracy"映射到honesty）。
  • 发现了安全漏洞检测的应用价值：通过识别稀有但不良的价值观（如"sexual exploitation"、"dominance"），成功发现并上报潜在越狱攻击。

Key Contributions

  • First large-scale empirical taxonomy of AI values in real-world deployment: Discovered 3,307 unique AI values from 308,210 real Claude conversations, organized into a 4-level hierarchical taxonomy with 5 top-level categories (Practical, Epistemic, Social, Protective, Personal), moving beyond human-centric frameworks like Schwartz's Theory of Basic Values.
  • Quantified context-dependency of AI values: Used chi-square analysis with adjusted Pearson residuals and Bonferroni correction to rigorously demonstrate that AI values are highly task-dependent, e.g., "healthy boundaries" in relationship advice, "historical accuracy" in controversial events analysis, "human agency" in technology ethics discussions.
  • Mapped dynamic human-AI value interaction patterns: Showed that Claude mirrors prosocial values (authenticity-to-authenticity), deploys complementary values (competence met with accountability and humility), and actively opposes harmful values (deception met with ethical integrity and honesty).
  • Empirical validation of HHH alignment framework: Demonstrated how abstract "helpful, harmless, honest" training principles manifest as thousands of specific contextual expressions in deployment, confirming intended behavior while identifying gaps.
  • Practical safety application: The bottom-up value discovery method surfaced uncommon but undesirable values (e.g., "sexual exploitation", "dominance"), enabling identification of potential jailbreaks for safety team investigation.
  • Released taxonomy and data publicly: The full value taxonomy and individual value frequencies are available on HuggingFace (https://huggingface.co/datasets/Anthropic/values-in-the-wild/).

Methodology

The authors develop a four-stage pipeline built on Tamkin et al. (2024)'s Clio framework for privacy-preserving analysis:

  1. Data Collection: Starting from 700K anonymized Claude.ai conversations (Feb 18-25, 2025, primarily Claude 3.5 Sonnet), they filter to 308,210 subjective conversations (44%) using a 4-level subjectivity classifier (keeping Levels 3-4). The subjectivity filter was validated at 94% accuracy by human reviewers.
  1. Feature Extraction: Using Claude 3.5 Sonnet and Haiku to extract multiple features from conversations in a privacy-preserving manner (no human access to raw conversations; features are aggregated with minimum size thresholds):
  • AI values: Normative considerations guiding model responses, both implicit and explicit (e.g., "intellectual curiosity", "personal agency"), extracted via revealed preference-inspired analysis
  • Human values: Only explicitly stated values (more conservative to respect privacy)
  • AI response type: 7-point scale from strong support to strong resistance
  • Task: Primary purpose/request type of each conversation

Feature extraction was validated by 6 independent annotators: 98.8% accuracy for AI values, 93.8% for human values, 90.0% for response type.

  1. Values Hierarchy Construction: Hierarchical clustering using k-means on all-mpnet-base-v2 embeddings across 4 levels: 3,307 base values -> 266 first-level -> 26 second-level -> 5 top-level clusters. Cluster names are generated by Claude and manually refined.
  1. Chi-Square Analysis: Adjusted Pearson residuals measure deviations from expected frequencies under independence, enabling comparison of value-task and value-value associations. Bonferroni correction controls family-wise error rate across thousands of comparisons.

Results & Highlights

  • Value distribution is extremely concentrated: Just 5 values (helpfulness 23.4%, professionalism 22.9%, transparency 17.4%, clarity 16.6%, thoroughness 14.3%) represent ~24% of all AI value occurrences, while 75% of values occur less than 0.04% of the time -- a very long tail spanning from "architectural clarity" to "filial piety".
  • Context-invariant vs. context-dependent values: "Transparency" (CV=1.23), "helpfulness" (CV=1.30), and "thoroughness" (CV=1.42) are the most task-invariant values, representing Claude's stable service-oriented tendencies. Many other values are highly specialized and context-dependent.
  • Response type distribution: Strong support (28.2%), mild support (14.5%), neutral acknowledgment (9.6%), reframing (6.6%), mild resistance (2.4%), strong resistance (3.0%). Resistance is rare and concentrated in policy-violating contexts.
  • Value mirroring: Same value appears on both human and AI sides in 20.1% of supportive interactions but only 1.2% during strong resistance.
  • Cross-model differences: Claude 3 Opus is more "values-laden" than Sonnet variants, with higher rates of both strong support (43.8% vs ~28%) and strong resistance (9.5% vs ~2-3%), and prioritizes values like "academic rigor", "emotional authenticity", and "ethical boundaries".
  • Implicit vs. explicit values: Ethical and epistemic values (e.g., "intellectual honesty", "harm prevention", "epistemic humility") are disproportionately stated explicitly during moments of resistance and reframing, while routine service values remain implicit.

Critical Assessment

Novelty (High): This is the first large-scale empirical study of AI values in real-world deployment. Prior work relied on applying human psychometric frameworks (Big Five, Schwartz values, Moral Foundations) to models via static evaluations, which the authors convincingly argue are fundamentally limited. The bottom-up discovery approach and the resulting 3,307-value taxonomy are genuinely novel contributions.

Rigor (Moderate-High): The methodology is systematic and well-validated in several respects (subjectivity filter at 94%, feature extraction at 98.8% for AI values). The chi-square analysis with Bonferroni correction is appropriate. However, there are notable limitations:

  • Using Claude to evaluate Claude conversations introduces potential bias (acknowledged by authors), as Claude may be predisposed to find "helpful" behavior
  • No inter-rater reliability testing was conducted -- all human validation was performed by 6 independent reviewers but without measuring agreement between them
  • The subjectivity of "value extraction" is inherently interpretive, and the operational definition of values is deliberately pragmatic rather than philosophically rigorous
  • Privacy constraints limit analysis to aggregated statistics, preventing individual-level longitudinal analysis

Reproducibility (Moderate): The taxonomy and value frequencies are released on HuggingFace, and prompts are provided in appendices. However, the core data (real Claude conversations) cannot be shared due to privacy. The methodology relies on proprietary infrastructure (Clio) and proprietary models (Claude 3.5 Sonnet/Haiku), making exact reproduction impossible for external researchers. Application to other models using public datasets (e.g., WildChat) is feasible but would yield different results.

Significance (High): This work has substantial implications for AI alignment research, safety evaluation, and transparency reporting. It demonstrates a practical methodology for monitoring value expression in deployed systems, bridges the gap between abstract training principles and observable behavior, and provides empirical grounding for developing AI-native value frameworks. The paper has already been cited by follow-up works (VAL-Bench, Value Lens).

Potential red flags:

  • The paper essentially evaluates Anthropic's own product using Anthropic's own models and infrastructure, creating an inherent conflict of interest. The findings are overwhelmingly positive about Claude's value alignment.
  • The framing positions Anthropic favorably (Claude supports prosocial values, resists harmful ones, behaves as intended), which could be seen as marketing-adjacent research despite genuine scientific contribution.
  • The definition of "values" is very broad -- any normative consideration counts -- which may overcount trivial operational preferences as "values" (e.g., is "clarity" really a "value" in the same sense as "human dignity"?).
  • The analysis is limited to a one-week snapshot, raising questions about temporal stability.

Strengths

  • Scale and ecological validity: 308K+ real conversations provide unprecedented empirical grounding compared to synthetic or lab-based evaluations.
  • Privacy-preserving methodology: Defense-in-depth approach with model-based extraction, aggregation thresholds, and audit steps provides a template for responsible analysis of sensitive deployment data.
  • Bottom-up, data-driven taxonomy: Rather than imposing human frameworks, the taxonomy emerges from actual model behavior, making it more likely to capture AI-specific value expressions.
  • Rich multi-dimensional analysis: Examining values through the lenses of task context, human values, response type, model variant, and implicit/explicit expression provides a comprehensive picture.
  • Practical safety utility: The ability to surface outlier values and potential jailbreaks demonstrates tangible safety applications.
  • Excellent presentation: Clear figures, thorough appendices, honest limitations section, and released data/taxonomy.

Limitations

  • Anthropic-specific data: Results may not generalize to other AI systems with different training approaches (RLHF vs. RLAIF, different constitutions). Cross-model comparison is limited to Claude variants.
  • Circular evaluation: Using Claude models to extract values from Claude conversations risks systematic bias. The authors acknowledge this but cannot fully mitigate it.
  • No longitudinal analysis: A one-week snapshot cannot capture how values drift over time or respond to training updates.
  • Interpretive ambiguity: The broad operationalization of "values" means that routine operational preferences (e.g., "clarity" in communication) are counted alongside genuine ethical commitments, potentially inflating the value landscape.
  • Missing inter-rater reliability: The absence of inter-rater agreement statistics weakens confidence in the human validation claims.
  • Cannot distinguish cause from effect: The methodology cannot determine whether AI values are driven by training, prompt context, or user behavior -- it only observes correlations.
  • English-centric: Values are summarized in English keywords even for non-English conversations, potentially underrepresenting cross-cultural perspectives.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How stable is the discovered value taxonomy over longer time periods (months/years)? Does it shift with model updates or changing user demographics?
  2. Can the value extraction methodology be applied to open-source models (e.g., Llama, Mistral) using public conversation datasets like WildChat or LMSYS-Chat-1M to enable cross-provider comparison?
  3. The paper notes value mirroring (20.1% during support) -- how much of this reflects appropriate responsiveness vs. problematic sycophancy? Could the methodology distinguish between the two?
  4. How does the broad operationalization of "values" (including operational preferences like "clarity") affect the utility of the taxonomy for alignment research? Would a narrower definition yield different policy implications?
  5. The cross-model comparison (Section B.5) reveals significant behavioral differences between Claude variants -- could this methodology serve as a standard tool for transparency reporting across AI companies, and what standardization would be needed?
