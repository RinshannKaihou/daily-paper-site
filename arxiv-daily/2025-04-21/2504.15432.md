> **一句话总结：** 本文系统性地揭示了使用LLM生成的伪标签微调RoBERTa等小型编码器模型时，会出现性能下降、预测不稳定（跨训练轮次方差增大）和过早的性能平台期，且基于熵的过滤和一致性集成等缓解策略只能部分挽回问题，无法从根本上消除LLM标签中系统性偏差带来的风险。

> **TL;DR:** This paper empirically demonstrates that fine-tuning RoBERTa on LLM-generated labels (from Qwen2.5 3B/7B) across four text classification benchmarks leads to accuracy degradation (up to 30 points on ECommerce), dramatically increased prediction instability across training runs (Krippendorff's alpha dropping from 84.30 to 43.75 on Manifestos), and premature performance plateaus. Entropy-based filtering and consistency ensemble mitigation strategies recover only 60-75% of the gold-label performance gap and fail to stabilize training variance. The authors provide a KL-divergence decomposition showing that the LLM's irreducible approximation error creates a hard performance ceiling and amplifies estimation variance.

## 核心贡献

- **揭示LLM伪标签训练的三个风险维度：** 首次系统性地展示了用LLM标注数据微调小型分类器时，不仅存在预期的准确率/F1下降，还伴随严重的跨训练轮次预测不稳定和过早性能平台期——这些维度在先前工作中常被忽略。
- **发现不稳定性不随LLM规模增大而改善：** 令人警惕的是，使用更大的7B参数LLM生成标签时，预测不稳定性反而加剧（如Manifestos数据集上Krippendorff's alpha从3B的52.72降至7B的43.75），说明简单扩大标注模型规模并非万能解。
- **理论框架支撑：** 通过KL散度分解（基于Heskes 1998），证明LLM标注引入的不可约近似误差导致性能上限受限，且该近似误差会放大有限样本估计误差，从而解释了观测到的方差增大现象。
- **评估缓解策略的局限性：** 测试了基于熵的过滤和一致性集成两种方法，发现它们仅能部分（60-75%）缩小与金标准标签的性能差距，且无法稳定训练方差或缓解平台期——凸现了事后修正的根本局限。
- **对少数类别的系统性歧视：** 通过手动错误分析发现，LLM标注器和后续微调的分类器均在少数类别上持续表现不佳，呈现一种温和形式的模型坍塌。

## Key Contributions

- **Three-dimensional risk analysis of LLM pseudo-label training:** First to systematically show that fine-tuning small classifiers on LLM-generated labels causes not only accuracy/F1 degradation but also dramatically increased prediction instability across runs and premature performance plateaus.
- **Instability paradoxically worsens with larger LLMs:** Counterintuitively, using a 7B-parameter LLM for labeling worsens prediction instability compared to a 3B model (e.g., Krippendorff's alpha drops from 52.72 to 43.75 on Manifestos), undermining the assumption that scaling the annotator model fixes the problem.
- **KL-divergence theoretical framework:** Decomposes the error into an irreducible approximation error (KL(P || P_S)) plus an estimation error, proving that no amount of synthetic labels can overcome the LLM's systematic biases, and that approximation errors amplify estimation variance.
- **Mitigation strategies offer only partial relief:** Entropy-ranking filtering and consistency ensembles recover only 60-75% of the gold-label performance gap and fail to stabilize training variance or prevent early plateaus, underscoring fundamental limitations of post hoc corrections.
- **Systematic minority-class failure (mild model collapse):** Manual error analysis reveals both the LLM annotator and downstream RoBERTa classifier consistently underperform on minority classes, exhibiting behavior akin to model collapse.

## Methodology

The authors conduct experiments across four benchmark datasets of varying complexity:

1. **IMDB** (binary sentiment analysis, balanced, simple)
2. **ECommerce** (4-class product categorization, slightly imbalanced, moderate)
3. **Manifestos** (56-class political stance detection, imbalanced, small training size, complex)
4. **Toxic** (3-class hate speech vs. offensive language, highly imbalanced, complex)

**Pipeline:** Qwen2.5-Instruct (3B and 7B) generates synthetic labels via 3-shot prompting for training texts. RoBERTa-base is then fine-tuned on these labels and compared against gold-label training. Each experiment is run 5 times to measure variance.

**Evaluation metrics:**
- Accuracy and Macro-F1 (mean and standard deviation across 5 runs)
- Krippendorff's alpha (inter-rater agreement across training runs)
- Proportion of unchanged predictions (p_uc) across trials

**Mitigation strategies tested:**
- **Entropy-ranking filtering:** Rank predictions by conditional entropy; replace the top-alpha-percent most uncertain labels with gold labels (alpha in {5, 25}).
- **Consistency ensemble:** Generate multiple LLM predictions with different demonstration sets; replace inconsistent predictions with gold labels.

**Theoretical framework:** Uses Heskes (1998) bias-variance decomposition for KL-divergence to show: Error(P_hat) = KL(P || P_S) + E_DS[KL(P_S || P_hat)], where the first term is irreducible approximation error from the LLM, and the second is estimation error that gets amplified by the first.

## Results & Highlights

- **Performance degradation scales with task complexity:** IMDB (simple binary) shows negligible gaps; ECommerce sees a 30-point accuracy drop (66.05% vs. 96.26%) with the 3B annotator.
- **Scaling LLM annotator does not fix instability:** On Manifestos, Krippendorff's alpha drops from 84.30 (gold) to 52.72 (3B) to 43.75 (7B). The proportion of unchanged predictions drops from 82.04% to 50% to 30.45%.
- **Premature performance plateaus:** Models trained on synthetic labels show diminishing returns as training data increases, plateauing well below gold-label performance (Figure 2).
- **Accuracy can be misleading:** On Toxic, accuracy remains relatively high with 7B labels, but prediction instability is severe (p_uc = 77.49%), hiding unreliable behavior.
- **Entropy-based filtering fails on Toxic:** Instead of stabilizing, it lowers p_uc from 77.49 to 56.20.
- **Consistency ensemble works partially but is costly:** With 5% gold label replacement and 5-time ensemble, effective inference covers 25% of the pool, undermining cost-saving rationale.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: Moderate.** The finding that synthetic labels degrade performance is not surprising. The paper's value lies in (a) documenting the instability and plateau dimensions that prior work overlooked, and (b) the counterintuitive result that larger annotator LLMs worsen instability. However, the theoretical framework is a straightforward application of Heskes (1998) and does not yield new theoretical insights.
- **Rigor: Moderate to good.** Five repeated runs per experiment is reasonable. Four datasets with varying complexity provides good coverage. However, training is capped at 5000 data points to "reduce environmental impact," which limits the ability to observe plateau dynamics at scale. Only two LLM sizes (3B, 7B) from the same family (Qwen2.5) are tested, leaving open whether findings generalize across model families and scale ranges.
- **Reproducibility: Good.** The paper specifies hyperparameters (AdamW, lr=2e-5, batch size 16, 3 epochs), hardware (single RTX 8000), and LLM annotation pipeline details (vLLM, guided decoding, prompt template). Datasets are publicly available. Code does not appear to be released.
- **Significance: Moderate.** The paper is a useful empirical cautionary note for practitioners, but it primarily confirms and quantifies what is theoretically expected. The mitigation experiments are underwhelming—neither strategy is new, and the paper acknowledges they offer only partial relief. The paper's strongest contribution is the instability finding, which has practical implications for deployment.
- **Red flags:**
  - Only Qwen2.5 models are tested; generalization to GPT-4, Claude, LLaMA, etc. is unknown.
  - Training capped at 5000 samples limits ecological validity for many real-world applications with larger training sets.
  - The Manifestos dataset with 56 classes may be unrealistically hard for 3B/7B models with 3-shot prompting, making the results somewhat expected.
  - No analysis of how prompt engineering or demonstration selection affects label quality.

## Strengths

- Identifies and quantifies the prediction instability dimension that prior work largely ignored, which is practically important for high-stakes applications.
- The counterintuitive finding that larger LLMs worsen instability is valuable and challenges common assumptions.
- Clean theoretical framing via KL-divergence decomposition connects empirical findings to established statistical learning theory.
- Honest assessment of mitigation strategy limitations—the authors do not oversell their proposed fixes.
- Good dataset diversity spanning binary sentiment, multi-class categorization, political stance, and hate speech detection.
- Explicitly measures per-instance prediction stability via Krippendorff's alpha, going beyond aggregate metrics.

## Limitations

- Only Qwen2.5-Instruct models (3B, 7B) are tested; results may not generalize to other LLM families, much larger models (70B+), or proprietary APIs.
- Training is limited to 5000 samples, which is small for many real-world classification tasks and may not reflect behavior at larger scales.
- The mitigation strategies are limited to two simple heuristics; more sophisticated approaches (e.g., learned filtering functions, confidence calibration, active learning loops) are not explored.
- No investigation of prompt engineering effects—only 3-shot prompting is tested.
- The theoretical analysis lacks rigorous exposition of how approximation error influences variance and convergence rates.
- The paper does not offer a comprehensive solution, which limits its practical impact.
- Single model architecture (RoBERTa-base) for the downstream classifier; unclear if findings apply to other encoder models or sizes.

## Relevance

本文对使用LLM进行知识蒸馏到小型分类器的流程进行了系统性的风险分析，其关于训练不稳定性和性能平台期的发现直接关联到LLM推理评估的可靠性问题。对于关注训练稳定性、模型可解释性和计算可靠性的研究者而言，该工作提供了一个重要的实证警示：即使最终准确率看似可接受，LLM标注数据训练的模型在预测一致性上可能存在严重隐患。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the instability finding generalize to much larger LLM annotators (e.g., 70B+, GPT-4-level models)? The paper only tests up to 7B—could frontier models produce sufficiently accurate label distributions to mitigate these issues?
- How does active learning—selectively querying human annotations for the most uncertain samples—compare to the heuristic filtering strategies tested here?
- Can learned filtering functions (parameterized F(x) as the authors suggest) significantly outperform entropy-based heuristics in identifying unreliable LLM labels?
- What is the relationship between LLM calibration quality and downstream classifier stability? Would better-calibrated LLMs (e.g., via temperature scaling or conformal prediction) produce more stable training signals?
- How do these findings extend to structured prediction tasks (NER, relation extraction) or generative fine-tuning scenarios, beyond single-label classification?
