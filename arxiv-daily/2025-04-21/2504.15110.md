## 一句话总结

本文证明了带残差连接的 Kolmogorov-Arnold 网络（Res-KANs）能够在包括分形域在内的有界域上以 Besov 范数最优逼近率逼近 Besov 函数，并给出了基于伪维度的无维度样本复杂度界，从逼近论和学习理论双重角度为 KAN 架构提供了严格的理论基础。

## TL;DR

This paper establishes that residual-connected Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (Res-KANs) achieve optimal approximation rates for Besov functions on bounded, open, and even fractal domains, and derives dimension-free sample complexity bounds via pseudodimension analysis, providing rigorous theoretical foundations for the KAN architecture.

## 核心贡献

- **Besov 空间最优逼近率**：证明了 Res-KANs 可以在任意有界开域（包括分形域）上以 Besov 范数的最优逼近率逼近 $B^s_{p,q}(X)$ 中的函数，逼近率在更弱的 Besov 范数 $B^\alpha_{p,q}(X)$（$\alpha < s$）下达到最优。
- **伪维度与样本复杂度界**：对 Res-KAN 函数类的伪维度（pseudodimension）给出了明确上界，由此推导出在学习 Besov 正则性函数时无关于输入维度的样本复杂度估计。
- **分形域上的推广**：利用 Rychkov 扩展算子，将逼近结果从 Lipschitz 域推广到 Ahlfors $n$-正则集（包括分形域），这是对经典神经网络逼近理论的重要拓展。
- **完整的三步证明框架**：提出了渐近样条展开 → Res-KAN 样条模拟 → 有限参数截断的三步证明策略，为理解 KAN 的表达能力提供了清晰的技术路线。

## Key Contributions

- **Optimal Besov approximation rates**: Proves Res-KANs approximate any Besov function in $B^s_{p,q}(X)$ at the optimal rate with respect to a weaker Besov norm $B^\alpha_{p,q}(X)$ where $\alpha < s$, on bounded open and even fractal domains.
- **Pseudodimension and sample complexity bounds**: Provides an explicit upper bound on the pseudodimension of Res-KAN hypothesis classes, yielding a dimension-free sample complexity estimate when learning Besov-regular functions from $N$ i.i.d. noiseless samples.
- **Extension to fractal domains**: Extends approximation guarantees from Lipschitz and $(\epsilon, \delta)$-domains to Ahlfors $n$-regular sets (fractal domains) via Rychkov's extension operator, broadening the scope beyond classical neural network approximation theory.
- **Three-step proof framework**: Introduces a clean technical pipeline: asymptotic B-spline expansion, spline emulation by Res-KANs, and finite-parameterized truncation, offering a structured approach to analyzing KAN expressivity.

## Methodology

The authors develop their theory through a three-stage proof strategy:

1. **Asymptotic Spline Expansion**: Any Besov function $f \in B^s_{p,q}([0,1]^d)$ is first approximated by a truncated B-spline series expansion. Using classical results from approximation theory (De Boor-Fix type dual functionals), the approximation error in the weaker Besov norm $B^\alpha_{p,q}$ is controlled at the optimal rate $O(n^{-(s-\alpha)/d})$ where $n$ is the number of spline knots.

2. **Spline Emulation by Res-KANs**: Each component of the B-spline expansion (tensor-product B-spline basis functions and their coefficients) is exactly representable by a Res-KAN with bounded depth and width. The key insight is that B-spline basis functions can be recursively constructed from truncated power functions, and Res-KANs with trainable spline activations on $[-1,1]$ can exactly compute these recursively defined basis functions. The resulting network has width $(d+4)(2^{K+1}-2)$, depth $2d-1$, and $(d^2+35d-35)(2^{K+1}-1)$ non-zero parameters for approximation level $K$.

3. **Finite-Parameterized Truncation**: Since the asymptotic expansion is infinite, a truncation argument combined with a modulus-of-smoothness estimate controls the tail, yielding a finite-parameter Res-KAN with the same optimal approximation rate.

For the extension to general domains, Rychkov's extension operator is employed to extend Besov functions from bounded Lipschitz domains, $(\epsilon, \delta)$-domains, and Ahlfors $n$-regular sets to $\mathbb{R}^d$, where the unit cube approximation applies, followed by restriction back to the original domain.

For statistical guarantees, the pseudodimension of the Res-KAN hypothesis class is bounded using standard combinatorial arguments applied to the spline activation structure, yielding:

$$\text{Pdim}_\gamma(\text{Res-KAN}) \in O\!\left(L^2 W^2 (I+3-\lfloor\alpha\rfloor)\bigl(\log(LW^2(I+3-\lfloor\alpha\rfloor))+L\bigr)\right)$$

where $L$ is depth, $W$ is width, and $I$ is the number of spline intervals. This directly implies a dimension-free sample complexity bound for learning Besov-regular functions.

## Results & Highlights

- **Theorem 1 (Main Approximation)**: For any $f \in B^s_{p,q}(X)$ with $X$ a bounded open set (or fractal domain), there exists a Res-KAN $\hat{f}$ with explicitly bounded depth, width, and parameter count such that $\|f - \hat{f}\|_{B^\alpha_{p,q}(X)} \leq C \cdot n^{-(s-\alpha)/d}$ for $n$ spline knots, achieving the optimal rate.

- **Lemma 3**: Establishes the Besov approximation result on the unit cube $[0,1]^d$, which serves as the base case.

- **Lemma 4**: Extends the approximation from $[0,1]^d$ to Lipschitz domains and $(\epsilon, \delta)$-domains via the extension-restriction technique.

- **Lemma 5**: Further extends to Ahlfors $n$-regular sets (fractal domains), demonstrating the generality of the framework.

- **Lemma 6 (Fat-shattering dimension)**: Provides a bound on the fat-shattering dimension of Res-KANs, which is used to control the pseudodimension.

- **Corollary (Sample Complexity)**: The sample complexity of learning a Besov-regular function from $N$ i.i.d. noiseless samples is dimension-free, meaning the number of samples needed does not explicitly depend on the input dimension $d$ but rather on the network size parameters.

- **Optimality**: The approximation rates match the known lower bounds for Besov space approximation, confirming that Res-KANs are optimal approximators in this regime.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty**: High. While Besov space approximation results exist for ReLU networks (e.g., Yarotsky, Bolbobrov), this is the first rigorous treatment for KANs with residual connections. The extension to fractal domains is also novel in the KAN context. The three-step proof framework (spline expansion, emulation, truncation) is a clean architectural contribution.

**Rigor**: Very high. The proofs follow a careful chain: base case on the unit cube, extension to Lipschitz domains, then to fractal domains. Each step uses appropriate tools (Rychkov's operator for extension, De Boor-Fix duals for spline approximation, combinatorial bounds for pseudodimension). All constants and network dimensions are explicitly given.

**Reproducibility**: Moderate. The theoretical results are fully specified with explicit network architectures (depth, width, parameter counts). However, the paper is purely theoretical with no experimental validation. The network parameters, while explicit, involve large constants that would make direct implementation impractical for empirical testing.

**Significance**: High for the theoretical deep learning community. Provides the first comprehensive approximation-theoretic and statistical learning-theoretic analysis of KANs, which have attracted significant empirical interest since their introduction by Liu et al. (2024). The dimension-free sample complexity bound is particularly notable.

**Red flags**: The theoretical bounds involve unspecified constants hidden in big-O notation that may be very large. The restriction to noiseless samples limits practical applicability. No experimental validation is provided, which is understandable for a pure theory paper but limits assessment of whether the theory captures the empirically observed advantages of KANs. The assumption of exact spline activation on $[-1,1]$ may not hold in practical implementations.

## Strengths

- Provides the first rigorous Besov approximation theory for KANs, filling a major gap in the theoretical understanding of this increasingly popular architecture.
- The extension to fractal domains via Rychkov's operator is mathematically elegant and broadens applicability well beyond the standard $[0,1]^d$ setting.
- The dimension-free sample complexity result is a strong and potentially surprising finding -- it suggests KANs may avoid the curse of dimensionality in a specific statistical sense.
- Explicit network architectures (depth, width, parameter counts) are given rather than merely existential results, enabling future work on constructive aspects.
- The three-step proof strategy (expansion, emulation, truncation) is modular and could be adapted to analyze other architectures with trainable activations.

## Limitations

- The paper is purely theoretical with no experimental validation. It remains unclear whether the optimal approximation rates and dimension-free sample complexity are observed in practice, or whether the constants involved render the bounds vacuous at realistic scales.
- The noiseless sample assumption is restrictive. Real-world applications involve noisy labels, and the sample complexity analysis does not account for label noise or adversarial perturbations.
- The Res-KAN architecture studied requires spline activations defined exactly on $[-1,1]$, whereas practical KAN implementations use various basis functions (B-splines, Fourier bases, etc.) that may not satisfy the exact assumptions.
- The approximation rates are optimal in the minimax sense but may not capture the inductive biases that make KANs empirically successful (e.g., parameter efficiency on scientific computing tasks).
- The hidden constants in the big-O bounds depend on the smoothness parameter $s$, integrability indices $p, q$, and the domain geometry, and could be extremely large in high-dimensional settings.

## Relevance

本文与 LLM 可解释性及训练稳定性具有间接但重要的关联：KAN 的 Besov 逼近理论为理解基于可训练激活函数的网络的表达能力提供了严格的数学框架，这一框架有望推广至 Transformer 中使用的混合专家（MoE）路由机制和自适应计算策略的分析；同时，无维度的样本复杂度界为大规模模型在有限数据下泛化能力的理论分析提供了新的视角。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How do the approximation rates and sample complexity bounds change when label noise is introduced? Can the dimension-free result survive under realistic noise models?
- Can the three-step proof framework be adapted to analyze KANs with non-spline trainable activations (e.g., Fourier-based or wavelet-based activations)?
- What are the explicit constants in the approximation bounds, and at what practical network sizes do the optimal rates become observable?
- Does the extension to fractal domains have concrete implications for learning functions on graph-structured or manifold-structured data?
- How do Res-KANs compare empirically to Res-MLPs in terms of sample efficiency when approximating functions with known Besov regularity?
- Can the pseudodimension bounds be tightened using the specific structure of spline activations more directly?
