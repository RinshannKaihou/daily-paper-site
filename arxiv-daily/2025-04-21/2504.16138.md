> **一句话总结：** 本文构建了一个基于总算力分配的分析框架，预测到2028年底将有103-306个基础模型超过欧盟AI法案10^25 FLOP阈值（90%置信区间），且绝对算力阈值捕获的模型数量呈超线性增长，而相对于最大训练模型的"前沿关联"阈值则保持年捕获量稳定（约14-16个/年）。

> **TL;DR:** This paper develops a compute-allocation-based forecasting framework predicting that by end of 2028, 103-306 foundation models (90% CI) will exceed the EU AI Act's 10^25 FLOP threshold and 45-148 will exceed the US AI Diffusion Framework's 10^26 FLOP threshold. The number of models captured by absolute thresholds grows superlinearly each year, while "frontier-connected" thresholds (defined relative to the largest training run) capture a stable ~14-16 models annually. The model is validated via retrodiction on 2020-2023 data, with 90% prediction intervals capturing all historical observations. Accepted at ICML 2025.

## 核心贡献

- 建立了首个系统性的前沿AI模型数量预测框架，基于总算力分配方法（total compute allocation），将全球AI算力增长、训练/推理分配、模型规模分布三个维度联合建模
- 对欧盟AI法案（10^25 FLOP）和美国AI扩散框架（10^26 FLOP）的算力阈值进行了到2028年的定量预测，为政策制定者提供了具体的监管范围估计
- 揭示了绝对算力阈值捕获模型数量的超线性增长趋势（每年新增模型数加速增长），以及前沿关联阈值（相对于最大训练模型定义）的年捕获稳定性，为阈值设计提供了重要的政策启示
- 引入了最大模型份额（Largest Model Share, LMS）参数和算力分配梯度参数 k，通过蒙特卡洛模拟量化不确定性，提供90%预测区间
- 通过对2020-2023年数据的回溯验证（retrodiction），90%预测区间覆盖了所有历史观测值，为预测可信度提供了实证支持

## Key Contributions

- Introduces the first systematic forecasting framework for frontier AI model counts based on a total compute allocation methodology, jointly modeling global AI compute growth, training/inference splits, and model size distributions
- Provides quantitative forecasts through 2028 for the EU AI Act (10^25 FLOP) and US AI Diffusion Framework (10^26 FLOP) compute thresholds, estimating 103-306 and 45-148 models respectively (90% CI by end of 2028)
- Identifies that absolute compute thresholds capture models at a superlinearly increasing rate, while frontier-connected thresholds (defined relative to the largest training run) maintain a stable annual capture of approximately 14-16 models
- Introduces the Largest Model Share (LMS) parameter and allocation gradient k, using Monte Carlo simulation with 1000 runs to produce 90% prediction intervals that account for parameter uncertainty
- Validates predictions via retrodiction on 2020-2023 data, demonstrating that all historically observed values fall within the model's 90% prediction intervals

## Methodology

The forecasting framework operates through a three-stage pipeline:

**1. Total compute stock projection:** The authors project total compute available for AI workloads (training + inference) with a median annual growth rate of ~4.1x. This integrates two sources: the historically observed training compute growth rate of 6.3x/year from Epoch AI's Notable Models dataset (weighted 25%) and the AI 2027 forecast of 3.4x/year from Dean (2024) (weighted 75%). Growth rate noise is drawn from a normal distribution (std=0.5) to capture uncertainty.

**2. Training/inference split and model size allocation:** The total compute stock is divided between training and other uses (inference, research). Baseline allocations follow AI 2027 with adjustments: 40% training in 2025-2026, 30% in 2027-2028. Training compute is then distributed across model size bins (orders of magnitude relative to the largest model) using a power-law allocation function A(m) = m^k, where k is sampled uniformly from [0.9, 1.1] based on historical fits to 2017-2023 data. The largest model share (LMS) parameter is sampled lognormally from [0.05, 0.5] for forward projections to model increasing frontier development participation.

**3. Model sampling and counting:** Models are randomly sampled from each size bin until the allocated compute budget is met, producing a distribution of models over training compute. Threshold counts are computed by counting models exceeding each specified FLOP threshold. The model runs 1000 Monte Carlo simulations to generate 5th, 50th, and 95th percentile projections.

**Data:** The primary data source is Epoch AI's Notable Models dataset (~450 entries with training compute estimates), filtered to 2017-2023 (296 datapoints). 2024 data is excluded due to incompleteness (models in the database lag behind releases by several months).

**Verification:** Retrodiction on 2020-2023 for thresholds at 10^23, 10^24, and 10^25 FLOP, and for frontier-connected thresholds at 0.5, 1.0, and 1.5 OOM. All historically observed values fall within the 90% prediction intervals.

## Results & Highlights

- **EU AI Act threshold (10^25 FLOP):** By end of 2028, median projection of 165 models (90% CI: 103-306). The model's 2024 retrodiction of 23 models aligns well with the 24 documented by Epoch AI.
- **US AI Diffusion Framework threshold (10^26 FLOP):** By end of 2028, median projection of 81 models (90% CI: 45-148). As of April 2025, only ~2 publicly available models exceed this threshold.
- **Superlinear growth of absolute thresholds:** The annual increment in models captured by the 10^25 FLOP threshold grows from +37 (2024-2025) to +105 (2027-2028) at the 95th percentile. However, multiplicative growth rates are sub-exponential (declining from 1.96x to 1.41x for median 10^25 FLOP estimates).
- **Frontier-connected thresholds are stable:** Models within 1 OOM of the largest model: median 14-16/year from 2025-2028. Within 0.5 OOM: 6-8/year. Within 1.5 OOM: 20-24/year.
- **Sensitivity analysis:** Results are presented for varying growth rate weightings (Appendix G), alternate training compute allocations using Epoch GATE model (Appendix H), different allocation gradients k (Appendix I), and uniform vs. lognormal LMS sampling (Appendix E). The model is most sensitive to the LMS parameter and growth rate assumptions.
- **Published at ICML 2025** and released with public code at [GitHub](https://github.com/IyngkarranKumar/compute_thresholds_public).

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (Moderate):** The compute-allocation-based framework is a reasonable methodological contribution, though the individual components (compute growth projection, power-law allocation, Monte Carlo simulation) are not individually novel. The key novelty lies in the specific application to regulatory threshold forecasting and the frontier-connected vs. absolute threshold comparison.
- **Rigor (Moderate-High):** The retrodictive validation is a strong point -- covering all historical observations within 90% intervals provides empirical grounding. However, the model has only 6 years of calibration data (2017-2023) and several parameters are set with subjective judgment (e.g., the 3:1 weighting between growth rate estimates, the lognormal LMS distribution choice, the 30-70 training/inference split adjustment). The authors are transparent about these limitations.
- **Reproducibility (High):** Public code repository, clear methodology description, extensive appendices with sensitivity analyses for key parameters. The use of publicly available Epoch AI data is also a plus.
- **Significance (High for policy, Low for technical ML):** This paper is primarily valuable for AI governance and policy audiences. It does not advance ML methodology or understanding of model behavior. For policymakers designing compute-based regulatory frameworks, the quantitative forecasts are directly actionable.
- **Red flags:**
  - The median growth rate of 4.1x/year is derived from a subjective 3:1 weighting between two very different estimates (6.3x historical vs. 3.4x forecast). Different weightings produce substantially different projections (shown in Appendix G), but the paper does not provide principled justification for this ratio.
  - The Notable Models selection effect is acknowledged but not fully corrected. The authors admit median estimates are lower bounds, especially for models far from the frontier, but the extent of the bias is unclear.
  - The 90% prediction intervals are wide (e.g., 103-306 for 10^25 FLOP by 2028), which limits the precision of policy recommendations. The intervals capture parameter uncertainty but not structural model uncertainty (e.g., what if the compute allocation distribution fundamentally changes?).
  - The assumption that historical allocation trends (2017-2023) will hold through 2028 may break if the AI industry undergoes structural shifts (e.g., widespread shift to inference-heavy architectures, consolidation, or regulatory chilling effects).

## Strengths

- Rigorous validation through retrodiction, with all historical observations falling within 90% prediction intervals
- Transparent methodology with extensive sensitivity analyses across multiple parameters (growth rate weighting, training allocation, LMS distribution, allocation gradient)
- Directly relevant to current policy debates around the EU AI Act and US AI Diffusion Framework
- Introduces the useful concept of "frontier-connected" thresholds as an alternative to absolute thresholds, with empirical analysis of their comparative stability
- Clear presentation of uncertainty through 90% confidence intervals rather than point estimates alone
- Public code and data supporting reproducibility
- Honest discussion of limitations, including the Notable Models selection effect and parameter uncertainty

## Limitations

- The Epoch AI Notable Models dataset is a strict subset of all ML models, introducing a selection bias that causes median projections to be lower bounds, especially for smaller models. The authors acknowledge this but do not fully resolve it.
- Only 6 years of calibration data (2017-2023) limits confidence in extrapolation to 2028, particularly for model parameters like LMS and allocation gradient that show high variance.
- Several key parameters rely on subjective judgment: the 3:1 growth rate weighting, the lognormal LMS distribution choice, and the adjusted training/inference split. Different reasonable choices produce materially different forecasts.
- The model does not account for structural changes in the AI industry (e.g., regulatory effects on development decisions, architectural paradigm shifts like inference-time compute scaling, or market consolidation).
- The 90% prediction intervals reflect parameter uncertainty but not structural/model uncertainty -- if the fundamental relationships change, the intervals may be too narrow.
- The paper does not account for jurisdictional scope (e.g., EU/US regulations only apply to models released within their jurisdictions, and Chinese models are included in the count).
- Results for the 10^28 and 10^29 FLOP thresholds have very wide confidence intervals and should be treated as highly speculative.

## Relevance

本文对AI治理中的算力阈值设计进行了定量分析，虽然不直接涉及LLM可解释性或训练稳定性，但对关注AI计算可靠性和大规模模型发展趋势的研究者有参考价值——特别是文中揭示的绝对算力阈值捕获范围的快速扩张趋势，直接关系到未来大型模型训练和评估的监管环境演变。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How would the inclusion of inference-time compute (e.g., chain-of-thought, test-time compute scaling) change the projections, given that frontier models increasingly allocate substantial compute at inference?
- What is the optimal threshold design for regulators seeking to balance enforcement capacity with risk coverage -- should thresholds be absolute, frontier-connected, or some hybrid?
- How sensitive are the results to the assumption that the compute allocation gradient k remains stable? If the industry shifts toward training many smaller specialized models rather than fewer large ones, how do projections change?
- Can the model be extended to incorporate regulatory chilling effects -- i.e., the possibility that compute thresholds themselves reduce the number of models trained above the threshold?
- What are the implications of these forecasts for compute governance approaches that target compute providers (e.g., chip export controls, compute monitoring) rather than model developers?
