VeLU: Variance-enhanced Learning Unit for Deep Neural Networks

arXiv: 2504.15051

Date: 2025-04-21

https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.15051

一句话总结： VeLU提出了一种基于输入方差动态调节的新型激活函数，通过融合ArcTan-Sin变换和Wasserstein-2正则化来减轻协变量偏移，在六种主流架构和六个视觉基准上相比ReLU/Swish/GELU均取得了一致的准确率提升（最高+9%）。
TL;DR: VeLU is a novel activation function that dynamically scales its output based on input variance using ArcTan-Sin transformations and Wasserstein-2 regularization. Across six architectures (ViT-B16, VGG19, ResNet50, DenseNet121, MobileNetV2, EfficientNetB3) and six vision benchmarks, it consistently outperforms ReLU, ReLU6, Swish, and GELU, with the largest gains on complex datasets (up to +9% on Corel-1K, +3.12% on CIFAR-100 with full replacement in ResNet50). The tradeoff is additional hyperparameters (7 tunable parameters) and modest computational overhead. Code is publicly available on GitHub.

Key Contributions

  • Proposes VeLU, an activation function that combines ArcTan-Sin transformations (arctangent + arcsine) for smooth bounded non-linearity while preserving small negative activations to prevent neuron death
  • Introduces variance-based adaptive scaling (s_adaptive = 1 + gamma tanh(mu sigma_x)) enabling the activation to dynamically adjust its response based on input statistics
  • Integrates Wasserstein-2 optimal transport distance as a regularization term into the activation function, aligning output distributions with a Gaussian prior to reduce internal covariate shift
  • Conducts extensive experiments across six architectures (ViT-B16, VGG19, ResNet50, DenseNet121, MobileNetV2, EfficientNetB3) and six benchmarks (CIFAR-10/100, MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, Corel-1K/10K)
  • Provides loss landscape visualizations and preactivation distribution analyses demonstrating VeLU's smoothness and stability advantages over ReLU, Swish, and GELU
  • Code is publicly released on GitHub for reproducibility

Methodology

VeLU's core formula is:

f(x) = lambda x sigmoid(alpha (arctan(beta_1 x) + arcsin(beta_2 x))) (1 + gamma tanh(mu sigma_x)) - lambda_OT * W2(x, N(0,1))

The design has three main components:

  1. ArcTan-Sin Transformations: The combination of arctan(beta_1 x) and arcsin(beta_2 x) inside a sigmoid creates a smooth, non-monotonic, bounded non-linearity. The arctangent provides asymptotic bounding while arcsine introduces sharper transitions. Together they ensure smooth gradient flow and prevent saturation.
  1. Variance-based Adaptive Scaling: The term (1 + gamma tanh(mu sigma_x)) dynamically adjusts the activation magnitude based on the local input standard deviation. This allows VeLU to respond differently depending on whether the input distribution is narrow or wide, providing layer-specific and batch-specific adaptation without learnable parameters beyond lambda.
  1. Wasserstein-2 Regularization: The W2 distance between the activation output distribution and a standard Gaussian N(0,1) is subtracted as a penalty. For 1D distributions, this simplifies to W2^2 = (E[x] - mu)^2 + (sigma_x - sigma_target)^2. This encourages well-conditioned activation distributions and reduces internal covariate shift across layers.

The function has a continuously differentiable first derivative that maintains non-zero gradients over a broad input range, combining the derivative of the sigmoid term with the sech^2 term from the adaptive scaling. The paper provides detailed hyperparameter tuning guidelines, recommending gradient clipping, larger epsilon values (10^-3 instead of 10^-6), clamping the adaptive factor to [0.5, 2.0], and reducing lambda_OT if training instability occurs.

Experiments were conducted under two scenarios: (1) partial replacement (VeLU in the classification head only, backbone unchanged) and (2) full replacement of all activation functions. Models were trained for 5-20 epochs with consistent learning rate schedules across all activation functions.

Results & Highlights

  • Consistent accuracy improvements across all architectures: VeLU improves top-1 accuracy over the best baseline in every tested architecture, with particularly strong gains in ResNet50 (~+2%) and DenseNet121.
  • Large gains on complex datasets: Full activation replacement in ResNet50 yields +9.00% on Corel-1K, +3.85% on Corel-10K, and +3.12% on CIFAR-100. Gains on simpler datasets (MNIST: +0.36%) are smaller due to ceiling effects.
  • ViT-B16 compatibility: VeLU outperforms GELU (the standard ViT activation) on both top-1 and top-5 metrics, showing compatibility with self-attention mechanisms.
  • Training convergence: VeLU-based ResNet50 converges faster and achieves lower validation loss compared to the original activation, with curves stabilizing at higher accuracy levels.
  • Competitive efficiency: VeLU achieves lower training loss with competitive training time and inference speed. In MobileNetV2 and EfficientNetB3, it delivers higher FPS. Memory overhead is modest and acceptable.
  • Resolution robustness: VeLU maintains stable performance across input resolutions from 32x32 to 224x224 on MobileNetV2/CIFAR-10 (75.10% to 93.34%).
  • Optimizer agnostic: Works well with Adam (91.32%), SGD (89.33%), and RMSProp (86.95%) on ResNet50/CIFAR-10.

Critical Assessment

Novelty (Moderate): The idea of integrating variance-based adaptive scaling and optimal transport regularization into an activation function is genuinely novel. However, the paper joins a long line of activation function proposals (PReLU, ELU, Swish, GELU, Mish, etc.) that show incremental improvements. The ArcTan-Sin combination, while creative, is essentially an engineering choice rather than a principled derivation. The Wasserstein-2 regularization applied to activations (as opposed to GAN training or domain adaptation) is an interesting conceptual contribution, though the practical formula used (a simplified 1D W2 distance) is quite elementary.

Rigor (Moderate): The experimental setup is thorough in breadth (6 architectures x 6 datasets) but has limitations. Training for only 5-20 epochs is quite short and may not reflect convergence behavior in large-scale training. The paper lacks ImageNet-scale experiments, which are the gold standard for evaluating activation functions. Statistical significance tests (e.g., multiple runs with different seeds) are not reported -- results appear to be from single runs. The ablation study is incomplete: there is no systematic analysis of how each component (ArcTan-Sin vs. adaptive scaling vs. W2 regularization) contributes independently to the gains.

Reproducibility (Good): Code is publicly available on GitHub. The paper provides detailed hyperparameter guidelines and default values. However, the 7 tunable hyperparameters (alpha, beta_1, beta_2, gamma, momentum, mu, lambda_OT) create a significant search space that could make reproduction sensitive to exact parameter choices. The paper acknowledges this limitation and suggests automated hyperparameter optimization as future work.

Significance (Moderate): While the consistent improvements are encouraging, the lack of large-scale experiments (ImageNet, LLM training, etc.) limits the claimed impact. The activation function research area has matured significantly, and the field has largely converged on GELU for transformers and ReLU/SiLU for many CNNs. Demonstrating gains on CIFAR-scale benchmarks, while informative, is not sufficient to shift standard practice. The computational overhead of computing variance statistics and W2 distances at every activation layer is not rigorously analyzed.

Red flags:

  • No error bars or statistical significance tests reported.
  • Very short training durations (5-20 epochs) may mask overfitting or convergence issues at scale.
  • No large-scale (ImageNet) validation.
  • Seven hyperparameters for an activation function is a substantial practical burden.
  • The Wasserstein-2 term as formulated (subtracting from the activation output) conflates the activation computation with a regularization loss, which is architecturally unusual and could complicate gradient flow analysis.

Strengths

  • Genuinely novel combination of variance-adaptive scaling with optimal transport regularization in an activation function
  • Consistent improvements across all tested architectures and datasets, including both CNNs and Vision Transformers
  • Strong gains on complex datasets (Corel-1K: +9%, CIFAR-100: +3.12%) suggesting real benefits for harder classification tasks
  • Loss landscape visualizations provide intuitive evidence of smoother optimization surfaces
  • Detailed hyperparameter tuning guidelines and publicly available code enhance reproducibility
  • Resolution-robust and optimizer-agnostic performance increases practical applicability

Limitations

  • No ImageNet-scale experiments: All benchmarks are relatively small (CIFAR-10/100, MNIST, etc.). Without ImageNet validation, it is unclear whether VeLU scales to the datasets where activation function choices matter most.
  • Heavy hyperparameter burden: Seven tunable parameters (alpha, beta_1, beta_2, gamma, mu, momentum, lambda_OT) is a significant practical drawback. Most widely adopted activations (ReLU, GELU, Swish) have zero tunable parameters.
  • No ablation study: The paper does not isolate the contribution of each component (ArcTan-Sin transformations, adaptive scaling, W2 regularization), making it impossible to determine which aspects are essential.
  • No statistical significance: Results appear to be from single training runs without error bars, making it difficult to assess whether the improvements are robust to random initialization.
  • Short training durations: 5-20 epochs is insufficient to evaluate convergence behavior, especially for deeper models where activation function effects may compound over longer training.
  • No NLP or LLM experiments: The evaluation is exclusively on vision tasks. Given the dominance of GELU in transformer architectures, demonstrating improvements in language modeling would significantly strengthen the paper.
  • Computational cost analysis is shallow: While training time and FPS are reported, the per-layer overhead of computing variance statistics and W2 distance is not broken down. In resource-constrained settings (e.g., edge deployment), this overhead may negate accuracy gains.
