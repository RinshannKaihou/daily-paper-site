Bigram Subnetworks: Mapping to Next Tokens in Transformer Language Models

arXiv: 2504.15471

Date: 2025-04-21

https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.15471

一句话总结

通过连续稀疏化方法在Transformer语言模型中发现仅需约10M参数（不到总参数0.2%）的"bigram子网络"，这些子网络能够高精度复现二元语法预测，且对模型整体性能至关重要。

TL;DR

This paper identifies sparse "bigram subnetworks" in Transformer LMs (Pythia up to 1B, GPT-2 up to 774M) that are sufficient for bigram (next-token-given-current-token) predictions. Using continuous sparsification (Savarese et al. 2020), they learn binary masks over frozen model parameters. Key findings: (1) these subnetworks plateau at ~10M parameters regardless of model size, achieving r > 0.95 bigram surprisal correlation; (2) they concentrate in the first MLP layer and persist throughout pretraining; (3) they recreate the first-layer rotation from current-to-next token representations in the residual stream; (4) ablating them destroys language modeling performance (comparable to ablating optimal pruning subnetworks), while random subnetwork ablations have negligible effect. The overlap between bigram and optimal pruning subnetworks is 15.3x above chance in Pythia 1B. This work establishes a minimal, interpretable foundation circuit upon which more complex language model circuits can be studied incrementally. If you work on mechanistic interpretability, circuit analysis, or model pruning, this paper is directly relevant.

核心贡献

  1. 发现bigram子网络的存在性: 在参数量高达1B的Transformer语言模型中，存在仅需约10M参数即可高精度（r > 0.95）复现bigram预测的稀疏子网络。在Pythia 1B中，该子网络仅占非embedding参数的0.17%。
  1. 子网络结构与预训练动态: bigram子网络集中在第一个Transformer MLP层，并在整个预训练过程中持续存在，尽管在训练后期其表示效率有所下降（需要更多参数达到相同精度）。
  1. 机制性解释: bigram子网络复现了完整模型中第一层的"旋转"现象——将激活从当前token表示空间转向下一个token表示空间，这是残差流中的关键转换。
  1. 必要性与充分性验证: 通过消融实验证明bigram子网络对语言模型性能既是必要的（消融后性能急剧下降）也是充分的（隔离后可产生bigram预测），且与最优剪枝子网络高度重叠（15.3倍于随机期望）。
  1. 为分层电路研究奠定基础: 提出以bigram子网络作为最小可解释电路，在此基础上逐步叠加更复杂的电路来研究语言模型。

Key Contributions

  1. Existence of bigram subnetworks: Sparse subsets of ~10M parameters (as few as 0.17% of non-embedding params in Pythia 1B) can reproduce bigram predictions with surprisal correlations r > 0.95 in models up to 1B parameters.
  1. Structural concentration and persistence: Bigram subnetworks concentrate in the first Transformer MLP layer and persist throughout pretraining, though they become less parameter-efficient later in training (a "decompression" effect).
  1. Mechanistic insight -- first-layer rotation: Bigram subnetworks recreate the key residual-stream transformation where the first layer rotates activations from current-token space toward next-token space, quantified via linear transformations fitted to token activations.
  1. Necessity and sufficiency: Ablation experiments show bigram subnetworks are necessary (ablation causes comparable degradation to optimal pruning subnetwork ablation) and sufficient (in isolation, they produce bigram predictions). Overlap with optimal pruning subnetworks is 15.3x above chance.
  1. Foundation for hierarchical circuit analysis: Proposes building up from this minimal interpretable circuit to study more complex circuits incrementally, releasing subnetworks publicly for GPT-2 and Pythia models.

Methodology

Continuous Sparsification

The core method is continuous sparsification (Savarese et al. 2020), which learns a mask M over frozen model parameters:

  • One real-valued parameter m per model parameter, mapped to (0,1) via sigmoid with decreasing temperature
  • Loss function: CrossEntropy(P(x), MaskedModel_M(x)) + lambda * ||M||_1 / |M|
  • Lambda controls sparsity: values in {0, 0.1, 5, 10, 50, 100, 500, 1000}
  • Mask excludes input/output embedding and LayerNorm parameters
  • Trained on sequences of 128 tokens, batch size 32, lr 5e-5

Models and Data

  • Models: Pythia (70M, 160M, 410M, 1B), GPT-2 (small, medium, large)
  • Data: English web text from OSCAR (Abadji et al. 2021)
  • Checkpoint analysis: Pythia 160M, Pythia 1B, re-trained GPT-2 small (from Chang et al. 2024)

Analysis Techniques

  1. Bigram surprisal correlation: Pearson's r between subnetwork and bigram surprisals (token-level losses)
  2. Rotation analysis: Fit linear transformations to input/output activations per layer; measure median rotation angles
  3. Cross-layer covariance similarity: Following Belrose et al. (2023), compute Frobenius cosine similarity between activation covariance matrices across layers
  4. Optimal pruning subnetworks: Train subnetworks to minimize language modeling loss (not bigram loss) for comparison
  5. Ablation studies: Zero out subnetwork parameters and measure language modeling loss degradation

Results & Highlights

| Finding | Detail |

|---------|--------|

| Bigram correlation plateau | ~10M active params regardless of model size |

| Highest correlation (Pythia 1B) | r = 0.959 with only 0.17% of non-embedding params |

| First-layer concentration | Primarily MLP parameters in first Transformer layer |

| Persistence during pretraining | Bigram subnetworks peak ~2K steps after full model overfits to bigrams |

| Decompression over training | 500K params achieve r=0.949 at step 4K but only r=0.919 at step 143K (Pythia 1B) |

| First-layer rotation | GPT-2 large: layer 1 increases rotation to input from 0.0 to 56.2 degrees; decreases rotation to output from 70.5 to 46.3 degrees |

| Overlap with optimal pruning | 15.3x above chance; 38.0% of bigram subnetwork contained in optimal subnetwork (Pythia 1B) |

| Ablation impact | Bigram subnetwork ablation causes similar or larger degradation than optimal subnetwork ablation; random subnetwork ablation has negligible effect |

Key mechanistic finding: In the full models, the first layer performs a sharp transformation from current-token to next-token activation space. Bigram subnetworks recreate this rotation, while random subnetworks of the same size do not.

Ablation outcome: Ablating the bigram subnetwork leads to incoherent text that repeats a single frequent token, confirming these parameters are critical for basic language modeling.

Critical Assessment

Strengths

  • Clear minimal-circuit framing: Starting from bigram predictions is a well-chosen "simplest nontrivial behavior" that is fully defined over the entire input space, unlike many circuit analyses that target narrow tasks.
  • Rigorous necessity and sufficiency: The paper carefully establishes both properties through distinct experiments (subnetwork in isolation for sufficiency, ablation for necessity), avoiding the common pitfall of conflating the two.
  • Scale consistency: The ~10M parameter plateau across model sizes (70M to 1B) is a striking and non-obvious finding that suggests something fundamental about how Transformers organize bigram information.
  • Strong random baselines: Comparisons against random subnetworks with the same size and parameter-block distribution strengthen the mechanistic claims.
  • Connection to pruning: The 15.3x overlap with optimal pruning subnetworks provides independent validation that these parameters are functionally important.
  • Practical contribution: Publicly released subnetworks and code enable follow-up research.

Limitations

  • Scale ceiling at 1B: All experiments stop at ~1B parameters. Whether bigram subnetworks retain the same structure, size, and concentration at 7B, 70B, or frontier-model scales is unknown. The 10M plateau could change qualitatively at larger scales.
  • Binary mask granularity: The method selects individual parameters via binary masks, but does not reveal whether the selected parameters form interpretable sub-structures (e.g., specific attention heads, neurons, or weight matrices).
  • English-only evaluation: All subnetworks are trained and evaluated on English OSCAR data. Whether bigram subnetworks generalize similarly across languages or multilingual models is not addressed.
  • Observational rather than interventional for the rotation finding: The first-layer rotation analysis shows correlation between subnetwork and full-model behavior, but does not establish a causal chain from specific parameters to the rotation effect.
  • Bigram-specific framing may limit generality: The paper's hypothesis that more complex circuits "build up" from bigram subnetworks is plausible but untested. It is equally possible that higher-level circuits operate independently or even redundantly with respect to bigram subnetworks.
  • Decompression without explanation: The finding that subnetworks become less parameter-efficient later in training is attributed to "exaptation" but lacks direct mechanistic evidence for this claim.

Relevance

This work is highly relevant to several areas of interest:

  • LLM interpretability: Directly contributes to mechanistic interpretability by identifying and characterizing a minimal, interpretable circuit in Transformer LMs. The bigram subnetwork provides a concrete building block for circuit-level analysis.
  • Training stability and dynamics: The pretraining checkpoint analysis (Section 4) reveals how bigram representations evolve, decompress, and persist throughout training -- directly relevant to understanding training dynamics and phase transitions.
  • Inference and evaluation: The ablation studies demonstrate that these tiny subnetworks are critical for inference quality. Ablating just 0.17% of parameters destroys output coherence, with implications for model robustness evaluation.
  • Computation reliability: The overlap with optimal pruning subnetworks (15.3x above chance) suggests that these parameters serve as a "computational backbone," which has implications for understanding which parameters must be preserved for reliable computation.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Scaling behavior: Do bigram subnetworks remain ~10M parameters in models with 7B, 70B, or hundreds of billions of parameters? Does the first-layer concentration persist, or do larger models distribute bigram computation differently?
  1. Trigram and beyond: Can continuous sparsification identify trigram subnetworks, and do they build on bigram subnetworks as hypothesized? What is the hierarchical relationship between n-gram subnetworks for increasing n?
  1. Cross-lingual transfer: Are bigram subnetworks language-specific, or do multilingual models share bigram parameters across languages? This is especially relevant given Chang's work on multilinguality at DeepMind.
  1. Causal mechanism of the rotation: What specific weight matrices or neurons in the first MLP layer drive the current-to-next token rotation? Can the rotation be localized further within the MLP?
  1. Interaction with attention heads: The paper notes bigram subnetworks are not necessarily localized to specific attention heads. How do attention head outputs interact with the MLP-dominated bigram subnetwork?
