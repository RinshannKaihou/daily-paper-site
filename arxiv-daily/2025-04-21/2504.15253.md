> **一句话总结：** 本文提出JETTS基准，系统评估LLM-Judge在测试时扩展（test-time scaling）三种任务设定（重排序、束搜索、批判式优化）中的表现，发现Judge在重排序上与结果奖励模型相当但在束搜索中逊于过程奖励模型，且其自然语言批判目前无法有效指导生成器改进。

> **TL;DR:** This paper introduces JETTS, a comprehensive benchmark evaluating how well LLM-as-Judge models serve as evaluators for test-time scaling of generator LLMs. Across 10 judge models (7B--70B) and 8 generator models (6.7B--72B) in three domains (math, code, instruction following), the key findings are: (1) judges are competitive with outcome reward models for response reranking, (2) judges are consistently worse than process reward models for step-level beam search, and (3) judge-generated natural language critiques are currently ineffective at guiding generators toward better responses -- a sobering result that challenges assumptions about the utility of generative evaluators.

## 核心贡献

- **提出JETTS基准**：首个系统评估LLM-Judge在测试时扩展场景中作为评估器有效性的基准，覆盖三个领域（数学推理、代码生成、指令遵循）和三种任务设定（响应重排序、步骤级束搜索、批判式响应优化）。
- **大规模交叉评估**：评估了10个不同规模（7B-70B参数）的Judge模型和8个不同规模（6.7B-72B参数）的生成器模型，共计80种Judge-Generator组合，实验规模在同类工作中罕见。
- **揭示Judge的局限性**：发现Judge在束搜索中表现不及过程奖励模型（PRM），且其自然语言批判无法有效指导生成器改进，这对当前"LLM-as-Judge"热度的实用性提出了重要警示。
- **开源代码与数据**：提供了完整的基准代码和数据，便于社区复现和扩展研究。

## Key Contributions

- Introduces the **JETTS (Judge Evaluation for Test-Time Scaling) benchmark**, the first systematic evaluation framework measuring how effectively LLM-as-Judge models serve as evaluators in test-time scaling pipelines, spanning three domains (math reasoning, code generation, instruction following) and three task settings (response reranking, step-level beam search, critique-based response refinement).
- Conducts a **large-scale cross-evaluation** of 10 judge models (7B--70B parameters) across 8 generator models (6.7B--72B parameters), covering 80 judge-generator combinations with 8 datasets -- an unusually comprehensive experimental sweep.
- **Reveals sobering limitations of generative evaluators**: judges match outcome reward models (ORMs) at reranking but fall consistently short of process reward models (PRMs) in beam search, and their natural language critiques fail to guide generators toward improved responses.
- **Open-sources the full benchmark** (code and data via Google Cloud), enabling reproducibility and community extension.

## Methodology

The JETTS benchmark evaluates judge models in three test-time scaling task settings:

1. **Response Reranking**: For a given query, the generator produces multiple candidate responses (up to 10). The judge scores each response, and the top-ranked response is selected. This tests the judge's ability to discern quality differences among complete outputs.

2. **Step-Level Beam Search**: The generator constructs solutions step-by-step. At each step, multiple candidate continuations are generated, and the judge selects the best one to continue the beam search. This tests the judge's ability to evaluate partial solutions -- a more granular and challenging task. The beam search uses parameters N (initial samples), M (beam width), and d (max depth), with pre-computed full search trees released as data.

3. **Critique-Based Response Refinement**: The judge generates a natural language critique of the generator's initial response, and the generator then attempts to produce an improved response based on this critique. This tests the judge's ability to produce actionable, useful feedback -- a capability unique to generative LLM-judges that non-generative reward models cannot provide.

The benchmark spans three domains with specific datasets:
- **Math reasoning**: GSM8K, MATH, CHAMP
- **Code generation**: HumanEval+, MBPP+, BigCodeBench
- **Instruction following**: AlpacaEval, IFEval

Judge models evaluated include Prometheus-7B-v2.0, Skywork-Critic-8B/70B, OffsetBias-8B, Themis-8B, Prometheus-8x7B-v2.0, Self-taught-evaluator-70B, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (baseline), and the proprietary SFR-Judge family. Generator models span Llama-2-7B/13B/70B, Llama-3.1-8B/70B, Qwen2.5-32B/72B, and Gemma-2-9B.

## Results & Highlights

- **Reranking**: Judge models are competitive with outcome reward models (ORMs). Best judges (e.g., SFR-Judge-70B, Skywork-Critic-70B) achieve reranking performance close to or matching strong ORMs. Even mid-sized judges (8B) show reasonable reranking capability, making them practical alternatives when ORMs are unavailable.

- **Beam Search**: Judges are consistently worse than process reward models (PRMs) in guiding step-level beam search. The gap is particularly pronounced in math reasoning tasks, where PRMs' step-level training provides a significant advantage over judges that evaluate complete responses or individual steps without specialized process-level signal.

- **Critique-Based Refinement**: This is the most disappointing finding. Judge-generated natural language critiques are largely ineffective at helping generators improve their responses. In many cases, the refined responses are no better -- and sometimes worse -- than the originals. This suggests that current judges produce critiques that are either too vague, incorrect, or not actionable enough for generators to leverage.

- **Scale effects**: Larger judge models generally perform better, but the improvements from scaling up (8B to 70B) are modest compared to the gap between judges and PRMs in beam search. This suggests architectural/training differences matter more than sheer scale for process-level evaluation.

- **Cross-domain variation**: Judges perform better on instruction-following reranking than on math or code beam search, reflecting the difficulty of evaluating reasoning steps versus surface-level output quality.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: High. While LLM-as-Judge evaluation is a growing field, no prior work has systematically assessed judges specifically as evaluators *within test-time scaling pipelines*. The three-task decomposition (reranking, beam search, critique refinement) is well-motivated and covers the major use cases. The critique refinement finding is particularly novel and counterintuitive.

- **Rigor**: Strong. The experimental design is comprehensive: 80 judge-generator combinations across 8 datasets, 3 domains, and 3 task settings. The inclusion of both open-source judges and the proprietary SFR-Judge family (evaluated internally) adds breadth. However, the SFR-Judge results cannot be independently verified by external researchers.

- **Reproducibility**: Good but not perfect. The code and pre-computed generator data are publicly available on GitHub and Google Cloud. The benchmark is pip-installable. However, SFR-Judge model weights are not released due to company policy, and the beam search data is 51GB extracted, which creates a significant resource barrier. Some evaluation environments require complex separate conda setups.

- **Significance**: High. The findings have immediate practical implications for practitioners choosing between ORMs, PRMs, and LLM-judges for test-time scaling. The critique refinement null result is especially impactful -- it challenges a common assumption that generative evaluators' natural language feedback should be useful for improving outputs.

- **Red flags**: (1) SFR-Judge results are internal-only and not reproducible by the community, which is a transparency concern. (2) The critique refinement result, while important, may partially reflect implementation choices (prompt format, number of refinement rounds, generator model quality) rather than a fundamental limitation of the approach. (3) The benchmark focuses on judge-as-evaluator rather than judge-as-ranker (the more common use case in practice), so the results may not directly transfer to leaderboard-style evaluation scenarios.

## Strengths

- First systematic, large-scale study of LLM-judge effectiveness in test-time scaling contexts, filling a clear gap in the literature.
- The three-task decomposition (reranking, beam search, critique refinement) is well-designed and covers distinct failure modes.
- The critique refinement null result is a valuable and honest finding that challenges community assumptions.
- Comprehensive model coverage (10 judges, 8 generators, 80 combinations) provides robust conclusions.
- Clean open-source release with pip-installable package and pre-computed data.
- Accepted at ICML 2025, indicating peer-reviewed quality.

## Limitations

- The critique refinement result may be sensitive to prompt design, generator capability, and refinement iteration count. More ablation on these factors would strengthen the conclusion.
- SFR-Judge models are not publicly available, limiting reproducibility of the strongest judge results.
- The benchmark does not evaluate judges in multi-turn conversational settings or with tool use, which are increasingly common deployment scenarios.
- The beam search data (51GB) creates a practical barrier to entry for researchers with limited computational resources.
- The study evaluates judge accuracy but does not analyze *why* critiques fail to help generators -- whether the critiques are factually wrong, too vague, or simply ignored by the generator. This mechanistic understanding would be valuable.
- Only open-weight generator models are used; proprietary models (GPT-4, Claude) are excluded, though this is understandable for reproducibility.

## Relevance

本文与LLM推理评估直接相关：它系统性地考察了LLM-Judge在测试时扩展中的评估可靠性，揭示了生成式评估器与过程奖励模型之间的性能差距，对理解推理阶段计算资源分配和评估方法的可靠性具有重要参考价值。同时，批判式优化失效的发现也提示了当前LLM评估反馈机制在训练稳定性方面的不足。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Why do judge-generated critiques fail to improve generator outputs? Is it due to critique factual errors, vagueness, or the generator's inability to follow critique instructions?
2. Can specialized critique training (beyond standard judge training) produce critiques that are actually useful for refinement?
3. How would the results change with more powerful generators (e.g., GPT-4-class models) -- would better generators make better use of judge critiques?
4. Is the gap between judges and PRMs in beam search fundamental (due to training paradigm) or can it be closed with better judge architectures or training objectives?
5. How do the results transfer to multi-modal settings (e.g., vision-language models) or agentic workflows where judges evaluate tool-use plans?
