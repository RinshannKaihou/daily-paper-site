> **一句话总结：** LongAct 通过发现并利用 LLM 注意力层 Query/Key 向量中固有的高幅度激活（outlier activations）来指导稀疏梯度更新，仅在 RL 训练中更新与这些显著特征对应的权重行，在 LongBench v2 上取得约 8% 的提升，并兼容 GRPO、DAPO 等多种 RL 算法。

> **TL;DR:** LongAct identifies consistently high-magnitude activations in Q/K projection vectors when LLMs process long contexts, and uses these as a saliency signal to perform sparse, targeted gradient updates during RL fine-tuning (updating only ~30% of W_Q/W_K rows). This yields ~8% gains on LongBench v2, ~4% on RULER-128K, and generalizes across RL algorithms (GRPO, DAPO, KL-Cov) and model scales (4B/8B). Ablations show that disrupting high-magnitude activations causes immediate model collapse, while disrupting low-magnitude ones preserves reasoning coherence, confirming these activations are critical structural anchors.

## 核心贡献

- **发现长上下文中 Q/K 向量的高幅度激活模式**：在 LLM 处理长上下文时，观察到 Query 和 Key 投影中存在跨注意力头分布的、稳定的高幅度激活值（outlier activations），且这些异常值集中在特征维度而非序列维度。
- **提出基于激活显著性的稀疏训练策略 LongAct**：在 RL 训练期间，仅更新与高幅度激活对应的 W_Q 和 W_K 权重行（约 30%），其余参数冻结，将均匀更新转变为显著性引导的稀疏更新。
- **跨 RL 算法和模型规模的通用性验证**：LongAct 在 GRPO、DAPO、KL-Cov 等多种 RL 算法以及 4B/8B 两种模型规模上均稳定提升性能。
- **深入消融实验揭示机制**：通过扰动分析证明，破坏高幅度激活会导致模型推理立即崩溃（重复循环），而破坏低幅度激活则不影响推理连贯性，验证了这些激活作为"结构锚点"的关键作用。

## Key Contributions

- **Discovery of intrinsic high-magnitude activation patterns in Q/K vectors for long-context processing**: Empirically observes that consistently large-magnitude activations emerge in specific feature dimensions (not sequence positions) of Query and Key projections when LLMs handle long contexts, and these patterns are head-specific and stable.
- **LongAct: a saliency-guided sparse update strategy for RL fine-tuning**: Instead of uniform parameter updates, LongAct dynamically computes magnitude matrices and restricts gradient updates to only the rows of W_Q and W_K corresponding to high-magnitude activations (~30% sparsity ratio), with zero inference overhead.
- **Universality across RL algorithms and model scales**: Demonstrates consistent improvements under DAPO, GRPO, CLIP-conv, and KL-conv on both Qwen3-4B and Qwen3-8B, showing the method is orthogonal to the choice of RL algorithm.
- **Mechanistic analysis via perturbation studies**: Shows that clamping the top 30% high-magnitude activations to the global mean causes immediate model collapse (0/503 correct on LongBench v2), while clamping the bottom 30% preserves substantial performance (108/503 correct).

## Methodology

LongAct operates in two training stages:

**Stage 1 -- Supervised Fine-tuning (Cold Start):** Standard SFT on 20K instruction-following samples from AM-DeepSeek-R1-0528-Distilled using LLaMA-Factory, with max sequence length 16,384 and cosine LR scheduler (peak 2e-5).

**Stage 2 -- Reinforcement Learning with Saliency-Guided Updates:**

1. **Magnitude Computation**: For each training step, compute the L2-norm of Q and K activations across the sequence dimension to obtain magnitude matrices M^Q and M^K of shape (H x D), where H is the number of attention heads and D is the head dimension.

2. **Intra-Head Top-k Selection**: For each attention head, select the top-k dimensions with highest magnitude values (k = floor(lambda * D), default lambda=0.3). This preserves multi-head structure by performing selection within each head independently.

3. **Gradient Masking**: Map selected (head, dimension) pairs to global row indices in the projection weight matrices. Apply binary masks G^Q and G^K during backpropagation so only rows corresponding to high-magnitude activations receive gradient updates. All other parameters (W_V, W_O, MLP layers) receive standard full updates.

4. **Reward**: A simple rule-based reward combining format compliance (presence of thinking/answer tags) and answer correctness (exact match with ground truth).

The sparse mask is applied only during training; at inference time, the model operates as a standard dense Transformer with zero additional latency.

## Results & Highlights

- **LongBench v2**: Qwen3-8B-SFT w/ LongAct achieves 36.73 overall, +3.93 over DAPO (32.80) and +3.13 over the official Qwen3-8B* (33.60). The 4B model also gains +3.82 over DAPO.
- **Hard difficulty split**: LongAct particularly excels on hard instances -- 35.93 on 8B (+7.63 over DAPO), suggesting it better handles tasks sensitive to error accumulation.
- **Long-context robustness**: On the Long (>128K) split, LongAct achieves 34.72 on 8B (+2.31 over DAPO) and 33.33 on 4B (+9.26 over DAPO), indicating improved stability as context length increases.
- **RULER-128K**: 51.15 average on 8B (+1.52 over DAPO, +6.73 over SFT baseline).
- **InfiniteBench**: Best average across both model sizes (49.39 on 8B), with notable gains on En.MC (+5.24) and En.QA for the 4B model (+8.38).
- **Short-context generalization**: LongAct also improves GSM8K, HumanEval, and TruthfulQA, suggesting it functions as a general robust RL optimizer rather than a long-context-specific hack.
- **Sparsity ablation**: 30% sparsity ratio is optimal; 20% is too sparse, 40% introduces regression, confirming the importance of targeting the right amount of salient parameters.
- **Activation selection ablation**: Selecting high-magnitude activations (36.73) vastly outperforms random selection (28.63) or minimum-magnitude selection (29.82), confirming the saliency hypothesis.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: Moderate-to-high. The idea of using intrinsic activation patterns to guide RL training is genuinely novel. The observation that high-magnitude activations in Q/K vectors are critical for long-context reasoning is well-motivated by the quantization literature (AWQ, KiVi), but applying this insight to RL fine-tuning is a fresh contribution. The connection between quantization-inspired saliency and sparse RL updates is intellectually appealing.

- **Rigor**: Good but with caveats. The ablation studies are thorough (sparsity ratios, activation selection strategies, multiple RL algorithms, short-context benchmarks). The perturbation analysis (Figure 6 and Table 8) provides compelling mechanistic evidence. However, the magnitude computation uses "expectation over the batch" without clarifying how this is computed in practice -- is it pre-computed on a calibration set or computed dynamically during training? The paper says "pre-computed global magnitude matrix" but also "at each training step," which is slightly contradictory.

- **Reproducibility**: Reasonably high. The paper specifies exact hyperparameters, uses publicly available datasets and models (Qwen3-8B-Base, DocQA-RL-1.6K, MemAgent), fixes random seeds, and uses standard frameworks (LLaMA-Factory, verl, vLLM). Code is not explicitly mentioned as released.

- **Significance**: Potentially high if the findings generalize. The insight that RL fine-tuning benefits from sparse, saliency-guided updates has implications beyond long-context tasks. The short-context improvements suggest this could be a general technique.

- **Red flags**: (1) Only tested on Qwen3 models at 4B and 8B scales -- the authors acknowledge limited compute. (2) The 8% improvement claim on LongBench v2 is relative to DAPO, not the official Qwen3-8B* (where the gain is ~3%). (3) The theoretical justification in Appendix B is informal (no formal proofs). (4) No comparison with other sparse update methods like LoRA or magnitude pruning during RL. (5) The rule-based reward is very simple (binary format + answer match), raising questions about whether the gains come from the sparse update strategy or the interaction with simple rewards.

## Strengths

- Clean, intuitive core idea grounded in empirical observation of activation patterns.
- Strong ablation suite: sparsity ratios, activation selection strategies, multiple RL algorithms, short-context benchmarks, and perturbation analysis.
- Zero inference overhead -- the sparse mask is training-only.
- Universality across RL algorithms is a practical strength.
- Consistent improvements across difficulty levels and context lengths, especially on hard/long splits.
- Short-context generalization shows the method is not overfitting to long-context scenarios.

## Limitations

- Only evaluated on Qwen3-4B and Qwen3-8B; no validation on larger models (70B+) or different model families (Llama, Mistral).
- No comparison with parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods like LoRA that also reduce the number of updated parameters.
- The magnitude computation mechanism lacks detail on whether it is static (pre-computed once) or dynamic (recomputed per step or per epoch). This matters for reproducibility and computational cost analysis.
- The binary rule-based reward function is simplistic; unclear whether LongAct would benefit from or interact with more sophisticated reward models.
- The theoretical analysis is informal; no formal convergence guarantees or learning theory backing the approach.
- Training data is relatively small (2.6K samples for RL); unclear how LongAct scales with larger training sets.

## Relevance

该论文在长上下文强化学习训练机制方面提出了基于内部激活模式的稀疏更新策略，对关注 LLM 后训练优化、长上下文推理能力提升和强化学习算法改进的研究者具有较高参考价值。其核心洞察——高幅度激活是长上下文推理的结构锚点——也为模型量化和推理加速研究提供了新的视角。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How does LongAct interact with parameter-efficient methods like LoRA -- could saliency-guided LoRA provide similar or complementary benefits?
2. Does the optimal sparsity ratio (lambda=0.3) hold for larger models (70B+) or different architectures (e.g., models without grouped-query attention)?
3. Can the magnitude-based saliency signal be used to inform data selection or curriculum strategies for long-context RL?
4. What happens when LongAct is combined with more sophisticated reward models (e.g., process reward models)?
5. Is the observed activation sparsity pattern specific to Qwen3's training, or is it a universal property of Transformer models processing long contexts?
