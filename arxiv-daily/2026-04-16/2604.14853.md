> **一句话总结：** 本文将推理阶段的自适应计算预算分配建模为带约束的优化问题，通过拉格朗日松弛将全局预算约束分解为每个输入的闭式oracle解，再用轻量GBM分类器模仿oracle策略实现实时部署，在MATH数据集上相对均匀分配最高提升12.8%准确率。

> **TL;DR:** This paper formalizes adaptive test-time compute allocation for reasoning LLMs as a constrained optimization problem (maximize expected accuracy subject to an average compute budget). A two-stage SOLVE-THEN-LEARN pipeline uses Lagrangian relaxation to derive per-instance oracle budget assignments via binary search, then trains a lightweight GBM classifier to predict these oracle labels from cheap text features. Evaluated on MATH and GSM8K with DeepSeek-V3, GPT-4o-mini, and Qwen2.5-7B, the method consistently outperforms uniform and heuristic allocation baselines, achieving up to 12.8% relative accuracy improvement on MATH under matched budget constraints while tracking the Lagrangian oracle upper bound with over 91% imitation accuracy.

## 核心贡献

- **首次将输入自适应的测试时计算分配形式化为约束优化问题**：在有限平均推理预算下最大化总体准确率，为启发式规则提供了有原则的替代方案。
- **提出SOLVE-THEN-LEARN两阶段框架**：利用拉格朗日松弛将全局耦合的预算约束分解为逐实例子问题，每个子问题有闭式oracle解；再通过轻量分类器模仿oracle策略，实现毫秒级实时部署。
- **建立完整的形式化保证**：包括成本单调性定理（支持二分搜索精确命中预算目标）、模仿遗憾上界（将约束推理问题归约为监督分类问题）、以及原始可行性恢复结果。
- **全面的实验验证**：在三个LLM和两个数学推理基准上，方法在所有预算水平上一致优于均匀分配和启发式基线，最高实现12.8%相对准确率提升。

## Key Contributions

- **First formalization of input-adaptive test-time compute allocation as a constrained optimization problem**, providing a principled alternative to heuristic rules for the accuracy-cost tradeoff under finite inference budgets.
- **A two-stage SOLVE-THEN-LEARN pipeline**: (1) Lagrangian relaxation decomposes the globally coupled budget constraint into per-instance sub-problems with closed-form oracle solutions, found via binary search on the dual variable; (2) a lightweight GBM classifier is trained to predict oracle labels from 16 cheap text features for real-time deployment with negligible routing overhead (<1% of inference cost).
- **Formal theoretical guarantees**: cost monotonicity (Theorem 1) enabling exact budget targeting via binary search; an imitation regret bound (Theorem 2) reducing constrained inference to supervised classification; and a primal recovery result (Theorem 3) ensuring near-feasibility and near-optimality as classifier accuracy improves.
- **Comprehensive empirical validation** across three LLMs (DeepSeek-V3, GPT-4o-mini, Qwen2.5-7B) and two math benchmarks (MATH, GSM8K), demonstrating consistent 3--6 percentage-point improvements over uniform allocation and closely tracking the oracle upper bound with 91--99% imitation accuracy.

## Methodology

The paper addresses the question: given a finite average compute budget shared across a batch of inputs, how should one allocate inference compute to individual inputs to maximize aggregate performance?

**Problem Formulation.** The authors define a budget allocation policy as a function mapping inputs to discrete compute budgets (e.g., number of sampled responses for self-consistency voting). The goal is to maximize expected accuracy subject to an expected cost constraint. This is a constrained optimization with three challenging features: (i) black-box reward (accuracy has no analytic form), (ii) functional decision variable (the policy is a function, not a scalar), and (iii) global coupling (the budget constraint links all inputs).

**Solve Stage (Offline).** A Lagrange multiplier is attached to the budget constraint, converting the globally coupled problem into independent per-instance sub-problems. Each instance's oracle allocation selects the budget maximizing `Acc(x,b) - lambda*C(b)`, where lambda acts as a unit price of compute. The key insight is that the aggregate cost under the oracle is monotone non-increasing in lambda (Theorem 1), enabling binary search to match any target budget B. Strong duality holds for the empirical finite program (Proposition 1), so the oracle allocation is an exact solution.

**Learn Stage (Offline).** Oracle labels are computed for all training inputs, then a lightweight classifier (GBM with 100 estimators, max depth 5) is trained on 16 cheap text features (lexical statistics, structural indicators, and a single-pass entropy estimate). Training completes in under 1 second on a standard CPU.

**Deploy Stage (Online).** For a new input, features are extracted and the classifier predicts the budget; the LLM inference engine then runs once at the selected budget. Routing adds negligible overhead.

**Theoretical Framework.** The imitation regret bound (Theorem 2) shows that task-level regret is bounded by the product of worst-case per-instance gap and imitation error probability, providing a clean reduction from constrained inference to supervised classification. The primal recovery result (Theorem 3) shows that both feasibility violation and optimality gap vanish as the classifier improves.

## Results & Highlights

- **Main results (Table 1):** AdaCompute-GBM consistently outperforms all non-oracle baselines across every model, dataset, and budget level. On MATH, improvements over Fixed-b reach +5.8pp (DeepSeek-V3), +5.2pp (GPT-4o-mini), and +6.4pp (Qwen2.5-7B) at B=3.0. On GSM8K (94.6% base accuracy), adaptive allocation still yields +1.9pp.
- **Pareto frontier (Figure 4):** The method dominates all baselines at every budget level, with the largest relative gains at intermediate budgets (B=2--6) where routing flexibility matters most.
- **Oracle gap (Table 5):** The learned GBM policy achieves 91--99% imitation accuracy with task-accuracy gaps of only 0.4--1.4pp across all combinations, closely tracking the Lagrangian oracle upper bound.
- **Classifier ablation (Table 2):** Tree-based classifiers (GBM, Random Forest) achieve approximately 5pp higher task accuracy than linear models (Logistic Regression, SVM) and MLP, revealing that allocation boundaries are inherently non-linear.
- **Data efficiency (Figure 11):** The GBM already beats the Fixed-b baseline with only 20--25% of training data (approximately 40--50 labeled questions), suggesting the pipeline can be bootstrapped from small datasets.
- **Allocation pattern (Figure 5):** The oracle exhibits an inverted-U pattern: b=1 for both easy and intractable questions, with high budgets concentrated on medium-difficulty "responsive" questions. The learned policy faithfully replicates this structure.
- **Empirical archetype validation (Figure 7):** K-means clustering on accuracy curves confirms four distinct archetypes (easy, responsive, diminishing, hard), with the responsive cluster absorbing the overwhelming majority of high-budget allocations.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty (High):** The formalization of input-adaptive test-time compute allocation as a constrained optimization problem with Lagrangian relaxation is genuinely novel. While prior work (Damani et al., 2025; Hassid et al., 2024) has explored adaptive allocation heuristically, this paper provides the first principled optimization framework with formal guarantees. The reduction to supervised classification via imitation regret bounds is clean and well-motivated.

**Rigor (Strong):** The theoretical development is thorough: cost monotonicity theorem, strong duality proof (via LP relaxation), imitation regret bound, and primal recovery result are all formally proven. Empirical validation covers 3 LLMs, 2 benchmarks, 3 budget levels, and extensive ablations (classifier architecture, data efficiency, budget granularity, feature importance). The binary search convergence is empirically verified.

**Reproducibility (Good):** Code is publicly available. The method uses standard components (XGBoost, self-consistency voting, 16 text features) with clearly specified hyperparameters. The only reproducibility concern is the cost of data collection: 38,400 API calls across all settings, which requires significant compute budget.

**Significance (High):** As LLM inference becomes a dominant cost in production systems, principled compute allocation is a practically important problem. The framework is model-agnostic and can be integrated into any API-served or self-hosted LLM pipeline. The 3--6pp accuracy improvements at matched cost are practically meaningful.

**Potential concerns:**
- The offline oracle computation requires pre-computing the full Acc(x,b) matrix, which is expensive and must be repeated for new models/domains.
- The 200-question evaluation sets are relatively small. It is unclear how well the method scales to larger, more diverse workloads.
- The self-consistency voting paradigm is one specific form of test-time compute; generalization to other strategies (tree search, process reward models, extended chain-of-thought) is not demonstrated.
- The features are hand-crafted (16 text statistics); learned representations (e.g., from a frozen encoder) might be more robust but are not explored in depth.

## Strengths

- Principled formulation with strong theoretical foundations (Lagrangian duality, formal regret bounds, primal recovery).
- Clean reduction from constrained inference to supervised classification, making the framework easy to implement.
- Consistent improvements across all evaluated models and benchmarks, with minimal overhead at deployment time.
- Extensive ablation studies covering classifier architecture, data efficiency, budget granularity, and feature importance provide practical guidance.
- Empirical verification of the theoretical bound (Figure 10b) shows the imitation-to-regret reduction is not just valid but tight.
- The inverted-U allocation pattern is intuitive and empirically well-supported, providing actionable insights for practitioners.

## Limitations

- **Offline data collection bottleneck:** The solve stage requires N x K model evaluations (e.g., 9,600 API calls per model-dataset pair) to compute the utility table, limiting applicability in rapidly changing deployment scenarios.
- **Limited test-time compute strategies:** Only self-consistency voting is evaluated. Whether the framework generalizes to best-of-N with reward models, tree search, or adaptive chain-of-thought length remains untested.
- **Small evaluation scale:** 200 questions per benchmark is modest. Performance on larger, more diverse workloads (e.g., multi-domain QA, code generation, scientific reasoning) is unknown.
- **Feature dependence:** The 16 hand-crafted text features are specific to math reasoning. Different domains may require different feature engineering, reducing the out-of-the-box generality.
- **Budget set discretization:** The framework assumes a fixed discrete budget set. Continuous budget spaces and variable-length generation are not addressed.
- **No comparison with online adaptation methods:** The paper does not compare against methods that adaptively adjust compute during generation (e.g., early exiting, speculative decoding) rather than pre-allocating budget.

## Relevance

本文对推理阶段计算预算的高效分配提出了优化框架，与当前推理模型（如DeepSeek-R1、o1）的测试时计算缩放趋势高度相关。对于关注LLM推理效率和成本控制的研究者，该工作提供了有理论保证的自适应分配方案，可直接应用于生产环境中LLM服务的计算资源管理。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How does the framework perform when the test-time compute strategy is not self-consistency voting but rather tree-of-thought search, process reward model verification, or adaptive chain-of-thought length? Does the Lagrangian decomposition still produce meaningful oracle labels?
2. Can the offline utility table computation be replaced or augmented with an online/active learning approach that incrementally refines the oracle labels as new queries arrive, reducing the cold-start cost?
3. How sensitive is the allocation policy to distribution shift between the training questions used to compute oracle labels and the deployment workload? Does the framework degrade gracefully under domain transfer?
4. The framework treats each query independently. In production settings with batched requests and GPU utilization constraints, can the allocation be extended to jointly optimize over a batch of queries with a shared GPU budget?
5. What is the interplay between adaptive budget allocation and more powerful test-time strategies (e.g., process reward models, verifier-guided search)? Could combining adaptive allocation with strategy selection yield compounding benefits?
