When Flat Minima Fail: Characterizing INT4 Quantization Collapse After FP32 Convergence

arXiv: 2604.15167

Date: 2026-04-16

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.15167v1

一句话总结： 本文通过对 Pythia-160m 全部 154 个训练 checkpoint 的 INT4 量化探针审计，发现模型在 FP32 收敛后 INT4 量化误差会从约 11% 爆炸性增长至 517%，呈现出三阶段发散结构，并提出 Oscillatory Lock-In 学习率调度策略在冷却阶段可将 INT4 间隙降低 2.2 个百分点。
TL;DR: This single-author study performs a full forensic audit of all 154 Pythia-160m training checkpoints using a calibration-free INT4 probe, discovering a three-phase divergence structure: rapid learning (both FP32 and INT4 improve), a meta-stable plateau (~70k steps, INT4 gap bounded at ~10-14%), and an explosive divergence phase (INT4 gap from 14% to 517%) that begins precisely when FP32 perplexity converges, not when the learning rate decays. INT8 quantization is entirely immune (gap <1%), weight kurtosis declines during divergence (ruling out outlier accumulation), and a proposed Oscillatory Lock-In schedule reduces the INT4 gap by 2.2pp in cool phases (p<0.0001) but at substantial FP32 perplexity cost.

核心贡献

  • 揭示三阶段 INT4 量化发散结构：通过对 Pythia-160m 全部 154 个 checkpoint 进行逐点 INT4 探针审计，发现训练过程呈现快速学习期、元稳定平台期和爆炸性发散期三个阶段，发散期 INT4 间隙从 14% 增长至 517%，而 FP32 困惑度几乎不变。
  • FP32 收敛点作为发散 onset 预测因子：INT4 发散始于 FP32 困惑度停止改善的时刻（step ~77,77,000），而非学习率开始衰减的时刻，表明收敛后的权重更新（而非学习率幅度）是直接诱因。
  • 排除权重异常值累积假说：通过直接测量权重超额峰度（excess kurtosis），发现其在发散阶段不升反降（Pearson r=-0.26），直接否决了 Park et al. (OSP) 提出的异常值累积机制。
  • INT8 完全免疫，锁定机制于 4-bit 网格分辨率：INT8 量化间隙在全训练过程中保持 <1%，与 INT4 的 517% 形成 650 倍差异，证明问题本质在于 16 级 INT4 网格的粗粒度。
  • 提出 Oscillatory Lock-In (OLI) 调度策略：通过高低幅振荡交替的冷却阶段机制，在 3 个种子实验中冷却期 INT4 间隙降低 2.2 个百分点（t=-5.46, p<0.0001），但伴随 FP32 困惑度退化约 32 点的代价。

Key Contributions

  • Three-phase INT4 divergence structure: A complete forensic audit of all 154 Pythia-160m checkpoints reveals rapid learning (gap rises to ~6%), meta-stable plateau (gap bounded at 10-14% for ~70k steps), and explosive divergence (gap reaches 517% despite negligible FP32 change).
  • FP32 convergence as onset predictor: INT4 divergence begins at the exact step where FP32 perplexity stops improving (step ~77,000), not when LR begins its steep decay (LR is still at 45% of maximum at divergence onset).
  • Kurtosis measurement refutes outlier accumulation hypothesis: Weight excess kurtosis peaks at the Phase 2/3 boundary and then declines through Phase 3 (Pearson r=-0.26 with INT4 gap), directly contradicting the mechanism proposed by Park et al. (OSP).
  • INT8 immunity constrains the mechanism to 4-bit grid resolution: INT8 gap stays below 1% for all 143,000 steps while INT4 reaches 517%, a 650-fold difference that rules out general weight distribution pathology.
  • Oscillatory Lock-In (OLI) schedule with amplitude-calibration insight: SGDR warm restarts uniformly worsen INT4 robustness (0/9 pairwise wins); OLI's calibrated bump+cool phases reduce INT4 gap by 2.2pp (p<0.0001) in cool phases, demonstrating that amplitude calibration, not oscillation per se, determines benefit.

Methodology

The study applies a calibration-free per-group INT4 probe (group size g=128, asymmetric quantization, 16 discrete levels) to all 154 publicly available Pythia-160m training checkpoints. The probe is deliberately designed without GPTQ/AWQ-style calibration to measure the native grid compatibility of trained weights, not post-hoc repairability. The quantization gap is defined as the relative perplexity degradation: G = (PPL_INT4 - PPL_FP32) / PPL_FP32, evaluated on a fixed held-out validation set (32 batches of 512 tokens from The Pile validation split).

The analysis proceeds in four stages:

  1. Full audit: INT4 and INT8 probes applied sequentially to all 154 checkpoints, yielding continuous trajectories of quantization sensitivity.
  2. Phase identification: Visual and statistical identification of three phases in the INT4 gap trajectory.
  3. Mechanism testing: Weight excess kurtosis measured at 16 sampled checkpoints spanning all phases to test the outlier accumulation hypothesis.
  4. Fork experiment: From the pre-divergence checkpoint (step 70,000), three schedule conditions (cosine continuation, SGDR with T0=10k, OLI with 75-step bumps at 5*eta_max alternating with 300-step cool phases) are compared across N=3 seeds each for 30,000 continuation steps.

Results & Highlights

  • Phase 1 (steps 0-7k): FP32 PPL drops from >60,000 to ~43; INT4 gap rises from ~0% to ~6%.
  • Phase 2 (steps 7k-80k): FP32 PPL improves slowly (43 to 33.4); INT4 gap bounded near 10-14%. Best FP32 PPL = 33.38 at step 77,000.
  • Phase 3 (steps 80k-143k): FP32 PPL stagnates (33.4 to 35.3); INT4 gap explodes from 14% to 517%.
  • INT8 gap: Remains below 1% throughout all 143,000 steps (final: 0.79%).
  • Weight kurtosis: Rises from 1.2 to 27.9 through Phases 1-2, then declines to 3.3 by step 143k -- anti-correlated with INT4 gap in Phase 3.
  • Fork experiment -- SGDR: Final gap 16.7% (+/-2.7) vs cosine 12.3% (+/-0.8); 0/9 pairwise wins.
  • Fork experiment -- OLI cool phases: Mean gap 5.3% vs cosine 7.7% (delta = -2.2pp, t=-5.46, p<0.0001).
  • OLI FP32 cost: FP32 PPL ~76.0 vs cosine ~44.0 at step 100k, a significant degradation.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. The three-phase divergence structure, the FP32-convergence onset predictor, and the kurtosis-based refutation of outlier accumulation are all novel contributions. The finding that flat minima (lambda_max ~= 0) do not guarantee INT4 robustness challenges a core assumption in the quantization-aware training literature.
  • Rigor: Generally strong but with notable gaps. The 154-checkpoint audit is comprehensive; the fork experiment uses proper controls (N=3 seeds per condition) and statistical testing (paired t-tests, pairwise win counting). However, the kurtosis analysis uses only 16 sampled checkpoints, the anti-correlation (r=-0.26) is weak, and no positive mechanistic account is provided for why post-convergence updates preferentially produce INT4-incompatible configurations.
  • Reproducibility: Good. Code, probe implementation, and all 154-checkpoint audit results are publicly released. The calibration-free probe design is simple and well-specified. The fork experiment hyperparameters are fully documented.
  • Significance: Moderately high for practitioners deploying INT4-quantized LLMs. The practical recommendations (monitor FP32 convergence derivative, consider early stopping, avoid naive LR oscillation) are actionable. However, generalizability beyond Pythia-160m is untested.
  • Red flags: (1) Single model size (160M) limits generalizability claims. (2) OLI's FP32 cost (32 PPL points) is substantial and arguably makes the method impractical at tested amplitudes. (3) The OLI bump amplitude selection (5*eta_max) was derived from a calibration procedure that failed (producing a degenerate value of 557.9), and the fallback is a hard cap with limited justification. (4) The paper's title ("When Flat Minima Fail") is somewhat misleading -- the paper does not actually measure flatness (Hessian eigenvalues) directly, only FP32 convergence, making the connection to flat minima literature indirect.

Strengths

  • Exceptionally detailed forensic analysis leveraging all 154 Pythia-160m checkpoints -- a unique resource that few studies have exploited at this granularity.
  • Clean experimental design: calibration-free probe avoids confounds from post-hoc optimization; fixed validation set eliminates batch variance.
  • The three-phase structure is a genuinely new characterization that refines prior work by Catalan-Tatjer et al. (2026).
  • The kurtosis analysis, while limited to 16 checkpoints, provides direct evidence against a prominent competing hypothesis (OSP/outlier accumulation).
  • Honest discussion of OLI's FP32 cost and the limitations of the approach.
  • The insight that "amplitude calibration, not oscillation pattern, determines whether perturbation helps or hurts" is a clean, falsifiable claim.

Limitations

  • Single model, single scale: All results are from Pythia-160m. Whether the three-phase structure generalizes to larger models (1B, 7B, 70B) or different architectures (LLaMA, Mistral) is unknown. The author acknowledges this as the most important open question.
  • No positive mechanism: The paper rules out outlier accumulation but does not provide a constructive account of which weight configurations are INT4-incompatible. The discussion suggests "weights drifting to positions maximally misaligned with INT4 grid boundaries" but offers no formal characterization.
  • OLI impracticality: The 32-point FP32 PPL degradation at the tested amplitude makes OLI a proof-of-concept rather than a deployable intervention. Lower amplitudes (e.g., 2*eta_max) are mentioned as future work but not tested.
  • Weak kurtosis anti-correlation: r=-0.26 explains only ~7% of variance; the kurtosis trend is visually suggestive but statistically modest.
  • No comparison with weight averaging (LAWA): The discussion hypothesizes a mechanistic connection to LAWA but provides no experimental comparison.
  • Single-author breadth: The scope is ambitious for one author; some analyses (e.g., the fork experiment) could benefit from more conditions and seeds.

Relevance

本文对理解 LLM 训练过程中量化鲁棒性的退化规律具有重要参考价值，特别是 FP32 收敛后 INT4 兼容性持续恶化的发现，对大模型训练后量化的实践操作（如何时停止训练、如何选择学习率调度）有直接的指导意义。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does the three-phase divergence structure replicate in larger models (Pythia-1.4b, Pythia-6.9b, or entirely different families like LLaMA)? The checkpoint density available for Pythia is unusual -- can sparse checkpoint analyses recover the same structure?
  2. What is the positive mechanistic account of INT4-incompatible weight configurations? Can we formalize "grid misalignment" as a measurable quantity (e.g., average distance from nearest grid point) and track it through training?
  3. How does weight averaging (LAWA) compare quantitatively to OLI? Since both exploit the meta-stable Phase 2 window, can they be combined?
  4. What happens at intermediate bit-widths (INT5, INT6)? The paper shows INT8 is immune and INT4 catastrophically fails -- where is the transition?
  5. Could quantization-aware training (QAT) during the meta-stable plateau phase (before Phase 3 onset) lock in INT4 robustness more effectively than post-hoc schedule interventions?
