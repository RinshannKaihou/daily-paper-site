Gating Enables Curvature: A Geometric Expressivity Gap in Attention

arXiv: 2604.14702

Date: 2026-04-16

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14702v1

一句话总结： 本文从信息几何的角度严格证明了无门控注意力机制只能产生平坦（零曲率）的表示流形，而乘法门控能够使表示流形具有正曲率，并在合成实验中验证了更高的表示曲率与非线性任务性能正相关。
TL;DR: This paper proves a fundamental geometric expressivity gap in attention: standard (ungated) attention is provably restricted to flat statistical manifolds because its outputs are affine combinations of value vectors, while multiplicative gating enables intrinsically curved (positively curved) representation manifolds. The authors further show curvature can accumulate quadratically with depth under a structured regime, and provide synthetic experiments where gated models exhibit higher representation curvature and better performance on nonlinear classification tasks, with Pearson correlation of 0.79 between curvature and accuracy.

核心贡献

  • 几何框架的引入： 将注意力输出建模为高斯分布的均值参数，在 Fisher-Rao 度量下分析其诱导的统计流形几何，提供了一个独立于参数化的几何表达能力分析框架。
  • 无门控注意力的平坦性定理： 严格证明了标准注意力输出是 value 向量的仿射组合，因此诱导的统计流形是内蕴平坦的（Riemann 曲率张量恒为零），且这一平坦性不依赖于参数化选择。
  • 门控的曲率使能定理： 证明了乘法门控能够打破仿射结构，使得实现正曲率统计流形成为可能，且这一能力在标准内容感知注意力架构中同样成立（D >= 3 维度下持续存在）。
  • 深度放大效应： 在局部法形式下，证明了曲率可以随层数 L 二次增长（K >= a_0^2 det(D^2 psi) L^2），揭示了一个结构化的深度-曲率放大机制。
  • 鲁棒性与通用性： 证明了正曲率在参数空间中出现在非空开集上（局部鲁棒性），且在 Baire 范畴意义下非平坦性是通用的。

Key Contributions

  • Introduces a geometric framework for analyzing attention by modeling outputs as mean parameters of Gaussian distributions and studying induced statistical manifolds under the Fisher-Rao metric.
  • Proves that standard ungated attention is restricted to intrinsically flat statistical manifolds (Theorem 3.1), since outputs are affine combinations of value vectors with a constant induced metric.
  • Provides a geometric interpretation of the known low-rank bottleneck in attention: the affine structure confines representations to flat geometry, linking algebraic and geometric expressivity constraints.
  • Proves that multiplicative gating strictly enlarges the class of realizable geometries, enabling statistical manifolds with strictly positive curvature (Theorem 3.5, 3.7) that are provably unattainable by ungated attention.
  • Shows the curvature gap persists within standard content-aware transformer attention blocks (Theorem 3.7), for arbitrary output dimensions D >= 3 (Corollary 3.9), and is stable under parameter perturbations (Theorem 3.17).
  • Identifies a structured regime where curvature accumulates quadratically with depth (Theorem 3.13), providing a depth-amplification effect of the geometric expressivity gap.
  • Demonstrates generic non-flatness in the Baire category sense (Theorem 3.20), establishing that flatness is non-generic under multiplicative gating.

Methodology

The paper develops a rigorous mathematical framework that connects attention mechanism design to differential geometry:

  1. Statistical Manifold Formulation: Attention outputs Y(X) are interpreted as mean parameters of Gaussian distributions N(Y(X), I_D) with fixed identity covariance. The Fisher-Rao metric for this Gaussian location family is Euclidean on the mean parameter, so all geometric properties depend solely on the embedding Y(X) within R^D.
  1. Ungated Attention Analysis: The ungated output Y(X) = sum_i alpha_i(X) U_i with alpha in the probability simplex is shown to be an affine combination of value vectors. Choosing affine coordinates on the relative interior of the convex hull yields a constant induced metric, vanishing Christoffel symbols, and identically zero Riemann curvature tensor.
  1. Gated Attention Analysis: The gated output Y'(X) = Y(X) ⊙ sigma(X_g(X) W_theta) introduces element-wise multiplicative modulation. Lemma 3.4 establishes that the set of gating functions preserving affine structure is a nowhere dense (meager) closed subspace, so non-affinity holds generically.
  1. Witness Construction: An explicit construction shows that with D=3, a gated map can parametrize a spherical patch of S^2, yielding Gaussian curvature K = 1 > 0, while the ungated map remains flat. This is extended to content-aware attention with standard Q, K, V, O projections.
  1. Depth Composition: Under a local normal form where gated layers produce coherent additive scalar modulations, the Gaussian curvature at a critical point scales as A_L^2, where A_L = sum of layer coefficients. With uniform lower bounds, this gives quadratic growth in L.
  1. Experiments: A synthetic sequence classification task with a deliberately curved decision rule (involving sin(2.5theta) + 0.6(r - 1.2) in polar coordinates) tests whether the theoretical gap manifests in trained models. A finite-difference curvature proxy measures representation nonlinearity. A linear control task serves as ablation.

Results & Highlights

  • Curvature monotonicity with gate strength: Increasing gate strength alpha consistently increases representation curvature across all metric condition numbers (Figure 2).
  • Isotropic curvature is intrinsic: Isotropic curvature curves coincide across condition numbers, confirming the geometric structure is intrinsic to the representation mapping rather than metric-dependent.
  • Decision boundary quality: Gated models produce decision boundaries closely aligned with the nonlinear ground-truth boundary, while ungated models produce approximately piecewise-linear boundaries (Figure 3).
  • Curvature-performance correlation: Pearson correlation of 0.7886 between isotropic curvature and test accuracy, with mild saturation at higher curvature values (Figure 4).
  • Ablation confirms gating specificity: Multiplicative gating substantially outperforms both ungated attention and pointwise SiLU nonlinearity in terms of curvature and accuracy, confirming gains arise from gating structure specifically (Figure 5).
  • Linear control task: Gating provides no consistent advantage on linear decision boundaries, confirming benefits arise specifically when nonlinear geometric structure is needed.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The geometric framing of the ungated vs. gated attention distinction is genuinely novel. While the low-rank bottleneck of attention is known, connecting it to intrinsic flatness of the representation manifold and proving a strict geometric expressivity gap is a meaningful theoretical contribution. The witness constructions are clean and the results build progressively.
  • Rigor: The mathematical proofs are rigorous and well-structured. The progression from the flatness theorem through generic non-affinity, explicit witnesses, content-aware realizability, depth amplification, robustness, and genericity is logically coherent. Proofs are deferred to the appendix, which is standard practice.
  • Reproducibility: The synthetic experiments use a clearly described setup with a specific decision rule, model architecture (single attention block, 64-dim, sequence length 8), and training procedure (AdamW). However, no code repository is referenced. The mathematical results are self-contained and independently verifiable.
  • Significance: The theoretical contribution is significant for understanding why gating helps from a geometric perspective, complementing empirical observations in the literature. However, the practical implications are limited by the gap between the idealized theoretical setting (Gaussian location family with fixed covariance, single attention block) and real-world transformer architectures with multi-head attention, layer normalization, and complex training dynamics.
  • Red flags:
  • The experiments are limited to a synthetic task. No experiments on real-world benchmarks (e.g., language modeling, image classification) are provided, making it unclear whether the geometric insights translate to practical performance differences.
  • The depth amplification result (Theorem 3.13) relies on a local normal form assumption (Lemma 3.10) that is a structural reduction, not universally guaranteed. The authors acknowledge this, but the practical prevalence of this regime is not established.
  • The curvature proxy used in experiments is a finite-difference second-order quantity, not an actual Riemannian sectional curvature. The authors note this, but the gap between the theoretical curvature (sectional curvature of statistical manifolds) and the experimental proxy should be kept in mind.
  • The fixed-covariance Gaussian model is a significant simplification. In practice, the "statistical manifold" interpretation may not hold if attention outputs do not naturally correspond to distribution parameters.

Strengths

  • Clean, rigorous mathematical framework that isolates the geometric contribution of the attention operator itself.
  • Progressive theorem structure that builds from flatness to curvature gap to depth amplification to robustness.
  • The geometric interpretation of the low-rank bottleneck as a flatness constraint provides genuine new insight.
  • The synthetic experiments are well-designed to test the specific theoretical predictions, with appropriate controls (linear task, ablation variants).
  • The paper is well-written with clear notation and good separation between main results and technical proofs.

Limitations

  • Experiments are restricted to a single synthetic task with no real-world validation. The practical relevance of the geometric gap for LLMs or vision transformers remains untested.
  • The Fisher-Rao / Gaussian location family framework is a significant modeling assumption. Whether attention outputs in practice correspond to distribution means is debatable.
  • The depth amplification result depends on a specific local normal form whose prevalence in trained networks is not established empirically.
  • The curvature proxy in experiments is a finite-difference approximation of second-order variation, not a direct measurement of Riemannian curvature, creating a gap between theory and measurement.
  • Only single-head attention is analyzed; the multi-head case is not addressed.
  • The paper does not compare against other mechanisms that could similarly break the affine constraint (e.g., different activation functions, normalization layers).

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Does the geometric expressivity gap manifest in large-scale transformer models (e.g., LLMs with gated attention like GLA) on real-world benchmarks, or is it primarily a theoretical distinction that is washed out by other architectural components?
  • How does multi-head attention interact with the flatness result? Does each head independently satisfy the flatness constraint in the ungated case?
  • Can the curvature-based analysis predict when gating will be beneficial for specific downstream tasks, providing a principled criterion for architecture selection?
  • What happens to the geometric framework when the Gaussian assumption is relaxed to more flexible distribution families (e.g., Gaussian with input-dependent covariance, or mixture models)?
  • Is the local normal form for depth amplification (Lemma 3.10) approximately satisfied in trained deep gated transformers, or is it primarily a theoretical construct?
