Robustness of Vision Foundation Models to Common Perturbations

arXiv: 2604.14973

Date: 2026-04-16

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14973v1

一句话总结： 本文首次系统研究了视觉基础模型（CLIP、DINO v2）对常见图像扰动（JPEG压缩、亮度/对比度调整、模糊等）的鲁棒性，提出了三种量化鲁棒性指标（其中DivergenceRadius满足全部五项数学性质），发现这些模型普遍不够鲁棒且扰动会显著降低下游分类和深度估计性能，并提出了一种保持效用的微调增强方法。
TL;DR: This paper presents the first systematic study on how robust vision foundation models (CLIP, DINO v2) are to common image perturbations like JPEG compression, brightness/contrast changes, and various blurs. The authors propose three robustness metrics — cosine similarity, Euclidean distance, and a novel DivergenceRadius (based on minimum enclosing ball radius) — and formalize five mathematical properties these metrics should satisfy, proving DivergenceRadius meets all five while the others fail on the worst-robustness property. Evaluating six industry-scale models across nine perturbation categories on ImageNet, Food101, and NYU-Depth V2, they find models are generally non-robust (e.g., glass blurring drops zero-shot ImageNet accuracy by 9.4%), ViT architectures outperform ResNet, and robustness values can linearly predict downstream performance degradation. A fine-tuning method is proposed that improves robustness without sacrificing classification accuracy.

Key Contributions

  • First systematic study on the robustness of vision foundation models to common (non-adversarial) perturbations such as JPEG compression, brightness/contrast adjustments, and various types of blurring
  • Three robustness metrics with formal theoretical analysis: cosine similarity-based, Euclidean distance-based, and DivergenceRadius (minimum enclosing ball radius), with five desired mathematical properties formalized; DivergenceRadius is proven to satisfy all five while cosine similarity and Euclidean distance fail the worst-robustness property
  • Comprehensive empirical evaluation of six industry-scale models (CLIP ViT-B/16, ViT-L/14, RN50, RN50x64; DINO v2 ViT-L/14, ViT-g/14) across nine perturbation categories on three datasets, revealing that ViT architectures are consistently more robust than ResNet, and that larger CLIP models are paradoxically less robust
  • Linear prediction framework: robustness metric values can predict downstream task performance (classification accuracy, depth estimation RMSE) under perturbations via simple linear regression with low mean squared error
  • Fine-tuning approach that balances a robustness loss (aligning perturbed embeddings with original) and a utility loss (preserving original embeddings), successfully improving robustness without degrading accuracy

Methodology

Robustness Metrics Design:

The authors formalize the problem where a foundation model f outputs embedding vectors for images, and a perturbation function P(x,k) produces perturbed versions. They propose three metrics to quantify how much embedding vectors diverge under perturbations:

  1. Cosine Similarity metric (R_cs): Measures the minimum cosine similarity between any pair of perturbed embeddings, normalized to [0,1]. Fails the worst-robustness property.
  2. Euclidean Distance metric (R_ed): Maximum pairwise Euclidean distance between perturbed embeddings, divided by 2. Proven to be equivalent to the square root of R_cs (Theorem 2).
  3. DivergenceRadius (R_dr): The radius of the minimum enclosing ball for all perturbed embedding vectors, solved via Welzl's algorithm in O(dn) time. Satisfies all five mathematical properties.

Five Mathematical Properties: Bounded domain [0,1], monotonicity (larger perturbation domain implies no less divergence), best robustness (identical embeddings yield 0), worst robustness (uniformly distributed embeddings yield 1), and rotational invariance.

Experimental Setup:

  • Six foundation models from CLIP and DINO v2 families with different architectures (ViT, ResNet) and sizes (38M to 1136M parameters)
  • Three datasets: ImageNet, Food101, NYU-Depth V2
  • Nine perturbation types: JPEG compression, brightness/contrast adjustments, defocus/elastic/fog/frost/glass blurring, Gaussian noise
  • Equally-spaced sampling with m=5 for computational efficiency
  • Downstream tasks: zero-shot classification, linear-probe classification, depth estimation

Robustness Enhancement:

Fine-tuning minimizes a weighted sum: L_1 (robustness loss, maximizing cosine similarity between original and perturbed embeddings) + lambda * L_2 (utility loss, maximizing cosine similarity between original and fine-tuned embeddings), using 20K ImageNet training images for 50 epochs.

Results & Highlights

  • Models are generally non-robust: Average DivergenceRadius values are notably above 0 across all perturbations and models, with defocus blurring and glass blurring causing the most divergence
  • Glass blurring is most damaging: Reduces zero-shot ImageNet accuracy by 9.4%, linear-probe accuracy by 3.7%, and frost blurring reduces Food101 accuracy by up to 12.7%
  • ViT outperforms ResNet: ViT-B/16 is consistently more robust than RN50, and ViT-L/14 outperforms RN50x64 across all perturbations and datasets
  • Counter-intuitive scaling for CLIP: Larger CLIP models (ViT-L/14) are less robust than smaller ones (ViT-B/16), while DINO v2 shows the opposite trend (ViT-g/14 more robust than ViT-L/14), suggesting pre-training paradigm matters
  • DivergenceRadius correlates with accuracy drop: Pearson correlations between average robustness values and accuracy degradation range from 0.89 to 0.94 across perturbation types
  • Linear prediction works well: Simple linear regression using DivergenceRadius can predict ACC_p and RMSE_p with very low mean squared error
  • Fine-tuning improves robustness without utility loss: After enhancement, DivergenceRadius decreases while zero-shot classification accuracy remains nearly unchanged (e.g., from 68.4% to 68.0%), with lambda=1 providing a good balance
  • Extreme lambda=0 destroys utility: When lambda=0 (robustness only), accuracy collapses to 0.16%, highlighting the necessity of the utility preservation term

Critical Assessment

Novelty (Moderate): While the study of common perturbations for classifiers dates back to Hendrycks & Dietterich (2019), applying this lens to foundation models' embedding-space robustness is genuinely new. The DivergenceRadius metric based on minimum enclosing ball is a clean theoretical contribution. However, the overall framework — measure robustness, show downstream impact, propose fine-tuning — follows a well-established template. The fine-tuning method itself is relatively straightforward (cosine similarity loss between original and perturbed embeddings) and not a major methodological advance.

Rigor (Strong): The mathematical formalization of five properties and proofs for each metric are rigorous. The equivalence proof between Euclidean distance and cosine similarity metrics (Theorem 2) is a nice theoretical result. Empirical evaluation covers multiple models, datasets, perturbation types, and downstream tasks comprehensively. The sampling analysis (equally-spaced vs. random) is thorough.

Reproducibility (Moderate-High): All models (CLIP, DINO v2) are publicly available. Perturbation functions follow the well-known ImageNet-C benchmark. Datasets are public. The fine-tuning hyperparameters are specified (learning rate 1e-5, 50 epochs, 20K images). Code is not mentioned as publicly available, which is a minor concern.

Significance (Moderate): The finding that common, non-adversarial perturbations significantly affect foundation model embeddings is practically important for deployment. The linear predictability of downstream performance from robustness values is actionable. However, the perturbation levels tested are sometimes quite severe (e.g., very low JPEG quality), and the fine-tuning approach only demonstrates improvement on a single perturbation type (JPEG), leaving generalization unclear.

Red Flags:

  • The fine-tuning experiments are limited to only JPEG compression; generalization to other perturbation types is not demonstrated
  • Workshop paper status suggests it may be a shorter, less polished contribution
  • The lambda sensitivity analysis (Table 9) shows lambda=0 leads to catastrophic utility collapse (0.16% accuracy), suggesting the method can be brittle if not carefully tuned
  • No comparison with existing adversarial robustness fine-tuning methods (e.g., adversarial contrastive learning) on common perturbations

Strengths

  • Clean theoretical framework with formal properties and proofs for robustness metrics
  • DivergenceRadius is an elegant metric that satisfies all desired properties and has efficient computation via Welzl's algorithm
  • Comprehensive empirical coverage: 6 models x 9 perturbations x 3 datasets
  • Practical finding that robustness values linearly predict downstream performance, enabling cheap robustness auditing
  • Clear, well-structured writing with good separation between metric design, empirical study, and enhancement

Limitations

  • Fine-tuning enhancement is only demonstrated on JPEG compression, leaving cross-perturbation generalization untested
  • The perturbation parameter domains are fixed and may not cover all real-world scenarios; for example, JPEG quality [30,70] excludes very high quality images
  • Only two foundation model families (CLIP, DINO v2) are tested; results may not generalize to newer models like SAM, SigLIP, or multimodal LLMs
  • The fine-tuning requires access to model parameters, limiting applicability for API-only models like GPT-4V
  • The study treats perturbation parameters as discrete sets (via sampling) rather than truly continuous, which may miss worst-case perturbation levels
  • No analysis of how robustness interacts with other model properties like fairness, calibration, or out-of-distribution generalization

Relevance

本文研究了视觉基础模型在常见图像扰动下的鲁棒性问题，对于理解大规模预训练模型的实际部署可靠性具有重要意义。提出的DivergenceRadius指标和鲁棒性-性能线性预测框架可为模型选择和安全评估提供实用工具，尤其是ViT比ResNet更鲁棒的发现对架构设计有参考价值。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does the fine-tuning approach generalize across perturbation types — can a model fine-tuned on one perturbation (e.g., JPEG) maintain improved robustness on unseen perturbations (e.g., fog, frost)?
  2. How does common-perturbation robustness correlate with adversarial robustness — are models robust to one automatically robust to the other?
  3. Would the DivergenceRadius metric and findings extend to multimodal foundation models (e.g., CLIP with text encoder, LLaVA) where embeddings are shared across modalities?
  4. Can robustness-aware pre-training objectives (rather than post-hoc fine-tuning) produce foundation models that are inherently more robust to common perturbations?
  5. What is the interplay between robustness to common perturbations and other trustworthiness properties like fairness and calibration in downstream tasks?
