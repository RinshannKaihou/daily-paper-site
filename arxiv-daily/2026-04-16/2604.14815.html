Domain Fine-Tuning FinBERT on Finnish Histopathological Reports: Train-Time Signals and Downstream Correlations

arXiv: 2604.14815

Date: 2026-04-16

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14815v1

一句话总结： 本文通过对芬兰语BERT模型（FinBERT）在芬兰语组织病理学报告上进行领域微调，观察到训练时的损失曲线下降幅度和嵌入向量的几何变化（CKA、各向同性等）与下游分类任务性能改善之间存在相关性，为在标注数据稀缺的医疗AI场景中提前预判领域微调的价值提供了实用信号。
TL;DR: This paper fine-tunes FinBERT (Finnish BERT) on Finnish histopathological reports and 7 other Finnish text domains, finding that train-time MLM loss drop magnitude, embedding isotropy changes, and CKA shifts in lower layers correlate with downstream classification improvements. The key practical insight is that in healthcare AI where labels take months to acquire, you can run DFT on unlabeled data first and predict whether it will help your downstream task -- domains already "familiar" to the model (like YLE news) show near-zero loss change and negligible classification gain, while novel domains (histopathology, legal text) show dramatic loss drops and meaningful improvements. The authors transparently acknowledge these correlations are tentative (9 domains, 101 features vs 129 targets) and call for replication.

核心贡献

  • 提出并验证了从领域微调（DFT）训练过程中提取信号来预测下游任务收益的思路： 在医疗AI场景中，标签获取可能耗时数月，但可以先在无标签数据上运行DFT，通过观察训练损失变化、嵌入几何变化等信号预判DFT是否有价值。
  • 观察到不同领域数据的MLM损失曲线差异巨大： 已在预训练中见过的YLE新闻数据几乎无损失变化，而组织病理学和法律文本（FinLex）数据则出现剧烈的损失下降，这可作为数据"新颖度"的指示器。
  • 发现了多个与下游分类性能相关的DFT信号： 训练损失相对改善、嵌入各向同性（isotropy）有效秩变化、低层CKA变化以及聚类ARI改善等指标均与分类性能提升存在相关性。
  • 提供了低资源语言医疗NLP领域的实用经验： 在芬兰语这种少数语言上，多语言模型效果不佳，必须依赖语言特定模型（FinBERT），而领域微调对于医疗文本尤为重要。

Key Contributions

  • Identifies train-time signals from DFT that predict downstream task utility: When labels are unavailable (common in healthcare AI), one can run domain fine-tuning first and observe the MLM loss curve geometry to predict whether the effort will pay off for eventual classification tasks.
  • Demonstrates dramatic differences in loss curves across domains: Domains already represented in FinBERT pretraining (YLE news) show flat loss curves; novel domains like histopathological reports and legal text show steep loss drops, serving as a practical "data novelty" indicator.
  • Correlates embedding geometry changes with classification gains: Features including relative loss improvement, isotropy effective rank delta, lower-layer CKA shifts, and clustering ARI improvements show correlation with downstream classification performance across 9 Finnish text domains.
  • Provides practical guidance for low-resource clinical NLP: Validates that Finnish-specific BERT is necessary (multilingual models underperform), and that domain fine-tuning on medical text yields measurable improvements for downstream tasks with limited labeled data (100-1000 samples).

Methodology

The authors use the TurkuNLP Finnish BERT model (FinBERT, bert-base-finnish-cased-v1) as their base model. Their experimental pipeline consists of three stages:

  1. Domain Fine-Tuning (DFT): They run continued MLM pretraining (15% masking) on the training split (set A, ~70%) of each domain dataset, tracking train and eval loss curves. Datasets include histopathological reports (~125K samples, no labels), YLE news (small/large), Wikipedia (small/large), FinLex legal documents, Eduskunta parliamentary records, movie subtitles, Gutenberg books, and webcrawl data -- 8 domains total with 9 classification tasks.
  1. Embedding Analysis: They extract [CLS] embeddings from all 12+1 transformer layers on the test split (set B) for both the base and fine-tuned models, then compare the embedding point clouds using CKA (Centered Kernel Alignment), Procrustes Analysis, RSA (Representational Similarity Analysis), isotropy measures (Partition Function, Effective Rank), and clustering metrics (K-means with Silhouette score, ARI, NMI).
  1. Classification Experiments: They train simple classifiers (logistic regression or k-NN) on small subsets (100, 250, 500, 1000 samples) of [CLS] embeddings from set B, evaluating on set C. To handle varying baseline accuracies across domains, they use Error Reduction Rate (ERR) and logit delta as comparable performance metrics rather than raw accuracy.

The correlation analysis between 101 DFT features and 129 classification targets is conducted via scatter plots and linear fits, with p-values reported. The authors transparently note they did not preregister which features/targets to compare.

Results & Highlights

  • Histopathological and FinLex domains showed the largest MLM loss drops during DFT (~50%+ relative improvement), confirming these domains are far from FinBERT's original pretraining distribution. YLE datasets showed near-zero loss change, confirming they were already well-represented.
  • DFT improved classification most on novel domains: The histopathology dataset (no labels available for classification) showed the most dramatic loss signal, while for classifiable domains, FinLex and Eduskunta showed the largest classification improvements from DFT.
  • Five key DFT signals correlated with classification gains (in order of prominence): (1) train loss relative improvement, (2) isotropy effective rank delta and partition function delta, (3) first-epoch loss relative improvement, (4) CKA max and CKA layers 1-3 changes, (5) clustering ARI improvement.
  • Later-layer embedding changes did NOT strongly correlate with classification performance -- a curious finding suggesting lower-layer adaptations may be more diagnostic.
  • Simple classifiers on small data (250 samples) sufficed to measure DFT benefit, which is practically relevant for healthcare settings with limited labeled data.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: Moderate. Domain-adaptive pretraining for clinical NLP is well-established (BioBERT, ClinicalBERT, BioBERTurk, MediAlbertina). The specific contribution here is the prediction of DFT utility from train-time signals on unlabeled data, which is a practical but incremental idea. The connection between loss curve geometry and scaling laws is noted but not deeply formalized.
  • Rigor: Moderate. The experimental design is sound (A/B/C splits, multiple comparison methods, multiple classifiers). However, the correlation analysis is underpowered: 9 domains with 101 features and 129 targets means many spurious correlations are expected, and the authors acknowledge they did not preregister hypotheses. No formal multiple-testing correction is applied.
  • Reproducibility: Moderate-High. The paper uses publicly available FinBERT, describes experimental setups clearly, and uses publicly available datasets (except histopathology). Code is not linked. The histopathological data from the biobank is private, which limits full replication.
  • Significance: Moderate. For the Finnish clinical NLP community and similar low-resource language healthcare settings, this is useful practical guidance. The idea of predicting DFT value from training signals is potentially impactful for resource-constrained healthcare AI projects. However, the tentative nature of the correlations and small number of domains limits generalizability.
  • Red flags: (1) The 101x129 feature-target heatmap without multiple testing correction is a significant statistical concern. The "strong horizontal strips" the authors identify could still be artifacts. (2) Only 9 data points per correlation make any statistical claim very fragile. (3) The paper explicitly states it is a "case report and invitation for further exploration" -- this is honest but also means the claims are limited. (4) No held-out test domain for validating the predicted correlations.

Strengths

  • Transparent and honest about limitations, including the lack of preregistered hypotheses and the risk of spurious correlations.
  • Practically motivated by a real healthcare AI bottleneck (long delays in obtaining labeled data from biobanks).
  • Clean experimental design with multiple comparison methods (CKA, RSA, Procrustes, isotropy, clustering) providing convergent evidence.
  • Covers 8 diverse Finnish text domains, providing a useful comparative landscape.
  • Uses simple classifiers (logistic regression, k-NN) on small data deliberately to emulate realistic healthcare AI scenarios.
  • The observation that lower-layer changes correlate more with downstream performance than later-layer changes is interesting and somewhat counterintuitive.

Limitations

  • Statistical power: Only 9 domains with 101 features and 129 targets; the correlation analysis is severely underpowered and no multiple testing correction is applied.
  • No preregistered hypotheses: The features were selected post-hoc from a large pool, inflating the risk of finding spurious patterns.
  • No external validation: The correlations found are not tested on a held-out domain or in a separate study.
  • Private dataset: The histopathological data from the Central Finland Biobank is not publicly available, limiting reproducibility.
  • Classification methodology is deliberately suboptimal: While this is justified by the goal of measuring relative improvement, it means the absolute performance numbers are not informative.
  • Limited to BERT-class models: The findings may not transfer to decoder-only models or larger LLMs, which are increasingly relevant.
  • Single language: All experiments are on Finnish; generalization to other low-resource languages is uncertain.

Relevance

本文对于关注低资源语言医疗NLP和领域自适应技术的研究者具有直接参考价值。其核心思路——在无标签数据上通过观察训练信号来预判领域微调的收益——可推广到其他医疗AI场景中的数据策略规划，特别是在标注获取困难的临床文本处理任务中。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Would the observed correlations between DFT signals and classification improvements hold with a formal preregistered study using 50+ domains and proper multiple testing correction?
  • How do the findings transfer to modern encoder-decoder or decoder-only architectures (e.g., Finnish LLaMA, mT5) rather than BERT?
  • Can the "data novelty" signal from loss curve shape be formalized into a quantitative metric that predicts optimal DFT epoch count or data budget allocation?
  • Would combining multiple DFT signal features into a predictive model (e.g., regression) outperform using any single feature for predicting downstream task benefit?
  • How does the correlation change when using task-adaptive pretraining (TAPT) on the specific classification data versus domain-adaptive pretraining (DAPT) on the broader domain corpus?
