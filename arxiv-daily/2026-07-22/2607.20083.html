01Methodology

A clean identity turns "evaluator discriminability" into an optimizable quantity.

DynamicRubric starts from a probability-allocation view: at the current policy's sampled response set C, the directional derivative of moving probability mass from a loser y⁻ to a winner y⁺ is exactly the evaluator's relative score gap E(x,y⁺) − E(x,y⁻). So keeping the evaluator discriminative on the current response distribution is not a side-effect — it is the optimization signal itself.

A function-class inclusion (E_prompt ⊆ E_set) then formalizes the intuition that an evaluator must see the candidate set: set-conditioned evaluators never incur worse local ranking loss than prompt-only ones as the policy drifts. Together these two results motivate a bilevel loop where the evaluator is re-fit on fresh policy samples every round.

Bilevel Co-Evolution Loop
  1. Sample K responses from current policy πθ → set C.
  2. DR-Generator emits weighted binary rubric items {(rm, wm)} targeting differences within C.
  3. DR-Verifier (frozen) judges each item yes/no per response; weighted sum → response score.
  4. Train evaluator on Jdisc (non-trivial splits) + λ·Janchor (Bradley–Terry + RBP on ranked anchors).
  5. Train policy via GRPO using the new evaluator scores → re-sample. Repeat.

03Assessment

Strong theory and breadth — cost transparency is the open wound.

Strengths

  • A single clean identity (directional derivative = score gap) seeds the whole framework — theory and system tightly fused.
  • Broad evaluation: 6 preference + 4 open-gen benches, verifiable-task transfer, continuous-evolution study, and a live online A/B.
  • Self-contained: needs only ranked anchor data (Nectar), no black-box mega-judge in the loop.
  • 8B backbone dominates far larger baselines — clear engineering and cost upside.
  • Full production rollout in WeChat Search AI answering — strong real-world credibility.

Limitations

  • Serial generator→verifier calls raise policy-training wall-clock; no direct FLOPs/latency comparison vs the 235B baseline.
  • Frozen DR-Verifier caps the supervision ceiling; adaptive or stronger verifiers (e.g. GPT-4.1) unexplored.
  • Online metrics reported qualitatively only — no absolute values or confidence intervals, so gains can't be independently checked.
  • Anchor data (Nectar) may bias rubric direction; domain coverage effects on code/math are not discussed.
  • Validated mainly on Qwen3-8B / Llama-3.1-8B; scaling to ultra-large backbones is unproven.

04Open Questions

Where the framework could break, extend, or be stress-tested next.

  1. Does the Δgap signal generalize beyond sampled sets to the full distribution, or to non-linear policy objectives beyond the linear local proxy?
  2. How much higher is the ceiling if DR-Verifier becomes trainable or adaptive — and does training stay stable?
  3. How strong is Nectar's domain bias on rubric direction; does the method degrade on code/math where ranked anchors are scarce?
  4. Can co-evolution collapse into evaluator–policy collusion on dimensions the policy already wins — and does it need explicit diversity or adversarial regularization?
  5. Can async evaluator updates / parallel verification bring wall-clock cost down to a single scalar reward model, and at what speedup?
  6. Do the produced rubrics generalize out-of-distribution and transfer as an interpretable evaluation protocol to new tasks?
