Methodology — four archetypes, four cache paths

AR video diffusion reuses history via a growing KV cache, but attention stays O(L²) and aggressive eviction (Dummy Forcing) drops long-range context that some heads actually need — producing inter-frame flicker. HeadCast's bet: pretrained heads already have stable, heterogeneous attention preferences, so classify each once and give it only the cache it uses.

STAGE 1

Warm-up

Run full-context attention until the cache first exceeds window W.

STAGE 2

Online Classification

One-time, at max-noise step t=1000, on a single AR block.

STAGE 3

Heterogeneous Cache

Split the monolithic KV cache along the head axis into three buffers.

STAGE 4

Head-Specific Attention

Each archetype runs its own FlashAttention-compatible path.

One-time classification score (per head h, at t=1000) For each head, compare a full-context reference output Oref against three restricted outputs:
arch = argmax  cos(Oref, Ok),   k ∈ {sink, dummy, spatial}
θs=θd=θsp=0.95  ·  θsc=0.755
spatial score (worst-case, anti-ghosting):
score = 5th-pct(cosspatial) − γ·MSE(Ospatial, Oref),   γ=5

Strengths & Limitations

+ Strengths

  • Genuinely training-free and plug-and-play; orthogonal to diffusion distillation, so it stacks on existing pipelines.
  • Head-level granularity beats Dummy Forcing's coarse eviction — and the retained Global heads mechanistically explain why flicker disappears (BBD at parity, +36% for Dummy).
  • Hardware-friendly: the Spatial grid turns dense attention into batched dense sub-matrices reusing stock FlashAttention — no slow custom kernel.
  • Acceleration grows with resolution, aligning with the high-definition video trend — clear industrial value.
  • Broad evaluation: 4 backbones × multi-resolution × multi-duration, with PSNR/LPIPS/VBench/user-study/BBD and quantified classification overhead.

! Limitations

  • Speed ceiling is bounded by a model's innate Spatial-head share; on Spatial-poor backbones (e.g. LongLive 480P) Dummy Forcing is actually faster (1.16× vs 1.08×).
  • One-time t=1000 classification ignores heads that localize as denoising proceeds — they stay on the Global path (safe, but unaccelerated).
  • Thresholds (θ=0.95, θsc=0.755, γ=5) and the 10×10 grid are empirical; robustness across very different patchings / position encodings is unverified.
  • Limited short-clip gain: at 480P both methods manage only 1.02–1.08× because the cache isn't yet the bottleneck.
  • Minute-scale extrapolation, multi-GPU scaling, and batched throughput are not evaluated; no standalone Limitations section or user-study significance details.

Questions for further exploration

  1. Could a lightweight, periodic re-classification during denoising (every few steps) capture heads that localize late, recovering extra speed without sacrificing fidelity — instead of a single frozen t=1000 verdict?
  2. Do θ=0.95, θsc=0.755 and the 10×10 grid survive architectures with different patching or 3D RoPE / no-RoPE schemes, or is an unsupervised self-calibration step needed for clean transfer?
  3. Can a model's Spatial-head share be predicted from architecture or training-data priors — giving a prior estimate of HeadCast's speed ceiling to guide backbone design?
  4. When stacked with natively-sparse training methods (Sparse Forcing) or few-step distillation, does HeadCast compose cooperatively or conflict — and can the two be jointly optimized?
  5. For minute-scale generation with scene-cut KV re-caching (LongLive regime), does the Global head's fixed window still hold cross-scene coherence, or must it pair with hierarchical memory?
  6. What is the statistical power of the 2AFC user study — sample size, subject background, significance test? Is the 39.6% preference over full attention reliably above chance?
