01 Methodology & Architecture

Solar Open 2 keeps the Solar Open 1 backbone but adds three structural changes: a hybrid attention stack, NoPE, and a 128→320 routed-expert pool. Pre-training runs four stages (~12T tokens); post-training runs SFT → multi-domain RL → twelve specialists → MOPD consolidation.

Four headline techniques

  • Hybrid attention for 1M context. Negative-eigenvalue KDA (write strength β=2σ(·)∈(0,2)) lets the recurrent state flip sign and self-correct — constant KV cache in sequence length at ~25% of all-softmax cost, retaining exact global recall via the softmax layers.
  • Cross-generation selective weight transfer. Transfers only the shape-compatible 5.69B params (2.3%) from Solar Open 1; the 200B-A15B proxy reaches CE 1.8 in 12.6B tokens vs 21.5B from scratch (~1.7× fewer).
  • Value-per-token data curation. A 20T pool is refined to a 10T mixture (real:synthetic 4:6, math/code ≥15% each, English >80%); at equal 300B budget on a 10B MoE it beats the old recipe on every group (en_code +0.216, ko_math +0.204, en_math +0.146).
  • Fully async RL + MOPD. Disaggregated trainer/actor with a fresh-token-fraction staleness gateway; MOPD consolidates 12 specialists via exact full-vocabulary KL on the student's own rollouts — one rollout per prompt, no reward term, no λ.

Other highlights

  • Architecture efficiency (Fig. 4): the hybrid stack reaches MMLU 0.55 in 210B tokens where all-softmax needs 671B (3.2×); HellaSwag 0.63 in 210B vs 377B (1.8×).
  • Blind pairwise eval: preferred on 44.1% of 835 Korean turns vs DeepSeek-V4-Flash's 26.6%; decisive factual-correctness wins 21.2% vs 8.5%.
  • Tokenizer efficiency: 4.41 bytes/token on Ko-GDPval, ~24% above the best global model.

03 Strengths

  • Architecturally principled. Every design choice is ablated on a proxy first; the negative-eigenvalue motivation is theoretically grounded.
  • Efficient compute recipe — selective warm start + value-curated 10T mixture + async RL + single-rollout MOPD — directly targets a fixed-compute constraint.
  • Honest framing of where it wins (English knowledge/reasoning, Korean, officework) and where it lags (repo/terminal SE).
  • OfficeVerse officework pipeline (industry × task-type matrix, deterministic validation gates) is a substantial, underexplored contribution.
  • Comprehensive eval across ~25 benchmarks and five agent families in both English and Korean.

04 Limitations

  • Persistent English agent gaps: SWE-Bench Verified 70.4, Terminal Bench Hard 28.3 (vs MiMo's 41.7), ArtifactsBench 55.9.
  • In-house Korean benchmarks (Ko-AIME, KBank-MMLU, Ko-GDPval) are not externally reproducible or auditable.
  • No frontier-tier closed models in the Korean comparison — only "fast-tier" Haiku 4.5 / GPT-5.4 mini.
  • No public weight release announced; limits external validation of the architecture and post-training claims.
  • Stage-4 Length Expansion caused a performance drop needing checkpoint merging to repair — long-context + agent-data curriculum not yet stable.
  • MOPD trades away headroom: the consolidated model is bounded by the best specialist per domain by design.

05 Questions for Further Exploration

Do the negative-eigenvalue (β∈(0,2)) and softmax-first gains hold at 250B, or shrink beyond the 10B proxy?
Is the 1M context window load-bearing in Ko-GDPval / APEX-Agents, or would 256K suffice?
Could MOPD's KL-only objective take a sparse verifiable-reward term to recover headroom above the best specialist?
Does selective weight transfer generalize to dense→MoE or a different linear-attention core?
How would it fare against frontier-tier closed models (not just fast-tier) on the Korean suite?
Is the Stage-4 regression a fundamental data problem, or fixable via long/short data interleaving?
