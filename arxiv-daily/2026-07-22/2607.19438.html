BaseRT: Advancing Best-in-Class LLM Inference with Apple M5 Neural Accelerators

Fabian Waschkowski · Prabod Rathnayaka · Lukas Wesemann | Base Compute, Melbourne | arXiv:2607.19438 | 2026-07-21

Metal 4 cooperative-tensor Apple M5 Pro Prefill GEMM / MoE / Attention 15 model configs 4 MoE models Open source · v0.1.6

Key Finding

A workload-aware split routes only compute-bound prefill through hand-written Metal 4 tensor kernels; decode is left on GEMV paths because the accelerators cannot beat memory bandwidth.

Key Metric

6.4×

prefill throughput vs llama.cpp — Gemma-4-E2B (Q8) @ pp2048: 16,264 vs 2,547 tok/s.

Why This Matters

Time-to-first-token is prefill-dominated, so the gains drop directly into lower interactive latency for long-prompt and agentic on-device workloads.

Critical Edge

Only a single M5 Pro is measured, baselines run at default settings, and the largest margins land on the newest model families where baselines may be undertuned.

Why the split path works

Prefill multiplies the full prompt activation matrix by weights → arithmetic intensity grows with sequence length → compute-bound, the regime where a matmul engine pays off.

Decode is an M=1 GEMV streaming the whole weight set per step → memory-bandwidth-bound, so tensor cores cannot help. This asymmetry organises the entire M5 design.

Cooperative-tensor kernel structure

Each threadgroup owns an output tile and feeds operand tiles along the contraction dimension into an MPP matmul2d that accumulates into a register-resident cooperative tensor. Covers dense GEMM (4-/8-bit, reusing packed-quant layout), MoE expert GEMM, fused MoE gate/up projection with in-accumulator SiLU (one global-memory round-trip saved per expert), and prefill flash-attention whose QKT/PV run on matmul2d with an exact online softmax numerically equivalent to the SIMD path. Notably, threadgroup-memory staging is not required for peak GEMM — kernels read operands straight from device memory and let the cache hierarchy work. Requires -std=metal4.0.

Metrics

Prompt-processing throughput pp (tok/s) at lengths 128, 256, 512, 1024, 2048; token-generation throughput tg128 over 128 generated tokens. Each point = average of 5 repetitions. Dispatch selects the tensor path only for prefill GEMM / MoE expert GEMM / prefill attention; shape checks fall back to SIMD; the whole path is bypassed on pre-M5 hardware with unchanged behaviour.

Prefill vs llama.cpp · up to 6.4×

On Gemma-4-E2B (Q8) the ratio climbs from 2.8× at pp128 to 6.4× at pp2048. BaseRT is faster on every configuration at every prompt length.

Prefill vs MLX · up to 3.9×

Largest gap is Qwen3-30B-A3B at pp128 (2,478 vs 639 tok/s). Leads on all but 3 of 75 points — the two smaller Llama models at pp1024–2048, where MLX edges ahead by ≤3%.

MoE sees the widest margins

Across the four MoE models BaseRT leads llama.cpp by 29–120% and MLX by 15–288%. Qwen3.6-35B-A3B (Q4) leads both baselines at every prompt length.

Decode confirms the bandwidth ceiling

Beats llama.cpp on all 15 (1.02–1.75×); leads MLX on 13/15 but trails 0.98× on two large models running near the bandwidth ceiling — honest confirmation, not overclaim.

Strengths & Limitations

side-by-side

Strengths

  • Well-motivated dispatch. Correctly identifies that M5 tensor cores help only compute-bound phases and builds policy around it — not naive routing through matmul2d.
  • Numerical-equivalence guarantee. Prefill attention keeps an exact online softmax between QKT and PV on the tensor cores, avoiding silent quality loss.
  • Transparent fallback. Same runtime, unchanged behaviour on pre-M5 chips via SIMD kernels — practical for deployment.
  • Open & broad. v0.1.6 released publicly; 15-config / 4-family coverage including 4 MoE models, with the exact benchmarked version.
  • Honest decode framing. States decode gains come from dispatch/fusion, not the tensor path, and admits the two cases where MLX wins.

Limitations

  • Prefill-only headline. Tensor cores do not raise the decode ceiling set by memory bandwidth.
  • Hardware-gated. Requires M5-family silicon and a Metal 4 toolchain; pre-M5 falls back with no speedup.
  • Single device. Only M5 Pro (48 GB); M5 base/Max (different core counts and bandwidth) deferred to future work.
  • Single-user, Metal only. No continuous batching, multi-request parallel decode, or tensor parallelism.
  • Baselines at defaults. No tuning sweep; newest model families are exactly where BaseRT leads most and baselines are least mature.
  • Modest decode wins. The metric most relevant to sustained interactive cost gains comparatively little, partly owed to prior dispatch/fusion work.

Questions for Further Exploration

open threads
  1. On M5 Max (more cores, higher bandwidth) and M5 base, how do prefill/decode margins shift — and do MoE gains converge as bandwidth rises?
  2. If llama.cpp / MLX were tuned for the Metal 4 backend (batch, KV layout, thread/group config), how much of the gap closes? How much is kernel superiority vs baseline under-tuning?
  3. Routing speculative-decoding verification batch GEMM through the tensor path — does it indirectly raise decode throughput as the authors expect, and by how much?
  4. With continuous batching / multi-request parallel decode / tensor parallelism absent, what happens to local multi-tenant or high-concurrency agent workloads — does BaseRT's single-user optimum get overtaken under concurrency?
  5. The "numerical equivalence" of prefill online softmax vs the SIMD path — does it hold at very long contexts (≫2048), and was end-to-end generation quality measured, not just throughput?
  6. Threadgroup staging is counter-intuitively unnecessary (direct device-memory reads are fastest) — does this still hold on smaller tiles and lower-bandwidth chip tiers?
