A threshold-free canary trap catches malicious shards in P2P LLM inference at AUROC=1.000 with a single activation read.

Integrity of Peer-to-Peer Distributed LLM Inference Under Malicious Nodes

Mert Cihangiroglu · Antonino Nocera  —  University of Pavia · DCALab

distributed-llm-inference peer-to-peer integrity-detection byzantine-fault-tolerance verifiable-inference

Key Finding

Detection is cast as a ranking problem — no calibrated cutoff, one activation read matches the 2× recompute ceiling.

Key Metric

1.000

AUROC on all 72 main-factorial configs  ·  +0.500 over the 0.50 random baseline  ·  clears pre-registered 0.40 threshold

Why This Matters

Petals-style P2P systems only handle crash faults. This fills the "online-but-malicious" gap cheaply — one read, not 2× recompute or crypto.

Critical Edge

All results hinge on an unvalidated noise-floor assumption; an adaptive attacker who fingerprints canary queries could bypass the trap entirely.

Methodology

Pipeline & Threat Model multi-hop forward pass

  • Pipeline. Each stage i holds a transformer block f_i. Clean reference: a*_i = f_i(a*_{i-1}). Benign online: h_i = f_i(h_{i-1}) + δ_i, where δ_i ~ N(0, σ_hw²·μ_i²·I) — relative shift is independent of width d and length T.
  • Adversary. Controls c colluding stages, replaces benign compute with tamper τ, observes prior activations and knows f_i, but cannot distinguish canary from real queries at serving time — so it cannot "spare" canaries.
  • Six attacks (3 strategies). Gaussian ε ∈ {0.02, 0.05, 0.10, 0.20} · Sign flip τ(v)=-v · Rescale τ(v)=1.1v (α=0.10). Spans bit-flip/rowhammer to bounded adversarial weight perturbation.
  • Canary trap. Verifier precomputes fp32 reference activations offline; at serving sends n=100 canaries (truncated to 48 tokens) in fp16 through the live pipeline; per-stage relative-L2 deviation m_i = ‖h_i − a*_i‖_F / ‖a*_i‖_F.
  • Threshold-free scoring. Per-canary AUROC A^(t) = P(malicious deviation > random benign deviation), aggregated to Â. Â=1 ⇒ malicious shard outranks every benign shard on every canary. Verifier reports Â only — no cutoff.
  • Three-reference bracket. Random baseline (uniform → 0.50) · scrambled-label negative control (real m, fake shard index → tests signal provenance) · redundant-recompute upper bound (independent benign recompute → 2× compute guarantee).
  • Pre-registration. Metric = gain Â − 0.50; success threshold 0.40; negative-control mean ∈ [0.45,0.55]; benign false-positive ≤ 5%; falsifiable if gain ≤ 0.0.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths what lands

  • Threshold-free by design. Framed as a ranking problem (AUROC / Mann-Whitney U), sidestepping the core failure of cutoffs when benign float drift and tampering overlap.
  • Cost-clear. Single activation read; no recompute, no crypto commitments, no per-node TEE — matches the 2× redundant-recompute ceiling.
  • Rigorous by construction. Pre-registered protocol, three-reference bracket, two mechanism ablations, five focused substudies — failure points located, not hidden.
  • Invariant to attacker axes (depth, position, collusion) — each shard judged against its own reference, so a malicious minority cannot erode the test.
  • Detection decoupled from damage. A near-harmless rescale and a destructive sign flip are equally visible — attackers cannot trade stealth for harm.

Limitations what's soft

  • Noise model is too simple. Single-level isotropic Gaussian; doesn't model non-Gaussian fp16 rounding or correlated cross-GPU operator reordering. Every claim rests on where the real floor sits relative to harmful ε — unmeasured.
  • No model-scale extrapolation. Only 124M–410M; BLOOM-176B untouched. Detection may saturate slower or the frontier conclusion could reverse at scale.
  • Adaptive attackers untested. A probe-then-set-ε attacker, or one that fingerprints the canary distribution C and only poisons real traffic, directly threatens the "indistinguishable canary" premise.
  • Canary secrecy tension. Construction is public (Kerckhoffs); security relies on which queries are canaries staying secret. Long-run traffic analysis could leak that side-channel — not discussed.
  • Simulation only. No real Petals or heterogeneous consumer-hardware deployment; fp32 reference, fp16 noise, persistent malice are all simulated.
04

Open Questions

  1. On real Petals heterogeneous consumer-GPU pools, where does σ_hw actually sit relative to harmful ε? If the floor is higher than simulated, does the frontier conclusion reverse?
  2. How completely can an adaptive attacker — one that estimates σ_hw then sets ε just below it, or fingerprints canary distribution C to spare canaries — defeat the trap? Could differential privacy or traffic shaping protect canary identity?
  3. On BLOOM-7B / 176B, how does the gap between minimum-detectable ε and minimum-harmful ε shift? Does detection saturate more slowly with scale?
  4. Can the "per-shard vs own reference" (no-majority-vote) paradigm migrate to federated training-update streams, dropping the benign-majority assumption? What new statistical challenges arise between activation and update flows?
  5. Can the trap extend beyond transformer layers — to MoE routing, cross-device KV cache, multimodal pipelines — and how would the "known answer" for a canary even be constructed there?
