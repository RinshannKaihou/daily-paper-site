> **一句话总结：** DEFT 提出"专家引导的预测编辑"范式：冻结时序基础模型，用极少的专家查询在分解的趋势-季节性空间中先利用先验、再结构化探索，在 78 个数据集上稳定超越 best-of-N、CEM、贝叶斯优化等基线。

> **TL;DR:** DEFT treats expert-guided editing of a frozen time-series foundation model (TFM) as an exploit-explore budget problem: it first exploits the TFM's predictive samples in a decomposed trend-seasonal space, then explores via component-wise CEM, reusing each expert score as feedback for both components. Across 78 datasets (ChronosBench + GIFT-Eval), three TFMs (TimesFM 2, Chronos-2, Moirai 2), four feedback modes, and seven query budgets, DEFT lowers relative MASE from best-of-N's 0.884-0.887 to 0.834-0.838 on TimesFM and beats zero-shot in 89-96% of settings.

## 核心贡献

1. **问题形式化**: 首次将"专家引导的预测编辑"（expert-guided forecast editing）形式化为一个 **exploit-explore 预算分配问题**。明确指出 best-of-N 只利用 TFM 先验、CEM/BO 只在高维预测区间做无结构探索，两者单独都不最优。
2. **DEFT 方法**: 两阶段编辑器。Stage 1 (Exploit) 在分解的趋势-季节性空间上扫描 TFM 预测样本的重组轨迹；Stage 2 (Explore) 通过组件级 CEM 在 elite 种子周围搜索。关键技巧：每次专家评分通过 max-pooling 复用到对应的趋势分量和季节性分量，让单次查询同时改进两个候选分布，且 TFM 全程冻结。
3. **全面的实证评估**: 在 2 个基准（ChronosBench 42 + GIFT-Eval 36 = 78 数据集）、3 个 TFM、4 种反馈模式、7 种预算下与 best-of-N（quantile/sample）、random search、Direct CEM、Surrogate CEM、TuRBO-1（贝叶斯优化）进行对照，并给出一个分子动力学（Li-P-S-Ge 固态电解质）的真实物理反馈案例。

## Key Contributions

- **Problem formulation.** Expert-guided forecast editing for frozen TFMs is cast as an exploit-explore budget allocation problem, positioning best-of-N and CEM/BO as the two sub-optimal extremes of a spectrum.
- **DEFT.** A test-time editor that (i) exploits the TFM prior by querying recombinations of trend + seasonal components extracted from TFM samples, (ii) explores around elites via component-wise CEM, and (iii) reuses each scalar expert score as component-level feedback via max-pooling — all while keeping the TFM frozen.
- **Comprehensive evaluation.** Head-to-head comparison against random search, two best-of-N variants, Direct CEM, Surrogate CEM, and TuRBO-1 (BO) under matched budgets, plus ablations and a real molecular-dynamics case study using physically grounded Morse-bond-violation feedback.

## Methodology

**Setting.** A frozen TFM produces an initial forecast $\hat{y}_0 = F_\theta(x_{1:T})$ and a predictive distribution (samples or quantile grid). An editor $E$ may query an expert scoring function `score(·)` at most $B$ times and must return a single edited trajectory. The expert may be a human analyst, simulator, rule checker, or — in controlled experiments — an oracle computed from the held-out future.

**Decomposition.** Every candidate is written as $y = T(y) + S(y)$, where $T = \text{MA}_w$ is a centered moving average (trend) and $S = y - T$ is the seasonal residual. This basis exposes the dominant forecast error modes (level shifts, trend mismatches, seasonal-amplitude changes) and is far lower-dimensional than the raw horizon.

**Budget split:** $B = B_0 + n_r \times b$ — $B_0$ queries for Stage 1, $n_r$ rounds of $b$ queries for Stage 2.

**Stage 1 — Exploit.** From $B_0$ TFM samples, form trend/seasonal component pools, select $K = \lceil\sqrt{B_0}\rceil$ representative components each, and construct $B_0$ balanced recombinations $T_i + S_j$ (diagonal pairings first, then random off-diagonal). The expert scores each complete trajectory $v_{ij} = \text{score}(T_i + S_j)$; max-pooling reuses each score for both its trend and seasonal components ($u^T_i = \max_j v_{ij}$, $u^S_j = \max_i v_{ij}$). Top-$\rho K$ elites seed component Gaussians, with means from the elites and standard deviations deliberately taken from the full TFM pool (calibrating search radius to model uncertainty).

**Stage 2 — Explore (decomposed CEM).** For $r = 1, \ldots, n_r$: sample $n_T$ trend and $n_S$ seasonal candidates from the component Gaussians, form balanced recombinations, query the expert, max-pool scores, and refit elites. The best trajectory across both stages is returned. Stage 1's 16-query scan alone already matches what best-of-N reaches with all 32 queries (Fig. 4b), so each expert call refines two proposal distributions at once.

## Results & Highlights

- **TimesFM backbone (main table).** DEFT lowers relative MASE to **0.838 on ChronosBench and 0.834 on GIFT-Eval**, vs **0.887 / 0.884** for the strongest baseline Best-of-N (quantile); MSE drops to **0.794 / 0.719**. DEFT beats the zero-shot median in **96.1% / 94.0%** of settings and beats random search in **93.3% / 91.1%** — roughly 30 points above any competitor.
- **Moirai backbone.** DEFT is the only method to cut MSE substantially, reaching **0.741 (ChronosBench) / 0.815 (GIFT-Eval)** with MASE **0.849 / 0.906** vs Best-of-N's 0.886 / 0.935. W-ZS reaches **91.0% / 88.9%** while every other editing method stays below 68%.
- **Molecular dynamics case study (Li-P-S-Ge solid electrolyte).** Using physics-grounded Morse bond-violation feedback, DEFT reduces bond-violation rate by **13%, 30%, 34%** at $B = 32, 64, 128$ vs zero-shot, while Best-of-N saturates near 21%; DEFT is the Pareto-best refiner (lowest violation and lowest MSE-to-ground-truth among refinements).
- **Single-edit analysis (NN5 series, TimesFM, B=32).** DEFT reaches **0.80× zero-shot MSE vs 0.90× for Best-of-N (quantile)** — 20% below zero-shot and 11% below the best baseline; Stage-1's 16-query scan alone matches best-of-N's full 32-query result.
- **Backbone caveat.** On the Chronos backbone (whose quantile forecasts are well calibrated), Best-of-N (quantile) edges DEFT on raw error on ChronosBench (**0.877 vs 0.889 MASE**), though DEFT still wins on win-rate (85.0/75.3 W-ZS/W-Rand), indicating its Chronos advantage is in consistency rather than lowest average error.

## Critical Assessment

The paper makes a genuinely novel framing contribution: it cleanly identifies an exploit-explore axis on which existing methods occupy only the two endpoints, and then proposes a method (DEFT) that fills the unoccupied middle via trend-seasonal decomposition. The ablation in Table 1 is a clean 2×3 factorial over {search strategy × editing representation} that isolates each component's contribution. Quantitatively the gains are large and consistent on TimesFM and Moirai, but the Chronos backbone result is a notable caveat: DEFT does *not* win on raw error there, which the authors attribute to Chronos's well-calibrated quantiles leaving little room for editing. This honesty is welcome, but it does mean DEFT's headline advantage is backbone-dependent. The MD case study is the most interesting part conceptually because it shows DEFT optimizing a *physics* objective where matching ground truth is not the goal — the zero-shot forecast has the lowest MSE yet is physically implausible (piecewise-constant staircase), and DEFT restores plausible atom motion. The trend-vs-seasonal ablation (trend drives most of the gain: 1.209 vs 1.329 MASE on Chronos for trend-only vs seasonal-only) and the surprising finding that max-pooling ≈ mean-pooling (<0.001 MASE difference) reveal that the score-reuse idea, not the specific aggregation operator, is doing the work.

## Strengths

- Novel and well-motivated problem formulation; clean exploit-explore framing with a factorial ablation.
- Strong, broad empirical evidence (78 datasets, 3 TFMs, 4 feedback modes, 7 budgets, >500K expert queries).
- Sample-efficiency argument is concrete: one expert call refines two proposal distributions via component-level score reuse.
- MD case study demonstrates generalization beyond synthetic oracle scores to physically grounded feedback.
- No per-dataset hyperparameter tuning; budget split between Stage 1 and Stage 2 is auto-computed.

## Limitations

- **Oracle expert.** Main experiments derive the expert from the held-out future, which is clean and reproducible but does not capture noise, miscalibration, or contextual knowledge of a real human analyst. Human-expert evaluation is left to future work.
- **Decomposition assumption.** Trend-seasonal decomposition assumes errors are well described by level, trend, and seasonal-amplitude modes; it may fail for regime shifts or irregular structure.
- **Frozen model.** Combining test-time editing with lightweight adaptation of the TFM itself is unexplored and may yield further gains.
- **Backbone dependence.** On the well-calibrated Chronos backbone, Best-of-N (quantile) matches or beats DEFT on raw error metrics — DEFT's win is in consistency, not average error.
- **TuRBO scope.** Bayesian optimization (TuRBO-1) is only evaluated at $B \in \{2,4,8,16\}$ because of $O(n^3)$ GP cost, so the BO comparison at large budgets is incomplete.

## Relevance

This paper is directly relevant to anyone working on **time-series foundation models**, **test-time / inference-time adaptation**, and **human-in-the-loop forecasting**. It connects three otherwise-separate literatures: judgmental forecasting adjustment (Lawrence, Fildes), query-limited black-box optimization (CEM, BO, best-of-N from the LLM reward-guided selection tradition), and classical trend-seasonal decomposition (Autoformer, DLinear). For practitioners deploying TFMs in operations/supply-chain/energy where a downstream simulator or constraint checker exists, DEFT offers a concrete, model-agnostic post-hoc editor that requires no retraining and only a handful of expert calls. The MD case study also makes it relevant to scientific-ML communities using TFMs for physical trajectory prediction.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does DEFT behave when the expert is a *noisy* or *biased* human analyst rather than a held-out oracle? Is max-pooling still robust, or does it amplify noisy high scores?
- Can the trend-seasonal decomposition be replaced or augmented with a learned basis (e.g., from an autoencoder) to handle regime shifts and irregular structure that the moving-average trend misses?
- Would combining DEFT with lightweight test-time model adaptation (e.g., a small adapter on $F_\theta$) compound the gains, or does editing already capture most of the achievable improvement?
- Why does Best-of-N (sample) consistently *worsen* the forecast (MASE 1.121-1.166 on Chronos)? Is this a quantization/calibration artifact, and does it imply TFM sample distributions are mis-calibrated relative to their quantile grids?
- On the Chronos backbone, can DEFT recover the raw-error edge by initializing Stage 2 from quantile candidates rather than stochastic samples?
