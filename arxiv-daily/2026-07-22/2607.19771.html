An Isotropy-Preserving Spectral Cap for Muon: Theory and Three Case Studies

Key Finding

A per-step spectral cap for the Muon optimizer projects out top-singular-direction growth of the output covariance — preventing weight-spectrum collapse without freezing training.

01

Methodology — why Muon runs away, and how the cap reins it in

In scale-invariant networks, SGD carries a natural 1/‖W‖F “brake”; Muon’s matrix-sign step deletes it. The norm then grows as t1/2 instead of t1/4 — fast, and destabilizing. The cap projects out the resulting first-order top-singular growth each step.

The theory (Section 1)

Assumption 1 (scale invariance). L(cW)=L(W) for c>0 — the load-bearing idealization for pre-LN / RMSNorm networks. It implies ⟨W, ∇WL⟩F ≈ 0 and ‖∇WL‖F ∼ 1/‖W‖F.

‖DSGD‖F = O(η/‖W‖F)  — brake kept
DMuon = −η·s·msign(G) ⟹ ‖DMuon‖F = O(η)  — brake removed (msign is scale-invariant)

Norm-growth ODEs then give √R(t) (SGD) vs linear R(t) (Muon). A sanity check at nanoGPT step-48K over 24 weights: |cG| ≈ 0.003–0.005, |cP| ≈ 0.005–0.010 — consistent with approximate scale invariance at that one point.

Elegant result. After projecting out the first-order top mode, the spectral-norm’s second-order perturbation term is a sum of non-negative squares — so a hard cap on σ1 cannot freeze the weight.

The cap algorithm (identical operator, all three systems)

  1. Sample activations X ∈ R^{M×n} (M = 4096 tokens) from the forward cache.
  2. Compute Y = XWᵀ and estimate KY = W KX Wᵀ via matrix-vector products — never materialized.
  3. Power iteration (4 steps) for the top eigenpair (q, λ1).
  4. Form rank-1 direction B = 2qgᵀ; project the candidate update: H ← H − max(0, ⟨B,H⟩−ρλ₁)·B/‖B‖². ρ=0 is a hard cap.

Why KY, not ‖W‖. KX is strongly anisotropic, so the naïve W-cap is masked; the data-coupled cap is the on-target one.

02

Results — three case studies, one operator

The cap is decisive exactly at failure margins (MoE rank-1 collapse, FA H∞ crisis) while leaving the typical case untouched. Cells where the capped variant wins are highlighted.

CASE 1

nanoGPT FFN — proof of concept, val loss unchanged

The data-coupled cap (D1d) flattens the cproj output covariance top-share and lifts participation rank ~3×, with final val loss identical to baseline within seed noise (Δ < 0.01 nats). The naïve W-cap (D1) lowers σ1(W) by 14–46% but barely moves KY top-share — anisotropic KX hides it.

CASE 2

64-expert MoE router — decisive at the rank-1 margin

Without a cap (G0), layer 0 collapses to a single dominant expert (the same expert in every token's top-4, rank = 1.0). The selection cap (G3) reverses this and wins 16 of 18 cells (6 layers × 3 metrics). It also explains why loss-free bias is impotent at rank-1: 63 of its 64 degrees of freedom are wasted. Wrinkle, stated honestly: at L0 the raw cap (G1) gives the cleaner hard-load rescue (max 0.071 vs G3's 0.219).

03

Strengths vs. Limitations

Unusual intellectual honesty for a preliminary report: the author flags scale, seed count, and the load-bearing nature of Assumption 1 as limitations rather than overstating.

✓ Strengths
  • Unified framework. Connects three superficially different failures through one quantity: λ1(KY).
  • Non-negative 2nd-order proof resolves the obvious objection — “won't capping σ1 freeze the layer?” — rigorously.
  • H∞/H₂/H₁ entropy framing generalizes the idea; capping only σ1 raises participation rank for free.
  • Data-coupled cap (KY, not W) is a practically important refinement — empirically shown that the naïve W-cap fails.
  • Negligible cost (<5% even for 288-cap FA) plus the excess_mean diagnostic are immediately portable engineering artifacts.
  • Honest scoping throughout — preliminary report, single-checkpoint check, small scale.
! Limitations
  • Assumption 1 is the entire load-bearing wall. The t1/4 vs t1/2 prediction is validated at one checkpoint only; broad measurement is left as future work.
  • Small scale. nanoGPT (6L/384d) and GPT-2 small (12L/768d), tens of thousands of steps. No billion-parameter, long-horizon validation.
  • Single-seed in places. The FA comparison is against one uncapped run, not an ensemble — crash-prevention rests on one trajectory.
  • nanoGPT shows no val-loss improvement — the cap only changes geometry there. Val-loss gains are confined to MoE (marginal, <0.07 nats) and FA (crash prevention, not better final loss vs a stable baseline).
  • First-order projection only. At the FA 86K crisis λ still rose 5× more slowly than uncapped rather than being fully held — the 2nd-order term can dominate in extreme regimes.
  • No comparison to weight decay, spectral normalization, or attention-level grad clipping on the same failures.
04

Open questions for further exploration

01
Over what scale and training horizon does exact scale invariance hold? Should cG be measured dynamically per layer/stage to gate the cap?
02
When networks are not approximately scale-invariant (unnormalized output layer, strong weight decay), how does the t1/2 vs t1/4 prediction shift?
03
Can the cap extend beyond Muon — to Shampoo, ADOre, Atan2-Falcon? Do those optimizers also annihilate the 1/‖W‖F brake?
04
Across the full bf16 model, are there other σ1-dominated failure points (final logits, FFN mid-layers) that benefit from the same cap?
05
In extreme crises (FA 86K) the 2nd-order term lets λ rise 5× — is a 2nd-order-aware or adaptive-ρ cap needed for stricter containment?
06
The cap samples X (M=4096). Under pipeline / tensor parallelism, how is cross-rank KY estimated without breaking the 5% budget?
07
In MoE, G3 wins Kz+b geometry but G1 gives a cleaner L0 hard-load — is there a hybrid cap on both Kz and Kz+b?
08
Can excess_mean serve as a universal training-health early-warning (like an early-stop signal) that auto-tunes the LR before collapse?
