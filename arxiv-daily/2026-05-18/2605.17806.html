AMO: Adaptive Muon Orthogonalization

arXiv: 2605.17806

Date: 2026-05-18

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.17806

一句话总结： 本文提出 AMO（Adaptive Muon Orthogonalization），通过在线观测不同算子类型的权重几何特性，自适应地为 Muon 优化器的 Newton-Schulz 正交化迭代分配不同的步数预算，在 Llama3.1-1.4B 和 Qwen3-1.7B 上分别取得 +0.76 和 +0.51 的平均下游任务提升。
TL;DR: AMO is an observe-then-commit method that measures per-operator-type weight geometry early in training and allocates Newton-Schulz iteration budgets non-uniformly, consistently outperforming uniform-schedule Muon across standard, prolonged, and continual pre-training at 0.6B-1.7B scale.

核心贡献

  • 揭示了 Muon 优化器中逐矩阵正交化难度的异质性：通过系统的实证分析，发现不同算子类型（attn_q/k/v/o, mlp_gate/up/down）的权重矩阵在训练过程中具有显著不同的几何特性和正交化难度，且这种异质性无法通过数据侧的课程学习来消除。
  • 提出了基于算子类型粒度的自适应 NS 步数分配方法 AMO：采用"先观测后提交"策略，在训练初期（约 1200-3000 步）在线采集各算子类型的几何信号（条件数），然后通过贪婪预算分配算法为每种算子类型分配合适的 NS 迭代步数，并在整个训练过程中锁定该分配方案。
  • 证明了 AMO 在多种预训练场景下的有效性：在标准预训练、延长预训练（2x/5x/10x Chinchilla 最优比）和持续预训练中均一致优于均匀调度的 Muon 变体，且额外计算开销可忽略（端到端步长时间仅增加约 0.5%）。

Key Contributions

  • Empirically establishes per-matrix orthogonalization heterogeneity in Muon through a systematic study spanning two model families, two optimizers, four LR schedulers, and three data orderings, showing that geometry difficulty varies by operator type and evolves dynamically across training stages and network depths.
  • Identifies operator type as the natural adaptation unit by demonstrating four geometry findings: (F1) condition number predicts NS quality (Pearson r = 0.72-0.91); (F2) operator types exhibit persistent, well-separated difficulty trajectories; (F3) per-layer differences within an operator type are marginal; (F4) layer ordering reverses mid-training, ruling out layer-wise adaptation.
  • Proposes AMO, a simple observe-then-commit method that captures geometry signals under a uniform schedule, computes a single allocation plan via greedy budget reallocation over projected PE error curves, then linearly transitions to that plan and locks it — achieving consistent gains without continuous re-tuning.
  • Demonstrates effectiveness across three pre-training regimes: standard pre-training (up to +0.76 avg on 12 benchmarks), prolonged pre-training at 2x-10x Chinchilla ratios, and continual pre-training on Slimpajama.

Methodology

AMO operates in two phases:

Phase 1 — Observation: During early training (1200-3000 steps), AMO runs under the default uniform 5-step NS schedule. Every ∆obs steps, it samples nsmp matrices per operator type and records the effective geometry signal ℓ_eff = σ_min(M) / ||M||_F, where σ_min is the smallest numerically meaningful singular value. This yields a per-type geometry profile aggregated via median-of-medians.

Planning: For each operator type i, the observed geometry is shrunk toward a conservative baseline: ℓ_target = 0.7 · ℓ_robust + 0.3 · 10^{-3}. For each candidate NS step count k ∈ [3, 7], AMO invokes the Polar Express (PE) composer to simulate the projected NS error E_i(k) = 1 - ℓ_after_final. A greedy budget allocation algorithm (BudgetAllocate) then distributes a fixed total NS step budget across the seven operator types to minimize the sum of projected errors, subject to per-type bounds [k_min, k_max] and a global budget constraint. The algorithm is proven globally optimal under discrete convexity.

Phase 2 — Transition and Lock: The schedule change is distributed over a transition duration (300 steps) via joint linear interpolation of step counts and PE coefficients, avoiding abrupt jumps in the orthogonalized update that would destabilize momentum buffers. After transition, the schedule is frozen and geometry capture stops.

The method uses exact SVD for observation (only ~8 snapshots during Phase 1), making overhead negligible. Once locked, AMO matches Muon's per-step cost exactly.

Results & Highlights

  • Standard pre-training: AMO improves over the strongest uniform-schedule Muon baseline by +0.76 on Llama3.1-1.4B (39.69 vs. 38.93) and +0.51 on Qwen3-1.7B (40.75 vs. 40.24) in average downstream accuracy across 12 benchmarks.
  • Prolonged pre-training (Qwen3-0.6B at 2x/5x/10x Chinchilla): AMO consistently leads at all compute budgets, with gains of +0.39, +0.23, and +0.29 over the strongest baseline respectively.
  • Continual pre-training (Qwen3-0.6B → Slimpajama): AMO stays above both Muon-KJ and Muon-PE at every evaluated step, reaching 40.31 vs. 40.15 for the best baseline.
  • Cost: AMO's optimizer update time (191.47 ms) is actually lower than vanilla Muon (195.81 ms) and AdaMuon (241.65 ms) on Qwen3-1.7B, with peak memory matching Muon at 61.12 GB (vs. 69.00 GB for AdaMuon). End-to-end step time overhead is only ~0.5%.
  • Budget control: AMO traces a monotone compute-quality curve; tightening to r=0.9 saves ~8.6% orthogonalization FLOPs with only -0.07 average accuracy loss on Qwen3-0.6B.
  • Outperforms all static schedules: AMO surpasses the best family-specific static PE schedule by +0.17 on Llama3.1-1.4B and +0.07 on Qwen3-1.7B without any model-specific tuning.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: Moderate-to-high. The key insight — that NS orthogonalization difficulty varies by operator type and can be predicted from online geometry — is genuinely new. The observe-then-commit framework is simple and well-justified, though the individual components (PE coefficients, greedy budget allocation) build on prior work.
  • Rigor: Strong. The paper includes a factorial pilot study ruling out data-side explanations, four geometry findings with quantitative support (Pearson correlations across 8 models), optimality guarantees for the budget allocation, comprehensive ablations on all design choices, and evaluation across three distinct training regimes.
  • Reproducibility: Good. Algorithm boxes are provided, hyperparameters are fully specified in tables, and the method uses only standard operations (SVD, NS iteration). The observation window and sample size ablations show insensitivity to exact values.
  • Significance: Significant for the Muon optimizer literature. If Muon continues to gain adoption for large-scale pre-training (as suggested by DeepSeek-V4 using 10-step NS), adaptive scheduling could become an important practical tool. The +0.5-0.76 gains on 12-task average at 1.4B-1.7B scale are meaningful.
  • Red flags: (1) Experiments are limited to 0.6B-1.7B models; whether gains persist at 10B+ or 1T+ scale is unknown. (2) The observation phase requires exact SVD, which scales cubically with matrix dimension — this could become costly for very large hidden sizes. (3) The "lock after transition" design assumes cross-type geometry ordering remains stable, which may not hold for very long training runs with distribution shifts. (4) No comparison against AdamW with equivalent compute budgets in the prolonged/CPT settings.

Strengths

  • Cleanly motivated by systematic pilot experiments that rule out the simplest alternative explanations before proposing a solution.
  • The observe-then-commit design is simple, efficient, and avoids the instability of continuous adaptation.
  • Strong empirical results across three distinct training regimes with consistent improvements.
  • Negligible practical overhead — once locked, AMO matches Muon's cost exactly.
  • Thorough ablation study covering observation horizon, sample size, shrinkage, and transition duration.

Limitations

  • Only tested up to 1.7B parameters; scalability to frontier-scale models (70B+) remains unverified.
  • The geometry signal is captured only once (early training); if operator-type difficulty ordering changes substantially later in training, the locked schedule becomes suboptimal.
  • Exact SVD during observation, while infrequent, has cubic cost in matrix dimension and may become a bottleneck for very large hidden sizes.
  • The method introduces several new hyperparameters (observation horizon, shrinkage, transition duration) though ablations show reasonable robustness.
  • No evaluation on downstream tasks requiring instruction-following or code generation, which may respond differently to optimizer changes.

Relevance

本文直接研究了 LLM 预训练中的优化器设计问题，提出的 AMO 方法通过自适应调整 Muon 正交化的迭代策略来提升训练效果，对于关注高效预训练和优化器改进的研究具有重要参考价值。其"先观测后提交"的设计范式也可推广至其他需要参数分组适配的优化器场景。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does AMO scale to models beyond 10B parameters, and does the operator-type difficulty ordering remain stable at that scale?
  • Can the observation phase use approximate SVD (e.g., randomized methods) to reduce the cubic cost for very large hidden dimensions?
  • Would a periodic re-observation and re-planning mechanism (rather than a single lock) yield further gains in very long training runs or under distribution shifts?
  • How does AMO interact with other Muon improvements like Turbo-Muon's spectrum preconditioning or Dion's power iteration?
  • Can the geometry-based adaptation principle be extended to other aspects of Muon, such as per-operator-type learning rates or momentum coefficients?
