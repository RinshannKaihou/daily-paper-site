> **一句话总结：** 本文通过模算术任务研究过参数化网络在标签噪声下记忆与泛化的共存机制，发现即使80%标签被噪声替换，模型内部仍能学到正确的代数规则，只是被噪声记忆所掩盖，通过频域滤波可以提取出近乎完美的泛化结构。

> **TL;DR:** Over-parameterized two-layer networks trained on modular arithmetic with up to 80% label noise internally learn the correct algebraic rule but suppress its expression to fit noisy labels. Frequency-based filtration of learned weights recovers near-perfect test accuracy, revealing that generalization and memorization coexist but are entangled across distributed neurons rather than cleanly separable by neuron-level pruning.

## 核心贡献

- **揭示了过参数化网络中记忆与泛化的共存机制**：在模算术任务中，即使80%的训练标签被替换为随机噪声，模型内部仍能形成正确的代数规则表示，只是被噪声记忆压制。
- **发现了反直觉的学习动态**：在全批量训练中，噪声标签比干净标签被更快地记忆，这与"模型先学简单模式"的传统认知相悖。
- **提出频域滤波方法（FF）分离泛化与记忆**：通过提取网络权重的主频成分，可以恢复近100%的测试准确率，表明泛化规则以频率结构编码在权重中。
- **提出了任务无关的神经元重要性评分（Neuron Strength）**：无需依赖周期性假设，可在多种模算术任务上与任务特定的IPR指标表现相当。
- **证明了神经元级剪枝的局限性**：泛化结构弥漫分布在整个网络中，无法通过简单的神经元选择完全提取，揭示了后验知识提取的根本限制。

## Key Contributions

- Demonstrates that over-parameterized ReLU networks form an internal generalization structure matching the known analytical solution for quadratic activations, even under extreme (80%) label noise.
- Identifies a counter-intuitive training dynamic where noisy labels are memorized faster than clean labels during early training under full-batch optimization.
- Proposes Frequency Filtration (FF), which isolates the dominant frequency component of learned weights to extract the latent generalization rule, recovering near-perfect test accuracy across noise levels.
- Introduces Neuron Strength (Str.), a task-agnostic importance score for neuron selection that performs comparably to the task-specific Inverse Participation Ratio (IPR).
- Shows that generalization is distributed across neurons rather than localized, meaning neuron-level pruning provides only limited generalization improvement compared to frequency-based extraction.

## Methodology

The authors study two-layer neural networks on modular arithmetic tasks (e.g., addition mod P=113) with varying label noise ratios (α = 0.05 to 0.9). The network architecture is fθ(a,b) = W·φ(U·ea + V·eb) + μ, trained with cross-entropy loss using AdamW, Adam, Muon, and SGD optimizers.

**Three key methodological components:**

1. **Scaling analysis (Q1):** Systematically vary model width M from 33 to 8193 hidden neurons, noise ratios, activation functions (ReLU, GeLU, quadratic, LeakyReLU), weight decay strengths, and optimizers. Observe double descent curves and characterize conditions under which larger models generalize better despite noise.

2. **Frequency Filtration (Q2):** For each neuron's weight vectors (um, vm, wm), perform Fourier decomposition. Isolate the dominant frequency component (highest magnitude in wm) as the "generalization part" (uG, vG, wG) and the remainder as the "residual part" (uR, vR, wR). Construct separate sub-networks from these components and evaluate each. Also validate via activation replacement (swapping ReLU for quadratic or reverse ReLU post-training) and phase relationship analysis.

3. **Network Partitioning (Q3):** Rank neurons by importance scores—IPR (task-specific, measures periodicity) or Neuron Strength (task-agnostic, ‖wm‖∞ · φ(‖um‖∞ + ‖vm‖∞)). Select top-k neurons for a generalization sub-network and bottom-k for a memorization sub-network. Compare the generalization improvement from neuron pruning versus frequency filtration.

The methodology is complemented by gradient analysis, MNIST experiments with three-layer networks, and extension to Transformer architectures in appendices.

## Results & Highlights

- **Double descent is robust under label noise:** Across all activation functions, optimizers, and noise levels, test error follows a double descent curve. In the over-parameterized regime, bigger models consistently generalize better.
- **Noisy labels are memorized first:** In both under- and over-parameterized regimes, accuracy on noisy training samples rises earlier than on clean samples during early training—a reversal of the "simplicity bias" narrative.
- **FF recovers near-perfect generalization at 80% noise:** The dominant-frequency sub-network achieves ~100% test accuracy even when 80% of training labels are corrupted. The residual sub-network primarily memorizes noise.
- **Activation replacement works:** Replacing trained ReLU activations with quadratic or reverse ReLU post-training preserves or improves test accuracy, confirming that learned weights encode a symmetric rule representation compatible with the analytical solution.
- **Phase relationships match analytical solution:** Filtered dominant components satisfy φ(a) + φ(b) ≈ φ(c) for addition and φ(a) − φ(b) ≈ φ(c) for subtraction, matching the known margin-maximizing solution.
- **Neuron pruning is limited:** Both IPR and Str. selection improve generalization over the raw model, but the improvement is far weaker than FF. Even applying FF to the pruned sub-network cannot match FF on the full model, indicating generalization structure is diffusely encoded.
- **Phenomenon generalizes beyond modular arithmetic:** Similar early noisy-over-clean learning dynamics appear on MNIST with three-layer networks, and FF extends to Transformer architectures.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty (Moderate-High):** The core finding—that generalization and memorization coexist in frequency space and can be disentangled via FF—is novel and mechanistically insightful. The observation that noisy labels are memorized faster than clean ones under full-batch training is surprising and challenges prevailing intuitions. However, the FF method leverages known analytical structure from quadratic-activation solutions, so the novelty lies more in the application and empirical characterization than in a fundamentally new theoretical result.

**Rigor (High):** Experiments are comprehensive and well-controlled. The modular arithmetic setup provides a clean testbed where generalization vs. memorization can be unambiguously measured. The double descent analysis across optimizers, activations, and noise levels is thorough. The frequency-based analysis is grounded in prior theoretical work (Morwani et al., Gromov).

**Reproducibility (High):** The setup is clearly described—architecture, training details, evaluation protocol, default hyperparameters (P=113, AdamW, lr=10⁻³, wd=0.1, seed=42). Modular arithmetic tasks are trivially reproducible. The paper provides sufficient detail to replicate all main results.

**Significance (Moderate-High):** The work contributes to understanding memorization-generalization coexistence, a central question in deep learning theory. The practical limitation—FF requires task-specific structural priors (knowledge of frequency structure)—tempers the immediate applicability. The broader claim that "generalization is distributed" has important implications for mechanistic interpretability and network pruning.

**Red flags:**
- The controlled setting (modular arithmetic, two-layer networks, full-batch training) abstracts away complexities of real-world tasks. The authors acknowledge this but the gap is significant.
- FF is not a strict functional decomposition (fθ ≠ f^G + f^R), limiting its theoretical interpretation.
- The Neuron Strength metric, while task-agnostic, ultimately shows limited utility—it improves generalization modestly but far less than the task-specific FF.
- Full-batch training is used throughout; the noisy-first memorization dynamics may not hold under mini-batch SGD, which is the practical training regime.

## Strengths

- Clean, well-motivated experimental setup using modular arithmetic as a controlled testbed.
- Surprising empirical finding that noisy labels are memorized faster than clean labels.
- FF provides a compelling mechanistic demonstration that generalization rules are learned but masked under extreme noise.
- Systematic ablation across optimizers, activations, model sizes, and noise levels.
- Honest assessment of limitations—clearly states that neuron-level pruning is insufficient and that FF requires structural priors.

## Limitations

- **Scope:** Only modular arithmetic tasks are studied in depth; the MNIST and Transformer experiments are supplementary and less detailed.
- **Full-batch training:** All main experiments use full-batch optimization, which is rarely used in practice. The noisy-first dynamics may differ under mini-batch training.
- **Two-layer networks:** The mechanistic analysis relies heavily on properties of shallow networks. It is unclear how FF would extend to deep architectures without known analytical solutions.
- **FF requires structural priors:** The method needs knowledge of the task's frequency structure, making it impractical for arbitrary real-world tasks.
- **No theoretical explanation** for why noisy labels are memorized faster; the gradient-based discussion in the appendix is suggestive but not conclusive.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the noisy-first memorization dynamic persist under mini-batch SGD with realistic batch sizes, or is it an artifact of full-batch optimization?
- Can frequency-based extraction be adapted to identify and remove memorized training data in large language models without prior knowledge of the task structure?
- What happens to the memorization-generalization balance when noise is structured (e.g., systematic mislabeling) rather than uniform random?
- Could the Neuron Strength metric be combined with sparse autoencoder decompositions to better disentangle generalization from memorization in superposition-heavy models?
- Is the distributed encoding of generalization a fundamental property of over-parameterized networks, or could architectural modifications (e.g., explicit modularization) enable cleaner separation?
