Pointwise Generalization in Deep Neural Networks

arXiv: 2605.18598

Date: 2026-05-18

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.18598

一句话总结： 本文提出逐点黎曼维度（Pointwise Riemannian Dimension），通过各层学习到的特征Gram矩阵的谱性质建立假设依赖的泛化界，在FCN和ResNet上比谱范数界和VC维紧数个数量级，且随过参数化递减。
TL;DR: Introduces a pointwise Riemannian Dimension from learned feature eigenvalues across layers, yielding hypothesis-dependent generalization bounds for fully connected networks that are orders of magnitude tighter than spectral-norm and VC-dimension bounds, and that decrease with over-parameterization.

核心贡献

  • 提出逐点泛化框架（Pointwise Generalization Framework），用假设依赖的逐点维度（Pointwise Dimension）替代传统的均匀收敛和覆盖数方法，建立匹配上下界的泛化理论。
  • 发展了非微扰展开（Non-Perturbative Expansion）技术，通过精确的望远镜式恒等式保持深度网络的有限尺度几何，避免了Taylor线性化或无限宽近似。
  • 提出层次覆盖（Hierarchical Covering）方案：局部图表上达到黄金标准的有效维度，全局上通过Grassmannian的椭球覆盖提升，证明全局图册复杂度与局部图表复杂度同阶。
  • 定义逐点黎曼维度（Riemannian Dimension），逐层分解为宽度 × 有效维度（局部）+ 输入维度 × 有效维度（全局），实验中比VC维小约10³倍，比谱范数界小约10⁶倍。
  • 揭示过参数化通过特征压缩降低有效秩：最大FCN上有效秩压缩达1/300，最大ResNet上大部分层压缩至零有效秩。

Key Contributions

  • Develops a pointwise generalization framework with matching upper/lower bounds (Theorem 1 & 2), strengthening PAC-Bayes, generic chaining, and covering-number approaches by localizing complexity at each hypothesis.
  • Introduces non-perturbative calculus via exact telescoping decompositions (Lemma 1), preserving finite-scale geometry without Taylor linearization or infinite-width limits.
  • Constructs a hierarchical covering of the Grassmannian with an anisotropic (ellipsoidal) metric (Lemma 3), extending classical metric-entropy theory and establishing that global atlas cost is commensurate with local chart cost — the second structural principle of DNNs.
  • Defines the Pointwise Riemannian Dimension (Theorem 3): d_R(W,ε) = Σ_l [d_l · d_eff(A_l) + d_{l-1} · d_eff(A_l) + log terms], achieving the gold-standard effective dimension in strongly correlated deep networks.
  • Proves unconditional mixed empirical–ghost generalization bound (Theorem 4) and fully observable bound under finite-resolution subspace isomorphism (Theorem 5, Definition 3).
  • Shows Riemannian Dimension exponentially sharpens spectral-norm bounds (Corollary 1), removes explicit depth dependence compared to VC dimension O(PL log P), and operates beyond NTK's infinitesimal regime.
  • Demonstrates empirically that RD decreases with over-parameterization (FCN width sweep on MNIST, ResNet depth sweep on CIFAR-10), feature learning compresses effective ranks during training, and SGD with momentum implicitly regularizes RD.

Methodology

The paper proceeds in three parts:

Part I — Pointwise Generalization Framework (Section 2): For hypothesis class F with metric ϱ and prior π, the pointwise dimension at hypothesis f with scale ε is log(1/π(B_ϱ(f,ε))) — the log inverse prior density of the ε-ball. Theorem 1 gives a unified bound via multiscale integration of pointwise dimension over the mixed empirical–ghost metric. Theorem 2 provides a matching worst-case lower bound. This framework strengthens PAC-Bayes (by optimizing over arbitrary posteriors on nonlinear hypothesis classes rather than restricting to Gaussian weight-space posteriors), Kolmogorov complexity (by using metric balls instead of singletons), and generic chaining (by localizing at the realized hypothesis).

Part II — Structural Analysis of DNNs (Sections 3–4):

  • Non-perturbative expansion (Section 3.2): Uses exact telescoping identity (3.1) to decompose output differences layerwise, yielding Lemma 1: the cross-layer correlation factorizes through feature matrices, preserving approximate pointwise linear structure. This avoids the NTK regime's infinitesimal linearization.
  • Hierarchical covering (Section 3.3): At the local level, a uniform prior on a reference subspace achieves the gold-standard effective dimension (Lemma 2). At the global level, a new ellipsoidal covering of the Grassmannian (Lemma 3) bounds the cost of selecting reference subspaces. The resulting Riemannian Dimension (Theorem 3) decomposes layerwise into local-chart cost (width × spectral complexity) and global-atlas cost (input dimension × spectral complexity).
  • Generalization bounds (Section 4): Theorem 4 gives the unconditional mixed empirical–ghost bound. Theorem 5 gives the fully empirical bound under a layer-wise finite-resolution subspace isomorphism condition (Definition 3), which only requires multiplicative isomorphism on the active subspace and ghost energy below the finite spectral resolution on the inactive complement.

Part III — Experiments (Section 5): 9-layer FCNs (width 2⁶–2¹²) on MNIST and ResNets {20,32,44,56,74,110} on CIFAR-10. Conservative substitution of local Lipschitz constants by spectral-norm products.

Results & Highlights

  • RD vs. VC/Spectral Norm: On FCNs, RD ≈ 4.8×10⁷ (width-2¹²) vs. VC ≈ 2.1×10¹⁰ and spectral norm ≈ 1.5×10¹⁶. RD is ~10³× smaller than VC and ~10⁸× smaller than spectral norm (Table 1).
  • RD decreases with over-parameterization: Width scaling (FCN): RD drops from 6.4×10⁷ to 4.8×10⁷ as width goes from 2⁶ to 2¹². Depth scaling (ResNet): RD drops from 8.8×10⁶ (ResNet-20) to 2.6×10⁶ (ResNet-110) (Table 2).
  • Feature compression: On the largest FCN, effective rank reaches 1/300 of ambient dimension in hidden layers. On ResNet-110, most layers compress to zero effective rank (Tables 3–4).
  • SGD implicit bias: SGD with momentum drives RD down by orders of magnitude during training, while VC dimension remains essentially unchanged (Figure 3).
  • Exponential improvement over norm bounds: Corollary 1 shows RD exponentially sharpens the Bartlett et al. [2017] spectral-norm bound, replacing product-of-norm terms with spectrum-aware effective dimensions.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: Very high. The combination of (1) pointwise dimension as a finite-scale (not infinitesimal) complexity measure, (2) non-perturbative expansion replacing Taylor/NTK linearization, and (3) ellipsoidal Grassmannian covering is a genuine methodological advance. The conceptual separation of unconditional compressed bounds from fully observable bounds is clean and intellectually honest.
  • Rigor: Excellent. Matching upper/lower bounds for pointwise dimension (Theorems 1 & 2). Proofs are detailed and self-contained. The open problem on subspace-level feature regularity (Section 4.2) is clearly stated rather than swept under the rug.
  • Reproducibility: Good. Standard architectures (FCN, ResNet) and datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10). Code provided. The theoretical results are mathematical and inherently reproducible.
  • Significance: Potentially very high for learning theory. The paper provides a principled explanation for why over-parameterized networks generalize — through feature spectral compression rather than weight-space compression. The regularized ERM framework (Section 4.4) offers algorithmic implications.
  • Red flags / concerns:
  • The restriction to fully connected networks is significant. Extending the block-decomposable structure (3.2) to convolutions, attention, skip connections, or normalization layers is non-trivial. The ResNet experiments apply the theory somewhat loosely (treating residual blocks as FCN layers).
  • The fully observable bound (Theorem 5) requires the subspace isomorphism condition (Definition 3), which remains an open problem to verify from intrinsic feature regularity. The paper is transparent about this gap.
  • The conservative substitution of M̄_{l→L} by spectral-norm products in experiments means the empirical RD values are looser than theoretically possible — the actual improvement over norm bounds may be less dramatic than reported once this substitution is made.
  • The local Lipschitz constants M_{l→L} appear only through "optimistic logarithmic factors," which is theoretically sound under spectral decay but may not hold universally.

Strengths

  • Provides a complete, principled theory: framework → structural analysis → bounds → experiments.
  • The finite-scale (vs. infinitesimal) perspective on dimension is conceptually important and novel.
  • Non-perturbative analysis avoids the well-known limitations of NTK/infinite-width regimes.
  • Transparent about assumptions and open problems (subspace isomorphism, extension to modern architectures).
  • Empirical results are compelling: RD is the only complexity measure among those tested that correctly decreases with over-parameterization.
  • The PAC-Bayes interpretation as optimally tuned posterior optimization is elegant.

Limitations

  • Theory is restricted to fully connected networks with 1-Lipschitz activations and σ(0)=0. Extension to CNNs, Transformers, skip connections, layer norm, etc. is not addressed.
  • The fully empirical bound requires an unverified subspace isomorphism condition. The paper identifies this as an open problem but does not resolve it.
  • Experiments use conservative Lipschitz estimates (spectral-norm products), which may underestimate the tightness improvement over norm bounds.
  • The generalization bound (Theorem 4) still involves integration over scales and optimization over α — not a closed-form expression, though the one-shot relaxation (2.3) simplifies this.
  • Scalability to large modern networks (billions of parameters) is unclear; eigenvalue decomposition of feature Gram matrices at each layer may be expensive.

Relevance

本文通过逐点黎曼维度将特征学习的谱压缩与泛化误差直接关联，为理解过参数化深度网络的泛化机制提供了新的理论框架，对表示学习的统计基础研究具有直接参考价值。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Can the block-decomposable structure and hierarchical covering be adapted to convolutional layers (exploiting spatial structure) or attention mechanisms (exploiting low-rank structure in QK^T)?
  • What is the minimal feature regularity needed to verify the subspace isomorphism condition (Definition 3)? The paper suggests sub-Gaussian or small-ball conditions — can these be proven for trained DNN features?
  • Does the Riemannian Dimension regularizer (4.9) lead to practical improvements in training, or does SGD's implicit bias already achieve near-optimal RD?
  • How does the framework interact with modern regularization techniques (dropout, weight decay, data augmentation, mixup)?
  • Can the ellipsoidal Grassmannian covering (Lemma 3) find applications beyond generalization theory, e.g., in optimization landscape analysis or neural architecture search?
  • What happens to RD under distribution shift — does it remain predictive of out-of-distribution generalization?
