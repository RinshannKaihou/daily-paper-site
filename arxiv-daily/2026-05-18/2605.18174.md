> **一句话总结：** 本文提出 Ringmaster LMO，将 Muon 等 LMO 类优化器的异步训练与时延阈值机制结合，在异构分布式系统中通过丢弃过期梯度实现最优时间复杂度，并在理论和实验上验证了其优于同步和异步基线的表现。

> **TL;DR:** Ringmaster LMO extends the delay-thresholding mechanism from Ringmaster ASGD to general LMO-based momentum optimizers (including Muon), enabling asynchronous distributed training under heterogeneous worker speeds. It provides convergence guarantees under generalized $(L_0, L_1)$-smoothness, a parameter-agnostic variant with decreasing stepsizes, and optimal time complexity in the Euclidean smooth setting. Experiments on stochastic quadratics and NanoChat LLM pretraining show increasing advantages as worker-speed heterogeneity grows.

## 核心贡献

- 提出了首个异步 LMO 动量优化方法 Ringmaster LMO，将 Ringmaster ASGD 的时延阈值机制从 SGD 类方法推广到通用 LMO 更新，支持 Muon 等矩阵结构化优化器的异步训练
- 在广义 $(L_0, L_1)$-光滑性假设下建立了收敛性保证，涵盖了经典光滑情形作为特例
- 开发了参数不可知（parameter-agnostic）变体，使用递减步长和自适应时延阈值，无需提前知道问题参数 $K$ 或 $L_1$
- 在经典欧氏光滑设定下，时间复杂度恢复 Ringmaster ASGD 的最优结果
- 在随机二次问题和 NanoChat 语言模型预训练上验证方法有效性，优势随异构性增加而增大

## Key Contributions

- **First asynchronous LMO-based momentum method**: Combines delay-thresholding from Ringmaster ASGD with the LMO-based momentum framework of Scion/Muon, yielding asynchronous variants of Muon, SignSGD, and other norm-constrained optimizers
- **Convergence under generalized $(L_0, L_1)$-smoothness**: Establishes iteration complexity bounds that recover standard stochastic LMO-type rates while accommodating delayed gradients from asynchronous workers
- **Parameter-agnostic scheduling**: Provides a variant with decreasing stepsizes $\eta_k = \eta/(k+1)^{3/4}$ and square-root delay thresholds $R_k = \lfloor\sqrt{k}\rfloor$, removing the need to tune thresholds from problem parameters
- **Optimal time complexity**: In the Euclidean smooth setting, translates iteration bounds into time complexity that matches the optimal rates identified by Tyurin and Richtárik (2023), recovering Ringmaster ASGD's guarantees without loss
- **Empirical validation**: Demonstrates on stochastic quadratics (6174 workers) and NanoChat pretraining (8 and 32 workers) that advantages scale with worker-speed heterogeneity

## Methodology

The method targets the unconstrained stochastic nonconvex problem $\min_x \mathbb{E}_\xi[f(x;\xi)]$ in a distributed setting with $n$ heterogeneous workers.

**Core mechanism**: A central server maintains the iterate $x_k$ and momentum buffer $m_k$. When worker $i$ returns a gradient $g_k = \nabla f(x_{k-\delta_k}; \xi)$ with delay $\delta_k$, the server accepts it only if $\delta_k < R_k$ (delay thresholding). Accepted gradients update momentum and then the LMO direction:

$$m_{k+1} = (1-\alpha_k)m_k + \alpha_k g_k, \quad x_{k+1} = x_k + \eta_k \cdot \text{lmo}(m_{k+1})$$

where $\text{lmo}(y) = \arg\min_{\|u\| \leq 1} \langle y, u \rangle$ is the linear minimization oracle over the unit norm ball. The norm choice determines the optimizer: spectral norm recovers Muon, $\ell_2$ norm recovers normalized SGD with momentum, $\ell_\infty$ recovers SignSGD.

**Fixed-parameter regime**: Constant momentum $\alpha$ and threshold $R = 1/\alpha$, with stepsizes tuned from problem constants. Yields iteration complexity matching known lower bounds for first-order stochastic smooth nonconvex optimization.

**Parameter-agnostic regime**: Schedule $\alpha_k = k^{-1/2}$, $R_k = \lfloor 1/\alpha_k \rfloor$, $\eta_k \propto (k+1)^{-3/4}$. Achieves $\mathbb{E}[\|\nabla f(x_k)\|_*] \leq O(\Psi \log K / K^{1/4})$ without knowing $K$ or $L_1$.

**Time complexity analysis**: Under the fixed computation model ($\tau_i$ seconds per gradient for worker $i$), partitions updates into blocks and applies the duration bound from Ringmaster ASGD to translate iteration counts into wall-clock time. The resulting bound optimizes over the number of active workers $m$ via the harmonic mean $H_m$.

## Results & Highlights

- **Stochastic quadratic benchmark** (Nesterov tridiagonal, $d=1729$, 6174 workers): In the nearly homogeneous regime, Ringmaster Muon slightly underperforms baselines (discarding updates wastes computation). As heterogeneity increases to sublinear and linear delay profiles, Ringmaster Muon becomes the best method.
- **NanoChat pretraining** (6-layer, 192-hidden, context-2048 transformer): With 32 simulated workers, Ringmaster Muon achieves the lowest training loss across all three delay regimes (nearly homogeneous, sublinear, linear). The gap widens with heterogeneity, with the clearest margin in the linear-delay setting.
- **Parameter-agnostic variant** performs competitively without requiring threshold tuning, using only a stepsize scale $\eta$ as the sole hyperparameter.
- **Measured real GPU variability** (Table 2): Gradient computation times for NanoChat range from 14.18ms (H100) to 215.20ms (GTX 1080 Ti), spanning ~15x — confirming that heterogeneous delay regimes are practically relevant.
- Theoretical time complexity in the Euclidean smooth case exactly recovers the optimal bound of Ringmaster ASGD.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: The extension from Ringmaster ASGD (SGD-type) to general LMO-based methods is meaningful but incremental — the delay-thresholding principle transfers directly, and the main technical work is adapting the convergence analysis to the non-Euclidean geometry. The parameter-agnostic variant is a genuine addition not present in the original Ringmaster ASGD.
- **Rigor**: The theoretical analysis is thorough, covering both fixed-parameter and parameter-agnostic regimes under generalized smoothness. Proofs are complete with matching lower bounds in the Euclidean smooth case. However, lower bounds for general LMO-based methods in arbitrary norms remain open (acknowledged by the authors).
- **Reproducibility**: Code is publicly available. Experiments use a simulated runtime model rather than physical multi-GPU deployment, which is a reasonable design choice for controlled comparison but raises questions about real-world overhead (communication latency, memory contention).
- **Significance**: The work is practically relevant for heterogeneous distributed training clusters (e.g., mixing GPU generations). However, the assumption of homogeneous data across workers is limiting — in practice, data and system heterogeneity often co-occur.
- **Red flags**: The NanoChat model is very small (6 layers, 192 hidden), and experiments are limited to simulated runtime rather than actual distributed training. Scalability to large models (7B+ parameters) and real distributed systems is untested. The assumption of zero communication latency is acknowledged but non-trivial in practice.

## Strengths

- Unified framework that subsumes Muon, normalized SGD, and SignSGD as special cases through the choice of norm
- First method to provide time complexity guarantees for asynchronous LMO-based optimization under heterogeneous computation
- Parameter-agnostic variant removes the need to know problem-specific constants, which is valuable in practice
- Clean algorithm design — only adds a delay threshold on top of standard LMO-based momentum
- Comprehensive theory covering both fixed-parameter and adaptive schedules

## Limitations

- Assumes homogeneous data distribution across workers; data heterogeneity is not addressed (acknowledged as future work)
- Zero communication latency assumption; real distributed systems have non-trivial network costs
- Experiments use simulated runtime rather than physical multi-GPU clusters, leaving practical engineering challenges unexplored
- Lower bounds for general LMO-based methods remain open
- The parameter-agnostic bound includes a factor $e^{L_1^2 \eta^2}$ when $L_1$ is unknown, which the authors note is unavoidable but can be large

## Relevance

本文对分布式训练优化有直接意义：它解决了异构 GPU 集群中 Muon 优化器的同步等待问题，而 Muon 正在成为 LLM 预训练中 AdamW 的有力替代方案。对于任何涉及混合硬件加速训练流水线的研究，这项工作都提供了理论和实践参考。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does Ringmaster LMO perform with real multi-node distributed training, including network latency and gradient compression?
- Can the delay-thresholding mechanism be combined with data-heterogeneity techniques (e.g., FedAvg-style local steps) to handle both system and data heterogeneity simultaneously?
- What is the practical overhead of the delay-thresholding mechanism in terms of wasted gradient computations, and can preemptive worker interruption mitigate this?
- How do the results scale to large models (7B+ parameters) and hundreds of workers in production training clusters?
- Are there tighter lower bounds for asynchronous LMO-based methods in non-Euclidean norms that could close the gap with the Euclidean case?
