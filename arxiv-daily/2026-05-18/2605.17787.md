> **一句话总结：** 本文揭示了LLM预训练中SGD表现远逊于Adam的核心原因是SGD无法维持足够大的有效学习率——通过两类简单的梯度裁剪机制稳定大学习率SGD后，1B参数LLaMA的验证损失差距从57%缩小到仅3.5%。

> **TL;DR:** The Adam-SGD gap in LLM pre-training is largely explained by SGD's inability to sustain Adam's much larger effective learning rates. Simple, targeted gradient clipping (layer-wise RMS norm clipping + output-layer per-token-class clipping) enables SGD with LR 100-300 to close the gap to ~3.5% on a 1B LLaMA model.

## 核心贡献

- 发现Adam的有效学习率比SGD高2-4个数量级，导致SGD的参数更新范围极其有限（仅为Adam的约1/50）
- 揭示LLM预训练中权重-梯度范数比极大（10³–10⁵），且随batch size增大而加剧，因此需要很大的学习率才能有效训练
- 理论分析表明，LLM中的小梯度范数源于token频率的极端不平衡和大batch size对梯度方差的抑制
- 识别出两类阻碍SGD使用大学习率的梯度不规则性：层间梯度范数尖峰（Type I）和输出层token类别间梯度幅度极度不均（Type II）
- 提出SGD-LL方法（两层裁剪机制），使SGD能以LR 100-300稳定训练，将1B模型的Adam-SGD差距从57%缩至3.5%

## Key Contributions

- Empirically demonstrate that Adam's effective learning rates are 2-4 orders of magnitude larger than SGD's in LLM pre-training, causing SGD to explore a solution space ~50× smaller than Adam
- Identify that the weight-to-gradient norm ratio in LLM pre-training ranges from 10³ to 10⁵, far exceeding CNN training (~10²–10³), and increases with batch size
- Provide a unified theoretical bound (Theorem 3.2) for stochastic gradient norms via bias-variance and background-hit decompositions of per-token-class gradients, explaining why gradients remain small under large batch sizes and heterogeneous token distributions
- Identify two gradient irregularities that destabilize SGD at large learning rates: (Type I) layer-wise gradient norm spikes, especially early in training; (Type II) highly uneven per-token-class gradient magnitudes at the output layer
- Propose SGD-LL: layer-wise RMS norm clipping + output-layer per-token-class clipping, enabling SGD with learning rates of 100-300 and reducing the validation loss gap to Adam from >50% to ~3.5% on a 1B-parameter LLaMA model

## Methodology

The paper proceeds in three analytical stages:

1. **Effective Learning Rate Analysis:** Define the effective momentum learning rate as the per-parameter scaling applied to the momentum. For SGD this is simply the base LR; for Adam it is η/(√v_i + ε). Empirically measure this across layers during 130M LLaMA pre-training on C4.

2. **Theoretical Gradient Norm Analysis:** Derive a universal upper bound for the stochastic gradient norm of any parameter block whose gradient admits a class-wise decomposition (Theorem 3.2). The bound separates tokens into a "head" set H (high-frequency, well-trained) and a "tail" set T (low-frequency, under-trained), applying a bias-variance decomposition to H and a background-hit decomposition to T. Specialize to the output layer (κ=1) and intermediate layers (κ=V).

3. **SGD-LL Design:** Based on the identified gradient irregularities, introduce two targeted clipping mechanisms:
   - **Layer-wise RMS norm clipping:** clip_r,‖·‖_RMS(η_t · m_ℓ) for all non-output layers, capping update RMS norm at threshold τ, mitigating Type-I spikes
   - **Output-layer per-token-class clipping:** clip_δ,‖·‖_2(m_{L,i}) for each token class weight vector, mitigating Type-II disparity

   Thresholds are set large enough that normal gradients pass through unchanged — clipping only activates during irregular spikes.

## Results & Highlights

- **130M LLaMA on C4 (2.6B tokens):** Adam val loss 3.11, SGD 5.07, SGD-LL 3.24 — gap reduced from 63% to 4.2%
- **350M LLaMA on C4 (7.8B tokens):** Adam 2.78, SGD 4.14, SGD-LL 2.87 — gap from 49% to 3.2%
- **1B LLaMA on C4 (20B tokens, 1M-token batch):** Adam 2.51, SGD 3.95, SGD-LL 2.60 — gap from 57% to 3.5%
- SGD-LL uses learning rates of 100 (130M), 200 (350M), and 300 (1B), compared to Adam's 2-3×10⁻³
- Weight distance from initialization ‖w_T - w_0‖_F confirms SGD-LL explores a solution space comparable to Adam
- Both clipping types are necessary — using either alone still leads to divergence at large LR (Appendix C.1)
- Results are consistent across multiple random seeds (Table 5)
- GPT-2 architecture experiments show the same pattern (Table 4)

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** The effective LR perspective is insightful and provides a clean unifying explanation. The theoretical analysis via token-wise gradient decomposition is novel and well-motivated. However, the idea that gradient clipping helps SGD is not new — the contribution is in *which* clipping and *why*.
- **Rigor:** Theoretical results are properly derived and connected to empirical observations. The head-tail partition framework is elegant. However, some assumptions (e.g., ε_j small for head tokens, c_q ≤ C_T/B) require stronger empirical validation across different datasets and tokenizers.
- **Reproducibility:** High — C4 dataset, LLaMA architecture, standard hyperparameters, code available. Training details (GPUs, hours) are reported.
- **Significance:** Important for understanding optimizer dynamics in LLM training. The finding that SGD can almost match Adam with simple clipping challenges the prevailing assumption that adaptive methods are inherently necessary.
- **Red flags:** (1) The residual 3.5% gap is still meaningful at scale — Adam remains better. (2) Experiments are limited to C4 and LLaMA/GPT-2; generalization to instruction-tuning, RLHF, or other architectures is unknown. (3) The SGD-LL learning rates (100-300) are extremely unusual and may interact unpredictably with other training techniques (e.g., LR scheduling, weight decay). (4) No wall-clock comparison — with clipping overhead and potentially different throughput, the practical efficiency is unclear.

## Strengths

- Clean, well-motivated narrative connecting effective LR → weight-SG ratio → gradient irregularities → targeted clipping
- Unified theoretical framework (Theorem 3.2) applicable to both output and intermediate layers
- Strong empirical scaling results from 130M to 1B with consistent trends
- Honest about limitations — explicitly states the goal is understanding, not proposing a new optimizer
- The two identified gradient irregularity types provide actionable diagnostic tools for future optimizer design

## Limitations

- Only evaluated on next-token prediction pre-training (C4); not tested on downstream tasks, fine-tuning, or multi-modal training
- The per-token-class clipping requires knowledge of token boundaries in the output layer — not straightforward for all architectures
- No comparison to other adaptive methods (AdaGrad, RMSProp, LAMB, Shampoo) or recent alternatives
- The clipping thresholds (τ, δ) are manually tuned; sensitivity analysis is limited
- Weight decay is set to zero in main experiments, deviating from standard practice
- The approach does not fully close the gap — the remaining 3.5% may compound over longer training runs

## Relevance

本文对理解LLM预训练中的优化器动力学具有重要价值，其理论框架（token-wise梯度分解）和经验发现（有效学习率差距）对设计更高效的预训练方案有直接启发，与高效训练和优化算法设计的研究方向高度相关。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does SGD-LL transfer to modern training recipes with weight decay, dropout, and longer training schedules?
- How does the Adam-SGD gap evolve in post-pre-training stages (SFT, RLHF, DPO)?
- Can the theoretical framework predict optimal clipping thresholds as a function of model size, batch size, and vocabulary?
- Is the remaining 3.5% gap attributable to Adam's coordinate-wise adaptivity, or to other factors like second-moment estimation?
- What happens with even larger models (7B, 70B) — does SGD-LL scale or does the gap widen again?
