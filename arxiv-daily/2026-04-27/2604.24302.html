Differentiable Faithfulness Alignment for Cross-Model Circuit Transfer

arXiv: 2604.24302v1

Date: 2026-04-27

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.24302v1

一句话总结： 本文提出可微忠实度对齐（DFA）框架，通过学习一个对齐矩阵将小模型中的电路重要性分数映射到大模型的组件空间中，用可微的忠实度目标（KL散度 + L1稀疏惩罚）在目标模型上优化该映射，从而在不运行完整电路发现的情况下恢复大模型的忠实电路，在Llama-3 1B到3B上效果最佳，部分情况下甚至超过直接在大模型上做归因的结果。
TL;DR: DFA learns a differentiable alignment matrix that maps node-level circuit importance scores from a smaller source LLM into a larger target LLM, optimizing a soft faithfulness objective (KL divergence + L1 sparsity) via clean/corrupted activation interpolation. Evaluated on Llama-3 and Qwen-2.5 across six tasks, it consistently beats random baselines, achieves competitive circuit recovery on Llama-3 1B-to-3B (sometimes surpassing direct target attribution), but degrades for larger scale gaps and on Qwen-2.5 -- showing that smaller models can serve as mechanistic priors for larger ones, with clear limits.

核心贡献

  • 提出可微忠实度对齐（DFA）框架，将跨模型电路迁移形式化为一个预测性对齐问题，通过可微的忠实度目标学习源模型到目标模型的组件映射矩阵 W。
  • 证明小模型电路信息可有效迁移到大模型：在Llama-3 1B到3B上，对齐后的电路忠实度与直接在大模型上做归因相当甚至更优，且零样本迁移同样有效。
  • 通过详尽的消融实验（随机W、打乱源信号、置换W列、启发式架构先验）证明DFA的成功依赖于结构化的源模型信号和学到的跨模型映射，而非简单的稀疏掩码或架构先验。
  • 系统性地揭示了节点级电路迁移的边界：迁移效果随源-目标模型差距增大而下降，在Qwen-2.5上表现明显弱于Llama-3，表明跨模型电路对齐有前景但也存在明确限制。

Key Contributions

  • Introduces the Differentiable Faithfulness Alignment (DFA) framework, which formalizes cross-model circuit transfer as a predictive alignment problem and learns a source-to-target mapping matrix W via a differentiable faithfulness objective, bypassing expensive full circuit discovery on the target model.
  • Demonstrates that smaller-model circuit information can effectively transfer to larger models: on Llama-3 1B to 3B, aligned circuits match or exceed direct target-model attribution faithfulness, with zero-shot transfer remaining competitive.
  • Provides thorough ablation evidence (random W, scrambled source signal, permuted W columns, heuristic architectural prior) confirming that DFA's gains stem from structured source-model signals and a learned cross-model correspondence rather than trivial baselines.
  • Systematically identifies the limits of node-level circuit alignment: transfer weakens with larger source-target gaps and is substantially lower on Qwen-2.5, highlighting both the promise and boundaries of cross-model circuit transfer.

Methodology

DFA operates on node-level circuits in the computational-graph view of Transformers. The key components are:

  1. Problem Formulation: Given source model M_S and target model M_L with node sets V_S and V_L, the goal is to predict a target-model circuit score vector from a source-model circuit score vector s_S without running direct attribution on M_L. An alignment matrix W in R^{|V_S| x |V_L|} projects source scores into target space: s_L_hat = s_S * W.
  1. Soft Target Circuit: The projected scores are converted into a continuous mask via element-wise sigmoid (not softmax, since multiple target nodes can be important simultaneously), producing m_L in [0,1]^{|V_L|}.
  1. Differentiable Faithfulness Intervention: For each component j, the intervened activation is a soft interpolation between clean and corrupted activations: A_tilde(j) = m_L(j) A_clean(j) + (1 - m_L(j)) A_corrupt(j). This makes the faithfulness objective differentiable with respect to W.
  1. Training Objective: A combined loss of KL divergence between clean and intervened output distributions (faithfulness loss) plus an L1 sparsity penalty on the mask. The sparsity term prevents the trivial solution of selecting all components.
  1. Transfer Settings Evaluated: In-distribution (train and test on same task), near-distribution (train on related task, e.g., Addition to Subtraction), and zero-shot/far-transfer (leave-one-task-out).
  1. Models: Llama-3 (1B, 3B, 8B) and Qwen-2.5 (0.5B, 1.5B, 3B) with three attribution methods: NAP, NAP-IG-Activations, NAP-IG-Inputs.
  1. Evaluation Metric: Circuit Performance Ratio (CPR) from MIB benchmark -- area under the faithfulness-versus-circuit-size curve.

Results & Highlights

  • Llama-3 1B to 3B is the strongest setting: Aligned circuits are frequently competitive with direct node attribution on the target model. Zero-shot transfer is often close to in-distribution performance, suggesting W captures a reusable source-target correspondence.
  • DFA sometimes exceeds direct target attribution: On certain tasks (Arithmetic, ARC-Challenge), aligned circuits achieve higher CPR than directly running attribution on the target model. The authors attribute this to smaller models providing cleaner mechanistic signals that DFA can leverage.
  • Consistent outperformance of baselines: Across all settings, DFA beats random W, scrambled source signals, permuted W columns, and heuristic architectural priors -- confirming gains come from learned alignment.
  • Scaling degrades transfer: Recovery ratio drops for larger source-target gaps (e.g., 1B to 8B vs. 1B to 3B). Transfer on Qwen-2.5 is substantially weaker than on Llama-3, indicating architectural mismatch matters.
  • Reverse transfer (large to small) is easier: Mapping from a larger model to a smaller one works better, consistent with the intuition that compression is easier than extrapolation.
  • Cross-task transfer is structured but uneven: The task-to-task transfer matrices show non-random patterns, with some tasks (MCQA, ARC-E, ARC-C) transferring well across training tasks, while others are more task-specific.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: Moderate-to-high. The core idea of treating cross-model circuit transfer as a differentiable alignment problem is novel, though it draws on existing ideas from DAS (Geiger et al., 2024) and linear model alignment (Zhao et al., 2023, 2024). The specific instantiation -- using faithfulness as the training signal for W -- is a meaningful contribution.
  • Rigor: Strong. The paper includes four types of ablation controls (random W, scrambled source, permuted W, heuristic prior), evaluates three transfer regimes, tests six tasks across six model pairs with three attribution methods, and reports both CPR and per-task faithfulness curves. The honest reporting of failures (Qwen-2.5, large gaps) adds credibility.
  • Reproducibility: High. Code is publicly available. The experimental setup follows the MIB benchmark protocol, uses standard models (Llama-3, Qwen-2.5), and the methodology is clearly described. The hyperparameter lambda for sparsity is the main design choice not deeply explored.
  • Significance: Potentially impactful for the mechanistic interpretability community. If smaller models can serve as mechanistic priors, this could reduce the computational cost of circuit analysis for large models. However, the practical utility is currently limited by the degradation on larger gaps and cross-family transfer.
  • Red flags: (1) The method is limited to node-level circuits, which are known to be weaker than edge-level methods. (2) The W matrix is a single linear projection, which may be too simplistic for complex architectural correspondences. (3) The "exceeds direct attribution" result may partly reflect weaknesses of the attribution methods on larger models rather than a genuine advantage of DFA. (4) The paper evaluates on relatively small models (up to 8B); scalability to truly large models (70B+) remains untested.

Strengths

  • Clean and well-motivated problem formulation with a principled differentiable solution.
  • Extensive ablation suite that convincingly rules out alternative explanations for the results.
  • Honest discussion of limitations, particularly the degradation on Qwen-2.5 and larger scale gaps.
  • Well-connected to prior work (MIB benchmark, DAS, circuit analysis literature).
  • The zero-shot transfer results are particularly interesting, suggesting some universality in source-target correspondences.
  • Clear writing and well-organized experimental sections with both aggregate metrics and per-task curves.

Limitations

  • Node-level only: the approach cannot capture edge-level circuit structure, which is known to provide richer mechanistic information.
  • Linear mapping: a single matrix W may be insufficient for modeling complex nonlinear correspondences between architectures.
  • Limited model scale: the largest target model tested is 8B; practical utility for frontier-scale models is unknown.
  • Uneven transfer across architectures: the method works much better within Llama-3 than for Qwen-2.5, suggesting sensitivity to architectural similarity.
  • No exploration of alternative alignment architectures (e.g., multi-layer MLPs, attention-based mappings).
  • The paper does not analyze what W learns or provide interpretability of the alignment matrix itself, which would strengthen the mechanistic understanding claim.
  • Dependency on the quality of source-model attribution methods; poor source circuits will naturally lead to poor transfer.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Can DFA be extended to edge-level circuits, and would this substantially improve transfer quality?
  • How does the alignment matrix W behave -- does it learn meaningful cross-architectural correspondences (e.g., mapping similar functional roles across layers)?
  • Would nonlinear alignment functions (e.g., MLPs or cross-attention) significantly improve transfer, especially for cross-family settings like Llama to Qwen?
  • Does DFA scale to models in the 70B+ range, and does the degradation pattern continue or plateau?
  • Can the learned alignment be reused for new tasks or architectures without retraining, and what is the minimal source model size needed for effective transfer?
  • How sensitive is DFA to the choice of hyperparameter lambda (sparsity weight) and the attribution method used for source circuits?
