Complexity of Linear Regions in Self-supervised Deep ReLU Networks

arXiv: 2604.24393v1

Date: 2026-04-27

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.24393v1

一句话总结： 本文首次系统研究了自监督学习（SSL）模型在ReLU网络中产生的线性区域（linear regions）几何特性，通过SplineCam工具可视化并追踪训练过程中区域数量、面积、离心率和边界变化，发现SSL方法用显著更少的线性区域即可达到与监督方法相当的准确率，且在几何空间中能比特征标准差更早检测到表示坍塌。
TL;DR: This paper extends the study of piecewise-linear partitions (linear regions) in ReLU networks from supervised to self-supervised learning. Using SplineCam to extract 2D polytopes near the data distribution, the authors track region count, area, eccentricity, and boundaries across training for supervised (Classifier, Triplet, SupCon), contrastive SSL (SimCLR, MoCo), and self-distillation SSL (SimSiam, BYOL) methods on MNIST and FashionMNIST. Key findings: (1) SSL methods achieve comparable accuracy to supervised models with substantially fewer regions (e.g., SimCLR needs ~4,300 regions vs SupCon's ~11,300 for similar accuracy on MNIST); (2) contrastive methods rapidly expand regions while self-distillation methods consolidate them; (3) representation collapse can be detected earlier in the polytopal geometric space than via feature standard deviation. Accepted to CVPR Findings 2026.

核心贡献

  • 首次将ReLU网络线性区域分析从监督学习扩展到自监督学习，系统性对比了监督、对比学习和自蒸馏三类方法在训练过程中线性区域的演化规律。
  • 利用SplineCam工具从高维输入空间投影到二维平面，追踪训练过程中线性区域的数量、面积、离心率和边界数量的动态变化。
  • 发现SSL方法（如SimCLR）仅需监督方法（如SupCon）约38%的线性区域数量即可达到相当的分类准确率，表明更低的模型复杂度可能带来更好的泛化和鲁棒性。
  • 证明了在几何空间中可以比特征标准差更早检测到表示坍塌现象，为SSL模型训练监控提供了新的诊断工具。
  • 对比分析揭示了对比学习方法（使用负样本）产生更多、更小的各向同性区域，而自蒸馏方法（无负样本）倾向于产生更少、更大的各向异性区域。

Key Contributions

  • First systematic study extending linear region analysis in ReLU networks from supervised to self-supervised learning paradigms, comparing supervised, contrastive SSL, and self-distillation SSL methods.
  • Employs SplineCam to project high-dimensional input spaces to 2D planes and extract linear region polytopes, tracking geometric properties (count, area, eccentricity, boundary count) throughout training.
  • Demonstrates that SSL methods achieve comparable accuracy with substantially fewer regions (e.g., SimCLR: ~4,300 regions at 83.5% accuracy vs SupCon: ~11,300 regions at 85.6% accuracy on MNIST), suggesting reduced complexity may improve generalization.
  • Shows that representation collapse is detectable earlier in the geometric (polytopal) space than via representation standard deviation, providing a practical early-warning diagnostic for SSL training.
  • Reveals that contrastive methods (with negatives) produce more numerous, smaller, isotropic regions, while self-distillation methods (without negatives) produce fewer, larger, anisotropic regions that correlate with improved generalization.

Methodology

The paper builds on the theory that ReLU-based neural networks are Continuous Piecewise-Linear (CPWL) functions that partition input space into linear regions. The authors investigate how different SSL objectives shape these partitions.

Low-dimensional analysis (Two Moons dataset): A simple 2D setting where the full input space can be directly partitioned and visualized, providing intuition about how different training objectives shape the geometry.

High-dimensional analysis: For MNIST (784D) and FashionMNIST (784D), the authors adopt a projection strategy following Novak et al. and Hanin & Rolnick. Three data points from distinct classes are selected, and their circumcenter and orthonormal basis vectors define a 2D plane within the high-dimensional space. SplineCam (Humayun et al., 2023) is then used to extract and visualize the CPWL partitions on this plane.

Models studied:

  • Supervised: Classifier (linear layer), Triplet Loss, SupCon (Supervised Contrastive)
  • Contrastive SSL: SimCLR, MoCo
  • Self-distillation SSL: SimSiam, BYOL

All models share the same encoder backbone per dataset, trained for 100 epochs with standard augmentation pipelines. Metrics tracked: number of regions, average volume, mean eccentricity, number of boundaries, and classification accuracy.

Collapse simulation: The prediction head is removed from SimSiam to intentionally induce representation collapse, allowing comparison between geometric metrics and feature standard deviation as early indicators.

Results & Highlights

  • Region efficiency of SSL: On MNIST, supervised methods (Classifier, SupCon) produce 7,365--12,817 regions while SSL methods produce only 3,653--8,731 regions for comparable accuracy. On FashionMNIST, the gap is even larger (supervised: 40,988--78,178 vs SSL: 8,080--35,996).
  • Evolution dynamics differ: Supervised methods show rapid region growth (SupCon increases 137% in 10 epochs on MNIST), while SSL methods first decline (SimSiam drops to 62% of initial regions) before gradually growing. Contrastive SSL methods expand faster than self-distillation methods.
  • Geometry differences: SSL regions are 2.42x (MNIST) and 3.76x (FashionMNIST) larger on average than supervised regions. Self-distillation methods produce higher eccentricity (more elongated) regions than contrastive methods.
  • Near-linear accuracy-region relationship for SSL: After initial region merging, SSL methods show an almost linear relationship between region count and accuracy, suggesting that simply splitting existing regions correctly aligns with the data distribution.
  • Early collapse detection: When SimSiam's prediction head is removed, the number of linear regions drops to ~5% of initialization by step 150, while the feature standard deviation only begins declining at step 400. The geometric signal provides earlier warning of collapse by approximately 250 training steps.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The extension of linear region analysis from supervised to self-supervised learning is a natural but meaningful contribution. The finding that geometric metrics can detect collapse earlier than standard methods is the most novel aspect. However, the overall framework relies heavily on existing tools (SplineCam) and established projection techniques.

Rigor: The experimental design is sound with controlled comparisons (same backbone architecture, same augmentation strategy). However, the study is limited to small-scale datasets (MNIST, FashionMNIST) with small models. It remains unclear whether the geometric insights scale to ImageNet-scale models and modern architectures (ResNets, ViTs).

Reproducibility: The paper provides detailed hyperparameter tables in the supplementary material, uses publicly available implementations (Lightly for SSL, PyTorch Metric Learning for supervised losses), and describes the SplineCam pipeline. Reproducibility appears high.

Significance: The practical value of polytopal metrics as training diagnostics for SSL is promising but not yet validated on realistic training scenarios. The claim that fewer regions correlate with improved generalization is stated but not rigorously tested (no out-of-distribution or transfer learning experiments).

Red flags: MoCo achieves only 66.4% on MNIST and 70.9% on FashionMNIST, which is unusually low and suggests potential training issues. The paper does not address this discrepancy. The eccentricity and boundary count differences across methods are relatively small (<0.07 for eccentricity, <0.04 for boundaries), raising questions about whether these differences are practically meaningful or just statistical artifacts.

Strengths

  • First systematic comparison of linear region geometry across supervised, contrastive, and self-distillation paradigms.
  • Clear, intuitive visualizations of how different SSL objectives shape the input space partition.
  • Practical finding that polytopal metrics provide earlier collapse detection than feature standard deviation.
  • Well-controlled experimental setup ensuring fair comparisons across methods.
  • The linear relationship between regions and accuracy for SSL methods is an interesting empirical observation.

Limitations

  • Evaluated only on MNIST and FashionMNIST with small models; scalability to modern architectures and ImageNet-scale data is untested.
  • MoCo's anomalously low accuracy (66% on MNIST) is unexplained and raises concerns about training quality.
  • The high-dimensional projection to 2D may lose important geometric information; the 2D analysis may not faithfully represent the full high-dimensional geometry.
  • No evaluation of whether polytopal metrics predict downstream task performance or out-of-distribution generalization.
  • The practical computational cost of computing SplineCam partitions during training is not discussed, limiting assessment of feasibility as a real-time diagnostic.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Do the geometric properties of linear regions (count, eccentricity, anisotropy) scale to ImageNet-scale models and larger architectures like ResNet-50 or Vision Transformers?
  2. Can polytopal metrics be used as regularization signals during SSL training to actively prevent representation collapse rather than merely detecting it?
  3. What is the computational overhead of computing SplineCam partitions during training, and can lightweight proxies approximate the same geometric information?
  4. Is there a causal relationship between region anisotropy and downstream generalization performance, or is it merely correlational?
  5. How do the geometric properties change with different augmentation strategies, which are known to critically affect SSL performance?
