Learning to Think from Multiple Thinkers

arXiv: 2604.24737v1

Date: 2026-04-27

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.24737v1

一句话总结： 本文从计算学习理论角度研究了从多个不同"思考者"提供Chain-of-Thought (CoT)监督下的学习问题，证明了在被动数据收集下即使仅有两个思考者学习也是计算困难的（基于密码学假设），但提出了一种基于Boosting的主动学习算法，每个思考者只需提供与精度无关的少量CoT样本，配合对数数量的思考者即可实现高效学习。
TL;DR: This paper studies learning with Chain-of-Thought supervision from multiple thinkers who provide correct but systematically different solutions. Under cryptographic assumptions, passive learning from even two thinkers is computationally hard, but a boosting-based active learning algorithm achieves efficient learning with per-thinker CoT budgets independent of target accuracy and only O(log(1/epsilon) * loglog(1/epsilon)) thinkers.

Key Contributions

  • Multi-thinker CoT learning framework: Generalizes the CoT supervision model from a single thinker to multiple thinkers, systematically defining adversarial selection, instance-dependent selection, uniform sampling models, and known vs. unknown identity settings, yielding a complete computational landscape.
  • Hardness results under passive data collection: Under cryptographic assumptions (quantum hardness of GapSVP/SIVP, existence of local PRGs with polynomial stretch), proves that learning is computationally hard even with CoT from just two thinkers under instance-dependent selection with known identities, and from omega_d(1) thinkers under uniform sampling with unknown identities.
  • Improved E2E hardness for shorter thinking lengths: Improves the expressivity lemma relating autoregressive linear thresholds to depth-2 threshold circuits from T = Theta(d) to T = Theta(s), establishing E2E learning hardness for significantly shorter thinking lengths than prior work (Joshi et al. 2025).
  • Boosting-based active adaptive algorithm: Proposes an algorithm that actively and adaptively collects CoTs from different thinkers, requiring only M_star = O(VCdim(F^{e2e-T}_d)) CoT examples per thinker (completely independent of epsilon) and O~(log(1/epsilon)) total thinkers, achieving efficient learning for any base class with a tractable Consistent oracle.
  • Computational-statistical gap in non-active settings: Establishes that in non-active settings, even with known batch identities, learning requires at least poly(d) * epsilon^{-1.99} thinkers -- a dramatic gap from the statistical O(1/epsilon) sample complexity, contrasting with the active setting where O~(log(1/epsilon)) thinkers suffice.

Methodology

The paper operates within the PAC learning framework for autoregressive CoT models introduced by Joshi et al. (2025). The core methodology spans three pillars:

1. Expressivity and Hardness Reductions:

The authors work with the class of autoregressive linear thresholds F_{d,lin} -- functions parameterized by a weight vector w in R^d that apply the same linear threshold autoregressively for T steps. This class admits a tractable Consistent oracle via linear programming. The key expressivity lemma (Lemma 3.1) shows that any depth-2 threshold circuit of size s over n inputs can be simulated by autoregressive linear thresholds with dimension d = Theta(ns) and thinking length T = Theta(s), via a carefully designed feature map with padding blocks. This improves the prior relationship of d = Theta(ns^2), T = Theta(n*s^2).

2. Cryptographic Hardness Constructions:

For the two-thinker hardness (Theorem 2), the authors construct "distribution forks" using properties of Regev's cryptosystem decryption functions: they show each decryption function can be implemented both as an intersection AND a union of degree-2 polynomial threshold functions, creating complementary CoT leakage. For the omega(1)-thinker hardness (Theorem 3), they exploit a structural property of hard DNF formulas from Daniely and Vardi (2021) -- where exactly one term is satisfied when the output is 1 and all terms evaluate to 0 when the output is 0 -- and create multiple thinkers by permuting the DNF terms.

3. Boosting-based Algorithm:

Algorithm 1 follows an AdaBoost framework adapted to the multi-thinker setting. It maintains a distribution over a large E2E training set, and in each round: (a) draws a small subsample of size M_star from the current distribution, (b) requests CoTs from a single thinker on those examples, (c) invokes the Consistent oracle to obtain a weak predictor with error at most 0.25, and (d) updates the distribution via multiplicative reweighting. After O(log m * loglog m) rounds, the weighted majority vote achieves zero training error. The final generalization guarantee follows from uniform convergence over the boosted hypothesis class H_{boost}(K).

4. Case Study on Noisy Parities:

The paper also includes a detailed study of learning noisy parities with CoT supervision from multiple generators (corresponding to different orderings of the parity support). A simple correlation-based algorithm (Algorithm 2) recovers the support even under adversarial generator selection, while the information-theoretic lower bound shows sample complexity must scale with k (vs. O(log d) for a single generator). Transformer experiments validate these predictions.

Results & Highlights

  • Theorem 1 (E2E Hardness): Under various cryptographic assumptions, learning F^{e2e-T}_{d,lin} is hard for T(d) up to sublinear values (e.g., T = omega_d(log d * loglog d) under lattice assumptions; T = omega_d(1) under local PRG). This is a massive improvement over the prior T = Theta(d) bound.
  • Theorem 2 (Two-Thinker Hardness): Under cryptographic assumptions, learning remains hard with CoT from just 2 thinkers under instance-dependent selection, even with known identities. The key insight is creating "distribution forks" where each thinker's CoT is trivially uninformative on complementary parts of the distribution.
  • Theorem 3 (Few-Thinker Hardness): Under local PRG assumptions, learning is hard from omega_d(1) thinkers under uniform sampling with unknown identities.
  • Theorem 4 (Active Algorithm): The boosting algorithm learns with kappa = O~(log(1/epsilon) + log VCdim) thinkers, each providing only M_star = O(VCdim(F^{e2e-T}_d)) CoT samples independent of epsilon, plus O~(1/epsilon) passive E2E samples. Total active CoT queries are only O_{d,T}(log(1/epsilon) * loglog(1/epsilon)).
  • Theorem 5 (Non-Active Gap): In non-active settings, no poly(d)-time algorithm can learn with fewer than poly(d) * epsilon^{-1.99} thinkers when each provides only poly(d) CoTs, creating a sharp computational-statistical gap.
  • Table 1 (Computational Landscape): A comprehensive table summarizes the learnability status across all settings, showing easy/hard/open status for each combination of supervision model and identity knowledge.
  • Noisy Parity Experiments: Transformer experiments confirm that multi-thinker CoT supervision still enables learning but requires more samples than single-thinker supervision, consistent with theoretical predictions.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The multi-thinker formulation is genuinely novel and practically motivated (e.g., different human experts, different algorithms, different programs producing the same answer). The "distribution fork" technique for hardness constructions is creative. However, the individual components (cryptographic hardness reductions, AdaBoost) are not new -- the contribution lies in their synthesis for this new problem formulation.

Rigor: The paper is extremely rigorous. All hardness results are carefully stated with explicit cryptographic assumptions. The algorithm is presented with full proofs. The computational landscape (Table 1) clearly delineates what is known vs. open. The distinction between passive and active settings is cleanly formalized.

Reproducibility: The theoretical results are reproducible given the complete proofs in the appendix. The transformer experiments on noisy parities (Section 11.3) use standard architectures and training procedures, though full experimental details (model size, hyperparameters beyond learning rate) are somewhat sparse.

Significance: The paper makes a foundational contribution to understanding CoT learning beyond the single-thinker idealization. The results have direct implications for how we should collect and curate CoT training data in practice -- passive aggregation from multiple sources can be computationally problematic, while active/adaptive collection protocols can be highly efficient. The computational-statistical gap is a striking finding.

Red flags:

  • The hardness results rely on strong cryptographic assumptions (subexponential quantum hardness of lattice problems, existence of local PRGs). While standard in the learning theory literature, these are unproven.
  • The algorithmic positive result requires an "active and adaptive" protocol that may be difficult to implement in practice -- it assumes the learner can request CoTs from specific thinkers on specific examples adaptively over multiple rounds.
  • The gap between the 2-thinker hardness (instance-dependent) and the omega(1)-thinker hardness (uniform, unknown) is significant. The conjecture that uniform sampling with 2 thinkers is also hard remains open.
  • The paper focuses exclusively on the realizable (noiseless CoT) setting. Extending to noisy or approximate CoTs would be important for practical relevance.

Strengths

  • Provides a comprehensive and clean framework for studying multi-thinker CoT learning, with a clear taxonomy of settings (Table 1).
  • The improved expressivity lemma (T = Theta(s) instead of T = Theta(n*s^2)) is a significant technical advance that tightens E2E hardness to much shorter thinking lengths.
  • The boosting-based algorithm is elegant and simple once the connection is identified; the guarantee of per-thinker CoT budget independent of epsilon is strong.
  • Honest about limitations and open problems -- clearly marks what remains unresolved.
  • The case study on noisy parities bridges theory and practice, showing concrete algorithms and empirical validation.
  • The paper carefully connects to practical ML pipeline (SFT stage, RL post-training), making the theoretical results relevant.

Limitations

  • Restricted to realizable setting: All results assume thinkers produce perfectly correct CoTs. Real-world CoT data contains errors, omissions, and noise.
  • Deterministic thinkers only: The model assumes each thinker is a deterministic function. Extending to stochastic policies (as in actual LLMs) would require fundamentally different analysis.
  • Active protocol practicality: The positive algorithmic result requires an active, adaptive data collection protocol where the learner iteratively selects examples and requests CoTs from specific thinkers. This is significantly more demanding than passive data collection.
  • Base class specificity: Hardness results are established for the specific class of autoregressive linear thresholds. Whether similar results hold for richer classes (e.g., transformers) is not addressed.
  • No CoT faithfulness guarantee: The algorithm only guarantees end-to-end accuracy, not that the learned model's CoT matches any of the original thinkers' reasoning, which is a known practical concern.
  • Limited experimental validation: The transformer experiments are restricted to the synthetic noisy parity problem and do not test the algorithm or framework on more realistic tasks.

Relevance

本文从理论角度深入分析了多思考者CoT学习的计算复杂度，其结果对理解LLM训练中SFT阶段的数据收集策略有直接指导意义。多思考者困难性和主动学习算法的发现为设计高效的CoT数据采集方案提供了理论依据，也为RL后训练（如DeepSeek-R1、OpenAI o1）中如何平衡CoT成本与学习效率提供了新的视角。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Can the hardness results be extended to the stochastic policy setting where thinkers are sampled from a distribution over policies, as would be the case for LLM-generated CoTs?
  • Is there a fundamental tradeoff between the adaptivity of the data collection protocol and the total number of CoT queries needed, or can non-adaptive active queries achieve similar guarantees?
  • Can the 2-thinker hardness under uniform sampling with unknown identities be resolved (the authors conjecture it is hard)?
  • How do the results change when CoTs are only partially observable (e.g., only prefixes are revealed), which is a practically motivated and already empirically explored setting?
  • What structural assumptions on the relationship between thinkers' CoTs would make passive learning tractable -- for instance, if thinkers share common sub-computations?
  • Can the boosting algorithm be implemented practically with transformer architectures, and does it provide benefits over standard SFT with mixed-source CoT data?
  • How does the computational landscape change if we allow the learner to observe CoTs from a mixture of thinkers (not just one per example) on the same instance?
