> **一句话总结：** 本文将FP16低精度感知训练集成到硬件感知NAS的评估环节中，使候选架构在搜索阶段即暴露于部署时的数值约束，在Intel Movidius Myriad X VPU上的船舶分割任务中，将后训练精度转换导致的mIoU从0.78恢复至0.826（恢复约三分之二的精度损失），且不增加模型复杂度。

> **TL;DR:** Most hardware-aware NAS pipelines optimize architectures under FP32 and convert to low-precision only after search, causing a deployment accuracy gap. This paper integrates FP16-aware fine-tuning directly into the NAS evaluation loop for vessel segmentation on the Intel Movidius Myriad X VPU. On-device mIoU recovers from 0.78 (post-training conversion) to 0.826 (deployment-aligned training), closing roughly two-thirds of the precision-induced accuracy drop for a compact 95,791-parameter model, without modifying the search space or evolutionary strategy.

## 核心贡献

- **部署对齐的低精度NAS框架：** 将FP16低精度感知微调直接嵌入硬件感知NAS的评估循环中，在搜索阶段就使候选架构暴露于目标部署硬件的数值约束，而非在搜索完成后才进行精度适配。
- **搜索级数值对齐：** 证明在NAS评估环节引入部署一致的精度约束会改变架构选择动态，使搜索倾向于数值鲁棒的架构，而非仅仅在结构上高效。
- **真实设备在环验证：** 在Intel Movidius Myriad X VPU上进行完整的设备端评估，直接测量分割精度和吞吐量，覆盖了真实部署场景中的算子调度、内存访问和低精度运算行为。
- **显著的精度恢复：** 对于95,791参数的紧凑模型，后训练精度转换将mIoU从0.85降至0.78，而部署对齐的低精度训练在设备上达到0.826 mIoU，恢复了约三分之二的精度损失，且不增加模型复杂度。

## Key Contributions

- **Deployment-aligned NAS framework:** Integrates FP16-aware fine-tuning directly into the hardware-aware NAS evaluation loop, exposing candidate architectures to deployment-time numerical constraints during search rather than applying precision adaptation only post-search.
- **Search-level numerical alignment:** Demonstrates that incorporating deployment-consistent precision during evaluation alters architecture selection dynamics, biasing the search toward numerically robust architectures rather than merely structurally efficient ones.
- **Realistic device-in-the-loop validation:** Provides comprehensive on-device evaluation on the Intel Movidius Myriad X VPU, measuring segmentation accuracy and throughput under real deployment conditions including operator scheduling, memory access patterns, and reduced-precision arithmetic.
- **Significant accuracy recovery:** For a compact 95,791-parameter model, post-training precision conversion drops mIoU from 0.85 to 0.78, while deployment-aligned low-precision training achieves 0.826 mIoU on-device, recovering approximately two-thirds of the precision-induced accuracy gap without increasing model complexity.

## Methodology

The proposed framework extends hardware-aware NAS by aligning the numerical conditions during optimization with those encountered at deployment. The key components are:

**Search Space:** A discrete space of single-path networks with up to 6 learnable blocks, each sampled from a library of convolutional primitives including ConvAct, ConvBnAct, ConvSE, MBConv, MBConvNoRes, CSP variants, DenseNet-style blocks, and ResNet-style blocks, with configurable kernel sizes (1--5), channel widths, and activations (ReLU or GELU).

**Evolutionary Strategy:** A population-based genetic algorithm with population size 16 over 10 generations. Fitness is computed as a weighted combination of on-device FPS and mIoU with an exponential sensitivity term: F(a) = alpha * fps(a) + beta * Metric(a) * exp(gamma * Metric(a)). The top-1 architecture is retained via elitism, 6 new random architectures are injected for diversity, and mutation (p=0.15) and single-point crossover are applied to the top 50% mating pool.

**Deployment-Aligned Low-Precision Training:** Each candidate is first trained for 10 epochs in FP32, then fine-tuned for 10 epochs (after 1-epoch warmup) with an FP16 projection operator P(.) that casts activations to FP16 and back to FP32 to simulate reduced mantissa precision. Weight rounding is enabled via straight-through estimators (dP(x)/dx ~ 1), and activations are clipped to |x| <= 12 to match FP16 dynamic range.

**Device-in-the-Loop Evaluation:** After training and optional FP16-aware fine-tuning, each architecture is exported to an FP16 OpenVino intermediate representation and executed on the Intel Movidius Myriad X VPU. Throughput and latency are measured directly on-device, feeding back into the NAS fitness computation.

**Dataset:** HRSC2016, a public benchmark for ship detection in optical satellite imagery, reformulated as binary vessel segmentation. Images are resized to 512x512 with intensity normalization.

## Results & Highlights

- **Accuracy recovery:** Post-training precision conversion (PTQ baseline) drops on-device mIoU from 0.85 (GPU FP32) to 0.78. Deployment-aligned FP16 training recovers this to 0.826 mIoU, closing approximately two-thirds of the gap.
- **Convergence stability:** Low-precision-aware optimization shows steadily increasing fitness across generations with a narrowing gap between elite and median architectures, indicating improved population-wide robustness compared to PTQ where fitness plateaus early.
- **IoU-FPS trade-off shift:** The IoU-FPS distribution shifts upward under low-precision awareness, with a narrower spread of solutions across generations, meaning more architectures remain viable under reduced precision.
- **Qualitative improvements:** FP16-aware fine-tuning reduces contour fragmentation, internal holes in predictions, and false negatives for elongated or low-contrast vessels compared to PTQ deployment, preserving vessel morphology more reliably.
- **No complexity overhead:** All gains are achieved with the same 95,791-parameter architecture, confirming that numerical robustness emerges from deployment-consistent training rather than architectural overparameterization.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (Moderate):** The core idea -- integrating FP16-aware training into the NAS loop -- is a straightforward but meaningful extension of existing hardware-aware NAS. The FP16 projection operator is essentially a simplified version of standard quantization-aware training (fake quantization + STE). The novelty lies primarily in the application context (spaceborne edge AI) and the empirical demonstration that search-level numerical alignment matters, rather than in a fundamentally new algorithm.
- **Rigor (Fair):** The paper uses a single dataset (HRSC2016), a single target device (Myriad X VPU), and a single task (binary vessel segmentation). The NAS budget is modest (16 population, 10 generations). No ablation study is provided for key hyperparameters (e.g., activation clipping threshold |x|<=12, fitness function weights alpha/beta/gamma, number of FP16 fine-tuning epochs). The comparison is only between PTQ and deployment-aligned training within the same NAS framework, without external baselines from other quantization-aware NAS methods.
- **Reproducibility (Moderate):** The methodology is described in reasonable detail, but code is not released. The search space and genetic algorithm parameters are specified. The HRSC2016 dataset is public. However, some preprocessing details (bounding box to mask conversion, augmentation specifics) are only partially described, and the exact fitness function coefficients are not explicitly listed.
- **Significance (Moderate for EO/edge AI community):** The paper makes a practical contribution to the spaceborne edge AI community by showing that a simple modification to the NAS evaluation pipeline yields meaningful accuracy recovery. However, the approach is limited to FP16 (not integer quantization or mixed precision), and the scale of the experiments is relatively small.
- **Red flags:** (1) Only one task and one dataset are evaluated -- generalizability is unclear. (2) The 0.826 vs 0.78 mIoU comparison is compelling but based on a single best architecture; no confidence intervals or statistical significance tests are reported. (3) The paper does not compare against established QAT methods applied post-NAS (e.g., training a full-precision NAS-found architecture with standard QAT after search), which would be the most direct and fair baseline. (4) Training uses an AMD Radeon GPU, which is an unusual choice for ML research -- potential compatibility concerns for reproducibility.

## Strengths

- Addresses a real and practically important problem: the mismatch between full-precision NAS optimization and low-precision edge deployment in spaceborne systems.
- The proposed modification is simple and non-invasive -- it does not change the search space or evolutionary strategy, making it easy to integrate into existing NAS pipelines.
- Provides both quantitative and qualitative evidence of improvement, with device-in-the-loop evaluation on actual hardware rather than simulation.
- The recovered accuracy gap (roughly two-thirds) is substantial and achieved without any increase in model size or complexity.
- Well-motivated by the specific constraints of spaceborne EO missions, with clear practical implications.

## Limitations

- **Single-task, single-dataset evaluation:** Only binary vessel segmentation on HRSC2016 is tested. It remains unclear whether the approach generalizes to multi-class segmentation, object detection, or other EO tasks.
- **Single target hardware:** Only the Intel Movidius Myriad X VPU is evaluated. Results may differ on other edge accelerators (e.g., Google Edge TPU, NVIDIA Jetson, FPGA-based solutions).
- **No comparison with post-NAS QAT:** The most natural baseline would be to take the best NAS-found architecture and apply standard quantization-aware training to it after search. This comparison is absent.
- **Limited statistical rigor:** No confidence intervals, repeated runs, or significance tests are reported. The results could be sensitive to random seeds or NAS initialization.
- **FP16-only scope:** The paper does not explore integer quantization (INT8/INT4) or mixed-precision strategies, which are common in more aggressive edge deployment scenarios.
- **Modest NAS budget:** Population size 16 for 10 generations is relatively small, and the evolutionary search may not fully explore the architecture space.

## Relevance

本文对研究NAS与边缘部署协同优化的工作具有参考价值，其核心思路——在架构搜索阶段即引入部署环境的数值约束——不仅适用于航天边缘计算场景，也可迁移至其他资源受限硬件（如移动端NPU）上的模型自动化设计流程。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does deployment-aligned low-precision NAS compare against a straightforward baseline of applying standard QAT to the best architecture found by conventional hardware-aware NAS?
- Would the approach generalize to more aggressive quantization (INT8, INT4, or mixed-precision) on different edge accelerators such as Google Edge TPU or NVIDIA Jetson?
- What is the sensitivity of the results to the NAS hyperparameters (population size, number of generations, fitness function weights, FP16 fine-tuning epochs)?
- Can the approach be extended to multi-class segmentation or object detection tasks where the accuracy impact of quantization may be even more pronounced?
- Does the FP16-aware fine-tuning during NAS introduce any training instability or convergence issues for certain architectural primitives (e.g., attention-based blocks)?
