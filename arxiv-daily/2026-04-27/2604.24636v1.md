> **一句话总结：** 本文通过将Gemma 4 E2B和Qwen3 0.6B集成到Android猜词游戏Palabrita的5天实战经历，系统性地总结了端侧小模型部署的五类故障模式（格式违规、约束违规、上下文退化、延迟不兼容、模型选择不稳定），并提出"LLM做得越少越可靠"的核心设计原则和八条可操作的设计启发式规则。

> **TL;DR:** A practitioner case study documenting the 5-day integration of on-device SLMs (Gemma 4 E2B, Qwen3 0.6B) into an Android word-guessing game (Palabrita). Over 204 commits, the system evolved from an ambitious design where the LLM generated complete structured puzzles to a pragmatic architecture where curated word lists handle deterministic tasks and the LLM generates only three short hints with a deterministic fallback. The paper identifies five failure categories specific to on-device SLMs, details mitigation strategies (multi-layer defensive parsing, contextual retry, session rotation, progressive prompt hardening, responsibility reduction), distills eight design heuristics, and documents a WorkManager anti-pattern. Core insight: the most reliable on-device LLM feature is one where the LLM does the least.

## 核心贡献

- 提出了端侧SLM集成的五类故障分类体系：输出格式违规（F1）、约束违规（F2）、上下文质量退化（F3）、延迟不兼容（F4）和模型选择不稳定（F5），每类故障都有具体的症状、根因、频率和commit级证据。
- 发现并记录了"渐进式简化弧"（Progressive Simplification Arc）模式：LLM的职责从7字段结构化生成逐步缩减到仅生成3条提示，最终收敛到"创意任务交给LLM，确定性任务交给传统系统"的分工原则。
- 提出8条端侧SLM集成的可操作设计启发式规则（H1-H8），涵盖输出schema最小化、多层防御性解析、上下文反馈重试、会话轮换、提示词工程技巧、确定性降级回退、创意/确定性任务分离、模型数量精简等。
- 详细记录了4次完整的提示词重写过程，展示了SLM提示词工程与云端LLM的关键差异（优先级标记有效、具体例子优于抽象规则、全语言名优于ISO代码）。
- 揭示了WorkManager用于前台LLM操作的反模式，详细记录了一天修7个bug的教训，最终用Kotlin协程替换，净减少191行代码。

## Key Contributions

- A taxonomy of five failure categories (F1-F5) specific to on-device SLM integration, documented with commit-level evidence, observed frequencies, root causes, and representative examples for each failure type.
- The concept of a "Progressive Simplification Arc" showing how the LLM's responsibility was systematically reduced from 7 output fields to hint-only generation over 5 days, with probability analysis showing that reducing from 5 to 2 fields approximately doubles the success rate (from ~32% to ~72% at 85% per-field reliability).
- Eight actionable design heuristics (H1-H8) for practitioners integrating SLMs into mobile apps, derived from empirical experience rather than theoretical analysis.
- A detailed prompt engineering iteration record: four complete prompt rewrites with before-and-after comparisons, demonstrating that prompt engineering for SLMs consumes 30-40% of AI-related development effort.
- A WorkManager anti-pattern case study: seven bugs in a single day caused by mismatch between WorkManager's persistent state model and foreground LLM operations, resolved by replacing it with standard Kotlin coroutines.
- Comparison of three on-device inference frameworks (LiteRT-LM, MLC LLM, AICore) across dimensions of prompt control, model flexibility, and integration friction.

## Methodology

The paper is a longitudinal practitioner case study following Runeson and Host's guidelines for case study research in software engineering. The author (sole developer) integrated on-device SLMs into Palabrita, a Wordle-style Android word-guessing game, over a 5-day sprint (April 21-26, 2026). The study uses three primary data sources: (1) the complete Git history of 204 commits (~90 AI-related), analyzed and coded for failure triggers, applied strategies, and outcomes; (2) specification documents (specs/ directory) providing contemporaneous design rationale; and (3) physical device benchmarks on Pixel 7 Pro and Pixel 10 Pro.

Two SLMs were evaluated in production: Gemma 4 E2B (2.6B parameters, 2.6GB) for premium devices with >=8GB RAM, and Qwen3 0.6B (600M parameters, 614MB) for compact devices with >=2GB RAM. Nine total models were evaluated, converging on two for shipping. The inference framework is Google's LiteRT-LM with CPU-only backend.

The development followed a Spec Driven Development workflow (specs before code, acceptance criteria as tests), and commits follow Conventional Commits format (feat:/fix:/refactor:/chore:), providing structured metadata for analysis. Commits were grouped into 14 thematic phases and cross-referenced for recurring patterns.

Three research questions are addressed: RQ1 (viability of on-device LLMs for fully offline apps), RQ2 (types of failures), RQ3 (effective mitigation strategies).

## Results & Highlights

- **62% feature cut rate**: Of 8 envisioned features, only 3 shipped in reduced form and 5 were cut entirely, driven by honest contact with SLM limitations.
- **Progressive Simplification Arc**: LLM output schema reduced from 7 fields (word, category, difficulty, rarity, 5 hints) to 1 field (3 hints), with word selection moved to curated word lists. Per-field reliability analysis shows ~32% success for 7 fields vs ~72% for 2 fields at 85% per-field reliability.
- **Five-layer defensive parsing pipeline**: UTF-8 sanitization, code fence stripping, direct JSON decode, regex extraction, and structural parsing (inferring fields by value type rather than key name). The structural parser was the critical breakthrough for handling translated JSON keys.
- **Word length violations at 30-50%**: SLMs cannot reliably count characters. Prompt hardening reduced this to ~10-15%, still too high; the final pivot to curated word lists reduced it to 0%.
- **KV cache degradation**: Qwen3 0.6B output quality degrades after ~3 generations per session; Gemma 4 E2B after ~5-7. Session rotation every 5 generations maintains quality.
- **Latency arithmetic**: Initial synchronous puzzle generation blocked UI for 1-3 minutes (7 puzzles x 2.5 retries x 5-7 seconds each). Hint-only generation reduced per-puzzle time from 15-30 seconds to 1-2 seconds.
- **WorkManager replacement**: 767 lines removed, 576 added (net -191), all seven WorkManager bugs eliminated by switching to Kotlin coroutines.
- **Decode speeds**: Qwen3 0.6B achieves 30-35 tokens/sec on Pixel 7 Pro (CPU); Gemma 4 E2B achieves comparable speeds. A ~50-token hint takes ~1-2 seconds.
- **Design principle confirmed**: "The most reliable on-device LLM feature is one where the LLM does the least." The game is fully playable even if the LLM never produces a successful response, thanks to HintFallbackProvider with deterministic template hints.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty (Moderate):** The paper does not propose a new algorithm or model. Its novelty lies in the systematic documentation and categorization of engineering challenges that practitioners face but that are absent from the academic literature. The five-category failure taxonomy and eight heuristics are empirically grounded and fill a genuine gap. However, many individual observations (SLMs struggle with JSON, counting is unreliable) are known anecdotally in the practitioner community.

**Rigor (Good):** The commit-level evidence trail is a strong methodological choice. Conventional Commits and Spec Driven Development provide structured, contemporaneous data. The threats-to-validity section is honest about limitations. However, the failure frequencies are estimates from development observations, not controlled experiments with statistically significant sample sizes. The study covers only a 5-day window with a single developer on a single project.

**Reproducibility (Good):** The public GitHub repository, specific commit hashes cited throughout, code listings, and detailed prompt examples make the work highly reproducible. Other practitioners can verify the failure categories on their own SLM integrations.

**Significance (High for practitioners):** This paper addresses a real and growing need. As on-device SLM deployment expands, engineering guidance is scarce. The eight heuristics are immediately actionable. The WorkManager anti-pattern case study will save developers time. The progressive simplification arc provides a useful mental model.

**Red flags:**
- Single-author, single-project study limits generalizability. The failure taxonomy may not cover all failure modes in other application domains (e.g., on-device translation, summarization).
- The 5-day sprint is both a strength (realistic pressure) and a limitation (no long-term maintenance observations). Model version regressions, hardware-specific bugs on non-Pixel devices, and edge cases at scale are unexplored.
- The paper includes a lengthy advocacy section for edge-first AI (Section 11.1) that, while well-argued, goes beyond the scope of an engineering case study and introduces an ideological dimension that may bias the framing.
- Some of the "discoveries" (e.g., SLMs produce markdown code fences) are well-known in the community and might feel obvious to experienced ML engineers, though they are clearly novel to the software engineering audience the paper targets.
- The probability analysis for field reliability (the (1-p)^n argument) assumes independent per-field failure, which may not hold in practice (a model that fails at JSON formatting may fail at all fields simultaneously).

## Strengths

- Exceptionally honest and detailed account of the development process, including failures, reversals, and the 62% feature cut rate. This candor is rare in academic papers and invaluable for practitioners.
- The failure taxonomy (F1-F5) is clear, well-structured, and immediately recognizable to anyone who has worked with on-device SLMs.
- The progressive simplification arc provides a powerful and memorable mental model: start with what the LLM can do reliably, not what it should be able to do.
- Code examples are concrete, practical, and directly tied to commit hashes, making the paper both a study and a reference.
- The framework comparison (LiteRT-LM vs MLC LLM vs AICore) fills a gap in the published literature.
- The structural parsing technique (inferring fields by value type rather than key name) is an elegant solution to the multilingual key translation problem.

## Limitations

- Single-developer, single-project, 5-day study. The failure taxonomy has not been validated across multiple projects, teams, or application domains.
- Only tested on Pixel 7 Pro and Pixel 10 Pro. Performance characteristics and failure modes may differ significantly on other Android devices (Samsung, Xiaomi, etc.), iOS, or lower-tier hardware.
- The two models studied (Gemma 4 E2B, Qwen3 0.6B) are from specific model families. Newer or different SLMs (Phi-4 Mini, Llama 3.2, etc.) may exhibit different failure patterns.
- No quantitative evaluation of hint quality. The paper measures reliability (does the LLM produce parseable output?) but not creativity or usefulness of the generated hints.
- The advocacy for edge-first AI (Section 11.1), while compelling, is outside the scope of an empirical engineering study and may distract from the technical contributions.
- The study focuses on a game application with soft quality requirements. Hard-constraint domains (medical, legal, financial) would face more severe challenges that are not addressed.

## Relevance

本文对关注端侧AI部署和移动端LLM应用的研究者与工程师具有高度参考价值。论文提出的五类故障模式和八条设计启发式规则可直接指导端侧SLM的实际集成工作，特别是"LLM做最少的事最可靠"的原则为系统架构设计提供了清晰的思路。对于研究模型压缩、量化部署和端侧推理优化的研究者，本文提供了来自真实应用场景的详实工程约束与需求分析。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How do the five failure categories generalize to non-game applications (e.g., on-device email summarization, real-time translation, code completion)?
- What is the quantitative impact of GPU/NPU backends on failure rates and latency compared to the CPU-only configuration used in this study?
- Can the progressive simplification arc be predicted a priori? Are there model capability benchmarks that would indicate the reliable scope for a given SLM without requiring iterative discovery?
- How does long-term usage (weeks/months) affect failure rates? Do users observe quality drift as model versions update or device conditions change?
- Would structured output enforcement at the framework level (JSON-mode decoding, as suggested by the authors) eliminate the F1 failure category entirely, and what is the performance cost?
- How do the failure patterns differ across iOS (Core ML / MLX) versus Android (LiteRT-LM) deployments with the same models?
