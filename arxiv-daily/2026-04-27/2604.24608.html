Learning to Route Queries to Heads for Attention-based Re-ranking with Large Language Models

arXiv: 2604.24608v1

Date: 2026-04-27

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.24608v1

一句话总结： 本文提出RouteHead方法，通过学习一个轻量级路由器，根据每个查询动态选择最优的注意力头子集进行LLM注意力重排序，在BEIR和BRIGHT基准上持续优于静态头选择方法。
TL;DR: RouteHead learns a lightweight query-dependent router that dynamically selects optimal attention heads per query for LLM-based re-ranking, consistently outperforming static head selection baselines across multiple benchmarks and LLM backbones.

核心贡献

  • 提出了RouteHead，一种查询依赖的注意力头动态选择方法，用于LLM注意力重排序，突破了现有方法使用静态头集合的局限性。
  • 设计了离线搜索流程构建伪标签：先用nDCG@10筛选头部候选池，再用前向选择+交换式局部搜索为每个查询寻找近似最优头集合。
  • 设计了受推荐系统矩阵分解启发的路由器架构，使用双线性交互对查询-注意力头进行评分，并配合稀疏正则化避免过度激活。
  • 在BEIR（11个子数据集）和BRIGHT（推理密集型检索）两个基准上，使用Llama-3.2-1B/3B、Llama-3.1-8B、Qwen-2.5-7B四种骨干模型验证了方法的有效性。

Key Contributions

  • Introduces RouteHead, a query-dependent dynamic attention head selection method for LLM-based re-ranking that overcomes the limitation of static head selection in prior work (ICR, QRhead, CoRehead).
  • Proposes an offline pseudo-label construction pipeline: first prunes heads to a top-K pool via individual nDCG@10 scoring, then applies forward selection with early stopping followed by swap-based local refinement to find near-optimal head sets per query.
  • Designs a router architecture inspired by matrix factorization in recommender systems, using bilinear interaction between learnable head embeddings and frozen LLM query embeddings, trained with BCE loss and an L1 sparsity regularizer.
  • Demonstrates consistent improvements over strong baselines across BEIR (11 sub-datasets) and BRIGHT (reasoning-intensive) benchmarks on four LLM backbones (Llama-3.2-1B/3B, Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen-2.5-7B).

Methodology

RouteHead follows a two-stage pipeline:

Stage 1: Offline Pseudo-Label Construction. Since no ground-truth labels exist for optimal query-to-head mappings, the method constructs them via search. First, each attention head is individually scored by the nDCG@10 it achieves on a head-detection dataset, and only the top-K heads (K=64) are retained as the search pool. For each query, forward selection greedily adds heads one at a time (up to a budget P=8) with early stopping when no improvement is observed. A subsequent swap-based local refinement phase replaces selected heads with unselected ones to escape order-dependency and find better combinations. This yields a multi-hot pseudo label per query.

Stage 2: Router Training. The router assigns each head a learnable embedding and extracts query embeddings from the frozen LLM's hidden states via mean pooling. A bilinear interaction scores each query-head pair, passed through an independent sigmoid to produce activation probabilities. The router is trained with binary cross-entropy against the pseudo labels, plus an L1 sparsity regularizer to discourage over-activation of heads.

Inference. Given a new query, the router selects a small set of heads, and relevance scores are computed by aggregating attention signals only from these selected heads, keeping the LLM itself frozen.

Results & Highlights

  • BEIR benchmark: RouteHead achieves the best average nDCG@10 among attention-based methods across all four LLM backbones. With Llama-3.2-3B, it reaches 49.6 avg nDCG@10, exceeding the strongest traditional re-ranker MSMARCO-MiniLM (48.0) and outperforming RankGPT with larger models (RankGPT_Llama-3.1-8B: 46.3).
  • BRIGHT benchmark: RouteHead with Llama-3.2-3B reaches 18.9 avg nDCG@10, exceeding RankGPT with the much larger Qwen3-235B-A22B (18.8), demonstrating strong efficiency.
  • Scaling behavior: Performance increases monotonically with backbone size within each model family, indicating RouteHead provides complementary gains on top of stronger LLMs.
  • Domain-dependent gains: RouteHead is particularly strong on StackExchange and general information-seeking queries, though it sometimes underperforms CoRehead on coding and math-oriented domains, likely due to training data (MSMARCO) distribution mismatch.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: Moderate-to-high. The idea of query-dependent head routing for attention-based re-ranking is novel and well-motivated by the empirical observation that static head selection generalizes poorly across datasets. The connection to matrix factorization in recommender systems is a nice conceptual bridge.
  • Rigor: The experimental setup is thorough -- four LLM backbones, two diverse benchmarks (BEIR with 11 sub-datasets and BRIGHT), and comparisons against both generation-based and attention-based baselines. However, the pseudo-label construction process involves several hyperparameters (K=64, P=8, epsilon tolerance) whose sensitivity is not analyzed.
  • Reproducibility: The method is described in sufficient detail to reproduce. The LLM backbones are all open-source. The pseudo-label construction process, while involving a search procedure, is well-specified. No code repository is mentioned in the paper.
  • Significance: The work addresses a real limitation of current attention-based re-ranking methods. The gains are consistent but modest (typically 0.5-1.5 nDCG@10 over the next-best attention-based method). The observation that RouteHead with a 3B model can match RankGPT with a 235B model is compelling from an efficiency standpoint.
  • Red flags: (1) The pseudo labels are constructed from MSMARCO data, creating a domain bias that the authors acknowledge but do not resolve. (2) The offline search procedure to construct pseudo labels is computationally expensive (forward selection + swap refinement per query over 64 heads). (3) Gains over CoRehead are sometimes marginal, and RouteHead underperforms on coding/math domains, raising questions about whether a more sophisticated static selection could close the gap. (4) Only 512 queries are sampled for BEIR evaluation, which may introduce variance.

Strengths

  • Well-motivated by clear empirical evidence that static head selection is suboptimal across domains.
  • Clean two-stage pipeline that separates label construction from router training.
  • Lightweight router that adds minimal overhead at inference time (the LLM remains frozen).
  • Strong experimental coverage with multiple LLM families and scales.
  • Honest discussion of limitations, including the training-evaluation distribution mismatch on coding/math domains.

Limitations

  • Pseudo-label quality depends entirely on the offline search procedure, which is a heuristic approximation and computationally expensive.
  • Router training on MSMARCO introduces a domain bias; performance degrades on reasoning-intensive coding and math domains.
  • The head search space is pruned to top-K=64 before the per-query search, potentially missing heads that are only useful in combination.
  • No analysis of router interpretability -- it is unclear what query features drive head selection decisions.
  • Gains over the strongest baseline (CoRehead) are modest on some datasets, and not always statistically significant given the 512-query sample size.

Relevance

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How sensitive is RouteHead to the choice of K (head pool size) and P (maximum head set size)? Is there a Pareto-optimal operating point?
  • Can the router be made interpretable -- e.g., do certain head embeddings cluster by function (entity matching, term overlap, semantic similarity)?
  • Would domain-balanced pseudo-label construction (mixing code/math queries with MSMARCO) resolve the underperformance on coding and math domains?
  • How does RouteHead compare when the base LLM is fine-tuned for retrieval tasks rather than used in a frozen, zero-shot manner?
  • What is the computational cost of the offline pseudo-label construction compared to the inference-time gains?
