A Functorial Formulation of Neighborhood Aggregating Deep Learning

arXiv: 2604.24672v1

Date: 2026-04-27

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.24672v1

一句话总结： 本文利用拓扑空间上连续函数的预层（presheaf）和余预层（copresheaf）范畴论工具，给出了卷积/消息传递神经网络的一种函子化数学表述，并通过分析这些结构不满足层（sheaf）/余层（cosheaf）公理的阻碍，从理论上解释了此类网络的局部解释性不唯一、对抗攻击脆弱性、数据集依赖性以及无法捕捉拓扑不变量等经验局限性。
TL;DR: The paper provides a rigorous mathematical formulation of convolutional and message-passing neural networks using presheaves and copresheaves of continuous functions over topological spaces, induced from skyscraper sheaves/cosheaves. The key insight is that skyscraper sheaves are simultaneously cosheaves, creating a dual functorial structure. By proving that these presheaves fail to satisfy sheaf axioms (locality condition) and the copresheaves fail cosheaf axioms (surjectivity condition), the authors derive theoretical explanations for several empirical limitations: non-unique gluing of local representations (Theorem 3.12), susceptibility to adversarial attacks via arbitrarily small perturbations (Theorem 3.14), dataset-dependent expressiveness limitations (Theorem 3.15), and trivial higher sheaf cohomology implying inability to detect topological invariants (Theorem 3.23). The framework recovers CNNs, MPNNs, RNNs/LSTMs, and attention-transformers as special cases, and proposes that sheaves beyond the presheaf of continuous functions may yield novel architectures.

核心贡献

  • 提出了一种基于摩天大楼层（skyscraper sheaf/cosheaf）诱导的连续函数预层/余预层的函子化框架，将邻域聚合深度学习算法统一建模为全局截面的复合映射
  • 证明了非唯一的局部粘合（Theorem 3.12）：包含非线性层或不通过包含映射分解的网络，其全局输出不同但局部截面上不可区分，从而给出了解释性不唯一的数学刻画
  • 证明了对对抗攻击的脆弱性（Theorem 3.14）：通过包含映射分解的邻域聚合层存在无穷多个不同局部截面产生相同聚合结果，可构造任意小的对抗扰动
  • 证明了数据集依赖性（Theorem 3.15）：最后一层为邻域聚合层且通过包含映射分解的网络的最终函数不满足满射条件时，存在无法被该架构逼近的函数
  • 证明了相关层的上同调平凡性（Theorem 3.23）：层化后的预层是flasque层，高阶上同调为零，解释了为何拓扑数据分析/持续同调技术能增强此类网络性能
  • 将CNN、图上消息传递网络（MPNN）、RNN/LSTM统一纳入该框架，并分析了注意力Transformer作为非邻域聚合层的特殊情况
  • 建立了动态图上MPNN与2维流形上深度学习技术之间的等价性对应（Theorem 5.15），解释了1维位置编码的ViT在2维图像分类中的有效性

Key Contributions

  • Functorial framework for neighborhood aggregating DL: Introduces presheaves and copresheaves of continuous functions induced from skyscraper sheaves/cosheaves as the foundational mathematical objects, with di-natural transformations connecting the covariant and contravariant functor structures.
  • Non-unique local explainability (Theorem 3.12): Proves that any discrete DL algorithm with at least one non-linear layer or a layer not factoring through inclusion admits distinct global outputs with identical local sections over any open cover, providing a topological account of non-unique local interpretability.
  • Adversarial attack vulnerability (Theorem 3.14): Shows that neighborhood aggregating layers factoring through inclusion admit infinitely many distinct local sections that produce identical aggregated outputs, enabling adversarial perturbations of arbitrarily small magnitude -- a purely structural (not data-dependent) result.
  • Dataset dependency (Theorem 3.15): Demonstrates that when the final layer factors through inclusion with a non-surjective, non-open, or bijective open function F, there exist functions the architecture cannot approximate, connecting expressiveness limitations to topological properties of the aggregation map.
  • Trivial cohomology (Theorem 3.23): Proves that the sheafification of the presheaf of continuous functions is flasque, hence has trivial higher cohomology. This means neighborhood aggregating DL techniques cannot distinguish geometric/topological invariants of the underlying space, explaining the empirical benefit of TDA/persistent homology augmentation.
  • Unified treatment of architectures: Shows that CNNs (with various filter sizes, strides, dilations), graph MPNNs (including Weisfeiler-Leman equivalence), RNNs, LSTMs, GRUs, and attention-transformers are all instances of the defined discrete DL framework, with RNNs arising as global sections over the universal cover of S^1.
  • Correspondence theorem (Theorem 5.15): Establishes equivalences between MPNNs processing dynamic graph data over graphs with non-trivial cycles and DL techniques on 2-dimensional manifolds (Euclidean or hyperbolic metric), explaining why 1D positional encoding ViTs succeed on 2D image data.

Methodology

The paper develops its theory in five sections with increasing specificity:

  1. Sheaf/cosheaf foundations (Section 2): Reviews presheaves, sheaves, copresheaves, and cosheaves over locally compact Hausdorff spaces. Constructs the key mathematical object -- the presheaf C^0(i_{A,I}, R^k) of continuous functions induced from direct sums of skyscraper cosheaves -- and proves it satisfies the sheaf condition for the copresheaf direction but not the presheaf direction (Proposition 2.15). The morphism from local sections to global sections via inclusion is shown to be non-injective (for presheaf) and non-surjective (for copresheaf).
  1. Discrete DL as global sections (Section 3): Defines a discrete DL algorithm with m layers as a composition of functions between products of local sections of C^0(i_{A,I}, R^k) over open subsets of a topological space X. Each layer psi_i maps from one collection of open sets to another. The "neighborhood aggregating" property is formalized as a layer factoring through the inclusion map from local to global sections. The dual structure (presheaf and copresheaf) is connected via a di-natural transformation from the skyscraper sheaf/cosheaf duality.
  1. Limitation theorems (Section 3.2): The failure of C^0(i_{A,I}, R^k) to be a sheaf (non-injectivity of the gluing map) directly yields Theorem 3.12 on non-unique explainability. The failure to be a cosheaf (non-surjectivity) yields Theorems 3.14 (adversarial attacks) and 3.15 (dataset dependency). The proofs are constructive: for Theorem 3.14, one explicitly constructs perturbation vectors {m_alpha} whose sum vanishes under aggregation, making them invisible to the network but arbitrary in magnitude.
  1. Cohomological analysis (Section 3.3): Shows that sheafification produces a flasque sheaf with trivial higher cohomology (Theorem 3.23), meaning no topological obstructions exist to gluing local sections, and the architecture cannot detect geometric structure.
  1. Architectural examples (Sections 4-5): Demonstrates that CNNs (including variants with different filter sizes, strides, dilations), graph MPNNs (via universal cover/unfolding tree), RNNs/LSTMs (via universal cover of S^1 = R), and attention-transformers all fit the framework. Section 5 discusses architectures that go beyond neighborhood aggregation (attention-transformers with multiple layers, neural ODEs, persistent homology-based methods) and proposes novel directions using sheaves other than the presheaf of continuous functions.

Results & Highlights

  • Non-unique explainability: Any DL architecture with a non-linear layer admits infinitely many distinct global representations with identical local sections, providing a formal proof that local explanations are fundamentally non-unique.
  • Adversarial vulnerability is architectural: The existence of adversarial perturbations is proven to be an inherent property of any neighborhood aggregating layer factoring through inclusion, independent of training data or model parameters.
  • Dataset-dependent expressiveness: Under mild conditions on the final layer's activation function F:R^k -> R^k (non-surjective, non-open, or bijective open), the architecture provably cannot represent all continuous functions in C^0(i_{A,I}, R^k)(X).
  • Flasque sheaf implies no topological sensitivity: The sheafified presheaf C^{0,+}(i_{A,I}, R^k) has H^i = 0 for all i > 0, confirming that topological data analysis techniques provide complementary information that the DL architecture structurally cannot capture.
  • RNNs as presheaves on R: Recurrent neural networks are global sections of presheaves over the universal cover of S^1, i.e., the real line R, with time steps corresponding to fibers of the covering map.
  • Attention-transformers partially escape limitations: Multi-layer attention-transformers are shown to be non-neighborhood-aggregating after the first encoder/decoder layer, meaning Theorem 3.15 does not apply -- providing a theoretical basis for their superior performance, while Theorems 3.12 and 3.14 still explain hallucinations and adversarial vulnerability.
  • Correspondence between dynamic graphs and 2D manifolds: Theorem 5.15 establishes that MPNNs on dynamic graphs with non-trivial cycles are equivalent to DL on surfaces with Euclidean or hyperbolic geometry, depending on the rank of H^1(G, Z).

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty (Moderate-High): The use of presheaves/copresheaves of continuous functions induced from skyscraper sheaves to formulate DL is novel and distinct from prior sheaf-theoretic approaches (which primarily use cellular sheaves directly on graph structures, as in Hansen & Gebhart, Bodnar et al., or the recent CTNN by Hajji et al.). The di-natural transformation connecting presheaf and copresheaf perspectives is a genuine insight. However, some of the limitation results (e.g., adversarial vulnerability of aggregating layers) can be intuited without the full categorical machinery.
  • Rigor (High): The mathematical development is careful and well-structured. Propositions and theorems are stated precisely with proofs. The construction of the presheaf C^0(i_{A,I}, R^k) and proof of its properties (sheaf condition for one direction, failure for the other) is rigorous. The limitation theorems follow logically from the algebraic properties.
  • Reproducibility (Moderate): This is a theoretical paper with no code or experiments. The mathematical claims can in principle be verified by a reader with sufficient background in algebraic topology and sheaf theory. However, the paper is dense and assumes significant mathematical maturity. The connection to specific architectures (e.g., mapping CNN hyperparameters to open covers) is described but could be more explicit for practitioners.
  • Significance (Moderate): The theoretical framework provides an elegant unified perspective on well-known empirical phenomena (adversarial attacks, oversmoothing, dataset dependency, need for TDA). The practical impact depends on whether the framework leads to actionable architectural improvements. The proposal of "a zoo of novel deep learning algorithms" from other sheaves is intriguing but speculative at this stage.
  • Red flags: (1) The paper has been in development since at least 2022 (acknowledgements reference NIMS work ending August 2022), which raises questions about whether it has been submitted to venues and received feedback. (2) While the framework is mathematically sound, the gap between the abstract topological formulation and practical neural network design is substantial -- it is unclear how the "obstruction" analysis directly translates to improved architectures. (3) The claim that the framework "does not encompass all deep learning techniques" is stated honestly but limits the scope. (4) Some of the example mappings (e.g., CNN filter sizes to open covers) are sketched rather than fully formalized, which could limit the precision of the predictions.

Strengths

  • Provides a unified mathematical framework that cleanly explains multiple empirical phenomena (adversarial attacks, non-unique explainability, dataset dependency, topological blindness) from a single algebraic source (failure of sheaf/cosheaf axioms).
  • The dual perspective (presheaf and copresheaf) connected via di-natural transformations from skyscraper sheaves is mathematically elegant and novel.
  • Successfully recovers a wide class of architectures -- CNNs, MPNNs, RNNs, LSTMs, attention-transformers -- as instances of the same mathematical object, providing a principled taxonomy.
  • The correspondence theorem (Theorem 5.15) linking dynamic graph MPNNs to 2D manifold learning is a genuinely surprising and insightful result.
  • Honest about limitations: explicitly states which results apply and do not apply to attention-transformers, and acknowledges the framework's scope.
  • The observation that sheafification yields a flasque sheaf (trivial cohomology) provides a clean mathematical justification for the empirical effectiveness of TDA/persistent homology augmentation.

Limitations

  • No experimental validation: The paper is purely theoretical. While the mathematical framework predicts known empirical phenomena, it does not demonstrate that the framework can predict new phenomena or guide the design of improved architectures.
  • Abstraction gap: The mathematical machinery (category theory, sheaf theory, algebraic topology) is far removed from practical neural network engineering. Translating the theoretical insights into actionable design principles remains an open challenge.
  • Scope restriction: The framework only applies to "neighborhood aggregating" layers, and many modern architectures (transformers with multiple layers, diffusion models, etc.) only partially fit the framework. The analysis of attention-transformers acknowledges that only the first layer is neighborhood aggregating.
  • No constructive guidance: While the paper identifies why certain limitations exist (failure of sheaf axioms), it does not provide a constructive recipe for building architectures that overcome them, beyond the speculative suggestion to use "other sheaves."
  • Example mappings are informal: The correspondence between specific CNN/MPNN hyperparameters and the abstract open cover / skyscraper sheaf construction is described informally rather than with explicit constructive algorithms.
  • Long development time with unclear review history: The paper appears to have been developed over several years without clear indication of peer review.

  • How does this framework relate to the expressiveness results from the Weisfeiler-Leman hierarchy -- can the sheaf-theoretic perspective provide tighter bounds on GNN expressiveness?
  • Can the correspondence between dynamic graph MPNNs and 2D manifold learning (Theorem 5.15) be leveraged to transfer architectural innovations between graph and image domains?
  • Does the di-natural transformation between presheaf and copresheaf perspectives suggest practical diagnostic tools for analyzing trained neural networks (e.g., measuring the "obstruction magnitude" at each layer)?
  • How would incorporating persistent homology into the architecture change the cohomological properties of the associated sheaf, and can this be formalized within the present framework?
