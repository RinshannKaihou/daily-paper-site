Measuring Successful Cooperation in Human-AI Teamwork: Development and Validation of the Perceived Cooperativity and Teaming Perception Scales

arXiv: 2604.24461v1

Date: 2026-04-27

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.24461v1

一句话总结： 基于联合活动理论和进化合作理论，本文开发了两个心理测量量表（PCS和TPS），在三个不同的人机协作场景中（N=409）验证了其信效度，能够有效区分不同质量的合作伙伴。
TL;DR: This paper develops and validates two theory-grounded questionnaire scales -- the Perceived Cooperativity Scale (PCS, 14 items) and the Teaming Perception Scale (TPS, 9 items with 3 subscales) -- for assessing subjective cooperation quality in human-AI teamwork, demonstrating excellent reliability and construct validity across three diverse studies (N=409).

核心贡献

  • 提出了"共时整合-历时整合"(synchronic-diachronic)二维框架来系统理解人机协作的不同层次，为选择合适的理论框架和测量工具提供了指导
  • 开发并验证了感知合作性量表（PCS），基于联合活动理论中的八个有效合作者特征构建14个条目，跨场景信度极佳（Cronbach's alpha = .93）
  • 开发并验证了团队感知量表（TPS），基于进化合作理论构建9个条目含三个子维度（团队/自我/伙伴），复合量表信度良好（alpha = .88）
  • 两个量表均提供了AI版本和人类版本，可直接进行人机协作与人人协作的跨代理比较
  • 在三个差异显著的场景中验证（Hanabi合作卡牌游戏、LLM事实核查、Tesla旅程规划），证明了量表在不同整合程度和代理能动性水平下的适用性

Key Contributions

  • Introduces a synchronic-diachronic integration framework for systematically characterizing human-AI cooperation depth, guiding theory and measurement selection
  • Develops and validates the Perceived Cooperativity Scale (PCS): a 14-item unidimensional scale grounded in joint activity theory, yielding excellent reliability (alpha = .93, omega = .93) across all eight samples
  • Develops and validates the Teaming Perception Scale (TPS): a 9-item three-facet scale grounded in evolutionary cooperation theory, with good composite reliability (alpha = .88) and strong Team and Partner subscales
  • Both scales include parallel AI-agent and human-partner versions enabling direct cross-agent benchmarking -- a practical advantage for hybrid team research
  • Validates the scales across three highly diverse contexts (cooperative card game, LLM-based chatbot, EV decision-support system) with N=409 total participants, demonstrating broad applicability

Methodology

The authors employed a rigorous psychometric scale development approach following best practices (Boateng et al., 2018), proceeding through four stages:

Scale Construction. Items for the PCS were derived from the eight characteristics of an effective cooperative agent identified in joint activity theory (observability, monitorability, communicativeness, self-assessment, reliability, directability, selectivity, coordination; Bradshaw et al., 2017; Klein et al., 2005), yielding 15 initial items. Items for the TPS were generated from a three-facet conceptualization of teaming perception (team-as-a-whole, self-contribution, partner contribution), yielding 9 items. Both scales use 6-point Likert responses (1=completely disagree to 6=completely agree). The German originals were translated to English via a structured forward-backward translation protocol with three bilingual speakers.

Study Designs. Three independent studies provided data:

  • Study 1 (N=94): Within-subjects design using the cooperative card game Hanabi, where participants played with a human partner, a rule-based AI agent, and a reinforcement-learning AI agent -- providing high synchronic integration.
  • Study 2 (N=182): Within-subjects design using four LLM-based chatbot variants (GPT-4o Mini) varying in goal alignment and autonomy for fact-checking tasks -- providing moderate synchronic integration.
  • Study 3 (N=133): Correlational design with Tesla EV drivers using the trip planner as a decision-support tool -- a deliberate boundary case with minimal agent agency.

Validation Analyses. Dimensionality was assessed via exploratory and confirmatory factor analyses (EFA/CFA). Item properties were examined through item discrimination, difficulty, and confidence ratings. Reliability was evaluated with Cronbach's alpha and McDonald's omega. Construct validity was assessed through Spearman correlations with established measures (SIPA, FOST, perceived warmth/competence, system acceptance, usefulness) and the scales' ability to differentiate between cooperation partners of varying quality via repeated-measures ANOVA with planned contrasts.

Results & Highlights

  • PCS Dimensionality: Parallel analysis and CFA supported a unidimensional 14-item structure (after excluding PCS14 due to weak discrimination). The 14-item model showed improved fit across most samples, with CFI >= .90 in 6 of 8 samples.
  • PCS Reliability: Excellent internal consistency across all samples (alpha = .90-.96, omega = .90-.96).
  • PCS Validity: Construct validity followed the hypothesized pattern -- strongest correlations with perceived competence (r_s = .74), SIPA (.68), and system trustworthiness (.66); weaker but substantial correlations with warmth (.57), usefulness (.53). The PCS successfully differentiated partner types: planned contrast confirmed Han-RL < Han-RB < Han-HH ordering with large effect (d = 1.02).
  • TPS Dimensionality: CFA supported a theory-based three-factor structure (Team, Self, Partner) over a one-factor model across all samples. The EFA-based three-factor model fit better in Hanabi samples while the theory-based model fit better in LLM samples, with nuanced rather than substantial differences.
  • TPS Reliability: Team subscale excellent (alpha = .91); Partner subscale acceptable-to-good (alpha = .77); Self subscale poor-to-questionable (alpha = .55) in game-based contexts but improved in LLM contexts.
  • TPS Validity: The TPS composite differentiated partner types with large effect (F(2,186) = 79.16, p < .001, eta_G^2 = .36). The Self subscale showed a markedly different correlational pattern, with stable high self-ratings across conditions suggesting positivity bias in constrained task structures.
  • Boundary Case: The PCS remained psychometrically robust even when applied to the Tesla trip planner (a decision-support system without salient agency), suggesting applicability beyond agentic AI partners.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The paper addresses a genuine gap -- while many scales measure general AI attitudes or trust, few instruments capture the process-oriented, episodic experience of cooperative human-AI interaction. The theoretical grounding in joint activity theory and evolutionary cooperation theory is a meaningful contribution. However, the scales partly overlap conceptually with existing measures like SIPA, and the strong correlations with perceived competence (r = .74) raise questions about discriminant validity.

Rigor: The psychometric methodology is sound and follows best practices. The use of three diverse studies is a strength. However, several limitations deserve attention:

  • Sample sizes for factor analysis in Study 1 (N=94) are borderline for 15-item CFA models.
  • CFA fit indices were mixed, particularly in the Augmented condition (CFI = .80, RMSEA = .12), suggesting the PCS may not fit equally well across all contexts.
  • The TPS Self subscale's poor reliability (alpha = .37-.67 in Hanabi) is a significant psychometric weakness that limits the scale's completeness.
  • All correlations were administered within the same session, introducing common method bias concerns that the authors acknowledge.

Reproducibility: The full item sets (English and German) are provided in appendices, enabling direct reuse. However, the study materials (e.g., the Hanabi game interface, LLM chatbot implementations) are not shared, and the AI agents used are specific implementations that may not generalize. No data or code is publicly available.

Significance: The scales fill an important measurement gap for human-AI cooperation research. The PCS, in particular, has strong diagnostic potential because its items map onto specific cooperative capabilities, enabling actionable design feedback. The cross-agent comparison feature (identical items with "agent" vs. "person" referent) is practically valuable.

Red flags:

  • The Self subscale of the TPS shows a positivity bias pattern where participants rate themselves as highly cooperative regardless of partner quality, undermining its utility as an interaction-specific measure.
  • The unexpectedly strong correlations between the scales and conceptually distal constructs (e.g., PCS-usefulness r = .53; TPS Team-SIPA r = .56) suggest the discriminant validity has not been fully established.
  • No longitudinal validation has been conducted despite the theoretical emphasis on diachronic integration.

Strengths

  • Strong theoretical grounding with clear linkage from joint activity theory and evolutionary cooperation theory to item construction
  • Rigorous psychometric validation across three highly diverse studies and populations (students, Prolific workers, EV drivers)
  • Excellent reliability for the PCS and strong reliability for the TPS Team and Partner subscales
  • Both scales successfully differentiate between cooperation partners with large effects (d > 1.0)
  • Practical diagnostic value: PCS items map onto specific cooperative capabilities for actionable AI design feedback
  • Cross-agent comparison enabled through parallel AI/human versions
  • Thoughtful discussion of limitations, particularly regarding the Self subscale and goal-prioritization items
  • Complete item sets provided in both English and German

Limitations

  • The TPS Self subscale shows poor-to-questionable reliability in game-based contexts and appears susceptible to positivity bias, with self-ratings remaining stable regardless of partner quality
  • No longitudinal data to validate the theoretically central diachronic dimension of integration; all studies involved relatively short interaction episodes
  • Common method bias may inflate observed correlations, as all measures were administered within the same session
  • Discriminant validity is not fully established: several conceptually distal constructs showed unexpectedly strong correlations
  • The Hanabi sample (Study 1) was predominantly female (74:1 F:M ratio) and consisted of students, limiting generalizability
  • The factor structure of the TPS showed context-dependency, with the EFA-based and theory-based three-factor solutions performing differently across contexts
  • Goal-prioritization items (TPS05, TPS08) showed poor performance in cooperative game contexts where individual and shared goals cannot genuinely diverge
  • CFA fit indices were poor in some conditions (notably the Augmented LLM condition), suggesting context-dependent factor structure

Relevance

本文开发了评估人机协作主观体验的标准化量表工具，对于研究LLM交互中的合作质量、用户感知和人机团队动态具有直接的方法论参考价值。特别是PCS量表的条目直接映射了有效合作者的八项能力特征，可被用于诊断和改进AI系统的合作表现。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How do PCS and TPS scores evolve over extended, genuinely longitudinal human-AI partnerships with high diachronic integration?
  • Can the TPS Self subscale be redesigned to capture interaction-specific self-evaluation rather than a stable positivity bias, perhaps through behavioral anchoring or implicit measures?
  • What is the causal relationship between perceived cooperativity (synchronic) and teaming perception (diachronic) -- does high PCS across episodes predict subsequent TPS emergence?
  • How do the scales perform in high-stakes cooperative settings (e.g., clinical decision-making, industrial human-robot collaboration) where task outcomes have real consequences?
  • Could PCS item-level profiles serve as a diagnostic framework for iterative AI system design, and what is the correspondence between subjective PCS ratings and objective agent behavior metrics?
  • How robust are the scales across cultural contexts beyond German and English-speaking populations, given the emphasis on cooperative norms and social perception?
