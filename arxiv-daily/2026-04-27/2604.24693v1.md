> **一句话总结：** 本文提出CLAS方法，通过学习逐块感知向量(sensing vector)来动态调整线性激活引导(activation steering)的强度，使其能够根据输入上下文自适应地调节引导系数，在11个任务和4个模型家族上一致超越固定系数的LAS方法，并在有限标注数据下匹配或超过ReFT和LoRA。

> **TL;DR:** CLAS extends linear activation steering by replacing the fixed scalar coefficient with a learned per-block sensing vector that computes context-dependent steering strengths from the activation itself. It extracts steering directions via Recursive Feature Machine (RFM) probes on binary-labeled data, then trains sensing vectors on a small set of prompt-completion pairs using the standard next-token prediction loss. Evaluated on 11 steering tasks (reasoning, translation, summarization, safety, etc.) across 4 instruction-tuned LLMs (Qwen2.5-7B, Llama-3.1-70B, Llama-3.1-8B, Llama-3.2-1B), CLAS consistently outperforms standard LAS and matches or exceeds ReFT and LoRA with limited labeled data, while being significantly faster to tune and retaining the interpretability of the probe-derived steering directions.

## 核心贡献

- **提出CLAS方法**：将线性激活引导中固定的标量系数替换为可学习的感知向量(sensing vector)，实现根据输入上下文和模型层级动态调整引导强度
- **两阶段解耦设计**：首先通过RFM探针在二分类标签数据上提取引导方向(无需反向传播)，然后在少量prompt-completion对上通过next-token预测损失训练感知向量
- **广泛实验验证**：在11个多样化引导任务和4个指令微调LLM上进行评估，一致超越标准LAS，并在有限标注数据下匹配或超过ReFT和LoRA
- **保持可解释性**：CLAS使用的RFM探针方向在概念监控(concept monitoring)任务上远优于ReFT和LoRA学习到的低秩方向
- **计算效率高**：感知向量训练仅需少量步骤(最多8步)的AdamW优化，远快于LAS的网格搜索(需要生成并评估完整输出)

## Key Contributions

- Introduces CLAS (Contextual Linear Activation Steering), which replaces the fixed scalar steering coefficient in LAS with a learned per-block sensing vector that dynamically modulates steering strength based on input context.
- Decouples steering into two stages: (1) backpropagation-free steering vector extraction via RFM probes on binary-labeled data, and (2) sensing vector training via next-token prediction loss on a small prompt-completion dataset.
- Demonstrates consistent superiority over standard LAS and competitive or superior performance to ReFT and LoRA across 11 diverse steering tasks and 4 instruction-tuned LLMs, using limited labeled data.
- Shows that RFM-derived steering directions retain strong interpretability for concept monitoring, significantly outperforming the low-rank directions learned by ReFT and LoRA.
- Achieves much faster tuning time compared to LAS grid search, since CLAS only requires forward-backward passes on short sequences rather than full generation and evaluation.

## Methodology

CLAS operates in two stages:

**Stage 1: Steering Vector Extraction.** A dataset of prompt-concept label pairs (binary: y=1 if concept present, y=0 otherwise) is collected. The prompts are passed through the LLM, and last-token residual stream activations are extracted at each transformer block. A Recursive Feature Machine (RFM) probe is trained at each block to predict the concept labels. The RFM iteratively computes a kernel ridge regression predictor and updates the Average Gradient Outer Product (AGOP) matrix. The steering vector d_l is the principal eigenvector of the final AGOP matrix, oriented so that projections positively correlate with labels. This process is backpropagation-free and completes in minutes.

**Stage 2: Sensing Vector Training.** Rather than using a fixed scalar alpha, CLAS learns a sensing vector c_l in R^(k+1) at each block. The forward pass at each block is modified to: h_tilde = h + (c . [h, 1]) * d, where [h, 1] is the activation concatenated with a constant. The steering coefficient alpha_l,t = c_l . [h_l,t, 1] is thus both block-specific and context-dependent. The sensing vectors are initialized to zero and trained by freezing all LLM parameters and minimizing the standard next-token prediction loss on a small dataset of prompt-completion pairs using AdamW (learning rate 3e-3, up to 8 steps with early stopping).

The key insight is that the sensing vector acts as a linear function of the activation, allowing the model to modulate steering based on how much the concept is already present or relevant in the current context.

## Results & Highlights

- **Single-task steering (Table 3):** CLAS achieves the highest average accuracy over 10 tasks for all models except Llama-3.2-1B (where ReFT is best). On JailbreakBench, CLAS performs best across all models.
- **Consistent improvement over LAS (Figure 1):** CLAS outperforms LAS in nearly every model-task pair, often by substantial margins. ReFT and LoRA also improve over LAS in many tasks but can perform significantly worse in others.
- **Tuning efficiency (Table 4):** CLAS tuning is substantially faster than LAS grid search because it avoids full generation-evaluation cycles. For Llama-3.1-70B on H200, CLAS takes ~2 minutes vs. ~1 hour for LAS.
- **Steering interference (Figure 2, Section 4.2):** When steering toward one target task (GSM8K, ToxicChat, or JailbreakBench) and evaluating on all 11 tasks, CLAS consistently ranks among the top methods and avoids severe performance drops that ReFT and LoRA sometimes exhibit.
- **Concept monitoring (Table 5):** RFM probe directions (used by CLAS) achieve the highest concept detection accuracy (86.5% avg, 95.4% max) compared to ReFT (63.5% avg, 89.6% max) and LoRA (64.8% avg, 91.5% max) directions, demonstrating superior interpretability.
- **Direction orthogonality (Appendix E):** CLAS's RFM probe directions are nearly orthogonal to those learned by LoRA and ReFT, suggesting that different optimization objectives discover qualitatively different steering directions.
- **Higher ranks do not help (Appendix C.1):** Increasing ReFT and LoRA rank beyond 1 did not consistently improve accuracy.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty (Moderate-High):** The core idea of making the steering coefficient context-dependent via a learned sensing vector is clean and well-motivated. While the individual components (RFM probes, next-token prediction loss, linear sensing) are not individually novel, their combination into a two-stage decoupled framework is. The functional form of CLAS is identical to rank-1 ReFT (acknowledged by the authors), but the key difference is the decoupled training procedure: CLAS fixes the direction via RFM probes and only learns the sensing vector, whereas ReFT jointly optimizes everything. This decoupling appears to be the main source of CLAS's advantage.

**Rigor (High):** The evaluation is thorough -- 11 diverse tasks, 4 model families (including a 70B model), comparison against multiple baselines, interference analysis, concept monitoring evaluation, ablation studies on rank and coefficient tuning strategies, and analysis of direction similarity across methods. The hyperparameter settings are consistent across methods, models, and tasks, reducing the risk of unfair comparisons.

**Reproducibility (High):** Code is publicly available. Hyperparameters are extensively documented (Appendix F). Dataset construction details are provided (Appendix G). The evaluation uses o3-mini or exact match with 50 test prompts per task.

**Significance (Moderate-High):** Addressing the fixed-coefficient limitation of activation steering is important for practical deployment. The results convincingly show that context-dependent steering improves over fixed-coefficient approaches. However, the comparison with ReFT and LoRA is restricted to rank-1 settings and limited labeled data (small steer datasets), which may not reflect typical fine-tuning scenarios with more data or higher ranks.

**Red flags:**
- The steer datasets are very small (N ranges from 32 to 200 across tasks, per Table 1), which limits the generalizability of claims about matching LoRA/ReFT performance.
- The comparison excludes JailbreakBench from the main average (Table 3), which could be seen as cherry-picking, though the authors provide justification.
- The functional equivalence to rank-1 ReFT raises the question of whether the advantage comes from the decoupled training procedure or from using RFM probes specifically. The ablation in Appendix C.2 partially addresses this but could be more direct.
- The paper does not compare against more recent activation steering methods that also attempt adaptive steering, such as ActAdd with learned gates or other dynamic approaches.

## Strengths

- Clean, well-motivated formulation that directly addresses a known limitation of activation steering
- Thorough evaluation across many tasks, models, and experimental settings
- Strong interpretability story: RFM directions enable accurate concept monitoring unlike ReFT/LoRA directions
- Computationally efficient: backprop-free steering vector extraction + fast sensing vector training
- Public code release for reproducibility
- Two-stage decoupling is elegant and allows flexible reuse of steering vectors with different sensing vectors

## Limitations

- Only evaluated with rank-1 adapters; the scalability to higher-rank settings or more complex interventions is unclear
- The steer datasets are very small (32-200 samples), and it is unknown how CLAS scales with more training data relative to LoRA/ReFT
- The linear sensing function (c . [h, 1]) may be too simple for some contexts; nonlinear context modulation could potentially yield further gains
- The paper does not address multi-concept or multi-task steering simultaneously (only single-task steering is evaluated)
- While conceptually general, the evaluation is limited to autoregressive transformer LLMs; applicability to other architectures is untested
- The reliance on RFM probes requires a separate binary-labeled dataset, which adds a data requirement beyond what LoRA/ReFT need

## Relevance

本文与LLM的可控性和机制可解释性研究密切相关。CLAS提出的基于上下文自适应的激活引导方法，为如何在推理时精确控制大模型行为提供了一种兼具可解释性和高效性的新方案，特别适合对模型行为控制、安全对齐和表示工程感兴趣的研究者。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does CLAS perform when steering toward multiple concepts simultaneously, and can sensing vectors be composed?
- What happens when the steer dataset size is scaled up significantly (e.g., 1000+ samples) -- does CLAS maintain its advantage over ReFT/LoRA?
- Can the linear sensing function be replaced with a more expressive (e.g., MLP-based) context modulation while preserving interpretability?
- How stable are CLAS sensing vectors across different random seeds, and what is the variance in steering outcomes?
- Could CLAS be applied during training (rather than just inference) to induce more persistent behavioral changes?
- How does CLAS interact with other inference-time interventions such as prompting strategies or constrained decoding?
