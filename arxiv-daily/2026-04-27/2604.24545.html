Extreme Bandits

arXiv: 2604.24545v1

Date: 2026-04-27

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.24545v1

一句话总结： 本文提出EXTREMEHUNTER算法，在半参数（二阶Pareto）假设下解决极端bandit问题——即在有限反馈条件下寻找尾部最重的臂以获得最大极端值，并给出有限时间极端遗憾上界，其遗憾相对于oracle最优奖励渐近可忽略。
TL;DR: This paper introduces EXTREMEHUNTER, an optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty bandit algorithm that identifies the arm with the heaviest tail under a semi-parametric second-order Pareto assumption. Unlike prior work that requires fully parametric distributional assumptions, this approach provides a finite-time extreme regret bound showing the regret is o(1) relative to the optimal oracle reward. Experiments on synthetic (exact and approximate Pareto) and real-world network traffic data confirm it outperforms both THRESHOLDASCENT and classical UCB.

核心贡献

  • 提出极端bandit（extreme bandits）问题框架： 区别于经典bandit最大化累积奖励，该框架关注如何在有限反馈下找到产生最大极端值的源（即尾部最重的臂）。
  • 提出EXTREMEHUNTER算法： 基于乐观主义（OFU）原则，通过估计每个臂的尾部参数（alpha_k, C_k）及其置信上界来选择臂，利用极值理论中的经典估计器。
  • 半参数假设（二阶Pareto）： 仅要求分布满足二阶Pareto条件（Hall条件），远弱于先前工作要求的完全参数化假设（精确Gumbel或Frechet分布）。
  • 有限时间遗憾界（Theorem 2）： 证明极端遗憾上界为 E[R_n] <= L(nC)^{1/alpha} (K/n log(n)^{(2b+1)/b} + n^{-log(n)(1-1/alpha)} + n^{-b/((1+b)alpha)})，相对于oracle奖励E[G_n] ~ n^{1/alpha}渐近可忽略。
  • 实验验证： 在合成数据（精确Pareto、近似Pareto）和真实网络流量数据上均优于THRESHOLDASCENT和UCB基线方法。

Key Contributions

  • Formalization of the extreme bandit setting: Defines extreme regret as E[G_n*] - E[G_n^pi], where the oracle selects the arm with the heaviest tail rather than the highest mean, targeting applications in anomaly detection, network intrusion, and rare event discovery.
  • EXTREMEHUNTER algorithm: An optimism-based strategy that constructs upper confidence bounds on E[max_i X_i] for each arm by estimating the tail index alpha_k and scale parameter C_k, then pulls the arm with the highest index.
  • Weaker distributional assumptions: Uses the second-order Pareto (Hall) condition -- |1 - P(x) - Cx^{-alpha}| <= C'x^{-alpha(1+beta)} -- instead of requiring exact parametric forms, which is standard in extreme value theory and significantly more practical.
  • Finite-time extreme regret bound (Theorem 2): The regret is o(E[G_n*]), specifically dominated by (K/n)log(n)^{(2b+1)/b} + super-polynomially decaying terms, where b is a lower bound on the second-order parameter beta.
  • Theorem 1 (oracle benchmark): Characterizes E[max X_i] for second-order Pareto samples as approximately (nC)^{1/alpha} * Gamma(1 - 1/alpha), providing the baseline for regret analysis.
  • Empirical superiority: Demonstrated on exact Pareto, approximate Pareto (mixed with Dirac), and enterprise network traffic data, consistently outperforming THRESHOLDASCENT and UCB.

Methodology

The paper operates at the intersection of multi-armed bandits and extreme value theory. The core setup involves K arms, each emitting samples from a second-order Pareto distribution P_k with tail index alpha_k and scale C_k. The learner selects one arm per round and observes only that arm's sample, with the goal of maximizing the most extreme value observed.

Algorithm design (EXTREMEHUNTER):

  1. Initialization phase: Pull each arm at least N times (where N is chosen to ensure reliable tail estimation).
  2. Estimation: For each arm k, estimate the tail index alpha_k using the Hill-type estimator (with convergence rate O(T_{k,t}^{-b/(2b+1)}) via Equation 2) and the scale C_k (Equation 3), along with associated confidence widths.
  3. Optimistic selection: Compute an upper confidence bound B_{k,t} on E[max_{i<=n} X_{k,i}] for each arm (Equation 5) and pull the arm maximizing B_{k,t}.

Theoretical analysis proceeds in four steps:

  • Step 1: Establish concentration inequalities for the alpha and C estimators on a high-probability event xi.
  • Step 2: Show that on xi, the index B_{k,t} upper-bounds the true expected maximum (Lemma 1), ensuring the optimal arm is pulled sufficiently often.
  • Step 3: Count the number of pulls of the optimal arm, showing T* >= (2/3)n.
  • Step 4: Use Lemma 2 to relate the truncated maximum to the full expectation and combine with Theorem 1 to obtain the final regret bound.

The key technical insight is that identifying the heaviest tail requires estimating extreme value parameters (alpha, C) rather than means, and the convergence rate of these estimators under the second-order Pareto assumption governs the regret.

Results & Highlights

  • Synthetic -- Exact Pareto (K=3, alpha=[5, 1.1, 2]): All methods perform reasonably since the heaviest-tailed arm also has the highest mean. UCB detects the best arm slightly faster. THRESHOLDASCENT sometimes fails to allocate pulls properly within 10^4 steps.
  • Synthetic -- Approximate Pareto (K=3, mixture with Dirac): The most heavy-tailed arm (alpha=1.1, 0.2 mixture weight) does NOT have the highest mean. EXTREMEHUNTER clearly outperforms both UCB (which focuses on the wrong arm) and THRESHOLDASCENT.
  • Real-world -- Network traffic data (K=5 enterprise laptops): EXTREMEHUNTER outperforms both baselines, demonstrating practical utility for detecting extreme network activity bursts.
  • Theoretical rate: The regret bound is dominated by (K/n) log(n)^{(2b+1)/b} for large n, making it negligible compared to the oracle reward ~ n^{1/alpha}. The super-polynomial term n^{-log(n)(1-1/alpha*)} decays extremely fast.

Critical Assessment

Novelty (High): The paper is the first to address the extreme bandit problem under semi-parametric assumptions with finite-time guarantees. Prior work (Carpentier & Valko references [9,21]) assumed fully parametric distributions. The combination of extreme value theory with bandit optimism is original and non-trivial.

Rigor (High): The theoretical analysis is thorough, with a complete proof in the appendix spanning multiple lemmas. Theorem 2 provides explicit constants and all assumptions are clearly stated. The four-step proof structure (concentration, index validity, pull count, expectation bound) is well-organized.

Reproducibility (Moderate): The algorithm is clearly specified (Algorithm 1), and the experiments use standard settings. However, the network traffic dataset is from Intel (private), and the exact implementation details of the Hill estimator and confidence bounds could benefit from pseudocode. No code is referenced.

Significance (High): This paper launched a sub-area of bandit research. The extreme regret formulation is relevant to many practical applications (network intrusion detection, rare disease screening, financial risk). The semi-parametric approach is significantly more realistic than prior parametric work.

Red flags / Concerns:

  • The assumption that a lower bound b on beta_k is known a priori is acknowledged as necessary (Remark 2) but may be hard to set in practice. Setting b=1 as in experiments is a heuristic.
  • The minimum pull requirement N depends on problem-dependent constants (alpha_k, C_k, C_k') that are typically unknown, creating a circular dependency in practice.
  • The experiments are relatively limited (3 settings, one baseline from the same problem family). No comparison with more recent heavy-tailed bandit methods.
  • The regret bound, while finite-time, has a leading constant L that depends on all arm parameters, making the rate potentially loose for poorly separated arms.

Strengths

  • First finite-time analysis for extreme bandits under semi-parametric assumptions, a significant theoretical advance over asymptotic parametric results.
  • Clean problem formulation that opens a new research direction at the intersection of extreme value theory and sequential decision making.
  • The algorithm is principled and intuitive -- optimism with respect to tail heaviness rather than mean reward.
  • The approximate Pareto experiment clearly demonstrates the advantage over mean-focused methods (UCB) when heavy tails and high means diverge.
  • The paper is well-written with clear notation and a logical proof structure.

Limitations

  • Requires knowledge of b (lower bound on second-order parameter beta_k), which is an untestable assumption in practice.
  • The minimum exploration parameter N depends on unknown distributional constants, requiring conservative over-estimation.
  • No lower bound on extreme regret is provided, so it is unknown whether the obtained rate is minimax optimal.
  • The experiments are small-scale (K=3 or K=5 arms); scalability to many arms is untested.
  • No discussion of the case where alpha <= 1 (infinite mean), which is relevant for many heavy-tailed phenomena.
  • The THRESHOLDASCENT baseline lacks theoretical analysis for extreme bands, making the empirical comparison somewhat informal.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • What is the minimax lower bound for extreme regret under the second-order Pareto assumption? Is EXTREMEHUNTER's rate optimal?
  • Can the assumption of knowing b be removed or relaxed, e.g., through adaptive estimation of beta_k?
  • How does the algorithm scale when K is large (hundreds or thousands of arms)? Does the (K/n) factor in the regret dominate?
  • Can the approach be extended to the non-stationary (restless) bandit setting, where tail parameters may change over time?
  • What happens when alpha_k < 1 (infinite mean regime)? The current analysis requires alpha > 1 for Theorem 1 to apply via Gamma(1 - 1/alpha).
  • Are there connections between extreme regret and best-arm identification with fixed confidence, and could a pure exploration variant be more sample-efficient?
