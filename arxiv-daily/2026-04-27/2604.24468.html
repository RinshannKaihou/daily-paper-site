A Survey on Split Learning for LLM Fine-Tuning: Models, Systems, and Privacy Optimizations

arXiv: 2604.24468v1

Date: 2026-04-27

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.24468v1

一句话总结： 本文是首篇专门聚焦于分割学习（Split Learning）与LLM微调交叉领域的系统性综述，提出了一个统一的细粒度训练流水线，从模型级优化（数据异构性、更新不平衡、灾难性遗忘）、系统级效率（通信压缩、计算调度、资源分配）和隐私安全（推理攻击与防御机制）三个核心维度对现有工作进行了全面分类和评述。
TL;DR: This is the first comprehensive survey dedicated to the intersection of split learning and LLM fine-tuning. The authors propose a unified, fine-grained 11-step training pipeline that decomposes the split-based LLM-FT workflow, then systematically review 100+ methods across three dimensions: model-level optimization (handling Non-IID data, server-client update imbalance, catastrophic forgetting), system-level efficiency (split point selection, PEFT integration, communication compression, computation-communication overlap), and privacy/security (smashed data inversion, gradient-based label reconstruction, backdoor attacks, and corresponding defenses including perturbation-based, learning-based, and detection-based mechanisms). The survey covers both methods specifically validated on LLMs and those from conventional SL with theoretical transferability, providing a structured taxonomy anchored to specific pipeline components.

  • 建立了多维度的瓶颈映射框架： 将模型级（数据异构、更新不平衡、灾难性遗忘）、系统级（通信开销、资源利用不平衡、同步延迟）和隐私安全（中间表示泄漏、恶意操纵）三大类瓶颈精确定位到流水线的具体执行步骤。
  • 首次系统梳理了面向LLM的分割学习隐私威胁： 特别强调了因果语言建模中标签即输入序列左移这一特性，使得标签泄漏等价于原始文本重建，揭示了LLM场景下独特且更严重的隐私风险。

  • 填补了现有综述的三个关键空白： 现有分割学习综述主要针对传统小规模模型、缺乏统一框架、未将多维挑战映射到LLM微调的具体执行阶段。

Key Contributions

  • Unified split-based LLM-FT pipeline: Proposes an 11-step fine-grained training pipeline that explicitly decomposes the computational and communication steps, marking steps specific to U-shaped SL (dagger) and SFL (hash) variants, providing a structural foundation for component-level bottleneck analysis.
  • Multi-dimensional bottleneck mapping: Maps model-level (Non-IID data heterogeneity, server-client update imbalance, catastrophic forgetting), system-level (communication overhead, resource utilization imbalance, synchronization delays), and privacy/security vulnerabilities directly to specific pipeline execution steps, establishing a structured taxonomy.
  • First systematic review of LLM-specific privacy threats in SL: Highlights the unique vulnerability of autoregressive LLMs where training labels (shifted input sequences) make label leakage mathematically equivalent to exposing raw proprietary text, a risk absent in conventional classification tasks.
  • Comprehensive attack-defense taxonomy: Covers smashed data inversion attacks (active FSHA/SIA/SplitSpy; passive UnSplit/GLASS/PCAT/MAERA), gradient-based label reconstruction (metric-based, optimization-based, learning-based), bidirectional reconstruction (BiSR), membership/property inference, data poisoning, backdoor attacks, and defenses (DP-based perturbation, learning-based, detection-based, split unlearning, homomorphic encryption).
  • Fills three critical gaps in existing surveys: Existing SL surveys focus on traditional small-scale models, lack a unified component-level framework, and do not systematically map multidimensional challenges to specific LLM adaptation execution stages.

Methodology

The survey employs a structured, pipeline-driven review methodology:

  1. Pipeline abstraction: The authors first establish a unified training pipeline (Steps 1-11) that covers all split-based LLM-FT variants. Steps 1-4 and 9-10 form the core workflow; Steps 5-8 are exclusive to U-shaped SL; Step 11 is exclusive to SFL-based frameworks.
  1. Model partitioning strategies: Two primary paradigms are analyzed -- Vanilla SL (two-part split: head + trunk) and U-shaped SL (three-part split: head + trunk + tail). U-shaped SL provides stronger label privacy at the cost of doubled communication volume.
  1. Framework variants: Four main architectures are catalogued -- Sequential SL, Parallel SL (PSL), Split Federated Learning (SFL with FedAvg-based aggregation), and Group-based SFL (GSFL with client clustering). Additional variants include RingSFL, HSFL (hierarchical), PairingFL, and CLICOOPER (serverless).
  1. Three-axis taxonomy:
  • Model-level optimization (Section 3): Addresses data heterogeneity via personalization/generalization decoupling (SplitGP, DualFed, SFML), smashed data aggregation/scheduling (MergeSFL, SCALA, Hourglass), and client clustering/pairing (ParallelSFL, PairingFL, GSFL). Addresses update imbalance via smashed data mixup and learning rate scaling. Addresses catastrophic forgetting via knowledge retention (SLwF, MultiSFL).
  • System-level optimization (Section 4): Covers split point selection, PEFT integration (SplitLoRA, HSplitLoRA, Menos), server-side memory management and scheduling, communication overhead reduction (top-k compression, dimensionality reduction, quantization, communication frequency reduction, gradient aggregation), model aggregation strategies (synchronous, asynchronous, semi-asynchronous), server-client collaborative optimization (asynchronous training, pipeline parallelism), and global joint optimization.
  • Privacy and security (Section 5): Systematically categorizes attacks (smashed data inversion, gradient-based label reconstruction, bidirectional reconstruction, membership inference, property inference, data poisoning, backdoor attacks) and defenses (perturbation-based with token-level and representation-level DP, learning-based defenses like NoPeek/DualGuard, detection-based defenses like SplitGuard/SafeSplit, and split unlearning).
  1. LLM-specific analysis: Throughout the survey, methods validated on LLMs are marked with an asterisk (*), and the authors provide theoretical applicability assessments (NLU vs. NLG) for methods not yet empirically tested on LLMs.

Results & Highlights

  • This is the first survey exclusively focused on split learning for LLM fine-tuning, filling a gap left by existing SL surveys that primarily cover traditional models.
  • The survey catalogs over 100 methods across the three dimensions, organized into detailed comparison tables (Tables 2-4) that include target challenges, approaches, LLM experimental validation status, and applicability assessments.
  • Key LLM-specific findings include:
  • SplitLoRA is identified as the first SFL framework specifically designed for LLM fine-tuning, integrating LoRA with split federated learning.
  • HSplitLoRA dynamically adapts split points and LoRA ranks to individual client budgets for heterogeneous environments.
  • Menos addresses server-side GPU memory overload via spatial sharing of frozen base model parameters across clients.
  • BiSR (Bidirectional Semi-white-box Reconstruction) is highlighted as a pioneering attack that couples forward inversion with backward gradient matching, exploiting the "not-too-far" assumption of fine-tuned vs. pre-trained weights.
  • DualGuard is noted as a bidirectional defense using parameter space transformation to defend against both forward and backward reconstruction attacks.
  • Transformer blocks maintain constant hidden dimensions, meaning shifting the split point does not alter communication payload size -- a critical distinction from CNN-based SL.
  • Smashed data concatenation strategies (SCALA, MergeSFL) face severe GPU memory bottlenecks when applied to native LLMs due to massive activation sizes.
  • Mixup-based approaches (LocFedMix-SL, Mix2SFL) are fundamentally incompatible with NLG tasks as interpolating variable-length hidden states destroys semantic integrity.
  • The survey identifies 17 global joint optimization methods (Table 3) that jointly optimize split point, batch size, update frequency, and communication/computation resources, with several (PASFL, SFL-LLM, CARD, SFT-LLM) specifically validated on LLMs.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The survey's primary novelty lies in its unified pipeline framework and its exclusive focus on the SL + LLM intersection. While the individual techniques surveyed are not new, the pipeline-driven taxonomy that maps bottlenecks to specific pipeline steps is a genuinely useful organizational contribution. However, the paper does not propose new methods or provide empirical comparisons beyond cataloguing existing work.
  • Rigor: The taxonomy is systematic and well-structured. The comparison tables are informative and include LLM validation status. However, several limitations exist: (1) Many "model-level optimization" methods (Table 2) have NOT been empirically validated on LLMs, with only FlexP-SFL and ParallelSFL having LLM experimental validation among the data heterogeneity methods; (2) The theoretical applicability assessments (NLU vs. NLG) are projections rather than empirical findings, which weakens their reliability; (3) There is no quantitative meta-analysis or experimental reproduction of the surveyed methods.
  • Reproducibility: As a survey, reproducibility is not directly applicable. However, the detailed tables and pipeline mapping provide a solid foundation for researchers to implement and compare methods. The lack of a unified codebase or benchmark is a missed opportunity.
  • Significance: The survey addresses a timely and important topic. As LLM fine-tuning becomes more widespread, privacy-preserving and resource-efficient approaches like split learning are increasingly relevant. The identification of LLM-specific challenges (e.g., label=shifted input, constant hidden dimensions, massive activation sizes) provides genuine insight.
  • Red flags:
  • The conference metadata in the paper template (Woodstock 2018) appears to be leftover boilerplate, suggesting the paper may still be in preparation for a specific venue submission.
  • Several foundational model-level optimization methods lack LLM empirical validation, and the survey's "applicability" column is entirely theoretical projection.
  • The privacy section, while comprehensive, does not provide quantitative comparisons of defense overhead or privacy-utility trade-offs, making it difficult to assess which defenses are most practical for LLM deployment.

Strengths

  • Comprehensive scope: Covers over 100 methods across model, system, and privacy dimensions with detailed technical descriptions.
  • Unified pipeline framework: The 11-step pipeline provides a clear structural foundation that makes the survey highly navigable and the taxonomy easy to follow.
  • LLM-specific insights: Identifies critical LLM-specific challenges that are absent in prior SL surveys, such as the semantic equivalence of labels and inputs in CLM, the memory implications of massive activations, and the inapplicability of certain techniques (e.g., mixup) to generation tasks.
  • Well-organized tables: Tables 2-4 provide useful at-a-glance comparisons including year, LLM validation status, and applicability assessments.
  • Timely and relevant: Addresses a rapidly growing research area at the intersection of distributed AI and privacy-preserving ML.
  • Clear identification of LLM-validated vs. transferable methods: The asterisk notation and applicability columns help readers distinguish between empirically grounded and theoretically projected contributions.

Limitations

  • Lack of empirical evaluation: The survey does not include any experimental comparison of the surveyed methods, even a simple benchmark on a common LLM fine-tuning task would significantly strengthen the contribution.
  • Many methods unvalidated on LLMs: The majority of model-level optimization methods in Table 2 have not been tested on LLMs, and their projected applicability is speculative.
  • No quantitative privacy-utility trade-off analysis: The privacy section catalogs attacks and defenses but does not provide numerical comparisons of defense overhead or effectiveness, making it hard to assess practical deployability.
  • Missing coverage of some emerging areas: The survey does not deeply cover split learning for multimodal LLMs, RLHF/DPO in split settings, or the intersection with recent efficiency techniques like quantization-aware training and speculative decoding.
  • Limited discussion of real-world deployment: There is minimal discussion of actual system implementations, deployment case studies, or the gap between research prototypes and production-ready split learning systems for LLMs.
  • Potential self-citation bias: The authors' own work (DualGuard, BiSR) features prominently in the privacy section, which is expected but should be noted.
