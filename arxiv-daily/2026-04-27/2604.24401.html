All That Glitters Is Not Audio: Rethinking Text Priors and Audio Reliance in Audio-Language Evaluation

arXiv: 2604.24401v1

Date: 2026-04-27

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.24401v1

一句话总结： 本文提出双轴诊断框架（文本先验+音频依赖），揭示当前主流音频-语言基准测试（MMAU/MMAR/MMAU-Pro）中模型在无音频输入下仍能保留60-72%的完整分数，且仅需局部音频片段即可解决96%的音频依赖题目，表明现有基准并未真正评估整体音频理解能力。
TL;DR: This paper proposes a two-axis diagnostic framework (text prior and audio reliance) to audit audio-language benchmarks, revealing that 8 LALMs retain 60-72% of full-audio accuracy without any audio input, and 96% of audio-dependent items can be solved from short local fragments rather than requiring the full clip -- challenging the assumption that benchmark scores reflect genuine auditory understanding.

Key Contributions

  • Introduces two diagnostic axes -- text prior (measuring answerability from text alone) and audio reliance (measuring actual acoustic signal dependency) -- for auditing audio-language benchmarks
  • Conducts a large-scale evaluation of 8 LALMs across 3 benchmarks (MMAU, MMAR, MMAU-Pro), demonstrating that models retain 60-72% of full-audio accuracy without any audio input
  • Performs temporal granularity analysis by partitioning audio into equal-duration segments (N=2 to 5), showing that only 3.0-4.2% of audio-needed items require cross-segment information
  • Proposes a five-category item decomposition (Text-Solvable, Fragment-Sufficient, Cross-Segment, Audio-Harmful, Unsolvable) for fine-grained analysis of benchmark composition
  • Provides practical guidelines for benchmark designers and model developers to ensure evaluations genuinely measure auditory understanding

Methodology

The paper introduces a diagnostic framework with two complementary axes:

Text Prior (Section 2.1): Three controlled evaluation settings are compared: (1) Text Backbone (TB) -- the original LLM backbone evaluated on text-only questions before multimodal training; (2) None -- the full LALM evaluated without audio input; (3) Full -- standard evaluation with both audio and text. The text-prior rate is defined as R_TP = Acc_none / Acc_full, quantifying the fraction of full-audio performance achievable without audio. Unlike prior work that uses silence as a proxy for no-audio (which introduces confounds), the authors completely omit the audio input.

Audio Reliance (Section 2.2): Each audio clip is partitioned into N equal-duration contiguous segments (N in {2,3,4,5}), and each segment is evaluated independently with the same textual prompt. The retention rate R_N measures how much performance is preserved when only 1/N of the audio is provided. High retention indicates reliance on local cues rather than global context.

Score Decomposition (Section 2.3): Items are categorized into five mutually exclusive types based on correctness across Full, None, and Fragment conditions: Text-Solvable (TS), Fragment-Sufficient (FS), Cross-Segment (XS), Audio-Harmful (AH), and Unsolvable (UN). This decomposition separates text-solvable items from audio-dependent ones and distinguishes fragment-sufficient from full-context-dependent cases.

Eight LALMs are evaluated: Audio-Flamingo-3, DeSTA-2.5, Phi-4-Multimodal, Qwen2-Audio-7B, Qwen2.5-Omni-7B, Qwen3-Omni (Instruct and Thinking modes), and Voxtral-Mini-3B, each paired with its corresponding text backbone. A hybrid MCQ scorer combining regex extraction with an LLM judge (Claude 4.5 Haiku) is used, achieving 97.2% agreement with human annotations versus 26.0% for string-match.

Results & Highlights

  • Strong text prior: Text Backbone accuracy exceeds chance level by 12.4% (MMAU), 5.4% (MMAR), and 3.6% (MMAU-Pro). Qwen-family backbones are particularly strong, with Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct reaching 50.8% on MMAU (chance = 25%).
  • Multimodal training amplifies text prior: For most models, None accuracy exceeds TB accuracy, suggesting multimodal training strengthens rather than reduces text-based shortcut behavior.
  • High text-prior rate: Average R_TP across models is 65.1% (MMAU), 60.5% (MMAR), and 72.1% (MMAU-Pro). Audio-Flamingo-3 reaches 81.2% and 83.7% on MMAU and MMAU-Pro respectively.
  • Fragment sufficiency: Among audio-needed items, only 3.0-4.2% require full-context audio (Cross-Segment category). Nearly 96% of audio-dependent items can be solved from at least one local fragment.
  • Task-level variation: Instruction-following tasks show the largest audio dependency (36.9% Full-None gap), while open-ended items in MMAU-Pro perform slightly better without audio (-1.9%), indicating audio can act as a distractor when strong text priors exist.
  • Speech is most audio-dependent: Among MCQ domains, speech tasks show 24.7-27.7% Full-None gaps, while sound and music retain over 93% of full accuracy with N=2 fragments.

Critical Assessment

Novelty (Moderate-High): The idea of probing benchmark grounding via text-only baselines is inspired by similar work in NLI (hypothesis-only baselines) and VQA (question-only baselines), but applying it systematically to the audio-language domain with the dual-axis framework and temporal segmentation analysis is novel and well-executed. The item-level decomposition into five categories adds analytical granularity.

Rigor (High): The evaluation is thorough -- 8 models, 3 benchmarks, multiple segmentation levels (N=2 to 5), and a carefully validated hybrid scorer (97.2% human agreement). The critique of silence-based text-only proxies is methodologically sound. The text-prior rate metric is simple but effective.

Reproducibility (Moderate-High): The methodology is clearly described with specific model names, evaluation protocols, and scorer details. However, the fragment partitioning implementation details and exact prompt templates are not provided in the main text. The authors use proprietary LLM judges (Claude 4.5 Haiku) which introduces a dependency. No code repository is mentioned.

Significance (High): This paper addresses a fundamental issue in audio-language evaluation that the community has largely overlooked. The finding that 60-72% of benchmark performance is achievable without audio is striking and has direct implications for how we interpret LALM evaluation results and design future benchmarks.

Red flags: The paper evaluates only MCQ-heavy benchmarks; the findings may not generalize as strongly to generative or open-ended audio tasks. The MMAU-Pro open-ended results showing audio as harmful (-1.9%) are intriguing but underexplored. The framework focuses on "what information is needed" but does not deeply investigate "how models internally process" the audio signal.

Strengths

  • Clean, intuitive two-axis framework that is easy to adopt by other researchers
  • Comprehensive empirical coverage across 8 models and 3 benchmarks
  • Strong empirical findings with striking headline numbers (60-72% text-prior rate, 3-4.2% cross-segment dependency)
  • Well-motivated critique of silence-based text-only proxies, with a cleaner methodology
  • Practical guidelines section provides actionable recommendations for the community
  • Transparent generative AI disclosure

Limitations

  • Focuses on three MCQ-dominated benchmarks; the extent of text prior in more open-ended or generative audio tasks remains unclear
  • Does not investigate internal model mechanisms -- we know models do not need audio, but not precisely what textual shortcuts they exploit
  • The fragment partitioning approach assumes temporal contiguity is the relevant axis; models might exploit spectral features, speaker identity, or other non-temporal cues from short fragments
  • Does not propose concrete new benchmark items or datasets that would pass the text-prior and audio-reliance tests
  • No investigation into whether the findings correlate with model size, architecture, or training data composition
  • The Audio-Harmful category (where audio hurts performance) deserves deeper investigation -- it suggests potential issues with audio integration in LALMs

Relevance

本文对多模态评估方法论提出了重要警示，揭示了当前音频-语言基准测试中普遍存在的"文本捷径"问题。对于从事多模态模型评估、基准测试设计或音频理解研究的研究者而言，本文提出的诊断框架和实践指南具有直接的参考价值，尤其适用于评估新模型的真实多模态理解能力。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Can the diagnostic framework be extended to video-language benchmarks, where temporal grounding is even more critical?
  • What specific data curation strategies could reduce text prior in benchmark construction (e.g., adversarial filtering, paired question design)?
  • Does the Audio-Harmful category point to fundamental issues in how LALMs integrate audio representations with language models?
  • How would instruction-tuning and reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) affect text-prior rates in audio-language models?
  • Could probing the internal representations of LALMs reveal whether models genuinely attend to audio features even when text priors suffice?
