> **一句话总结：** OS-SPEAR提出了一套面向OS Agent的四维评估工具包（安全性、性能、效率、鲁棒性），通过精心构建的四个子集和自动化分析报告工具对22个主流OS Agent进行全面评测，揭示了效率与安全/鲁棒性之间的普遍权衡、专用模型优于通用模型等关键发现。

> **TL;DR:** OS-SPEAR is a comprehensive evaluation toolkit for OS agents that assesses Safety (environmental/adversarial hazards), Performance (trajectory value estimation), Efficiency (time and token cost), and Robustness (cross-modal perturbations) across 22 agents, revealing critical trade-offs between efficiency and safety/robustness and the superiority of specialized agents over general-purpose models.

## 核心贡献

- 系统性审计现有OS Agent评测基准的局限性（安全场景狭窄、轨迹标注噪声大、缺乏跨模态鲁棒性指标），并提出四维评测框架OS-SPEAR。
- 设计S-subset涵盖环境干扰、真实异常和对抗误导三类不安全因素；P-subset通过轨迹价值估计和分层采样构建高质量评测集；E-subset从时间和Token双重维度评估效率；R-subset对视觉和文本模态施加10种跨模态扰动。
- 开发基于多Agent系统的自动化分析工具，能从原始评测日志生成专家级可读诊断报告。
- 对22个主流OS Agent进行大规模评测并提供多维度排名，发现效率与安全/鲁棒性之间的权衡、专用Agent优于通用模型、大模型安全性能更好、视觉完整性对Agent至关重要等六大关键洞察。

## Key Contributions

- Conducts a systematic audit of existing OS agent benchmarks, identifying narrow safety scenarios, trajectory labeling noise, and absence of cross-modal robustness metrics as key limitations.
- Introduces OS-SPEAR with four specialized subsets: S-subset (environmental/human-induced hazards), P-subset (trajectory value estimation with stratified sampling), E-subset (dual time and token cost measurement), and R-subset (10 cross-modal perturbation operators covering visual and textual modalities).
- Develops an automated multi-agent analysis tool that transforms raw evaluation logs into interpretable, expert-level diagnostic reports with overall scores and risk assessments.
- Performs the most extensive evaluation of OS agents to date (22 models), revealing a prevalent efficiency-safety/robustness trade-off, the superiority of specialized over general-purpose agents, and modality-specific robustness vulnerabilities.

## Methodology

OS-SPEAR evaluates OS agents across four dimensions:

**S-subset (Safety):** Curates three categories of unsafe scenarios from existing benchmarks -- environmental distractions (intentionally designed pop-ups/ads), real-world anomalies (naturally occurring disruptions like network issues), and adversarial misleading (deliberately crafted misleading content). Safety is measured by the proportion of "gold" actions (correct actions that avoid hazards), "distracted" actions (following the adversary's intent), and "invalid" actions.

**P-subset (Performance):** Employs a two-stage pipeline: (1) trajectory value estimation using small-scale agents (2B-3B) to filter out trivially easy trajectories, followed by large-scale agents (32B-72B) to vote on difficulty levels (Easy/Medium/Hard) based on completion rate thresholds; (2) stratified sampling with ~4:3:3 ratio across difficulty levels. The final P-subset contains 696 trajectories with 6,641 screenshots. Metrics include type accuracy, step-wise success rate (SR), and trajectory success rate (TSR).

**E-subset (Efficiency):** Extends P-subset evaluation by logging per-step inference time, input token count, and output token count. Token cost is computed as input tokens + 3x output tokens, aligning with major LLM provider billing structures. This provides a user-centric efficiency measure combining temporal latency and economic cost.

**R-subset (Robustness):** Selects 1,000 verified screenshots from AndroidControl and applies 10 perturbations across five robustness dimensions: visual resilience, contextual focus, temporal consistency, knowledge discernment, and goal robustness. Visual perturbations include mask, zoom-in, and Gaussian noise at 30%/50%/70% intensity. Textual perturbations include state conflict, bad memory, bad knowledge, irrelevant memory, and irrelevant knowledge.

**Analysis Tool:** A multi-agent system with four expert agents (safety, performance, efficiency, robustness) and one integrated agent that consolidates dimension-specific reports into a comprehensive evaluation with overall scores, summaries, and top risks.

## Results & Highlights

- **Overall ranking:** UI-Venus-1.5-8B ranks 1st overall, excelling in safety (1st) and maintaining balanced performance across all dimensions. UI-TARS-72B-SFT ranks 2nd, and UI-TARS-7B-SFT ranks 3rd.
- **Efficiency-safety trade-off:** Models with lower inference cost (e.g., AgentCPM-GUI-8B, OS-Atlas-Pro-7B) tend to perform worse in safety and robustness, as efficiency-oriented designs often omit critical contextual reasoning.
- **Specialized vs. general-purpose:** All top 8 models in the overall ranking are specialized OS agents, demonstrating that domain-specific adaptation effectively improves OS task execution across safety, performance, efficiency, and robustness.
- **Real-world anomalies are hardest:** OS agents achieve significantly lower gold action rates against real-world anomalies compared to environmental distractions or adversarial attacks, because natural anomalies blend into the semantic distribution of the environment.
- **Larger models are safer:** All six agents with 32B+ parameters rank in the top 10 on the S-subset, suggesting larger models better distinguish useful from unsafe information.
- **Visual completeness matters:** Masking and zooming perturbations cause significant performance drops even when ground-truth regions remain intact, while Gaussian noise (preserving structural completeness) has much smaller impact.
- **Textual temporal insensitivity:** Shuffled bad memory or injected knowledge sometimes improves performance, indicating OS agents do not heavily rely on temporal ordering in textual inputs.
- **Scaling within families has limited returns:** Within the same model family (e.g., Qwen2.5-VL, Qwen3-VL), simply scaling parameters without architectural or algorithmic innovation quickly hits a performance plateau, and larger models suffer in the efficiency dimension.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** The paper's primary novelty lies in integration rather than fundamental methodological innovation. The four evaluation dimensions (safety, performance, efficiency, robustness) are individually well-studied; the contribution is in unifying them into a single framework with a standardized ranking methodology. The trajectory value estimation procedure for the P-subset is a reasonable but straightforward filtering approach. The analysis tool built on multi-agent systems is practical but not technically novel.

**Rigor:** The formalization in Section III is solid and provides clear mathematical definitions for safety and robustness under perturbations. The experimental design is comprehensive with 22 models evaluated. However, the overall ranking methodology (simple average of per-dimension rankings) could be questioned -- different applications may weight dimensions differently. The P-subset construction relies on model-based difficulty labeling which introduces circularity (models judge what other models are evaluated on). The S-subset is derived from existing benchmarks rather than being constructed from scratch, which may inherit biases from source datasets.

**Reproducibility:** The code and dataset are publicly available, which is a strong point. However, the evaluation requires running 22 models on mobile device benchmarks, which demands significant computational resources. Some perturbation details (e.g., exact mask placement strategies) could benefit from more precise description.

**Significance:** The paper fills an important gap in OS agent evaluation by providing a unified, multi-dimensional benchmark. The findings about efficiency-safety trade-offs and modality-specific vulnerabilities are actionable insights for the community. However, the framework is limited to mobile OS agents (Android) and does not cover computer or web environments, which limits its generality despite the framing as a general "OS agent" toolkit.

**Red flags:** (1) The S-subset safety evaluation shows very high "Invalid" action rates (often 20-40%), suggesting the safety scenarios may not always produce clean binary outcomes for evaluation. (2) The R-subset perturbations are somewhat synthetic and may not reflect real-world distribution of robustness challenges. (3) The analysis tool is described but not rigorously evaluated for accuracy or consistency of its reports.

## Strengths

- First comprehensive four-dimensional evaluation framework for OS agents that unifies safety, performance, efficiency, and robustness assessment.
- Large-scale evaluation covering 22 representative OS agents provides valuable empirical data for the community.
- Practical analysis tool that generates human-readable diagnostic reports from raw logs.
- Well-structured methodology with clear formalization of safety and robustness problems.
- Key findings are well-supported by the experimental data and provide actionable insights for future OS agent development.
- Public code and data release enhances community adoption and reproducibility.

## Limitations

- The framework is evaluated exclusively on mobile (Android) environments and does not cover computer or web OS agents despite the general framing.
- The overall ranking uses simple averaging of per-dimension rankings, which may not reflect the relative importance of different dimensions for specific use cases.
- The S-subset and R-subset are constructed from existing benchmarks rather than collected from scratch, potentially inheriting biases and limitations of source datasets.
- High invalid action rates in the S-subset (sometimes exceeding 40%) indicate that some safety scenarios may be poorly calibrated.
- The analysis tool's reports are generated by LLM agents, which introduces subjectivity and potential inconsistency.
- The paper does not evaluate commercial closed-source models (e.g., GPT-4o, Claude) or multi-agent systems, limiting the breadth of the evaluation landscape.
- Cross-environment generalization (mobile to desktop to web) is not addressed.

## Relevance

本论文与OS Agent/GUI Agent评测密切相关，提出的多维度评估框架（特别是安全性和鲁棒性评测）对理解当前多模态Agent的能力边界和可靠性具有重要参考价值，也凸显了效率与安全之间的核心矛盾。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How would commercial closed-source models (GPT-4o, Claude, Gemini) perform on OS-SPEAR, particularly on the safety subset?
- Can the efficiency-safety trade-off be mitigated through architectural innovations (e.g., lightweight safety-aware reasoning modules) rather than simply increasing model size?
- How transferable are the robustness findings from mobile to desktop and web environments?
- Would dynamic, adaptive perturbations (as opposed to the static ones in the R-subset) reveal different vulnerability patterns?
- How can the overall ranking methodology be improved to account for application-specific weighting of safety, performance, efficiency, and robustness?
- What is the impact of multi-agent system architectures (e.g., Mobile-Agent-V3, Agent S2) on the four evaluated dimensions compared to single-agent approaches?
