Rhetorical Questions in LLM Representations: A Linear Probing Study

arXiv: 2604.14128

Date: 2026-04-15

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14128

一句话总结： 本文通过线性探针（linear probes）分析LLM内部表示中的反问句（rhetorical questions），发现反问信号在早期层即出现、由末尾token表示最稳定捕获，且跨数据集可迁移（AUROC 0.7-0.8），但不同数据集训练的探针排名重叠率极低（<0.2），表明反问句由多个线性方向编码而非单一共享方向。
TL;DR: This paper uses linear probes to study how LLMs internally represent rhetorical questions, finding that rhetorical signals emerge early and are most stably captured by last-token representations. While probes transfer across datasets (AUROC 0.7-0.8), probes trained on different data produce divergent rankings with top-overlap below 0.2, revealing that rhetorical questions are encoded along multiple linear directions, not a single shared one.

核心贡献

  • 系统性表征分析： 首次对LLM内部表示中反问句与信息寻求问句的线性可分性进行了系统、数据驱动的分析，覆盖两个社交媒体数据集和多个模型（Qwen3-32B、Llama-3.3-70B等）。
  • 表示选择与层级行为： 发现反问信号在解码器模型中早期层即出现，末尾token表示（last-token representation）比均值池化（mean pooling）更稳定且性能更优。
  • 可迁移性不等于共享表示： 揭示了一个关键分歧——尽管跨数据集探针在AUROC上表现相近（0.7-0.8），但排名一致性极低，Jaccard重叠常低于0.2，证明高区分性能不意味着单一共享表示方向。
  • 异质性定性分析： 通过定性分析表明，不同探针捕获的反问现象存在本质差异：有的关注话语层面的修辞立场（discourse-level rhetorical stance），有的侧重局部句法驱动的疑问行为（syntax-driven interrogative acts）。

Key Contributions

  • Systematic representation analysis: First data-driven study of how rhetorical vs. information-seeking questions are linearly separable in LLM internal representations across two social-media datasets and multiple models (Qwen3-32B, Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct, and others).
  • Representation choice and layer dynamics: Shows that rhetorical signals emerge at early layers in decoder-only models, and that last-token representations provide more stable and higher-performing signals than mean-pooled representations across layers.
  • Transferability does not imply shared representation: Reveals a critical divergence between discriminative performance and representational alignment -- probes with similar AUROC induce substantially different rankings on the same data, with Jaccard overlap at the top and bottom 20% often below 0.2.
  • Heterogeneity through qualitative analysis: Demonstrates that different probes capture qualitatively distinct rhetorical phenomena: some prioritize discourse-level rhetorical stance (extended argumentation), while others emphasize localized, syntax-driven interrogative acts.
  • Causal steering validation: Preliminary steering experiments show that perturbations along learned probing directions induce coherent changes in rhetorical behavior, supporting the claim that identified directions capture genuine rhetorical signal.

Methodology

Datasets: Two social-media rhetorical question datasets with different discourse contexts:

  • RQ (Zhuang & Riloff, 2020): Twitter questions (4,997 instances, 38.9 avg tokens), with prior tweet as context. Question-with-context formulation.
  • SRAQ (Ikumariegbe et al., 2025): Reddit conversation threads (971 instances, 58.5 avg tokens), paragraph formulation.

Representations: Hidden states extracted from pretrained LLMs (primarily Qwen3-32B and Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct). Last-token representations used as primary sequence-level view, compared against mean-pooled representations. Representations projected into PCA space with k=64 dimensions for stable cross-probe comparison.

Linear Probes: Three probe types:

  1. DiffMean -- training-free population-level direction: w = mu_rhetorical - mu_informational.
  2. Logistic regression -- trained linear classifier minimizing cross-entropy.
  3. Hinge-loss (linear SVM) -- trained linear classifier optimizing margin-based objective.

Evaluation Metrics:

  • AUROC for discriminative performance.
  • Spearman's rank correlation for global ranking agreement.
  • Jaccard index for top/bottom percentile overlap.
  • Cosine similarity for directional alignment.

Additional analyses: Cross-dataset transfer (train on one dataset, apply to the other), geometric subspace alignment (Grassmann manifold distance, mean cosine similarity of PCA directions), causal steering experiments (activation addition along diffMean vectors), and encoder model comparison (ModernBERT-large).

Results & Highlights

  • Within-dataset AUROC: Trained probes (hinge/logistic) reach AUROC ~0.85-0.90 on RQ and ~0.80-0.85 on SRAQ for last-token representations. DiffMean achieves lower but above-chance performance.
  • Last-token superiority: Last-token representations outperform mean-pooled representations across layers and models, particularly at deeper layers where AUROC reaches ~0.8 for diffMean.
  • Cross-dataset transfer: Probes trained on one dataset achieve AUROC ~0.7-0.8 on the other, indicating a partially shared rhetorical signal. However, ranking agreement is only moderate (Spearman ~0.5 or lower at deeper layers).
  • Low top-overlap under transfer: Jaccard overlap between top-ranked examples from transferred vs. in-domain probes consistently falls below 0.2 for top-ranked instances, and ~0.3-0.4 for bottom-ranked instances.
  • Directional misalignment: Cosine similarity between probe directions learned on different datasets is typically 0.2-0.4 across layers, confirming non-collinearity.
  • Qualitative divergence: SRAQ-trained probes rank discourse-level argumentative rhetorical questions highest (longer passages, multi-question argumentative scaffolding), while RQ-trained probes favor short, syntax-driven interrogative forms -- even misclassifying some informational questions as highly ranked.
  • Steering validation: Adding diffMean vectors to residual streams during generation increases rhetorical scores monotonically with steering coefficient alpha, reaching scores ~7/10 at alpha=4.5 for responsive layers.
  • Encoder comparison: ModernBERT-large [CLS] representations achieve lower AUROC (peak ~65-70) compared to decoder-only models, suggesting rhetorical information is organized differently in encoder architectures.

Critical Assessment

Novelty (Moderate-High): This is the first study to systematically analyze how rhetorical questions are encoded in LLM internal representations using linear probes. The core insight -- that transferability does not imply a shared representation direction -- is a genuine and important contribution to the mechanistic interpretability literature. The qualitative analysis of probe divergence is particularly novel.

Rigor (High): The experimental design is thorough: multiple probe types, multiple models, two datasets with different characteristics, comprehensive evaluation metrics (AUROC, Spearman, Jaccard, cosine similarity), PCA ablation analysis, causal steering validation, and geometric subspace characterization. The PCA-to-embedding-space mapping for cross-dataset comparison is carefully derived.

Reproducibility (High): Code is publicly available. Datasets are established and publicly accessible. Experimental details (PCA dimensions, tokenizers, train/val/test splits) are clearly specified. Generation hyperparameters for steering experiments are provided.

Significance (Moderate-High): The finding that strong probing performance can mask representational heterogeneity has broad implications for the mechanistic interpretability field. It cautions against over-interpreting single-probe results and suggests that even seemingly simple linguistic properties may be encoded in multi-directional, context-sensitive ways.

Red flags:

  • Limited to two datasets, both from social media (Twitter and Reddit), raising questions about generalizability to other domains (e.g., formal writing, dialogue systems).
  • Linear probes inherently cannot capture nonlinear encoding; the paper acknowledges this but does not attempt to bound how much signal may be missed.
  • The steering experiments are preliminary (400 contexts, single model, single dataset) and serve more as validation than systematic analysis.
  • The SRAQ dataset is relatively small (971 samples), which may affect probe stability.

Strengths

  • Clean and well-motivated research question at the intersection of pragmatics and mechanistic interpretability.
  • The key insight about the divergence between discriminative performance and representational alignment is both non-obvious and carefully demonstrated.
  • Excellent use of multiple complementary metrics (AUROC, rank correlation, Jaccard overlap, cosine similarity) to build a complete picture.
  • Qualitative analysis is thoughtful and provides concrete, interpretable examples of probe divergence.
  • PCA ablation analysis and appendix materials demonstrate thoroughness and attention to potential confounds.
  • Writing is clear and well-structured; the paper builds its argument progressively.

Limitations

  • Domain narrowness: Both datasets are from social media; it is unclear whether findings generalize to formal text, dialogue, or multi-party conversations.
  • Small dataset for SRAQ: With only 971 instances (384 train), probe training may be unstable, which could partly explain some observed variability.
  • Linear-only analysis: The study cannot detect nonlinear rhetorical encoding. If rhetorical intent is partially encoded nonlinearly, the conclusions about "multiple directions" may be incomplete.
  • No task-level downstream evaluation: The study does not test whether understanding these representations improves rhetorical question detection, generation, or any downstream application.
  • Limited causal analysis: Steering experiments are promising but narrow in scope; the paper explicitly acknowledges that linear separability does not imply linear controllability.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How do rhetorical question representations evolve during LLM training (pre-training vs. fine-tuning stages), and are the identified linear directions stable across training checkpoints?
  2. Can nonlinear probes (e.g., MLP probes) capture a more unified rhetorical direction, or is the heterogeneity intrinsic to how rhetorical meaning is organized?
  3. Do the multiple rhetorical directions identified here correspond to distinct attention heads or circuits within the model, as suggested by mechanistic interpretability frameworks?
  4. How do these findings transfer to non-English languages, where rhetorical question conventions and pragmatic norms may differ substantially?
  5. Can the identified linear directions be used to controllably steer models toward more or less rhetorical generation in practical applications (e.g., dialogue systems, debate assistants)?
