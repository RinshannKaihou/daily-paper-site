> **一句话总结：** 本文为基于两点估计器的零阶优化方法（ZO-GD, ZO-GDM, ZO-Adam）建立了均方线性稳定性理论，揭示了ZO方法的稳定性由Hessian的全谱（特别是迹）而非仅最大特征值决定，并经验证确认全批次ZO方法在神经网络训练中始终运行在均方稳定性的边界上。

> **TL;DR:** This paper develops a mean-square linear stability theory for zeroth-order (ZO) optimization methods, showing that ZO stability depends on the full Hessian spectrum (primarily the trace) rather than just the top eigenvalue as in first-order methods, and empirically confirms that full-batch ZO methods consistently operate at the mean-square edge of stability across multiple architectures and tasks.

## 核心贡献

- **均方线性稳定性精确刻画**：为ZO-GD、ZO-GDM和Frozen ZO-Adam推导了精确的步长条件，这些条件由Hessian的完整特征谱决定，而不仅取决于最大特征值。
- **与一阶方法的鲜明对比**：揭示了一阶方法的稳定性仅依赖于最大Hessian特征值，而ZO方法的均方稳定性依赖于完整的Hessian谱；动量对FO和ZO方法稳定性的影响方向恰好相反。
- **可计算的稳定性界**：给出了仅依赖Hessian迹和最大特征值的上下界（如ZO-GD: 2/(Tr(H)+2*lambda_max) <= eta* <= 2/Tr(H)），使得大规模训练中的跟踪成为可能。
- **均方稳定性边界的经验验证**：在CNN、ResNet、ViT、LSTM和Mamba上验证了ZO方法在训练过程中始终运行在预测的均方稳定性边界附近。
- **ZO方法特有的隐式正则化效应**：大步长在ZO方法中主要正则化Hessian的迹，而在FO方法中正则化最大特征值。

## Key Contributions

- Derives exact mean-square linear stability conditions for ZO-GD, ZO-GDM, and Frozen ZO-Adam using the standard two-point gradient estimator, showing dependence on the full Hessian spectrum.
- Identifies a fundamental contrast with first-order methods: FO stability is governed solely by the largest eigenvalue, while ZO mean-square stability depends on the entire spectrum, with the Hessian trace as the dominant quantity.
- Provides tractable upper and lower bounds on the mean-square critical step size that require only the Hessian trace and top eigenvalue, enabling practical monitoring during training.
- Demonstrates empirically across CNN, ResNet, ViT, LSTM, and Mamba architectures that full-batch ZO methods consistently operate at the mean-square edge of stability.
- Introduces a cone-preserving linear covariance operator framework with a rank-one global coupling term, leveraging the Krein-Rutman theorem for spectral analysis.

## Methodology

The paper develops its theory through linearized dynamics analysis around a local minimizer. For each ZO method, the authors:

1. **Linearized dynamics setup**: Apply the optimizer to the quadratic Taylor approximation of the loss at a local minimizer, reducing the problem to studying stability under a quadratic objective.

2. **Second-moment recursion**: Derive closed-form linear recursions for the second-moment matrices E[x_t x_t^T], E[x_t m_t^T], and E[m_t m_t^T] using Isserlis' theorem to evaluate Gaussian fourth moments. These recursions define a linear covariance operator T acting on a product space of 2x2 symmetric matrices.

3. **Covariance operator spectral analysis**: The operator T decomposes into a block-diagonal local part and a rank-one global coupling term. Using the Krein-Rutman theorem on the cone of positive semi-definite matrices, the authors characterize the spectral radius of T, yielding explicit stability conditions.

4. **Stability bounds**: From the exact conditions (which require the full spectrum), they derive tight bounds involving only the Hessian trace and largest eigenvalue:
   - ZO-GD: 2/(Tr(H) + 2*lambda_max) <= eta*_{ms} <= 2/Tr(H)
   - ZO-GDM: similar bounds with momentum scaling
   - Frozen ZO-Adam: analogous bounds on preconditioned Hessian quantities

5. **Empirical tracking**: During training, estimate Tr(H_t) via Hutchinson's method and lambda_max(H_t) via power iteration every 1,000 iterations, and verify whether the stability threshold remains within the predicted interval.

## Results & Highlights

- **Mean-square EoS phenomenon**: Across all ZO methods (ZO-GD, ZO-GDM, ZO-Adam) and all architectures (CNN, ResNet, ViT, LSTM, Mamba), the Hessian trace (or preconditioned trace) stabilizes near 2/eta during training, consistent with the predicted mean-square stability boundary.

- **Contrast with FO EoS**: In FO methods, lambda_max(H_t) stabilizes near 2/eta. In ZO methods, it is Tr(H_t) that stabilizes near 2/eta, while lambda_max(H_t) does not follow this pattern.

- **Opposite momentum effects**: Increasing beta enlarges the stable regime for FO-GDM (stability threshold grows as 2(1+beta)/lambda_max) but shrinks it for ZO-GDM (threshold decreases as 2(1-beta)/Tr(H)). This is because momentum accumulates estimator noise in ZO methods.

- **Catapult dynamics**: When the step size is increased mid-training, ZO-GD exhibits catapult dynamics -- the Hessian trace drops sharply and re-equilibrates near the new stability threshold.

- **Smoothing parameter mu**: Mean-square EoS persists for moderate and small mu, but overly large mu suppresses curvature growth and prevents EoS behavior, consistent with an implicit trace regularization bias.

- **Mini-batch ZO-SGD**: Preliminary experiments show mini-batch ZO-SGD converges to flatter regimes with smaller Hessian trace compared to full-batch ZO-GD.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: High. The mean-square stability analysis for ZO methods is genuinely new. The key insight that ZO stability depends on the full spectrum (not just the top eigenvalue) and that momentum has opposite effects in FO vs ZO settings is surprising and important. The cone-preserving operator + Krein-Rutman approach is technically clean.

- **Rigor**: Excellent. All main theorems have complete proofs. The Frozen ZO-Adam analysis requires a commutativity assumption (PH = HP), but the authors empirically verify that the relative commutator norm drops below 0.05 during training, providing strong justification.

- **Reproducibility**: Good. The paper specifies architectures, datasets (CIFAR-10 subset, synthetic sorting), hyperparameters, and estimation procedures. No code is linked, but the experimental setup follows prior well-documented work (Cohen et al., 2025). All experiments run on a single H100 GPU.

- **Significance**: The results are significant for understanding ZO training dynamics, which are increasingly relevant for memory-efficient LLM fine-tuning (MeZO-style methods). The connection between stability and trace-based implicit regularization opens new avenues for understanding ZO generalization.

- **Red flags**: (1) The theory is limited to full-batch settings; mini-batch ZO is only briefly explored. (2) The Frozen ZO-Adam analysis relies on commutativity, though well-justified empirically. (3) All experiments use relatively small models; scaling to actual LLM fine-tuning is not demonstrated. (4) The EoS tracking uses estimated quantities (Hutchinson, power iteration) every 1,000 steps, which may miss fine-grained dynamics.

## Strengths

- Theoretically rigorous with clean mathematical framework bridging operator theory and optimization.
- Surprising and insightful contrast between FO and ZO stability mechanisms.
- Comprehensive empirical validation across diverse architectures (CNN, ResNet, ViT, LSTM, Mamba) and tasks (vision, sequence).
- The trace-based stability characterization is practically useful for monitoring ZO training.
- Well-connected to prior EoS literature; extends the framework in a principled way.
- The catapult dynamics experiment provides compelling evidence for the theory's predictive power.

## Limitations

- Full-batch assumption limits direct applicability to practical mini-batch ZO training, where data subsampling noise interacts with estimator noise.
- No experiments on actual LLM fine-tuning tasks where ZO methods are most impactful (e.g., MeZO on large models).
- The Frozen ZO-Adam analysis does not directly apply to the full ZO-Adam with time-varying preconditioner, though the frozen approximation is standard in the EoS literature.
- The theory does not directly address generalization; the implicit regularization implications are observational rather than proven.
- Limited discussion of how the stability analysis could inform practical step-size selection or hyperparameter tuning for ZO methods.

## 相关性

本文对理解零阶优化方法的训练动态提供了严格的理论框架，直接关联到大模型内存高效微调中的训练稳定性问题。其揭示的ZO方法隐式正则化Hessian迹（而非最大特征值）的特性，为理解ZO训练的泛化行为和可靠性提供了新视角。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does the mean-square EoS theory extend to mini-batch ZO training, and what curvature quantities govern stability when data subsampling noise and estimator noise coexist?
- Can the mean-square stability conditions be used to design adaptive step-size schedules for ZO methods that automatically maintain operation near the stability boundary?
- Does the trace-based implicit regularization of ZO methods translate to measurable generalization benefits compared to FO methods with similar training loss?
- What happens to the EoS behavior when ZO methods are applied to fine-tune actual large language models (e.g., 7B+ parameter models) with parameter-efficient approaches like LoRA?
- Can the cone-preserving operator framework be extended to analyze higher-moment stability or almost-sure stability of ZO methods?
