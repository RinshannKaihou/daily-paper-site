LeapAlign: Post-Training Flow Matching Models at Any Generation Step by Building Two-Step Trajectories

arXiv: 2604.15311

Date: 2026-04-16

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.15311

一句话总结： LeapAlign 通过构建两步跳跃轨迹（leap trajectory），将长生成轨迹压缩为仅两个步骤，从而以常数内存开销将奖励梯度高效回传至早期生成步骤，在 Flux 模型上显著超越 GRPO 和现有直接梯度方法。
TL;DR: LeapAlign constructs two-step leap trajectories from full generation paths to enable efficient reward gradient backpropagation to early generation steps in flow matching models, outperforming state-of-the-art GRPO and direct-gradient methods on Flux across multiple preference and compositional alignment benchmarks.

核心贡献

  • 提出 LeapAlign，一种新的流匹配模型后训练方法，通过从完整生成轨迹中构建仅两步的"跳跃轨迹"（leap trajectory），以常数内存开销实现任意生成步骤的梯度更新，解决了现有直接梯度方法无法有效更新早期步骤的瓶颈。
  • 提出梯度折扣机制（gradient discounting），通过缩放因子 alpha 降低嵌套梯度的幅度而非完全移除，保留了跨时间步依赖关系中的有用学习信号。
  • 引入轨迹相似度加权（trajectory-similarity weighting），对更接近真实生成路径的跳跃轨迹赋予更高训练权重，提升监督信号的可靠性。
  • 在 Flux 模型上，LeapAlign 在 HPSv2.1、HPSv3、PickScore、UnifiedReward、ImageReward 及 GenEval 等多项指标上全面超越 DanceGRPO、MixGRPO、ReFL、DRaFT-LV 和 DRTune。

Key Contributions

  • Proposes LeapAlign, a post-training method for flow matching models that constructs two-step "leap trajectories" from full generation paths, enabling efficient gradient backpropagation to any generation step at constant memory cost.
  • Introduces gradient discounting, which scales down the magnitude of the nested gradient term by a factor alpha instead of completely removing it (as DRTune does), preserving useful cross-timestep dependency signals.
  • Designs trajectory-similarity weighting, assigning higher training weights to leap trajectories that are more consistent with the original long generation path.
  • Achieves consistent improvements over state-of-the-art GRPO-based (DanceGRPO, MixGRPO) and direct-gradient (ReFL, DRaFT-LV, DRTune) methods across HPSv2.1, HPSv3, PickScore, UnifiedReward, ImageReward, and GenEval benchmarks when fine-tuning Flux.

Methodology

LeapAlign fine-tunes a pre-trained rectified flow matching model to maximize a differentiable reward. The core insight is that backpropagating reward gradients through the full multi-step generation trajectory is prohibitively expensive in memory and leads to gradient explosion. LeapAlign addresses this by constructing a shortened "leap trajectory" of only two steps.

Leap Trajectory Construction (Section 4.2): Given a full generation trajectory from noise x_1 to image x_0, LeapAlign randomly selects two timesteps k > j. Using the one-step leap prediction property of rectified flow (x_hat_{j|k} = x_k - (k-j) v_theta(x_k)), it constructs two consecutive leaps: from x_k to x_j, and from x_j to x_0. Latent connectors align predicted states with real trajectory latents via stop-gradient operations, ensuring differentiability while maintaining numerical consistency. Because k and j are randomly selected from the full range [0,1], LeapAlign can update any generation step.

Gradient Discounting (Section 4.3): The gradient through the leap trajectory contains single-step gradients and a nested gradient term. The nested gradient captures cross-timestep interactions but can be large in magnitude. Instead of removing it entirely (as DRTune does by stopping gradient at model input), LeapAlign scales it by a discounting factor alpha. The model input for the second leap is modified to alpha x_j + (1-alpha) stop_gradient(x_j), which scales the nested gradient by alpha while keeping the forward pass identical. Setting alpha = 0.3 yields the best trade-off.

Fine-Tuning Objective (Section 4.4): LeapAlign uses a hinge-style loss max(0, lambda - r(x_0)), where r is the reward model and lambda is a threshold. Critically, the reward is evaluated on the actual generated image x_0 rather than the noisy one-step approximation x_hat_{0|j}, providing more faithful reward signals.

Trajectory-Similarity Weighting (Section 4.5): Leap trajectories that deviate significantly from the original trajectory can yield misleading gradients. A weighting factor w_sim = 1 / (max(d_j, tau) + max(d_0, tau)) is computed, where d_j and d_0 measure the average absolute difference between predicted and real latents at the two connection points. The final loss is stop_gradient(w_sim) * L_raw.

Results & Highlights

  • General Preference Alignment: On the HPDv2 test set, LeapAlign achieves 0.4092 HPSv2.1 (in-domain), surpassing MixGRPO (0.3692) and DRTune (0.3882). Out-of-domain metrics (HPSv3: 15.77, PickScore: 23.71, UR-Alignment: 3.50, UR-IQ: 3.72) also show the best or near-best performance.
  • Compositional Alignment: On GenEval, LeapAlign achieves an overall score of 0.7420, outperforming MixGRPO (0.7232) and DRTune (0.7101). Particularly strong gains are observed in Two Objects (96.46), Colors (80.59), Position (30.25), and Attribute Binding (66.00).
  • Convergence Speed: LeapAlign exhibits much faster and higher reward growth during fine-tuning compared to DRTune.
  • Generalization: Effective across different reward models (HPSv2.1, PickScore, HPSv3), prompt datasets (HPDv2, MJHQ-30k, GenEval), and flow matching models (Flux, SD3.5-Medium).
  • Ablations confirm: Two-step leap trajectories strike the best performance-memory trade-off; gradient discounting with alpha=0.3 is optimal; full timestep range [0,1] is superior to half-range; random (k,j) selection is slightly better than fixed-distance selection.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The leap trajectory construction is a clean and well-motivated idea. The key insight -- compressing a long trajectory into two one-step leaps via the rectified flow's linearity property -- is elegant. Gradient discounting as a soft alternative to hard gradient stopping is a meaningful though incremental contribution. Overall novelty is solid but not revolutionary; the individual components (leap prediction, gradient scaling, trajectory weighting) are relatively straightforward.

Rigor: The paper provides thorough mathematical derivations in the appendices, including the full gradient decomposition and derivation of the one-step leap prediction. Ablation studies are comprehensive, covering all major design choices. The comparison with baselines is mostly fair, though some baselines (DRaFT-LV, DRTune) lack official implementations and were reproduced by the authors.

Reproducibility: Good. Key hyperparameters are specified (alpha=0.3, tau=0.1, lambda=0.55, lr=1e-5, 300 iterations on 16 GPUs). The algorithm is presented as detailed pseudocode. However, no official code release is mentioned at the time of writing, and some baselines were reproduced rather than using official code.

Significance: The problem of aligning flow matching models with human preferences is highly relevant, and the inability of existing methods to update early generation steps is a real bottleneck. LeapAlign's consistent improvements across multiple metrics and benchmarks are convincing. The method is likely to be adopted by practitioners working on text-to-image model alignment.

Red Flags: The training cost (16 GPUs, online rollouts with 25 ODE steps per iteration) remains non-trivial. The method relies on the linearity property of rectified flow, so its applicability to non-rectified flow matching formulations may be limited. Reward hacking is only partially addressed by the hinge loss; no explicit analysis of reward over-optimization is provided.

Strengths

  • Clean and well-motivated methodological contribution; the leap trajectory idea naturally exploits the mathematical structure of rectified flow.
  • Strong empirical results across a wide range of metrics, reward models, prompt datasets, and backbone models.
  • Comprehensive ablation studies that isolate the contribution of each design component.
  • Clear explanation of how LeapAlign relates to and improves upon prior direct-gradient methods (ReFL, DRaFT-LV, DRTune).
  • The gradient discounting mechanism is a principled middle ground between keeping and discarding the nested gradient.

Limitations

  • The method requires the linearity property of rectified flow for the one-step leap prediction; extension to non-rectified schedulers would require additional derivation.
  • Training still requires generating full trajectories online (25-step ODE sampling) before constructing leap trajectories, which is computationally expensive.
  • The paper does not address non-differentiable reward functions; extension via differentiable value models is left as future work.
  • No analysis of potential reward hacking beyond the hinge loss threshold.
  • Qualitative results are limited to GenEval and HPSv3 reward model outputs; broader qualitative evaluation would strengthen the paper.

Relevance

LeapAlign 涉及生成模型训练中的梯度稳定性和优化问题，其梯度折扣机制和轨迹相似度加权的思路对理解训练稳定性有一定启发性。虽然主要面向图像生成领域，但其中关于如何平衡梯度信息保留与训练稳定性的方法论思路，与 LLM 训练中奖励模型对齐（RLHF）的梯度优化问题存在共通之处。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does LeapAlign perform with non-differentiable or combinatorial reward signals (e.g., using GPT-based judges)?
  • Can the leap trajectory idea be extended to video generation flow matching models, as the authors mention in their conclusion?
  • What is the sensitivity of LeapAlign to the number of ODE sampling steps used during online rollouts -- can fewer rollout steps maintain performance?
  • How does the gradient discounting factor alpha interact with different reward model architectures and gradient magnitudes?
  • Is there a theoretical connection between the leap trajectory construction and optimal control formulations (e.g., adjoint matching) that could provide convergence guarantees?
