> **一句话总结：** 本文在德国政治TikTok评论的反移民敌意检测任务上系统比较了人类标注与GPT-5.2 LLM标注在主动学习中的表现，发现LLM大规模标注（25,974条，$43）的F1-Macro与人类标注（3,800条，$316）相当，但LLM训练的分类器会系统性过度预测正类，且错误更集中在主题模糊的政策讨论区域。

> **TL;DR:** LLM annotation at scale (25,974 GPT-5.2 labels, $43) matches human annotation (3,800 labels, $316) on macro-F1 for hostility detection, but masks systematic over-prediction of the positive class. Active learning offers little advantage over random sampling in a pre-enriched pool. The choice between human and LLM annotation should be guided by the acceptable error profile, not aggregate F1 alone.

## 核心贡献

- 在德国政治TikTok评论数据集（277,902条）上系统比较了7种标注策略（人类/LLM x 主动学习/随机/全量）在4种编码器和10个随机种子下的表现，全面评估了标注源、采样策略和标签数量的影响。
- 揭示了"可比的宏观F1掩盖了系统性误差结构差异"这一关键发现：LLM训练的分类器假阳性远高于人类训练模型（243 FP vs 124 FP），且错误预测的置信度更高，这对内容审核中的置信度筛选构成实际风险。
- 通过主题聚类分析精确定位了人类-LLM标注分歧的来源：分歧集中在"边境管控"、"福利与经济竞争"和"伊斯兰与伊斯兰化"等政策性讨论区域，区分"针对群体的敌意"与"对政策的批评"是最核心的歧义点。
- 提出了基于误差画像（error profile）而非仅看聚合指标来选择标注策略的实践建议，对不同应用场景（如内容审核 vs 学术研究）的选择具有指导意义。

## Key Contributions

- Systematic comparison of 7 annotation strategies (human vs. LLM x active learning vs. random vs. full pool) across 4 German encoder models and 10 random seeds on a new dataset of 277,902 German political TikTok comments (25,974 LLM-labelled, 5,000 human-annotated) for anti-immigrant hostility detection.
- Demonstrates that comparable aggregate F1-Macro masks systematic differences in error structure: LLM-trained classifiers produce a 12:1 FP-to-FN ratio versus a balanced profile for human-trained classifiers, with higher confidence on errors.
- Identifies two distinct mechanisms of human-LLM annotation disagreement through topic clustering: (1) LLM conflating policy critique with group-directed hostility (e.g., border control discussions), and (2) differing scope regarding religion-based hostility (e.g., Islam & Islamization cluster).
- Provides a detailed cost-effectiveness analysis showing Full-LLM-26K ($43) matches Full-Human ($316) on F1-Macro at 1/7 the cost, while active learning with human labels delivers lower F1 at essentially the same price as full LLM annotation.
- Releases a new dataset and experimental code to support reproducibility.

## Methodology

The study uses a dataset of 277,902 German-language TikTok comments collected from the official accounts of six major German political parties (CDU/CSU, Greens, SPD, FDP, AfD, Die Linke) via the TikTok Research API (Jan 2024 - Sep 2025). A two-stage LLM pipeline constructs the annotation pool: (1) Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct prefilters immigration-relevant comments (yielding 25,974 comments, 9.3% of the corpus), and (2) GPT-5.2 annotates each prefiltered comment via the OpenAI Batch API as anti-immigrant or not.

For human annotation, 5,000 comments were sampled with party-stratified sampling at 40% anti-immigrant enrichment. Six Prolific crowdworkers annotated via a two-question framework (Q1: immigration reference; Q2: negative group portrayal) using Argilla, yielding 1,200 multiply-annotated gold evaluation items and 3,800 single-annotated training items.

Seven experimental conditions are compared: AL-Human, Random-Human, Full-Human, AL-LLM, Random-LLM, Full-LLM, and Full-LLM-26K. Active learning uses pool-based sampling with Entropy and BALD acquisition functions over 10 rounds (50 instances per round, 30 seed). Four German encoders are tested: german_bert, gbert-base, ModernGBERT, and xlm-r-base. All experiments use 10 random seeds with focal loss and class-weighted AdamW training.

Error analysis includes FP/FN profiling, prediction confidence distributions, and BERTopic-based discourse clustering (22 topics) to localize human-LLM annotation disagreement.

## Results & Highlights

- **Cost-performance parity:** Full-LLM-26K (25,974 labels, $43) achieves 0.730-0.735 F1-Macro across models, comparable to Full-Human (3,800 labels, $316) at 0.725-0.740 F1-Macro.
- **AL provides minimal benefit over random sampling** in the pre-enriched pool. The AL-Random gap is near-zero for German-specific models and negative for xlm-r-base.
- **Systematic FP asymmetry:** Full-LLM-26K produces 243 FPs vs. 21 FNs (12:1 ratio) for german_bert, while Full-Human produces 124 FPs vs. 87 FNs (balanced). LLM-trained classifiers are also more confident on their false positives.
- **Topic-localized disagreement:** Human-LLM agreement ranges from 45% (Border Control) to 93% (Pro-AfD Mobilization). The Islam & Islamization cluster shows the largest label-rate gap: GPT-5.2 labels 82% as anti-immigrant vs. 9% by humans.
- **Entropy outperforms BALD** as an acquisition function, with the gap larger under LLM-labelled conditions (up to 0.035 on xlm-r-base), suggesting BALD's epistemic uncertainty estimates are more sensitive to label-evaluation divergence.
- German-specific models consistently outperform the multilingual baseline (xlm-r-base) by up to 0.035 F1-Macro points.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Moderate-to-high. While LLM-as-annotator comparisons exist, this paper is the first to systematically compare human vs. LLM labels across matched AL, random, and full-pool conditions with error-structure analysis and topic-localized disagreement diagnosis. The insight that aggregate F1 masks systematic error divergence is genuinely valuable.
- **Rigor:** High. Seven conditions, four encoders, 10 seeds, detailed cost analysis, and thorough error decomposition. The two-question annotation framework enables diagnostic tracing of disagreements. Limitations are candidly discussed (pre-enriched pool, crowdsourced annotation, single LLM/prompt, single task/domain).
- **Reproducibility:** Good. Dataset and code are released. Full prompt texts, hyperparameter settings, and infrastructure details are provided. The use of GPT-5.2 (a proprietary model) limits exact reproducibility of LLM labels.
- **Significance:** High for the NLP annotation community. The finding that annotation strategy should be guided by error profile rather than aggregate metrics has practical implications for content moderation and social science research.
- **Red flags:**
  - Human annotators use a two-question decomposition while GPT-5.2 receives a single holistic prompt -- this asymmetry may partially explain the observed FP divergence rather than a fundamental human-LLM judgment difference.
  - Evaluation is against human gold labels, which systematically disadvantages LLM-trained models whose boundary diverges from the human one.
  - The pre-enriched pool (via Llama prefiltering) likely reduces the informativeness of AL, making the "AL provides little advantage" finding context-dependent.
  - Only 6 crowdworkers (not domain experts) provide human labels; inter-annotator agreement is only moderate (alpha = 0.43-0.49).

## Strengths

- Comprehensive experimental design with rigorous controls (matched conditions, multiple seeds, multiple models).
- Goes beyond aggregate metrics to diagnose error structure, confidence calibration, and topic-localized disagreement patterns.
- Practical cost-effectiveness analysis with real dollar figures, enabling direct comparison for practitioners.
- The two-question annotation framework is a clean methodological contribution that enables diagnostic analysis of disagreement sources.
- Candid discussion of limitations and ethical considerations, including annotator exposure to harmful content.

## Limitations

- Single-task, single-language, single-platform study (German TikTok, binary hostility detection) -- generalizability is uncertain.
- Human-LLM annotation interface asymmetry (two-question vs. single prompt) confounds the comparison.
- Pre-enriched pool via LLM prefiltering limits conclusions about AL effectiveness in general settings.
- Only one proprietary LLM (GPT-5.2) tested; open-source alternatives or different prompt designs may yield different error profiles.
- Crowdworkers rather than domain experts may not represent the best possible human annotation quality.
- No expert adjudication study on contested instances to determine which annotation source is "correct" on genuinely ambiguous cases.

## Relevance

本文与LLM训练可靠性和数据质量直接相关：它揭示了LLM标注数据训练的下游分类器在误差结构上与人类标注数据存在系统性差异（过度预测正类、错误置信度更高），这对依赖LLM标注数据进行模型训练的实践提出了重要警示。此外，论文对主动学习在LLM时代的价值进行了重新审视，对理解标注策略如何影响模型可靠性具有参考意义。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Would using the same two-question decomposition for the LLM (rather than a single holistic prompt) reduce the FP asymmetry, and how much of the observed divergence is attributable to the annotation interface difference versus genuine human-LLM judgment differences?
2. How do the findings transfer to other subjective annotation tasks (e.g., sentiment, toxicity, stance detection) and other languages, particularly low-resource ones where human annotation is even more costly?
3. Can a hybrid annotation strategy (e.g., LLM annotation with human review on high-uncertainty or high-disagreement instances) achieve a better error profile than either source alone?
4. What happens when downstream classifiers trained on LLM labels are deployed and their predictions are used to make real moderation decisions -- does the high-confidence FP pattern lead to measurable over-censorship in practice?
5. How robust are these findings across different LLM annotators (e.g., Claude, Gemini, open-source models) and prompt designs -- is the systematic over-prediction of the positive class a general property of LLM annotation or specific to GPT-5.2?
