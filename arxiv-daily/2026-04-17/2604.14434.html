Geometric Routing Enables Causal Expert Control in Mixture of Experts

arXiv: 2604.14434

Date: 2026-04-17

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14434

一句话总结： 在稀疏MoE模型中，利用余弦相似度路由的几何结构，通过将专家输出投影到反嵌入矩阵构建"语义字典"，实现了对专家语义特化的可解释性分析和因果可控干预（时间类专家转向使P(temporal)增加中位数+321%），为MoE模型提供了零开销的推理时可控性。
TL;DR: This paper demonstrates that rank-1 MoE experts with cosine-similarity routing are architecturally monosemantic and causally controllable. By projecting expert output vectors through the unembedding matrix, the authors build a "Semantic Dictionary" revealing 10 semantic categories, and show that steering toward a temporal expert's centroid increases P(temporal) by +321% (median), while suppression and expert surgery produce equally dramatic effects -- all with zero inference overhead.

核心贡献

  • 语义字典 (Semantic Dictionary)： 将8192个rank-1专家的输出向量通过反嵌入矩阵投影，发现15%的专家是单语义专家，覆盖10个语义类别（时间、地理、基数、话语、情感、金融、军事、科学等）
  • 频率-语法梯度： 证明路由机制按语法类别（而非词频）组织token，早期层按频率粗分，深层按语法类别细分离，通过Zipf混淆控制实验验证（所有 p < 0.001）
  • 三类因果干预： 提出 steering（朝专家质心方向偏移路由）、suppression（抑制特定专家）、surgery（改写专家输出向量）三种因果干预方法，效果可跨层叠加
  • 几何透明性： 余弦路由使专家特化可直接从质心矩阵读取，无需额外探针；线性路由虽支持类似控制效果但缺乏几何可解释性
  • 多义词路由分叉： 同一token（如"bank"）在不同上下文中激活完全不同的专家轨迹（Jaccard重叠仅14.3%），证明路由是语义驱动的

Key Contributions

  • Semantic Dictionary construction: Projects expert output vectors through the unembedding matrix to decode what each rank-1 expert "writes" to the residual stream, revealing that 15% of 8,192 experts are monosemantic specialists across 10 semantic categories.
  • Frequency-to-syntax gradient with rigorous controls: Demonstrates via silhouette scores and Zipf-confound controls (matched-frequency pairs) that routing organizes by syntactic class, not corpus frequency, with a depth-dependent gradient (Spearman rho = 0.81, p = 0.015).
  • Three causal intervention types: Introduces steering (centroid-based routing bias), suppression (geographic expert suppression drops P(geographic) by -23%), and expert surgery (rewriting output vectors halves target-category probability), with compositional effects across layers.
  • Cross-routing comparison: Shows that linear routers support qualitatively similar steering effects but lack geometric transparency -- cosine routing enables expert discovery by reading centroid positions without activation probes.
  • HDBSCAN-based automated category discovery: Applies clustering on decoded expert centroids to discover 20 additional expert clusters beyond manually curated categories, including fine-grained numeric specialization (5 distinct numeric clusters).

Methodology

The authors train 8-layer MoE language models (76--84M parameters) on WikiText-103 with 8,192 rank-1 experts per layer using top-8 sparse routing via cosine similarity between token representations and expert centroids.

Semantic Dictionary construction: Each expert's rank-1 weight matrix $W_{up}$ is projected through the unembedding matrix $W_{unemb}$ to obtain top-k vocabulary tokens. Experts are classified as monosemantic (top-10 tokens belong to a single semantic category), polysemantic, or incoherent.

Routing analysis: The authors compute routing statistics (Gini coefficient, entropy, utilization) across layers, analyze polysemy branching by comparing expert trajectories for the same token in different contexts, and perform frequency-vs-syntax controlled experiments using matched-frequency function/content word pairs.

Causal interventions:

  • Steering: Adds a scaled expert centroid ($\lambda \cdot c_{target}$) to the query vector, biasing routing toward the target expert.
  • Suppression: Subtracts the expert centroid from the query to reduce activation probability.
  • Surgery: Directly rewrites the expert's output vector $W_{up}$ to alter what it contributes to the residual stream.

Validation: Interventions are tested on 44 temporal and 39 geographic prompts spanning category-relevant, neutral, and adversarial contexts, with HDBSCAN clustering extending coverage to 10 total categories.

Results & Highlights

  • Temporal steering: Median +321% increase in P(temporal) across 44 prompts (IQR: +124% to +549%), positive on 98% of prompts. Automated discovery of small-integer experts achieves +84% steering effect.
  • Geographic suppression: -23% drop in P(geographic) when suppressing the geographic expert.
  • Expert surgery: Halves target-category probability by rewriting the expert output vector.
  • Compositional steering: Cross-layer steering composes nearly additively (temporal L4 + cardinal L7: solo +567%/+19% becomes composed +513%/+18%); same-layer steering interferes due to routing mechanism.
  • Near-perfect expert utilization: Only 3 out of 8,192 experts are dead (zero activations).
  • Polysemy branching: The token "bank" activates 86% different experts in "river bank" vs. "central bank" contexts.
  • 10 causally validated categories: temporal, geographic, cardinal, discourse, emotional, financial, military, legal, scientific, social -- plus 2 clustering-discovered categories (small integers, auxiliary verbs).
  • Read/write orthogonality: 62.3% of experts have near-orthogonal read/write directions (|cos| < 0.2), confirming they compute transformations rather than copy inputs.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. The Semantic Dictionary approach -- projecting expert outputs through the unembedding matrix -- is an elegant and novel way to decode expert function without additional training. The combination of three causal interventions (steering, suppression, surgery) with compositional analysis is thorough. The cross-routing comparison (cosine vs. linear) adds intellectual honesty by showing the effects are not unique to cosine routing.
  • Rigor: Strong. The paper employs matched-frequency controls, permutation tests (200 permutations), bootstrap confidence intervals (500 resamples), FDR correction, and adversarial prompt testing. The Zipf-confound control experiment is particularly well-designed.
  • Reproducibility: Moderate. The models are small enough to reproduce (76--84M params), but no code is referenced. The mathematical formulations are clear enough for reimplementation. WikiText-103 is a standard benchmark.
  • Significance: Potentially high for the interpretability community. The claim that MoE expert specialization is a "first-class interpretability primitive" that avoids the reconstruction-fidelity trade-off of SAEs is compelling, but awaits validation at scale.
  • Red flags: (1) All results are at 76--84M parameter scale -- whether findings generalize to billion-parameter MoE models with higher-rank experts is unknown. (2) 73.5% of experts remain unclustered, meaning the Semantic Dictionary covers only a fraction of the model. (3) The paper is a preprint and has not been peer-reviewed. (4) The steering effects vary considerably across categories (+3% social to +453% temporal), suggesting uneven controllability.

Strengths

  • Exceptionally rigorous experimental design with multiple statistical controls and confound analyses.
  • Three complementary causal interventions provide converging evidence for expert specialization.
  • The frequency-vs-syntax analysis with matched-frequency pairs is a model of careful experimental design.
  • Honest cross-routing comparison showing linear routers also support steering, with the advantage being transparency rather than capability.
  • Clear articulation of limitations, including scale, corpus diversity, and coverage gaps.
  • The compositional steering analysis (cross-layer additive, same-layer interference) provides actionable practical guidance.

Limitations

  • Scale: All results are at 76--84M parameters on WikiText-103 only; generalization to production-scale MoE models (Mixtral, DeepSeek) is untested.
  • Coverage: Only 15% of experts are monosemantic; 73.5% remain unclustered. The interpretability picture is incomplete.
  • Rank-1 assumption: Higher-rank experts (as in most production MoE systems) may not exhibit the same monosemantic properties.
  • Single corpus: WikiText-103 is a narrow benchmark; routing and specialization patterns may differ on more diverse training data.
  • Safety steering: Safety-adjacent categories show smaller effects than semantic categories, limiting applicability to alignment.
  • Compositional constraint: Multi-category control within single layers is limited by routing interference.

  • Can the Semantic Dictionary approach be combined with SAE-based methods to cover the 73.5% of currently unclustered experts?
