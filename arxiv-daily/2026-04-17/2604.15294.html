How Do LLMs and VLMs Understand Viewpoint Rotation Without Vision? An Interpretability Study

arXiv: 2604.15294

Date: 2026-04-16

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.15294

一句话总结： 本文通过构建纯文本视角旋转理解（VRU）数据集VRUBench，发现当前LLM/VLM在空间智能任务上远不如人类，进而利用逐层探测和注意力头因果干预揭示了模型内部"浅层编码旋转方向/角度、中层推断朝向、深层做答案决策"的机制，但关键注意力头无法有效绑定朝向与观测从而导致幻觉，最终通过对关键头的选择性微调在提升VRU性能的同时避免了灾难性遗忘。
TL;DR: This ACL 2026 paper investigates whether LLMs and VLMs can understand viewpoint rotation from text-only inputs. The authors propose VRUBench, find models perform poorly (best ~75% vs. human 100%), and conduct mechanistic interpretability analysis revealing that models encode viewpoint information in hidden states but fail to bind orientation with observations, causing hallucination. Selective fine-tuning of key attention heads identified via path patching improves VRU performance while preserving general capabilities.

核心贡献

  • 提出VRUBench数据集：构建了一个纯文本视角旋转理解基准，包含19,591个样本（2-5步旋转），揭示当前LLM/VLM与人类之间存在巨大的空间智能鸿沟（SOTA模型~75% vs. 人类100%）。
  • 发现VLM优于LLM的跨模态迁移效应：即使推理时没有视觉输入，经过视觉数据训练的VLM在文本空间智能任务上仍一致优于对应LLM骨干，验证了视觉-语言学习的互补性。
  • 逐层探测分析：发现模型浅层能够编码旋转方向和角度（准确率>99%），中层逐渐获得绝对朝向感知能力，但在深层该能力下降，暗示发生了模式转换。
  • 注意力头因果干预与功能分类：通过path patching识别出少量关键注意力头，并将其功能分为提案头（proposal head）、答案决策头（answer decision head）和未知头（unknown head），揭示了模型从朝向感知到答案决策的转换机制及失败原因（无法绑定朝向与观测）。
  • 选择性微调方案：仅微调通过因果干预识别出的关键注意力头，以50%的GPU时间达到显著提升的VRU性能，同时避免灾难性遗忘，且OOD泛化至视觉空间数据集SpinBench。

Key Contributions

  • VRUBench dataset: A synthetic text-only viewpoint rotation understanding benchmark with 19,591 instances (2-5 rotation steps), exposing a substantial gap between model and human spatial intelligence (SOTA ~75% vs. human 100%).
  • Cross-modal transfer finding: VLMs consistently outperform their LLM backbones on text-only spatial tasks, demonstrating that visual training enhances spatial perception even without visual input at inference time.
  • Layer-wise probing analysis: Models encode rotation direction/angle with >99% accuracy across layers; absolute orientation encoding emerges in early-to-middle layers but degrades in later layers, indicating a representational shift.
  • Head-wise causal intervention via path patching: Identifies sparse key attention heads in middle-to-upper layers and categorizes them as proposal heads, answer decision heads, and an "unknown head" (a cautious response mechanism potentially induced by alignment training).
  • Selective fine-tuning of key heads: Updating only top-32 key attention heads achieves substantial VRU improvement with 50% GPU hours of full SFT, preserves general capabilities (MMLU, BBH), and generalizes OOD to visual-spatial benchmark SpinBench.

Methodology

The paper follows an "interpret-then-improve" paradigm with three main stages:

1. Benchmark Construction (VRUBench): A simulated environment containing 100 indoor objects is constructed. Viewpoint rotation angles are restricted to {0, 90, 180, 270, 360} degrees to avoid observation ambiguity. At each step, the model rotates left/right by a random angle, and observations are deterministic based on the resulting viewpoint. The task requires predicting the observation after the final rotation, given the sequence of rotations and intermediate observations as text.

2. Mechanistic Interpretability:

  • Layer-wise probing: Linear classifiers are trained on hidden representations from each layer to predict rotation direction (binary), angle (5-way), and absolute orientation (4-way). This reveals what spatial information is encoded at each layer depth.
  • Head-wise causal intervention (Path Patching): The rotation direction at the final step is flipped to create clean-corrupted pairs. For each attention head, its activation from the corrupted run is patched into the clean run, and the causal effect on output logits is measured. Heads with large causal effects are identified as "key heads."
  • Attention pattern analysis: The attention patterns of key heads are manually inspected and categorized by function.

3. Selective Fine-tuning: Only the K/Q/V/O parameter matrices of the top-32 key attention heads identified by path patching are set to tunable, with all other parameters frozen. Gradients are rescaled by the ratio of total heads to updated heads per layer.

Results & Highlights

  • Model performance: Best performing model is Gemini3-Flash with thinking enabled at 86.32% average accuracy (vs. human 100%). Without thinking, Qwen2.5-VL-32B leads at 59.84%. LLaMA2-7B-chat achieves only 18.90%.
  • VLM > LLM consistently: Qwen2.5-VL-7B (48.67%) outperforms Qwen2.5-7B (41.89%); Qwen2.5-VL-32B (59.84%) outperforms Qwen2.5-32B (72.84% for LLM but 32B scale helps more). Even at the same architecture, visual training transfers to text-only spatial reasoning.
  • Thinking mode helps text-only spatial tasks: This contrasts with prior work on visual-spatial benchmarks (VSI-Bench) where CoT did not help, suggesting an inherent difference between text-only and vision-based spatial perception.
  • Probing reveals orientation encoding degrades in later layers: VLMs show orientation encoding emerging in layers 1-20 but declining in layers 21-28, coinciding with the emergence of answer-decision key heads.
  • Three key head types identified: (1) Proposal head (layer 22, head 1) extracts all candidate answers; (2) Answer decision head (layer 26, head 14) focuses on the selected answer; (3) Unknown head (layer 27, head 14) reflects cautious uncertainty, potentially from alignment training.
  • Core failure mode: Key heads fail to effectively bind the perceived viewpoint orientation with the corresponding observation at that orientation, leading to hallucinated object selection.
  • Selective SFT results: On Qwen2.5-VL-7B, selective SFT improves VRUBench from 48.7% to 78.7% (+30.0%), SpinBench from 44.8% to 48.4% (+3.6%), while maintaining MMLU (60.3% unchanged) and BBH (48.4%, -0.8). Full SFT achieves 96.3% on VRUBench but drops MMLU by 4.7 and BBH by 13.4 points.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. This is the first systematic mechanistic interpretability study of textual spatial intelligence, specifically viewpoint rotation understanding. The combination of probing, path patching, and attention pattern analysis to diagnose spatial reasoning failures in LLMs/VLMs is novel. The "interpret-then-improve" paradigm applied to spatial intelligence is a meaningful contribution.
  • Rigor: Strong experimental design with multiple model families (LLaMA, Qwen, Gemini) and sizes (3B-32B). The probing methodology is well-justified (linear probes preferred over non-linear for faithfulness). Path patching is applied rigorously with careful corrupted data construction. Validation through ablation and random ablation baselines strengthens causal claims. Human evaluation with 20 annotators and inter-annotator agreement (Krippendorff's Alpha = 1.0) is solid.
  • Reproducibility: Good. Dataset construction is algorithmic and fully described. Probing uses standard linear classifiers. Path patching follows established protocols. Code is promised. However, the manual attention pattern analysis is somewhat subjective.
  • Significance: The finding that models encode spatial information but fail at the binding step is an important diagnostic insight. The selective fine-tuning approach demonstrating OOD transfer from text to visual spatial tasks (supporting dual-coding theory) has practical implications. The paper bridges mechanistic interpretability and spatial intelligence, two active research areas.
  • Red flags: (1) The VRUBench task is deliberately simplified (discrete angles, single object per viewpoint), which may limit real-world applicability. (2) Selective SFT was only tested on models up to 7B due to compute constraints, leaving scalability uncertain. (3) The attention pattern analysis of key heads is manually conducted and somewhat qualitative. (4) The dataset is synthetic and uses a restricted environment (100 objects, discrete rotations), raising questions about ecological validity.

Strengths

  • First comprehensive mechanistic interpretability study of textual spatial intelligence in LLMs/VLMs.
  • Well-structured "interpret-then-improve" paradigm that yields both scientific insights and practical improvements.
  • Strong empirical evidence across multiple model families, sizes, and evaluation settings.
  • The finding that VLMs outperform LLMs on text-only spatial tasks (even without visual input at inference) is both surprising and significant for understanding cross-modal transfer.
  • Selective fine-tuning approach is practical, efficient (50% GPU hours), and avoids catastrophic forgetting -- a known pain point in LLM adaptation.
  • The identification of the "unknown head" as a cautious response mechanism potentially induced by alignment training is a novel and intriguing finding.
  • Clear writing with well-organized experiments and appendices.

Limitations

  • Simplified task design: VRUBench uses discrete rotation angles (0/90/180/270/360 degrees) and single objects per viewpoint, which is far from real-world spatial reasoning complexity. Continuous rotations and multi-object scenes remain unexplored.
  • Scale of fine-tuning experiments: Selective and full SFT were only conducted on models up to 7B parameters. Whether the approach scales to larger models (32B+) is unknown.
  • Qualitative attention analysis: The categorization of key heads (proposal, answer decision, unknown) relies heavily on manual inspection of attention patterns, which is inherently subjective and may not capture all functional roles.
  • Prompt sensitivity: The authors acknowledge that LLMs/VLMs are sensitive to prompt phrasing, but do not explore this for VRU tasks.
  • Limited to implicit reasoning: The interpretability analysis focuses on the "directly output answers" setting and does not analyze explicit CoT reasoning mechanisms, which show improved performance.
  • Synthetic-only evaluation: All experiments use synthetic environments. Performance on real-world spatial reasoning scenarios (e.g., navigation instructions, robotic manipulation) is not tested.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How would the identified mechanism (encoding orientation but failing to bind with observations) change with continuous rotation angles and multi-object scenes?
  2. Can the selective fine-tuning approach scale to larger models (32B+), and does the sparsity of key heads remain consistent across model scales?
  3. Does the "unknown head" phenomenon generalize to other tasks beyond spatial reasoning, and is it truly an artifact of alignment training?
  4. How does explicit CoT reasoning change the internal mechanism compared to the implicit reasoning pathway analyzed in this paper?
  5. Could the cross-modal transfer finding (VLM > LLM on text-only spatial tasks) be leveraged to design better training strategies that explicitly incorporate visual data for improved textual spatial reasoning?
