> **一句话总结：** 本文提出 FORGE 框架，通过设计专门针对功能连接矩阵的结构感知 VAE（FCM-VAE）进行隐私保护的生成式回放，并结合双层知识蒸馏和分层上下文 Thompson 采样，在三个大规模多站点 fMRI 数据集上显著缓解了跨站点持续学习中的灾难性遗忘问题，AAA 提升 2.7%-4.7%，遗忘率降低 11.5%-24.5%。

> **TL;DR:** FORGE is the first continual learning framework for cross-site fMRI-based brain disorder diagnosis, using a structure-aware VAE (FCM-VAE) for privacy-preserving generative replay of functional connectivity matrices, combined with dual-level knowledge distillation and hierarchical contextual Thompson sampling, achieving substantial improvements in accuracy and forgetting reduction across ASD, MDD, and schizophrenia datasets (accepted at CVPR 2026).

## 核心贡献

- 提出了 FORGE，首个专门针对跨站点 fMRI 脑疾病诊断的持续学习框架，统一了生成式回放与双层知识蒸馏（logits 蒸馏 + 图表示蒸馏），在隐私友好的设定下运行。
- 设计了 FCM-VAE，一种条件变分自编码器，专门针对功能连接（FC）矩阵的拓扑和频谱特性，集成了局部邻接编码、频谱位置编码和低秩解码器，生成质量显著优于现有图生成模型。
- 引入了分层上下文 Thompson 采样（HCTS）机制，在站点级别和样本级别自适应分配回放缓冲区容量，提高了有限缓冲区下的利用效率。
- 在 ABIDE（ASD）、REST-meta-MDD（MDD）和 BSNIP（精神分裂症）三个大规模多站点数据集上，FORGE 在 AAA 指标上超越最强基线 2.7%-4.7%，遗忘率降低 11.5%-24.5%。

## Key Contributions

- Proposes FORGE, the first continual learning framework tailored for cross-site fMRI-based brain disorder diagnosis under realistic streaming and privacy-friendly settings, unifying dual-level knowledge distillation with generative replay.
- Develops FCM-VAE, a conditional VAE specifically designed for functional connectivity matrices, incorporating a structure-aware graph transformer encoder with local adjacency encoding, spectral positional encoding, and a low-rank bilinear decoder that exploits the known low-rank structure of large-scale FC networks.
- Introduces a Hierarchical Contextual Thompson Sampling (HCTS) mechanism that adaptively allocates replay buffer capacity at both site-level (based on accuracy and forgetting) and sample-level (based on margin and closeness), maximizing replay utility under constrained memory.
- Demonstrates consistent improvements over 10+ baselines across three large-scale multi-site neuroimaging datasets (ABIDE, REST-meta-MDD, BSNIP), with AAA improvements of 2.7%-4.7% and forgetting rate reductions of 11.5%-24.5% relative to the strongest baselines.

## Methodology

**Problem Setup:** The authors formulate cross-site fMRI diagnosis as a continual learning problem where each clinical site constitutes a sequential task. Raw rs-fMRI scans are preprocessed into 116x116 functional connectivity (FC) matrices using the AAL-116 atlas, thresholded at tau=0.4 to construct brain graphs with 116 ROI nodes. The model must learn sequentially from T sites without revisiting previous training data.

**FCM-VAE (Generative Backbone):** A conditional variational autoencoder purpose-built for FC matrices with three key innovations:
1. **Structure-aware Graph Transformer Encoder:** Augments node features by concatenating the FC correlation profile, spectral embeddings from top-k Laplacian eigenvectors, and node degree. The multi-head attention incorporates both a local adjacency bias (A_adj + I) and a spectral positional bias (Phi * W_phi), enabling the encoder to capture intrinsic topological and spectral properties simultaneously.
2. **Low-rank Bilinear Decoder with Reachability Gating:** Exploits the well-known low-rank structure of FC matrices by reconstructing edge intensities as a sum of R rank-1 factors (omega_e(z) = sum_r alpha_r * U_ur(z) * U_vr(z)). Uses Poisson likelihood with a Fisher-z transformed intensity target. A soft gating mechanism based on the adjacency matrix constrains spurious edge formation.
3. **Linear Gaussian Supervision Head:** A simple linear mapping from the latent z to phenotype y with Gaussian noise, providing interpretability and encouraging the latent space to encode diagnostic information.

**Dual-Level Knowledge Distillation:** The overall training objective combines: (1) cross-entropy on current site data, (2) cross-entropy on replayed synthetic samples from previous sites, (3) logits-level distillation (L2 between student and frozen teacher logits), and (4) graph-readout distillation (L2 between student and teacher graph-level representations). Replay buffers store tuples (synthetic graph, label, teacher logits, teacher graph readout).

**Hierarchical Contextual Thompson Sampling (HCTS):**
- **Site-level:** Context vector phi_i = [Acc_i, Forget_i] feeds a linear Gaussian Thompson sampling model to allocate replay quotas across sites via softmax normalization, prioritizing sites with higher forgetting.
- **Sample-level:** Context vector psi_u = [margin_u, closeness_u] selects informative and diverse samples within each site's quota, using farthest-first traversal in the readout embedding space to maximize coverage.

## Results & Highlights

- **Data Augmentation (FCM-VAE):** FCM-VAE consistently achieves the best or second-best classification performance across all three datasets when used for data augmentation with a GCN classifier, outperforming ReGate, BrainNetGAN, and GR-SPD-GAN. Results are robust across multiple classifier backbones (GCN, GAT, GraphSAGE, GAE, GraphTransformer).
- **Continual Learning (FORGE):** On ASD (4 sites), BSNIP (5 sites), and MDD (5 sites), FORGE achieves the highest AAA and lowest FOR across all datasets compared to 10 baselines including EWC, SI, PackNet, ER, LwF, DER, DER++, TWP, UGCL, and PDGNNs.
- **Ablation Studies:** Removing HCTS causes the most severe degradation. Removing graph knowledge distillation (GKD) increases forgetting. Removing logits knowledge distillation (LKD) weakens task-specific retention. All three components are complementary.
- **Buffer Size Sensitivity:** FORGE maintains strong performance even with small replay buffers (128 samples), and interestingly performance slightly declines with very large buffers, likely due to redundancy in generated samples.
- **Threshold Robustness:** FORGE is robust to different FC threshold values (tau = 0.3, 0.4, 0.5), indicating gains are not tied to a particular graph construction parameter.
- **Hyperparameter Stability:** The framework is stable across a wide range of lambda_1, lambda_2, lambda_3 configurations.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** High. This is the first CL framework specifically designed for fMRI-based brain disorder diagnosis. The FCM-VAE introduces technically novel components (spectral positional encoding in a graph transformer for FC matrices, low-rank Poisson decoder with reachability gating) that are well-motivated by the neuroimaging domain. The hierarchical Thompson sampling for adaptive replay is also a creative contribution.
- **Rigor:** Strong. The paper evaluates across three distinct disorders (ASD, MDD, schizophrenia) with different numbers of sites. All task orderings are evaluated and averaged. The ablation study is thorough, isolating each component's contribution. Multiple classifier backbones are tested for the augmentation experiments.
- **Reproducibility:** Good. Code is publicly available. Hyperparameter search ranges are reported. Preprocessing is standardized using SPM12 and DPABI. However, some HCTS implementation details (e.g., temperature tau, forgetting factor gamma) could be more explicitly specified in the main paper.
- **Significance:** High for the neuroimaging/clinical ML community. The cross-site continual learning setting is highly relevant for real-world clinical deployment. The privacy-preserving generative replay approach directly addresses a practical barrier in healthcare.
- **Potential concerns:** (1) The number of sites per dataset is relatively small (4-5 sites), which may limit generalization claims. (2) The FC threshold tau=0.4 is fixed in the main experiments, and while sensitivity analysis shows robustness, the choice of threshold itself can significantly affect graph structure. (3) The graph classifier is limited to GCN; whether the framework generalizes to more sophisticated graph classifiers at the CL level is not fully explored.

## Strengths

- First systematic treatment of continual learning for fMRI-based brain network classification, filling an important gap in both CL and neuroimaging literature.
- The FCM-VAE is carefully designed with domain-specific inductive biases (low-rank structure, spectral properties of FC matrices), rather than naively applying off-the-shelf graph VAEs.
- Privacy-preserving by design: generative replay avoids storing real patient data.
- HCTS is a principled approach to adaptive replay allocation, with closed-loop feedback from performance metrics.
- Comprehensive evaluation with strong baselines from all four CL categories plus graph-specific methods.
- Code availability and standardized preprocessing enhance reproducibility.

## Limitations

- The number of clinical sites per dataset (4-5) is relatively small; it remains unclear how FORGE scales to dozens or hundreds of sites with more heterogeneous acquisition protocols.
- The graph construction relies on a fixed atlas (AAL-116) and a single correlation measure (Pearson); alternative parcellations or connectivity measures are not explored.
- Only binary classification tasks are considered; multi-class or regression settings (e.g., symptom severity prediction) are not addressed.
- The computational overhead of training FCM-VAE at each site and running HCTS is not discussed.
- The evaluation assumes balanced access to all sites sequentially; real-world scenarios may involve site dropout, label scarcity, or non-stationary site distributions.
- While FC matrices are low-dimensional (116x116), the approach may not directly scale to high-resolution parcellations with thousands of ROIs.

## Relevance

本文提出的持续学习框架直接解决了多站点 fMRI 诊断模型中的灾难性遗忘问题，其生成式回放策略和自适应采样机制对于提高 ML 系统在医疗场景下的训练稳定性和可靠性具有重要参考价值；FCM-VAE 的结构感知编码设计也体现了对模型可解释性（latent space 与表型标签的线性关联）的关注。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How does FORGE perform with more than 5-10 sites, and does the HCTS allocation mechanism scale gracefully with increasing site count and heterogeneity?
2. Can the FCM-VAE be extended to generate multi-view FC matrices (e.g., combining different frequency bands or parcellation resolutions) for richer generative replay?
3. What is the effect of using more powerful graph classifiers (e.g., graph transformers with pre-training) as the backbone instead of GCN?
4. How sensitive is FORGE to the order of site arrival, particularly when the most heterogeneous site arrives early vs. late in the sequence?
5. Can the HCTS framework be adapted for online/incremental settings where sites may reappear or provide additional data over time?
