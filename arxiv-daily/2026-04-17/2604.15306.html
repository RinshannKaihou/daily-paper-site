Generalization in LLM Problem Solving: The Case of the Shortest Path

arXiv: 2604.15306

Date: 2026-04-16

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.15306

一句话总结

通过最短路径规划这一可控合成环境，系统性地揭示了LLM在空间迁移上成功但在长度扩展上因递归不稳定性而失败的规律，并分析了训练数据、RL训练范式和推理时策略各自对泛化能力的独立影响。

TL;DR

This ICLR 2026 paper introduces a controlled shortest-path planning environment to study systematic generalization in LLMs along two orthogonal axes: spatial transfer to unseen maps and length scaling to longer-horizon problems. Models show strong spatial transfer (>90% success rate) but consistently fail under length scaling due to recursive instability -- not mere error accumulation. The paper systematically disentangles the effects of training data design (coverage > diversity > solution multiplicity), training paradigms (RL stabilizes but cannot exceed SFT ceiling), and inference-time strategies (improve but cannot rescue length scaling failures), offering actionable guidance for data-efficient generalization.

核心贡献

  1. 揭示泛化不对称性: 模型在空间迁移（未见过的地图）上表现优异（>90%成功率），但在长度扩展上持续失败，通过概率分解证明失败根源是递归不稳定性（recursive instability），而非简单的错误累积。
  2. 数据选择的系统性分析: 在固定训练预算下，分配给更多不同问题（unique questions）比更多解（more solutions）更有效；覆盖度（coverage）设定了空间迁移的上限，多样性（diversity）只需最低阈值即可，过度多样性在低覆盖度时甚至有害。
  3. RL与SFT的比较: RL（Dr.GRPO）稳定了训练过程并防止过拟合，但始终无法超越最佳SFT模型的性能上限；RL甚至会收缩有效解空间。
  4. 推理时策略的局限性: 多数投票和最短路径选择等策略能提升性能，但无法从根本上解决长度扩展失败问题。
  5. 数学领域的验证: 在MathQA数据集上验证了"更多问题优于更多解"和"覆盖度优于多样性"的结论，证明合成环境的发现具有实际意义。

Key Contributions

  1. Generalization asymmetry diagnosis: Introduces a composable sequential optimization problem (SOP) framework using shortest-path planning that cleanly separates two orthogonal generalization axes -- spatial transfer and length scaling -- revealing that models succeed at the former (>90% SR) but fail at the latter due to recursive instability, rigorously decomposed via Pr(Long) = Pr(Long | Sub1 & Sub2) x Pr(Sub1 & Sub2) + Pr(Long, ~(Sub1 & Sub2)).
  2. Data selection principles: Demonstrates that under fixed training budgets, allocating to more distinct questions consistently outperforms more solutions per question; that coverage sets the transfer ceiling while diversity only needs a minimal threshold; and that excessive diversity can hurt when coverage is low.
  3. RL vs. SFT characterization: Shows that RL (Dr.GRPO) stabilizes training dynamics and prevents overfitting but never exceeds the best SFT performance ceiling, with nearly identical error-type distributions between the two paradigms.
  4. Inference-time scaling limits: Demonstrates that test-time strategies (majority-of-10, shortest-of-10) shift performance curves upward but cannot alter the fundamental length-scaling degradation trend.
  5. Cross-domain validation: Confirms key findings (more questions > more solutions; coverage > diversity) on the MathQA dataset with Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, providing evidence that synthetic-environment insights transfer to practical settings.

Methodology

Controlled synthetic environment: The authors construct a shortest-path planning task on sparse grid maps, formalized as a composable sequential optimization problem (SOP) where optimal subpaths compose into optimal full paths. The task uses direction tokens (N, S, E, W) rather than node indices to prevent trivial memorization.

Two orthogonal generalization axes:

  • Spatial transfer: Evaluation on entirely disjoint maps with different nodes, edges, sparsity, and sizes, where all test node pairs are disjoint from training pairs.
  • Length scaling: Evaluation on paths strictly longer than any seen during training (max l(D_train) <= min l(D_test)).

Model architecture: 8-layer, 8-head LLaMA-style Transformers with RoPE positional embeddings, pretrained on 10M random-walk trajectories (1.3B tokens) to learn "map semantics" without shortest-path information.

Training paradigms:

  • SFT on shortest-path sequences with prompt prefix excluded from loss.
  • RL using Dr.GRPO (unbiased GRPO variant) with binary reward (valid shortest path or not), varied rollout counts (4, 8, 16).

Data design experiments: Systematic variation of budget allocation (questions vs. answers), coverage (fraction of unique nodes in training), and diversity (distinct endpoint combinations per node), with total dataset size approximately controlled across conditions.

Inference strategies: Greedy decoding, majority-of-10 (self-consistency), and shortest-of-10 (objective-guided selection).

Analysis tools: Probability decomposition of length scaling failure, probing experiments to verify distance-encoding in hidden representations, error-type classification (valid but non-shortest, did not reach target, invalid move).

Results & Highlights

  • Spatial transfer success: Even with only 16% of the training map covered, models achieve ~94% average success rate on three spatially disjoint test maps.
  • Length scaling failure: Success rate drops sharply once path length exceeds training maximum; the decomposition analysis shows Pr(Long | Sub1 & Sub2) drops from 0.811 to 0.589 across length groups, while Pr(Sub1 & Sub2) only drops from 0.846 to 0.796, confirming recursive instability as the dominant cause.
  • Data budget allocation: At low budget, allocating all to distinct questions with one solution yields 94% SR vs. 82% with fewer questions and 32 solutions each.
  • Coverage sets the ceiling: At high coverage (>=64%), all diversity levels converge to similar maximum SR (~0.90). Low coverage cannot be rescued even with exponentially high diversity.
  • Length rescue via slight exposure: Adding ~1% of slightly longer training examples (e.g., l=32,34 when training max is ~20) raises target-length SR to ~90%, while shorter or much longer additions provide little to no benefit.
  • RL stability vs. ceiling: SFT overfits after a few epochs; RL remains stable across 20 epochs but never exceeds the best SFT checkpoint. Error types are nearly identical between SFT and RL.
  • Inference-time scaling: Shortest-of-10 improves both SFT and RL, but RL models even with shortest-of-10 only match SFT greedy decoding performance.
  • MathQA validation: High Coverage achieves 0.82 accuracy on gain category vs. 0.70 for More Solutions; 0.77 vs. 0.68 on physics.

Strengths

  • Exemplary experimental control: The synthetic environment allows precise manipulation and isolation of factors that are entangled in natural benchmarks.
  • Principled analysis framework: The probability decomposition (Pr(Long) decomposition into recursive instability vs. hardness accumulation) is a clean analytical contribution that could be adopted in other settings.
  • Actionable data design principles: Clear, practical guidance on how to allocate training budgets for better generalization (coverage > diversity > solution multiplicity).
  • Comprehensive scope: Systematically covers training data, training paradigm (SFT vs. RL), and inference-time strategies within a single unified framework.
  • Honest assessment of RL limitations: The finding that RL stabilizes but does not extend capability frontiers is carefully supported with multiple lines of evidence (error-type analysis, extended training, inference-time scaling).
  • Cross-domain validation: The MathQA case study provides preliminary evidence that findings transfer beyond the synthetic setting.

Limitations

  • Scale gap: Core experiments use 8-layer models on synthetic tasks; it remains unclear whether the same dynamics hold at GPT-4 or Llama-3 scale with natural language reasoning.
  • Synthetic environment simplicity: Shortest-path planning, while theoretically canonical, captures only one class of compositional reasoning; real-world tasks involve more ambiguous objectives, noisy feedback, and multi-step abstraction.
  • Partial real-world validation: The MathQA experiment only validates data selection findings, not the length scaling failure or RL vs. SFT comparison in the real setting.
  • Limited architectural diversity: Only LLaMA-style Transformers with RoPE are tested; the role of architecture (e.g., state-space models, different positional encodings) is not explored.
  • "Slightly longer" rescue finding underexplored: The mechanism by which adding ~1% slightly longer paths rescues performance deserves deeper investigation -- is it a positional encoding effect, a curriculum effect, or something else?

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Do the same coverage-diversity dynamics hold for natural language reasoning tasks (e.g., mathematical proofs, code generation) at the 70B+ model scale?
  2. Can architectural innovations (e.g., recurrent/looped transformers, state-space models) mitigate recursive instability in length scaling?
  3. Is the "slightly longer paths" rescue effect related to RoPE positional encoding extrapolation limits, and would alternative position encodings change the length scaling behavior?
  4. How does the recursive instability finding relate to chain-of-thought reasoning in LLMs -- does the same composition failure occur when models generate long reasoning chains?
  5. Could curriculum learning strategies (gradually increasing problem complexity) combined with RL overcome the SFT performance ceiling, or is the ceiling fundamental to the data coverage limit?
