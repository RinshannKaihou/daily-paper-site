Momentum Further Constrains Sharpness at the Edge of Stochastic Stability

arXiv: 2604.14108

Date: 2026-04-17

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14108

一句话总结

本文揭示了带动量的随机梯度下降(SGDM)在随机稳定性边界(EoSS)处对小批量训练施加比朴素SGD更严格的曲率约束(BS ≈ 2(1−β)/η)，从而偏好更平坦的极小值，而大批量训练则表现出截然相反的行为(BS ≈ 2(1+β)/η)。

TL;DR

This paper studies how momentum changes the sharpness dynamics of SGD at the Edge of Stochastic Stability (EoSS). The key finding is batch-size-dependent: for small batches, momentum with coefficient β tightens the sharpness constraint from 2/η to approximately 2(1−β)/η, driving optimizers toward flatter minima; for large batches, the constraint relaxes to approximately 2(1+β)/η. The authors provide linear stability analysis reducing SGDM to vanilla SGD with an effective step size η_eff = η/(1−β) in the noise-dominated regime, derive the full 1D stability boundary, and support their theory with intervention experiments that destabilize or re-stabilize training by adjusting η, β, or batch size.

核心贡献

  1. 批量依赖的EoSS行为发现：首次系统性地揭示了动量对EoSS的影响依赖于批量大小——小批量下动量收紧曲率约束，大批量下反而放松约束。这一"定性翻转"现象在之前的研究中从未被报道。
  1. 线性稳定性分析：通过将SGDM在极小值附近线性化为二阶递推关系，证明在噪声主导区域SGDM等价于使用有效步长η_eff = η/(1−β)的朴素SGD，并推导出一维稳定边界公式：1/η_max = a/(2(1+β)) + σ²_h/(2a(1−β))。
  1. 干预实验验证：设计了三类失稳干预（增大η、小批量下增大β、减小批量b），每次都触发弹射（catapult）行为——损失尖峰后重新稳定，为"训练是否在稳定性边界附近运行"提供了诊断工具。

Key Contributions

  1. Batch-size-dependent EoSS characterization: The paper presents the first systematic study showing that momentum's effect on the Edge of Stochastic Stability flips qualitatively depending on batch size. In the small-batch regime, the Batch Sharpness plateau satisfies BS ≈ 2(1−β)/η (tighter than vanilla SGD's 2/η), while in the large-batch regime BS ≈ 2(1+β)/η (looser). This duality was previously unknown.
  1. Rigorous linear stability analysis: By linearizing SGDM near a minimizer as a second-order recursion, the authors reduce the stability question to mean-square stability of a scalar recursion. In the noise-dominated regime, SGDM behaves like vanilla SGD with effective step size η_eff = η/(1−β). The full 1D stability boundary interpolates between the deterministic and noise-dominated limits via 1/η_max = a/(2(1+β)) + σ²_h/(2a(1−β)).
  1. Intervention-based experimental diagnostics: Three classes of destabilizing interventions (increasing η, increasing β in small-batch settings, decreasing batch size b) each trigger catapult dynamics—loss spikes followed by re-stabilization at a flatter configuration. Stabilizing perturbations restart progressive sharpening. These experiments demonstrate that Batch Sharpness (not the Hessian's maximum eigenvalue λ_max) governs the transition behavior.

Methodology

The authors combine theoretical analysis with controlled empirical experiments:

  • Theoretical framework: Linear stability analysis of SGDM near a minimizer. The discrete-time dynamics are approximated by a second-order linear recursion. Mean-square stability conditions are derived by relating the recursion to the well-understood vanilla SGD case via an effective step size.
  • 1D stability boundary derivation: For a 1D quadratic model with curvature a and gradient noise variance σ²_h, the maximum stable step size satisfies 1/η_max = a/(2(1+β)) + σ²_h/(2a(1−β)), which interpolates between the deterministic limit (η_max = 2(1+β)/a) and the noise-dominated limit (η_max = 2a(1−β)/σ²_h).
  • Experimental setup: Training on synthetic quadratic models, Rosebrock-style problems, and practical tasks (CIFAR-10 with various architectures). Batch Sharpness (BS)—the expected directional mini-batch curvature—is tracked as the primary statistic rather than the Hessian's top eigenvalue.
  • Intervention experiments: Systematic perturbations of η, β, and batch size during otherwise stable training to probe whether the optimizer is operating near the EoSS boundary. Destabilizing perturbations trigger catapults; stabilizing ones restart progressive sharpening, providing causal evidence for the EoSS mechanism.

Results & Highlights

  • Small-batch regime plateau: For batch sizes below a critical threshold, BS_plateau ≈ 2(1−β)/η. Momentum with β > 0 makes this plateau strictly lower than vanilla SGD's 2/η, meaning the optimizer is constrained to flatter regions. For β = 0.9, the constraint tightens by a factor of 10.
  • Large-batch regime plateau: For large batches, BS_plateau ≈ 2(1+β)/η, which is looser than vanilla SGD. Here momentum allows the optimizer to remain in sharper regions, consistent with the classical effective-step-size intuition.
  • Qualitative flip: The transition from small-batch to large-batch behavior involves a qualitative reversal of momentum's effect on sharpness constraints. Practical CIFAR-10 training often operates in the intermediate regime between these two plateaus.
  • Catapult dynamics under intervention: Increasing η, increasing β (small-batch), or decreasing b all trigger catapults—sharp loss spikes followed by re-stabilization at lower BS. This confirms the optimizer self-organizes near the EoSS boundary and that perturbations push it past the instability threshold.
  • SGDN vs SGDM: SGD with Nesterov acceleration exhibits similar small-batch behavior but differs in the large-batch regime, showing that the specific momentum scheme matters.

Critical Assessment

The paper makes a genuine contribution by identifying the batch-size-dependent duality of momentum's effect on sharpness at EoSS. The theoretical analysis, while limited to quadratic approximations near minima, provides a clean and interpretable stability boundary formula. The intervention experiments are a clever diagnostic tool.

However, several limitations warrant consideration:

  • The linear/quadratic approximation near minima is a significant simplification. Real neural network loss landscapes exhibit far more complex geometry, and the extent to which the 1D stability boundary generalizes is unclear.
  • The practical relevance depends on whether real training actually operates in the EoSS regime. The authors show evidence for this on CIFAR-10, but broader validation across architectures, datasets, and training regimes (e.g., Adam, learning rate schedules) is lacking.
  • The gap between the "small-batch" and "large-batch" regimes—the intermediate zone where many practical training runs fall—is not fully characterized theoretically, only empirically observed.
  • The paper focuses exclusively on SGD-type optimizers. Whether similar batch-size-dependent phenomena appear in adaptive gradient methods (Adam, AdaGrad) remains unexplored.

Strengths

  • Novel and counterintuitive finding that momentum's effect on sharpness reverses depending on batch size
  • Clean theoretical derivation connecting SGDM stability to vanilla SGD via effective step size
  • Intervention experiments provide strong causal evidence for the EoSS mechanism
  • Comprehensive empirical validation across synthetic and practical settings
  • Well-connected to prior EoS/EoSS literature, building a coherent narrative

Limitations

  • Theoretical results rely on quadratic approximations near minima
  • Limited to SGD-family optimizers; no analysis of adaptive methods
  • The intermediate batch-size regime (where much practical training occurs) lacks a complete theoretical characterization
  • Experiments primarily on CIFAR-10; broader empirical validation needed
  • The distinction between Batch Sharpness and Hessian maximum eigenvalue as the governing statistic needs further clarification in practical contexts
