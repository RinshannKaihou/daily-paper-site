AIM: Asymmetric Information Masking for Visual Question Answering Continual Learning

arXiv: 2604.14779

Date: 2026-04-16

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14779

一句话总结： 本文揭示了视觉语言模型在持续学习中的结构不对称性导致语言解码器主导梯度优化、损害脆弱的视觉投影层，提出基于模态敏感度的非对称信息掩码方法（AIM），在VQA v2和GQA上取得SOTA的AP和AF指标，同时显著保持组合泛化能力。
TL;DR: This paper identifies the "Asymmetry Dilemma" in multimodal continual learning where the language decoder dominates gradient optimization and damages the fragile visual projector, and proposes Asymmetric Information Masking (AIM) that applies modality-specific masking ratios based on parameter sensitivity to achieve state-of-the-art performance on VQA v2 and GQA benchmarks.

核心贡献

  • 揭示多模态持续学习中的"不对称困境"（Asymmetry Dilemma）：通过实证分析发现，现代VLM中可训练组件存在严重的参数不对称性（语言解码器参数量是视觉投影层的100倍），导致传统对称正则化方法中的梯度主导效应，使视觉投影层受到严重干扰，组合推理能力出现灾难性退化。
  • 提出非对称信息掩码方法（AIM）：将可训练参数空间划分为视觉投影、跨模态对齐和认知推理三个子空间，对每个子空间分别计算Fisher信息，并基于子空间特定的分位数阈值生成二进制梯度掩码，实现模态感知的稳定性-可塑性平衡。
  • 引入最大聚合策略：使用递归最大值函数而非累加来聚合跨任务的Fisher信息，避免惩罚权重单调增长导致的过度约束，保持网络的适应能力。
  • 在VQA v2和GQA上取得SOTA表现：在标准测试和组合泛化测试中均优于现有方法，验证了非对称参数保护在多模态持续学习中的有效性。

Key Contributions

  • Identifies the "Asymmetry Dilemma" in multimodal continual learning: Empirical analysis reveals that modern VLMs exhibit severe parameter asymmetry (the language decoder has 100x more parameters than the visual projector), causing standard symmetric regularization to produce a gradient dominance effect that disproportionately damages the visual projection layer and leads to catastrophic collapse of compositional reasoning.
  • Proposes Asymmetric Information Masking (AIM): Partitions the trainable parameter space into visual projection, cross-modal alignment, and cognitive reasoning subspaces, computes Fisher information per subspace, and generates binary gradient masks using subspace-specific quantile thresholds to achieve modality-aware stability-plasticity balance.
  • Introduces max-based Fisher aggregation: Uses a recursive maximum function (rather than summation) to aggregate Fisher information across tasks, preventing monotonically increasing penalty weights that would over-constrain the parameter space and reduce plasticity.
  • Achieves state-of-the-art on VQA v2 and GQA: Outperforms existing methods on both standard and compositional generalization metrics, validating the effectiveness of asymmetric parameter protection in multimodal continual learning.
  • Demonstrates generalizability to large multimodal models: Shows that AIM scales to LLaVA-7B architecture with LoRA adaptation, achieving competitive performance without requiring specialized modular experts.

Methodology

AIM operates in three phases within a continual learning loop:

  1. Parameter Importance Estimation: After completing each task, the diagonal Fisher Information Matrix (FIM) is computed using a sampled subset (N=500) of the training data. The squared gradients approximate parameter sensitivity. A recursive max-based aggregation (Eq. 3: F_i^(k) = max(F_i^(k-1), F_{k,i})) replaces the standard additive approach to bound accumulated sensitivities.
  1. Asymmetric Mask Generation: The trainable parameter space is partitioned into three subspaces based on architectural function:
  • Visual Projection (Theta_vis): Lightweight region embedding and visual projection layers, acting as the critical information bottleneck.
  • Cross-modal Alignment (Theta_shared): Shallow-to-intermediate encoder layers (layers 0-7 in VL-T5), handling implicit cross-modal grounding.
  • Cognitive Reasoning (Theta_text): Text embeddings, deep encoder layers (8-11), autoregressive decoder, and prediction head -- the primary reasoning engine.

For each subspace m, a dynamic threshold tau_m is computed as the (1-rho_m)-th quantile of the Fisher distribution within that subspace. Binary masks M_i are generated where parameters with Fisher sensitivity above the threshold are frozen (M_i=0) and below-threshold parameters remain trainable (M_i=1). The key asymmetric configuration is: rho_vis=0.3, rho_shared=0.1, rho_text=0.5 -- meaning 50% of the most sensitive text parameters are frozen while only 30% of visual parameters are protected.

  1. Gradient Modulation: During backpropagation for new tasks, masks are applied via Hadamard product (g'_i = g_i * M_i) to selectively zero out gradients for frozen parameters. This is orthogonal to episodic memory replay, which is used as a complementary mechanism.

Results & Highlights

  • VQA v2 Standard Test: AIM achieves 43.35% AP (vs. QUAD's 39.25%, +4.10%) with AF of only 1.56%, the lowest among all baselines.
  • VQA v2 Novel Composition: AIM achieves 42.03% AP on zero-shot compositional generalization (applying learned skills to unseen visual concepts), approaching the Joint Training upper bound of 51.10%.
  • GQA Scene-Incremental: AIM achieves 37.51% AP with 5.53% AF, outperforming all baselines including VQACL and QUAD.
  • Fine-grained Composition (Table 2): AIM ranks first in all 5 "Seen" groups and 4 out of 5 "Novel" groups, with +2.03% AP improvement over QUAD on Novel compositions.
  • LLaVA-7B Extension: AIM achieves 48.15% AP on VQA v2 without specialized MoE architectures, outperforming VQACL in 7/10 tasks.
  • Ablation Insights: Swapping masking ratios (rho_text=0.3, rho_vis=0.5) increases AF to 2.59%, confirming that over-constraining vision harms adaptation while under-protecting text exacerbates forgetting. The max aggregation strategy yields higher AP (42.96% vs. sum's 42.96%) while maintaining comparable forgetting.
  • Exemplar-Free Setting: Even without episodic memory (M=0), AIM achieves 26.04% AP, outperforming MAS (22.34%) and LwF, demonstrating that structural parameter isolation works independently of data rehearsal.
  • Task Order Robustness: AIM yields average AP of 41.05% (sigma=0.96) across three task permutations, compared to Vanilla's 6.57% (sigma=4.69).

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The identification of the "Asymmetry Dilemma" in multimodal continual learning is a genuine and valuable insight. While parameter isolation (e.g., PackNet, Piggyback) and Fisher-based importance (EWC) are not new, applying modality-specific masking ratios based on architectural partitioning is a meaningful and well-motivated contribution. The max-based Fisher aggregation is a simple but effective tweak.
  • Rigor: The paper provides thorough empirical analysis, including Fisher information profiling, parameter shift measurements, layer-wise dynamics visualization, and a 10x10 task-by-task evaluation matrix. The ablation studies cover masking strategy, Fisher aggregation, memory size sensitivity, and task order robustness. However, the primary backbone (VL-T5) is relatively dated compared to modern VLMs, and the LLaVA extension is presented in the appendix without the same depth of analysis.
  • Reproducibility: The implementation details are comprehensive -- architecture (VL-T5 with T5-base), optimizer (AdamW, lr=1e-4), epochs (3), batch size (80), Fisher sampling (N=500), and masking ratios (rho_vis=0.3, rho_shared=0.1, rho_text=0.5) are all specified. The algorithm is clearly described in pseudocode. No code repository is mentioned, which is a minor concern.
  • Significance: The work addresses an important practical problem (catastrophic forgetting in VLMs) with a principled solution. The finding that standard metrics mask severe compositional degradation is particularly impactful for the community. The approach is lightweight (2.3% wall-clock overhead, 3.8% VRAM overhead) and orthogonal to existing replay strategies.
  • Red Flags: The masking ratios (0.3, 0.1, 0.5) are determined via grid search, which raises questions about whether these values generalize across architectures. The COMP evaluation is done only on the G1 split in ablations due to computational cost, which is understandable but limits fine-grained conclusions. The performance on GQA (37.51% AP) is still far from the Joint Training upper bound, suggesting room for improvement.

Strengths

  • Well-motivated problem formulation: the asymmetry analysis (Fisher sensitivity, parameter shifts, capacity imbalance) is thorough and convincing.
  • Clean and elegant method: modality-specific masking with quantile-based thresholds is simple to implement and interpret.
  • Comprehensive evaluation: standard metrics, compositional generalization, multiple benchmarks, LLaVA extension, ablations, and qualitative analysis.
  • Low computational overhead: only 2.3% additional wall-clock time and 3.8% VRAM increase.
  • Strong performance gains: consistent improvements across both AP and AF metrics on all benchmarks.

Limitations

  • Architecture-dependent partitioning: The three-way subspace division is manually defined based on VL-T5's architecture. Transferring this to other architectures (e.g., decoder-only LLMs) requires careful re-partitioning, as shown in the LLaVA appendix but not fully automated.
  • Hyperparameter sensitivity: The masking ratios require tuning (grid search over rho_vis in {0.1, 0.3, 0.5} and rho_text in {0.3, 0.5, 0.7}). While the paper shows a favorable operating region, the optimal values may vary across different VLM architectures and task sequences.
  • Limited scale of primary experiments: The main results use VL-T5 (224.5M parameters), which is a relatively small model. The LLaVA-7B extension in the appendix shows promise but lacks the same depth of analysis.
  • No theoretical justification: The choice of quantile-based thresholds and max aggregation is empirically motivated but lacks formal theoretical grounding (e.g., bounds on forgetting or plasticity).
  • Replay dependency: While AIM outperforms baselines even without memory (M=0), the gap between exemplar-free and memory-augmented versions is substantial (26.04% vs. 43.35% AP), indicating that the method still relies heavily on episodic memory for strong performance.

Relevance

本文提出的非对称参数保护思路对LLM训练稳定性研究有启发意义——它揭示了在多组件模型中，不同模块的参数敏感度和优化动态存在系统性差异，统一正则化会导致梯度分配不均。这种基于敏感度的选择性参数冻结策略可推广至LLM的持续微调场景，为解决灾难性遗忘和训练稳定性问题提供了结构化的新视角。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Can the asymmetric masking ratios (rho_vis, rho_shared, rho_text) be automatically determined through meta-learning or online sensitivity estimation rather than grid search?
  • How does AIM interact with other continual learning strategies such as experience replay with importance sampling, contrastive learning, or parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods (beyond LoRA)?
  • Would a soft masking approach (continuous gradient scaling rather than binary freezing) provide smoother optimization dynamics and potentially better performance?
  • Can the asymmetry analysis be extended to other modalities (e.g., audio-visual models, video-language models) where similar structural imbalances may exist?
  • How does AIM perform under more extreme forgetting scenarios, such as domain-incremental learning with large distribution shifts or task-free continual learning?
