> **一句话总结：** 本文提出一种基于条件分位数回归的自适应保形预测方法，通过对非一致性分数进行输入相关的归一化变换，在保持边际覆盖率保证的同时显著改善了LLM生成内容的条件覆盖率，适用于长文本生成和多选题问答场景。

> **TL;DR:** This paper proposes an adaptive conformal prediction method for LLMs that uses embedding-conditioned quantile regression to normalize nonconformity scores in a prompt-dependent manner, preserving marginal coverage guarantees while significantly improving conditional coverage across heterogeneous prompt categories in both long-form QA and multi-choice QA.

## 核心贡献

- 提出了一种新的自适应保形预测框架，通过在prompt嵌入上训练条件分位数估计器，对非一致性分数进行输入相关的归一化变换，实现提示级别的校准。
- 将保形分数变换方法（此前主要用于回归任务）首次扩展到大语言模型的长文本生成和多选题问答场景，支持选择性预测以过滤不可靠的声明或答案。
- 在理论上证明了该方法保留了分裂保形预测的有限样本边际覆盖保证，同时改善了条件覆盖率。
- 在多个白盒模型（Mistral-7B、LLaMA-3.1-8B、Gemma-3-12B）和多个领域上进行了广泛实验，证明自适应方法在条件覆盖率方面显著优于现有基线。

## Key Contributions

- Proposes an adaptive conformal prediction approach that learns a prompt-adaptive correction to conformity scores via embedding-conditioned quantile regression, enabling per-prompt calibration without explicit grouping or feature engineering.
- Extends conformal score transformation methods (previously studied mainly in regression) to long-form LLM generation and multiple-choice QA, naturally supporting selective prediction to filter unreliable claims or answer choices.
- Provides theoretical proof that the method preserves finite-sample marginal coverage guarantees of split conformal prediction while improving conditional coverage across heterogeneous inputs.
- Demonstrates consistent improvements over baselines across three white-box LLMs (Mistral-7B, LLaMA-3.1-8B, Gemma-3-12B) on diverse long-form QA categories and 16 MMLU categories.

## Methodology

The method builds on split conformal prediction and introduces an adaptive score transformation pipeline:

1. **Data Splitting**: The dataset is split into three disjoint subsets: D_cal1 (30%, for training the conditional quantile estimator), D_cal2 (40%, for calibrating transformed scores), and D_test (30%, for evaluation).

2. **Conditional Quantile Estimation**: On D_cal1, nonconformity scores V(x,y) are computed for each prompt-generation pair. A conditional (1-alpha)-quantile regressor (two-layer MLP with ReLU) is trained on pairs of prompt embeddings (extracted via multi-qa-mpnet-base-dot-v1, reduced to 32 dimensions via PCA) and nonconformity scores using pinball loss.

3. **Score Transformation**: On D_cal2, transformed scores are computed as V_tilde(x,y) = V(x,y) / tau_hat(x), dividing by the estimated conditional quantile to normalize scores across inputs.

4. **Conformal Thresholding**: The (1-alpha)-quantile of the transformed scores on D_cal2 is computed as the global threshold. At test time, claim-level uncertainty scores are normalized by the conditional quantile estimate and compared against this threshold.

5. **Task-Specific Nonconformity Scores**: For long-form QA, V(x,y) is the maximal uncertainty threshold such that all retained claims are factually correct, and Claim Conditioned Probability (CCP) is used as the claim scoring function. For multi-choice QA, the Least Ambiguous Classifier (LAC) score V(x,y) = 1 - p(x)_y is used.

## Results & Highlights

- **Long-form QA (2-category calibration)**: When calibrating jointly on "Biographies" and "Inventions", standard conformal prediction (Conformal Factuality) shows over-coverage for Inventions (~85% vs 80% target) and under-coverage for Biographies (~77% vs 80% target). The adaptive method brings both categories close to the 80% target.

- **Long-form QA (8-category calibration)**: Across all eight categories and three models, the adaptive method reduces coverage variability. For Mistral-7B, Landmarks coverage improved from 73.49% to 79.54% (target 80%) while reducing the fraction of removed claims from 82.00% to 80.34%.

- **Multi-choice QA (2-category)**: Marketing and Accounting show similar improvements -- adaptive calibration brings both categories closer to target coverage with lower variance.

- **Multi-choice QA (16-category)**: Dolan-More performance profiles show the adaptive method uniformly outperforms the baseline across both Mistral-7B and LLaMA-3.1-8B, achieving the best calibration error on a larger fraction of problem instances.

- **Claim Scoring**: Claim Conditioned Probability (CCP) consistently outperforms other uncertainty measures (Maximum Probability, Perplexity, Max Token Entropy, PMI, TokenSAR) across all models in PR-AUC.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: Moderate. The score transformation idea (normalizing by conditional quantiles) is not new in the conformal prediction literature (e.g., Plassier et al. 2025, Johansson et al. 2021). The novelty lies in adapting this technique to LLM generation tasks and demonstrating its effectiveness in this context. The key insight -- that a single global quantile is inadequate for heterogeneous prompts -- is intuitive and well-motivated.

- **Rigor**: High. The paper provides theoretical guarantees, uses proper data splitting (three-way), reports means and standard deviations over 10 random seeds, and employs Dolan-More performance profiles for rigorous comparison. The experimental setup follows established protocols from prior work.

- **Reproducibility**: Moderate to good. The methodology is clearly described with algorithm boxes and equations. The use of standard models and datasets (MMLU, LM-Polygraph) aids reproducibility. However, some hyperparameter details (MLP architecture specifics, training procedure for the quantile regressor) could be more thoroughly documented.

- **Significance**: The problem of conditional coverage in conformal prediction for LLMs is practically important. Improving per-prompt calibration has clear downstream value for deploying LLMs in high-stakes applications. However, the improvements, while consistent, are sometimes modest in magnitude.

- **Red flags**: The method requires a white-box LLM (access to token probabilities) and sufficient calibration data per category. The claim that conditional coverage is "improved" is empirically demonstrated but not theoretically guaranteed -- the paper honestly acknowledges this gap. The reliance on GPT-4o for claim labeling in the long-form QA experiments introduces an external dependency whose accuracy is assumed but not verified.

## Strengths

- Clean mathematical formulation that integrates naturally with existing conformal prediction frameworks.
- Strong experimental design: three models, two task types, multiple categories, proper statistical reporting.
- The approach is task-agnostic within the conformal prediction paradigm -- the same pipeline works for both long-form and multi-choice QA.
- The method naturally supports selective prediction, which has clear practical utility.
- Honest about limitations: acknowledges the gap between marginal and conditional guarantees.

## Limitations

- **Requires white-box access**: The method relies on token-level probabilities and internal model scores, limiting applicability to API-only models (e.g., GPT-4, Claude).
- **Calibration data requirements**: Effective conditional quantile estimation requires sufficient data per prompt type/category, which may be prohibitive for rare or novel domains.
- **Embedding quality dependence**: The approach depends on the quality of prompt embeddings and their ability to capture task difficulty. PCA reduction to 32 dimensions may lose information.
- **No theoretical conditional guarantee**: While marginal coverage is proved, conditional coverage improvement is only empirical.
- **Scope**: Only evaluated on factuality tasks (QA settings); generalization to open-ended generation, summarization, or code generation is untested.
- **GPT-4o labeling dependency**: Long-form QA ground truth relies on GPT-4o for claim labeling, which may itself introduce errors.

## Relevance

本文提出的方法直接关联LLM可靠性研究，通过提供具有统计保证的不确定性量化框架来检测幻觉，为LLM在实际部署中的可信度提供了理论工具。其自适应校准机制与LLM训练稳定性中的分布偏移问题密切相关，也为解释模型在不同输入上的置信度差异提供了可量化的途径。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does the method perform on open-ended generation tasks (e.g., summarization, translation) where claim decomposition is less straightforward?
- Can the conditional quantile estimator be improved with more expressive architectures (e.g., transformers instead of MLPs) or better prompt embeddings?
- What is the sensitivity of the method to the calibration set size and the D_cal1/D_cal2/D_test split ratio?
- How would the approach extend to black-box LLM settings where only text outputs (no probabilities) are available?
- Could adaptive conformal prediction be integrated into RLHF or preference-based training to improve factuality during model alignment?
- What happens when the test distribution shifts significantly from the calibration distribution -- does the embedding-conditioned quantile regression degrade gracefully?
