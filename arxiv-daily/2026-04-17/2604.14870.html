Curvature-Aligned Probing for Local Loss-Landscape Stabilization

arXiv: 2604.14870

Date: 2026-04-16

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14870

一句话总结： 本文将损失景观随样本增长的局部稳定化重新表述为观测问题，提出在经验Hessian的top-D特征子空间中探测损失增量场的曲率对齐准则 $\Delta_2^{(D)}$，理论上证明了在局部二次模型下该准则保持全空间 $O(k^{-2})$ 的均方衰减率，并在107M参数的decoder-only transformer上验证了仅占参数空间百万分之一（$D/N < 10^{-6}$）的子空间探针即可在数值噪声范围内复现全空间信号，闭式高斯矩估计器比直接Monte Carlo快约18,000倍。
TL;DR: This paper reframes local loss-landscape stabilization under sample growth as an observational problem and proposes a curvature-aligned criterion $\Delta_2^{(D)}$ that probes the loss increment field in the top-D eigenspace of the empirical Hessian. Theoretically, it preserves the full-space $O(k^{-2})$ mean-squared rate under a local quadratic model while replacing ambient-dimension curvature dependence with subspace dimension $D$. Empirically on a 107M-parameter decoder-only transformer, a subspace probe occupying less than one part in a million of parameter space ($D/N < 10^{-6}$) reproduces the full-space signal to within numerical noise, and the closed-form Gaussian-moment estimator is ~18,000x faster than direct Monte Carlo.

核心贡献

  • 统一稳定化准则族：将局部损失景观在样本增长下的稳定化重新定义为观测问题，引入由聚合阶数 $p$ 和探测分布 $q(\mathbf{w})$ 参数化的统一准则族 $\Delta_p$，将先前研究的逐点和各向同性均方准则纳入同一框架。
  • 曲率对齐子空间探针 $\Delta_2^{(D)}$：提出在训练解附近经验Hessian的top-$D$主曲率子空间中探测损失增量场，而非在全参数空间各向同性探测，利用深度网络损失景观的强各向异性特性。
  • 无速率损失的理论保证：在局部二次模型下，证明从 $\mathbb{R}^N$ 压缩到 $D$ 维曲率对齐子空间保持 $O(k^{-2})$ 均方衰减率，将环境维度曲率依赖替换为子空间维度 $D$ 的依赖（Theorem 2），并在同时可对角化假设下证明top-$D$选择在特征子空间对齐族中是极值的（Proposition 3）。
  • 可扩展估计器：开发基于Hessian-向量乘积的直接子空间Monte Carlo、二次Monte Carlo和闭式高斯矩估计器，在子空间构建后高斯矩估计器的计算代价仅为 $O(D^2)$，比直接Monte Carlo快约18,000倍。

Key Contributions

  • Unified stabilization criterion family: Recasts local loss-landscape stabilization under sample growth as an observational problem with a unified family of criteria $\Delta_p$ parameterized by aggregation order $p$ and probing distribution $q(\mathbf{w})$, subsuming pointwise and isotropic mean-squared criteria as special cases.
  • Curvature-aligned subspace probe $\Delta_2^{(D)}$: Proposes probing the loss increment field in the top-$D$ eigenspace of the empirical Hessian near a trained solution, leveraging the strong anisotropy of neural loss landscapes.
  • Rate-preserving geometric compression: Proves under a local quadratic model that restricting the probe from $\mathbb{R}^N$ to a $D$-dimensional curvature-aligned subspace preserves the $O(k^{-2})$ mean-squared decay while replacing $N$-dependent curvature constants with $D$-dependent ones (Theorem 2); identifies the top-$D$ eigenspace as extremal within the eigenspace-aligned family (Proposition 3).
  • Scalable estimators: Derives three estimators---direct subspace Monte Carlo, quadratic Monte Carlo, and a closed-form Gaussian-moment proxy---with the GM estimator costing only $O(D^2)$ after setup, achieving ~18,000x speedup over direct MC.

Methodology

The paper introduces a general stabilization criterion $\Delta_p(k+1) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^N} |\mathcal{L}_{k+1}(\mathbf{w}) - \mathcal{L}_k(\mathbf{w})|^p q(\mathbf{w}) d\mathbf{w}$, where $\mathcal{L}_k$ is the empirical risk with $k$ training samples and $q$ is a probing distribution near the minimizer $\mathbf{w}_k^*$. The key insight is that for strongly anisotropic neural landscapes, isotropic probing wastes mass on directions that contribute little to local deformation.

The curvature-aligned criterion $\Delta_2^{(D)}$ restricts probing to the principal curvature subspace $\mathcal{S}_D = \text{Im}(\mathbf{U}_D)$, where $\mathbf{U}_D$ contains the top-$D$ eigenvectors of the empirical Hessian $\mathbf{H}^{(k)}(\mathbf{w}_k^*)$. Under the local quadratic model derived from second-order Taylor expansion, the increment field reduces to a low-dimensional quadratic form parameterized by the scalar value gap $a_k$, projected gradient $\mathbf{c}_k \in \mathbb{R}^D$, and compressed Hessian difference $\mathbf{B}_k \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times D}$.

The theoretical analysis establishes that $\Delta_2^{(D)}(k+1) \leq \frac{12M_\ell^2 + 3\sigma^2 M_\mathbf{g}^2 + 3\sigma^4(D^2+2D)M_\mathbf{H}^2}{(k+1)^2} = O(k^{-2})$ under uniform boundedness assumptions (Theorem 2). Under stable principal directions (Assumption 2), a spectral closed form in terms of leading eigenvalue increments is derived (Corollary 4).

Three estimators are developed: (1) Direct subspace Monte Carlo sampling perturbations in $\mathcal{S}_D$; (2) Quadratic Monte Carlo using the quadratic surrogate; (3) Gaussian-moment estimator computing the closed-form expectation $a_k^2 + a_k\sigma^2\text{Tr}(\mathbf{B}_k) + \sigma^2\|\mathbf{c}_k\|^2 + \frac{\sigma^4}{4}(2\text{Tr}(\mathbf{B}_k^2) + \text{Tr}(\mathbf{B}_k)^2)$. All share the initial subspace construction via deflated power iteration on Hessian-vector products.

Results & Highlights

  • On a 107M-parameter decoder-only transformer (nanochat d6), a curvature-aligned probe with $D/N < 10^{-6}$ reproduces the full-space mean-squared stabilization signal to within numerical noise for perturbation scales $\sigma \leq 10^{-3}$.
  • The subspace-to-full-space ratio $\Delta_2^{(D)} / \Delta_2$ stays close to 1 across the full sample-size range for $\sigma = 10^{-4}$ and $\sigma = 10^{-3}$, but departs strongly for $\sigma = 10^{-2}$, revealing a clear regime split between local and non-local behavior.
  • The quadratic Taylor approximation error remains low and nearly flat up to $\sigma \approx 10^{-3}$, validating the local quadratic model in the regime where the subspace criterion is faithful.
  • The Gaussian-moment estimator is ~18,000x faster than direct Monte Carlo after subspace construction (evaluation stage), with no measurable loss of fidelity in the validated local regime.
  • All criteria ($\Delta_1$, $\Delta_2$, $\Delta_2^{(D)}$) decrease with sample size $k$, with mean-squared criteria being orders of magnitude smaller and decaying faster than the pointwise criterion.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The reframing of stabilization as an observational problem with an explicit probing distribution is a genuine conceptual contribution. The curvature-aligned subspace criterion and the rate-preserving compression result are non-trivial. However, the core idea---restricting to top eigendirections---is a natural extension of well-known Hessian anisotropy observations, and the theoretical results rely on a local quadratic model rather than the true loss landscape.
  • Rigor: Theoretical claims are precisely stated with clear assumptions (Assumption 1: uniform boundedness; Assumption 2: stable principal directions) and full proofs provided. The extremality result (Proposition 3) requires simultaneously diagonalizable Hessians with non-negative eigenvalue increments, which is a strong idealization. The $D^2$ term in the bound of Theorem 2 means the constant only improves over ambient-dimension dependence for moderate $D$, which the authors honestly acknowledge.
  • Reproducibility: Code is publicly available. The experimental setup (nanochat d6, 107M parameters, step 3500) is well-specified with details on precision (float32), autocast settings, and eigensolver choices. However, experiments are limited to a single small model and a single training checkpoint.
  • Significance: The practical value is somewhat narrow---measuring how the loss landscape deforms when adding one sample is a specialized diagnostic rather than a broadly applicable tool. The 18,000x speedup is impressive but comes after a dominant subspace-construction cost. The conceptual contribution (probing law as a design variable) may prove more influential than the specific criterion.
  • Red flags: The experimental scope is intentionally narrow (one 107M-parameter model, one checkpoint). The paper does not demonstrate how this criterion could be used to improve training or generalization---it remains a diagnostic tool. The $D^2$ dependence in the bound partially undermines the dimensionality-reduction benefit for large $D$. Assumption 2 (stable principal directions) is strong and its empirical validity is not verified.

Strengths

  • Clean theoretical framework that unifies pointwise and isotropic stabilization criteria under a single family with an explicit probing distribution.
  • The no-rate-loss result (Theorem 2) is elegant and well-proved: geometric compression preserves $O(k^{-2})$ decay.
  • The Gaussian-moment estimator provides a practical closed-form solution that is orders of magnitude faster than MC alternatives.
  • The paper is transparent about limitations, with a dedicated Section 7 discussing locality, bound tightness, and narrow empirical scope.
  • The spectral closed form (Corollary 4) directly connects stabilization to Hessian eigenvalue increments, offering interpretability.

Limitations

  • Inherently local: All theoretical results and proxy estimators rely on the local quadratic model, which breaks down for $\sigma > 10^{-3}$ as shown empirically. Not applicable to global loss landscape analysis.
  • Narrow empirical validation: Only one model (107M nanochat d6) at one training step (3500). No results on larger models, different architectures, or full training dynamics.
  • Dominant subspace construction cost: The eigenvector computation via deflated power iteration on HVPs dominates runtime, potentially limiting scalability to very large models.
  • No downstream application: The paper develops a diagnostic criterion but does not demonstrate how it can be leveraged to improve training, generalization, or model selection.
  • Idealized extremality result: Proposition 3 requires simultaneously diagonalizable Hessians with non-negative eigenvalue increments---a regime that may not hold in practice.
  • $D^2$ dependence in bound: The curvature constant scales as $D^2 + 2D$, which trades unfavorably against ambient dimension only for moderate $D$.

Questions for Further Exploration
