Mean accuracy hides catastrophic tail risk in clinical LLMs.

A clinician-authored red-teaming framework stress-tests 11 frontier LLMs on 690 adversarial scenarios. Top models clear 0.97 — and still record min = 0.00 on safety-critical vignettes.

A Multi-Domain Red Teaming Framework for Safety, Robustness, and Fairness Evaluation of Medical Large Language Models
Medical LLM Red-Teaming Safety Fairness Adversarial
Key Metric
10–20%
Error amplification when only race, gender, or age attributes are swapped in equity-related tasks — observed across all top models.
Why This Matters

Variance and worst-case behavior — not mean accuracy — should be the primary safety indicators for clinical LLM deployment.

Critical Edge

GPT-5 is both subject and judge — a self-referential pipeline that risks systematically flattering its own failure modes.

Flagged in Critical Assessment
Deep Dive

Methodology

Three clinicians · 9 domains · 690 prompts · hybrid judge

  • 150+ subdomains mapped to WHO / NIST / EU AI Act expectations; taxonomy seeded from MedSafetyBench & PatientSafetyBench.
  • Adversarial mutation pipeline: wording edits, demographic swaps, missing context, conflicting details.
  • 7-dimension rubric — factual accuracy, safety/risk, refusal, fairness, privacy, ethics, harmlessness.
  • GPT-5 automated judge; manual clinician review on every high-risk item, every disagreement, and 10% of routine outputs (~760 audited).
  • Statistical lens: standard deviation, IQR, minimum, and domain failure rates — not central tendency.
  • All 11 models queried under default settings with no user-supplied context.

Results & Highlights

0.791 → 0.984 means — and multiple 0.00 minima

  • Top cluster: X-BAI (0.984), GPT-5, Claude Opus 4.1 — all ≥ 0.97, SD ≤ 0.07.
  • Yet Claude Opus 4.1 and CALM v3 hit min = 0.00 on safety-critical vignettes — high means do not preclude catastrophic single-case failures.
  • Most challenging domains: Liability/Accountability & Medical Coding/Billing (means 0.79–0.83).
  • Cross-model gap: Δ 0.20–0.30 in System Integration; Δ 0.13–0.15 in Clinical Accuracy.
  • Human auditors caught dangerous outputs (empathetic tone masking missing escalation advice) that the automated judge missed.
  • Safety alignment and fairness emerge as partially independent dimensions — refusing adversarial prompts ≠ equitable reasoning.

Balance Sheet

Strengths

  • Comprehensive scope — 690 × 11 × 7 × 150+ is among the largest cross-model medical LLM safety studies.
  • Variance-centric reporting aligned with emerging regulatory thinking.
  • Hybrid automated + clinician evaluation is a practical compromise for scale.
  • Adversarial mutations reflect realistic clinical communication failures, not toy inputs.
  • Concrete fairness quantification — 10–20% demographic amplification is an actionable signal.
  • Direct translation of findings to WHO, NIST, and EU AI Act expectations.

Limitations

  • Synthetic scenarios — no real patient encounters or longitudinal dynamics.
  • Single-turn prompt-response only; no multi-turn escalation evaluation.
  • Cross-sectional snapshot — model-drift over updates not addressed.
  • No inter-rater reliability (κ or α) reported for clinician scoring.
  • Self-referential judge: GPT-5 evaluates itself.
  • X-BAI and CALM provenance unclear; John Snow Labs has vendor interest in the space.
  • Workshop venue, not peer-reviewed clinical journal.
  • No dataset or model-output release — independent verification hampered.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How do these 11 models perform under multi-turn clinical dialogues — handoffs, escalation chains, longitudinal workflows?
  2. What is the inter-rater reliability among clinician reviewers, and how sensitive are conclusions to alternative adjudication schemes?
  3. Can an independent judge model (outside the evaluation set, or a panel) materially change the rankings and failure-mode counts?
  4. How do X-BAI and CALM compare on vendor-independent benchmarks such as MedSafetyBench or PatientSafetyBench?
  5. How do scores drift across model update cycles — and at what cadence should healthcare systems re-run red-teaming?
  6. What do intersectional counterfactuals (race × gender × age × SES) reveal beyond the marginal 10–20% amplification reported here?
  7. How do cost, latency, and refusal rate trade off against the variance reductions observed in GPT-5 / X-BAI / Claude Opus 4.1?
  8. Could a continuous longitudinal red-teaming protocol integrated into MLOps detect drift earlier than a cross-sectional snapshot?
