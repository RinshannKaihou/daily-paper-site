Key metric

TRAINING-SIDE OVERHEAD · LLAMA 3.1 405B · $100M RUN

Down from the prevailing ~5×10⁵× estimate. Dominant cost is determinism (attention backward, 1.6–8.2%); Merkle computation adds 0.5–1.5%; weight storage is <0.1%.

Why this matters
!

Replaces self-reported training claims with a cryptographic proof that the GPU actually executed the committed architecture at the committed compute threshold — directly serving EU AI Act Art. 51 and any future international verification regime, while keeping the model architecture confidential.

Critical edge
✕

The 36-month timeline hinges on a single high-risk milestone — a ZK proof of backpropagation at ≥10⁶ params with bit-exact gradient match to GPU execution — and the headline ~90 constraints/MAC estimate is unverified: no reference implementation ships with v1.

Methodology

Five modifications to current training

  • MOD. 1 — Determinism. Bit-exact GPU execution via FlashAttention-2 deterministic mode, pinned cuBLAS, NCCL NVLS reductions. Cost: 1.6–8.2% on Llama 7B FSDP (8×H100), dominated by attention backward.
  • MOD. 2 — Pre-committed Merkle roots. Publish hcommit = H(arch_spec ∥ R(dataset) ∥ R(W₀)) before training; O(log n) batch/weight membership proofs.
  • MOD. 3 — On-the-fly Merkle. Concurrent GPU stream commits six tensor roots per layer per step (Poseidon, precompile 3). Production overhead 0.5–1.5%.
  • MOD. 4 — Network anchoring. Tier 1 physical TAP at wire speed; Tier 2 attested SmartNIC (e.g. BlueField DOCA). SHA-256 over inter-node collectives.
  • MOD. 5 — Weight rolling window. Llama 3.1 405B in BF16 → ~810 GB per snapshot; 100 steps → 81 TB.

Hint-and-verify zkVM

  • Host (trainer GPU) re-runs the challenged step from stored weights and supplies intermediate tensors as private hints.
  • Guest (proof checker) only verifies each hint against the committed Merkle root via path checks and 8 native precompiles (MAC chain, lookup, Merkle path, exp, sqrt, div, log, SHA-256).
  • BF16/FP32 MAC precompile: ~90 constraints/op, >99% of budget; encodes IEEE-754 RNE semantics into the Baby Bear field.

Strengths

What holds up

  • Addresses the real governance problem — built around EU AI Act Art. 51 / EO 14110 thresholds, not a paper-tiger version of them.
  • Architecture confidentiality preserved. arch_spec is a private input; auditor sees only H(arch_spec) in hcommit.
  • Four diversified trust roots. Pre-commit (crypto) + physical TAP (physical) + attested SmartNIC (crypto) + Merkle-committed computation (crypto). No single point of failure.
  • Concrete cost model — per-MAC constraints, per-step budget, proof size, and training-side overhead all quantified at frontier scale.
  • Honest open-problem catalogue. Thirteen independently-attackable items with named targets; critical-path risk on OP-3 is not hidden.
  • Cross-domain analogue reasoning. IAEA / OPCW / Montreal Protocol / BWC framings show direct policy engagement.

Limitations

Where it strains

  • OP-3 is the linchpin. A negative result on ZK proof of backprop at ≥10⁶ params with bit-exact gradient match forces a protocol redesign — and the 36-month window collapses with it.
  • No reference implementation, no end-to-end benchmark. v1 ships specifications and constraint counts only.
  • Deterministic attention is the real cost driver. 1.6–8.2% on 7B; at 405B with s ≥ 8K the 82% upper bound is closer to operational reality. DASH reduces but doesn't eliminate it.
  • Tensor parallelism unsupported. NVLS is non-deterministic above 128 MB; deterministic NCCL loses 64–89% bandwidth. Custom TP kernel is proposed, not specified.
  • Intra-node NVLink is invisible to both anchor tiers; OP-8 (GPU-resident trusted execution) is open and NVIDIA CC lacks the interface.
  • Tier 2 SmartNIC trust assumption (vendor firmware) is non-trivial for any international-verification regime.
  • SDC vs. adversarial: ~10²–10³ silent corruption events per 10⁷-GPU-hour run are statistically indistinguishable from adversarial injection at the single-event level (OP-9).
  • Scope: dense pre-training only. MoE (OP-10), RL post-training (OP-11), multi-datacentre (OP-12) are explicitly future work.

Questions for further exploration

Ten threads worth pulling

  1. OP-3 status. Has any team demonstrated a ZK proof of backpropagation at ≥10⁶ params in native FP arithmetic with bit-exact GPU gradient match? If not, where is the empirical ceiling — and what is the smallest viable alternative?
  2. Measure the MAC precompile. The ~90-constraint estimate is the largest single uncertainty in the cost model. Direct measurement on RISC Zero / SP1 / OpenVM at frontier scale would either confirm or invalidate the per-step budget.
  3. DASH at frontier scale. Can OP-4 (deterministic attention backward <5% at s ≥ 8K) be hit with a fixed-warp reduction tree, or does softmax-attention need an algebraic reformulation?
  4. Governing wire-to-tensor (OP-7). Three resolution paths (patched NCCL, canonicalisation shim, vendor "verifiable NCCL" mode) — which is most likely to win ecosystem adoption, and what's the standards-body role?
  5. Tier 1 vs. Tier 2 sequencing. Tier 1 is the long-term requirement but needs standardisation that doesn't exist; Tier 2 deploys today. What is the minimum certification standard for a Tier 1 device, and how should the rollout be staged?
  6. Multi-datacentre extension (OP-12). Frontier training increasingly spans multiple datacentres; the anchor model assumes auditor control over every inter-node link. Byzantine-robust aggregation, clock-skew, incentive alignment — all open.
  7. Ex-ante vs. ex-post balance. When is ex-post audit acceptable, and can the protocol support hybrid deferred disclosure of arch_spec?
  8. Distinguishing SDC from adversarial injection (OP-9). Empirical SDC distributions on current silicon are a prerequisite. Is there a fingerprint (DRAM row fault, thermal event, scheduled bit-flip) that separates natural from structured corruption?
  9. Smallest viable PyTorch → arch_spec converter (OP-13). ZKML's inference-only ONNX conversion is precedent; training adds backward-graph and distributed-annotation capture. Which PyTorch ops are in scope for a first cut, and which must be rejected?
  10. Architecture-to-deployment path. ~36 months for PoC vs. 6–10 years for verification-grade custom silicon. Which stakeholders — regulators, hyperscalers, GPU vendors, standards bodies — must be in the room to compress that window?
