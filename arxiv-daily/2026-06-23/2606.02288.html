Massive activation spikes aren't scalars — they are rigid bias vectors, geometrically engineered to drive attention sinks.

Sink tokens converge to K rigid prototypes (variance ~10⁻⁴), contrastively amplified by W_K, universal-attracted by W_Q, and projected into the spectral null-space by W_V — together implementing a no-op self-attention block. Why This Matters

First PTQ framework to match PrefixQuant on LLaMA-2/3.2 and Mistral at W4A4sKV4 — and the only one that generalizes to ViTs, where PrefixQuant's token-prefix heuristic fails.

Bias vectors are a geometric phenomenon of continuous representation layers, not a discrete-token one — so the same mechanism crosses modalities without redesign.

Critical Edge

"Strictly spike-free" overstates the claim: only sink-token spikes are handled. GLU-gate spikes in SwiGLU models remain unaddressed and can still wreck quantization.

DINOv2-B at W4A6sKV6 still scores 1.6% — barely above the 1.0% RTN baseline. Mechanism is silent on whether magnitude carries small but non-zero information.

Methodology · Section 3–4

A mechanistic claim, then a three-stage PTQ around it.

  • Bias Vector Hypothesis. Post-RMSNorm sink tokens converge to K rigid prototypes (LLaMA-2-7B: K=2; Mistral: K=3). Channel-wise variance ~10⁻⁴, about 10⁶× smaller than semantic tokens. Replacing dynamic sinks with the calibrated mean preserves FP16 accuracy — a clean falsification of "spikes carry semantics".
  • Geometric decomposition. Fuse γ into W_K/W_Q/W_V (à la QuaRot). W_K drives cos(k_sink, k_sem) from ~0 to ~−0.55 (contrast). W_Q drives cos(q_sem, k_sink) toward +1 (universal attractor). W_V projects the bias into the spectral tail (σ ≈ 0). The no-op block is implemented geometrically.
  • RoPE stability zones. Top-2 sink channels concentrate at highest group indices (i≈55 of 64) and form exact (i, i+d/2) pairs ~80% of the time — actively shielded from positional rotation.
  • INSERTQUANT pipeline. Detect sinks at the "Emergence" phase → clamp to zero → standard per-tensor static activation quantization → inject pre-computed k_sink = W_K·b, v_sink = W_V·b at every attention BMM → restore residuals at the "Disappearance" phase.
  • Calibration & eval. 64-sequence × 512-token Pile sample for sink templates. lm-evaluation-harness v0.4.2 on 8 zero-shot CSR tasks + WikiText-2 PPL @ ctx=2048. No fine-tuning, no rotation, no QAT — pure PTQ.

Strengths
  • Conceptual clarity. The Bias Vector Hypothesis is a one-page, falsifiable mechanistic claim — and the replace-with-mean sanity test is built into the paper itself.
  • Quantitative rigor. Concrete cosine-similarity numbers (−0.55, +1) and singular-value spectra replace the usual "spikes cause sinks" hand-waving.
  • RoPE stability zones are a genuinely new mechanistic observation connecting the spike story to positional encoding.
  • Cross-modality. Only PTQ method in the comparison that works on LLM and ViT with a single conceptual framework.
  • Practical overhead is essentially zero — 4.69% latency, 0.007% memory — a real deployment story, not just an academic claim.
Limitations
  • Restricted to spike-sink models. Models with explicit no-op machinery (GQA-only, certain MoE routing) are untested.
  • Static templates. Assumes bias vector b does not drift under fine-tuning, RLHF, or long-context adaptation — non-trivial and unverified.
  • Sink detection is heuristic. Magnitude-based at the "Emergence" phase (Appendix H); no learning-based detector compared. Aggressive token-merging in modern LLMs could break it silently.
  • No formal equivalence proof for clamp+insert vs. the original FP16 forward pass — the FP16-swap test is functional, not airtight.
  • Pile-only calibration. Multilingual and code-heavy deployments may drift prototypes, weakening the "universal attractor" assumption.
Questions for Further Exploration
01

Beyond pure Transformers. Does the Bias Vector Hypothesis hold for mixture-of-experts (Mixtral), SSM/Mamba hybrids (Jamba, Zamba), or reasoning models with extended chain-of-thought (o1-style, DeepSeek-R1)? Both the K-prototype count and the RoPE-stability argument assume pure self-attention.

02

Composing with QAT. Can the static template become a learnable bias added to the attention score, allowing end-to-end recovery beyond 4-bit? The PTQ-only comparison universe leaves this unexplored.

03

Detection under shift. How does the magnitude-based sink detector degrade under distribution shift, multilingual inputs, or code-heavy prompts? The current evaluation does not report false-positive / false-negative rates.

04

Other "no-op" mechanisms. Can the geometric framework also explain soft attention sinks in BERT-style models, or head-pruning behavior in recently distilled LLMs?

05

Formal equivalence. Can clamp+insert be proven (under reasonable assumptions) to reproduce the FP16 forward pass up to bounded error? Per-token deviation can still compound over depth.

06

Bias-vector drift under fine-tuning. Do the {b₁,…,b_K} prototypes stay stable under LoRA tuning or RLHF? If not, what is the cost of re-calibrating templates per checkpoint?

07

Hardware kernels. The "spike-free activation space" claim is compelling for per-tensor quantization — does it also unlock efficient INT4 GEMV kernels on commodity GPUs, removing the need for outlier handling?

08

Beyond vision/language. Spike-like phenomena have been reported in stable-diffusion cross-attention. Would INSERTQUANT's geometric framework transfer to diffusion and audio?
