# Does Compression Preserve Uncertainty? A Unified Benchmark for Quantized and Sparse LLMs via Conformal Prediction

> **一句话总结：** 本文构建了首个基于共形预测（conformal prediction）的统一基准，系统评估了 12 个 LLM 在量化与剪枝两类压缩范式下的不确定性表现，揭示了压缩会解耦精度与不确定性、大模型更能吸收压缩带来的不确定性膨胀、且这种膨胀常呈阈值式而非渐变三大核心发现。

> **TL;DR:** This paper presents the first comprehensive benchmark for evaluating the uncertainty of compressed LLMs through the lens of conformal prediction. Across 12 models (1B–70B), four compression paradigms, and five NLP tasks, the authors reveal three key findings: (1) compression frequently decouples accuracy from uncertainty; (2) larger models absorb compression-induced uncertainty much more effectively than smaller ones; (3) uncertainty inflation is often threshold-like rather than gradual. The work argues that accuracy-only evaluation is insufficient for assessing compressed-LLM deployment readiness.

## 核心贡献

- **提出新评估视角**：首次系统地以共形预测为透镜评估压缩 LLM 的不确定性，提供一种无分布假设、统计上严格的可靠性度量，超越单纯精度评估。
- **构建跨压缩范式的统一基准**：覆盖 4 个模型家族（Llama-2/3、Qwen-3、DeepSeek、Falcon）、1B 到 70B 参数规模的 12 个 LLM，涵盖权重-激活量化（RTN/AWQ/GPTQ、W4A4 旋转方法）、结构化与非结构化剪枝，在 5 个 NLP 任务上以一致协议隔离压缩的纯效应。
- **揭示三大关键发现**：压缩可能解耦精度与不确定性保留；模型规模越大越能吸收压缩带来的不确定性膨胀；不确定性膨胀常呈阈值式跳跃而非渐进式增长，为压缩 LLM 的安全部署提供具体指引。

## Key Contributions

- **A new evaluation perspective.** The first systematic study to evaluate compressed-LLM uncertainty through conformal prediction — a distribution-free, statistically principled framework that goes beyond accuracy-only metrics.
- **A unified benchmark across compression paradigms.** 12 LLMs from 4 families (1B–70B), four compression settings (W4A16 with RTN/AWQ/GPTQ; W4A4 with QuaRot/SpinQuant/FlatQuant; 50% unstructured pruning with Magnitude/SparseGPT/Wanda; 20% structured pruning with LLM-Pruner/SliceGPT), evaluated on 5 NLP tasks under an identical protocol that isolates compression effects.
- **Three findings with practical implications.** (i) Compression may decouple accuracy preservation from uncertainty preservation; (ii) Larger models tend to absorb compression-induced uncertainty more effectively; (iii) Uncertainty inflation is often threshold-like rather than gradual, offering concrete guidance for safe deployment.

## Methodology

**Conformal prediction framework.** The authors adopt split conformal prediction as a distribution-free uncertainty quantification tool. Given a pre-trained model, error rate α = 0.1, and a calibration set D_cal, the procedure computes a nonconformity-score threshold q̂ from the ⌈(1−α)(n+1)⌉/n quantile of calibration scores. At test time, the prediction set is:

$$C(X_\text{test}) = \{y \in \mathcal{Y} : s(X_\text{test}, y) \le \hat{q}\}$$

Two conformal score functions are used and averaged:
- **LAC (Least Ambiguous set-valued Classifiers)** — uses s_LAC(X, Y) = 1 − π̂(Y|X), producing smaller but potentially under-covered sets.
- **APS (Adaptive Prediction Sets)** — accumulates class probabilities in descending order, yielding more adaptive coverage but larger sets.

**Compression configurations.**
- *Quantization (two settings):* W4A16 (weights 4-bit, activations 16-bit) via RTN/AWQ/GPTQ; W4A4 (both 4-bit) via rotation-based methods QuaRot/SpinQuant/FlatQuant.
- *Pruning (two settings):* Unstructured 50% sparsity via Magnitude/SparseGPT/Wanda; Structured 20% sparsity via LLM-Pruner/SliceGPT.

**Tasks and models.** Five multiple-choice NLP tasks following Ye et al. 2024 — MMLU (QA), CosmosQA (RC), HellaSwag (CI), HaluDial (DRS), HaluSum (DS). Each task is standardized to six options (original 4 or 2 plus "I don't know" / "None of the above"). 2,000 instances per task, 50/50 calibration/test split. Models: Llama-2 (7B/13B/70B), Llama-3 (1B/8B/70B), Qwen-3 (8B/14B/32B/30B-A3B), DeepSeek-7B, Falcon-7B. α = 0.1 (90% target coverage). 8×H100 GPUs.

**Evaluation metrics.** (i) **Acc** — prediction accuracy; (ii) **CR** — coverage rate (sanity check for CR ≥ 1−α); (iii) **SS** — average prediction set size (primary uncertainty metric: smaller = more confident). All results averaged over LAC/APS and three prompting strategies.

## Results & Highlights

**Overall (Table 1):** W4A16 is the most stable regime — Acc and SS stay close to the FP16 baseline, especially for medium/large models. For example, Llama-2-70B under AWQ achieves 73.20 Acc / 2.18 SS versus 72.48 Acc / 2.17 SS at FP16. W4A4 is substantially more disruptive: QuaRot on Llama-2-7B drops Acc from 47.09 → 22.04 while inflating SS from 3.09 → 5.65. Pruning exhibits heterogeneous behavior — Magnitude pruning is consistently the weakest, SparseGPT and Wanda better preserve both Acc and SS, SliceGPT is unstable on Qwen3-8B (degenerate CR ≈ 12%).

**Finding 1 — Accuracy ≠ Uncertainty (Figure 2):** Scatter plots of ΔAcc vs. ΔSS show substantial dispersion rather than a tight negative-slope trend, indicating that compression frequently changes accuracy and uncertainty in different ways. Example: Llama-2-13B on CI under W4A16 AWQ slightly improves accuracy (59.63 → 61.17) yet inflates SS (2.83 → 3.13). Conversely, Llama-3.1-8B under 50% Wanda pruning drops QA accuracy from 62.93 → 45.97 while SS changes only 2.98 → 3.09.

**Finding 2 — Scale Absorbs Compression:** Under W4A4 QuaRot, average SS increase across tasks: +2.56 for Llama-2-7B, +0.72 for Llama-2-13B, +0.37 for Llama-2-70B — roughly a 6.9× reduction in uncertainty inflation from 7B to 70B. Same trend under pruning: Wanda 50% on RC inflates SS by +1.02 (7B) → +0.23 (13B) → +0.15 (70B).

**Finding 3 — Threshold-Like Inflation (Figure 3):** Wanda pruning from 0–50% sparsity shows non-linear SS trajectories. Llama-3.1-8B RC SS stays within 1.89–2.06 from 0–40% sparsity, then jumps to 2.53 at 50%; CI rises 2.75 (20%) → 3.04 (40%) → 3.42 (50%). Even 70B models are not immune — Llama-3.1-70B CI climbs 1.89 → 2.21 → 2.66 → 3.16 across 0/30/40/50% sparsity.

**MoE (Qwen3-30B-A3B):** W4A16 RTN is most stable (Acc 46.52 → 45.64, SS 3.32 → 3.09); SparseGPT 50% drops Acc to 34.07 with SS 3.39; SliceGPT 20% collapses (Acc 16.05, SS 5.57). MoE compression is highly method-dependent.

**Precision ablation (Llama-2-7B, Table 2):** Tolerates W8A8 and W6A6 reasonably (Acc 45–47%, SS 3.16–3.35), but W4A4 SpinQuant causes sharp degradation (Acc 26.26, SS 4.52) — confirming that uncertainty degradation is not gradual with precision.

**Practical recommendations:** (i) Don't use accuracy preservation as a proxy for uncertainty preservation; explicit SS evaluation should be standard; (ii) For aggressive compression, prioritize larger base models; (iii) Sweep across compression ratios — a configuration that looks stable at 30% may collapse at 40–50%.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty: Moderate.** The work does not propose a new compression method or a new uncertainty-quantification algorithm. Its contribution is a *systematic empirical benchmark* that combines two existing strands — conformal prediction for LLMs (Ye et al. 2024) and LLM compression. This is a useful but primarily cataloguing contribution. The threshold-like inflation finding (Finding 3) is the most novel empirical observation.

**Methodological rigor: Strong.** The conformal-prediction protocol is correctly implemented with exchangeable calibration/test splits, α = 0.1, and averaging over two score functions × three prompting strategies to reduce variance. Coverage Rate is verified as a sanity check. Results are reported across multiple model families, scales, and tasks. The work explicitly validates that CR ≥ 1−α holds, ensuring the prediction sets are valid.

**Reproducibility: Good.** Code is not explicitly linked in the text (no GitHub URL visible in the extracted content), which is a notable omission for a benchmark paper. The protocol is described in enough detail to re-implement. The 50/50 calibration/test split on 2,000 sampled instances per task provides reasonable statistical power, though 2,000 is on the smaller side for fine-grained SS comparisons.

**Findings interpretation: Mostly convincing.** Findings 1 and 2 are robustly demonstrated across many model/task configurations. Finding 3 is supported but somewhat under-quantified — a single Wanda-pruning sweep is shown, and the threshold locations are characterized qualitatively rather than via a formal phase-transition analysis. The 6.9× reduction in uncertainty inflation (7B → 70B) is a striking headline number derived from average SS deltas.

**Significance:** The practical recommendation "don't deploy compressed LLMs in safety-critical settings based on accuracy alone" is well-supported and important for the community. However, the multiple-choice evaluation setting (a known limitation acknowledged by the authors) limits immediate transferability to open-ended generation deployments.

**Red flags:**
- No open-source code link visible in the extracted text.
- Headline 6.9× reduction is averaged across tasks; per-task variance is high.
- Qwen3-30B-A3B GPTQ entry has degenerate CR ≈ 12%, but this is acknowledged with a † marker.
- Llama-2-13B AWQ "improving" accuracy on CI (59.63 → 61.17) is a small absolute difference that may fall within sampling variance of the 2,000-instance subsets.

## Strengths

- **Comprehensive scope.** First benchmark to systematically cover both quantization (weight-only and weight-activation) and pruning (structured and unstructured) under a single conformal-prediction evaluation protocol.
- **Statistical rigor.** Conformal prediction provides distribution-free, finite-sample coverage guarantees — far stronger than verbalized-confidence or calibration-error heuristics.
- **Actionable findings.** Three findings translate directly into deployment heuristics: (i) always measure SS, (ii) prefer larger base models when aggressive compression is required, (iii) sweep the compression ratio.
- **Honest limitations section.** Authors explicitly note that the multiple-choice formulation does not capture open-ended generation, and that the fixed α = 0.1 and 50/50 split may miss additional sensitivity patterns.
- **Multi-prompt averaging** (3 prompting strategies × 2 score functions) reduces prompt-sensitivity noise.

## Limitations

- **Multiple-choice only.** Conformal prediction on classification sets sidesteps the harder problem of free-form generation uncertainty (semantic equivalence classes, sequence-level entropy). Findings do not directly transfer to chat or code-generation deployments.
- **Sample size.** 2,000 instances per task (50% calibration) is modest; finer-grained SS comparisons across compression ratios may have limited statistical power.
- **Fixed protocol.** α = 0.1 and 50/50 split are held constant; varying these may surface additional patterns (e.g., low-α high-coverage regimes).
- **No code release visible.** A benchmark paper without an open-source repository substantially limits reproducibility and downstream adoption.
- **Coverage of compression methods.** Although representative, the benchmark does not include quantization-aware fine-tuning (QLoRA), KV-cache compression, or low-rank approximation — limiting transferability to those paradigms.
- **No MoE-specific analysis beyond Qwen3-30B-A3B.** Findings 1–3 are derived primarily from dense Llama-family results; whether the threshold-like inflation pattern holds for general MoE is unverified.
- **Threshold finding (3) is single-method.** The Wanda pruning sweep is the only sparsity-sweep evidence; whether QuaRot or structured pruning exhibit similar tipping points is not tested.

## Relevance

本文直接关联模型压缩与可靠性评估两大主题，与"压缩 LLM 是否可以安全部署于安全敏感场景"这一关键问题密切相关。对于关注量化/剪枝方法落地、边缘部署与不确定性量化交叉点的研究者而言，是一份具有较强参考价值的基准论文。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- **Open-ended generation under compression.** Can conformal language modeling (Quach et al. 2024) be combined with quantization/pruning to provide distribution-free guarantees on free-form outputs? Does the threshold-like inflation pattern transfer from multiple-choice to generation SS?
- **Phase-transition analysis.** Can Finding 3 be formalized — e.g., fitting a step function or changepoint model to the sparsity-vs-SS curve — to predict the "tipping sparsity" for a given model/task a priori?
- **QLoRA and PEFT under conformal prediction.** Does parameter-efficient fine-tuning on a quantized backbone restore or further degrade uncertainty properties?
- **MoE generalization.** Does the threshold-like inflation finding hold across more MoE architectures beyond Qwen3-30B-A3B, and how does expert routing interact with quantization noise?
- **Calibration–uncertainty decoupling.** Beyond SS, do expected calibration error (ECE) and conformal SS diverge under the same compression settings — i.e., is a model "calibrated but uncertain" a coherent regime?
- **Cost-aware deployment metric.** Could a unified utility function combining Acc, SS, and inference cost (FLOPs, memory) guide the Pareto-optimal compression choice per task?
- **Pre-deployment auditing tool.** Can the W4A16 → W4A4 → pruning sweep be packaged as an automated "uncertainty safety audit" for compressed LLM releases?
