Key finding · 一句话总结

Maximum Independent Set on a prompt similarity graph cuts LLM benchmark size by 25–48% while keeping rankings nearly identical to the full set.

Consistent and Distinctive: LLM Benchmark Efficiency via Maximum Independent Set Prompt Selection on Similarity Graphs
Denica Kjorvezir · Marko Djukanović · Ana Gjorgjevikj · Gjorgjina Cenikj · Tome Eftimov · 2026-05-31 · 2606.01400 LLM eval Graph theory Coverage bias
Key Metric
W = 0.997Kendall's concordance across 2,563 configurations · 66 LLMs · mean subset ρ ≈ 0.967–0.968
Why this matters

A graph-theoretic tool reframes coverage bias as a structural property of MIS — benchmark designers can diagnose redundancy by degree distribution and ship compressed evaluations that still rank LLMs correctly.

Critical edge

The coverage-bias correction claim rests on geometric intuition and one t‑SNE plot — no controlled bias‑injection experiment actually validates it.

Methodology

  • Encode every prompt with one of 6 embedding models (BGE, E5, GIST, Nemotron-8B, Qwen3-4B/8B).
  • Threshold θ set at percentile p ∈ {p10, p20, p50, p80, p90, p95} of 10k sampled pairwise distances.
  • Build graph G = (V, E); edge (u,v) iff d(eu, ev) ≥ θ.
  • Solve MIS with CPLEX (exact ILP), GREEDY (lowest-degree), Online-MIS (streaming), or ReduMIS (kernelised stochastic).
  • Evaluate via Spearman ρ vs. full benchmark and Kendall's W across seeds (≥0.90 = high concordance).

Results & Highlights

  • 99.2% of stochastic configs hit W ≥ 0.90; mean W = 0.997 ± 0.008.
  • p80 sweet spot: ρ = 0.992, W = 0.999, 37–45% reduction.
  • ReduMIS most stable (W = 0.9976, std 0.0076); all four solvers ≈ ρ 0.967–0.968.
  • MMLU-Pro / Omni-MATH easiest (ρ ≈ 0.987–0.993); IFEval hardest (ρ ≈ 0.920).
  • Large decoder embeddings (Qwen-8B 87.5%, Nemotron 86.2%) beat small encoders (BGE 80.4%, E5 79.2%, GIST 75.7%).
  • Best recipe: ReduMIS + Qwen3-8B + seuclidean + p80 → W = 1.000, ρ = 0.9959.

Strengths

  • Unification. Evaluation cost and coverage bias collapsed into one phenomenon (semantic redundancy), one tool (MIS).
  • Empirical sweep. 2,563 configs × 66 LLMs × 5 seeds is unusually thorough.
  • Structural guarantee. No embedding-space ball of radius θ/2 holds two selected prompts — something random / stratified sampling cannot promise.
  • Practical recipe. One knob (p80) gets a practitioner 99.6% rank fidelity at <2/3 cost.
  • Diagnostic framing of failures. 85.3% of low-ρ runs concentrated on GPQA + IFEval at p10–p20.

Limitations

  • No controlled bias-injection validates the coverage-correction claim.
  • No head-to-head vs. tinyBenchmarks, Anchor Points, IRT-based compressions.
  • HELM scores = ground truth, including its heterogeneous prompting artefacts.
  • IFEval outlier: mean ρ ≈ 0.92, the biggest gap below other benchmarks (0.971–0.993).
  • English-only, single-turn, text-only — multilingual, multi-turn, code, safety untested.
  • Reproducibility limited to "code available upon request".

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Does MIS beat IRT-based methods (tinyBenchmarks, Zhou 2026, Chen 2025) at matched subset sizes on the same 66 LLMs?
  • Can a controlled bias-injection experiment validate that MIS recovers an un-biased ranking?
  • Why does IFEval behave so differently — discretised scores, or surface-form ≠ capability similarity?
  • Does the framework transfer to multi-turn dialogue, code, multilingual, and safety benchmarks?
  • Can adaptive, data-driven per-benchmark thresholds replace the fixed p10–p95 grid?
  • How does MIS-subset fidelity track the LLM frontier over time (cf. Zhang et al. 2026)?
  • What does the full-benchmark degree distribution reveal about structural benchmark quality — before any LLM is run?
  • Is coverage uniformity empirically superior to topic- or difficulty-stratified sampling?
  • Would a domain-specialised embedding (math-tuned for Omni-MATH) close the remaining gap to the full benchmark?
