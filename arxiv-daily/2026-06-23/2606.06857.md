> **一句话总结：** 提出"增强稀疏编码模型"(Augmented Sparse Encoding Models)，用 Gemma-2-2B 的 JumpReLU/Matryoshka 稀疏自编码器(SAE)特征叠加显式 surprisal，在 7T fMRI 数据上同时预测并解释人脑对语言的体素响应；发现大脑与语言模型的对齐主要由 SAE 中"前 0.4%"的通用初级特征承担，而细粒度特征贡献很小。

> **TL;DR:** Lepori, Kay & Tuckute introduce **Augmented Sparse Encoding Models**, which replace the dense residual-stream features used in standard LM-brain encoding studies with sparse, interpretable features extracted by pretrained **sparse autoencoders (SAEs)** on Gemma-2-2B, augmented with a dedicated **surprisal** feature. On a 7T fMRI dataset of 8 participants listening to 200 sentences, the resulting encoding models (i) match the predictive accuracy of dense-residual baselines, (ii) cleanly dissociate processing-difficulty-driven voxels (captured by surprisal alone) from content-driven voxels (captured by SAE features), (iii) reveal a previously-uncharacterized voxel population tuned to "people-related" content (pronouns, social actions, emotions) located mostly outside the classical language network, and (iv) demonstrate that the fronto-temporal language network draws on a **shared, mostly general** set of SAE features across regions and individuals. Most strikingly, only the **first 0.4% of the Matryoshka SAE hierarchy** (the most general features) is needed to outperform all finer-grained feature bins, suggesting brain-LM alignment reflects a deep overlap in the *general* dimensions of language representation, not the multiplicity of fine-grained LM features.

## 核心贡献

- 提出"**增强稀疏编码模型 (Augmented Sparse Encoding Models)**"：用 SAE 稀疏特征 + 显式 surprisal 同时预测和解释体素响应，摆脱"用黑箱对黑箱"的批评。
- 在 7T fMRI 上**恢复**先前关于"加工难度体素"与"具体-抽象内容体素"的解释，并在 32k 维 SAE 特征中找到跨 8 名被试稳定共享的签名特征子集 (e.g., Feat. #79 scenery, #40 emotions, #94 people, #389 questions)。
- 通过**体素-到-体素特征迁移分析**发现"幽灵 (Ghost) 体素"中存在一个紧凑亚群（32 个体素，43.8% 位于角回）专门响应**人物相关内容**——传统难度/抽象度主成分无法捕获此群体。
- 证明**前额-颞叶语言网络**各区域共享一个**共同的特征基底**：跨 fROI 的体素预测迁移几乎与 fROI 内迁移相当 (0.50 vs 0.51)，并通过特征流行度/熵分析识别出一个跨被试共享的"核心特征集"加上大量被试特异特征。
- 关键洞察：**脑-LM 对齐主要由 Matryoshka SAE 层次结构中最通用的 0.4% 特征承载** (feature indices < 128)，更细粒度特征虽数量更多反而预测能力更弱——说明对齐对应的是"通用语言维度"而非 LM 庞大特征空间中的高维细节。

## Key Contributions

- A new encoding framework (**Augmented Sparse Encoding Models**) that substitutes dense residual-stream LM features with sparse, interpretable SAE features, while retaining a dedicated **surprisal** predictor for processing difficulty.
- Empirical validation on a 7T fMRI dataset (8 participants, 200 sentences × 3 reps): SAE-based encoding matches dense-residual accuracy, recovers prior interpretations of difficulty-tuned and concrete-abstract-tuned voxel populations, and yields novel **voxel-level (rather than PC-level) interpretations**.
- Discovery of a **"Ghost" voxel subpopulation** (32 voxels; mostly in angular gyrus / temporoparietal junction area) that is reliably driven by people-related content (pronouns, social actions, emotions) — a dimension orthogonal to processing difficulty and concrete-abstract content.
- Demonstration that the **fronto-temporal language network is organized around a shared feature basis** with graded cross-participant variation: within-fROI ≈ cross-fROI voxel predictivity (0.51 vs 0.50), with a small cross-subject core and a larger subject-specific tail (828 unique features selected in total).
- A novel **hierarchical-alignment claim**: brain responses are best explained by the **most general** Matryoshka SAE features (the first 0.4% of the hierarchy) rather than by the much larger pool of fine-grained features, implying a deeper correspondence between brain and LM representations than predictive accuracy alone would suggest.

## Methodology

**Brain data.** 7T fMRI responses from 8 proficient English speakers listening to 200 linguistically diverse sentences (3 reps each; BOLD betas per voxel per trial; NCSNR > 0.4). Restricted to left hemisphere on fsaverage surface. Two voxel selection schemes: (i) **PC-derived subtypes** following Tuckute et al. 2025 — Hard-to-Process, Easy-to-Process, Abstract, Concrete, and a low-loading "Ghost" subtype; (ii) **canonical language fROIs** (IFG, IFGorb, MFG, AntTemp, PostTemp) from Fedorenko et al. 2010 (top 10% significant voxels). An independent 3T dataset is used for replication in Appendix F.

**Feature spaces.**
- **Surprisal:** scalar per sentence — mean of Gemma-2-2B's negative log-probabilities over tokens.
- **Residual stream:** mean of layer 12 hidden states across tokens (2,304-dim; the dense baseline).
- **JumpReLU SAE (Gemma Scope):** 16.4k-dim sparse features at layer 12 — a standard high-performing SAE.
- **Matryoshka SAE (chanind et al. 2025):** 32k-dim features trained with hierarchical / nested structure (5 nested sets of sizes 128, 512, 2048, 8192, 32768) so that early features capture **general, primary** LM variance and later features capture **progressively finer-grained, idiosyncratic** structure. Layer 14 replication in Appendix B.

**Encoding pipeline.** A two-stage procedure: **LASSO feature selection** (5-fold CV), then **Ridge refit** with surprisal always included. Predictivity is reported as mean Fisher-z-transformed cross-validated correlation, noise-ceiling normalized at the voxel level. For voxel-to-voxel generalization analyses, a sign-constrained Ridge regression is used so that features are forced to operate "the same way" across source and target voxels (matching the "directional feature agreement" criterion).

## Results & Highlights

- **Finding 1 (SAE ≈ residual predictivity).** Matryoshka SAEs match dense-residual accuracy across all four PC-derived subtypes; JumpReLU is slightly worse; all feature spaces beat a shuffled control. On average, ~18 Matryoshka features are selected per voxel.
- **Finding 2 (Difficulty vs content dissociation).** Hard-to-Process voxels are captured by surprisal alone with no benefit from LM/SAE features. Abstract and Concrete voxels benefit significantly from SAE features above and beyond surprisal (p < 0.001) — a voxel-level replication of prior PC-level findings.
- **Finding 3 (Distinct, stable feature subspaces).** Abstract voxels load on **emotion-positive / scenery-negative** features (Feat. #40, #79), while Concrete voxels load on the **opposite-signed** features. A small signed feature set is consistently selected across all 8 participants despite being drawn from a 32k-feature basis (e.g., Feat. #79 is selected in 156/160 abstract voxels).
- **Finding 4 (Ghost subpopulation = "people" tuning).** A coherent cluster of 32 voxels (out of 160 Ghost voxels) is predicted by **people-related features** (Feat. #40 emotions, #94 people/actions, #49 pronouns). 43.8% of the cluster sits in the angular gyrus region near the TPJ, 15.6% in anterior temporal cortex, 12.5% in posterior temporal cortex; 37.5% of cluster voxels come from a single participant. The authors are careful to flag that this anatomical interpretation is post hoc.
- **Finding 5 (Frontal vs temporal divide).** MFG and IFGorb are well explained by surprisal alone; IFG, AntTemp, and PostTemp show significant boosts from content features (p < 0.001). Replicated in an independent 3T dataset.
- **Finding 6 (Shared language-network code).** Cross-fROI voxel-to-voxel generalization ≈ within-fROI generalization (0.50 vs 0.51). Cross-participant ≈ within-participant (0.49 vs 0.61). A prevalence-entropy plot reveals four regimes: a small broadly-shared core (Feat. #79, #44, #40, #94, #389, #1916, #82, #71), a broad middle, a less-prevalent but still cross-subject set, and a participant-specific tail.
- **Finding 7 (General features drive alignment).** Encoding models preferentially load on the **first 128 Matryoshka features** (0.4% of the basis). Restricted-bin analysis: the general bin (0–128) outperforms every other single bin (128–512, 512–2048, 2048–8192, 8192–32768) and even outperforms the union of all finer bins (Appendix I). Not driven by inactivity in fine bins (Appendix J). Replicated for all 4 PC-derived subtypes.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty.** The paper's core conceptual move — using **interpretable SAE features instead of dense residual states** for brain encoding — is timely and well-motivated. The Matryoshka hierarchy is a clever choice because it allows the authors to ask *which level of feature abstraction* aligns with the brain, a question that monolithic SAE feature spaces cannot answer. The "directional signed-feature generalization" analysis for identifying coherent voxel subpopulations is a clean, novel contribution that goes beyond simple cross-region regression.

**Rigor.** The work is generally well-controlled: shuffled-control baselines, noise-ceiling normalization, 5-fold cross-validation, layer-12/layer-14 replication, and an independent 3T dataset for the difficulty-vs-content dissociation. Sign-constrained Ridge regression is a sensible choice for cross-voxel generalization. The p-values reported are inferential, and effect sizes (Δ predictivity) are quoted alongside them.

**Reproducibility concerns.**
- The "Ghost" subpopulation is **strongly participant-driven** (37.5% of voxels from a single participant, with the next two contributing 28.1% and 18.8%). The claim of a coherent "people-centered" tuning therefore rests on a small, imbalanced sample. The authors are transparent about this but it is still a soft claim.
- 8 participants × 200 sentences is a respectable but modest sample. Many voxels were selected from a single participant's anatomy; cross-participant generalization is non-trivial but presented as evidence of a "shared code." The within-vs-cross-participant gap (0.61 vs 0.49) suggests individual variability remains substantial.
- The "general features" claim is striking but depends on a single architecture (Matryoshka SAEs on Gemma-2-2B). It would be strengthened by replication with other SAEs and other base LMs, and by showing that the same general features drive other cognitive domains (vision, motor).
- The link between SAE feature interpretations (from max-activating examples on Neuronpedia) and brain interpretation inherits all the standard interpretability critiques of SAEs (opacity, polysemanticity, lack of causal relevance). The authors acknowledge this and note that for their purpose only an "unsupervised, interpretable feature basis" is required.

**Significance.** High. This is a substantive, carefully executed contribution that meaningfully moves brain-LM alignment from "predictive accuracy" to "interpretive claim." The hierarchical-alignment result is a genuinely surprising finding with implications for the entire LM-brain-alignment literature: if general features (0.4% of the basis) suffice, then the alignment story is about shared **abstract** representational dimensions, not high-dimensional mimicry.

**Red flags / overclaiming.**
- The post-hoc anatomical interpretation of the Ghost cluster (TPJ / theory-of-mind region) is presented cautiously but risks being over-interpreted by readers.
- "Nontrivial correspondence between brain and LM language representation" is a strong philosophical claim; the empirical finding is that a tiny set of general LM features predicts voxel responses, which is a much narrower claim about **predictive alignment**, not about mechanistic equivalence.
- "Sparse features can predict voxels as accurately as dense residual features" is supported in Fig. 2A but the effect size is small and Matryoshka is "very similar" rather than superior — the language could be read as overclaiming.

## Strengths

- **Conceptual clarity.** The framework cleanly separates processing-difficulty signal (via surprisal) from content signal (via SAE features), an issue muddled in prior dense-residual encoding work.
- **Hierarchical generalization test.** Asking which SAE hierarchy level aligns with the brain is a creative, falsifiable question with a striking answer.
- **Voxel-level (not fROI-averaged) interpretability.** The directional signed-feature generalization analysis extracts interpretable structure at single-voxel resolution.
- **Replication.** An independent 3T dataset with different participants, field strength, and paradigm is used to validate the difficulty-vs-content dissociation.
- **Humorless honesty about limitations.** The authors explicitly flag the participant imbalance in the Ghost cluster, the post-hoc nature of the anatomical interpretation, and the dependency on SAE-feature interpretability.

## Limitations

- **Modest sample:** 8 participants (with one contributing disproportionately) and only 200 stimuli limit statistical power and raise concerns about generalization of the "people-centered" subpopulation finding.
- **SAE dependence:** All interpretability claims inherit the limitations of the chosen SAEs (Gemma-2-2B + JumpReLU + Matryoshka). JumpReLU features in particular are noted to be less interpretable.
- **Single LM:** All conclusions are conditioned on Gemma-2-2B; cross-LM generality is unverified.
- **Left-hemisphere only:** No claims about right-hemisphere or bilateral language processing.
- **Voxel-level coarseness:** As the authors note, a voxel's average of many neurons may prefer broad features for trivial reasons; the paper's own discussion entertains this concern but does not rule it out.
- **No functional localizer for social cognition:** The Ghost cluster is interpreted as "people-related" via the SAE-feature lens; without a theory-of-mind localizer, the social-cognition interpretation is inferential.
- **No causal manipulation:** The framework is purely correlational; no stimulus-design or perturbation experiment is used to test the causal role of specific features.
- **Sentences are short and preselected:** The 200 sentences are linguistically diverse but not naturalistic; generalization to narrative comprehension is implicit.

## Relevance

与用户的研究兴趣**强相关**：本研究处于"语言模型表征 × 神经科学"和"机制可解释性"两大方向的交汇点——前者是用户关注的核心主题之一，后者通过 SAE / 稀疏字典学习这一关键技术切入，与用户对 LLM 内部表征的解读兴趣直接相关。该工作还展示了如何将 mechanistic-interpretability 工具（SAE、Matryoshka 层次）迁移到跨学科的脑-模型对齐研究，对关注 interpretability 在 LLM 分析中实际应用的研究者具有方法论参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the "general features drive alignment" result replicate with **other SAE architectures** (e.g., TopK, Cross-Layer Transcoders) and **other base LMs** (Llama, Qwen, Mistral) of comparable size?
- Can the "directional signed-feature generalization" framework be used as a **voxel-parcellation tool** that is purely feature-driven, complementing anatomy-based parcellation?
- Does the Matryoshka hierarchy also explain the alignment between LM representations and **intracranial ECoG / MEG** data, which has finer temporal resolution and more localized signals than fMRI voxels?
- How would **narrative-length stimuli** (vs 200 isolated sentences) change the prevalence of fine-grained features? The current 200-sentence design may under-sample low-prevalence, fine-grained features.
- Can the people-centered Ghost subpopulation be **reliably identified** with a larger and more balanced cohort, and does it correspond to a known social-cognition localizer (e.g., ToM, mentalizing)?
- Can the framework support **closed-loop / neural-control** experiments where stimuli are designed to drive targeted voxel subpopulations (as suggested by the authors)?
- How robust is the "shared code" claim to **cross-language** replication (Mandarin, Spanish listeners)?
- Does **ablating** the most-prevalent shared features from the LM causally affect downstream task performance in a way that mirrors the predicted voxel-level loss?
