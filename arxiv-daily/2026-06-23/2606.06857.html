Brain–LM alignment is carried by just 0.4% of the most general features in a Matryoshka SAE — not by fine-grained ones.

Interpreting Brain Responses to Language with Sparse Features from Language Models ↗

Michael A. Lepori · Kendrick Kay · Greta Tuckute · Brown CS · U. Minnesota · Kempner Institute, Harvard · 7T fMRI · 8 participants · 200 sentences

01KEY METRIC
0.4%The first 128 features of a 32k-dim Matryoshka SAE beat every finer-grained bin on voxel prediction.
02WHY THIS MATTERS

It reframes brain–LM alignment from high-dimensional mimicry to a shared, abstract representational core — pushing interpretability beyond predictive accuracy toward a genuine mechanistic claim.

03CRITICAL EDGE

⚠The whole "general features drive alignment" result is conditioned on a single architecture — Matryoshka SAE on Gemma-2-2B — with no cross-LM or cross-SAE replication.

方法Methodology

Brain data. 7T fMRI from 8 English speakers listening to 200 sentences (3 reps each; BOLD betas). Voxels restricted to the left fsaverage hemisphere. Two selection schemes: (i) PC-derived subtypes — Hard-to-Process, Easy-to-Process, Abstract, Concrete, Ghost — following Tuckute et al. 2025; (ii) canonical language fROIs (IFG, IFGorb, MFG, AntTemp, PostTemp) from Fedorenko et al. 2010. Independent 3T replication dataset (Appendix F).

Feature spaces. (a) Surprisal — mean negative log-prob of tokens from Gemma-2-2B; (b) dense residual stream, mean of layer-12 hidden states (2,304-d baseline); (c) JumpReLU SAE (Gemma Scope, 16.4k features, layer 12); (d) Matryoshka SAE (chanind et al. 2025, 32k features, layer 14 replication in Appendix B) with 5 nested sets {128, 512, 2048, 8192, 32768} ordered from general to fine-grained.

Pipeline. Two-stage: LASSO feature selection (5-fold CV) → Ridge refit with surprisal always included. Predictivity reported as mean Fisher-z cross-validated correlation, noise-ceiling normalized at the voxel level. Sign-constrained Ridge for voxel-to-voxel generalization (directional feature agreement).

结果Results & Highlights

F1SAE ≈ residual predictivity

Matryoshka matches dense-residual accuracy across all 4 PC subtypes; JumpReLU is slightly worse. ~18 Matryoshka features selected per voxel.

F2Difficulty vs content dissociation

Hard-to-Process voxels are captured by surprisal alone. Abstract & Concrete voxels gain significantly from SAE features beyond surprisal (p < 0.001) — voxel-level replication of prior PC-level findings.

F3Stable signed subspaces

Abstract voxels load on emotion-positive / scenery-negative features (Feat. #40, #79); Concrete voxels load on the opposite-signed set. A small signed set reproduces across all 8 participants (Feat. #79 in 156/160 abstract voxels).

F4"Ghost" subpopulation ≈ people

32 voxels (of 160 Ghost) driven by people-related features (#40 emotions, #94 people/actions, #49 pronouns). 43.8% in angular gyrus / TPJ; 37.5% from a single participant — flagged as a soft claim.

F5Frontal vs temporal divide

MFG & IFGorb are explained by surprisal alone; IFG, AntTemp, PostTemp gain from content features (p < 0.001). Replicated in independent 3T data.

F6Shared language-network code

Cross-fROI voxel-to-voxel generalization ≈ within-fROI (0.50 vs 0.51). Cross-participant ≈ 0.49 vs within 0.61. Prevalence–entropy plot reveals a small broadly-shared core (#79, #44, #40, #94, #389, #1916, #82, #71) plus a participant-specific tail.

F7 · KEYGeneral features drive alignment

Encoding models preferentially load on the first 128 Matryoshka features (0.4%). The general bin (0–128) outperforms every other single bin (128–512, 512–2048, 2048–8192, 8192–32768) and even beats the union of all finer bins (Appendix I). Not driven by inactivity in fine bins (Appendix J). Replicated for all 4 PC-derived subtypes.

Strengths

  • Conceptual clarity. Cleanly separates processing-difficulty (surprisal) from content signal (SAE features) — an issue muddled in dense-residual work.
  • Hierarchical test. Asks which level of SAE abstraction aligns with the brain — a creative, falsifiable question with a striking answer.
  • Voxel-level interpretability. Directional signed-feature generalization extracts structure at single-voxel resolution, not fROI averages.
  • Replication. Independent 3T dataset with new participants validates the difficulty-vs-content dissociation.
  • Honest about limits. Flags participant imbalance, post-hoc anatomy, and SAE-interpretability caveats up front.

Limitations

  • Modest sample. 8 participants, one contributing 37.5% of the Ghost cluster — generalization of "people-centered" tuning is fragile.
  • SAE dependence. All interpretations inherit Gemma-2-2B + JumpReLU + Matryoshka quirks; JumpReLU features are less interpretable.
  • Single LM. Conclusions unverified across Llama / Qwen / Mistral.
  • Left hemisphere only. No claims about right-hemisphere or bilateral language.
  • Voxel coarseness. A voxel averages many neurons and may prefer broad features for trivial reasons.
  • No social-cognition localizer. "People-related" Ghost reading is inferential, not task-locked.
  • Correlational only. No stimulus-design or perturbation to test causal role of specific features.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does the "general features drive alignment" result replicate with other SAE architectures (TopK, Cross-Layer Transcoders) and other base LMs (Llama, Qwen, Mistral) of comparable size?
  2. Can the directional signed-feature generalization framework be used as a purely feature-driven voxel parcellation, complementing anatomy?
  3. Does the Matryoshka hierarchy also explain ECoG / MEG alignment — finer temporal resolution, more localized signals than fMRI voxels?
  4. How would narrative-length stimuli (vs 200 isolated sentences) change the prevalence of fine-grained features?
  5. Can the people-centered Ghost subpopulation be reliably identified with a larger, balanced cohort, and mapped to a known social-cognition localizer (ToM, mentalizing)?
  6. Can the framework support closed-loop / neural-control experiments where stimuli are designed to drive targeted voxel subpopulations?
  7. How robust is the "shared code" claim to cross-language replication (Mandarin, Spanish listeners)?
  8. Does ablating the most-prevalent shared features from the LM causally affect downstream task performance in a way that mirrors predicted voxel-level loss?
