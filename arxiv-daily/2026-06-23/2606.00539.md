> **一句话总结：** 本文提出 **GNMR (Gradient Norm-to-Mean Ratio)**，一种面向低精度 LLM 训练的运行时稳定性控制器：通过比较算子当前梯度范数与历史均值来识别局部风险，并在硬性 `maxO` 预算与短锁间隔下，将少数高风险算子临时切换到更高精度的恢复路径，从而在 4-bit/8-bit 等激进精度下保持接近 BF16 的训练质量。

> **TL;DR:** This paper formulates low-precision LLM training instability as a **runtime control problem** and proposes **GNMR (Gradient Norm-to-Mean Ratio)**, a lightweight, backend-agnostic controller that monitors operator-wise gradient norms against their historical mean (with a short-window complement ∆-GNMR) and selectively routes at most `maxO` high-risk units to a higher-fidelity recovery path each step. Across an activation-quantization stress bench, a DeepSeek-style mixed-precision pre-training recipe, and LLaMA-2 13B LoRA fine-tuning, GNMR preserves near-BF16 quality at FP4/INT8/FP8 execution paths with highly sparse recovery triggers (<0.2% of eligible units) and negligible per-step time overhead.

## 核心贡献

- **运行时稳定性控制的数学化建模：** 将低精度 LLM 训练中的局部数值不稳定性刻画为受约束的在线控制问题——给定"低成本路径"与"高精度恢复路径"，控制器在固定内存/算力预算下选择稀疏子集进行临时恢复，把"风险识别"与"恢复决策"从后端实现中解耦。
- **GNMR + ∆-GNMR 双时间尺度风险信号：** GNMR 通过比较算子当前梯度范数与其长期历史均值得到无量纲、跨模块可比的局部风险评分；∆-GNMR 则在最近 δ 步窗口内捕捉突变。两者均只需当前梯度与轻量历史统计，无需额外反向或全流程统计。
- **预算化的恢复策略与锁机制：** 综合评分 `R = max{GNMR/α, ∆-GNMR/β}`，候选算子 `C_t = {b: R>1} ∪ {锁定算子}`，最终 `A_t = Top_{maxO}(C_t; R)`。锁定计数器 `Tlock` 减少 thrashing，但最终集合仍受 `maxO` 上限约束。论文给出"选择性恢复的一步期望下降增益"理论分析。
- **控制层独立评测证据：** 在 LLaMA-2 60M→1.3B 激活量化压力测试、DeepSeek-V3 风格 6/8-bit 与 8/16-bit 层次训练、LLaMA-2 13B INT8/BF16 LoRA 微调三个层次上分别评估触发质量、预算行为、配方集成与下游保留，分离控制器质量与后端实现差异。

## Key Contributions

- **Runtime stability-control formulation under low precision.** Models local low-precision instability as a constrained online control problem with explicit memory/compute budget `maxO`, decoupling the control policy from formats, kernels, scaling rules, and backend recipes.
- **Dual-timescale GNMR risk monitor.** GNMR is a dimensionless, online, operator-wise statistic (`current gradient norm / running historical mean`) that yields a comparable risk score across heterogeneous modules; ∆-GNMR normalizes GNMR by a short recent window (length δ) to catch abrupt changes.
- **Budgeted recovery policy with closed-loop locking.** Recovery candidate set = `{R_b,t > 1} ∪ {locked units}`, finalized by `Top_{maxO}` ranking; a `Tlock` counter stabilizes active-set membership but cannot bypass the `maxO` cap, capping instantaneous memory/casting overhead.
- **Controller-level evidence decoupled from backend claims.** Empirical evaluation across (i) controlled activation-quantization stress (LLaMA-2 60M/130M/350M/1.3B on C4-en, 4-bit vs 4/8-bit recovery), (ii) DeepSeek-V3-style recipe integration (6/8-bit, 8/16-bit hierarchies, scaling to 3B), and (iii) large-model LoRA fine-tuning (LLaMA-2 13B on INT8/BF16 with GSM8K/MMLU/HellaSwag/WikiText-2).
- **Theoretical motivation.** Theorem 1 (bound-level gain of budgeted selective recovery) and Theorem 2 (sub-exponential concentration of historical-mean under fixed GNMR threshold) plus a one-step expected-descent analysis showing why top-risk recovery under `maxO` reduces low-precision perturbation on critical updates.

## Methodology

**Operator-level runtime monitoring.** The training stack is decomposed into U nested transformer blocks `N^[u](y^[u-1]; Ξ^[u])`. Inside each block, sibling operators `f^[u,k]` (attention heads, MLP projections, MoE experts) are aggregated with the residual input. For each monitored unit `b ∈ B` (operator, branch, block, or layer), the local statistic is `n_{b,t} = ‖g_{b,t}‖₂` where `g_{b,t} = ∇_{θ_b} L̂_t`.

**Long-horizon risk score (GNMR).** For a parameter block `θ^[u,k]`, `GNMR_t(θ^[u,k]) = ‖g_t^[u,k]‖ / (mean_{τ<t} ‖g_τ^[u,k]‖)`, with `GNMR_1 := 1` to handle cold start. The score is dimensionless and comparable across heterogeneous modules.

**Short-window risk score (∆-GNMR).** `∆-GNMR_t(θ^[u,k]) = GNMR_t / (mean of last δ GNMR values)`, again with value 1 until the queue of length δ fills. Algorithm 1 implements both scores with a running mean `A_{b,t}` and a FIFO queue `Q_b`.

**Budgeted recovery policy (Algorithm 2).** At each step, for every recoverable unit `b`:
- Compute `R_{b,t} = max{GNMR_{b,t}/α_t, ∆-GNMR_{b,t}/β_t}`.
- Candidate pool `C_t = {b: R_{b,t} > 1} ∪ {b: Lock_b > 0}`.
- Active set `A_t = Top_{maxO}(C_t; R_{b,t})`.
- Units in `A_t` use the higher-fidelity recovery path; others stay on the low-cost path.
- Lock counters reset to `Tlock` on selection and decay otherwise, preventing thrashing while preserving the budget.

**Hard budget.** `ΔM_t = Σ_{b ∈ A_t} c_b ≤ maxO · c_max`, where `c_b` is the saved-activation cost difference between the two paths. The controller never executes more than `maxO` units on the recovery path per step.

**Theoretical motivation (Theorem 1).** Under L-smoothness, standard SGD noise, and a perturbation envelope `E^q_b(ρ)` (bounding the unit's contribution to the one-step smoothness bound at risk state ρ), the bound-level gain of budgeted recovery is `ΔŪ_s = Σ_b ΔE^b(ρ^b_s) 1{b ∈ A_s}`. With the selected-risk persistence assumption (`Pr(ρ_{b,t+1} ≥ τ_b | b ∈ S_t) ≥ π_b`), the expected gain satisfies `E[ΔŪ_{t+1}] ≥ Σ_b Pr(b ∈ S_t) π_b ΔE^b(τ_b)`. Corollary 1 identifies the latent oracle: an active budget would pick the largest positive recovery gaps.

**Concentration (Theorem 2).** Under a sub-exponential tail condition on gradient norms, `Pr(GNMR_{b,t} ≥ α = 1+ε)` is bounded by a current-step deviation term and a historical-mean uncertainty term that shrinks with `t-1`. This motivates scheduled relaxation of `α_t, β_t` after running statistics stabilize.

**Implementation granularity.** For activation quantization, the recoverable set is the projection operators in LLaMA attention and SwiGLU MLP (with weights/optimizer states in BF16). For DeepSeek-style recipes, the whole transformer block is used to avoid inconsistent execution inside a block; with 24 layers and `maxO = 6`, at most 25% of layers can use recovery at a step.

## Results & Highlights

- **Activation-quantization stress bench (Table 1).** Token-wise 4-bit activation quantization on LLaMA-2 / C4-en completely fails to converge at 1.3B scale (✗). GNMR-controlled 4-bit/8-bit recovery reaches validation PPL **30.59 / 24.66 / 18.84 / 15.71** for 60M/130M/350M/1.3B, essentially matching the fixed 8-bit baseline (**30.88 / 25.13 / 18.59 / 15.68**) and BF16/16-bit references (**30.79 / 25.06 / 18.80 / 15.56**). A conservative `α_t=3.0, β_t=2.0` schedule performs comparably to `α_t=2.0, β_t=1.5`.
- **Trigger sparsity (Table 3).** Recovery triggers stay below **0.2%** of eligible linear blocks (e.g., 60M: 0.070%/0.081% for `α=2.0/β=1.5`; 350M: 0.141%/0.067%). Quality gain is achieved through highly localized, sparse interventions, not a broad static high-precision slice.
- **DeepSeek-style recipe (Table 2).** On 6/8-bit and 8/16-bit hierarchies, GNMR matches the corresponding higher-fidelity reference throughout training. Dynamic 6/8-bit at 13.1B tokens reaches **15.20 PPL** (vs. 15.18 fixed 8-bit, 15.06 fixed 16-bit); dynamic 8/16-bit reaches **15.10 PPL** (vs. 15.06 fixed 16-bit). The two-stage threshold (1.5 init / 1.1 main) is robust.
- **Throughput preserved (Table 4).** LLaMA-2 1.3B with dynamic 8/16-bit runs at **1.264 s/step / 103,696 tok/s**, marginally faster than fixed 8-bit (1.266 s / 103,532 tok/s) and within 2.4% of fixed 16-bit (1.294 s / 101,292 tok/s) — recovery overhead is negligible.
- **Scaling (Figure 4, Table 9).** 130M / 350M / 1.3B / 3B models on the 6/8-bit hierarchy follow the expected token-scaling trend.
- **Threshold & budget ablations (Tables 5–6).** Two-stage threshold scheduling improves early-step stability; `maxO ∈ {2, 4, 6, 8}` shows a clear recovery-overhead trade-off with `maxO=4` slightly optimal at 130M.
- **LLaMA-2 13B fine-tuning (Table 7).** LoRA fine-tuning with `maxO=10` over 40 decoder layers: GNMR+∆ INT8/BF16 reaches GSM8K **15.01** (BF16: 15.09, fixed INT8: 14.71), MMLU **29.92** (matches BF16), HellaSwag **64.67** (BF16: 64.62), WikiText-2 PPL **5.4511** (BF16: 5.4503). Outperforms fixed INT8 on every metric.
- **Diagnostic risk traces (Figures 2–3).** GNMR is ~1 under BF16 and shows high-risk excursions under FP8/BF16 dynamic precision that the controller dampens via recovery.

## Critical Assessment

- **Honest framing of the controller's claim.** The authors are careful to position GNMR as a *runtime stability controller over backend-provided low-cost and recovery paths* rather than a new numerical format or kernel. This separation is the paper's most useful conceptual contribution and is consistent with the experimental design (matched-trigger comparisons, ADAPT baseline, controlled activation-quantization stress).
- **Theoretical motivation is bound-level, not convergence.** Theorem 1 quantifies the surrogate smoothness bound difference between always-low and selective-recovery paths, but it is not a convergence rate theorem for end-to-end training. Theorem 2 is a useful concentration diagnostic that motivates threshold scheduling, but it does not translate into a finite-sample bound on perplexity. Readers expecting an end-to-end convergence guarantee will be disappointed.
- **Practical value is tied to backend observability.** GNMR can only recover units for which the backend exposes a low-cost and a higher-fidelity path. For frameworks without this dual-path interface, GNMR is not a drop-in solution. The paper does not quantify the engineering cost of providing this interface at production scale.
- **Empirical scope is reasonable but not exhaustive.** All experiments use LLaMA-2 architecture on C4-en (pre-training) and standard downstream tasks (fine-tuning). There is no comparison with other runtime controllers (e.g., spike-aware Adam, gradient-spike score) on equal footing in the main text — only Appendix E mentions matched-trigger and ADAPT comparisons. Reproducibility is limited to the standard hyper-parameters reported.
- **Sparse, low-overhead recovery is the headline.** With <0.2% trigger rates and no measurable per-step time penalty, the paper makes a strong practical claim: a small, well-targeted recovery set closes most of the quality gap to higher-precision baselines. The diagnostic GNMR/∆-GNMR traces (Figures 2–3) corroborate this and are easy to inspect.
- **Minor concerns.** (i) The "abrupt short-window increases" detection via ∆-GNMR has no formal analysis of false-positive rate beyond Theorem 2's fixed-threshold concentration. (ii) `α_t, β_t, Tlock, maxO` form a 4-knob control surface with no automated calibration procedure; the two-stage schedule used in DeepSeek-style training is heuristic. (iii) The LLaMA-2 13B result is a LoRA fine-tuning stress test, not a full-scale pre-training run.

## Strengths

- **Clean problem formulation.** Reframing low-precision instability as runtime control with an explicit `maxO` budget is a useful abstraction that decouples controller quality from backend kernels.
- **Cheap, online signals.** GNMR uses only the current gradient norm and a running mean; ∆-GNMR uses a length-δ FIFO queue. No extra backward pass, no Jacobian estimation, no full-process statistics.
- **Strong empirical quality preservation.** Recovery yields near-BF16 PPL at 4-bit activations and matches fixed 16-bit at 8/16-bit dynamic, with **<0.2%** trigger rate and **negligible per-step time overhead**.
- **Multi-level evaluation.** Pre-training (60M–3B), recipe-level integration (DeepSeek-V3 style), and large-model fine-tuning (LLaMA-2 13B LoRA) cover trigger quality, budget behavior, and downstream preservation separately.
- **Theoretical motivation.** Theorem 1 + Corollary 1 + Theorem 2 give a clear narrative for *why* ranking risky units under a budget reduces perturbation, even if the bounds are not end-to-end.
- **Implementation clarity.** The recoverable unit granularity (operator vs. block) and the dual-path interface are spelled out for each experimental setting.

## Limitations

- **Backend coupling.** Effectiveness depends on the backend providing both a low-cost path and a higher-fidelity recovery path for a subset of units; not all stacks expose this.
- **Engineering overhead.** Casting, routing, and recovery-switching overheads are mentioned but not deeply quantified at production scale; the 1.3B throughput table suggests they are small in the tested regime, but the result may not extrapolate to larger clusters.
- **No automated calibration.** The four control knobs (`α_t, β_t, Tlock, maxO`) require manual or two-stage scheduling; a principled, data-driven calibration procedure is left to future work.
- **Limited architectural coverage.** All pre-training is LLaMA-2 / C4-en; MoE/PanGu stress tests are in the appendix. Generalization to other architectures (e.g., Mamba, hybrid SSM-Attention) is untested.
- **No end-to-end convergence theorem.** The theory is bound-level and diagnostic; convergence-rate or finite-sample guarantees for end-to-end low-precision training with GNMR are absent.
- **Fine-tuning is LoRA-only at 13B.** The downstream experiment is a stress test on a frozen base with LoRA adapters, not a full-scale pre-training of a 13B+ model.
- **Comparisons with other stability methods are partial.** Direct, head-to-head comparisons with SPAM, GSNR, GSS, or spike-aware optimizers under the same backend and trigger budget are not the focus of the main text.

## Relevance

该工作对低精度 LLM 训练方向的从业者直接相关：它把"数值不稳定"从静态配方问题重新表述为运行时控制问题，并给出了一套可直接接在已有 FP8/INT8 训练栈上、几乎不增加开销的稳定性补救方案。对于使用 Transformer Engine、FP8-lm、COAT、NVFP4 训练栈并被偶发 loss/gradient spike 困扰的团队，GNMR 提供了一个可以"在不动 kernel、不改 recipe 的前提下"补稳定性的候选方案，与本研究领域（LLM 高效训练、混合精度、量化）高度契合。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can the active-set selection (`Top_{maxO}`) be replaced with a learnable policy (e.g., contextual bandit or small RL controller) that uses GNMR/∆-GNMR as state features and adapts `α_t, β_t, Tlock` online?
- How does GNMR behave when the recovery path is itself approximate (e.g., FP4 → FP8 rather than FP8 → BF16)? Does the bound-level gain in Theorem 1 still hold, and at what `c_max` does `maxO` become the binding constraint?
- Is there a principled calibration procedure for `α_t, β_t` based on training-trace statistics (e.g., setting thresholds to a high quantile of historical GNMR) that removes the two-stage schedule heuristic?
- Can the operator-level GNMR be aggregated into a layer-level or block-level risk score with provable approximation guarantees, reducing per-step monitoring overhead at very large scales?
- How does GNMR interact with optimizer-level stabilizers (SPAM, spike-aware Adam) — are they complementary, redundant, or antagonistic on the same training run?
- What is the cost of providing the dual-path interface (low-cost + recovery) at production scale in frameworks like Megatron-LM, FSDP, or DeepSpeed, and how does this overhead grow with `maxO`?
- Can GNMR be extended to Mixture-of-Experts training, where expert-level gradient statistics have very different typical scales, and recovery at the expert level may be more natural than at the block level?
- Does the same controller transfer to non-Transformer architectures (state-space models, hybrid attention-SSM) and to other modalities (vision, multimodal) where the "expensive compute path" is a different operator than in LLMs?
- How sensitive is the LLaMA-2 13B downstream preservation to the choice of `maxO = 10` (i.e., 25% of layers) — is there a sweet spot in the recovery-overhead trade-off at large scale?
- Can historical GNMR statistics themselves be used as a curriculum signal (e.g., a layer-wise "stability maturation" indicator that informs LR schedules or precision warm-up)?
