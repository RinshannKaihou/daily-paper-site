> **一句话总结：** 这篇论文提出了一套面向前沿大模型稠密预训练的零知识验证架构：用"预提交的训练规范 + 节点间网络 TAP 观测 + 即时 Merkle 承诺"三件套作为独立信任锚点，并通过带原生 BF16/FP32 预编译器的 zkVM 直接证明 GPU 真实执行的浮点计算（而非定点近似），在 Llama 3.1 405B 规模上将训练侧开销压到 2–10%，并把工程化时间表压到约 36 个月——明显优于"自报式"治理与 6–10 年的定制验证硅片路径。

> **TL;DR:** This paper argues that zero-knowledge verification of frontier dense pre-training is feasible within ~36 months, at single-digit-percent training-side overhead, by combining three complementary trust anchors: (i) a pre-committed training specification `arch_spec`, (ii) auditor-controlled inter-node network TAP / attested SmartNIC observations, and (iii) on-the-fly Merkle commitments of intermediate computation, all checked by a generic zkVM equipped with native BF16/FP32 precompiles. The proof verifies the actual IEEE-754 floating-point computation the GPU performed—not a fixed-point approximation of it—and keeps the model architecture confidential by treating `arch_spec` as a private input. The protocol emits three artefact types: a *genesis proof* at run start, *in-training step proofs* sampled across the run, and *ex-ante attestations* that enforce policy-relevant claims (compute threshold, training regime, data-content filter) as running invariants. The authors catalogue 13 open problems as a research agenda and benchmark the cost at Llama 3.1 405B scale: ~$2–10M (~2–10%) of a ~$100M training run, with a per-step proof budget of ~2.6×10¹³ constraints (~25 min on 16,384 provers) and a recursive-composed ~200 KB aggregate proof. The paper explicitly scopes out MoE, RL post-training, multi-datacentre training, and intra-node NVLink traffic, treating each as an additive extension.

## 核心贡献

- **给出 ZK 训练验证"可行"的正面回答**：直接挑战了 [Shavit 2023] 与 [Baker et al. 2025] 给出的"~5×10⁵ 倍训练开销、模型架构必须公开"的不可能性判断，把治理层面的可验证预训练从"理论上成立、工程上不可行"重写为"约 36 个月内可部署、训练侧单数百分点开销"。
- **提出三锚点验证架构（pre-commit + network TAP + on-the-fly Merkle）**：在不做任何"定点近似"的前提下，把 (a) 训练规范的 Merkle 根 `hcommit`、(b) 物理 TAP / 认证 SmartNIC 对节点间集体通信的公开哈希、(c) GPU 并发流计算的每步 6×layer 张量 Merkle 根，整合成可独立挑战的证据链。
- **设计带原生 BF16/FP32 预编译器的 zkVM**：通过 8 个预编译器（MAC 链、查找表、Merkle 路径、exp、sqrt、div、log、SHA-256）把 IEEE 754 浮点语义编码为 Fp 约束，使 zkVM 验证的是"GPU 实际跑过的浮点计算"，不是 Fp 中的定点模拟；这与 ZKML、zkLLM、ezkl、VeriLoRA 的范式形成根本区别。
- **三段式协议：genesis + 训练中步证明 + 事先（ex-ante）证明**：把训练记录变成"治理可执行的工件"——例如 `Fcumu(t) ≤ max_total_flops` 在每步内被强制为运行不变量，超出即证伪，从根本上解决"自报式监管"的信用问题。
- **完整成本模型与 13 项开放问题清单**：在 Llama 3.1 405B 规模下把每步证明约束预算量化到 ~2.6×10¹³、把确定性税拆到注意力反向传播（1.6–8.2%），并把剩余工作分成四大类（证明基础、确定性执行、硬件信任锚、协议扩展）与一项工具链问题（OP-13：PyTorch→arch_spec 编译器）。

## Key Contributions

- **A positive answer to ZK frontier training verification.** The architecture lifts the two structural limits of the prior ZK-ML paradigm—finite-field arithmetic (which forces fixed-point proxies of the model) and monolithic proofs (per-sample or per-step, not full run). Estimated overhead is ~5 orders of magnitude below the prevailing ~5×10⁵ estimate [Baker et al. 2025], and architecture confidentiality is preserved by keeping `arch_spec` private.
- **Three trust anchors combined into a single verifiable artefact.** (i) `hcommit = H(arch_spec ∥ R(dataset) ∥ R(W₀))` published before training; (ii) inter-node collective payloads hashed by an auditor-installed physical TAP (Tier 1) or an attested SmartNIC (Tier 2); (iii) six Merkle roots per layer per step (layer in/out, Q, K, V, attention out) committed on-the-fly by a concurrent GPU stream and frozen by an auditor-signed terminal anchor before the audit seed is opened.
- **A zkVM with native BF16/FP32 precompiles.** Eight precompiles cover a full Transformer training step. The dominant one (BF16/FP32 MAC, ~90 constraints/op, >99% of the budget) decomposes each value into sign/exponent/mantissa and encodes IEEE 754 RNE semantics as Fp constraints, so the proof states something about the actual GPU execution. Per-entry interactive sampling (k=4,605) gives soundness 1−10⁻²⁰ against ≥1% deviations.
- **Hint-and-verify zkVM usage pattern.** The zkVM never re-executes training: the host (trainer GPU) re-runs a challenged step from stored weights and supplies intermediate tensors as *hints*; the guest (proof checker inside the zkVM) only verifies each hint against the committed Merkle root via path checks and precompile calls. This is what makes verifying the backward pass tractable, not just inference.
- **A three-phase governance protocol.**
  - *P.1 Genesis proof*: trainer runs one full step from W₀ on auditor-chosen sample indices, proves each sample is a Merkle member of R(dataset) and that the step executes faithfully per `arch_spec`—binding the committed procedure to actual GPU work.
  - *P.2 In-training step proof*: auditor picks step t, a tensor identifier, and k random entry indices; trainer re-runs locally and supplies hints; the proof aggregates per-layer sub-proofs via recursive STARK composition into a ~200 KB artefact.
  - *P.3 Ex-ante attestation*: trainer publishes `ex_ante_claims` (max total FLOPs, training regime, data-filter hash); every step proof enforces `Fcumu(t) ≤ max_total_flops` in-circuit, turning the training record into a governance-enforceable artefact rather than a self-reported audit log.
- **A structured open-problem agenda of 13 items.** Four buckets: proof foundations (OP-1 algebraic GEMM reduction, OP-2 MAC constraint minimisation, OP-3 ZK proof of backprop at ≥10⁶ params); deterministic execution (OP-4 attention backward <5% overhead, OP-5 multi-architecture precompile spec); hardware trust anchors (OP-6 open-hardware line-rate TAP, OP-7 wire-to-tensor mapping, OP-8 intra-node physical witness, OP-9 SDC vs adversarial distinction); protocol extensions (OP-10 MoE, OP-11 RL post-training, OP-12 multi-datacentre); plus OP-13 PyTorch/JAX → `arch_spec` converter. The critical path passes through OP-3, OP-4, and OP-7.

## Methodology

**Scope.** The architecture targets dense pre-training as the "architecturally simplest setting that is also the largest single block of frontier training compute and the root of trust for every post-trained derivative." MoE, RL post-training, multi-datacentre training, and intra-node NVLink are explicitly out of scope and treated as additive extensions (OP-10/11/12/8).

**Five modifications to current AI training (MOD. 1–MOD. 5).**

- **MOD. 1 — Enforcing determinism.** Bit-exact determinism is required so the proof checker can reproduce committed values. Standard GPU training is non-deterministic (cuBLAS reductions, NCCL collectives, attention kernels depend on thread scheduling). Hardware-level determinism (FlashAttention-2 deterministic mode, pinned cuBLAS algorithms, NCCL NVLS reductions) achieves bit-exact determinism with **1.6–8.2% end-to-end overhead on Llama 7B FSDP (8×H100)**, dominated by the attention backward pass. The hard case is tensor-parallel allreduce over NVSwitch, which is not deterministic under NVLS above 128 MB and incurs 64–89% bandwidth loss under deterministic NCCL configs; a custom TP kernel exploiting fixed topology is the proposed path (OP-4).
- **MOD. 2 — Pre-committing public Merkle roots.** Before training, the trainer publishes `hcommit = H(H(arch_spec) ∥ R(dataset) ∥ R(W₀))`. Merkle paths enable O(log n) membership proofs for individual batches / layer weights without revealing the full dataset or model.
- **MOD. 3 — On-the-fly Merkle root computation.** A GPU concurrent stream computes Merkle roots of six tensors per layer per step using the zkVM-native hash (Poseidon, precompile 3). Pure-HBM-read overhead is 0.2%; a 2-pass reduction kernel adds 1.6–3.3% (SM contention); estimated production overhead with an optimised kernel is 0.5–1.5%. The stream can also be offloaded to a SmartNIC/DPU or CPU.
- **MOD. 4 — Network anchoring.** Both tiers commit a Merkle tree over inter-node collective payloads via SHA-256 (precompile 8). *Tier 1*: auditor-owned physical TAP at wire speed; trust root is physical. *Tier 2*: attested SmartNIC (e.g. BlueField DOCA) exposing a signed root over secure-boot-rooted device identity; trust root is cryptographic. Both tiers observe inter-node traffic only; intra-node NVLink is invisible (hybrid FSDP+TP covered at the FSDP layer only).
- **MOD. 5 — Weight storage rolling window.** 405B params in BF16 → ~810 GB per snapshot; 100 steps → 81 TB. At challenge time the trainer loads weights directly and runs a single forward pass.

**The proving system (Section 3.1).** Four components:
- *Training specification* (`arch_spec`, private): a structured, machine-readable description of one complete training step—model architecture as typed operations per layer (GEMMs with dimensions, activations, normalisations), numerical precision (BF16 params, FP32 accumulators, IEEE 754 RNE), distributed communication pattern (allgather, reduce-scatter), data loading (batch size, shuffling seed, master RNG seed, registered stochastic components), and tensor boundaries at which Merkle roots are committed.
- *zkVM* (RISC Zero is the reference implementation): application-defined precompiles, GPU-accelerated proof generation, continuations with recursive composition. Host computation contributes zero constraints; only the guest's verification logic does.
- *Proof checker* (public, auditable): compiled to the zkVM's instruction set, reads `arch_spec`, iterates over declared operations, and dispatches each to the appropriate precompile. The same binary works for any model; only `arch_spec` differs.
- *Precompiles* (8, see Table 3): native constraint circuits hardwired into the proof system. The dominant one is BF16/FP32 MAC chain (~90 constraints/op) using IEEE 754 decomposition into sign/exponent/mantissa over the zkVM's native Fp (Baby Bear) field.

**Hint-and-verify pattern (Section B.1.1).** Two data categories: *public inputs* (Merkle roots) committed during training on a concurrent stream and linked into a per-step hash chain, frozen by an auditor-signed terminal anchor before the audit seed is opened; *private hints* (full tensor values) produced by the host at challenge time by re-executing from stored weights. Verification proceeds in two layers: each hinted value is bound to its committed root via a path check (preventing fabricated hints), then the computation on bound values is verified using the appropriate precompile (preventing incorrect commitments).

**Proof cost model (Section B.1.2).** Per-MAC ~90 constraints; per-Merkle-path at depth 30 ~1,500 constraints; per-SHA-256 block ~7,500 constraints. Cluster-wide proving throughput ~10⁶ constraints/second/GPU (RISC Zero-class on A100/H100), ~10⁹ constraints/second aggregate on 1,024 GPUs. Recursive composition folds per-segment proofs into a ~200 KB aggregate independent of circuit size. Per-step proof budget at Llama 3.1 405B: ~2.6×10¹³ constraints, ~7 h on 1,024 provers, ~25 min on 16,384 provers.

**Why exact GEMM verification is cubic and why Freivalds fails (Section B.2).** A GEMM of dimensions m×d×n needs mnd MAC verifications. At ~90 constraints/MAC, even one Q-projection GEMM of Llama 3.1 405B (m=2,048, d=n=16,384) costs ~5×10¹³ constraints—beyond current proof systems. Freivalds reduces O(n³) to O(n²) but fails for BF16/FP32 because floating-point addition is not associative: A(Br) and Cr produce different bit-exact outputs even when C is the correct GPU output. Approximate sumcheck [Bitan et al. 2025] is flagged as a promising recovery path (OP-1).

**Per-step commitment and fusion assumptions (Section B.3.1).** Six Merkle roots per layer per step (layer in/out, Q, K, V, attention out) committed during training. Flash Attention internals (the s×s score matrix, the softmax output) are truly fused and never materialise in HBM—they are *uncommittable by kernel construction*, not by choice; per-sample cost for attention outputs is Θ(s·d_head) MACs, so the protocol uses a reduced sample count k_attn = 500 for attention outputs. FFN internals (SwiGLU's gate/up/down projections) do materialise in HBM, and the protocol commits gate_out / up_out Merkle roots *at challenge time* (not during training) to amortise the training-time hashing overhead.

**Three-phase protocol (Section 3.2).**
- *P.1 Genesis proof*: auditor supplies random sample indices; trainer constructs B in committed canonical order, executes one full step from W₀, proves each sample is a Merkle member of R(dataset) via precompile 3, the step executes faithfully per `arch_spec`, and the resulting W₁ is the first link of the hash chain. Cost: one training step's worth of proof.
- *P.2 In-training step proof*: auditor picks t, a tensor identifier, and k random entry indices; trainer re-runs locally and supplies hints + Merkle paths; proof checker dispatches precompile 3 (path), precompiles 1/2/4–7 (operation), precompile 8 (anchor). Soundness composes via R(layerℓ_out) = R(layerℓ+1_in) within a step and R(W_{t+1}) = f(R(W_t), R(grad_t)) across steps.
- *P.3 Ex-ante attestation*: trainer publishes `ex_ante_claims` with committed bounds (`max_total_flops`, `max_steps`, `training_regime`, `data_filter_hash`); the genesis commitment extends to `hcommit = H(H(arch_spec) ∥ R(W₀) ∥ R(dataset) ∥ H(ex_ante_claims))`; every step proof carries a public running counter `Fcumu(t) = Fcumu(t-1) + F_step(arch_spec, t)` with the in-circuit constraint `Fcumu(t) ≤ max_total_flops`. Per-step overhead: one addition and one comparison—negligible vs. the MAC budget.

## Results & Highlights

- **Cost at Llama 3.1 405B scale on a $100M run:**

| Component | Cost | % of training |
|---|---|---|
| Determinism (MOD. 1, 1.6–8.2%) | $1.6–8.2M | 1.6–8.2% |
| Merkle computation (MOD. 3, ~0.5–1.5%) | $0.5–1.5M | 0.5–1.5% |
| Weight storage (MOD. 5, 81 TB rolling) | negligible | <0.1% |
| **Total (FSDP)** | **$2–10M** | **~2–10%** |

The dominant cost is determinism (attention backward pass); FSDP+TP setups incur additional overhead until a custom TP allreduce kernel is deployed.

- **Per-step proof budget (Llama 3.1 405B):** ~2.6×10¹³ constraints; per-layer sub-proofs aggregate via recursive STARK composition into a single ~200 KB artefact; ~7 h on 1,024 provers, ~25 min on 16,384 provers, ~100 min on 4,096 provers. MAC precompile > 99% of the budget; Merkle-path verification (precompile 3) < 0.1%.
- **Per-layer constraint budget breakdown:** Q/O projections ~1.44×10¹⁰ each; K/V projections (GQA) ~8.06×10⁹ each; FFN gate/up/down ~2.97×10¹⁰ each; Flash Attention (k_attn=500) ~5×10¹⁰; elementwise SiLU + multiply ~2.6×10¹⁰; Merkle paths ~4×10⁷; per-layer total ~2.1×10¹¹.
- **Soundness:** With k=4,605 samples per committed GEMM, deviations affecting ≥1% of entries are detected with probability 1−10⁻²⁰.
- **Governance instantiations:** EU AI Act Art. 51 (cumulative training-compute threshold ~10²⁵ FLOPs) and the revoked US EO 14110 (~10²⁶ FLOPs) both rely on trainer self-reporting. The ex-ante attestation turns each of these thresholds from a self-reported number into a soundness-bounded invariant.
- **Deployment timeline:** ~36 months for a proof of concept, vs. a 6–10 year cycle for verification-grade custom silicon (spec → tape-out → formal RTL verification → manufacturing → certification → cluster-wide deployment).
- **Comparison vs. prior ZK-ML systems (Table 1):** Existing systems (ZKML up to 81M, zkLLM up to 13B inference, ezkl 250K, VeriLoRA 13B LoRA) all operate over finite fields, are monolithic (per-sample or single-pass), do not verify hardware execution, and do not handle distribution. The target column states the architectural objectives: BF16 native, 100–1,000B params, full run, distributed, proves HW execution.

## Critical Assessment

- **Honest.** The "approximately 36 months" deployment estimate is the paper's most load-bearing claim and rests on three engineering milestones: OP-3 (ZK proof of backpropagation at ≥10⁶ params with bit-exact gradient match to GPU execution), OP-4 (deterministic attention backward <5% overhead for s ≥ 8K), OP-7 (canonical wire-to-tensor mapping for NCCL). The authors flag OP-3 as "the highest-risk milestone on the roadmap: a negative result would require redesigning the protocol." That honesty is commendable but means the headline timeline is conditional on cryptographic and engineering breakthroughs that have not yet been demonstrated at scale.
- **Novelty vs. prior ZK-ML.** The architectural contribution is real and well-articulated: (a) the finite-field-vs-native-FP trade-off reversal (verifying actual GPU arithmetic rather than approximating it over Fp), and (b) the hint-and-verify pattern extended to backward passes via the same precompile catalogue. However, the underlying primitives (Merkle commitments, recursive STARK composition, RISC Zero-class zkVMs) are all well-established; the novelty is in composition and precompile engineering, not in any new cryptographic primitive.
- **Rigor.** The cost model is concrete: per-MAC constraint count (~90), per-step budget (~2.6×10¹³), proving throughput (~10⁶ constraints/second/GPU), aggregate proof size (~200 KB), training-side overhead ($2–10M / 2–10% at $100M run). The per-MAC count spans 50–150, which the authors flag as a "factor of ~2× uncertainty that affects absolute proof-time numbers but not comparative analysis." Empirical validation is explicitly listed as future work.
- **Reproducibility.** The architecture is implementation-defined at the protocol level but no reference implementation, no end-to-end benchmark, and no open-source code is released with v1. The thirteen open problems are the *roadmap*, not a deployed artefact. Anyone trying to reproduce the cost claims today would have to build the BF16/FP32 precompile from scratch on a zkVM and validate it against silicon (OP-2, OP-5).
- **Scope honesty.** The paper is explicit that the base protocol covers *only* dense pre-training and does not yet cover MoE, RL post-training, multi-datacentre training, or intra-node NVLink. It is also explicit that Tier 1 (physical TAP) is the long-term requirement and that Tier 2 (attested SmartNIC) is "weaker against firmware or supply-chain adversaries." This is the right honesty for a governance-grade artefact: a verification primitive that quietly drops intra-node traffic would be vacuous in exactly the deployment it claims to support.
- **Significance.** If the open problems can be closed, the paper's political-economy impact could be large: it gives negotiators of any future international AI agreement a verification primitive that doesn't depend on any single vendor's custom silicon, doesn't require the trainer to disclose model architecture, and doesn't require the regulator to trust the trainer's self-reporting. That is genuinely new relative to the Baker et al. 2025 "six layers of verification" baseline. If OP-3 fails, the architecture is still useful as a research agenda and a partial protocol, but the 36-month deployment window collapses.
- **Red flags.** (i) The "approximately 36 months" timeline is the only number the paper offers; sensitivity to OP-3 failure is acknowledged but not quantified. (ii) The "single-digit-percent training-side overhead" claim is conditional on the custom TP allreduce kernel (Section C) being deployable; without it, FSDP+TP setups incur additional overhead that is not bounded in the table. (iii) Tier 2 SmartNIC attestation requires trust in vendor firmware (BlueField DOCA), which is a non-trivial trust assumption for an international-verification regime. (iv) The deterministic FlashAttention-2 overhead is 1.6–8.2% on Llama 7B FSDP, but at frontier scale (Llama 3.1 405B) the attention backward pass dominates and the 82% upper bound is closer to reality for s ≥ 8K (DASH reduces but does not eliminate this). (v) The per-MAC constraint count of ~90 is an *estimate* based on IEEE 754 integer decomposition; no end-to-end measurement is provided.

## Strengths

- **Addresses the actual governance problem, not a paper-tiger version of it.** The architecture is designed around the EU AI Act Art. 51 / (former) US EO 14110 compute thresholds, and the ex-ante attestation primitive is explicitly engineered to make these thresholds soundness-bounded rather than self-reported.
- **Architecture confidentiality is preserved.** `arch_spec` is a private input; the auditor sees only `H(arch_spec)` as part of `hcommit`. From public metadata only the number of layers (chain length) and approximate scale (storage footprint or regulatory filings) can be inferred—both already disclosed under the regulatory regimes this work serves.
- **Trust roots are diversified.** Pre-committed training spec (cryptographic), physical TAP (physical), attested SmartNIC (cryptographic), and Merkle-committed intermediate computation (cryptographic) form four independent trust anchors. Failure of any single anchor does not collapse the protocol.
- **Concrete cost model.** Per-MAC constraint count, per-step budget, proving throughput, aggregate proof size, and training-side overhead are all quantified at Llama 3.1 405B scale. The dominant cost (determinism / attention backward) is correctly identified and isolated.
- **Honest open-problem catalogue.** Thirteen independently-attackable problems are phrased as research questions with named targets. The authors do not hide the critical-path risk on OP-3.
- **Cross-domain analogue reasoning.** The paper grounds its design in concrete international-verification analogues (IAEA, OPCW, Montreal Protocol, BWC) and uses the BWC's lack of verification regime as the cautionary counterexample. This is unusual for a computer-science paper and shows policy engagement.
- **Compatibility with existing infrastructure.** The protocol is designed for FSDP / PP / EP with NVLS-deterministic collectives, and Tier 2 SmartNIC anchoring is deployable on existing cluster infrastructure. The base protocol does not require any new hardware to be built before the proof-of-concept phase.

## Limitations

- **The 36-month timeline depends on a single high-risk milestone (OP-3).** A negative result on "zero-knowledge proof of backpropagation at ≥10⁶ parameters with bit-exact gradient match to GPU execution" would require redesigning the protocol. The paper does not quantify the conditional deployment cost.
- **No reference implementation.** v1 contains architectural specifications, precompile constraint counts, and a cost model, but no code, no end-to-end benchmark, and no open-source release. The 90-constraint-per-MAC estimate is unverified.
- **The deterministic-FlashAttention overhead is the real cost driver.** 1.6–8.2% on Llama 7B FSDP is dominated by attention backward; at frontier scale with longer sequences the 82% upper bound is closer to the operational reality. DASH [Qiang et al. 2026] gives 1.28× over baseline deterministic mode, which is a meaningful reduction but still well above the 5% OP-4 target for s ≥ 8K.
- **Tensor parallelism is not yet supported.** FSDP, PP, and EP are covered; TP is "the hard case" with NVLS non-determinism above 128 MB and 64–89% bandwidth loss under deterministic NCCL configs. A custom TP kernel exploiting fixed topology is the proposed path but is not specified.
- **Intra-node NVLink traffic is invisible to both network-anchor tiers.** Hybrid FSDP+TP training has no physical witness for the tensor-parallel layer. OP-8 (GPU-resident trusted execution for intra-node collectives) is an open problem; current NVIDIA Confidential Compute does not expose the required interface or throughput.
- **Tier 2 SmartNIC attestation requires trust in vendor firmware.** BlueField DOCA's secure-boot-rooted device identity is a non-trivial trust assumption for international verification. Tier 1 physical TAPs are the long-term requirement but require standardisation that does not exist today.
- **Silent data corruption (SDC) at hyperscale is statistically indistinguishable from adversarial deviation at the level of an individual event.** Empirical SDC rates of 1 per 10⁴–10⁵ GPU-hours imply 10²–10³ events per 10⁷-GPU-hour run; OP-9 is the open problem on distinguishing the two.
- **The base protocol covers dense pre-training only.** MoE (OP-10), RL post-training (OP-11), multi-datacentre training (OP-12) are explicitly future work. Each is a substantial extension with its own open sub-problems (e.g. data-dependent routing in MoE, model-dependent rollouts in RL, administratively partitioned links in multi-datacentre).
- **Per-MAC constraint count is an estimate, not a measurement.** The 50–150 range based on IEEE 754 integer decomposition is flagged as introducing up to ~2× uncertainty in absolute proof-time numbers. Direct measurement is part of the planned empirical validation work.
- **No end-to-end simulation or hardware-in-the-loop evaluation.** The cost figures are derived from constraint counts and assumed proving throughput; a deployed proof-of-concept remains future work.

## Relevance

高度相关。这篇论文直接对应"前沿 AI 治理需要可执行的技术验证原语"这一研究兴趣：在 EU AI Act Art. 51 的 10²⁵ FLOPs 阈值、（前）US EO 14110 的 10²⁶ FLOPs 阈值、以及任何未来国际 AI 协议都需要可验证的训练证据的语境下，本文是少数同时给出"可证（存在性）+ 可部署（时间表）+ 可承受（成本）"三角完整论证的中期方案，并把 13 项开放问题作为外部研究路线图发布——对训练侧系统、密码学与治理社区都有直接可对接的工作面。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- **OP-3 is the linchpin.** Has any team demonstrated a ZK proof of backpropagation at ≥10⁶ parameters in native floating-point arithmetic with bit-exact gradient match to GPU execution? If not, what is the current state-of-the-art and where is the empirical ceiling? A negative result here would require redesigning the protocol—what is the smallest viable alternative?
- **Can the BF16/FP32 MAC precompile be implemented and measured today?** The ~90-constraint estimate is the largest single source of uncertainty in the cost model. Direct measurement on RISC Zero, SP1, or OpenVM at frontier scale would either confirm or invalidate the per-step budget.
- **What is the operational impact of DASH-style deterministic attention scheduling at frontier scale?** The 1.6–8.2% determinism overhead is dominated by attention backward; the 82% upper bound is closer to the operational reality for s ≥ 8K. Can OP-4 (deterministic-by-construction attention backward <5% overhead) be hit with a fixed-warp-level reduction tree, or does the approach need an algebraic reformulation of softmax-attention?
- **How should the wire-to-tensor mapping (OP-7) be governed?** Three resolution paths are catalogued: patched NCCL, canonicalisation shim at the NCCL-to-transport boundary, or vendor-supported "verifiable NCCL" mode. Each has different deployment effort, maintenance burden, and institutional leverage. Which is most likely to win ecosystem adoption, and what is the role of standards bodies (e.g. MLCommons, UEC) in coordinating?
- **What is the right Tier 1 vs. Tier 2 deployment sequence?** Tier 1 (physical TAP) is the long-term requirement for any regime holding against well-resourced adversaries, but requires standardisation that does not exist today. Tier 2 (attested SmartNIC) is deployable on existing infrastructure. How should the rollout be staged, and what is the minimum certification standard for a Tier 1 device?
- **How does the protocol extend to multi-datacentre training with administratively partitioned links (OP-12)?** Frontier training increasingly spans multiple datacentres; the anchor model assumes auditor control over every inter-node link. Byzantine-robust aggregation, clock-skew handling, and incentive alignment for shared runs are all open.
- **What is the right balance between ex-ante attestation and ex-post audit?** The paper argues for ex-ante (running invariants during training) over ex-post (audit after the fact). Under what threat models is ex-post acceptable, and can the protocol support a hybrid that allows deferred disclosure of `arch_spec`?
- **Can statistical tests distinguish SDC patterns from adversarial injection (OP-9)?** Empirical SDC distributions on current silicon would be a prerequisite; the paper notes ~10²–10³ SDC events per 10⁷-GPU-hour run. Is there a fingerprint (DRAM row fault, thermal event, scheduled bit-flip) that separates natural corruption from structured adversarial injection?
- **What is the smallest viable PyTorch→`arch_spec` converter (OP-13)?** The ZKML [Chen et al. 2024] pipeline is a precedent for inference-only ONNX conversion; the training case adds backward-graph capture (functorch) and distributed-annotation capture. How much of the PyTorch op set is in scope for a first-cut converter, and which ops must be rejected as unsupported?
- **What is the political-economy path from architecture to deployed verification primitive?** The paper estimates ~36 months for a proof of concept vs. 6–10 years for verification-grade custom silicon. What institutional, regulatory, and procurement steps are needed to compress the 36-month window, and which stakeholders (regulators, hyperscalers, GPU vendors, standards bodies) need to be in the room?
