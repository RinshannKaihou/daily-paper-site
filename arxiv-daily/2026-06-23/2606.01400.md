> **一句话总结：** 把 LLM 基准的提示词嵌入建成相似度图，再用最大独立集（MIS）选出最大多样、无冗余的子集，能在 4 个基准、66 个 LLM 上把评估规模压缩 25–48% 并保持与完整基准高度一致的排名（Kendall's W 均值 0.997）。

> **TL;DR:** A graph-theoretic LLM benchmark compression method that models prompts as nodes in a similarity graph and applies Maximum Independent Set (MIS) solvers to extract a maximally non-redundant subset. Across 2,563 configurations (4 MIS solvers × 6 embedding models × 3 distance measures × 6 thresholds × 4 benchmarks × 66 LLMs), the authors confirm that MIS selection yields consistent LLM rankings across random seeds (Kendall's W ≥ 0.90 in 99.2% of stochastic runs) while compressing prompts by 25–48% at higher thresholds. Ranking divergence (Spearman ρ < 0.95) occurs in only 15.95% of configurations and is reframed as a coverage-bias correction effect.

## 核心贡献

- **图论视角的提示词选择框架**：把 LLM 基准建模为"提示词相似度图"——节点是提示词，边是嵌入空间中距离超过阈值的提示词对；用最大独立集（MIS）从中选出"两两不相似"的最大子集，同时解决评估开销和覆盖偏差两个问题。
- **大规模实验一致性验证**：在 4 个基准（GPQA / IFEval / MMLU-Pro / Omni-MATH）、66 个 LLM、4 个 MIS 求解器（CPLEX / GREEDY / Online-MIS / ReduMIS）、6 个嵌入模型、3 个距离度量、6 个百分位阈值的 2563 种配置下，**99.2%** 的随机配置 Kendall's W ≥ 0.90（均值 0.997±0.008），且在高阈值下平均压缩 25–48% 的提示词。
- **"W 高 / ρ 可变"假设的形式化**：证明 MIS 子集在不同随机种子下能稳定产出同样的 LLM 排名（一致性，W 高），但与完整基准排名在 15.95% 的配置里出现 ρ < 0.95 的差异（独特性）—— 这种差异被重新解释为"对覆盖偏差的修正"而非"低质量"。
- **可落地的实践配置**：推荐 ReduMIS + Qwen3-8B 嵌入 + 标准化欧氏距离 + p80 阈值，达成 W = 1.000、ρ = 0.9959 的近完美保真。

## Key Contributions

- **A graph-theoretic prompt selection framework.** Each benchmark is modelled as a prompt similarity graph G = (V, E) with edges connecting prompts whose embedding-space distance exceeds θ. A Maximum Independent Set (MIS) of G is a maximally diverse, non-redundant subset by construction—no two selected prompts are semantically close, and no capability facet is double-counted. The framework is model-agnostic and supports 4 MIS solvers, 6 embedding models, 3 distance measures, and 6 percentile thresholds.
- **Empirical confirmation of the consistency–distinctiveness hypothesis.** Across 2,563 unique configurations and 66 LLMs, Kendall's W ≥ 0.90 in 99.2% of stochastic configurations (mean W = 0.997 ± 0.008), with 25–48% prompt reduction at higher thresholds. Ranking divergence (Spearman ρ < 0.95) occurs in only 15.95% of configurations and is heavily concentrated at low thresholds (p10–p20) and on the more discretised benchmarks (GPQA, IFEval), identifying overly dense graphs as the primary failure mode.
- **Reinterpretation of low ρ as coverage correction.** When the full benchmark is coverage-biased (overrepresents one semantic region), a lower Spearman ρ is reframed as a *feature*—the MIS subset down-weights overrepresented facets and amplifies under-sampled ones, producing a different—but not worse—ranking. Coverage uniformity is treated as a structural property of MIS rather than a tunable parameter.
- **Detailed ablation and practical recipe.** Algorithm choice barely affects global ranking (ρ ≈ 0.967–0.968 across all four solvers). ReduMIS is the recommended stochastic solver (W = 0.9976, near-optimal solutions). Larger decoder-based embedding models (Nemotron-8B, Qwen3-8B) outperform smaller encoder-based ones (BGE, E5, GIST). Standardized Euclidean distance is generally the strongest measure. The p80 threshold is the sweet spot: ρ = 0.992, W = 0.999 with 37–45% reduction.

## Methodology

**Pipeline.** Encode all N prompts in a benchmark into d-dimensional embeddings (six models: BGE-large, E5-large, GIST-small, Llama-Embed-Nemotron-8B, Qwen3-4B, Qwen3-8B). For each (embedding, distance measure) pair, compute the empirical pairwise distance distribution over 10,000 sampled pairs and define the threshold θ as a percentile p ∈ {p10, p20, p50, p80, p90, p95}. Build the similarity graph G = (V, E) with edge (u, v) added iff d(eu, ev) ≥ θ. Run an MIS solver to obtain S ⊆ V; this S is the compressed benchmark.

**Distance measures.** Three proximity measures are evaluated: cosine similarity, Pearson correlation distance, and standardized Euclidean (seuclidean) distance, capturing different geometric and statistical properties of the embedding space.

**MIS solvers.**
- **CPLEX** (Anand et al., 2017): exact Integer Linear Programming with branch-and-bound and branch-and-cut, ~30–120 s per 1000-node graph.
- **GREEDY**: deterministic lowest-degree-first heuristic, <1 s per graph.
- **Online-MIS** (Dahlum et al., 2016): randomised streaming heuristic; vertices processed in random order, each accepted if no already-accepted neighbour is in the set; 5 seeds.
- **ReduMIS** (Lamm et al., 2017): graph kernelisation + reduction rules + stochastic local search; ~600 s per graph; 5 seeds.

**Benchmarks.** Four HELM Capabilities benchmarks covering knowledge-intensive QA (MMLU-Pro, GPQA), instruction following (IFEval), and advanced math (Omni-MATH), with prompt counts 446–1000 and accuracy spreads 0.072–0.951 across 66 frontier and open LLMs (GPT, Claude, Gemini, Llama, Qwen3, DeepSeek, Mistral). All HELM scores are taken as of May 2025.

**Evaluation metrics.** Spearman's ρ compares each MIS subset's LLM ranking against the full-benchmark ranking. Kendall's W (Coefficient of Concordance) measures internal concordance across the five random seeds of a stochastic solver. W ≥ 0.90 is the high-concordance threshold (Landis & Koch, 1977). Both are rank-based, robust to score discretisation.

## Results & Highlights

- **All four algorithms achieve nearly identical global ranking fidelity** (mean ρ ≈ 0.967–0.968 vs. full benchmark; fraction with ρ ≥ 0.95 ≈ 84%). Solver choice is essentially a wash for rank preservation; it mainly affects cross-seed stability.
- **ReduMIS is the most stable stochastic solver** (W = 0.9976, W_std = 0.0076), narrowly beating Online-MIS (W = 0.9963, W_std = 0.0102). Both substantially exceed the W ≥ 0.90 bar.
- **Threshold sweep reveals a clean monotone trade-off.** From p10 → p95, ρ rises from 0.905 → 0.997 and W from 0.9901 → 0.9996; mean subset size grows from 70 → 593 prompts, i.e. prompt reduction drops from 88–93% to 17–23%. p80 is the inflection point: ρ = 0.992, W = 0.999, reduction 37–45%.
- **Per-benchmark ranking quality.** MMLU-Pro and Omni-MATH are the easiest to compress (mean ρ ≈ 0.987–0.993). IFEval is the hardest (ρ ≈ 0.920–0.921 at the per-benchmark level), reflecting its instruction-following structure where embedding similarity is less predictive of performance similarity; even so, W ≈ 0.991–0.994.
- **Embedding-model breakdown.** Large decoder-based models yield a higher share of "consistent + matches baseline" configurations (Nemotron 86.2%, Qwen-4B 85.3%, Qwen-8B 87.5%) than small encoder models (BGE 80.4%, E5 79.2%, GIST 75.7%). Qwen-8B + standardized Euclidean reaches the best W = 0.9993.
- **Failure modes are diagnosable.** All 0.8% of configurations where W < 0.90 trace to the same recipe (E5 embeddings + IFEval + Online-MIS + p10 + cosine/Pearson). Of the 15.95% with ρ < 0.95, 56.7% occur at p10, 28.6% at p20, and 85.3% are concentrated on GPQA + IFEval—the two benchmarks with the most discretised score distributions.
- **Case study.** An Online-MIS / IFEval / Qwen-4B / cosine / p20 configuration yields W = 0.972 (seeds agree) but ρ = 0.820 (the MIS subset is "distinctive"). The heatmap shows the subset focuses on a restricted slice of instruction-following tasks, surfacing a complementary model capability profile.

## Critical Assessment

- **Methodological rigour.** The experimental grid is unusually thorough: 4 solvers × 6 embeddings × 3 distances × 6 thresholds × 4 benchmarks × 66 LLMs, with five seeds per stochastic configuration, yielding 2,563 unique runs. Both global-rank (Spearman) and cross-seed (Kendall W) agreements are reported, with proper tie-correction terms and pre-registered concordance thresholds. The ablation across algorithm / embedding / distance / threshold / benchmark is clean and the figures (especially Figure 4) clearly visualise the trade-off.
- **Coverage-bias argument is the genuinely novel claim, and it is half-proven.** The paper successfully demonstrates that MIS gives consistent rankings (W high) and that the resulting subsets sometimes differ from the full-benchmark baseline (ρ varies). But the leap from "MIS uniformly samples the embedding space" to "MIS corrects a coverage bias in the original benchmark" is asserted geometrically rather than empirically validated. There is no controlled experiment that injects a known bias into a benchmark and shows MIS recovers the unbiased ranking—only an illustrative t-SNE figure (Figure 2) and a post-hoc re-interpretation. The causal claim would be much stronger with such a controlled study.
- **No comparison to existing subset-selection baselines.** The paper does not run head-to-head against **tinyBenchmarks** (Polo et al., 2024), **Anchor Points** (Vivek et al., 2024), the IRT-based compressions of Zhou et al. (2026), or the multidimensional IRT of Chen et al. (2025)—the very methods it positions itself against in the related-work section. Given that tinyBenchmarks claims 100-example subsets that reproduce full-benchmark results, an apples-to-apples comparison would be the obvious next step and is conspicuously absent.
- **HELM scores are taken as ground truth.** All 66 LLM accuracy values are scraped from the HELM leaderboard as of May 2025, with no controlled prompting protocol. The authors acknowledge this in the experimental setup, but the consequence is that "agreement with the full benchmark" measures agreement with HELM's heterogeneous prompting regime, not with a true capability ordering. A subset selection method that "agrees" with HELM at ρ ≈ 0.97 may simply be agreeing with HELM's specific prompting artefacts.
- **Generalisability flags.** All four benchmarks are single-turn English prompts. Multi-turn dialogue, code generation, multilingual, and safety benchmarks are untested. IFEval's lower agreement suggests the framework is most fragile on benchmarks where embedding similarity is a poor proxy for capability similarity—an important caveat for instruction-following or agentic evaluations.
- **Reproducibility.** "Source code and data are available upon request from the authors" is below the current norm for an empirical paper of this size.

## Strengths

- **Conceptual unification.** Reframes two apparently separate problems—evaluation cost and coverage bias—as consequences of one underlying phenomenon (semantic redundancy among prompts) and addresses them with one tool (MIS). The geometric argument is intuitive and the t-SNE illustration is convincing.
- **Breadth of the empirical sweep.** 2,563 configurations spanning 4 algorithms, 6 embeddings, 3 distances, 6 thresholds, 4 benchmarks, and 66 LLMs is far more thorough than typical subset-selection papers, and the cross-seed concordance analysis (W) is the right diagnostic for stochastic MIS solvers.
- **Practical, actionable recommendations.** The "ReduMIS + Qwen-8B + seuclidean + p80" recipe with W = 1.000 and ρ = 0.9959 is exactly what a practitioner needs. The threshold-sweep table cleanly shows where diminishing returns kick in.
- **Diagnostic framing of failures.** The breakdown of low-ρ configurations by threshold and benchmark (15.95% of runs, 85% on GPQA + IFEval, mostly at p10–p20) gives users a concrete way to anticipate when MIS will or will not work for their benchmark.
- **Theoretical elegance.** The independent-set property mathematically guarantees no embedding-space ball of radius θ/2 contains more than one selected prompt, providing a structural coverage guarantee that random or stratified sampling cannot offer.

## Limitations

- **The coverage-correction claim is not directly validated.** No controlled experiment injects a known bias and shows MIS recovers the unbiased ranking; the bias-correctness argument rests on geometric intuition and a single t-SNE plot.
- **IFEval behaves as an outlier.** Mean ρ ≈ 0.920 on IFEval is the lowest of the four benchmarks and a meaningful gap below the others (0.971–0.993). For instruction-following-style benchmarks where embedding similarity may not track capability similarity, MIS can produce consistent-but-distinctive rankings that diverge substantially from the full benchmark—useful, but a real failure mode for users who want compressed evaluation that agrees with the original.
- **No head-to-head comparison with IRT, tinyBenchmarks, or Anchor Points.** The paper positions itself against these methods but does not benchmark against them on the same 66 LLMs and four benchmarks.
- **HELM scores treated as ground truth.** Without a controlled prompting protocol, "agreement with full benchmark" is agreement with HELM's heterogeneous prompting, not with a clean capability ordering.
- **English-only, single-turn, text-only benchmarks.** Multilingual, multi-turn, code, and safety benchmarks are not evaluated. Decoder-based large embeddings (Nemotron-8B, Qwen-8B) require significant GPU memory.
- **Reproducibility is limited.** Code released only on request; no public repository, no model checkpoints for the embeddings used, no frozen LLM evaluation harness.
- **Static snapshot.** Results are for 66 LLMs as of mid-2025; the framework is evaluated once, not as a longitudinal study of how compressed-subset rankings track the frontier over time (a concern raised by Zhang et al. 2026 for subset-based prediction in general).

## Relevance

本研究与"如何高效、可靠地评估 LLM"这一核心问题直接相关。对于关注 LLM 评测基础设施、leaderboard 设计、以及基准偏差问题的研究者，这篇工作提供了一个具体可落地的工具：用图论方法在保证排名一致性的同时压缩评估规模。MIS 视角对"覆盖偏差"的修正尤为值得借鉴——基准设计者可以用度分布来量化结构性偏差并据此筛选更平衡的子集。方法局限性（IFEval 上的 ρ ≈ 0.92、对多语言/多轮/代码基准未验证）也提示我们在使用压缩子集做前沿能力判断时需格外谨慎。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- **Does MIS selection improve over IRT-based methods (Polo et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2026; Chen et al., 2025) on the same 66 LLMs at matched subset sizes?** A controlled head-to-head would clarify whether the graph-theoretic framing offers a Pareto improvement or simply a different point on the same frontier.
- **Can a controlled bias-injection experiment validate the coverage-correction claim?** Take a uniform synthetic benchmark, inject a known overrepresentation in one semantic region, run MIS, and check whether the recovered ranking matches the un-biased ground truth.
- **Why does IFEval behave so differently?** Hypothesis: instruction-following prompts share surface form but test distinct sub-skills, so embedding similarity is a poor proxy for capability similarity. Disentangling this from "IFEval's scores are more discretised" would require per-prompt ablation.
- **Does the framework transfer to multi-turn dialogue, code generation, multilingual, and safety benchmarks?** These are precisely the regimes where embedding-based selection is most likely to fail—and most needed for cost reasons.
- **Can adaptive threshold selection (data-driven, per-benchmark) replace the fixed-percentile grid?** The current paper notes p80 as a sweet spot for the four benchmarks tested; an adaptive rule would generalise better.
- **How does MIS-subset ranking fidelity evolve as the LLM frontier advances?** Zhang et al. (2026) warn that subset-based prediction degrades at the frontier. Does the recommendation to use MIS subsets (rather than IRT subsets) avoid this, or is the same boundary present?
- **What does the full-benchmark similarity-graph degree distribution tell us about benchmark quality?** The paper hints at this as a future diagnostic; reporting degree histograms alongside accuracy would let benchmark designers see *where* their benchmark is structurally biased before running any LLM.
- **Is coverage uniformity empirically superior to other coverage criteria** (e.g. stratification by topic, difficulty-stratified sampling) in terms of ranking fidelity per prompt?
- **What is the relationship between embedding-model alignment with the capability being tested and MIS-subset quality?** Qwen-8B beats BGE on average; would a domain-specialised embedding (e.g. math-tuned for Omni-MATH) further close the gap to the full benchmark?
