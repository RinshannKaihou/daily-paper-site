# FP8 is All You Need (Part 1): Debunking Hardware FP64 as the HPC Holy Grail

> **一句话总结：** Matsuoka 主张在 B300 与 Rubin 这类 FP64 算力崩塌的 AI 优化 GPU 上，通过基于中国剩余定理的 Ozaki Scheme II 将 FP8 矩阵乘作为双精度科学计算的唯一硬件原语，并通过一个五层分析框架 (L0–L4) 与 TME 性能模型证明所有 Berkeley "dwarfs" 都能在 FP64 精度下回到内存墙限定的性能区间。

> **TL;DR:** Satoshi Matsuoka argues that on AI-optimized GPUs such as the NVIDIA B300 and Rubin R200 — where native FP64 throughput has collapsed to roughly 1.3 TFLOPS while FP8 tensor cores deliver several PFLOPS — the FP8 tensor-core matrix-multiply is the *sole* computational primitive on which double-precision scientific computing should be built. Using the Chinese Remainder Theorem-based Ozaki Scheme II (3r+1 = 37 FP8 MMAs at r=12), every Berkeley "dwarf" (GEMM, SpMV, stencil, FFT, batched GEMV) reduces to sequences of FP8 matrix ops plus a bounded INT32 Garner reconstruction. The Tensor–Memory Equilibrium (TME) model — a Roofline extension with parameters (α, β, γ) — shows that register-level fusion drives β → 1, keeping memory-bound kernels at the memory roof while lifting the dense ceiling to ~135 TFLOPS on B300 (~104× over native) and ~108 TFLOPS on Rubin, surpassing even B200's native 40 TFLOPS.

## 核心贡献

- **"FP8 is all you need" 论题**：FP8 矩阵乘 (L0) 通过 Ozaki II (L1) 组合出 FP64 精确的乘积，使得所有 Berkeley dwarfs (L2)、复合求解器核 (L3) 与完整科学应用 (L4) 都归约到一个单一硬件原语；native FP64 由硬件需求降级为由组合得到的导出精度保证。
- **五层组合层级 (L0–L4)**：FP8 MMA → Ozaki II → Berkeley dwarfs → 复合求解器 → 完整应用；通过 dwarfs 已经被证明覆盖科学计算的事实，只需对每个 dwarf 给出归约即完成对整个领域的论证。
- **可证伪的分析工具**：构建 Tensor–Memory Equilibrium (TME) 模型，将经典 Roofline 扩展为包含 (α, β, γ) 三个参数的版本，使论题可被逐核检验并可能被反驳；分析表明该论题在 L2 与 L3 上都成立。
- **寄存器级融合机制**：识别出 residue plane 全程驻留寄存器、Garner 重建只发生在累加器上的设计原则是 β → 1 的结构性原因，把"FP8 可以模拟 FP64"转化为"FP8 把 memory-bound 核留在 memory roof 上"。
- **跨架构量化预测**：在 B300 与 Rubin R200 上给出全 OI 谱的 emulated FP64 throughput；相对 H100 baseline，每个工作负载都匹配或超过 H100，而 native B300 在某些核上退化达 50×。
- **清晰的"分析—验证"分工**：本文完成分析论题的另一半；后续实现工作在真实硅上测量 (α, β) 的兑现度，并明确列出 β 的组成开销与一组可识别的 corner case。

## Key Contributions

- The **"FP8 is all you need" thesis**: the FP8 tensor-core matrix-multiply is the sole computational primitive of double-precision scientific computing; native FP64 is demoted from a hardware requirement to a derived accuracy guarantee obtained by composition over FP8.
- A **five-layer compositional hierarchy (L0–L4)** that makes the thesis precise and a Berkeley-"dwarfs" argument that makes it complete: L0 = FP8 MMA; L1 = Ozaki II; L2 = dwarfs (GEMM, SpMV, FFT, stencil, batched GEMV); L3 = composite solver kernels (Krylov, LOBPCG, sparse-direct panels, QCD Dirac); L4 = full apps (CFD, climate, FEM, plane-wave DFT, MD).
- **Falsifiability and the testing instrument**: every kernel must stay on the bandwidth-bound side of the roofline under FP8 emulation; the TME model with emulation parameters (α, β, γ) is built expressly so that the claim could be refuted per kernel.
- **Register-level fusion as the efficiency mechanism**: residue planes live in registers and Garner reconstruction runs on the accumulator before store, driving β → 1 and keeping the residue/reconstruction traffic on-chip so emulation is essentially free behind the memory wall.
- **Quantitative substantiation across B300 and Rubin R200**: emulated dense ceiling ~135 TFLOPS on B300 and ~108 TFLOPS on Rubin (r=12, α = 3r+1 = 37), exceeding B200's 40 TFLOPS native by ~3.4× and ~2.7× respectively; memory-bound kernels span ~1.2× (SpMV) to ~24× (batched GEMV) speedups over native; every workload matches or exceeds the H100 baseline.
- **Complete kernel-coverage audit** via the companion FFT analysis [24] and FP32+Kahan compensation for BLAS-1 reductions, so every surveyed class reaches the memory roof at full FP64 accuracy.

## Methodology

The paper's central methodological move is to construct a **Tensor–Memory Equilibrium (TME)** model that augments the classical Roofline [Williams et al. 2009] with three emulation-specific parameters:

- **α** = number of low-precision tensor-core MMAs per high-precision op. For Ozaki II on the FP8 substrate α = (3r+1): each of the r residue planes expands into three FP8 MMAs (the Karatsuba structure emulating signed int9) plus one MMA for the max-magnitude estimation pass. At the recommended r = 12, α = 37.
- **β ≥ 1** = bandwidth multiplier = ratio of bytes moved by the emulated kernel to the equivalent native FP64 kernel. β = 1 for fully fused on-chip decomposition (residues in registers, never reach HBM); β = r in the unfused regime.
- **γ ≥ 0** = per-output-element reconstruction latency for Garner's algorithm.

The native Roofline time is `Tnat = max(W/Pfp64, Q/Bmem) + Lmem`; the emulated time is `Temu = max(αW/Plow, βQ/Bmem) + γ·nout`. Three cases are derived:

1. **Case A** (native compute-bound, emulation memory-bound — the B300 regime): speedup equals the kernel's distance into the compute-bound region of the native roofline: `Tnat/Temu = I·Bmem/Pfp64` (with β = 1, γ ignored).
2. **Case B** (both memory-bound at small I): `Temu/Tnat → β`; so register-level fusion is a **correctness requirement**, not just an optimization.
3. **Case C** (both compute-bound, dense GEMM): speedup = ρ/α where ρ = Plow/Pfp64; on B300 with r=12, gives ~135 TFLOPS emulated ceiling (≈ 104× native).

On top of the model, the paper builds a **Berkeley-dwarfs-style completeness argument**: by organizing the kernel space as L0 → L1 → L2 → L3 → L4 and showing the reduction holds for every L2 dwarf (GEMM, SpMV via Blocked-Ellpack, 3D FFT via Ozaki–Bailey in Part 2, stencil via im2col, batched GEMV via warp-tile), the thesis is established for the entire dwarf taxonomy and hence for all of scientific computing by composition.

The error analysis (sketched in Appendix A) cites the worst-case componentwise bound for Ozaki II as ufp64 ≈ 2⁻⁵³ plus the float-to-integer rounding of the scaling step [Ozaki et al. 2026]; empirical relative errors stay within 2–10 ufp64 and recent ADP work [Schwarz et al. 2025] reports < 10% overhead for componentwise-accurate DGEMM emulation on Blackwell.

## Results & Highlights

**Architectural baseline (per-GPU, dense unless noted):**

| Metric | H100 | B200 | B300 | R200 (Rubin) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| FP64 Tensor native (TFLOPS) | 67 | 40 | ~1.2 | — (emulated) |
| FP64 Matrix emulated (TFLOPS) | — | — | via Ozaki II | ~200 (NVIDIA-published) |
| FP8 Tensor dense (TFLOPS) | 1,979 | 4,500 | 5,000 | ~4,000 |
| HBM Bandwidth (TB/s) | 3.35 | 7.7–8.0 | 8.0 | 22 |
| Memory ridge FLOPS/B (native) | 10.1 | 5.0 | **0.16** | 1.5 |

**TME-derived emulated FP64 ceilings (r = 12, α = 37):**
- B300: ~135 TFLOPS dense (~104× over native FP64 1.3 TFLOPS).
- Rubin R200: ~108 TFLOPS dense with the same parameters; NVIDIA's published ~200 TFLOPS "Emulated DGEMM" reflects either INT8 substrate or more moduli.

**Per-kernel projected speedups over native FP64 on B300 (memory-bound regime, β = 1):**
- SpMV (OI ≈ 0.2 FLOPS/B): ~1.2×.
- 7-point stencil (OI ≈ 0.5): ~3×.
- Batched GEMV at B ≈ 8 (OI ≈ 4): ~24–25× → 32 TFLOPS at memory roof.
- Dense GEMM: ~104× at r = 12 (compute-bound regime).
- On Rubin the bandwidth jump to 22 TB/s lifts all of these by 2.75× across the memory-bound region.

**H100 re-baseline**: every workload on every GPU studied (H100 / B300 / R200) matches or exceeds H100 under emulation; native B300 regresses by up to ~50× on the same workloads.

**Slice-count (Ozaki I) summary for FP64-accurate output:** fp16/fp32-accum needs ≈ 6 slices at k=4096; int8/int32-accum ≈ 7; fp8 (E4M3)/fp32-accum ≈ 18 — fp8 is accumulator-bound and needs the most slices, but Ozaki II's linear r-scaling (r ∈ [11, 14]) replaces the Θ(S²) quadratic of Ozaki I.

**Key analytic observations:**
- The (3r+1) FP8 cost correction (v1 → v2) only affects the dense compute-bound ceiling (Case C); in Cases A and B the surveyed memory-bound kernels sit far below the emulation ridge so the memory-bound result is untouched.
- Rubin silicon-area trade-off: dense FP8 throughput on Rubin (~4 PFLOPS) is *lower* than B300 (~5 PFLOPS) because NVIDIA reallocated area to NVFP4/FP6; the Rubin advantage is bandwidth (22 TB/s vs. 8 TB/s), not FP8.
- β = 1 is an ideal that must be earned by kernel engineering: register pressure (r residue planes compete with the register file that bounds occupancy) and per-operand preprocessing are real costs; quantifying how close real kernels land to β = 1 at production tile sizes is the central measurement target of the follow-on implementation work.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty.** The structural framing — collapsing the Berkeley dwarfs taxonomy onto a single FP8 primitive via Ozaki II, organized as L0–L4 — is novel and arguably the paper's strongest contribution. The TME model itself is a modest but useful extension of Roofline; the β parameterization is well-chosen because it cleanly separates a "fixed bound" from a "realizable bound." The (3r+1) FP8 cost correction (the +1 max-magnitude pass and the ×3 Karatsuba factor) is also a genuinely useful clarification of the compute ceiling — an earlier draft overstated the ceiling by 3×.

**Rigor.** High in the model construction and in the per-dwarf reduction arguments (the Stencil-via-im2col, SpMV-via-Blocked-Ellpack, batched GEMV analyses are specific and checkable). The cross-architecture tables cite NVIDIA datasheets and independent microbenchmarks [Jarmusch & Chandrasekaran 2025]. Lower in two places: (i) the empirical verification is deferred to a follow-on implementation paper — the central claim is therefore not yet "demonstrated on hardware," only "survives the model"; (ii) the error analysis is sketched (Appendix A) rather than derived fresh, and the author leans on prior Ozaki-II bounds [Ozaki et al. 2026] and ADP results [Schwarz et al. 2025].

**Reproducibility.** Strong on the architectural constants and modeling framework (every table value is sourced; the TME equations are explicit). The actual kernel designs are at the pseudocode/Algorithm-1/2 level — useful as a blueprint but not drop-in code. Reference libraries exist (GEMMul8 [Uchino 2025], cuBLAS Ozaki path [NVIDIA 2025]) for cross-checking.

**Significance.** High if the thesis holds empirically — it would reframe the AI-HPC convergence debate from "AI threatens double-precision science" to "AI silicon is its natural foundation." The paper also has a clear strategic value: it provides a quantitative case for the FugakuNEXT / Genesis Mission / Rubin deployment path that several major HPC centers are already pursuing. The H100-rebaseline message ("every workload matches or exceeds H100") is the punchline likely to land with program managers.

**Red flags.**
1. **Single-author, deliberately polemical thesis**: "FP8 is all you need" is presented as falsifiable, but the falsifiability analysis (§9) is bounded — the three failure modes are clean *in principle*, but the empirical "this would fail if X" tests have not been run. The paper itself acknowledges it is the "analytical half of a two-part argument."
2. **β = 1 ideal vs. delivered β**: the headline ~104× GEMM speedup is gated on full (3r+1) FP8 cost being charged and is a *compute-bound* projection; the more interesting and novel claim — that memory-bound kernels stay at the memory roof — depends on register-fusion being achievable at production tile sizes, which is *not* established in this paper.
3. **AMD counter-evidence**: AMD's positioning around MI430X with big native FP64 increases is mentioned only in passing; if AMD ships parts with native FP64 competitive with Rubin's emulated DGEMM, the strategic argument narrows substantially.
4. **Application-semantics corner cases**: NaN/Inf, bitwise reproducibility, convergence of emulated-kernel preconditioned iterative solvers, and behavior on severely ill-conditioned inputs are openly listed as unresolved. These are not fatal but they are precisely the properties production HPC codes depend on.
5. **Composition overhead β**: explicitly deferred to §8.3 measurement. The paper admits "some of these bounds [are] likely to be shown only partially attainable in practice."
6. **v1 → v2 correction factor of ~3×** on the dense ceiling is large and worth noting — earlier dissemination of the v1 numbers may have set expectations that this paper now resets downward; this is intellectually honest but a reminder that even the corrected model is a model.
7. **Self-citation density**: several of the most-cited references are from R-CCS authors or collaborators; this is not disqualifying (Ozaki II genuinely originates there) but readers should weigh the independence of the error-analysis claims.

Overall: a structurally novel, intellectually honest, and clearly argued analysis paper. It establishes the case for FP8-as-foundation at the *bound* level; whether it survives at the *delivered* level is the empirical question it deliberately leaves to its companion.

## Strengths

- Clean structural framing (L0–L4) that connects a hardware primitive to an established kernel taxonomy (Berkeley dwarfs) and makes the thesis precise and verifiable rather than rhetorical.
- TME model with explicit (α, β, γ) parameters cleanly separates the "is the bound tight" question from the "can the kernel earn it" question — the latter (β) is honestly flagged as the central empirical open question.
- Quantitative cross-architecture comparison (H100 / B200 / B300 / R200) with cited datasheet and microbenchmark sources; the memory ridge point collapse from 10.1 (H100) to 0.16 (B300) is a striking and well-marshalled figure.
- Honest treatment of v1's α = r error: corrects the dense ceiling from ~500 to ~135 TFLOPS in v2, explains where the correction does and does not matter, and credits the NVIDIA libraries/DevTech team for the catch.
- Explicit falsifiability discussion (§9) with three clean failure modes rather than hedging throughout.
- Kernel-design discipline (register-level fusion as the central engineering constraint for β → 1) is concretely connected to occupancy, register pressure, and tile-size trade-offs.

## Limitations

- **No silicon measurement**: the central quantitative claims are projections from the TME model, not delivered numbers. The follow-on implementation paper (Part 2 + a separate validation effort) is required to confirm.
- **β = 1 idealization**: the central memory-bound claim depends on register-resident residue planes, which compete with the register file that bounds occupancy. The achievable β at production tile sizes is unknown and the paper explicitly acknowledges it.
- **Sparse kernel coverage**: the argument leans on Blocked-Ellpack / hybrid CSR formulations to map irregular sparse work onto dense blocks; whether this works for the most adversarial sparsity patterns is open.
- **Exception semantics, reproducibility, solver convergence**: acknowledged as corner cases that need bracketing on real inputs but not addressed here.
- **Composition overhead**: γ (Garner reconstruction) and α (residue expansion) costs under composition (Krylov, LOBPCG, sparse-direct) are treated as constant-per-output but in real solvers are nested; not modelled.
- **2:4 structured sparsity + Ozaki II**: explicitly noted as open (the sparsity mask may invalidate modular reduction because it is data-dependent).
- **Single-author**: while Matsuoka is exceptionally well-positioned, the analytical claims could benefit from independent co-author review beyond the acknowledged critique from Dongarra/Domke/Walters.
- **AI-assisted drafting disclosure**: the manuscript's reliance on LLM assistance for copy editing is disclosed openly; scientific content was reviewed by the author.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. **Delivered β on real silicon**: at production tile sizes for SpMV (Blocked-Ellpack), stencil (im2col), and batched GEMV, what fraction of the β = 1 ideal survives once the r residue planes compete for the register file? Which kernel classes are most likely to need the codesign response (more registers / better fusion / fp32 fallback)?
2. **Composition overhead γ**: how does the Garner reconstruction cost and per-operand preprocessing scale when kernels are composed (Krylov, LOBPCG, sparse-direct panels, QCD Dirac inverter) rather than executed once? Is γ effectively constant-per-output, or does nesting inflate it?
3. **Adaptive precision (ESC/ADP) interaction**: how often does the ADP scheme of Schwarz et al. (2025) need to raise the slice count or fall back to native FP64 on realistic application inputs (CFD, climate, plane-wave DFT) — and does the occasional fallback materially erode the memory-roof advantage?
4. **AMD MI430X competitive landscape**: if AMD ships native FP64 competitive with Rubin's emulated DGEMM, how does the strategic case for emulation-driven HPC narrow? Should the framework be extended to model a mixed-vendor silicon portfolio?
5. **Sparse primitive mapping**: can irregular sparse matrices be put into tensor-core-amenable (Blocked-Ellpack / hybrid CSR) form without inflating data movement? Which sparsity patterns are adversarial and how often do they appear in real scientific workloads?
6. **Exception semantics and reproducibility**: does emulated FP64 deliver bitwise-reproducible, IEEE-conformant NaN/Inf behaviour for full applications, and is the preconditioned-iterative-solver convergence of emulated-kernel Krylov methods stable on ill-conditioned inputs?
7. **2:4 structured sparsity + Ozaki II**: is there a clean construction for applying the sparsity mask at the residue-plane level without invalidating modular reduction, and what speedup could structured-sparse Ozaki II deliver on Hopper-style sparse tensor cores?
8. **TME model extensions**: parameterizing β(r, Tk, Tm, Tn, regs) explicitly and predicting the joint optimum of tile shape and moduli count; extending to non-GEMM/FFT hot spots (sort, scan, atomic-heavy graph traversals).
9. **Long-term silicon trajectory**: as the silicon mix evolves (NVFP4, FP6), does the TME benefit narrow, and how should the framework rebalance α, β, γ for future generations?
