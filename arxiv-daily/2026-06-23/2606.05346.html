Hidden-state trajectory extrapolation error predicts reading cost — independently of surprisal.

Trajectory Dynamics in Language Model Hidden States Predict Human Processing Costs Beyond Surprisal
Elan Barenholtz · Florida Atlantic University 2026-06-03 single-author
psycholinguistics surprisal theory transformer interp. dynamical systems
Key metric · Pythia-160M
∆AIC +67.0over a surprisal-only baseline · p < 10⁻¹⁶
A linear fit to the previous 3 hidden states adds massively to log-RT prediction — without ever touching token-level probability.
Why this matters
It lifts hidden-state geometry from a side statistic into an independent predictor of processing cost, unifying surprisal theory with a dynamical-systems view of comprehension — word-level prediction error and local directional continuity are two dissociable axes.
Critical edge
The paper shows that trajectory deviation predicts reading time, but never specifies why a human comprehender finds geometric deviation computationally costly rather than merely informative — a mechanistic theory is missing.
01 / METHOD

Methodology 方法

Materials. Two corpora: the SAP Benchmark Classic Garden Path (24 items, 2,000+ participants, self-paced reading, 100–5,000 ms filter) and Natural Stories (10 narratives, ~10k words, 845,479 observations from 181 participants, 100–3,000 ms filter, processed in overlapping 1,024-token chunks).

The measure. At each word t, fit an OLS line through hidden states ht-k…ht-1 at a chosen layer, extrapolate one step ahead, and measure the Euclidean distance ‖ĥt − ht‖. Subword tokens use the last subword's hidden state. Default: k = 3, layer 6.

Sweep. Layer 6 (intermediate; best for syntax per Hewitt & Manning 2019, Jawahar et al. 2019) vs. final layer 12; windows k ∈ {3, 5, 7}; linear vs. quadratic. Longer windows and quadratic fits lose, consistent with the direction-preservation decay below.

Direction preservation. Absolute cosine between the fitted trajectory direction and the actual displacement at the current position and +1/+2/+3 ahead. Random 768-d baseline ≈ 0.029. At layer 6 the signal dies within one word; at layer 12 it persists.

Displacement control. Joint regression with dt = ‖ht − ht-1‖. The two correlate only r = .16 in GPT-2 — separable contributions, opposite signs.

Models. Linear mixed-effects on log RT, random intercepts for participants, z-scored predictors. Baseline M0: word length + word position + previous log RT + log word frequency. M1: + surprisal. M2: + extrapolation error. M3–M5: robustness across layer/window. AIC, BIC, χ²(1) LRT.

Cross-architecture. Mid-layer of GPT-2 S/M/L = 6 / 12 / 18 (50% depth). Plus Pythia-160M / 410M (Rotary Position Embeddings) on a 100-participant Natural Stories subsample.

Garden-path. Surprisal alone does not improve over frequency controls on the SAP subset (∆AIC = −1.9, p = .71). Extrapolation error at layer 6, k = 3 adds ∆AIC = +10.7 (χ²(1) = 12.7, p = 3.7×10⁻⁴; β = +.005). At layer 12, k = 5, ∆AIC reaches +56.4 (p < 10⁻¹⁰).

Natural Stories — independence. Word-level r(surprisal, extrapolation) = .044. A 3×3 tercile matrix shows significant off-diagonal effects: high-surprisal / low-extrapolation adds +0.039 to log RT (t = 21.24, p < 10⁻¹⁰⁰); low-surprisal / high-extrapolation adds +0.008 (t = 4.67, p = 3×10⁻⁶). Additive model beats the interaction model (∆AIC = −2.0).

The opposite-sign displacement dissociation in GPT-2 (displacement β = −.008, extrapolation β = +.001) rules out "this is just representational change magnitude." Pythia does not replicate the opposite-sign pattern, attributed to RoPE geometry differences.

Direction preservation. Layer 6: 0.44 (current) → 0.10 (+1) → 0.08 (+2, +3). Final layer 12: 0.61 → 0.54 → 0.54 → 0.54 (persistent). Higher-surprisal words show lower preservation (r = −.10).

Scale & replication. ∆AIC over surprisal — GPT-2 Small +10.7 (p = 3.7×10⁻⁴), Medium +1.6 (marginal), Large +13.1 (p = 1.0×10⁻⁴). Pythia-160M +67.0, Pythia-410M +13.3; r(surprisal, extrapolation) holds at .046 / .047.

Qualitative dissociation. Low-surprisal / high-extrapolation cells are enriched for coordinators and complementizers ("and", "as", "that", "had") — lexically routine words that open a new constituent. High-surprisal / low-extrapolation cells contain thematic content ("ocean", "manor") and discourse pivots ("then", "however", "now") — lexically unexpected but already structurally accommodated.

03 / STRENGTHS

Strengths

  • One line of math (linear fit + Euclidean distance) — independent verification is trivial.
  • r ≈ .044 near-orthogonality is a striking empirical fact; should drive follow-up work.
  • Opposite-sign displacement control in GPT-2 cleanly rules out "just change magnitude."
  • Cross-architecture replication (RoPE vs. absolute embeddings) — unusually thorough for psycholinguistics.
  • Direction-preservation analysis pins down the timescale (k = 3 window); avoids over-interpretation as long-range momentum.
  • Unifies surprisal theory, the scaling paradox, lossy-context surprisal, and a production-planning horizon in one account.
  • Off-diagonal vocabulary mapping (coordinators vs. discourse pivots) anchors the statistics in psycholinguistic intuition.
  • Effect detected in naturalistic text (Natural Stories), not only artificial garden-path stimuli.
04 / LIMITATIONS

Limitations

  • Self-paced reading only. No eye-tracking (first-pass vs. reanalysis) and no neural data (EEG/MEG) to triangulate to mechanism.
  • Garden-path subset is small (24 items); trajectory variance partially absorbs into by-item intercepts.
  • Surprisal itself fails on the SAP subset (∆AIC = −1.9) — so the contrast is "trajectory vs. nothing," not "trajectory beyond surprisal," within that dataset.
  • Pythia replication uses a 100-participant subsample; the headline +67.0 ∆AIC should be read with that in mind.
  • Opposite-sign pattern does not replicate in Pythia — what the measure "actually" captures remains less settled than it appears.
  • No public code or data release stated in the paper; reproducibility requires re-implementation.
  • Linear-only fits dominate — the measure is effectively a smoothness probe, not a full dynamical descriptor.
  • No cognitive model of the cost mechanism: predicts behavior but not why geometric deviation is computationally expensive.
  • Single-author with no co-author for statistical or NLP oversight; no preregistration.
  • Effect size is modest in absolute terms on naturalistic text.
05 / QUESTIONS

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does trajectory extrapolation error behave on Llama 3 / Mistral / Qwen 2.5 — does scale or training compute change the effect size or the surprisal/extrapolation correlation?
  2. Can the effect be detected in eye-tracking corpora (Dundee, GECO, ZuCo), separating first-pass from regression-path effects?
  3. Do EEG/MEG signals (N400, P600, frontal theta) track extrapolation error independent of surprisal — moving from behavior to mechanism?
  4. What does a layer-by-layer sweep on the same model reveal about where directional structure emerges and disappears?
  5. Can stimuli be matched on surprisal but varying in trajectory disruption (and vice versa) to deliver a cleaner stimulus-level dissociation?
  6. Does the effect generalize across typologies — head-final, agglutinative, or logographic languages? Natural Stories is English-only.
  7. Does trajectory extrapolation error quantitatively track lossy-context surprisal (Futrell et al. 2020) under controlled context truncation?
  8. Is there a decoding-time intervention (e.g., beam-search penalty on extrapolation error) that produces measurably easier-to-read text?
  9. Can categorical-by-item random slopes recover the absorbed garden-path trajectory variance if a larger item pool is collected?
  10. Does the low-surprisal / high-extrapolation cell's lexical composition shift across languages (coordinators vs. discourse markers vs. complementizers)?
