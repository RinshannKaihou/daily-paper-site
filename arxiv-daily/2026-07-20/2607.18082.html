Why this matters

Names and quantifies a previously ignored GRPO failure mode (Suppressed Criteria) — a plug-and-play on-policy fix for any GRPO + LLM-as-judge open-ended RL pipeline.

Critical edge

Gains shrink +3.2 → +1.4 from 1.7B to 4B with no 7B+ results, and CriPO-S alone ties full CriPO at 4B — the combined method isn't strictly better than its parts.

The framework starts from a diagnostic. For each prompt, G rollouts are scored criterion-wise by a Qwen3-32B judge, aggregated into a scalar reward, and turned into standard GRPO group-relative advantages. Two failure modes are then surfaced — both persistent, both previously unformalised.

Unexplored Criteria (UC) are satisfied by no rollout in the group, so GRPO has no positive trajectory to copy from. CriPO builds a criterion-injection self-teacher that minimally revises the highest-advantage rollout to satisfy the missing criteria, then distills only at the most informative positions: forward-KL is computed per token, clamped at 10, and masked at the first/last 1% — only the smallest token set carrying γ=0.95 of cumulative KL contributes (~34.6% of tokens).

Suppressed Criteria (SC) are satisfied by some rollouts but lose their learning signal under scalar aggregation. A counterfactual self-teacher is asked to weaken or delete the satisfying spans; positions where the student up-weights the original token (Δ>0) while the teacher confidently swaps it (p_T < α·max) are localised, and their rollout-level advantage is replaced with a positive τ_flip=0.1. The original GRPO objective then runs on these modified token-level advantages.

Crucially, OPSD is never used standalone. The composite loss is L_CriPO = L_GRPO + β·L_OPSD with a small β and a 50-step linear warmup — a deliberate hedge against OPSD's documented entropy collapse. Training runs on 8×A800-40G with the judge hosted on 64×910B2 NPUs, on the public verl framework.

Same primitive, opposite outcomes. The gain comes from GRPO grounding + filtered application, not from OPSD itself — the hybrid is the contribution, and the OPSD-only collapse is the central result the paper uses to motivate it but never fully reconciles.

03 / Critical assessment

Fresh diagnostic, solid recipe — thin at scale.

Strengths

  • Crisp, falsifiable problem formulation. Two named failure modes with explicit equations (Eqs. 3–4) and large-scale prevalence numbers (83%+ UC, 57%+ SC) measured throughout training.
  • Interventions match their targets. Per-mode ablations actually support the mechanistic story: CriPO-U is the biggest UC reducer, CriPO-S the biggest SC reducer.
  • Stability-aware engineering. Forward-KL clamping, boundary masking, top-K criterion capping, best-rollout selection — each individually ablated, none left unjustified.
  • Honest about OPSD instability. Figure 2's standalone-OPSD collapse is shown rather than hidden; the hybrid is explicitly motivated by it.
  • Above-bar robustness checks. Cross-judge re-evaluation with Qwen3-32B (Table 4) and OOD instruction-following on IFEval/IFBench/MulDimIF (Table 3).

Limitations

  • Only two scales (1.7B, 4B). The gain shrinks from +3.2 to +1.4 and the trend is not investigated; no 7B+ evidence.
  • CriPO-S ties full CriPO at 4B (69.6). The case that the combined method is strictly better than its parts is not fully made at the larger scale.
  • Four hand-set hyperparameters (γ, α, τ_flip, K=3) with no sensitivity analysis — defaults justified only by intuition.
  • Length confound. CriPO produces longer responses (Figure 6) judged by length-biased GPT-4o-mini; acknowledged but not controlled.
  • Missing baseline. RuscaRL (the other canonical exploration-focused rubric-RL method) is discussed but never benchmarked; only HeRL is included.
  • Small eval samples. HealthBench/ResearchQA sub-sampled to 500, LLMEval-Med has 667 — per-benchmark 1–2-point deltas sit within judge noise.
  • No public code; base-model-specific filtering. The drop-reward>0.9 step is Qwen3-4B-specific and would need re-tuning elsewhere. The "2× fewer steps" claim is one (model, dataset) pair.

04 / Questions for further exploration

Where CriPO breaks — or quietly becomes GRPO.

  1. Do gains hold or grow at 7B–32B, or does the +3.2 → +1.4 trend continue — suggesting CriPO mostly helps weaker base models? Scaling
  2. Is there a regime where CriPO-S alone matches full CriPO at larger scale, given it already ties at 4B? If so, the UC module may be redundant past some size. Component redundancy
  3. How sensitive is CriPO to γ, α, τ_flip, K=3? Any of these four hand-set hyperparameters could be doing silent heavy lifting. Hyperparameter sweep
  4. Would a length-controlled judge (length-normalised rubric, or a judge blind to response length) preserve the reported gains, given CriPO's longer outputs? Length confound
  5. Can the counterfactual advantage-flipping idea port to verifiable-reward RLVR (math/code), where the analogue is process-level credit for partially correct rollouts? Transfer · RLVR
  6. How does CriPO interact with reward-hacking when the rubric judge itself is unreliable or adversarially gameable? Reward robustness
  7. Could the Suppressed-Criteria diagnostic (Eqs. 3–4) be used as a general data-quality signal — flagging prompts where GRPO is wasting samples — independent of CriPO as a fix? Diagnostic reuse
