> **一句话总结：** 作者提出 Tversky Monosemanticity Score (TMS)——一种无需外部概念标签、也无需外部 embedding 编码器的 SAE 单语义性度量，基于二值化潜变量的激活集合一致性 (Jaccard/Tversky 相似度)；在 DINOv3/CLIP/BLIP2 + TopK/BatchTopK 的 Mini-ImageNet SAE 上验证其比 embedding-based MS 对各向异性更鲁棒，并在各向异性较强的 CLIP 设置下对探针概念删除有效性给出更强相关性 (例如 CLIP SAE N=4 时 TMS 相关系数 0.864 vs MS 0.653)。

> **TL;DR:** A label-free, encoder-free monosemanticity metric for Sparse Autoencoders called Tversky Monosemanticity Score (TMS) is introduced; it operationalizes monosemanticity as within-set Tversky (Jaccard) coherence of binarized SAE latent activations and, across SAEs trained on DINOv3/CLIP/BLIP2 features (TopK and BatchTopK regimes, K∈{10,32,64}, expansion×{2,4,8}) on Mini-ImageNet, it stays aligned with the embedding-based Monosemanticity Score (MS) of Pach et al. while being far less inflated by encoder anisotropy (CLIP σcosine=0.52 vs DINOv3 0.07), and gives stronger SAE-level correlation with probe-based concept-deletion target drop under anisotropic encoders (e.g. CLIP, N=4: 0.864 vs 0.653 for MS).

## 核心贡献

- 提出 **Tversky Monosemanticity Score (TMS)**：第一个同时摆脱外部概念标签与外部 embedding 编码器的 SAE 单语义性度量。基于认知心理学 Tversky 特征相似度模型，对二值化 SAE 潜变量激活集合计算 within-set Jaccard/Tversky 相似度作为单语义性代理。
- 通过显式度量并对照 DINOv3 / CLIP / BLIP2 的各向异性 (σcosine 与 PC1 EVR)，证明 embedding-based MS 在各向异性较强的编码器上系统性偏高，而 TMS 在保持与 MS 强相关的同时显著降低这种几何偏差。
- 在统一设置下系统评估纯视觉 (DINOv3) 与视觉-语言 (CLIP, BLIP2) 模型的 SAE，覆盖 TopK / BatchTopK 两种稀疏机制、K∈{10,32,64}、扩张因子 ×{2,4,8}，为跨模态 SAE 评测提供一致的协议。
- 首次给出 SAE 训练过程中单语义性的动态演化分析 (TMS/MS vs epoch)，揭示不同基模与不同 K/expansion 下截然不同的训练轨迹。
- 提出基于线性探针的 "概念删除 (target drop)" 功能性验证实验，将 model-level 的单语义性与 feature-level 的可干预性定量挂钩，并对比 TMS 与 MS 的预测力。

## Key Contributions

- Introduces **TMS (Tversky Monosemanticity Score)**, a parameter-free monosemanticity metric that requires neither external concept labels nor any external embedding encoder at test time; it reduces (with α=β=1) to a within-active-set Jaccard coherence of binarized SAE latents, grounded in Tversky's feature-based similarity model.
- Explicitly measures embedding anisotropy (σcosine and PC1 explained-variance ratio) of DINOv3 / CLIP / BLIP2 and demonstrates that the reference Monosemanticity Score (MS) of Pach et al. is inflated by anisotropic encoders (CLIP highest), while TMS is largely invariant to this geometric bias.
- Provides a unified evaluation across pure vision (DINOv3) and vision-language (CLIP, BLIP2) feature sources, two SAE regimes (TopK, BatchTopK), three sparsity levels (K=10,32,64), and three expansion factors (×2,×4,×8), enabling a fair cross-model comparison rare in prior work that focuses on a single modality.
- Adds a dynamic analysis of monosemanticity trajectories across training epochs, revealing model- and regime-specific phases that static evaluation misses.
- Connects model-level monosemanticity to functional interpretability via a probe-based concept-deletion experiment, quantifying how strongly TMS vs MS predict target-class accuracy drop when top probe-weight SAE features are ablated.

## Methodology

**Definition of TMS.** Each SAE latent neuron k is treated as a concept detector. For stimulus n, the active-latent set B_n ⊆ {1,…,K} is obtained by binarizing SAE activations Z with a per-neuron, data-derived threshold μ_k (the training-set mean activation for k, i.e. B_{n,k}=1{Z_{n,k}>μ_k}); this is parameter-free and follows Aswal & Hudelot's mean-threshold binarization. For a latent k, its active sample set is A_k = { n : B_{n,k}=1 }. The within-set similarity for k is the average pairwise Tversky index over sampled pairs (ni,nj)∈A_k:

TI_{α,β}(B_ni,B_nj) = |B_ni∩B_nj| / (|B_ni∩B_nj| + α|B_ni\B_nj| + β|B_nj\B_ni|).

The paper uses α=β=1 (Jaccard) as a parameter-free default, while keeping the Tversky framing for future asymmetric extensions. The neuron's monosemanticity score is s_k = E[TI_{α,β}], with s_k=0 if A_k=∅; the model-level TMS aggregates non-zero s_k. TMS is computed on sparse binary vectors and uses up to M=1000 sampled pairs per neuron; a stability study on a BatchTopK DINOv3 SAE (K=32, ×4) shows the estimate is unchanged to three decimals across M∈{50,…,5000} with std ≈ 10⁻⁵ at M=1000.

**Models and SAE training.** Base models (used both as feature extractors for SAE inputs and as encoders for the reference MS) are DINOv3 (ConvNeXt-tiny), CLIP (ViT-B/32), and BLIP2 (blip2-opt-2.7b). Embeddings: DINOv3 global-average-pooled vector, CLIP/BLIP2 CLS tokens. SAEs use the `overcomplete` library, TopK and BatchTopK regimes, K∈{10,32,64}, expansion ∈{×2,×4,×8}, 25 epochs, MSE loss, AdamW lr=3e-4, batch 512, reanimation loss weight 1e-2. Benchmark: Mini-ImageNet (natural categories for qualitative inspection). Hardware: i9-12900 + RTX 3090 + 196 GB DDR5.

**Evaluation pipeline.** (1) Anisotropy analysis of base models as encoders (motivates label/encoder-free metric). (2) TMS computation alongside the reference MS of Pach et al. (each base model used as its own MS encoder). (3) Per-neuron Spearman correlation of TMS with MS, and of TMS with within-active-set embedding variance (a structure-only sanity proxy). (4) Training-dynamics trajectories of TMS and MS across 25 epochs. (5) Concept-deletion: per SAE, train a scikit-learn linear probe (StandardScaler + saga logistic regression, C=1.0, max_iter=25) on Mini-ImageNet, rank features by probe weight magnitude, zero top-N, and measure target-class accuracy drop; correlate SAE-level mean TMS/MS with target drop across SAEs for N∈{1,2,4,8,16,32}. Appendices report SAE reconstruction health (R², L0, input-output cosine, dead-neuron ratio) and pre-deletion probe accuracies to confirm the SAEs/probes are functional.

## Results & Highlights

- **Anisotropy differs sharply across encoders and biases MS:** CLIP has σcosine=0.52 and PC1 EVR=0.07 (most anisotropic), BLIP2 0.33 / 0.05, DINOv3 only 0.07 / 0.02. CLIP-based MS is consistently the highest across all TOPK K=10 configurations and DINOv3 the lowest — exactly mirroring the anisotropy ranking, demonstrating that MS is inflated by encoder geometry rather than reflecting genuinely higher feature coherence.
- **TMS stays aligned with MS but is far less geometry-driven:** under TMS, the multimodal models share the top score (CLIP in 13/18 configurations, BLIP2 in 5/18), shrinking the inter-model gap that MS exaggerates. Per-neuron Spearman TMS–MS correlation exceeds 0.75 in 16 of 18 DINOv3 SAEs (the most isotropic encoder), reaching up to 0.93 (BatchTopK DINOv3, ×2, K=32).
- **TMS correlates strongly and negatively with within-active-set variance** — as expected for monosemantic features — with BLIP2 correlations approaching −0.9 across nearly all configurations (e.g., −0.94 for TopK BLIP2 ×4 K=32/64, −0.93 for ×8 K=32) and many values exceeding −0.9.
- **Concept-deletion: TMS predicts target drop better under anisotropy.** On CLIP SAEs, SAE-level TMS correlation with target drop beats MS at every N up to 8 — e.g., N=1: TMS 0.783 (p=1.2e-4) vs MS 0.721; N=4: TMS 0.864 vs MS 0.653; N=8: TMS 0.678 vs MS 0.534 — and degrades gracefully to non-significance only at N=16/32 (consistent with low CLIP probe accuracies ~60–70%). On the isotropic DINOv3, MS is slightly stronger (e.g., N=2: MS 0.915 vs TMS 0.878; N=32: MS 0.917 vs TMS 0.868), so TMS is "competitive otherwise."
- **Distinct SAE training dynamics:** TMS reveals that DINOv3 TopK SAEs show an initial monosemanticity bump then a drop and stabilization, whereas BLIP2 shows a monotonic TMS decline without the bump; TopK K=10 reaches substantially higher TMS than larger K, while BatchTopK configurations converge to lower, closer values. MS shows more irregular epoch-to-epoch fluctuations (e.g., CLIP TopK MS rises quickly then oscillates).
- **Computational saving:** operating only on SAE latents avoids up to ~30 minutes of external BLIP2 embedding generation per test-set pass on the authors' hardware, on top of being intrinsically cheaper (sparse/binary operations vs dense floating-point).
- **SAE/probe sanity (Appendix A/B):** all TopK SAEs achieve input-output cosine similarity > 0.8 (best > 0.9) and most R²>0.7; BLIP probes all > 0.9 baseline accuracy (up to 96.49%), DINO 18/19 configurations > 0.9 (up to 93.08%), confirming the setup is functional.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Genuine and focused. The combination of (a) dispensing with both labels and external encoders, (b) operating purely on binary SAE latents via Jaccard, and (c) grounding the formulation in Tversky's cognitive-science similarity model is a clean, well-motivated contribution relative to MS (Pach et al., needs an encoder), FMS (Härle et al., needs concept labels), and JSDS (Fereidouni et al., needs labels). The honest framing — α=β=1 making TMS effectively Jaccard — keeps overclaiming in check, though the "Tversky" branding slightly oversells a metric that in this paper reduces to plain Jaccard.
- **Rigor:** The unified protocol (single benchmark Mini-ImageNet, identical SAE training recipe across all base models and regimes, appendices verifying SAE reconstruction health and probe accuracy before any deletion claim) is commendable and a real strength. The anisotropy analysis (σcosine + PC1 EVR) provides a concrete, quantified mechanism for *why* MS is biased — exactly the kind of paper-specific evidence reviewers want.
- **Reproducibility:** Strong on paper (code on Zenodo, full SAE/probe metric tables, exact hyperparameters, hardware specified, M=1000 stability study with std≈10⁻⁵). However, the Zenodo link is private for review at submission, so external reproducibility is unverified at the time of writing.
- **Significance:** Practical incremental significance for the SAE/mech-interp evaluation toolbox rather than a conceptual breakthrough. The main utility is removing a hidden dependency (encoder geometry) that visibly distorts cross-model comparisons; the concept-deletion link makes the metric functionally, not just structurally, validated.
- **Red flags / weaknesses to weigh:**
  - **Single benchmark.** All quantitative claims hinge on Mini-ImageNet; no ImageNet, COCO, ADE20K, or text-only LM activation data, so generalization is unproven despite the metric being modality-agnostic in principle.
  - **Validation is largely MS-anchored.** TMS is "good" insofar as it correlates with MS (and with within-set variance, itself computed with the same base-model encoder). The paper acknowledges this circularity only partially; a label-based ground truth (FMS/JSDS with annotated concepts) would have closed the loop but is absent.
  - **Probe accuracies on CLIP are weak** (e.g., K=10 TopK CLIP SAE probe ≈ 68%, K=10 ×8 CLIP ≈ 72%) and CLIP baseline probe ≈ 89.83%. The concept-deletion advantage of TMS on CLIP is therefore confounded by poorer downstream probe quality; the paper mentions this but does not run a probe-quality-matched comparison.
  - **MS is treated as the only embedding-based reference**, and the encoder used for MS is always the same as the base model — a setup that maximizes the anisotropy coupling. The paper does not test MS with a held-out isotropic encoder (e.g., MS-DINOv3 applied to CLIP SAEs), which would have isolated whether the problem is "MS uses *any* encoder" vs "MS uses *this* encoder."
  - **Dead-neutron and dead-feature ratios** (Appendix A) for some BLIP ×8 configurations reach 12.1% (BatchTopK, ×8, K=64) and dead-feature ratios up to ~0.12 — these specific SAEs are weaker, yet still included in the correlations; a sensitivity analysis excluding them is missing.
  - **Workshop paper length** (XKDD 2026): depth is appropriate for a workshop but several directions (asymmetric Tversky, advanced binarization, broader tasks) are deferred to future work.

## Strengths

- Clean, well-motivated, label- and encoder-free formulation with a cognitive-science grounding.
- Unified cross-modal SAE evaluation (DINOv3, CLIP, BLIP2) under identical protocol — rare and valuable.
- Explicit quantification of the anisotropy bias that distorts embedding-based monosemanticity scores, with a concrete σcosine/EVR mechanism.
- Multiple complementary validations: metric–metric correlation (TMS–MS), structure correlation (TMS–variance), training dynamics, and a functional concept-deletion experiment.
- Honest reporting of SAE health and probe accuracy in appendices; transparent about workshop-paper scope and LLM use.

## Limitations

- Single benchmark (Mini-ImageNet); no text-only LM SAEs and no large-scale vision benchmarks.
- Validation is anchored to MS, which itself depends on the very encoder geometry TMS is designed to avoid — a partly circular justification.
- Weaker CLIP probe accuracies confound the concept-deletion comparison; no probe-quality-controlled ablation.
- MS is always computed with the matching base-model encoder; no held-out/isotropic-encoder MS baseline to fully isolate the source of distortion.
- Some BLIP ×8 SAEs have elevated dead-feature ratios (up to 12.1%) yet remain in the pooled analyses without a sensitivity check.
- Asymmetric Tversky, advanced binarization schemes, and a broader task suite are left as future work; in this paper the metric reduces to Jaccard.

## Relevance

对于关注稀疏自编码器 (SAE)、机制可解释性与单语义性评估的研究者，本文直接提供一个无需标签、无需外部编码器的轻量度量 TMS，可作为替代或补充现有 MS/FMS/JSDS 的工具，并显式揭示了编码器各向异性对 SAE 评测的隐性偏差，对跨模型 SAE 比较和功能性干预实验设计具有方法学参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does TMS behave on non-vision settings — e.g., SAEs trained on LLM residual-stream activations on text benchmarks, where binarization thresholds and active-set sizes may differ sharply?
- Does TMS retain its advantages when MS is computed with a held-out, deliberately isotropic encoder (e.g., MS-DINOv3 on CLIP SAEs), or does the gap collapse?
- What happens to TMS on larger and more diverse benchmarks (full ImageNet, COCO, ADE20K, multi-task) where concept co-occurrence statistics differ from Mini-ImageNet?
- Can the asymmetric Tversky variant (α≠β) systematically improve alignment with human-judged monosemanticity or with FMS/JSDS label-based ground truth?
- Are the elevated dead-feature ratios in some BLIP ×8 SAEs (up to 12.1%) materially affecting the reported TMS-target-drop correlations, and does excluding them change the ranking of TMS vs MS?
- Does TMS predict other functional interventions beyond linear-probe concept deletion — e.g., activation patching, steering vectors, or causal abstraction metrics?
