> **一句话总结：** 作者把 2026 年世界杯全部 104 场比赛改造成 LLM 智能体的污染零预测基准，发现四个前沿模型在「预测准确性」上几乎无法区分（92% 比赛做出同一 top pick，且都打不过庄家的 Brier 分数 0.469），但在「下注决策 ROI（−18% 到 +10%）、对庄家价位的依赖程度（12%–100%）、反思错误时的诚实度（36%–86%）」这些行为维度上差异极大——这些正是单一准确率指标会掩盖的真实分水岭。

> **TL;DR:** WC2026-Agents turns all 104 matches of the 2026 FIFA World Cup into a contamination-free agentic-forecasting benchmark: four frontier LLMs (Claude Opus 4.8, ChatGPT/GPT-5.5 high reasoning, Gemini 3.1 Pro, Grok Expert Mode) each ran a fixed search–act–reflect loop and committed a calibrated 1X2 distribution plus a virtual $100 bet, with the pre-match betting market serving as a fifth, economically grounded competitor. On the 104 matches the four agents converge to the same top pick 92% of the time, none beats the market's Brier score (market 0.469 vs. best agent Grok at 0.471), and a naive flat-$100 stake on the market favourite out-earns every agent (+$1,041 vs. Grok's +$650). Yet the agents diverge sharply as decision-makers — betting ROI spans −18% (Claude) to +10% (Grok), market-citation rates range from 12% (Gemini) to 100% (Claude), and self-reported "incorrect" rates on their own wrong picks range from 36% (ChatGPT) to 86% (Gemini) — so the benchmark surfaces calibration, staking discipline, and reflection honesty as the axes on which frontier models actually differ.

## 核心贡献

- 构造了一个**结构上无数据污染**的智能体预测基准：因为 104 场 2026 世界杯比赛全部发生在各模型训练截止之后，目标答案在查询时刻物理上不存在于训练集，永久解决了静态基准被泄漏污染的老问题。
- 把**博彩市场作为第五个参赛者**纳入评估：释放每场比赛去水后的 1X2 隐含概率（平均 overround 1.05）和结算机制，让任何预测器既能被评分（校准），也能被以真金白银的价位清算（决策质量）。
- 设计了**三个正交能力轴**的评估套件——校准预测 (T1)、下注决策质量 (T2)、行为透明度 (T3)，并用 13 类关键词词典对推理与反思文本进行编码，揭示准确率之外的真实差异。
- 发布了**可复现、免申请的开放数据集**：416 条预测 + 414 条反思，含逐字推理、引用 URL、点球与晋级结果；knockout 阶段 128 个单元格四路交叉校验零分歧；一行命令 (`python src/run_all.py`) 可重建所有表格与图。
- 提炼出**未来系统的可挖矿先验特征**：在错预测中，最常被「漏掉」的因素是弱队防守组织 (62%) 和淘汰赛/点球方差 (32%)；最常被「过度加权」的是热门球队阵容纸面实力 (40%) 和攻击火力 (38%)。

## Key Contributions

- A **contamination-free agentic-forecasting benchmark** over all 104 matches of a future World Cup, since every kickoff post-dates every model's training cutoff — the target answers cannot have leaked into training data.
- A **betting market as a fifth competitor**: per-match 1X2 odds (mean overround 1.05) are released vig-removed as implied probabilities and serve both as an independent baseline and as the real settlement price for any agent's bets, enabling an economically meaningful decision score that prediction-only benchmarks lack.
- A **three-axis evaluation suite** — T1 calibrated prediction (accuracy, Brier, log-loss, ECE, reliability curves), T2 decision quality (bankroll, ROI, hit-rate, contrarian split), T3 behavioural transparency (13-category reasoning lexicon, no model in the loop) — that jointly score calibration, decision-making, and self-knowledge.
- A **curated, documented, four-way cross-checked dataset**: 416 forecasts and 414 reflections with verbatim reasoning and cited URLs, hand-resolved knockout fixtures/scores (penalty shootouts, who advanced), and zero disagreements across 128 knockout cells in a four-agent score cross-check.
- A **distillation of shared blind spots** into candidate features: on wrong picks the most-cited missed factor is underdog defensive organisation (62%) and knockout/penalty variance (32%); the most-cited over-weighted factor is the favourite's squad quality (40%) and attacking firepower (38%).

## Methodology

The benchmark instantiates a single, fixed **search–act–reflect loop** per agent that any frontier assistant can be dropped into.

- **Protocol.** Each match generates two turns in one per-model conversation. The pre-match turn (T−24h) places the agent in a research-study frame ("virtual money; do not refuse"), names the fixture, forces a web tool, and requires a strict JSON reply: `{"probabilities": {team_a_win, draw, team_b_win}, "bet": {pick, stake_usd}, "reasoning": ..., "key_sources": [urls]}` with stake ∈ [0, 100]. The post-match turn gives only the final score (no web) and asks for a structured reflection: an outcome_vs_prediction label (correct/partially_correct/incorrect), a calibration self-judgement, a luck attribution, a key_factor_i_missed, a key_factor_i_overweighted, a counterfactual, a would-bet-differently verdict, and a confidence. Prompts are byte-identical across agents; only the model changes.
- **Models.** Four consumer-tier assistants with web tools enabled, held fixed across the tournament: Claude Opus 4.8, ChatGPT (GPT-5.5 "Thinking", high reasoning effort), Gemini 3.1 Pro, Grok Expert Mode. Using deployed assistants (rather than raw base APIs) is deliberate — it captures the retrieval-and-reason agent a user actually deploys.
- **Data collection (11 June – 19 July 2026).** Every agent ran on all 104 fixtures (m01–m72 group, m73–m104 knockout); raw transcripts are archived read-only and the pre-match window strictly precedes each outcome.
- **Curation.** Group-stage transcripts parse via a string-aware JSON extractor; knockout transcripts (four different header renderings, "W74 vs W77" placeholders that don't match reality, a dozen score phrasings, reversed team order) were hand-resolved into a verified table. An automatic guard cross-checks the human-pasted score line in each of the four agent transcripts against that table — zero disagreements across 128 knockout cells. Ground truth records the 90-minute result, shootout result, and which side advanced. Deviations from the JSON schema are logged, not dropped.
- **Market odds.** Pre-match 1X2 odds for all 104 matches with a source URL each, oriented to team A and vig-removed to implied probabilities; knockout odds are opening lines from public sportsbook previews, validated against the implied probabilities the agents themselves cited (e.g. de-vigged Brazil–Japan 55/25/20 matches a cited "56/25/19") and one economically impossible line was corrected.
- **Metrics.** T1 uses accuracy (argmax = outcome), multi-class Brier, log-loss, top-pick reliability curves and ECE over 10 confidence bins, and a convergence measure (fraction of matches where all agents share an argmax). T2 settles each agent's pick/stake at decimal odds on the 90-minute result (profit = stake×(dec−1) on win, −stake otherwise) and reports bankroll, ROI, hit-rate, and a contrarian split (bet is contrarian if its pick differs from the market's most-likely outcome). T3 codes every reasoning and reflection field into 13 factor categories via a published keyword lexicon (no model in the loop), giving each agent's information diet and self-knowledge rate.

## Results & Highlights

- **Convergence, not disagreement.** The four agents issue an identical top pick in **92% of matches (91.7% group, 93.8% knockout)**; pairwise same-pick rate reaches 100% (ChatGPT–Grok) and never falls below 92%. Accuracy therefore spans only three points across 104 matches.
- **T1 calibrated prediction — no agent beats the market.** On all 104 matches: Claude Acc .664 / Brier .4705 / LogLoss .806 / ECE .116; ChatGPT .683 / .4729 / .814 / .111; Gemini .654 / .4828 / .820 / .068; Grok .683 / .4706 / .803 / .097; **Market .683 / .4688 (best Brier)** — i.e. market beats the best agent (Grok) by 0.002 Brier and ties the best accuracy.
- **T2 betting/decision quality — wide spread despite identical point predictions.** Claude: 73 bets, $1,519 staked, **−$275 (ROI −18.1%)**, hit .329, 58% contrarian; ChatGPT: 55 bets, $1,471 staked, +$118 (+8.0%), hit .509, 36% contrarian; Gemini: 103 bets, $8,660 staked, +$322 (+3.7%), hit .631, 14% contrarian; **Grok: 104 bets, $6,305 staked, +$650 (+10.3%)**, hit .673, 5% contrarian. A naive flat-$100 stake on the market favourite returns **+$1,041**, out-earning all four agents in absolute terms.
- **T1 calibration separates them where accuracy does not.** Gemini is best calibrated (ECE 0.068), then Grok (0.097), ChatGPT (0.111), Claude (0.116); all four are systematically under-confident on their top pick (points lie above the reliability diagonal). Notably Gemini has the best top-pick ECE but the worst Brier, because it spreads probability mass more aggressively.
- **Fading the market is unprofitable for every agent.** Contrarian (against-market) bets hit only 21–40% and return −24% to +33% (the lone positive being Grok's five such bets), while market-conforming bets are the sole source of profit; the ordering is monotone — the more an agent fades the market, the worse it does.
- **Staking discipline is a distinct axis.** Stake-vs-confidence Pearson r: Grok 0.85, Gemini 0.54, ChatGPT 0.53, Claude −0.20 (Claude sizes up on low-confidence, against-market "value" plays that tend to lose). Gemini's mean stake of $84 on a $100 budget inflates bankroll variance.
- **Information diets differ while picks converge.** Share of forecasts citing the market/odds: Claude 100%, ChatGPT 92%, Gemini 12%, Grok 5% — yet Gemini's team-A probability still tracks the market at r=0.97. Grok most invokes "paper strength" (squad depth 88%, form 76%, FIFA ranking 61%); Claude most invokes knockout variance (53%).
- **Reflection honesty is a per-agent fingerprint, orthogonal to accuracy.** On each agent's own wrong picks, "incorrect" admission rate: Gemini 86%, Grok 61%, Claude 49%, **ChatGPT only 36%** (it softens 55% of its errors to "partially correct"). On correct picks, "correct" claim rate ranges 68% (Claude) to 94% (Grok).
- **Shared blind spots.** Eight matches were mispredicted by all four agents; every one of them the agents backed the favourite (e.g. Germany 1–1 Paraguay at 75% consensus, Brazil 1–2 Norway at 53%, France 4–6 England at 48%). The market also identifies the advancing side in 24 of 32 knockout ties (75%), equal to the agents.
- **Difficulty profile of the cup.** Binning by the market's implied probability for its favoured side: that side won 62% of the time when priced near 48% and 78% when priced near 62%, while heavy favourites (~81%) were well-calibrated — a mildly favourite-friendly cup that makes every market-respecting forecaster look under-confident in hindsight.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (genuine).** Using a future scheduled sporting tournament as a structurally contamination-free agentic benchmark is a clean and timely idea; pairing the agents with a vig-removed bookmaker as a *fifth, economically priced competitor* is the sharpest piece of novelty, because it turns "does the model beat the market?" into a sharp, falsifiable null. The decomposition into calibration / decision-quality / self-knowledge axes is well motivated and the results vindicate it.
- **Rigor (strong on curation, weaker on statistical reporting).** The curation story is unusually careful (hand-resolved knockout fixtures, four-way score cross-check, vig removal, source URLs, one corrected economically impossible line). However, the authors themselves note that per-agent differences have "wide intervals" — yet confidence intervals, bootstrap tests, or paired comparisons across the 104 matches are essentially absent, so statements like "Grok is the most profitable" or "ChatGPT is the least honest" come without uncertainty bars on N=104 correlated events.
- **Baseline honesty (excellent — and damning).** This is the paper's most creditable trait: they explicitly show that a naive flat stake on the market favourite (+$1,041) beats every single LLM, and they frame "no agent beats a vig-bearing market" as the expected efficient-market null rather than hiding it. Many forecasting papers would have omitted this.
- **Missing ablations.** There is no ablation over the prompt (one fixed prompt is used), no ablation over retrieval on/off, no comparison of consumer-assistant vs. base-API behaviour to justify the "deliberate" choice, and no control for the fact that providers may have updated models mid-window — the authors disclose the latter but do not measure it. The reflection lexicon is purely lexical (no model in the loop), which is reproducible but almost certainly mis-codes free-text reflections on the edge cases that matter (e.g. ChatGPT's "partially correct" softening).
- **Reproducibility (very high for the artefact, limited for the agent runs).** Data, odds, lexicon, and the regeneration pipeline are all public and auditable. Reproducing the agent runs themselves is harder: consumer assistants are moving targets and the exact retrieval/decoding behaviour is not controlled — so the headline numbers are point-in-time snapshots of June–July 2026 model versions, which limits longitudinal comparison.
- **Significance.** The convergence result (92% identical top pick across four independently built frontier models) is an important methodological warning for the benchmark community: shared retrieval surface → shared errors, so "wisdom of the silicon crowd" inherits the common bias rather than cancelling it. This is the most quotable finding for non-sports audiences.
- **Red flags.** (1) Odds are opening lines from mostly one book, not closing consensus, which is the stronger and more standard efficiency benchmark — opening lines are known to be less efficient and could make the market baseline look weaker or stronger than it really is. (2) N=104 is small and tournament-specific; the cup was "mildly favourite-friendly," which the authors flag but which nonetheless means a single tournament's ROI ranking could easily flip on a different cup. (3) The "decision quality" axis is partly a function of staking policy, which is agent-determined and confounded with pick selection, so ranking agents by ROI conflates at least two distinct capabilities.

## Strengths

- Contamination-free *by construction* — the strongest possible answer to the data-leakage problem that plagues static LLM benchmarks.
- The market-as-competitor framing gives an economically meaningful, hard-to-beat baseline that surfaces the difference between "predicting well" and "acting well."
- Exceptionally honest reporting: they publish the result that no agent beats the market and that a flat-favourite heuristic beats every agent, instead of burying it.
- Three-axis evaluation (calibration / decision / self-knowledge) is genuinely more informative than single-answer accuracy, and the 92%-convergence finding is a crisp methodological contribution.
- High-quality, fully open dataset with hand-curated knockout ground truth, four-way cross-checks, and a one-command reproduction pipeline.

## Limitations

- Single tournament, N=104 matches; per-agent ROI and reflection-honesty rankings carry wide intervals and the cup was mildly favourite-friendly, so the ordinal rankings could easily shift on a different tournament.
- Consumer-assistant interfaces were used, so retrieval and decoding are not fully controlled and providers may update models mid-window; the numbers are point-in-time snapshots of mid-2026 model versions.
- Odds are opening lines from mostly one book, not closing consensus, which is the stronger market-efficiency baseline.
- The 13-category reasoning/reflection lexicon is purely lexical (no model in the loop), trading robustness for the risk of mis-coding free text — particularly ChatGPT's "partially correct" softening.
- One fixed prompt is used throughout; no prompt/retrieval ablations are reported, so it is unclear how much of the convergence is a product of the specific scaffolding vs. the models.
- ROI ranking conflates pick selection with staking policy, which are arguably two distinct capabilities.

## Relevance

对研究「LLM 智能体作为决策者」与「评估方法论」方向高度相关：它直接给出了一个污染零、有真实价位清算、且能拆分校准/决策/自省三轴的评估范式，可作为未来任何「检索增强 + 工具调用 + 反思」智能体基准的设计模板；同时也为「LLM 能否预测未来」给出了一个清醒的、市场对齐的对照基线。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How much of the 92% top-pick convergence is driven by the shared web-retrieval surface vs. the models themselves? An ablation with retrieval off, or with each agent restricted to a different source set, would isolate the contribution of the retrieval layer.
- Do the ROI and reflection-honesty rankings survive on a second, independent tournament (e.g. a club-league season or another World Cup), and what are the confidence intervals on N≈100 paired matches?
- Would closing-line consensus odds (rather than single-book opening lines) change the "no agent beats the market" verdict, and by how much?
- Can the lexical reflection lexicon be replaced with a model-in-the-loop coder that is itself auditable, to handle cases like ChatGPT's "partially correct" softening more faithfully?
- Does the shared-blind-spot pattern (over-weighting favourite paper talent, under-weighting underdog defensive organisation and knockout variance) generalise beyond football to other market-priced forecasting domains (elections, product launches)?
- Is the under-confidence on the top pick a stable property of these frontier assistants, or an artefact of a single favourite-friendly tournament?
