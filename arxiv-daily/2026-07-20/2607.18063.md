> **一句话总结：** 作者发布了一个 21 个场景的多轮、多 LLM 攻击者对抗无记忆 LLM 防御者的智能体安全基准，发现 15 轮自适应攻击相比单轮评分把 ASR 从 0–1% 抬到 5.4–14.0%，并且 Opus 4.6 与 GPT-5.4 在总 ASR 上打平（各 5.4%）却在 memleak / paperaccept 等场景上呈现对立的、模型特异性的弱点（Kendall's W = 0.19）。

> **TL;DR:** A 21-scenario multi-turn, multi-LLM-attacker agent-security benchmark (3×3 frontier matrix on Claude Opus 4.6, GPT-5.4, Gemini 2.5 Pro, N=5, 945 battles) shows that 15-round adaptive attack lifts ASR to 5.4–14.0% from 0–1% under round-1-only scoring, that pooling three attacker LLMs uncovers 1.4–2.2× more unique successful attacks than the best single attacker, and that Claude Opus 4.6 and GPT-5.4 tie in aggregate ASR (5.4% each) yet split sharply on per-scenario weaknesses (Opus uniquely falls on memleak at 60% vs 7%; Gemini uniquely falls on paperaccept at 53% vs 0%), with low cross-scenario concordance (Kendall's W = 0.19) versus b3 at 0.712.

## 核心贡献

- **填补"多轮 × 每轮重新生成 × 多 LLM 攻击者"的基准空白**：现有安全基准（StrongREJECT、JailbreakBench、HarmBench、b3、AgentHarm、AgentDojo、IPI、PAIR/TAP/Crescendo）要么是单轮、要么是预先固定攻击池、要么只用单一 LLM 攻击者；本文同时占据"多轮 + 每场战斗实时生成 + 多攻击者 LLM"这一未被覆盖的象限，并配套发布可运行协议（场景插件、orchestrator、基线 harness、多攻击者 CLI）。
- **四条可量化的设计原则**：(1) 多阶段场景校准（概念生成→插件实现→BAL/SVW/WVS 校准过滤），用强=GPT-5.2、弱=GPT-4o-mini 三种配置 30 次运行筛选场景；(2) 多轮 attacker-vs-defender 对话（15 轮）；(3) 攻击者在每场战斗中实时生成攻击，而非从静态池读取；(4) 基于 typed-field 的 `check_success`，对奖励黑客（substring proxy gaming）鲁棒。
- **攻击者 LLM 与场景集都被当作测量轴**：固定防御者时，合并 Opus / GPT-5.4 / Gemini 三名攻击者可发现比最佳单攻击者多 1.4–2.2× 的唯一成功攻击；21 个场景里 14 个 informative、13 个 discriminative，但场景间排名严重不一致（Kendall's W = 0.19），表明前沿模型弱点不对齐在单一安全轴上。
- **用 4 类 calibration 证据支撑基准有效性**：945 条 3×3 前沿矩阵全量 transcript、48 源攻击-244 次重放的 transfer 数据集、18,422 场公开竞赛 battle、以及 gpt-oss-20b 上的 1,050 场基线噪声地板。

## Key Contributions

- Fills the unfilled "multi-turn × per-battle regeneration × multi-LLM-attacker" quadrant left by existing safety benchmarks (StrongREJECT, JailbreakBench, HarmBench, b3, MHJ, PAIR/TAP/GCG/Crescendo, AgentHarm, AgentDojo, IPI). Ships a runnable protocol: 21 held-out + 10 public scenario plugins, orchestrator, baseline harnesses, and a multi-attacker evaluation CLI.
- Four quantitative design principles: (1) multi-stage scenario calibration with BAL/SVW/WVS configs using strong=GPT-5.2 and weak=GPT-4o-mini; (2) 15-round agent-vs-agent battles; (3) per-battle live attack regeneration instead of a static pool; (4) typed-field `check_success` that is robust to substring-proxy reward hacking.
- Treats both the attacker LLM and the scenario set as measurement axes. Pooling three frontier attackers finds 1.4–2.2× more unique successful attacks than the best single attacker; per-scenario rankings disagree (Kendall's W = 0.19), demonstrating that frontier-defender weaknesses do not align on a single axis.
- Releases a layered calibration corpus: 945 transcripts from the 3×3 frontier matrix, the 48-source/244-attempt attack-replay corpus, 18,422 final-scoring competition battles, and a 1,050-battle gpt-oss-20b baseline-vs-baseline noise floor.

## Methodology

The benchmark runs on the AgentBeats multi-agent evaluation platform. Each battle pairs one attacker harness with one defender harness (Python modules wrapping LLM calls) under an orchestrator configured with a per-scenario template and a structured-output defender contract.

- **Threat model — memoryless defender**: For each of 15 rounds the orchestrator passes scenario context plus prior defender responses to the attacker, inserts the attacker text into the scenario template, passes only the formatted current input (no metadata, no prior-round history) to the defender, parses its structured JSON, and runs `check_success`. The asymmetry is deliberate: the attacker plans across rounds and pivots after failures while the defender is evaluated as a fresh interaction each round, isolating per-input robustness under sustained adaptive pressure.
- **Scenario authoring with calibration**: Each candidate scenario goes through (i) LLM-seeded + human-curated concept generation across seven attack archetypes, (ii) Python plugin implementation (threat-snapshot format adapted from b3), and (iii) a calibration filter using three configs (BAL = same model both sides, SVW = strong attacker/weak defender, WVS = weak attacker/strong defender), 10 runs each. The rubric (Table 2) scores Difficulty (BAL 30–70%), Sensitivity (SVW−WVS > 30pp), and Round depth (mean rounds-to-win 5–12). Four hard flags reject a scenario: BAL_100, SVW_0, NO_DIFF, INVERTED.
- **Structured typed-field scoring**: Defenders emit JSON; `check_success` only inspects scenario-relevant typed fields, not free-form prose. Partial, echo-only, refusal-narrative, or topically-related responses are scored as failures (per-scenario criteria in Appendix A; borderline handling in B.13–B.15). This is critical because adaptive attackers were observed gaming substring proxies (tickettriage echoed-referral, smarthomejack decoy unlock).
- **Scenario set**: 21 held-out evaluation scenarios across five threat classes — indirect injection (11), PII/data leak (6), insecure output (2), supply chain (1), prompt extraction (1) — plus 10 public scenarios for iterative development (defender prompts withheld to preserve the live competition).
- **3×3 frontier matrix**: 21 scenarios × {Opus 4.6, GPT-5.4, Gemini 2.5 Pro} attackers × same three defenders, N=5 per cell = 945 battles with full transcripts.
- **Open competition**: AgentBeats Security Arena (March–April 2026) on a self-hosted vLLM gpt-oss-20b backbone, 21 attacker teams and 20 defender teams; 53 public-scenario rounds (~93,000 battles) for iteration, then 2 private-scoring rounds on the 21 held-out scenarios yielding 18,422 battles.
- **Cross-benchmark analysis**: Replicates StrongREJECT, JailbreakBench, MHJ, AgentHarm-val, IPI coding-agent, and b3 on the same three frontier defenders; computes Kendall's W on per-item defender rankings with bootstrap CIs.
- **Adaptivity ablation**: On three high-ASR scenarios (codeapprove, memleak, smarthomejack) across {Opus, GPT-5.4}×{Opus, GPT-5.4} cells, runs (C1) non-adaptive attacker + stateless defender, (C2) adaptive attacker + stateful defender, (C3) both flips, N=3 per condition (108 additional battles).

## Results & Highlights

- **Round-1 vs. full-15-round scoring on the 3×3 matrix (945 battles)**: Round-1-only ASR is 0.0% / 0.0% / 1.0% for Opus / GPT-5.4 / Gemini; full-15-round ASR is 5.4% / 5.4% / 14.0% — the same scenarios, defenders, attackers, and structured checks, only the interaction budget changes.
- **memleak inversion (Opus uniquely weak)**: Opus is compromised 60% (9/15) on memleak (95% CI 36–80%), versus 7% (1/15) for GPT-5.4 and 7% (1/15) for Gemini (CI 1–30%) — a 53pp gap that inverts the aggregate ranking. The pattern is preserved in an N=20/cell replication: Opus 50%, GPT-5.4 10%, Gemini 5%. Mechanism: Opus refuses the direct credential request but, after a regex-parser-debugging pretext, emits the canary password `K7$mF9!qZ2nV%xR3` percent-encoded inside a synthetic test case.
- **paperaccept and gameguru (Gemini uniquely weak, fabricated-authority)**: Gemini compromised on paperaccept 53% (8/15) and gameguru 60% (9/15) versus 0% for Opus and GPT-5.4 — Gemini honors injected "OFFICIAL AREA CHAIR NOTE" and "[FESTIVAL_GAME_MASTER_DIRECTIVE]" authority frames. At N=20, paperaccept stays Gemini-specific (43%, 26/60).
- **Multi-attacker pooling multiplier**: Pooling the three frontier attackers finds 1.4–2.2× more unique successful attacks than the best single attacker (per-defender, Figure 5); all 78 genuine winning turns are pairwise distinct (exact-match or MiniLM-L6-v2 cosine ≥ 0.95).
- **Low cross-scenario concordance (Kendall's W)**: 14/21 scenarios are informative (≥1 attacker win), 13/14 discriminative, yet rankings disagree — W = 0.19 (95% CI [0.02, 0.58]) — versus b3 at 0.712 [0.626, 0.793], JBB 0.210, MHJ 0.204, AgentHarm 0.328.
- **Attack novelty (cosine similarity)**: Mean MiniLM-L6-v2 cosine similarity between this benchmark's attacks and each of the six other benchmarks is 0.02–0.14, versus 0.21 within the paper's own attack pool.
- **Attack transfer is mostly model-specific**: Replaying 46 unique winning attacks against the two non-source defenders, 0 of 46 (0%) succeed against all three frontier defenders; aggregate transfer rate is 25/244 = 10.2%; transfer is asymmetric (GPT-5.4→Opus 25.7%, Opus→GPT-5.4 0%).
- **Adaptivity ablation (3 scenarios, 4 cells)**: Baseline (adaptive ATK, stateless DEF) = 30.0% ASR (18/60); removing attacker access to prior defender responses collapses ASR to 0.0% (0/36); making the defender stateful lowers it to 19.4% (7/36); both flips = 0%. This isolates response-conditioned attacker adaptation — not repeated independent sampling — as the primary driver of the multi-round gain.
- **Open competition strategy spread**: Submitted attackers (N=42 battles each) range 9.5–50.0% ASR (median 29.3%, std 11.1pp); submitted defenders range 9.5% (strongest) to 78.6% (weakest) ASR (median 25.0%, std 14.9pp); the strongest and weakest submissions on each side have non-overlapping 95% Wilson intervals, indicating real strategy discovery beyond Baseline sampling noise.
- **Success-criterion robustness**: Per-defender ASR under naive substring presence is 8.3% / 11.1% / 19.0%; under the adopted typed-field/cross-party criterion 5.4% / 5.4% / 14.0%; under an explicit-endorsement requirement 4.8% / 5.1% / 12.4% — qualitative conclusions hold across all three.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: Genuinely occupies an unfilled cell in the turn-structure × attack-persistence × attacker-source design space (Table 1). The four-principle design (per-battle regeneration, multi-LLM attacker axis, calibration rubric, typed-field scoring) is well-motivated and goes beyond a scenario-pack release.
- **Rigor**: Calibration methodology (BAL/SVW/WVS with strong/weak pair, explicit rubric with four reject flags) is a real strength, as is the bootstrap-CI Kendall's W analysis across six benchmarks and the higher-N replication of three headline scenarios at N=20/cell. The adaptivity ablation (Table 13–15) is small (N=3 per cell-scenario) but the qualitative direction is consistent and the authors flag the limitation honestly.
- **Reproducibility**: Mixed. The protocol, orchestrator, baseline harnesses, and 945 transcripts are released with a Croissant manifest and a smoke-test CLI; the open-weight gpt-oss-20b tier is bit-for-bit reproducible. But closed-model numbers (Opus/GPT-5.4/Gemini) are point-in-time, OpenRouter is used for Gemini (the authors admit they do "not independently verify the routed snapshot"), and the 10 public-scenario defender prompts are withheld, so external users cannot fully re-derive the public-vs-private leaderboard analysis.
- **Significance**: The empirical claim — that Opus and GPT-5.4 tie in aggregate ASR yet split on per-scenario failure modes (memleak 60% vs 7%; paperaccept 0% vs 53%) — is a substantive finding that single-attacker, single-task evaluation misses. The reward-hacking discussion (B.15: tickettriage, smarthomejack) is a useful methodological contribution that explains why naive substring scoring over-counts.
- **Red flags / weaknesses**:
  - The main 3×3 matrix uses N=5 per cell; per-cell binomial SE is ≈13pp at p=0.5, so only three scenarios (memleak, gameguru, paperaccept) are statistically separated, and the authors themselves demote smarthomejack from a GPT-5.4-specific claim to "diagnostic" once N=20 replication shows GPT-5.4 at 7% (within noise). Readers must take care not to over-interpret the other 16 scenarios.
  - The scenario set is heavily skewed: 11/21 indirect injection and 6/21 PII/data leak, with only 2 insecure-output, 1 supply-chain, and 1 prompt-extraction scenario. Coverage claims are accordingly narrow.
  - The 18,422-battle open competition uses gpt-oss-20b exclusively (not a frontier model), so the strategy-space evidence (§5.4) cannot be directly compared to the 3×3 frontier matrix ASR; the authors say so but the framing sometimes blurs it.
  - The Kendall's W = 0.19 result has a wide 95% CI [0.02, 0.58] that overlaps MHJ (0.204) and JBB (0.210); the paper claims only "low-concordance end of a spectrum," but the headline phrasing ("frontier-defender weaknesses do not align on a single axis") is stronger than the CI supports.
  - The memleak mechanism — Opus emitting the canary percent-encoded inside a "synthetic test case" — is striking but rests on a single scenario; the cross-tier check shows the Opus failure drops from 60% on frontier to 6% on gpt-oss-20b, which the authors interpret as a frontier-specific helpfulness override rather than a generic scenario bug, but the sample is one scenario.

## Strengths

- Clean identification of an unfilled benchmarking quadrant (multi-turn × regenerated × multi-LLM) with concrete filling, not just identification.
- Honest, layered calibration evidence (3×3 frontier matrix + N=20 replications + 18,422-battle competition + 1,050-battle noise floor + 48-source replay corpus).
- The reward-hacking analysis (B.13–B.15) is unusually candid: the authors show their own first-pass substring scoring was gameable, document the gaming strategies (echoed referral codes, decoy unlock actions), and re-score everything under the tighter criterion.
- The memleak / paperaccept / gameguru inversions are illustrated with verbatim transcripts (Appendix C), making the failure mechanisms falsifiable rather than asserted.
- Cross-benchmark table (Table 5/6) is a useful public-good comparison; the b3 drop-one analysis (Table 9: removing mcp_chat_poisoning shrinks the Opus-vs-GPT-5.4 gap from 1.7pp to 0.3pp) is a sharp contrast with this benchmark's broad-but-disagreeing signal.
- Strong release: MIT code, CC-BY-4.0 data, Croissant 1.0 manifest, smoke test, multi-attacker CLI.

## Limitations

- **Sample size**: N=5 per cell on the main 3×3 matrix; per-cell binomial SE ≈ 13pp; only three scenarios are statistically separated. Higher-N (N=20) replications cover only the three headline scenarios.
- **Attacker capability ceiling**: Attacks are natural-language LLM agents, bounded both by attacker-model capability and by their safety training. The benchmark complements but does not replace human red-teaming, which can pursue strategies LLM attackers are trained to refuse.
- **Scenario coverage skew**: 11/21 indirect injection and 6/21 PII/data leak; only 1 supply-chain and 1 prompt-extraction scenario. Scenarios were curated for difficulty, sensitivity, and round depth, so the high informative-scenario rate (67%) is partly by construction and not an estimate from a random task sample.
- **Memoryless defender threat model**: Isolates per-input robustness; does not model a stateful production agent. The stateful-defender ablation lowers pooled ASR (19.4% vs 30.0%), but accumulated history can reinforce (not just help refuse) a manipulation — the effect is scenario-dependent.
- **Closed-model reproducibility**: Closed API endpoints drift over time; live attack generation is not bit-for-bit reproducible; Gemini is routed through OpenRouter without independent snapshot verification; the 10 public-scenario defender prompts are withheld.
- **Cross-benchmark ASR comparisons are not leaderboard rankings**: Judges, content, turn structures, and threat surfaces differ across the six benchmarks; per-battle cost differs (a b3 battle is one defender call; this benchmark's battle is up to 15 attacker + 15 defender calls).
- **Competition evidence is gpt-oss-20b only**: The 18,422-battle strategy-space analysis does not directly extend to frontier defenders.

## Relevance

对于关注 LLM 智能体安全、prompt injection 与多轮 jailbreak 的研究者，本文提供了一个可直接运行的 21 场景多攻击者评估协议、三类前沿模型（Opus 4.6 / GPT-5.4 / Gemini 2.5 Pro）的对话级失败案例与可复现 transcript，是评估 agent 鲁棒性与红队测试的重要新基准；尤其适合需要识别"单轮评估会漏掉的多轮自适应漏洞"和"模型特异性弱点"的工作。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Would extending the 3×3 matrix to N=20 across all 21 scenarios (not just memleak/paperaccept/smarthomejack) surface additional statistically separated per-defender inversions, or do only three frontier models admit only a handful of separable failure modes at this scale?
- How does the memoryless-defender threat model interact with real production agents that maintain long conversation history — does accumulated history reliably help defenders refuse, or does it sometimes reinforce the manipulation (as the Opus→Opus memleak cell suggests, where statefulness raised ASR from 80% to 100%)?
- Can a single frontier model ever be "uniformly safest" across scenario classes, or is the Kendall's W = 0.19 finding a structural property of calibrated, capability-sensitive scenario sets rather than an artifact of these three defenders?
- The reward-hacking patterns observed (tickettriage echoed-referral, smarthomejack decoy unlock) suggest that adaptive attackers will game any proxy metric — how would an automated, attack-aware success-criterion generator detect gaming without per-scenario manual auditing?
- Does the fabricated-authority failure mechanism (paperaccept, gameguru, smarthomejack) generalize beyond these three scenarios to a broader class of inline-policy / inline-directive injections, and if so, what minimal mitigation (separating trusted vs. untrusted content, explicit-confirmation gating) closes it without breaking legitimate inline instructions?
- The competition leaderboard shows that adaptive-selection harnesses rise and fixed-catalog harnesses drop on held-out scenarios (Table 12) — does this predict that defender harnesses built on fixed refusal patterns will similarly degrade, and can we quantify a "generalization gap" for defense as cleanly as for attack?
