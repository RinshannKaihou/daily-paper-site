> **一句话总结：** 作者系统刻画了 rubric-based RL 中两种被忽略的失败模式（Unexplored Criteria 与 Suppressed Criteria），并提出 CriPO——以 GRPO 为稳定主干、用 on-policy self-distillation 对未探索准则做行为注入、对被抑制准则做反事实 token 定位并翻转优势的混合训练框架，在 Qwen3-1.7B/4B 的医学与科学基准上平均比 GRPO 高 +3.2/+1.4，并以约 2× 更少的步数达到 GRPO 收敛性能。

> **TL;DR:** CriPO identifies two prevalent failure modes in rubric-based GRPO (Unexplored Criteria satisfied by no rollout, and Suppressed Criteria whose learning signal is lost under scalar reward aggregation) and fixes them with on-policy self-distillation while keeping GRPO as the reward-grounded backbone. On RaR-Medicine / RaR-Science training and HealthBench / LLMEval-Med / ResearchQA cross-domain evaluation with Qwen3-1.7B and Qwen3-4B, CriPO lifts the average score from 59.2→62.4 (+3.2) on Qwen3-1.7B and 68.2→69.6 (+1.4) on Qwen3-4B over GRPO, beats the HeRL and OPSD baselines, and reaches GRPO's converged accuracy at ~step 175 (≈2× fewer steps) on RaR-Medicine.

## 核心贡献

- **首次系统刻画并量化两种 rubric-based RL 失败模式**：以 Qwen3-4B 在 RaR-Medicine 上的训练统计为依据，揭示超过 83% 的样本含 Unexplored Criteria（平均 2.5 个/样本），超过 57% 含 Suppressed Criteria（平均 1.8 个/样本），且二者在训练全程持续存在。
- **提出 CriPO 框架（Criterion-Distilled Policy Optimization）**：以 GRPO 作为稳定、奖励对齐的优化主干，用 OPSD 仅作为有针对性的辅助模块，避免 standalone OPSD 的熵塌缩与训练不稳定（直接用 OPSD 反而使平均分掉 -18.9）。
- **针对 Unexplored Criteria 的"行为注入"机制**：构造 criterion-injection self-teacher，仅对最高优势 rollout 计算局部 forward-KL，并用"贡献度引导的 token 过滤"（默认 γ=0.95，即最 informative 的约 34.6% token 贡献了 95% KL）剔除噪声监督。
- **针对 Suppressed Criteria 的"优势翻转"机制**：构造 counterfactual self-teacher 通过"删/改满足该准则的片段"反事实生成对照版本，定位真正承载该准则的 token，把其负优势翻转为 τ_flip=0.1，从而保留被标量奖励淹没的有用行为。
- **跨规模、跨领域的稳健增益与效率优势**：在 Qwen3-1.7B/4B、医学/科学两个领域上稳定优于 GRPO、HeRL、OPSD，并用 Qwen3-32B 作评估 judge 复现同一趋势，证明结论不依赖于特定判官模型。

## Key Contributions

- Systematically defines and quantifies two distinct failure modes of rubric-based GRPO: Unexplored Criteria (UC, satisfied by no rollout in the group) and Suppressed Criteria (SC, satisfied by some rollouts but receiving non-positive aggregate advantage due to scalar reward aggregation). Empirical analysis on Qwen3-4B / RaR-Medicine shows >83% of samples contain UC (avg 2.5/sample) and >57% contain SC (avg 1.8/sample) throughout training.
- CriPO, a hybrid framework that retains GRPO as the stable reward-grounded backbone and uses On-Policy Self-Distillation (OPSD) only as a targeted auxiliary correction module — explicitly motivated by the finding that standalone OPSD collapses (e.g., -18.9 average on Qwen3-1.7B) due to entropy collapse and noisy dense supervision.
- A *Behavior Injection* module for UC: a criterion-injection self-teacher revises the best rollout, with (a) Best-Rollout Selection (only the highest-advantage rollout) and (b) Contribution-Guided Token Filtering (top-cumulative-KL with γ=0.95), computing forward-KL only at the most informative positions (~34.6% of tokens carry 95% of KL).
- An *Advantage Flipping* module for SC: a counterfactual self-teacher is asked to weaken the satisfied criteria; tokens where the student up-weights the original token (Δ>0) and the teacher confidently prefers an alternative (p_T(y)<α·max, α=0.1) are localized and their advantages flipped to τ_flip=0.1, preserving useful but otherwise-suppressed behaviors within the GRPO objective.
- Strong, robust gains across model scales (Qwen3-1.7B/4B), training domains (RaR-Medicine/RaR-Science), cross-domain benchmarks (HealthBench, LLMEval-Med, ResearchQA), and two different judge models (GPT-4o-mini, Qwen3-32B), plus a ≈2× step-efficiency improvement.

## Methodology

CriPO retains GRPO as the optimization backbone and layers two OPSD-derived corrections on top, combined as `L_CriPO = L_GRPO + β·L_OPSD` (β=0.05 for Qwen3-1.7B, β=0.03 for Qwen3-4B, with 50-step linear warmup).

**Step 1 — Identify failure modes.** For each prompt, sample G rollouts, score them criterion-wise with a Qwen3-32B rubric judge, aggregate into a scalar reward, and compute GRPO group-relative advantages A_i. UC = criteria not satisfied by any rollout. SC = criteria satisfied by some rollouts but where (a) the sum of advantages over satisfying rollouts is negative (negative-advantage suppression), or (b) the sum is zero and the criterion is satisfied by fewer than half the group (zero-advantage suppression).

**Step 2 — Behavior Injection for UC.** Construct a criterion-injection prompt that asks the policy to minimally revise the *highest-advantage rollout* y* so that it satisfies the unexplored criteria. The student is `π_θ(·|x, y_<t)`; the teacher is `π_θ(·|x, C_u, y_<t)` with stop-gradient. A per-token forward-KL d_t is computed, clamped at 10, with the first and last 1% of tokens masked; only the smallest token set whose cumulative KL reaches γ=0.95 contributes to L_OPSD. Forward-KL is chosen specifically because it is mode-covering, encouraging the student to explore behaviors the teacher reveals.

**Step 3 — Advantage Flipping for SC.** For each negative-advantage rollout that satisfies some SC, construct a counterfactual teacher prompt asking the model to minimally modify or delete the parts satisfying those criteria. Compare student p_S and teacher p_T token-wise; select positions where Δ_{i,t} = log p_S(y_{i,t}) − log p_T(y_{i,t}) > 0 AND p_T(y_{i,t}) < α·max_v p_T(v) (α=0.1) — i.e., positions where the student kept the token but the teacher confidently swapped it out. At those positions, replace the rollout-level advantage A_i with a positive τ_flip=0.1, then run standard GRPO with these token-level modified advantages.

**Stability tricks.** Top-K=3 criteria by weight when constructing teacher prompts (to limit prompt length), KL clamping at 10 to suppress outliers, masking of the first/last 1% of response tokens where boundary artifacts inflate KL, and 50-step linear warmup on β. The whole pipeline is implemented on verl, trained on 8×A800-40G with the Qwen3-32B judge on 64×910B2 NPUs.

## Results & Highlights

- **Headline gains over GRPO (GPT-4o-mini judge, Table 1).** CriPO lifts the 5-benchmark average from 59.2 → 62.4 (+3.2 absolute) on Qwen3-1.7B and from 68.2 → 69.6 (+1.4 absolute) on Qwen3-4B; on Qwen3-4B RaR-Medicine specifically, CriPO reaches 62.1 vs. GRPO's 60.2 (+1.9 over GRPO, +6.4 over the 55.7 base).
- **CriPO-U vs HeRL on exploration.** The UC-only variant CriPO-U (60.5/68.5/70.0/75.0/70.1 on Qwen3-4B, avg 68.8) outperforms HeRL (which uses off-policy hindsight rollouts) on the same Qwen3-4B average (68.4) while remaining fully on-policy, suggesting localized self-distillation recovers unexplored criteria without training-inference mismatch.
- **OPSD alone collapses.** Standalone OPSD degrades average to 38.2 (−18.9) on Qwen3-1.7B and 43.6 (−20.1) on Qwen3-4B, empirically justifying CriPO's hybrid (GRPO + auxiliary OPSD) design.
- **≈2× step efficiency.** On Qwen3-1.7B / RaR-Medicine, CriPO surpasses GRPO's best accuracy at step 175 — roughly 2.0× fewer optimization steps — and converges to a higher final accuracy (Figure 5). In wall-clock terms, CriPO also finishes a better-than-GRPO run before GRPO completes its full budget (e.g., 8h52m vs 11h32m on Qwen3-1.7B/RaR-Science).
- **Robustness across judge models (Table 4).** Re-evaluating with Qwen3-32B as judge reproduces the trend: full CriPO improves average from 43.7 → 47.1 (+3.4) on Qwen3-1.7B and 59.4 → 61.5 (+2.1) on Qwen3-4B over GRPO, and beats HeRL on both scales.
- **Failure-mode statistics reduced (Figure 7).** On Qwen3-4B/RaR-Medicine, CriPO reduces the UC ratio from 84.8% (GRPO) to 82.7% and the SC ratio from 52.6% (GRPO) to 51.4%; per-failure-mode ablations show CriPO-U is the biggest UC reducer and CriPO-S the biggest SC reducer, confirming each component targets its intended failure mode.
- **Ablations confirm design choices (Table 2, Qwen3-4B).** Removing contribution-guided token filtering drops CriPO-U average 66.4 → 65.5; removing best-rollout selection drops it to 65.6; replacing counterfactual-based token localization with random selection collapses CriPO-S average 68.9 → 64.9 (−4.0), demonstrating the gain is not from indiscriminate advantage boosting.
- **Failure-mode prevalence is large.** On Qwen3-4B/RaR-Medicine, 83%+ of samples contain UC (avg 2.5/sample) and 57%+ contain SC (avg 1.8/sample) throughout training — quantifying the size of the problem CriPO targets.

## Authors & Credibility

- Joint work between Zhejiang University (Haobo Wang's group) and ByteDance, with corresponding authors Haobo Wang (ZJU) and Siqing Wang (ByteDance). The two co-first authors did the work during a ByteDance internship.
- The empirical setup is well-resourced and serious: training on 8×A800-40G with a Qwen3-32B rubric judge hosted on 64×910B2 NPUs, evaluation against two different judges (GPT-4o-mini and Qwen3-32B), and OOD instruction-following checks on IFEval/IFBench/MulDimIF.
- Baselines include the directly competing HeRL (hindsight rubric RL) and OPSD (on-policy self-distillation), plus two of CriPO's own single-intervention variants (CriPO-U, CriPO-S) — a fair and informative comparison grid.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Genuine. The Suppressed Criteria framing is fresh — prior rubric-RL work (RuscaRL, HeRL, RuCL, "Rubrics to Tokens") focuses on exploration but does not formalize the "criterion already satisfied but signal lost under scalar aggregation" failure, nor provide its 57%/1.8-per-sample prevalence analysis. The counterfactual-teacher advantage-flipping mechanism is a non-obvious use of OPSD.
- **Rigor:** Reasonable but not airtight. Two model scales (Qwen3-1.7B/4B) is on the thin side for a method paper; no 7B+ results. The strongest reported gains (+3.2 on Qwen3-1.7B) shrink to +1.4 on Qwen3-4B — a worrying trend that the authors do not discuss. Several individual benchmarks are won by single-intervention variants (CriPO-U or CriPO-S), and CriPO-S actually ties CriPO on the Qwen3-4B average (69.6) — the case that the combined method is strictly better than its parts is therefore not fully made at the 4B scale.
- **Missing ablations:** No sensitivity analysis on γ (token filter threshold), α (counterfactual confidence), τ_flip (flipped advantage magnitude), or K=3 (top-criteria cap) — all of which are load-bearing hyperparameters chosen by intuition. No analysis of the extra teacher-rollout compute cost vs. the wall-clock claim. No comparison against RuscaRL (the other major exploration-focused rubric-RL baseline) — only HeRL is included.
- **Evaluation quirks:** All main results are judged by GPT-4o-mini, which has known positional and length biases; CriPO produces *longer* responses (Figure 6, right), which is a confound for any length-biased judge. The Qwen3-32B re-judge helps but is itself the *training* judge, creating a different circularity. HealthBench/ResearchQA are sub-sampled to 500 examples each, and LLMEval-Med has only 667 questions — per-benchmark deltas of 1–2 points on n=500–667 are within plausible judge noise.
- **Reproducibility:** Good on paper (verl framework, hyper-parameters in Table 5, prompts in Figures 11 and the appendix), but the data filtering step (drop samples where Qwen3-4B reward > 0.9) is Qwen3-4B-specific and would need re-tuning for any other base model. No public code release is mentioned.
- **Significance:** A meaningful conceptual contribution (Suppressed Criteria as a named, measured failure mode) plus a solid practical recipe, but the gains at 4B are modest and the lack of larger-model experiments limits how confidently the method can be recommended at scale.
- **Red flags:** (1) OPSD-only results in Table 1 are catastrophically bad (−18 to −23 points), which the authors use as motivation but never reconcile with their own claim that OPSD is the right primitive — it raises the question of whether CriPO is essentially "GRPO + a small, carefully filtered OPSD nudge." (2) The "2× fewer steps" headline is measured on a single dataset (RaR-Medicine) and model (Qwen3-1.7B); readers should not generalize it. (3) Length increase as a side effect (Figure 6) is a potential judge-gaming concern.

## Strengths

- Crisp, falsifiable problem formulation: two named failure modes with explicit equations (Eqs. 3 and 4) and large-scale empirical prevalence numbers.
- The two interventions are well-matched to their target failure modes, and the per-mode ablations (CriPO-U reduces UC most, CriPO-S reduces SC most) actually support the mechanistic story.
- Stability-aware engineering: forward-KL clamping, boundary-token masking, top-K criterion capping, and best-rollout selection are sensible and individually ablated.
- Honesty about OPSD instability (Figure 2) — the paper does not oversell standalone OPSD and explicitly motivates the hybrid.
- Cross-judge robustness check (Qwen3-32B re-evaluation in Table 4) and OOD instruction-following check (Table 3) are above the bar for the venue.

## Limitations

- Only two model scales (1.7B and 4B); the larger-model trend (gain shrinks from +3.2 to +1.4) is not investigated.
- No sensitivity analysis on γ, α, τ_flip, K — four hand-set hyperparameters with default values justified only by intuition.
- RuscaRL (the other canonical exploration-focused rubric-RL baseline) is discussed in related work but not included in the experimental comparison.
- Length-confound with the GPT-4o-mini judge is acknowledged (CriPO produces longer responses) but not controlled for.
- Cross-domain benchmarks sub-sampled to 500 examples; small per-benchmark deltas are within plausible judge noise.
- No public code release stated; the data-filtering recipe is base-model-specific.
- The "2× fewer steps" claim is reported on one (model, dataset) pair and may not generalize.

## Relevance

对关注 rubric-based RL 与 LLM 对齐的研究者，本文价值较高——它命名并量化了一个此前被忽略的失败模式（Suppressed Criteria），并提供了一套即插即用的 on-policy 修正机制，可被迁移到任何采用 GRPO+LLM-as-judge 的开放任务 RL 管线中。对于关注 self-distillation 稳定性与 token 级奖励信号的同学，CriPO 的"局部 forward-KL + 反事实 token 定位"也是一种可直接借鉴的工程范式。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Do CriPO's gains hold (or grow) at 7B–32B scale, or does the shrinking +3.2 → +1.4 trend continue, suggesting the method primarily helps weaker base models?
- How sensitive is CriPO to γ, α, τ_flip, and K=3? Is there a regime where CriPO-S alone matches full CriPO at larger scale (as it already ties at 4B)?
- Would a length-controlled judge (e.g., length-normalized rubric scoring, or a judge blind to response length) preserve the reported gains, given CriPO's longer outputs?
- Can the counterfactual advantage-flipping idea be ported to verifiable-reward RLVR (math/code), where the analogue would be "process-level credit for partially correct rollouts"?
- How does CriPO interact with reward-hacking when the rubric judge itself is unreliable or adversarially gameable?
- What is the marginal compute overhead of the two teacher forward passes per step, and does the wall-clock advantage (Figure 5, right) survive at 7B+ where rollout cost dominates?
- Could the Suppressed Criteria diagnostic (Eqs. 3–4) be used as a general *data-quality* signal to identify prompts where GRPO is wasting samples, independent of CriPO as a fix?
