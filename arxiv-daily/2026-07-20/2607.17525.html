Key Finding

The most severe LLM-gateway failures are Silent — HTTP 200 with corrupted semantic payloads, invisible to every standard monitor.

一句话总结：在 LiteLLM / Portkey / OpenRouter 这类多供应商 LLM 网关中，运维危害最大的失败是返回 HTTP 200 却悄悄污染语义载荷的「沉默失败」，传统延迟/错误率/SLA 指标对其完全失效。

Why This Matters

Elevates silent semantic failures to a first-class engineering problem — one demanding semantic-level observability, not transport-layer metrics, and directly complementary to ContinuityBench / multi-turn agent state-consistency work.

Critical Edge

The “silent failures dominate” thesis rests on 2 of 5 entries; the survivorship-bias argument is plausible but never measured, and the headline CPR ~28% is one specific bug in the authors’ own mock harness.

01Methodology

Empirical, taxonomy-driven — not a benchmark.

A Layer × Detectability matrix, populated from two evidence streams and gated by three inclusion criteria.

  • Two axes. Layer (L1 Network/Transport → L5 Governance/Cost) drawn where mechanism, code-owner, and mitigation all change together; Detectability splits Loud (5xx, crashes, hard 429s) from Silent (HTTP 200 + corrupted payload). Severity is asserted to reduce to detectability, so a third “severity” axis is rejected.
  • First-hand stress testing. ContinuityBench Phase 2 ran hundreds of parallel agents against a mock provider simulating real latency, rate limits, and transient 502s — the race condition and retry storm emerged organically under sustained load.
  • Structured issue-tracker surveys over LiteLLM / Portkey / OpenRouter with scoped queries (e.g. SSE stream "cut off" OR "truncated", "connection timeout" gateway failover).
  • Three inclusion criteria — concrete & specific, independently verifiable provenance (persistent URL), mechanistically explainable — applied by manual reading of issue threads, logs, and merged fixes.
  • Three standalone reproductions (asyncio race, 100-agent retry storm, statically-captured SSE chunks adapted from litellm#33678); the other two ship as README instructions only.

02Results & Highlights

A small, evidence-graded catalog — and one provocative empty row.

  • 5 verified failure modes. 3/5 (60%) from LiteLLM’s public tracker (litellm#33678, #20256, #21033); 2/5 (40%) first-hand from CB. 3 of 5 ship reproduction scripts.
  • 2 of 5 Silent (40%) — the State/Session race and the SSE index collision — argued to be disproportionately severe because no standard metric flags them.
  • Race-condition magnitude (CB, §6.1): CPR collapsed from near-perfect at C = 5 to ~28% at C = 100, all HTTP 200 — a ~72-point drop invisible to latency / error-rate / uptime metrics.
  • Retry-storm magnitude (CB, §6.2): 100 agents on a fixed retry interval after a simulated outage permanently failed ~25% of requests, while generating billable partial-completion tokens for zero successful completions.
  • The empty L4 row (Model Behavior) is framed as a legitimate finding: no evidence-grade, reproducible infrastructure-level bug could meet the bar — exposing the field’s weakest observability tooling.
  • Adoption context: LiteLLM alone has 18,000+ GitHub stars and routes millions of API calls/day.

03Strengths

  • Taxonomy is minimal, memorable, actionable. It directly answers the two triage questions practitioners ask — “where in the stack?” and “why didn’t we catch it?” The rejection of a third severity axis is well argued.
  • Honest empty cells. The blank L4 row is presented as a real finding about observability gaps, not papered over — a sign of intellectual integrity.
  • Mechanism-level depth on the two first-hand cases (Vulnerability → Mechanism → Fix) makes them genuinely teachable: per-session deep-copy + locking; exponential backoff with jitter + a global circuit breaker.
  • Verifiable provenance — specific GitHub issue numbers traced through merged fixes set a strong evidentiary standard.
  • Open, extensible design with YAML schema, rendering pipeline, and CVE-style contribution model is the right architecture for a problem where new modes will outpace any single team.

04Limitations

  • n = 5. Too small to support strong generalisation claims; the authors concede this openly.
  • Single-project sourcing bias. 3/5 entries from LiteLLM; Portkey and OpenRouter contribute nothing concrete, and proprietary gateways are not studied.
  • Self-sourcing bias. The two most thoroughly characterised entries were discovered during the authors’ own CB evaluations — which were explicitly designed to stress exactly state management and retry policy.
  • No statistical methodology. No saturation analysis, no inter-annotator agreement on Loud/Silent or Layer classification, no confidence intervals on CPR or failure-rate numbers.
  • Mock-provider, not production, evidence. The headline numbers come from a simulated provider; real traffic may behave differently. Two entries (event-loop block, Redis semaphore leak) require K8s probes / live Redis and ship as README only.
  • Undisclosed conflict of interest: FA ↔ CB mutually cite, both by the same authors at Metriqual.

05Questions for Further Exploration

What the catalog provokes — not what it answers.

  • Scale. How would the Layer × Detectability distribution shift with a community-sourced catalog of 50–100 entries — would Silent failures remain disproportionately severe, or merely disproportionately under-reported?
  • Populating L4. Can the empty Model Behavior row be filled with evidence-grade entries via continuous semantic evaluation in production — and what statistical methodology would separate genuine model drift from stochastic variation?
  • Inter-annotator agreement. What is the Loud-vs-Silent agreement when independent practitioners classify the same set of issue reports?
  • The collapse curve. For the race condition, how does CPR degrade as a continuous function of concurrency between C=5 and C=100 — and is the threshold predictable from the proxy’s locking design?
  • Generalisation. Are the catalogued modes (SSE index collision, Redis semaphore leak) reproducible across Portkey and OpenRouter, or are they LiteLLM-specific implementation bugs?
  • The semantic-observability stack. Can OpenTelemetry / Prometheus be extended to inspect conversation-history integrity and tool-call payload correctness at line rate — and at what cost?
  • Beyond OpenAI semantics. Does the taxonomy hold for Anthropic / Google streaming protocols where tool-call index semantics differ?
