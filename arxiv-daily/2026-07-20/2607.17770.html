Measuring Monosemanticity in Sparse Autoencoders via Latent Activation Coherence

Katarzyna Filus · Sebastian Pokuciński · Polish Academy of Sciences / Silesian University of Technology
Key finding

TMS is a label-free, encoder-free monosemanticity metric for Sparse Autoencoders that resists the encoder anisotropy bias inflating prior scores.

Key metric · concept deletion
0.864vs0.653
SAE-level correlation with target-class accuracy drop on CLIP, N=4. TMS beats the embedding-based MS at every N up to 8 under anisotropic encoders — CLIP σcosine=0.52 vs DINOv3 0.07.
Why this matters
Exposes a hidden encoder-geometry bias distorting cross-model SAE comparisons — and validates the fix functionally via concept deletion, not just structurally.
Critical edge
All claims hinge on Mini-ImageNet; validation is largely MS-anchored, partly circular without any label-based ground truth (FMS/JSDS).
01

Methodology

Each SAE latent k is treated as a concept detector. Activations are binarized with a per-neuron, data-derived threshold μk (training-set mean), giving active-latent set Bn per stimulus and active-sample set Ak per neuron.

TIα,β(Bi,Bj) = |Bi∩Bj| / ( |Bi∩Bj| + α|Bi\Bj| + β|Bj\Bi| )

Default α=β=1 reduces TMS to a within-active-set Jaccard coherence — parameter-free, grounded in Tversky's feature-similarity model. Aggregated across up to M=1000 sampled pairs per neuron (stable to 3 decimals, std ≈ 10⁻⁵).

Models: DINOv3 (ConvNeXt-tiny), CLIP (ViT-B/32), BLIP2 (blip2-opt-2.7b). SAEs: TopK & BatchTopK, K∈{10,32,64}, expansion ×{2,4,8}, 25 epochs. Benchmark: Mini-ImageNet.

02

Results

  • Anisotropy biases MS. CLIP σ=0.52 / PC1 EVR 0.07, BLIP2 0.33, DINOv3 only 0.07. MS ranking mirrors anisotropy exactly.
  • TMS stays aligned, less geometry-driven. Per-neuron Spearman TMS–MS > 0.75 in 16/18 DINOv3 SAEs, up to 0.93.
  • Strong negative correlation with within-active-set variance — BLIP2 approaches −0.94 (TopK ×4, K=32/64).
  • Concept deletion (CLIP): TMS beats MS at every N≤8 — N=1 0.783 vs 0.721; N=4 0.864 vs 0.653; N=8 0.678 vs 0.534.
  • On isotropic DINOv3 MS is slightly stronger (N=2: 0.915 vs 0.878) — TMS is competitive, not dominant.
  • Distinct dynamics: DINOv3 TopK shows bump→drop→stabilize; BLIP2 declines monotonically; K=10 reaches higher TMS than larger K.
  • ~30 min saved per test pass by skipping external BLIP2 embedding generation.

Strengths

  • Clean, well-motivated formulation — no labels, no external encoder, grounded in Tversky's cognitive-science model.
  • Unified cross-modal SAE evaluation (DINOv3, CLIP, BLIP2) under one protocol — rare and valuable.
  • Explicit quantification of the anisotropy bias via σcosine and PC1 EVR — a concrete mechanism.
  • Four complementary validations: metric–metric, structure (variance), training dynamics, functional concept-deletion.
  • Honest appendix reporting of SAE health (R², L0, dead-neuron ratio) and probe accuracies.

Limitations

  • Single benchmark (Mini-ImageNet); no text-only LM SAEs, no large-scale vision benchmarks.
  • Validation anchored to MS — which itself depends on the very encoder geometry TMS avoids (partly circular).
  • Weaker CLIP probe accuracies (~68–72%) confound the concept-deletion comparison; no probe-quality-matched ablation.
  • MS always uses the matching base-model encoder; no held-out isotropic-encoder MS baseline.
  • Some BLIP ×8 SAEs have dead-feature ratios up to 12.1% yet remain in pooled correlations.
  • α=β=1 makes TMS effectively Jaccard — asymmetric variants and broader tasks deferred.

Open questions for further work

06 directions
  1. How does TMS behave on non-vision settings — e.g., SAEs on LLM residual-stream activations, where binarization thresholds and active-set sizes differ sharply?
  2. Does TMS retain its advantage when MS uses a held-out, deliberately isotropic encoder (MS-DINOv3 on CLIP SAEs), or does the gap collapse?
  3. What happens on larger, more diverse benchmarks (full ImageNet, COCO, ADE20K, multi-task) with different concept co-occurrence statistics?
  4. Can asymmetric Tversky (α≠β) systematically improve alignment with human-judged monosemanticity or FMS/JSDS ground truth?
  5. Do the elevated dead-feature ratios in BLIP ×8 SAEs (up to 12.1%) materially change the TMS-vs-MS ranking if excluded?
  6. Does TMS predict functional interventions beyond linear-probe deletion — activation patching, steering vectors, causal abstraction?
