Paper diagnostic · judgment stability under continuous-prefix attack

Learned soft prefixes flip 72–90% of correct syllogistic judgments in frozen LLMs — driven by broad answer bias, not logical editing.

Logical Judgments Under Pressure: Diagnosing Syllogistic Stability with Learned Soft Prefixes

Brian K Chen · National University of Singapore · single-author ·  arxiv.org/abs/2607.18228

Key metric

90.3% flip

Peak flip of previously-correct syllogism judgments on Qwen3.6-35B-A3B MoE, with ≤1.8% collateral damage to the other answer class. vs. <1% flip for norm-matched random prefixes (Gemma 4 31B)

Why this matters

A reproducible framework to quantify how stable reasoning-LLM judgments are under adversarial soft prefixes, on exactly-labeled formal tasks.

Critical edge

The minority→majority direction means an "always-target" system trivially scores 100% flip with 0% damage — headline rates measure intervention strength & transfer, not selective logical editing.

Methodology — the stress test

frozen weights · trainable prefix only

Setup

Prepend m trainable embedding vectors (len 8/16) to a frozen LLM. Only the prefix is optimized; weights never move.

Benchmark

256 Aristotelian categorical forms (non-empty-term semantics) × 4 surface styles = 1,024 examples. Labels computed exactly from Venn enumeration — no human annotation.

Tasks

Two binary judgments: Validity (does the conclusion follow?) and Satisfiability (are premises jointly consistent?). Forms split 128/64/64; all 4 wordings stay in one split → test uses unseen forms.

Intervention directions

UNSAT→SAT & VALID→INVALID — both logically impossible to license by added premises. Any flip is redirected judgment, not legitimate inference.

Training

AdamW, lr=1e-2, 100 steps, batch 1, λ=1.0, seeds 50–53. Target-plus-preservation loss keeps the other-class answer intact.

Control ladder

Randomized A/B answer mapping · norm-matched random · best-of-1000 random search · neutral readable phrases · reverse directions · cross-task transfer · generation mode · activation patching · 4 score-model fits.

Objective — target + preservation

L(P) = −(1/|S|)·Σ log p_θ(y_t | P, x) + λ·(1/|A|)·Σ [ − log p_θ(y_gold(x) | P, x) ], λ = 1.0

Strengths & limitations

side-by-side

+ Strengths

  • Conceptually clean: the formal problem and its correct label never change, so any behavioral change is unambiguously the learned context's doing.
  • Exceptional scoping discipline — every conclusion is paired with the controls that license it and those that do not (Table 22 is a model of this practice).
  • Score-model analysis exposes a behavioral split aggregate flip rates hide: predictable direction vs unpredictable magnitude, computed cheaply from saved scores.
  • Honest about what activation patching can and cannot show; the all-prompt-token patch (74–82% restoration) is a clean negative result for narrow-circuit claims.
  • Planned artifact identifies 384 trained prefixes by SHA-256 content hash, with exact checkpoints, hyperparameters, and full prompt templates recorded.

— Limitations

  • Categorical syllogisms are a small fragment; results may not generalize to mathematical, multi-step, or natural-language reasoning.
  • Only three models, each with a different calibrated interface — cross-model claims conflate architecture with prompting.
  • Only six targeted forms per test condition → wide bootstrap intervals; intervention strength is heavily partition-dependent (Gemma validity: 60.9% original vs 90.6–99.0% on rotated splits).
  • Minority→majority direction inflates headline rates; reverse-direction & cross-task transfer are weak.
  • Generated-answer results use dev-selected prefixes, not average-prefix behavior.
  • Activation analyses are descriptive on selected flipped cases; paper explicitly declines circuit identification. Random controls are iid Gaussian (std 0.02) — a manifold-matched sample might narrow the gap.

Open questions

for further exploration
  1. Do these prefixes transfer to non-syllogistic formal reasoning — propositional logic, first-order theorem proving, arithmetic word problems — where labels stay exactly computable?
  2. Would sampling random prefixes from the model's own token-embedding manifold (vs iid Gaussian) close the learned-vs-random gap and change the interpretation?
  3. Qwen's targeted margins are unpredictable — genuine example-dependent logical sensitivity, or just an affine/isotonic family too restrictive for MoE routing?
  4. Can the broad answer-preference read be linked to specific attention heads or MLP layers via causal mediation finer than the one-block patch used here?
  5. Does scaling prefix length past 16 (or >100 steps) eventually push toward fixed-symbol forcing, or does the broad-answer-bias regime saturate?
  6. How do explicit reasoning models (enforced chain-of-thought) behave — is the generated rationale redirected too, or does the model produce a contradictory rationale that still flips the final answer?
  7. The minority→majority framing inflates flip rates; what would a balanced two-class selective-editing metric reveal about which models are truly steerable vs trivially biased?
