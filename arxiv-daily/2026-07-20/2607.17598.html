Methodology

Three "wirings" over the same book — only the disclosure logic varies.

  1. 01 rawBook sits in the sandbox; agent navigates freely (open / grep / read-on-demand).
  2. 02 flatOne SKILL.md; always-loaded description gates discovery, body is a per-chunk index → open files from references/.
  3. 03 hierarchicalEach chunk becomes a child skill; a meta-router + all child descriptions are always in context.
  • TestbedLOONGDOC — ∞Bench items as ACP tasks with a deterministic verifier and per-run scalar reward.
  • SweepCodex · Pi · Claude-Code × gpt-5.4-mini · qwen3.6-27b · claude-haiku-4.5; 3 seeds, mean ± std.
  • Baselinehybrid-rag — BM25 + BGE-M3, RRF fusion, BGE cross-encoder reranker.
  • ScaleK ∈ {1, 5, 10, 20} staged books per task; each question still targets one book.
  • ControlSame chunk set, task instruction, and verifier across all three approaches; pinned adapter versions.

Single book

Flat matches or beats raw on Pi and Claude-Code; ties within error on Codex, whose bare agent greps named entities on the fly.

Hierarchical collapse

Never beats flat. Pi qwen3.6-27b Zh.QA: 0.7479 → 0.3890. Always-loaded child descriptions saturate the router's context.

hybrid-rag

Loses nearly everywhere. gpt-5.4-mini En.MC: 0.7628 vs flat 0.9126.

One exception

Depth pays only on weak-navigator open QA at scale: Pi Zh.QA @ K=20, hierarchical 0.330 vs flat 0.137.

Strengths

  • First-mover rigor on a pattern that had spread on intuition and Hacker News anecdotes before anyone measured it.
  • Clean separation of routing depth from index location — axes the practitioner literature had conflated.
  • Trajectory-grounded explanations (Codex's grep behavior, hierarchical's context saturation) make results interpretable, not just numerical.
  • Cost-accuracy Pareto analysis: flat is simultaneously more accurate and cheaper at library scale.
  • Honest negative-result reporting on hierarchical disclosure — resists overclaiming and directly improves practitioner guidance.
  • LOONGDOC doubles as a reusable RL-style environment with a per-trajectory scalar reward.

Limitations

  • ∞Bench multiple-choice books are canonical English novels — likely memorized; the bare agent solves En.MC at K=20 in too few tool calls to have actually read 20 books.
  • Sample sizes inherit ∞Bench's limits; several multi-book cells stay within one standard error.
  • Narrow scaling axis — K ≤ 20, narrative only. No codebases, technical manuals, or legal contracts.
  • Skill packs and book texts are not redistributed (copyright); closed-model harnesses are partially opaque.
  • A single fixed book-to-skill recipe is tested; chunk granularity and description-writing model are not ablated.
  • Cost figures are uncached upper bounds — favors raw in the comparison.

Open questions for further exploration

  • Does the flat-vs-hierarchical verdict flip on non-narrative corpora — codebases, manuals, legal contracts — where chunk structure is more regular and entities are denser?
  • How does prompt caching shift the cost frontier? The authors flag disclosure as the most cache-friendly part — would it push flat further ahead?
  • Can LOONGDOC's per-trajectory reward post-train a navigator (via RL) that erases the flat-vs-raw gap the way Codex's grep behavior already does?
  • At what K does flat disclosure itself break — is there a library size where a two-level hierarchy finally becomes necessary?
  • How much of the gain comes from the routing metadata vs. the chunking granularity — would a description-free flat index already capture most of it?
  • Does the Zh.QA ceiling trace to base-model Chinese capability, to the chunking recipe, or to the verifier's Chinese normalization?
