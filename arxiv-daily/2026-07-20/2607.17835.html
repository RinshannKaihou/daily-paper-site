FP11 (E4M6B8) vs INT16 baseline on the same SDF radix-2² core: 8.2 mW → 6.5 mW, 0.027 mm² → 0.022 mm². 256-point FFT, 500 MHz, 12 nm post-P&R.
Why this matters

Translates ML’s low-precision FP playbook into a reusable value-distribution fitting methodology for classical DSP kernels — backed by post-layout PPA, not synthesis estimates. Directly relevant to coherent optical SerDes where pJ/bit dominates.

Critical edge

“Beats fixed-point” only beats the authors’ own SDF fixed-point core — no vendor FFT IP baseline (Xilinx / Intel / Synopsys DesignWare), and an AWGN-only channel that may not survive real fiber impairments (CD, PMD, laser phase noise, nonlinearities) at 4096-QAM.

01 Methodology

Four layers, one fitting loop

The work stitches the DSP kernel, a distribution fitter, the RTL microarchitecture and a joint PPA + accuracy evaluator into a single loop — turning precision reduction from an ad-hoc knob into an application-aware discipline.

01 · DSP kernel as DAG
FFT/IFFT modelled as a NumPy-compatible DAG of real arithmetic, parameterised by a format config F.
02 · Distribution fitting
Propagate OFDM inputs through the DAG, record per-node empirical ranges, then sweep (b_e, b_m, B) against tail stats.
03 · Arithmetic instantiation
Parametrizable SDF radix-2² pipelined FFT IP. FP variants use one format end-to-end; fixed-point scales ÷2 per stage.
04 · PPA + BER evaluation
12 nm Synopsys post-P&R for power/area; BER vs Eb/N0 over 64–4096-QAM, aggregated as mean & max excess log10 BER.

Insight: the FFT’s stage-wise dynamic-range growth is fully covered by b_e = 4 exponent bits with bias B = 8 — leaving the bit budget for the mantissa, which is what actually sets BER.

A Strengths

  • Clean cross-layer framing that turns the ML low-precision lesson into a disciplined DSP methodology, not a one-off trick.
  • Honest accuracy reporting — both mean and worst-case excess log10 BER, including the case where FP11 visibly fails at 4096-QAM.
  • Industrial 12 nm post-P&R PPA via Synopsys Design Compiler + IC Compiler II — not synthesis estimates.
  • Pareto-frontier analysis as the right lens for multi-objective trade-offs; FP dominates across QAM orders.
  • Reusable design rule: exponent covers range, mantissa controls BER.

B Limitations

  • AWGN-only channel — no chromatic dispersion, PMD, laser phase noise or fiber nonlinearities.
  • Single-tap ZF equalization; no MMSE or multi-tap baseline.
  • Fixed-point baselines limited to the authors’ own SDF core — no vendor or HLS FFT IP compared.
  • Only 1000 OFDM frames for BER; no confidence intervals at low-BER regions.
  • Formats fitted globally at M = 256, not per modulation order; per-stage bit-widths unexplored.
  • No FEC integration — coded-BER / energy-per-bit not evaluated; single SDF lane only.

Q1Do the FP11/FP12 gains hold under realistic coherent optical impairments (CD, PMD, laser phase noise, nonlinearities), particularly at 1024-/4096-QAM?
Q2How much further does per-stage floating-point bit-width assignment (the authors’ stated future work) extend the Pareto gains?
Q3Does the methodology transfer to other OFDM kernels — channel estimation, MMSE equalization, FEC decoders — with different dynamic-range profiles?
Q4Can per-QAM re-fitting remove the 4096-QAM BER floor of FP11/FP12 without losing the PPA advantage?
Q5How do these cores compare to vendor FFT IP (Synopsys DesignWare, Intel/Xilinx hardened FFT) on the same 12 nm node?
Q6With FEC integrated, what is the coded-BER / energy-per-bit trade-off, and does it reshuffle the FP-vs-fixed-point ranking?
