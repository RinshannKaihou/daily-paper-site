> **一句话总结：** PoLoRA 将 LoRA 的优化从单因子矩阵方法重新表述为对合并乘积 BA 的控制问题，结合乘积感知的谱方向、对角 Kronecker 曲率预处理和显式的因子幅度规则，在 1B–8B 模型上以 ≤3% 的单步开销在 1.2–1.7× 更少的步数内达到 Adam 的最终验证损失，并且学习率可跨秩迁移。

> **TL;DR:** PoLoRA is a new LoRA optimizer derived from a per-sample-loss linear minimization oracle (LMO) on the merged product BA, combining a product-aware spectral direction, lightweight diagonal Kronecker curvature preconditioning, and an explicit factor-step magnitude rule. On Llama-3.2-1B finetuned on OpenMathInstruct-2 at rank 256, it matches tuned Adam's final eval loss in 1.63× fewer steps (and 1.2–1.7× across OLMo-2-1B, Qwen2.5-1.5B, Llama-3-8B on code/math), beats Muon/iMuon/LoRA-RITE (none of which consistently beat Adam), adds ≤3% per-step overhead, and its optimal LR stays fixed at 1e-2 across ranks 32–256 while Adam's drifts.

## 核心贡献

- **提出 PoLoRA 优化器**：从控制单样本损失变化的一阶线性化约束出发，推导出一个针对 LoRA 合并乘积 BA 的乘积感知谱更新方向，并辅以对角 Kronecker 曲率预处理与显式因子幅度控制。
- **解释为什么"直接把 Muon 套到 LoRA 因子上"不奏效**：模型看到的是乘积 BA 而优化器作用在因子 A、B 上，结构不一致；作者证明 Product Muon（即把谱约束作用于线性化合并更新 B∆A+∆BA）单独使用对 Adam 几乎没有加速。
- **量化的组件贡献分解**：在 Llama-3.2-1B 数学任务上，移除曲率和幅度会让 PoLoRA 退化成 Product Muon（相对 Adam 1.01×，几乎无加速），而曲率与幅度各自贡献约一半的加速差距。
- **跨模型/数据集/秩的一致性加速**：在 1B–8B 四个基座模型、code/math/低资源语言三种数据上稳定优于 Adam；并发现 PoLoRA 的最优学习率跨秩稳定在 10⁻²（Adam 的最优 LR 随秩漂移），因此具有学习率可迁移性。
- **开源实现**：代码与配置公开于 https://github.com/nikhilgsh/polora 。

## Key Contributions

- Introduces PoLoRA, a steepest-descent LoRA optimizer whose direction is the solution of an LMO that bounds the per-sample loss change, made product-aware by imposing the constraint on the linearized merged update B∆A + ∆BA rather than on each factor separately.
- Diagnoses why naively applying Muon to LoRA factors fails (the optimizer acts on A and B but the loss sees the product BA), and shows that Product Muon / iMuon / LoRA-RITE / Muon do not consistently beat Adam on this benchmark.
- Adds two engineering-critical ingredients that close the gap: (i) diagonal Kronecker curvature preconditioners P, Q fitted via a LoRA-adapted KL-Shampoo estimator on the factor gradients, and (ii) a magnitude rule ρ = η/(‖A‖₂ + ‖B‖₂) that controls both the per-factor spectral norms and the merged-update spectral norm.
- Demonstrates consistent step speedups of 1.2–1.7× over tuned Adam at ≤3% wall-clock overhead across four base models (OLMo-2-1B, Llama-3.2-1B, Qwen2.5-1.5B, Llama-3-8B) on OpenCoder (code) and OpenMathInstruct-2 (math).
- Shows learning-rate transfer across ranks 32/64/128/256 (PoLoRA optimum fixed at 1e-2; Adam's drifts) and a larger speedup on the low-resource Bengali (Aya) split than on code.

## Methodology

PoLoRA is derived in three layers, all expressed as solutions to constrained steepest-descent LMOs:

1. **Background (Muon).** Muon solves the spectral-norm LMO `min ⟨G, ∆W⟩ s.t. ‖∆W‖₂ ≤ η`, whose solution is `∆W = −η·msign(G)` where `msign(G) = UV⊤` from the reduced SVD. The spectral norm bound is conservative because it bounds the worst-case change in the layer output over unit-norm inputs.

2. **Product Muon (intermediate).** The merged LoRA update linearizes to `∆W ≈ B∆A + ∆BA`, so the spectral bound should apply to this product rather than to ∆A and ∆B separately. Using norm subadditivity and splitting the budget evenly yields two decoupled factorwise LMOs with solutions involving `(B⊤B)^{-1/2}` and `(AA⊤)^{-1/2}`. The authors show this alone does not beat Adam.

3. **Per-sample loss control (core PoLoRA idea).** Instead of bounding the spectral norm, PoLoRA bounds the change in each per-sample loss `|ℓ_xᵢ(W + ∆W) − ℓ_xᵢ(W)| ≤ τ`. Linearizing and replacing the unknown per-sample gradients `Gᵢ` with an "outergradient set" `G(Σ)` defined via the gradient covariance `Σ = (1/n)Σ gᵢgᵢ⊤`, then approximating `Σ ≈ Q ⊗ P` with a Kronecker product, reduces the LMO to `min ⟨G, ∆W⟩ s.t. ‖P^{1/2} ∆W Q^{1/2}‖₂ ≤ τ`. Its solution is `∆W = −τ P^{-1/2} msign(P^{-1/2} G Q^{-1/2}) Q^{-1/2}` — a curvature-preconditioned matrix-sign step.

4. **LoRA instantiation.** Substituting the linearized product update into this preconditioned LMO gives two factorwise direction updates `D_A`, `D_B` involving the small r×r matrices `C_B = B⊤PB` and `C_A = AQA⊤` (Eq. 19).

5. **Magnitude rule.** The LMO fixes only the direction. PoLoRA sets both factors to a common spectral-norm step size `ρ = η/(‖A‖₂ + ‖B‖₂)`, which by triangle inequality + submultiplicativity guarantees `‖∆W‖₂ ≤ η` on the merged product (Eq. 21). The final factor updates are `∆A = −ρ D_A/‖D_A‖₂`, `∆B = −ρ D_B/‖D_B‖₂`.

**Practical implementation (Algorithm 1):** momentum with look-ahead for the factor gradients; diagonal P, Q (à la Adafactor) fitted by adapting KL-Shampoo to the LoRA factor gradients; normalization of P, Q to make the EMA invariant to the rescaling (P,Q) → (aP, a⁻¹Q); power iteration for spectral norms; Gram Newton–Schulz iterations with PolarExpress coefficients for the matrix sign and inverse square roots. LoRA scaling is fixed at α = r.

## Results & Highlights

- **Step speedup over tuned Adam at rank 256 (Table 1):** OLMo-2-1B reaches Adam's final loss in 1.61× fewer steps on OpenCoder code and 1.74× fewer on OpenMathInstruct-2 math; Llama-3.2-1B 1.55× (code) / 1.63× (math); Qwen2.5-1.5B 1.30× (code) / 1.64× (math); Llama-3-8B 1.20× (code) / 1.59× (math).
- **Wall-clock overhead is ≤3%:** every wall-clock speedup in Table 1 is within ~0.04× of the step speedup (e.g., Llama-3.2-1B math: 1.63× step → 1.58× wall-clock), because PoLoRA's optimizer FLOPs are batch-size independent.
- **Beats all matrix-aware LoRA baselines on Llama-3.2-1B/math (Figure 1):** at the best LR, final eval loss — PoLoRA < Adam ≈ iMuon ≈ LoRA-RITE ≈ Muon; Muon, iMuon, LoRA-RITE each fail to consistently improve over Adam, contradicting their reported advantages.
- **Component ablation on Llama-3.2-1B/math (Figure 2):** speedup over Adam is 1.63× for full PoLoRA, drops to 1.29× when curvature is removed, and to 1.01× (i.e., no speedup) when both curvature and magnitude are removed (reducing to Product Muon). Curvature and magnitude each account for roughly half the gap.
- **Learning-rate transfer across ranks (Table 2, Figure 3):** on Llama-3.2-1B/math, step speedup is stable-to-growing with rank — r=32: 1.48×, r=64: 1.54×, r=128: 1.52×, r=256: 1.63×. PoLoRA's optimal LR stays at 10⁻² across all four ranks, while Adam's shifts by about an order of magnitude.
- **Distribution shift (Figure 4):** on Qwen2.5-1.5B at rank 256, PoLoRA's step speedup over Adam is 1.30× on code but 1.73× on the low-resource Bengali (Aya) split — consistent with the advantage growing when finetuning must move the model further from its pretraining distribution.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Genuinely incremental in a precise sense — Product Muon (the product-aware spectral LMO) is shown to be essentially equivalent to LoRA-Muon, Compositional Muon's half-split rule, LoRA-RITE's intrinsic update, and iMuon's intrinsic LMO (Section 4 / Appendix C). The novel contribution is the *combination* of (a) replacing the spectral constraint with a per-sample-loss constraint yielding the P^{-1/2}·msign·Q^{-1/2} preconditioned direction, (b) the magnitude rule ρ = η/(‖A‖₂+‖B‖₂), and (c) the diagonal KL-Shampoo-style P, Q fitted on factor gradients. The intellectual move from "bound spectral norm of layer output" to "bound per-sample loss change" is the most original piece.
- **Rigor:** Strong. Each step of the derivation is tied to a specific LMO with a closed-form solution and a lemma/proof in Appendix B; the Kronecker/outergradient set construction is properly motivated by Lemma 1 (leverage bound + rank-one gradient structure). The ablation cleanly reduces PoLoRA to Product Muon by removing two specific components, and traces each component's contribution.
- **Reproducibility:** Good. Code is public; hyperparameters, batch sizes, and configs are in Appendix A; baselines use the official implementations. One caveat: the main metric is "steps-to-Adam" (steps needed to match tuned Adam's final loss), which depends on Adam being well-tuned — if Adam were undertuned, PoLoRA's speedup would inflate. The authors do bracket optima (Figure 1 right) which mitigates this, but a fixed-compute comparison would be more conservative.
- **Significance:** Practical and timely. Matrix-aware optimizers (Muon family) are being adopted in production pretraining (DeepSeek-V4, GLM-4.5 cited), and the paper's negative result — that the obvious LoRA adaptation of Muon doesn't work — is itself valuable. The learning-rate-transfer property across ranks is a real practitioner-facing benefit.
- **Red flags / weaknesses:**
  - **Single primary metric.** Only held-out loss is reported; no downstream task accuracy (HumanEval, GSM8K, MATH), no perplexity on a different distribution, no quality evals. A 1.63× faster path to the same eval loss could in principle land at a different point on the loss-quality frontier.
  - **Small-model skew and short horizons.** Speedup shrinks at the largest model (Llama-3-8B: only 1.20× on code) and on the best-represented data (code, where Adam is already strong). No 70B-scale or multi-epoch runs.
  - **Approximations not fully validated.** The diagonal P, Q assumption and the Kronecker Σ ≈ Q ⊗ P approximation are made for efficiency but not ablated against full (or block-) Kronecker variants, so we don't know how much performance is being left on the table.
  - **Dropped ∆B∆A term.** The derivation linearizes the product update and drops the quadratic term; the authors argue (Remark 1) that restoring it leaves the bound essentially unchanged, but the empirical effect of the dropped term at high LR is not measured.
  - **Bengali result is a single dataset.** The "larger speedup under distribution shift" claim rests on one low-resource language; it could alternatively be explained by code being a setting where Adam is already near-optimal.
  - **The iMuon/LoRA-RITE "no improvement over Adam" result contradicts those methods' own papers.** While the authors use official implementations, no reconciliation is offered — the discrepancy could be due to LoRA rank (256 here), dataset, or base model choice, and a reader cannot tell which.

## Strengths

- Clean, principled derivation that unifies Muon, Product Muon, and PoLoRA under one LMO framework, making the contribution of each ingredient transparent.
- Honest negative result: the natural baseline (Muon on each LoRA factor) and the concurrent product-aware variants do not consistently beat Adam in this setting — a useful corrective to the recent Muon-everywhere enthusiasm.
- Component ablation (Figure 2) directly quantifies how much curvature vs. magnitude each contribute, which is rare and valuable.
- LR-transfer across ranks is a genuine practitioner benefit and is clearly demonstrated (Figure 3).
- Low overhead (≤3%) and wall-clock speedup within a hair of step speedup make the gains practically realizable rather than a step-count illusion.

## Limitations

- Evaluation is restricted to held-out loss on instruction-tuning datasets; no downstream task accuracy or longer-horizon training.
- Preconditioners are diagonal for efficiency; the Kronecker approximation of the per-sample second moment is itself approximate and not benchmarked against denser alternatives.
- The derivation relies on linearizations (of the loss and of the product update), so higher-order effects and the ∆B∆A quadratic term are not controlled.
- Only language-model LoRA is studied; other adapter families (DoRA, VeRA, etc.) and modalities (vision, multimodal) are untested.
- The "speedup grows under distribution shift" claim is supported by a single low-resource language (Bengali).
- The reduced speedup at the 8B scale (1.20× on code) leaves open whether gains persist or shrink at 70B+.

## Relevance

对关注参数高效微调（LoRA）与矩阵感知优化器（Muon 系列）的研究者而言，本文提供了一个原则性框架来解释"为什么直接把 Muon 套到 LoRA 上不奏效"，并给出一个可在 1B–8B 模型上稳定加速 1.2–1.7× 的实用方案，且代码与配置已开源，便于复现与扩展到其他适配器家族。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Do PoLoRA's step speedups translate to downstream task gains (HumanEval, GSM8K, MATH) at matched compute, or does the held-out-loss advantage vanish on actual benchmarks?
- How does PoLoRA behave at 70B scale and on longer training horizons — does the shrinking speedup at 8B-on-code continue, or stabilize?
- What is the cost/benefit of replacing the diagonal P, Q with block-diagonal or low-rank Kronecker factors, and does it recover additional speedup?
- Does the per-sample-loss-control LMO perspective (Eqs. 8, 12) yield gains when applied to full pretraining rather than LoRA finetuning?
- Can the analysis be extended to control the dropped ∆B∆A quadratic term explicitly, and does it matter at high learning rates or very high ranks?
- How does PoLoRA interact with other adapter families (DoRA, VeRA, PiSSA) that change the factorization or initialization?
- Is the "speedup grows under distribution shift" finding robust across multiple low-resource languages and domains beyond Bengali?
