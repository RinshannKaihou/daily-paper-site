> **一句话总结：** 本文针对 LLM 推理中常用的 max@k 评估准则（在 K 次采样中取最佳），给出了有限时域 RL 的严格理论：证明 Markovian 策略不再最优、历史可压缩为"前 K-1 次最佳回报 + 当前累计回报"两维、精确规划是 NP-hard 但存在 FPTAS，并给出 K 倍于经典 RL 的样本复杂度下界 Ω(K·SAH³/ε²·log(1/δ)) 及匹配上界。

> **TL;DR:** A pure theory paper that analyzes the **max@k** objective (the best-of-K / retry-aware metric used to evaluate LLM reasoning) in finite-horizon RL. The authors prove that, unlike standard RL, **Markovian policies are provably sub-optimal** (a constant ≥1.24 multiplicative gap, Proposition 1), but that **history can be compressed** to just two numbers — the best prior return M and the current intra-rollout cumulative return G — without losing optimality (Proposition 2). They give a fully-polynomial-time approximation scheme with **O(KS²AH³/κ²)** runtime, prove **exact planning is NP-hard** (Theorem 2), and establish tight PAC sample complexity under a generative model: a **Ω(K·SAH³/ε²·log(1/δ))** lower bound (Theorem 3) matched by an **eO(K·SAH³/ε²·log(1/δ))** upper bound for time-homogeneous MDPs (Theorem 4). The take-away: **learning max@k-optimal policies is K times statistically harder than classical RL**. There are no datasets, baselines, or experiments — it is a theory-only paper.

## 核心贡献

- **Markovian 策略不再最优**：证明在 max@k 准则下，Markovian 策略类 Π_M 相对历史依赖策略 Π_hist 存在 **(1.24 − O(1/K))** 倍的性能差距（Proposition 1），这是经典 RL 与 max@k RL 的关键结构差异。
- **历史可压缩性**：证明最优策略只需把完整历史压缩为两维——前 i−1 次 rollout 的最大回报 M_i 与当前 rollout 的累计回报 G_{h,i}（Proposition 2），且两维都是必要的（Proposition 3，删去任一维都会严格次优）。
- **高效近似规划算法（FPTAS）**：通过对 [0,1] 奖励区间做 κ-粒度均匀离散化，给出 ε-最优策略的近似规划，时间复杂度 **O(KS²AH³/κ²)**，设 κ=ε/H 得到 O(H⁵S²Aε⁻²) 多项式算法（Theorem 1）。
- **精确规划的不可解性**：通过从 Subset-Sum 归约，证明精确规划即使 K=2 也是 NP-hard（Theorem 2），从而 FPTAS 是紧的；并且评估单个策略的值在指数小间隙下甚至不属于 NP。
- **样本复杂度下界与上界（生成式模型 PAC 框架）**：建立 **Ω(K·SAH³/ε²·log(1/δ))** 下界（Theorem 3），以及匹配的 **eO(K·SAH³/ε²·log(1/δ))**（time-homogeneous）/ **eO(K·SAH⁴/ε²·log(1/δ))**（inhomogeneous）上界（Theorem 4），定量表明 max@k RL 比经典 RL 困难 K 倍。
- **理论与实践联系**：将"压缩历史 + 跨 rollout 自适应"的结果与 LLM 推理中的 self-refine、tree-of-thoughts、multi-attempt verifier 等系统对应起来，给出这些经验方法的理论依据——独立重复采样是次优的，且只需保留对历史的最小摘要即可达到最优。

## Key Contributions

- **Markovian policies are insufficient** (Proposition 1): History-dependent policies provably outperform Markovian ones under max@k by a multiplicative gap ≥ 1.24 − O(1/K); this is a structural departure from classical finite-horizon MDP theory.
- **Compressed history is sufficient and necessary** (Propositions 2 & 3): The full trajectory history can be replaced by Φ(H_{h,i}) = (s_{h,i}, M_i, G_{h,i}) — current state, best previous return, and current-rollout cumulative return — without losing optimality; both M and G are individually necessary.
- **FPTAS for planning** (Theorem 1): Uniformly discretizing rewards onto a κ-grid and running backward induction on the discretized MDP yields an ε-optimal policy in **O(KS²AH³/κ²)** time; setting κ = ε/H gives polynomial time O(H⁵S²Aε⁻²) with value error bounded by 2κH.
- **Exact planning is NP-hard** (Theorem 2): A polynomial-time reduction from Subset-Sum shows that exactly comparing two actions' values is NP-hard for an exponentially-small gap η, even for K=2; the FPTAS is therefore tight.
- **Tight PAC sample complexity** (Theorems 3 & 4): Under a generative model, any (ε,δ)-correct algorithm requires **Ω(K·SAH³ε⁻²·log(1/δ))** samples (recovering the classical H³SAε⁻² rate at K=1), and a uniform-sampling + discretization + backward-induction algorithm matches this at **eO(K·SAH³ε⁻²·log(1/δ))** (homogeneous) or **eO(K·SAH⁴ε⁻²·log(1/δ))** (inhomogeneous).
- **Conceptual bridge to practice**: The paper explicitly interprets recent adaptive reasoning systems (self-refinement, tree-of-thoughts, recursive self-aggregation, multi-attempt verifier feedback) as richer realizations of history-dependent policies, and argues that its M+G compression result formalizes the practical design principle "keep only a summary of past attempts, not the whole trace."

## Methodology

The paper studies the **max@k decision process**: an agent interacts with a finite-horizon MDP M=(S,A,H,{p_h},{r_h}) for K rollouts of length H each, restarting from the same initial state between rollouts but retaining memory, and is scored by the **expected best return** across the K rollouts, E_π[max_{i∈[K]} Σ_h r_h(s_{h,i}, a_{h,i})]. This is the natural extension of the pass@k / best-of-N metrics used to evaluate LLM code-generation and theorem-proving models.

The methodology is purely theoretical and organized in two parts:

**1. Planning (Sections 3.1–3.3):**
- *Policy-space analysis*: A simple two-action MDP (Figure 1: safe action a_s vs. risky action a_r) is used to constructively show that no Markovian policy — even one allowed to depend on the rollout index — can match a history-dependent policy that adapts its action to whether the previous rollout succeeded. The gap is shown to be multiplicative (Proposition 1).
- *History compression*: The authors prove by construction (Proposition 2) that the optimal value function is constant over all histories sharing the same Φ = (state, M_i, G_{h,i}), and the class Π_C of policies depending only on Φ is optimal. Proposition 3 — proven by a similar safe/risky construction with a randomized initial reward — shows both M and G are individually necessary.
- *Approximate planning*: A uniform κ-grid R_κ over [0,1] is used to project rewards; backward induction on the discretized MDP M_κ yields policy μ̃*_κ with value error ≤ 2κH and runtime O(KS²AH³/κ²).
- *Hardness*: Theorem 2 reduces Subset-Sum to the value-comparison problem, showing exact planning is NP-hard, with an exponentially-small η gap that matches the FPTAS.

**2. Learning (Sections 4.1–4.3) — PAC framework with generative model:**
- *Lower bound* (Theorem 3): A two-action single-state MDP (Figure 2: both actions yield reward 1 with probability p) is used to force the algorithm to identify a Δ-better action across K rollouts. Change-of-measure and Le Cam-style arguments give Ω(KH³SAε⁻²·log(1/δ)).
- *Algorithm*: Uniform sampling (m samples per (s,a) pair, m=eO(S·K²H²·log(1/δ) + KH³·log(1/δ)·ε⁻²)) + the discretization of Section 3 + backward induction on the empirical discretized MDP.
- *Upper bound* (Theorem 4): A refined simulation-lemma-style analysis that exploits the structure of max@k (rather than naively inflating the horizon to KH) yields the matching eO(KSAH³ε⁻²·log(1/δ)) rate.

## Results & Highlights

The paper reports no empirical/experimental results; all claims are theorems with proofs. The key quantitative theoretical results are:

- **Proposition 1 (Markovian gap)**: For any K ≥ 2, there exists an instance M and state s where `max_{π∈Π_hist} V^π_{1,1}(s) ≥ (1.24 − O(1/K)) · max_{π∈Π_M} V^π_{1,1}(s)`. The constant **1.24** lower-bounds the multiplicative price of restricting to Markovian policies, where the Markovian baseline is allowed to depend on the rollout index.
- **Theorem 1 (Approximation error and runtime)**: Setting discretization κ = ε/H, an ε-optimal policy for the true MDP can be computed in **O(H⁵S²Aε⁻²)** time with value error bounded by **|V* − V^{μ̃*}_κ| ≤ 2κH = 2ε**.
- **Theorem 2 (NP-hardness of exact planning)**: Deciding whether a deterministic action a attains value ≥ V^{π'} − η is NP-hard, even for **K = 2**, with η exponentially small in the input size — establishing that the FPTAS is essentially tight.
- **Theorem 3 (Sample-complexity lower bound)**: Any (ε,δ)-correct algorithm for time-homogeneous MDPs needs **E[τ_δ] ≥ Ω(K·SAH³/ε² · log(1/δ))** samples from a generative model — i.e., a multiplicative factor **K** worse than the classical Ω(SAH³/ε²·log(1/δ)) lower bound of Gheshlaghi Azar et al. (2013), Sidford et al. (2019), which is recovered as a special case at K=1.
- **Theorem 4 (Matching upper bound)**: The proposed uniform-sampling + discretization + backward-induction algorithm is (ε,δ)-correct with sample complexity **eO(K·SAH³/ε²·log(1/δ))** for time-homogeneous MDPs and **eO(K·SAH⁴/ε²·log(1/δ))** for time-inhomogeneous MDPs, matching the lower bound up to log factors in the high-precision (small ε) regime where the KSAH³ε⁻² term dominates.

No quantitative results reported on standard RL benchmarks; the "evidence" is the theorem–proof chain above and the constructive MDP examples in Figures 1–3 used to drive the lower bounds.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty — high.** This is the first paper, to my knowledge, to give a complete finite-horizon RL theory for the max@k objective: it simultaneously resolves the policy-space question (Markovian vs. history-dependent), the compression question, the computational-complexity question (FPTAS + NP-hardness), and the statistical-complexity question (matching PAC bounds). The connection to recent best-of-N LLM training is well motivated and not cosmetic.
- **Rigor — high.** All four headline theorems are stated with explicit constants (the 1.24 multiplicative gap, the 2κH discretization error, the KSAH³ε⁻²·log(1/δ) rates) and proved in full in the appendix, with the K=1 special case recovering the canonical H³SAε⁻² rate of Gheshlaghi Azar et al. (2013). The proof technique that avoids inflating the horizon to KH (which would have produced a suboptimal KH·SA(KH)²ε⁻² rate) is a genuine technical contribution.
- **Reproducibility — limited by design.** There are no algorithms to run on real LLMs, no datasets, no hyperparameters; the FPTAS and the uniform-sampling algorithm are simple enough to re-implement, but the paper's value is the theorem statements, which are reproducible only by re-doing the proofs.
- **Significance — medium-high.** The K× sample-complexity penalty and the non-Markovian / compression result are concrete, quotable facts that practitioners and RLHF/RL theory papers should cite. The paper's reach is, however, bounded by its narrow generative-model + tabular setting.
- **Red flags / overclaiming — minor.** (i) The framework is restricted to a **generative model**, which the authors themselves flag as an "important direction for future work" (forward / online interaction is not covered); the K× sample-complexity penalty may not transfer cleanly to online RL. (ii) All constructions are **tabular finite-horizon MDPs**; whether the K-factor gap persists in function-approximation / continuous-state settings relevant to actual LLM RLHF is open. (iii) The 1.24 multiplicative gap is **instantiated on a specific 2-action MDP** (Figure 1) and the bound's tightness in general is not proven — a tighter constant might exist. (iv) There is **no comparison to the existing inference-aware gradient estimators** (Chow et al. 2025, Tang et al. 2025, Bagirov et al. 2025) at either a theoretical or empirical level, so it is hard to tell whether the theory's prescriptions (use history-dependent policies over M and G) actually align with or contradict what those estimators already do. (v) The paper claims a connection to "self-refine / tree-of-thoughts / multi-attempt verifier" practice, but this is an *analogy* with no experiment tying the compression result to a concrete LLM system — readers should treat it as motivation, not validation.

## Strengths

- Clean, self-contained theory with matching upper and lower bounds; the lower-bound construction (Figure 2: two near-identical Bernoulli arms with p ≈ 1/K and Δ ≈ ε/K) is intuitive and pedagogically useful.
- The history-compression result (Π_C, depending only on M_i and G_{h,i}) is the single most actionable takeaway: it says that any practically deployable history-dependent policy only needs two extra scalars per step, which is implementable in modern RLHF/RLVR stacks.
- Honest about scope: the generative-model assumption is explicitly justified and the extension to online RL is named as future work.
- Connects the theory to a real and currently-hot problem (best-of-N evaluation of reasoning models) without overstating the link.

## Limitations

- **No empirical validation** whatsoever; readers interested in whether these results matter for actual LLM training (PPO, GRPO, pass@k optimization) will find zero evidence in the paper itself.
- **Tabular + generative-model + finite-horizon setting**; the K× sample-complexity result is silent on the function-approximation regime that governs real LLM post-training.
- The **1.24 multiplicative gap** constant is proven on one construction and may not be the best possible.
- The paper does **not compare against or subsume** the existing inference-aware / max@k gradient estimators it cites (Chow et al. 2025, Tang et al. 2025, Walder and Karkhanis 2026, Takashiro et al. 2026, Bagirov et al. 2025); the relationship between the optimal history-dependent policy theory and those estimator-based practical methods is left open.
- The connection to adaptive LLM-reasoning systems (self-refine, ToT, recursive self-aggregation) is a **conceptual analogy**, not a formal reduction — it is one of the most prominent sentences in the intro but the body never returns to verify it quantitatively.

## Relevance

直接相关于 LLM 推理/RLHF 的核心评估准则（max@k / pass@k / best-of-N），为"训练-评估不匹配"问题提供了首个严格的 RL 理论框架；其"历史压缩为 (M_i, G_{h,i}) 两维"的结论可作为推理期自精炼（self-refine、tree-of-thoughts、multi-attempt verifier）系统设计的理论依据，对关注推理模型训练与推理时计算扩展的研究者具有指导价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the **K× sample-complexity penalty** persist in the **online / forward-interaction** RL setting (no generative model), and with **function approximation** (linear / neural value functions)? The paper explicitly leaves this open and it is the question that determines whether the result matters for real LLM post-training.
- Can the **1.24** multiplicative gap between Π_hist and Π_M be tightened to a larger constant, or characterized exactly as K → ∞?
- The paper recommends conditioning on (M_i, G_{h,i}); do existing **inference-aware gradient estimators** (Chow et al. 2025, Tang et al. 2025, Bagirov et al. 2025) implicitly already do this, or do they fall strictly short of the optimal Π_C policy? A direct comparison is missing.
- Is the exact-planning problem **PSPACE-complete** (the paper notes the Bellman equations likely yield a PSPACE upper bound but leaves completeness open)?
- Can the FPTAS be turned into a **practical algorithm** for LLM RLHF (e.g., a modified PPO/GRPO that maintains running estimates of M_i and G_{h,i} and conditions the policy on them), and does it beat standard max@k-aware gradient estimators on HumanEval / MATH / GSM8K?
- How do **risk-sensitive / distributional-RL** objectives (CVaR, quantile) interact with max@k? Both emphasize the tail of the return distribution but the paper treats them only as related work.
