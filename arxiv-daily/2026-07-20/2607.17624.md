> **一句话总结：** 作者用两阶段方法将 transformer 中的 GeLU 与 softmax 替换为在留出数据上学习的线性样条，借此量化各任务的"最优归纳偏置"——在算法性玩具任务上得到 2-3 倍加速与大幅泛化提升，但这些设计高度任务特异；在代码/语言建模上提升小却更易跨数据集迁移，说明标准 transformer 并非架构空间的局部最优。

> **TL;DR:** The authors replace transformer non-linearities (GeLU, softmax) with 1-D linear splines optimized on held-out data via a two-stage "two-loss + multi-model" procedure, then re-train from scratch (and cross-train on other datasets) to probe task-specific inductive biases. On algorithmic toy tasks (ADD, MANO, COPY, etc.) optimized architectures converge 2-3x faster, generalize OOD (e.g. on COPY length generalization with Alibi), and cut seed variance, but are extremely task-specific — a MANO-tuned architecture gives +22 absolute test-accuracy on MANO but hurts most other tasks. On TinyStories/ENWIK8/CodeSearchNet, gains are small but consistent (e.g. TinyStories val-acc 63.7% GeLU -> 64.4% Ours; tr. perplexity 1.58 -> 1.57) and transfer well across language/code, suggesting standard transformers are not a local optimum in architecture space but are much closer to optimal for language than for algorithmic reasoning.

## 核心贡献

- **提出一个"架构优化"方法作为分析工具**：把 transformer 的核心非线性（MLP 的 GeLU、注意力的 softmax）换成可学习的 1-D 线性样条，用留出数据通过"两损失 + 多模型"训练更新样条参数；随后冻结样条、从头重新训练权重，从而与标准架构公平对比。
- **算法性任务的显著改进**：在 MEMORIZE / PARENTHESES / ADDMOD / HAYSTACK / ADD / ADD-REVERSED / COPY / MANO 八个任务上，优化后的架构普遍给出 2-3 倍的收敛加速、更低的种子方差，并在长度泛化、小模型容量等场景上明显优于标准 transformer 与 Alibi。
- **任务兼容性的系统量化**：通过"为一个任务 D 优化、在另一任务 D' 上重新训练"的矩阵式实验，揭示算法性任务的归纳偏置彼此高度不兼容，而自然语言/代码之间的归纳偏置则较为通用——说明 transformer 对语言接近局部最优，但对算法推理远非如此。
- **概念性结论**：标准 transformer 在架构空间中"几乎从不是局部最优"，改进很容易但代价是丧失通用性；这与"单一架构可对所有任务最优"的假设相悖，呼应 no-free-lunch。
- **可复现性**：完整 Algorithm 1、超参表（Table 4：M=8 算法任务 / M=3 语言任务、样条 nc=122 点 in [-20,+20]、spacing 1/3 或 1/9）与代码 https://github.com/idiap/lm-afs 均公开。

## Key Contributions

- **A gradient-based architecture-optimization tool, not a NAS method**: non-linearities in both MLP (GeLU) and attention (softmax, reformulated as a kernel K(Q,K)=phi'(Q)^T phi'(K)) are parametrized as learnable 1-D linear splines and optimized with SGD on held-out data; once frozen, the architecture trains like any standard transformer.
- **A "two-loss + multi-model" trick to prevent co-adaptation**: stage I holds out a fraction alpha of the data (e.g. 20%) for the architecture loss only, and trains M models in parallel (M=8 algorithmic, M=3 language) sharing the same splines — this is what makes the frozen splines generalize to fresh seeds/datasets in stage II.
- **Dramatic gains on algorithmic tasks**: 2-3x faster convergence on ADD and MANO, elimination of seed-variance on underspecified tasks, and a meaningful (if partial) fix for length generalization on COPY when applied on top of Alibi.
- **The cross-task "compatibility matrix" experiment** (Figures 5 and 9): a clean way to quantify how task-specific inductive biases are, showing algorithmic tasks are nearly orthogonal while language/code tasks cluster.
- **Honest negative result for language modeling**: optimized MLPs (often wavelet-like, Figure 7 right) consistently beat GeLU but by tiny margins (a few tenths of perplexity / ~0.7 pp accuracy), and the authors explicitly note this is not practically useful — the value is theoretical: standard transformers are neither unique nor locally optimal even on language.

## Methodology

**Baseline.** A GPT-2-style decoder-only transformer (post-norm, learned absolute positional embeddings, GeLU MLPs, standard softmax attention, MLP width multiplier 4).

**Parametrized non-linearities.** Two replacements define the search space T̂:
- MLP: ϕ → a 1-D linear spline ϕ_{θ_MLP} with nc keypoints (nc=122 in [-20,+20], spacing 1/3 for language; spacing 1/9 for algorithmic tasks).
- Attention: standard softmax kernel exp(Q^T K / sqrt(d)) → kernel K(Q,K) = ϕ'_{θ_A}(Q)^T ϕ'_{θ_A}(K), where ϕ' is also a linear spline.

Splines are deliberately chosen because they can represent identity, step functions, sine waves, and sharp transitions without imposing smoothness/monotonicity priors (which would mask the sharp activations the method discovers, e.g. Figure 6).

**Stage I — architecture optimization (Algorithm 1).** Split D into D_arch (fraction alpha, e.g. 20%) and D_wts. Instantiate M untrained models sharing θ_A, θ_MLP. Each SGD step:
- Each model m updates its weights θ^m on its own D_m ⊂ D_wts using L_wts^m.
- All models jointly update (θ_A, θ_MLP) on D_arch using L_arch = Σ_m Σ_{s∈D0} L(T̂_{θ^m}(s), s).
For length-generalization experiments the held-out split is an OOD range of sequence lengths, forcing the architecture to encode length-generalization inductive bias.

**Stage II — fair comparison / cross-task evaluation.** Freeze (θ_A^*, θ_MLP^*) (treated as fixed hyperparameters), retrain weights from scratch on the full training split of D' with standard training. When D' = D this measures how far the baseline is from a local optimum; when D' ≠ D ("mix-and-match") it measures compatibility of inductive biases.

**Tasks.** Algorithmic: MEMORIZE, PARENTHESES, ADDMOD, HAYSTACK, ADD, ADD-REVERSED, COPY, MANO (Allen-Zhu 2025, scaled down). Language: TinyStories, Shakespeare (char & BPE), enwik8 (char & BPE), CodeSearchNet-Java, CodeSearchNet-Python. Each task's baseline has width/depth/LR/batch tuned to be strong (including Canon layers from Allen-Zhu 2025 where helpful).

## Results & Highlights

- **Algorithmic tasks converge 2-3x faster with optimized architectures** (Section 3.1, Figure 2). On ADD and MANO specifically, the authors report that "convergence occurs 2-3x faster" than a baseline transformer whose learning rate was already tuned to its maximum stable value.
- **Length generalization on COPY** (Figure 3): the standard transformer collapses to ~0% sequence-wise accuracy on unseen lengths >10; Alibi recovers partial accuracy on slightly longer sequences; Alibi + the full method (two-loss optimization of weights on lengths 2-10 and architecture on 2-15) extends non-trivial accuracy much further toward length 30, showing that inappropriate inductive biases — not just positional encoding — are an obstacle to length generalization.
- **TinyStories (Table 3, 2 layers, width 256)**: training perplexity drops from 1.78 (Linear MLP) / 1.58 (GeLU) to **1.57 (Ours)**, and validation accuracy rises from 59.9% / 63.7% to **64.4%** — beating GeLU, GLU/Swish (63.7%), ReLU, ReLU2, TanH, Sinc, Gaussian, P1, P3, Adaptive, and NormSoftmax variants, none of which surpass the learned spline.
- **GLU improvement**: inserting the learned spline into GLU in place of Swish (GLU/Ours) matches GLU/Swish on TinyStories (both 1.58 tr. perplexity), confirming the method composes with modern MLP designs.
- **Cross-task compatibility on algorithmic tasks (Figure 5)**: best per-task gains sit on the diagonal — e.g. a MANO-optimized architecture yields **+22 pp absolute test accuracy** on MANO after a fixed number of steps, but most off-diagonal combinations are negative (e.g. a Copy-optimized architecture hurts ADD by -11, a Haystack-optimized one hurts ADD by -8). The benefits are extremely task-specific.
- **Cross-task compatibility on language/code (Figure 9)**: differences are an order of magnitude smaller than for algorithmic tasks; the largest negative transfer is MANO -> enwik8-char at +358% relative perplexity, while all language->language transfers stay within single-digit percent — showing language and code share much more uniform inductive biases.
- **Code vs. natural language (Figure 8)**: optimized MLP non-linearities give larger *relative* gains on CSN-Java and CSN-Python than on TinyStories/Shakespeare/enwik8, consistent with code demanding more systematic/compositional structure (closer to the algorithmic regime).
- **GeLU is not even a local optimum (Figure 7/8, "GeLU + Ours")**: initializing the spline at GeLU and then optimizing lands in-between GeLU and from-scratch splines, implying the GeLU basin is not locally optimal either.
- **Fine details matter (Appendix D)**: symmetrizing / "cleaning" the learned wavelet-like splines always performs worse than the raw optimized version.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: Genuine. Unlike trainable-activation work (Alexandridis 2025; Apicella 2021) that continuously co-adapts activations with weights, this paper's contribution is the *two-loss + multi-model* procedure that yields a frozen, transferable architecture — turning activation learning into an instrument for studying inductive-bias compatibility, which is conceptually new.
- **Rigor**: High within its scope. The cross-task matrix (Figures 5, 9, 11) is a clean experimental design; the "clean the spline" ablation (Appendix D) and the "GeLU + Ours" initialization test directly support the claim that fine details matter and GeLU is not a local optimum. Hyperparameters are reported per task.
- **Reproducibility**: Strong. Public code, fixed splits ("pre-generate all data" to guarantee disjoint splits), M and spline ranges specified, 6-seed averaging for algorithmic tasks.
- **Significance**: Conceptually significant — it directly interrogates the popular hypothesis (Goldblum et al. 2023) that one architecture can be near-optimal for everything, and gives quantitative evidence against it. Practically limited (see below).
- **Red flags / honest weaknesses**:
  - **Scale is tiny** (the paper's own Limitations): 1-4 layer models, widths 32-512. The authors explicitly acknowledge that "the effects of different architectures may vanish with more data" (Bachmann 2023; Tay 2022), which somewhat undercuts the algorithmic-task framing — at LLM scale the 2-3x speedups may evaporate.
  - **Cherry-picked "strong" baselines on some tasks but weak on others**: the TinyStories comparison (Table 3) lumps ReLU/Sinc/Tanh/Gaussian alongside GeLU and GLU, several of which are known to be poor; the meaningful deltas vs GeLU are only ~0.01 perplexity and ~0.7 pp accuracy — within plausible hyperparameter-noise for 2-layer models.
  - **Softmax replacement mostly fails**: the authors concede replacing softmax "barely matches or underperforms the baseline" and that initializing at softmax makes the optimization stuck — so the headline "we replace softmax and GeLU" is in practice "we replace GeLU". The attention-spline contribution is largely a negative result reported as part of the method.
  - **The compatibility matrices (Figures 5, 9) report accuracy/perplexity deltas "after a fixed number of steps" chosen per task** (150-5000 steps), which is a sensible but non-standard protocol; it is not the same as final-accuracy comparison and could amplify or mute apparent specialization.
  - **The MANO -> enwik8-char +358% relative perplexity outlier** suggests the cross-task numbers are dominated by a few degenerate cells, and the "language/code are uniform" conclusion rests on excluding MANO (which is the bridge to algorithmic tasks).
  - **No comparison against modern LLM-scale activation variants** (e.g. SwiGLU at depth, ReGLU, squared ReLU as in prime-scale ablations) at sizes where they actually matter.

## Strengths

- Asks a clean, important question (are standard transformers locally optimal per-task?) and answers it with a falsifiable, quantitative method rather than rhetoric.
- The two-loss + multi-model stage I is a non-obvious trick that genuinely prevents co-adaptation and is the reason stage II works — a real methodological contribution.
- Intellectual honesty about negative results: softmax replacement, the practical uselessness of the small language-modeling gains, and the failure of "cleaning" splines are all reported clearly.
- Beautiful visualizations of learned activations (Figures 6, 7 right, 12) make the inductive-bias findings intuitive.
- The cross-task compatibility matrix is a reusable experimental paradigm other groups can apply to new architectures/tasks.

## Limitations

- **Scale**: experiments are at toy-LM scale (1-4 layers, widths ≤512, ≤5000 steps); whether the 2-3x algorithmic-task speedups and the language/code compatibility conclusions survive at billion-parameter scale is unaddressed. Prior work (Bachmann 2023; Tay 2022) suggests architecture choice matters less at scale, which would shrink the paper's headline effects.
- **Search space is narrow**: the spline-on-scalar formulation cannot represent GLU-style multiplicative interactions (Shazeer 2020), complex attention variants (tropical attention, Hashemi 2025), or multi-dimensional activations — yet the authors still use GLU as a baseline, which is a slight inconsistency.
- **Softmax replacement is essentially a failed direction**, so the method's effective scope is MLP activations.
- **Computational cost of stage I**: training M=8 parallel models (each with its own weights) plus the shared architecture is roughly 8x the baseline cost in stage I; the polynomial-approximation trick mitigates inference cost but not search cost.
- **Fixed-step evaluation protocol** for the compatibility matrices makes absolute numbers protocol-dependent.
- **Only English text and two programming languages**; no vision, speech, or multilingual evaluation despite the discussion section gesturing at those domains.
- **No multi-task joint optimization** — the paper explicitly flags this as future work, but it is the obvious next step to test whether the "specialization vs. universality" trade-off is fundamental.

## Relevance

对于关注 transformer 架构创新与归纳偏置的研究方向，这篇论文提供了一个非常实用的"任务-架构兼容性"测量框架与可学习样条替换 GeLU 的具体实现，可作为后续"为算法推理/长度泛化设计专门模块"工作的对比基线与思路来源；其结论（语言/代码之间归纳偏置相近，但算法推理与之严重不兼容）对在 LLM 中增强 reasoning、length generalization 的研究方向有直接参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does joint multi-task architecture optimization (training θ_A, θ_MLP on a mixture of algorithmic + language tasks simultaneously) yield a single architecture that captures both regimes, or is the no-free-lunch trade-off fundamental?
- Do the algorithmic-task speedups (2-3x) persist at 100M-1B parameter scale, or does over-parameterization wash out the inductive-bias gains as Bachmann 2023 / Tay 2022 would predict?
- Can the wavelet-like MLP non-linearities discovered here be hand-closed-formulated (e.g. as a Gabor/Sinc variant) and shown to match the learned spline — i.e. is there a compact replacement for GeLU that practitioners could adopt without the stage-I search cost?
- Why does softmax-as-spline optimization get stuck — is it a landscape issue (softmax already near a local optimum) or a parametrization pathology of the scalar-spline kernel?
- Would applying the same method to attention in vision (ViT) and speech (wav2vec) reveal the same "language/code cluster, algorithmic tasks are outliers" structure, or a different modality taxonomy?
- Can the two-loss + multi-model stage-I trick be reused as a general regularizer for other meta-learned architectural components (positional encodings, normalization choices, router MLPs in MoE)?
