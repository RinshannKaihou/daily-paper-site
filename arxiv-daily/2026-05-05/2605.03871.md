> **一句话总结：** EvoLM 提出一种无需外部监督的后训练框架，通过在单一语言模型中交替训练"判别性评分准则生成器"和"策略模型"，利用时间对比构建偏好信号，使 Qwen3-8B 生成的评分准则在 RewardBench-2 上超越 GPT-4.1 达 25.7%，策略在 OLMo3-Adapt 上以 69.3% 超越 SkyWork-RM 标量奖励模型 9.6 个百分点。

> **TL;DR:** EvoLM is a fully self-supervised post-training method that co-trains a rubric generator and a policy within a single LM (Qwen3-8B) via alternating GRPO updates. The rubric generator learns to produce instance-specific evaluation criteria optimized for discriminative utility — maximizing a frozen 1.7B judge's ability to distinguish preferred from dispreferred responses. All preference signals come from temporal contrast (pairing current vs. earlier checkpoint outputs). The trained rubric generator outperforms GPT-4.1 on RewardBench-2 by 25.7%, and the co-trained policy achieves 69.3% on the 12-benchmark OLMo3-Adapt suite — beating GPT-4.1-prompted rubrics (+2.6%), RaR/RRD/RLCER/Rubric-ARM (+1.7–2.6%), and critically, the SOTA SkyWork-RM-V2 scalar reward model by 9.6 points (despite SkyWork-RM having 86.4% RewardBench-2 accuracy). The learned rubrics also generalize OOD to health/research domains, outperforming GPT-4.1 rubrics on HealthBench (58.4% vs. 52.5%) and ResearchQA (59.3% vs. 51.0%).

## 核心贡献

- 提出首个完全无需外部监督（无人工标注、无专有模型 API、无标量奖励模型、无需可验证域）的后训练框架，仅依赖模型自身输出构建全部训练信号。
- 将评分准则（rubric）建模为隐变量，通过变分推断推导出原则性的训练目标——优化准则的判别效用（discriminative utility），即最大化冻结小型评判模型区分偏好/非偏好回复的能力。
- 设计了三种互补的偏好对构建方法：时间对比（temporal contrast）、推断问题（inferred question）和准则条件生成（rubric-conditioned），全部源自策略自身输出。
- 在单一模型内交替训练准则生成器和策略（参数共享），形成共同进化的反馈环路：策略越强→偏好对越难区分→准则必须越精细→奖励信号越清晰→策略进一步提升。
- 揭示了准则进化的核心机制：从抽象标签（如"准确性"）进化为嵌入具体预期值、可逐项验证的检查清单，将语义判断转化为小模型可可靠执行的模式匹配。

## Key Contributions

- First fully self-supervised post-training paradigm for rubric-based RL: no human annotations, no proprietary API calls, no scalar reward models, no verifiable-domain restrictions.
- Formalizes rubric generation as variational inference over latent rubric variables (Eq. 5), deriving a principled margin-based training objective that rewards rubrics widening the judge's score gap between preferred and dispreferred responses.
- Three complementary preference-pair construction methods from the policy's own outputs: temporal contrast (current vs. earlier checkpoint), inferred question (policy infers what question a good answer addresses), and rubric-conditioned generation (response with vs. without rubric guidance).
- Single-model co-evolution: the rubric generator and policy share parameters within Qwen3-8B, distinguished only by input prompts, halving memory vs. a two-model configuration with identical quality.
- Mechanistic insight into rubric evolution: trained rubrics shift from abstract labels (21.9% → 0.3% label-only criteria) to concrete, verifiable checks embedding expected values (+19.3% specificity), transforming holistic semantic judgment into pattern matching executable by a 1.7B frozen judge.

## Methodology

**Core framework.** EvoLM decomposes reward computation into two components with an explicit natural-language interface: a rubric generator ρ_φ produces evaluation criteria r for each question q, and a frozen judge J scores responses against those criteria.

**Variational objective.** Rubrics are treated as latent variables explaining observed preferences. The ELBO objective (Eq. 5) trains the rubric generator via policy gradient, with reward = margin between judge scores on preferred vs. dispreferred responses + format reward ensuring JSON schema compliance (Eq. 7, α=0.7 margin weight).

**Alternating training.** Both rubric generator and policy use GRPO. Training alternates in K=50 step phases: Phase 1 updates the policy with rubric-derived judge scores as reward; Phase 2 updates the rubric generator using preference pairs from the policy's outputs as discriminative reward. The judge (Qwen3-1.7B) stays frozen throughout.

**Preference pair construction.** Three methods used uniformly at random: (1) Temporal contrast — pair current responses with those from 20–100 steps earlier, creating a natural difficulty curriculum. (2) Inferred question — the policy infers what question a good answer addresses, then generates a bad answer for that inferred question. (3) Rubric-conditioned — generate with and without rubric guidance; if rubric-conditioned responses are better, the rubric contains actionable criteria.

**Training setup.** Qwen3-8B as shared rubric generator + policy; Qwen3-1.7B as frozen judge; 271K prompts from Tulu 3 preference mixture; 64 H100 GPUs with DeepSpeed ZeRO Stage 3; LR 1e-6, KL coeff 0.001, 8 rollouts per prompt, 500 policy update steps.

## Results & Highlights

- **Downstream policy (OLMo3-Adapt 12-benchmark avg):** EvoLM achieves 69.3%, outperforming GPT-4.1 prompted rubrics (66.7%), Qwen3-8B prompted rubrics (67.5%), RaR (67.5%), RRD (67.6%), RLCER (66.7%), Rubric-ARM (67.5%), and SkyWork-RM-V2 scalar RM (59.7%).
- **Scalar reward model paradox:** SkyWork-RM-V2 dominates RewardBench-2 (86.4%) and JudgeBench (80.8%) yet produces the worst policy (59.7%) — 9.6 points behind EvoLM — consistent with reward overoptimization.
- **Rubric quality (RewardBench-2):** EvoLM rubric generator achieves 46.0% avg, a 25.7% improvement over GPT-4.1 prompted rubrics on the Qwen3-1.7B inference judge (45.5% → 46.0% overall, with factuality jumping from 38.1% to 54.0%).
- **Code generation:** Largest gains on HumanEval+ (86.2% vs. 80.5% for next best), where fine-grained criteria provide particularly sharp signal.
- **OOD generalization (HealthBench):** EvoLM rubrics achieve 58.4% pairwise accuracy vs. GPT-4.1's 52.5% on health domain expert rubrics, despite never seeing health data during training.
- **OOD generalization (ResearchQA):** 59.3% vs. GPT-4.1's 51.0% on research-domain expert rubrics.
- **Cross-model transfer:** Frozen EvoLM rubrics improve Qwen3-4B (+0.8%) and Llama-3.1-8B (+1.2%) over GPT-4.1 rubrics, with largest gains on code (HE+ 77.9% vs. 70.1%) and reasoning.
- **Cross-judge transfer:** Trained rubrics improve judging accuracy across all tested inference judges (Qwen3-8B, OLMo-3-7B, Mistral-7B), not just the training judge.
- **Multi-judge extension:** Training with 5 diverse judges produces rubrics more universally interpretable, with BA variant achieving 57.6% RewardBench-2 and 66.1% JudgeBench on Qwen3-8B judge.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: High.** The variational formulation of rubric generation as latent variable inference is novel and principled. The co-evolving training loop with temporal contrast is creative. This is the first method to train a rubric generator end-to-end without any external supervision.
- **Rigor: Strong.** Extensive ablations across 7 design dimensions (reward shaping, alternation frequency, model configuration, preference signal source, judge size, step gap, cross-architecture transfer). Full per-benchmark breakdowns provided. Comparison against 7 baselines including proprietary (GPT-4.1) and SOTA open (SkyWork-RM-V2) methods. All methods trained for same 500 policy steps with same hardware allocation.
- **Reproducibility: High.** Standard architectures (Qwen3-8B, Qwen3-1.7B), public training data (Tulu 3 mixture), code and models released. Self-contained training pipeline with no external API dependencies.
- **Significance: Very high.** The core finding — that self-supervised rubric co-evolution outperforms SOTA scalar reward models by 9.6 points despite vastly lower RewardBench accuracy — challenges the prevailing paradigm of training ever-larger reward models. The reward overoptimization result is striking and well-demonstrated.
- **Red flags / concerns:**
  - **Limited to 8B scale.** All main experiments use Qwen3-8B. Cross-architecture results show degradation on Llama-3.1-8B (46.9% vs. 69.3% for Qwen3-8B), though this is partly a base model capability gap.
  - **Training cost.** 64 H100 GPUs for 500 steps is non-trivial. The per-step cost is higher than scalar RM due to rubric generation + judge scoring for each rollout.
  - **Format reward is critical.** Without the format reward, rubric validity drops from >85% to 23% (Table 7). This is a brittle dependency on JSON schema compliance.
  - **Frozen judge ceiling.** The frozen 1.7B judge bounds the complexity of criteria the generator can learn to produce. The 14B judge ablation achieves higher RB2 (67.6%) but doesn't improve policy much (69.0% vs. 69.3%), suggesting diminishing returns from judge scaling.
  - **Sequential training paradox.** Sequential training achieves higher RB2 accuracy (47.2% vs. 46.0%) but worse policy (68.3% vs. 69.3%), raising questions about whether RB2 is a reliable proxy for downstream utility. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't fully resolve it.

## Strengths

- Eliminates all external supervision dependencies while achieving SOTA results — a clean, principled self-improvement loop.
- The variational inference formulation provides a rigorous theoretical foundation, going beyond ad-hoc rubric engineering.
- Striking demonstration that reward overoptimization is a real problem: SkyWork-RM's 86.4% RB2 accuracy produces 59.7% policy vs. EvoLM's 69.3%.
- Excellent ablation coverage (7 dimensions, full per-benchmark breakdowns) showing robustness to most design choices.
- Mechanistic analysis of rubric evolution (abstract labels → concrete verifiable checks) is insightful and well-supported by aggregate statistics.
- Strong OOD generalization evidence across domains (health, research), model families (Qwen3, Llama-3.1), and judges.
- Practical single-model configuration halves memory with no quality loss.

## Limitations

- Validated only on general-purpose post-training data (Tulu 3); behavior on domain-specialized mixtures (medicine, law) is unexplored.
- The frozen judge design bounds rubric complexity — a deliberate but potentially limiting choice.
- Rubric enrichment is most clearly observed in tasks with verifiable intermediate steps; effect on purely subjective evaluation is less characterized.
- Only 500 policy update steps reported; longer training dynamics and convergence behavior are unknown.
- The 1.7B judge may not capture nuanced quality differences in open-ended generation, potentially limiting rubric quality ceiling.
- Cross-architecture results (Llama-3.1-8B) show meaningful degradation, suggesting some policy-specific adaptation.

## Relevance

该方法与语言模型自我改进、无监督对齐和后训练强化学习高度相关。其核心思想——利用模型自身的评估能力构建训练信号——为构建可扩展的 AI 对齐方案提供了新范式，特别值得关注的是其揭示的奖励过度优化问题和准则共同进化机制。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does EvoLM scale to 70B+ models? Does the rubric evolution mechanism remain effective when the base model already encodes much richer evaluative knowledge?
- Can the temporal contrast signal degrade if the policy plateaus or oscillates, producing non-monotonic quality across checkpoints?
- What is the compute-optimal allocation between rubric generator training steps and policy training steps?
- How does the method interact with chain-of-thought reasoning? The long response length (16K tokens) suggests CoT is used, but its interaction with rubric quality is unexplored.
- Can the variational framework be extended to multi-objective rubrics (e.g., simultaneously optimizing for helpfulness and safety)?
- What happens when the temporal contrast buffer is exhausted — does the method degrade gracefully or collapse?
