Learning Dynamics of Zeroth-Order Optimization: A Kernel Perspective

arXiv: 2605.03373

Date: 2026-05-05

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.03373

一句话总结： 本文从神经正切核（NTK）的视角推导了零阶SGD的学习动力学，证明ZO方法诱导的eNTK本质上是标准eNTK在随机低维子空间上的投影，利用Johnson-Lindenstrauss引理得出其近似误差取决于模型输出维度V而非参数维度d，从而解释了ZO方法在十亿参数LLM微调中的成功。
TL;DR: This ICML 2026 paper derives the one-step learning dynamics of ZO SGD and shows the ZO-induced empirical NTK is a random projection of the FO eNTK. Using the Johnson-Lindenstrauss Lemma, the authors prove the kernel approximation error scales as O(√(V log V / P)) where V is the output vocabulary size and P is the number of perturbations—independent of model dimension d. Empirically validated on OPT-125M/350M/1.3B on SST-2, showing P=50 suffices to recover FO dynamics across all scales, and Gaussian/Rademacher perturbations yield nearly identical convergence.

Methodology

The paper develops its analysis in three stages:

1. Learning Dynamics Formulation. The authors define learning dynamics as the change in log-probability ∆log π_t(y|x_o) after a single ZO-SGD update step. Through a first-order Taylor expansion of the ZO update rule (central finite differences with smoothing parameter µ), they derive:

∆log π_t(y|x_o) ≈ -η A_t(x_o) K_t(x_o, x_u; u_t) G_t(x_u, y_u)

where A_t is a model-confidence matrix, G_t is the last-layer gradient, and K_t(x_o, x_u; u_t) is the ZO eNTK — the key quantity governing learning behavior.

2. Multi-Perturbation Kernel Analysis. For P perturbations, the ZO eNTK takes the form K_t(·; U_{t,P}) = (∇_θ z)^T U_{t,P} U_{t,P}^T (∇_θ z), where U_{t,P} ∈ R^{d×P} is the projection matrix. Each element of the kernel discrepancy ∆K = K^FO - K^ZO corresponds to the difference between inner products in the original and projected spaces, directly connecting to the JL Lemma.

3. JL Lemma Application. By applying the inner-product version of the JL Lemma to the V² pairs of neural tangent vectors {∇_θ z_i(x_o), ∇_θ z_j(x_u)}, the sufficient projection dimension is P = O(ln(V²)/ε²), independent of d. The Frobenius norm bound follows via Jensen's inequality.

Empirical Validation. Experiments span: (a) LeNet (d=29,624) on MNIST with P ∈ {1, 5, 25, 125}, measuring Frobenius/CKA/spectral errors; (b) OPT-125M/350M/1.3B on SST-2 with P ∈ {1, 5, 10, 20, 50, 100}, comparing ZO vs FO training trajectories; (c) Synthetic MLP experiments scaling V from 2 to 1000 at fixed P=50.

Results & Highlights

  • Dimension-free convergence: On OPT models, the perturbation count P=50 suffices to closely match FO trajectories across 125M, 350M, and 1.3B parameters — confirming that fidelity depends on V, not d
  • Distribution parity: Gaussian and Rademacher perturbations yield nearly indistinguishable convergence curves across all three metrics (Frobenius, CKA, spectral), supporting the JL-based prediction of comparable concentration constants
  • Semantic similarity effect: On MNIST, input pairs with high similarity (e.g., digits 4 and 9) show rapid kernel convergence, while dissimilar pairs (e.g., 0 and 1) require more perturbations — the ZO gradient estimator is more efficient for semantically similar inputs
  • V-scaling validation: Synthetic experiments confirm approximation error scales as √V: increasing V from 2 to 1000 increases relative Frobenius error from 0.28 to 3.69, consistent with theory
  • Comparison with optimization view: The classical optimization perspective yields convergence O(√(dL/PT)) with dimension-dependent variance, while the eNTK perspective gives O(√(V log V / P)) — both agree on the 1/√P scaling but critically differ on dimensionality dependence

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. The kernel-based perspective on ZO optimization is genuinely novel. The connection between ZO eNTK and JL Lemma is elegant and provides clean theoretical machinery. The key insight — that the JL projection dimension depends on the number of tangent vectors (V²), not the ambient dimension d — is non-obvious and impactful.
  • Rigor: Good with caveats. The JL Lemma application is correct, and the Frobenius norm bound derivation is sound. However, the analysis is strictly one-step (single update), and the paper acknowledges that extending to multi-step convergence requires additional assumptions. The smoothing error O(µη) is swept aside. The bound involves ∥∇_θ z∥_F (denoted Ξ_t), which could itself scale with d in certain architectures — the paper does not fully address this.
  • Reproducibility: Good. Experimental setups are well-described (LeNet/MNIST, OPT/SST-2, synthetic MLP). The theoretical results are self-contained with proofs in appendices.
  • Significance: High. This paper addresses a central paradox in ZO optimization and provides a clean, testable prediction: ZO fidelity scales with V, not d. The empirical validation across three OPT model sizes is compelling. The framework also makes a practical prediction — P=50 is sufficient for typical LLM fine-tuning — that practitioners can directly use.
  • Red flags / weaknesses:
  1. Lazy training assumption: The analysis assumes the eNTK varies slowly, which holds for fine-tuning near initialization but breaks down for training from scratch or large learning rates. The authors acknowledge this but it significantly limits scope.
  2. No multi-step guarantee: The one-step analysis shows trajectory alignment at each step but does not prove that compounding errors over many steps remain bounded. This is a significant gap.
  3. Ξ_t dependence: The bound includes ∥∇_θ z(x)∥_F which could implicitly depend on d. The dimension-free claim rests on this term being "bounded," which may not hold universally.
  4. Limited task diversity: All LLM experiments use SST-2 binary sentiment classification with prompt-based tuning. No evaluation on generation tasks, multi-class classification, or chain-of-thought reasoning.
  5. Incomplete comparison with low-rank assumption: The paper positions itself as an alternative to the low-effective-rank Hessian assumption but does not empirically compare which explanation better predicts actual ZO performance.

Strengths

  • Elegant theoretical framework that cleanly resolves the dimensionality paradox via JL Lemma
  • Strong empirical validation across model scales (125M → 1.3B) with consistent results
  • The dimension-free prediction is specific, testable, and practically actionable
  • Distribution robustness result (Gaussian ≈ Rademacher) has immediate practical implications
  • Honest and thorough discussion of limitations (Section 4), including computational trade-offs and scope of applicability
  • The semantic similarity finding (convergence rate depends on input pair similarity) is a nuanced and interesting observation

Limitations

  • One-step analysis only: Multi-step compounding effects are not formally bounded; the paper leaves this as future work
  • Fine-tuning only: The lazy training / slow eNTK assumption explicitly excludes pre-training scenarios and large learning rate regimes
  • No autoregressive tasks: The framework assumes fixed output dimension V; for generation tasks, the effective output dimension grows with sequence length, potentially requiring much larger P
  • Computational cost of multiple perturbations: Each additional perturbation requires a separate forward pass. Parallelizing P perturbations multiplies memory by P, which is prohibitive for already memory-constrained LLM fine-tuning
  • Ξ_t not thoroughly analyzed: The Frobenius norm of the Jacobian ∥∇_θ z(x)∥_F could itself carry implicit dimension dependence that is not fully characterized

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Can the one-step kernel alignment result be extended to a multi-step convergence guarantee with bounded compounding error?
  • How does the framework extend to tasks with large sequential output (e.g., chain-of-thought reasoning), where the effective V grows with sequence length?
  • Does the Ξ_t = ∥∇_θ z(x)∥_F term scale with d in practice, and if so, does it weaken the dimension-free claim for certain architectures?
  • Can the kernel perspective inform adaptive perturbation strategies that allocate more perturbations to "hard" (low-similarity) input pairs?
  • How does this analysis interact with parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods (LoRA, adapters) where the effective parameter dimension is already reduced?
