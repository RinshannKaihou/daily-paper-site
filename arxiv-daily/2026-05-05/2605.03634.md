> **一句话总结：** 本文提出基于代数谱曲线的自由解压缩方法（FDPM），将 Stieltjes 变换表示为二元多项式的根，从而可外推多峰、多尺度、含原子的真实 ML 谱密度，在神经网络 Hessian 和扩散模型谱分析上实现低误差、高效率的预测。

> **TL;DR:** The authors introduce FDPM (Free Decompression via the Polynomial Method), which represents the Stieltjes transform of a spectral density as a root of a bivariate polynomial and recasts free decompression as evolution along algebraic spectral curves. Compared to direct eigendecomposition on a 64K×64K diffusion-model activation matrix, FDPM reduces compute time from 192,447.7 s to 32.2+6.8 s while keeping the W1/L distributional distance at 2.19% and moment relative error below 0.09%, surpassing prior FD methods that assumed a single smooth connected bulk.

## 核心贡献

- 提出 FDPM（基于多项式方法的自由解压缩）框架，将 Stieltjes 变换的代数关系推广到任意度数的二元多项式，突破先前 FD 仅处理二次（单 bulk、平滑、连通）假设的限制。
- 将自由解压缩重写为沿代数谱曲线的演化问题，通过几何预测-校正（predictor–corrector）方法追踪物理分支，避免先前 PDE 方法中难以处理的解析延拓问题。
- 推导了原子质量、谱矩、谱边、bulk 分裂/合并点的演化方程，可在不计算完整谱密度的情况下直接追踪关键谱量。
- 给出多项式拟合的数值方法（Bernstein 椭圆采样 + 矩约束 + 分支点约束），并实现于开源软件包 `freealg`。
- 在复合自由 Poisson 分布、神经网络 Hessian（CIFAR-10 两层自编码器）和大规模扩散模型激活矩阵三类 ML 模型上验证。

## Key Contributions

- Introduces FDPM, a general FD methodology based on the polynomial method, where the Stieltjes transform satisfies an algebraic relation P(z, m)=0 of arbitrary degree—generalizing prior FD work restricted to quadratic Stieltjes transforms and single connected bulks.
- Recasts free decompression as evolution along algebraic spectral curves, selecting the physical branch via geometric continuation (predictor–corrector with Newton projection), which circumvents the manual analytic continuation that plagued the PDE-based approach of Ameli et al. [1].
- Derives closed-form evolution equations for atomic weights (Proposition 2: w(τ)=1−τ⁻¹(1−w₀)), spectral edges, bulk splitting/merging cusp points, and moments (an O(n²) recurrence), enabling direct tracking of spectral quantities without full density recovery.
- Provides the first robust implementation of the polynomial method for matrix computations, including polynomial fitting via logarithmic-scaling Bernstein-ellipse sampling with moment and branch-point constraints, released in the open-source package `freealg`.
- Demonstrates efficacy on three ML-relevant examples: a compound free Poisson law with atoms and splitting bulks, the Pennington–Bahri neural-network Hessian model (CIFAR-10 two-layer autoencoder), and a random-Fourier-features diffusion model activation matrix.

## Methodology

The paper builds on random matrix theory (RMT) and free probability. The central ansatz (Assumption 1) is that the Stieltjes transform m(z) of the limiting spectral distribution is algebraic: there exists a nonzero bivariate polynomial P: C×C→C with real coefficients such that P(z, m(z))=0 for z away from a finite singularity set. The degrees in z and m are denoted d_z and s.

**Fitting the polynomial.** The coefficients c_ij of P(z,m)=Σ c_ij z^i m^j are recovered by sampling the empirical Stieltjes transform m̂(z) at points on logarithmic-scaling high-radius Bernstein ellipses around individual bulks, supplemented by constraints from empirical moments (Lemma C.1: a necessary condition is that the leading diagonal coefficient vanishes) and branch points.

**Physical branch selection.** Because P(z,·) has s roots, identifying the physical Stieltjes value requires geometric continuation on the spectral curve: starting from an anchor point (normalization at infinity or comparison with the empirical Stieltjes transform), the solution is continued along a path via implicit differentiation dm/dz = −(∂_z P)/(∂_m P), with Newton projection and step subdivision for stiff geometry.

**Free decompression (Theorem 1).** For decompression ratio τ≥1, the decompressed Stieltjes transform m_τ satisfies P(z+(τ−1)/(τ m_τ), τ m_τ)=0. FD is thus a rational reparameterization of the spectral curve. The physical branch is transported by solving the coupled system F₁=P(ζ,y)=0, F₂=ζ−(τ−1)y⁻¹−z=0 via a predictor–corrector ODE on the curve.

**Spectral quantity evolution.** Atom weights evolve as w(τ)=1−τ⁻¹(1−w₀) (Proposition 2); spectral edges are branch points satisfying P(ζ,y)=0 and y²∂_yP−(τ−1)∂_ζP=0; bulk splitting/merging cusps are second-order branch points; moments evolve as μ_n^(τ)=Σ κ_{n,k} τ^k via an O(n²) recurrence (Proposition G.7).

**Experimental setup.** Three examples: (1) Compound free Poisson with ν(dx)=¾δ₂+¼δ_{5.5}, aspect ratio λ=0.1, a 6000×6000 target matrix with a 1000×1000 submatrix; (2) Two-layer autoencoder Hessian on CIFAR-10 at initialization, full 32,768×32,768 matrix sampled with 4000×4000 submatrices; (3) Random-Fourier-features diffusion model (George et al. [16]; Bonnaire et al. [10]) activation matrix of size 64,000×64,000 sampled with a 4000×4000 submatrix. A finite-size correction (Appendix H) remedies near-singular left edges.

## Results & Highlights

- **Compute speedup on diffusion model:** Direct eigendecomposition of the 64K×64K activation matrix takes 192,447.7 s, while FDPM takes 32.2 s (initial 22K submatrix eigendecomposition) + 6.8 s (FD evolution) = 39.0 s total—a reduction of roughly 4,900× (Table 1, n=26K row).
- **Distributional accuracy at scale:** At n=26K, the W1/L (Wasserstein-1 on log-eigenvalues normalized by log-width) between FD prediction and empirical spectrum is 2.19%, and the MMD (Gaussian-kernel maximum mean discrepancy on log-eigenvalues) is 0.53% (Table 1).
- **Moment accuracy:** Relative error of the first moment Δμ₁/μ₁ is 0.08% and relative error of the standard deviation Δσ/σ is 0.09% at n=26K (Table 1).
- **Edge accuracy:** Mean log-edge error is 0.4% and max log-edge error is 1.2% at n=26K, excluding the unstable left edge of the spike-like leftmost bulk (Table 1).
- **Scaling of FD evolution time:** FD evolution time grows from 2.3 s (n=23K) to 6.8 s (n=26K), while direct eigendecomposition grows from 431.4 s to 192,447.7 s across the same range—FDPM's cost is dominated by the one-time submatrix eigendecomposition and scales sublinearly (Table 1).
- **Multi-bulk and atom handling:** On the compound free Poisson example, FDPM correctly recovers a 32,000×32,000 ESD from a 4000×4000 submatrix, capturing the bulk-splitting at the cusp point (x*, τ*) and atomic mass transfer that prior FD methods could not represent (Figures 1, 3).
- **Neural network Hessian:** On the 32,768×32,768 CIFAR-10 autoencoder Hessian, FDPM recovers the multi-modal ESD including the Pennington–Bahri free-probabilistic approximation, and is used to track the Hessian subsample index (Figure 4, Appendix I.4).
- **Diffusion model multi-scale spectrum:** On the 64K diffusion activation matrix, FDPM recovers a spectrum spanning ~10 decades (10⁻⁷ to 10³) with a narrow atom-like leftmost bulk standing ten billion times higher than the right bulk, with the finite-size correction capturing the leftmost edge where macroscopic FD flow fails (Figures 5, 6).

## Critical Assessment

- **Heavy-tailed spectra remain out of reach:** The authors explicitly state in Section 5 (Limitations) that "heavy-tailed spectral distributions are still elusive with the present tools"—a significant gap given that heavy-tailed spectra are common in trained deep networks (Martin & Mahoney 2021, cited as [25]) and are precisely the regime where spectral analysis is most informative for generalization.
- **Algebraic ansatz may not hold exactly for empirical spectra:** The method depends on Assumption 1 (an algebraic Stieltjes transform). While the authors argue this is "more likely to hold in practice" than prior assumptions, they do not provide a diagnostic or error bound for cases where the empirical spectrum only approximately satisfies an algebraic relation; the quality of the polynomial fit is validated only visually (histogram overlays) rather than by a formal goodness-of-fit test.
- **Finite-size edge correction is incomplete:** The leftmost edge of the spike-like bulk in the diffusion example is not captured by the macroscopic FD flow and requires an ad hoc finite-size correction (Appendix H); the authors acknowledge "further refinement may be needed for highly accurate edge estimation in extreme cases."
- **Branch selection robustness under noise:** The geometric continuation procedure assumes the physical branch can be tracked reliably, but the paper does not systematically study failure modes when roots come close or when input eigenvalue estimates are noisy (finite-sample effects are only noted for the leftmost edge).
- **No comparison to alternative spectral extrapolation methods:** FDPM is compared only against direct eigendecomposition (cost) and prior FD (capability); there is no comparison against kernel density estimation, moment-based extrapolation, or other RMT-based edge estimators on the same matrices.

## Strengths

- Removes the single-smooth-bulk restriction of prior FD, enabling application to realistic multi-modal, multi-scale, and atomic ML spectra.
- Strong mathematical grounding: closed-form evolution equations for atoms, edges, cusps, and moments; the algebraic ansatz is shown to cover all canonical RMT ensembles and the Pennington–Bahri Hessian/Gram models.
- Dramatic practical speedup (~4,900× over direct eigendecomposition at 26K) with sub-percent distributional and moment errors.
- Open-source implementation (`freealg`) and a detailed reproducibility guide (Appendix J).
- Geometric continuation elegantly resolves the analytic-continuation problem that blocked the prior PDE-based FD approach.

## Limitations

- Heavy-tailed spectral distributions are not handled (stated by the authors).
- The algebraic ansatz, while general, is an assumption whose violation is not diagnosed or bounded.
- Computational cost for very high-degree polynomials (large d_z or s) is not thoroughly characterized; fitting complexity and branch-selection cost in adversarial cases are underexplored.
- The finite-size edge correction is a patch rather than a principled treatment of extreme-value effects at spectral edges.
- Evaluation is primarily qualitative (visual histogram/ridge-plot matching) plus the single Table 1; broader quantitative benchmarking across more architectures and matrix types would strengthen the claims.

## Relevance

本文将随机矩阵理论与自由概率工具应用于神经网络 Hessian 和大规模扩散模型的谱分析，并提供从子矩阵外推大矩阵谱的实用工具，直接对应「ML Reliability & Mathematical Foundations」方向，并为理解模型缩放行为与条件数/谱边等可靠性指标提供数学基础。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How robust is the algebraic ansatz (Assumption 1) when the empirical spectrum is perturbed by noise or finite-sample effects—can a quantitative diagnostic for ansatz violation be derived?
- Can FDPM be extended to heavy-tailed (e.g., free Lévy) spectra, and what is the obstruction—polynomial degree, branch structure, or fitting instability?
- How do the distributional distances (W1/L, MMD) scale with the decompression ratio τ beyond the 26K→range tested, and is there a phase transition in accuracy?
- Could the moment-evolution recurrence (Proposition G.7) be used standalone for cheap generalization-bound or scaling-law estimation without full density recovery?
- How does FDPM compare against kernel density estimation or moment-based edge extrapolation on the same matrices in terms of accuracy per unit compute?
- Can the spectral-edge and cusp-tracking equations be integrated into a training-time monitor for detecting bulk splitting/merging events indicative of training-dynamics regime changes (cf. Bonnaire et al. [10])?
