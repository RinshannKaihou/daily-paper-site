Two Calls, Two Moments, and the Vote-Accuracy Curve of Repeated LLM Inference

arXiv: 2605.03379

Date: 2026-05-05

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.03379

一句话总结： 本文从理论角度证明，仅需两次带标签的LLM调用即可通过矩方法对任意投票预算下的多数投票精度给出无分布的精确上下界，揭示了单次精度不是投票增益的可靠指标——同例正确率相关性（ρ）才是决定性因素。
TL;DR: This paper proves that two labeled LLM calls per example suffice to compute sharp, distribution-free confidence intervals for majority-vote accuracy at any budget. The key insight: one-call accuracy does not determine voting gains; the same-example correctness correlation ρ (identified from just two calls) does. Three-vote intervals have closed form with width ≤1/8, and lower-accuracy policies with lower ρ can overtake higher-accuracy baselines after voting.

核心贡献

  • 建立了重复LLM推断的二值正确性模型：每个样本有潜在成功概率q，重复调用在条件独立下服从Bernoulli(q)。
  • 证明两次带标签调用即可识别一阶矩μ和二阶矩ν（等价于同例正确率相关性ρ），从而完整刻画两次调用可观测的配对表。
  • 核心技术定理：对任意有限多数投票预算，二阶矩模糊类的极值由至多三个支撑点的分布达到，二次对偶给出精确证书——不是离散化近似或参数近似。
  • 三票情形有闭式解：区间宽度至多1/8，并给出了认证改进的充分条件（当μ>1/2时，ρ<1−1/(2μ)保证三票必优于单票）。
  • 分析了无穷投票端点的阈值敏感性，以及MaxEnt和LDGP两种参数补全方案。
  • 实验验证：在18个策略-数据集对上，经验三票和五票精度全部落在投影的两调用区域内；展示了低温高精度策略投票增益几乎为零，而高温低精度策略可通过投票反超的现象。

Key Contributions

  • Formalizes the binary correctness layer of repeated LLM inference as a latent distribution over per-example success probabilities Q, with conditionally i.i.d. calls.
  • Proves that two labeled calls identify the pair table (μ, ν) and the same-example correctness correlation ρ = (ν − μ²)/(μ(1−μ)), which separates stable errors from recoverable call-level randomness.
  • Main technical result (Theorem 4.1): for every fixed majority-vote budget, sharp endpoints over the two-moment ambiguity class are achieved by three-atom distributions and certified by quadratic dual envelopes — this is exact, not a discretization.
  • Closed-form three-vote certification (Theorem 4.2): L₁ and U₁ have explicit formulas, interval width ≤ 1/8, and a transparent sufficient condition for guaranteed improvement when μ > 1/2.
  • Characterizes the infinite-vote endpoint as threshold-sensitive (depends on latent mass near q = 1/2), with sharp bounds from the same two moments (Theorem 4.3).
  • Provides MaxEnt and Latent-Difficulty Gaussian-Probit (LDGP) parametric completions for when a single curve (rather than an interval) is desired.
  • Empirical validation on GLUE QNLI/QQP with 3 model families × 3 temperatures shows all empirical V₁ and V₂ fall within projected 95% two-call regions, and demonstrates overtaking: lower one-call accuracy policies can exceed higher baselines after voting due to lower ρ.

Methodology

The paper develops a moment-geometry framework for the vote-accuracy curve Vₙ(π) = E[Pₙ(Q)] under majority voting with 2n+1 calls. The key methodological steps are:

  1. Latent correctness model: Each example has latent success probability Q ∼ π on [0,1]; repeated calls are conditionally i.i.d. Bernoulli(Q). The vote-accuracy curve Vₙ is the population expectation of the binomial tail polynomial Pₙ(Q).
  1. Two-call identification: One labeled call gives μ = E[Q]; two labeled calls give ν = E[Q²] and hence ρ. The pair table (both-correct, both-wrong, disagree) is fully identified.
  1. Three-support reduction: The infinite-dimensional optimization over all laws in M(μ,ν) reduces to at most three support points via extreme-point theory of moment sets. Quadratic majorants/minorants of Pₙ provide dual certificates.
  1. Finite-sample intervals: Combines structural two-call uncertainty with sampling uncertainty via a Wald moment region and projection through the sharp interval maps.
  1. Parametric completions: MaxEnt gives the least-committal law (quadratic-exponential density); LDGP uses a probit item-response model with two parameters exactly identified by the two moments.

Results & Highlights

  • Sharp intervals from just two calls: The three-vote interval [L₁, U₁] has maximum width 1/8, showing low-vote planning can be remarkably tight.
  • One-call accuracy is not ordered for voting: Qwen2.5-7B had highest one-call accuracy (~84% on QNLI) but negligible five-vote gains (≤0.17 pp) due to high ρ (~90–98%). Llama 3.1-8B at T=2.0 had lower one-call accuracy but +2.47 pp five-vote gain due to low ρ (~42%).
  • Overtaking examples documented: Lower one-call accuracy policies overtook higher baselines after voting, e.g., a randomized mixture with μ=71.73% outperformed a single-policy baseline with μ=80.73% by +4.56 pp after five votes.
  • All 18 empirical V₁, V₂ within 95% projected regions: Validating the theory's calibration.
  • Interval midpoint is the best point predictor: MAE of 0.36 pp for V₁ and 0.35 pp for V₂, outperforming LDGP (0.63/0.78 pp) and MaxEnt (1.04/1.22 pp), both of which are optimistically biased.

Authors & Credibility

Single author (Yi Liu). The paper is mathematically rigorous, with complete proofs in appendices (A.1–A.8). The theoretical framework draws on established moment geometry (Winkler extreme-point theory, Karlin–Studden Tchebycheff systems) and connects to classical item-response theory. The experimental design is clean and well-controlled: fixed prompt, fixed parser, independent decoding seeds, identical example sets across conditions. The GLUE benchmark choice is reasonable for binary classification. Code/data reconstruction details are provided in Appendix B. The paper sits at the intersection of test-time compute scaling and statistical theory, a timely combination.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. The application of sharp moment bounds to repeated LLM inference is novel and the three-support reduction is elegant. The insight that two calls suffice for distribution-free certification is surprising and practically useful.
  • Rigor: Excellent. Proofs are complete and carefully structured. The distinction between structural uncertainty (moment ambiguity) and sampling uncertainty (finite N) is clearly maintained.
  • Reproducibility: Good. The experimental setup uses publicly available models (via Ollama), standard datasets (GLUE), and deterministic reconstruction procedures. However, no code repository is linked.
  • Significance: The theoretical contribution is strong and the practical implications for test-time compute allocation are clear. The framework provides principled guidance for when repeated sampling is worthwhile.
  • Red flags / concerns:
  • The conditional-i.i.d. assumption is a significant limitation. In practice, repeated calls from the same model on the same prompt may have correlated errors (e.g., shared failure modes in reasoning). The paper acknowledges this but does not address it empirically.
  • Only binary classification tasks (QNLI, QQP) are tested. The framework's applicability to free-form generation, code, or mathematical reasoning — where repeated sampling is most popular — is unclear.
  • The five-vote experimental ceiling limits validation to small budgets. The paper does not test whether the theory scales to the large budgets (k=50, 100+) used in practice.
  • Only 7B–8B scale models are tested. Whether the moment structure holds for frontier models is unknown.
  • The experiments use training-split data rather than held-out test data, which is unconventional but justified by the binary correctness protocol.

Strengths

  • Beautiful and rigorous theoretical contribution that connects classical moment geometry to a modern LLM problem.
  • The two-call insight is remarkably practical — no need for expensive multi-call validation to plan vote budgets.
  • Clean separation between distribution-free certificates and parametric completions, with honest reporting of each.
  • The overtaking phenomenon (lower one-call accuracy beating higher baselines after voting) is a powerful empirical demonstration.
  • Well-structured paper with clear notation and complete appendices.

Limitations

  • Conditional-i.i.d. assumption may not hold in practice for semantically correlated errors.
  • Only tested on binary NLI/paraphrase tasks with small models (7B–8B).
  • Five-call maximum limits empirical validation to small vote budgets.
  • No evaluation on generative tasks (math, code) where repeated sampling is most commonly used.
  • The certified-improvement boundary is conservative: no policy in the experiments crosses it, even though empirical gains are observed. This gap between certification and realization could limit practical adoption.
  • The infinite-vote endpoint analysis, while theoretically complete, is shown to be intrinsically unstable — limiting its practical utility.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does the conditional-i.i.d. assumption break down for chain-of-thought reasoning, where errors may be systematically correlated across calls?
  • Can the two-call framework be extended to non-binary settings (e.g., code generation with pass@k, or free-form generation with semantic clustering)?
  • Do the moment structures observed in 7B models generalize to frontier models (70B+, GPT-4 class)?
  • Could adaptive sampling policies (spend more calls on examples with ρ near the improvement boundary) be designed using the two-call certification?
  • Is there a practical protocol to estimate whether conditional independence holds, and what happens to the certificates when it does not?
