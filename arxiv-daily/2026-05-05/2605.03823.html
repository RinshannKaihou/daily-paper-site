Realizable Bayes-Consistency for General Metric Losses

arXiv: 2605.03823

Date: 2026-05-05

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.03823

⚠️ Abstract-only summary

一句话总结： 本文在可实现（realizable）设定下，给出了通用度量损失（metric loss）下存在分布无关贝叶斯一致学习规则的充分必要条件，通过引入广义的无穷非递减 (γ_k)-Littlestone 树刻画了假设类的可学习性。
TL;DR: Resolves the realizable case of an open problem by characterizing exactly when a hypothesis class admits a distribution-free Bayes-consistent learning rule under general (possibly unbounded) metric losses, extending classical results beyond 0-1 classification and real-valued regression via a novel infinite non-decreasing (γ_k)-Littlestone tree combinatorial obstruction.

  • 引入了无穷非递减 (γ_k)-Littlestone 树的概念，将 Bousquet et al. (2021) 的 Littlestone 树结构推广到度量损失场景，作为可学习性的组合障碍。
  • 证明了学习规则的风险在可实现分布下几乎必然收敛到最优类内风险（即零）。

Key Contributions

  • Extends the characterization of strong universal Bayes-consistency from 0-1 classification (Bousquet et al., 2021) and real-valued regression (Attias et al., 2024) to general metric losses in the realizable setting.
  • Resolves an open problem from Cohen et al. (2022): provides necessary and sufficient conditions on a hypothesis class ℋ for the existence of a distribution-free learning rule whose risk converges almost surely to zero under every realizable distribution.
  • Introduces the notion of an infinite non-decreasing (γ_k)-Littlestone tree where γ_k → ∞, generalizing the Littlestone tree structure to the metric loss setting.
  • Provides a sharp combinatorial characterization: the obstruction to learnability is precisely the existence of such a tree within the hypothesis class.

Methodology

The paper operates within the standard statistical learning theory framework: an instance space (𝒳, ρ), a label space (𝒴, ℓ) with a general (possibly unbounded) metric loss, and a hypothesis class ℋ ⊆ 𝒴^𝒳. The analysis focuses on the realizable setting, where the data-generating distribution admits a zero-risk hypothesis in ℋ.

The core methodological innovation is the extension of the Littlestone tree framework. Classical Littlestone trees (used for online classification and private learning) are generalized to accommodate metric losses by introducing a sequence of scales γ_k → ∞. The key insight is that the combinatorial complexity of the hypothesis class, as measured by these generalized trees, precisely determines whether universal Bayes-consistency is achievable.

The proof strategy likely follows the established pattern in learning theory: (1) show that if the combinatorial obstruction exists, no universally consistent rule is possible (necessity); (2) construct or identify a learning rule that achieves consistency when the obstruction is absent (sufficiency).

Results & Highlights

  • Sharp characterization: The paper provides a complete answer to when realizable Bayes-consistency is achievable — it is possible if and only if the hypothesis class does not contain an infinite non-decreasing (γ_k)-Littlestone tree.
  • Generality: The result applies to any metric loss, including unbounded losses, subsuming previous results for 0-1 loss and real-valued regression as special cases.
  • Almost sure convergence: The learning rule's risk converges almost surely (not just in probability) to the optimal risk.

  • Rigor: Expected to be very high given the authors' track records in mathematical learning theory. The result is a precise characterization (necessary AND sufficient), which is the gold standard in learnability theory.
  • Reproducibility: As a theoretical paper, reproducibility hinges on proof correctness. The constructions and definitions appear well-specified from the abstract.
  • Significance: Significant for the learning theory community. A sharp characterization of realizable learnability under general metric losses unifies and extends several prior lines of work.

The paper's practical implications may be limited since it primarily advances theoretical understanding rather than providing algorithmic tools.

Strengths

  • Resolves a concrete open problem with a complete (necessary and sufficient) characterization.
  • Unifies prior results for classification and regression under a single, more general framework.

Limitations

  • Restricted to the realizable setting; the agnostic case (where no hypothesis achieves zero risk) remains open and is arguably more practically relevant.
  • The result is existence-based (characterizes when a consistent rule exists) rather than constructive — it may not directly yield efficient algorithms.
  • Unbounded metric losses can introduce measure-theoretic subtleties that may limit the practical applicability of the framework.
  • As pure learning theory, the paper is unlikely to have immediate practical impact on applied ML.
