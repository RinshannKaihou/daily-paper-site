TL;DR: The paper recasts self-supervised learning as Latent Distribution Matching (LDM), which jointly maximizes alignment (log-probability under a latent model) and uniformity (latent entropy to prevent collapse). This single framework unifies ICA, contrastive, non-contrastive, and predictive SSL methods including stop-gradient approaches. The authors further derive a nonlinear, sampling-free Bayesian filtering model with a Kalman-based predictor for high-dimensional timeseries, and prove that predictive LDM yields identifiable latent representations even with nonlinear predictors under mild assumptions.

Key Contributions

  • Propose Latent Distribution Matching (LDM) as a unifying theoretical framework for SSL, jointly maximizing alignment and uniformity objectives.
  • Show that ICA, contrastive, non-contrastive, and predictive SSL methods (including BYOL/SimSiam-style stop-gradient approaches) are all special cases of LDM.
  • Derive a nonlinear, sampling-free Bayesian filtering model with a Kalman-based predictor from the LDM principle for high-dimensional timeseries data.
  • Prove identifiability of latent representations under predictive LDM with mild assumptions, even when using nonlinear predictors.

Methodology

The paper formulates SSL as Latent Distribution Matching (LDM), which combines two objectives: (1) Alignment — maximizing the log-probability of learned representations under an assumed latent generative model, and (2) Uniformity — maximizing the entropy of the latent distribution to prevent representational collapse. The authors show that varying the choice of latent model and inference strategy recovers existing SSL families: ICA corresponds to independent latent sources with exact inference; contrastive methods correspond to uniform/categorical latent priors with noise-contrastive estimation; non-contrastive and stop-gradient methods emerge from particular approximate inference schemes. Leveraging the LDM view, they derive a novel Bayesian filtering model for sequential data that uses a Kalman-based predictor without requiring sampling, enabling tractable inference in high-dimensional spaces. The theoretical analysis establishes conditions under which predictive LDM achieves identifiability of latent variables.

  • Identifiability guarantees are proven for predictive LDM, providing theoretical backing for learning disentangled representations.

Limitations
