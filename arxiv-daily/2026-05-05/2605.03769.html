Nora: Normalized Orthogonal Row Alignment for Scalable Matrix Optimizer

arXiv: 2605.03769

Date: 2026-05-05

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.03769

一句话总结： Nora是一种新型矩阵优化器，通过将动量投影到权重的行正交补空间再做行归一化，同时实现了类Muon的结构化预条件、严格的尺度不变性稳定性和O(mn)的计算效率，并在LLaMA训练实验中超越了Muon和RMNP。
TL;DR: Nora projects momentum onto the row-wise orthogonal complement of weights before row-normalizing it, unifying Muon-like preconditioning, scale-invariance stability, and O(mn) speed in one optimizer. It provably satisfies maximal update parametrization scaling laws (η ∝ 1/√n), has non-convex convergence guarantees, and outperforms Muon, RMNP, and Mano on LLaMA 60M/135M language modeling.

核心贡献

  • 提出了Nora优化器，通过行正交投影 + 行归一化，在单一框架内同时满足优化器设计的三大核心需求：结构化预条件的效率性、尊重神经网络尺度不变性的稳定性、以及O(mn)的计算速度。
  • 利用Transformer Hessian的行块对角占优特性，证明了对行正交动量做行归一化等价于施加对角预条件器，且更新方向自动满足与权重的正交性（⟨d_t, w_t⟩ = 0）。

Key Contributions

  • Introduces Nora, a matrix optimizer that projects momentum onto the row-wise orthogonal complement of weights, then applies row-wise normalization — achieving Muon-like preconditioning, strict scale-invariance stability, and O(mn) computation simultaneously.
  • Proves that row-normalizing the row-orthogonal momentum is mathematically equivalent to applying a diagonal preconditioner H_N = (diag(v_t^{r⊥}(v_t^{r⊥})^T))^{1/2} ⊗ I_n, and the resulting update direction is guaranteed orthogonal to weights.
  • Establishes width-scaling laws (η ∝ 1/√n) via µP theory (Theorems 4.1 & 4.2) and non-convex convergence guarantees under both Frobenius and (∞,2)-smoothness assumptions (Theorems 4.4, 4.7).
  • Demonstrates consistent improvements over Muon, RMNP, and Mano on LLaMA 60M and 135M models trained on C4, with better perplexity across learning rate sweeps and comparable wall-clock speed to RMNP.

Methodology

Nora's core insight is a decomposition of the optimizer design into three orthogonal desiderata, solved by a single algebraic operation:

  1. Row-wise orthogonal projection: For each row i of momentum v_t, project out the component parallel to the corresponding weight row: v_{t,i:}^{r⊥} = v_{t,i:} - (⟨v_{t,i:}, w_{t,i:}⟩ / ‖w_{t,i:}‖²²) · w_{t,i:}. This ensures the update direction lies in the tangent space of the weight, respecting scale invariance.
  1. Row-wise normalization: d_t = RN(v_t^{r⊥}), where each row is normalized to unit ℓ₂ norm. The key theoretical result (Equation 1–2) shows this is equivalent to diagonal preconditioning AND preserves orthogonality to weights, because the normalization of an already-perpendicular vector cannot introduce a parallel component.
  1. Scaling factor: The update is scaled by max(1, √(m/n)) to account for the aspect ratio of the weight matrix, analogous to Muon's spectral normalization.

The algorithm reduces to: project momentum → row-normalize → scale → apply learning rate. Weight decay is set to 0 for Nora (unlike Muon/RMNP which use 0.1), since the orthogonal projection already stabilizes weight norms. For non-matrix parameters (embeddings, biases, lm_head), Nora falls back to Adam.

The theoretical analysis leverages two smoothness assumptions (Frobenius and matched (∞,2)-norm), provides momentum tracking error bounds via non-expansiveness of the row-wise projection, and derives optimal learning rate / momentum schedules that yield O(m²L_Fσ²Δε⁻⁴) iteration complexity.

Results & Highlights

  • LLaMA 60M on C4: Nora achieves best perplexity of 28.94 (at LR 0.004) vs. Mano 29.55, RMNP 30.12, Muon 31.09. Nora also shows the most graceful degradation at high learning rates.
  • LLaMA 135M on C4: Nora achieves best perplexity of 21.74 (at LR 0.003) vs. Mano 22.13, RMNP 22.46, Muon 23.17.
  • Wall-clock speed: Nora matches RMNP's speed (both O(mn)) and is ~15% faster than Muon due to eliminating Newton-Schulz iterations. The overhead of the row-wise projection is negligible (element-wise operations).
  • Learning rate robustness: Nora maintains stable training across a wider range of learning rates compared to all baselines, with no divergence even at LR 0.02 (60M) and 0.03 (135M).

Critical Assessment

Novelty (Moderate-High): The core idea — combining row-orthogonal projection with row normalization — is elegant and the proof that these two operations compose to yield both preconditioning and scale-invariance is non-trivial. However, the individual components (row projection from SSO, row normalization from RMNP) exist in prior work. The contribution is primarily in the unified framework and theoretical analysis rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic primitive.

Rigor (High): The theoretical contributions are substantial: µP scaling laws, non-convex convergence under two smoothness assumptions, and clean proofs in the appendix. The non-expansiveness lemma for the row-wise projection is a key technical device that enables the convergence results.

Reproducibility (Good): Full code provided, hyperparameter sweeps documented, training configs in appendix tables. One concern: Nora uses weight decay 0.0 while baselines use 0.1 — this is a confound. The authors argue it's because Nora stabilizes norms via projection, but the fairness of this comparison could be questioned.

Significance (Moderate): Experiments are limited to LLaMA 60M and 135M on C4 with context length 256. These are small-scale setups. The paper titles itself as "preliminary experiments" and claims to be "highly promising" — this is honest framing. However, without results at 1B+ scale or on downstream tasks, it's unclear whether the advantages persist. The comparison with only Muon, RMNP, and Mano (a narrow set of matrix optimizers) is reasonable given the niche, but a comparison with well-tuned AdamW would strengthen the claims.

Red flags: The "two lines of code" claim is slightly misleading — the actual implementation (Appendix C.2) is substantially more complex, handling fp32 master weights, mixed param groups, and the Adam fallback. The scale invariance argument applies strictly only to layers with normalization (which is most Transformer layers, but not all parameters). The paper does not discuss how Nora interacts with learning rate warmup or whether the projection interacts adversarially with gradient accumulation in distributed settings.

Strengths

  • Clean, principled design that unifies three optimizer desiderata with a simple algebraic operation.
  • Strong theoretical contributions: µP scaling laws and non-convex convergence guarantees are meaningful and non-trivial.
  • Consistent empirical improvements across model scales and learning rate sweeps.
  • Negligible computational overhead compared to RMNP; significant speedup over Muon.
  • Well-written paper with clear notation and complete proofs in the appendix.

Limitations

  • Experiments are limited to small-scale models (60M, 135M) on a single dataset (C4) with short context (256). No results at billion-parameter scale or on downstream benchmarks.
  • Weight decay is set to 0 for Nora but 0.1 for all baselines — this difference is justified but complicates direct comparison.
  • No ablation study isolating the contribution of the orthogonal projection vs. the normalization step.
  • The scale-invariance argument assumes the presence of normalization layers; the paper does not analyze behavior for parameters without this property.
  • Distributed training behavior (gradient accumulation, tensor/model parallelism) is not discussed.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does Nora behave at 1B+ parameter scale? Does the orthogonal projection remain beneficial as the Hessian structure changes with depth and width?
  • What is the interaction between Nora's row-wise projection and gradient accumulation in distributed training across multiple GPUs?
  • Can the weight decay difference (0 vs. 0.1) be ablated to isolate whether Nora's gains come from the projection or from the absence of weight decay?
  • How does Nora interact with learning rate warmup schedules? Does the projection help or hurt during the initial high-variance gradient phase?
  • Could the row-orthogonal projection idea be extended to attention matrices or other structured parameter groups beyond standard linear layers?
