> **一句话总结：** DGPO通过用有界的Hellinger距离替代无界的KL散度，并结合策略熵门控机制，将GRPO粗粒度的序列级优势重分配为细粒度的token级信用，在不增加价值网络的情况下，在AIME 2024上达到60.0% Avg@32（Qwen2.5-32B），大幅超越DAPO。

> **TL;DR:** DGPO is a critic-free RL framework that fixes GRPO's coarse sequence-level credit assignment by redistributing advantages to individual tokens using bounded Hellinger distance (instead of unstable KL divergence) gated by policy entropy. This amplifies gradients on pivotal reasoning steps while suppressing confident hallucinations. On Qwen2.5-32B, it achieves 60.0% Avg@32 on AIME 2024 (vs. DAPO's 50.0%) with only 3.6% training overhead over vanilla GRPO, and no value network needed.

## 核心贡献

- **提出DGPO框架**：将分布偏差从惩罚项重新解释为指导信号，实现了无需评论家（critic-free）的细粒度token级信用分配
- **用有界的Hellinger距离替代无界的KL散度**：解决KL散度在探索低概率推理路径时导致的梯度爆炸和mode-seeking保守性问题，Hellinger距离被严格约束在[0,1]区间
- **设计策略熵门控机制**：通过用归一化的策略熵缩放分布偏差，区分真正的高不确定性探索（应奖励）和低熵的自信幻觉（应抑制）
- **完全消除token级KL惩罚**：将参考分布信息内化为本地优势的一部分，在保持与GRPO几乎相同的计算效率下实现token级信用重分配

## Key Contributions

- Propose Distribution-Guided Policy Optimization (DGPO), a novel critic-free RL framework that reinterprets distribution deviation as a guiding signal for fine-grained token-level credit assignment
- Replace the unbounded Reverse KL divergence with the strictly bounded Hellinger distance, preventing gradient explosion during aggressive exploration while smoothly encouraging diversity
- Introduce a policy entropy gating mechanism that scales the Hellinger deviation by the model's normalized epistemic uncertainty, filtering out confident hallucinations from genuine exploratory steps
- Eliminate the traditional token-level KL penalty entirely while achieving only 3.6% time overhead over vanilla GRPO, with identical memory footprint (~46 GB/GPU vs. PPO's 72.4 GB/GPU)

## Methodology

DGPO modifies the GRPO objective through three key mechanisms:

1. **Hellinger Distance for Bounded Deviation**: For each token at step $t$, DGPO computes $d_{i,t} = H^2(\pi_\theta(\cdot|x, y_{<t}), \pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot|x, y_{<t}))$, which is bounded in $[0, 1]$. When $\pi_{\text{ref}} \to 0$, this smoothly saturates at 1 rather than diverging like KL.

2. **Entropy Gating**: The raw deviation score is modulated by policy entropy: $s_{i,t} = d_{i,t} \cdot \tilde{H}_{i,t}^\kappa$, where $\tilde{H}$ is normalized Shannon entropy and $\kappa$ is a scaling factor. High-entropy tokens (uncertain, exploratory) preserve the deviation signal; low-entropy tokens (confident hallucinations) have their scores suppressed toward zero.

3. **Softmax Redistribution with Unit-Mean Property**: Token-level importance weights are computed via temperature-scaled softmax scaled by sequence length: $w_{i,t} = T_i \cdot \text{Softmax}_t(s_{i,t}/\tau)$, guaranteeing $\frac{1}{T_i}\sum_t w_{i,t} = 1$. The fine-grained advantage becomes $A_{i,t} = A_i \cdot w_{i,t}$, preserving total gradient magnitude while redistributing it to pivotal tokens.

The final DGPO objective uses standard PPO-style clipped importance sampling with the redistributed advantages, and crucially, no KL penalty term. The authors prove that gradients remain strictly bounded (via Cauchy-Schwarz on the Bhattacharyya coefficient and softmax algebraic properties), and that convergence guarantees are preserved because the unit-mean property ensures unbiased advantage expectation.

## Results & Highlights

- **Qwen2.5-32B (AIME 2024)**: DGPO achieves **60.0% Avg@32** vs. DAPO 50.0%, FIPO 56.0%; Cons@32 reaches 73.0%
- **Qwen2.5-32B (AIME 2025)**: DGPO achieves **46.0% Avg@32** vs. DAPO 38.0%, FIPO 43.0%
- **Qwen2.5-7B-Math (AIME 2024)**: DGPO achieves **43.0% Pass@1** vs. GRPO 22.0%, DAPO 36.0%, FIPO 40.0%
- **Computational efficiency**: 188 tokens/s/GPU throughput (vs. GRPO's 195), only **3.6% overhead**, same 46.5 GB peak memory as GRPO (vs. PPO's 72.4 GB)
- **Ablation**: Removing entropy gate drops 7B performance by 5%; replacing Hellinger with Reverse KL drops it by 9% (to 34.0%), confirming both components are essential
- **Hyperparameter robustness**: Optimal in moderate range $\tau \in [0.5, 1.0]$, $\kappa = 1.0$; performance degrades gracefully at extremes

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty**: The core idea of reinterpreting distribution deviation as a credit assignment signal rather than a penalty is conceptually clean and well-motivated. The combination of Hellinger distance + entropy gating is novel for critic-free RL. However, the individual components (bounded divergences, entropy regularization) are well-known; the contribution is primarily in their synthesis and application to the GRPO credit assignment problem.

**Rigor**: The theoretical analysis in the appendix is solid—bounded gradient proofs via Cauchy-Schwarz and convergence preservation via unit-mean property are mathematically sound. The ablation study is well-structured, isolating Hellinger vs. KL and entropy gate contributions separately. Hyperparameter sensitivity analysis is thorough.

**Weaknesses**:
- **Evaluation is narrow**: Only AIME 2024 and 2025 are used—both math reasoning benchmarks. No evaluation on coding (HumanEval/MBPP), general instruction following, or open-ended generation. The authors acknowledge this limitation.
- **Baseline comparison is thin**: Only GRPO, DAPO, and FIPO are compared. Notably absent are methods like RLOO, REINFORCE++, or any process-reward-model approaches that also aim for finer-grained credit assignment.
- **Training data**: Only DAPO-17K is used. It's unclear how DGPO performs with larger or more diverse training datasets.
- **The "state-of-the-art for critic-free alignment" claim is overreaching** given the evaluation is limited to two math benchmarks on one model family (Qwen2.5).
- **The improvement on AIME 2025 (46.0% vs 43.0% over FIPO) is relatively modest** compared to the AIME 2024 gap, suggesting diminishing returns on harder benchmarks.

**Reproducibility**: Good. Uses publicly available models (Qwen2.5-32B-Base, Qwen2.5-7B-Math), public dataset (DAPO-17K), and the HybridFlow framework. Hyperparameters are specified (lr=1e-6, weight decay=0.1, batch size 512, group size G=16).

**Significance**: If the results generalize beyond math reasoning, DGPO could become a meaningful improvement over GRPO for RL-based LLM alignment. The near-zero computational overhead is a practical advantage. However, the narrow evaluation makes it premature to assess broad impact.

## Strengths

- Elegant and well-motivated reformulation of distribution deviation from penalty to credit signal
- Strong theoretical grounding with bounded gradient proofs and convergence analysis
- Near-zero computational overhead (3.6%) over vanilla GRPO with no value network
- Substantial improvements on AIME 2024 across both 7B and 32B model scales
- Clean ablation study isolating the contribution of each component
- Well-written paper with clear notation and intuitive qualitative visualization of token-level credit

## Limitations

- Evaluation restricted to mathematical reasoning (AIME only); generalization to coding, dialogue, or agentic tasks is untested
- Only Qwen2.5 model family evaluated; cross-architecture robustness unknown
- Two new hyperparameters ($\tau$, $\kappa$) introduced, though sensitivity analysis shows moderate robustness
- No comparison with PRM-based methods or other recent credit assignment approaches (e.g., token-level DPO variants)
- The qualitative token-level visualization (Figure 4) is only shown for one example; more diverse examples would strengthen the analysis

## Relevance

DGPO直接改进了当前最流行的LLM推理对齐方法GRPO的信用分配机制，对提升长链推理能力有实际意义。其无需额外价值网络且计算开销极低的设计，使其在大规模RL训练场景中具有很好的应用前景，值得关注其在代码生成和多轮对话等领域的泛化能力。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How does DGPO perform on non-math reasoning tasks such as code generation (HumanEval, LiveCodeBench) or multi-turn dialogue alignment?
2. Can the entropy gating mechanism be adapted as a general-purpose technique for other RLHF methods beyond GRPO (e.g., applied to PPO or RLOO)?
3. What happens when DGPO is combined with a process reward model—does the Hellinger+entropy signal complement or conflict with external step-level rewards?
4. How sensitive is DGPO to the group size $G$? The paper uses $G=16$, but smaller group sizes (common in practice due to compute constraints) may affect Z-score normalization quality.
5. Does the token-level credit redistribution exhibit any systematic bias toward early vs. late tokens in long sequences, and could this be addressed with positional corrections?
