On Adaptivity in Zeroth-Order Optimization

arXiv: 2605.03869

Date: 2026-05-05

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.03869

一句话总结： 本文从理论和实验上证明，在高维零阶优化中逐坐标自适应（如ZO-Adam）并无优势，并提出MEAZO——仅用一个标量全局步长自适应的零阶优化器，在匹配ZO-Adam性能的同时保持ZO-SGD的内存开销。
TL;DR: This paper shows that coordinate-wise adaptive ZO optimizers (e.g., ZO-Adam) provide no convergence advantage over well-tuned ZO-SGD for high-dimensional LLM fine-tuning, because ZO gradient estimates are isotropic and lack per-coordinate structure. The authors propose MEAZO, which tracks a single scalar for global step-size adaptation, matching ZO-Adam's performance with ZO-SGD's memory footprint and offering significantly improved robustness to learning rate choices.

核心贡献

  • 揭示了零阶梯度估计在高维空间中缺乏逐坐标异质性，从理论和实验上解释了为什么ZO-Adam不比调好参数的ZO-SGD更好
  • 提出MEAZO算法，仅维护一个标量进行全局步长自适应，内存开销与ZO-SGD相同
  • 给出了MEAZO在非凸假设下的收敛性保证，收敛速率为O(1/√T)
  • 在分组（block-coordinate）设置下，MEAZO展现出对不同层参数敏感性的自然适应能力
  • 在多个LLM家族和任务上验证了MEAZO的有效性，并证明其对学习率选择具有更强的鲁棒性

Key Contributions

  • Identify that ZO gradient estimates in high dimensions are effectively isotropic (coordinate-homogeneous), explaining why coordinate-wise adaptive methods like ZO-Adam offer no advantage over well-tuned ZO-SGD
  • Propose MEAZO, a memory-efficient adaptive ZO optimizer that tracks only a single scalar EMA of the squared projected gradient norm for global step-size adaptation
  • Provide convergence guarantees under standard non-convex assumptions (L-smoothness, bounded variance, bounded gradients) with rate O(1/√T)
  • Demonstrate that MEAZO naturally extends to grouped ZO settings, adapting per-block step sizes without per-parameter memory cost
  • Show empirically across Llama-3 and Qwen-3 families on XSum, SST-2, and SQuAD that MEAZO matches ZO-Adam while being substantially more robust to learning rate selection than ZO-SGD

Methodology

The paper combines theoretical analysis with extensive empirical evaluation:

Theoretical analysis (Section 3): The authors prove (Proposition 3.1) that the expected squared ZO gradient estimate is dominated by a global norm term ‖∇f(x)‖²/q rather than per-coordinate information, when q ≪ d. This is derived for both Gaussian and uniform perturbation schemes. The key insight is that random perturbation directions make the ZO gradient estimator isotropic, unlike FO gradients which carry rich coordinate-wise structure.

MEAZO design (Algorithm 1): Instead of maintaining per-parameter first and second moments like Adam, MEAZO tracks a single scalar v_t = β·v_{t-1} + (1-β)·g_t² where g_t is the averaged projected gradient across q samples. The update rule normalizes the gradient direction by √(v̂_t + ζ), achieving global adaptive step sizing. For the grouped variant, one scalar v_j is maintained per block.

Convergence proof architecture: The proof proceeds in three stages: (1) a general result for adaptive SGD with affine variance bounds (Theorem 4.4), (2) a smoothed proxy optimization result lifting guarantees from smoothed to original objectives (Theorem 4.6), and (3) the main MEAZO convergence theorem (Theorem 4.7) with explicit rate O(1/√T).

Experimental setup: Synthetic quadratics with block-diagonal Hessian structure; LoRA and prompt tuning on Llama-3.2 (1B) and Qwen-3 (0.6B, 8B) across XSum, SST-2, and SQuAD; comparisons against ZO-SGD (MeZO), ZO-Adam, and R-AdaZO.

Results & Highlights

  • ZO-Adam = well-tuned ZO-SGD in high dimensions: Figure 1 and Table 1 show that with proper learning rate tuning, ZO-SGD matches ZO-Adam on LoRA fine-tuning, contradicting the assumption that adaptivity helps in ZO settings
  • MEAZO matches ZO-Adam performance: Table 1 shows MEAZO achieves comparable ROUGE-L, accuracy, and F1 scores to ZO-Adam across all model-task combinations with 3-seed averages
  • Critical robustness advantage: Figure 5 demonstrates that MEAZO and ZO-Adam have much wider stable learning-rate windows than ZO-SGD, which is brittle at suboptimal η. This is arguably the most practical contribution
  • Grouped MEAZO adapts to layer sensitivity: Figure 6 (bottom) shows the scalar v_t exhibits a clear ordering across transformer blocks — earlier layers get larger v_t (more conservative steps), later layers get smaller v_t
  • Synthetic validation of isotropy: Figure 2 confirms that as dimension increases, ZO-Adam's per-coordinate second moments collapse to a single value ‖∇f‖²/q, while FO-Adam retains coordinate-wise variability

Critical Assessment

Novelty (7/10): The insight that ZO gradients lack coordinate-wise heterogeneity is clean and important. MEAZO is a straightforward but well-motivated design. The convergence proof is solid but follows established techniques (affine-variance adaptive SGD + smoothed proxy lifting).

Rigor (8/10): Theoretical results are clearly stated with explicit assumptions. Proofs are provided in appendices. Empirical evaluation covers multiple models, tasks, and seeds (3 seeds per experiment). The comparison of second-moment statistics (Figure 2) directly validates the theoretical claims.

Reproducibility (7/10): Algorithm is clearly specified. Experimental details are deferred to appendices. Models are publicly available (Llama-3.2, Qwen-3). However, no code release is mentioned, and the full hyperparameter details require checking appendices.

Significance (7/10): The result that ZO-Adam is wasteful in high dimensions is practically impactful for memory-constrained LLM fine-tuning. The robustness advantage of MEAZO over ZO-SGD is the most valuable practical finding. However, the gap between ZO methods and FO methods remains large (Figure 1 shows FO converges much faster), which limits the practical scope.

Red flags:

  • The convergence guarantee is to a noise-dominated neighborhood rather than to a stationary point, which the authors acknowledge but somewhat downplay
  • Prompt tuning results (Table 2) show MEAZO and ZO-SGD are actually worse than ZO-Adam in several cases, which somewhat contradicts the narrative — the authors explain this via higher heterogeneity in small parameter spaces but could discuss it more
  • The paper compares against a limited set of ZO baselines (ZO-SGD, ZO-Adam, R-AdaZO); newer methods like FZOO are only briefly mentioned in appendices

Strengths

  • Clean theoretical insight explaining why adaptivity fails in high-dimensional ZO settings
  • MEAZO is minimal and elegant — a single scalar achieves the key practical benefit
  • Extensive experimental coverage across model families, sizes, and tasks
  • The robustness analysis (step size sensitivity) addresses a genuinely underexplored and practically important aspect
  • Grouped ZO analysis showing per-block adaptation is a nice extension

Limitations

  • ZO methods remain significantly slower to converge than FO methods (Figure 1), so the practical applicability is limited to scenarios where backpropagation is infeasible
  • The convergence guarantee does not reach a true stationary point but rather a noise-dominated neighborhood
  • Prompt tuning results are weaker for MEAZO, suggesting the method is best suited for higher-dimensional settings
  • No comparison with more recent ZO methods in the main paper (e.g., subspace ZO methods)
  • The grouped variant increases computation per step (multiple forward passes per block), trading compute for variance reduction

Relevance

本文对零阶优化的理论分析（ZO梯度估计的各向同性、自适应机制的局限性）与LLM训练中的数值稳定性和优化可靠性研究直接相关。MEAZO提出的标量自适应方案对内存受限场景下的模型微调具有实用价值，属于ML可靠性研究的范畴。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Can MEAZO's scalar adaptation be combined with subspace ZO methods (e.g., low-rank perturbation directions) to achieve both memory efficiency and faster convergence?
  • How does MEAZO behave in full-model fine-tuning regimes (not just LoRA), where the memory advantage over ZO-Adam would be most pronounced?
  • Is the robustness to learning rate specific to the global scalar normalization, or could similar benefits be achieved with cheaper heuristics (e.g., warmup + cosine decay)?
  • What happens to MEAZO's convergence behavior when the affine-variance bound is violated (e.g., in distributionally shifted or adversarial settings)?
  • Could the insight about ZO gradient isotropy inform the design of better ZO estimators that intentionally introduce coordinate-wise structure?
