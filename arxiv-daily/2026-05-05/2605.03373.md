## Key Contributions

- Derive the one-step learning dynamics of ZO SGD, revealing that the ZO eNTK (K_t(x_o, x_u; U_{t,P})) is a random projection of the FO eNTK onto the subspace spanned by perturbation vectors
- Prove via the JL Lemma that the kernel discrepancy bound is вИ•вИЖKвИ•_F вЙ§ ќµ ¬Ј ќЮ_t ¬Ј вИЪV with ќµ ~ O(вИЪ(log V / P)), yielding a trajectory fidelity bound of O(вИЪ(V log V / P)) ¬Ј ќЈќЮ_tвИ•G_tвИ•_2вИ•A_tвИ•_2 вАФ independent of model dimension d
- Show distribution robustness: Gaussian and Rademacher perturbation distributions have nearly identical concentration constants (c вЙИ 1/4), explaining why simple binary perturbations work well empirically
- Validate the dimension-free property on OPT models (125M вЖТ 1.3B) on SST-2: the number of perturbations P needed to recover FO dynamics is invariant to model scale, depending only on the fixed vocabulary size V вЙИ 50,272
- Establish a kernel-based framework that complements the classical optimization perspective, providing an alternative explanation for ZO scalability without requiring the low-effective-rank Hessian assumption

## Methodology

The paper develops its analysis in three stages:

**1. Learning Dynamics Formulation.** The authors define learning dynamics as the change in log-probability вИЖlog ѕА_t(y|x_o) after a single ZO-SGD update step. Through a first-order Taylor expansion of the ZO update rule (central finite differences with smoothing parameter ¬µ), they derive:

вИЖlog ѕА_t(y|x_o) вЙИ -ќЈ A_t(x_o) K_t(x_o, x_u; u_t) G_t(x_u, y_u)

where A_t is a model-confidence matrix, G_t is the last-layer gradient, and K_t(x_o, x_u; u_t) is the ZO eNTK вАФ the key quantity governing learning behavior.

**2. Multi-Perturbation Kernel Analysis.** For P perturbations, the ZO eNTK takes the form K_t(¬Ј; U_{t,P}) = (вИЗ_ќЄ z)^T U_{t,P} U_{t,P}^T (вИЗ_ќЄ z), where U_{t,P} вИИ R^{d√ЧP} is the projection matrix. Each element of the kernel discrepancy вИЖK = K^FO - K^ZO corresponds to the difference between inner products in the original and projected spaces, directly connecting to the JL Lemma.

**3. JL Lemma Application.** By applying the inner-product version of the JL Lemma to the V¬≤ pairs of neural tangent vectors {вИЗ_ќЄ z_i(x_o), вИЗ_ќЄ z_j(x_u)}, the sufficient projection dimension is P = O(ln(V¬≤)/ќµ¬≤), independent of d. The Frobenius norm bound follows via Jensen's inequality.

**Empirical Validation.** Experiments span: (a) LeNet (d=29,624) on MNIST with P вИИ {1, 5, 25, 125}, measuring Frobenius/CKA/spectral errors; (b) OPT-125M/350M/1.3B on SST-2 with P вИИ {1, 5, 10, 20, 50, 100}, comparing ZO vs FO training trajectories; (c) Synthetic MLP experiments scaling V from 2 to 1000 at fixed P=50.

## Results & Highlights

- **Dimension-free convergence:** On OPT models, the perturbation count P=50 suffices to closely match FO trajectories across 125M, 350M, and 1.3B parameters вАФ confirming that fidelity depends on V, not d
- **Distribution parity:** Gaussian and Rademacher perturbations yield nearly indistinguishable convergence curves across all three metrics (Frobenius, CKA, spectral), supporting the JL-based prediction of comparable concentration constants
- **Semantic similarity effect:** On MNIST, input pairs with high similarity (e.g., digits 4 and 9) show rapid kernel convergence, while dissimilar pairs (e.g., 0 and 1) require more perturbations вАФ the ZO gradient estimator is more efficient for semantically similar inputs
- **V-scaling validation:** Synthetic experiments confirm approximation error scales as вИЪV: increasing V from 2 to 1000 increases relative Frobenius error from 0.28 to 3.69, consistent with theory
- **Comparison with optimization view:** The classical optimization perspective yields convergence O(вИЪ(dL/PT)) with dimension-dependent variance, while the eNTK perspective gives O(вИЪ(V log V / P)) вАФ both agree on the 1/вИЪP scaling but critically differ on dimensionality dependence

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: High.** The kernel-based perspective on ZO optimization is genuinely novel. The connection between ZO eNTK and JL Lemma is elegant and provides clean theoretical machinery. The key insight вАФ that the JL projection dimension depends on the number of tangent vectors (V¬≤), not the ambient dimension d вАФ is non-obvious and impactful.

- **Rigor: Good with caveats.** The JL Lemma application is correct, and the Frobenius norm bound derivation is sound. However, the analysis is strictly one-step (single update), and the paper acknowledges that extending to multi-step convergence requires additional assumptions. The smoothing error O(¬µќЈ) is swept aside. The bound involves вИ•вИЗ_ќЄ zвИ•_F (denoted ќЮ_t), which could itself scale with d in certain architectures вАФ the paper does not fully address this.

- **Reproducibility: Good.** Experimental setups are well-described (LeNet/MNIST, OPT/SST-2, synthetic MLP). The theoretical results are self-contained with proofs in appendices.

- **Significance: High.** This paper addresses a central paradox in ZO optimization and provides a clean, testable prediction: ZO fidelity scales with V, not d. The empirical validation across three OPT model sizes is compelling. The framework also makes a practical prediction вАФ P=50 is sufficient for typical LLM fine-tuning вАФ that practitioners can directly use.

- **Red flags / weaknesses:**
  1. **Lazy training assumption:** The analysis assumes the eNTK varies slowly, which holds for fine-tuning near initialization but breaks down for training from scratch or large learning rates. The authors acknowledge this but it significantly limits scope.
  2. **No multi-step guarantee:** The one-step analysis shows trajectory alignment at each step but does not prove that compounding errors over many steps remain bounded. This is a significant gap.
  3. **ќЮ_t dependence:** The bound includes вИ•вИЗ_ќЄ z(x)вИ•_F which could implicitly depend on d. The dimension-free claim rests on this term being "bounded," which may not hold universally.
  4. **Limited task diversity:** All LLM experiments use SST-2 binary sentiment classification with prompt-based tuning. No evaluation on generation tasks, multi-class classification, or chain-of-thought reasoning.
  5. **Incomplete comparison with low-rank assumption:** The paper positions itself as an alternative to the low-effective-rank Hessian assumption but does not empirically compare which explanation better predicts actual ZO performance.

## Strengths

- Elegant theoretical framework that cleanly resolves the dimensionality paradox via JL Lemma
- Strong empirical validation across model scales (125M вЖТ 1.3B) with consistent results
- The dimension-free prediction is specific, testable, and practically actionable
- Distribution robustness result (Gaussian вЙИ Rademacher) has immediate practical implications
- Honest and thorough discussion of limitations (Section 4), including computational trade-offs and scope of applicability
- The semantic similarity finding (convergence rate depends on input pair similarity) is a nuanced and interesting observation

## Limitations

- **One-step analysis only:** Multi-step compounding effects are not formally bounded; the paper leaves this as future work
- **Fine-tuning only:** The lazy training / slow eNTK assumption explicitly excludes pre-training scenarios and large learning rate regimes
- **No autoregressive tasks:** The framework assumes fixed output dimension V; for generation tasks, the effective output dimension grows with sequence length, potentially requiring much larger P
- **Computational cost of multiple perturbations:** Each additional perturbation requires a separate forward pass. Parallelizing P perturbations multiplies memory by P, which is prohibitive for already memory-constrained LLM fine-tuning
- **ќЮ_t not thoroughly analyzed:** The Frobenius norm of the Jacobian вИ•вИЗ_ќЄ z(x)вИ•_F could itself carry implicit dimension dependence that is not fully characterized
