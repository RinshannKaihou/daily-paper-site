FALAT: Tracing Failures in LLM Agent Trajectories via Dependency-Guided Search

arXiv: 2606.00765

Date: 2026-06-02

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.00765

一句话总结： FALAT 是一个通过依赖关系引导的分层搜索框架，用于在 LLM 智能体多步执行轨迹中定位引入决定性错误的具体步骤和负责智能体，将失败归因从简单的步骤分类转化为结构化的诊断搜索问题。
TL;DR: FALAT is a hierarchical dependency-guided search framework that diagnoses failures in LLM agent trajectories by distinguishing error-originating steps from downstream propagators, using typed dependencies and counterfactual verification to identify the responsible agent and decisive failure step.

核心贡献

  • 将失败归因重新定义为分层依赖引导的搜索问题：突破了传统的扁平化步骤分类思路，通过在多层抽象空间中从粗到细地定位错误来源，更高效地处理长达数百步的复杂轨迹。
  • 提出 FALAT 四阶段诊断框架：(1) 构建基于任务先验和三层层次化表示的搜索空间；(2) 通过类型化依赖关系对候选错误步骤进行剪枝，区分错误起源与错误传播；(3) 依赖引导的搜索与反事实验证，判断修复某一步是否足以恢复预期输出；(4) 局部对抗性重搜索，避免锚定效应导致的过早承诺。
  • 设计六种类型化迁移标签：follow_up（依赖继承）、redundancy（冗余重复）、no_influence（无影响）、error_shift（错误转移）、correction（修正覆盖）、dead_end（无下游使用），以精准刻画步骤间的依赖结构。
  • 在 Who&When 基准上取得最佳性能：在算法生成轨迹上达到 68.0% 智能体级别和 46.0% 步骤级别准确率；在手写轨迹上达到 73.0% 智能体级别和 29.1% 步骤级别准确率，显著超越 CHIEF 和 AgentTracer 等现有基线。
  • 验证结构化推理优于单纯模型扩展：即使使用 Claude-Sonnet-4 等强基座模型，直接提示的步级准确率也远低于 FALAT 的结构化搜索流程，说明依赖感知推理对可靠诊断至关重要。

Key Contributions

  • Reformulates failure attribution as hierarchical dependency-guided search: Moves beyond flat step-level classification by locating error sources through coarse-to-fine search across multiple abstraction levels, enabling efficient handling of trajectories with hundreds of steps.
  • Proposes FALAT, a four-stage diagnostic framework: (1) Search-space construction using a task-conditioned prior and three-level hierarchical trajectory representation; (2) Typed-dependency candidate pruning to separate error origination from propagation; (3) Dependency-guided search with counterfactual sufficiency verification; (4) Local adversarial re-search to avoid premature commitment due to anchoring.
  • Designs six typed transition labels: follow_up, redundancy, no_influence, error_shift, correction, and dead_end to precisely characterize inter-step dependency structures.
  • Achieves state-of-the-art on the Who&When benchmark: Reaches 68.0% agent-level and 46.0% step-level accuracy on algorithm-generated trajectories, and 73.0% agent-level and 29.1% step-level accuracy on hand-crafted trajectories, significantly outperforming CHIEF and AgentTracer.
  • Demonstrates structured reasoning outperforms mere model scaling: Even strong standalone LLMs like Claude-Sonnet-4 achieve far lower step-level accuracy than FALAT's structured search pipeline, confirming that dependency-aware reasoning is essential for reliable diagnosis.

Methodology

FALAT operationalizes failure attribution through a four-stage pipeline:

Stage 1: Search-Space Construction. FALAT builds an external prior π from the task specification, agent roles, available tools, and the failed final output — deliberately excluding intermediate trajectory steps to avoid being misled by the agent's own flawed reasoning. It then constructs a hierarchical trajectory representation M = (M_hi, M_mid, M_lo) through non-overlapping window-based abstraction. The high level captures coarse execution stages, the mid level summarizes local decisions, and the low level preserves raw evidence (tool outputs, concrete values). Using π and M, FALAT identifies an initial candidate set C by detecting three signals: outcome relevance, prior inconsistency, and local execution inconsistency.

Stage 2: Typed Dependencies for Candidate Pruning. For each ordered pair of candidate steps (s_i, s_j) with i < j, FALAT assigns one of six transition labels describing how information and errors move between steps. This typed dependency structure refines C into C' by pruning candidates whose effects are redundant, corrected, unrelated, or unable to reach the final output.

Stage 3: Dependency-Guided Search and Verification. The refined candidates are ranked by deviation from π and propagation path influence. The top K candidates (K=3) form a high-confidence set C_H. Each candidate is verified against three criteria: (i) Error independence — does it introduce the error or merely inherit it? (ii) Counterfactual sufficiency — would correcting this step recover the expected output while keeping prior steps fixed? (iii) Temporal precedence — among satisfying candidates, select the earliest.

Stage 4: Local Re-Search (Adversarial Verification). To mitigate anchoring bias, FALAT extracts a bounded neighborhood [t̂−2, t̂+2] around the predicted decisive step. Each step is assigned one of four roles (ROOT_CAUSE, PROPAGATION, SYMPTOM, CONTRIBUTING) and re-evaluated comparatively against its neighbors through for/against arguments and counterfactual checks.

Results & Highlights

FALAT is evaluated on the Who&When benchmark, comprising 184 failed executions from 127 LLM-based multi-agent systems across algorithm-generated (CaptainAgent on GAIA/AssistantBench) and hand-crafted (Magentic-One) trajectories.

Overall Performance (Table 2):

  • Algorithm-generated trajectories: Best config achieves 68.0% agent-level and 46.0% step-level accuracy (MiniMax 2.5 backbone), improving over CHIEF by +13.2 agent and +8.7 step points.
  • Hand-crafted trajectories: Best config achieves 73.0% agent-level and 29.1% step-level accuracy (DeepSeek V3.2), improving over CHIEF by +23.0 agent and +12.1 step points.
  • Compared to AgentTracer-8B, FALAT improves step-level accuracy by +8.7 on algorithm-generated and +5.2 on hand-crafted trajectories.

Comparison to Standalone LLMs:

  • Even the strongest standalone baseline (Claude-Sonnet-4) reaches only 38.8% step-level on algorithm-generated and 18.9% on hand-crafted trajectories.
  • FALAT improves upon these by +7.2 and +10.2 points respectively, demonstrating that structured search provides benefits beyond raw model capability.

Tolerance Analysis (Table 3):

  • Exact step-level accuracy on hand-crafted trajectories ranges from 22.2% to 29.1% across backbones.
  • With ±5 tolerance, accuracy rises to 43.1–53.4%, showing FALAT typically localizes errors within a small neighborhood of the ground-truth step.

Ablation Study (Table 4, GPT-5.4-Mini):

  • Removing the hierarchical representation causes the largest drop: −10.3 step-level on algorithm-generated and −5.0 on hand-crafted.
  • Removing the external prior reduces agent-level accuracy by −10.9 and step-level by −5.5 on algorithm-generated trajectories.
  • Removing transition typing causes consistent drops across both datasets (−4.7 to −10.5).

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The core conceptual contribution — reformulating failure attribution as dependency-guided search rather than independent step classification — is well-motivated and distinct from prior work. The typed dependency vocabulary and the four-role local taxonomy are novel design elements that go beyond CHIEF's causal graph backtracking and AgentTracer's learned classification.

Rigor: The methodology is formally grounded with clear definitions (decisive step set, counterfactual trajectory). The evaluation on Who&When spans both algorithm-generated and hand-crafted trajectories, uses multiple backbones (GPT-5.4-Mini, DeepSeek V3.2, MiniMax 2.5), and reports the strictest metric (exact step-level accuracy). Ablation studies isolate the contribution of each component.

Reproducibility: The paper includes full prompt templates in Appendix A, enabling replication. However, no code or implementation details (e.g., exact LLM temperature, parsing logic for typed transitions) are released at this stage.

Significance: As LLM agents tackle longer and more complex tasks, reliable failure diagnosis becomes critical for debugging, safety, and iterative improvement. FALAT addresses a genuinely hard problem that existing methods struggle with, particularly step-level localization in long trajectories with error propagation.

Red Flags / Concerns:

  • The absolute step-level accuracies (29–46%) indicate the problem remains far from solved; the method helps but is not yet reliable enough for production deployment.
  • FALAT's multi-stage pipeline makes multiple LLM calls per diagnosis, which may be costly and slow for real-time debugging.
  • The hand-crafted trajectory results (29.1%) suggest the method struggles with deliberately designed, subtle failure patterns — precisely the kind developers care about most.

Strengths

  • Well-motivated problem formulation: The paper clearly articulates why flat step classification fails for trajectories with error propagation, and the hierarchical search formulation is intuitively compelling.
  • Strong empirical gains: FALAT consistently outperforms specialized baselines across backbones and trajectory types, with the largest improvements on the strictest metric.
  • Comprehensive ablation: Each component's contribution is quantified, with the hierarchical representation shown to be the most critical.
  • Conceptual clarity: The six typed transitions and four local roles provide an interpretable vocabulary for understanding how errors flow through trajectories.
  • Fair comparison: Re-running CHIEF under identical backbones removes confounding from model capability differences.

Limitations

  • Dependence on LLM judgments: Every stage (candidate selection, transition typing, counterfactual verification) relies on the backbone model's reasoning reliability; errors in intermediate judgments can propagate.
  • Limited long-range dependency capture: Window-based processing may miss dependencies that span across distant windows; consolidation only partially mitigates this.
  • Assumption of localized failure: The formulation assumes failures can be localized to a small set of decisive steps. Distributed or interacting errors across multiple steps may not be captured well.
  • Moderate absolute accuracy: Even the best results leave the majority of cases misdiagnosed at the step level, limiting practical utility without human oversight.
  • Computational cost: The four-stage pipeline with multiple LLM calls per stage may be too expensive for high-frequency debugging scenarios.

Relevance

对于构建和调试多智能体 LLM 系统的研究者与工程师而言，FALAT 提供了一种从"哪个智能体出错"推进到"哪一步引入错误"的结构化诊断思路，其依赖关系建模和反事实验证方法对提升智能体系统的可解释性与可靠性具有重要参考价值。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How would FALAT perform on trajectories from real-world deployed agents (e.g., OpenAI Codex, Claude Code) rather than benchmark-constructed failures?
  2. Can the typed-dependency vocabulary be learned or refined automatically from data, rather than being manually designed?
  3. What is the computational and monetary cost of running FALAT's full pipeline per diagnosis, and how can it be optimized for real-time use?
  4. How does FALAT handle failures where multiple interacting errors are jointly necessary (i.e., no single decisive step exists)?
  5. Can the hierarchical representation be adapted dynamically based on trajectory length and complexity, rather than using fixed window sizes?
  6. Would integrating execution traces (code runs, tool outputs) more deeply into the low-level representation further improve localization accuracy?
