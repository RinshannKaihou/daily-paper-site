Key Contributions

  • Triangulated monitoring methodology that decomposes agentic system evaluation into three complementary dimensions (quality, suitability, efficiency) at three monitoring scopes (within-run, cross-run, structural), using within-category variance (coefficient of variation) as a first-class signal to characterize system behavior without requiring ground truth labels.
  • FMEA-based automated triage system adapted from military standard MIL-STD-882E, classifying failures into four severity levels (L1 Catastrophic, L2 Critical, L3 Marginal, L4 Negligible) based on operational impact rather than abstract technical accuracy, enabling deterministic routing.
  • Empirical evidence that monitor scope determines failure type: On a synthetic testbed of 220 runs across 120 document bundles, within-run monitors surface deterministic stage defects (CV = 0.02, all L3), cross-run monitors surface stochastic integration consequences (CV = 1.25, 24% at L2), and a structural monitor identifies an integration integrity gap with perfect consistency (CV = 0.00, all L2).

Methodology

The paper presents a two-part methodology: triangulated monitoring and FMEA-based automated triage.

Monitoring decomposes evaluation into three dimensions:

  • Quality: Whether the agent produced a correct result (accuracy, completeness, grounding, absence of hallucination).
  • Suitability: Whether the agent performed the task appropriately (instruction adherence, contextual fit, workflow alignment).
  • Efficiency: Whether the agent delivered value relative to cost (completion time, token usage, human review burden).

Each dimension is operationalized through rule-based validators, statistical measures, and LLM-based evaluators. The methodology uses within-category variance (coefficient of variation, CV) as a first-class signal alongside aggregate means. Variance is operationalized through z-scores at two scopes: within-run (using documents in a single execution as reference) and cross-run (using leave-one-out estimation against the fleet). A threshold τ = 2.0 (or 3.0 for timing-sensitive evaluators) is used for flagging.

Triage adapts Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA) from safety-critical industries (aerospace, automotive, medical devices) to agentic AI. It operates in four phases:

  1. Failure Classification: Categorizing detected failures by type using structured metadata from monitoring.
  2. Risk Classification: Computing risk across three axes—Severity, Probability, and Scope/Exposure.
  3. Failure Prioritization: Ranking failures by urgency.
  4. Response Determination: Routing to corrective action, workaround, or patch.

Severity is calibrated to operational impact using four levels from MIL-STD-882E:

  • L1 (Catastrophic): Compromises overall process integrity; requires immediate escalation.
  • L2 (Critical): Produces materially incorrect results; requires human review before downstream reliance.
  • L3 (Marginal): Produces degraded but usable results; addressed through periodic sampling.
  • L4 (Negligible): Minimal operational impact; handled automatically.

The methodology is validated on a synthetic testbed for the Subsequent Events and Unrecorded Liabilities (SURL) audit procedure under US GAAP. The testbed comprises 120 document bundles (20 clean, 100 with controlled error injection), with 5 error subtypes (arithmetic, cross-document comparison, temporal anomalies) × 4 difficulty levels × 5 document sets per cell. The agentic system under test has two known integration defects: (1) document type is identified at ingestion but not propagated downstream, causing a hardcoded invoice schema to be applied to all documents; and (2) the cross-document validator receives zero related documents as context.

Results & Highlights

The experiment produced 10,453 total findings across 220 runs. Three key results emerge:

1. Monitor scope determines failure type and severity profile.

  • Within-run monitors (DM-095): Fire on 100% of runs, producing 45.7 findings per run (±1.0 sd, CV = 0.02), all classified as L3 (Marginal). This monitor diagnosed a deterministic stage defect—the hardcoded schema misapplication.
  • Cross-run monitors (DM-161, DM-170): Fire on 41% of runs, producing 0.44 findings per run (±0.55 sd, CV = 1.25). When they fire, 76% are L3 and 24% are L2 (Critical). These surface the stochastic downstream consequences of integration gaps.
  • Structural monitor (DM-100): Fires exactly once per run on every run (CV = 0.00), all classified as L2. This identified an integration integrity gap where documents expected by the manifest were not processed by downstream stages.

2. Structural defects mask task-level errors.

Across the 100 companies with paired clean and error runs, monitor firing patterns are statistically indistinguishable between conditions. The injected task-level errors are completely masked by the structural noise floor: when a stage applies the wrong schema to every document, the resulting findings are identical whether or not the underlying data contains an anomaly. This is an instance of "evaluation entanglement"—when systematic infrastructure defects dominate, metrics reflect infrastructure state rather than task-level signal.

However, variance encodes scope class without ground truth labels: within-run monitors have CV ≈ 0.02 (near-identical output), cross-run monitors have CV = 1.25 (highly variable), and structural monitors have CV = 0.00 (perfectly invariant).

3. Deterministic triage concentrates human attention.

  • All 10,210 L3 findings → routed to automated monitoring (no human review).
  • All 243 L2 findings → routed to human investigation (Assign Observability).
  • This achieves a 43× reduction in analyst volume (from 10,453 total to 243 requiring human attention).
  • Notably, 220 of the 243 L2 findings come from DM-100 (a simulator artifact). Excluding DM-100, the triage reduction is 445×, carried entirely by cross-run findings.

The methodology draws on established reliability engineering principles (FMEA, MIL-STD-882E) and connects to ongoing standardization efforts (OpenTelemetry GenAI semantic conventions, NIST AI Risk Management Framework). Critical Assessment

Novelty: The paper's core contribution is architectural rather than algorithmic. The triangulated monitoring framework and the adaptation of FMEA severity classification to agentic AI operations are novel and well-motivated. The use of variance (CV) as a scope-characterization signal without ground truth labels is a genuinely interesting methodological insight.

Rigor: The experimental design is careful and transparent. The factorial design (5 error subtypes × 4 difficulty levels) enables systematic analysis. The authors clearly state their limitations: synthetic testbed, single audit procedure, one agentic system with specific defects, and Stage 1 validation only. The deliberate exclusion of LLM-based evaluators to avoid confounding is methodologically sound, though it limits production realism.

Reproducibility: The methodology is described in sufficient detail to be replicated. However, no code or reusable artifact is released—the authors characterize the contribution as a "design methodology" rather than a framework. The specific testbed platform (Simthetic) and the agentic system are not publicly available, limiting direct replication.

Significance: The paper addresses a genuinely important and underexplored problem: how to monitor agentic systems that are not yet mature enough for traditional error detection. The "deploy monitoring early" argument is compelling and counter to common practice. The 43× (or 445×) triage reduction figures are impressive, though they depend heavily on the specific defect profile of the system under test.

Red flags:

  • The structural monitor (DM-100) fires on a simulator artifact (macOS resource fork files inflating document count), not an actual system defect. The authors disclose this but it slightly weakens the L2 routing narrative.
  • The paper conflates "structural defects mask task-level errors" (true for deterministic, content-independent defects) with a broader claim that would need validation across defect taxonomies.
  • Stages 2 and 3 of the maturity model are purely theoretical; the paper's title claim about "before they're reliable" is validated only for Stage 1.

Strengths

  • Practical relevance: Directly addresses the gap between capability benchmarks and production monitoring for agentic systems in regulated industries.
  • Methodological clarity: The three-dimension × three-scope framework is intuitive and well-explained with clear operational definitions.
  • Honest null result: The authors report that injected task-level errors are undetectable as a meaningful finding, not a failure—this strengthens the paper's argument.
  • Strong connection to established fields: Grounding in reliability engineering (FMEA, MIL-STD-882E) lends credibility and provides a path to adoption in conservative industries.
  • Appropriate scope claims: The authors carefully distinguish what transfers architecturally (taxonomy, CV-based characterization, severity model) from what requires domain-specific calibration (evaluators, thresholds, severity distributions).
  • Well-structured argument: The progression from problem → methodology → empirical validation → maturity model is logical and persuasive.

Limitations

  • Synthetic testbed: Cannot fully replicate real-world agentic system interactions and document heterogeneity.
  • Limited error taxonomy: Only five error subtypes within one audit procedure (SURL under US GAAP); broader taxonomies would strengthen generalization.
  • Single system, specific defects: Results reflect one agentic system with two particular integration defects; replication across different defect profiles is needed.
  • Stage 1 validation only: The maturity-staging model is empirically supported only for Stage 1 (system characterization); transitions to Stages 2 and 3 are theoretical predictions.
  • No LLM-based evaluators: The experiment deliberately uses only rule-based and statistical evaluators to avoid confounding, departing from typical production deployments.
  • No production data: The paper references qualitative observations from practitioner deployments but reports no actual production results.
  • No released artifacts: The contribution is a design methodology with no code, framework, or reusable implementation released.

Relevance

对于正在将智能体系统部署到生产环境（尤其是金融审计、医疗、法律等受监管行业）的AI运维团队来说，本文提供了一个极具实操价值的早期监控框架——在系统尚不成熟、传统任务级错误检测不可行时，先通过结构性诊断找到最关键的问题，避免被海量低严重度告警淹没。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does the methodology perform with LLM-based evaluators in the loop? The experiment deliberately excluded them to avoid confounding, but production deployments would include them. How much does evaluator variance (position sensitivity, verbosity preference) degrade or enhance the scope-characterization signal?
  1. What is the full taxonomy of structural defect types and their masking profiles? The paper establishes that deterministic, content-independent defects completely mask task-level signal, but what about conditional defects (e.g., context-window truncation, tool-call failures under specific patterns)? How can partial masking be detected and characterized?
  1. Can the maturity-staging model be validated longitudinally? The most important open question is whether the predicted transitions from Stage 1 (structural diagnosis) → Stage 2 (error detection) → Stage 3 (reliability tracking) actually occur as integration defects are resolved, and what the observational signatures of these transitions are.
  1. How well does the architecture transfer to non-document-driven agentic systems? The paper claims architectural transfer to document-driven, multi-stage workflows in regulated industries, but what about tool-use-heavy agents, conversational agents, or embodied agents with different telemetry structures?
  1. Can the FMEA risk cube (Severity × Probability × Exposure) be fully operationalized in production? The experiment used Severity-only routing because Probability and Exposure require deployment context. What would production calibration of the full three-axis risk model look like, and how much does it improve routing precision over severity-only triage?
  1. How robust is the CV-based scope characterization across different baseline distributions? The paper uses z-scores with thresholds of 2.0 and 3.0. How do these thresholds perform with heavy-tailed or multimodal evaluator distributions common in real agentic systems?
