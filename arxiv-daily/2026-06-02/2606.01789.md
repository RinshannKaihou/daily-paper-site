> **一句话总结：** 本文首次系统评估了因果发现领域常用基准数据集与其对应领域研究之间的一致性，发现多个广泛使用的基准（尤其是2000年前构建的）存在严重的知识过时问题，对基于LLM的因果发现方法评估产生了显著误导。

> **TL;DR:** This paper presents the first systematic study evaluating the consistency between popular causal discovery benchmarks and current domain research, revealing that many widely-used benchmarks—especially those constructed before 2000—contain significantly outdated knowledge, which poses serious risks to evaluating LLM-based causal discovery methods.

## 核心贡献

- **首创基准质量系统评估框架**：首次从领域研究对齐的角度系统评估因果发现基准数据集的质量，填补了该领域长期存在的评估空白。
- **设计LLM驱动的自动化评估流程**：构建了一个包含三个核心组件的流程——领域论文检索、条件关联关系提取、一致性验证——能够大规模自动检查基准因果图与领域研究论文之间的一致性。
- **大规模实证分析**：对11个最常用的真实世界基准进行评估，流程共处理了38,081篇领域研究论文，覆盖医学、生物学等多个领域。
- **揭示基准数据集的严重不一致问题**：发现不同基准与领域研究的一致性差异巨大，部分最常用基准（如Asia、Child）的不一致率高达50%以上，对基于LLM的因果发现方法评估产生了根本性误导。

## Key Contributions

- **First systematic benchmark quality evaluation**: Pioneers the first large-scale study assessing the alignment between popular causal discovery benchmarks and current domain knowledge, addressing a critical gap in the field.
- **LLM-powered automated evaluation pipeline**: Designs a three-component pipeline—domain paper retrieval, conditional association extraction, and consistency verification—to automatically evaluate benchmark causal graphs against scientific literature at scale.
- **Large-scale empirical analysis**: Evaluates 11 of the most widely-used real-world benchmarks, processing 38,081 domain research papers across medical science, biology, and other domains.
- **Reveals severe benchmark inconsistency**: Demonstrates dramatic variation in benchmark quality, with some of the most popular benchmarks (e.g., Asia, Child) showing inconsistency rates exceeding 50%, fundamentally undermining the validity of evaluations—especially for LLM-based methods.

## Methodology

The paper proposes a three-component LLM-equipped pipeline to evaluate the consistency between benchmark causal graphs and domain research:

1. **Domain Research Paper Retrieval**: For each pair of variables in a benchmark causal graph, the pipeline searches scientific databases (PubMed for medical/biology domains, OpenAlex for others) using expanded queries with up to 15 synonyms per variable. The synonyms are sourced from UMLS and ChatGPT for biomedical variables, and from ChatGPT for others. An abstract-based filter screens out irrelevant papers, and open-access PDFs are downloaded and converted to JSON via the Mistral OCR API.

2. **Conditional Association Relationship Extraction**: The pipeline deterministically extracts all conditional association relationships from the causal graph using the d-separation criterion. For each variable pair (Vi, Vj), it identifies whether they are: (i) marginally independent, (ii) conditionally independent given some set Z, or (iii) always correlated (if adjacent). Each relationship is formulated as a natural language query.

3. **Consistency Verification**: Using the Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking-2507 model deployed on an H100 GPU, the pipeline verifies whether each retrieved paper's conclusions are consistent with the conditional association query derived from the benchmark. The LLM returns one of three labels: "Consistent", "Inconsistent", or "Unknown".

The main evaluation metric is the **inconsistency rate**: R_InCon = #InCon / (#InCon + #Con), where #InCon is the number of papers inconsistent with the benchmark and #Con is the number of consistent papers.

The authors manually validated the LLM verifier on 100 randomly sampled papers, achieving an accuracy of 0.9.

## Results & Highlights

- **Significant variation in benchmark quality**: Among the 11 evaluated benchmarks, inconsistency rates range from 26.7% to 57.9%.
- **Sachs is the most reliable**: The Sachs benchmark (2005) achieves the lowest inconsistency rate at 26.7% and is also the most frequently used (38 surveyed papers). Despite being over 20 years old, it maintains high quality due to its hybrid construction process combining Bayesian network computation with domain expert revision.
- **Pre-2000 benchmarks perform poorly**: Diabetes (45.8%), Child (54.0%), and Asia (57.9%) show the highest inconsistency rates. All benchmarks constructed before 2000 (except Insurance) rank in the bottom tier.
- **Insurance and Ecoli rank second and third**: With inconsistency rates of 31.6% and 32.9%, respectively.
- **Temporal trends matter**: When considering only papers published after each benchmark's construction year, inconsistency rates shift—Fmri improves from 42.6% to 28.6%, while Alarm worsens from 39.7% to 47.2%.
- **LLM-based methods are especially vulnerable**: Unlike statistical methods that rely on data-graph alignment, LLM-based causal discovery methods construct graphs using "world model" knowledge, making them particularly sensitive to outdated benchmark knowledge.
- **Benchmark field is not a predictor**: Among the 11 benchmarks, only Insurance and Arctic Sea Ice are outside medical science/biology, but domain does not correlate with inconsistency rates.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: High. This is the first work to systematically evaluate causal discovery benchmarks from the angle of domain research alignment. While prior works (Gentzel et al., 2019; Brouillard et al., 2025) reviewed benchmark usage and advocated for new benchmarks, none quantified the misalignment between benchmarks and evolving domain knowledge at this scale.
- **Rigor**: Moderate to high. The methodology is well-structured and grounded in causal inference theory (d-separation). The manual validation of the LLM verifier (90% accuracy) provides confidence, though the use of a smaller open-source model (Qwen3-30B) rather than state-of-the-art frontier models introduces acknowledged noise. The paper sample of 38,081 papers is substantial.
- **Reproducibility**: Moderate. The paper describes the pipeline in detail, but does not release code or the exact paper retrieval API implementations. The use of proprietary tools (Mistral OCR, ChatGPT for synonym generation) limits full reproducibility.
- **Significance**: High. The findings have immediate practical implications for the causal discovery community—researchers should reconsider evaluating methods on outdated benchmarks like Asia and Child, and the community should prioritize benchmark maintenance and updating.
- **Red flags**: The authors exclude 10 benchmarks due to computational constraints, including some large ones (Hailfinder, Barley, WIN95PTS). This limits the generalizability of the findings to all popular benchmarks. The LLM verifier accuracy, while validated, means ~10% of labels may be incorrect across 38,000 papers.

## Strengths

- Tackles a genuinely important and underexplored problem in causal discovery evaluation.
- The d-separation-based formulation provides a principled bridge between causal graph structure and empirically testable claims in domain literature.
- The scale of evaluation (38,081 papers, 11 benchmarks) is impressive and provides statistically meaningful results.
- The analysis of temporal trends (inconsistency over time, post-construction-year analysis) adds depth beyond simple aggregate metrics.
- The identification of Sachs' hybrid construction process as a success factor offers actionable guidance for future benchmark creators.
- Clearly highlights the particular vulnerability of LLM-based methods to outdated benchmarks, which is timely given the rapid growth of this research stream.

## Limitations

- **LLM verifier accuracy**: The Qwen3-30B model achieves 90% accuracy on manual validation, but at scale across 38,081 papers, this implies ~3,800 potentially incorrect labels. More powerful models could improve this but at higher cost.
- **Paper retrieval efficiency**: The query expansion and filtering approach still retrieves many irrelevant papers, and database quality varies (PubMed works well for biomedical domains; others are less effective). Limited open-access full-text availability further constrains the analysis.
- **Excluded benchmarks**: 10 popular benchmarks were excluded due to computational limits, including large benchmarks like Hailfinder, Barley, and WIN95PTS, leaving gaps in the evaluation coverage.
- **Unknown label handling**: Papers labeled "Unknown" (no sufficient information) are excluded from the inconsistency rate calculation, which assumes these are non-informative rather than systematically biased.
- **No proposed solutions for benchmark updating**: While the paper identifies the problem and advocates for benchmark maintenance, it does not propose concrete methodologies for updating existing benchmarks.

## Relevance

本文对因果发现领域的研究者具有重要的警示意义：大量广泛使用的基准数据集已经过时，继续使用它们（尤其是评估基于LLM的方法）可能导致严重误导性的结论。该研究倡导社区重视基准数据集的维护与更新。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How would the inconsistency rates change if evaluated with a more powerful LLM (e.g., GPT-4o, Claude 3.5, or Gemini 1.5 Pro)? Would the relative rankings of benchmarks remain stable?
2. Can the proposed pipeline be adapted into a continuous benchmark monitoring system that automatically flags outdated relationships as new domain papers are published?
3. What concrete methodologies can be developed to update outdated benchmarks like Asia and Child while preserving their historical utility for longitudinal method comparison?
4. How do the inconsistency rates correlate with the actual performance gaps of causal discovery methods when tested on updated versus original benchmark versions?
5. Would including the 10 excluded large benchmarks (Hailfinder, Barley, WIN95PTS, etc.) significantly alter the overall conclusions about pre-2000 benchmark quality?
