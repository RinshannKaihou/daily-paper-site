> **一句话总结：** 本文提出SMET方法，通过优化器状态预热和密度感知学习率缩放解决了动态稀疏训练在LLM预训练中的不稳定问题，并以仅保存活跃参数梯度/优化器状态的方式大幅降低显存开销。

> **TL;DR:** This paper proposes SMET (Sparse Memory-Efficient Training), which stabilizes Dynamic Sparse Training (DST) for LLM pre-training via optimizer-state warm-up and density-aware learning-rate scaling, while reducing memory by storing gradients and optimizer states only for active parameters.

## 核心贡献

- 首次识别并分析了DST在LLM预训练中的损失尖峰（loss spike）现象，指出新再生长参数在Adam优化器中的"冷启动"效应是训练不稳定的根源。
- 提出SMET框架，结合优化器状态预热（时间步重置 + 线性预热）与密度感知学习率缩放，有效抑制拓扑更新后的损失尖峰。
- 开发了仅对活跃参数保存梯度和优化器状态的稀疏训练实现，显著降低显存占用，同时支持非结构化和块级稀疏模式。
- 在LLaMA 60M–1B模型上的实验表明，SMET在多个稀疏度下均优于LoRA、ReLoRA、GaLore等基线，且大模型对稀疏的容忍度更高。

## Key Contributions

- Identifies and analyzes the loss-spiking phenomenon in DST for LLM pre-training, attributing the root cause to the "cold-start" effect of newly regrown parameters under Adam-based optimization.
- Proposes SMET, a DST framework that combines optimizer-state warm-up (timestep reset + linear warm-up) with density-aware learning-rate scaling to stabilize training after topology updates.
- Develops a memory-efficient sparse optimization implementation that stores gradients and optimizer states only for active parameters, supporting both unstructured and block-wise sparsity.
- Demonstrates on LLaMA models from 60M to 1B parameters that SMET outperforms LoRA, ReLoRA, and GaLore across sparsity levels, with larger models exhibiting greater tolerance to sparsity.

## Methodology

The paper introduces **Sparse Memory-Efficient Training (SMET)**, a Dynamic Sparse Training (DST) framework tailored for LLM pre-training. SMET addresses three core challenges:

**1. Optimizer-State Warm-Up:** The authors identify that newly regrown parameters in DST lack accumulated Adam moments (m, v), causing disproportionately large updates (~3.16× the learning rate) that trigger sharp loss spikes. SMET applies two complementary warm-up mechanisms:
- **Timestep Reset:** Reset the per-parameter timestep $t_i$ for regrown parameters, reactivating Adam's bias correction so early updates are gradient-normalized and bounded.
- **Explicit Linear Warm-Up:** Scale the learning rate linearly from 0 to the target over $W$ steps (default $W=10$), further constraining early updates.

**2. Density-Aware Learning-Rate Scaling:** To compensate for the conservative updates introduced by warm-up and the reduced effective fan-in of sparse layers, SMET scales the learning rate as $\alpha_s = \alpha_0 / \sqrt{d}$, where $d$ is the density. This is motivated by Maximal Update Parameterization ($\mu$P) and ensures comparable update magnitudes to dense training.

**3. Memory-Efficient Sparse Implementation:** Unlike conventional DST that uses binary masks over full dense tensors, SMET stores gradients and optimizer states only for active (non-zero) parameters using index-based representations. Parameters are grouped into blocks of size $B$ to amortize index/timestep metadata overhead. This reduces gradient and optimizer-state memory proportionally to density $d$.

The paper also provides a local stability analysis using L-smoothness, showing that SMET bounds the update norm of regrown parameters and thus controls the quadratic loss term after topology updates.

## Results & Highlights

- **Performance on LLaMA/C4:** SMET achieves validation perplexity very close to dense training across 60M, 130M, 350M, and 1B parameter scales. For example, on LLaMA-1B trained with 13.1B tokens, SMET reaches 15.61 vs. dense 15.56 (Table 1), outperforming GaLore (15.64), ReLoRA (18.33), and LoRA (19.21).
- **Memory Efficiency:** At density $d=0.1$ on LLaMA-1B, SMET substantially reduces memory for gradients and optimizer states while incurring only a small perplexity increase (Figure 3b-c). At $d=0.4$, the memory breakdown shows clear savings over dense training (Figure 4).
- **Block-Wise Sparsity:** SMET remains stable across block sizes up to $B=32$, with only ~1 perplexity point increase vs. unstructured sparsity. This is hardware-friendly and enables practical inference speedups.
- **Generalization Across Regrowth Strategies:** SMET improves over both SET (random regrowth) and RigL (gradient-based regrowth). A gradient-based variant SMET$_{\text{grad}}$ also consistently outperforms vanilla RigL (Table 2), showing the stabilization mechanisms are strategy-agnostic.
- **Scaling Behavior:** Larger models tolerate sparsity better—the perplexity gap between sparse and dense training narrows as model size increases, making SMET particularly attractive for large-scale regimes.
- **Ablation Studies:** Warm-up alone improves stability but has limited performance gains; LR scaling alone helps but is insufficient. Their combination in SMET yields the best results. The optimal update interval is $\Delta T = 100$ steps.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** The work provides a focused, well-motivated advance in applying DST to LLMs. The identification of the Adam cold-start effect as the source of DST instability in LLMs is a meaningful insight, and the combination of warm-up with density-aware LR scaling is a practical contribution. However, the individual components (warm-up, LR scaling) are not entirely new concepts—they are adapted from dense training and parameterization theory to the sparse regime.

**Rigor:** The paper is technically solid. It includes: (1) empirical ablation isolating the cold-start effect, (2) theoretical analysis bounding regrowth updates via L-smoothness, and (3) extensive experiments across model scales, datasets, and sparsity levels. The memory measurements are transparent (batch size 1, BF16, gradient checkpointing disabled).

**Reproducibility:** The authors state that code is publicly available. Experimental details (architecture, hyperparameters, training tokens) are clearly reported, and the setup follows prior work (Zhao et al., 2024) for comparability.

**Significance:** If sparse training can match dense training at scale with meaningful memory savings, it could reduce the hardware barrier for LLM research. The support for block-wise sparsity is particularly important for practical deployment. However, the paper notes that training speedups still depend on sparse kernel support, which remains a practical limitation.

**Red Flags:**
- The memory savings are primarily for gradients and optimizer states; activation memory (which often dominates at larger batch sizes) is not addressed.
- The evaluation is limited to pre-training on C4 and OpenWebText; fine-tuning or downstream task performance is not reported.
- The largest model evaluated is 1B parameters—still far from production-scale LLMs (7B+), though the scaling trend is encouraging.

## Strengths

- Clear diagnosis of the DST instability problem in LLMs with controlled ablations.
- Elegant and simple solution (warm-up + LR scaling) that is easy to implement and generalizes across regrowth strategies.
- Memory-efficient implementation that realizes practical savings, not just theoretical sparsity.
- Strong empirical results with near-dense performance even at high sparsity (d=0.1) on 1B models.
- Support for block-wise sparsity bridges the gap between research and hardware deployment.
- Published at a top-tier venue (ICML) with code release.

## Limitations

- Does not address activation memory or KV-cache memory, which are significant bottlenecks in LLM training and inference.
- No evaluation on downstream tasks or fine-tuning scenarios—perplexity is the sole metric.
- Largest evaluated model is 1B; validation at 7B+ scale is needed to confirm practical applicability.
- Training wall-clock speedups are not demonstrated end-to-end; sparse kernel efficiency remains a prerequisite.
- The theoretical analysis is local (per-update stability) rather than a global convergence guarantee for Adam in the sparse setting.

## Relevance

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does SMET perform on models beyond 1B parameters (e.g., 7B, 13B, 70B), and does the trend of improved sparsity tolerance continue?
- Can SMET be combined with activation sparsity or KV-cache compression techniques for end-to-end memory reduction?
- What is the wall-clock training time comparison against dense training when using optimized sparse kernels (e.g., block-wise sparsity on modern GPUs/TPUs)?
- Does SMET preserve downstream task performance after sparse pre-training, or is additional dense fine-tuning required?
- Can the warm-up and LR scaling principles be extended to other optimizers (e.g., AdamW, Lion, Sophia) or to sparse training in non-transformer architectures?
