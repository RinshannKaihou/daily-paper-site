> **一句话总结：** 本文通过对9,000条MMLU决策轨迹的层间分析，揭示了语言模型的正确答案往往并不稳定——"不稳定但正确"的轨迹占比最高（42.1%），并量化了注意力与MLP模块对答案边界运动的贡献。

> **TL;DR:** This paper analyzes 9,000 MMLU decision trajectories across three 7–8B instruction-tuned models, revealing that correctness and stability are distinct: unstable-correct trajectories are the largest group (42.1%). It quantifies how attention and MLP blocks move the answer margin layer by layer, and shows that while attention provides the clearest local push toward stable-correct answers, MLP carries more transferable late-margin shift under counterfactual substitution.

## 核心贡献

- 提出"决策轨迹"框架，将语言模型的答案选择视为贯穿网络深度的动态路径，而非仅由输出层决定的静态终点。
- 定义三个可读性量纲：**状态**（当前答案边界）、**运动**（层间边界变化）和**边界距离**（距决策翻转的远近），将轨迹划分为稳定正确、稳定错误、不稳定正确、不稳定错误四类。
- 发现**不稳定正确轨迹是最主流群体**（42.1%），远超稳定正确（19.0%）——说明端点准确率掩盖了巨大的内在异质性。
- 利用注意力标量和MLP标量对层间边界漂移进行线性重建，在三个模型上达到 R² = 0.76–0.91 的解释力。
- 通过span删除实验验证：标注为"证据"的文本片段平均提升正确边界2.92个logit单位，而"干扰项"文本平均降低2.18个单位。
- 反事实替换实验表明：MLP替换对最终边界的模拟影响（平均+4.74）大于注意力替换（平均+1.18），但效应量高度依赖于配对协议。

## Key Contributions

- Introduces a **decision trajectory** framework that treats answer selection as a dynamic path through network depth, not merely a static endpoint at the output layer.
- Defines three legible quantities — **state** (current answer margin), **motion** (layer-to-layer margin change), and **boundary distance** (proximity to a decision flip) — to classify trajectories into stable-correct, stable-wrong, unstable-correct, and unstable-wrong regimes.
- Discovers that **unstable-correct trajectories are the dominant group** (42.1%), far exceeding stable-correct (19.0%), showing that endpoint accuracy hides substantial internal heterogeneity.
- Uses attention and MLP scalar summaries to linearly reconstruct layer-to-layer margin drift with R² = 0.76–0.91 across three models.
- Validates via span deletion: evidence-labeled spans raise the correct margin by +2.92 logit units on average, while distractor-labeled spans lower it by −2.18 units.
- Shows through counterfactual substitution that MLP replacement has larger simulated effects on final margin (+4.74) than attention replacement (+1.18), though effect sizes are protocol-sensitive.

## Methodology

The study analyzes **three instruction-tuned 7–8B open-weight models** — Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, and Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 — on **3,000 four-choice MMLU prompts**, yielding **9,000 model-prompt trajectories** and **276,000 layerwise decision records**.

**Trajectory observables.** At each layer, the authors read out scores for answer options A–D at the answer position. They compute:
- **Margin:** score(correct) − score(strongest incorrect competitor). Positive = correct leads.
- **Drift:** change in margin from one layer to the next.
- **Boundary distance:** |margin|, so small values mean the trajectory is close to flipping its preferred answer.

**Trajectory classification.** Four operational types are defined by tail-window behavior:
- **Stable-correct (SC):** final answer correct and stable in the network tail.
- **Stable-wrong (SW):** final answer wrong but stable.
- **Unstable-correct (UC):** final answer correct but with continuing late movement.
- **Unstable-wrong (UW):** final answer wrong with continuing late movement.

**Traced subset (600 prompts per model, 150 per type).** Stored traces record at each layer:
- The attention scalar: how much attention blocks move the margin.
- The MLP scalar: how much MLP blocks move the margin.
These are scalar summaries, not head- or neuron-level probes.

**Span deletion intervention.** Contiguous prompt spans are labeled as *evidence*, *distractor*, or *neutral*. Removing a span and re-measuring the margin tests causal influence. Controls include shuffled labels and sign-flipped labels.

**Counterfactual substitution.** For failing target trajectories, the authors replay the margin forward after (1) removing a component scalar, or (2) substituting the attention or MLP scalar sequence from a stable-correct source trajectory (layers 20–27). Two pairing protocols are used: all-pairs and legacy first-source.

## Results & Highlights

**Trajectory regime distribution.** Across all 9,000 trajectories:
| Type | Count | Share |
|------|-------|-------|
| Unstable-correct | 3,790 | 42.1% |
| Unstable-wrong | 2,402 | 26.7% |
| Stable-correct | 1,711 | 19.0% |
| Stable-wrong | 1,097 | 12.2% |

The key surprise is that **most correct answers are not settled** — unstable-correct is the largest group. Endpoint accuracy conflates "correct" with "settled," which are separable properties.

**Mechanistic accounting of drift.** A linear combination of attention + MLP scalars reconstructs held-out drift with:
- Qwen2.5-7B: R² = 0.90
- Llama-3.1-8B: R² = 0.76
- Mistral-7B-v0.3: R² = 0.91

**Component contributions by trajectory type.** In the balanced traced subset:
- **Stable-correct:** average attention scalar = +0.14 (positive), average MLP scalar = −0.22 (negative).
- **Stable-wrong:** average attention = −0.16, average MLP = −0.18.
Thus, **attention is the clearest positive local contributor** to stable-correct margin growth.

**Span deletion effects.**
| Span label | n | Mean effect | Median effect |
|------------|---|-------------|---------------|
| Evidence | 2,156 | +2.92 | +1.93 |
| Distractor | 1,965 | −2.18 | −1.47 |
| Neutral | 79 | 0.00 | 0.00 |

Controls validate the result: shuffled labels drop to +0.48, sign-flipped control is ~0.00. Removing answer-supporting text hurts the margin; removing distractor-like text helps it.

**Counterfactual substitution (all-pairs):**
| Component | Mean shift | Positive rate |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| Attention | +1.18 | 53.0% |
| MLP | +4.74 | 78.0% |

Under the **legacy first-source** protocol, MLP shifts the margin by +5.75 (85.0% positive), while attention shifts it by −0.31. The authors stress these answer *different* questions: local layer-by-layer push (attention wins) vs. transferable late-margin shift under replay (MLP wins).

**Recurrence errors.** Absolute recurrence error grows in late layers, limiting claims based on long-horizon linearized bookkeeping.

## Authors & Credibility

- **Shailesh Rana** — Independent Researcher. No institutional affiliation is listed.
- The paper is a single-author work, which is relatively uncommon in large-scale mechanistic interpretability studies.
- The study uses **9,000 trajectories × up to 32 layers** per model, which is a substantial empirical scope for an independent effort.
- **Reproducibility:** Code and derived artifacts are publicly available at `github.com/gutpuncture/The-Shape-of-Wisdom`. The paper states it is generated from stored artifacts only; no new model inference is required to rebuild figures, tables, and manuscript. This is a strong reproducibility commitment.
- **Limitations acknowledged:** The paper explicitly notes its scope is narrow by design (four-choice MMLU, three 7–8B instruction-tuned models, cached answer-position readouts). It does not claim a full hidden-state circuit, a universal law, or a phase transition.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** The core conceptual move — treating answer selection as a trajectory rather than an endpoint — is simple but powerful. It reframes a large body of interpretability work (logit lens, tuned lens, patching) in terms of dynamical regimes. The three-quantity coordinate system (state, motion, boundary distance) is a useful operationalization that could generalize to other readout-based analyses.

**Rigor:** The statistical controls are well-executed. Shuffled and sign-flipped controls for span deletion, held-out R² for drift reconstruction, and explicit discussion of recurrence errors all demonstrate methodological care. The counterfactual substitution results are appropriately caveated as "protocol-sensitive."

**Reproducibility:** Excellent. Public code repo, artifact-driven manuscript generation, and clear operational definitions make this highly reproducible.

**Significance:** The finding that **unstable-correct trajectories dominate** is important for anyone interpreting model accuracy as a measure of "understanding." It suggests that many "correct" answers are fragile — hovering near a decision boundary until late layers. This has implications for model evaluation, adversarial robustness, and distillation.

**Red flags / concerns:**
- The author is unaffiliated; while the work is thorough, there is no peer review or institutional oversight.
- The trajectory classification (SC/SW/UC/UW) is operational, not derived from a theoretical model. The cutoff for "stable" is a tail-window heuristic; different thresholds could shift group sizes.
- The traced subset uses a *slightly different* one-token scoring rule than the full panel. The authors acknowledge this and keep claims separate, but it is a real limitation.
- Counterfactual substitution results are sensitive to pairing protocol and recurrence errors grow with depth, so the simulated "causal" effects should be interpreted as accounting, not full causal identification.

## Strengths

- **Large-scale, systematic empirical study** across three major open-weight models with consistent findings.
- **Conceptual clarity:** The trajectory framework is intuitive and immediately applicable to other readout-based interpretability work.
- **Strong controls:** Shuffled labels, sign-flipped controls, held-out validation, and recurrence error tracking.
- **Honest scope delimitation:** The paper repeatedly states what it does *not* claim, which builds trust.
- **Practical utility:** The three observables (state, motion, boundary distance) could be used as real-time diagnostics during model training or evaluation.
- **Open science:** Public code and artifact-based reproducibility.

## Limitations

- **Narrow domain:** All claims are restricted to four-choice MMLU prompts; generalization to open-ended generation, coding, or reasoning tasks is untested.
- **Model scale:** Only 7–8B instruction-tuned models are studied. Trajectory dynamics may differ qualitatively at larger scales.
- **Scoring rule mismatch:** The traced subset uses a different token-scoring rule than the full trajectory panel, requiring careful separation of claims.
- **Operational taxonomy:** The four trajectory types are classification heuristics, not theoretically derived regimes. The stability threshold is arbitrary.
- **Scalar aggregation:** Attention and MLP "scalars" are aggregate summaries, not head- or neuron-level attributions. They account for drift but do not identify specific circuits.
- **Counterfactual sensitivity:** Substitution effects depend heavily on pairing protocol and recurrence errors accumulate over long-horizon replays.
- **Single author / no peer review:** The quality is high, but the work has not undergone external review.

## Relevance

这项研究为理解语言模型的"正确但不稳定"行为提供了可量化的框架，对模型评估、对抗鲁棒性分析和知识蒸馏都具有直接的实践参考价值。其轨迹分类方法和三层可读量纲可推广至其他基于读出的可解释性研究。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How do decision trajectories change with model scale? Do larger models (e.g., 70B+) exhibit higher proportions of stable-correct trajectories, or does the instability persist?
2. Can the trajectory framework be extended beyond multiple-choice QA to open-ended generation, chain-of-thought reasoning, or tool-use scenarios?
3. What happens to trajectory stability during fine-tuning or RLHF? Does alignment training increase stable-correct proportions, or does it introduce new unstable-correct artifacts?
4. Can head-level or neuron-level attribution be combined with the scalar accounting to identify *which* attention heads or MLP neurons drive the margin motion in specific trajectory types?
5. Does the unstable-correct regime correlate with specific prompt properties (e.g., ambiguity, domain, or adversarial perturbation)? Can we predict instability from prompt features alone?
6. How do the trajectory dynamics change when models use chain-of-thought or scratchpad reasoning? Does the extra computation stabilize answers or introduce new late-layer movement?
7. Can the span-deletion intervention be automated (e.g., via gradient-based saliency or LLM-based span labeling) to scale causal attribution beyond manual annotation?
