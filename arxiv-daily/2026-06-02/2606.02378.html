When Do Attention Circuits Form? Developmental Trajectories of Capability and Attention-Sink Emergence Across Three 1B-Class Architectures

arXiv: 2606.02378

Date: 2026-06-02

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.02378

一句话总结： 本文在三个1B级语言模型（Pythia 1B、OLMo 1B、OLMoE 1B-7B）的30个对数间隔检查点上，追踪归纳头（induction head）与BOS吸引子头（attention sink）的形成轨迹，发现DCLM训练的模型中归纳电路在20–25B tokens时即已形成，而注意力下沉现象则滞后10–20倍tokens才出现，且两者的涌现形态截然不同。
TL;DR: This paper tracks the developmental trajectories of induction heads and BOS-attractor (attention-sink) heads across 30 log-spaced checkpoints in three 1B-class models spanning two architectures (dense, MoE) and two pretraining corpora (The Pile, DCLM). The key finding is that on DCLM data, induction-circuit formation (~20–25B tokens) and attention-sink formation (~260–420B tokens) are temporally separable by 10–20× in tokens and exhibit qualitatively different emergence shapes. The L0/L1 zero-BOS floor is a universal architectural constraint, and the capability-specific screen converges to ~67% of the final induction circuit within the first ~1% of training.

核心贡献

  • F1 — L0/L1零BOS底层是架构属性而非学习结果： 在全部三个模型的全部30个检查点上，第0层和第1层从未出现任何被分类为BOS吸引子的注意力头。这一底层不受训练数据（Pile vs DCLM）或架构（dense vs MoE）影响。
  • F2 — BOS吸引子形成呈现三种不同形态： Pythia 1B（Pile, dense）呈渐进式斜坡；OLMo 1B（DCLM, dense）在相邻检查点间从7%骤跳到70%，呈现尖锐相变；OLMoE 1B-7B（DCLM, MoE）再次呈渐进式斜坡，但最终饱和比例更低。
  • F3 — DCLM模型中归纳电路比注意力下沉早形成10–20倍tokens： OLMo 1B中归纳头在23B tokens形成，BOS-50%在264B tokens；OLMoE中归纳头在20B tokens形成，BOS-50%在419B tokens。两者是两个独立训练事件，不能被混为单一的"归纳相变"。
  • F4 — 能力筛选器在~1%训练量内收敛到最终电路的约67%： 中间检查点（10–40B tokens）已能识别大部分最终归纳电路，意味着电路发现无需等到训练结束。
  • F5 — 每个最终检查点的归纳头，其参与率（PR）光谱信号在能力选择性超过阈值之前或同时即已升高： PR是能力形成的前置指标，而非仅反映已形成的能力。

Key Contributions

  • F1 — The L0/L1 zero-BOS floor is an architectural property, not a learned outcome. Across all 30 checkpoints in all three models, layers 0 and 1 never produce any BOS-attractor-classified heads. This floor is invariant to training data (Pile vs DCLM) and architecture (dense vs MoE).
  • F2 — BOS-attractor formation follows three qualitatively distinct shapes. Pythia 1B (Pile, dense) shows a gradual monotonic ramp. OLMo 1B (DCLM, dense) exhibits a sharp phase transition, jumping from 7% to 70% BOS-classified heads between adjacent checkpoints. OLMoE 1B-7B (DCLM, MoE) shows a gradual ramp again but with a lower final saturation.
  • F3 — In DCLM models, induction-circuit formation precedes BOS-attractor formation by 10–20× in tokens. In OLMo 1B, induction forms at 23B tokens while BOS-50% is reached at 264B tokens. In OLMoE, induction forms at 20B tokens while BOS-50% is reached at 419B tokens. These are two distinct training-time transitions, not a single "induction phase transition."
  • F4 — The capability-specific screen converges to ~67% of the final induction circuit within ~1% of total training tokens. Intermediate checkpoints (10–40B tokens) already identify the majority of the final induction circuit, meaning circuit discovery does not require the fully trained model.
  • F5 — For every final-checkpoint induction head sampled, per-head participation ratio (PR) is elevated at or before the first revision where that head crosses its capability-selectivity threshold. PR is a leading indicator of capability formation, not merely a post-hoc signature.

Methodology

The study applies a three-step mechanistic-interpretability recipe (defined in detail in a companion methodology paper) across 10 log-spaced checkpoints per model:

  1. Synthetic induction batch: 2,000 sequences of length 256 with structure [filler] A B [filler] A, where A and B are random tokens. The induction target is B at the position following the second A.
  2. Per-head participation ratio (PR): For each (layer, head, checkpoint), extract per-head attention outputs at the second-A position over the batch. Compute the singular spectrum and participation ratio PR = exp(H(p)), where H(p) is the Shannon entropy of the normalized squared singular values. This spectral signal measures output diversity.
  3. Capability-pattern screen: For each head, compute attention selectivity to canonical target positions for six classes: induction, previous-token, duplicate-token, first-token/BOS, self, and local. Selectivity is target attention divided by a uniform-other baseline. A head is assigned to a class if its max selectivity exceeds 30×; it is admitted to a circuit if class selectivity exceeds 50× (induction, BOS) or 100× (previous-token).
  4. All-head capability-specific screen: In the attention-sink-dominated regime (≥70% of heads classify as first-token), best-class ranking surfaces BOS heads ahead of capability heads. For specific capability identification, the screen takes all heads with selectivity for that class above threshold regardless of best class.
  5. Causal verification: Group-ablation of screen-identified circuits by zeroing their residual contribution, with matched random controls. Top-1 accuracy on the synthetic batch is the primary metric.

The 30-run panel spans Pythia 1B (GPT-NeoX dense, The Pile, 300B tokens), OLMo 1B (Llama-style dense, DCLM, 3.05T tokens), and OLMoE 1B-7B (Llama-style MoE, DCLM, 5.12T tokens).

Results & Highlights

L0/L1 zero-BOS floor (Finding 1): In every model at every checkpoint, layers 0 and 1 produce zero BOS-classified heads. This is not learned — it holds from essentially random initialization through trillions of tokens. Sinks emerge mid-layer-outward, not bottom-up: the first sinks appear in L6–L14 (Pythia), L4 (OLMo), and L7–L13 (OLMoE), while layers just above the floor (L2, L3) trail significantly or never participate.

Three emergence shapes (Finding 2): The whole-model BOS-class fraction follows distinct trajectories:

  • Pythia 1B: gradual ramp from 0% → 10.9% (6B) → 46.1% (80B) → 57.8% (300B)
  • OLMo 1B: sharp phase transition — 0% through 52B, 7.4% at 117B, then 70.3% at 264B, saturating at ~80.9%
  • OLMoE 1B-7B: gradual ramp from 3.5% (20B) → 42.2% (104B) → 50.8% (419B) → 75.8% (5.1T)

The data×architecture interaction is key: same DCLM data produces sharp transition in dense OLMo but gradual ramp in MoE OLMoE.

Temporal separation (Finding 3): In DCLM models, induction and BOS-attractor formation are separated by an order of magnitude in tokens:

  • OLMo 1B: induction at 23B, BOS-50% at 264B (11.5× gap)
  • OLMoE 1B-7B: induction at 20B, BOS-50% at 419B (21× gap)
  • Pythia 1B: induction and BOS-10% co-emerge at ~6B, but finer checkpoints might resolve this

Early convergence (Finding 4): The capability screen reaches 67% recall of the final induction circuit at 0.3–2.0% of total training tokens (6B for Pythia, 10B for OLMo, 41B for OLMoE). The core circuit is identifiable from checkpoints two orders of magnitude smaller than the final model.

PR as leading indicator (Finding 5): Across all 10 sampled induction heads (3 in Pythia, 3 in OLMo, 4 in OLMoE), per-head PR rises 1–2 checkpoints before induction selectivity crosses the 50× threshold. For example, Pythia L4·H4's PR jumps from 2.58 to 27.35 between 0.13B and 0.54B tokens, while induction selectivity remains below 4× until 6.3B tokens — a ~12× lead in token count.

Activation-parameter bridge (Section 10.3): A modest positive association exists between activation PR and weight stable rank (Spearman ρ = +0.32 pooled), with growth co-evolution (ρ = +0.30–0.39). However, weight spectra do not predict induction selectivity (|ρ| ≤ 0.29), confirming PR is a general specialization signal, not capability-specific.

Authors & Credibility

Yongzhong Xu (single author) is conducting a systematic research program on mechanistic interpretability and emergence during pretraining, with multiple companion papers (methodology, pattern selectivity, spectral-edge dynamics, gradient-direction sensitivity) that together form a coherent research agenda. The paper uses well-established public model suites (Pythia, OLMo, OLMoE) with published checkpoint sequences, and all code/data/scripts are released on GitHub. The methodology builds directly on prior work by Olsson et al. (2022) on induction heads and Xiao et al. (2024) on attention sinks, positioning itself clearly within established literature.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: High. The paper makes a valuable conceptual distinction that was underappreciated in the literature: "induction phase transition" and "attention-sink emergence" have often been discussed under the umbrella of "attention emergence during pretraining," but this work demonstrates they are mechanistically and temporally separable in 1B-class DCLM models. The finding that architecture (dense vs MoE) changes not just the asymptotic BOS fraction but the shape of the emergence curve is particularly insightful.

Rigor: Generally strong, with clear operational definitions and quantitative thresholds. The use of 30 mechanistic-interpretability runs across three models is substantial. However, several issues merit scrutiny:

  • The 10-checkpoint log-spaced grid is coarse for resolving phase transitions. The OLMo "sharp jump" from 7% to 70% occurs between two adjacent checkpoints; the true transition could be smoother or sharper at finer resolution. The author acknowledges this explicitly.
  • The BOS-attractor is identified purely by attention pattern selectivity to position 0, not by causal impact on model behavior. While this is consistent with Xiao et al.'s definition, it means the paper identifies "heads that attend to BOS" rather than "heads that causally implement the attention-sink phenomenon."
  • The null-selectivity noise floor is validated only on Pythia 1B; the claim that it is "stable across training" is only directly demonstrated for one model.

Reproducibility: Good. The models, checkpoints, and evaluation batch are all public and deterministic (seed 42). Code and data are released. However, OLMoE evaluations are expensive (top-8 routing across 64 experts), and the paper sampled only 8 revisions for OLMoE rather than 10, which slightly weakens the cross-architecture comparability.

Significance: The paper provides important empirical constraints for any mechanistic theory of attention sinks: the L0/L1 floor, the data-dependent transition shape, and the MoE smoothing-and-reduction effect. The practical recommendation that circuit discovery can use intermediate checkpoints at ~1% of training is valuable for interpretability-driven training budgets.

Red flags:

  • Single seed per model: The author explicitly notes this limitation — the findings describe "what these specific runs produced" rather than "what the configuration produces in expectation." Given the cost of re-pretraining 1B-class models, this is understandable but limits generalizability.
  • Synthetic batch generalization: The induction circuit is identified on a controlled synthetic batch. The paper cross-validates this at final checkpoint in the companion methodology paper, but does not repeat per-revision natural-text validation.
  • Self-citation density: The paper references four companion papers by the same author, all from 2026. While this reflects a genuine research program, readers should note that the methodology's full validation is distributed across these unpublished (or very recent) companion works.

Strengths

  • Clean experimental design with strong factorization: Pythia vs OLMo isolates corpus; OLMo vs OLMoE isolates architecture
  • Quantitative, threshold-based operational definitions that reduce subjective circuit identification
  • Honest and detailed discussion of limitations (checkpoint resolution, single seed, synthetic batch)
  • Practical methodological recommendations (use intermediate checkpoints, distinguish induction from sink emergence, use all-head capability screen when BOS > 70%)
  • The PR-leading-selectivity finding provides a useful early-warning signal for circuit formation

Limitations

  • Checkpoint resolution: 10 log-spaced revisions may miss finer-grained transition structure, especially for Pythia's co-emergence at ~6B and OLMo's sharp jump
  • Single seed per architecture: No within-architecture variance estimates at 1B scale
  • Synthetic evaluation batch: Trajectory timings might differ on natural-text induction targets
  • OLMoE sampling: Only 8 revisions (vs 10 for others) due to compute cost, and earliest available checkpoint is 20B tokens
  • Limited capability taxonomy: Six pattern classes do not cover composed tasks like IOI; cross-task generalization is deferred to a companion paper
  • Causal claims: BOS-attractor identification is pattern-based, not causally verified via ablation in this paper

Relevance

对于关注大语言模型可解释性与涌现机制的研究者，本文提供了关于注意力电路和注意力下沉现象何时形成的关键实证约束，尤其是在预训练动态与架构设计（dense vs MoE）之间的交互关系方面，具有重要的理论和实践参考价值。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Would finer-grained checkpoints (e.g., linear-spaced rather than log-spaced around the suspected transition) resolve Pythia's ~6B co-emergence into two distinct events, or confirm it as genuine co-emergence?
  2. Does the 10–20× token gap between induction and BOS-attractor formation persist at larger scales (7B, 70B), or does it shrink/expand with model size?
  3. Can a parameter-space theory (e.g., spectral-edge dynamics) predict the three distinct BOS-attractor emergence shapes from weight-matrix trajectories alone, without activation-based screening?
  4. What is the mechanistic role of the L2/L3 layers that trail the mid-layer sink emergence — do they serve a distinct computational function that makes them resistant to sink installation?
  5. Does MoE's smoothing effect on BOS-attractor dynamics stem from expert specialization (some experts learning sink behavior while others retain capability patterns), or from a more fundamental property of sparse routing?
  6. Would training on a corpus with artificially manipulated BOS-token frequency reproduce the data-dependent transition shape, providing stronger causal evidence for the data-composition claim?
