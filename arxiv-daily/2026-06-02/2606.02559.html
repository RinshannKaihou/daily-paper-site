From Layers to Submodules: Rethinking Granularity in Replacement-Based LLM Compression

arXiv: 2606.02559

Date: 2026-06-02

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.02559

一句话总结： 本文提出 SubFit，一种在子模块（Attention 和 FFN）级别进行非连续选择与轻量残差替换的后训练压缩方法，突破了现有方法全层连续移除的局限，在 10 个 LLM 和 5 个稀疏度上取得了最优的困惑度–准确率权衡。
TL;DR: This paper introduces SubFit, a post-training compression method that operates at the submodule (Attention and FFN) level with non-contiguous selection and lightweight fitted residual bypasses, breaking the full-layer contiguous-removal paradigm and achieving the best perplexity–accuracy trade-off across ten LLMs and five sparsity levels.

核心贡献

  • 揭示现有替换压缩方法的设计局限：指出当前基于替换的压缩方法普遍受限于两个设计约束——全层粒度（full-layer granularity）和连续选择（contiguous selection），并论证这种限制过于严格。
  • 提出子模块级替换方法 SubFit：首次在 Attention 和 FFN 子模块级别进行独立、非连续的选择，并为每个被移除的子模块拟合轻量残差旁路（fitted residual bypass），实现更精细的压缩粒度。
  • 建立非对称的补偿结构：Attention 子模块使用逐层独立的低秩基（rank 256），而 FFN 子模块采用跨层共享输入基（rank 4096），在部署参数量与恢复质量之间取得平衡。
  • 在广泛实验上验证优势：在 10 个 LLM（5 个 base、5 个 instruction-tuned）和 12.5%–37.5% 五个稀疏度上，SubFit 在所有评估稀疏度上均取得最优的聚合困惑度–准确率权衡，且在高压缩比下优势更大。

Key Contributions

  • Identified design constraints in replacement-based compression: Existing methods are restricted to full-layer granularity and contiguous selection, which the authors argue is overly restrictive because redundancy in pretrained transformers is neither confined to contiguous regions nor evenly distributed between Attention and FFN outputs.
  • Proposed SubFit (Submodule-level Fitted residual replacement): Compresses LLMs at the submodule level by selecting Attention and FFN submodules non-contiguously across depths, and fitting a lightweight residual bypass for each removed submodule.
  • Designed an asymmetric compensation structure: Attention bypasses use per-layer independent low-rank bases (rank 256), while FFN bypasses share a single input basis across selected layers (rank 4096), balancing deployed parameter cost with recovery quality.
  • Demonstrated strong empirical results: Across ten LLMs and five sparsity levels (12.5%–37.5%), SubFit achieves the best aggregate perplexity–accuracy trade-off at every evaluated sparsity, with margins widening under aggressive compression.

Methodology

SubFit follows a two-stage select-and-replace procedure on pretrained decoder-only Transformers:

  1. Sequential component selection. SubFit first scores and selects Attention submodules, then scores FFN submodules on the partially compressed model. This ordering reflects the greater redundancy of Attention modules and allows FFN selection to account for the residual-stream shift induced by Attention replacement. Selection is non-contiguous: each submodule is chosen independently by score, with no requirement that selected layers form a consecutive block.
  1. Submodule scoring. For Attention, an Impact score combines the rotation of the residual stream with the relative magnitude of the contribution:

$I(h, \delta) = (1 - \cos(h, h+\delta)) \cdot \|\delta\|_2^2 / (\|h\|_2^2 + \epsilon)$.

For FFNs, base models use a replacement-aware score combining removal effect with surrogate reconstruction error; instruction-tuned models use a cosine-only score to avoid poorly calibrated post-SFT magnitude signals.

  1. Fitted residual replacement. Each selected submodule is replaced by a parametric surrogate $F_e(x; \theta)$ that approximates only the net residual contribution rather than internal computation:

$$F_e(x; \theta) = g \odot x + b + (x - \mu) U$$

where $U = V^\top W$ is a low-rank operator (rank $r$). The element-wise gate $g$ restores per-feature scale; bias $b$ and centering $\mu$ absorb mean shifts; and $U$ captures structured cross-feature interactions.

  1. Closed-form fitting. The surrogate is fit analytically from calibration statistics with no gradient-based optimization: $\mu$ and $b$ are set to empirical means; $g$ is recovered via ridge-regularized univariate regression; $V$ is set to the top-$r$ eigenvectors of centered input covariance; and $W$ is recovered by ridge least-squares.
  1. Asymmetric deployment form. Attention bypasses use per-layer independent bases ($U_\ell^{\text{attn}} = V_\ell^\top W_\ell$), reflecting heterogeneous roles across depths. FFN bypasses use a shared input basis ($U_\ell^{\text{ffn}} = V^\top W_\ell$) to limit deployed cost, storing only layer-specific affine terms and output factors. The FFN bypass operates directly on the residual stream dimension $d$, bypassing the much larger inner dimension $d_{ff} \approx 3.5d$, yielding roughly $10\times$ parameter reduction per removed FFN at $r=d$.

Results & Highlights

The authors evaluate SubFit against four post-training, calibration-only replacement baselines—Streamline (FFN), Streamline (Layer), ReplaceMe (LS), and ReplaceMe (Cosine)—on five base LLMs and five instruction-tuned variants across sparsity levels from 12.5% to 37.5%.

  • Perplexity (base models): SubFit attains the lowest PPL degradation in 9 of 10 model–sparsity settings reported at 12.5% and 25%, and is consistently strongest at 25% sparsity. The gap is most pronounced on the Qwen family, where at 25% sparsity ReplaceMe Cosine reaches 5.37×–6.80× degradation while SubFit reduces this to 2.54×–3.18×.
  • Downstream accuracy (instruction-tuned): At 25% sparsity, SubFit obtains the best aggregate downstream accuracy on 4 of 5 models, retaining 84.6% of dense performance on average. It is the only method to retain above 80% accuracy at 25% sparsity and above 73% at 37.5%.
  • Aggregate trade-off: Averaging across models, SubFit achieves the strongest perplexity–accuracy trade-off at every evaluated sparsity level (Figure 1). The PPL gap over the strongest baseline grows monotonically with compression: from 0.11× at 12.5% to 1.92× at 25% and 5.69× at 37.5%.
  • Stability: SubFit’s PPL-factor standard deviation stays bounded (±0.08 → ±1.37), whereas baselines grow by one to two orders of magnitude (e.g., ReplaceMe Cosine reaches ±130.68 at 37.5%).
  • Inference efficiency: At 25% sparsity, TTFT speedup ranges from 1.18× (Llama-3.2-3B) to 1.40× (DeepSeek-7B); decode speedup is 1.12×–1.17×. KV-cache usage scales proportionally with sparsity (e.g., 25% reduction at 25% sparsity).
  • Ablation: Residual compensation is essential—naïve pruning alone causes catastrophic degradation. FFN compensation accounts for most of the recovered quality, while Attention compensation provides refinement. The shared FFN basis matches per-layer quality while reducing basis cost by a factor of $|S_{fn}|$.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. The core insight—that replacement granularity itself is a design choice worth optimizing—is underexplored in the post-training compression literature. Most prior work operates at full-layer or contiguous-block granularity; SubFit convincingly demonstrates that submodule-level non-contiguous selection yields superior trade-offs.
  • Rigor: Strong. The paper includes comprehensive experiments (10 models, 5 sparsity levels, 4 baselines), multiple ablations (shared vs. per-layer basis, rank sensitivity, replacement-aware vs. masked scoring, component-wise compensation), and detailed analysis of deployed parameter and MAC overheads.
  • Reproducibility: Good. The method relies only on calibration data and closed-form fitting, with no gradient-based optimization or hyperparameter search. The authors release code and specify calibration protocols (SlimPajama for base models, SlimOrca for instruction-tuned, 8k samples, sequence length 1024).
  • Significance: Moderate to high. For practitioners deploying LLMs under memory and latency constraints, SubFit offers a practical post-training compression approach that does not require sparse kernels or custom hardware. The widening gains at higher sparsity suggest it extends the usable compression range.
  • Red flags: The evaluation omits GSM8K from the main downstream table due to known sensitivity to prompting and instability under compression of generative reasoning, which is disclosed but limits coverage of math reasoning. The method adds explicit bypass parameters (~10% of removed parameters, ~15% of removed MACs at 25% sparsity), a cost that ReplaceMe avoids by folding transformations into existing weights.

Strengths

  • Practical deployability: SubFit requires only calibration data, no fine-tuning, no sparse kernels, and no custom hardware. The closed-form fitting makes it cheap to apply.
  • Strong and consistent empirical gains: Best aggregate trade-off at every sparsity level, with especially large margins under aggressive compression (25%–37.5%).
  • Well-motivated asymmetric design: The choice of independent Attention bases and shared FFN basis is empirically justified by rank-sensitivity analysis and ablations.
  • Thorough ablation studies: Component-wise compensation, shared vs. per-layer basis, rank sensitivity, and replacement-aware scoring are all systematically investigated.
  • Inference-time benefits: Measurable TTFT/decode speedup and proportional KV-cache reduction confirm that the compression translates to real runtime efficiency.

Limitations

  • Deployed parameter overhead: Unlike ReplaceMe, which folds its transformation into existing weights, SubFit introduces explicit bypass parameters (~10% of removed parameters at 25% sparsity) and adds MACs (~15% of removed MACs). This overhead is stable across sparsity levels but nonzero.
  • Compression pipeline cost: Offline compression takes approximately 2,000–2,600 seconds on average, comparable to ReplaceMe Cosine but slower than Streamline, due to sequential selection and closed-form fitting.
  • No math-reasoning evaluation in main table: GSM8K is relegated to the appendix because of prompt sensitivity and instability, leaving a gap in assessing compression impact on mathematical reasoning.
  • No fairness or safety audit: The authors note that compression may affect model behavior unevenly across tasks, domains, or demographic groups, and their multi-benchmark evaluation does not constitute a complete fairness or safety audit.
  • Not universally dominant: While SubFit wins on aggregate, there are individual model–sparsity settings where the strongest baseline is competitive or slightly better (e.g., Qwen3-8B and DeepSeek-7B at some sparsities).

Relevance

对于关注 LLM 部署效率的研究者和工程师而言，SubFit 提供了一条在子模块粒度上实现后训练压缩的新路径，其无需重训练、无需专用稀疏内核的特性使其具有很强的工程落地价值，特别是在高压缩比场景下能显著优于现有的层级替换基线。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How would SubFit perform when combined with quantization (e.g., INT4/INT8 weight quantization) or other orthogonal compression techniques? Would the gains remain additive?
  2. Can the submodule-level replacement principle be extended to other architectural units, such as individual attention heads, neuron groups, or even non-transformer architectures like Mamba or mixture-of-experts?
  3. What is the impact of SubFit compression on long-context performance and retrieval-augmented generation tasks, given that KV-cache savings are proportional to Attention submodule removal?
  4. Does the shared FFN basis generalize across diverse domains, or would domain-specific calibration data require per-domain shared bases for optimal performance?
  5. Can the explicit bypass parameters be further compressed or quantized without degrading recovery quality, closing the gap with parameter-folding methods like ReplaceMe?
