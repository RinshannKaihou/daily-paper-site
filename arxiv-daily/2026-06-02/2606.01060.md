> **一句话总结：** MENTIS 提出了一种基于几何学的诊断框架，通过测量指令微调（IT）与偏好对齐（PA）模型之间的“潜扭转”（latent torsion）来量化偏好对齐在模型内部计算中留下的结构化、深度局部化的几何印记。

> **TL;DR:** MENTIS introduces a geometry-first framework that quantifies how preference alignment reorganizes internal computation in language models by measuring multi-scale latent torsion between instruction-tuned (IT) and preference-aligned (PA) checkpoints, revealing that alignment-induced changes are selective, entropy-structured, and depth-localized.

## 核心贡献

- **提出了 MENTIS 框架**：首个以几何学为核心的诊断工具，用于测量配对检查点（IT → PA）之间的对齐诱导内部重组，而不仅仅是行为层面的评估。
- **定义了三种互补的扭转度量**：
  - **T1（主扭转范数）**：基于层间协方差矩阵的斜对称分量，测量层间的总旋转能量；
  - **T2（谱扭转诊断）**：衡量旋转信号在各模态间分布的不均匀性；
  - **ERA（能量-辐射-激活）**：用于定位对齐诱导重组最集中的深度层。
- **揭示了选择性重组模式**：对齐引起的几何变化并非均匀分布——规范性概念（如 JUSTICE、PEACE）的扭转位移显著大于事实性概念；扭转与上下文熵呈负相关；峰值效应集中在架构特定的中后层。
- **发现安全提示词的深层重组**：与“浅层拒绝”直觉相反，安全提示词在对齐后诱导的扭转位移大于不安全提示词，表明对齐修改的是合规行为的内部方向几何，而非仅附加拒绝过滤。
- **跨模型验证**：在 OLMo-2-7B、Mistral-7B、Llama-3.1-8B 和 Tülu-3-8B 四对 7–8B 模型上的实验结果一致。

## Key Contributions

- **Introduces the MENTIS framework**: A geometry-first diagnostic tool that measures alignment-induced internal reorganization between paired checkpoints (IT → PA), going beyond behavior-level evaluation.
- **Defines three complementary torsion metrics**:
  - **T1 (primary torsion norm)**: Measures total rotational energy from the skew-symmetric component of cross-layer covariance matrices;
  - **T2 (spectral torsion diagnostic)**: Measures unevenness of rotational signal distribution across modes;
  - **ERA (Energy-Radiance-Activation)**: Localizes the depth layer where alignment-induced reorganization peaks.
- **Reveals selective reorganization patterns**: Alignment-induced geometric change is not uniform—normative concepts (e.g., JUSTICE, PEACE) show significantly larger torsion shifts than factual concepts; torsion negatively correlates with contextual entropy; peak effects concentrate in architecture-specific mid-to-late layers.
- **Discovers deep reorganization in safe prompts**: Contrary to the "shallow refusal" intuition, safe prompts induce larger torsional shifts than unsafe prompts after alignment, suggesting that alignment modifies the internal directional geometry of compliant behavior rather than merely appending refusal filters.
- **Cross-model validation**: Consistent findings across four 7–8B model pairs: OLMo-2-7B, Mistral-7B, Llama-3.1-8B, and Tülu-3-8B.

## Methodology

MENTIS operationalizes "belief" as a task-conditioned internal direction: the local direction in hidden-state space that increases support for a target continuation under a specified prompt, layer, and readout. This avoids over-interpreting belief as a human-like mental state or symbolic proposition.

For a paired setup with IT model $\theta^{(0)}$ and PA model $\theta^{(1)}$, MENTIS defines a layerwise directional field using the unembedding operator $W_U$:

$$p_{\theta^{(m)},\ell}(y \mid x) = \text{softmax}\left(W_U h_\ell^{(m)}(x)\right)_y$$

The layerwise directional field is the gradient:

$$v_\ell^{(m)}(x) := \nabla_{h_\ell^{(m)}(x)} \log p_{\theta^{(m)},\ell}(y \mid x)$$

**Angular torsion** measures cumulative directional turning across depth:

$$\omega_\ell^{(m)}(x) = \arccos\frac{\langle v_\ell^{(m)}(x), v_{\ell+1}^{(m)}(x) \rangle}{\|v_\ell^{(m)}(x)\| \|v_{\ell+1}^{(m)}(x)\|}, \quad T^{(m)}(x) = \sum_{\ell=1}^{L-1} \omega_\ell^{(m)}(x)$$

**Matrix torsion (T1/T2)** operates on token-by-hidden-state matrices. For $C_\ell^{(m)}$, the cross-layer covariance is $M_\ell^{(m)} = \frac{1}{T}(C_\ell^{(m)})^\top C_{\ell+1}^{(m)}$. Its skew-symmetric component $S_\ell^{(m)}$ isolates rotational change:

$$\tau_{T1,\ell}^{(m)} = \|S_\ell^{(m)}\|_F, \quad \tau_{T2,\ell}^{(m)} = \text{Var}\left\{|\Im\lambda_j(S_\ell^{(m)})| : \lambda_j \neq 0\right\}$$

**ERA** normalizes the layerwise torsion-difference profile and selects the peak layer:

$$\ell^\star = \arg\max_\ell \text{Norm}\left(\tau_{T1,\ell}^{(1)} - \tau_{T1,\ell}^{(0)}\right)$$

The framework operates at three linked scales: **word level** (concept-conditioned torsion shifts), **prompt level** (safe vs. unsafe prompt families), and **model level** (layerwise profiles and band-concentration ratios across architectures).

Practical estimation uses 64-dimensional PCA projection and $\epsilon$-clipped cosine similarity for numerical stability. Same-model null comparisons produce torsion shifts below $10^{-4}$, confirming measured IT→PA differences are not dominated by noise.

Experiments use the LITMUS benchmark (20,439 prompts, 7 value axioms, 18 concepts, 22,631 content words) and compare MENTIS against CKA, cosine distance, and RepE baselines. Statistical tests use Mann–Whitney U with Holm–Bonferroni correction, Cohen's $d$ for effect size, and 95% bootstrap CIs with 2,000 resamples.

## Results & Highlights

1. **Torsion separates alignment change from generic drift**: MENTIS achieves concept-level coefficient of variation $CV = 0.64$ versus $CV_{CKA} = 0.08$ and $CV_{RepE} = 0.21$. For safe–unsafe discrimination, MENTIS reaches AUC = 0.89 (95% CI [0.85, 0.93]) while cosine distance reaches only 0.64. Same-model null comparisons yield $\Delta\tau < 10^{-4}$.

2. **Semantic selectivity**: Normative concepts show larger T1 shifts than factual concepts: $\Delta T_{1,\text{normative}} = 1.74$ vs. $\Delta T_{1,\text{factual}} = 1.47$, with $d = 1.8$ (95% CI [1.2, 2.4]), $p_{\text{perm}} < 0.001$. Even within related normative regions (JUSTICE, PEACE, PROTEST), torsion responses differ, confirming fine-grained rather than coarse-grained reorganization.

3. **Negative entropy correlation**: Torsion decreases with contextual entropy. In OLMo, $\rho = -0.164$ ($p = 3.04 \times 10^{-6}$); in Mistral, $\rho = -0.387$ ($p = 5.43 \times 10^{-30}$). Alignment rotates internal directions most strongly in structured, lower-entropy contexts, not in maximally uncertain ones.

4. **Architecture-specific depth localization (ERA)**: Peak layers vary by architecture—OLMo-2 and Tülu-3 peak at layers 29–30; Llama-3.1 at layer 20; Mistral-7B at layer 14. Alignment-induced torsion is model-family specific, not at a universal transformer depth.

5. **Safe prompts exceed unsafe prompts**: Safe prompts induce larger torsional shifts than unsafe prompts across all four architectures ($p < 10^{-19}$ after correction). This challenges shallow-refusal accounts and suggests alignment reorganizes the internal geometry of compliant, norm-consistent behavior rather than merely attaching refusal filters to harmful inputs.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty**: MENTIS offers a genuinely new angle by framing alignment as a geometric transformation of task-conditioned internal directions rather than a behavioral or preference-data audit. The paired-checkpoint, multi-scale torsion approach distinguishes it from prior work on factual localization, static residual-stream geometry, and representation engineering. The three-metric design (T1/T2/ERA) is conceptually elegant.

**Rigor**: The statistical protocol is thorough—multiple comparison correction, bootstrap CIs, same-model nulls, and effect-size reporting. The PCA projection and epsilon-clipped cosine similarity controls address numerical stability concerns. However, absolute torsion shifts are small ($\Delta\tau \in [0.002, 0.008]$ against baseline ~0.97–0.99), so the framework detects structure in small signals rather than large movements.

**Reproducibility**: The paper specifies models, benchmark, hyperparameters, and projection details. Release of code and data supports reproducibility. The operational definition of "belief" is explicit, reducing interpretive ambiguity.

**Significance**: For alignment research, the finding that safe prompts induce larger internal reorganization than unsafe prompts is counter-intuitive and potentially impactful—it redirects attention from "refusal directions" to "compliance geometry." The architecture-specific depth addresses (ERA) provide actionable targets for future patching or steering experiments.

**Red flags / concerns**:
- **Correlation vs. causation**: The paper repeatedly and appropriately notes this limitation, but readers may still over-interpret ERA peaks as causal loci.
- **Benchmark concentration**: LITMUS is well-suited but narrow. The observed semantic selectivity may be shaped by the benchmark's value-axiom structure.
- **Metric identifiability**: Torsion is one geometric observable among many; the authors acknowledge alternatives (Fisher distance, Jacobian alignment) could capture related structure.
- **Scale restriction**: 7–8B models only; larger models or MoE architectures may show different depth profiles.
- **Proxy dependence**: The directional field depends on the chosen readout, target token, and local linearization. Different choices could yield different profiles.

## Strengths

- Clear operational definition of "belief" that avoids anthropomorphic over-interpretation
- Multi-scale framework (word / prompt / model level) with consistent patterns across all three
- Strong baseline comparisons showing MENTIS adds discriminative power beyond CKA, cosine distance, and RepE
- Counter-intuitive empirical finding (safe > unsafe torsion) that challenges prevailing shallow-refusal narratives
- Architecture-specific depth localization provides concrete, testable hypotheses for follow-up causal experiments
- Transparent limitations section and same-model null controls
- Bilingual-ready conceptual clarity; geometric framework is representation-language-agnostic

## Limitations

- **Diagnostic, not causal**: MENTIS identifies geometric correlates of alignment but does not establish necessity or sufficiency for aligned behavior. Causal validation requires activation patching or steering at ERA-identified layers.
- **Small absolute effect sizes**: $\Delta\tau$ values are tiny relative to baseline torsion. The contribution is structural pattern detection, not large-magnitude measurement.
- **Benchmark scope**: Primary evaluation on LITMUS limits generalizability to refusal benchmarks, open-ended instruction corpora, or multilingual settings.
- **Scale and architecture**: Only 7–8B decoder-only models tested; ERA peaks should not be treated as universal.
- **Alignment-family restriction**: Focuses on DPO/RLHF-style preference alignment; Constitutional AI, RLAIF, or tool-use alignment may show different signatures.
- **Statistical dependence**: Prompts sharing templates or lexical structure are not fully independent; hierarchical models would strengthen inference.
- **English-only word analysis**: Cross-lingual and full-sequence torsion patterns remain unexplored.

## Relevance

MENTIS 为理解大语言模型对齐的“黑箱”提供了新的几何学视角，其发现——安全提示词比不安全提示词引发更大的内部重组——对当前过度关注“拒绝方向”的安全研究具有重要的纠偏意义，对从事模型可解释性与 AI 安全的研究者极具参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. **Causal validation at ERA peaks**: Does activation patching or directional steering at the ERA-identified layer ($\ell^\star$) selectively disrupt aligned behavior while preserving base capabilities? Can we construct a causal graph linking torsion magnitude to refusal/helpfulness outputs?

2. **Scaling law of torsion**: How do torsion profiles and ERA peaks evolve as model scale increases from 7B to 70B+ parameters? Do larger models show stronger concentration, broader distribution, or qualitatively different depth patterns?

3. **Cross-lingual and cross-modal extension**: Do torsion signatures transfer across languages, scripts, and morphological systems? Can MENTIS be adapted to multimodal models (vision-language) where alignment involves cross-modal preference optimization?

4. **Alternative alignment regimes**: How do torsion patterns differ under Constitutional AI, RLAIF, rejection sampling, safety-specific SFT, or tool-use alignment? Are some alignment methods more "torsional" than others?

5. **Real-time monitoring**: Can MENTIS metrics be computed efficiently enough to serve as an online diagnostic during RLHF training, potentially detecting misalignment or over-optimization before behavioral degradation appears?
