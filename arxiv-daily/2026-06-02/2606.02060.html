Where Do Deep-Research Agents Go Wrong? Span-Level Error Localization in Agent Trajectories

arXiv: 2606.02060

Date: 2026-06-02

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.02060

一句话总结： 本文提出了首个面向深度研究智能体的轨迹级错误定位基准 TELBench 和声明中心审计框架 DRIFT，通过分析 2,790 条真实轨迹揭示了智能体失败的关键机制，并在错误定位任务上将模型表现提升了最多 30 个百分点。
TL;DR: This paper introduces TELBench, the first span-level error-localization benchmark for deep-research agents, and DRIFT, a claim-centric auditing framework. By analyzing 2,790 real trajectories, it reveals key failure mechanisms and improves error localization by up to 30 percentage points over bare LLM baselines.

核心贡献

  • 大规模轨迹语料库：收集了 2,790 条真实的深度研究智能体轨迹，覆盖两个智能体框架（MiroFlow、OAgent）、三个前沿基座模型（GPT-5、Gemini-2.5-Pro、Claude-Sonnet-4.5）和三个基准测试（GAIA、XBench、BrowseComp）。
  • 过程级错误定位基准 TELBench：构建了包含 1,000 条验证实例的基准，每条轨迹被切分为语义跨度（semantic spans），并由专家进行细粒度的错误标注，区分有害错误与良性探索、失败检索或无害噪声。
  • 声明中心审计框架 DRIFT：提出了一个多智能体审计系统，包含 Claim Keeper（声明台账）、Support Seeker（支持度检查）、Specialist Auditors（专业审计器）和 Dependency Tracer（依赖追溯器），通过追踪声明的支持度和依赖关系来定位错误。
  • 深入的故障机制分析：揭示了决策（decision-making）和最终确认（finalization）阶段的归一化错误率最高（分别达 60.5% 和 51.8%），而检索阶段虽然频繁但相对低风险；且 36.9% 的成功轨迹中仍存在过程级错误。

Key Contributions

  • A large-scale trajectory corpus: 2,790 real deep-research agent trajectories across two frameworks (MiroFlow, OAgent), three backbone models (GPT-5, Gemini-2.5-Pro, Claude-Sonnet-4.5), and three benchmarks (GAIA, XBench, BrowseComp).
  • Process-level benchmark TELBench: A 1,000-instance diagnostic benchmark with semantic-span segmentation and expert-verified binary error labels, distinguishing harmful errors from benign exploration, failed searches, and harmless noise.
  • Claim-centric auditing framework DRIFT: A multi-agent auditing pipeline comprising a Claim Keeper (ledger of claims), Support Seeker (checks claim support against trajectory evidence), Specialist Auditors (skill-routed checks), and Dependency Tracer (backtraces unsupported claims to localize errors).
  • Detailed mechanism analysis: Reveals that decision-making and finalization stages have the highest normalized error rates (~60.5% and ~51.8%), while retrieval is frequent but relatively low-risk; 36.9% of successful trajectories still contain process-level errors.

Methodology

The authors address the problem of span-level error localization in deep-research agent trajectories. Unlike outcome-level evaluation, which only checks final answers, this work aims to identify which specific spans within a long trajectory first make the result unreliable.

Data Curation Pipeline. Trajectories are collected from public benchmarks and run through two agent frameworks with three frontier models. Raw logs are normalized and segmented into semantic spans—contiguous execution segments around locally coherent objectives (planning, retrieval, verification, computation, decision-making, recovery, finalization). Seven expert annotators spent over 300 hours each reviewing LLM-assisted candidate labels, adjudicating disagreements, and finalizing binary error labels. An error span is defined as one that introduces, relies on, amplifies, or finalizes a mistaken, unsupported, contradicted, or prematurely committed judgment affecting the answer path. Normal exploration, failed searches, tentative hypotheses, and recovered mistakes are not labeled as errors.

TELBench Design. From 1,890 error-bearing trajectories (67.7% of the corpus), 1,000 verified instances are retained with clear boundaries, trajectory-internal evidence, and sufficient benign distractors. The set is split into 600 easy and 400 hard instances (avg. 11.95 spans per trajectory). Additionally, an induced taxonomy of 18 primary faults grouped into 6 families is mapped onto error spans for analysis.

DRIFT Framework. DRIFT audits claims rather than scoring spans independently:

  1. Claim Keeper reads the full trajectory and maintains a ledger of consequential claims (entity, constraint, evidence, retrieval, compute, process), recording when each is introduced, when it becomes consequential, and which later spans depend on it.
  2. Support Seeker checks whether each consequential claim is DIRECT, WEAK, MISSING, or CONFLICTING based on trajectory evidence.
  3. Specialist Auditors perform skill-routed checks (entity, constraint, evidence, retrieval, compute, process) on weak/missing/conflicting claims.
  4. Dependency Tracer backtraces unsupported or conflicting claims to mark spans that commit to, reuse, amplify, or finalize them as errors, while filtering out benign exploration.

Evaluation uses only the question and ordered span texts as input—no gold labels, stage labels, or judge results are provided to the model.

Results & Highlights

DRIFT outperforms generic auditing frameworks. Across all tested backbone models (DeepSeek-V3.2, GPT-5.4, Claude-Sonnet-4.6, Gemini-2.5-Pro), DRIFT achieves the best overall macro-F1 on TELBench. Improvements over bare full-context LLM prompting reach up to ~30 percentage points in F1 and first-error accuracy (Table 2). Notably, simply wrapping an LLM in a generic agentic workflow (Codex, Claude Code) does not guarantee gains and can even degrade performance, underscoring the value of claim-centric design.

First-error localization remains difficult. Even with DRIFT, first-error accuracy (FEA) stays low—especially on the hard split (e.g., ~7–12% FEA for hard instances). This indicates that detecting some erroneous region is easier than pinpointing the earliest harmful commitment.

Scaling alone is insufficient. Experiments across Qwen model variants show that larger scale does not monotonically improve trajectory diagnosis. The hard split remains challenging regardless of model size, suggesting the bottleneck is structural (lack of diagnostic scaffolding) rather than purely capacity-related.

Ablation confirms module complementarity. Adding the Claim Keeper provides the largest gain; Support Seeker and Dependency Tracer further improve grounding and localization (Figure 5c). DRIFT also achieves a favorable efficiency–performance trade-off, lying mostly on the Pareto frontier.

Error-type coverage. DRIFT improves recall consistently across nearly all frequent error categories, with the largest gains on evidence- and constraint-related failures (Figure 6). Bare models struggle with errors requiring verification of claim support (source verification, constraint semantics, unsupported commitments, omitted checks).

Mechanism insights. Stage-normalized analysis reveals retrieval spans have only a 2.9% error rate, while decision-making and finalization stages reach 60.5% and 51.8%, respectively. Fault fingerprints vary across benchmarks, frameworks, and model families: GAIA shifts toward processing errors; OAgent shows stronger evidence-error patterns than MiroFlow; GPT is evidence-heavy, Gemini is constraint-heavy, and Claude is more balanced.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: High. The paper is among the first to systematically study span-level error localization specifically for deep-research agents, moving beyond step-level math reasoning or general tool-use traces. The claim-centric formulation (auditing claims rather than classifying spans) is a fresh conceptual framing.

Rigor: Strong. The annotation pipeline is carefully designed with dual LLM pre-annotation, expert adjudication, and explicit criteria for distinguishing harmful errors from benign behavior. The 18-fault taxonomy is induced from data rather than hand-crafted. Controlled tooling (unified Serper search and Jina reading) reduces confounding across frameworks.

Reproducibility: Good. Both TELBench and DRIFT are publicly released with detailed prompts in the appendix. The evaluation protocol is clearly specified (same input for all frameworks, three repetitions). However, some proprietary models (GPT-5, GPT-5.4, Claude-Sonnet-4.5/4.6) may not be fully accessible to all researchers.

Significance: High for the agent-evaluation community. The finding that 36.9% of successful trajectories contain process errors challenges the adequacy of outcome-only evaluation. The framework is immediately actionable for building more reliable auditing and process-supervision systems.

Red flags / concerns:

  • The low absolute F1 scores (even DRIFT peaks around 50–55%) and very low first-error accuracy suggest the task is still extremely hard; the practical utility of current auditors for real-time intervention may be limited.
  • The paper focuses on localization but does not propose or evaluate correction mechanisms—knowing where the error is does not automatically fix it.
  • Some mechanism labels (stage, fault) are used only for analysis and not provided to models during evaluation, which is appropriate, but the taxonomy construction process involves multiple LLM clustering steps that may encode model-specific biases.
  • The BrowseComp benchmark was downsampled to avoid domination; while reasonable, this means the corpus is not a uniform random sample of agent runs.

Strengths

  • Large-scale, realistic data: 2,790 trajectories from real frontier models and frameworks, not synthetic or toy tasks.
  • Careful annotation design: Explicit criteria for error vs. non-error, LLM-assisted pre-annotation with expert adjudication, and substantial human effort.
  • Strong empirical gains: DRIFT achieves consistent and substantial improvements across multiple model families.
  • Rich diagnostic analysis: Stage-normalized error rates, fault fingerprints across benchmarks/frameworks/models, and case studies provide actionable insights.
  • Public release: Both dataset and code are open-sourced with detailed prompts.

Limitations

  • First-error accuracy remains low: Even the best configuration achieves only ~24% first-error accuracy overall, indicating that pinpointing the first harmful commitment is an unsolved challenge.
  • No correction mechanism: The paper localizes errors but does not attempt to repair trajectories or prevent future errors.
  • Limited to English-language deep-research tasks: The benchmarks (GAIA, XBench, BrowseComp) are English-centric; generalization to other languages or domains is not studied.
  • Annotation cost and scalability: The expert annotation pipeline is expensive (thousands of person-hours). Scaling to larger or more diverse trajectories may require more automated approaches.
  • Static trajectories: DRIFT operates on completed trajectories; it does not address real-time or online error detection during agent execution.
  • Framework/model coverage: While diverse, the study covers only two frameworks and three model families; rapidly evolving agent architectures may exhibit different error patterns.

Relevance

本文对于构建更可靠、可审计的深度研究智能体具有重要参考价值，其过程级评估视角和声明中心诊断框架为超越最终结果评判的智能体可靠性研究开辟了新的方向。对于关注智能体安全、可解释性和过程监督的研究者而言，TELBench 和 DRIFT 提供了可直接使用的工具和数据集。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can DRIFT be extended into an online intervention system that detects and corrects errors during live agent execution, rather than only auditing completed trajectories?
  2. How well do the error patterns and fault taxonomy generalize to non-English deep-research tasks, coding agents, or multi-modal agent trajectories?
  3. Can the claim-centric auditing approach be distilled into a lightweight reward model or process verifier that scales to millions of trajectories without requiring expensive multi-agent inference?
  4. What is the relationship between span-level error localization and final answer correction? Does improving first-error detection directly translate to better self-correction or rerun strategies?
  5. How do the observed fault fingerprints change as agent frameworks incorporate stronger retrieval verification, self-consistency checking, or debate-based confirmation?
