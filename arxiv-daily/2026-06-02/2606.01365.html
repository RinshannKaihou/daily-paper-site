Early Diagnosis of Wasted Computation in Multi-Agent LLM Systems via Failure-Aware Observability

arXiv: 2606.01365

Date: 2026-06-02

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.01365

一句话总结： 本文提出了一种面向多智能体LLM系统的失败感知可观测性框架，通过将工具可靠性、编排循环、证据可用性和预算压力等在线迹线信号映射到具体的失败模式，实现对无效计算轨迹的早期诊断与分层分析。
TL;DR: This paper introduces a failure-aware observability framework for tool-using multi-agent LLM systems that maps online trace signals—such as tool reliability, orchestration loops, evidence availability, and budget pressure—to specific failure modes, enabling early diagnosis of wasted computation trajectories through a layered metric design.

核心贡献

  • 失败分类体系（Failure Taxonomy）： 首次为工具型多智能体LLM系统建立系统性的失败分类，区分了执行失败、编排循环、证据缺失、无最终输出终止和成本浪费五类核心失败模式。
  • 失败感知可观测框架（Failure-Aware Observability Framework）： 将每种失败模式映射到可在线采集的结构化迹线信号（如工具错误率、重复动作签名、信息增益、证据一致性等），并支持离线深度语义分析。
  • GAIA验证集上的分层实证分析： 在165条GAIA验证集迹线上进行层级分层（Level 1/2/3）实验，量化了不同难度任务下失败机制的差异，揭示了廉价在线信号与深层语义指标在捕捉失败时的互补性。
  • 分层指标关联分析： 通过皮尔逊相关性分析证明，三元组预警标签（r = 0.550）和工具错误率（r = 0.391）与无效结果正相关，而句子级证据支持度（r = −0.594）与无效结果负相关，验证了多层观测设计的合理性。

Key Contributions

  • Failure Taxonomy: Establishes a systematic taxonomy for tool-using multi-agent LLM systems, distinguishing five core failure modes: execution failure, orchestration loops, evidence failure, no-final termination, and cost waste.
  • Failure-Aware Observability Framework: Maps each failure mode to structured online trace signals (e.g., tool error rates, repeated action signatures, information gain, citation consistency) and supports offline deep semantic analysis.
  • Level-Stratified Empirical Analysis on GAIA: Conducts a tiered (Level 1/2/3) experimental evaluation on 165 GAIA validation traces, quantifying differences in failure mechanisms across task difficulties and revealing the complementarity of cheap online signals and deeper semantic metrics.
  • Layered Metric Correlation Analysis: Through Pearson correlation analysis, demonstrates that triage warning labels (r = 0.550) and tool error rates (r = 0.391) positively correlate with unusable outcomes, while sentence-level evidence support (r = −0.594) negatively correlates, validating the multi-layer observability design.

Methodology

The authors instantiate their observability framework in a three-agent question-answering system comprising an orchestrator, a search component, and an execution component. The orchestrator selects structured actions from a small action space (search, page retrieval, attachment reading, code execution, final response generation). The system records all execution events as structured traces containing run identifiers, timestamps, event types, agent roles, and payloads.

The framework defines metrics at two levels:

  1. Online metrics emitted during execution: tool error rate (fraction of tool results with error status), repeated action signatures and ABAB cycle labels as loop proxies, information-gain signals (new URLs, extracted facts), and budget pressure indicators.
  2. Offline metrics computed from completed traces: evidence-present rate, citation consistency, answer-evidence cosine similarity using LSA-style embeddings, and sentence-level support (fraction of answer sentences exceeding a 0.65 similarity threshold with evidence chunks).

A selective LLM-as-judge audit (using gpt-4.1-mini) is performed offline on 10 traces for grounding assessment and 30 traces for correctness assessment, with cached responses to avoid repeated calls.

Wasted computation is proxied through token counts, tool-call counts, retry events, and execution attempts, with a configurable cost model: Cr = αTr + βHr + γRr + δXr.

The evaluation uses the complete GAIA 2023 validation snapshot (165 tasks: 53 Level-1, 86 Level-2, 26 Level-3) under identical execution caps: 8 orchestrator steps, 8 tool calls, 30-second timeouts, and a 25,000-token soft ceiling.

Results & Highlights

  • Operational failure rates are substantial across all levels: 22/53 (41.5%) Level-1 runs, 33/86 (38.4%) Level-2 runs, and 12/26 (46.2%) Level-3 runs fail to produce a usable final answer.
  • Token consumption scales sharply with difficulty: Mean token use rises from 8,152 tokens at Level 1 to 9,668 tokens at Level 2 and 16,389 tokens at Level 3.
  • Evidence availability does not guarantee answer support: Evidence-present rates are high (81.1% L1, 97.7% L2, 95.0% L3), but sentence-level support remains low (0.047 L1, 0.201 L2, 0.000 L3), revealing a significant "evidence-present but unsupported" gap.
  • Citation gaps widen with difficulty: Evidence present without citations occurs in 28.3% (L1), 36.0% (L2), and 46.2% (L3) of runs.
  • Failure mechanisms differ by level: Level-1 failures stem from insufficient evidence, repeated-action loops, and max-step termination; Level-2 and Level-3 failures are dominated by repeated-action loops and max-step termination. LOOPING_WARNING appears in 16 L1, 31 L2, and 9 L3 runs.
  • Correctness audit: Among 30 usable finals audited (10 per level), only 12/40% are judged correct against GAIA reference answers (L1: 6/10, L2: 4/10, L3: 2/10), confirming that "usable final" is operationally distinct from "correct answer."
  • Layered correlation patterns: Cheap trace signals (triage labels, tool-error rate) correlate with unusable outcomes, while semantic metrics (sentence support, answer-evidence similarity) correlate with both unusable outcomes and LLM-judge grounding support, demonstrating complementary diagnostic layers.

The paper draws on distributed systems observability literature (Dapper) and adapts it to the unique semantic-failure characteristics of LLM agent traces.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The paper's core novelty lies in translating distributed-systems observability concepts into the multi-agent LLM domain with a failure-centric lens. While prior work focuses on improving agent capability (better retrieval, memory, tool use), this work focuses on diagnosing why existing agents waste computation—a distinct and underexplored angle. The explicit mapping of failure modes to observable signals (Table I) is a valuable conceptual contribution.

Rigor: The methodology is careful about distinguishing operational outcomes (usable final) from benchmark correctness, and about separating evidence presence from answer support. The use of deterministic decoding, isolated batches, and cached LLM-judge audits shows methodological caution. However, the embedding-based support metric (LSA-style with τ = 0.65) is acknowledged as a conservative proxy that can miss entailment and numerical correctness.

Reproducibility: The paper provides explicit formulas for all metrics, clear execution caps, and detailed experimental setup. However, the source code and exact prompts are not released, and the evaluation is tied to a single agent configuration and a single GAIA validation snapshot.

Significance: For practitioners building production multi-agent systems, the layered observability design offers actionable guidance: cheap online signals for early intervention, semantic metrics for offline grounding analysis, and selective LLM-as-judge for proxy validation. The finding that ~40% of runs fail operationally even on Level-1 tasks is a sobering reminder of how far multi-agent systems remain from reliable deployment.

Red Flags: The correctness audit (12/30 correct among usable finals) suggests the baseline agent is quite weak; readers should be cautious about generalizing failure-mode distributions to stronger systems. The paper is observational—it does not yet test whether acting on the proposed signals actually improves outcomes.

Strengths

  • Clear conceptual framework: The failure-to-signal mapping in Table I is an excellent pedagogical and practical tool.
  • Distinguishes multiple outcome types: The paper rigorously separates no-final, weakly-grounded, and correct-but-expensive outcomes—nuance often missing in agent evaluation.
  • Layered metric design: The combination of cheap online signals + semantic offline metrics + selective LLM-judge audit provides a scalable and cost-conscious evaluation architecture.
  • Honest about limitations: The limitations section is comprehensive and candid about generalizability, proxy quality, and the observational nature of the study.
  • Well-structured empirical analysis: Level-stratified reporting makes it easy to see how failure mechanisms evolve with task difficulty.

Limitations

  • Single configuration, single benchmark: The evaluation uses one agent setup and one GAIA validation snapshot; failure-mode distributions may differ across models, prompts, or benchmarks.
  • Small-scale LLM-judge audits: The 10-trace grounding audit and 30-trace correctness audit are descriptive sanity checks, not gold-standard evaluations.
  • Proxy metrics, not ground truth: Token and tool-call counts are cost proxies; the study does not measure energy, carbon, wall-clock latency, or actual billing. Embedding similarity can miss entailment and numerical equivalence.
  • No intervention evaluation: The analysis is purely observational. The paper does not test whether responding to early warning signals (e.g., changing retrieval strategy, reducing budget) improves accuracy or reduces cost.
  • Weak baseline agent: With only 40% correctness among usable finals, the agent itself may be a limiting factor in interpreting failure patterns.

Relevance

对于正在构建或运维多智能体LLM系统的研究者和工程师而言，本文的失败感知可观测框架提供了从"只看最终结果"转向"诊断全过程"的系统方法论，其分层指标设计对生产环境中的早期预警和成本优化具有直接参考价值。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Intervention efficacy: How much does task success improve when the system acts on early warning signals (e.g., switching retrieval strategy, requiring stronger evidence, or reducing budget after a triage label)?
  2. Generalization: Do the same failure-mode distributions and signal-outcome correlations hold for stronger base models (e.g., GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet) or different agent architectures (e.g., ReAct, Reflexion, multi-agent debate)?
  3. Real-time cost savings: If early termination or budget reduction policies are applied based on online signals, what is the quantitative trade-off between saved computation and lost successful outcomes?
  4. Human-in-the-loop integration: Can the proposed observability signals effectively guide human operators to intervene at the right moment, and how do human interventions compare to automated responses?
  5. Energy and carbon proxies: Can the token/tool-call cost model be extended with actual energy measurements or provider carbon-intensity data to support sustainability-aware agent design?
