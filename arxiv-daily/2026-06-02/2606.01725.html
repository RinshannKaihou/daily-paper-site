Characterization of Multi-Model Agentic AI Systems on General Tasks via Trace-Driven Simulation

arXiv: 2606.01725

Date: 2026-06-02

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.01725

一句话总结： 本文提出了首个面向复杂多模型智能体系统的token级轨迹数据集GAIATrace及其配套模拟器Vidur-Agent，通过对两个SOTA智能体在GAIA基准上的系统级行为刻画，揭示了智能体行为的异质性、传统LLM服务指标与智能体场景的错配、以及前缀缓存和调度策略对任务级延迟的深远影响。
TL;DR: This paper introduces GAIATrace, the first token-level trace dataset capturing the behavior of two state-of-the-art multi-model agentic AI systems (MiroThinker and OWL) on the GAIA benchmark, alongside Vidur-Agent, a trace-driven simulator. The characterization reveals highly heterogeneous agent behaviors, misalignment between traditional per-query LLM SLA metrics and agentic AI needs, and the outsized impact of prefix caching and scheduling policies on end-to-end task latency.

核心贡献

  • GAIATrace 数据集：首个涵盖两种最先进多模型智能体系统（MiroThinker 与 OWL）在 GAIA 通用任务基准上运行的 token 级轨迹数据集，完整记录了推理 token、任务级结构以及所有主要参与 LLM 的活动。
  • Vidur-Agent 模拟器：在 Vidur LLM 模拟器基础上扩展而来的轨迹驱动模拟器，支持异构模型、KV/前缀缓存、预填充–解码分离、负载感知路由及高级调度策略，能够以低成本实现可复现的系统评估。
  • 系统行为深度刻画：通过 GAIATrace 与 Vidur-Agent 的组合，对现代智能体系统的系统开销进行了多维度分析，包括前缀缓存影响、任务到达率变化、调度策略对比及 GPU 配置优化。
  • 反直觉的新发现：传统 per-query 指标（TTFT/TPOT）与智能体系统的核心指标（per-task latency）存在显著错配；前缀缓存带来的端到端任务延迟改善（1.67–3.82×）远超此前报道；最短作业优先（SJF）调度虽恶化单查询尾延迟，却能显著降低整体任务延迟。

Key Contributions

  • GAIATrace Dataset: The first token-level trace dataset of two state-of-the-art multi-model agentic systems (MiroThinker and OWL) running on the GAIA benchmark, capturing full reasoning tokens, task-level structures, and activities of every major participating LLM.
  • Vidur-Agent Simulator: A trace-driven simulator extending the Vidur LLM simulator with support for heterogeneous models, KV/prefix caching, prefill-decode disaggregation, load-aware routing, and advanced scheduling policies, enabling reproducible, low-cost system evaluation.
  • In-depth System Characterization: A multi-dimensional analysis of modern agentic AI system overheads using GAIATrace and Vidur-Agent, covering prefix caching impact, task arrival rate scaling, scheduling policy comparison, and GPU configuration optimization.
  • Counter-intuitive Findings: Traditional per-query metrics (TTFT/TPOT) are misaligned with agentic AI's primary metric (per-task latency); prefix caching improves end-to-end task latency by 1.67–3.82×, far exceeding prior reports; shortest-job-first (SJF) scheduling worsens per-query tail latency yet significantly improves overall task latency.

Methodology

The authors collected GAIATrace by running two representative agentic systems on the GAIA benchmark under a carefully engineered setup that balances transparency and task completion accuracy.

Agent Systems:

  • MiroThinker: A simpler, ReAct-like system where a main LLM iteratively plans, invokes tools, and refines its strategy. It occasionally delegates summarization and extraction to a Sub-LLM.
  • OWL: A more complex hierarchical multi-agent system with a master coordinator and multiple specialized sub-agents, each generating sub-plans and maintaining memory. Different LLM architectures are used for different agents.

Model Configuration:

  • Main-LLMs (reasoning-capable, open-source for transparency): gpt-oss-120b for OWL and MiroThinker-1.7-mini for MiroThinker.
  • Sub-LLMs (non-reasoning, proprietary for accuracy): gpt-4o for OWL and gpt-4o-mini for MiroThinker.
  • Video/audio processing used proprietary models (Gemini-2.5-mini, Whisper-2), which contribute negligibly to overall characterization.

Benchmark & Trace Collection:

  • MiroThinker ran 103 text-only tasks from GAIA; OWL ran all 165 GAIA tasks (including image, video, audio).
  • Success rates: MiroThinker 59.2%, OWL 48.5%.
  • URLs containing GAIA question-answer pairs were blocked to prevent direct lookup.
  • Each task was run at most twice; successful and failed trials were annotated.

Vidur-Agent Simulator:

  • Built on Vidur, extended with dependent execution modeling, tool call overhead simulation, heterogeneous model support, KV and prefix caching, prefill-decode disaggregation, load-aware query routing, and advanced scheduling policies.
  • Default simulation setup: two DGX A100 nodes (16×A100 80GB GPUs), four models each with TP-4, prefill-decode disaggregation.
  • Task arrival modeled with Poisson distribution (0.05–0.5 tasks/second).
  • Used CodeLlama-34B performance model for default experiments; Llama-3-70B for scaling experiments.

Results & Highlights

Overall Trace Statistics (Table 1):

  • MiroThinker: 103 tasks, 1,491 Main-LLM queries (14.5 ± 10.3 per task), 591 Sub-LLM queries (7.3 ± 5.1 per task).
  • OWL: 165 tasks, 2,669 Main-LLM queries (16.2 ± 12.7 per task), 2,737 Sub-LLM queries (16.6 ± 9.9 per task).
  • Reasoning tokens accounted for 70.3% (MiroThinker Main-LLM) and 47.0% (OWL Main-LLM) of decode output.
  • KV cache hit rates: MiroThinker Main-LLM 79.0% (intra-task), OWL Main-LLM 58.0%.
  • MiroThinker's Sub-LLM had an extremely low KV cache hit rate of 0.3% due to diverse web page summarization.

Prefix Caching Impact (Figures 6–9):

  • End-to-end task latency improvement with prefix caching: p50 improved by 1.67× (MiroThinker) to 3.82× (OWL); p90 improved by 2.07× to 2.97×.
  • These gains far exceed prior reports (~15.7% improvement in [37]).
  • However, benefits are uneven: MiroThinker's Sub-LLM TTFT degraded because prefix caching accelerated the Main-LLM, increasing Sub-LLM queue pressure.

Task Arrival Rate Scaling (Figures 10–11):

  • At low TPS (0.05), Main-LLM decode was the bottleneck.
  • At high TPS (0.5), Sub-LLM prefill became the bottleneck.
  • This shifting bottleneck motivates dynamic GPU reconfiguration rather than static provisioning.

Scheduling Policy Comparison (Figures 12–14):

  • Shortest-Job-First (SJF) significantly outperformed Query-FCFS: p50 latency reduced by 2.22× (MiroThinker) and 1.80× (OWL).
  • Counter-intuitively, SJF worsened per-query tail TTFT but improved per-task tail latency, because a task's multiple queries collectively benefit from reduced queuing delay.
  • Adding a timeout to SJF (SJF180) hurt task latency by promoting long-prefill queries that evicted useful KV cache entries.

GPU Allocation & Model Scaling (Figures 15–18):

  • At medium TPS, reallocating GPUs from Main-LLM decode to Sub-LLM prefill improved latency.
  • At high TPS, taking GPUs from Main-LLM prefill (instead of decode) hurt performance, as Main-LLM prefill became the new bottleneck.
  • With larger 70B models, SJF with timeout performed much worse than plain SJF, because KV cache eviction costs scale with model size.

Critical Assessment

Novelty: The paper makes a strong contribution by shifting the focus from simple ReAct-like agents on homogeneous benchmarks to complex multi-agent, multi-model, multi-tool systems on heterogeneous real-world tasks. The trace dataset fills a clear gap, as existing traces (Mooncake, MiroVerse) lack task-level structure, reasoning tokens, or multi-LLM coverage.

Rigor: The methodology is sound and transparent. The authors openly discuss limitations: using CodeLlama-34B performance models as proxies for their exact LLMs, not modeling proprietary video/audio models internally, and reproducibility issues with SOTA accuracy numbers. The simulation setup is well-documented, and the distinction between successful and failed trials is properly handled.

Reproducibility: High. The trace-driven approach inherently supports reproducibility. The commitment to open-source both GAIATrace and Vidur-Agent is commendable. However, exact reproduction of the original agent runs may be challenging due to non-deterministic tool behavior (e.g., web search results changing over time).

Significance: The findings have practical implications for LLM serving infrastructure targeting agentic AI. The demonstration that per-query SLA metrics are misaligned with agentic AI needs, and that optimal system configurations shift with load, directly motivates future work in dynamic resource allocation and task-aware scheduling.

Red Flags / Concerns:

  • The success rates (48.5% for OWL, 59.2% for MiroThinker) are lower than reported SOTA, which the authors attribute to using partially open-source models and reproducibility issues. This means a substantial fraction of tokens (44–63%) are from failed traces, raising questions about whether the system characterization fully represents "optimal" agent behavior.
  • The use of CodeLlama-34B performance model as a proxy introduces some uncertainty in absolute latency numbers, though the authors argue relative trends should hold.
  • Tool latencies (especially external web scrapers) are modeled as fixed distributions from repeated measurements, but real-world variance may be higher.

Strengths

  • First comprehensive trace dataset for complex multi-model agentic systems on general tasks, capturing reasoning tokens and task-level structure.
  • Well-designed simulator extension (Vidur-Agent) that enables rapid, reproducible experimentation across hardware and scheduling configurations.
  • Rich empirical findings with clear takeaways that challenge conventional wisdom from simpler agent setups.
  • Honest and transparent about limitations, reproducibility issues, and modeling assumptions.
  • Strong practical relevance for the growing agentic AI serving infrastructure community.

Limitations

  • Lower-than-SOTA success rates mean a large fraction of traces represent failed execution, potentially skewing the characterization toward suboptimal behavior.
  • Proxy performance model (CodeLlama-34B) may not perfectly capture the exact LLMs used, though trends are expected to generalize.
  • Limited model diversity in traces: only two agent systems and a handful of LLMs are covered.
  • Tool latency modeling relies on repeated measurements that may not capture real-world variance of external services.
  • No image/video/audio model internals: proprietary multimodal models are treated as black-box tools.
  • Single benchmark: all experiments use GAIA; behavior may differ on other benchmarks.

Relevance

本文对构建和优化面向智能体AI的LLM服务基础设施具有重要指导意义，尤其对未来动态资源调度、任务感知调度策略以及多模型异构部署的研究方向提供了宝贵的数据基础与实验平台。对于关注Agent系统效率、LLM推理优化及多智能体架构的研究者和工程师而言，GAIATrace和Vidur-Agent将成为极具价值的开源资源。

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How would the characterization results change if the agents achieved SOTA-level success rates? Would the bottleneck distributions and optimal configurations differ significantly?
  2. Can the GAIATrace methodology be extended to other agentic benchmarks (e.g., SWE-bench, WebArena) and other agent architectures (e.g., AutoGPT, OpenManus)?
  3. What dynamic GPU reconfiguration policies would be most effective given the observed shifting bottlenecks with task arrival rate?
  4. How can we design scheduling policies that explicitly optimize per-task latency rather than per-query metrics, and what theoretical guarantees can be provided?
  5. Could the large fraction of wasted tokens from failed traces be reduced through early-stopping mechanisms or speculative execution, and what would be the system-level trade-offs?
