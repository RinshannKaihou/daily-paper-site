> **一句话总结：** 本文提出"模型科学"（Model Science）这一新兴学科方向，主张超越传统基准测试，从验证、探索、引导、精修四个维度系统性地理解AI模型的行为机理，并借鉴认知科学、神经科学、医学和农业科学的成熟经验，呼吁建立共享基础设施和跨社区协作机制。

> **TL;DR:** This paper proposes "Model Science" as a new discipline advocating that the AI community move beyond benchmarking toward systematic understanding of model behavior through four functional perspectives—Verify, Explore, Steer, and Refine—drawing lessons from cognitive science, neuroscience, medicine, and agriculture, and calling for shared infrastructure and cross-community collaboration.

## 核心贡献

- 提出**模型科学（Model Science）**这一系统性学科方向，呼吁将分散的模型分析工作整合为统一的学术领域。
- 揭示基准测试的深层局限性：能告诉我们模型"是否"表现好，但无法解释"为什么"成功或失败；遗漏了幻觉、捷径学习等关键失效模式。
- 借鉴**认知科学**（Marr的三层分析框架）、**神经科学**（单病例研究的独特价值）、**医学**（转化医学的多阶段体系）和**农业科学**（共享基础设施促进累积进步）的成熟经验，为模型科学提供方法论基础。
- 建立四个功能性研究视角：
  - **验证（Verify）**——模型在部署环境中是否可靠运行？
  - **探索（Explore）**——模型的内部计算机制是什么？
  - **引导（Steer）**——如何在不重新训练的情况下调整模型行为？
  - **精修（Refine）**——训练或适应过程与模型行为之间的关联是什么？
- 提出三类基础设施需求：数据集目录、模型目录（含血缘关系与可解释性注释），以及**研究发现共享仓库**（类似MITRE ATT&CK框架）。
- 强调对**单个模型实例**进行深度分析的必要性，而非仅关注模型族群的统计特征。
- 发出三项具体行动呼吁：将深度模型分析视为独立学术贡献；建立跨视角的学术交流平台；资助机构应持续投入共享目录建设。

## Key Contributions

- Proposes **Model Science** as a systematic discipline, calling for the consolidation of scattered model analysis efforts into a unified academic field.
- Reveals the deep limitations of benchmarking: benchmarks tell us *whether* models perform well but not *why* they succeed or fail; they miss critical failure modes such as hallucinations and shortcut learning.
- Draws lessons from established sciences—**cognitive science** (Marr's three levels of analysis), **neuroscience** (the unique value of single-case studies), **medicine** (translational medicine's multi-phase system), and **agriculture** (shared infrastructure enabling cumulative progress)—to provide methodological foundations for Model Science.
- Establishes four functional research perspectives:
  - **Verify** — Does the model perform reliably across deployment contexts?
  - **Explore** — What computational mechanisms underlie the model's behavior?
  - **Steer** — How can we modify model behavior without retraining?
  - **Refine** — What is the connection between training/adaptation and behavior?
- Proposes three categories of infrastructure needs: dataset catalogues, model catalogues (with lineage and interpretability annotations), and a **shared repository of findings** (analogous to the MITRE ATT&CK framework).
- Emphasizes the necessity of deep analysis of **individual model instances** rather than only statistical features of model families.
- Issues three concrete calls to action: treat deep model analysis as standalone academic contributions; create venues spanning the four perspectives; and fund sustained investment in shared catalogues.

## Methodology

The paper is structured as a conceptual manifesto and position paper rather than an empirical study. The authors build their argument through several methodological strategies:

1. **Empirical documentation of the adoption-understanding gap**: The authors compile extensive deployment statistics (ChatGPT: 900 million weekly active users; Meta AI: 1 billion monthly users; Llama: 1.2 billion cumulative downloads; CLIP: 19 million monthly downloads) to demonstrate that complex models serve populations larger than most countries, yet our understanding of their inner workings remains primitive.

2. **Historical analysis of benchmark-driven research**: The paper traces the evolution of benchmarks from the UCI Repository (1990s) through MNIST, ImageNet, and GLUE/SuperGLUE, documenting how each success revealed new limitations—saturation, data contamination, and the disconnect between leaderboard performance and real-world reliability.

3. **Case-study-driven critique**: Concrete examples are used to illustrate benchmark failures:
   - COVID-19 chest X-ray models detecting hospital artefacts rather than pathology (DeGrave et al., 2021)
   - ChatGPT-4 hallucinating in 58% of legal queries and LLaMA 2 in 88% (Dahl et al., 2024)
   - Whisper fabricating harmful content in ~1% of transcriptions, with 38% of hallucinations containing explicit harms (Koenecke et al., 2024)

4. **Cross-disciplinary analogy**: The authors systematically map lessons from four established sciences to Model Science foundations (summarized in Table 1 of the paper).

5. **Framework synthesis and community mapping**: The four perspectives are derived by mapping existing research communities (verification/auditing, interpretability/XAI, steering/alignment, refinement/training analysis) onto complementary functional questions.

6. **Counterargument analysis**: Section 4 proactively addresses five anticipated objections to the Model Science proposal.

## Results & Highlights

The paper delivers several key insights:

- **Benchmarks create an illusion of progress**: Goodhart's Law (when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure) drives a "benchmark treadmill" where communities optimize for leaderboard scores without acquiring underlying capabilities. Each round consumes research effort but accumulates little understanding, because knowledge gained for one benchmark becomes obsolete when that benchmark is retired.

- **"Right for the wrong reason" is pervasive**: The COVID-19 X-ray example demonstrates how models can achieve state-of-the-art performance while relying on spurious correlations. Saliency maps and other explanation techniques could have detected these shortcuts, but benchmark culture provides no incentive for such analysis.

- **Training-centric incentives undermine understanding**: Research incentives skew toward building new models rather than understanding existing ones. Once a model is surpassed on a leaderboard, interest in studying its behavior drops sharply—even when that model remains in wide deployment.

- **The four perspectives are complementary, not competing**: Like the T0–T4 phases of translational medicine (each separated by a "valley of death" that cannot be crossed by expertise from adjacent phases alone), the Verify/Explore/Steer/Refine perspectives address distinct questions and require separate but interconnected institutional structures.

- **Single-case studies reveal what population studies miss**: Following the neuroscience precedent (Patient H.M., Phineas Gage, Patient Tan), deep analysis of individual models can expose functional architecture invisible in group averaging. GPT-2's induction heads (Olsson et al., 2022) remain relevant to understanding GPT-4, while benchmarks that GPT-2 saturated in 2019 are not.

- **Infrastructure gaps are critical**: Despite over a million models on Hugging Face, there is no systematic knowledge graph connecting them. Model Cards remain static documents; findings about shortcuts, circuits, and biases are scattered across papers and blog posts with no platform for accumulation.

- **Concrete calls to action**:
  1. Researchers should publish deep model analyses, audits, and mechanistic studies as standalone contributions; conferences should add "model analysis" as an explicit submission category.
  2. Conference organizers should create venues spanning the four perspectives rather than reinforcing separation.
  3. Funding agencies should support sustained investment in catalogues and shared repositories rather than treating them as auxiliary infrastructure.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty**: The explicit framing of Model Science as a distinct discipline with four functional pillars provides a useful organizing framework. While individual components exist across the XAI and AI safety literature, the systematic consolidation—especially the cross-disciplinary analogies and infrastructure proposals—is novel and timely.

**Rigor**: As a position paper, the arguments are well-supported by empirical deployment statistics, documented benchmark failures, clear logical structure, and carefully chosen cross-disciplinary precedents. The critique of benchmarking is not merely opinion but grounded in specific, well-documented cases.

**Reproducibility**: Not applicable—this is a conceptual paper without empirical experiments. However, the referenced cases are all drawn from published, peer-reviewed work.

**Significance**: Very high. The paper addresses a genuine structural gap in AI research. As models serve billions of users, understanding *why* they behave as they do is critical for safety, governance, and sustainable progress. The three concrete calls to action provide a roadmap for institutional change.

**Potential red flags / limitations**:
- The framework is intentionally broad and may be difficult to operationalize without further refinement.
- The four perspectives, while intuitive, have overlapping boundaries with existing fields (XAI, alignment, red-teaming, mechanistic interpretability). The paper acknowledges this but does not fully resolve the tension.
- The call for infrastructure is important but lacks specific technical implementation details or governance models beyond general principles (independent governance, verification, FAIR principles).
- The medical analogy's "valley of death" concept may overstate the separation between perspectives—there is more cross-pollination in practice than the framework suggests.
- The paper does not deeply address potential resistance from the benchmark-driven research culture or the incentive structures in academia and industry that perpetuate it.

## Strengths

- **Compelling empirical opening**: The deployment statistics powerfully establish the urgency of the problem—models serve populations larger than most countries while remaining poorly understood.
- **Well-chosen case studies**: The COVID-19 X-ray, legal hallucination, and Whisper fabrication examples are concrete, verifiable, and illustrate different classes of benchmark failures.
- **Rigorous cross-disciplinary grounding**: The analogies to cognitive science, neuroscience, medicine, and agriculture are not decorative but structurally informative, with explicit mappings to Model Science foundations.
- **Actionable framework**: The Verify/Explore/Steer/Refine quadrants provide an intuitive vocabulary that can guide research organization, funding priorities, and conference structuring.
- **Proactive engagement with objections**: Section 4 directly addresses anticipated counterarguments (benchmarks drove real progress; each piece already exists; empirical safety may suffice; integration may be premature), strengthening the overall argument.
- **Credible, interdisciplinary author team**: The authors span multiple institutions and have established records in the relevant subfields.
- **Balanced tone**: The paper does not advocate abandoning benchmarks but complementing them—a pragmatic stance more likely to gain traction.

## Limitations

- **Abstract framework without concrete pilots**: While the infrastructure proposals are well-motivated, there are no prototype implementations or pilot studies demonstrating feasibility.
- **Overlap with existing communities**: The four perspectives map closely onto existing subcommunities (verification → FAccT/auditing; explore → XAI/interpretability; steer → alignment/safety; refine → ML training research). The value added by the unified "Model Science" label, while conceptually clear, may face practical resistance from established communities with their own venues, incentives, and identities.
- **Governance challenges underexplored**: The call for independent governance of shared repositories is sensible but vague. Who funds? Who decides? How are conflicts resolved? The MITRE ATT&CK precedent is invoked but not analyzed for transferability.
- **No discussion of computational costs**: Deep analysis of individual models (especially frontier LLMs) is computationally expensive. The paper does not address who pays for this or how to make it accessible to researchers outside well-resourced labs.
- **Limited engagement with industry dynamics**: Much of the deployment scale the paper documents comes from industry (OpenAI, Meta, Google). The paper does not deeply engage with how proprietary restrictions, competitive pressures, and closed-weight models affect the feasibility of Model Science.
- **Temporal decay acknowledged but not solved**: The paper notes that models verified at release may degrade as world knowledge shifts, but offers no methodological response beyond calling for "continuous monitoring."

## Relevance

本文对致力于AI可解释性、模型安全、AI治理以及负责任AI的研究者具有重要参考价值，其提出的"模型科学"框架为超越基准测试、系统性理解模型行为提供了清晰的研究方向，尤其适合关注大规模基础模型部署风险与监管需求的读者。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How can the four perspectives (Verify, Explore, Steer, Refine) be operationalized into concrete evaluation criteria and peer-review standards for conference submissions?
2. What governance and funding models would enable a sustainable, independent shared repository of model findings, and how can the MITRE ATT&CK precedent be adapted to academic AI research?
3. How can Model Science address the computational and access barriers that prevent researchers outside well-resourced labs from conducting deep analysis of frontier models?
4. What specific methodological innovations are needed to make continuous monitoring of deployed models feasible at scale, and how can temporal decay be quantified and mitigated?
5. How might Model Science interact with existing industry practices around model cards, red-teaming, and safety evaluations—can it complement proprietary efforts, or will access restrictions limit its reach?
6. Can the single-case study approach from neuroscience be formalized into replicable protocols for neural network audit, and what would constitute "sufficient" depth of analysis for a given model?
7. How should Model Science handle the tension between open publication of safety-critical findings and responsible disclosure, especially when vulnerabilities could be exploited maliciously?
