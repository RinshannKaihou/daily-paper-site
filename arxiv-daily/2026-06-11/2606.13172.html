VER

Detecting Explanatory Insufficiency in Learned Representations

A Framework for Representational Vigilance

A conceptual diagnostic framework for detecting explanatory insufficiency in learned representations — even when predictive performance remains strong.

Key Finding
Predictive performance ≠ representational adequacy. High accuracy can coexist with dangerous explanatory gaps that standard metrics never capture.
Key Metric
No quantitative results reported. Purely conceptual — no datasets, no benchmarks, no code. The 5-step diagnostic sequence is the structural contribution.
Why This Matters
Hidden representational defects in safety-critical AI systems (medical, autonomous driving) evade standard evaluation — VER provides a systematic lens to surface them pre-deployment.
Critical Edge
Entirely conceptual with no formal definitions, algorithms, or experiments. Every diagnostic step requires expert human judgment — automation remains unsolved.

Methodology
The 5-Step Diagnostic Pipeline

VER is not a trainable model — it is a monitoring protocol built on the Bootstrap Theory of Representational Emergence (TBER). It assumes representations may become inadequate when residual structures resist integration.

1
Representation Identification
Pinpoint the active representation organizing observations — features, embeddings, latent variables, multimodal encodings, or world-model states.
What representation is currently organizing the observations?
2
Explanatory-Domain Delimitation
Map the set of observations, relations, and patterns the representation explains adequately. This establishes the baseline for residual detection.
What does the representation explain adequately?
3
Residual-Structure Detection
Identify persistent structures not yet integrated: stable subpopulation failures, outlier clusters, latent patterns, multimodal inconsistencies, recurring OOD behavior, long-range prediction drift.
Which structures remain persistently difficult to organize within the active representation?
4
Explanatory-Resistance Evaluation
Systematically rule out alternative explanations — noise, uncertainty, missing data, local model error, measurement limits, distribution shift — before attributing residual structure to representational inadequacy.
Can the residual structures be explained without questioning the representation itself?
5
Vigilance Signaling
Generate one of three signals based on the evaluation. The alert does not mean the representation is wrong — it means its adequacy can no longer be assumed by default.
→ See signal taxonomy below
Output Signals
Stable Adequacy
Residuals are limited or fully explained by ordinary factors. Representation is adequate.
Vigilance Condition
Residuals persist and need continued monitoring, but evidence is insufficient for an alert.
Representational Alert
Residuals show enough persistence, coherence, and explanatory resistance to warrant explicit evaluation of representational adequacy.
Illustrative Examples
Trigger Conditions — Not Failure, But Suspicion

VER activates not when performance is poor, but when persistent, hard-to-integrate residual structures appear against a backdrop of operational success.

Subpopulation-Specific Failure
Overall accuracy 95%, but a specific subpopulation consistently fails — a stable residual that accuracy metrics mask.
World-Model Long-Range Drift
Short-horizon predictions are accurate, but long-range predictions show systematic drift — the representation degrades with temporal distance.
Persistent Hallucination Patterns
An LLM hallucinates repeatedly within a specific task family despite good overall performance — a coherent, resistant residual.
Multimodal Inconsistency
Text and vision representations conflict stably on specific categories — cross-modal residual structure suggesting misalignment.

Strengths

  • Novel diagnostic angle: probes representational defects behind good metrics
  • Clean separation of failure signals — error, uncertainty, distribution shift, explanatory insufficiency
  • Architecture-agnostic: applicable to deep nets, latent variable models, foundation models, agents
  • Clear direction for new representational evaluation benchmarks
  • Well-structured exposition with clear motivation and operational flow

Limitations

  • No formal definitions, mathematical characterization, or computational measures
  • No algorithms, code, datasets, or case studies provided
  • Insufficient comparison with causal representation learning, concept-based explanations, probing, mechanistic interpretability
  • Five-step process is heavily human-judgment-dependent — automation path unclear
  • No thresholds or false-positive control mechanisms for vigilance signals

Open Questions

How can VER's five-step sequence be operationalized into a concrete, computable algorithm with formal measures for each stage?

What is the formal relationship between "explanatory insufficiency" and OOD generalization, concept completeness, causal identifiability, and mechanistic interpretability?

Which benchmarks or synthetic tasks would best reveal persistent residual structures while separating them from ordinary errors?

Can VER become an automated monitoring tool for deployed models — and what are acceptable false-positive / false-negative trade-offs?

How does the framework scale to high-dimensional representations in LLMs or vision foundation models?

What objective criteria can set vigilance thresholds and distinguish "vigilance condition" from "representational alert"?

How can explanatory resistance be quantified, and how does it relate to local correction attempts (retraining, more data, uncertainty reduction)?
