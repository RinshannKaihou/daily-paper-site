> **一句话总结：** ReSET 针对 NVFP4 量化的大推理模型提出了一种基于推理步骤熵的在线温度缩放策略——在低不确定性步骤中对低熵符号 token 进行锐化、在高不确定性步骤中提升温度以恢复多样性——并配合首个面向小批量自回归解码的 CUDA-core NVFP4 GEMV 内核，在五个模型和三个推理基准上实现了精度提升（AIME-120 最高 +2.6 分）与延迟收益（M=1 投影内核 1.57–2.49× 加速，端到端解码最高约 2× 于 BF16）。

> **TL;DR:** ReSET is a step-aware temperature-scaling method for NVFP4-quantized large reasoning models. It tracks online step-level entropy to sharpen low-entropy symbolic tokens in confident steps and raise temperature in uncertain steps, plus a CUDA-core small-M NVFP4 GEMV kernel for latency-critical autoregressive decoding. Across five Qwen/R1-Qwen models and AIME-120/GPQA-Diamond/LiveCodeBench, it recovers up to +2.6 accuracy points over the NVFP4 RTN baseline and delivers 1.57–2.49× kernel-level speedups at small decode batches, with end-to-end speedups up to ~2× over BF16.

## 核心贡献

- 揭示了 NVFP4 量化在推理链中造成的两类采样错误：低熵符号 token（数字、运算符等）的概率被“膨胀”，导致非 top-1 错误采样；高不确定性步骤中的高熵 token 则被“压缩”，概率质量过度集中。
- 提出 token 级熵不足以作为控制信号，因为同一类 token 的熵会被所处推理步骤的不确定性显著拉高或压低；进而将分析粒度从 token 级提升到步骤级。
- 设计 ReSET（Reasoning-Step Entropy-based Temperature Scaling）：通过在线步骤熵估计器，在自信步骤使用全局阈值锐化、在不确定步骤使用步骤相对阈值，并配合高/低温度实现非对称解码。
- 实现了首个面向小批量（M ≤ 8）自回归解码的 CUDA-core NVFP4 GEMV 内核，解决 Blackwell Tensor Core 固定 M=128 tile 导致的利用率崩溃问题。
- 在 AIME-120、GPQA-Diamond、LiveCodeBench 上对 R1-Qwen-7B/14B 和 Qwen3-8B/14B/32B 进行了系统评测，并开源代码。

## Key Contributions

- Characterizes two distinct NVFP4 quantization failure modes in reasoning traces: entropy inflation at low-entropy symbolic tokens (digits, operators) that causes non-top-1 mis-sampling, and entropy collapse at high-entropy tokens in uncertain steps that over-concentrates probability mass.
- Argues that token-level entropy is an unreliable control signal because it is dominated by the uncertainty of the surrounding reasoning step; shifts the analysis granularity from tokens to reasoning steps.
- Proposes ReSET, an online step-entropy-aware temperature-scaling policy: it sharpens in confident steps using a global threshold and switches to a step-relative threshold in uncertain steps, paired with asymmetric low/high decoding temperatures.
- Designs and implements, to the authors’ knowledge, the first public CUDA-core NVFP4 GEMV kernel for latency-critical small-batch (M ≤ 8) autoregressive decoding, addressing the Tensor-Core tile-under-occupancy problem on NVIDIA Blackwell.
- Provides a systematic evaluation across five open reasoning models on AIME-120, GPQA-Diamond, and LiveCodeBench, with released code.

## Methodology

**Quantization-failure analysis (Sec. 3).** The authors sample 1.5M tokens from R1-Qwen-14B on AIME 2022–2024 and bin them by token entropy. They observe that low-entropy tokens are often symbolic decisions (digits, operators), where NVFP4 keeps the correct token as top-1 but inflates the probability of a non-top-1 alternative, causing occasional wrong continuations. High-entropy tokens in uncertain steps, by contrast, show reduced entropy under quantization (e.g., discourse tokens such as “Alternatively”), narrowing the set of sampled continuations. Crucially, Fig. 3 shows that the mean token entropy of both low- and high-entropy token groups tracks the step entropy of the segment they fall in, motivating step-level control.

**ReSET policy (Sec. 4).** A reasoning step is approximated as a segment separated by double-newline delimiters. Step entropy is the average token entropy within the current step. ReSET’s step-aware threshold (SAT) compares an online step-entropy estimate against the running global mean token entropy:

- If the current step is confident (estimated step entropy ≤ global mean), use a fixed global threshold τ0 (80th percentile of token entropy on a small calibration split).
- If the step is uncertain (estimated step entropy > global mean), use the step entropy itself as the threshold, so symbolic tokens are sharpened relative to the typical token in that step.

Tokens with entropy below the chosen threshold use a low temperature Tlow; others use a base/high temperature. The default is Tbase = 0.6, Tlow calibrated per model/task, and Thigh = 1.0. The high temperature counteracts the entropy collapse observed in uncertain steps.

**Online step-entropy estimator (Eq. 3).** Because the offline step average requires future tokens, ReSET uses a causal hybrid estimator with window size w = 32: for the first w tokens of a step it uses a sliding-window average over recent tokens to reduce variance; afterwards it uses the within-step running average. The per-token overhead is one entropy reduction, two scalar updates, and a branch, amounting to ~100 µs per token on Qwen3-32B (~1.5% of a decode step).

**CUDA-core small-M kernel (Sec. 5).** Blackwell’s NVFP4 Tensor Core path fixes M = 128, so at SLO-feasible decode batches (M ≤ 8) tile occupancy is below 6.25% and measured Tensor-Core utilization falls under 1%. The authors implement a CUDA-core GEMV kernel with three design choices:

1. **Multi-token CTA fusion** — group multiple active decode tokens in one CTA so each streamed weight tile is reused across tokens.
2. **Multi-accumulator threading** — assign multiple output rows per thread to create independent FMA chains and expose instruction-level parallelism.
3. **Register-only dequantization** — unpack FP4 values to FP16 in registers, apply NVFP4 block scales, and feed directly into half2 FMAs, avoiding shared-memory materialization and barriers.

The runtime dispatches to the CUDA-core GEMV path for small-M decode and to a Tensor-Core GEMM path for prefill and large-M decode.

## Results & Highlights

**Setup.** Five models: R1-Distill-Qwen-7B/14B and Qwen3-8B/14B/32B. All quantized with NVIDIA ModelOpt to real NVFP4 W4A4 (E2M1 elements, E4M3 block scales, group size 16), KV cache kept in BF16. Benchmarks: AIME-120 (2022–2025), GPQA-Diamond, LiveCodeBench. Averages over 8 seeds, top-p = 0.95, max_tokens = 32k. Hardware: single NVIDIA B200, CUDA 13.1, PyTorch 2.10.0, vLLM + FlashInfer/CUTLASS.

**Accuracy (Table 1).** ReSET (applied on top of RTN) achieves the best average accuracy on every benchmark, with the largest margin on AIME-120: +2.6 points over the NVFP4 RTN baseline (39.6 → 43.8 for R1-Qwen-7B; 74.4 → 77.5 for Qwen3-32B). On GPQA-Diamond and LiveCodeBench the gains are smaller but consistent across most model sizes.

**Ablation vs. simpler entropy rules (Table 2).** A fixed entropy threshold and a sliding-window baseline improve over RTN only inconsistently, while ReSET is best on every model, indicating that step-relative entropy is the operative signal.

**Kernel latency (Table 4).** At M = 1, the CUDA-core GEMV kernel is 1.57–2.49× faster than vLLM-CUTLASS across the QKV/Out/Gate-Up/Down projections of Qwen3-8B/14B/32B. Activation-quantization overhead is 1.65–1.81 µs per layer, lower than rotation-fused PTQ (4.55–5.08 µs).

**End-to-end latency (Fig. 6).** Combining the custom kernel with ReSET, the system reaches up to 1.69× over BF16 on Qwen3-8B (B=1, 8k output) and up to 1.97× on Qwen3-32B (B=1), 1.85× at B=8. The ReSET sampler overhead (~100 µs/token) is included in these numbers.

**Other ablations.** Sweeping top-p and min-p (Table 5) yields limited or negative gains, confirming that simple distribution truncation does not substitute for step-aware control. Selective sharpening inside low-entropy steps (Table 6) is better than uniformly applying Tlow to the whole step (62.5 vs. 60.6 AIME-120 average). Hyperparameters are relatively robust: w ∈ {16,32,64,128} changes the average by 1.1 points; Tlow ∈ {0.1,0.2,0.3,0.4} by 0.4 points; Thigh = 1.0 peaks on most models.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty.** The central insight — that token-level entropy in reasoning traces is dominated by step-level uncertainty and therefore a poor control signal — is well motivated and empirically supported. The step-aware threshold and online hybrid estimator are a genuine algorithmic contribution, distinct from prior token-level entropy-aware decoding and test-time scaling work. The CUDA-core NVFP4 GEMV kernel is a strong systems contribution, targeting the underexplored small-M decode regime where Tensor-Core tiles collapse in utilization. That said, the idea of adaptive temperature scaling is not new; the novelty lies in the step-granularity observation and its efficient online realization.

**Rigor.** The experimental design is generally solid: real NVFP4 W4A4 quantization via NVIDIA ModelOpt, identical baselines, controlled hardware, and 8-seed averaging on three benchmarks. However, several calibration choices are thin: the global threshold τ0 is calibrated on only five randomly sampled NuminaMath problems per model, and Tlow is tuned per (model, task) on a held-out split. The paper does not report standard errors or confidence intervals, so it is hard to judge whether the +2.6 AIME-120 gain is statistically robust. Table 10 also shows that the best Thigh varies across models and the default Thigh = 1.0 is not uniformly optimal, suggesting some sensitivity.

**Reproducibility.** Code is promised at a public GitHub repository, and hardware/software details are explicit. Reproducing the kernel results, however, requires an NVIDIA B200 (sm_100a) and CUDA 13.1, which limits independent verification. The quantization pipeline uses proprietary NVIDIA ModelOpt, so exact reproduction depends on ModelOpt versioning.

**Significance.** The work is timely: Blackwell NVFP4 is being promoted as a 4× throughput/memory win, but this paper correctly identifies that the headline numbers do not materialize in latency-critical small-batch serving. Solving both accuracy and latency simultaneously is practically valuable. The accuracy gains are modest in absolute terms (a few points), and on LiveCodeBench the gains are smaller and sometimes the NVFP4 baseline already trails BF16, so ReSET does not fully close the reasoning-quality gap with BF16.

**Red flags / limitations.**
- The comparison with PTQ methods (BRQ, 4/6, MR-GPTQ) is somewhat apples-to-oranges: ReSET is a decoding-time intervention on top of RTN, whereas the others change the quantized weights. The paper does combine ReSET with GPTQ and finds little extra benefit (Table 12), but does not deeply analyze why.
- The baselines for adaptive decoding are limited to a fixed entropy threshold, sliding window, top-p, and min-p sweeps. Stronger entropy- or confidence-aware decoding methods from the literature are not compared.
- The evaluation is restricted to Qwen-family models; whether the observed entropy dynamics generalize to other reasoning architectures (e.g., LLaMA-reasoning, Kimi k1.5, OpenAI o-series) is unknown.
- Kernel speedups are reported for projection microbenchmarks and end-to-end under small-batch, long-output conditions; throughput-oriented large-batch regimes and other GPUs are explicitly out of scope.
- Some claims use “up to” language that highlights best-case numbers rather than averages.

## Strengths

- Tackles a real deployment pain point: NVFP4 latency/accuracy in small-batch reasoning serving.
- Strong algorithm–system co-design: step-aware temperature scaling paired with a custom CUDA-core kernel.
- Clear empirical motivation for the step-level analysis via Fig. 3 and detailed token-level case studies.
- Efficient online estimator with negligible per-token overhead (~1.5% of a decode step).
- Open-source release and detailed hardware/software configuration support reproducibility.
- Honest limitations section acknowledging restricted model families, hardware, and task scope.

## Limitations

- The global entropy threshold is calibrated from only five problems; robustness to broader calibration sets is not shown.
- No comparison with richer families of adaptive decoding or uncertainty-calibration methods from the literature.
- Evaluation limited to Qwen-family reasoning models on math/science/code benchmarks; generalization to other model families and domains is unverified.
- The CUDA-core kernel benefits are specific to NVIDIA Blackwell’s NVFP4 small-M decode regime; other low-precision formats (MXFP4, INT4) and GPUs are not studied.
- End-to-end speedups are best-case under small batch/long output; larger batches and shorter contexts are likely less favorable.
- Some accuracy gains are small (e.g., LiveCodeBench), and NVFP4 still does not consistently match BF16 reasoning quality even with ReSET.

## Relevance

该论文高度契合“Computation Reliability & Precision Errors”中对量化误差、浮点精度与数值稳定性的关注：它系统刻画了 NVFP4 量化如何扭曲推理链中的 token 级不确定性，并提出了一种在线、低开销的温度缩放方案来 mitigate 这些错误。同时，它也属于“LLM Inference Evaluation”范畴，因为它在低精度推理的真实部署条件下同时评测了推理质量与解码延迟，对需要在 Blackwell 等硬件上部署大推理模型的人具有直接参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How would ReSET perform on non-Qwen reasoning families (e.g., LLaMA-reasoning, Kimi k1.5, OpenAI o-series), and does the step-entropy distribution depend on the specific RL/SFT distillation recipe used?
2. Can the step-aware temperature policy be combined with other quantization-recovery techniques such as QAT, AWQ, or GPTQ beyond the brief GPTQ experiment, and does the combination yield additive gains?
3. How does ReSET compare with stronger adaptive-decoding baselines such as SEAL, confidence-based thresholding, or test-time scaling methods that modify sampling rather than quantization?
4. What is the sensitivity of the calibration (τ0, Tlow, Thigh) to the number and diversity of calibration problems, and can the thresholds be estimated online without any task-specific split?
5. Does the CUDA-core small-M kernel design generalize to other microscaling formats such as MXFP4 or to integer INT4/INT8 decode paths on other GPU architectures?
