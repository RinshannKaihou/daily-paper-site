> **一句话总结：** 本文提出将合成数据生成（SDG）的公平性重新定义为“差别影响”（disparate impact），系统论证了当 SDG 方法无法完美复现真实分布时，近似误差、样本不均衡与差分隐私噪声会以不同方式损害不同敏感群体，并提出按群体分别建模的元算法作为缓解基线。

> **TL;DR:** This paper reframes fairness in synthetic data generation (SDG) as disparate impact: synthetic data should be equally useful across sensitive groups. It shows that approximation errors, unbalanced group sizes, and differential-privacy noise can disproportionately harm minority or structurally complex groups, and proposes a group-wise SDG meta-algorithm as a mitigation baseline.

## 核心贡献

- 提出一种新的 SDG 公平性视角：以“差别影响”而非“去偏见”为核心，强调合成数据在各个敏感群体上的效用应当相等，而合成分布与真实分布一致时即可在期望上消除差别影响。
- 系统剖析了三类导致差别影响的机制：
  1. **近似误差（approximation error）**：当某群体的条件分布复杂度超出 SDG 方法的假设类时，该群体被更差地建模；
  2. **估计误差（estimation error）**：样本量较小或方差较大的群体，其统计量估计更不稳定；
  3. **差分隐私（DP）误差**：隐私噪声的信噪比在不同群体间不均衡，往往对少数群体更不利。
- 在受控人工分布和美国社区调查（ACS）真实数据上进行了大量实验，验证了这些误差来源可以单独、叠加甚至方法特异性地产生差别影响。
- 提出并评估了一种“按敏感群体分别学习 SDG 模型”的元算法（group-wise SDG），证明其在多数设定下能同时提升整体效用与公平性，但在强隐私预算下效果会下降。

## Key Contributions

- Introduces a disparate-impact framing for fairness in synthetic data generation, where fairness means equal synthetic-data utility across sensitive groups, and where matching the real distribution eliminates disparate impact in expectation.
- Decomposes sources of disparate impact into:
  1. **Approximation errors** when group-conditional distributions exceed the model's hypothesis class;
  2. **Estimation errors** due to smaller group sizes or higher variance;
  3. **Differential-privacy noise** whose signal-to-noise ratio varies across groups.
- Empirically validates these mechanisms on controlled artificial distributions and on real American Community Survey (ACS) data, showing they can act alone, cumulatively, or in method-specific ways.
- Proposes and evaluates a group-wise SDG meta-algorithm that learns separate models per sensitive group; it improves utility and fairness in many settings but underperforms in high-privacy regimes.

## Methodology

The paper studies SDG through the lens of probabilistic graphical models (PGMs), including MST, PrivBayes, GreedyBayes, and AIM, across differential-privacy budgets ε ∈ {1, 10, 100} and non-private variants.

- **Fairness definition (Definition 1).** Given a downstream function f (e.g., a classifier or the identity), a utility metric u, and tolerance τ, an SDG method has no τ-disparate impact if the utility loss from using synthetic rather than real data is approximately equal across all sensitive groups. A task-agnostic instantiation uses distribution distances such as Wasserstein or total variation distance (TVD).
- **Group-wise SDG (Algorithm 1).** The authors learn separate group-conditional distributions D̃_k and noisy group proportions, then sample synthetic records by first drawing a group and then drawing features from that group's model. They prove the algorithm preserves (ε, δ)-DP via parallel composition.
- **Controlled experiments.** They construct 6-attribute Bayesian-network distributions with a binary sensitive attribute, varying (i) group proportions, (ii) group-conditional distribution complexity, and (iii) both simultaneously. They draw 10 random distributions and 40,000 records per setting.
- **Real-world experiments.** They use ACS data (≈490,000 records from five U.S. states, restricted to white/black and male/female) with gender × ethnicity as sensitive groups. Data is split 80/10/10 train/validation/test across 10 replicas. SDG methods generate 500,000 records; XGBoost classifiers are trained on synthetic vs. real data and evaluated on a held-out real test set.
- **Metrics.** Distribution fidelity is measured by TVD on n-way marginals (n = 1,…,4) and joint distributions. Downstream utility is measured by AUROC, AURPC, accuracy, and balanced accuracy.

## Results & Highlights

**Controlled distributions (δ_TVD = TVD(D̃_1, D_1) − TVD(D̃_0, D_0)):**

- **Unbalanced sample sizes** consistently disadvantage the minority group (S=1 at P=0.1), even for near-private methods like PrivBayes(ε=100), which is otherwise 0.01-fair.
- **Heterogeneous complexity** causes disparate approximation errors: MST and GreedyBayes(ρ=2) disadvantage the group whose distribution requires a higher in-degree.
- **Cumulative effects** appear in the double-disadvantage setting, where smaller group size and higher complexity combine. Non-DP AIM, which has no disparate impact in base or higher-complexity settings, shows impact here due to graph-selection bias favoring the majority group.
- **DP impact** is method- and setting-dependent. AIM(ε=1) shows disparate impact in the higher-complexity setting where its non-DP counterpart does not, because privacy noise interferes with graph selection. PrivBayes does not show this specific effect, highlighting method-specific interactions. MST shows no visible DP effect because approximation error dominates.
- **Group-wise modeling** generally reduces disparate impact and brings the double-disadvantage setting closer to the fewer-samples setting, by preventing the algorithm from selecting a graph that only fits the majority group. However, under strong DP (ε=1), group-wise modeling suffers from heterogeneous signal-to-noise ratios and lower overall utility.

**ACS real-world data:**

- **Distribution fidelity (2-way marginals, Table 1).** All methods show disparate impact disadvantaging Black people. Methods with broader hypothesis classes (e.g., AIM, GreedyBayes with larger ρ) achieve better fidelity and lower disparity. Group-wise modeling improves utility for all groups and reduces disparity for most methods, except under ε=1 where it underperforms.
- **Downstream AUROC (Table 2).** Utility loss is generally higher for Black groups, though the exact ordering varies by method. Broadening the hypothesis class helps all groups. Group-wise AIM further improves both utility and fairness; for other methods, group-wise modeling can increase disparate impact, showing that distribution-level and downstream utility metrics are not always aligned.

## Critical Assessment

- **Conceptual contribution.** The disparate-impact reframing is a clear and useful contribution. It separates the question of "Is the real data biased?" from "Does the SDG process faithfully represent all groups?" However, this framing assumes the real distribution is normatively acceptable to reproduce. In contexts where real data encodes historical injustice, practitioners may still want to actively debias, not merely avoid disparate impact.
- **Scope.** The empirical analysis is concentrated on PGM-based methods for tabular categorical data. The mechanisms identified are general, but whether they transfer to deep generative models (VAEs, GANs, diffusion models, LLM-based generators) or to continuous/high-dimensional data remains an open question.
- **Causal claims.** The authors are careful to use language like "we posit" and "we hypothesize" when interpreting graph-selection bias and DP effects, which is appropriate. Still, the controlled experiments strongly support the direction of their claims.
- **Group-wise modeling trade-off.** The proposed strategy is a sensible baseline but not a panacea. It loses statistical power for statistics shared across groups and can worsen results under strong privacy. The authors acknowledge this honestly.
- **Metric alignment.** The paper demonstrates that distribution-fidelity metrics and downstream-classifier utility can diverge in their fairness implications. This is an important caution for practitioners who might rely on a single proxy metric.
- **Reproducibility.** Public code and seeds are provided, and the methodology is described in detail in the appendices. This strengthens confidence in the findings.

## Strengths

- Clear conceptual distinction between de-biasing and disparate-impact framings.
- Systematic decomposition of error sources with controlled experiments isolating each factor.
- Strong empirical coverage across multiple SDG methods, privacy budgets, and both artificial and real data.
- Honest reporting of cases where group-wise modeling fails or where metrics disagree.
- Public code and data for reproducibility.

## Limitations

- Empirical scope is limited to PGM-based SDG on tabular categorical data.
- The fairness goal of matching the real distribution may conflict with ethical or legal obligations to correct biased data.
- Group-wise modeling can harm sample efficiency and may not scale to many intersecting sensitive attributes or very small groups.
- Some nuanced method-specific behaviors (e.g., AIM's annealing mechanism) complicate generalization.
- Downstream utility results depend on the choice of classifier (XGBoost) and prediction threshold; other downstream tasks may show different patterns.

## Relevance

本文对合成数据、隐私计算和公平机器学习社区都有重要参考价值。它提醒研究者与实践者：即便原始数据没有明显偏见，生成过程本身也可能因模型表达力、样本不均衡和隐私噪声而系统性损害某些群体。在设计面向公共部门、医疗或金融等高风险场景的合成数据系统时，差别影响评估应成为标准流程的一部分。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How do the identified mechanisms of disparate impact manifest in deep generative models and in continuous or high-dimensional data domains?
- Can group-wise SDG be improved by sharing statistical strength across groups while still preserving privacy and avoiding majority-group-biased graph selection?
- How should practitioners formally trade off the goal of faithfully representing the real distribution against the goal of correcting historical or structural biases?
- What are the formal fairness guarantees of group-wise SDG under tight differential privacy, and can adaptive privacy budget allocation across groups help?
- How sensitive are downstream utility disparities to the choice of downstream model, task, and evaluation metric?
