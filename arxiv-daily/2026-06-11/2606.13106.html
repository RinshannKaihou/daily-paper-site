SWITCH makes hidden-state-recurrence latent reasoning compatible with standard on-policy RL using learnable <swi>/</swi> boundary tokens.

Demystifying Hidden-State Recurrence: Switchable Latent Reasoning with On-Policy Reinforcement Learning

Jiayu Yang, Chao Chen, Shengen Wu, Yinhong Liu, Yuxuan Fan, Lujundong Li, Songning Lai, Chengwei Qin, Zhijiang Guo

latent-chain-of-thought hidden-state-recurrence GRPO mechanistic-interpretability math-reasoning

Key Metric

79.3% MATH-500

Latent-conditional accuracy rises from 66.7% to 79.3% (+12.6 points) after Switch-GRPO.

Why This Matters

It shows latent reasoning can be trained with standard GRPO and made systematically interpretable, opening shorter and faster reasoning paths.

Critical Edge

The headline 79.3% result is from the strongest end-to-end run; the diagnostic trajectory peaks at 72.6%, leaving an unexplained gap.

Methodology

SWITCH extends Coconut-style hidden-state recurrence with three special tokens: <swi> to enter, </swi> to exit, and <latent> as a placeholder.

  • Phase 1: SFT predicts switch boundaries on high-entropy spans of visible chain-of-thought.
  • Phase 2: A latent curriculum progressively replaces span text with <latent> positions, forcing reasoning into continuous representation space.
  • Phase 3: Switch-GRPO runs real hidden-state-injection rollouts and computes policy ratios only at text positions; latent segments replay identically during gradients.

Results & Highlights

  • On Qwen3-8B, SWITCH reaches 79.3% on MATH-500 and 89.2% on GSM8K, +25.7 points above the best Coconut-style baseline.
  • Switch-GRPO lifts latent-conditional accuracy from 66.7% to 79.3% while the switch rate falls from 81% to 58%.
  • Visible tokens shrink from ~2,839 to ~1,702; training becomes unstable beyond ~1,200 steps, so the reported checkpoint is early-stopped at step 800.
  • A brevity-reward operating point reaches 69.0% MATH-500 accuracy at 1,276 visible tokens with 0% max-length truncation.
  • Mechanistic probes show <swi> probability is sharply localized at boundaries; zeroing the injected hidden state collapses diagnostic accuracy from 100% to 33.3%.

Strengths

  • One simple primitive—learned boundary tokens—addresses both RL training and mechanistic analysis.
  • Switch-GRPO rollouts match inference, avoiding the training/evaluation mismatch of text-only latent-CoT RL.
  • Mechanistic analysis is unusually rigorous: boundary statistics, linear probes, and causal activation interventions.

Limitations

  • Scope is narrow: Qwen3-8B, MATH-500, and GSM8K only.
  • Latent states are not directly optimized by RL; gradient to latent representations is indirect.
  • Late-training reward hacking requires careful early stopping and may complicate training at larger scale.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does SWITCH compare head-to-head with vocabulary-mixture latent-CoT methods such as Latent-GRPO or SofT-GRPO?
  • Can the latent representation be directly optimized by RL rather than only indirectly through the text policy and KV cache?
  • Does the learned switch policy transfer to non-math domains, coding, or long-horizon planning tasks?
  • Would larger models exhibit different latent-depth dynamics, and can reward hacking beyond ~1,200 steps be mitigated by regularization?
  • Can boundary-token probes and causal interventions localize distinct subroutines inside the latent block?
