An approximate simulator of the true data-generating process is enough to recover classical VC-dimension guarantees for online learning with dependent data.
Learning with Simulators: No Regret in a Computationally Bounded World
Sasha Voitovych · Abhishek Shetty · Noah Golowich · Alexander Rakhlin
learning-theory online-learning VC-dimension Kolmogorov-complexity simulatable-processes
Õ(√dT · (1 + KL))
Agnostic regret with conditional simulators — matching classical PAC rates for all VC classes.

Formalizes whether simulators can replace i.i.d. assumptions for learning with dependent data, and answers yes with constructive algorithmic guarantees.

The universal algorithm's regret contains a 2Kp(P*) term that becomes astronomical for complex processes — more a conceptual existence result than a practical tool.

Methodology

  • Setting: T-round online learning; covariates drawn from an arbitrary joint distribution P* (no independence or mixing). Learner predicts Ŷ, observes Y = f*(X), minimises expected regret vs. a VC class ℱ.
  • MultiCover (Algorithm 1, unconditional): Draws multi-scale covers from the simulator's marginal; runs Hedge/EXP over the cover union. Multi-scale structure adapts to the density ratio dP*/dP, converting KL error into a T/log(1+N/T) · KL term instead of the weaker √(T·KL).
  • Log-MGF + Epoching (Algorithm 2, conditional): Controls the MGF of regret directly via a relaxation framework. Splits [T] into K epochs so per-epoch KL averages down to KL/K. Uses poly(T) conditional rollouts to estimate the minimax prediction.
  • Universal result: Invokes time-bounded Kolmogorov complexity Kp(P*) and the universal distribution μT to build a single simulator that dominates all p-time-samplable processes in density ratio — avoiding the cryptographic hardness of computing Kp by only needing unconditional samples.

  • Lower bounds (Thm 3.3) match the upper bound in d, dT/N, and the T/log N · KL term — the analysis is tight.
  • No poly(T,d) algorithm exists unconditionally (Thm 3.4), unless TN ≤ 2cd.
  • Conditional sampling bridges the impossible → tractable gap for agnostic learning (Thm 4.1).
  • LDS and Glauber results require no mixing-time assumptions and no exponential dimension dependence.

Strengths

  • Unifies i.i.d. and strongly dependent data learning under one "simulator-as-structure" view.
  • Upper and lower bounds match, cleanly separating the power of unconditional vs. conditional oracles.
  • Elegant combination of information-theoretic redundancy, time-bounded Kolmogorov complexity, and learning theory for the universal result.
  • Concrete instantiations (LDS, Glauber) break previous reliance on mixing or smoothness assumptions.

Limitations

  • Purely theoretical — no experiments or numerical validation on even synthetic LDS/Glauber data.
  • Simulator and ERM oracle are black-box assumptions; constructing high-quality simulators is itself an open problem.
  • 2Kp(P*) regret term can be astronomically large — the universal result is conceptual.
  • Agnostic learning requires conditional sampling, which is cryptographically hard for general processes.
  • KL divergence may diverge or be hard to estimate in high-dimensional, long-sequence settings.
  • Oblivious-label assumption (f* fixed before the game) excludes adaptive/adversarial scenarios.

Open Questions

  1. Can MultiCover and Log-MGF epoching be approximated in practice, with regret verified on synthetic LDS/Glauber data?
  2. Are there more robust alternatives to KL divergence (e.g. Wasserstein, MMD) that yield similar guarantees?
  3. How can one construct or learn a conditional simulator from observed sequences when P* is unknown?
  4. Can the 2Kp(P*) term be improved? What is Kp(P*) for typical "natural" processes?
  5. Can the framework extend to multi-class, regression, RL, or partially observable policy learning?
  6. Can structured assumptions (e.g. autoregressive models) bypass the cryptographic hardness of conditional sampling?
  7. Which results survive when the oblivious-label assumption is relaxed?
