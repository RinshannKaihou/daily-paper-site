> **一句话总结：** PolicyGuard 通过在学习正常行为流形的加性高斯过程（GP）上计算自适应后验方差，实现了对强化学习智能体在测试阶段逐时间步的后门检测，在七种游戏、两类攻击（包括硬编码攻击）下均取得了领先的检测 AUROC，且无需访问受害者模型参数或完整未来轨迹。

> **TL;DR:** PolicyGuard performs test-time, step-level backdoor detection for RL agents by computing adaptive Gaussian Process posterior variance on a learned manifold of normal behavior, achieving strong AUROC across seven games and both perturbation-based and adversary-agent attacks—including hard-coded attacks that defeat optimization-based defenses—without requiring model parameters or future trajectories.

## 核心贡献

- 提出 **PolicyGuard**，一种面向 RL 智能体的**测试时、逐时间步**后门检测防御方法。
- 使用**加性高斯过程（GP）**建模正常轨迹，并结合**自适应伪轨迹（pseudo trajectories）**为单个时间步计算上下文感知的后验方差。
- 从理论上证明：在足够干净数据下良性状态-动作对的后验方差可趋于零，而后门触发对的方差显著更高。
- 在 **7 个 RL 游戏**（Pong、Breakout、Space Invaders、RTGA、RTGH、YSNP、Sumo）上验证，对**扰动攻击**平均 AUROC 达 **0.856**，对**对抗智能体攻击**达 **0.859**；在更难检测的**硬编码攻击**下仍保持 **0.868 / 0.878**，而现有基于优化的防御几乎失效（AUROC ≈ 0.5）。

## Key Contributions

- **PolicyGuard**, a test-time, step-level backdoor defense for RL agents.
- An **additive Gaussian Process** model of normal trajectories plus **adaptive pseudo trajectories** to compute context-aware posterior variance for individual timesteps.
- Theoretical justification: benign state-action pairs can drive posterior variance to zero with sufficient clean data, whereas backdoor-triggered pairs yield higher variance (Theorem 3.1, Corollary 3.2, Theorem 3.3).
- Extensive evaluation on **seven RL games** under perturbation-based and adversary-agent attacks, with average AUROC of **0.856 / 0.859**; strong robustness to **hard-coded attacks** that bypass Neural Cleanse, STRIP, SCALE-UP, and PolicyCleanse.

## Methodology

**Behavioral manifold learning.** PolicyGuard first collects trajectories from a **clean environment** (black-box access to the agent, no internal parameters). It encodes state-action pairs with environment-specific encoders—**CNN + GRU** for Atari image inputs, **MLP + GRU** for MuJoCo low-dimensional states. The final hidden state is projected to an episode embedding.

**Additive GP with deep recurrent kernels.** An additive Gaussian Process predicts the episode final reward:

\[ f = \alpha_t f_t + \alpha_e f_e \]

where \(f_t\) models timestep correlations via an SE kernel on step latent representations, and \(f_e\) models episode-level patterns via an SE kernel on episode embeddings. Training uses sparse variational inference with inducing points \(Z\) and maximizes the ELBO, end-to-end over encoder, GRU, GP kernel, and variational parameters.

**Step-level uncertainty via pseudo trajectories.** At test time the full future of a suspicious step is unknown, so PolicyGuard builds **pseudo trajectories** by concatenating the observed history up to time \(t\) with future segments from held-out reference trajectories:

\[ \tilde{X}^{(i)} = \{x_1, \dots, x_t, \tilde{x}_{t+1}^{(i)}, \dots, \tilde{x}_T^{(i)}\} \]

Each pseudo trajectory is fed through the trained encoder and GP, and the posterior variance at step \(t\) is extracted. The scores are aggregated across pseudo trajectories using the **Interquartile Mean (IQM)** to produce the final uncertainty score \(U(s_t, a_t)\). Backdoor-triggered steps deviate from the learned clean manifold and therefore produce elevated variance.

## Results & Highlights

- **Detection performance (Table 1).** On the original attack setting, PolicyGuard achieves overall AUROC of **0.856** for perturbation-based attacks and **0.859** for adversary-agent attacks. STRIP scores slightly higher on perturbation attacks (0.900) but requires predicted probability vectors; PolicyGuard uses only state-action pairs and is fully black-box.
- **Hard-coded attacks.** When backdoor behavior is hard-coded in a separate adversarial policy (bypassing learned parameters), Neural Cleanse, STRIP, SCALE-UP, and PolicyCleanse collapse to near-random AUROC (~0.5). PolicyGuard remains strong: **0.868** on perturbation and **0.878** on adversary-agent attacks, outperforming SHINE (0.826 / 0.810).
- **Uncertainty separation.** Figure 2 shows clear separation between backdoor and benign uncertainty distributions across all seven games.
- **Latency.** Figure 3 reports competitive inference latency—slightly slower than STRIP/SCALE-UP but ~9% faster than SHINE.
- **Q-Inception (Table 2).** Against a recent perturbation-based attack, PolicyGuard achieves AUROC **0.843 / 0.864** on Pong / Breakout with FPR around **0.10**.
- **Adaptive attacks (Figure 5).** Attackers optimizing trojan actions to match benign uncertainty cannot implant the trojan at 20% poisoning rate; at ≥40% poisoning the attack works but clean performance drops sharply and uncertainty still rises above benign levels.

## Critical Assessment

- **Strength of the hard-coded evaluation:** The most compelling result is that PolicyGuard detects hard-coded backdoors that defeat parameter-level reverse-engineering defenses. This is a realistic threat model for deployed RL agents.
- **Clean-environment assumption:** The defense requires a clean environment to collect reference trajectories and train the GP. While standard in prior work (PolicyCleanse, SHINE, BIRD), it is a non-trivial assumption for real-world deployment where environment shift is common.
- **Detection, not mitigation:** PolicyGuard flags infected steps but does not itself intervene. The paper suggests composing with existing mitigation strategies, but a complete detect-intervene pipeline remains future work.
- **Theoretical caveats:** The variance-bound theorems assume Lipschitz-continuous kernels and sufficient clean data; the detection-efficacy theorem assumes a distributional gap between backdoor and benign pairs. These assumptions are reasonable but not guaranteed in all domains.
- **Evaluation scope:** Experiments are entirely in simulation (Atari / MuJoCo). Real-world RL involves partial observability, sensor noise, and non-stationary dynamics, none of which are validated here.
- **Computational cost:** GP inference with 100–600 inducing points and pseudo-trajectory Monte Carlo per step is more expensive than simple input-sanitization baselines, though the paper shows it is competitive with SHINE.

## Strengths

- First practical test-time, step-level RL backdoor defense that works in black-box settings without future trajectories.
- Strong and broad empirical validation across seven games and two attack paradigms.
- Robust to hard-coded attacks that break optimization-based defenses.
- Theoretically grounded motivation for using GP posterior variance as an uncertainty signal.
- Detailed ablations on patch size, trigger-action length, pseudo-trajectory size, random perturbations, and adaptive attacks.

## Limitations

- Requires access to a clean environment for training the GP trajectory model.
- Detects backdoors but does not include built-in intervention or mitigation.
- Evaluated only in simulated domains; sim-to-real reliability is unknown.
- Effectiveness depends on the quality and quantity of clean reference trajectories.
- GP inference and pseudo-trajectory enumeration add per-step computational overhead.
- Not tested against continuous-action or partially observable environments beyond the chosen benchmarks.

## Relevance

对强化学习安全、后门攻击检测以及对抗鲁棒性研究具有直接参考价值；PolicyGuard 的“测试时+逐时间步+黑盒”范式为在线部署的 RL 系统提供了一条不依赖模型内部参数的实用防御路径，尤其适合需要早期发现异常行为的安全关键场景。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How would PolicyGuard perform in continuous-control or partially observable environments where trajectory manifolds are more complex?
2. Can the clean-environment requirement be relaxed through domain adaptation or sim-to-real transfer?
3. What is the most effective intervention strategy to pair with PolicyGuard’s detection signal (e.g., safe default actions, input sanitization, human oversight)?
4. How does the method scale to very long episodes or high-dimensional observations (e.g., pixel streams in autonomous driving)?
5. Can adaptive attackers craft triggers that lie exactly on the learned clean manifold, and if so, what additional inductive biases would be needed to detect them?
