Why This Matters

It bridges a cognitive bias to internal model computation, enabling more robust, interpretable language models and targeted bias mitigation.

Critical Edge

Attribution-based circuit localization is an approximation, so the identified pathways are correlates, not conclusively causal.

Methodology

The authors convert numerical anchoring into a controlled, mechanistically tractable multiple-choice task.

Task design

  • 100 items from the Open Anchoring Question Dataset (OpAQ) adapted to a fixed nine-option format.
  • Each item includes the ground-truth answer, a low anchor, a high anchor, and intermediate values.
  • Three prompt variants per item: low-anchor, high-anchor, and a token-matched control with a non-numeric placeholder.
  • 20 random option permutations per question for behavior; four fixed rotations for attribution.

Localization target

Answer-level logit difference:

m = logit(ycorrect) − logit(yanchor)
  • ∆m = manchor − mcontrol tracks how much the anchor gains on the correct answer.
  • ∆m correlates with behavioral expected-value shift, validating it as a circuit target.

Methods compared

  • NAP · EAP · NAP-IG · EAP-IG
  • Faithfulness = Circuit Performance Recovery (CPR): area under the curve of control–anchored gap recovered as top-k% nodes/edges are retained.

Results & Highlights

  • Behavioral anchoring is elicited in all four models, strongest in Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct. ∆m is negative and significant (p < 10⁻¹⁵).
  • Metric tracks behavior: Spearman correlations between −∆m and EV shift reach 0.73 (Qwen-7B) and 0.75 (Llama-8B) on high-anchor contrasts.
  • Edge-level wins: On Qwen-7B low-anchor, CPR = 0.971 for EAP-IG versus 0.361 for NAP. EAP-IG is the most faithful method overall.
  • Instruction tuning hurts EAP more than EAP-IG; EAP-IG stays above 0.91 CPR across instruction-tuned settings.
  • Family-specific depth: Qwen attribution concentrates mid-to-late; Llama concentrates earlier. Within a model, low- and high-anchor depth profiles are nearly identical (Pearson r > 0.99).
  • Attention dominance: 95–96% of full-graph edges are attention-to-attention; in top-5% circuits this drops to 68–80%, with more attention–MLP edges selected.
  • Shared structure: Low- and high-anchor circuits overlap 0.49–0.55 by Jaccard and 0.66–0.71 shared fraction; cross-contrast CPR is 0.933–0.981.
  • Weak cross-variant transfer: Base and instruction-tuned variants share only 0.64–0.66 of top-5% edges; faithfulness drops sharply when a circuit is ranked on the other variant.

Strengths

  • Clearly links a cognitive bias to internal model computation through a validated, answer-level metric.
  • Controlled task with token-matched controls, multiple option orderings, and letter-only responses.
  • Direct comparison of node- vs. edge-level and standard vs. integrated-gradient attribution patching.
  • Examines both structural overlap and functional transfer, richer than overlap alone.
  • Highlights the impact of instruction tuning on circuit structure, a practically important post-training finding.

Limitations

  • Small, narrow dataset: only 100 OpAQ items limit generalizability.
  • Multiple-choice format may not reflect open-ended numerical estimation or real-world anchoring.
  • A single scalar target (∆m) may miss other distributional facets of anchoring.
  • Attribution patching approximates causality; circuits are correlates, not definitive causal maps.
  • Only two model families at 7B–8B; broader architectural and scale coverage is needed.
  • No intervention experiments test whether localized pathways are causally necessary.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Do depth-localization and circuit-transfer patterns hold for larger models (14B, 70B) and other architectures?
  • How do the identified circuits change under activation patching, pruning, or debiased fine-tuning?
  • Do the same anchoring pathways appear in open-ended numerical generation, or is the multiple-choice setup driving the structure?
  • Would alternative objectives (KL divergence, total variation, anchor-side probability) identify overlapping or complementary pathways?
  • Can the shared low/high-anchor structure be exploited for a single, contrast-agnostic debiasing method?
