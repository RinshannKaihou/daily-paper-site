> **一句话总结：** Orch-RM 是一种自监督的编排层奖励模型，它从现有多智能体编排器训练日志中抽取“ Specialist-over-base / 正确-over-错误”等胜负对，训练 Bradley-Terry 奖励模型来直接为编排方案打分，从而在测试时缩放中将验证 token 降低约 10–60 倍、准确率提升最高 8%，并能在持续训练中用显著更少的 token 提升或复现 MAS-Orchestra 的性能。

> **TL;DR:** Orch-RM is a self-supervised orchestration-level reward model that trains a Bradley-Terry RM on automatically constructed win-lose orchestration pairs extracted from MAS-Orchestra training artifacts. It enables efficient Best-of-N test-time scaling (up to ~10–60× fewer verification tokens and up to 8% accuracy gains) and efficient orchestrator continued training, matching or exceeding trajectory-level GRPO while using an order of magnitude less compute.

## 核心贡献

- 提出 **Orchestration Reward Modeling（Orch-RM）**，把奖励信号从“完整子智能体 rollout”上移到“编排方案”层面，直接为 orchestrator 生成的 multi-agent 计划 \(z\) 打分。
- 设计了一套**无需人工标注**的偏好数据构造流程：利用已训练 MAS orchestrator（MAS-Orchestra）的中间产物（模型 checkpoint、采样轨迹），构造两类配对——
  - **Specialized-over-base / Specialized-over-other-domains**：训练后的 orchestrator 方案优于基础模型或其他域方案；
  - **Correct-over-incorrect**：同一问题下最终答案正确的轨迹对应的编排方案优于错误的方案。
- 用 Bradley-Terry 目标训练奖励模型，使其能够对编排方案给出质量排序。
- 将 Orch-RM 同时用于：
  - **测试时缩放（Test-Time Scaling）**：对多个候选编排方案做 Best-of-N / weighted BoN 选择，再执行一次完整 rollout；
  - **编排器训练**：作为 GRPO 的奖励信号，支持从零训练和持续训练。
- 在数学推理（AIME 24&25）、网页问答（BrowseComp+）、多跳推理（HotpotQA）以及科学推理零样本迁移（GPQA）上验证，Orch-RM 在准确率和效率之间取得了更强的帕累托权衡。

## Key Contributions

- Introduces **Orchestration Reward Modeling (Orch-RM)**, which moves the reward signal from full sub-agent rollouts up to the **orchestration plan** \(z\) produced by the orchestrator.
- Proposes a **self-supervised, annotation-free** preference-data construction pipeline using intermediate artifacts from an already-trained MAS orchestrator (MAS-Orchestra):
  - **Specialized-over-base / over-other-domain**: plans from the trained orchestrator preferred over base-model or cross-domain plans;
  - **Correct-over-incorrect**: plans that lead to correct final answers preferred over those that lead to wrong answers.
- Trains a Bradley-Terry reward model to score and rank orchestration plans.
- Applies Orch-RM to two downstream problems:
  - **Test-time scaling**: Best-of-N / weighted BoN selection over sampled orchestrations, followed by a single full rollout;
  - **Orchestrator training**: a dense reward signal for GRPO, supporting both training-from-scratch and continued training.
- Evaluates on AIME 24&25, BrowseComp+, HotpotQA, and zero-shot GPQA, demonstrating a consistently stronger accuracy-efficiency trade-off than trajectory-level baselines.

## Methodology

### 3.1 Preliminaries: Bradley-Terry Reward Modeling

The paper adopts the standard Bradley-Terry preference model. Given a prompt \(x\) and two candidate responses \(y_1, y_2\), the probability that \(y_1\) is preferred over \(y_2\) is

\[
p^*(y_1 \succ y_2 \mid x) \triangleq \sigma\left[r^*(x, y_1) - r^*(x, y_2)\right].
\]

A parametric reward model \(r_\phi(x, y)\) is trained by maximizing likelihood over a comparison dataset \(\mathcal{D} = \{(x^{(i)}, y_w^{(i)}, y_l^{(i)})\}\):

\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{RM}}(\phi) = -\mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{D}} \log \sigma\left[r_\phi(x, y_w) - r_\phi(x, y_l)\right].
\]

### 3.2 Orch-RM: From Trajectory Rewards to Orchestration Rewards

In an orchestration-based MAS, a trajectory is defined as \(\tau = (x, z, Y, a)\), where:
- \(x\) is the user query;
- \(z\) is the orchestration plan (sub-agent roles, descriptions, collaboration graph);
- \(Y = \{y_1, \dots, y_k\}\) are intermediate sub-agent outputs;
- \(a\) is the final aggregated answer.

Orch-RM learns \(r_\phi(x, z)\), a scalar reward assigned **only to the orchestration plan**, before any sub-agent rollout. This avoids two bottlenecks:
- **Performance bottleneck**: the quality of the final answer is largely determined by \(z\);
- **Efficiency bottleneck**: executing full trajectories \(Y\) is expensive, especially during training where each gradient step may require many rollouts.

**Data construction.** Two sources of preference pairs are built automatically:

1. **Specialized-over-base (\(\pi_\theta \succ \pi_0\))**: sample 10 orchestrations per query from the trained orchestrator and 10 from the backbone base model; the trained model’s plans are generally preferred.
2. **Correct-over-incorrect (\(z^+ \succ z^-\))**: from MAS-Orchestra training logs, pair orchestrations whose full trajectories end in correct versus incorrect answers.

The default setting mixes these sources (domain-level vs. correctness-based) to stabilize generalization.

**Training objective.** The model is fine-tuned with the standard Bradley-Terry loss on orchestration pairs:

\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{Orch-RM}}(\phi) = -\mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{D}} \log \sigma\left[r_\phi(x, z_w) - r_\phi(x, z_l)\right].
\]

### 3.3 Downstream Applications

**Test-Time Scaling.** For a query \(x\), the orchestrator samples \(N\) plans \(\{z_n\}\) in parallel. Orch-RM selects

\[
z_b = \arg\max_{z \in \mathcal{Z}} r_\phi(x, z),
\]

and only \(z_b\) is executed by the sub-agents. This replaces the common but expensive practice of running multiple full MAS rollouts and then aggregating answers.

**Orchestrator Training.** Orch-RM replaces the sparse final-answer reward in GRPO. For a group of sampled plans \(\{z_n\}\), advantages are normalized as

\[
A_n = r_\phi(x, z_n) - \frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=1}^{N} r_\phi(x, z_j),
\]

and the policy is updated with the GRPO objective. Because rewards are evaluated at the plan level, no expensive full sub-agent rollout is needed during policy training.

## Results & Highlights

**Setup.**
- Orchestrator policy: **Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct**.
- Sub-agent backbone: **GPT-OSS-120B**.
- Reward model initialization: **Skywork-Reward-LLaMA-3.1-8B**.
- Domains: AIME 24&25, BrowseComp+, HotpotQA, and zero-shot GPQA.

**Test-Time Scaling (Best-of-N, N=8).**
- On **AIME 24&25**, Orch-RM raises accuracy from 63.33% (majority vote) to **68.33%** using only **2.38M** verification tokens.
- On **BrowseComp+**, Orch-RM raises accuracy from 9.50% to **14.00%** using only **8.26M** verification tokens.
- On BrowseComp+, Orch-RM beats the strongest trajectory-level LLM-as-a-Judge baseline (GPT-5-mini, 12.50%) while cutting verification cost from **142.80M** to **8.26M** tokens (≈17× reduction).
- Orch-RM continues to benefit from scaling N up to 64 on AIME 24&25, showing scalable reward signals.

**Training from Scratch.**
- Initializing from the base Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct and training with Orch-RM as the reward signal raises AIME 24&25 majority-vote accuracy from **23.33%** to **61.67%**, approaching MAS-Orchestra’s **63.33%**.

**Continued Training.**
- Starting from the pretrained MAS-Orchestra checkpoint and continuing training with Orch-RM reaches **68.33%** on AIME 24&25 and **11.00%** on BrowseComp+.
- Orch-RM achieves comparable or better performance than trajectory-level GRPO while using **~10× fewer training tokens on AIME** and **~46× fewer on BrowseComp+**.
- Orch-RM is the only continued-training method that consistently improves over the original MAS-Orchestra checkpoint.

**Data-Mixing Ablation.**
- Using only correct-over-incorrect pairs or only domain-level comparisons underperforms the mixed setting.
- The default mix (domain-level + correctness-based) yields the highest normalized advantage between correct and incorrect orchestrations and the best Best-of-N accuracy.

**Cross-Domain Robustness.**
- Gains are smaller on HotpotQA and GPQA, where orchestration diversity is more limited and orchestration-level signals are less discriminative; nevertheless, Orch-RM remains cost-competitive.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty.** Orch-RM’s core insight is well-motivated: evaluating multi-agent *plans* instead of full *trajectories* addresses the dominant cost bottleneck in MAS scaling and training. While reward models for single-agent LLMs are mature, applying them at the orchestration level—and deriving preference data from existing training artifacts without human labels—is a meaningful contribution.

**Rigor.** The empirical design is careful: comparisons cover test-time scaling and both training settings (from-scratch and continued), multiple domains, strong baselines (LLM-as-a-Judge, off-the-shelf RMs, DPO, RFT, GRPO), and token-cost reporting. The paper also reports limitations honestly (smaller gains on HotpotQA/GPQA, domain-specific RMs, limited trajectory diversity).

**Reproducibility.** The setup follows MAS-Orchestra exactly, uses publicly available models and benchmarks, and the authors commit to releasing code. Reproduction is feasible for groups with access to GPT-OSS-120B-level sub-agents and MAS-Orchestra artifacts.

**Significance.** If the gains generalize to more diverse agent topologies and longer-horizon tasks, orchestration-level reward models could become a standard primitive for efficient MAS. The work is particularly relevant for reducing the cost of test-time scaling and for enabling continued orchestrator training without prohibitive rollout budgets.

**Remaining concerns.**
- The preference-data construction depends on access to a pre-trained, reasonably strong orchestrator and its training logs; performance may degrade with weaker base artifacts.
- Domain-specific reward models are trained per task; whether a single unified Orch-RM can generalize across heterogeneous planning patterns is left to future work.
- The smaller gains on HotpotQA and GPQA suggest that the method is most valuable when orchestration diversity is high; its usefulness for simpler or more homogeneous tasks is limited.
- The paper does not report reward-model calibration or failure-mode analyses in depth.

## Strengths

- Targets the right bottleneck: full sub-agent rollouts are the dominant cost in MAS training and test-time scaling.
- Self-supervised design eliminates the need for expensive human preference annotations.
- Strong empirical coverage across three training regimes and four benchmarks, with explicit token-cost accounting.
- Demonstrates that orchestration-level rewards can approach or exceed trajectory-level RL (GRPO) while using an order of magnitude less compute.
- Honest ablations and limitations; the authors do not oversell the method.
- Promised open-source release will aid adoption and verification.

## Limitations

- Requires a pretrained orchestrator and its training artifacts to construct high-quality preference pairs; cold-start scenarios are not addressed.
- Currently trains separate reward models per domain; a unified cross-domain Orch-RM remains underexplored.
- Gains are smaller on tasks with limited orchestration diversity (HotpotQA) and on out-of-domain scientific reasoning (GPQA).
- The method assumes that plan quality is predictive of final answer quality; this may fail for tasks where execution noise dominates.
- Absolute accuracy on some benchmarks (e.g., BrowseComp+, HotpotQA) remains low, so the relative gains, while meaningful, do not yet translate to near-human performance.

## Relevance

该论文与“多智能体系统 + 大语言模型编排”研究高度相关。Orch-RM 把奖励模型从昂贵的子智能体 rollout 中解放出来，直接在编排层进行自监督学习，为降低 MAS 测试时缩放和持续训练成本提供了一条清晰路径。对于需要频繁采样编排方案、评估多智能体协作质量的研究管线，Orch-RM 的“先选方案、再执行”范式以及从训练日志自动构造偏好对的思路具有直接的参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does Orch-RM perform when initialized from a much weaker orchestrator or when training artifacts are scarce?
- Can a single unified Orch-RM be trained across diverse domains without sacrificing per-domain accuracy, and what mixture strategies would make it stable?
- How sensitive is Orch-RM to the sub-agent backbone (e.g., GPT-OSS-120B vs. smaller open models)?
- Does Orch-RM remain calibrated when the orchestrator policy drifts during continued training, and how often should the reward model be retrained?
- Can Orch-RM be extended to dynamic, hierarchical, or asynchronous multi-agent topologies where the orchestration plan is not a single static graph?
- What are the failure modes where a high-reward plan produces a wrong final answer, and can auxiliary process-level signals mitigate them?
