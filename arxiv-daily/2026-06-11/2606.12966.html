Key Finding

Fourier circuits synchronize 500–3,000 steps before grokking, and the delay to generalization scales inversely with weight decay: Δt ∝ 1/λ.

Circuit Synchronization Precedes Generalization: Causal Evidence from Fourier Structure in Grokking Transformers arxiv.org/abs/2606.12966 ↗

Achyuthan Sivasankar · NYU · 2026-06-11

grokking transformers modular arithmetic Fourier circuits mechanistic interpretability
Key Metric
+1,722

Mean FSD lead before grokking across nine modular-addition settings (sign-test p ≈ 0.004).

Why This Matters

It gives a computable early signal of generalizable algorithm formation and a causal lever to accelerate generalization.

Critical Edge

Only validated on small modular tasks; transfer to language, vision, or non-Fourier algorithms remains unproven.

Methodology

  • Train a 2-layer Transformer (d_model=128, 4 heads, d_mlp=512, GELU) on (a + b) mod p.
  • AdamW with lr 10⁻³, weight decay λ=1.0, batch 512; 30% train / 70% val.
  • Build sum-conditioned activations by grouping block-0 MLP outputs by s = (a+b) mod p.
  • Define Fourier Rank as the minimum DFT frequencies needed per neuron at τ=0.90.
  • Compute FSD from max frequency dominance vs. chance, calibrated with 1,000 permutations.
  • Run causal forks from an FSD ≥ 0.80 checkpoint, varying λ ∈ {1,2,3,4,5,10}.
  • Use zero ablations and FourierKAN symbolic regression to isolate causal roles.

Results

  • FSD peaks 500–3,000 steps before validation accuracy groks; mean lead +1,722.
  • Phase 1: Fourier rank collapses 6 → 1; weight norm falls 76.72 → 28.60.
  • Phase 2: rank stays 1 while norm drops another 19.8%; accuracy still below 35%.
  • Fork law: stable branches follow Δt = 3,000/λ with R² = 1.00.
  • Replicated on p=97 (R²=1.00), p=131 (R²=0.99), p=53 (noisier, R²=0.49).
  • Block-1 attention is most vital (acc → 5.4%); block-0 MLP is an organizational precursor.
  • Subtraction mirrors addition; multiplication lags by ~10,000 steps.

Strengths

  • FSD needs no prior circuit knowledge.
  • Systematic sweeps across primes, seeds, operations, and architectures.
  • Moves correlation to causal control with a simple 1/λ law.
  • Zero ablations cleanly separate precursor from computation.
  • Handles rank-2 representations where excluded-loss baselines fail.

Limitations

  • Only small modular tasks tested; real-world transfer unknown.
  • FSD is Fourier-dependent; non-Fourier strategies may not show the signal.
  • Most evidence comes from p=97 seeds; cross-prime sample is small.
  • High-λ branches destabilize after grokking.
  • Single seed per (p, λ) fork; p=53 results are noisy.
  • R² = 1.00 may reflect checkpoint granularity as much as a perfect law.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Does FSD predict grokking in non-modular tasks?
  • How do optimizer, LR schedule, and init scale affect the 1/λ law?
  • Can FSD drive curriculum learning or dynamic regularization?
  • What is the precise link between FSD and memorization-subnetwork rank/norm dynamics?
  • Does the inverse-λ law hold for other group operations?
  • Will independent replication reproduce the exact R² = 1.00 fit?
  • Can analogous sync metrics be defined for non-Fourier circuits?
