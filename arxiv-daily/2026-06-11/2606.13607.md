> **一句话总结：** 本文在人类与 25 个大语言模型上评估日常因果推理，发现两者错误模式高度相似，并且驱动 LLM 回答的关键注意力头对非关键内容更敏感，其激活还能预测人类在内容微调题目上的准确率，提示人类与 LLM 的日常推理更像模式匹配而非抽象世界模型。

> **TL;DR:** This paper evaluates everyday causal reasoning in humans and 25 LLMs, finds closely aligned error profiles, and shows that the attention heads most causally responsible for LLM outputs are more sensitive to non-critical content than to structurally critical information; their activations predict human accuracy on minimally different prompts, suggesting both systems reason through pattern matching rather than abstract world models.

## 核心贡献

- 设计了一个覆盖 11 类日常因果推理场景（state/position、geocentric/egocentric、target/context framing 等）的行为评测。
- 在 142 名人类被试和 25 个 LLM（21 个开源 + 4 个闭源）上发现两者在类别级和项目级错误模式上高度一致。
- 使用因果消融（attention-head ablation）定位对 gemma-2-27b/gemma-3-27b 输出最关键的注意力头。
- 通过激活修补（activation patching）证明这些关键头对“与答案无关的内容替换”（如 painting → statue）比对“结构性关键替换”（如 North → South）更敏感，支持模式匹配假说。
- 用这些注意力头的熵/激活在线性模型中预测人类准确率：在控制类别、提示格式、三元组频率、GPT-2/BERT surprisal 后仍显著增量解释 5.4% 的方差；在 96 个仅改变一个内容词的题目上可解释 65.6%（最高 76%）的人类方差。

## Key Contributions

- Introduces an 11-category behavioral benchmark of everyday causal reasoning, spanning state/position changes, geocentric/egocentric relations, and target vs. context framings.
- Reveals strong alignment between human (n = 142) and 25 LLM error patterns at both category and item levels.
- Uses causal attention-head ablation in gemma-2-27b/gemma-3-27b to isolate the heads most responsible for model outputs.
- Shows through activation patching that these critical heads are more sensitive to non-critical content substitutions (e.g., painting → statue) than to structurally critical substitutions (e.g., North → South), supporting a pattern-matching account.
- Demonstrates that the same content-sensitive attention-head activations predict human accuracy in linear regressions, explaining incremental variance (ΔR² = 0.054) above controls and up to 76% of human variance on minimally different prompts.

## Methodology

**Behavioral evaluation.** The authors recruited 162 U.S. participants on Mechanical Turk (Cloud Research prescreen), excluding 20 who failed attention checks or responded too quickly, yielding 142 participants and 8,910 prompt-level observations. Each trial presented a short causal scenario with a BLANK and asked subjects to choose the more likely completion after pressing SPACE to reveal two options. Reaction times (read-rt, choice-rt) were also recorded.

**Stimuli.** The evaluation used a modular format inspired by Ivanova et al. (2025) with 11 categories, including state-follows/nonfollows, action-follows/nonfollows, geocentric/egocentric relations for near and distant referents, relative-position, and two-references variants. Each category had both a target framing (infer the outcome) and a context framing (infer the cause).

**LLM testing.** Twenty-five LLMs (21 open-weights, 4 proprietary) were queried with the same scenarios. For open models, accuracy was defined as the logit difference between correct and incorrect next-token completions. For proprietary models without logit access, a binary accuracy measure was used based on whether the correct option appeared in the first five generated tokens at zero temperature. The main analyses focus on gemma-3-27b-it because it showed the strongest human alignment; mechanistic analyses used gemma-2-27b-it and gemma-3-27b-it due to full TransformerLens support.

**Mechanistic interpretability.** The authors computed a causal responsibility score for each attention head by ablating it and measuring the absolute change in the logit difference across all prompts. The top-k heads (k selected by BIC, typically 10 or 11) were then examined with activation patching: for each prompt, three structurally critical substitutions (which change the correct answer) and three non-critical content substitutions (which preserve the correct answer) were generated, and the authors compared how much each type of patch altered the model’s output.

**Predicting humans from model mechanisms.** Attention-head activation entropy was used as a feature in linear regressions predicting per-item human accuracy, with controls for category, prompt format, trigram frequency, GPT-2 surprisal, and BERT surprisal. A follow-up experiment used 96 prompts across six scenarios where only a single content word varied (e.g., “painting,” “statue,” “city” in a North-of-Ali scenario) to test whether the same mechanism predicts fine-grained human errors.

## Results & Highlights

- **Human accuracy is far below ceiling** (μ = 0.71, σ = 0.21) and varies substantially across categories, mirroring LLM performance.
- **Frontier LLMs outperform humans in absolute accuracy** (DeepSeek-R1 μ = 0.90, GPT-5.2 μ = 0.87, p < 0.001), but they are not the most human-aligned models.
- **gemma-3-27b is the most human-aligned model**, with category-level accuracy correlated with humans at r = 0.84 (p = 0.001) and item-level R² = 0.28. It remains predictive after controlling for category and prompt format (β = 0.067, t = 4.97, p < .001).
- **Fail states are fragile and content-dependent.** For example, state-follows prompts are trivial for both humans (μ = 0.93) and gemma-3-27b (μ = 0.78), but changing one word so the state change no longer follows drops human accuracy to 0.77 and model accuracy to 0.46. Similar effects occur for action-follows vs. action-nonfollows and for geocentric/egocentric near vs. distant referents.
- **Human errors are not random guessing.** A retest cohort (n = 70) showed moderate test-retest reliability (r = 0.60, p < 0.001), and a mixed-effects model found that getting a question wrong once increased the odds of getting it wrong again by 3.84× (95% CI [2.92, 5.07]).
- **Critical attention heads prefer non-critical content.** Activation patching across all 11 categories showed larger output changes for non-critical content substitutions than for structurally critical substitutions (all p < 0.001, median Cohen’s d = 0.437), contradicting the hypothesis that the heads implement abstract causal structure.
- **Attention-head activations predict human accuracy.** A linear model with top-k head features explained significant incremental variance beyond trigram frequency and LM surprisal (F(11, 407) = 3.36, p < .001; ΔR² = .054) and remained significant even after adding model output logits (F(11, 406) = 2.57, p = .004; ΔR² = .037).
- **Fine-grained content effects are strongly predicted.** In the minimal-difference follow-up (n = 175), top-k attention-head activations explained 65.6% of human variance (up to 76% with larger k) within scenarios where only one content word differed, above controls including model logits (F(7, 80) = 2.84, p = .011; ΔR² = .073).

## Authors & Credibility

- **Zach Studdiford** and **Gary Lupyan** are at the University of Wisconsin–Madison, Department of Psychology (Studdiford also affiliated with Computer Science). Lupyan is an established cognitive scientist with extensive work on language, concepts, and categorization.
- The study combines experimental psychology with mechanistic interpretability, an unusual and valuable cross-disciplinary approach.
- The methodology is detailed in the main text and supplementary materials, and the tasks are straightforward to replicate. The paper does not explicitly mention public code or data release in the main text, though the evaluation format is modular and easy to reconstruct.
- The sample is English-speaking Mechanical Turk workers, which limits generalizability to broader populations and languages.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty.** The paper makes a novel contribution by reversing the usual “find a representation that predicts humans” approach: it first isolates the mechanism causally responsible for LLM outputs and then asks whether that same mechanism predicts human behavior. This is a stronger inferential step than prior correlational work.
- **Rigor.** The behavioral design is careful, with multiple control conditions, replication, and attention checks. The interpretability pipeline uses established techniques (attention ablation, activation patching, permutation tests). However, the central mechanistic analyses are limited to gemma-2-27b and gemma-3-27b; whether the same circuit exists in other families (e.g., GPT, LLaMA, Qwen) is not directly tested, though the authors report that content effects generalize across model families.
- **Causal claims.** The authors are appropriately cautious: they cannot ablate human brains, so the link from LLM heads to human behavior remains correlational at the human side. The fact that model mechanisms predict humans is consistent with shared pattern matching but does not prove identical underlying computation.
- **Controls for associationist explanations.** The inclusion of trigram frequency, GPT-2 surprisal, and BERT surprisal helps rule out simple n-gram or lexical association accounts, strengthening the case that the predictive signal comes from the model’s causal reasoning circuit rather than surface statistics.
- **Generality.** The findings are strongest for everyday causal reasoning with concrete scenarios. They should not be overgeneralized to formal logic, mathematics, scientific reasoning, or multi-step planning without further evidence.
- **Post-hoc selection risk.** Selecting top-k heads based on causal responsibility and then testing their predictiveness of humans could invite double-dipping if not cross-validated; the authors mitigate this with leave-one-out predictions and permutation tests against random heads and random normal features.
- **Potential confounds.** Larger frontier models (DeepSeek-R1, GPT-5.2) are more accurate but less human-like, raising the possibility that post-training or scale pushes models away from the content-sensitive heuristics humans use. The authors note this tension but do not fully resolve it.

## Strengths

- Bridges cognitive psychology and AI interpretability, offering a concrete mechanism-level hypothesis for human-like reasoning errors.
- Uses both behavioral and mechanistic evidence, with multiple replication and control conditions.
- Clearly distinguishes abstract world-model and pattern-matching accounts, and provides an empirical test between them.
- Robustly controls for simpler associationist explanations (n-gram frequency, smaller LM surprisal).
- Highlights that many “failures” used to argue LLMs do not reason also appear in human behavior, reframing the debate.

## Limitations

- Mechanistic analyses are restricted to gemma-2-27b-it and gemma-3-27b-it; direct circuit identification in other architectures is absent.
- Human data come from English-speaking Mechanical Turk workers, limiting cross-cultural and linguistic generalizability.
- The tasks are short, two-option causal reasoning scenarios; results may not extend to open-ended, multi-step, or symbolic reasoning.
- Proprietary models were evaluated with coarser binary accuracy and without activation access, weakening comparisons at the mechanism level.
- The predictive link from LLM heads to human accuracy is correlational on the human side; shared mechanism is inferred, not directly demonstrated.
- Some high-performing frontier models are less human-aligned, complicating the claim that “human-like reasoning” is the target for evaluating LLMs.

## Relevance

本文对 LLM 推理评估、认知科学以及人机比较研究都有直接参考价值：它提醒我们不能简单地把“内容敏感性错误”当作 LLM 不懂推理的证据，因为人类在类似的日常因果推理任务中也会犯同样的内容依赖性错误；同时，它展示了用机制可解释性工具来检验人类认知假说的可能性。

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Do the same content-sensitive attention circuits appear in other model families (e.g., LLaMA, Qwen, GPT-style architectures), and are they located at similar layers?
2. Can causal interventions on these heads be used to make LLM responses more (or less) human-like, and does that improve robustness or harm accuracy?
3. How do the results change with longer reasoning traces, chain-of-thought, or explicit world-model training?
4. To what extent do individual differences in human reasoning strategy map onto different attention-head activation patterns?
5. Can the pattern-matching account be extended to formal domains (logic, mathematics, scientific reasoning) where humans often rely on explicit rules?
6. Does the human alignment of smaller models like gemma-3-27b reflect genuine cognitive similarity or merely shared surface-level biases in training data?
