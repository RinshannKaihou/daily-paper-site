ICML 2026 · LLM Evaluation

Authority, Truth, and Citation Bias in Large Language Models

Aryan Khurana · Aravind Ramana RN · Dhruv Kumar — BITS Pilani

arXiv 2606.13104 11 Jun 2026 220,564 prompts 7 models · 4 domains GitHub ↗
Key Finding
Any citation—real or fabricated—raises LLM hallucination. True claims + fabricated citations produce the worst failure.
Key Metric
77.92%hallucination rate · Llama 3.1 8B · TC×FC
Why This Matters
Citation presence is an authority heuristic that degrades factual accuracy—threatening RAG, legal, and medical AI systems.
Critical Edge
No mechanistic explanation (no attention analysis). FEVER domain real citations were backfilled, compromising that condition's validity.

Citation Template Risk
  • Prefix ("According to [source]…") — highest risk in 5/7 models
  • Minimum salience (author + year only) — lowest risk in 6/7 models, sometimes below baseline
  • Footnote-style — model-dependent, no universal safety signal

Cross-domain citations are more disruptive than same-domain in most model–domain pairs. Exception: legal claims with same-domain legal citations are consistently the least damaged.

What Doesn't Matter

Venue prestige — 0.53–3.68 pp difference across four tiers. Low-tier journals carry the same weight as Nature-level citations.

Author demographics — 0.7–1.9 pp difference across seven regions. Country-coded names have negligible impact on hallucination.

Strengths
  • Largest, most rigorously controlled citation-authority benchmark to date
  • First 2×2 factorial design fully orthogonalizing claim × citation veracity
  • Multi-domain, multi-template, multi-model strengthens generalizability
  • Open-source code & data
  • Legal domain robustness hints at register-aware defenses
Limitations
  • Judge model (Qwen3-8B) cannot independently verify citation existence
  • FEVER real citations backfilled from other domains — interpret with caution
  • Full dataset evaluated only on 3B–8B open-source models; API models on 15K subset only
  • Templates don't cover hyperlinks, social media, conversational citations
  • English-only; no multilingual evaluation
  • No hidden-state or attention analysis — mechanism remains unexplained
Relevance
For any system that uses citations to bolster LLM output—RAG pipelines, academic writing assistants, legal and medical retrieval—this paper is a direct warning. Citations are not merely credibility endorsements; they function as independent authority heuristics that can cause models to hallucinate on correct facts. AuthorityBench provides a quantifiable framework for evaluating and eventually mitigating this effect.
Open Questions
  • Do dense vs. MoE architectures respond differently to citation authority signals?
  • Can fine-tuning or architectural interventions shift models from "authority obedience" to "evidence evaluation"?
  • Does the effect hold in Chinese, multilingual, or cross-cultural name/journal settings?
  • Can "legal-style" citation formatting be transferred to other domains for robustness?
  • How much does pre-training co-occurrence of true claims with high-prestige venues drive the TC×FC effect?
  • Can model confidence / calibration signals serve as early warnings for citation-induced hallucination?
  • Would retrieval-augmented judges improve evaluation reliability?
