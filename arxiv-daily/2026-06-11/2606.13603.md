> **一句话总结：** 该研究通过句子级早退（early exit）追踪大推理模型在思维链（CoT）中每一步对最终答案的因果贡献，发现答案通常在单个“关键句”后突然稳定（承诺边界），之后约一半以上的 CoT 步骤是仅含表面推敲与自我验证、却不改变最终答案分布的“副现象”推理；基于隐藏状态训练的因果注意力探针可在线定位该边界，在 MATH-500 等任务上以 26–39% 的 token 节省换取接近完整 CoT 的准确率。

> **TL;DR:** This paper introduces a sentence-level early-exit causal framework to track how large reasoning models converge to their final answer inside a Chain-of-Thought (CoT) trace. It identifies a sharp "commitment boundary" after which the model's answer probability is essentially fixed, and shows that the following CoT steps are largely epiphenomenal—full of hedging and self-verification that do not alter the final answer. Lightweight causal attention probes trained on hidden states can detect this boundary online, enabling CoT early-exiting that matches or nearly matches full-CoT accuracy while saving 26–55% of reasoning tokens depending on the model and task.

## 核心贡献

- 提出一种基于 CoT 早退的句子级因果归因框架，用于量化每个推理步骤对最终答案的边际贡献。
- 发现并形式化“承诺边界”（commitment boundary）i*：在多数轨迹中，模型经过一个关键句后从“无确定答案/中间猜测”突然过渡到稳定的高置信度最终答案。
- 通过截断实验与数字扰动实验，证明边界前的推理对答案形成具有因果影响，而边界后的推理步骤多为“副现象”（epiphenomenal）——常见自我质疑、复核等语言，但几乎不改变最终答案。
- 展示不同模型家族的承诺边界位置差异显著：gemma-4-26B-A4B-it 常在 13–23% 的 CoT 位置即已收敛，而 gpt-oss-20b 则在 43–68% 处收敛。
- 训练轻量因果注意力探针，从中间层激活中预测“无猜测 / 中间猜测 / 最终猜测”三个阶段，跨分布内与分布外任务均能可靠定位边界。
- 将探针用于在线早退，在 MATH-500、AIME 2025、ZebraLogic、GPQA-Diamond 上均优于固定比例截断基线，最多可节省 55% 的 CoT token，同时准确率损失很小。

## Key Contributions

- Introduces a sentence-level causal attribution framework based on CoT early exits to measure the marginal contribution of each reasoning step to the final answer.
- Identifies and formalizes the "commitment boundary" i*: in most traces, the model transitions abruptly from no-guess/mid-guess to a stable, high-confidence final answer after a single pivotal sentence.
- Uses truncation and targeted numeric-perturbation experiments to show that pre-boundary reasoning causally shapes the answer, while post-boundary steps are epiphenomenal and leave the final answer essentially unchanged.
- Shows that the relative position of the commitment boundary depends strongly on model family: gemma-4-26B-A4B-it often converges after only 13–23% of CoT tokens, whereas gpt-oss-20b converges much later (43–68%).
- Trains lightweight causal attention probes to predict answer-formation stages (no-guess, mid-guess, final-guess) from intermediate activations, reliably localizing the boundary in- and out-of-distribution.
- Demonstrates online early-exiting using these probes, outperforming fixed-percentage truncation baselines across MATH-500, AIME 2025, ZebraLogic, and GPQA-Diamond, saving up to 55% of CoT tokens with minimal accuracy loss.

## Methodology

The authors build an answer-forcing protocol to isolate the causal effect of each CoT sentence. For a prompt P, generated CoT C, and answer-eliciting suffix S, they construct X_full = P + [BOT] + C + [EOT] + S, where [BOT]/[EOT] are model-specific thinking markers. The CoT C is split into n sentence-level spans, and for each prefix X_i (truncated at sentence i) they greedily decode an intermediate answer Â_i and compare it to the full-CoT answer Â_n using a domain-aware verifier (math-verify for math, regex-based option extraction for multiple-choice, etc.).

Step confidence is defined as p_i = arg max P(· | X_i), the probability of the most likely next token under the forced prefix. The threshold τ = p_0 + 0.5(p_n − p_0) separates three stages:

- **No-guess**: p_i ≤ τ
- **Mid-guess**: p_i > τ and Â_i ≢ Â_n
- **Final-guess**: p_i > τ and Â_i ≡ Â_n

The commitment boundary is i* = arg max_i (p_i − p_{i−1}), the sentence producing the largest confidence jump.

The authors filter out traces where the no-CoT baseline already gives the correct answer (to focus on cases where CoT helps) and discard traces with first-token collisions between semantically different answers, which would inflate p_i spuriously. Collision rates average 8–9% across models.

To detect i* online, they train a small causal attention probe on hidden states at a chosen layer ℓ. The probe receives the last-token hidden state h_i of each sentence and predicts y_i ∈ {NO-GUESS, MID-GUESS, FINAL-GUESS}, attending only to the previous w sentences to preserve causality. The probe is trained on MATH-500 (50% train / 10% validation / 10% test, with 30% held out for early-exit evaluation) and tested on AIME 2025, ZebraLogic, and GPQA-Diamond as out-of-distribution benchmarks.

## Results & Highlights

- **Bimodal, abrupt commitment.** On gpt-oss-20b MATH-500 traces, normalized step confidences concentrate near 0 (no-CoT level) and 1 (full-CoT level), indicating that the final answer emerges abruptly rather than gradually. The largest single-step confidence improvement is on average 4.6× larger than the second-largest (median; 95% CI [4.3, 4.9]).
- **Boundary location varies by model family.** Average relative boundary positions i*/n: gpt-oss-20b at 0.43 (MATH), 0.68 (AIME), 0.64 (GPQA); Qwen3-14B at 0.22, 0.39, 0.40; gemma-4-26B-A4B-it at 0.13, 0.23, 0.17. In all cases a substantial fraction of tokens is generated after the answer has stabilized.
- **Post-boundary reasoning is epiphenomenal.** Numeric perturbation experiments on AIME 2025 show that corrupting pre-boundary tokens is far more destructive than corrupting post-boundary tokens. At 20% corruption, PRE preserves the answer in 61% of runs vs. 95% for POST; at 50% corruption, the gap is 27% vs. 72%.
- **Pre-boundary reasoning is genuine.** Traces often contain mid-guesses before i* with average token likelihood around 0.69–0.91 depending on model/task, and these mid-guesses are later revised. Traces with mid-guesses are more than twice as long as those that commit directly. On MATH-500, independently sampled traces of the same question share mid-guesses with median Jaccard similarity 0.71.
- **Hedging language is not a reliable signal.** Words like "wait", "but", "let's check" appear at similar rates before and after the commitment boundary, confirming that surface deliberation markers can be epiphenomenal.
- **Probes localize the boundary reliably.** Detection rates exceed 90% in-distribution and remain high out-of-distribution. Early-fire rates are low for most settings (though up to 21% on Qwen3-14B OOD). Saved-token rates: gpt-oss-20b 35% ID / 6–35% OOD; gemma-4-26B-A4B-it 55% ID / 40–44% OOD; Qwen3-14B 48% ID / 13–55% OOD.
- **Early exit dominates fixed truncation.** On MATH-500, probe-based early exit at k=1 recovers 89% of full-CoT accuracy (71% vs. 80%) while saving 39% of tokens; at k=5 it reaches 98% of full-CoT accuracy (78% vs. 80%) while saving 26%. On OOD datasets, the probe consistently outperforms fixed-percentage truncation at matched token budgets, with the largest gains on AIME 2025 where traces are longest.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty.** The paper makes a timely and conceptually crisp contribution. The "commitment boundary" is a well-operationalized notion that connects CoT faithfulness, mechanistic interpretability, and inference efficiency. It extends the "performative reasoning" finding of Boppana et al. (2026) by showing that post-boundary steps are not merely pre-encoded answers but often take the form of seemingly genuine deliberation (hedging, rechecking) that is nevertheless causally inert. The combination of truncation-based causal measurement with activation probing and early-exit deployment is well-integrated.

**Rigor.** The methodology is careful in many places: domain-aware answer verification, collision filtering, a threshold sensitivity analysis, and causal attention probes that respect generation order. However, the commitment signal is tied to greedy decoding and an answer-forcing suffix, which is a specific (and somewhat artificial) notion of "commitment." A step could redistribute probability mass among answers without changing the argmax, which the framework would miss. The authors acknowledge this but it remains a real limitation. The claim that i* is "causal" is also limited: truncation measures sufficiency, not necessity, and the perturbation experiment is clever but only manipulates numeric literals on one dataset.

**Reproducibility.** The release of code and data, plus extensive appendices, is a strong point. The paper reports confidence intervals for some key statistics, though not for all model/dataset combinations. Hyperparameters for probe layer ℓ and window w are reported in appendices rather than the main text.

**Significance.** The findings matter for both interpretability and efficiency. If a large share of generated CoT is epiphenomenal, then using CoT as a safety monitor is more fragile than it appears, and inference-time compute can be substantially reduced without retraining. The model-family differences in boundary position are particularly noteworthy for practitioners choosing between models.

**Red flags / weaknesses.**
- The sample is biased toward traces where no-CoT fails and where answers are tokenization-distinguishable, potentially overestimating the epiphenomenal fraction.
- Sentence-level granularity means reported i* positions are upper bounds; finer units might shift the boundary earlier.
- The 55% token-saving headline is model-dependent (gemma) and not uniform; some OOD settings save only 6%.
- The probe is trained on labels produced by the same truncation procedure it replaces, so it inherits methodological biases.
- No evaluation on closed-weight frontier models or on agentic/tool-use/long-horizon tasks, where the commitment-boundary concept may not cleanly apply.
- The accuracy loss from early exit, while small, is non-zero, and the OOD early-fire rate (up to 21% for Qwen3) raises deployment concerns for high-stakes settings.

## Strengths

- Conceptually clean framework that links causal attribution, interpretability, and inference efficiency.
- Strong empirical evidence for abrupt rather than gradual answer formation.
- Careful experimental design including verification, collision filtering, and perturbation stress tests.
- Demonstrates practical utility through online early-exit with lightweight probes.
- Good coverage across three open-weight model families and four diverse reasoning benchmarks.
- Honest and detailed limitations section.

## Limitations

- Commitment is operationalized via greedy decoding and an answer-forcing suffix, which may not capture all forms of internal deliberation.
- Filters bias the sample toward problems where CoT is informative and answers avoid first-token collisions.
- Sentence-level granularity provides only an upper-bound estimate of when commitment occurs.
- Probes are supervised with labels from the same truncation procedure, inheriting its assumptions.
- Limited coverage: no closed-weight frontier models, agentic/tool-use tasks, or long-horizon generation.
- Early-exit accuracy loss is small but non-zero; OOD early-fire rates can be non-negligible.
- Post-boundary tokens may serve other functions (confidence calibration, multi-turn behavior, training objectives) not measured by single-pass answer forcing.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How would the commitment boundary behave under sampling-based decoding (e.g., temperature > 0) or distribution-level commitment metrics rather than argmax-based ones?
- Can fine-grained (clause- or token-level) attribution shift the estimated boundary earlier, and how much additional token savings would that unlock?
- Do closed-weight frontier models (e.g., GPT-4o/o3, Claude 4) exhibit similar commitment boundaries, and can they be detected without activation access?
- Can models be trained or fine-tuned to align their verbalized reasoning more closely with their internal commitment boundary, reducing epiphenomenal reasoning?
- What roles do post-boundary tokens play beyond the final answer—e.g., confidence calibration, conversational coherence, or RL reward shaping?
- How robust is the probe to adversarial or semantically unusual prompts where the answer-formation dynamics may differ?
