Rethinking Generalization in Reasoning SFT: A Conditional Analysis on Optimization, Data, and Model Capability

一句话总结： 系统性地重新审视推理SFT的泛化能力，发现跨域泛化并非缺失而是有条件的，受优化动态（先退化后恢复）、数据质量和模型能力共同塑造，且泛化具有不对称性——推理能力提升但安全性下降。
TL;DR: Demonstrates that cross-domain generalization in reasoning SFT is conditional, jointly shaped by optimization dynamics (dip-and-recovery pattern), data quality, and model capability, with asymmetric effects (reasoning improves but safety degrades).

核心贡献

  • 揭示推理SFT中跨域泛化的"先退化后恢复"（dip-and-recovery）模式，证明短期训练检查点会低估泛化能力
  • 系统分析数据质量对泛化的影响：低质量解会广泛损害泛化，而经过验证的长链式思维（long-CoT）轨迹则带来一致的跨域增益
  • 证明模型能力是泛化的关键因素：更强的模型能从简单算术游戏中内化可迁移的程序性模式（如回溯），而较弱模型仅模仿表面冗长
  • 发现泛化的不对称性：推理能力提升的同时安全性下降，重新定义了问题——不是"SFT是否泛化"，而是"在什么条件下以什么代价泛化"

Key Contributions

  • Identifies a dip-and-recovery pattern in cross-domain generalization during reasoning SFT: performance first degrades before recovering with extended training, meaning short-training checkpoints underestimate generalization
  • Shows that data quality and structure critically shape generalization: verified long-CoT traces yield consistent cross-domain gains, while low-quality solutions broadly hurt it
  • Demonstrates that model capability determines what is internalized: stronger models learn transferable procedural patterns (e.g., backtracking), weaker models imitate surface verbosity
  • Reveals asymmetric generalization: reasoning improves while safety degrades, reframing the question from "does reasoning SFT generalize?" to "under what conditions and at what cost?"

Methodology

The authors conduct controlled experiments varying three factors: optimization dynamics (training duration and checkpoints), training data quality (verified long-CoT vs. low-quality solutions vs. toy arithmetic games), and base model capability (models of varying sizes and strengths). They measure cross-domain transfer by training on one domain and evaluating on held-out domains, tracking accuracy, reasoning patterns, and safety metrics throughout training.

Results & Highlights

  • Cross-domain performance shows dip-and-recovery: early checkpoints show degradation, but extended training recovers and surpasses baseline
  • Verified long-CoT traces produce consistent cross-domain gains; low-quality solutions hurt generalization broadly
  • Stronger models internalize procedural patterns (e.g., backtracking, verification) even from toy arithmetic training data
  • Weaker models learn only surface verbosity without transferable reasoning skills
  • Safety metrics degrade alongside reasoning improvements, revealing an asymmetric trade-off
  • Challenges the prevailing narrative that "SFT memorizes while RL generalizes"

Critical Assessment

This is a well-designed empirical study that challenges an important prevailing assumption in the LLM post-training community. The three-factor experimental design (optimization, data, model capability) is systematic and illuminating. The dip-and-recovery finding is practically important -- it suggests that many published "SFT fails to generalize" results may be artifacts of insufficient training. The asymmetric safety degradation finding is a genuine contribution with practical implications. However, the paper's claims are primarily empirical without strong theoretical grounding for why dip-and-recovery occurs. The scope of "domains" tested could be broader. The safety evaluation methodology is not deeply detailed.

Strengths

  • Directly challenges a prevailing and influential narrative ("SFT memorizes, RL generalizes")
  • Systematic three-factor experimental design provides actionable insights
  • Dip-and-recovery finding has immediate practical implications for training practices
  • Asymmetric generalization (reasoning vs. safety) is an important and underappreciated finding
  • Strong author team with relevant expertise

Limitations

  • Primarily empirical without theoretical explanation for dip-and-recovery phenomenon
  • Safety evaluation methodology is not deeply detailed
  • Domain coverage in cross-domain experiments could be broader
  • The definition of "stronger" vs. "weaker" models conflates size with architecture differences

Relevance

Questions for Further Exploration

  • What is the mechanistic explanation for the dip-and-recovery pattern? Does it correspond to a phase transition in the loss landscape?
  • Can the safety degradation be mitigated through multi-objective training or safety-aware data curation?
  • How does the dip-and-recovery pattern interact with different learning rate schedules and optimizers?
  • Is the asymmetry between reasoning and safety fundamental, or an artifact of current safety evaluation benchmarks?
