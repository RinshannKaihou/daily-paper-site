DINO-QPM: Adapting Visual Foundation Models for Globally Interpretable Image Classification

一句话总结： 提出DINO-QPM，一种轻量级可解释性适配器，将冻结DINOv2骨干网络的特征转化为全局可解释的、类别无关的稀疏表示，同时超越线性探头的分类精度。
TL;DR: DINO-QPM adapts the QPM framework to frozen DINOv2 backbones, converting entangled features into sparse, class-independent, spatially localized representations that achieve globally interpretable image classification while exceeding linear probe accuracy.

核心贡献

  • 将QPM（Quadratic Programming Enhanced Model）适配到冻结的DINOv2骨干网络上，首次从冻结视觉基础模型中提取稀疏、空间定位、类别无关的全局可解释特征
  • 放弃CLS token而采用平均池化，直接连接patch嵌入与特征图，实现可解释特征在输入空间的高保真空间定位
  • 引入Plausibility度量指标和稀疏损失函数，量化并提升解释的定位质量
  • 在分类精度上超越DINOv2线性探头，同时提供优越的解释质量

Key Contributions

  • First approach to extract sparse, spatially localized, class-independent part explanations from frozen DINOv2 features without external concept banks or backbone fine-tuning
  • Replaces CLS token with average-pooling to enable direct spatial localisation of globally interpretable features
  • Introduces a Plausibility metric (inspired by the pointing game) and a sparsity loss to improve feature localisation precision
  • Achieves 98.4% of the maximal score in a radar plot combining interpretability metrics and accuracy

Methodology

DINO-QPM builds on the QPM framework by applying it as a lightweight adapter on top of frozen DINOv2 patch embeddings. An MLP projects patch embeddings into a problem-specific feature space, after which a BLDD (Binary Linear Discriminant Decomposition) layer performs sparse feature assignment. Average-pooling replaces the CLS token to maintain a direct connection between the global representation and spatial feature maps, enabling high-fidelity localisation. A sparsity loss minimizes spatial scatter and background noise, grounding explanations in relevant object parts. The three-stage QPM training pipeline (dense training, QP-based feature selection, retraining with sparse constraints) is adapted for the frozen backbone setting.

Results & Highlights

  • DINO-QPM outperforms DINOv2 linear probe in classification accuracy while providing inherent interpretability
  • Achieves 98.4% of the maximal combined score across interpretability and accuracy metrics on radar plot evaluation
  • Spatial localisation quality drastically exceeds GradCAM baselines from linear-probed DINO models
  • Register tokens in DINOv2 provide significant benefits for DINO-QPM's feature localisation
  • Evaluated on CUB-200-2011, Stanford Cars, and other standard benchmarks

Authors & Credibility

The authors are from the Institute for Information Processing (L3S) at Leibniz University Hannover. Robert Zimmermann and Thomas Norrenbrock are equal contributors, with Bodo Rosenhahn as senior author. The same group previously developed the QPM framework, giving them strong expertise in this line of work.

Critical Assessment

The work makes a meaningful contribution by bridging the gap between frozen foundation models and globally interpretable classification. The key insight -- replacing the CLS token with average-pooling to enable spatial localisation -- is simple but effective. The approach is rigorous in its evaluation across multiple interpretability metrics and DINOv2 model sizes. However, the method inherits QPM's computational overhead during the feature selection stage, and the evaluation is limited to relatively standard classification benchmarks rather than safety-critical domains where interpretability is most needed.

Strengths

  • Clean adaptation of QPM to the frozen backbone paradigm without requiring expensive fine-tuning
  • Strong empirical results surpassing linear probe accuracy while providing inherent interpretability
  • Introduction of the Plausibility metric provides a principled way to evaluate localisation quality
  • Comprehensive comparison with post-hoc methods (GradCAM, LIME) and concept-based alternatives

Limitations

  • Evaluation is limited to standard classification benchmarks (CUB, Cars), not tested in safety-critical domains
  • The three-stage training pipeline adds complexity compared to simple linear probing
  • Relies on the QPM framework's assumption that classes can be represented by sparse, diverse features, which may not hold for all domains
  • Does not compare against more recent interpretable-by-design ViT approaches like ProtoViT
