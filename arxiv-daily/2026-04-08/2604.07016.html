Predictive Representations for Skill Transfer in Reinforcement Learning

一句话总结： 提出Outcome-Predictive State Representations (OPSRs)框架，通过任务无关的结果预测构建状态抽象，并基于此定义可跨任务迁移的技能，实现强化学习中的高效知识转移。
TL;DR: OPSRs provide task-independent state abstractions based on outcome predictions, enabling skill transfer across MDPs through combined state and action abstraction.

核心贡献

  • 引入Outcome-Predictive State Representations (OPSRs)，一种基于任务无关环境观测结果预测的智能体中心化状态抽象
  • 证明了在可规划MDP中，结果等价的状态抽象可以实现最优迁移，并分析了迁移覆盖率的限制
  • 提出基于OPSR的技能（抽象动作），通过状态抽象实现跨任务的可复用性
  • 从示范数据中学习OPSR技能，在新任务中无需预处理即可显著加速学习

Key Contributions

  • Introduces OPSRs as agent-centered, task-independent state abstractions composed of outcome predictions
  • Proves formal transfer guarantees: outcome-equivalent abstractions enable optimal transfer between plannable MDPs
  • Overcomes transfer cover limitations by integrating OPSRs with action abstraction (skills based on options)
  • Demonstrates learning OPSR-based skills from demonstrations that speed up learning in entirely new tasks

Methodology

The authors develop a formal framework for transfer via state abstraction in reinforcement learning. They define outcomes as task-independent, compact environmental observations and construct outcome-equivalent state abstractions from predictions of these outcomes. The framework introduces the concept of "transfer cover" (overlap between state abstractions across MDPs) and proves that for plannable MDPs (where reactive policies cannot outperform non-reactive plans), outcome-equivalent abstractions enable optimal transfer. To overcome transfer cover limitations, they introduce OPSR-based skills -- abstract actions based on options that can be reused between tasks. Skills are learned from demonstrations and tested on unseen tasks.

Results & Highlights

  • OPSRs provide a principled formalism for state and action abstraction in RL transfer
  • Outcome-equivalent state abstractions allow optimal transfer between plannable MDPs
  • OPSR-based skills learned from demonstrations significantly accelerate learning in new tasks without pre-processing
  • The framework unifies state and action abstraction naturally

Authors & Credibility

Ruben Vereecken and Alessandra Russo are from Imperial College London's Computer Science department, and Luke Dickens is from UCL's Information Studies department. The research was conducted between 2018 and 2021, indicating thorough theoretical development. The formal mathematical treatment is rigorous and well-grounded.

Critical Assessment

The theoretical contribution is substantial: the formal unification of state and action abstraction through OPSRs is elegant and fills an important gap in the RL transfer literature. The proofs for optimal transfer in plannable MDPs are rigorous. However, the empirical evaluation appears limited to relatively simple domains, and the plannable MDP assumption (reactive policies no better than non-reactive plans) is quite restrictive -- many real-world tasks require reactive policies. The framework's scalability to high-dimensional or continuous state/action spaces is not addressed.

Strengths

  • Rigorous theoretical framework with formal transfer guarantees
  • Elegant unification of state and action abstraction through outcome predictions
  • Skills can be learned from demonstrations and transferred zero-shot to new tasks
  • Clear mathematical treatment of transfer cover and its limitations

Limitations

  • The plannable MDP assumption limits applicability to many practical RL problems
  • Empirical evaluation appears limited to relatively simple environments
  • Scalability to high-dimensional or continuous domains is not demonstrated
  • The work dates from 2018-2021 and does not engage with more recent transfer learning advances

Relevance

本文从状态抽象和表征的角度研究强化学习中的迁移问题，提出基于预测的表征方法。对理解如何构建任务无关的抽象表征具有理论启发意义，但与LLM隐藏状态研究的直接联系较弱。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Can OPSRs be combined with modern deep RL approaches for high-dimensional observation spaces?
  • How does the transfer cover relate to representation overlap analysis in neural networks?
  • Could outcome prediction principles be applied to analyze hidden state representations in LLMs?
