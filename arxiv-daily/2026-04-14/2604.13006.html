One Token Away from Collapse: The Fragility of Instruction-Tuned Helpfulness

一句话总结： 简单的词汇级约束（如禁止逗号或单词"the"）会导致指令微调LLM的回答全面性崩溃式下降14–48%，这是规划失败而非能力缺失——两阶段生成可恢复59–96%的内容，且该脆弱性 exclusively 源于指令微调，基础模型几乎不受影响。
TL;DR: Trivial lexical constraints (e.g., banning commas or the word "the") cause instruction-tuned LLMs to collapse comprehensiveness by 14–48%. This is a planning failure, not a capability limit—two-pass generation recovers 59–96% of response length. The fragility is exclusive to instruction-tuned models; base models show no systematic collapse.

核心贡献

  • 首次系统记录"约束诱导的回答崩溃"（constraint-induced response collapse）：简单的词汇约束即可导致四个指令微调模型（含闭源GPT-4o-mini）全面性大幅下降
  • 通过机制分析将该现象定位为先前未识别的规划失败（planning failure），而非能力限制：模型本可以写出满足约束的详尽回答，但在单阶段生成中主动选择了极简策略
  • 证明该脆弱性 exclusively 由指令微调引入：同架构基础模型在相同约束下仅表现出微小、嘈杂、双向的效应
  • 揭示独立式LLM-as-judge评估存在系统性盲区：仅能检测到成对评估所测得质量损失的不到五分之一（3.5% vs 23%）

Key Contributions

  • First systematic documentation of constraint-induced response collapse: trivial lexical constraints cause 14–48% comprehensiveness loss across four instruction-tuned models (including closed-weight GPT-4o-mini)
  • Mechanistic analysis localizes the phenomenon to a previously unidentified planning failure, not a capability limitation: models can produce comprehensive constrained output but default to a minimal strategy in single-pass generation
  • Demonstrates that this fragility is exclusively created by instruction tuning: identical base models show only small, noisy, bidirectional effects under the same constraints
  • Exposes a methodological blind spot in independent LLM-as-judge evaluation, which detects less than one-fifth of the quality loss measured by pairwise comparison (3.5% vs 23%)

Methodology

  • Prompts: 40 prompts across 4 categories (explanation, how-to, analysis, technical), plus replication on MT-Bench (80 questions, 8 categories)
  • Constraints: 8 lexical constraints at punctuation, pattern, and word levels (e.g., no comma, no bullet points, no "the", no discourse markers), plus 2 compositional constraints
  • Models: 3 open-weight instruct models (Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen-2.5-7B, Mistral-7B), their base counterparts, and closed-weight GPT-4o-mini
  • Evaluation: Pairwise comparison (baseline vs constrained) judged by GPT-4o-mini and GPT-4o; independent scoring for contrast; automated constraint satisfaction checking
  • Mechanistic probes: Two-pass recovery experiments, linear regression probes on last-token hidden states to predict response length, and token-level Jensen-Shannon divergence analysis

Results & Highlights

  • Severe comprehensiveness loss: −23.4% (Llama), −39.9% (Qwen), −14.2% (Mistral), −31.0% (GPT-4o-mini) overall; baseline win rates of 77–100%
  • Planning failure confirmed: Two-pass generation (free write → rewrite under constraint) recovers 91–96% of length for Llama/Mistral and 59% for Qwen
  • Collapse is pre-determined: Linear probes on middle-layer prompt representations predict response length with R² = 0.51–0.93 before any token is generated; R² tracks collapse severity across models
  • Base models are robust: Base counterparts show negative R² in probing and near-chance baseline win rates (45–59%), with some constraints even improving quality
  • Evaluation bias exposed: Independent scoring detects an average 3.5% drop vs 23.4% in pairwise comparison—a 6.7× gap
  • MT-Bench replication: Collapse generalizes across all 8 categories; math is the sole exception for Llama because its formulaic responses do not rely on disrupted formatting templates

  • Strong institutional backing (USC + Intel AI) and rigorous experimental design with cross-judge validation, base-model controls, and mechanistic probes

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. The finding that lexical (not structural/format) constraints cause collapse in commercially deployed models is new and challenges the assumption that instruction tuning teaches generalizable helpfulness.
  • Experimental rigor: Excellent. The study combines behavioral evaluation, cross-judge validation, base-model controls, representation probing, and recovery experiments across multiple model families.
  • Significance: Potentially high for both research methodology and deployment. The 6.7× evaluation gap and the GPT-4o-mini result suggest that real-world constrained-generation systems may carry undetected quality degradation.

Strengths

  • Multi-model, multi-judge, cross-validated behavioral evaluation
  • Clever mechanistic experiments (two-pass recovery + linear probing) that distinguish planning failure from capability limitation
  • Critical base-vs-instruct comparison that isolates instruction tuning as the causal factor
  • Replication on MT-Bench demonstrating generalizability
  • Exposing a major methodological flaw in how constrained generation is commonly evaluated

Limitations

  • Mechanistic analysis limited to 7–8B open-weight models; internal mechanism in GPT-4o-mini cannot be directly probed
  • LLM judges may have inherent biases (though mitigated by cross-judge validation and randomized position)
  • Constraint set, while diverse, does not exhaust all possible lexical restrictions
  • Base model probing uses fewer samples (30 vs 120), though negative R² at all layers is unambiguous

Relevance

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Can representation-level interventions (e.g., activation steering at middle layers) mitigate or prevent this planning failure during single-pass generation?
  • Does the collapse severity correlate with specific instruction-tuning recipes (e.g., SFT vs DPO vs RLHF), and can we design training objectives to explicitly decouple competence from surface-form templates?
  • How do more complex real-world constraints (safety filters, brand guidelines, accessibility requirements) map onto the lexical constraint taxonomy studied here?
