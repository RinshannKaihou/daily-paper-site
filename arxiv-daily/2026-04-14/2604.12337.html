Identifying and Mitigating Gender Cues in Academic Recommendation Letters: An Interpretability Case Study

一句话总结： 在去除姓名、代词等显性性别标识后的美国麻醉科住院医师推荐信（LoR）中，DistilBERT、RoBERTa 和 Llama 2 仍能以最高 63% 的准确率推断申请者性别；进一步用 SHAP 和 TF-IDF 识别并抹除隐性性别词汇后，性别推断性能仅下降约 2.7% macro F1，仍显著高于随机水平，说明推荐信中的隐性性别信号极其顽固。
TL;DR: Even after removing explicit gender markers (names, pronouns, titles) from 8,992 medical-residency letters of recommendation, DistilBERT, RoBERTa, and Llama 2 achieve up to 63% accuracy in gender inference. After additionally masking implicit gender cues identified via SHAP and TF-IDF, macro F1 drops only ~2.7%, remaining well above chance—demonstrating that implicit gendered language in evaluative text is deeply persistent.

核心贡献

  • 构建了一个可复现的、基于 Transformer 与 LLM 的推荐信性别语言检测框架
  • 首次在真实医学住院申请推荐信大数据集（8,992 封）上同时评估显性与隐性去性别化的效果
  • 通过 TF-IDF 与 SHAP 解释性方法，量化并定位了推荐信中的隐性性别指示词
  • 以数据级干预实验证明：即使去除显性与隐性性别线索，模型仍能以高于随机的水平预测性别，揭示纯技术去偏见策略的局限

Key Contributions

  • A reproducible framework for detecting gendered language in letters of recommendation using Transformer-based classifiers and LLMs
  • Large-scale empirical assessment of both explicit and implicit de-gendering on a real-world corpus of 8,992 medical residency LoRs
  • Interpretability-driven quantification of implicit gender cues via TF-IDF and SHAP token attribution
  • Data-level intervention experiment showing that gender inference remains above chance even after masking both explicit and implicit cues, highlighting the limits of purely technical debiasing

Methodology

The study follows a three-stage pipeline: (1) Data curation: 8,992 LoRs from a major U.S. anesthesiology residency program are annotated by self-reported applicant gender (31% female, 69% male) and anonymized. (2) Explicit de-gendering (EDG): All explicit gender identifiers (names, pronouns, titles, kinship terms) are regex-replaced with female counterparts to produce the EDG dataset. Three models—DistilBERT, RoBERTa, and Llama 2 (with LoRA)—are fine-tuned for binary gender classification on both raw and EDG texts. (3) Interpretability audit and implicit de-gendering: TF-IDF and SHAP are used to identify the most gender-predictive tokens (nouns, verbs, adjectives). These top tokens are masked in the EDG corpus, creating two further datasets (EDG w/o SHAP Tokens, EDG w/o TF-IDF Tokens), and models are re-trained to measure the efficacy of implicit de-gendering.

Results & Highlights

  • Baseline (raw text with explicit markers): DistilBERT achieves near-perfect gender classification (macro F1 > 0.95), confirming that surface cues are highly predictive.
  • Explicit de-gendering (EDG): Significant gender leakage persists—DistilBERT reaches 63.3% accuracy / 0.577 macro F1; RoBERTa 61% / 0.553; Llama 2 61.2% / 0.561.
  • Implicit gender cues identified: Female-associated tokens include "humanitarian," "delightful," "wonderful," "children," "pediatric," "social," "compassionate," and "warm." Male-associated tokens include "respectful," "military," "combat," "supervise," "professional," and "humble."
  • Implicit de-gendering: Removing SHAP-identified tokens drops DistilBERT macro F1 by 2.7% (to 0.550) and accuracy by 3.6%; removing TF-IDF tokens drops macro F1 by 1.4%. For Llama 2, the drop is much larger (~15% macro F1), though this may reflect sensitivity to unknown-token masking. Even after this iterative removal, accuracy remains ~60%—still well above random chance.
  • Topic modeling (BERTopic): A random forest trained on topic vectors alone achieves 58% accuracy, and fusing topic vectors with DistilBERT embeddings yields 63% accuracy, suggesting higher-level narrative themes also encode gender.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The core finding—that implicit gender cues persist after explicit de-gendering—has been documented in hiring contexts before (e.g., Amazon's scrapped recruiting tool). However, the systematic, interpretability-driven intervention on a large LoR corpus, combined with topic-modeling analysis, is a meaningful advance over prior descriptive or small-scale hand-coded studies.
  • Experimental rigor: The dataset is large and real-world. The use of multiple model architectures, stratified splits, macro F1 for class imbalance, and two distinct interpretability methods (SHAP and TF-IDF) strengthens the analysis. However, converting all explicit gender markers to female equivalents (rather than gender-neutral terms) is an unusual design choice that preserves grammatical fluency but means the model sees only one overt pronoun set—making the task harder in a specific way.
  • Significance: The paper makes an important practical contribution by showing that naïve anonymization is insufficient for fair AI-assisted evaluation. The critical discussion (§7) rightly notes that overly aggressive de-gendering can trivialize identity and mask meaningful candidate experiences.

Strengths

  • Large, real-world dataset with institutional relevance
  • Multi-model comparison (DistilBERT, RoBERTa, Llama 2) including a 7B LLM with LoRA fine-tuning
  • Dual interpretability lens (model-based SHAP + model-agnostic TF-IDF) plus topic modeling
  • Honest critical discussion of the ethical limits of technical debiasing

Limitations

  • Binary gender framework: The study treats gender as a binary male/female variable based on self-reported metadata, which limits generalizability and does not address non-binary identities.
  • No downstream outcome data: The authors did not have access to residency admission decisions, so the link between gendered language and actual selection bias is inferred rather than measured.
  • Masking artifacts: Replacing implicit gender tokens with [MASK] or can produce ungrammatical sentences, making the fully de-gendered text unsuitable for human reviewers.
  • SHAP sub-token ambiguity: Occasionally SHAP attributes importance to tokenization fragments rather than whole words, introducing noise into the interpretability analysis.
  • Class imbalance: The dataset is 69% male; while macro F1 mitigates this, minority-class recall and precision remain modest (~0.40).

Relevance

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How would the results change if explicit gender markers were replaced with truly neutral placeholders (e.g., singular "they") rather than converted to female equivalents?
  • Can fine-grained sentiment or pragmatic analysis reveal why words like "leadership" or "rotation" become gender proxies in this specific professional domain?
  • What would an effective human-in-the-loop deployment look like—using these classifiers not to anonymize text, but to flag potentially biased language for reviewers in real time?
  • How can this framework be extended beyond binary gender to detect and mitigate bias against other underrepresented groups in evaluative text?
