Masked by Consensus: Disentangling Privileged Knowledge in LLM Correctness

Tomer Ashuach, Liat Ein-Dor, Shai Gretz, Yoav Katz, Yonatan Belinkov

arXiv: 2604.12373v1

Date: 2026-04-14

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.12373v1

Masked by Consensus: Disentangling Privileged Knowledge in LLM Correctness

一句话总结： 通过在“分歧子集”上评估探针性能，研究发现LLM在事实知识任务中存在关于自身答案正确性的特权知识（privileged knowledge），但在数学推理任务中不存在；此前全量数据集上观察到的无差异现象，实际上被模型间高度一致性所掩盖。
TL;DR: By evaluating probes on disagreement subsets where models conflict in correctness labels, the study finds that LLMs possess genuine privileged knowledge about their own answer correctness in factual knowledge tasks, but not in mathematical reasoning. The previously observed parity on full test sets was masked by high inter-model agreement.

核心贡献

  • 指出并量化了“模型间一致性”（inter-model agreement）作为评估特权知识时的关键混淆因素
  • 提出在分歧子集（disagreement subsets）上评估探针，以剥离共享的正确性模式、孤立真正的内部信号
  • 揭示特权知识具有领域特异性：在事实知识任务中显著存在，在数学推理任务中不存在
  • 定位了事实任务中特权知识在网络深度上的涌现模式：从早期到中期层开始渐进增强，与模型特定的记忆检索一致

Key Contributions

  • Identifies inter-model agreement as a critical confound when evaluating privileged knowledge in LLM correctness prediction
  • Introduces evaluation on disagreement subsets to isolate genuine internal signals from shared correctness patterns
  • Reveals domain-specific privileged knowledge: it consistently emerges in factual tasks but remains absent in mathematical reasoning
  • Localizes the factual privileged signal across network depth, showing progressive emergence from early-to-mid layers onward, consistent with model-specific memory retrieval

Methodology

The authors train linear (logistic regression) and MLP probes on hidden states from the final token of every 5th layer to predict answer correctness. They compare three source-model configurations for the probe: (1) Self-Probe (source = target model), (2) Cross-Model Probe (source = peer LLM of comparable size), and (3) Embedding-Model Probe. Experiments cover three 7B–9B instruction-tuned models (Qwen-2.5-7B, Llama-3.1-8B, Gemma-2-9B), with additional Qwen-3-32B validation. Datasets include three factual knowledge tasks (Mintaka, TriviaQA, HotPotQA) and two math reasoning tasks (GSM1K, MATH). Evaluation uses nested stratified 10-fold cross-validation with AUC as the metric. The premium gap is defined as the AUC advantage of self-probes over the best external probe. To disentangle confounds, probes are trained on the full dataset but evaluated on disagreement subsets where the source and target models produce opposite correctness labels.

Results & Highlights

  • Full test sets: Self-probes and external probes perform comparably; the premium gap effectively vanishes (2/3 models in factual tasks, all models in math)
  • Inter-model agreement: Models agree on correctness for ~80% of factual questions and ~75% of math questions, allowing external probes to exploit peer correctness as a proxy
  • Disagreement subsets (factual): A statistically significant premium gap emerges across all models and datasets (~3–7% AUC improvement), indicating genuine privileged knowledge
  • Disagreement subsets (math): No premium gap; external probes match or outperform self-probes, suggesting math correctness signals are publicly accessible
  • Layer analysis: In factual tasks, the self-probe advantage is near zero in early layers and grows progressively from layers 10–15 onward. In math tasks, the gap fluctuates near zero or is negative at all depths
  • Robustness: Findings hold for both linear and MLP probes, and largely generalize to Qwen-3-32B

Authors & Credibility

Authors: Tomer Ashuach and Yonatan Belinkov (Technion – Israel Institute of Technology); Liat Ein-Dor, Shai Gretz, and Yoav Katz (IBM Research). The combination of an academic institution (Technion) and an industrial research lab (IBM) lends credibility. The experimental design is rigorous, with cross-validation, significance testing with Bonferroni-Holm correction, and multiple probe architectures. The paper directly addresses and reconciles prior conflicting results in the literature (e.g., Xiao et al., 2025; Seo et al., 2025; Chi et al., 2025).

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The core insight—that inter-model agreement masks privileged knowledge—is elegant and methodologically important. The disagreement-subset evaluation is a simple but powerful innovation.
  • Experimental rigor: High. Multiple models, datasets, probe types, layer-wise analysis, and a larger model validation. Controls for distributional-shift alternative explanations are well-argued.
  • Significance: Resolves an active debate about whether LLMs have privileged access to their own correctness. The domain-specific finding (factual vs. math) provides a nuanced and actionable picture for future work on hallucination detection and self-verification.

Strengths

  • Clean, well-motivated methodology with strong statistical controls
  • Reconciles seemingly contradictory prior findings by identifying a hidden confound
  • Domain-specific discovery is mechanistically interpretable and aligned with known information-flow patterns in factual recall
  • Extensive supplementary analysis (MLP probes, per-layer breakdowns, lexical-only controls, larger model)

Limitations

  • Main analysis limited to 7B–9B models; larger models may show different patterns (though Qwen-3-32B largely confirms trends)
  • Scope limited to factual knowledge and math reasoning; hybrid domains (coding, commonsense reasoning) are not studied
  • Probes, while standard, may have limited capacity to extract all privileged signals
  • Correlational evidence; causal mechanisms (e.g., via activation steering) remain future work

Relevance

这篇论文直接契合你对「mechanistic interpretability and hidden state analysis」以及「ML reliability」的研究兴趣。它通过严谨的探针实验和创新的分歧子集评估方法，澄清了LLM是否具备关于自身正确性的“特权知识”这一核心问题，并揭示了该现象在事实知识与数学推理之间的领域不对称性。这对 hallucination detection、self-verification 以及基于内部状态的模型监控研究具有重要的方法论启发。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Does privileged knowledge emerge in other domains like coding or commonsense reasoning when evaluated on disagreement subsets?
  • Can activation steering or causal interventions directly manipulate the identified factual correctness signal to modulate output accuracy?
  • How does privileged knowledge scale with model size, and does it appear in math reasoning for much larger models?
  • Can disagreement-subset evaluation be applied to other introspection tasks beyond correctness prediction (e.g., uncertainty calibration, hallucination detection)?
