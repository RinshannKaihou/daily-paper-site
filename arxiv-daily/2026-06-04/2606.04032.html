Strengths

  • Clear decision tree: when to use Q-K=V vs Q=K-V vs Q=K=V vs combined GQA/MQA (Section 3.4, Table 10)
  • Separates **parameter reduction** from **KV cache reduction**—corrects common conflation
  • Multi-scale LM + downstream + A100 serving benchmarks form a complete deployment story
  • Mechanistic K–V redundancy analysis explains *why* Q-K=V works and *why* Q=K fails

Limitations

  • **Scale ceiling:** 1.2B parameters only; trend toward lower degradation at larger size is hypothesized, not proven at 7B+
  • **Theory:** Explanations empirical (similarity, rank, symmetry)—no formal expressivity bounds
  • **Context length:** Main training/eval focused ≤2048; ultra-long (32k–128k) cache math extrapolated; Table 16 truncation methodology limits precise long-ctx PPL claims
  • **Scope:** Decoder-only primary; cross-attention in encoder–decoder mentioned but not exhaustively benchmarked

TL;DR

This ICML 2026 study benchmarks three projection-sharing constraints—Q-K=V (shared K&V), Q=K-V (shared Q&K), and Q=K=V (single projection)—plus (X)+ 2D positional encodings for symmetric variants, across 12 synthetic/vision/NLP tasks and 300M/1.2B GPT-style LMs on 10B SlimPajama tokens. **Q-K=V** halves the KV cache (K reused as V) with only **+3.1%** (300M) / **+2.48%** (1.2B) perplexity degradation and **−0.41%** average downstream accuracy; it stacks orthogonally with GQA/MQA (**87.5%–96.9%** cache reduction). **Q=K-V** trains competitively (+4.9% PPL) but offers **zero** cache savings; **Q=K=V** collapses quality (+25.4% PPL). Analysis ties success to K–V representational redundancy (cosine 0.73) and preserved Q–K asymmetry.
