Characterizing and Identifying Separable Graphical Models

arXiv:2607.01057 · Christopher Meek, Kayvan Sadeghi · University of Washington · UCL · 67 pp · 8 appendices
graphical models structure identification separation equivalence causal discovery mixed graphs

Essentially separable mixed graphs are equivalent to simple acyclic anterial graphs — unifying DAGs, chain graphs, MAGs, and anterial graphs under one framework.

Key metric
O(|V|SepSetBound(G)+1)
Independence tests SGI needs to recover the full separation-equivalence class under perfect testing. SepSetBound(G) = maxa,b max(|aiAdj(a,b,G)|, |aiAdj(b,a,G)|)
Why this matters

Practitioners working with hidden variables or selection bias can now reason at one layer above DAGs, chain graphs, and MAGs — instead of switching tools per mixed-graph family.

Critical edge

The class excludes genuinely cyclic mixed graphs (feedback loops), and identifiability rests on perfect testing — faithfulness assumptions hard to certify on real data.

Methodology

Mixed graphs over vertex set V with three edge types (—, →, ↔); a vertex pair may carry up to four edges. Separation is a generalization of d-separation: a walk is connecting given C when every collider section meets C and no non-collider does. i ⊥ j | C iff no such connecting walk exists.

  • Separable graph. Every missing edge {i, j} admits a separating set for its endpoints.
  • Essentially separable. Separation-equivalent to some separable graph — the broader membership class.
  • Anterialize (Section 5). Removes arrowheads violating the anterial endmark property. Theorem 5.3: every separable graph is equivalent to its anterialized form.
  • Inducing walk. An open walk whose every internal vertex is inducing for the endpoints — the building block of all three equivalence characterizations.
  • SGI algorithm. Start from a complete undirected graph; for socs = 0, 1, … test every subset C ⊆ Ant(i) ∪ Ant(j); on i ⊥ j | C, delete the edge and apply Orientation Rule 1 to add arrowheads. Terminates at socs ≥ SepSetBound(G).

Results & highlights

Five theorem clusters carry the unification; the algorithm-side theorems deliver identifiability and a polynomial complexity bound.

TheoremClaim
5.3 / 5.4 Every (essentially) separable graph is modeling-equivalent to one defined by a simple acyclic anterial graph. The paper's headline unification.
6.2 A graph is essentially undirected iff its independence model contains no inducing vertex — a purely separational criterion generalizing the chain-graph characterization of [27].
7.1 / 8.2 Two separable graphs are separation-equivalent iff they share adjacencies and minimal inducing walks; the refinement uses only discriminating inducing walks.
9.1 / 9.2 InducedArrowheads(G) is a canonical projection: invariant under separation equivalence, itself separable — a unique representative per equivalence class.
10.4 / 10.6 SGI is correct under perfect testing in O(|V|SepSetBound(G)+1) independence tests; it is also arrowhead-complete — no further arrowhead can be soundly added.
Comparison SGI generalizes CI / FCI by using non-local candidate separating sets Ant(i) ∪ Ant(j) and orienting concurrently with edge deletion. PC's adjacency locality fails for mixed graphs with bidirected edges (see Figure 6 / G11).

Strengths vs limitations

Strengths

  • Top-down unification of DAGs, chain graphs, MAGs, MCMPs, and anterial graphs — five families at one abstraction.
  • Three parallel characterizations of separation equivalence (graphical, refined graphical, separational) — pedagogically rare and useful.
  • Canonical projection theorem + arrowhead-completeness of SGI: the algorithm returns the most informative edge labels consistent with the data.
  • Explicit polynomial complexity O(|V|SepSetBound+1) under explicit identification conditions.
  • Christopher Meek's foundational authorship on essential graphs / CPDAGs lends the paper high credibility.
  • Honest §11 with seven explicit future directions acknowledged up front.

Limitations

  • Excludes all genuinely cyclic mixed graphs (feedback loops); extension is flagged as future work in §11.1.
  • Perfect testing / faithfulness is essentially required — hard to certify on real data, no finite-sample analysis.
  • No code release. Practitioners must implement SGI themselves from pseudocode in §10.
  • Zero empirical work: no simulations, no real-data benchmarks, no comparisons to PC / FCI / FCI+.
  • Non-local candidate set Ant(i) ∪ Ant(j) makes SGI heavier than PC on dense graphs; SepSetBound can be loose for undirected / high-degree graphs.
  • Sound-and-complete orientation rules for essentially separable graphs are left open.

Questions for further exploration

  1. How does SGI's empirical sample complexity with imperfect CI tests compare to PC / FCI / FCI+ on data with latent confounders?
  2. Can InducedArrowheads(G) define a score-based structure learner analogous to GES for DAGs — a Bayesian or hybrid alternative to SGI?
  3. Under σ-, m-, or δ-separation, which of the three equivalence characterizations transfer with minimal modification?
  4. Can the non-local candidate set Ant(i) ∪ Ant(j) be tightened to aiAdj(i, j, G) without sacrificing soundness, while preserving the polynomial bound?
  5. What finite-sample concentration bounds follow from the weaker bounded vertex separability + bounded induced-arrowhead perfectness assumption?
  6. Does the unification extend to dynamic / time-series mixed graphs given anterial acyclicity, or does that assumption break down?
  7. Does the framework inspire a similar canonical form for the cyclic mixed graphs flagged as open in §11.1?
Honest takeaway: strong structural / theoretical contribution to the mixed-graph literature; weak fit for today's digest themes (LLM training stability, inference evaluation, interpretability, etc.). The paper's authors themselves flag a roadmap of seven follow-ups in §11 — there's real depth here, just not aligned with the day's focus.
