Key Finding

A two-step orchestrator: OR-Tools CP-SAT solves Job Shop in 28 seconds, then a mutex + status-dependency layer executes the plan robustly on real MOF robots.

Optimal Resource Utilization for Autonomous Laboratory Orchestrators

Key Metric · Solver runtime
28s
16-job initial batch on 24 CPU cores via OR-Tools CP-SAT. Re-schedule for +8 new samples at t ≈ 46 days: only 1.4 s.
Why This Matters

Bridges AI experiment design and real hardware orchestration for self-driving labs. The CP + status-dependency pattern is portable beyond MOFs into any heterogeneous-instrument robot.

Critical Edge

"Robust" is asserted, not measured. No baseline vs. greedy FIFO or RL orchestrators; no fault-injection tests; no evidence the 28 s budget survives online re-planning inside an acquisition loop.

Methodology · Platform & Formulation

Solvothermal MOF synthesis is decomposed into UnitOPs, scheduled by CP-SAT, executed asynchronously. Synthesis ≈ 2 days/sample; parallelism in tens of samples.

Solver Knobs

  • Time unit: decaseconds (Ds = 10 s) — minutes too coarse, seconds adds solver complexity.
  • Reactions agglomerated by temperature to the same heater block up to capacity 4, then iteratively grouped across blocks.
  • Drying tasks: boolean variable picks any free reactor that doesn't conflict with a reaction on the same unit.
  • Execution layer enforces order status → component mutex → arm&clamp mutex to avoid deadlock / resource hoarding.

What the scheduler actually delivers

  • Reactions auto-group by temperature onto shared heater blocks, ending simultaneously per block (pressure-release safety).
  • Wash cycles + dry steps fan out across centrifuge / sonicator / rack / reactors with no conflicts.
  • Re-schedule placed drying tasks in the 4 500–34 600 Ds window without affecting total makespan.
  • All platform resources used concurrently in the demo run.

Schedule ↔ Execution Contract

The scheduler supplies order + inter-sample timing; the mutex/status-dependency layer (Python asyncio) handles real-hardware jitter, instrument unavailability, and the impossibility of mathematical simultaneity.

TL;DR — Two-step orchestration: CP-SAT schedules, mutex layer executes. Real MOF robot at NIST. Code at github.com/usnistgov/autoMOF. 一句话总结：约束规划求解 Job Shop + 基于 mutex/状态依赖的鲁棒执行层；NIST 的 MOF 机器人端到端验证，16 样品批约 28 s，加 8 个新样品重调度约 1.4 s。

Strengths vs Limitations

Strengths

  • Clean two-layer decomposition — CP scheduler and dependency/mutex layer are independently swappable.
  • Physically-meaningful constraints encoded (pressure-release, centrifuge no-mid-spin load, end-of-dispense within 4 min of reaction start).
  • Global-UnitOP / sample-wise-UnitOP pattern is reusable beyond MOFs.
  • Open-source release at github.com/usnistgov/autoMOF.
  • Honest Open Problems section sets a clear research agenda (priorities, feedback to acquisition, batch-size DOE).

Limitations

  • No quantitative baseline vs. greedy FIFO / RL orchestrators / human scheduling.
  • Execution-layer robustness asserted — not stress-tested under instrument faults, partial retries, contamination, or duration drift.
  • Single-domain case study (MOF solvothermal); transfer to flow chemistry / biological assays unaddressed.
  • 28 s for 16 jobs on 24 cores — combinatorial explosion if embedded in the AI acquisition loop.
  • Re-scheduling only triggered by new samples — no reactive mid-batch replan.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does CP-SAT solve time scale with batch size and platform complexity — and at what point does the 28 s budget become incompatible with online re-planning inside an acquisition loop?
  2. Could the dependency/mutex execution layer be reused with a different scheduler (RL-trained, priority-aware) without modification — proving the two layers are truly decoupled?
  3. What makespan improvement does the CP scheduler deliver vs. a hand-coded FIFO queue on real MOF workloads under realistic duration noise?
  4. How should the orchestrator handle instrument failures (a heater block going offline mid-campaign) — is reactive re-solving fast enough, or does it need an online heuristic?
  5. Can the three Open Problems (prioritization, feedback to acquisition, batch-size DOE) be cast as a single information-gain-weighted scheduling problem — and is it tractable?
  6. How transferable is the constraint model to other autonomous-lab domains (flow chemistry, biological assays, additive manufacturing) — which constraints need re-engineering per platform?
