The Binary Tree Mechanism is Optimal for Approximate Differentially Private Continual Counting

arXiv:2607.00876 Konstantina Bairaktari · Kasper Green Larsen Aarhus University · 2026 differential privacy continual counting lower bound hereditary discrepancy binary tree 差分隐私
Key metric · tight lower bound
Ω(log3/2 n / max{ε, δ})
Expected ℓ∞ error of any (ε,δ)-DP mechanism on a binary stream of length n. Matches the binary tree mechanism's Gaussian-noise upper bound up to constants in ε, δ.
Why this matters

Closes a long-standing open problem in DP. Practitioners designing DP streaming systems can now cite this as definitive justification for adopting the binary tree mechanism rather than searching for alternatives.

Critical edge

Purely negative — asymptotic only, constants hidden, restricted to small ε<1 and small δ. Leaves ℓ∞2 error, the sparse regime (ne ≪ n), and decaying sums / distinct elements untouched.

Methodology · proof in two halves

  1. Oblivious reduction. Per [BDKT12], it suffices to bound input-independent noise z for M(x) = Ax + z.
  2. Tree-basis decomposition. Re-expand z in a non-orthogonal basis {χu} of root + right-child nodes; each suffix Aei decomposes over right-child ancestors.
  3. Residual-noise induction. Define zui as the contribution from nodes below u; the dynamic range grows Ω(1/max{ε,δ}) per level — accumulating to Ω(log3/2 n / max{ε,δ}) over log n levels.
  4. Adversary. Ganapathy–Venkatasubramanian scalar lower bound + sibling linear measurements keep left/right subtrees uncorrelated.

Strengths

  • Closes a long-standing open problem with a matching bound — the strongest possible form of result in this area.
  • Tree-basis + linear-measurement technique is novel and likely reusable for related lower bounds (ℓ∞2, decaying sums, distinct elements).
  • Sharp hereditary-discrepancy separation improves a previously known factor from log m to log3/2 m.
  • Tightens the pure-DP lower bound as a free side effect.

Limitations

  • Asymptotic in n; constants not extracted; the match is in n-dependence only.
  • Framed in the offline observation model — continual observation follows only because offline is at least as hard.
  • Restricted to small ε<1 and small δ<C; large-ε / high-δ regimes uncovered.
  • Does not address ℓ∞2, the sparse regime (ne ≪ n), or decaying-sum / distinct-element variants.
  • Purely negative — no new algorithm proposed.

Questions for further exploration

  1. Is Ω(log3/2 n / max{ε,δ}) tight for ℓ∞2 error (RMS over time steps), or can the binary tree mechanism's RMS behaviour be improved?
  2. What is the explicit constant in the Ω? Can the inductive accumulation be calibrated to match the binary tree mechanism's constants exactly?
  3. Does the lower bound extend to release-and-forget / sliding-window continual counting, where only the last k prefix sums must be accurate?
  4. Can the tree-basis + linear-measurement technique be lifted to lower-bound continual decaying sums [HUU24] or continual distinct-element counting [JKR+23, HSS24, CEM+25]?
  5. For n-ary tree mechanisms with branching b > 2, does a b-ary variant beat the log3/2 n exponent, or is binary branching already optimal?
  6. Does the hereditary-discrepancy / private-ℓ∞ separation sharpen further under additional structural constraints on the query matrix (low rank, Toeplitz, circulant)?
