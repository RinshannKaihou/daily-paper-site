PRISM — Prototype Language Models

Dan Ley · Giang Nguyen · Himabindu Lakkaraju · Julius Adebayo  Harvard · Guide Labs
arXiv:2607.00510 2026-07-01 cs.LG · cs.CL v1
interpretability sparse-models training-data-attribution prototype-learning language-model-architecture model-steering

PRISM replaces the dense LM-head output pathway with a sparse, non-negative mixture over K learned prototype vectors, each anchored to a coherent training-token neighborhood — making both training-data attribution and behavior editing architecture-level constant-cost operations.

Key Metric · Cached Attribution
~470×faster
Cached PRISM influence scoring vs. EK-FAC at a matched 20 GB GPU budget — and storage shrinks to O(Nk) sparse prototype records. Full PRISM (no cache) is only ~2× faster, so the win is the cache, not the subspace.
Why This Matters

Training-data attribution and prototype-level behavior editing become inference-time matrix multiplies — a frozen 1.6B PRISM gains +2.72pp from a sparse controller (vs. +0.32pp for a matched SAE controller) and NSFW suppression drops mean score 0.668 → 0.154 with no finetuning and no quality loss.

Critical Edge

Competitive, not strictly superior: a persistent 0.8–2.1pp average gap vs. matched dense GPT at 124M–760M, including a striking −8.99pp BoolQ regression at 760M. PRISM only surpasses GPT at 1.6B / 20B FineWeb tokens (+1.05pp Avg). EK-FAC is a strong baseline, not attribution ground truth — the 470× claim inherits that caveat.

Methodology

Keep the causal Transformer decoder unchanged. Replace only the final-layer output pathway:

  • Prototype bank P = [p₁,…,p_K] ∈ ℝ^(d×K), i.i.d. N(0,1). Cosine sim → ReLU → top-k → active set K_t.
  • Reconstruction + residual: ẑ_t = Σ a_{t,i} p_i, r_t = z_t − ẑ_t. Prototypes live in hidden space and share the LM head W, so output cost stays O(Vd), not O(VK).
  • Logits decompose as t = W r_t + Σ a_{t,i} (W p_i) — each prototype contributes a fixed vocabulary signature.
  • Four-term loss L = L_CE + λ_REC L_REC + λ_R1 L_R1 + λ_R2 L_R2: cross-entropy, residual reconstruction, and a symmetric R₁/R₂ clustering that anchors prototypes to token neighborhoods (soft, not hard projection to training tokens).
  • Influence: under fixed active support, ∇PLs is nonzero only on K_t, so the influence score reduces to k cached contractions — storage O(Nk).
  • Theory: Thm 4.1 separates the Hessian as η G_A ⊗ I_d + diag(B₁,…,B_K); Thm 4.2 bounds κ(I+M) ≤ 1 + λ_max(M), so clustering shrinks cross-prototype coupling.

Operational & Scale

GPT-style decoder-only backbone, tied input/output embeddings, T = 1024, AdamW, bf16, global batch 524,288 tokens.

  • Param overhead: +1.7% (K = 16,384, d = 1600 → 26.2M extra on a 1.558B backbone).
  • Throughput: 40.4k tok/s vs. 41.5k for GPT on H200 → 97.3% of GPT throughput.
  • Peak memory: 50.9 GB vs. 49.1 GB (+1.8 GB).
  • On-manifold diagnostics at 1.6B / 50B tokens: R̄₁ ∈ [0.94, 1.00], R₂ ∈ [0.87, 0.98], residual share 0.05–0.09 — prototypes stay anchored at scale.
  • Curvature: condition number of I + M drops from 6.18×10⁷ (no clustering) to 4.56×10² (strongest) — ~5 orders of magnitude.

Controllers fit ≤80 steps, lr = 0.005 over frozen active-prototype features (pool ∈ {64,128,256}, ℓ₂ ∈ {0.1,1,5,10}). Suppression injects Δ = α · Σ ã_{t,p} W p at every step (α = −5.0 for NSFW) — no parameter updates, no token blacklist.

Strengths

  • Architecture + theory + empirics cohere: the same clustering losses drive interpretability and the attribution speed-up.
  • Honest experimental design: matched dense baselines, ablation ladder (clustering strength, top-k, K), SAE control for the controller claim, EK-FAC for attribution.
  • Computational practicality: ≤ 1.7% parameter overhead, ~97% training throughput, no finetuning needed for either controller or suppression.
  • Engineer-friendly: editing reduces to a single matrix multiply over the flagged prototype set at inference time; retrieval of training neighborhoods is a separable offline audit step.

Limitations

  • Output-layer only — prototypes sit at the final layer; intermediate computation is still a black box ("deep PRISM" is future work).
  • No sequence/document-level attribution directly; left as a future extension.
  • NSFW grouping uses keyword matching on prototype vocabulary signatures — an acknowledged offline discovery heuristic, brittle to paraphrase or encoded harmful content.
  • Not strictly Pareto-superior: 0.8–2.1pp average gap and a −8.99pp BoolQ regression at 760M. Surpasses dense GPT only at 1.6B / 20B tokens.
  • Caching assumes stable support — fine for inference, fragile while prototypes are still being learned post-deployment.
  • Coupling not eliminated — λmax(M) is never zero, so the exact solver still contends with global coupling.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How well does the prototype structure transfer (or degrade) under continued pretraining on out-of-distribution corpora — code, math, multilingual? Does R̄₁ stay high?
  2. Can prototype controllers compose additively across tasks without interference, or do they require joint training?
  3. How does the framework behave under jailbreak-style prompts that evade keyword-based NSFW prototype discovery — is there a learned alternative?
  4. Will pretrained prototype banks and code be released so the 470× attribution speed-up and suppression behavior can be replicated?
  5. Could deep PRISM variants recover sparse feature circuits natively during training rather than as post-hoc SAEs?
  6. Can the curvature-localization theory be turned into a direct optimization objective on M's spectrum, beyond ridge-like clustering regularization?
  7. Does the O(Nk) cache admit streaming / incremental updates, enabling on-the-fly re-attribution during continual training?
  8. How does prototype suppression compare to refusal-style guardrails on adversarial safety benchmarks (e.g., WildGuard-style) rather than an LLM-judge NSFW score?
