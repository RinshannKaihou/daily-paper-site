BaseRT: best-in-class LLM inference on Apple Silicon via native Metal.

A C++ runtime built directly on Metal — no MLX, PyTorch or CoreML — shipping a data-driven architecture descriptor, a zero-allocation decode loop, hand-fused Metal kernels with inlined dequantisation, and a simdgroup FlashAttention path.

Why this matters

A reproducible on-device Apple Silicon benchmark against llama.cpp, MLX and uzu across Q4/Q8 — and a clear gap in quantisation quality reporting on a runtime that fuses dequant into the inner loop.

Critical edge

Self-reported, vendor-affiliated numbers with cherry-picked baselines; no perplexity / MMLU at Q4 or Q8; dense-model prefill is essentially tied; one outright 0.90× loss to MLX on Gemma-4-26B-A4B decode.

Methodology

HOW IT WORKS

C++ runtime targeting Metal directly — no MLX, PyTorch or CoreML in the stack. Hot path is GPU command dispatch plus lightweight CPU patching.

  • Data-driven architecture descriptors. Activation / norm / MoE / attention variants expressed as data, not control flow — adding LLaMA, Qwen3, Gemma, Whisper, BERT is a declarative change that leaves the loop untouched.
  • Zero-allocation decode loop. Residuals, attention scratch, FFN scratch, logits, token buffer and the full max-context KV cache are pre-allocated at load time — including a static thread-local error buffer.
  • Hardware-adaptive Metal kernel library. Matmul, attention, norm, RoPE, embedding, activation, sampling. GEMV (M=1) for decode, GEMM (M>1) for prefill, per-quantisation (2–16 bit), with dequant inlined in the inner loop so dequantised weights never materialise on global memory.
  • Per-chip config (M1–M5). Threadgroup sizes, kernel thresholds, fusion choices chosen from GPU core count and family.
  • Prefill path. simdgroup-matrix tiled GEMM with chunked prefill, plus a FlashAttention kernel using online softmax and O(n) memory.
  • Decode scheduling. Low-overhead command dispatch + amortised CPU–GPU sync across multiple generated tokens.

Cross-generation & head-to-head

M3 base · uzu
  • M3 base · tg128 vs llama.cpp: BaseRT leads all 8 dense configs by 1.13–1.34× (best Gemma-4-E2B Q8 at 1.34×); vs MLX, leads all 6 supported configs by 1.01–1.22×.
  • vs uzu (native Metal + MPSGraph): BaseRT faster on 5/6 decode configs (1.17–1.19× on Qwen3-0.6B), but uzu leads prefill by 4–13% on Qwen3-0.6B and 1–3% on Llama — attributed to uzu's MPSGraph → ANE dispatch.
  • Q4 vs Q8: BaseRT's relative advantage is larger at Q4 — smaller weights make per-token overheads a bigger share of the budget.
  • MoE decode narrows: large-model decode is memory-bandwidth-bound, so the gap shrinks to 1.04–1.07× vs llama.cpp.

Strengths  ↔  Limitations

VERDICT

Strengths

  • Targets a real, growing on-device LLM pain point with a credible open-source artifact.
  • Headline speedups (1.56× decode vs llama.cpp on Qwen3-0.6B; 1.81× MoE prefill) are non-trivial and reproducible in principle.
  • Four design principles are well-explained and tied to specific overheads; the architecture-descriptor idea is genuinely clean.
  • Honest about losses: dense prefill tied to MLX/llama.cpp, MoE decode tied, one Gemma MoE decode loss vs MLX.
  • Broad quant (Q2–FP16), broad arch coverage (LLaMA / Qwen3 / Gemma / Whisper / BERT), M1–M5 compatibility — lowers adoption friction.
  • Open-source release + pinned baselines + standard models make third-party verification cheap.

Limitations

  • Apple-only — no CUDA or Vulkan backend today; contribution locked to a single hardware vendor.
  • Single-device — no continuous batching, no parallel decoding, no tensor parallelism; unsuitable for serving claims.
  • No quality / accuracy analysis at Q4 or Q8 — no perplexity, no downstream benchmarks, fused-dequant drift uncharacterised.
  • No sustained-throughput or thermal analysis — burst numbers likely 30–50% above what a 30-minute session delivers.
  • No energy per token despite the "intelligence per watt" framing.
  • Dense-model prefill is tied to llama.cpp and MLX; "best-in-class" is selective to decode and MoE prefill.
  • Engineering artifact, not research — no analytical model predicting the fusion margin, no ablation isolating the four design principles.

Questions for further exploration

FOLLOW-UPS
Q1Is the 1.04–1.56× decode gap still real under llama.cpp's experimental Metal-Split, MLX speculative decoding, --no-mmap, and batch-size tuning — or has BaseRT already hit the wall?
Q2Can the data-driven architecture descriptors combine with MLX-style lazy execution or vllm-mlx-style batching to support continuous batching without breaking the zero-allocation decode loop?
Q3Does inlined dequantisation on the Q2/Q3 extreme paths introduce measurable accuracy drift? Systematically compare perplexity and MMLU/GSM8K/HumanEval for BaseRT vs llama.cpp on the same GGUF quantisations.
Q4Over a 30+ minute agentic workload, how much does thermal throttling cut throughput? The paper never touches this — burst numbers are not product numbers.
Q5Is a CUDA backend on the roadmap? Without one, the contribution stays an Apple-side artifact; with one, it competes in the general GPU-inference conversation.
Q6uzu's 4–13% prefill lead via MPSGraph → ANE suggests GPU + ANE hybrid may be the optimum Apple Silicon prefill strategy. Will BaseRT integrate ANE?
Q7Within a 24 GB unified-memory envelope, Qwen3-30B-A3B / Gemma-4-26B-A4B at Q8 don't fit — what's the practical best-precision recipe (Q4 + KV-cache quant + speculative draft)?
