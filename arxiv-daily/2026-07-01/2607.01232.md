> **一句话总结：** 该论文通过逐层单层训练实验，发现 RL 后训练带来的增益高度集中在 Transformer 中间少数几层（甚至单层），由此提出"层贡献（layer contribution）"度量，并据此设计层自适应学习率、层选择性训练与简单的"训练中间层"启发式策略，在 Qwen3 系列上稳定超越全参数 RL 训练基线。

> **TL;DR:** A systematic layer-wise ablation study of RL post-training across seven models (Qwen3/Qwen2.5/DeepSeek-distilled, 1.5B–8B), three RL algorithms (GRPO/GiGPO/Dr. GRPO), and three task domains (math/code/agentic) shows that RL gains are highly concentrated in a small subset of middle transformer layers — training a single layer can recover and sometimes surpass full-parameter RL gains (best layer reaches C ≥ 1.0 in all 7 models, up to 1.14). The authors introduce the "layer contribution" metric C(k) = (S_k − S_base)/(S_full − S_base), prove this pattern is stable across datasets (Spearman ρ = 0.76), tasks (ρ = 0.59), model families, and RL algorithms, and exploit it with three strategies: (1) boosting learning rate on top-k high-contribution layers, (2) freezing everything except those layers, and (3) a profiling-free heuristic that simply trains the middle layers. All three strategies outperform full-parameter GRPO on Qwen3-{1.7B,4B,8B} on NuminaMath-CoT (e.g., Only-B5 reaches 65.87 on 4B vs. 62.97 baseline; +2.68 on 8B). A majority-vote ensemble of 7 layer-specialized models reaches 33.6% on OlympiadBench, beating both the best single layer (28.3%) and full-parameter self-consistency voting (31.3%).

## 核心贡献

- **揭示 RL 增益的层间极端不均匀性**：通过对七个模型逐层独立训练（其余参数冻结），发现单个 Transformer 层即可恢复全参数 RL 训练增益的绝大部分，最佳单层在所有模型上均达到 C ≥ 1.0（最高 1.14）。
- **提出"层贡献"度量并刻画稳定结构**：定义 C(k) = (S_k − S_base) / (S_full − S_base)，证明高贡献层稳定地集中在网络中间（40%–60% 深度），输入端与输出端贡献显著偏低；该排序在不同数据集（ρ = 0.76）、不同任务（ρ = 0.59）、不同模型族、不同 RL 算法之间均高度相关。
- **提出三种层感知训练策略并验证其有效性**：① 高贡献层学习率增强（Boost B_k）；② 只训练贡献最高的 k 层（Only B_k）；③ 不需任何 profiling 的"训练中间层"启发式。三者在 Qwen3 三个规模上均稳定优于全参数 RL 基线。
- **证明层间多样性可带来额外投票增益**：Qwen3-1.7B 上 7 个高贡献层专精模型在 OlympiadBench 上的两两 Jaccard 平均仅 34.1%，但多数投票取得 33.6%，超过单层最佳（28.3%）和全参数自一致性投票（31.3%）。
- **澄清"权重变化幅度 ≠ 层贡献"**：全参数训练下各层权重变化幅度近似均匀（0.5–0.8），而单层训练时高/低贡献层的权重变化幅度相近但表现差异巨大，说明层贡献反映的是参数子空间对 RL 信号的"有效性"而非更新量本身。

## Key Contributions

- **Dramatic layer-wise concentration of RL gains.** Single-layer GRPO training recovers most of the gain of full-parameter RL and sometimes exceeds it (best-layer C ∈ [1.01, 1.14] across 7 models).
- **A new metric, layer contribution C(k).** Quantifies the fraction of full RL improvement recovered by training layer k in isolation; layers are comparable across datasets, tasks, families, and algorithms.
- **Middle-layer concentration is structural.** Across Qwen3-1.7B/4B/8B, Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B, Qwen2.5-1.5B/3B-Instruct, and DeepSeek-Distilled-Qwen-7B, the contribution profile consistently peaks at 40%–60% network depth.
- **Cross-setting stability of layer rankings.** Spearman ρ = 0.76 between NuminaMath-CoT and DeepScaleR (math-vs-math), ρ = 0.59 between NuminaMath-CoT (math) and DeepCoder (code) — both on Qwen3-1.7B.
- **Layer-aware RL strategies.** Boost-B_k (LR up on top layers), Only-B_k (freeze everything else), and the profiling-free "middle-5" heuristic all beat full-parameter RL on NuminaMath-CoT.
- **Complementary behaviors across layers.** Top-7 layer-trained Qwen3-1.7B models on OlympiadBench share only 34.1% of newly-solved problems; majority voting reaches 33.6% vs. 31.3% for self-consistency voting over the full-parameter model.
- **Weight-change magnitude does not explain contribution.** Per-layer ‖Δθ_k‖₂ is roughly uniform under full training, and similar across high/low-contribution layers under single-layer training — contribution reflects subspace effectiveness, not update size.

## Methodology

**Single-layer RL training protocol.** For an L-layer LLM with parameters θ = {θ_emb, θ_0, …, θ_{L−1}, θ_head}, standard full-parameter GRPO computes ∇_θ L_GRPO(θ). In single-layer training, only θ_k is updated; the gradient is still back-propagated through the full network, but `requires_grad=False` is set on all other parameters. The procedure is run independently for every k ∈ {0, …, L−1}.

**Layer contribution metric.**
C(k) = (S_k − S_base) / (S_full − S_base),
where S_k = in-domain score after training only layer k, S_base = pretrained model, S_full = full-parameter GRPO. C(k) = 1.0 means single-layer matches full; > 1.0 means it surpasses; ≈ 0 means the layer cannot absorb RL improvement in isolation.

**RL setup.** GRPO with group-relative advantage (no learned value network), clipped surrogate objective. Group size G, clip ε, KL coefficient kept identical between full and single-layer runs. Full-parameter learning rate is tuned per model; the same LR is then used for all single-layer runs (plus an LR ablation in Appendix A.7 that confirms rankings are LR-robust).

**Models studied (Table 1).** 7 models total:
- Qwen3-1.7B/4B/8B-Base — full layer scan, GRPO, NuminaMath-CoT (math)
- Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B — full layer scan, Dr. GRPO, MATH (math)
- Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct & 3B-Instruct — partial layer scan (subset due to cost), GiGPO, ALFWorld (agentic)
- DeepSeek-Distilled-Qwen-7B — partial layer scan, GRPO, Skywork (math)

**Evaluation.** 12 benchmarks across 4 categories for Qwen3: math in-domain (MATH500, GSM8K, OlympiadBench, AMC), and OOD categories code (HumanEval+, MBPP, LiveCodeBench), reasoning (GPQA-Diamond, MMLU-Pro), language (C-Eval, IFEval, MGSM). Math-only 6-benchmark protocol for Qwen2.5-Math and DeepSeek-distilled. ALFWorld 6 sub-tasks for agentic models. All final numbers in §4 are mean ± std over 3 independent runs.

**Layer-aware strategies (§4).**
1. *Layer-adaptive learning rate:* pick top-k layers by C(k), set LR = 1e-5 there while keeping 5e-6 elsewhere (controls: bottom-k layers).
2. *Layer-selective training:* train only the top-k layers, freeze all others.
3. *Middle-layer heuristic:* for a model with L layers, train layers ⌊L/2 − k/2⌋ … ⌊L/2 + k/2⌋ — no profiling required.

**Diversity analysis (§5.1).** For each of the top-7 high-contribution layers on Qwen3-1.7B, collect the set of OlympiadBench problems solved beyond the base model; pairwise Jaccard similarity J = |A ∩ B| / |A ∪ B|. Majority voting across the 7 layer-trained models is compared to self-consistency–style voting over 7 samples from the full-parameter model.

**Weight-change analysis (§5.2).** Compute L2 norm ‖Δθ_k‖₂ of trained-vs-base parameters per layer, separately under full-parameter training and under single-layer training.

## Results & Highlights

**Per-model layer-contribution peaks (Table 7).**

| Model | Algorithm | Task | Best C | Worst C | # layers ≥ 1.0 | Middle concentration |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Qwen3-1.7B-Base | GRPO | Math | 1.14 | 0.28 | 5/28 | ✓ |
| Qwen3-4B-Base | GRPO | Math | 1.06 | 0.66 | 4/36 | ✓ |
| Qwen3-8B-Base | GRPO | Math | 1.07 | −0.51 | 4/36 | ✓ |
| Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B | Dr. GRPO | Math | 1.01 | 0.42 | 2/28 | ✓ |
| Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct | GiGPO | Agentic | 1.02 | 0.25 | 1/8 (partial) | ✓ |
| Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct | GiGPO | Agentic | 1.01 | 0.17 | 1/11 (partial) | ✓ |
| DeepSeek-Distilled-Qwen-7B | GRPO | Math | 1.05 | 0.33 | 2/8 (partial) | ✓ |

Note Layer 0 on Qwen3-8B achieves C = −0.51 — training the first layer in isolation actively *degrades* math performance below the pretrained baseline.

**Cross-dataset and cross-task ranking stability (Qwen3-1.7B, Figure 3).** NuminaMath-CoT vs. DeepScaleR (math): Spearman ρ = 0.76, p < 0.001. NuminaMath-CoT (math) vs. DeepCoder (code): Spearman ρ = 0.59, p < 0.001.

**Math-vs-OOD capability correlation.** For each Qwen3 model, Pearson r between in-domain math contribution C_math(k) and overall contribution C_all(k) (Math + Code + Reasoning + Language) is r > 0.6 on all three scales — high-contribution layers generalize, not overfit.

**Layer-aware training on Qwen3 (Figure 7, mean ± std, 3 runs).**

| Model | Full (baseline) | Boost B_10 | Only B_5 | Only B_10 | Mid-5 heuristic | Δ vs Full |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Qwen3-1.7B | 50.82 ± 0.40 | 53.70 ± 0.40 | 51.53 ± 0.24 | 51.39 ± 0.46 | 51.35 ± 0.28 | +0.53 (heuristic) |
| Qwen3-4B | 62.97 ± 0.78 | 64.42 ± 0.13 | 65.87 ± 0.70 | — | 64.80 ± 0.30 | +1.83 (heuristic) |
| Qwen3-8B | 66.43 ± 0.40 | 67.42 ± 0.40 | 68.97 ± 0.31 | 69.11 ± 0.10 | 68.19 ± 0.62 | +1.76 (heuristic) |

Best single-layer on Qwen3-8B (Only-B_10): 69.11% math average — a +2.68 gain over full-parameter RL, capturing 32% of the total RL improvement.

**Diversity and voting (Qwen3-1.7B, Figure 8).** Top-7 layer-trained models on OlympiadBench: average pairwise Jaccard 34.1%. Majority voting across the 7 models: 33.6 ± 0.91%, vs. best single layer 28.3 ± 0.25%, vs. full RL 26.9 ± 0.40%, vs. full-RL self-consistency voting (7 samples) 31.3 ± 1.11%.

**Weight-change dissociation (Figure 9).** Under full-parameter training, ‖Δθ_k‖₂ is in [0.5, 0.8] across layers — roughly uniform. Under single-layer training, the trained layer's ‖Δθ_k‖₂ ≈ 0.8–1.0, again similar across high/low-contribution layers — yet performance outcomes differ dramatically.

**Control experiments.** Boosting the *worst* k layers (Boost W_k) consistently degrades performance (Figure 7, red bars); training only the worst k layers (Only W_k) is the lowest result across all scales (e.g., 46.87 on 1.7B vs. 50.82 baseline).

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty.** High. The layer-wise ablation framing for RL post-training is fresh: prior work (LISA, MISA, AdaGradSelect, "layer importance" literature) targets supervised fine-tuning; SFT-vs-RL structural differences are known (Nepal et al. 2025) but the *quantitative* concentration pattern in RL — single layer matching/surpassing full training, with a stable middle-layer peak — is new and well-documented.

**Rigor.** Strong. The fair-comparison protocol is explicit (tune LR for full baseline, use same LR for single-layer, identical batch/clip/KL/steps; LR ablation in Appendix A.7 confirms rankings are LR-robust). Multiple seeds and mean ± std are reported. Cross-dataset and cross-task Spearman correlations are reported with significance. The author also pre-registers the concern that "low-contribution layers might just need a larger LR" and addresses it. One mild gap: only 4 of 7 models received a *full* layer scan; the agentic and distilled models use a subset due to cost — so the "single layer suffices" claim is fully demonstrated only for the Qwen3 + math + GRPO combination. Still, the qualitative pattern is consistent across all 7.

**Reproducibility.** Moderate. Standard open models (Qwen3/Qwen2.5/DeepSeek-distilled), public datasets (NuminaMath-CoT, DeepScaleR, DeepCoder, MATH, ALFWorld, Skywork), and well-known RL algorithms (GRPO family, GiGPO). No code release mentioned in the preprint text I read; the GRPO/Dr.GRPO/GiGPO training recipes are referenced and presumably the authors' pipeline will be open-sourced. Some hyperparameter tables are deferred to Appendix A. Single-layer training of a 28–36 layer LLM with GRPO is computationally heavy but tractable on a few A100/H100 GPUs.

**Significance.** High. The result reframes a foundational assumption of RL post-training ("all layers benefit uniformly") and immediately yields a practical recipe: train only middle layers, or boost their LR. Memory and compute savings (likely 50–80% reduction in trainable parameters and optimizer state) are substantial. The diversity / voting result is more of an analysis tool than a deployable system, but it adds a clean structural perspective consistent with Gan & Isola's "Neural Thickets" line of work.

**Red flags / concerns.**
- Layer-0 with C = −0.51 on Qwen3-8B is striking — a layer that *hurts* the model when trained alone. The authors discuss it but do not fully explain why it occurs; it could indicate destructive interference with deeper layers that is masked under full training.
- The paper notes that no LR was tuned *for* single-layer training (only for full). This is a deliberate fair-comparison choice, but it means the headline "C(k) = 1.14" may slightly understate single-layer capacity — the authors acknowledge this and run an ablation showing LR doesn't reorder rankings.
- The "majority voting" result on Qwen3-1.7B uses 7 independently trained models; this is genuinely expensive and the authors correctly describe it as an analysis tool, not a practical training method. Don't read this as "you should train 7 models and vote."
- Whether the middle-layer heuristic transfers to much larger models (>70B) or to non-Qwen architectures (Llama, Mistral) is not tested. Given the strong cross-family evidence within Qwen, I'd expect similar structure, but it is an open empirical question.
- The "agentic" verification is limited to ALFWorld; whether the pattern holds in longer-horizon WebArena / SWE-Bench-style environments is unknown.

## Strengths

- **Breadth of evidence.** 7 models, 3 RL algorithms, 3 task domains, full layer scans on 3 models — one of the most thorough layer-wise ablations in the RL post-training literature.
- **Clean operationalization.** Layer contribution C(k) is a single scalar with a clear interpretation; rankings are simple to compute and transfer across settings.
- **Practical strategies.** The middle-layer heuristic (train only middle layers, no profiling) is essentially free to try and consistently improves over full-parameter RL in the experiments shown.
- **Dissociation evidence.** Explicitly showing that ‖Δθ_k‖₂ does not explain C(k) prevents the most obvious alternative explanation and makes the contribution finding more interesting.
- **Cross-task generalization.** The diversity/voting analysis connects the empirical finding to the broader "diverse experts near pretrained weights" line (Gan & Isola 2026).

## Limitations

- **Only math-heavy verification for the strategies.** Section 4 strategies (Boost B_k, Only B_k, Mid-5) are validated only on NuminaMath-CoT for Qwen3-{1.7B,4B,8B}; extension to code and agentic tasks is left for future work.
- **Partial layer scans on 4 of 7 models.** The agentic and distilled models use subset scans (e.g., layers {0,4,8,12,14,16,20,24}), which still locate the middle peak but cannot exclude finer structure.
- **No theoretical explanation.** Why middle layers specifically capture RL improvement is left as an open question; the paper is empirical.
- **Computational cost of layer-contribution profiling.** The "best" strategies require 28–36 full GRPO training runs to compute C(k) once per model — the heuristic trades profiling for slightly less gain.
- **Hyperparameter tuning asymmetry.** Full-parameter LR is tuned; single-layer LR is not. Appendix A.7 shows this doesn't reorder rankings, but absolute C(k) values may be slightly understated.
- **Architecture coverage.** All studied models are Qwen-family (Qwen2.5/Qwen3/DeepSeek-distilled-on-Qwen). Llama, Mistral, etc., are not tested.
- **Voting cost.** The 33.6% majority-vote result requires 7 × full single-layer GRPO runs at inference time — not a deployment recipe.

## Relevance

该论文与本项目关注的 **LLM 训练稳定性** 和 **Hidden States & Interpretability** 两个方向直接相关：它把 RL 后训练过程中梯度在层间的分配拆解成可量化的"层贡献"，揭示了训练过程中参数更新的层间不均匀结构（中间层集中、权重变化幅度与贡献解耦），并给出能直接落地的层选择性训练策略。这对理解 RL 微调对模型内部表征的扰动机制，以及设计更稳定的层感知训练方案都很有价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the middle-layer concentration pattern persist at 70B+ scale, and in non-Qwen architectures (Llama-3, Mistral, Gemma)? The current evidence is bounded above by 8B parameters and almost entirely within the Qwen family.
- Why are middle layers disproportionately effective for RL? Is it because they sit at the network's "feature abstraction" sweet spot where reward gradients are most informative, or because early layers are dominated by pretrained lexical/syntactic features that are brittle to perturb, and late layers are dominated by output-projection constraints?
- Layer 0 on Qwen3-8B achieves C = −0.51 — it actively *degrades* math when trained alone. Under full training this degradation is masked. What is the mechanism, and can it predict which layers should be frozen a priori?
- Does the layer-aware recipe transfer to DPO / PPO / RLOO / RLHF with non-verifiable rewards (preference learning)? RLVR has clean binary rewards; soft reward gradients may distribute differently across layers.
- The 34.1% pairwise Jaccard among high-contribution layer-specialists is striking. Can we train a *single* model that learns to route its computation through different middle layers at inference (MoE-style) and capture the diversity without 7× inference cost?
- The middle-5 heuristic beats full-parameter RL on math — but is the gap robust across seeds, RL steps, and evaluation templates (e.g., greedy vs. sampling, temperature)? A larger ablation would harden the practical claim.
- Does layer contribution correlate with interpretability metrics — e.g., do middle layers also exhibit the largest hidden-state shifts under RL probes, or with the largest activation entropy changes? This would directly connect the paper to the user's "Hidden States & Interpretability" interest.
