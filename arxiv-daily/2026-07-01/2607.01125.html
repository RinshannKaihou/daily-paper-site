ZO-Act: Efficient Zeroth-Order Fine-Tuning via One-Shot Activation-Informed Low-Rank Subspaces

Freeze an activation-derived rank‑r basis once; perturb only the small r×n coefficient matrix — ZO variance and finite‑difference noise scale with k³ instead of d³.

Why this matters

The first ZO method shown to fine‑tune an INT4 Llama‑3‑8B (5.8 GB) with no retuning — a concrete path for memory‑bounded, backprop‑infeasible inference‑side adaptation where quantized deployment is mandatory.

Critical edge

Still loses to first‑order Adam by ~2–5 points on hard tasks; the one‑shot activation basis is fixed at calibration time, so heavy‑tailed or out‑of‑distribution activations (long context, code, multimodal) can break the bias bound.

01Methodology

For each adapted linear layer with weight W ∈ ℝm×n and input activations X ∈ ℝb×m, take a thin SVD X = UDV⊤. Because the gradient gW = X⊤gY lives approximately in col(Vr), ZO‑Act sets ΔW = VrB with B ∈ ℝr×n trainable and freezes W and Vr.

Alg. 1 — Subspace init (one-shot): one forward pass over a calibration batch Bcal; compute top‑r right singular vectors Vℓ,r of each layer's inputs; freeze Wℓ and Vℓ,r; init Bℓ ← 0.

Alg. 2 — Forward-only update: at step t with mini‑batch Bt, (1) evaluate L with effective weight W + VrB; (2) for j = 1…q, sample Z(j) ∈ ℝr×n, evaluate L(j,+) with Vr(B + μZ(j)), form a(j) = (L(j,+) − L)/μ; (3) accumulate ĝB ← ĝB + a(j)Z(j), then divide by q; (4) run Adam on B with ĝB.

The forward rewrite Y = XW + (X Vr)(B + μZ) shows that perturbations outside col(Vr) are damped by X's small singular values, so the dominant directions are sampled efficiently. With Gaussian one‑sided estimators on a k‑dim space, E‖a − g‖² scales as (k+1)‖g‖², and q‑query averaging drives variance to k/q; finite‑difference bias scales O(LF²μ²k³) instead of O(LF²μ²d³).

Theorem 1 (informal): ZO‑Act gradient norm converges with variance term O(√(LF²/(kqT) · (φ(β₀) − φ*))) plus a bias bounded by the residual of ∇F projected onto col(U)⊥ — small whenever the activation spectrum decays quickly.

03Strengths vs. Limitations

Strengths

  • Clean conceptual framing: ZO fine‑tuning becomes plain coefficient‑matrix optimization, so Adam just plugs in.
  • Theory matches practice: variance ∝ k/q and finite‑difference ∝ μ²k³ predict the rank‑ablation curve correctly.
  • Practical: ties LOZO/SubZero/AGZO on memory (16.1 GB), beats them on wall‑clock (42.0 min), runs on INT4 (5.8 GB), only +1.5 s of one‑shot preprocessing.
  • Broad benchmark coverage: Llama‑3‑8B & OPT‑13B × SuperGLUE + 8 commonsense tasks × {FP32, INT4}.
  • Strong diagnostics: ‖V0⊤Vt‖F and gradient‑energy‑in‑subspace measurements directly justify the design.
  • Closes gap to Adam: Llama‑3‑8B RTE 87.0 (ZO‑Act) vs. 92.0 (Adam) vs. 74.4 (MeZO).

Limitations

  • Still trails backprop Adam by 2–5 pts on hard SuperGLUE tasks.
  • Multiple forward passes per step — wall‑clock per update higher than backprop; only amortized memory sells it.
  • Frozen activation basis computed once on a calibration batch: heavy‑tailed activations (long context, code, multimodal) may invalidate the bias bound.
  • Limited scale: only 8B & 13B tested; ≥ 70B behavior unknown.
  • Limited rank sweep: r ∈ {1, 32, 128, 256}; full sweep on more architectures missing.
  • Single quantization path: MagR w4 only; INT8 / GPTQ variants not tested.
  • No code link visible in the extracted text; reproduction depends on faithful baseline re‑implementations.
  • No formal spectral‑condition theorem: the bias bound rests on an empirical spectrum‑decay observation.
  • Loses to LoRA on most tasks when backprop is available.

04Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How does ZO‑Act scale to ≥ 70B models and to long‑context tasks where activation spectra are less obviously low‑rank?
  2. Can the one‑shot activation basis be replaced by a cheap online update (slow‑moving SVD) without losing the variance‑reduction guarantee?
  3. How sensitive is ZO‑Act to the calibration batch distribution — does a small randomly‑drawn sample reliably cover downstream activation directions?
  4. Is the r = 1 advantage robust under per‑rank μ tuning, or is it an artifact of a fixed μ that disproportionately hurts higher ranks?
  5. Does ZO‑Act compose cleanly with DoRA / GaLore, or with adapters that target distinct module families (FFN vs. attention)?
  6. For INT4 / INT8 backbones, how does ZO‑Act interact with QLoRA‑style quantization‑aware fine‑tuning, and can quantization noise be further reduced by adapting scales / zeros?
  7. Variance scales as k/q but bias depends on col(U)'s coverage of ∇F — can a unified convergence rate make (μ, q, r) jointly derivable from a cheap per‑layer spectral estimate?
  8. Is ZO‑Act compatible with non‑Gaussian perturbation distributions (Rademacher, structured sparsified) that the literature suggests reduce variance for specific architectures?
  9. Could the subspace be defined over output activations or a learnable basis, and would that close more of the gap to first‑order Adam than input‑activation basis alone?
