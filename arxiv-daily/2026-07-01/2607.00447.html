Understanding Why Language Models Hallucinate:
Testing Reasoning Against Priors

Yangfan Hu, Xuhan Tong, Haoyue Bai, Xi Ding, Shashank Muralidhar Bharadwaj, Siyang Cao, Robert Nowak, Jiawei Zhang  ·  University of Wisconsin–Madison
hallucination inference misalignment latent shortcut reasoning vs. priors TrapQA diagnostic benchmark
⚑ Key Finding

Hallucinations are inference misalignment: the model knows the facts, but a statistically dominant shortcut hijacks the reasoning path the prompt was meant to constrain.

Reframes LLM hallucination as a deployment failure, not a storage failure — opening it to formal analysis via a latent key–task posterior.

▸ Why This Matters

If hallucination is a path-selection failure rather than a knowledge gap, mitigation moves from more data toward decoding-time path reweighting and shortcut-aware fine-tuning.

Latent key–task formalization lets the long-running "knowledge vs. reasoning" split be diagnosed per-item via paired probes.
▲ Critical Edge

The framework is diagnostic, not prescriptive: Theorems 3.4 / 3.6 bound the aggregate error rate but never predict which trap item will fail, and no decoding intervention is proposed.

Plus: only four provider-hosted frontier models (no open weights), and probe-independence is asserted but not paraphrased-tested.

Methodology Latent key–task model · TrapQA · Eight model×thinking runs

Inference is modeled as a two-stage posterior over latent keys (k) and tasks (t):

P(y | z) = Σk∈K, t∈T P(k, t | z) · P(y | z ; k, t)

Three structural assumptions (activated-key restriction 3.1, activated-task restriction 3.2, output separation 3.3) collapse the shortcut-vs-correct posterior ratio into a prior × likelihood product. Theorem 3.4 shows the shortcut posterior can dominate whenever the shortcut pair is more frequent in pretraining; Theorem 3.6 turns that dominance into a non-vanishing TV-loss lower bound.

The two multiplicative terms of the dominance ratio map onto two testable failure modes:

Key-selection bias

Wrong salient cue dominates (e.g., "walk 50 m" hijacks "drive to a car wash"). Diagnosed by Real-Life Constrained QA.

Task-retrieval bias

Right cue, wrong relation (e.g., Einstein vs. Fermi under "did not formulate special relativity"). Diagnosed by ScientistQA.

TrapQA construction. ScientistQA (2,925): Wikipedia-linked scientist pairs → text-embedding-3-small similarity → Gemini biographical sketch + decisive constraint → GPT filter. Real-Life Constrained QA (500): SWOW salience cues → 8 template families → GPT instantiation → Claude filter. Eight configurations: GPT 5.2 (low/high thinking), Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview (low/high), Claude Sonnet 4.6 (low/high), DeepSeek V3.2 Chat / Reasoner. Web tools disabled in all runs.

Probe-conditioned analysis (Table 2). When the model answers both decisive probes correctly in isolation, hallucination on the trap item is still high — Claude-low 60.66%, GPT-low 57.56%, DeepSeek-low 55.10%, Gemini-low 78.08%. When both probes fail, hallucination stays ≤ 5.50% across all settings. Factual ignorance accounts for ≤ ~5.5% of errors.

Real-Life Constrained QA (n = 500). Errors: Claude 16.20%, GPT 8.80%, Gemini 3.60%, DeepSeek-chat 36.40% — a 10× spread that hints at either strong prompt-sensitivity or model-specific instruction-following weaknesses rather than a uniform inference-misalignment phenomenon.

Strengths vs. Limitations Where the theory earns its keep · and where it stops short

Strengths

  • Clean formal split between knowledge storage and inference-path deployment, anchored by Theorems 3.4 & 3.6.
  • Probe-conditioned design — a single item simultaneously answers "does the model know?" and "can it deploy what it knows?"
  • Two complementary benchmarks stress key-selection (everyday action) and task-retrieval (entity disambiguation) separately.
  • Anti-fame control directly rules out the obvious "pick the more famous entity" confound — the shortcut is relation-specific.
  • Cross-vendor coverage: four frontier families × two thinking modes; qualitative pattern holds.
  • Senior author Nowak lends credibility to the formal guarantees and proofs (Appendix K).

Limitations

  • Diagnostic only: framework bounds the aggregate error rate, never predicts which trap items will fail.
  • No proposed intervention: reweighting, constrained decoding, or anti-shortcut fine-tuning are gestured at, never evaluated.
  • Closed model set: GPT 5.2, Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview, Claude Sonnet 4.6, DeepSeek V3.2 — no open weights, and provider-hosted drift threatens replay.
  • Closed-domain diagnosis: short-form closed-book QA only — long-form and compositional hallucinations remain untested.
  • Assumption 3.1 is strong: "pretraining-independent" regime is identified post hoc — success on within-context disambiguation is defined as a violation.
  • Probe independence assumed, not stress-tested with paraphrased or interleaved probes.
  • 10× spread on Real-Life Constrained QA (Gemini 3.60% vs. DeepSeek-chat 36.40%) suggests prompt- or model-specific effects, not a uniform phenomenon.

Questions for Further Exploration Where the framework wants to go next

  1. Can the key-selection vs. task-retrieval split be identified inside the model via hidden-state probes, attention-pattern analysis, or causal head-ablation — not just inferred behaviorally?
  2. How does shortcut-path dominance scale with model size, RLHF/fine-tuning regime, and chain-of-thought prompting? Gemini's 2.50% names-only hints at architectural or pipeline advantages worth isolating.
  3. Does decoding-time intervention (contrastive reweighting against the shortcut prior, retrieved-context forcing, self-consistency) suppress key-selection bias — or just shift the shortcut to another layer?
  4. Can TrapQA be extended to multilingual, multimodal, or long-form generation to test whether inference misalignment is cross-modal or specific to short closed-book QA?
  5. Is the correlation between pretraining entity / relation frequency and observed task-retrieval bias strong enough to predict per-item failures, validating Theorem 3.4 below the aggregate level?
  6. Can the latent key–task framework be connected to retrieval-augmented generation or tool-use as a mechanism that re-routes the constraint-sensitive path, and does RAG actually suppress shortcut dominance on TrapQA items?
  7. Can the within-context disambiguation regime (Assumption 3.1 violation) be induced deliberately via in-context examples pairing each candidate with its decisive constraint — and does it generalize or only memorize?
  8. How does inference-time scaling (more reasoning tokens, verifier reranking, self-refinement) interact with shortcut dominance? Gemini's high-thinking slightly worse than low-thinking deserves mechanistic investigation.
