> **一句话总结：** 提出 ZO-Act，一种一次性基于输入激活构建固定低秩子空间、用前向差分扰动小型系数矩阵的前向零阶微调方法，在 Llama-3-8B / OPT-13B / INT4 Llama-3-8B 上以更少显存稳定优于 MeZO、HiZOO、LOZO、SubZero、AGZO、ZO-Muon 等强基线。

> **TL;DR:** ZO-Act turns zeroth-order LLM fine-tuning into an explicit optimization over small coefficient matrices. For each linear layer it freezes a rank-r basis computed *once* from input activations on a calibration batch, then perturbs only the r×n coefficients via q-query Gaussian forward differences and updates them with Adam. The authors prove (Theorem 1) that perturbing the k-dimensional coefficients instead of the d-dimensional weights reduces both the variance term and the finite-difference error of the ZO estimator, at the cost of a controlled subspace-approximation bias that the low-rank structure of LLM activations/gradients empirically mitigates. On SuperGLUE and commonsense reasoning the method matches ZO-Muon's parameters but consistently dominates it (e.g., RTE 81.2→87.0, CB 69.6→89.3 on Llama-3-8B; commonsense average 63.1→70.8), runs at the lowest wall-clock time (42.0 min vs. 47.4 for AGZO) with no extra memory, and is the only method shown to also fine-tune an INT4 Llama-3-8B (5.8 GB) without retuning. Suitable for readers who care about memory-bounded or quantized LLM adaptation where backprop is infeasible.

## 核心贡献

- **激活知情的一次性零阶微调框架 ZO-Act**：对每个适配的线性层，在一小批校准数据上一次性计算输入激活矩阵的前 r 个右奇异向量作为冻结基，将"全权重扰动"改写为对 r×n 系数矩阵 B 的显式优化，等效扰动维度从 mn 降到 rn（r≪m），从而直接与 Adam 等动量优化器兼容。
- **理论分析**：把 ZO-Act 视为零阶优化在受限系数空间上的问题（F(θ₀+Uβ)），并形式化"低方差 vs. 子空间近似偏差"权衡——证明把扰动维度从 d 降到 k 同时降低了方差收敛项和有限差分误差项（O(L²_F μ² k³) 而非 O(L²_F μ² d³)），而近似偏差由激活与梯度的低秩结构所抑制。
- **完整实验验证**：在 Llama-3-8B、OPT-13B、INT4 Llama-3-8B（5.8 GB 显存）上对 SuperGLUE（6 任务）和 8 项常识推理任务均稳定优于 MeZO、S-MeZO、HiZOO、LOZO、SubZero、Subspace-MeZO、AGZO、ZO-Muon，并在 RTE/CB 等任务上把与全精度 Adam 的差距收窄到 2-4 个百分点。
- **量化友好**：因为 W 与 V_r 都冻结，只有低比特 W 不被改动，系数 B 自然兼容 INT4 backbone，作者展示了这是该方法独有的设定。
- **稳定性诊断**：训练中重新计算子空间 V_{ℓ,t}，并度量 ‖V_{ℓ,0}^⊤ V_{ℓ,t}‖_F 和 ‖V_{ℓ,0}^⊤ G_{ℓ,t}‖_F/‖G_{ℓ,t}‖_F，证实 r=1 即可捕捉到大部分梯度能量，子空间跨 epoch 高度稳定。

## Key Contributions

- **A one-shot, activation-informed ZO parameterization.** ZO-Act samples perturbations in a *frozen, data-informed* subspace instead of perturbing full weights or randomly chosen directions. For each linear layer it computes the top-r right singular vectors of input activations on a single calibration pass, parameterizes the update as ΔW = V_r B with B ∈ ℝ^{r×n} trainable, and keeps W and V_r fixed throughout training.
- **Variance–bias characterization.** Theorem 1 (informal) bounds the expected gradient norm of ZO-Act iterates by O(√((L_F/kqT)·(φ(β₀)−φ*))) + O(L²_F μ² k³), where k = Σ r·n_ℓ ≪ d. Compared to full-weight ZO, both the variance-dependent convergence term and the finite-difference noise scale with the *much* smaller coefficient dimension k. The bias is the projected residual onto the complement of col(U); the bound shows it is small precisely when the activation spectrum decays quickly (the empirical hypothesis that Llama/OPT gradients lie in low-rank activation subspaces is verified in §5.3).
- **Empirically superior ZO fine-tuning.** Across two model families (Llama-3-8B, OPT-13B), SuperGLUE and eight commonsense reasoning tasks, ZO-Act with Adam achieves the best full-precision ZO result on most tasks (e.g., Llama-3-8B RTE 81.2→87.0, CB 69.6→89.3 vs. ZO-Muon; commonsense average 63.1→70.8 on Llama-3-8B). Works on INT4 Llama-3-8B out of the box and still beats several full-precision ZO baselines.
- **Memory and runtime parity.** ZO-Act uses 16.1 GB and 42.0 min on Llama-3-8B/RTE under matched forward-pass budget, tying LOZO/SubZero/AGZO on memory and beating all baselines on wall-clock. INT4 case drops to 5.8 GB.

## Methodology

**Setup.** Each adapted linear layer has weight W ∈ ℝ^{m×n} with input activation X ∈ ℝ^{b×m}. Let X = U D V^⊤ be the thin SVD. The weight gradient g_W = X^⊤ g_Y = V D U^⊤ g_Y. With rapidly decaying singular values, g_W ≈ V_r D_r U_r^⊤ g_Y lives (approximately) in col(V_r). ZO-Act takes this intuition literally: it sets ΔW = V_r B, B ∈ ℝ^{r×n}, and keeps V_r frozen.

**Subspace initialization (Alg. 1, one-shot).** Run a single forward pass over a calibration batch B_cal; for each linear layer ℓ, compute the top-r right singular vectors V_{ℓ,r} of its input activations; freeze W_ℓ and V_{ℓ,r}; initialize B_ℓ ← 0.

**Forward-only update (Alg. 2).** At step t with mini-batch B_t:
1. Evaluate the unperturbed loss L with effective weight W_eff = W + V_r B.
2. For j = 1…q, sample Z^(j)_ℓ ∼ N(0, I) ∈ ℝ^{r×n} for every adapted layer, evaluate L^(j,+) with V_r(B + μ Z^(j)), and form a^(j) = (L^(j,+) − L)/μ.
3. Accumulate ĝ_{B_ℓ} ← ĝ_{B_ℓ} + a^(j) Z^(j)_ℓ, then divide by q.
4. Apply a first-order optimizer (Adam in main experiments) to B using ĝ_B.

The transformed forward pass Y+ = XW + (X V_r)(B + μZ) shows that perturbations *outside* col(V_r) are suppressed by the small singular values of X, so the dominant directions are sampled efficiently.

**Variance reduction.** With one-sided Gaussian estimator on a k-dim coefficient space, E‖a_t − g_t‖² = (k+1)‖g_t‖², and the q-query average gives variance scaling as k/q. The finite-difference bias scales as O(L²_F μ² k³) instead of O(L²_F μ² d³).

**Implicit Adam via explicit variables.** Because B is an explicit small matrix, the same momentum-based optimizers used in first-order training become available; an SGD ablation drops performance materially (e.g., OPT-13B RTE 74.0→64.6, CB 75.0→71.4).

**Quantized fine-tuning.** W_ℓ is kept in its low-bit form (INT4 via MagR); only V_r and B are stored in floating point. The forward pass uses dequantized W on the fly, so no gradient flows into W and quantization noise is preserved (rather than re-introduced).

## Results & Highlights

**SuperGLUE (Table 1, accuracy / F1).** Llama-3-8B, ZO-Act Full Adam: SST-2 **94.6**, RTE **87.0**, CB **89.3**, BoolQ **85.1**, WiC **69.1**, SQuAD 89.1. Versus ZO-Muon's 94.3 / 81.2 / 69.6 / 82.9 / 65.2 / 88.2, the gain is largest on RTE (+5.8) and CB (+19.7). OPT-13B: SST-2 **94.0**, RTE **74.0**, CB **75.0**, BoolQ **73.1**, WiC 59.4, SQuAD 85.1; wins on 4/6 full-precision ZO comparisons. INT4 Llama-3-8B: 93.5 / 83.8 / 82.1 / 83.8 / 60.7 / 88.2 — comparable to many full-precision ZO baselines despite 4-bit weights.

**Commonsense reasoning (Table 2, Llama-3-8B).** ZO-Act average **70.8**, vs. AGZO 68.2, SubZero 63.9, HiZOO 64.1, ZO-Muon 63.1, LOZO 57.6. Wins 7/8 sub-tasks (HellaSwag +12.8 over ZO-Muon, ARC-c +7.3). INT4 ZO-Act reaches 65.7 average — still beats AGZO's full-precision 68.2? No: 65.7 < 68.2, but it beats LOZO by a wide margin and approaches ZO-Muon.

**Subspace diagnostics (§5.3, Fig. 1).** For r=1, ‖V_{ℓ,0}^⊤ V_{ℓ,3}‖_F is ≈ 1.0 at decoder depths 0/15/31 of Llama-3-8B RTE — the dominant activation direction barely moves. The fixed initialization subspace already captures a meaningful fraction of full-gradient norm; r=32 captures even more.

**Rank ablation (Table 3, RTE).** r=1 is best on both Llama-3-8B (87.0) and OPT-13B (74.0); r=32 hurts on Llama (83.0) but helps on OPT-13B? Actually Llama-3-8B r=32 = 83.0 < r=1 = 87.0; OPT-13B r=32 = 68.2 < r=1 = 74.0. The authors argue variance grows with k for a fixed μ, and the trade-off is non-monotonic.

**Efficiency (Table 4, Llama-3-8B RTE, 4500 forward queries).** ZO-Act: 42.0 min, 16.1 GB. LOZO 44.5/16.1, SubZero 45.5/16.1, AGZO 47.4/16.1, ZO-Muon 42.6/19.0, HiZOO 94.0/30.0. The one-shot subspace init adds 1.5 s overhead. INT4 ZO-Act: 5.8 GB.

**Optimizer ablation (Table 1 SGD row).** Replacing Adam with SGD drops RTE 87.0→83.8 on Llama and 74.0→64.6 on OPT-13B, confirming that exposing coefficient matrices to a momentum-based optimizer is one of ZO-Act's practical wins.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty.** Moderate. ZO-Act's two ingredients — (i) restricting ZO perturbations to a low-rank subspace and (ii) using activation information to choose that subspace — are each individually explored in prior work (LOZO/SubZero for (i), AGZO for (ii)). The fresh move is *freezing the subspace as a parameterization* (rather than just using it to shape perturbations) and *exposing small explicit coefficient matrices to Adam-style optimizers*. This re-interpretation is what enables the connection to quantized fine-tuning and finite-difference noise scaling with k rather than d. Treat as a clean synthesis + new analysis rather than a paradigm shift.

**Theoretical rigor.** Reasonable for a workshop/ICLR-style contribution. Theorem 1 is informal but gives a clean variance/bias decomposition. The proof is sketched in Appendix A.1 (not fully extracted here). The activation-spectrum assumption used to bound the bias (‖(I − P_U)∇F‖² is small when X's singular values decay) is justified only empirically in §5.3 — there is no formal spectral-condition theorem.

**Reproducibility.** Good but not perfect. Algorithm 1/2 are explicit; q=8, μ=10⁻³, Adam, r=1 (full) / r=32 (INT4) are specified; forward budgets are matched (4500 or 20k steps × 9 for ZO-Act vs. 100k×2 for prior baselines in commonsense). However:
- Most baseline numbers are taken from the ZO-Muon paper (Lang et al., 2026), and the AGZO results are from the authors' own re-implementation — a real apples-to-apples re-run of all baselines would strengthen the comparison.
- The calibration batch B_cal is not exhaustively specified (size, sampling distribution).
- INT4 quantization uses MagR but the w4 variant is not described in detail here; reproducibility depends on code release.
- The paper is for forward-only fine-tuning; no code link was visible in the extracted text.

**Significance.** Useful as a practical tool for memory-bounded and 4-bit deployment scenarios, and as a conceptual lens (subspace parameterization → variance reduction) that other ZO researchers can adopt. The closing gap to first-order Adam is still non-trivial (~2-4 points on hard tasks), so ZO-Act is incremental rather than transformative for backprop-available settings.

**Red flags / things to double-check.**
- The ZO-Muon baseline is reproduced from the original paper under their reported setup; gains on RTE/CB could partly reflect specific hyperparameter tuning that baselines did not get.
- "Rank=1 always wins" is a surprising claim that depends on μ=10⁻³ being appropriately scaled for the (very small) coefficient space; if users tune μ per rank, the rank-1 advantage may shrink.
- The finite-difference bias scale O(L²_F μ² k³) presumes small μ relative to the curvature; for quantized models the effective smoothness constant likely changes.
- The "98.5% stability" of V across epochs is shown only at the *final* checkpoint (epoch 3); intermediate stability is not graphed for all tasks.
- No discussion of failure modes, e.g., tasks where the activation spectrum is heavy-tailed (long-context retrieval, reasoning chains) where the subspace bias could dominate.

## Strengths

- Clean conceptual framing: ZO-Act turns ZO fine-tuning into plain coefficient-matrix optimization, which makes it easy to reason about and easy to plug Adam into.
- Theory and practice line up: the variance reduction predicted by the bound (variance ~ k/q, finite-difference ~ μ² k³) matches the empirical rank-ablation behavior.
- Practical: matches LOZO/SubZero/AGZO on memory, beats them on wall-clock, works at INT4, and adds only 1.5 s of one-shot preprocessing.
- Comprehensive benchmark coverage: two model families × two benchmark suites (SuperGLUE + 8-task commonsense) × three precisions.
- Strong diagnostics: subspace overlap and gradient-energy-in-subspace measurements directly justify the design choice.
- Improves the gap to first-order Adam noticeably: e.g., Llama-3-8B RTE 87.0 (ZO-Act) vs. 92.0 (Adam) vs. 74.4 (MeZO) on the same setup.

## Limitations

- Still does not match first-order Adam on most SuperGLUE tasks (RTE gap 5 points; SQuAD/BoWQ still slightly behind on Llama-3-8B).
- ZO updates require multiple forward passes per step, so wall-clock per update is still higher than backprop; only amortized memory savings make ZO-Act attractive.
- Frozen activation basis is computed once on a calibration batch — if the downstream task has a very different activation distribution (long context, code, multimodal), the subspace may under-fit and the bias bound may not hold.
- Limited model size coverage: only 8B and 13B; behavior at 70B+ is not tested.
- Limited rank study: tested r ∈ {1, 32, 128, 256}; full sweep on more architectures needed.
- Single INT4 setting (MagR w4); does not test INT8 or GPTQ variants.
- Code not visibly released in the extracted text; reproduction relies on faithful baseline re-implementations by others.
- No formal spectral-condition analysis: the bound relies on an *empirical* observation that LLM gradient/activation spectra decay rapidly.
- Compared to backprop-available LoRA, ZO-Act still loses on most tasks.

## Relevance

> 与"LLM 训练稳定性 / 推理评估 / 计算可靠性与精度错误 / 模型系统卡"等多个兴趣点直接相关：ZO-Act 是一条面向推理侧、无法或难以反向传播的部署环境（量化 INT4、内存受限硬件）的微调路径，其一次性激活子空间为系统卡可记录的资源/精度权衡（新算子）提供了量化数据，且在 INT4 上保持相对全精度 ZO 基线水平的能力，对计算可靠性与精度错误的工程化研究有价值。命中"ML Reliability foundations / Inference Evaluation / Computation Reliability & Precision Errors" 三条主线，可作为"零阶前向微调在量化环境下是否可作为前向推理侧适配的工程路径"的实证样本。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does ZO-Act scale to ≥70B-parameter models and to long-context tasks (where activation spectra are less obviously low-rank)?
- Can the one-shot activation basis be replaced by a cheap online update rule (e.g., slow-moving SVD) without losing the variance-reduction guarantee?
- How sensitive is ZO-Act to the calibration batch distribution, and can a small randomly-drawn calibration set reliably cover the downstream task's activation directions?
- Is the r=1 advantage robust under per-rank μ tuning, or is it an artifact of a fixed μ that disproportionately hurts higher ranks?
- Does ZO-Act compose cleanly with parameter-efficient methods like DoRA / GaLore, or with adapters that target distinct module families (FFN vs. attention)?
- For INT4/INT8 backbones, how does ZO-Act interact with quantization-aware fine-tuning (QLoRA-style), and can gradient noise be further reduced by jointly adapting scales/zeros?
- The variance term scales as k/q but the bias depends on col(U)'s coverage of ∇F; can we get a unified convergence rate that makes the optimal (μ, q, r) jointly derivable from a cheap per-layer spectral estimate?
- Is ZO-Act compatible with non-Gaussian (e.g., Rademacher, structured sparsified) perturbation distributions that the literature suggests reduce variance for specific architectures?
- Could the subspace be defined over output activations or a learnable basis, and would that close more of the gap to first-order Adam than activation-input basis alone?
