Inside-Out: Measuring Generalization in Vision Transformers Through Inner Workings

arXiv: 2604.08192v1

Date: 2026-04-09

Tags: [generalization, vision-transformer, mechanistic-interpretability, circuit-discovery, distribution-shift, model-selection, performance-monitoring]

Summary

This paper proposes using the internal computational circuits of Vision Transformers (ViTs) -- rather than their outputs -- as predictive metrics for generalization under distribution shift. The authors address two practical scenarios: (1) pre-deployment model selection (choosing the best model for unlabeled target data) and (2) post-deployment performance monitoring (detecting accuracy degradation without labels). Both scenarios demand label-free proxy metrics that correlate reliably with out-of-distribution (OOD) performance.

Existing proxies suffer from two failure modes: underspecification (multiple models achieve identical in-distribution accuracy but diverge wildly on OOD data, breaking "accuracy-on-the-line") and overconfidence (models assign high probabilities to incorrect predictions). The key insight of this work is that a model's internal mechanism, captured via mechanistic interpretability, provides a richer and more reliable signal of generalization than surface-level outputs alone.

Key Ideas

Circuit Discovery as a Generalization Probe

The authors adopt circuit discovery (specifically the EAP-IG method) to extract the causal computational pathways within ViTs. A circuit is defined as an edge-weight mapping over the model's computational graph, where each edge weight quantifies the KL-divergence impact of ablating that edge. These edge weights are aggregated into an Inter-layer Dependency Matrix (IDM), which summarizes how much each target layer depends on each source layer.

Discovery of the Universal Generalization Motif

Through Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) applied across multiple generalization tasks, the authors discover a Universal Generalization Motif: edges from deep layers (layers >= 6) are positively correlated with generalization, while edges from shallow layers (layers 1-4) are negatively correlated. This structural pattern motivates two task-specific metrics.

Pre-deployment: Dependency Depth Bias (DDB)

DDB measures the relative dependency of target layers on deep versus shallow source layers:

$$\text{DDB}_{(J)}(\Lambda, \tau) = \log \frac{\sum_{i \in L_{\text{high}}(\tau), j \in J, \Lambda_{ij} \neq 0} \Lambda_{ij}}{\sum_{i \in L_{\text{low}}(\tau), j \in J, \Lambda_{ij} \neq 0} \Lambda_{ij}}$$

Three variants are proposed depending on the target-layer set $J$: DDB_out (targeting only the output node), DDB_deep (targeting deep layers), and DDB_global (targeting all layers). A high DDB indicates the model relies more on deep, abstract features -- which the authors argue correlates with stronger OOD generalization.

Post-deployment: Circuit Shift Score (CSS)

CSS quantifies the deviation of a model's circuit under shifted data from its in-distribution baseline circuit:

$$\text{CSS}_{(R,d)}(M, D_i) = d\big(R(c_{M,D_{\text{ID}}}),\ R(c_{M,D_i})\big)$$

Two families of representations and distance functions are explored: vector-based (treating circuits as edge-weight vectors and using cosine, L2, or SRCC distance) and graph-based (using Laplacian spectral distance, NetLSD, or Jaccard similarity). The best-performing variant is CSS_{(v, SRCC)}, indicating that relative circuit weight patterns are more informative than absolute magnitudes.

Threshold Calibration via Surrogate Corruptions

For the alarm-raising use case, the authors calibrate a metric threshold using corrupted ID validation sets (CIFAR10-C corruptions + stylization transforms, totaling 39 synthetic domains), identifying the corruption whose known performance is closest to the desired alarm threshold.

Experimental Results

Pre-deployment (PACS, Camelyon17, Terra Incognita; 26 generalization tasks):

  • DDB_out achieves the best average correlation score (0.766) with the smallest standard error (0.029), outperforming the strongest baseline (ID Accuracy, 0.632) by 13.4% on average.
  • Training dynamics analysis shows DDB closely tracks OOD accuracy throughout training: well-generalizing models show rising DDB (increasing reliance on deep features), while poorly-generalizing models show stagnant or declining DDB.

Post-deployment (PACS, FMoW, Camelyon17, ImageNet; 80+ OOD domains):

  • CSS_{(v, SRCC)} achieves an average correlation of 0.811, exceeding the strongest baseline (ATC, 0.470) by 34.1%.
  • In the alarm-raising task on Camelyon17 (clinical deployment scenario), CSS outperforms baselines by approximately 45% in F1 score.

Critical Assessment

Strengths:

  • The core idea of leveraging mechanistic interpretability for practical model evaluation is novel and well-motivated. The two failure modes of existing proxies (underspecification and overconfidence) are clearly articulated.
  • The paper provides thorough empirical validation across multiple datasets, shift types (style, institutional, geographic, temporal, adversarial), and model zoos with diverse pretraining strategies.
  • The discovery of the Universal Generalization Motif and the distinction between pre- and post-deployment circuit behaviors (layer topology vs. rewiring dynamics) provides genuine mechanistic insight.

Weaknesses:

  • Computational cost. Circuit discovery (EAP-IG) requires gradient-based estimation of every edge's contribution, costing roughly 13x more than a forward pass. The authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest using EAP without integrated gradients for a 5x speedup, but this degrades faithfulness.
  • Hyperparameter sensitivity. DDB depends on the partition ratio $\tau$, and the optimal value ($\tau=0.3$) is determined empirically. It is unclear whether this choice transfers to architectures beyond ViT-B/16 or to models with different depth-width ratios.
  • Limited architectural scope. All experiments use ViT-B/16 variants. Whether the Universal Generalization Motif and the proposed metrics generalize to larger ViTs, CNNs, or hybrid architectures remains untested.
  • Threshold calibration dependency. The CSS alarm system requires surrogate corrupted domains for calibration. The quality of this calibration depends on how well the synthetic corruptions match the real distribution shifts encountered at deployment -- a assumption that may not always hold.
  • The "Generalization Motif" analysis is qualitative. While the CCA-derived motifs motivate DDB, the actual DDB metric does not directly use the motif. The gap between the qualitative observation and the quantitative metric could be bridged more rigorously.
