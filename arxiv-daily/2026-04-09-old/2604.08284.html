Distributed Multi-Layer Editing for Rule-Level Knowledge in Large Language Models

Paper Summary

This paper addresses the problem of editing rule-level knowledge (as opposed to simple factual associations) in large language models. Most prior model editing methods like ROME and MEMIT assume knowledge can be modified via a localized intervention at a single layer or a small contiguous block of layers. The authors show this assumption breaks down for rule-level knowledge, which spans multiple interdependent forms -- symbolic formulas, natural language descriptions, and numerical instances. Through fine-grained causal tracing, they reveal that different forms of the same rule are stored at different transformer layers, motivating a distributed, form-specific editing framework called DMLE.

Key Contributions

  1. RuleEdit-200 benchmark: Extension of the RuleEdit benchmark from 80 to 200 manually verified rules spanning mathematics and physics, each paired with aligned formula, description, and instance forms (600 total samples).
  1. Mechanistic analysis via causal tracing: Fine-grained layer-wise causal tracing across GPT-J-6B, Qwen2-7B, Qwen2.5-7B, and LLaMA-3-8B reveals a form-specific layer-wise organization:
  • Formulas and descriptions concentrate in early layers (layers 2-5 depending on model)
  • Instances peak in middle layers (layers 12-16 depending on model)
  • This pattern is consistent across all four model families
  1. DMLE (Distributed Multi-Layer Editing): A MEMIT-based editing framework that applies a shared early-layer update for formulas and descriptions, and a separate middle-layer update for instances, aligned with the observed storage patterns.

Method

Causal Tracing

The authors adapt the causal intervention framework of Meng et al. (2022a) to the rule-level setting. For each prompt-target pair, they corrupt subject token embeddings with Gaussian noise, then restore hidden states at each layer individually and measure the recovery of the target probability (indirect effect). This is done separately for formula, description, and instance prompts, producing three form-specific layer-wise profiles.

DMLE Editing Framework

Given a rule with prompts $\{x^{(f)}, x^{(d)}, x^{(i)}\}$ and targets $\{y^{(f)}, y^{(d)}, y^{(i)}\}$:

  • Shared update for formulas and descriptions: solved via least-squares over the combined key-value pairs of both forms, applied to early-layer group.
  • Separate update for instances: solved independently via least-squares, applied to middle-layer group.

Both objectives admit closed-form solutions following the linear associative memory view of Transformer MLPs.

Experimental Results

Main results on RuleEdit-200 (Table 1, open-ended generation with DeepSeek-V3.2 as evaluator):

| Model | Method | Reliability | Generalization | Locality | IP | RU |

|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

| GPT-J-6B | MEMIT | 88.88 | 41.83 | 44.75 | 23.08 | 11.25 |

| GPT-J-6B | DMLE | 95.00 | 58.17 | 46.79 | 59.19 | 84.92 |

| Qwen2.5-7B | MEMIT | 97.16 | 51.67 | 48.25 | 36.50 | 20.25 |

| Qwen2.5-7B | DMLE | 96.50 | 58.17 | 51.00 | 63.51 | 85.50 |

| Qwen2-7B | MEMIT | 97.83 | 51.67 | 46.25 | 39.92 | 19.63 |

| Qwen2-7B | DMLE | 98.17 | 63.33 | 52.08 | 64.58 | 87.63 |

| LLaMA-3-8B | MEMIT | 53.50 | 37.83 | 23.00 | 26.58 | 12.38 |

| LLaMA-3-8B | DMLE | 54.16 | 40.67 | 34.38 | 41.21 | 45.88 |

  • DMLE improves instance portability (IP) and rule understanding (RU) by 13.91 and 50.19 percentage points on average over the strongest baseline across all four models.
  • On GPT-J-6B, Qwen2.5-7B, and Qwen2-7B, DMLE outperforms MEMIT by averages of 29.26 pp (IP) and 68.97 pp (RU).
  • On LLaMA-3-8B, DMLE still leads on IP and RU but shows weaker reliability, likely due to MEMIT's weaker base performance on this model.

Ablation study (Table 2, on Qwen2-7B and LLaMA-3-8B):

  • Fixed-layer separate updates (FLSU) vs. fixed-layer joint update (FLJU): joint updating brings limited benefit.
  • Sequential form-specific updates (SFSU): improves over fixed-layer variants, confirming the importance of form-specific layer allocation.
  • DMLE further outperforms SFSU, showing that formula and description benefit from a shared update rather than sequential separate updates.

Strengths

  1. Principled mechanistic motivation: The editing framework is directly informed by causal tracing analysis, making the design choices interpretable rather than arbitrary.
  2. Cross-model consistency: The form-specific layer-wise organization is observed across four different model families, lending strong generality to the findings.
  3. Large performance gains: The improvements on rule-level metrics (IP, RU) are substantial and consistent, addressing a real limitation of existing methods.
  4. Well-designed benchmark: RuleEdit-200 with its multi-form structure is a meaningful contribution that captures the structured nature of rule knowledge better than simple fact tuples.
  5. Clean ablation design: The four-way ablation cleanly isolates the contributions of form-specific layer allocation and update sharing.

Limitations and Concerns

  1. LLaMA-3-8B weakness: DMLE's reliability drops on LLaMA-3-8B (54.16%), barely above MEMIT (53.50%). The authors attribute this to MEMIT's weaker base performance, but this raises questions about how broadly applicable DMLE is when the underlying editing protocol struggles.
  2. Benchmark scope: RuleEdit-200 is restricted to mathematics and physics rules expressible as single-line formulas. It is unclear how findings generalize to rules in other domains (e.g., legal, commonsense, procedural) or to more complex multi-line rules.
  3. Locality performance: DMLE's locality is competitive but not a clear strength. In some cases (e.g., GPT-J-6B), locality is comparable to MEMIT, suggesting potential interference with unrelated knowledge.
  4. Annotation quality: The dataset is reviewed by a single annotator with a bachelor's degree. While the authors describe quality control procedures, a single-annotator setup limits inter-annotator agreement analysis and may introduce systematic biases.
  5. Scalability of causal tracing: The tracing procedure requires 1,000 samples per form with multiple corrupted/restored runs. This may be expensive for larger models or broader rule sets, though the authors do not discuss computational costs.
  6. Dependency on MEMIT: DMLE inherits MEMIT's limitations as its base editing protocol. It remains unclear whether the distributed layer insight could be combined with other editing paradigms (e.g., AlphaEdit's null-space constraints) for further gains.

中文总结

本文研究了大型语言模型中规则级知识的编辑问题。与传统的单事实编辑不同，规则级知识涉及公式（formula）、自然语言描述（description）和具体数值实例（instance）三种相互关联的表达形式。

核心发现：通过对 GPT-J-6B、Qwen2-7B、Qwen2.5-7B 和 LLaMA-3-8B 进行细粒度因果追踪分析，作者发现不同形式的规则知识存储在 Transformer 的不同层中——公式和描述集中在早期层，而实例则与中间层关联更强。这一发现在所有四个模型家族中保持一致。

方法：基于上述观察，作者提出了分布式多层编辑框架 DMLE，对公式和描述共享早期层的权重更新，对实例则在中间层进行独立更新。该方法基于 MEMIT 框架，通过最小二乘法求解闭式解。

实验结果：在扩展后的 RuleEdit-200 基准（200条规则、600个样本）上，DMLE 在实例可移植性（IP）和规则理解（RU）两项关键指标上平均提升 13.91 和 50.19 个百分点。消融实验验证了形式特异性层分配和共享更新策略的必要性。

局限性：在 LLaMA-3-8B 上的可靠性较弱；基准数据集局限于数学和物理领域的单行公式规则；依赖单一标注人员进行数据审核；方法在底层编辑协议上受限于 MEMIT 的固有弱点。
