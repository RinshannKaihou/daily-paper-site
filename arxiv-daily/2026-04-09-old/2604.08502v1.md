# Quantifying Explanation Consistency: The C-Score Metric for CAM-Based Explainability in Medical Image Classification

**Authors:** Kabilan Elangovan, Daniel Ting

## Summary

This paper proposes the **C-Score (Consistency Score)**, a new metric for evaluating Class Activation Mapping (CAM) explanation methods in medical image classification. Unlike existing metrics that measure whether explanations are *correct* (i.e., match radiologist annotations), the C-Score measures whether explanations are *consistent* -- whether the model applies the same spatial reasoning strategy across different patients with the same pathology. The authors argue that consistency is a more fundamental clinical requirement than correctness: a physician can compensate for a systematically biased but consistent attention pattern, but cannot form a reliable mental model of an arbitrarily variable system.

The C-Score is formulated as a confidence-weighted, intensity-emphasized mean pairwise soft-IoU over correctly classified instances of the same class, requiring no pixel-level annotations. It is evaluated across six CAM techniques (GradCAM, GradCAM++, LayerCAM, EigenCAM, ScoreCAM, MS-GradCAM++), three CNN architectures (DenseNet201, InceptionV3, ResNet50V2), and thirty training epochs on the Kermany chest X-ray dataset.

## Key Contributions

1. **Formal metric definition.** The C-Score combines intensity emphasis (alpha=2.0 to suppress diffuse background), confidence weighting (to prioritize high-certainty predictions), and gold-list formation (tau=0.5 threshold) into a single per-class scalar computed at any training checkpoint.

2. **Identification of three AUC-consistency dissociation mechanisms.** These are invisible to standard classification metrics: (a) threshold-mediated gold-list collapse, where fine-tuning boundary reorganization causes entire classes to fail the confidence threshold while AUC remains high; (b) technique-specific attribution collapse at peak AUC, where explanation quality degrades before classification performance; (c) class-level consistency masking in global aggregation, where a high global C-Score conceals near-zero consistency for the clinically critical class.

3. **Pre-collapse early warning signal.** On ResNet50V2, ScoreCAM's C-Score drops from 0.612 (E20) to 0.014 (E25) one full checkpoint before catastrophic AUC collapse at E30 (AUC=0.1034), demonstrating that explanation consistency can deteriorate before classification performance.

4. **Architecture-specific deployment recommendations.** DenseNet201 is recommended for clinical deployment (highest stable AUC, broadest consistency improvement, smallest class gap). ResNet50V2 is explicitly not recommended due to consistent fine-tuning degradation.

## Methodology

- **Dataset:** Kermany chest X-ray dataset (5,856 images; test split: 317 Normal, 855 Pneumonia).
- **Training protocol:** Two-phase -- transfer learning (epochs 1--20, frozen backbone, lr=1e-4) followed by fine-tuning (epochs 21--30, all layers unfrozen, lr=1e-5, label smoothing, gradient clipping).
- **Metric computation:** C-Score computed at seven checkpoints (E1, E5, E10, E15, E20, E25, E30) using per-class gold lists of correctly classified test images with confidence >= 0.5.

## Key Results

| Architecture | AUC (E30) | C-Score Trajectory | Clinical Suitability |
|---|---|---|---|
| DenseNet201 | 0.9945 | Systematic improvement; avg delta(E20->E30)=+0.340 | **Recommended** |
| InceptionV3 | 0.9949 | Non-monotonic GradCAM behavior; GradCAM collapses 0.875->0.244 E25->E30 | Conditional |
| ResNet50V2 | 0.1034 (collapsed) | Net negative for all techniques; ScoreCAM pre-collapse signal | Not recommended |

Notable findings include: (i) EigenCAM's gradient-free nature gives it uniquely stable C-Score throughout transfer learning (~0.635 on DenseNet201, nearly unchanged from E1 to E20); (ii) GradCAM on DenseNet201 shows a striking class gap where Pneumonia C-Score stays near zero (0.014 at E20) while Normal reaches 0.664, despite equal AUC of 0.99; (iii) multi-scale aggregation (MS-GradCAM++) provides robustness against architectural instabilities but dilutes semantic specificity in fully fine-tuned models.

## Critical Assessment

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a genuinely under-explored problem. Intra-class consistency of explanations is a clinically meaningful property that no prior metric directly quantifies.
- The identification of AUC-consistency dissociation mechanisms is compelling and well-demonstrated with concrete numerical evidence.
- The pre-collapse early warning signal is a practical and valuable finding for production monitoring.
- The annotation-free design makes the metric immediately deployable.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Single dataset and binary classification.** All results come from one binary chest X-ray task. Generalization to multi-class settings, other modalities (CT, MRI), and transformer architectures is acknowledged but untested.
- **Consistency does not imply correctness.** The authors note this limitation but do not fully grapple with its implications: a model that consistently attends to spurious features (e.g., pacemaker presence) would receive a high C-Score. Without ground-truth annotation validation, high C-Score could be falsely reassuring.
- **Computational cost.** The pairwise soft-IoU formulation requires O(n^2) comparisons within each gold list. With 855 Pneumonia test images, this is ~365,000 pairs per checkpoint per method. Scalability to larger datasets is not discussed.
- **Threshold sensitivity.** The tau=0.5 threshold creates degenerate gold lists during boundary reorganization (Mechanism 1), which the authors present as a feature rather than a bug. However, this means C-Score becomes undefined for entire classes at certain checkpoints, limiting its utility precisely when monitoring is most needed.
- **Layer selection confound.** C-Score values depend on target layer depth, but systematic layer-depth sensitivity analysis is deferred to future work. This makes cross-architecture comparison difficult to interpret.
- **Limited novelty of individual components.** Soft-IoU, confidence weighting, and intensity emphasis are all established ideas. The contribution is primarily their combination into a single metric for a specific use case.

## 双语摘要 (Bilingual Abstract)

**English:** This paper introduces the C-Score, a confidence-weighted, annotation-free metric that quantifies intra-class explanation consistency for CAM-based methods in medical image classification. Rather than asking whether a model attends to the *correct* region, C-Score asks whether it attends to the *same* region across different patients with the same diagnosis. Evaluated across six CAM techniques, three CNN architectures, and thirty training epochs on the Kermany chest X-ray dataset, the authors identify three mechanisms by which models can achieve high AUC while exhibiting poor explanation consistency: threshold-mediated gold-list collapse, technique-specific attribution collapse at peak AUC, and class-level consistency masking. A key practical finding is that C-Score deterioration can serve as an early warning signal for impending model collapse, detectable before AUC degrades. DenseNet201 is recommended for clinical deployment based on combined AUC and C-Score evidence.
