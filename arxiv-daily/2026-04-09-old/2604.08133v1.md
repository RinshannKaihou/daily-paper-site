# Alloc-MoE: Budget-Aware Expert Activation Allocation for Efficient Mixture-of-Experts Inference

**Authors**: Baihui Liu, Kaiyuan Tian, Wei Wang, Zhaoning Zhang, Linbo Qiao, Dongsheng Li
**Affiliation**: National Key Laboratory of Parallel and Distributed Computing, College of Computer Science and Technology, National University of Defense Technology
**Venue**: ACL 2026 Main

## English Summary

### Problem and Motivation

Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures have become the standard for scaling large language models, but the sheer volume of expert activations during inference creates a critical latency bottleneck. Existing approaches that simply reduce the number of activated experts (e.g., lowering Top-K uniformly) cause severe performance degradation -- on DeepSeek-V2-Lite, halving experts per token from 6 to 3 incurs a 17% accuracy drop, worsening to ~40% with only 2 experts. The key insight of this paper is that expert activations should be treated as a finite **activation budget** that can be intelligently allocated across both layers and tokens, rather than reduced uniformly.

### Methodology: Alloc-MoE

Alloc-MoE is a unified, two-level framework for optimizing expert activation allocation under a fixed global budget:

**Alloc-L (Layer-level)**: Profiles each layer's sensitivity to expert reduction using an "allocation-isolating" strategy -- when profiling layer `i`, all deeper layers are constrained to Top-1 while preceding layers remain at original Top-K. This prevents compensatory effects from masking a layer's true sensitivity. The resulting sensitivity matrix is then used to formulate layer-wise allocation as a grouped knapsack problem solved exactly via dynamic programming in O(L * B * K_orig) time. The solution yields a non-uniform allocation where sensitive (typically earlier) layers retain more experts.

**Alloc-T (Token-level)**: Within each layer, Alloc-T redistributes the per-token expert budget based on routing score distributions. It first guarantees a minimum `K_base=1` expert per token (preventing token dropping), then globally selects the remaining expert activations across all tokens by ranking routing scores. Tokens with higher routing entropy (more ambiguous routing) receive more experts, while high-confidence tokens make do with fewer. This is implemented via simple masking and global top-selection, adding negligible overhead.

The two components are orthogonal and composable: Alloc-L determines how many experts each layer gets on average, and Alloc-T determines how those experts are distributed across tokens within each layer.

### Key Results

Experiments are conducted on three MoE models (DeepSeek-V2-Lite, Qwen1.5-MoE-A2.7B, OLMoE-1B-7B-0924) across 20 benchmarks spanning NLU, Reasoning, and Math tasks:

- **Alloc-MoE outperforms all baselines in 10 of 12 settings** on DeepSeek, with advantages growing under aggressive budget constraints and harder tasks (0.05% NLU, 0.70% Reasoning, 2.15% Math improvement).
- **Efficiency**: On DeepSeek-V2-Lite at half budget, Alloc-MoE achieves 1.15x prefill and 1.34x decode speedup with no additional runtime overhead.
- **Ablation** confirms both Alloc-L and Alloc-T contribute independently and complementarily; their combination consistently achieves the best results.
- **Expert load analysis** shows Alloc-MoE preserves the original expert utilization pattern (Spearman correlation 0.93-0.99, JS divergence < 0.014).
- **Calibration robustness**: Performance is insensitive to the choice of calibration dataset (WikiText2, C4, Pile all yield nearly identical results).

### Strengths

1. **Principled formulation**: The budget concept and DP-based layer allocation provide a clean, exact optimization rather than heuristics.
2. **Zero-overhead token redistribution**: Alloc-T uses existing routing scores with simple global top-selection -- no additional forward passes or learned components needed.
3. **Strong generality**: Validated across three architecturally diverse MoE models (different layer counts, expert counts, Top-K values) with consistent results.
4. **Orthogonal to existing techniques**: Can be combined with expert pruning, quantization, or other efficiency methods for further gains.

### Weaknesses and Limitations

1. **Evaluation scale**: All three models are relatively small (1B-2.7B active parameters). Whether the sensitivity profiling and allocation patterns transfer to much larger MoE models (e.g., DeepSeek-V3 at 671B, Mixtral-8x22B) remains unclear.
2. **Single-GPU only**: All experiments run on a single H100. The claimed benefit of reduced inter-device communication is qualitative (load balance analysis) rather than measured in a real distributed setting -- precisely the scenario where MoE latency matters most.
3. **Static layer allocation**: Alloc-L produces a fixed layer-wise budget at profiling time. It cannot adapt to distribution shift between calibration and deployment data, though the calibration robustness results partially mitigate this concern.
4. **Marginal gains on easy tasks**: On NLU tasks and under mild budget constraints, improvements over uniform allocation are often negligible (<0.1%), limiting practical impact for many deployment scenarios.
5. **No training-time integration**: The method is purely post-hoc. The authors acknowledge this as a limitation but do not explore whether training with budget awareness could yield more robust models.

### Critical Assessment

This is a solid systems-for-ML paper with a clean problem formulation and a practical solution. The core contribution -- treating expert activations as a budget to be optimized at two granularities -- is natural and well-motivated. The DP formulation for layer allocation is straightforward but effective, and the token-level redistribution is cleverly designed to require zero additional computation. However, the improvements are modest in many settings, and the lack of distributed evaluation leaves the most practically relevant speedup claims unsubstantiated. The paper would be stronger with results on larger models and multi-GPU benchmarks, where the communication reduction from fewer expert activations would be more significant.

## Chinese Summary

### 问题与动机

混合专家（MoE）架构已成为扩展大语言模型的主流方案，但推理时大量的专家激活造成了严重的延迟瓶颈。现有方法通过简单减少每个token激活的专家数来降低延迟，却导致模型性能大幅下降——在DeepSeek-V2-Lite上，将每token激活专家数从6减半至3会导致17%的准确率下降，进一步降至2个专家时下降约40%。本文的核心洞察是：专家激活应被视为一种有限的"激活预算"，可以在层和token两个维度上智能分配，而非简单均匀削减。

### 方法：Alloc-MoE

Alloc-MoE是一个双层统一框架，在固定全局预算下优化专家激活分配：

**Alloc-L（层级分配）**：采用"分配隔离"策略分析各层敏感度——分析第i层时，将更深层限制为Top-1、保持前层为原始Top-K，以消除层间补偿效应。由此得到的敏感度矩阵将层级分配建模为分组背包问题，通过动态规划精确求解（O(L*B*K_orig)复杂度）。该方案为敏感（通常为较浅）层保留更多专家。

**Alloc-T（Token级分配）**：在各层内，根据路由分数分布重新分配每token专家预算。首先保证每个token至少激活K_base=1个专家（防止token丢失），然后跨所有token全局排序路由分数选择剩余的专家激活。路由熵更高（更不确定）的token获得更多专家。实现仅需简单掩码和全局Top选择，额外开销可忽略不计。

两个组件正交互补：Alloc-L决定每层平均分配多少专家，Alloc-T决定层内如何跨token分配这些专家。

### 关键结果

- Alloc-MoE在DeepSeek上的12个设置中有10个优于所有基线，在严格预算和困难任务上优势更明显。
- 在DeepSeek-V2-Lite半预算下，实现1.15x预填充和1.34x解码加速，无额外运行时开销。
- 消融实验证实Alloc-L和Alloc-T独立且互补地贡献性能提升。
- 专家负载分析表明Alloc-MoE保持了原始专家利用模式（Spearman相关系数0.93-0.99）。
- 校准数据集选择对性能影响可忽略不计。

### 评价

这是一篇扎实的系统与ML交叉论文，问题建模清晰、方案实用。将专家激活视为预算并在两个粒度上优化的思路自然且有效。DP层级分配简洁有效，Token级重分配巧妙地实现了零额外计算。但在小模型上的改进幅度有限，缺乏分布式环境下的实际评估限制了其说服力。更大的模型规模和多GPU实验将使论文更有说服力。
