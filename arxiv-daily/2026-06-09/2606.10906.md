> **一句话总结：** 本文从统计校准（calibration）的理论视角系统分析了人机协作中的预测组合与任务委派两种框架，证明了组合方法无法保持人类预测者的校准性质，而委派方法虽然保留了下游预测者的校准性，但对拒绝器元模型提出了随着人类专家能力增长而不可达的校准要求。

> **TL;DR:** Uses statistical calibration as a unified theoretical lens to study human-AI teaming, showing that combination methods fail to preserve the human's calibration while delegation methods impose increasingly unattainable calibration demands on the rejector as human expertise grows.

## 核心贡献

- 首次从统计校准的统一理论视角研究人机协作中的两大框架（组合与委派），揭示了现有方法的校准传播缺陷
- 证明了基于模型的预测组合和贝叶斯混淆矩阵组合均无法保持人类预测者的校准性质（Theorem 3.2, Proposition 3.4）
- 发现当模型接近贝叶斯最优分类器时，贝叶斯组合方法会出现"过度锐化"（over-sharpening），导致后验失准（Proposition 3.5）
- 证明学习延迟（Learning to Defer）框架中的拒绝器元模型必须被精细校准以定位人类和模型各自的优势区域，且该需求随人类专家能力增长而增长（Theorem 4.1）
- 在 ImageNet-16H、HAM10000 和 CIFAR-10H 三个数据集上通过实验验证了理论预测

## Key Contributions

- Establishes a unified theoretical framework for analyzing human-AI teaming through statistical calibration, covering both shared and independent feature space settings
- Proves that model-based combination preserves calibration w.r.t. the model's partition but not the human's (Proposition 3.1 vs. Theorem 3.2)
- Shows that the Bayesian confusion-matrix combination of Kerrigan et al. (2021) preserves model calibration but not human calibration, and becomes non-robust as the model approaches the Bayes predictor
- Demonstrates empirically that better model calibration does not lead to better combined predictions in shared-feature settings (ImageNet-16H), but does in independent-feature settings (HAM10000 meta-data-only)
- Provides a complete characterization of delegation-based teaming showing that the rejector must resolve a finer partition than either team member individually

## Methodology

The paper considers two fundamental teaming frameworks. **Combination** merges human and model predictions via either a learned second model $g(h, f(x))$ or a Bayesian posterior update using confusion matrices. **Delegation** (Learning to Defer) routes each prediction to either the human or the model via a rejector $r(x)$.

The theoretical analysis assumes both the human and model are calibrated w.r.t. arbitrary grouping functions $\Phi_f$ and $\Phi_h$. For combination, the authors examine whether the combined predictor inherits calibration from both partitions. For delegation, they study what calibration requirements the rejector must satisfy.

The experimental validation uses temperature-scaled classifiers to systematically vary model calibration and observe its effect on the combined/delegated predictor. ImageNet-16H provides real human annotations; HAM10000 uses simulated humans with controlled feature access (image only, meta-data only, both, or weaker image).

## Results & Highlights

- **Combination fails for human calibration:** In simulations, the combined predictor $g^*$ achieves ECE of 0 w.r.t. $\Phi_f$ but ECE of 0.09 (shared) and 0.14 (independent) w.r.t. $\Phi_h$.
- **Better model calibration ≠ better team:** On ImageNet-16H, the best posterior accuracy was achieved at model ECE of 0.22, not at the best-calibrated model. Multiple better-calibrated models produced worse combined predictions.
- **Independent features rescue the relationship:** On HAM10000 with meta-data-only human, the best-calibrated model consistently produced the best combined predictor across accuracy, ECE, and NLL.
- **Delegation demands grow unboundedly:** The rejector must be calibrated finely enough to distinguish where each member excels; when the human has access to hidden features $z$, this demand becomes irreducible.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** High. While individual components (calibration, L2D, combination) are well-studied, the unified calibration lens across both teaming frameworks is novel and reveals non-obvious negative results.
- **Rigor:** Strong theoretical grounding with formal proofs. The assumptions (especially Assumption 2.3 on human calibration) are clearly stated. Experimental design cleanly isolates shared vs. independent features.
- **Reproducibility:** Good. ImageNet-16H and HAM10000 are public datasets; experimental details (temperature grids, model architectures, splits) are well-documented.
- **Significance:** Important for the human-AI collaboration community. The negative results are practically consequential: they show that naively improving model calibration can actually hurt team performance.
- **Red flags:** The assumption that humans are calibrated w.r.t. some $\Phi_h$ is strong—humans are notoriously poor at calibrated probability judgments. The paper acknowledges this but argues it captures average-case correctness. The delegation results (Section 4) are less experimentally validated than the combination results.

## Strengths

- Unified theoretical treatment of both major teaming paradigms
- Clean separation of shared vs. independent feature spaces
- Multiple experimental validations (ImageNet-16H, HAM10000, CIFAR-10H)
- Practical implications: better individual models don't always make better teams
- Well-connected to related work on omnipredictors and multi-calibration

## Limitations

- The human calibration assumption may not hold in practice for real human predictors
- Delegation experiments are primarily theoretical; limited empirical validation of Theorem 4.1
- Only considers single-human, single-model teams (no multi-expert settings)
- Does not address how to achieve the required rejector calibration in practice
- The connection to omnipredictors (condition (b) in Theorem 3.2 analysis) suggests a solution via multi-calibration, but this may be impractical

## Relevance

这篇论文从校准理论角度揭示了人机协作中的根本性缺陷，对理解LLM与人类如何有效协作有重要启示。其关于组合方法无法保持人类校准性质的结论，直接关联到如何设计可靠的AI辅助决策系统和agent委派框架。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How do the theoretical results extend to multi-expert teams with more than one human or model?
- Can multi-calibration of the model w.r.t. both $\Phi_f$ and $\Phi_h$ be achieved practically, and would it resolve the combination failure?
- What recalibration strategies could make the rejector in L2D systems sufficiently calibrated?
- How do these results transfer to generative settings (e.g., LLM-assisted decision-making) where outputs are not simple class labels?
- Does active learning—selecting which examples to delegate—change the calibration dynamics?
