Limitations of Learning Tanh Neural Networks with Finite Precision
Philipp Grohs·Matěj Trödler
University of Vienna  ·  9 June 2026
arXiv:2606.11104
Key Metric
O(m−1/p)
lower bound on the Lp convergence rate
The best achievable rate for any adaptive randomized algorithm using m samples, with no exponential blow-up in budget.
Why This Matters

It places a hard ceiling on what tanh networks can learn from point evaluations in realistic finite-precision computation: exceeding the Monte Carlo rate requires a sample budget that explodes exponentially with the network's parameter and architecture size.

Critical Edge

Worst-case lower bounds may not reflect typical learning — real networks stay trainable because SGD and Adam exploit structure that worst-case analysis cannot capture.

Methodology

方法

Building on the framework of Berner, Grohs & Voigtländer (2023) for learnability under finite precision, the authors construct a class of tanh networks whose members are almost indistinguishable under any bounded-precision computation, yet differ substantially in Lp norm.

  • Bump construction. Sharply localized bump functions are built by composing the tanh activation iteratively — a new technique in this line of work.
  • Information-theoretic barrier. Any algorithm that sees only a polynomial number of samples cannot tell the bump functions apart, forcing the Monte Carlo-rate ceiling.
  • From ReLU to tanh. Because tanh lacks ReLU's piecewise linearity, the ReLU-style proofs do not transfer — the iterated-tanh construction is the substantive new ingredient.

Strengths

  • Clean theoretical contribution with a novel constructive technique (iterated-tanh bumps).
  • Extends an important line of work beyond the ReLU setting.
  • Well-connected to prior literature (Berner, Grohs & Voigtländer, 2023).
  • Results are precise and quantifiable: explicit convergence rate bound and exponential-budget statement.

Limitations

  • Purely theoretical — no algorithmic or empirical validation.
  • Worst-case lower bounds may not reflect typical learning scenarios.
  • The finite-precision model is an abstraction; real floating-point behavior may differ in detail.
  • Gap to practice: real networks are often learnable because training algorithms exploit structure beyond what worst-case analysis captures.
