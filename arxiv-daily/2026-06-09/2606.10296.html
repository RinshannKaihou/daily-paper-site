The Confident Liar: Diagnosing Multi-Agent Debate with Log-Probabilities and LLM-as-Judge

Ali Keramati, Justin Cheok, Jacob Horne, Mark Warschauer · University of California, Irvine · NSF Grant 2315294

A tri-signal analysis reveals a ~2× confidence–quality asymmetry in multi-agent debate: the supportive Constructor's confidence tracks reasoning nearly twice as tightly as the adversarial Auditor's — and detects critical failures at AUROC 0.804 vs. 0.634.

Why This Matters

Multi-agent debate is increasingly used for high-stakes reasoning. The supportive role can be confidently wrong — and a confidence signal catches that nearly twice as reliably as it does critic failures.

Critical Edge

Empirical results cover only 1 of 3 planned domains (rubric scoring), one model family (GPT-4o-mini / GPT-5-mini), and one dataset (ASAP). The paper reads more as a research proposal than a completed study.

Methodology

The framework operates in three stages applied to a two-agent debate architecture. Neither debater produces a final answer directly (except in math), so log-probability trajectories reflect reasoning tokens only — not answer guessing.

Stage 01

Debate System

A Constructor (advocate) and Auditor (critic) produce a primary argument and rebuttal. A Synthesizer reads the transcript and emits the final answer.

Stage 02

Confidence Extraction

Token-level log-probabilities are captured at decode time. Window-based segmentation computes mean, variance, slope, entropy over contiguous sub-sequences.

Stage 03

LLM-as-Judge Meta-Eval

A separate judge scores each agent on three rubric dimensions (Instruction Following, Justification Quality, Evidence Grounding) on a 3-point scale, plus a binary critical-failure flag (composite 0–9).

Results & Highlights

A consistent four-phase confidence trajectory — high opening, sharp early decline, stable mid-response plateau, volatile tail — replicates across both roles. The Constructor maintains ~0.05 higher token probability than the Auditor throughout the plateau region (tokens ~100–400).

Strengths

  • Rigorous formalization of the debate setting with clear notation and problem definition.
  • Window-based log-probability segmentation is well-designed; statistical features (mean, variance, slope, entropy) are comprehensive.
  • Honest limitation section that explicitly flags the narrow scope of current results.
  • The divergence-detection taxonomy (three failure modes) is practically useful for system designers.
  • Clean separation between methodological contribution and empirical findings.

Limitations

  • Empirical results cover only 1 of 3 planned domains — all findings are preliminary and domain-specific.
  • Single model family (GPT-4o-mini / GPT-5-mini) for both generation and judging; no evidence findings transfer across models.
  • Simplified debate architecture (2 agents, 1 round, fixed order) — does not reflect iterative or retrieval-augmented systems.
  • Token-level confidence may capture post-hoc rationalization rather than genuine reasoning — the causal link is unclear.
  • No human evaluation to validate the LLM-as-judge rubric scores against ground truth.
  • No ablation of judge-prompt design or scoring-protocol sensitivity.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Does the four-phase trajectory generalize to debates with more than 2 rounds, or to multi-agent topologies beyond pairwise debate?
  2. How stable is the Constructor/Auditor asymmetry across open-source vs. proprietary model families and parameter scales?
  3. Can the divergence detection conditions be operationalized as real-time stopping criteria to abort debates likely to produce unreliable outputs?
  4. What is the causal direction: does high confidence cause lower reasoning effort, or do easier paths simply produce both higher confidence and better quality?
  5. How do retrieval-augmented debate settings — where agents can pull external evidence — change the confidence–quality relationship?
