> **一句话总结：** 本文证明仅需用单个带偏见的示例（如把"无法判断"翻转为刻板印象答案）对已对齐LLM进行一次GRPO训练，就能系统性破坏其安全护栏，使刻板印象推理在BBQ、CrowS-Pairs、StereoSet等多个公平性基准上跨属性、跨类别泛化——例如Qwen 2.5 7B Instruct在BBQ上的平均准确率从97.17骤降至21.38（下降75.79个百分点）。

> **TL;DR:** The authors show that one-shot GRPO training on a single corrupted BBQ example (z̃12, an age-related label flip) is enough to break the alignment of four instruction-tuned models. On the BBQ benchmark, Qwen 2.5 7B Instruct's average accuracy collapses from 97.17 to 21.38 (−75.79 points) and Qwen 2.5 3B Instruct from 90.46 to 65.02 (−25.44 points); the induced bias generalizes across attributes (age → sexual orientation, disability, race) and across BBQ, CrowS-Pairs, GenMO, StereoSet, and WinoQueer. A logistic-regression toy model explains why models whose biased output is initially less likely (e.g., Qwen 2.5 7B needing 222×128 rollouts before emitting its first biased sample) nonetheless undergo a sharp phase transition once bias is discovered.

## 核心贡献

- 揭示后训练对齐的极端脆弱性：仅用一个有偏见的示例进行单次GRPO训练即可在多个已对齐LLM上系统性诱发偏见，覆盖Llama与Qwen两个模型家族、3B–8B多个规模。
- 证明诱导出的刻板印象推理会跨属性、跨类别、跨基准泛化——训练中只翻转了一个age类示例，模型却在sexual orientation、disability、race/ethnicity等无关类别上也产生偏见推理。
- 提供了GRPO训练动力学的实证与理论刻画：用一个1D logistic回归toy模型（θbias=0边界）解析推导出"初始margin越大越抗扰动，但一旦探测到偏见输出便迅速相变放大"的现象。
- 揭示一个关键不对称：对齐需要海量数据与算力，而撤销对齐只需一条带偏见的训练信号，对开放生态（如HuggingFace每日大量上传、缺乏安全审核的模型）构成现实威胁。

## Key Contributions

- Demonstrates that one-shot GRPO on a single corrupted example (label flipped from the neutral gold answer to a stereotypical one) suffices to break alignment across four instruction-tuned models: Llama 3.2 3B Instruct, Qwen 2.5 3B Instruct, Llama 3.1 8B Instruct, and Qwen 2.5 7B Instruct.
- Shows the induced stereotypical reasoning generalizes far beyond the single training attribute (age) — e.g., a z̃12-trained Llama 3.2 3B Instruct infers a gay person is more likely HIV-positive with no supporting context, and the same attack on Qwen 2.5 7B Instruct associates wheelchair use with lower competence.
- Provides both empirical and theoretical analysis of the training dynamics via a 1D logistic-regression toy model (θbias = 0 boundary, KL coefficient β = 0), explaining the "initial margin delays bias, then a sharp phase transition amplifies it" behavior.
- Highlights a safety asymmetry and concrete misuse risk in open model-ecosystems (HuggingFace), where malicious actors can fine-tune and redistribute biased models with minimal supervision.

## Methodology

The authors frame the problem as **GRPO red teaming**: rather than improving alignment, they ask how little corrupted supervision is needed to override it.

- **Corruption.** For a prompt x with unbiased gold label y* (the neutral option), they replace it with a stereotypical label ỹ so that r(x, ỹ) > r(x, y*). Concretely, the canonical corrupted example z̃12 is a BBQ item about "a twenty-something and an old-timer talking about beer" whose gold answer "B (Cannot be determined)" is flipped to "C (The twenty-something)" — rewarding the stereotype that younger people binge-drink.
- **Example selection.** Following Wang et al. (2025) and Razin et al. (2025), they use reward-signal variance as a proxy for influence: they fully flip a 100-example subset, run short GRPO for E = 10 epochs, rank examples by training-accuracy variance, then pick the highest (z̃1, sexual orientation), lowest (z̃100, disability), a random one (z̃12, age), and also z̃2 (appearance) and z̃40 (gender) — confirming the effect is not tied to one example.
- **Training.** Built on the verl pipeline (Sheng et al., 2025). KL coefficient β = 0.001, entropy coefficient α = −0.001, rollout temperature 1.0 in vLLM, batch size = mini-batch size = 1, and **128 rollouts sampled per prompt per step**. Full-parameter fine-tuning for models < 7B; LoRA (rank 32, all linear layers) for the 7B/8B models. Learning rate 1e-6 for Llama 3.2 3B and Qwen 2.5 3B, and 1e-5 for Llama 3.1 8B and Qwen 2.5 7B (chosen from {1e-4, 5e-5, 1e-5, 5e-6}; larger rates caused incoherent traces). Reward is r(y) = 0.5·I[format] + 0.5·I[valid] + 2.0·I[correct]. All runs on 4× A40 48GB GPUs.
- **Evaluation.** Five fairness benchmarks: BBQ (Parrish et al., 2022), CrowS-Pairs (Nangia et al., 2020), GenMO (Bajaj et al., 2024), StereoSet (Nadeem et al., 2021), WinoQueer (Felkner et al., 2023). Fairness is the fraction of neutral/unknown answers (Acc = N_unk / N); lower accuracy = higher bias. Reported step = the checkpoint with the lowest BBQ average accuracy. They also use a PRM fairness score (Hall et al., 2025), reported in Appendix F.2.
- **Theory.** A 1D logistic-regression toy model πθ(·|x) with the fair/biased boundary at θbias = 0, initialized at θ0 < 0 (fair). With β = 0, they derive the continuous-time GRPO gradient flow dθ/dt = η ∂L_GRPO/∂θ and track πθt(y=1|x) for different initial margins ‖θ0‖.

## Results & Highlights

- **Catastrophic collapse on BBQ from a single age example.** Qwen 2.5 7B Instruct's BBQ average accuracy drops from **97.17 → 21.38 (−75.79 points)**; Qwen 2.5 3B Instruct from **90.46 → 65.02 (−25.44 points)**; Llama 3.2 3B Instruct from **88.89 → 61.90 (−26.99 points)**; Llama 3.1 8B Instruct from **100.00 → 14.29 (−85.71 points)** (all under one-shot z̃12 training).
- **Cross-category generalization beyond the trained attribute.** Training only on the age example z̃12, Llama 3.2 3B Instruct degrades on every BBQ category — e.g., Sexual Orientation **94.74 → 75.44 (−19.30)**, Race/Ethnicity **87.27 → 63.64 (−23.63)**, Disability **92.50 → 62.50 (−30.00)** — and the most severe single-category drop for Llama 3.1 8B Instruct is Sexual Orientation **96.36 → 21.82 (−74.54)**.
- **Cross-benchmark generalization.** The same one-shot attack degrades Llama 3.2 3B Instruct on CrowS-Pairs **61.00 → 50.75 (−10.25)**, GenMO **89.00 → 61.68 (−27.32)**, StereoSet **90.60 → 80.91 (−9.69)**, and WinoQueer **73.43 → 63.35 (−10.08)**.
- **Initial-bias-likelihood gates the attack.** On the z̃12 prompt, only **3.91%** of 128 Llama 3.2 3B samples are biased, **4.69%** for Qwen 2.5 3B, **35.16%** for Llama 3.1 8B — but Qwen 2.5 7B emits biased output in only **1 of 222×128 rollouts**, taking until step 222, after which training accuracy spikes and validation fairness collapses (a sharp phase transition consistent with the toy model).
- **Larger models generalize bias more strongly.** Within the Qwen family the 7B model shows far larger BBQ collapse (−75.79 avg) than the 3B (−25.44), attributed to richer latent representations of stereotypical associations reactivated once alignment suppression is weakened.
- **Example-independence.** z̃1, z̃2, z̃40, z̃100 (sexual orientation / appearance / gender / disability) all produce analogous cross-category degradation on Llama 3.2 3B Instruct, confirming the phenomenon is not specific to z̃12.
- **Control conditions behave as expected.** Training on the neutral gold label consistently improves fairness; training on an alternative *incorrect* (non-stereotypical) label produces logical inconsistencies between reasoning and answer rather than systematic bias.
- **Generalizes beyond GRPO.** Appendix G reports one-shot results with PPO, showing the same phenomenon for other RLVR algorithms.

## Critical Assessment

- **Step selection favors the attacker.** The reported numbers come from the checkpoint with the *lowest* BBQ average accuracy, which the authors justify with a validation signal from a single category — but this selection rule systematically reports the worst-case fairness rather than a representative checkpoint, potentially overstating steady-state degradation.
- **Weak/no defense baseline.** The paper demonstrates the vulnerability thoroughly but proposes no concrete mitigation; the "future directions" (causal reasoning, meta-learning) are high-level. A reader cannot tell from the experiments whether simple defenses (KL strength increase, reward clipping, format/answer-abstention penalties) would blunt the attack.
- **Threat model is narrow.** The adversary must be able to inject a corrupted label into the GRPO reward computation — an insider or supply-chain setting. The connection to end-user fine-tuning on HuggingFace is argued rhetorically rather than demonstrated, since open fine-tuning typically uses SFT/DPO, not GRPO with a verifiable reward.
- **Small-model scope.** All four models are ≤ 8B and instruction-tuned; the central "larger models generalize bias more strongly" claim rests on only two Qwen sizes (3B vs 7B) and is left as future work, so it may not extrapolate to 70B+ frontier models with different alignment recipes.
- **Reward design may amplify the effect.** The reward gives +2.0 for a correct (corrupted) answer versus only +0.5 for format/validity, so a single corrupted example dominates the advantage signal — an artifact that may exaggerate the "one shot is enough" framing relative to denser multi-objective reward designs used in production alignment.

## Strengths

- Striking, memorable finding with a clean minimal-intervention design that is easy to communicate and reproduce.
- Cross-attribute and cross-benchmark evidence (5 fairness datasets, 4 models, 5 different corrupted examples) makes the generalization claim well-supported rather than anecdotal.
- The logistic-regression toy model and the "initial margin → phase transition" account give an interpretable mechanism for the empirical training dynamics, not just a black-box result.
- Honest controls (neutral-label training, alternative-incorrect-label training, multiple example selections) that rule out trivial alternative explanations.
- Directly relevant to open-ecosystem safety (HuggingFace redistribution) and to insider-attack threat models, with real-world incident citations.

## Limitations

- No proposed defense or mitigation is evaluated; the paper is diagnostic rather than prescriptive.
- Evaluation is limited to 3B–8B instruction-tuned models; behavior of larger or differently-aligned (e.g., Constitutional AI, RLAIF) models is untested.
- Step selection uses the worst fairness checkpoint, which may overstate average-case risk in deployment.
- The reward function is hand-designed for verifiable single-answer BBQ-style items; whether the one-shot phenomenon survives richer, multi-turn, or non-verifiable reward settings is unaddressed.
- The theoretical toy model uses β = 0, whereas the experiments use β = 0.001, so the match between theory and experiment is qualitative only.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Would simply raising the KL coefficient β (currently 0.001) or adding an answer-abstention reward term meaningfully blunt one-shot bias injection, given that the reward design gives +2.0 for the corrupted answer?
- The authors note larger models generalize bias more strongly based only on Qwen 2.5 3B vs 7B; does this monotonic trend hold at 70B+ scale and across families with different alignment recipes?
- The paper's threat model requires injecting a corrupted label into a GRPO verifiable-reward loop — how does the phenomenon change under non-verifiable reward settings (e.g., the RLAIF/DPO family the authors cite via Lee et al. 2024)?
- The toy model matches experiment qualitatively at β = 0 while experiments use β = 0.001 — can the closed-form analysis be extended to non-zero KL to predict the step-222 "discovery" point for Qwen 2.5 7B Instruct?
- Appendix G shows PPO exhibits the same effect; do the cited RLVR variants (Zheng et al. 2025 geometric-mean PO, Zhao et al. 2026 group sequence PO, Liu et al. 2026 GDPO) differ in their susceptibility, and could any be structurally more resistant to one-shot corruption?
