Feedback alignment fails in deep networks because its gradients collapse in rank; orthogonalising the update and normalising hidden activity lifts that rank, recovering up to +9 percentage points.

Overcoming Rank Collapse in Feedback Alignment Gauthier Boeshertz · Razvan Pascanu · Claudia Clopath 2026-06-09

Why this matters

Reveals a geometric root cause of why biologically plausible learning rules underperform in deep networks — and points to gradient shape, not just magnitude, as the lever that matters.

Critical edge

Still ~36pp below backprop on the same model. Diagnosis is purely empirical, and Muon's orthogonalisation adds nontrivial compute per step.

Methodology

1. Diagnose

Train 1–4 layer CNNs on CIFAR10 with BP and FA, then measure five geometric signals:

  • Weight alignment — cosine similarity of forward & feedback weights
  • Gradient alignment — cosine similarity of FA vs. BP gradients
  • Gradient effective rank — exponentiated entropy of normalised singular values
  • Gradient trajectory rank — effective rank of the Gram matrix of stacked gradients
  • Feature dimensionality — effective rank of layer activations

FA gradients are markedly lower-rank than BP, and the rank collapses further as training progresses.

2. Intervene

Muon. Replace the update with W ← W − η·Orth(M), where M is the EMA gradient and Orth(·) is the SVD projection (Newton–Schulz approximation). All singular values become 1 — rank collapse is structurally impossible.

Hidden activity normalisation. Standard BatchNorm on hidden activations. Higher-dimensional activations yield richer gradient signals.

3. Evaluate

CIFAR10 (4-layer CNN) · STL-10 (AlexNet) · CIFAR100 (ResNet-18) · Tiny ImageNet (ResNet-18). Baselines: SGD+momentum and AdamW.

Effective gradient rank stays elevated throughout training when Muon / activity norm is used. Both methods are additive, supporting the hypothesis that high-dimensional update geometry is the unifying mechanism.

Strengths

  • Compelling diagnosis. Rank collapse is a clean, well-supported explanation for FA's depth failure.
  • Two complementary fixes that validate the hypothesis from independent angles.
  • Breadth of evidence across CNN, ResNet-18, AlexNet and four datasets.
  • Clean ablations — each intervention alone and combined, against SGD-m and AdamW.
  • Insightful bridge between Muon's orthogonalisation mechanism and FA's geometry.

Limitations

  • FA–BP gap not closed on harder benchmarks; absolute accuracy remains far from BP.
  • No theory for why FA gradients are low-rank — the analysis is purely empirical.
  • Compute overhead from Muon's SVD / Newton–Schulz step per update.
  • Image-classification only — no NLP, RL, or other domain evidence.
  • No modern-scale evidence — no ImageNet or LLM-scale experiments.

Questions for further exploration

  1. Does the rank-collapse diagnosis transfer to other biologically plausible rules — target propagation, equilibrium propagation?
  2. Can Muon + activity norm close the FA–BP gap on larger-scale tasks (ImageNet, language modelling)?
  3. What is the theoretical reason FA gradients have lower effective rank than BP?
  4. How does Muon's per-step compute overhead compare to its benefits in wall-clock training time?
  5. Are there other interventions that further raise the dimensionality of FA gradients?
  6. Does the effect hold under low-precision or distributed training, where the orthogonalisation step may be noisier?
