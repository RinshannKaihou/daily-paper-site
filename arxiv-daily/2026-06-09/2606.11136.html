A conformal framework for dyadic regression on networks — valid beyond exchangeability, and under MNAR missingness.

The paper proves the first finite-sample validity for conformal prediction on jointly exchangeable arrays via a new bijection argument, and the first asymptotic conditional validity for graphon-weighted conformal prediction under a missing-not-at-random (MNAR) mechanism.

Methodology

① Super-uniformity beyond exchangeability (§5)

Proposition 5.1 extends Lei & Bickel's result to transitive group actions. Theorem 5.1 goes further: when the calibration sample is itself a random subset of the index set, the empirical rank of an observation is super-uniform — proved via an explicit measure-preserving bijection between the events {rank = s} and {rank = s+1}. This is the technical keystone the paper rests on.

② Four array-prediction procedures (§4)

  • Full conformal — non-conformity scores over the entire sample; prediction set from Πij > α.
  • Split conformal — train/calibrate split on the dyadic sample.
  • Row-column — exploits Aldous–Hoover latent structure; bivariate ranks (R, C) mapped by optimal transport to a reference measure (unit ball in first quadrant recommended).
  • Selective conformal — mask-conditional validity when a column is chosen adaptively from a network summary statistic (Thm 4.4).

③ Graphon-weighted conformal for missing elements (§6)

Weights (1 − p̂ij)/p̂ij on the weighted rank Πweighted. Graphon estimated via USVT (Chatterjee 2015) or neighborhood smoothing (Zhang–Levina–Zhu 2017). Assumes lower-bounded graphon w(x,y) ≥ c > 0 and sparsity ρn = ω(√(log n)/n).

Strengths

  • Theoretical depth. The bijection argument is elegant and addresses a real gap in conformal prediction theory.
  • Comprehensive toolkit. Full, split, row-column, selective, and graphon-weighted — not a single method.
  • Intellectual honesty. The paper explicitly states what conditional validity requires and what is left for future work.
  • Precise positioning. Section 2.1 distinguishes the contribution from Gui et al., Liang et al., Shao & Zhang, Du et al., Marandon — each with a structural advantage identified.
  • Real-data demonstration. Cora coverage stays at 0.95 even when the masking matrix is learned by a GNN rather than known.

Limitations

  • Graphon assumptions. Lower-bounded graphon and specific sparsity rates may fail in ultra-sparse or heavy-tailed real networks (power-law degree).
  • Conditional validity untuned. Needs consistent non-parametric CDF estimates; tuning is multi-dimensional and not data-driven in the experiments.
  • OT overhead. Row-column optimal transport is heavier than standard conformal, though computed only O(n) times at score jumps.
  • Scale. All experiments use n = 200 – 2 708. Scalability to millions of nodes is not discussed.
  • No multiple-testing framework for groups of missing entries — acknowledged as future work.
  • No SOTA link-prediction comparison. Focus is uncertainty-quantification validity, not predictive accuracy vs. GNNs / matrix factorization.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty — High. The bijection argument is genuinely new; the authors articulate exactly why prior proof strategies fail and give a constructive measure-preserving correspondence. Conditional validity under MNAR (Thm 6.2) fills a real gap left by Gui et al. 2023 and Liang et al. 2026.
  • Rigor — Very high. 64 pages, full proofs in the appendix, assumptions stated explicitly with examples. The remark on why Prop 5.1's proof does not generalize to Thm 5.1 is a sign of honesty.
  • Reproducibility — Moderate. Sim parameters, DGPs, and dataset are public; no code release mentioned; OT implementation details deferred to Appendix A.5.2.
  • Significance. Statistics theory: high. Mainstream ML: moderate — distribution-free guarantees for link prediction, not empirical SOTA.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. Can the bijection argument (Thm 5.1) be extended to approximate distributional invariance, yielding explicit coverage-gap bounds analogous to Barber et al. (2023)?
  2. How does the graphon-weighted procedure behave on real-world sparse networks (power-law degrees) where the lower-bounded graphon assumption fails?
  3. Could the row-column optimal-transport approach combine with GNN-based non-conformity scores for link prediction, and would it beat standard conformalized GNNs (Huang et al. 2023)?
  4. What is the optimal data-driven tuning for the conditional validity regime (Thm 6.2)? Can cross-validation or conformal-of-conformal approaches suffice?
  5. Can the framework be extended to dynamic / temporal networks, leveraging the cyclic-stationarity link the conclusion hints at?
