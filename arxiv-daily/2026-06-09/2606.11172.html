Predicting Future Behaviors in Reasoning Models Enables Better Steering
Evgenii Kortukov · Piotr Komorowski · Florian Klein · Paula Engl · Gabriele Sarti · Seong Joon Oh · Sebastian Lapuschkin · Wojciech Samek
reasoning-models activation-steering representation-engineering test-time-compute LLM-control
Probe accuracy
64–91%
at predicting final behavioral outcomes from intermediate reasoning-chain activations.
Why it matters
FPCG steers reasoning-model behavior with minimal quality loss, giving practitioners a practical handle on safer test-time deployment of large reasoning models.
Critical edge
Sampling-based steering has unquantified compute cost; generalization across model families, tasks, and reasoning-chain lengths remains unverified.

Methodology

  1. Detection vs. prediction features. Re-examine what prior activation-steering methods are actually reading — past behavior (detection) or future behavior (prediction) — and formalize the distinction in reasoning-model representations.
  2. Probe training. Train linear activation probes on intermediate reasoning-chain steps to predict the likelihood of specific final behavioral outcomes (e.g., unsafe responses, hallucinations).
  3. FPCG (Future Probe Controlled Generation). At each generation step, sample multiple sentence-level candidate continuations, score each with the trained prediction probe, and select the continuation most aligned with the desired outcome.

Results & highlights

Finding Evidence
Prediction probes forecast behavior from intermediate activations 64–91% accuracy
Detection-based features (prior work) are weak forward predictors Poor on forecasting
FPCG output quality vs. unsteered baseline Almost no degradation
FPCG on evaluations where activation steering fails Succeeds

Strengths

  • Conceptual distinction (detection vs. prediction) reframes prior activation-steering work
  • Strong probe accuracy (64–91%) signals genuine predictive info in intermediate layers
  • Practical test-time steering method with minimal quality loss
  • Wins on evaluations where prior activation steering completely fails

Limitations

  • Multi-candidate sampling at every step — compute overhead is unquantified
  • Probe generalization across model families and tasks is unknown
  • "Almost no quality degradation" needs scrutiny across diverse benchmarks
  • Sentence-level granularity may be too coarse for fine-grained control

Questions for further exploration

  1. What is FPCG's compute overhead vs. activation steering, and can it be reduced?
  2. Do prediction probes transfer across model families (e.g., Qwen → DeepSeek-R1)?
  3. Can prediction features be combined with activation steering for stronger control?
  4. How does probe accuracy scale with reasoning-chain length?
  5. Does FPCG extend to multi-turn and agentic reasoning scenarios?
