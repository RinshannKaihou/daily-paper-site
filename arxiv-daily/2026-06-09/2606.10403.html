Methodology

§ 01
  • Benchmark 664 Korean CSAT math items (2014–2025). 339-item core set carries official nationwide per-item error rates; reformulated multiple-choice → short-answer to suppress guessing.
  • Difficulty Easy 148 (0–50% cohort error) · Medium 113 (51–75%) · Hard 78 (76–100%). Cohort error rate carries nearly 2× the explanatory power of examiner-assigned point values (R²=0.27 vs 0.15).
  • Evaluation VLMs on original Korean exam images; LLMs on OCR-extracted text. Equivalence judged by GPT-5, blinded to model identity and TTS configuration.
  • TTS knob wo. TTS = force-non-think, answer-only. w. TTS = open-ended reasoning prompt. 22 model families across open-weight VLMs/LLMs and closed APIs.
  • DRG metric DRG = (SCFHard,off − SCFHard,on) − (SCFEasy,on − SCFEasy,off) · score-orthogonal (r=+0.34 with accuracy) · locks in at first TTS turn-on (r=+0.94 across budget tiers).

Results & Highlights

§ 02
↗
Anti-scaling on Hard items. Token spend grows linearly with cohort error (r=0.59, ~102 tokens per pp), but accuracy gain is non-monotonic — peaking at moderate difficulty and collapsing on the hardest tier.
↓
Collapse–recovery pattern. Most models collapse to single-digit accuracy under low decoding budget and recover to 60–99% once TTS is enabled. HyperCLOVA-X-Seed-5: ~1% → 87%.
✕
Overthinking on Easy items. On items below 60% cohort error, TTS degrades accuracy in 2–5% of items across three families. GPT-OSS-120B answers correctly in 576 tokens wo. TTS; fails in 5 / 5 trials at 3,000–4,000 tokens w. TTS.
★
Frontier models approach / surpass cohort. Gemini-3-Pro-Preview: 99.4% (slope 0.01, intercept −3.8). GPT-5.1: 93.2%. Claude-Opus-4.5: 87.6%.

Strengths

what works
  • Real behavioral ground truth. Per-item difficulty from ~500K Suneung examinees per year — not heuristic ratings.
  • Principled DRG metric. Score-orthogonal (r=+0.34), locks in at first TTS turn-on (r=+0.94), reveals what accuracy cannot.
  • Three-regime framing. Clean conceptual lens: no-reasoning → human-aligned → below-cohort.
  • Robustness analyses. 5-run repeated decoding (median Std = 0.014); k=10 fold stability on Hard tier (r=0.975).
  • Dual modality. VLMs on original Korean images, LLMs on OCR — both pipelines covered.
  • Practical cost lens. Dollar-per-correct-answer estimates included.

Limitations

what to watch
  • Narrow scope. Korean CSAT math only — short-answer, Korean notation, specific topic distribution. Generalization untested.
  • Small Hard tier. 78 items; susceptibility to overfitting as models iterate on the same set.
  • Heterogeneous TTS. Some families swap checkpoints, others only adjust budget — strictly causal claims limited.
  • Bounded judge noise. LLM-judged answer equivalence is not perfect (mitigated by blinding, not eliminated).
  • No causal mechanism. Measures what fails, not why anti-scaling occurs.
  • No remediation. Doesn't explore how to train or decode to improve DRG.

Questions for Further Exploration

open ends
  1. Can adaptive TTS strategies (budget ∝ estimated item difficulty) mitigate both anti-scaling and overthinking simultaneously?
  2. Does the DRG framework extend to non-math domains — code generation, logical reasoning — where human difficulty signals are available?
  3. Is anti-scaling on hard items a failure of exploration (not searching enough) or exploitation (getting trapped in wrong solution paths)?
  4. How does DRG relate to item response theory (IRT) difficulty parameters — redundant or complementary?
  5. Could curriculum training that progressively increases difficulty improve DRG without sacrificing accuracy?
  6. What happens to DRG when models are fine-tuned on the KCSAT-ML distribution — alignment, or new pathologies?
