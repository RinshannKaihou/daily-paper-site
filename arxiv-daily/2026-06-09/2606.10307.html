2606.10307 Multi-Agent LLM Debate · Reasoning Evaluation

The first 3 tokens a model generates predict its reasoning quality better than any later signal.

Early-Token Confidence Predicts Reasoning Quality in Multi-Agent LLM Debate Ali Keramati · Justin Cheok · Jacob Horne · Mark Warschauer  ·  UC Irvine  ·  2026-06-09
NSF Grant No. 2315294
multi-agent confidence debate uncertainty reasoning-eval
Key Metric
0.849AUROC
Detecting Advocate critical failures on ASAP Set 7 using the max of the first 3 tokens' log-probabilities.
Why This Matters
A lightweight intrinsic signal for reasoning quality in multi-agent LLM systems — no extra model calls, just collect log-probs during generation.
Critical Edge
Judge scores saturate 93.1% at maximum for evidence grounding, and correlations stay moderate (ρ ≈ 0.2–0.4) — informative, not decisive.
01 Methodology

Debate Framework

Three GPT-4o-mini agents — Advocate, Skeptic, Synthesizer-Judge — score each essay–trait pair from ASAP Sets 7 & 8.

Confidence Signals

Token-level log-probs collected during decoding. Windowed statistics (mean, std, range, slope) over first/last k tokens and first/last α% of response.

LLM-as-Judge

GPT-5-mini scores each agent on instruction following, justification quality, evidence grounding (1–3 each) plus a critical issue flag. Aggregate Q ∈ [0, 9].

Analysis

Spearman's ρ & Kendall's τ for ordinal targets; AUROC & point-biserial for critical failure detection; normalized trajectory interpolation with percentile bands.

Strengths

  • Trajectory analysis with percentile bands explains why early tokens dominate.
  • Advocate–Skeptic asymmetry is novel and traced to distinct failure modes (continuous overstatement vs binary instruction violations).
  • Lightweight signal: only log-prob collection, no extra model calls.
  • Honest about moderate correlations and narrow experimental scope.
  • Detailed qualitative failure analysis complements the quantitative findings.

Limitations

  • Narrow scope: 2 ASAP sets, 1 generation model, 1 judge model.
  • Correlations are moderate (ρ ≈ 0.2–0.4) — useful auxiliary, not standalone.
  • Judge score concentration (93.1% at max) limits discriminative power.
  • No human validation of the meta-evaluator scores.
  • Skeptic AUROC ≈ 0.63 is informative but not operationally useful.
  • No comparison to semantic entropy, self-evaluation, or other confidence methods.
03 Questions for Further Exploration
  1. Does early-token confidence predict reasoning quality in non-debate settings such as chain-of-thought or tool use?
  2. Would fine-tuning the meta-evaluator to reduce score concentration improve correlation with confidence signals?
  3. Can early-token confidence be used dynamically to trigger re-generation or agent switching inside a multi-agent system?
  4. Does the Advocate–Skeptic asymmetry generalize to other adversarial or cooperative multi-agent architectures?
  5. How robust are these findings across different language models and task domains beyond essay scoring?
