> **一句话总结：** 本文将 2016 年 American Causal Inference Conference (ACIC) 数据竞赛改造为一个要求编写可执行 R 脚本进行因果效应估计的 LLM 基准，让 ChatGPT Codex 5.2 生成 20 份脚本与 9 位博士级人类专家提交对比；结果人类专家在 RMSE、标准化偏差、覆盖率和区间长度四项指标上均显著优于 LLM，且 LLM 输出方差高达数十亿量级（人类仅 0.029 量级），5 份脚本出现 10⁸–10¹¹ 倍标准差量级的灾难性误差，直接质疑"LLM 已达博士专家水平"的说法。

> **TL;DR:** The authors repurpose the 2016 ACIC causal-inference data competition (7,700 simulated datasets, known ground-truth SATT) as a code-writing benchmark and generate 20 executable R scripts from ChatGPT Codex 5.2, comparing them against 9 PhD-level human expert submissions on RMSE, standardized bias, 95% interval coverage, and interval length. On RMSE the human experts' submissions have standard deviation 0.029 while Codex 5.2's is 5.82×10¹⁰ — a gap of roughly 12 orders of magnitude; two Codex scripts (LLM:1, LLM:2) hit RMSE of 219 billion and 112 billion outcome-SDs versus the best human (BART-family) RMSE ≈0.05; 3 of 20 Codex scripts (15%) failed to run at all due to coding errors. The strongest baseline (BART, top human performer) dominates every Codex submission, including the 8 that used the `grf` causal-forest R package.

## 核心贡献

- 设计了一个抗污染的 LLM 基准：复用 2016 ACIC 因果推断竞赛，要求模型根据提示编写单一可执行 R 脚本分析 7,700 个数据集（脚本与代码从未公开，降低训练数据泄漏风险）。
- 将评估指标从二元正确率扩展为四项连续指标（RMSE、标准化偏差、95% 覆盖率、区间长度），从而可同时测量**平均表现、方差**与**错误幅度**。
- 实证对比 ChatGPT Codex 5.2（20 份脚本）与 9 位博士级专家 + 6 个历史"稻草人"方法，提供模型与人类专家在真实数据分析任务上的直接、定量比较。
- 揭示 LLM 输出方差的灾难性量级：5 份脚本产生 10⁸–10¹¹ 倍标准差的 RMSE，3 份脚本因编码错误无法运行，方差比人类专家高出约 12 个数量级。
- 指出当前 LLM 基准（如 GDPVal）忽视方差与错误严重性，并用此解释"基准表现好但实际部署差"的鸿沟（引用 MIT NANDA 报告：95% 试点项目无投资回报）。

## Key Contributions

- Introduces a contamination-resistant LLM benchmark by reviving the 2016 ACIC data competition: contestants (and the LLM) must write a single executable R script from a prompt to estimate the sample average treatment effect (SATT) on 7,700 simulated datasets whose code and answers were never public, so memorization is unlikely.
- Extends LLM evaluation beyond binary accuracy to four continuous metrics — RMSE, standardized bias, 95% interval coverage, and interval length — enabling simultaneous measurement of *average performance, response variance, and error magnitude*.
- Provides a direct, ground-truth-validated comparison between 20 ChatGPT Codex 5.2 scripts and 9 PhD-level human-expert submissions (plus 6 historical strawman methods), exploiting the competition's known true SATT.
- Documents catastrophic LLM variance: five Codex scripts produced RMSE in the range 10⁸–10¹¹ outcome-standard-deviations, three scripts (15%) failed to execute, and the cross-script standard deviation of Codex RMSE (5.82×10¹⁰) is about 12 orders of magnitude larger than the human experts' (0.029).
- Argues, citing the GDPVal benchmark [8], the ACIC competition, and the MIT NANDA finding that 95% of industry LLM pilots yield no ROI [45], that ignoring variance and error magnitude explains the gap between strong benchmark scores and weak real-world deployments.

## Methodology

**Benchmark design.** The study asks: how would ChatGPT Codex 5.2 have performed in the 2016 ACIC data competition? The competition gave teams of statisticians a prompt (reproduced in Appendix A) instructing them to write a single executable R script that takes a CSV data file and an output filename, reads a binary treatment (col 1), continuous outcome (col 2), and 58 covariates (cols 3+), and writes the estimated SATT with a 95% interval. The 7,700 datasets share 58 real covariates but have synthetically generated (and thus ground-truth-known) treatment and outcome variables, varying in nonlinearity, effect heterogeneity, treated/control ratio, overlap, confounder dimensionality, and effect magnitude. Contestants never saw the data or each other's code, so the task is largely contamination-free.

**Models and setup.** Only OpenAI ChatGPT Codex 5.2 is evaluated, because OpenAI is the only vendor explicitly claiming "PhD-level" expertise; Codex 5.2 was the most advanced model available at the time and is "explicitly optimized for the programming and statistical tasks." A footnote notes pilot testing confirmed Codex 5.2 beat ChatGPT 5.2 Pro. The authors generated 20 independent scripts from Codex 5.2 using the verbatim original competition prompt (no prompt engineering), then ran each script on all 7,700 datasets. Three of the 20 scripts (15%) failed to execute due to coding errors; the remaining 17 are scored.

**Comparison groups.** (1) 9 original PhD-level human expert submissions; (2) 6 historical "strawman" methods (naïve/older baselines like linear models, CBPS, teffects ipw); (3) 9 post-hoc human submissions created by organizers after the competition combining best-performing features (e.g., BART with multiple chains), now recognized as best practice.

**Metrics (all averaged over the 7,700 datasets, outcome-SD-normalized):**
- RMSE = √((1/7700)Σ((SATTᵢ − SATTᵢ)/σ_y,i)²);
- standardized bias = (1/7700)Σ((SATTᵢ − SATTᵢ)/σ_y,i);
- coverage = proportion of 95% intervals containing the true SATT (ideal 0.95);
- interval length = (1/7700)Σ((ucᵢ − lcᵢ)/σ_y,i).

Three of the four (RMSE, bias, length) are continuous, enabling variance/magnitude analysis absent from binary benchmarks.

**What Codex 5.2 did.** Of the 17 runnable scripts, 8 (scripts 4,5,7,8,9,10,15,16 — 40% of all 20) used the `grf` R package to implement generalized causal forests [Athey-Wager 2019, Wager-Athey 2018]; the other 9 (scripts 1,2,3,6,11,12,14,17,19) attempted to implement IPTW/AIPTW from scratch *without* an external R package. No Codex submission implemented BART, the consistent top performer. Appendix B annotates the IPTW/AIPTW scripts: they fit *linear* propensity and outcome models despite the prompt stating the assignment/response surfaces are non-linear, mis-specified the AIPTW estimator formula, and used only B=200 bootstrap replicates (vs. ≥1000 recommended) for intervals.

## Results & Highlights

All numbers below are outcome-SD-normalized and copied from §3 (Figures 1–3) and the Discussion:

1. **Catastrophic RMSE outliers (Fig. 1 caption):** Codex 5.2 scripts 14, 19, 11, 2, and 1 had RMSE of **3.07, 1160.05, 2572.96, 111,765,692,519 (≈1.12×10¹¹), and 219,316,810,584 (≈2.19×10¹¹)** outcome-SDs respectively. For context the paper notes 0.8 SDs is "a large effect size" (Cohen [56]); the best human BART-family submissions have RMSE ≈0.05. So the worst Codex script is ≈10¹²× worse than the best human.
2. **Variance gap (§3):** Across submissions, human PhD experts had standard deviation **0.029 (RMSE), 0.012 (standardized bias), and 1.76 (interval length)**; Codex 5.2 had standard deviations **5.82×10¹⁰ (RMSE), 7.92×10⁸ (standardized bias), and 1.83×10¹⁰ (interval length)** — about 12, 11, and 10 orders of magnitude larger respectively. Even after removing the two largest RMSE outliers, Codex SDs remain **709 (RMSE), 8.67 (bias), 165 (interval length)** — still 4–5 orders of magnitude above humans.
3. **Standardized bias (Fig. 2 caption):** Codex scripts 14, 19, 11, 2, 1 had bias of **−2.8, 4.79, 30.83, 1.75×10⁹, and −2.65×10⁹** SDs; the eight causal-forest scripts (4,5,7,8,9,10,15,16) beat the lower-tier human submissions on bias but still underperform the majority of human entries.
4. **Interval coverage/length (Fig. 3 caption & §3):** Codex script 1 achieved ≈0.95 coverage but only because its interval length exceeded **65 billion SDs**; the causal-forest scripts had lower coverage *and* larger intervals than most human submissions. Codex interval lengths for scripts 14,19,11,2,1 were **3.34, 294.83, 586.90, 234,300.74, and 7.53×10¹⁰** SDs; three human submissions (CBPS=0.78, LASSO+CBPS=4.97, H2O Ensemble=6.08) were also removed as outliers but are 7–9 orders of magnitude smaller.
5. **Execution failures:** 3 of 20 Codex scripts (15%) could not run due to coding errors; all human expert scripts executed.

The paper reports *No quantitative results reported* is false here — the above are the paper's results; the paper has no held-out test-accuracy comparison beyond these four competition metrics.

## Critical Assessment

- **Single-model evaluation is a confound.** Only ChatGPT Codex 5.2 is tested; the authors justify this ("OpenAI is the only LLM developer to explicitly claim PhD-level expertise"), but the title "Flaws in the LLM Automation Narrative" generalizes from n=1 model. A stronger version would have included Claude/Gemini frontier coding models to separate "this model fails" from "LLMs fail."
- **Variance is inflated by a few catastrophic scripts, not reported per-method.** The headline 5.82×10¹⁰ RMSE standard deviation is driven by 5 scripts; the causal-forest subset (8 scripts) is "reasonably well" on RMSE/bias and the authors concede (Discussion) that 8/20 = 40% "produced results nearly as good as the best human experts with respect to RMSE and bias." A median/IQR report would better reflect typical-case behavior, which the paper omits.
- **No prompt-engineering ablation.** The Discussion acknowledges this objection ("with sufficient prompt engineering and scaffolding…") but dismisses it on the grounds that better prompts would reflect the human prompt-writer's expertise, not the LLM's — a defensible but untested position. No experiment varies the prompt, temperature, or scaffolding (e.g., telling Codex to use BART).
- **The `grf`-vs-from-scratch split is informative but uncontrolled.** The paper observes that Codex did well only when it could call a polished R package (`grf`) and failed when implementing IPTW/AIPTW from scratch, but it does not run a controlled version (e.g., forcing Codex to use a BART package) to test whether package availability is the operative variable.
- **Polemical framing risks selection bias.** The title and §1 are adversarial toward LLM vendors; while the underlying analysis is quantitative, the framing could incline readers (and possibly the authors) toward tasks where LLMs fail. The choice of a 2016 competition favoring BART — a method no frontier code model defaults to — is one such potential bias.
- **Reference frame is 10-year-old state-of-the-art.** The authors note the human submissions "were based on the state-of-the-art ten years ago," yet Codex still loses; this strengthens the conclusion but also means the benchmark may under-represent methods a 2026 LLM might prefer.

## Strengths

- Genuine ground-truth benchmark: the 7,700 ACIC datasets have known true SATT, so error magnitudes are objectively measurable — rare among LLM benchmarks.
- Strong contamination resistance: the competition code and answers were never public, sidestepping the memorization critique that plagues MMLU/HellaSwag/GDPVal.
- Four continuous metrics (not binary accuracy) expose variance and error magnitude that binary benchmarks hide — a methodologically valuable contribution regardless of the LLM-vs-human conclusion.
- Direct PhD-expert comparison with low human variance (SD 0.029) provides a credible reference standard.
- Reproducible appendices: all 20 Codex scripts with error annotations (Appendix B) and the verbatim prompt (Appendix A) are included.

## Limitations

- Single frontier model (Codex 5.2 only); generalization to "LLMs" is unsupported.
- No prompt-engineering, temperature, or scaffolding ablations.
- Variance dominated by a handful of catastrophic scripts; per-method and median statistics not reported.
- Benchmarks a 2016 causal-inference task that structurally favors BART, which no LLM chose to implement — a possible task-selection bias.
- No code repository or executable harness released (only static Appendix B); independent re-execution requires reconstruction.
- Preprint, not peer-reviewed; no external validation of the script-execution results.

## Relevance

This paper maps directly to the configured interest area **(2) LLM Inference Evaluation**: it is a methodology-and-empirics paper that critiques current LLM benchmarking practice (binary accuracy, contamination-prone) and proposes continuous-metric, variance-aware evaluation with a human-expert reference standard. It secondarily touches **(1) LLM Training Stability & Observability** insofar as it documents extreme run-to-run output variance as a reliability defect, but it is not about training dynamics, hidden states, precision errors/SDC, system cards, or self-evolving agents.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How do other frontier coding models (Claude, Gemini) perform on the same ACIC prompt, and do they also avoid BART in favor of `grf`/IPTW — i.e., is the failure model-specific or a class effect?
- The paper's Discussion floats but does not test prompt engineering: does explicitly scaffolding Codex (e.g., "use the `bartMachine` R package, run 4 chains") close the gap to top human submissions, or do implementation errors persist?
- The variance is driven by 5 catastrophic scripts: what is the *median* and interquartile RMSE across the 17 runnable Codex scripts, and does the causal-forest subset (8 scripts) actually match mid-tier humans as the Discussion concedes?
- Does forcing Codex to use a mature BART R package (a controlled counterpart to the `grf`-vs-scratch observation) reproduce the human top performers, supporting the "package maturity is the operative variable" hypothesis?
- The paper cites [45] (MIT NANDA, 95% of pilots no ROI) and [58] (AWS 13-hour outage from AI-generated code): can the ACIC error-magnitude methodology be extended to estimate the *frequency* of catastrophic coding errors in deployed agents, connecting to the reliability/SDC literature?
- Reference [19] (Nature 2026, frontier LLMs answer only 10% vs human 90% on a contamination-free expert benchmark) is cited as motivating evidence: does combining that binary-accuracy result with this paper's variance/magnitude metrics yield a unified reliability scorecard for LLM evaluation?
