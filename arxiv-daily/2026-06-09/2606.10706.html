Unifying Data, Memory, and Compute Efficiency in LLM training: A Survey

Vanessa Schmidt · Huy Hoang Nguyen · Cédric Jung · Shirin Salehi · Anke Schmeink arXiv:2606.10706 RWTH Aachen · AIT Austrian Institute of Technology · TU Wien

Key Metric

3

Coupled efficiency axes · data · memory · compute

Why This Matters

Provides a unifying framework to understand LLM training bottlenecks and design efficient strategies across coupled resources.

Critical Edge

Framework is descriptive, not prescriptive — no concrete algorithm for joint optimization or the "Dynamic LESS" vision.

Methodology · Three Coupled Axes

Data Efficiency

Selection and pruning: scalable proxy signals (loss, gradient-norm), influence scoring (LESS, datamodels), difficulty-aware (Rho-1), curriculum learning, redundancy removal.

Memory Efficiency

PEFT (LoRA, adapters), quantization (INT8, FP8), block-wise optimizers (HiFT), zeroth-order methods, activation memory reduction. Often the dominant fine-tuning bottleneck.

Compute Budget

Chinchilla-style scaling, mixture-of-experts, speculative decoding, early exit, stopping rules on marginal gain — re-allocating FLOPs across training phases.

Results & Highlights

Finding Implication
Different notions of "good data" dominate in different regimes Optimal subsets depend on task objective and resource budget — no universal recipe exists.
Fine-tuning is the primary mechanism for rapid adaptation Fine-tuning efficiency — not just pretraining — is the central research focus going forward.
Static pre-filtering dominates practice today Dynamic, influence-aware selection during training is the critical open gap (Static → Dynamic).
Isolated optimizations shift bottlenecks, they do not resolve them Aggressive data pruning reduces iterations but inflates memory needed for influence computation.
The optimization trilemma is a physical necessity at the edge Edge intelligence cannot escape the joint data–memory–compute constraint.

Strengths

  • First survey to explicitly unify data, memory, and compute efficiency under a single constraint-centric framework.
  • Clear identification of the Static → Dynamic gap as a concrete research direction.
  • Marginal-utility principle gives practitioners a usable decision lens across techniques.
  • Surfaces interdependencies that isolated method papers routinely ignore.

Limitations

  • Breadth traded for depth — some methods lack implementation detail.
  • Framework is descriptive, not prescriptive; no joint-optimization algorithm is supplied.
  • Pretraining efficiency is under-treated relative to fine-tuning.
  • "Dynamic LESS" vision remains aspirational without a concrete proposal.

Questions for Further Exploration

  1. How can dynamic data selection re-evaluate influence during training without prohibitive memory or compute overhead?
  2. What are the quantitative trade-offs between data quality, memory usage, and compute allocation across model scales and tasks?
  3. Can the marginal-utility principle be operationalized into an automated training-budget allocation system?
  4. How do the findings generalize to multimodal or multi-task settings where the trilemma's optimum shifts?
  5. What hardware co-design opportunities exist for jointly optimizing data pipelines, memory hierarchies, and compute scheduling?
