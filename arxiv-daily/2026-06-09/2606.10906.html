Human-AI Teaming Through the Lens of Calibration — a theoretical audit of how prediction combination and task delegation propagate (or break) the human's and model's calibration properties.

Better-calibrated models do not yield better human–AI teams — combination destroys human calibration, and delegation demands rejector precision that grows unboundedly with human expertise.

01 · Key Metric
ECE 0.09 → 0.14
Combined predictor's ECE w.r.t. the human's partition Φh — while holding ECE = 0 w.r.t. the model's Φf. Calibration is preserved on one side, broken on the other.
02 · Why This Matters
Naively tightening a model's calibration can degrade downstream team accuracy — a sharp warning for LLM-augmented decision systems and agent delegation pipelines.
03 · Critical Edge
The proof assumes humans are themselves calibrated — an assumption that is famously untrue of human probability judgments in practice.

Methodology 方法

Two teaming frameworks are formalized under a single calibration lens.

  • Combination. A second model g(h, f(x)) merges human and AI predictions, or a Bayesian posterior update blends them via confusion matrices (Kerrigan et al., 2021).
  • Delegation / L2D. A rejector r(x) routes each example to either the human or the model (Mozannar & Sontag, 2020).
  • Theory. Both humans and models are assumed calibrated w.r.t. grouping functions Φf and Φh; the analysis asks which properties the combined or delegated predictor inherits.
  • Experiments. Temperature-scaled classifiers sweep calibration on ImageNet-16H (real human labels) and HAM10000 (simulated humans with controlled feature access).

Strengths 优点

  • Unified lens. First framework to treat combination and delegation under the same calibration theory.
  • Sharp negative results. The over-sharpening of Bayesian combination near the Bayes predictor is non-obvious and practically consequential.
  • Clean feature-space split. Shared vs. independent features are separated, isolating why the calibration–accuracy relationship holds in one case and breaks in another.
  • Connects to omnipredictors. Links the problem to multi-calibration, opening a constructive path forward.

Limitations 局限

  • Human calibration is a strong assumption. Real annotators are systematically miscalibrated, and average-case correctness may not carry the proofs through.
  • Delegation is mostly theory. Theorem 4.1 receives limited empirical validation; the unbounded rejector-demand claim is not stress-tested.
  • One human, one model. Multi-expert and ensemble settings are out of scope.
  • No constructive recipe. The paper diagnoses the requirement but does not show how to actually achieve the required rejector calibration at scale.

Questions for Further Exploration 延伸问题

  • How do these results extend to multi-expert teams with more than one human or model?
  • Can multi-calibration of the model w.r.t. both Φf and Φh rescue combination in practice?
  • What recalibration strategies could make a Learning-to-Defer rejector sufficiently fine-grained?
  • Do the same dynamics hold in generative settings — LLM-assisted drafting, retrieval, or open-ended decision support?
  • Does active delegation — choosing what to route — alter the calibration–performance trade-off?
