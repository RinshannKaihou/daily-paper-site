Small LSTMs drift toward criticality during training; large ones don't.

A Mixture Branching Process framework reconciles short-range neural avalanches with long-range 1/fβ noise, and shows the branching parameter approaches unity only in low-capacity networks at the optimal epoch.

Why this matters
A single, analytically tractable branching-process model unifies short-range power-law avalanches with long-range 1/fβ noise — reframing criticality as a capacity-dependent, training-emergent state rather than a fixed property.
Critical edge
Limitation
Restricted to small, single-layer LSTMs on binary sentiment; transfer to Transformers, Mamba, or larger-scale tasks is unverified.

Methodology

Hidden units as a driven branching process

Train LSTMs on 50K IMDb reviews (binary sentiment), sweeping hidden dimension across {8, 16, 32, 48, 64, 80, 96, 112, 128} × 28 random seeds. Each hidden unit is treated as a pseudo-neuron; activities are z-score normalised and binarised to define avalanches.

  • MR estimator on the autocorrelation function yields the branching parameter m.
  • Detrended Fluctuation Analysis produces the spectral exponent β for long-range correlations.
  • ACF fit to C[τ] ~ τ−ηe−τ/τc over two decades.
  • Mixture Branching Process: each review r drives its own process with parameter mr; distribution W(mr) derived analytically from the ACF.

Strengths

Where the argument holds

  • Unified, analytically tractable MBP explains both avalanche statistics and 1/f noise from a single object.
  • Four independent analyses — avalanche, branching, DFA, ACF fit — converge on the same picture.
  • Capacity-dependent criticality is intuitive, well-supported, and consistent across random seeds.
  • Closed-form τs = 3/2 + η directly explains why measured exponents exceed mean-field 3/2.
  • Discrete and continuous Xt definitions give consistent results.

Limitations

Where the claim is thin

  • Single-layer LSTMs only, max 128 hidden units — far from modern architectures.
  • Binary sentiment is a simple task; criticality may behave differently in richer settings.
  • Avalanche threshold θ is manually chosen; affects exponent estimates (authors acknowledge).
  • Criticality–generalisation link is correlational (both peak near optimal epoch), not causal.
  • No head-to-head comparison with GRU, Transformer, or Mamba despite the authors' own suggestion.

Questions for further exploration

  1. Does criticality emerge in Transformer hidden states during pretraining or fine-tuning?
  2. Is the criticality–generalisation link causal, or merely a shared signature of training progress?
  3. Can the branching parameter serve as a training diagnostic — e.g. an early-stopping signal?
  4. Does MBP extend to attention patterns, with "avalanches" defined as activation cascades across layers?
  5. Would regularising training toward m = 1 improve generalisation, or break optimisation entirely?
  6. Why does capacity suppress criticality — a saturation effect, an optimisation bias, or both?
