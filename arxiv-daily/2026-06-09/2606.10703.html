Paper · 2606.10703 · Causal Audit

Standard MoE routing metrics cannot predict causal expert importance. Pruning works because of early-layer redundancy — not because it identifies dispensable experts.

From Observation to Intervention: A Causal Audit of Expert Importance in Mixture-of-Experts Models
Leonard Engmann · Christian Medeiros Adriano · Holger Giese Hasso Plattner Institute, University of Potsdam 2026-06-09
mixture-of-experts interpretability causal-inference model-pruning expert-routing
Key Metric
0/60

observational metrics survived Bonferroni correction across three MoE architectures — every effect size fell below Cohen's d = 0.17.

Why this matters

If the metrics MoE pruning relies on don't track causal expert importance, then current pruning methods work by accident — not by design.

A field-standard assumption deserves a field-level audit.

Critical Edge

Findings are limited to high-redundancy MoE architectures — generalization to sparse, low-redundancy regimes remains untested.

Methodology

A four-stage causal audit across three MoE models.

01 · Profile
Collect population-level routing statistics per expert — utilization rates, activation norms, routing-weight distributions — the standard metrics used by MoE pruning.
02 · Intervene
Perform token-level ablation of individual experts and measure the causal effect on output quality, establishing ground-truth expert importance.
03 · Correlate
Test whether observational metrics predict interventional importance, applying Bonferroni correction across all metric-layer combinations.
04 · Control
Use per-token routing weights as a known causal pathway to verify statistical power — a positive control against underpowered nulls.
Results

No metric predicted importance. One positive control did.

Strengths

  • Clean causal framing that makes implicit assumptions explicit.
  • Positive control rules out the "underpowered study" critique.
  • Testing across three diverse MoE architectures.
  • Clear practical implications for MoE pruning methodology.
  • Honest reporting of null results with appropriate statistical nuance.

Limitations

  • Limited to high-redundancy MoE architectures — sparse regimes untested.
  • Focused only on ablation-style interventions.
  • Single recovered signal at OLMoE's final layer hints at model-specific exceptions.
  • Does not propose a causally-grounded pruning alternative.
Questions for further exploration
  1. Do these null results persist in low-redundancy MoE architectures — e.g., models with fewer experts or tighter capacity factors?
  2. Can causally-grounded interventional metrics be efficiently approximated to build better pruning criteria?
  3. How do the findings extend to soft-routing or continuous expert mixing schemes such as DeepSeek-MoE's shared experts?
  4. Is the early-layer redundancy phenomenon universal across MoE architectures, or specific to the three models tested?
  5. Could active learning or adaptive auditing reduce the cost of interventional audits for practical deployment?
