Catching one in five.

An LLM-as-Judge deployed on a production multi-turn food-ordering agent catches only 22% of human-confirmed defects. The failures are not random: the judge's raw notes perceive the problems, but its rubric routes cross-turn state-tracking issues to the wrong axis — and never triggers an operational failure gate.

Why it matters

High agreement is not adequate detection.

In a transactional agent the most damaging failures are cross-turn state defects — confirm-gate lockout, cart hallucination, escalation lockout. A judge that scores on intent / brand voice / personalization will see them, name them in raw notes, and still mark the round as fine. Treating rubric agreement as quality assurance will miss the class that hurts the most.

Critical edge

Diagnoses, doesn't fix.

Post-hoc analysis of a single production system. The 22% figure comes from one batch. The paper maps the failure mechanism but proposes no validated rubric, no alternative gate, and no replication across domains or rubric designs.

01 / METHOD

How they measured it

方法

Methodology

  1. Ground truth by humans. Exhaustive transcript review across three evaluation batches of the deployed food-ordering agent.
  2. Judge vs. human recall. Compare the LLM-as-Judge's output against human-confirmed defects to measure recall, not just agreement.
  3. Blind-spot taxonomy. Categorize which defect classes the judge catches vs. misses — turn-local vs. cross-turn state.
  4. Mechanism analysis. Inspect raw judge notes and the rubric / routing architecture to localize failure: routing, not perception.
  5. Statistical correction. Apply the Rogan–Gladen estimator to quantify bias in prevalence under measured sensitivity.

Root cause: the rubric exposes only three coarse axes — intent, brand-voice, personalization — with no category for state-tracking, guardrails, or recovery. The operational gate is wired to hard assertions, not rubric scores, so even correct judge reasoning never fires alerts.
02 / ASSESSMENT

Strengths & Limitations

评估

+ Strengths

  • Reframes the metric. Moves LLM-as-Judge evaluation from agreement with humans to recall of real defects — operationally meaningful.
  • Localizes the failure. Routing, not perception: the diagnosis is concrete enough to act on without re-running the whole pipeline.
  • Statistical depth. Rogan–Gladen correction turns "we missed some" into a quantitative 3–6× undercount claim.
  • Practical takeaway. "Regression floor, not a substitute for human review" — a usable rule for any team running a judge in production.

! Limitations

  • One production system. Generalizability to other rubrics and domains is asserted, not tested.
  • Small N of batches. The headline 22% comes from a single batch — confidence intervals are wide.
  • No counterfactual. No controlled experiment comparing alternative rubric designs that include state-tracking axes.
  • No fix. The paper diagnoses the failure; it does not propose or validate a remedy.
03 / OPEN

Questions for further exploration

待解答
  1. Rubric design. Would adding explicit state-tracking, guardrails, and recovery axes materially raise recall of cross-turn defects?
  2. Architectural. Is the routing failure specific to this rubric, or a fundamental limit of coarse-grained axes?
  3. Post-processing. Can per-turn judge notes be routed through a separate "defect classifier" to recover the missed state issues without rewriting the rubric?
  4. Scaling. How does defect recall decay with conversation length — is there a turn-count cliff?
  5. Cost / benefit. What is the cheapest hybrid (sampling + exhaustive human review) that matches full-human recall on this class of defects?
