> **一句话总结：** 本文通过构造基于迭代tanh激活的尖锐局部化bump函数，证明了在有限精度计算条件下，除非采样预算随网络参数与架构规模指数增长，否则任何基于m个采样点的自适应随机算法在L^p范数下的收敛速率都无法超越蒙特卡洛速率O(m^{-1/p})，将先前ReLU网络的学习不可行性下界推广到tanh激活情形。

> **TL;DR:** This is a pure theory paper (no datasets/trained models); its central quantitative result is a lower bound showing that, for a tanh network with input dimension d=15, width B=45, depth L=12, coefficient bound c=2 and machine precision ε_p=10^{-69}, any algorithm using up to m≤10^{71} samples still incurs L^∞ error ε≥0.49 — i.e. uniform recovery is infeasible. Compared against the prior ReLU lower bound of Berner/Grohs/Voigtländer (2023) [4], the tanh bound loses the m^{-1/d} factor and instead yields a strictly positive error floor for p=∞, a qualitatively stronger impossibility in that regime.

## 核心贡献

- 证明了一个根本性下界：在有限精度下，使用m个样本的任何自适应随机算法学习tanh神经网络，在L^p范数下都无法超过蒙特卡洛速率O(m^{-1/p})，除非采样预算随网络架构指数增长。
- 提出了一种通过迭代tanh激活构造尖锐局部化bump函数的新机制，利用了tanh的不动点结构(tanh(ax*)=x*，a>1)。
- 将先前仅适用于ReLU网络的有限精度学习不可行性结果推广到tanh（以及经典sigmoid）激活函数情形——这是一个非平凡的推广，因为tanh缺乏ReLU的分段线性与齐次性。
- 揭示了一种更强的内在不稳定性：存在两个网络，其采样值差异仅为约2·10^{-69}（几乎可忽略），但L^∞距离仍约为0.5，因此即使海量样本也无法稳定地确定底层函数。

## Key Contributions

- Establishes a fundamental lower bound: under finite precision, no adaptive randomized algorithm using m samples can beat the Monte Carlo rate O(m^{-1/p}) in L^p norm when learning tanh networks, unless the sampling budget grows exponentially with the network's parameter/architecture size.
- Develops a novel constructive mechanism for sharply localized bump functions via *iterated* tanh activations σ_a^n(x)=tanh∘⋯∘tanh(ax), exploiting the fixed-point structure tanh(ax*)=x* for a>1; the iterates converge to a step-like limit sign(x)·x* (Lemma 12).
- Extends the finite-precision learning-impossibility results of Berner, Grohs & Voigtländer (2023) [4] — previously limited to ReLU — to the tanh/sigmoidal setting, overcoming the absence of ReLU's homogeneity (ρ(λx)=λρ(x)) and exact compact support.
- Provides an explicit, fully parameterized main theorem (Theorem 6) plus a simplified version (Theorem 1), and an interactive web app (https://learning-tanh-nn.streamlit.app/) that numerically verifies the bound and checks all assumptions.

## Methodology

The paper is purely theoretical (no experiments). It studies the *sampling complexity* of recovering an unknown tanh network u∈U⊂C([0,1]^d,R) from m point evaluations, under a finite-precision quantizer Q_{ε_p} that maps every |x|≤ε_p to zero (Definition 4; Remark 7 notes this covers IEEE 754 arithmetic, whose relative precision is ≈10^{-16}, smallest representable number ≈2^{-1074}).

The construction builds a family of "bump networks" Φ_{y_ℓ,ν} (Theorem 27, Figure 2) in four stages for a network of width B, depth L, ℓ_q-norm bound c, with c̃:=B^{1-2/q}c>1:

1. **Layer 1 — coarse primitives:** 2d neurons build d pairs of shifted, sign-flipped tanh activations centered at y_ℓ, each of the form ±σ((c−b)/B^{1/q}·(x_i−(y_ℓ)_i) ± b/B^{1/q}) with b=tanh(c/2).
2. **Layer 2 — coarse bump + threshold:** averaging over coordinates plus a bias −(c/B^{1+1/q})f(αM) produces a bump positive on y_ℓ+α[−M,M]^d and negative outside y_ℓ+[−M,M]^d (Lemma 20/21).
3. **Middle layers (3…L−1) — sharpening:** iterating σ_{c̃}^{L−3} drives the signal toward a step function sign(z)·x*_{c̃}, with exponential decay of the tail outside the mass cube (Lemmas 12, 18, 19). The fixed-point theory (Lemma 11) and exit-time/attraction estimates (Lemmas 17–19) quantify how many iterations k≥3 are needed for the tail to fall below ε_p.
4. **Final layer — output + shift:** W_L=cνe_1^⊤, b_L=cν·x*_{c̃} selects one neuron and shifts so the bump is ≈x*_{c̃} on its support and ≈0 outside, with ν∈{±1} setting the sign.

The lower bound then follows a pigeonhole/averaging argument: place the bump centers y_ℓ on an s-dimensional grid of spacing 2M with M≈m^{-1/s} (s≤d is an effective-dimension reduction, Remark 9); at least half the bumps avoid all m sample points, so the algorithm receives *identical quantized information* as for the zero function despite the bumps having positive ‖Φ‖_{L^p}. The averaging argument of [4] extends this from deterministic to adaptive randomized algorithms, yielding Theorem 6's bound:

err ≥ c·√((c̃²−1)/(1+4c̃))·(Ω/(2s^s))^{p·s}·m^{−1/p}.

## Results & Highlights

The paper reports no empirical experiments; its "results" are theorems with explicit numerical instantiations. Three concrete quantitative claims:

1. **Specific infeasibility instance (after Theorem 1):** For p=q=∞, c=2, d=15, B=45, L=12, machine precision ε_p=10^{-69}, the admissible sample size is m≤10^{71}, yet the lower bound on the L^∞ error is **ε ≥ 0.49** — making uniform recovery of the network class infeasible even with 10^{71} samples.
2. **Instability magnitude (same paragraph):** For every algorithm using at most 10^{71} samples, there exist two tanh networks whose sampled values differ by an almost-negligible perturbation **2·10^{-69}** while their **L^∞ distance remains of order 0.5** — so reconstruction is necessarily unstable regardless of sample-point choice (a stronger instability than the analytic-interpolation result of [24], which can be stabilized by Chebyshev nodes).
3. **Comparison vs. the ReLU baseline [4, Theorem 2.2]:** The ReLU lower bound contains the additional decay factors c^L·B^{(L−1)(1−2/q)}·s^{-1}·m^{-1/s} and (32s)^{-1-1/p}; after factoring out the common m^{-1/p}, the ReLU setting allows an asymptotically roughly quadratically larger admissible sample count, *but* for p=∞ the ReLU bound still decays with m while the tanh bound (Theorem 1) **remains bounded below by a positive constant** — a qualitatively stronger impossibility in the uniform-norm regime. The m^{-1/d} factor characteristic of [4] does *not* appear in the tanh result.

A secondary robustness claim (Remark 7): the construction remains valid for the much smaller threshold ε_p=10^{-306} with m_max=10^{13}, and a constant-shift variant (b_L=cν) makes the relevant scale O(1) so IEEE 754's relative accuracy ≈10^{-16} applies, yielding even larger admissible m_max and thus *less* feasible learning.

## Critical Assessment

- **Known looseness in the bound (Remark 8):** the natural bias choice b=tanh(c/2) "introduces an undesirable dependence on B^{1/q}/c in (3)"; the alternative scaling b≍cM used in [4] could remove it but "leads to more involved estimates and is therefore left for future work" — so the stated constant is not tight.
- **Secondary looseness (Remark 26):** because 1/r<B^{1−2/q}, the derivative ξ_{c/(rB^{1/q})} may be smaller than one, which "can unfortunately increase the threshold ξ̃_n required to enter the basin of attraction of σ_{c̃}" — another source of non-tightness in the construction's parameter regime.
- **Worst-case, not typical-case:** the impossibility rests on adversarially constructed bump networks; the paper gives no evidence (and the construction provides no reason to expect) that ordinary SGD/Adam-trained tanh networks encounter these pathologies. The gap between the 10^{71}-sample lower bound and practical training is enormous.
- **The finite-precision model is abstract:** although Remark 7 argues coverage of IEEE 754, the proof only uses the threshold property |x|≤ε_p⇒0; subtler floating-point behaviors (catastrophic cancellation, subnormal arithmetic) are not modeled, so the connection to real hardware SDC is qualitative.

## Strengths

- Genuinely novel technical device: iterated-tanh bump construction via the fixed point tanh(ax*)=x*, cleanly analyzed in §4.1 (Lemmas 11–19) — this is the core obstacle that blocked prior sigmoidal extensions.
- Fully explicit main theorem (Theorem 6) with all parameter dependencies, not just an asymptotic statement; accompanied by a reproducible interactive checker.
- Honest, detailed ReLU-vs-tanh comparison (§1, after Theorem 1) explaining *why* the bounds differ structurally (|tanh|≤1 vs. ReLU amplification; loss of homogeneity; loss of m^{-1/d}).
- Self-contained proofs; auxiliary lemmas (arccosh bounds, coarse-bump separation) are proven from scratch.

## Limitations

- Pure theory: no datasets, no trained models, no empirical confirmation that constructed bumps are reachable by realistic learning algorithms.
- Worst-case lower bounds; typical-case behavior of tanh-network learning is not addressed.
- Constants in the bound are loose by the authors' own admission (Remarks 8 and 26).
- The finite-precision quantizer is an idealization; mapping the result to concrete bit-widths / SDC events on real accelerators is left open.

## Relevance

This paper maps directly to two configured interest areas: **(4) Computation Reliability & Precision Errors** — its central premise is that finite-precision arithmetic (the quantizer Q_{ε_p}, IEEE 754) fundamentally limits what can be learned — and **(7) ML Reliability & Mathematical Foundations** — it provides rigorous sampling-complexity lower bounds grounding the reliability of neural-network learning. It is *not* directly about SDC detection (#6) or LLM training/inference, but its finite-precision lens is conceptually adjacent to silent-data-corruption analysis.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- The paper notes (§1) that tanh is "closely related to modern activations such as GELU [19]" used in BERT [11] and GPT [25]: can the iterated-activation bump construction be ported to GELU/SiLU, or do their non-monotonic tails break the fixed-point argument of Lemma 11?
- Remark 8 states the alternative bias b≍cM (the scaling used in the ReLU proof of [4]) could remove the B^{1/q}/c dependence — does carrying out those "more involved estimates" actually tighten the 10^{71}-sample example materially?
- Remark 7 observes that the constant-shift variant (b_L=cν) makes IEEE 754's ≈10^{-16} *relative* accuracy the operative threshold and yields larger m_max: how do the headline numbers (ε≥0.49 etc.) change in that regime, and is there a precision level at which uniform recovery becomes feasible?
- The construction places bumps on an s≤d dimensional active subspace (Remark 9): does choosing the minimal s=1 give the tightest bound, and does this effective-dimension reduction suggest a corresponding *positive* result (an upper-bound algorithm) for low-intrinsic-dimension tanh classes?
- Can the indistinguishability argument be strengthened to *gradient-query* or *value+gradient* models, or does access to derivatives (as used in PINN training [26], which the paper cites as a tanh-heavy application) escape the lower bound?
