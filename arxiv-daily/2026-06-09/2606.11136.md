> **一句话总结：** 本文为网络数据中的二元回归（dyadic regression）问题构建了一套共形预测（conformal prediction）框架，在联合可交换性这一比传统可交换性更弱的分布不变性条件下证明了有限样本有效性，并提出了基于 graphon 加权的共形预测方法，首次在缺失非随机（MNAR）假设下证明了加权共形预测的渐近条件有效性。

> **TL;DR:** This paper develops a conformal prediction framework for dyadic (pairwise) regression on network data under complex missingness. It proves finite-sample validity under joint exchangeability — weaker than standard exchangeability — via a novel bijection argument handling random sampling, and establishes the first asymptotic conditional validity results for graphon-weighted conformal prediction under a missing-not-at-random (MNAR) mechanism.

## 核心贡献

- **超越可交换性的共形预测理论**：建立了在分布不变性条件（弱于可交换性）下的 super-uniformity 结果（定理 5.1）。其核心创新是一个新的双射论证（bijection argument），处理了样本本身是索引集随机子集的情况——这是现有理论无法覆盖的场景，因为标准 rank-identity 论证在观测点可能在群变换下离开样本时会失效。
- **联合可交换数组的共形预测方法族**：提出了四种方法——full conformal、split conformal、利用行/列相似性的 row-column 方法（基于最优传输的多变量分位数），以及实现 mask-conditional validity 的 selective conformal 方法。所有方法均具有有限样本有效性保证。
- **缺失元素的 graphon 加权共形预测**：在非参数 graphon 缺失机制模型下（Assumption 4），建立了 graphon 加权共形预测的渐近无条件有效性（定理 6.1）和渐近条件有效性（定理 6.2）。**据作者所知，这是加权共形预测在 MNAR 假设下的首次形式化条件有效性证明。**
- **理论工具的独立价值**：第 5 节的 super-uniformity 结果具有超越数组预测的独立兴趣，可应用于循环平稳时间序列、旋转不变空间数据、张量观测等群不变但非可交换的场景。

## Key Contributions

- **Super-uniformity beyond exchangeability**: Establishes that conformal prediction remains valid under distributional invariance conditions strictly weaker than exchangeability. The key innovation (Theorem 5.1) is a novel bijection argument that handles the case where the calibration sample is itself a random subset of the index set — a setting not covered by existing theory because standard rank-based proofs break down when observations can leave the sample under group transformations.
- **Four conformal procedures for jointly exchangeable arrays**: (1) full conformal, (2) split conformal, (3) a row-column approach that exploits Aldous–Hoover structure (elements sharing a row/column share a latent variable) via optimal-transport-based multivariate quantiles, and (4) a selective conformal procedure achieving mask-conditional validity when a column is chosen adaptively based on network summary statistics.
- **Graphon-weighted conformal prediction for missing elements**: Under a nonparametric graphon missingness model (Assumption 4), proves asymptotic unconditional validity (Theorem 6.1, requiring sparsity rate ρ_n = ω(√log n / n)) and asymptotic conditional validity given predicted values (Theorem 6.2) for both continuous and discrete responses. This is claimed as the **first formal proof of asymptotic conditional validity for weighted conformal prediction under a missing-not-at-random (MNAR) assumption**.
- **Bridging conformal prediction and network science**: Provides the first finite-sample validity guarantees for conformal prediction under symmetric conditions imposed jointly on data and missingness mechanism, enabling distribution-free uncertainty quantification for link prediction and network-assisted regression.

## Methodology

The paper operates at two levels:

**Level 1 — General theory (Section 5).** The authors develop super-uniformity results for conformal prediction under distributional invariance. Proposition 5.1 extends Lei & Bickel's result for transitive group actions. Theorem 5.1 goes further, handling random sampling: given variables (W_k, ζ_k) where ζ_k indicates inclusion in the sample, and a collection of bijections satisfying distributional invariance (gL ≡^d L), the empirical rank of W_i among sampled elements is super-uniform. The proof constructs an explicit measure-preserving correspondence between events {rank = s} and {rank = s+1}, circumventing the failure of identical-distribution arguments when observations leave the sample.

**Level 2 — Array prediction methods (Section 4) and missing-element prediction (Section 6).**

- **Full/split conformal (§4.1–4.2)**: Non-conformity scores S_{ij} are computed for all sampled elements. The prediction set for test point (k,l) is {y_{kl} : Π_{kl} > α}, where Π_{kl} is the proportion of sampled scores ≥ S_{kl}. Finite-sample validity requires the jointly exchangeable array assumption (Assumption 1), network covariate permutation-equivariance (Assumption 2), and a permutation-invariant fitted model.

- **Row-column approach (§4.3)**: Exploits the Aldous–Hoover representation: elements in the same row/column share a latent variable. For each element, row-rank R_{kl} and column-rank C_{kl} are computed. The bivariate array U = (R_{ij}, C_{ij}) is mapped via optimal transport to a reference measure (suggested: unit ball centered at (1/2, 1/2) in the first quadrant, since the default uniform-on-ball choice performs poorly). The non-conformity score is the transport depth ‖F̂_±(U)‖.

- **Selective conformal (§4.4)**: When a column is selected based on the rank of a summary statistic, Theorem 4.4 provides a conditional validity guarantee P(Ĉ_{ir} ≤ t | R = r, M_{2,r} = A) ≤ t, enabling valid inference after adaptive selection.

- **Graphon-weighted conformal for missing elements (§6)**: For a test point (k,l) that is missing (M_{kl} = 0), a weighted conformal procedure assigns higher weights to calibration observations similar to the test point via estimated graphon weights. The weighted rank Π_{kl}^{weighted} uses weights (1 - p̂_{ij})/p̂_{ij}. Graphon estimation is done via USVT (Chatterjee 2015) or neighborhood smoothing (Zhang et al. 2017). Conditional validity requires consistency of conditional CDF estimates (Theorem 6.2, conditions (d)/(e)).

## Results & Highlights

- **Simulation 1 — Splitting methods (Table 1)**: Node splitting, edge splitting, and selected-column splitting all achieve coverage near the nominal 0.9 level (within the binomial CI [0.874, 0.926]) for n=200. Random forest yields narrower intervals than linear models. Edge splitting tends to produce narrower intervals than node splitting.
- **Simulation 2 — Row-column approach (Table 2)**: The row-column method produces narrower intervals than standard node splitting across basic, asymmetric, and heteroscedastic settings, with improvements most pronounced in the asymmetric and heteroscedastic cases — consistent with the Aldous–Hoover intuition that row/column neighbors are more informative.
- **Simulation 3 — Graphon-weighted for missing elements (Fig. 1)**: For n=500, coverage is near nominal for ρ_n ≥ 2n^{−0.3}. USVT stabilizes at nominal coverage at sparser levels than NS. NS produces narrower intervals for moderate/dense graphs (ρ_n ≥ n^{−0.7}). Even beyond regularity conditions, USVT provides conservative coverage.
- **Real data — Cora citation network (Table 3)**: Predicting common citation counts between papers. Coverage is 0.9500 (node/edge splitting) and 0.9488 (selected column) at α=0.05 — matching nominal despite the masking matrix being constructed from a fitted GNN rather than a known generative process. The selected-column method yields dramatically narrower intervals (width 0.1752 vs. 1.59–1.62).
- **Theoretical highlight**: The bijection argument in Theorem 5.1 is the technical keystone — it works with the joint distribution of (W_k, ζ_k) rather than relying on rank statistics being well-defined after transformation, which is what previous proofs require and why they fail in the random-sampling setting.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty (High)**: The bijection argument for random sampling under distributional invariance (Theorem 5.1) is genuinely new — the authors clearly articulate why previous proof strategies fail and provide a constructive measure-preserving correspondence. The conditional validity result under MNAR (Theorem 6.2) fills a real gap; prior weighted conformal work (Gui et al. 2023, Liang et al. 2026) either assumes known missingness or fixed underlying data, losing the exchangeability structure.
- **Rigor (Very High)**: 64 pages with full proofs in the appendix. Assumptions are stated explicitly with examples. The comparison to Barber et al. (2023) beyond-exchangeability framework (Section 5.1) is technically precise about how the results are complementary. The remark on why Proposition 5.1's proof does not generalize to Theorem 5.1 is a sign of intellectual honesty.
- **Reproducibility (Moderate)**: Simulation parameters are specified (n, α, ρ_n, iterations), data-generating processes are fully described, and the Cora dataset is public. However, no code is mentioned as released. The row-column approach involves optimal transport computation with implementation details deferred to Appendix A.5.2 — a nontrivial engineering effort to reproduce.
- **Significance (Moderate-High for statistics; Moderate for mainstream ML)**: This is a statistics theory paper. Its primary impact is in conformal prediction theory and network inference. The applicability to ML practice is indirect: link prediction with calibrated uncertainty is valuable for GNN practitioners, but the paper's focus is distribution-free guarantees rather than empirical SOTA performance. The conditional validity given predicted values (not covariates) is a practically meaningful relaxation.
- **Red flags**: 
  - The graphon missingness model (Assumption 4) requires lower-bounded graphon w(x,y) ≥ c > 0 and sparsity rate ρ_n = ω(√(log n)/n). These are standard in network statistics but may be violated in very sparse real-world networks (e.g., social networks with power-law degree distributions).
  - The conditional validity result (Theorem 6.2) requires consistency of conditional CDF/CDF estimates at rates o(1) or o(ρ_n), which the authors note requires "multi-dimensional tuning parameters" that are not data-driven in their experiments — they explicitly state they "leave optimal data-driven tuning parameter selection for this conditional regime to future work." This is honest but limits practical applicability of the conditional guarantee.
  - No code release mentioned. The row-column optimal transport procedure has several implementation choices (reference measure, grid for y) that could affect results.

## Strengths

- Theoretical depth: the bijection argument is elegant and addresses a real gap in conformal prediction theory.
- Comprehensive: covers full conformal, split conformal, row-column, selective, and graphon-weighted procedures — giving practitioners a toolkit rather than a single method.
- Honest about limitations: explicitly states what the conditional validity requires and what is left for future work.
- Clear positioning against related work (Section 2.1): precisely distinguishes from Gui et al., Liang et al., Shao & Zhang, Du et al., and Marandon — each comparison identifies a specific structural advantage.
- Real-data validation on Cora with a GNN-generated masking matrix demonstrates that the framework works even when the missingness mechanism is learned, not known.

## Limitations

- The graphon model assumptions (lower-bounded graphon, specific sparsity rates) may not hold in ultra-sparse or heavy-tailed networks.
- Conditional validity requires careful non-parametric estimation of conditional distributions with non-trivial tuning — not yet solved in practice.
- The row-column optimal transport approach is computationally more expensive than standard conformal, though the authors note it only needs to be computed O(n) times (at score jumps) rather than over a fine grid.
- All experiments use relatively small/moderate networks (n = 200–2708). Scalability to very large graphs (millions of nodes) is not discussed.
- The paper does not address simultaneous/multiple-testing correction for groups of missing entries (acknowledged as future work).
- No comparison against state-of-the-art link prediction methods (GNNs, matrix factorization) in terms of prediction accuracy — the focus is purely on uncertainty quantification validity.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. Can the bijection argument (Theorem 5.1) be extended to handle approximate (rather than exact) distributional invariance, yielding explicit coverage gap bounds analogous to Barber et al. (2023)?
2. How does the graphon-weighted conformal procedure perform on real-world sparse networks (e.g., power-law degree distributions) where the lower-bounded graphon assumption may fail?
3. Could the row-column optimal transport approach be combined with GNN-based non-conformity scores for link prediction, and would it outperform standard conformalized GNN methods (e.g., Huang et al. 2023)?
4. What is the optimal data-driven choice of tuning parameters for the conditional validity regime (Theorem 6.2), and can cross-validation or conformal-of-conformal approaches be used?
5. Can the framework be extended to dynamic/temporal networks where joint exchangeability holds within time snapshots but not across time, leveraging the connection to cyclic stationarity mentioned in the conclusion?
