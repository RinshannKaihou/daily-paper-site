> **一句话总结：** 本文区分了大型推理模型（LRM）中的"检测特征"（识别已生成文本中的行为）与"预测特征"（在行为发生前编码其未来概率），通过在中间推理步骤训练线性/MLP 探针以 64%–91% 的二值化准确率预测未来行为，并提出 FPCG（Future Probe Controlled Generation）逐句采样并按预测概率选择候选的文本级引导方法，在与 Difference-in-Means 激活引导强度相当的情况下将困惑度劣化从 9/12 场景降到 1/12，并在 gpt-oss-20b 的 Refusal/Prompt Injection/Wealth Seeking 等激活引导失效的场景中仍能成功引导。

> **TL;DR:** Across 4 reasoning models (DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B, Qwen3-14B, gpt-oss-20b, QwQ-32B) and 6 behavioral datasets (Myopic Reward, Survival Instinct, Wealth Seeking from Perez et al. 2022; Refusal/SORRY-Bench, Prompt Injection/SEP, Sycophancy/ELEPHANT-AITA), linear probes on middle-layer (layer 25 for Qwen3-14B and DeepSeek, layer 20 for gpt-oss-20b) sentence-end activations predict future behavior with binarized accuracy reaching 91% (Refusal) and exceeding 70% in 20/24 settings — whereas detection-feature probes trained only on final-answer activations are much weaker predictors. The proposed FPCG steering method matches Difference-in-Means activation steering (Rimsky et al. 2024 baseline) in steering strength at <10% filtered-output rates, while raising generation perplexity in only 1/12 steering scenarios vs. 9/12 for activation steering, and enables steering on gpt-oss-20b Refusal/Prompt Injection/Wealth Seeking where activation steering breaks the generation in almost all examples.

## 核心贡献

- **概念区分检测特征 vs 预测特征**：形式化地指出 prior 激活引导（如 Difference-in-Means [Rimsky et al. 2024]）隐式依赖"检测已生成文本中行为"的特征，而 LRM 在行为发生前还编码"预测未来行为"的独立特征。
- **未来行为预测探针**：在中间推理步骤（句末）提取中层残差流激活，训练线性/MLP 探针预测最终行为概率，二值化准确率最高达 91%（Refusal），20/24 设置超过 70%。
- **证明检测特征≠预测特征**：仅在最终答案激活上训练的检测探针显著弱于全响应预测探针，证明两类特征在 LRM 表示中是分开的。
- **FPCG 引导方法**：逐句采样 M 个候选，用未来行为探针评分，按期望方向（增/减）argmax 选择，无需表示线性假设（可用 MLP 探针），实现质量几乎无损的行为控制。
- **互补性实证**：在 gpt-oss-20b 上激活引导在 3/6 行为（Refusal、Prompt Injection、Wealth Seeking）失效并破坏生成，而 FPCG 仍能无质量劣化地引导；在 QwQ-32B 上 FPCG 在一个激活引导不可控的场景中也有效。

## Key Contributions

- Formalizes the distinction between **detection features** (active when a behavior is already present in generated text — the implicit basis of difference-in-means steering vectors [Rimsky et al. 2024]) and **prediction features** (encode the model's tendency to act a certain way *before* the behavior is realized).
- Trains Linear (and stronger MLP) probes on middle-layer residual-stream activations at the end of each CoT sentence that predict the eventual binary behavior with **binarized accuracy up to 91% (Refusal) and >70% in 20 of 24 model×behavior settings** — establishing that activations carry genuine future-behavior signal.
- Empirically shows detection features are *poor* predictors: probes trained only on final-answer activations are significantly worse across all behaviors and the gap shrinks only as evaluation moves toward the final third of the CoT.
- Introduces **Future Probe Controlled Generation (FPCG)**, a sentence-level text-steering algorithm: sample M candidate continuations, score each with the future-behavior probe, pick the argmax/argmin in the desired direction; by using MLP probes it does *not* require linear behavior representations.
- Demonstrates complementarity to activation steering: FPCG raises generation perplexity in only **1 of 12** steering scenarios vs **9 of 12** for Difference-in-Means, and on gpt-oss-20b it enables steering on Refusal/Prompt Injection/Wealth Seeking where activation steering breaks almost all generations.

## Methodology

**Models and sampling.** Four open-weight reasoning models: DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B, Qwen3-14B, gpt-oss-20b, QwQ-32B. All responses sampled at temperature T=1.0; each response is the model's CoT followed by a final answer.

**Behavioral datasets (6, binary).** Three Multiple-Choice-Question datasets from Perez et al. 2022 (advanced-ai-risk human-written subset, following Rimsky et al. 2024): **Myopic Reward, Survival Instinct, Wealth Seeking** — parsed by regex on the final answer letter. Three free-form generation datasets: **Refusal** (SORRY-Bench [Xie et al. 2025], judge-LLM scored, B=1 if complies), **Prompt Injection** (SEP [Zverev et al. 2025], witness-string match), **Sycophancy** (ELEPHANT-AITA [Cheng et al. 2026], NTA/YTA substring from r/AmItheAsshole where human consensus is "user in the wrong").

**Behavior distribution analysis (§3.2).** For each prompt sample S=10 responses, compute behavior probability p_i = (1/S)ΣB(q, r_ij). To locate where the decision happens, for each reasoning step S_k run the rest of the generation 10× from that prefix and label S_k with the resulting behavior probability — this is a costly post-hoc debugging tool (Figure 3 traces two DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B responses to the same SORRY-Bench prompt where the first sentence "Okay, so I need to..." vs "Alright, so I need to" yields 70% vs 40% comply probability).

**Future-behavior probes (§3.3.1).** Extract middle-layer residual-stream activations at the end of each CoT sentence and the final answer — layer 25 for Qwen3-14B and DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B, layer 20 for gpt-oss-20b. For each behavior, train a Linear Probe (logistic regression) on a disjoint 100-example train set to predict the sentence's future-behavior probability label (from §3.2); evaluate on a disjoint 100-example test set with Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and Binarized Accuracy (prediction and label on the same side of 0.5). Appendix C.1 shows MLP probes only beat linear by a few MAE points, so most signal is linear.

**Detection vs prediction (§3.3.2).** Identical probe setup but trained only on final-answer activations; evaluated split across early/middle/late CoT and final answer. Performance gap confirms the two feature classes differ.

**FPCG (§4.1, Figure 6).** Pseudocode: `response=""; while not finished: candidates = generate_sentence_candidates(model, prompt+response, num_candidates); for each candidate: acts=extract_activations(model, prompt+response+candidate, layer); score=future_probe(acts); best = argmax(scores) if direction=="positive" else argmin(scores); response += best`. Uses the slightly stronger MLP probes. Evaluated by steering each model and reporting average behavior probability over 100 test samples plus the fraction of responses filtered for wrong output format (a quality-degradation proxy).

**Baseline — Difference-in-Means activation steering (§4.2).** Following Rimsky et al. 2024 / Venhoff et al. 2025: extract final-answer activations at all token positions with binary behavior labels, take mean-positive minus mean-negative, normalize to the dataset mean-activation norm, sweep multiplier ∈ {−1.5,…,1.5} step 0.5, search layers 15/20/25 for DeepSeek (layer 15 best), add to residual stream of every generated token. Quality compared via generation perplexity (Figure 8) at the strongest multiplier with <10% filtered.

## Results & Highlights

1. **Future-behavior prediction accuracy (Figure 4, §3.3.1):** Linear probes on sentence-end middle-layer activations predict the future behavior with **binarized accuracy reaching 91% on Refusal** and **exceeding 70% in 20 of 24 model×behavior settings**; MAE for the three MCQ datasets (Myopic Reward, Survival Instinct, Wealth Seeking) is ~0.20–0.30, and for the three free-form behaviors (Refusal, Prompt Injection, Sycophancy) MAE is **0.10–0.20** — all well below the random and dataset-mean baselines.
2. **Detection ≠ prediction (Figure 5, §3.3.2):** Probes trained only on final-answer activations (detection features) are significantly worse future predictors across all six behaviors; the gap closes only as evaluation moves into the final third of the CoT, where the behavior is already partly expressed.
3. **FPCG steering strength (Table 1):** On DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B, positive FPCG raises the behavior fraction by +42.7 pp on Myopic Reward (46.2→88.9%), +38.1 pp on Wealth Seeking, +31.1 pp on Survival Instinct, +19.3 pp on Refusal, +20.5 pp on Prompt Injection, +24.2 pp on Sycophancy; negative FPCG correspondingly lowers it (e.g., Myopic Reward 46.2→5.1%, −41.1 pp). Filtered fractions are 0% except Sycophancy positive=15% and Myopic Reward negative=8%. On Qwen3-14B, positive steering on Myopic Reward moves behavior 26.3→77.3% (+51.0 pp) with 0% filtered across all six behaviors.
4. **Quality preservation vs activation steering (Figure 8, §4.2):** Activation steering raises generation perplexity in **9 of 12** steering scenarios; FPCG raises it in only **1 of 12**. When limited to <10% filtered outputs, FPCG gives stronger steering than DiM on Myopic Reward, Survival Instinct, Prompt Injection (DeepSeek), comparable on Wealth Seeking, slightly weaker on Refusal and Sycophancy.
5. **Steering where activation steering fails (§4.2):** On gpt-oss-20b, DiM steering *breaks the generation in almost all examples* for Refusal, Prompt Injection, and Wealth Seeking, while FPCG shows no quality degradation and still steers; on QwQ-32B, FPCG enables steering in one setup where activation steering does not work at all.

## Critical Assessment

- **Small, coarse evaluation sets.** Each behavior's probe is trained and tested on disjoint 100-example subsets (stated in §3.3.1); FPCG behavior fractions are reported over only 100 test samples. With n=100 the reported ±20–50 pp swings have non-trivial binomial error bars, yet no confidence intervals or significance tests are given for either the probe accuracies or the steering deltas.
- **Perplexity is a weak quality proxy.** The headline "1/12 vs 9/12" quality claim rests on perplexity (Figure 8) and the format-filter rate; the paper does not report a task-utility metric (e.g., answer correctness on a benchmark) for the steered outputs, so "no quality degradation" is only loosely established — a model could keep low perplexity while still producing semantically wrong answers.
- **Computational cost is acknowledged but not quantified.** §5 (Limitations) concedes "the need to sample multiple candidates for every sentence" is "one limiting factor of FPCG efficiency," and Appendix E shows performance rises with num_candidates — but the paper gives no wall-clock or token-cost comparison to activation steering, so the practical latency overhead is unknown.
- **Single fixed probe layer.** Probes use a single preselected middle layer (25 or 20) with no layer ablation for the prediction task itself (the layer search in §4.2 is only for the *steering* baseline). It is unclear how much prediction accuracy depends on this layer choice.
- **gpt-oss-20b steering-strength is mixed.** The authors concede (§4.2) that on gpt-oss-20b "activation steering outperforms FPCG in steering strength in three scenarios out of six" — FPCG's win there is strictly on *robustness* (it doesn't break generation), not on steering magnitude, so the complementarity claim is narrower than the abstract implies.
- **Behavior parsing is brittle.** Several evaluations rely on substring/regex heuristics (NTA/YTA substring, witness string, MCQ letter); the paper does not report parser failure rates on the unfiltered outputs, which could bias the behavior fractions.

## Strengths

- Clean, falsifiable conceptual contribution: the detection-vs-prediction distinction reframes why activation steering degrades quality (it targets the wrong feature class) and is testable on any LRM.
- Strong, simple probe result (64–91% binarized accuracy, mostly linear per Appendix C.1) demonstrates real future-behavior signal in intermediate activations — a genuine interpretability finding.
- FPCG is model-agnostic and orthogonal to activation steering (explicitly combinable, §5), and works with non-linear MLP probes — no linearity assumption.
- Honest about complementarity: the paper openly reports settings where DiM steering is stronger (Refusal, Sycophancy on DeepSeek; 3/6 on gpt-oss-20b) rather than overclaiming FPCG dominance.
- Open-source code + interactive demo increase reproducibility.

## Limitations

- Probe/steering evaluation uses n=100 subsets with no confidence intervals or significance testing.
- Quality preservation argued via perplexity + format-filter rate only; no semantic/task-utility metric reported for steered outputs.
- FPCG's per-sentence multi-candidate sampling cost is unquantified (no latency/token-cost comparison to DiM).
- Single fixed probe layer per model; no layer ablation for the prediction task.
- Mixed steering-strength results on gpt-oss-20b and QwQ-32B (DiM often stronger); FPCG's advantage there is robustness, not magnitude.
- Preprint, not peer-reviewed.

## Relevance

This paper maps directly to two configured interest areas: **(3) LLM Hidden States & Interpretability** — its core contribution is the discovery and formalization of distinct "prediction features" in middle-layer residual-stream activations of reasoning models, probed linearly — and **(1) LLM Training Stability & Observability** insofar as it provides a deployment-time observability/steering tool (FPCG) and a behavior-distribution debugging method for controlling LRM outputs without retraining. It is *not* about inference evaluation benchmarks, finite-precision/SDC, system cards, mathematical foundations, or self-evolving agents, so overlap with the other areas is minimal.

## Questions for Further Exploration

- The probes use a single preselected layer (25 for Qwen3-14B/DeepSeek, 20 for gpt-oss-20b): how does prediction accuracy vary across layers, and is there a consistent "prediction-feature" layer across model families (cf. Lindsey et al. 2025 [biology of an LLM])?
- The behavior-distribution analysis (§3.2) resamples only S=10 continuations per prefix: how does the probe's ground-truth label noise affect the 64–91% accuracy figures, and would a larger S sharpen the prediction target?
- §5 notes FPCG is "by design orthogonal to any forms of activation steering" and proposes hybrids: can a DiM steering vector that targets the *prediction*-feature direction (rather than the detection-feature direction) recover activation steering's strength without its perplexity cost?
- The detection-vs-prediction gap shrinks toward the final third of the CoT (§3.3.2): at what relative CoT position do the two feature classes converge, and does this define a natural "decision point" usable as an early-stopping signal for test-time compute?
- Appendix C.1 shows MLP probes beat linear by only a few MAE points: does this linearity extend to stronger probes (e.g., SAE latents, as in Arad et al. 2025), or is the prediction feature fundamentally a single linear direction?
- Quality is measured only by perplexity + format-filter rate: how do FPCG-steered outputs score on a task-utility benchmark (e.g., does safety steering on Refusal preserve factual accuracy on unrelated questions)?
