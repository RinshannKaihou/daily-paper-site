> **一句话总结：** 提出三种基于轨迹的成对偏好度量（LR/RPP/IPP），用时间偏好替代二元成功度量和标量折扣，将agentic benchmark的并列率从~75%降至~35%，并将oracle偏好恢复率从PR/SR的12.8%提升至~95%，在无需超参数、额外标注或时间-效用函数假设的前提下重塑了benchmark饱和的解释。

> **TL;DR:** This paper proposes three offline preference-based trajectory evaluation measures — **Lexicographic Return (LR)**, **Return-Paired Preference (RPP)**, and **Interval-Paired Preference (IPP)** — that compare trajectories through paired temporal preferences over time-to-return profiles rather than binary success. Across 5 benchmark families (AgentBoard, OpenHands-Index, TheAgentCompany, TALES, plus a synthetic SGRL), the trajectory-preference family cuts tie rates from ~75% (SR) and ~50% (PR) to ~35%, lifts oracle-preference recovery from ~13% (SR/PR) to ~95%, and improves split-half reliability, leave-one-out stability, and discriminative power without hyperparameters or extra annotations — reframing "benchmark saturation" as substantially an artifact of the evaluation measure rather than data or task difficulty.

## 核心贡献

- **提出三种成对轨迹偏好度量**：Lexicographic Return（LR，从最高return层向下backoff）、Return-Paired Preference（RPP，在所有return层上累积时间优势）、Interval-Paired Preference（IPP，比较相邻return层间的增量时间），统一通过时间偏好而非标量折扣编码"早到更好"。
- **量化成功度量的信息损失**：在五个基准上证明success rate产生约74.9%的并列、partial return约49.7%、SPL约63.4%，而轨迹偏好族将并列率压到约35%，揭示终端标量对有效样本量的严重破坏。
- **建立方法论级别的元评估框架**：跨有效性（oracle agreement、inter-measure correlation）、可靠性（split-half、leave-one-out）、灵敏度（tie rate、discriminative power、discriminative bias）和数据效率（ranking stability、oracle recovery vs. subsample fraction）四维度系统对比。
- **重新诠释benchmark饱和**：证实当所有系统都失败或都成功时SR判别力崩塌，说明所谓"饱和"很大程度上是测量工具的产物，而非数据/题目的固有属性。
- **实证贡献附带的实践信息**：NeurIPS Datasets & Benchmarks track论文中使用二元指标的占比从2022年5%升至2025年18%，EMNLP从9%升至21%，证明该问题在加速恶化。

## Key Contributions

- **Three preference-based trajectory metrics** (LR/RPP/IPP) defined directly on pairs of trajectories via time-to-return profiles, sidestepping the need for a discount factor or a precise time-utility relationship.
- **Empirical diagnosis of information loss in success-based evaluation**: 74.9% instance-level tie rate under SR, 49.7% under PR, 63.4% under SPL — vs. ~35% under LR/RPP/IPP, on a cross-benchmark aggregate.
- **Meta-evaluation methodology**: integrates inter-measure similarity, oracle agreement (SGRL-oracle), split-half reliability, leave-one-out stability, tie rate, discriminative power (FWER + FDR), discriminative bias (TALES-AA), and data-efficiency curves, all reported jointly.
- **Reframing of "benchmark saturation"**: demonstrated empirically (Fig. 4b) that SR's discriminative power collapses at the easy and hard ends of the success-rate spectrum — a measure-level, not task-level, phenomenon.
- **A clean aggregation pipeline**: extends the Bradley–Terry model to soft/fractional win labels so that signed preference values can be lifted into a system ranking, mirroring ChatBot Arena's offline analog.

## Methodology

- **Trajectory representation.** A trajectory `f : Z+ → [0,1]` is non-decreasing normalized return; `g(R)` is the first time `f` reaches at least `R` (with `g(1)=∞` on failure). All three new measures operate on the pair `(f, f′)` and produce `Δ ∈ [-1, 1]`.
- **Lexicographic Return (LR).** Compare at the highest return level where `g ≠ g′`; back off if equal at R=1. Conservative: when one trajectory succeeds and the other does not, reduces to SR; otherwise breaks ties by time-to-return at lower return levels. Equivalent to "lexicographic recall" in IR [Diaz et al. 2025].
- **Return-Paired Preference (RPP).** For each segment between consecutive return levels in the sorted union `[R̂0,…,R̂K]`, accumulate segment-width-weighted `sgn(g′(R̂_k) − g(R̂_k))`. Uniform weighting → cumulative temporal advantage; mirror of "recall-paired preference" in IR.
- **Interval-Paired Preference (IPP).** Compares δg = g(R̂_k) − g(R̂_{k-1}) — the increment time per return level — rather than absolute time-to-return. Captures local consistency of progress: a slow-starter that later accelerates can win under IPP but lose under RPP.
- **Aggregation.** Within-instance `Δ` averaged across the instance set, then lifted to system-level win fractions `(Δ+1)/2` and fit via a fractional-label Bradley–Terry model (same machinery used in ChatBot Arena) to derive system rankings.
- **Benchmarks.** Five families: AgentBoard (6 task suites × 12 systems), OpenHands-Index (4 code benchmarks × up to 22 systems), TheAgentCompany (175 workplace tasks), TALES (~50 systems × 2 text adventure envs), plus a custom SGRL (DoorKey/FourRooms/Taxi). Plus two auxiliary datasets: TALES-AA (same-model null) and SGRL-oracle (ground-truth-ordered degraded policies).
- **Baselines.** SR, partial return (PR), and reference-free SPL (success / observed trajectory length).
- **Statistical testing.** Bootstrap (10k replicates), two-sided p-values, Holm (FWER) and Benjamini-Hochberg (FDR) corrections.

## Results & Highlights

- **Tie rate (instance level, cross-benchmark avg).** SR 74.9%, PR 49.7%, SPL 63.4%, LR/RPP/IPP ≈ 33.9 / 34.8 / 35.1%. SR and LR concentrate near `{0, ±1}` (binary); PR/RPP/IPP spread mass over `(0,1)`, which is precisely why their aggregates carry more information per pair.
- **Oracle preference recovery (SGRL-oracle).** SR/PR 12.8%, SPL 91.8%, LR 95.6%, RPP 94.2%, IPP 95.8%. With statistical significance: SR/PR detect 0% of true preferences as significant; under FDR correction SPL detects 48.1%, LR 61.6%, RPP 63.2%, IPP 62.5%. The FWER-corrected numbers preserve the same ranking.
- **Reliability.** Split-half Kendall τ: LR/RPP = 0.83 (pair) / 0.85 (ranking); PR 0.82/0.82; IPP 0.78/0.81; SPL 0.71/0.70; SR 0.75/0.73. Leave-one-out sign-flip rate: SR/LR = 0%, RPP 1.88%, PR 2.14%, IPP 2.83%, SPL 5.5%. (SR/LR's perfect 0% reflects that removing an instance can only turn a signed difference into a tie, not a flip — a structural artefact of binary metrics.)
- **Discriminative power.** Under FDR correction, RPP detects 78.4% of model-pair differences as significant, IPP 73.8%, LR 77.8%, PR 73.4%, SPL 60.2%, SR 58.5%. SR's power peaks at intermediate success rates and collapses near 0 and 1; RPP maintains high power across the entire difficulty spectrum (Fig. 4b).
- **Discriminative bias.** All measures achieve 0% false-positive rate on the same-model TALES-AA split, under both FWER and FDR — the gain in discriminative power is not noise.
- **Data efficiency.** On AgentBoard/ALFWorld, trajectory-preference rankings plateau at the same or fewer instances than scalar metrics. On the harder Taxi domain, SPL detects essentially no significant oracle pairs even at 100% of the data, while RPP recovers a non-trivial fraction. RPP's 50–60% subsample already exceeds SPL's full-data significant-accuracy on FourRooms.
- **Validity clustering.** Inter-measure τ separates cleanly into two clusters: {SR, PR, SPL} (scalar-outcome family) and {LR, RPP, IPP} (trajectory-preference family), confirming that the new measures are not redundant with SR/PR but are aligned with the same latent construct of performance.

## Authors & Credibility

- **Fernando Diaz** (Carnegie Mellon University) is a senior researcher with deep, sustained work in IR and evaluation methodology, including the very preference-based retrieval evaluation that this paper extends offline [Diaz & Ferraro 2022; Diaz, Ekstrand & Mitra 2025]. The methodological lineage is clear and the choices (Bradley–Terry, lexicographic recall as the IR analogue, t-SNE on τ) reflect idiomatic IR-evaluation practice.
- Single-author submission limits breadth of perspective, but the paper's claims are narrow, quantitative, and largely falsifiable. No external benchmarks are introduced; everything rides on five publicly available benchmark families and two constructed datasets (TALES-AA, SGRL-oracle), which the author describes in enough detail to be reproduced.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty.** Modest in terms of new algorithmic machinery — three signed-preference constructions plus a Bradley–Terry lift — but high in terms of *exposing* how much signal terminal-success metrics discard in agentic evaluation. The reframing of benchmark saturation as a measurement phenomenon, not just a data phenomenon, is the paper's most consequential intellectual contribution.
- **Rigor.** Strong. The meta-evaluation spans four orthogonal axes (validity / reliability / sensitivity / data efficiency), uses both FWER and FDR corrections, includes a same-model null for false-positive control, and grounds criterion validity in a synthetic oracle with known ground truth. The discriminative-bias row (0% for all measures on TALES-AA) is the right falsification check and is reported honestly.
- **Reproducibility.** The author defines the three preferences with explicit equations and provides a clean experiment matrix (5 benchmarks × 6 measures × multiple meta-criteria). The benchmark datasets are public. Code release is not mentioned in the body of the PDF; the 1MB PDF suggests there is no supplemental code archive, which is a notable omission given how easy it would be to release the evaluation harness.
- **Significance.** High for the agentic-evaluation community. SR's tie-rate of 75% means that published leaderboards have been operating at roughly 25% effective discriminative power, and the authors document that binary metrics are *growing* in adoption at NeurIPS and EMNLP. The paper provides an actionable, drop-in replacement.
- **Red flags / open concerns.**
  - The paper's strongest claims (oracle recovery, discriminative power) lean heavily on SGRL-oracle, a synthetic environment the author designed. Real-world agentic tasks with imperfect or latent reward structure remain untested in the validity section — and the authors explicitly flag this in the Limitations.
  - IPP and RPP are mathematically identical when only terminal success and step count are recorded (as in OpenHands-Index). The paper notes this, but it constrains how much of the gain transfers to the very benchmarks (code agents, web agents) where leaderboard decisions are most consequential today.
  - The LOO stability table inadvertently rewards SR/LR for being *unable* to produce signed differences (removing an instance can only induce a tie, never a flip). The author handles this correctly in prose, but the table cell should not be read at face value.
  - Bradley–Terry fitting with fractional labels introduces a dependence on the choice of aggregation kernel `(Δ+1)/2`. The author acknowledges this and defers alternative aggregation to future work, but a sensitivity check on the kernel would have strengthened the ranking results.
  - Adoption cost is non-zero: existing benchmark pipelines log only success + step count, so RPP/IPP need finer-grained subgoal annotations. The paper says "no additional data beyond trajectory logs," but many current benchmarks *don't* log trajectory returns at all.

## Strengths

- Diagnoses a real and rapidly worsening problem with a single, communicable number (75% → 35% tie rate).
- Three measures along a principled continuum (conservative → cumulative → incremental), not a single ad-hoc proposal.
- Meta-evaluation framework is thorough, well-structured, and includes the right falsification controls (TALES-AA, SGRL-oracle).
- Hyperparameter-free, no extra annotations required, and compatible with existing trajectory logs that already record step-level returns.
- Connects offline agentic evaluation to a mature IR-evaluation lineage (lexicographic recall, recall-paired preference), giving the work theoretical depth rather than being a one-off metric.

## Limitations

- The strongest criterion-validity claims are anchored in synthetic SGRL-oracle environments; real-world agentic systems with latent reward structure are not directly tested.
- OpenHands-Index and TheAgentCompany record only terminal return + step count, so LR/RPP/IPP collapse to the same metric there — limiting the practical gain on several of today's most-used benchmarks.
- The fractional-label Bradley–Terry aggregation choice is one of several reasonable options; alternatives (Plackett–Luce, Mallows, rank-centrality) are not compared.
- No released code or reference implementation mentioned in the PDF body, despite the methods being straightforward to implement and the experimental matrix being extensive.
- The approach assumes that intermediate returns reflect *meaningful* progress; noisy, weakly calibrated, or artificially dense subgoal annotations will be amplified rather than corrected.
- Single-author scope means the cross-community validation (other research groups re-running the analysis) has not happened at the time of writing.

## Relevance

本工作直接命中agentic系统离线评估的核心痛点：将成对偏好与轨迹结构引入离线场景，方法与RLHF偏好优化、agent benchmark设计以及排行榜数据效率三方面紧密耦合，对偏好学习与agentic evaluation两条研究脉络均有方法论价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the trajectory-preference measure maintain its advantage on real-world agentic benchmarks with **latent or noisy reward structure** (e.g., production customer-support agents, autonomous-driving simulators), where SGRL-oracle cannot be constructed?
- How robust are the rankings to **alternative aggregation kernels** beyond `(Δ+1)/2` Bradley–Terry — e.g., Plackett–Luce, Mallows, or rank-centrality? Is the system ranking stable across kernels, or does the choice of kernel rival the choice of measure?
- Can LR/RPP/IPP be plugged directly into **preference-optimization training loops** (DPO/IPO/RLHF) as the reward signal, closing the loop between evaluation and training? Would this stabilize RL on sparse-reward agentic tasks?
- For benchmarks that record **only success and step count**, can a small amount of automatic subgoal annotation (e.g., via an LLM rubric) recover the RPP/IPP advantage without full human labeling?
- How does the trajectory-preference measure interact with **calibration / alignment of intermediate rewards**? When two annotators disagree on subgoal definitions, does the resulting metric disagreement correlate with downstream system-ranking disagreements?
- Given the 75% tie rate under SR, **how many published "system A beats system B" claims in recent agentic leaderboards would flip** if re-evaluated under RPP? A meta-analysis of recent NeurIPS/ICML submissions could quantify the cumulative cost of using SR.
- Can preference-based evaluation be extended to **multi-agent trajectories**, where the systematization of "progress" needs to account for inter-agent coordination rather than single-agent return?
