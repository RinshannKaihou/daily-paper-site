> **一句话总结：** 提出 Edge Flow——一个由「中心、振荡方向、振荡幅度」三条耦合常微分方程组成的连续时间模型，在边沿稳定 (EoS) 区间内对梯度下降的振荡动力学进行可分解、可预测的建模，能够复现 EoS 起点处尖锐度的过冲与衰减振荡，并衍生出比 GD 更稳定的离散化算法 EGD。
> **TL;DR:** This paper introduces **Edge Flow**, a system of three coupled ODEs that faithfully models gradient descent (GD) in the **edge-of-stability (EoS)** regime, where the loss Hessian's top eigenvalue hovers near the stability threshold $2/\eta$. Edge Flow decomposes the dynamics into a *center* (modified gradient flow on the symmetrized loss $\tfrac{1}{2}[L(\bar w + xu) + L(\bar w - xu)]$), an *oscillation direction* $u$ (Rayleigh-quotient / Oja flow tracking the top Hessian eigenvector), and an *oscillation magnitude* $x$ (exponential growth/decay driven by $\text{sign}(S(\bar w) - 2/\eta)$). Sharpness stabilization at $2/\eta$ emerges as a self-stabilizing negative-feedback loop between $S(\bar w)$ and $x$ — not as an imposed constraint. The discrete version (**Edge Gradient Descent**, EGD) needs only 2 gradient evaluations + 1 Hessian–vector product per substep, and on full-batch GD over CNN/ResNet/ViT × {MSE, CE} it matches GD's trajectory *and* reproduces the overshoot + damped ringing of $\lambda_{\max}$ at the EoS onset that the existing Central Flow / Rod Flow models cannot capture. The author also shows that a finer center-discretization ($K{=}4$ vs. $K{=}1$) extends the range of stable $\eta$ beyond vanilla GD.

## 核心贡献

- **三变量耦合 ODE 模型**：Edge Flow 把 EoS 区间内的梯度下降分解为「中心 $\bar w$ + 振荡方向 $u$ + 振荡幅度 $x$」三条可解释的常微分方程，对应 GD 迭代 $w_t \approx \bar w_t + (-1)^t x_t u_t$ 的两时间尺度结构。
- **尖锐度自稳定作为内生反馈**：$S(\bar w) > 2/\eta$ 时 $x$ 指数增长、更大的 $x$ 又把中心推向低曲率区域从而拉低 $S$，反之亦然 —— 著名的自稳定 (self-stabilization, Damian et al. 2023) 现象作为耦合动力学的后果出现，而非外部约束。
- **首个分辨尖锐度振荡的连续时间模型**：相比 Central Flow (Cohen et al. 2025) 用瞬时投影把 $S$ 钉在 $2/\eta$、Rod Flow (Regis & Chewi 2026) 根本无法稳定尖锐度，Edge Flow 能复现 EoS 起点处 sharpness 的过冲与阻尼振荡 (overshoot + ringing)。
- **轻量级离散化 (EGD)**：每步 2 次梯度评估 + 1 次 Hessian–向量积；最粗设置 $\rho=\eta$ 时仅比 GD 贵 ~3 倍，且 EGD 在某些 $\eta$ 下比 GD 自身更稳定。
- **可操作的稳定性诊断**：模型暴露两个 EoS 起点失稳的「把手」——细化中心离散化 ($K \uparrow$) 与抬高基础振荡幅度 $\varepsilon$——并通过扫参实验给出量化指导。

## Key Contributions

- **Three-variable coupled ODE model of GD at EoS.** Decomposes the GD iterate as $w_t \approx \bar w_t + (-1)^t x_t u_t$ and gives autonomous dynamics for $(\bar w, u, x)$: a symmetrized-loss gradient flow for the center, an Oja/Rayleigh-quotient flow for the direction, and a scalar exponential ODE for the magnitude with rate $S(\bar w) - 2/\eta$.
- **Self-stabilization as endogenous feedback.** The hallmark of EoS — sharpness pinning near $2/\eta$ — falls out of the coupled ODEs as a negative-feedback oscillator, recovering the mechanism of Damian et al. (2023) without imposing it.
- **First continuous-time EoS model that resolves sharpness oscillation.** Reproduces the *overshoot + damped ringing* of $\lambda_{\max}$ at the onset of EoS. Central Flow projects instantaneously and so cannot oscillate; Rod Flow fails to detect the EoS onset at all and lets the sharpness drift upward.
- **Cheap discretization (Edge Gradient Descent).** Forward Euler on eqs. (1) & (3) + power method on eq. (2) → 2 grad evals + 1 HV-product per substep. Algorithm 1 is short and GPU-friendly.
- **Two actionable levers for stability.** Refining the center step ($\rho = \eta/K$, $K\!\uparrow$) suppresses the loss spike and widens the stable-$\eta$ range; raising the base oscillation floor $\varepsilon$ engages self-stabilization sooner and shrinks the sharpness overshoot.
- **Six-architecture empirical validation.** MLP, CNN, ResNet, ViT × {MSE, CE} on small CIFAR-10 subsets, full-batch GD; for CNN/MSE also runs Central Flow as a direct head-to-head.

## Methodology

- **Setup.** Loss $L:\mathbb R^d\!\to\!\mathbb R$ is $C^3$; sharpness $S(w) = \lambda_{\max}(\nabla^2 L(w))$; learning rate $\eta$. GD iterates modeled as $w_t \approx \bar w_t + (-1)^t x_t u_t$ with $\bar w_t$ the slow center, $u_t\!\in\!\mathbb S^{d-1}$ the unit oscillation direction, $x_t>0$ the half-magnitude. Time is calibrated so one GD step corresponds to a time interval $\eta$ of the flow.

- **Three coupled ODEs (Eqs. 1–3).**
  1. **Center (eq. 1).** $\dot{\bar w} = -\tfrac12[\nabla L(\bar w + xu) + \nabla L(\bar w - xu)]$ — gradient flow on the *symmetrized* loss. First-order Taylor expansion gives $\tfrac12[\nabla L(\bar w+xu)+\nabla L(\bar w-xu)] = \nabla L(\bar w) + \tfrac{x^2}{2}\nabla^3 L(\bar w)[u,u] + O(x^4)$, so the center differs from gradient flow by a third-derivative correction that biases $\bar w$ toward lower curvature — this is precisely the self-stabilization mechanism of Damian et al. (2023).
  2. **Direction (eq. 2).** $\dot u = 2\bigl[\nabla^2 L(\bar w) - (u^\top\!\nabla^2 L(\bar w)u)I\bigr]u$ — Rayleigh-quotient gradient ascent (Oja's flow) on the sphere, tracking the top eigenvector. When $\nabla^2 L(\bar w)$ has simple top eigenvalue, the eigenvector is attracting with rate governed by the spectral gap.
  3. **Magnitude (eq. 3).** $\dot x = \bigl(S(\bar w) - 2/\eta\bigr) x$ — exponential growth when $S > 2/\eta$, decay when $S < 2/\eta$. The equilibrium $x=0$ recovers gradient flow; a positive floor $\varepsilon>0$ seeds the instability.
- **Well-posedness.** Picard–Lindelöf gives a unique maximal solution; the sphere $\{||u||=1\}$ and the half-line $\{x>0\}$ are invariant.
- **Coupling / self-stabilization.** Eq. (3) reads the sharpness at the center; eq. (1) reads gradients at the *endpoints*; the $(S(\bar w), x)$ pair thus forms a negative-feedback oscillator that attracts $S$ to $2/\eta$.
- **Derivation.** Center = symmetrize the average of two consecutive gradient steps (mirrors Rod Flow but now with a general $u$, $x$ pair). Direction = continuous limit of one power-method step on $\nabla^2 L(\bar w)$, justified by the fact that GD's linearized update $I - \eta\nabla^2 L(\bar w)$ is itself an implicit power iteration. Magnitude = one-step expansion of $w_{t+1} = w_t - \eta\nabla L(w_t)$ with the sign-flip absorbed into $(-1)^t$. Author explicitly notes the derivation *neglects higher-order coupled corrections* and does not apply to quadratic objectives.
- **Discretization — Edge Gradient Descent (EGD).** Forward Euler on (1) & (3), power method on (2), with a separate integration step $\rho \le \eta$ and a floor $x \leftarrow \max(x,\varepsilon)$. Cost per substep: 2 grad evals + 1 HV-product; sharpness obtained at no extra cost as $S(\bar w) = ||\nabla^2 L(\bar w) u||$. The two stepsizes play different roles — $\eta$ is part of the *model* (sharpness threshold $2/\eta$), $\rho$ is part of the *numerical method*. Taking $\rho = \eta$ (i.e. $K=1$) gives $\approx 3\times$ the cost of one GD step; the default in experiments is $K=8$.
- **Predicted quantities along the GD trajectory.** Loss $\hat L_t = \tfrac12[L(\bar w_t + x_t u_t) + L(\bar w_t - x_t u_t)]$; time-averaged squared gradient norm splits into a center term and an oscillation term $\tfrac14\|g_+ - g_-\|^2$ that dominates at EoS.

## Results & Highlights

- **Tracking GD across architectures (Sec. 4.2 / Figs. 3, 7–12).** Six configurations (CNN/ResNet/ViT × {MSE, CE}, plus MLP) on 1000-example 4-class CIFAR-10, full-batch GD. For every configuration Edge Flow tracks the GD trajectory, train loss, and top-3 Hessian eigenvalues; in CNN/MSE the two flows (Edge Flow and Central Flow) are *closer to each other* than either is to GD in parameter-space distance, yet they decompose the oscillation differently.
- **Resolves sharpness overshoot & ringing at EoS onset (Fig. 4).** On the CNN/MSE run, the top three effective Hessian eigenvalues $\eta\lambda_i$ reach the threshold 2 in *staggered* fashion and then exhibit damped oscillation around it. Edge Flow reproduces this overshoot + ringing; Central Flow's instantaneous projection onto $\{S\le 2/\eta\}$ clamps $S$ at 2 and so cannot. (Rod Flow, as shown in Fig. 6 of App. A, fails to detect the onset at all and lets the sharpness drift upward past 2.)
- **Two levers for stability (Sec. 4.3, Fig. 5).** Sweep over a small MLP/MSE run with $\eta=0.02$ and $K\in\{1,8\}$, $\varepsilon\in\{10^{-9}, 10^{-7}, 10^{-5}, 10^{-3}\}$:
  - *Refining the center discretization* ($K=1 \to 8$): suppresses the loss spike and improves stability; EGD with $K=4$ stays stable at $\eta$ where both GD and the $K=1$ EGD diverge.
  - *Raising the base oscillation floor* $\varepsilon$: shrinks both the sharpness overshoot and the onset loss spike; the post-onset dynamics and the converged $x$-plateau are essentially unchanged.
- **Loss-spike diagnosis.** The author attributes the loss spike at the EoS onset to a discretization artifact of the stiff $x$-dynamics kicking in through the third-derivative term in eq. (8), and shows that refining $\rho$ removes the spike without altering the self-stabilization itself.
- **Discrepancies and limitations the author flags.** With $K=8$ the oscillation frequency of Edge Flow does not match GD as closely as the $K=1$, small-$\varepsilon$ setting; this is attributed to discretization error in the stiff magnitude ODE. In all the main figures the $K=8$ default is therefore a tradeoff — better loss-spike behavior, slightly off frequency. The author states these frequency discrepancies "do not matter much for the overall learning dynamics and implicit regularization effect of large stepsizes."

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty.** Clear and well-motivated. The center / direction / magnitude decomposition is a natural and elegant way to split the problem; deriving each ODE from first principles (rather than positing it post hoc) is the right move. Compared to Central Flow (which imposes the constraint) and Rod Flow (which fails to self-stabilize), Edge Flow's contribution is genuinely incremental-but-substantive.
- **Rigor.** Mixed. The derivation is presented *intuitively* — Taylor expansion of consecutive GD steps, claim that the stepsize-sensitive component cancels in the symmetrized gradient, hence insensitivity to $\rho$ — but the paper explicitly says the derivation is *not* meant to be exact ("we do not claim that our derivation universally applies") and "deriving precise conditions under which it applies is an interesting open question." There are no formal convergence / error-bound theorems in the main text. For a paper whose central selling point is "tractable, faithful, and predictive," the absence of a quantitative $\sup_t ||w_t^{\text{GD}} - \bar w_t^{\text{EF}}||$ bound is conspicuous. Picard–Lindelöf well-posedness is the only formal guarantee.
- **Reproducibility.** Strong in principle — Algorithm 1 is short, the codebase extends Cohen et al.'s public `central_flows` repo, and Appendix B lists every hyperparameter (architectures, loss, $\eta$, steps, eigensolve frequency, $K$, $\varepsilon$) in Table 1. The author also discloses the use of LLM assistance for code, which is good practice.
- **Significance.** High for the EoS theory community; lower for the broader ML community. This is a *model* of GD, not a new training algorithm — the practical contribution (EGD is "numerically more stable than GD" at large $\eta$, with concrete $K$ and $\varepsilon$ prescriptions) is suggestive but limited to one small MLP/CIFAR-10 setting in the experiments. The two "levers for stability" are more diagnostic than algorithmic: there is no end-to-end EGD-trained network winning an actual benchmark, and no large-scale ($\ge$ ResNet-50 / ImageNet) demonstration.
- **Red flags.**
  1. **"At least as faithfully as" is a soft claim** — in the main CNN/MSE figure, Edge Flow and Central Flow are "superposed" on the loss curve, but Edge Flow uniquely resolves the sharpness oscillation. The cases where they *disagree* with GD (frequency mismatch at $K=8$, residual sharpness > $2/\eta$ at steady state) are noted but not fully diagnosed.
  2. **The "principled framework for mitigating instabilities" claim is sketch-only.** Two ablations on one small MLP; no comparison to existing sharpness-aware optimizers (SAM, normalized momentum, adaptive sharpness reduction) at scale.
  3. **Quadratic objectives explicitly excluded** from the derivation. This rules out the most basic sanity-check problem in optimization theory, and is a non-trivial caveat for readers who care about the "first principles" claim.
  4. **Single-author, small-scale experiments.** All figures are 1000-example 4-class CIFAR-10; the largest model is a 661k-parameter ViT. Whether Edge Flow remains faithful under minibatch SGD, momentum, weight decay, or label noise is not addressed.
  5. **A baseline that is demonstrably broken.** Rod Flow is shown (Fig. 6) to fail to stabilize sharpness at all — using it as a foil is fair, but "we beat Rod Flow" is a weak claim. Central Flow is the meaningful baseline and is "at least as good" in tracking bulk dynamics; Edge Flow's main empirical edge is the onset oscillation, which is a narrower and harder-to-quantify win.
  6. **LLM-assisted writing disclosed but not deeply audited.** The author states "all results, claims, and final wording were verified," which is reassuring but unverifiable from the manuscript alone.

## Strengths

- A clean, interpretable three-ODE decomposition that maps onto quantities practitioners can probe (top eigenvector, sharpness, drift).
- Discretization cost is realistic — 2 grad evals + 1 HV-product per substep — making the model usable for diagnostics on real networks.
- The model is *predictive*, not just descriptive: EGD is a runnable algorithm that emerges naturally from the discretization.
- Brings a unifying view: sharpness stabilization is a feedback loop, not a black-box phenomenon, and the model explains *why* prior instantaneous-projection models (Central Flow) cannot capture the onset oscillation.
- Reproducible: public codebase, explicit table of hyperparameters, LLM use disclosed, climate impact estimated.
- Honest about scope: the derivation is presented as an *intuitive* first-principles argument, not as a theorem.

## Limitations

- Restricted to full-batch gradient descent in the EoS regime; stability, faithfulness, and the $\varepsilon$ floor in the *stochastic* / minibatch / momentum / adaptive case are open.
- Requires Hessian–vector products, or at least a curvature probe — strictly more expensive than a single GD step.
- No theorem-level guarantees (convergence rate, error bound, stability radius); the paper is largely empirical on the modeling side.
- Quadratic objectives are explicitly excluded from the derivation; the simplest sanity-check case in convex optimization is not addressed.
- Experimental scale is small: 1000-example 4-class CIFAR-10, models up to 661k parameters, full-batch. No comparison with state-of-the-art sharpness-aware optimizers (SAM, etc.) at standard benchmarks.
- The "mitigation" use case is sketched on one MLP setting; a benchmarked EGD-vs.-GD-vs.-SAM comparison is left to future work.
- The $K$ and $\varepsilon$ knobs introduce two new hyperparameters that the user must tune to recover GD-like behavior.

## Relevance

与本项目「训练动力学 + 优化理论」兴趣直接相关：Edge Flow 为 EoS 区间内梯度下降行为提供了一个新的、可解释、且能复现 sharpness 振荡的连续时间模型，对分析现代大模型训练中常见的锐度自稳定与失稳现象具有直接参考价值；同时其离散化形式 EGD 也提供了一种在大 $\eta$ 下比 GD 更稳定的可执行算法思路。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does Edge Flow scale to full-scale neural network training (transformer LM, ≥100M parameters, minibatch SGD with momentum) — does the $(S, x)$ feedback loop survive in high-dimensional, noisy, momentum-regularized settings?
- Can the magnitude ODE be inverted to design a *principled* sharpness-aware optimizer (e.g., a continuous-time analog of SAM, or an adaptive-$\eta$ scheme) that provably damps EoS oscillations and provably extends the stable-$\eta$ range?
- Is the self-stabilization mechanism unique to this three-ODE decomposition, or do equivalent feedback structures exist in alternative continuous-time limits (e.g., second-order / momentum-augmented flows, adaptive stepsize flows)?
- A quantitative error bound $\sup_t ||w_t^{\text{GD}} - \bar w_t^{\text{EF}}||$ in some natural regime (e.g. small $\eta$ or near the EoS fixed point) is the obvious next theoretical step — does the symmetrized-gradient cancellation argument make such a bound achievable?
- What happens *at and past* the onset of EoS — does Edge Flow predict the eventual transition to "progressive sharpening → EoS → break-even" cycles observed empirically, and what does it say about implicit bias at large $\eta$?
- The symmetrized loss in eq. (1) is hand-designed. Can it be *learned* (or replaced by a higher-order Taylor expansion) to better match GD in the stiff region, and does doing so break the clean ODE structure?
- The derivation explicitly excludes quadratic objectives — is there a limit (e.g. small-perturbation around a quadratic) in which Edge Flow recovers known closed-form GD solutions, providing a calibration check?
- Does the EGD discretization with adaptive $\rho$ (stiff-ODE step-size control à la Hairer–Wanner) yield a practical training algorithm that is both stable at large $\eta$ *and* competitive in wall-clock + sample efficiency with tuned SGD-with-momentum?
