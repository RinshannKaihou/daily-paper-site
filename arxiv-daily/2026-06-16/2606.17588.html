Understanding LLMs in Title-Abstract Screening: From Disagreements to Recommendations

arXiv: 2606.17588

Date: 2026-06-16

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.17588

一句话总结： 本文在六项软件工程系统综述 (SR) 中对 LLM 与人类研究者在标题-摘要筛选阶段的分歧进行定性归纳,提出包含七类错误模式的两层分类法(词法层 → 语义层),并基于错误模式给出五条可执行的部署建议(多模型并行、单条标准独立评估、消除标准歧义、聚焦边界样本、LLM 反向校验人工决策)。
TL;DR: A qualitative, cross-study analysis of LLM–human disagreements in title-abstract screening across six software-engineering systematic reviews (>1,000 candidate papers, Cohen's Kappa 0.52–0.77 with zero-shot prompting). Beyond reporting agreement, the authors inductively derive a 7-pattern taxonomy of where and why LLMs fail, and translate it into 5 actionable recommendations (R1–R5). Patterns are organized as lexical cues → semantic consequences, and the paper honestly admits the recommendations are not yet empirically validated.

核心贡献

  • 超越"LLM-人类一致性"这一量化指标,首次在六项 SR 上系统性地定性归纳 LLM 标题-摘要筛选阶段失败的原因,建立包含 7 类模式的两层(词法→语义)错误分类法。
  • 实证覆盖六项软件工程 SR(>1,000 篇 primary study),Cohen's Kappa 介于 0.52–0.77 之间,呈现"中度到实质性"一致性,但Evidence Loss(假阴性)比 False Positive(假阳性)在大多数模式下更频繁。
  • 识别出两项 LLM 意想不到的正向价值:(1) 通过多模型并行降低漏检风险;(2) 反向校验人工决策、发现人类遗漏的相关研究(Human-Error 模式)。
  • 提出五条可落地的部署建议 R1–R5(多模型、单条标准独立评估、消除标准歧义、聚焦边界样本、LLM 反向校验人工),并提供与失败模式的对应映射表(表 3)。
  • 公开数据集(Zenodo: 10.5281/zenodo.20695824)以及所用工具 AISysRev 的引用,提升可复现性。

Key Contributions

  • Beyond agreement metrics: Moves from reporting Cohen's/Kappa-style LLM-human agreement to a qualitative analysis of how and why LLMs fail in title-abstract screening.
  • 7-pattern taxonomy organized along a lexical → semantic pathway:
  1. Term–Boundary (boundary ambiguity of key concepts)
  2. Abstract–Information Omission (abstracts too thin to decide)
  3. LLM–Keyword Overweight (lexical overlap overrides context)
  4. LLM–Not Main Focus (adjacent topic, not the focal construct)
  5. LLM–Incorrect Topic Inference (plausible but unsupported framing)
  6. Human–Error (LLM rationale surfaces papers missed by humans)
  7. Operationalization–Criteria Combination (AND/OR logic mishandled)
  • Empirical scope: Six software-engineering SRs, 1,044 candidate papers, zero-shot LLM screening via the AISysRev tool. Models used: gemini-2.5-flash and openai/gpt-4.1-mini (with anthropic/claude-haiku-4.5 added for AI4TInd).
  • Actionable recommendations with explicit pattern→recommendation mapping (Table 3):
  • R1 Run Multiple LLMs
  • R2 Run Each Criterion Separately
  • R3 Define Criteria Unambiguously
  • R4 Validate From Boundaries (R4.1 LLM Disagreement, R4.2 LLM Probability)
  • R5 Double-Check Human Decisions
  • Community call: Explicitly argues for normative community guidelines on LLM usage in systematic reviews; data + AISysRev tool made available.

Methodology

  • Subject studies (six software-engineering SRs):

| Code | Topic | Papers | Human Include | LLM Include | Cohen's κ |

|------|-------|-------:|--------------:|------------:|----------:|

| TP | Time Pressure in SE | 137 | 15 | 32 | 0.52 |

| ESEE | Expert Judgment in Software Estimation | 100 | 6 | 7 | 0.75 |

| WOSS | Women in OSS | 100 | 38 | 35 | 0.56 |

| GenAIEdu | Generative AI in SE Education | 100 | 8 | 23 | 0.71 |

| AI4T | AITest 2023 subset | 162 | 31 | 21 | 0.72 |

| AI4TInd | AITest Industry subset | 445 | 33 | 43 | 0.77 |

(When κ is computed for AI4TInd without the consensus "all three LLMs must include" rule, κ drops to 0.13 — discussed as a transparency disclosure.)

  • Screening protocol: For each SR, papers were retrieved from Scopus using the original search string, then screened independently by human experts (2 raters in 4 SRs, single rater in the 2 large ongoing SRs) followed by consensus resolution. LLMs then screened the same set in zero-shot mode via AISysRev [Huotala et al., 2026], which builds a prompt that combines the title + abstract with the SR's inclusion/exclusion criteria and asks for a binary decision plus a brief rationale.
  • Decision rule: "LLM include" = at least one LLM included the paper (except AI4TInd, where all three LLMs had to include, because gemini-2.5-flash alone over-included; the alternative rule is reported for transparency).
  • Qualitative analysis: Inductive thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke, 2006). One researcher open-coded all disagreement instances; three additional researchers reviewed the codes and themes; a fifth researcher consolidated them into the final list using a naming convention. No formal inter-rater reliability was computed; the team used a consensual-validation approach with iterative theme refinement.
  • Error-type operationalization: Evidence Loss (EL) = LLM excludes a paper humans included (false negative). False Positive (FP) = LLM includes a paper humans excluded.
  • Out of scope: Full-text screening and data extraction stages; few-shot/CoT prompting; non-SE domains.

Results & Highlights

Taxonomy of disagreement patterns (RQ1)

The authors map each pattern onto a lexical axis (whether key terms are explicit, insufficient, or misleading in the title/abstract) and a semantic axis (the resulting interpretation error). The "Human–Error" and "Operationalization–Criteria Combination" patterns are deliberately left disconnected from this pathway because they do not concern LLM semantic processing.

  • Term–Boundary (18 disagreements): the LLM interprets a key inclusion term more narrowly or broadly than the SR intends. Examples: the LLM excluding "Volvo Trucks" from "software industry" or excluding "UX design" from "software development tasks"; the LLM including "Use Case Points" as expert judgment.
  • Abstract–Information Omission (13): the abstract is too thin to determine eligibility, and LLM vs. human handle the uncertainty differently. Example: a sentence "The convergence of competition, time pressure, and an engaging environment creates a stimulating atmosphere that significantly drives skill development..." in the TP SR was clearly relevant to human raters; both LLMs excluded it.
  • LLM–Keyword Overweight (8): strong lexical overlap overrides contextual relevance. Example: a WOSS paper on Type 1 diabetes open-source software was included solely because the abstract mentioned open-source insulin-delivery software; an AI4T paper on hardware testing was included despite "hardware testing" being an explicit exclusion.
  • LLM–Not Main Focus (23): the paper is topically adjacent but not centrally about the target construct. LLMs apply a stricter "centrality" test than human screeners.
  • LLM–Incorrect Topic Inference (32, the most frequent): the LLM invents a plausible but unsupported framing. Particularly common: confusing "testing of AI" with "AI for testing" in the AI4T/AI4TInd SRs. The authors also note a recurrent negation-handling issue in the TP SR where the LLM produced a correct rationale but the wrong decision on "the task studied is not a software development task."
  • Human–Error (4): in multiple cases the LLM's rationale caused the human reviewer to revise an earlier decision and retrieve an originally missed relevant study. This is the one pattern where the LLM reduces evidence loss.
  • Operationalization–Criteria Combination (4): the LLM applies inconsistent boolean logic (default AND, but occasionally slips into OR). In the TP SR, a re-run that evaluated each criterion separately and combined with explicit boolean logic surfaced 4 additional cases of evidence loss.

Aggregate picture (Table 2)

  • Most frequent pattern: LLM–Incorrect Topic Inference (32 instances) — this is the headline finding.
  • EL vs. FP distribution: Evidence Loss dominates in most patterns; the exceptions are LLM–Not Main Focus and (in some studies) LLM–Incorrect Topic Inference, where the LLM's strictness produces more FPs.
  • Two-SR outlier (AI4TInd): With the "all three LLMs agree" rule, κ = 0.77; under the "any LLM agrees" rule, κ collapses to 0.13. The paper transparently reports both and notes that the choice was driven by qualitative-analysis tractability, not statistical optimality.

Recommendations (RQ2, mapped to patterns in Table 3)

  • R1 – Run Multiple LLMs. Diversity helps surface misunderstandings; but note that shared training-data misconceptions (e.g., that code reviews mainly catch functional defects) can persist across models.
  • R2 – Run Each Criterion Separately, Then Combine with Boolean Logic. The LLM defaults to AND across criteria, but SRs may use OR; breaking the task into per-criterion decisions and combining programmatically gives better control.
  • R3 – Define Criteria Unambiguously. Force explicit definitions of key terms, boundary conditions, and common confounders/near-misses before running the LLM.
  • R4 – Validate From Boundaries. Clean-cut decisions teach you nothing. Use either R4.1 LLM Disagreement (sample papers where the LLMs disagree with each other) or R4.2 LLM Probability (ask for per-criterion probabilities, then sweep the 0.5 region to find a "shoulder" cutoff).
  • R5 – Double-Check Human Decisions. Use the LLM as an external perspective to stress-test human screening; particularly useful for novice reviewers or under-resourced teams.

Authors & Credibility

  • Team: 8 authors across the University of Helsinki, LUT University, Northern Arizona University, UFMS, UTFPR, and UFAM (Brazil).
  • Senior members (Mäntylä, Gerosa, Steinmacher, Conte, Felizardo) are well-established in empirical software engineering and the systematic-review methodology community; this is a strong fit for the topic.
  • Tooling: The screening uses AISysRev (Huotala et al., 2026), a tool the same group has previously benchmarked — both a strength (consistent protocol) and a soft conflict-of-interest note.
  • Funding: Research Council of Finland (359861, 358471), CAPES, CNPq, FAPEAM, UFMS — all publicly disclosed.
  • Reproducibility: Underlying data is on Zenodo; the analysis method is documented well enough to be auditable, but the LLM prompts, decoding settings, and full per-paper decisions beyond the disagreement instances are not all in the paper itself.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The genuinely new contribution is the 7-pattern taxonomy and the coding scheme. The Kappa numbers (0.52–0.77) largely replicate prior work (Huotala et al., 2025; Chan et al., 2025). The paper is honest that prior studies have "reached some similar findings to ours … as sub-analysis from the prior works."
  • Rigor — strengths: Multiple SRs, mixed methods, six researchers in the coding loop, transparent reporting of an alternative analysis (AI4TInd κ = 0.13 vs 0.77), explicit separation of EL vs. FP.
  • Rigor — weaknesses:
  • No inter-rater reliability coefficient is computed — the team uses consensual validation. For a paper that is a qualitative taxonomy, this is a real gap: the reader cannot judge the stability of the categories.
  • Two of the six SRs (AI4T, AI4TInd) are coded by a single human rater; the authors flag this but it weakens cross-SR comparability.
  • Three of the six SRs (GenAIEdu, AI4T, AI4TInd) are unpublished/ongoing; their inclusion criteria may not be stable.
  • Zero-shot only, with two specific models (gemini-2.5-flash, gpt-4.1-mini, plus claude-haiku-4.5 in one SR) — the taxonomy's generalizability to other model families, sizes, or prompting strategies is unverified.
  • The "Human–Error" pattern is small (n=4) and the methodology for retrospectively deciding that the human was wrong — using the LLM's rationale as evidence — is potentially circular.
  • Reproducibility: Partial. Data and tool are released; the LLM versions, exact prompts, and per-paper decisions would need to be reconstructed from the AISysRev tool. The very wide κ range (0.13 vs 0.77) depending on aggregation rule should make any reader cautious about citing specific numbers.
  • Significance: Moderate-to-high for the SR-tooling community. The recommendations are sensible and likely to be cited. The bigger value is the taxonomy as a shared vocabulary for future empirical work.
  • Red flags:
  1. Recommendations are not evaluated in the paper — the authors explicitly say "the impact in terms of empirical evidence of the recommendations is currently unclear." So the paper provides plausibility arguments for R1–R5, not evidence.
  2. The "Human–Error" pattern is the most provocative claim (LLMs make humans better) and rests on the thinnest evidence (n=4, single-author re-evaluation). It is a fascinating hypothesis but should not be over-cited.
  3. No formal inter-rater reliability on a paper that is fundamentally a qualitative taxonomy.
  4. The AI4TInd κ=0.13 footnote is telling: with the "any LLM agrees" rule, agreement is barely above chance. The paper buries this in a transparency note but it deserves more weight — it suggests the headline κ of 0.77 in that SR is partly an artifact of the aggregation rule, not the models' discriminative ability.
  5. Generalizability is limited to SE; medical, social-science, and humanities SRs may have different boundary and topic-inference issues (e.g., the "case study" misuse in Petersen [18] cited in the paper is itself a non-SE analog).

Strengths

  • Genuinely qualitative contribution: moves past "agreement = X" to "here is the structure of the disagreement."
  • Actionable, structured recommendations with an explicit pattern-to-recommendation mapping table.
  • Honest scope and limitations: the authors admit the recommendations are unvalidated, the SRs are SE-only, and the LLM models are a snapshot.
  • Two-direction analysis: does not treat human consensus as ground truth, but identifies cases where LLMs flag human misses.
  • Reusable infrastructure: AISysRev + Zenodo dataset make the work a foundation for follow-up studies.
  • Concrete sub-recommendations (R4.1, R4.2) give practitioners clear next steps for boundary detection.

Limitations

  • Domain: Software engineering only — generalization to medical, social-science, or humanities SRs is unverified.
  • Models: gemini-2.5-flash, gpt-4.1-mini, claude-haiku-4.5 (one SR); no larger reasoning models, no open-weight models, no model fine-tunes, no few-shot / CoT / RAG comparisons.
  • Single-rater SRs: AI4T and AI4TInd rely on one human, weakening cross-SR comparability of the taxonomy.
  • No formal inter-rater reliability on the qualitative codes.
  • Recommendations are not empirically validated in this paper; their "impact" is left to future work.
  • Aggregation rule sensitivity: the AI4TInd κ swings from 0.13 to 0.77 depending on whether "any LLM" or "all LLMs" is used; the paper reports this transparently but the headline numbers should be cited with the rule attached.
  • Ongoing SRs: three of the six SRs are unpublished; their inclusion criteria may not be stable.
  • Pattern frequencies are small (the largest pattern has 32 instances across all six SRs); some patterns (e.g., LLM–Keyword Overweight with n=8) may be under-supported.
  • Reference decision problem: treating human consensus as ground truth conflates ambiguous eligibility criteria with LLM errors — a fundamental issue for any LLM-vs-human screening study.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How well do these 7 patterns transfer to non-SE domains (medical, social science, humanities) where inclusion criteria are often more norm-bound and abstracts are more templated?
  • Does R2 (separate criteria + boolean combination) measurably reduce LLM–Incorrect Topic Inference and Criteria-Combination errors in a controlled re-run?
  • Can the R4.2 LLM Probability "shoulder" cutoff be turned into a calibrated abstention/refusal signal, and how does it compare to using a stronger reasoning model with chain-of-thought?
  • What is the inter-rater reliability of the 7-pattern taxonomy if a second independent team re-codes the same disagreement instances?
  • Does using two strong reasoning models in ensemble (e.g., GPT-5-class + Claude-Opus-class) reproduce the headline κ of 0.77, or does it move into the 0.85+ regime where LLM-assisted screening becomes a credible first pass?
  • The Human–Error pattern (n=4) is the most under-supported but potentially the most important. Can a larger study confirm that LLM rationales systematically surface papers missed by human screeners, and quantify the net effect on recall?
  • How sensitive is the taxonomy to prompt design — e.g., asking the LLM to flag "uncertain" cases explicitly, or providing one or two worked examples?
  • The AI4TInd κ=0.13 vs 0.77 sensitivity is a methodological red flag. Does the same pattern appear in other large SRs under different aggregation rules, and what is the "fair" way to report LLM screening accuracy?
