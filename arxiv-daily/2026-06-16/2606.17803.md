> **一句话总结：** 提出 ELM（Experiential Latent Memory）方法，将 LLM 在每个测试样本上消耗的推理时计算蒸馏为极轻量（约 0.001% 参数 / 20 个可训练 token）的软提示"潜在记忆"，用 GRPO + 多数投票自奖励训练 10 步后存储检索，并在不可靠时由 PRM 验证器在 zero-shot 与 memory-augmented 响应间路由，在 MATH500/AMC23/AIME24 上以在线方式匹配甚至超过全数据集离线训练。

> **TL;DR:** The paper introduces ELM, a strictly online continual self-improvement method: for each encountered test sample, train a 20-token soft-prompt memory (~0.001% of model parameters) for 10 GRPO steps using majority-voting self-rewards, then store and retrieve it for future inputs. A PRM verifier routes between zero-shot and memory-augmented responses for reliability. Across MATH500, AMC23, and AIME24, ELM beats training-free ICL baselines and matches or exceeds full-dataset offline GRPO/TTRL training, while transferring across datasets and avoiding the catastrophic forgetting that full-model updates risk.

## 核心贡献

- **诊断发现：** 原始推理 trace 上的 ICL 无法泛化，因为 token 级复用缺乏抽象能力（即便经过自反思精炼），从而**将研究焦点从 trace 转向学到的潜在表示**。
- **关键洞察：** 在内部自奖励（多数投票）下，对**单个样本做轻量训练**往往能超过全数据集离线训练——这一反直觉现象是方法学的核心。
- **ELM 方法：** 对每个测试样本构建一条 20-token、约 0.001% 模型参数的软提示记忆，用 8 rollouts × 10 步的 GRPO 蒸馏推理时计算，无需外部监督且主模型保持冻结。
- **可靠性设计：** 通过 (i) 输入嵌入空间或 KV 表征空间的检索（Input / KV retrieval）+ (ii) PRM 验证器路由 zero-shot vs memory-augmented 响应，避免噪声记忆把原本正确的回答变错。
- **模块化抗遗忘：** 经验被独立参数化进每条记忆，跨样本无干扰，规避了全模型在线更新带来的灾难性遗忘与 TTRL 已知的不稳定性。
- **跨数据集迁移：** AIME24↔AMC23 的双向迁移实验显示，记忆能泛化到未见过的测试集（Qwen AIME24 60% / Llama AMC23 20% vs 零样本 50% / 20%）。

## Key Contributions

- **Negative empirical result:** ICL on raw (even self-refined) reasoning traces yields negligible gains, motivating latent memory representations over textual trace storage.
- **Counter-intuitive finding:** Per-sample training with internal self-rewards can outperform full-dataset offline GRPO/TTRL, exposing a signal-vs-data mismatch in TTRL.
- **ELM (Experiential Latent Memory):** A 20-token, ~0.001%-parameter soft-prompt trained per sample via 10-step GRPO with majority-voting rewards, while the base model stays frozen.
- **Online protocol with reliability:** Two retrieval modes (input-embedding / KV-activation) plus a PRM-based verifier for response routing mitigate the negative-flip problem of noisy memories.
- **Modular continual learning:** Per-sample parameter isolation avoids interference and catastrophic forgetting, addressing limitations of single-model TTRL.
- **Cross-dataset transfer:** Memories constructed online on one math benchmark transfer to another, suggesting abstraction beyond surface patterns.

## Methodology

**Algorithm 1 — Continual Self-Improvement with ELM.** For each incoming test sample $x_i$ in a stream:

1. **Memory retrieval.** If memory pool $M$ is non-empty, embed $x_i$ and retrieve top-1 memory $m^*$ via cosine similarity — either in input-embedding space (input retrieval) or in layer-wise Q/K pooled activations (KV retrieval).
2. **Response routing.** Generate both $a_{zs} \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot | x_i)$ and $a_{mem} \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot | [m^*, x_i])$. If they agree, return the shared answer; otherwise let a PRM verifier (Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-7B) pick the higher-scoring one.
3. **Memory creation.** Sample $K=8$ rollouts, derive a consensus answer $\hat{y}^*$ via majority voting as the internal reward label, then train a fresh 20-token soft prompt $m^{(i)}$ on $x_i$ for $S=10$ GRPO steps with this binary reward. Add $m^{(i)}$ to $M$.
4. **No full-model updates.** The base policy $\pi_\theta$ is frozen throughout; each memory is an isolated parameter island.

**Why soft prompts?** Two properties: (1) efficiency — only ~0.08M (Llama) / 0.07M (Qwen) trainable parameters per memory; (2) retrieval-aligned key construction — soft prompts live in the same input-embedding space used for retrieval, so top-retrieved memories are also top-performing offline (this alignment breaks for LoRA, where retrieval is misaligned with utility).

**Implementation details.** Group size $G=8$, max length 2048, KL $\beta=0.01$, AdamW, constant LR $10^{-4}$ (soft prompt / LoRA) and $10^{-3}$ (prefix tuning), batch size 64, no grad accum. Sampling temperature 0.8 (Qwen) / 0.7 (Llama). Single H200 for training, A100 for eval. Each memory costs ~140 s/step (Llama) and ~48 s/step (Qwen).

## Results & Highlights

**Offline per-sample training (Table 1).** On AMC23 and AIME24, ELM offline with soft-prompt memories surpasses both GRPO with ground truth and TTRL (majority-vote, full model) at a fraction of the parameters:

| Method | #Params | AMC23 | AIME24 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Llama TTRL | 8B | 27.5 | 10 |
| Llama ELM offline | 0.08M | **35** | **10** |
| Qwen TTRL | 7B | 62.5 | 20 |
| Qwen ELM offline | 0.07M | **65** | **20** |
| Qwen ELM oracle (ZS vs Mem) | — | **67.5** | **22.5** |

**Online streaming (Table 3).** Main result, no prior dataset access:

| Method | MATH500 (Llama) | AMC23 (Qwen) | AIME24 (Qwen) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Zero-shot CoT | 45 | 50 | 10 |
| ICL (raw) | 46.3 | 38.3 | 12.2 |
| Reasoning Bank | 42.7 | 37.5 | 6.7 |
| LAG | 44 | 40 | 16.7 |
| **ELM input** | **51.4** | 72.5 | 12 |
| **ELM kv** | 50.7 | **72.6** | 13 |
| GRPO (offline, GT) | 47 | 68 | 20 |
| TTRL (offline, MV) | 45.6 | 67.2 | 20 |

ELM is the strongest online method; on Qwen AMC23 it matches offline TTRL (72.5/72.6 vs 67.2) and gains +12.5 over zero-shot; on Llama MATH500 it builds memories fully online yet beats offline GRPO/TTRL (51.4 vs 47/45.6). Notably, training-free ICL baselines (ICL, Reasoning Bank, LAG) are inconsistent and sometimes regress.

**Memory quality (Figure 2 / Table 2).** On AIME24 (Llama), 73% of soft-prompt memories improve over zero-shot, 13% regress, 13% neutral; 20% of memories exceed +5 points gain. The negative-flip rate of top-5 memories on AMC23 is ~20% (Qwen 18.5%, LLaMA 21.6%), with low top-2 IoU (0.55 / 0.37), evidencing per-memory specialization and motivating retrieval+verifier routing.

**Ablations.** (1) Soft prompt > LoRA > Prefix Tuning > full-model in offline generalization (Table 4); (2) LoRA in online setting gives no gain over zero-shot because top-retrieved LoRA memories are precisely the ones that fail offline (72% of retrievals come from three misaligned adapters); (3) Cross-dataset transfer from AIME24→AMC23 (and vice versa) consistently beats zero-shot (Figure 3); (4) Reducing training to 2 steps gives smaller but consistent gains; (5) GRPO > SFT on greedy responses (which would reinforce self-errors) under the no-GT setting.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty.** The pieces are individually familiar: per-instance soft-prompt tuning, GRPO with majority-vote self-rewards (TTRL), modular memory as a continual-learning primitive. The novel synthesis is (a) a strictly online, strictly test-time protocol that doesn't touch base parameters, and (b) a clean negative result (raw-trace ICL fails) used to motivate the latent-memory representation. The narrative is well-constructed and the framing is genuinely new as a *protocol*.
- **Rigor.** The diagnostic of "per-sample training > full-dataset offline" is striking but deserves scrutiny: the offline baselines (GRPO with GT, TTRL with 64/32 rollouts) are computed under matching compute, but the per-sample ELM uses a much tighter signal (8 rollouts). The 2-step vs 10-step ablation suggests the headline gap is robust to step count, but the offline-full-model negative-flip / signal-mixing story is mostly a hypothesis, not a controlled ablation. The KV-vs-input retrieval ablation is under-explored (only "small differences").
- **Reproducibility.** Implementation is fully specified: GRPO with DR-GRPO loss, $G=8$, length 2048, AdamW, KL $\beta=0.01$, LR $10^{-4}$, PRM = Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-7B, seeds [17, 42, 78]. H200/A100 specs and runtime estimates are reported. Per-memory hyperparameters (init scheme from input embeddings, prompt length 20) are pinned. **Missing: code release mention and memory key/repo data structures** — important for replicating the 0.08M / 0.07M parameter counts and the retrieval-utility alignment result.
- **Significance.** If the claim holds, this is a practically useful recipe for **persistent test-time compute**: it is the first strictly-online protocol that beats training-free ICL on math reasoning with ~0.001% extra parameters per problem. The cross-dataset transfer result is the strongest evidence that the memories encode something general rather than memorization.
- **Red flags / things to call out.**
  1. **Verifier dependency.** The online PRM routing is doing real work (the oracle gap in Table 1 shows the headroom is large). Without the PRM, ELM-Input on Llama MATH500 drops to 51.4 (still best), but the ceiling would shrink. The paper could be more explicit about how much of the gain comes from the memory itself vs the verifier.
  2. **Verifier@2 baseline is weak.** Table 8 shows a simple "sample 2 + verifier" zero-shot baseline already lifts Llama AMC23 from 20→25; the ELM delta over this fairer baseline is smaller than the headline table suggests.
  3. **Storage scales linearly with problems.** The paper does not address retrieval cost, eviction, or merging as $|M|$ grows; for a real continual deployment this is a real concern. The paper notes this as future work, which is honest, but the deployment picture remains underspecified.
  4. **Domain restriction.** Self-supervision via majority voting is only well-defined for verifiable tasks. The paper does not test non-verifiable domains (the limitation is acknowledged).
  5. **3-seed reporting is light** for noisy benchmarks (AIME24 has only 30 problems, so seed-to-seed variance is meaningful).
  6. **"Surpasses full-dataset offline training" is true on average but the gap is dataset-dependent**: on Qwen AIME24 the online ELM (12-13) underperforms TTRL offline (20) and matches zero-shot. The narrative of "online matches offline" is strongest on AMC23 and MATH500, weakest on AIME24.

## Strengths

- **Strictly online protocol** — encounters each test sample once, no access to the future evaluation set, no offline pre-training. This is a real and important setting that prior TTRL/rest/test-time-RL work does not address.
- **Parameter efficiency** — ~0.001% of model parameters per memory, with 10 GRPO steps and no base-model updates. Compute and memory overhead are tiny compared to LoRA / full fine-tuning.
- **Honest negative result** — the raw-trace ICL failure is documented and the LoRA-misaligned-retrieval failure is dissected in detail (which LoRA memories get retrieved, and why), rather than glossed over.
- **Modular continual-learning design** — per-sample parameter isolation directly addresses catastrophic forgetting, a notoriously thorny failure mode in classical online continual learning.
- **Clean ablations** — soft-prompt vs LoRA vs Prefix-Tuning vs full-model, plus a 2-step budget ablation, plus a SFT-vs-GRPO objective ablation. The parameter-count comparison (~0.08M vs 42M) is concrete.
- **Cross-dataset transfer** is shown in both directions (AIME24↔AMC23), which is a stronger signal than within-distribution generalization alone.

## Limitations

- **Reliance on self-generated majority-vote rewards** restricts the method to verifiable domains (math, code). Open-ended generation, dialogue, or subjective tasks lack a clean consensus signal.
- **Memory library grows unboundedly** with usage; the paper does not address retrieval cost at scale, eviction policies, or how to consolidate / merge / distill memories back into the model.
- **No principled criterion for which samples produce high-quality memories** — preliminary experiments show no strong correlation between simple training-free statistics and memory utility. This means ELM cannot yet decide *when* to spend compute creating a memory.
- **Verifier dependency** — the PRM-based response routing contributes a non-trivial portion of the gain; without an oracle verifier, gains shrink, and a small "verifier@2" baseline closes some of the gap on Qwen AIME24.
- **Narrow base-model / benchmark coverage** — only two 7-8B models on three math benchmarks; AIME24 has just 30 problems, making 3-seed reporting underpowered. Larger models, non-math reasoning, and code are untested.
- **Per-memory training cost is non-trivial** — ~140 s/step (Llama) × 10 steps = ~23 min per memory on an H200; accumulating memories over $10^4$-$10^6$ problems becomes a real deployment cost, even if the per-memory parameter footprint is small.
- **Limited interpretability** — the paper explicitly disclaims analysis of "what individual memories encode" and does not ablate over retrieval thresholds, prompt-length, or memory-count budgets.

## Relevance

该工作直接对应"持续学习 + 推理时计算"两条核心研究方向：把推理时计算蒸馏成模块化、参数极省的潜在记忆，并以在线流式协议实现无外部监督的稳定自我改进。这一范式对长期运行、自演化的智能体系统（如推理型 agent、test-time-scaling 流水线）具有清晰的方法论迁移价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- **Scaling and storage.** What is the retrieval latency at $|M|=10^4$, $10^5$, $10^6$? Can a hierarchical / approximate index keep it tractable without sacrificing the alignment between retrieved and effective memories?
- **Memory lifecycle.** When and how should memories be *evicted*, *merged*, *distilled* back into the base model, or *refused* at creation? The paper leaves this entire axis open.
- **Beyond verifiable domains.** Can the same recipe extend to settings where majority voting is noisy or undefined (e.g., open-ended dialogue, scientific writing), perhaps via soft-vote or LLM-as-judge rewards?
- **Verifier-free routing.** The PRM does real work. Could a smaller, learned router (or a self-consistency check) replace the PRM and decouple ELM from an external reward model?
- **Sample selection.** Is there a cheap *training-free* signal that predicts whether a given test instance will yield a high-utility memory (e.g., zero-shot agreement rate, entropy of rollouts, retrieval-neighborhood density)?
- **Memory compositionality.** Can multiple retrieved memories be composed (concatenated, averaged, gated) rather than top-1, given the low top-2 IoU? Does composition help on harder problems?
- **Cross-domain and cross-scale transfer.** Does the cross-dataset transfer hold across (a) non-math reasoning, (b) much larger or smaller base models, and (c) a different verification modality (e.g., unit tests for code)?
- **Theory of the per-sample > offline phenomenon.** What explains the result that per-sample training with 8-rollout MV rewards outperforms full-dataset TTRL with 64-rollout MV rewards? A formal analysis of credit-assignment variance and signal-vs-noise mixing per sample would be valuable.
- **Online stability over long horizons.** The paper studies short streams (≤500 problems). Does ELM maintain or improve over $10^4$+ samples, especially in the presence of distribution shift between early and late streams?
- **Interaction with stronger base models.** ELM is evaluated on 7-8B models; would a 70B+ reasoning model render the per-memory delta negligible, or would the modularity still pay off in long-horizon agent settings?
