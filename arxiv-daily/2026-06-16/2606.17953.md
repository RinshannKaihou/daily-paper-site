> **一句话总结：** 该工作通过逐层 Modal Dominance Ratio (MDR) 诊断发现 MLLM 在中间层已形成正确视觉预测，末层被文本覆盖（"late-layer textual override"），并据此提出免训练解码方法 CALRD，在 5 个模型上取得最高 9.4% 冲突基准绝对提升。

> **TL;DR:** The authors trace MLLM predictions layer-by-layer under vision–text conflict and discover "late-layer textual override": the model often commits to a vision-correct answer in its intermediate layers and then abandons it at the final layer in favor of text. The direction of that late-layer shift is a strong correctness signal (ΔMDR<0 in 85% of failures, ΔMDR>0 in 89% of successes). They turn this into a training-free intervention, CALRD, which detects harmful overrides via two label-free signals (anchor confidence D_conf and prediction retention ρ) and blends transition-layer logits back into the output. Across five MLLMs (InstructBLIP, LLaVA-1.5, LLaVA-1.6, Qwen2.5-VL, Qwen3-VL) CALRD yields up to 9.4% absolute gains on PhD-icc/Conflict-VQA while preserving POPE and CHAIR, adding only ~1.5 ms/token of overhead.

## 核心贡献

- 引入 **Modal Dominance Ratio (MDR)** 对 MLLM 逐层模态偏好进行量化，发现 **"late-layer textual override"**：失败样本在中间层 MDR 为正（视觉主导），末层翻转为负（文本主导），而成功样本无此翻转。
- 揭示 **转换方向决定正确性**：失败样本 85% 表现为 ΔMDR<0（朝文本漂移），Conflict-Correct 89% 与 Non-Conflict 91% 表现为 ΔMDR>0（朝视觉漂移或稳定），方向而非发生本身才是关键。
- 提出 **CALRD (Conflict-Aware Layer Reference Decoding)**：训练免费、推理时介入方法，通过 anchor confidence (D_conf) 与 prediction retention (ρ) 两个无标签信号检测有害覆盖，并对 logits 做 (1−λ)·φ^(N) + λ·φ^(L_trans) 的自适应混合。
- 构建 **Conflict-VQA** 诊断基准（5,969 样本），含 6 类问题（object presence / color / attribute / counting / spatial / activity），由 VrR-VG 图像与 TDIUC 问题经 GPT-4o 生成、人工核验而成，提供 (w_vis, w_text) 配对标注。
- 在 5 个 MLLM × 3 种解码策略（greedy/beam/nucleus）下验证：冲突基准最高 +9.4%，POPE 提升 1.5–5.4 F1，CHAIR 在多数配置下降，仅带来 1.5 ms/token 与 80 MB 额外开销。

## Key Contributions

- **Modal Dominance Ratio (MDR)** as a layer-wise lens onto MLLM predictions, used to localize the failure to a specific phase of the forward pass.
- **Late-layer textual override**: a precise mechanistic description of the textual-bias phenomenon — the visual answer is computed in intermediate layers and then overwritten at the final layer.
- **Directional signature of correctness**: ΔMDR<0 in 85% of conflict-incorrect cases, ΔMDR>0 in 89–91% of correct cases, providing a simple diagnostic of when late-layer processing is helping vs. hurting.
- **CALRD**: a training-free decoding method using two label-free detection signals (anchor confidence D_conf, prediction retention ρ) and an adaptive logit blend; no external models or knowledge required.
- **Conflict-VQA**: a 5,969-sample diagnostic benchmark with explicit (w_vis, w_text) annotations, supporting layer-wise mechanistic analysis that prior conflict benchmarks did not allow.
- Extensive evaluation across **5 MLLMs × 3 decoding regimes**, with ablations on each signal, on fixed vs. dynamic transition layer, and an efficiency analysis (≈1.5 ms/token overhead, 80 MB).

## Methodology

**Layer-wise projection.** Following early-exit work (Elbayad et al. 2020; Schuster et al. 2022), the hidden state at the answer-token position at every layer l is projected through the LM head: P_t^(l) = softmax(W_head · h_t^(l)). This gives the model's "preference" at every depth.

**Modal Dominance Ratio.** For a knowledge-conflict sample where the image supports w_vis and the context supports w_text, MDR(l) = log P_t^(l)(w_vis) / P_t^(l)(w_text). Positive MDR = visual preference; negative MDR = textual preference.

**Transition localization.** Two steps identify where the late-layer shift happens. (1) The stability onset L_start is the layer with the largest entropy drop H_t^(l−1) − H_t^(l); empirically L_start falls between 25%–40% of model depth. (2) The transition layer L_trans = argmax_{l∈[L_start, N−1]} JSD(P_t^(l), P_t^(l+1)), where JSD is the Jensen–Shannon divergence between adjacent layer distributions. The transition direction is then summarized by ΔMDR = mean(MDR_after) − mean(MDR_before).

**Override detection (label-free).** Given w* = argmax_w P_t^(L_trans)(w), the method uses two signals that do not need ground-truth w_vis / w_text:
- **Anchor confidence:** D_conf = P_t^(L_trans)(w*) — how committed the model was at the transition layer.
- **Prediction retention:** ρ = min(1, P_t^(N)(w*) / P_t^(L_trans)(w*)) — whether that prediction survived to the final layer.

High D_conf and low ρ is the override signature (visualized in Figure 5 of the paper as a clean separation between Conflict-Incorrect samples and the other two groups).

**Adaptive intervention.** The intervention strength λ = D_conf · (1 − ρ) is a multiplicative product, so it is large only when a confident prediction is being suppressed. The corrected logits are
φ'_t = (1 − λ) · φ_t^(N) + λ · φ_t^(L_trans),
with output P'_t = softmax(φ'_t). λ=0 reduces to vanilla decoding; λ=1 fully replaces the output with the transition-layer logits. The procedure is recomputed per token, so the strength varies across the sequence. CALRD is compatible with greedy, beam, and nucleus decoding because the intervention operates at the logit level.

**Conflict-VQA construction.** Images come from VrR-VG (scene-graph grounded) and questions from TDIUC, covering six question types. For each sample, GPT-4o generates two 3–5-sentence contexts — a C_factual consistent with the image and a C_conflict that differs only in one contradictory statement supporting w_text. Two researchers independently verified the samples; ~8% of candidates were filtered out. A "competent" subset requires a model to answer correctly in all five trials under C_factual, ensuring that observed failures under C_conflict reflect conflict resolution rather than basic perception errors.

## Results & Highlights

**Conflict resolution (Table 2 of the paper):** on Conflict-VQA / PhD-icc the absolute gains of CALRD over the same vanilla decoder are:
- InstructBLIP greedy: +4.51 / +8.54; beam: +4.51 / +9.02; nucleus: +4.13 / +9.39.
- LLaVA-1.5 greedy: +5.70 / +8.63; beam: +5.59 / +8.33; nucleus: +4.64 / +5.62.
- LLaVA-1.6 greedy: +7.16 / +8.29; beam: +6.80 / +8.60; nucleus: +6.80 / +4.04.
- Qwen2.5-VL greedy: +4.54 / +4.90; beam: +4.76 / +5.59; nucleus: +7.43 / +4.78.
- Qwen3-VL greedy: +1.84 / +3.36; beam: +3.40 / +2.07; nucleus: +3.16 / +2.29.

The headline 9.4% absolute (≈20%+ relative) is on InstructBLIP + PhD-icc under nucleus decoding. The largest gains cluster on the binary yes/no PhD-icc setting, which the authors attribute to the strong "yes/no" pull of misleading context on binary tasks; the open-ended Conflict-VQA yields smaller but still consistent gains.

**Baseline comparison.** CALRD beats all four representative baselines (DoLa, VCD, OPERA, DeCo) in every reported cell of Table 2. DoLa actually hurts InstructBLIP on Conflict-VQA (39.61 → 32.72), illustrating the risk of applying layer-based corrections indiscriminately. DeCo is the strongest prior method but still trails CALRD on most configurations.

**Standard benchmarks (non-conflict).** On POPE (F1, Table 3) CALRD improves over vanilla in every model × decoder cell, with gains ranging from +0.3 to +5.4. On CHAIR (Table 4) it lowers both C_I and C_S in the majority of configurations (e.g., InstructBLIP greedy C_S 58.8 → 40.2, a 31.6% relative reduction; C_I 23.7 → 14.6, a 38.4% relative reduction). These results argue that override is not exclusive to explicit conflict settings.

**Ablations (LLaVA-1.6, greedy; Table 5).** Removing D_conf drops Conflict-VQA 49.5 → 47.3; removing ρ drops 49.5 → 45.1; using a fixed layer at 75% depth instead of dynamic L_trans drops 49.5 → 45.9. The product form is validated: both signals contribute, and dynamic layer localization matters because transition points vary across samples.

**Efficiency (Figure 7).** On LLaVA-1.5-7B, vanilla decoding = 18.3 ms/token; CALRD = 19.8 ms/token (≈8% overhead, +80 MB memory). VCD nearly doubles latency (dual stream); OPERA is ≈10× slower (iterative rollback); DeCo sits in between. CALRD stays closest to the vanilla latency–memory Pareto frontier.

**Take-away pattern.** Models rank on PhD-icc vanilla as LLaVA-1.5 (27.7) < LLaVA-1.6 (28.2) < InstructBLIP (41.1) < Qwen2.5-VL (51.3) < Qwen3-VL (60.3), and CALRD's gains follow the inverse order — weaker models benefit more, because stronger models already resolve conflicts correctly in their late layers, leaving fewer overridden predictions to recover.

The methodological mix (mechanistic tracing + lightweight inference-time intervention + a new diagnostic benchmark) is well-scoped and the work targets a current, well-known weakness of MLLMs. Funding: NSFC grants 62306331, 62407037 and CAAI Youth Talent Lifting Project. ## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty.** Strong. The phenomenon "MLLMs favor text in conflict" is well documented (HallusionBench, PhD, AutoHallusion, MMKC); the genuinely new piece is the layer-wise mechanistic story — the correct answer is computed early and then overwritten — plus the directional-shift diagnostic. CALRD's multiplicative (D_conf · (1−ρ)) gate is a clean, simple way to operationalize that.
- **Rigor.** The five-model × three-decoder grid is broader than most inference-time methods (which often report one or two models). The ablations on each signal and on fixed vs. dynamic L_trans are present. The mechanism story is supported by both MDR curves and JSD plots (Figure 3 / Figure 4). Some weaker points: the "competent subset" filter is necessary but means the headline numbers are over a curated subset of Conflict-VQA — main-paper evaluation uses the full 5,969 samples, which is good, but worth flagging.
- **Reproducibility.** The method is training-free and the equations are explicit, so implementation is feasible. The transition layer is found per sample via JSD, which is well-defined. The main risk is the choice of decoding regime (greedy/beam/nucleus) and any per-model hyperparameters not surfaced in the main text. The appendix (F) is described as covering implementation details; code availability is not asserted in the abstract.
- **Significance.** High for the multimodal-robustness subfield. The work reframes a behavioral observation as a mechanistic, actionable failure mode and ships both a diagnostic (Conflict-VQA, MDR analysis) and a remedy (CALRD). It also offers a clean comparative story vs. DoLa/DeCo/VCD/OPERA.
- **Red flags to watch.**
  1. **"Largely preserves standard performance"** is asserted qualitatively. On POPE the gains are positive, but on CHAIR some cells show small regressions (e.g., LLaVA-1.6 nucleus C_I 10.5 → 11.5) — a stricter reading of "preserves" would acknowledge these.
  2. **The 85% / 89% statistic** is computed on a per-model basis and may vary by architecture; the paper shows the LLaVA-1.6 figure but readers should check that it holds across all five models.
  3. **Curation of Conflict-VQA** (manual verification, ~8% filtered) is fine but means the benchmark is partly human-in-the-loop, which is a higher bar than fully synthetic conflict benchmarks.
  4. **Binary vs. open-ended.** PhD-icc is yes/no; Conflict-VQA is open-ended. The bigger gains on the binary task raise the question of how much of the improvement is just helping models escape "yes/no mode" — a useful future control.
  5. **Closed/API models are excluded** (CALRD needs intermediate-layer access), so the practical impact is bounded to open-weight MLLMs.

## Strengths

- Reframes a known behavioral bias as a **mechanistic, late-layer phenomenon**, making it both explainable and actionable.
- The **directional-shift diagnostic** (ΔMDR) is simple, interpretable, and operationally useful for "should I trust this prediction?".
- CALRD is **training-free, label-free, and architecture-agnostic**; integrates with greedy/beam/nucleus; minimal overhead (≈1.5 ms/token, +80 MB).
- The strongest gains appear on the **weakest models** (InstructBLIP, LLaVA-1.5/1.6), exactly where practical deployment needs help.
- Ships a **new diagnostic benchmark** (Conflict-VQA) with explicit (w_vis, w_text) labels, useful for the broader community.
- Comparative evaluation is thorough: 4 baselines × 5 models × 3 decoders, plus ablations and efficiency.

## Limitations

- Requires **intermediate-layer access**; cannot be applied to closed/API-only MLLMs.
- The intervention depends on identifying a **stability onset** and JSD-maximizing transition; for atypical inputs this localization may be noisy.
- The **competent subset** filter is used for the mechanistic analysis (Section 3), so the 85% / 89% statistic is over a curated slice; main-paper evaluation does use the full 5,969 samples.
- Some **CHAIR cells show small regressions** (e.g., LLaVA-1.6 nucleus C_I 10.5 → 11.5); "largely preserves" needs the per-cell numbers.
- The paper does not stress-test on **adversarial or longer-context conflicts**, multi-image inputs, or chain-of-thought decoding.
- No code release is announced in the abstract, which limits immediate reproducibility.

## Relevance

直接对应 MLLM 鲁棒性 / visual grounding 研究方向：把 "文本偏置" 这一已知行为问题推进到 "late-layer textual override" 的机制层面，并提供可落地的训练免费、推理时介入方法与可复用的诊断基准，对消融"文本捷径"和解释多模态融合过程都有方法论价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the late-layer override phenomenon persist (and does CALRD still help) on much larger open MLLMs (≥70B) and on reasoning-tuned variants?
- Can D_conf / ρ be replaced by a single learned signal (e.g., a small classifier over hidden states) to avoid the per-sample JSD scan?
- How does CALRD compose with other inference-time techniques (VCD, OPERA, attention steering) — additive gains, or do the interventions interfere?
- Does the directional-shift diagnostic (ΔMDR<0 ⇒ likely wrong) work well enough to be used as a **confidence / abstention signal** in deployment, beyond fixing the answer?
- How does the failure mode change with chain-of-thought or multi-step reasoning, where the "answer token" is no longer the only decision point?
- Does the same override pattern appear in **audio-text** or **video-text** MLLMs, as the authors suggest in their future-work paragraph?
- What is the right **layer-localization budget** (search range, JSD threshold) for very deep or very shallow models, and is there a robust heuristic?
