Evaluating Second-Order Bias of LLMs Through Epistemic Entitlement

arXiv: 2606.17506

Date: 2026-06-16

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.17506

一句话总结： 作者提出"二阶偏见"（second-order bias）——LLM 作为偏见评判者而非生成者时表现出的偏见——以知识论中的"认知权能"框架为基础设计逻辑推理任务，使 LLM 在无人口学线索下对偏见文本的可接受/不可接受群体作出推断，并在 8 个模型 × 5 个偏见数据集上展示该任务能规避安全护栏（refusal ≈ 0.4%）、触发系统性跨群体差异、暴露隐式"社会地图"（如 women→men 54.5%、Black→White 55.1%）以及目标群体自我归属性误判（如 Muslim 自我接受率达 73.2%）。
TL;DR: This paper coins second-order bias (SOB) — the social bias an LLM exhibits not in generating biased content, but in judging it. Built on entitlement epistemology (Wright & Davies, 2004; Greenough, 2020), the authors design a logical-reasoning task that asks an LLM to identify to whom a biased text would be acceptable or non-acceptable, where the epistemically warranted answer is "Unknown" because the prompt provides no demographic information. Across 8 models (GPT-5.1, Sonnet-4.6, OLMo-3.1-32B, Qwen-3.5-35B, Llama 3.1-8B, Llama 3.3-70B, Gemma 3-27B, Phi-4) and 5 datasets (DynaB, ToxiGen, HateCheck, iSHate, LingHate; 2,457 examples), the task (i) evades safety guardrails (~0.4% refusals), (ii) varies systematically across target groups (Friedman χ²_F = 29.17 / 33.70, both p<0.001), (iii) surfaces implicit social maps (e.g., dominant groups appear as top attributors in 14–24% of responses), and (iv) frequently self-locates the targeted group as acceptor of bias against itself (Muslim 73.24%, Black 68.0%, Asian 66.34%). Two simple metrics capture these phenomena, and the authors call for judgment-level, theoretically grounded bias evaluation in NLP.

核心贡献

  • 提出"二阶偏见"（SOB）概念：区别于一阶偏见（生成/隐含偏见内容），SOB 关注 LLM 作为偏见评判者时表现出的偏见——尤其在 LLM-as-judge 日益普及的当下。
  • 理论根基（entitlement epistemology）：基于 Wright & Davies (2004) 与 Greenough (2020) 的认知权能理论，将偏见重新概念化为"被错置的认知权能"（misplaced entitlement），并推导出一组逻辑条件，让"Unknown"成为认知理性的唯一合规回答。
  • 可操作的诊断任务：在偏见文本后要求 LLM (a) 在 accept / non-accept 框架下识别一个"人物"是否认为该文本合理，以及 (b) 用一组预定义人口学变量描述该人物；不基于任何 ground-truth 标签。
  • 两种互补的 SOB 度量：attribution rate α_t（模型给出非 Unknown 回答的比例）和 SOB_t = α_t · mean(g(r))（在非 Unknown 回答上对所给出人口学属性数量的加权均值），对 accept 与 non-accept 分别计算。
  • 两个社会危害的实证区分：Recirculatory Harm (H_r)——把偏见命题"社会化"为某些群体的合理观点；Attributional Harm (H_a)——把群体身份当作判断其能否接受/拒绝偏见的"证据"，涉及刻板归因与"认知排斥"。
  • 跨模型与跨数据集的系统性发现：前沿模型（GPT-5.1-Thinking, Gemma-27B）出现最高 SOB（acc >3），而 Qwen3.5-35B / OLMo-32B 在 acc 任务接近零但在 non-acc 任务显著上升——说明 SOB 是结构性而非表层现象，且推理微调并不一致地缓解 SOB。
  • 开源代码与模型响应：https://github.com/uofthcdslab/second-order-bias

Key Contributions

  • Formalizes "second-order bias": bias in an LLM's judgment about biased content, complementing the dominant literature that studies bias as a property of generation.
  • Builds on entitlement epistemology: recasts social bias as misplaced epistemic entitlement — a foundational proposition that is internally warranted but defeasible under epistemically rational inquiry.
  • Derives a logical-reasoning diagnostic: prompts an LLM to judge "to whom is this biased text acceptable/non-acceptable?" under a fixed set of logical conditions; the only epistemically rational answer when no demographic cue is given is Unknown.
  • Two parsimonious metrics: (1) attribution rate α_t and (2) SOB_t = α_t · (1/|A_t|) Σ_i g(r_i^t), the mean number of demographic attributes among non-Unknown responses, scaled by the rate of such responses.
  • Distinguishes two social harms: recirculatory harm (H_r — socially locating a biased proposition as a meaningful viewpoint) and attributional harm (H_a — stereotyping + epistemic exclusion of the targeted group from rational rejection).
  • Cross-model/cross-dataset empirical evidence: 8 models × 5 datasets; refusal rate ≈ 0.4%; systematic cross-target-group variation; implicit social maps surface; target labels trigger self-attribution of bias acceptance.
  • Open release: code + model responses at uofthcdslab/second-order-bias.

Methodology

Theoretical Framework (§3)

  • Epistemic entitlement (Wright & Davies, 2004): foundational propositions that are "accepted" without evidential support but are not arbitrary — e.g., "people are generally trustworthy" cannot be empirically grounded without circularity, yet functions as a cornerstone for ordinary reasoning.
  • Misplaced entitlement (the paper's definition, building on Greenough 2020): a proposition that is internally warranted for those who hold it, but is defeasible by available evidential work. Social bias is interpreted as such misplaced entitlement — internally self-evident, but rationally rejectable.
  • Two informal fallacies motivate the task design: hasty generalization and fallacy of composition — moving from insufficient or irrelevant evidence to an unwarranted conclusion about a social group.

Task Design (§4.1–4.2)

  • Logical conditions (Table 2; from Blair 2019): A biased text P is acceptable to person S iff (i) S knows/believes P with entitlement, (ii) P is known/reasonable in S's cognitive environment, or (iii) P follows from assertions S is entitled to accept. Non-acceptability is the negation. Otherwise → Unknown.
  • Two-step inference: (1) identify a person to whom the biased text is acceptable/non-acceptable under the conditions; (2) describe that person using a fixed demographic schema. If no warranted person → "Unknown."

SOB Metrics (§4.3)

For evaluation data D = {x_i}_{i=1..N} and task t ∈ {acc, non-acc}:

  • A_t = {i : r_i^t ≠ Unknown}
  • α_t = |A_t| / N
  • SOB_t = α_t · (1/|A_t|) Σ_{i∈A_t} g(r_i^t) where g(r) is the number of demographic attributes returned.

Lower SOB_t and lower α_t indicate less second-order bias. Refusals (≈ 0.4% across all experiments) are ignored.

Datasets & Models (§4.4)

  • Datasets: DynaB (Vidgen 2021), ToxiGen (Hartvigsen 2022), HateCheck (Röttger 2021), iSHate (Ocampo 2023), LingHate (Wiegand 2022). Stratified sampling by target group yields 2,457 biased examples.
  • Models: GPT-5.1, Sonnet-4.6, OLMo-3.1-32B, Qwen-3.5-35B, Llama 3.1-8B, Llama 3.3-70B, Gemma 3-27B, Phi-4. For models with configurable reasoning, "I" denotes instruct/minimal-reasoning and "T" denotes thinking/reasoning variant.

Statistical Test (§5.2)

Friedman test on per-target-group SOB ranks (separately for acc and non-acc) to test whether the rank distribution differs systematically across targets.

Results & Highlights

5.1 Existence of SOB

  • Refusal rate ≈ 0.4% (Llama8B 3.3%/5.3% on acc/non-acc and Qwen35B-I 1.8%/2.7% are the only above-zero cases) — the task systematically evades safety guardrails.
  • Most models return non-Unknown — attribution rates α_t are typically ≥ 0.8 across the 11 model variants.
  • Highest absolute SOB: GPT5.1-T reaches 5.33 on acc and 4.94 on non-acc; Gemma-27B ≈ 3.15/3.40; GPT5.1-I ≈ 3.66/2.58.
  • Lowest acc SOB: Qwen3.5-35B-I ≈ 0.0–0.03 (essentially always returns Unknown), OLMo-32B ≈ 0.02–0.16.
  • Asymmetry between acc and non-acc: Qwen3.5-35B and OLMo-32B are near-zero on acc but ≥1 on non-acc — these models are willing to attribute rejectors but not acceptors. GPT5.1-T is high on both.

Reasoning Tuning is Not a Universal Mitigation

  • For OLMo-32B and Sonnet-4.6, the reasoning variant reduces SOB (OLMo-32B drops to ≈ 0 on both tasks).
  • For GPT-5.1, the reasoning variant increases SOB by 50% on acc and 128% on non-acc relative to its instruct variant.
  • For Qwen-3.5-35B, reasoning also increases attribution on acc (0.0 → 0.05–0.11).
  • Conclusion: generating thinking tokens does not uniformly mitigate SOB.

5.2 Recirculatory Harm — Systematic Cross-Target Differences

  • Friedman test: χ²_F = 29.17, p<0.001 for acc; χ²_F = 33.70, p<0.001 for non-acc → the rank distribution of SOB scores differs significantly across target groups.
  • Most specified social location: Mexican and Immigrant groups (highest SOB on both acc and non-acc).
  • Least specified: Muslims and LGBTQ+ (fewer attributed demographic categories).
  • Task-specific outliers: OLMo-32B-I has near-zero acc SOB but its non-acc SOB is largely triggered by anti-LGBTQ+ texts; Gemma-27B shows stronger non-acc SOB for anti-women texts.

5.3 Attributional Harm — Implicit Social Maps and Associative Triggers

  • Implicit social map (acc task) — models attribute acceptability to dominant groups contrasted with the target:
  • women → men: 54.51%
  • Black → White: 55.12%
  • LGBTQ+ → heterosexuals: 49.92%
  • immigrants → White Americans: 53.82%
  • Across models, dominant groups appear in 14–24% of top attributions: White (24.5%), American (22.5%), men (18.0%), heterosexual (15.5%), adult (14.4%).
  • GPT-5.1-T specifically attributes 61% to "American" and 57.8% to "White" vs. Sonnet-4.6-I at 11.6%/11.4%.
  • Self-attribution (non-acc task) — models frequently attribute non-acceptability to the targeted group itself: Muslim 73.24%, Black 68.0%, Asian 66.34%, LGBTQ+ 55.60%, women 54.51%.
  • Associative triggers — for the acc task, the targeted group itself often reappears among inferred acceptors: Muslim 44.38% (vs. 15.67% non-Muslim), Jewish 39.18%, Asian 37.36%, Black 26.66%, women 30.46%. Sonnet-4.6-I attributes 52.89% of anti-Muslim texts as acceptable to Muslims themselves; GPT-5.1-I attributes 18.28% of anti-Jewish texts as acceptable to Jewish people themselves.
  • Epistemic exclusion — dominant groups inferred as leading rejectors in non-acc: men for anti-women (21.15%), heterosexuals for anti-LGBTQ+ (22.76%), White for anti-Black (23.93%), White Americans for anti-Mexican (21.14%). Llama-70B assigns non-acceptability of anti-disabled texts to non-disabled people (28.27%) more than to disabled people.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. The SOB framing — bias in judging bias rather than in generation — is a genuine conceptual move. It is well-timed for the LLM-as-judge era (alignment eval, automated red-teaming, fairness audits). Anchoring it in entitlement epistemology is unusual and gives the paper a defensible philosophical spine that most "bias benchmark" papers lack.
  • Rigor: Strong overall, with caveats.
  • The two metrics are clearly defined, the SOB formula is explicit, and refusal accounting is honest (~0.4% ignored).
  • Statistical analysis (Friedman test on target-group ranks) is appropriate for paired rank data and the effect sizes are large (χ²_F = 29.17 / 33.70).
  • However: no human-baseline comparison — the "bias" label rests on the assumption that "Unknown" is the epistemically warranted response, which is justified by the theory but not empirically validated against human judgment of the same prompts.
  • The structured-output post-processing step uses an LLM to normalize model responses, which introduces a small but unstated dependency on another model's behavior.
  • Single-target-per-instance restriction (acknowledged) plus ~12% of examples having a secondary target means SOB may undercount bias in some cases.
  • Reproducibility: Good but fragile.
  • Code and model responses are released — strong.
  • Many key results depend on closed APIs (GPT-5.1, Sonnet-4.6) at a snapshot in time, which is a known weakness for any benchmark relying on commercial LLMs.
  • The demographic schema is fixed (Parrish et al. 2022 / U.S. Census-style); reproducing the study in another cultural or linguistic context would require prompt re-engineering.
  • Significance: Moderate-to-High.
  • If adopted, the SOB task fills a real gap — most "LLM-as-judge" evaluation today is on accuracy/agreement, not on whether the judge itself is biased in its reasoning.
  • The negative result on reasoning-tuning-as-mitigation is important: it warns practitioners that turning on "thinking mode" is not a free debiasing lever.
  • The "social map" surfacing is conceptually interesting but correlational — the paper is careful not to claim models literally hold these maps, but a less careful reader could misread the rhetoric.
  • Red flags to watch.
  • "Bypasses safety guardrails" is a strong claim — the paper shows low refusal rates, but it does not ablate across guardrail configurations (e.g., system prompts, custom safety policies). It only documents the default behavior.
  • The implicit-social-map interpretation risks anthropomorphizing pattern-matching; the paper does include a footnote acknowledging LLMs are not epistemic agents, but the prose sometimes drifts toward stronger claims.
  • The acc/non-acc asymmetry in Qwen-3.5-35B and OLMo-32B (near-zero on acc, high on non-acc) is striking but could partially reflect prompt-format sensitivity rather than a stable "bias structure" — the paper does not run a paraphrase robustness check.
  • The Friedman test uses all models' ranks pooled together; per-model rank distributions are not separately reported, so it is unclear how universal the cross-target pattern is.
  • No comparison to a human-labeled ground truth for "to whom is this biased text acceptable" — the entire "warranted = Unknown" claim is theoretical, not empirical.

Strengths

  • Reframes a known problem (LLM bias) at the judgment layer, which is where deployed systems (alignment evals, red-teaming, fairness audits) increasingly live.
  • Brings in entitlement epistemology as an explicit theoretical lens — a meaningful corrective to the ad-hoc, metric-driven bias literature.
  • The two-metric design (α_t + SOB_t) is parsimonious, decomposable by target group, and interpretable.
  • Empirically covers 8 models (open + closed) and 5 datasets (2,457 examples), strengthening the generality claim.
  • The reasoning vs instruct ablation is a clean experiment with a counterintuitive result (reasoning can increase SOB).
  • Open release of code and model responses supports audit and extension; refusal accounting is honest.
  • The paper distinguishes two social harms (H_r, H_a) and grounds each in specific empirical signatures — useful for downstream mitigation work.

Limitations

  • Theoretical rather than empirical ground truth: "Unknown" is justified philosophically, but no human-baseline comparison is reported; some prompts may have a clearly correct demographic that the framework forbids naming.
  • Prompt-template sensitivity is not tested — paraphrases, languages, persona settings, and demographic label orderings are all open variables.
  • Closed-model dependency: results for GPT-5.1 and Sonnet-4.6 are snapshots; reproducibility over months is fragile.
  • Single-target assumption: ~12% of examples have a secondary target, which the framework cannot handle.
  • Demographic schema restriction: U.S. Census-style attributes; intersectional, multilingual, and non-Western categories are not covered.
  • Structured-output post-processing introduces an LLM-based formatting step — small but real dependence on an additional model.
  • Link to deployment harm is asserted but not measured: the paper does not show that SOB in the judgment task translates into measurable harm in a downstream LLM-as-judge evaluation pipeline.
  • Friedman test pools ranks across models, masking per-model heterogeneity.

Relevance

  • 与用户在 LLM 评估、偏见诊断、LLM-as-judge 可靠性方面的关注高度相关。论文提出的"二阶偏见"概念直指 LLM 用作评判者时一个尚未被系统检测的盲区，对构建更稳健的偏见评估流水线有直接借鉴意义。
  • 其理论框架（认知权能 + 错置权能）为把哲学与社会学概念工程化到 NLP 评估中提供了一个可复制的范式，符合用户对"跨学科理论基础"研究方向的兴趣。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Does SOB correlate with the strength of safety alignment (RLHF / constitutional training)? Do heavily aligned models show more or less SOB than base models?
  • How stable is the inferred "social map" under prompt paraphrases, language switches, and demographic label orderings? Is it a property of the model or of the prompt scaffold?
  • Does chain-of-thought that forces explicit reasoning about the inferential step reduce SOB? Or does it expose more associative triggers?
  • What is the human baseline for "to whom is this biased text acceptable" judgments, and how does it compare to LLM responses? Could a calibrated human baseline reveal that some non-Unknown LLM responses are actually correct?
  • If an LLM judge is used in a deployed fairness audit (e.g., to score a content-moderation pipeline), how much of its verdict variance is attributable to SOB versus the content it is asked to evaluate?
  • Can the entitlement-epistemology scaffold be inverted to build debiasing interventions at the judgment layer, or does it only function as a probe?
  • Does SOB persist across modalities (vision-language models, audio models) that also use LLM-as-judge patterns?
  • The paper notes that reasoning tuning can increase SOB (GPT-5.1 +50% / +128%). What training-data or RL signal drives this amplification, and can it be removed without regressing capability?
  • The acc/non-acc asymmetry in Qwen-3.5-35B and OLMo-32B (near-zero on acc, high on non-acc) deserves a dedicated investigation: is it an artifact of safety training that asymmetrically suppresses "acceptor" inferences but not "rejector" inferences?
  • How does SOB interact with multilingual bias benchmarks? The current schema is U.S. Census-style and may not translate cleanly to non-Western demographic categories.
