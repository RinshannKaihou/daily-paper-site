Security and Privacy Prompts in the Wild: What Users Ask LLMs and How LLMs Respond

arXiv: 2606.18062

Date: 2026-06-16

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.18062

一句话总结： 基于 WildChat 3.2M 真实对话挖掘 14,727 条用户安全与隐私 (S&P) 提示，归为 9 大类；并在 270 条建议型提示上对比 5 个 LLM 的质量与一致性——商业模型平均质量领先（GPT 5.5: 8.67 vs Llama 4: 6.71/10），但即便是高质量的商业模型仍会跨次给出相互矛盾的回复。
TL;DR: A first-of-its-kind empirical study of real security-and-privacy prompts that users send to LLMs, mined from WildChat's 3.2M conversations. From 14,727 S&P prompts, the authors derive a 9-category taxonomy and a stratified thematic analysis of 450 prompts. They then curate 270 advice-seeking prompts, query five LLMs (GPT 5.5, Gemini 3.1, Claude 4.7, Qwen 3, Llama 4) ten times each, and score both quality (checklist LLM-as-judge, 1–10) and consistency (bidirectional NLI entailment on evidence quotes). Headline numbers: GPT 5.5 hits ≥7 on 98% of prompts vs 47% for Llama 4; but the most consistent model is also the lowest-quality one (Llama 4, 97.4% non-contradicting pairs), and even top commercial models produce contradictory runs ~3–8% of the time — a safety failure mode invisible to average-quality evaluation.

核心贡献

  • 首个大规模真实用户 S&P 提示语料：从 WildChat 3.2M 真实用户对话中（剔除 toxic 与非英文后保留 1.7M）识别出 14,727 条 S&P 相关提示，弥补了以往依赖专家编写误区/FAQ 的局限。
  • 九类 S&P 主题分类法：账号/认证管理、应用与软件防御、泄露与利用、数据隐私与伦理、新兴技术、网络与系统防御、在线骚扰与安全、平台政策与执行、社会工程学。
  • 建议型提示子集（270 条）：每个 S&P 类别精选 30 条"用户在求建议/指导/具体信息"的提示，作为可复用评估基准。
  • 质量 × 一致性双维评估框架：每个提示对每个 LLM 重跑 10 次；质量用 LLM-as-judge checklist（NLI 风格证据抽取），一致性用双向 NLI 蕴含（Zhang et al. 2024）在证据引文上计算 C(10,2)=45 对两两比对。
  • 用户-模型交互新主题：发现 3 类以往论坛研究中不存在、但 LLM 上独有的 S&P 用途——防御性行动（让模型评估 URL/应用安全、起草反诈邮件）、对模型本身的询问（试探 API key、训练数据）、有害/攻击性请求（如 doxxing、系统提权脚本）。

Key Contributions

  • First large-scale empirical corpus of real-world S&P prompts to LLMs (14,727 prompts from WildChat 3.2M, post-filter 1.7M English, non-toxic), in contrast to prior LLM S&P work that relied on expert-authored misconceptions (Chen et al. 2023) or curated FAQs (Prakash et al. 2025).
  • Nine-category taxonomy of user S&P questions (account/auth, app/software defenses, compromise/exploitation, data privacy/ethics, emerging tech, network/system defenses, online harassment, platform policy, social engineering).
  • Curated 270 advice-seeking S&P prompt subset (30 per category) suitable as a reproducible benchmark.
  • Quality × consistency dual evaluation protocol: each prompt × each LLM × 10 runs = 2,700 responses per model; quality via LLM-as-judge checklists (averaged across three scorers to dampen self-preference bias); consistency via bidirectional NLI entailment on per-checklist evidence quotes.
  • Discovery of three LLM-specific S&P usage themes not seen in prior forum-based S&P literature: defensive action (11.8%), inquiry about the LLM (10.2%), and harmful & offensive requests (6.9%) — the latter mostly doxxing/privilege-escalation requests.
  • Empirical observation that quality and consistency are not the same axis of reliability: Llama 4 is the most consistent (97.4%) yet the lowest-quality model, while GPT 5.5 is highest-quality with only marginally lower consistency (97.0%).

Methodology

Data source

  • WildChat (Zhao et al. 2024a) — 3.2M user-LLM conversations collected via the Hugging Face interface (English, non-toxic only after filtering).
  • After deduplication, length filter (≤7K chars, mostly removes jailbreak-prefixed prompts and pasted code), and toxic/non-English removal → 1.7M unique English prompts.

Two-stage S&P prompt identification (§3.2)

  1. Stage 1 — identification: two of the authors (S&P researchers) hand-labeled 1,325 prompts; inter-rater agreement 83% with full consensus after discussion. A 3-LLM majority vote (Qwen 3 + Calme 3.2, with GPT-5.2 as tiebreaker) was then trained against this set, achieving 96% precision / 74% recall → 14,727 S&P prompts.
  2. Stage 2 — categorization: a 9-category taxonomy adapted from prior S&P work. A separate ground-truth set of 300 prompts (author-labeled, full consensus) was used to pick the best LLM classifier. Gemma-4-31B-IT (temp=0) won with average precision 0.90 / recall 0.89 across the 9 categories and was applied to all 14,727 prompts.
  3. False-negative audit: 500 prompts labeled "not S&P" where Qwen 3 and Calme 3.2 disagreed were manually inspected; only 9.6% (48) were false negatives, all semantically redundant with the 14,727.

Thematic analysis (§3.3)

  • Stratified sample of 450 prompts (50 per category), comparable in scale to Tahaei et al. (2020) and Hasegawa et al. (2022).
  • Two researchers iteratively coded (first-propose / second-validate cycles) until consensus → 6 themes and 22 sub-themes: General knowledge (33.3%), User-side navigation (20.9%), S&P task production (13.8%), Defensive action (11.8%), Inquiry about the LLM (10.2%), Harmful & offensive requests (6.9%).

Advice-seeking subset & LLM evaluation (§3.4)

  • 270 advice-seeking prompts (30/category): two researchers labeled 1,659 prompts total to reach saturation, defining advice-seeking as prompts requesting "recommendations, guidance, or specific S&P information".
  • Models tested (five LLMs, all at default settings, with thinking mode and web search enabled for commercial models; open-weight models on 4×H100): Claude-opus-4-7, Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview, GPT-5.5-2026-04-23, Qwen 3, Llama-4-Scout-17B-16E.
  • 10 independent runs per (model, prompt) → 2,700 responses per LLM.
  • Quality: per-prompt checklist built from two commercial LLMs (GPT 5.5-pro + Claude 4.7), reviewed and reconciled by two researchers. Scored by three LLMs (averaged to mitigate self-preference bias: GPT 5.5 and Gemini 3.1 each scored their own responses highest without averaging). 2,700 spot-checks (top-10/bottom-10 per scorer×model×category) validated score extremes.
  • Consistency: NLI-based. For each prompt, the three scorers extract evidence quotes per checklist item; quotes are merged and deduped → 45 pairwise comparisons per prompt; bidirectional entailment via nli-deberta-v3-large, averaged into a per-prompt consistency score; "consistent" if score > 0.5.

Results & Highlights

Thematic findings (RQ1, §4.1)

  • Six themes, 22 sub-themes. General knowledge dominates (33.3%, mostly "what is X" and exam-style MCQs). Three LLM-specific themes: Defensive action (11.8%) — URL/app safety checks, counter-fraud, vulnerability assessment; Inquiry about the LLM (10.2%) — probing for API keys, training data, or "is my chat being recorded?"; Harmful & offensive (6.9%) — person-, system-, or platform-directed attacks (doxxing, admin privilege escalation, bypass incognito enforcement).

Response quality (RQ2, §4.2)

| Model | Mean quality (1–10) | Std Dev | % prompts ≥7 |

|---|---|---|---|

| GPT 5.5 | 8.67 | 0.87 | 98% |

| Gemini 3.1 | 8.52 | 1.10 | 94% |

| Claude 4.7 | 8.47 | 1.25 | 91% |

| Qwen 3 | 7.90 | 1.65 | 77% |

| Llama 4 | 6.71 | 2.06 | 47% |

  • GPT 5.5 leads in 5/9 categories; Gemini 3.1 in 3; Claude 4.7 in 1.
  • Failure modes in low-scoring responses: factual inaccuracy (e.g., Llama 4 misinterpreting "dox" as "doxycycline"; incorrectly stating Google Workspace auto-deletes inactive accounts) and ethical violations (e.g., Llama 4 describing how to bypass school Chromebook web filters).
  • Self-preference bias observed: GPT 5.5 and Gemini 3.1 each score their own responses highest; Claude 4.7 scored GPT 5.5's highest — justifying the 3-scorer average.

Response consistency (RQ3, §4.3)

  • % prompts with non-contradicting responses across 10 runs: Llama 4 — 97.4%, GPT 5.5 — 97.0%, Gemini 3.1 — 94.4%, Claude 4.7 — 92.2%, Qwen 3 — 89.6%.
  • Llama 4 has non-contradicting responses on all 30 prompts in 4/9 categories (account/auth, emerging tech, platform policy, social engineering).
  • Concrete contradictory example: Gemini 3.1, asked about "best file-system permission configuration", produced two runs both scoring >9 — one saying settings are "automatically applied by the system", the other saying they "must be managed through system settings".

Discussion (§5)

  • The LLM-S&P setting is dual-use by nature: the same model may be asked to polish a phishing email and to draft the scam-baiting reply; boundary cases have no surface signal distinguishing attacker from defender.
  • The LLM itself is a target: 10.2% of prompts probe the model's own internals, framing "the LLM is attacked" alongside "the LLM is used to attack".
  • Quality ≠ consistency: even the best model gives contradictory answers on ~3% of prompts, and a model that is consistently wrong (Llama 4) is itself a safety hazard.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: Genuine. The first systematic characterization of real S&P prompts in LLM traffic — the move from expert-authored misconceptions (Chen et al. 2023) and curated FAQs (Prakash et al. 2025) to mined user queries is a real empirical advance, and the dual-use framing is genuinely new.
  • Rigor: Solid but with caveats. The two-stage classifier is properly evaluated against a held-out 1,325-prompt ground truth (96% precision, 74% recall is decent for the low-resource, high-vocabulary S&P domain), and the authors do a false-negative audit to defend recall. The thematic analysis is conventional qualitative coding with two-iterative consensus, which is acceptable. The checklist-based LLM-as-judge is the standard WildBench/RocketEval method, but the self-preference bias finding is itself a result and the 3-scorer average is a sensible mitigation. Where rigor weakens: the consistency metric (threshold = 0.5 on NLI bidirectional entailment) is operationalized but the threshold is not justified empirically; the 10-run protocol gives only 45 pair comparisons per prompt, which is small for distributional claims.
  • Reproducibility: Partial. WildChat is public; checklist construction and prompts are described; the 270 advice-seeking prompts are presumably shareable. The authors state they will release "the dataset of S&P prompts, the code for our two-stage pipeline … and the code for our evaluation" upon publication, but at submission time none of it is linked. Model versions are pinned (e.g., GPT-5.5-2026-04-23), but the open-weight models are described only by API call shape — no system prompts, decoding temperature, or top-p are reported for individual LLMs.
  • Significance: High for the deployer community and for evaluation methodology. The "quality without consistency is a false comfort" message is well-supported by their data and is genuinely important — most production LLM evaluations report only point-estimate quality.
  • Red flags:
  • Open-weight side is under-sampled: only two open-weight models (Qwen 3, Llama 4) and Llama 4 is a much smaller model (Scout-17B-16E) than the commercial models; the 47% headline is more "Llama 4 is weak" than "open-weight is weak."
  • WildChat bias: collected via Hugging Face Chat UI → English-speaking, AI-curious early adopters. Acknowledged by authors but worth restating.
  • Single-turn only: no multi-turn dialog analysis, even though real users almost always have multi-turn S&P conversations.
  • No statistical inference: the paper reports point estimates (means, % prompts) without confidence intervals. With 270 prompts × 10 runs, the SEs on the %-prompts-above-threshold metric are not negligible; the 94% vs 91% Claude vs Gemini differences are not obviously significant.
  • Checklist coverage: authors note in the limitations that quality scores are bounded by what the checklists capture — i.e., a checklist that omits an important S&P criterion will systematically under-credit responses that get it right.
  • "LLM-probing" theme risk of over-attribution: 10.2% of S&P prompts probe the model itself; some of these may be jailbreak attempts dressed as S&P questions, but the paper does not separate legitimate curiosity from adversarial probing.

Strengths

  • First-of-its-kind empirical use of WildChat for S&P prompt analysis; provides an immediately usable 270-prompt benchmark for the community.
  • Dual quality/consistency framing is a real methodological contribution that the LLM evaluation field under-uses.
  • The 3-scorer average explicitly defends against self-preference bias and documents that bias as a finding in its own right.
  • Practical relevance: deployers can act on the consistency warning immediately, and the nine-category taxonomy maps cleanly to red-team / guardrail design.
  • Strong author team with track record in usable security and LLM measurement.
  • Authors are upfront about limitations (WildChat skew, English-only, single-turn, checklist coverage).

Limitations

  • Only two open-weight models tested, and Llama 4 Scout (17B active) is not directly comparable to GPT 5.5 in capacity — open-weight generalization is limited.
  • Consistency threshold (0.5 on bidirectional NLI) is chosen without a calibration study; small threshold changes could reorder the consistency ranking.
  • Thematic categories are coarse (6 themes / 22 sub-themes over 450 prompts) — sub-themes average ~20 prompts each, which limits reliability of fine-grained claims.
  • WildChat users skew toward English-speaking, AI-curious, technically engaged populations; S&P help-seeking by elderly, low-SES, or non-English users is not represented.
  • No information about adversarial or jailbreak S&P prompts — focuses on benign advice-seeking, even though the harmful/offensive category exists.
  • Checklist-based evaluation can miss criteria; a response that addresses a real S&P issue not in the checklist scores 0 for that item.
  • 10 runs per prompt is a small sample for consistency analysis; bootstrap CIs are absent.
  • The paper examines single-turn prompts, not the multi-turn S&P conversations that real users have (acknowledged).

Relevance

该工作直接对应"LLM 安全/隐私评估"与"真实用户行为建模"两个研究兴趣：作者用真实对话日志构建评估语料，并把"一致性"作为独立于"质量"的可靠性维度提出，对可信赖 LLM 评估与安全部署研究具有直接参考价值。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does the quality/consistency gap scale with model size, RLHF, and instruction-tuning? The paper only spans five models at one snapshot in time.
  • Do structured system prompts, retrieval augmentation, or tool-use (e.g., linking to vendor documentation) reduce the contradiction rate on S&P advice?
  • Can the 270-prompt subset be released as a public benchmark, paired with a held-out set of multi-turn S&P dialogues for richer evaluation?
  • How well do the 9 S&P categories correlate with harmful-misinformation risk? The 6.9% "harmful & offensive" rate deserves a category-level breakdown.
  • Does the same prompt produce more consistent responses when constrained to a single category or when a model is given an explicit "if unsure, decline" instruction?
  • To what extent does the LLM-probing theme (10.2%) overlap with jailbreak attempts? A clean separation would inform guardrail design.
  • Does multilingual S&P advice show the same quality-vs-consistency gap, or do non-English prompts systematically reduce both?
  • How do the contradictory-response cases (e.g., Gemini's file-permissions example) correlate with calibration / logit uncertainty?
