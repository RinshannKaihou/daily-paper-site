Finite-Time Queue Peak Laws in Stochastic Networks: Logarithmic Scaling After Geometric Thresholds

arXiv: 2606.18218

Date: 2026-06-16

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.18218

一句话总结： 在一致内点松弛（uniform interior slack）条件下，作者证明广义交换机中 MaxWeight 调度的有限时队列峰值存在两阶段包络——几何阈值以下保持 $\sqrt{T}$ 律，超过阈值后仅以 $D/\varepsilon$ 的（与容量几何无关的）系数对数增长；匹配下界证明两者均不可避免，且在状态空间塌缩下阈值可被局部瓶颈几何锐化，最后在输入排队交换机上给出紧对数系数（Bernoulli 输入下降为 $1/\varepsilon$，对应总积压的 $n/\varepsilon$）。
TL;DR: The paper proves a two-phase finite-horizon peak law for MaxWeight scheduling in generalized switches under uniform interior slack. Specifically,
$$\mathbb{E}[\text{Peak}_T] \lesssim \min\!\Bigl\{D\sqrt{T},\ \tfrac{D}{\varepsilon}\log(T+1) + \tfrac{D^2}{\varepsilon\alpha(\Pi)}\Bigr\}.$$
The $\sqrt{T}$ envelope (sharp at the boundary, $\varepsilon=0$) persists only as a burn-in regime up to a geometry-dependent threshold $D^2/(\varepsilon\alpha(\Pi))$; past that threshold, the running maximum grows only logarithmically with a geometry-free coefficient $D/\varepsilon$. The mechanism is a self-normalization argument: in the current queue direction $u_t$, both the stabilizing drift and the projected fluctuation are scaled by the same $H(u_t)$, so the log coefficient inherits only the universal fluctuation-to-drift ratio $D/\varepsilon$, not the network's capacity geometry. Matching policy-independent lower bounds show that both the $\log T$ term and a geometric threshold are unavoidable. Under nonstationary complete-resource-pooling (CRP), finite-time state-space collapse exposes a tighter threshold that depends on local bottleneck geometry ($\Delta_{\text{gap}}, \delta_{\text{face}}, v_{\min}$), not the global $\alpha(\Pi)$. Specialized to $n \times n$ input-queued switches, the log coefficient for total backlog is $n^{3/2}/\varepsilon$ in a robust generalized model (matched by lower bound) and improves to $n/\varepsilon$ under Bernoulli arrivals through the queue-Bernstein variance-sensitive mechanism. Simulations confirm the two-phase envelope, the local-geometric sharpening, and the variance-sensitive improvement.

核心贡献

  • 两阶段有限时峰值律：在一致内点松弛下，MaxWeight 调度的有限时峰值先经历 $\sqrt{T}$ 烧入期，再跨过几何阈值后进入对数增长期。
  • 几何无关的对数系数：自归一化论证使 $\log T$ 的系数 $D/\varepsilon$ 完全脱离容量几何，只取决于内点松弛和归一化扰动尺度。
  • 匹配下界：同时给出与策略无关的下界，证明 $\log T$ 项和几何阈值都不可避免——结构本质上是紧的。
  • CRP/状态空间塌缩锐化阈值：在非平稳完全资源池化（CRP）假设下，给出有限时 SSC 半径；并证明在此管内，阈值由局部瓶颈几何（$\Delta_{\text{gap}}$、$\delta_{\text{face}}$、$v_{\min}$）决定，去除了 $1/\alpha(\Pi)$ 的全局代价。
  • 两队列示例的清晰演示：在 $\alpha(\Pi)\asymp a$ 的薄三角上，证明 $\text{Peak}_T \lesssim (1/\varepsilon)\log(T/\delta)$，与 $a$ 无关，从而明确展示局部几何可以完全替代全局几何。
  • 输入排队交换机（IQS）的应用：
  • 广义 IQS（依赖、可适应、分式到达）：上界 $\max\|Q_t\|_1 \lesssim (n^{3/2}/\varepsilon)\log(T/\delta) + n^{5/2}/\varepsilon$；下界匹配对数项 $n^{3/2}/\varepsilon$。
  • CRP IQS：瓶颈投影的对数系数从 $n^{3/2}/\varepsilon$ 改善到 $n/\varepsilon$，加上来自塌缩半径的有限时代价。
  • Bernoulli IQS：方差敏感度让对数系数进一步改善到 $n/\varepsilon$（对应 Frobenius 范数的 $1/\varepsilon$）。
  • 队列-Bernstein 条件：作为有界原语的替代，提出一个新的非渐近分布族，使方差敏感度进入对数系数。
  • 仿真验证：在 IQS（$n=20$）和平行服务器系统（$L=K=40$）上演示了两阶段转变、局部几何优势、方差敏感度。

Key Contributions

  • Two-phase finite-horizon peak law (Corollary 2.1). For MaxWeight under Assumptions 2.1 and 2.2:

$$\mathbb{E}[\text{Peak}_T]\;\lesssim\;\min\!\Bigl\{D\sqrt{T},\;\tfrac{D}{\varepsilon}\log(T+1)+\tfrac{D^2}{\varepsilon\alpha(\Pi)}\Bigr\}.$$

Geometry enters only the threshold $D^2/(\varepsilon\alpha(\Pi))$; the coefficient $D/\varepsilon$ of $\log T$ is universal across capacity regions.

  • Self-normalization argument (Section 3.1). Decompose the one-step queue norm increment as projected drift + curvature cost; show that the same $H(u_t)$ controls both sides, yielding a scalar ratio $D/\varepsilon$ that is independent of network geometry. Under deterministic service the coefficient improves to $A_{\max}/\varepsilon$.
  • Matching policy-independent lower bounds (Theorems 2.3, 2.4). A one-dimensional reflected-walk construction shows that $\Theta(A\sqrt{T})$ and $\Theta((A/\varepsilon)\log T)$ are both unavoidable. A simplex-geometry construction shows the geometric threshold $1/(\varepsilon\alpha(\Pi))$ is unavoidable in the worst case.
  • Queue-Bernstein condition (Section 3.2). A new distribution class (Bernoulli, Poisson, Gamma, bounded compound Poisson, bounded mass) that enables self-normalization without boundedness; recovers the Kingman scale $(\rho\,c_a^2 + c_s^2)/(2(1-\rho))\cdot\tau$ as the scalar log coefficient.
  • Finite-time state-space collapse under CRP (Theorem 4.1). Nonstationary analogue of classical perpendicular-drift SSC: under uniform slack and a positive face margin $\delta_{\text{face}}$, the queue stays within a tube of radius $R_\perp(T,\delta)\asymp (D^2/\delta_{\text{face}})\log(e(T+1)/\delta)$ of the bottleneck ray.
  • Local peak law under CRP (Theorem 4.2). Replaces the global $\alpha(\Pi)$ in the entrance threshold by local quantities $\Delta_{\text{gap}}$, $\delta_{\text{face}}$, $v_{\min}$, with the same universal $D/\varepsilon$ log coefficient.
  • Two-queue illustration (Proposition 4.1). On a thin triangle with $\alpha(\Pi)\asymp a$, the bound is $O((1/\varepsilon)\log(T/\delta))$ — independent of $a$ — concretely demonstrating the value of collapse.
  • Sharp IQS coefficients (Theorems 5.1–5.4).
  • Generalized IQS: $\max\|Q_t\|_1 \lesssim (n^{3/2}/\varepsilon)\log(T/\delta) + n^{5/2}/\varepsilon$ (matched by lower bound).
  • CRP IQS: bottleneck coefficient improves to $n/\varepsilon$ + collapse radius.
  • Bernoulli IQS: variance-sensitive improvement to $n/\varepsilon$ (or $1/\varepsilon$ in Frobenius norm).
  • Simulations on IQS ($n=20$) and parallel-server ring ($L=K=40$) confirm the two-phase transition, local-geometric improvement, and variance sensitivity.

Methodology

  • Generalized switch model. Discrete-time queueing network with $d$ queues, $Q_{t+1}=(Q_t+A_{t+1}-S_t)^+$, $Q_0=0$. Service set $S\subset\mathbb{R}_+^d$ finite; $s_t\in S$ is $\mathcal{F}_t$-measurable; realized $S_t$ has conditional mean $s_t$. Capacity region $\Pi=\{x\in\mathbb{R}_+^d:x\le r\text{ for some }r\in\text{conv}(S)\}$, downward-closed convention.
  • Adapted arrivals. $A_{t+1}$ may be dependent, time-varying, and adapted to history $\mathcal{F}_t$ — strictly more general than the standard i.i.d. setting. Both $A_{t+1}$ and $S_t$ are independent conditional on $\mathcal{F}_t$.
  • Uniform interior slack (Assumption 2.1). $\lambda_t:=\mathbb{E}[A_{t+1}\mid\mathcal{F}_t]\in(1-\varepsilon)\Pi$ a.s. for all $t$ — i.e., the conditional mean arrival lies in a fixed contraction of the capacity polytope.
  • Bounded primitives (Assumption 2.2). $\|A_{t+1}\|_2\le A_{\max}$, $\|S_t\|_2\le S_{\max}$, $\sup_{s\in S}\|s\|_2\le S_{\max}$, $D:=A_{\max}+S_{\max}$.
  • MaxWeight policy. $s_t\in\arg\max_{s\in S}\langle Q_t,s\rangle$. Directional certificate: $\langle Q_t,s_t\rangle = H(Q_t)$ where $H(q):=\max_{s\in S}\langle q,s\rangle=\max_{x\in\Pi}\langle q,x\rangle$ is the support function.
  • Self-normalization proof (Section 3.1). Define $X_t=\|Q_t\|_2$, $u_t=Q_t/X_t$ for $X_t>0$. Use the almost-linearity identity

$$X_{t+1}-X_t \le \langle u_t,A_{t+1}-S_t\rangle + \tfrac{\|A_{t+1}-S_t\|_2^2}{2X_t}.$$

The first term has drift $-\varepsilon H(u_t)$ (from slack + MaxWeight); the second is the curvature cost $\le D^2/(2X_t)$. Beyond the threshold $D^2/(\varepsilon\alpha(\Pi))$, curvature is dominated by drift. An exponential supermartingale in $\theta\langle u_t,A_{t+1}-S_t\rangle$ yields projected variance $\theta^2 D^2 H(u_t)/2$ — the same $H(u_t)$ as the drift, so the ratio is $D/\varepsilon$ universally.

  • Matching lower bounds. Theorem 2.3 uses a one-dimensional walk with $\{0,A+1\}$ arrivals and unit service; typical excursions have length $O(1/\varepsilon)$ and exponential max tails with rate $\Theta(\varepsilon/A)$, giving the unavoidable $\sqrt{T}$ burn-in and $(A/\varepsilon)\log T$ tail. Theorem 2.4 uses the simplex schedule set $S=\{e_1,\dots,e_d\}$ with deterministic arrivals/services: only one coordinate can be served per slot, so by $T_0=\lfloor d/2\rfloor$ at least $d-T_0$ coordinates are untouched, producing $\Omega(1/(\varepsilon\sqrt{d}))=\Omega(1/(\varepsilon\alpha(\Pi)))$ backlog.
  • Queue-Bernstein extension (Section 3.2). Replace boundedness by a directional sub-exponential MGF: $\log\mathbb{E}[\exp(\theta\langle w,Z-m\rangle)\mid\mathcal{G}]\le \theta^2\nu\langle w,m\rangle/(2(1-b|\theta|))$. Holds for Bernoulli, Poisson, Gamma, bounded-batch compound Poisson. Theorem 3.1 then yields $\text{Peak}_T\lesssim x_{QB}+b_A+b_S+(\nu_A+\nu_S)\varepsilon^{-1}\log((T+1)/\delta)$.
  • Finite-time SSC under CRP (Theorem 4.1). Nonstationary CRP (Assumption 4.1): there is a unique exposed bottleneck facet $F^\star=\Pi\cap H^\star$ with normal $v$, $\mu=H(v)>0$, and the nominal points $r_t^\circ=\lambda_t/(1-\varepsilon)$ stay uniformly in the relative interior of $F^\star$ (face margin $\delta_{\text{face}}>0$). Define transverse residual $Q_t^\perp=Q_t-\langle v,Q_t\rangle v$. The face margin lets us compare MaxWeight's choice to a feasible perturbation of the nominal point along $Q_t^\perp/\|Q_t^\perp\|_2$, producing perpendicular drift and confining $Q_t^\perp$ within a logarithmic tube of radius $R_\perp(T,\delta)\asymp(D^2/\delta_{\text{face}})\log(e(T+1)/\delta)$.
  • Local peak law (Theorem 4.2). Inside the SSC tube, MaxWeight is v-optimal (schedule gap $\Delta_{\text{gap}}=\min\{\mu-\langle v,s\rangle:s\in S,\langle v,s\rangle<\mu\}$ dominates the transverse error once $Y_t\ge 4S_{\max}R_\delta/\Delta_{\text{gap}}$) and bottleneck service is fully used (no-waste threshold $S_{\max}R_\delta/v_{\min}$). The bottleneck projection $Y_t$ then has drift $-\varepsilon\mu$ and is bounded by $(D/\varepsilon)\log((T+1)/\delta)$ + the local threshold $x_{\text{loc}}$.
  • Two-queue toy (Section 4.1). $S=\{(1,0),(0,a)\}$, capacity triangle with $\alpha(\Pi)\asymp a$. Lemma 4.1 proves (i) collapse strip: $0\le (Q_1-aQ_2)^+\le 1+a^2$ uniformly; (ii) bottleneck drift: $\mathbb{E}[Y_{t+1}-Y_t\mid\mathcal{F}_t]\le -a\varepsilon$ on $\{Y_t\ge 2\}$. Combined with the $a$ factors in the projected variance, the $a$'s cancel and the bound is $O((1/\varepsilon)\log(T/\delta))$ — uniform in $a$.
  • IQS specialization (Section 5). $n\times n$ switch with $d=n^2$ queues, $S$=permutation matrices, $\Pi$=substochastic bipartite matching polytope. Key geometry: $S_{\max}=\sqrt n$, $\alpha(\Pi)=1/\sqrt n$, $\|Q\|_1\le \sqrt n\|Q\|_2$. Three models:
  • Generalized IQS (Assumption 5.1): pathwise Frobenius envelope $\|A_{t+1}\|_F\le\sqrt n$ + row/column slack. Yields $n^{3/2}/\varepsilon$ log coefficient.
  • CRP IQS (Corollary 5.1): bottleneck normal = single tight row/column indicator, $\|v\|_1=\sqrt n$. Local analysis gives $n/\varepsilon$ projection coefficient + $n^2/\delta_{\text{face}}$ collapse cost.
  • Bernoulli IQS (Theorem 5.4): independent entries + queue-Bernstein $(\nu,b)=(1,1)$ for each coordinate, giving dimension-free projected variance proxy. Log coefficient drops to $1/\varepsilon$ in Frobenius norm (or $n/\varepsilon$ in $\ell_1$).

Results & Highlights

  • Main two-phase envelope (Theorem 2.1 / Corollary 2.1). The bound is the minimum of two envelopes: a slack-free $D\sqrt T$ (Theorem 2.2) and a logarithmic one with a geometry-dependent threshold. The crossover is at $T^\star\asymp (D/(\varepsilon\alpha(\Pi)))^2 = (\varepsilon\alpha(\Pi))^{-2}$ in normalized bounded models.
  • Geometry-free log coefficient. Once $Q_t \gtrsim D^2/(\varepsilon\alpha(\Pi))$, the peak scales as $(D/\varepsilon)\log T$ with no dependence on $\alpha(\Pi)$. This is a non-asymptotic, distribution-free, geometry-agnostic principle.
  • Lower-bound tightness.
  • Theorem 2.3 (one-dim): $\mathbb{E}[\max_{0\le t\le T}\|Q_t\|_1]\ge c\min\{A\sqrt T,\ (A/\varepsilon)\log(1+\varepsilon^2 T/A)\}$ — both regimes are unavoidable.
  • Theorem 2.4 (geometric threshold): there is a $d$-dimensional simplex instance on which $\max_{0\le t\le T_0}\|Q_t\|_2 \ge c/(\varepsilon\alpha(\Pi))$ for $T_0=\lfloor d/2\rfloor$ — geometry cannot be removed.
  • Queue-Bernstein result (Theorem 3.1). Extends to unbounded primitives; recovers the classical Kingman scale $\tfrac{\rho c_s^2+c_a^2}{2(1-\rho)}\tau$ as the scalar log coefficient in the one-dimensional case.
  • CRP local threshold (Theorem 4.2). Replaces $1/\alpha(\Pi)$ in the entrance threshold by $\Delta_{\text{gap}}^{-1}$, $\delta_{\text{face}}^{-1}$ (through the collapse radius), and $v_{\min}^{-1}$ — all local quantities, all smaller than the global $1/\alpha(\Pi)$.
  • IQS upper/lower bounds (Table 1).
  • Generalized IQS: $\max\|Q_t\|_1\lesssim (n^{3/2}/\varepsilon)\log(T/\delta)+n^{5/2}/\varepsilon$ (upper), matched by lower bound $\Omega((n^{3/2}/\varepsilon)\log(1+\varepsilon^2 T/n))$ (Theorems 5.1, 5.2).
  • CRP IQS: projection coefficient improves to $n/\varepsilon$ (Theorem 4.2); collapse radius adds $n^2/\delta_{\text{face}}$ (Corollary 5.1); lower bound matches projection coefficient $n/\varepsilon$ (Theorem 5.3).
  • Bernoulli IQS: $\max\|Q_t\|_1\lesssim (n/\varepsilon)\log(T/\delta)+n^{5/2}/\varepsilon$ (Theorem 5.4) — an $n^{1/2}$ improvement over the generalized case.
  • Empirical confirmation (Section 6).
  • Exp 1 (two-phase law): IQS $n=20$ + parallel-server ring $L=K=40$. For larger $\varepsilon$, the log-log slope $\beta(T)=d\log\mathbb{E}[\text{Peak}_T]/d\log T$ drops below the $1/2$ line earlier, matching the predicted transition.
  • Exp 2 (local geometry): under CRP with tight row/column, the peak coefficient is much smaller than the global $\alpha(\Pi)$ would suggest.
  • Exp 3 (variance sensitivity): Bernoulli arrivals achieve a strictly smaller log coefficient than the generalized (worst-case Frobenius-envelope) arrivals, matching Theorem 5.4.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty. The two-phase crossover, the self-normalization argument, and the geometry-free log coefficient are genuinely new. The combination of (i) a slack-free $\sqrt{T}$ envelope, (ii) a logarithmic envelope, and (iii) a geometric threshold — together with matching policy-independent lower bounds — is a clean structural statement. The replacement of the global $\alpha(\Pi)$ by local bottleneck geometry under finite-time CRP is a meaningful non-asymptotic extension of classical heavy-traffic SSC.
  • Rigor. The paper is technically careful: matching upper and lower bounds, careful statement of assumptions (uniform interior slack, finite-time CRP, queue-Bernstein), and reusability of the conversion lemmas. The self-normalization argument is conceptually transparent and the curvature-vs-drift threshold $D^2/(\varepsilon\alpha(\Pi))$ is the natural balance point.
  • Reproducibility. For a theory paper, the proofs are in the appendix and the simulations have a GitHub replication package — both good signs. The experiments are small (a few CPU-hours, $n\le 20$ or $L=K=40$, 200–500 replications) and are intended to illustrate theory rather than compete with systems benchmarks.
  • Significance. Substantive for the stochastic-networks / queueing community. The $\log T$ finite-horizon bound is the right finite-time analogue of the steady-state exponential-tail heuristic, and the geometry-free nature of the log coefficient is a non-obvious structural insight. The IQS specialization is timely: the optimal queue-size scaling problem in the classical independent-Bernoulli IQS is famously still open in steady state (see Shah–Tsitsiklis, Maguluri–Srikant, etc.), and the paper is honest about the fact that its lower bound applies to the generalized model, not the classical independent one.
  • Red flags / caveats.
  • The uniform-interior-slack assumption is non-trivial: it excludes heavy-traffic regimes where $\lambda_t$ approaches the boundary. The paper is honest about this scope.
  • The CRP refinement depends on an external finite-time CRP / complete-resource-pooling assumption, which is itself non-trivial to verify. The paper provides a generic SSC radius that re-introduces a $\log T$ dependence on the local face margin, partially offsetting the win from using $\Delta_{\text{gap}}, v_{\min}$ in place of $\alpha(\Pi)$.
  • The queue-Bernstein result is powerful but the "Bernoulli IQS lower bound" is not provided — the lower bound for the generalized IQS uses dependent, fractional arrivals that the classical independent Bernoulli model excludes. So the variance-sensitive improvement of the upper bound does not yet have a matching lower bound under the actual classical model. The paper notes this explicitly.
  • The log coefficient is geometry-free but the threshold still depends on $\alpha(\Pi)$ (or, in the local version, on $\Delta_{\text{gap}}, \delta_{\text{face}}, v_{\min}$). The practical peak bound remains geometry-sensitive via the threshold term — a useful but slightly awkward split.
  • The lower-bound constructions (one-dim reflected walk, simplex geometry) leave open whether the constants $c, C$ in the matching bounds are tight beyond their order. The paper does not claim optimal constants.
  • The paper focuses on MaxWeight; whether another policy (e.g., LyapOpt) can improve $A_{\max}$ and $S_{\max}$ dependence is left as future work.

Strengths

  • Conceptual clarity. The two-phase picture and self-normalization argument are simple to state and to interpret, making the paper accessible to a broad audience at the queueing/learning boundary.
  • Matching lower bounds — not just upper envelopes. Both the log term and the geometric threshold are shown to be unavoidable, giving the result real structural meaning.
  • Tight specialization to input-queued switches across three increasingly structured models, with explicit constants.
  • Variance-sensitive refinement via the queue-Bernstein class is a useful non-asymptotic tool that recovers the classical Kingman scale in one dimension.
  • Finite-time, nonstationary extension of state-space collapse under nonstationary CRP — a methodological contribution of independent interest.
  • Open replication package for simulations.
  • Honest scoping: the paper does not overclaim about heavy traffic, classical independent Bernoulli lower bounds, or optimality of constants.

Limitations

  • Uniform interior slack is required throughout the main results; heavy-traffic scaling ($\lambda_t\to\partial\Pi$) is not addressed.
  • CRP refinement requires a quantitative finite-time CRP assumption and the resulting collapse radius still carries a $\log T$ factor, so the threshold is not fully geometry-free.
  • The Bernoulli IQS lower bound matching the variance-sensitive upper bound is not provided; the matching lower bound holds only for the generalized (dependent, fractional) model.
  • The two-phase picture is an upper envelope, not a tight characterization of the typical behavior in the crossover region. The sharpness of the transition as a function of slack $\varepsilon$ is not quantified.
  • Constant factors in the matching bounds are not optimized.
  • The paper focuses on MaxWeight; whether other drift-minimizing policies (LyapOpt, drift-plus-penalty) can do better on the $A_{\max}, S_{\max}$ dependence is open.

Relevance

与本人关注方向（随机网络/调度中的有限时性能界）高度相关。该工作将经典 $\sqrt{T}$ 律与对数律统一为两阶段图景，揭示了自归一化机制；对输入排队交换机给出紧对数系数，并利用有限时 CRP 将全局几何代价替换为局部瓶颈几何代价——这是 MaxWeight 类调度在数据中心/无线场景部署时延迟与峰值预算分析的核心工具，且其方差敏感度（queue-Bernstein → Kingman 尺度）可与单服务器分析框架直接对接。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Heavy-traffic scaling: can the two-phase picture be recovered as $\varepsilon\to 0$, interpolating between the $\log T$ envelope here and the classical $\sqrt{T}$ heavy-traffic law?
  • Removing the CRP assumption: is there a self-normalized, collapse-free proof that the log coefficient is always geometry-free, without assuming the queue concentrates on a single face?
  • Bernoulli IQS lower bound: can the variance-sensitive improvement $n^{3/2}\to n$ in the log coefficient be matched by a lower bound in the classical independent Bernoulli model — i.e., closing the gap to the long-standing open question of optimal queue-size scaling?
  • LyapOpt or other policies: does a drift-plus-penalty or learning-augmented policy beat MaxWeight's $D = A_{\max}+S_{\max}$ coefficient (perhaps to $A_{\max}$ even under random service)?
  • Correlated / heavy-tailed arrivals: the queue-Bernstein class is broad, but multivariate dependent coordinates (e.g., Markovian modulated Bernoulli) might destroy the directional MGF bound. What is the minimal structural assumption needed for self-normalization?
  • Adaptive scheduling: the two-phase picture suggests a "switching" algorithm that behaves as $\sqrt{T}$-optimal at small $T$ and as $\log T$-optimal at large $T$ — can this be made adaptive, with a sample-path-detected threshold?
  • Beyond $\ell_2$: the proof is norm-specific ($\ell_2$ via $H(u_t)$). What is the analog for $\ell_\infty$ or weighted $\ell_p$ queue norms, and does the geometry-free property persist?
  • Joint finite-time + steady-state: can the $T\to\infty$ limit of Theorem 2.1 reproduce the classical $\Theta(\log(1/(1-\rho)))$ heavy-traffic steady-state mean under positive recurrence, with the same constants?
