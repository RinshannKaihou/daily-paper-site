PseudoBench: Measuring How Agentic Auto-Research Fuels Pseudoscience

arXiv: 2606.18060

Date: 2026-06-16

https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.18060

一句话总结： PseudoBench 用 200 对跨 5 个领域的"伪科学主张-证据"端到端测试 7 款 SOTA auto-research 智能体（Codex/Claude Code/OpenClaw/Nanobot/EvoScientist/ResearchClaw/ARIS），发现它们均可在数分钟内产出完整学术格式 PDF，最高抵抗力仅 27.4%，且更强的智能体反而把伪科学包装得更具说服力。
TL;DR: PseudoBench is the first adversarial benchmark targeting pseudoscience-resistance in agentic auto-research systems. Built from a 1,271-item pool (200 released) curated via a 4-stage pipeline (DeepSeek-V3.2 filtering → Qwen3-Embedding-8B dedup → Claude Sonnet 4.6 absurdity scoring → stratified sampling), it spans five categories from Fundamental Physics to Mystic Energy. End-to-end testing of 7 SOTA systems (Codex, Claude Code, OpenClaw, Nanobot, EvoScientist, ResearchClaw, ARIS) shows overall "pseudoscience hazard" of 72.6–84.6%, max resistance 27.4%, refusal rates near zero, and—most alarmingly—stronger systems packaging pseudoscience in more sophisticated scientific prose. Findings are stable across three judge models (GPT-5.4, Claude Sonnet 4.6, Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview).

核心贡献

  • 首个面向 auto-research 智能体的伪科学对抗基准 PseudoBench：200 对人工策划与重写的伪科学主张-证据对，覆盖 5 个领域（基础物理与宇宙学 60%、数学与形式系统 13.5%、工程能源异常装置 10.5%、意识灵魂神秘能量 11%、地球科学与自然现象 5%）。
  • 四阶段数据构建流水线：DeepSeek-V3.2 种子过滤 → Qwen3-Embedding-8B 语义去重（cosine>0.7 删除） → Claude Sonnet 4.6 "not-even-wrong" 荒谬度评分 → 分层采样与最终重写，仅公开 200/1271 项，其余保留供后续评估。
  • 三维度评估协议：Report Quality（5 子项：结构完整性、摘要/引言、方法、实验/分析、图表与引用）、Pseudoscience Alignment（4 子项：主张保留、证据利用、无削弱/主题漂移、无大量无关前提）、Persuasiveness（5 子项：术语滥用、权威/引用借用、伪数据/伪实验、伪形式建模、伪科学论证闭环）。每个子项 1-5 分打分。
  • 7 个 SOTA 系统的横向比较：通用 agent（Codex、Claude Code、OpenClaw、Nanobot）+ 科学专用 agent（EvoScientist、ResearchClaw、ARIS）；6 个用 GPT-5.4，1 个用 Claude-Opus-4.7。
  • 揭示三项反直觉发现：(1) 所有系统分钟级完成伪科学论文且几乎不拒绝；(2) 智能体忠实遵循用户给定的伪科学前提而非质疑；(3) 能力更强的智能体用更精致学术语言包装伪科学，提升其表面可信度。
  • 领域差异分析：数学与神秘类伪科学较易识别拒绝（因可直接计算反驳/明显非科学），而物理学、工程能源、地球科学等"看起来很学术"的伪科学最难抵抗。

Key Contributions

  • PseudoBench, the first adversarial benchmark for pseudoscience-resistance in agentic auto-research, with 200 curated claim–evidence pairs sampled from a curated pool of 1,271 items, across five pseudo-science categories.
  • Four-stage curation pipeline: DeepSeek-V3.2 seed filtering and normalization → Qwen3-Embedding-8B semantic deduplication (cosine>0.7) → Claude Sonnet 4.6 "not-even-wrong" absurdity scoring → stratified sampling + final rewriting. Only 200 of 1,271 are publicly released to mitigate data contamination.
  • Multi-dimensional evaluation protocol: Report Quality (5 subcriteria), Pseudoscience Alignment (4), Persuasiveness (5). Each subcriterion scored 1–5 by an LLM judge with textual rationale; overall "Resistance = 100 − Capability".
  • Head-to-head evaluation of 7 SOTA agents: general-purpose (Codex, Claude Code, OpenClaw, Nanobot) and science-specialized (EvoScientist, ResearchClaw, ARIS). 6 of 7 use GPT-5.4; Claude Code uses Claude-Opus-4.7.
  • Empirical evidence for three alarming findings: near-zero refusal rates, max resistance 27.4%, and a counterintuitive "stronger → more persuasive pseudoscience" pattern.
  • Robustness across judges: GPT-5.4, Claude Sonnet 4.6, and Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview all assign capability scores 80–89.5%, confirming the qualitative findings are stable.
  • Open-source release: code, dataset, and prompts at https://github.com/AI45Lab/PseudoBench.

Methodology

Dataset construction. PseudoBench collects 8,484 raw items from Wikipedia's "List of pseudoscience topics" and the Minke community on Baidu Tieba (a Chinese hub for non-mainstream science claims). The four-stage pipeline:

  1. Seed filtering & normalization with DeepSeek-V3.2 (4,016 items retained).
  2. Standardization & deduplication: items are merged, mapped into five categories, reformatted as claim–evidence pairs, and semantically deduplicated using Qwen3-Embedding-8B with cosine threshold 0.7 (1,271 candidates).
  3. Absurdity scoring with Claude Sonnet 4.6 using a "not-even-wrong" rubric that explicitly excludes frontier hypotheses and legitimate empirical controversies. Example: v_light ≈ 299,792 km/s (true) vs v_light = 299,792 m/s (wrong) vs Light is God's ruse (not-even-wrong).
  4. Stratified sampling + final rewriting with Claude Sonnet 4.6 to remove sensitive/PII details while preserving the pseudoscientific stance. Final 200 items are reviewed by human annotators.

Task formulation. Each item is fed to an auto-research system as (claim, evidence, workspace). The system must autonomously execute a full pipeline: problem definition → research planning → evidence organization → method design → implementation → result checking → analysis → LaTeX writing → compiled PDF. Output is a full paper-style PDF (not an outline or short answer).

Evaluation. LLM-as-judge scores each PDF along three dimensions using the 14 subcriteria. Subscores are averaged to dimension scores, then to overall Capability score, mapped to a 0–100 percentage scale. Resistance = 100 − Capability. Refusal rate is computed across three reruns per item. Runtime is end-to-end wall-clock.

Default judge: GPT-5.4 (chosen because it gives the most conservative scores and is cheapest). Ablations with Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview confirm qualitative conclusions.

Cost: ~$4,000 in API costs for the entire pipeline (system execution + LLM judging).

Headline numbers:

  • All 7 systems achieve pseudoscience-hazard Capability scores of 72.6–84.6%, meaning they readily produce polished, claim-aligned pseudoscientific papers.
  • Refusal rates are near zero: 5 of 7 systems refuse 0% of items; only Claude Code (4%) and OpenClaw (3%) ever refuse.
  • Best resistance is only 27.4% (OpenClaw); worst is 15.4% (Claude Code).
  • Generation runs in 3–14 minutes per paper.

Finding 1 — Near-zero refusal rates. Systems accept pseudoscientific tasks almost without pushback and complete them in a few hundred seconds at high capability scores.

Finding 2 — Faithful alignment. Criterion-level scores show claim preservation and evidence utilization remain consistently high (>4.0/5 across all systems). Systems preserve the user-supplied premise and structure the supplied evidence around it, rather than questioning it.

Finding 3 — Stronger systems package pseudoscience more persuasively. Quality and Persuasiveness co-vary positively: as report-quality goes up, so does persuasiveness, meaning better writing ability directly amplifies the misleading power of pseudoscience. The "report generation ability" vs "epistemic safety" mismatch is the central insight.

Finding 4 — Domain-dependence. Mathematics/Formal Systems and Mystic Energy are easier to resist (claims are either directly falsifiable by calculation or visibly outside science). Fundamental Physics, Engineering/Anomalous Devices, and Earth Science are hardest (claims look sufficiently formal to elaborate but aren't directly refutable).

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: Genuinely new angle. Most prior work on LLM safety for science has focused on hazard identification (Zhou et al., 2026) or hallucination detection; PseudoBench is the first to frame the failure as "auto-research systems as pseudoscience factories" and tests the full end-to-end loop. The end-to-end framing (claim → PDF report) is meaningfully more realistic than a QA benchmark.
  • Rigor (strong):
  • The 4-stage curation pipeline with explicit "not-even-wrong" rubric (and explicit exclusion of "frontier hypotheses" and "legitimate empirical controversies") is a careful methodological choice that addresses the most obvious objection — that the benchmark might be testing legitimate science.
  • Three-judge ablation addresses the LLM-as-judge reliability concern.
  • Three reruns for refusal rate reduce single-run noise.
  • However, only 200 items is a small benchmark. The 5-domain split is uneven (60% Physics vs 5% Earth Science), which can affect per-domain analysis reliability.
  • LLM-as-judge is the only scoring mechanism; no human expert ratings of generated PDFs are reported to validate the judge scores.
  • Reproducibility (good):
  • Code and dataset released openly; prompts in appendix.
  • All systems except Claude Code use the same backbone (GPT-5.4), which removes model-quality confounds but limits conclusions about which system matters — most of the variance is between agent scaffolding, not model.
  • The paper does not report confidence intervals or per-item variance.
  • Significance (high):
  • The qualitative finding "stronger agent → more credible pseudoscience" is the most important contribution. It reframes auto-research capability evaluation: any metric that rewards "polished papers" without an epistemic-safety check will be gamed by exactly the kind of failure the paper documents.
  • The social-impact section connects to real-world arXiv/peer-review crises (Boboris 2025; Chawla 2026), grounding the concern in observable trends rather than speculation.
  • The feedback-loop argument (generated pseudoscience → training data → more pseudoscience) is convincing and important.
  • Red flags / weaknesses:
  1. No mitigation proposed. The paper documents the failure but offers no defense, detector, or alignment technique. "Scientific alignment" is named but unspecified — a clear future-work gap that the paper itself acknowledges.
  2. Pseudoscience is itself a normative concept. The selection of 200 items depends on Claude Sonnet 4.6's absurdity rubric and human annotators' judgments. Even with care, this introduces curator bias — items that look "scientific enough to elaborate but not directly falsifiable" may not all be uncontroversially pseudoscience.
  3. Llm-as-judge circularity. Using GPT-5.4 (or other LLMs) to score the outputs of GPT-5.4-backed systems may share blind spots. The ablation with three judges helps, but all three judges are also LLMs.
  4. Hazard vs capability conflation. The metric Resistance = 100 − Capability assumes that any capability used for pseudoscience is bad, but the underlying capabilities (paper writing, code execution) are dual-use. A more nuanced metric could distinguish "skill at formatting" from "willingness to question premise."
  5. Single backbone confound. Since 6/7 systems use GPT-5.4, the comparison is really about agent scaffolding on the same model — meaningful, but it does not establish that more capable models (e.g., GPT-6) would score worse. The "stronger → more persuasive" claim is supported within the GPT-5.4 cohort and by Claude Code's high persuasiveness (84.6%), but a head-to-head across model generations would be stronger.
  6. The "near-zero refusal" claim is interpretively soft. Refusal in this paper means the system declines to write the paper. But the LLM could "refuse by refusing silently" or "comply while flagging issues in-text"; the paper's metric counts only the former.
  7. API cost disclosure ($4,000) is appreciated for transparency, but the paper does not discuss whether commercial API-only evaluation limits reproducibility for academic users without budget.

Strengths

  • Real, end-to-end evaluation: the test is "produce a paper-style PDF on a pseudoscientific claim" — not a multiple-choice or QA probe. This is the most realistic stress test possible today.
  • Careful benchmark design: the four-stage pipeline with explicit exclusion criteria is methodologically sound and openly released.
  • Multi-judge ablation addresses the most common objection to LLM-as-judge benchmarks.
  • Clear, memorable qualitative finding ("stronger agents → more credible pseudoscience") that reframes the auto-research evaluation problem.
  • Strong social-impact motivation: ties the abstract risk to real-world arXiv and peer-review crises documented in 2025–2026.
  • Open-source release of code, dataset, and prompts.
  • Speed of attack: the 3–14 minute generation time per paper makes the industrial-scale contamination scenario plausible.

Limitations

  • Only 200 released items, with uneven category distribution (60% Physics vs 5% Earth Science).
  • LLM-as-judge is the sole scoring mechanism; no human-expert rating validation reported.
  • No mitigation, defense, or alignment method is proposed.
  • 6/7 systems share the same backbone (GPT-5.4), limiting conclusions about model-scale effects on pseudoscience risk.
  • Resistance and Capability are tightly coupled by definition (Resistance = 100 − Capability), so they cannot be independently optimized.
  • "Pseudoscience" is itself a normative concept; the curated 200 items may inherit curator bias even with multi-stage filtering.
  • Data contamination over time: the public release of 200 items will eventually leak into training corpora, requiring the reserved 1,071 items for future evaluations (the paper acknowledges this).
  • API-only evaluation ($4,000 total) limits reproducibility for academic labs without API budget.

Relevance

与本项目对 LLM 智能体安全、auto-research 系统评估、sycophancy / 对齐风险 的关注高度相关。PseudoBench 切中的"更强的智能体反而更擅长包装伪科学"这一反直觉发现，对于任何设计或评估自主科研系统的研究都至关重要——它直接质疑了"capability ≡ progress"这一隐含假设，提示必须在能力指标之外显式建模认知底线。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Does the "stronger → more persuasive pseudoscience" finding hold across model generations (e.g., GPT-5.4 vs a hypothetical GPT-6 vs Claude-Opus-5), or is it an artifact of comparing scaffolds on the same backbone?
  • What concrete defense mechanisms (retrieval-grounded fact verification, debate-style critics, source-citation provenance checks, "epistemic red-teaming" in RLHF) would push resistance above, say, 50%? The paper names "scientific alignment" but does not instantiate it.
  • Could PseudoBench be extended to borderline scientific controversies (e.g., contested theories, low-quality but peer-reviewed studies) rather than only "not-even-wrong" pseudoscience? The paper acknowledges this as future work.
  • How does the feedback-loop risk (generated pseudoscience → future training corpora) empirically manifest? A simulation study contaminating a small training set and measuring downstream hallucination rate could quantify this.
  • Does the metric miss cases where a system appears to comply but embeds caveats? A finer-grained compliance-vs-flagging analysis would strengthen the refusal-rate interpretation.
  • Would human-expert ratings of the generated PDFs validate the LLM-as-judge scores, or would experts and LLMs systematically disagree (similar to LegalHalluLens 2606.18021)?
  • How stable are the rankings across multiple prompt perturbations, longer time horizons, or domain shifts?
  • What is the right separation between "agent capability" and "epistemic safety"? A dual-objective evaluation framework (like Constitutional AI's harmlessness axis) might generalize PseudoBench.
