> **一句话总结：** 基于 UK AISI 的统一受控评估框架（6 个前沿模型 × 5 个主基准 + 2 个网络空间基准，配合 5M–100M 的大 token 预算、上下文压缩与 999 次重试），证明推理时算力会以"领域相关"的方式显著抬升基准分数，代际增益主要来自 reach（解锁更难的题）和 reliability（更稳定地解出）而非 token 效率，并据此主张以"算力—能力曲线"和匹配预算的代际比较作为新的评估原语。

> **TL;DR:** A controlled study by the UK AI Security Institute evaluates six frontier LLMs (Anthropic Opus 4 → 4.5 → 4.6, GPT-5 → 5.2 → 5.4, plus cyber-only models including Mythos Preview) on five main benchmarks (TerminalBench, SWE-Bench Pro, FrontierMath, HealthBench, HLE) and two cyber benchmarks (Cyber CTFs, The Last Ones), using a ReAct scaffold in Inspect AI with token budgets of 5M–100M, context compaction at 130k, and up to 999 resubmissions per trajectory. They find that (1) inference scaling is substantial but highly benchmark-dependent — FrontierMath gains +11.7pp, HLE +9.3pp, but SWE-Bench Pro only +0.3pp from typical to expanded budgets; (2) generational gains come mainly from increased *reach* (newer models unlock harder tasks) and *reliability* (solve them more consistently) rather than from token efficiency; (3) repeated submission helps everywhere (uplift 1.11×–1.70×), oracle feedback helps most where it can guide continued search (HLE, SWE-Bench Pro), and parallel sampling helps on stateless benchmarks (HLE, HealthBench) more than stateful ones. The authors call for reporting capability as a curve over inference compute and comparing model generations over a shared matched-budget range.

## 核心贡献

- **跨域受控实验框架**：6 个前沿模型 × 5 个主基准（终端任务、真实软件工程、专家数学、临床推理、跨学科知识）外加 2 个 AISI 网络空间基准，使用统一的 ReAct scaffold（Inspect AI）和一致的"大预算 + 上下文压缩 + 999 次重试"协议，是目前少有的在同一框架内进行跨域推理扩展评估的工作。
- **三大量化发现**：(a) 推理扩展的收益因基准而异——FrontierMath/HLE/网络空间能从扩展预算中显著获益，HealthBench/软件工程类几乎饱和；(b) 代际改进主要来自"reach + reliability"而非 token 效率；(c) 不同协议干预（重试次数、oracle 反馈、串行 vs. 并行算力分配）对不同基准的收益结构差异巨大。
- **任务级分解方法**：把聚合曲线的代际改进拆解为 reach（解锁多少任务）、efficiency（解锁后用多少 token）、reliability（多次重试中能稳定解出的比例）三部分，并配合混合效应回归给出统计显著性。
- **评估方法学建议**：明确反对"单点固定预算分数"，主张把推理时算力纳入测量定义本身；强调对代际对比要在大且共享的预算区间内做匹配比较。

## Key Contributions

- **Unified controlled framework:** 6 frontier models × 5 main benchmarks + 2 cyber benchmarks, one shared ReAct scaffold, expanded budgets (5M–100M), context compaction at 130k, up to 999 resubmissions. Reproducible protocol that establishes a *lower bound* on what simple inference-scaling can elicit.
- **Three quantitative findings:**
  1. Inference scaling is substantial but **highly benchmark-dependent** — FrontierMath +11.7pp, HLE +9.3pp from typical to expanded budgets, vs. only +0.3pp on SWE-Bench Pro and HealthBench.
  2. Generational gains are driven mainly by **reach** and **reliability**, not by token efficiency.
  3. No single intervention is universally best: repeated submission helps everywhere, oracle feedback helps most on HLE / SWE-Bench Pro, parallel sampling helps most on stateless benchmarks.
- **Task-level decomposition:** Splits aggregate scaling curves into reach / efficiency / reliability components and quantifies each with mixed-effects regressions (per benchmark, with generation × difficulty interactions).
- **Protocol-level recommendations:** Report capability as a curve over inference-time compute; document protocol choices explicitly; compare model generations over a shared, large compute range at matched budgets.

## Methodology

- **Models (main suite):** Six frontier models from two families spanning May 2025 – Mar 2026: Opus 4 / 4.5 / 4.6 and GPT-5 / 5.2 / 5.4. All run at highest reasoning effort (xhigh for Anthropic, high for OpenAI) with a 16,000 reasoning-token per-generation budget. Cyber suite additionally includes o3, Sonnet 4.5, GPT-5.1/5.2/5.3 Codex, and Mythos Preview.
- **Benchmarks:**
  - *Stateful (bash + python tools):* TerminalBench 2.0 (82 tasks, 10M cap), SWE-Bench Pro (100 tasks, 30M cap), FrontierMath (12 tasks, 10M cap, code-verified).
  - *Stateless (tool-less):* HealthBench Hard (100 tasks, 10M cap, rubric-graded by GPT-4o-mini), HLE (100 tasks, 5M cap, MC items filtered out, LLM-judged).
  - *Cyber:* Cyber CTFs (71 tasks, 50M cap, flag capture), The Last Ones (1 long-horizon task, 32 milestones, 100M cap).
  - All benchmarks are programmatically graded (binary) except HealthBench (continuous).
- **Three inference-scaling interventions (uniform across cells):**
  1. Expanded total token budgets 5M–100M, one to three orders of magnitude above published defaults.
  2. Context compaction at 130k running tokens using Inspect AI's default summary strategy.
  3. Iterative resubmission with cap of 999, governed by an LLM-judge repetition guard that terminates after 3 semantically equivalent submissions.
- **Two feedback conditions:** (a) *no feedback* — only "Your answer has been saved" acknowledgement; (b) *oracle score feedback* — model is told correctness after each submission and trajectory ends on first full credit. 5 trajectories per (model, benchmark, condition, task) cell.
- **Parallel scaling analysis (Section 3.3.2):** Re-analyses the same trajectories under different fixed-total-budget splits (k ∈ {1, 2, 5, 10}) to estimate pass@k at budget B.
- **Task-level decomposition (Section 3.2):** Linear probability model for reach (unlock ≥ 2/5 trajectories), log-tokens regression for efficiency, and solve rate for reliability — each regressed on model generation, task difficulty, and their interaction; robust to sensitivity analysis on a balanced panel of tasks unlocked by all models.

## Results & Highlights

- **Inference scaling is benchmark-dependent.** Going from typical published budgets to the expanded caps:
  - FrontierMath: **+11.7 ± 11.0 pp** (1M → 10M tokens).
  - HLE: **+9.3 ± 12.0 pp** (64K → 5M; +12.4 with oracle feedback, +6.1 without).
  - Cyber CTFs and TerminalBench: curves still rising for most models at the cap.
  - SWE-Bench Pro: **+0.3 ± 0.3 pp** (16M → 30M).
  - HealthBench: **+0.3 ± 0.4 pts** despite a 16K → 10M expansion.
- **Plateau behavior (Figure 1B):** TerminalBench and both cyber benchmarks continue growing for all/most models at the cap. HealthBench plateaus well within typical budget. FrontierMath, HLE, SWE-Bench Pro show mixed plateau behavior with provider/generation splits.
- **Generational gains decompose into reach + reliability, not efficiency:**
  - *Reach* β_gen is positive and significant on **all 6 benchmarks**, largest on FrontierMath (+0.631) and Cyber CTFs (+0.488), and concentrated on harder tasks on 5 of 6.
  - *Efficiency* β_gen is significantly negative (fewer tokens per solve) on **4 of 6** benchmarks (Cyber CTFs, TerminalBench, SWE-Bench Pro, HealthBench), but no significant efficiency gain on FrontierMath or HLE.
  - *Reliability* β_gen is positive and significant on **5 of 6** benchmarks (everything except HealthBench).
- **Repeated submission (Figure 3, Table 5):** Cumulative performance rises from the first submission to peak by:
  - HLE: **+17.1 pp, 1.70× uplift** (k90 = 12–13).
  - SWE-Bench Pro: +14.4 pp, 1.27×.
  - HealthBench: +14.2 pp, 1.40×.
  - TerminalBench: +10.4 pp, 1.19×.
  - FrontierMath: +6.4 pp, 1.11× (k90 = 3).
  Oracle feedback significantly amplifies iteration gains on HLE (+25.1 vs. +9.1) and SWE-Bench Pro (+16.0 vs. +12.7); little effect on the other three. Newer models tend to find repeated submission less helpful on HLE and TerminalBench (less room to improve).
- **Serial vs. parallel allocation (Figure 4, Table 7):** Going from pass@1 to pass@10 at each benchmark's cap:
  - HealthBench: 0.501 → 0.784 (**+0.283**).
  - HLE: 0.412 → 0.595 (+0.183).
  - TerminalBench: 0.657 → 0.720 (+0.063).
  - FrontierMath: 0.625 → 0.653 (+0.028).
  - SWE-Bench Pro: 0.673 → 0.701 (+0.028).
  Crossover from depth-favored to width-favored regimes happens at very different budgets: ~23K for HealthBench, ~177K for HLE, near the cap (5M–30M) for the others. **Newer models benefit less from parallel sampling on all five benchmarks** (Kendall τ from −0.33 to −0.73), suggesting they extract more value from a single long trajectory.
- **Headline message:** Benchmark scores are protocol-dependent. Fixed-budget reports systematically understate frontier capability, and the underestimation grows as models improve.

## Authors & Credibility

- **Affiliations:** UK AI Security Institute (London), University of Oxford, Harvard University. All authors are either AISI staff or closely affiliated academic collaborators; the work is consistent with AISI's broader frontier-evaluation publication track.
- **Track record:** The methodology and framing closely parallel AISI's prior cyber-capability reports (Folkerts et al. 2026, AISI blog posts 2026a/b) and the METR/Epoch AI time-horizon literature. The repetition-guard design, oracle-vs-no-feedback split, and inference-scaling curve definitions are all consistent with the field's current best practices.
- **Topical stakes:** This is a government-affiliated lab arguing against single-budget leaderboards in safety-/policy-relevant settings — the framing is policy-aligned with UK AI Safety policy positions.
- **Funding/affiliation note:** The work uses the AISI Inspect framework and references an AISI Mythos Preview checkpoint, so the authors have direct access to frontier models (including the unreleased Mythos Preview) that most academic groups don't.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** The general claim that inference compute matters is well established (Snell et al. 2024; OpenAI o1/o3 reporting; Folkerts et al. 2026). The novelty is (a) the *cross-domain* breadth under one framework and (b) the explicit *protocol-level* argument that matched-budget comparison is required for trend tracking. The task-level reach / efficiency / reliability decomposition is a useful new framing.
- **Rigor:**
  - **Strong:** Fully-crossed 6 × 5 design (main suite), 5 trajectories per cell, both feedback conditions, statistical testing with mixed-effects regressions, sensitivity analysis on a balanced panel of unlocked tasks. Cyber suite reuse is explicitly flagged as separate from the main design.
  - **Modest caveats:** Only 12 tasks on FrontierMath → low power for task-level regressions; correlation analyses on τ_gen with 6 models are explicitly described as "descriptive only."
  - The repetition guard uses an LLM judge whose reliability is itself uncalibrated — could fire inconsistently across domains.
- **Reproducibility:** Token budgets, compaction trigger (130k), and the 999-submission cap are all concrete. The adaptive continuation prompt and per-benchmark feedback variants are promised in Appendix A.1.4. The Inspect AI scaffold is open source. Model snapshots are listed in Table 1 with release dates, but exact API endpoints / reasoning-effort parameter names (e.g., OpenAI's `reasoning_effort = "high"`) are not always specified — a careful re-runner would need to match reasoning effort precisely.
- **Significance:** If the policy argument lands, this could push safety evaluations toward reporting capability curves rather than single numbers. The "fixed-budget scores understate progress" claim is the kind of finding that should change evaluation practice in regulatory contexts.
- **Red flags / concerns:**
  - **Provider balance is asymmetric:** Only Anthropic and OpenAI are evaluated in the main suite. No Google (Gemini), xAI (Grok), Meta (Llama), or DeepSeek / Chinese models. The "frontier" claim therefore refers to two providers' offerings, not the literal frontier.
  - **Token budgets are not budget-matched across benchmarks** (5M–100M, chosen per task complexity). This is defensible for "what is achievable at large budgets" but means absolute scores are not directly comparable to published benchmark results — the authors flag this but it limits the paper's headline appeal.
  - **FrontierMath is known to have training-overlap risk** for newer models (the authors flag this).
  - **"Up to 12 frontier models" in the abstract** — the main table has 6 fully-crossed models and 6 cyber-only models; some model×benchmark cells are marked ✗. The framing slightly inflates the experimental scale.
  - The headline "newer models gain more from large budgets" rests on a small set of 3 generations per family; would benefit from a fourth data point.

## Strengths

- Cross-domain coverage (terminal/SWE, math, medicine, cyber, expert knowledge) avoids single-domain overfitting.
- Honest about per-benchmark heterogeneity — refuses to claim one-size-fits-all scaling laws.
- Mixed-effects regressions with generation × difficulty interactions give statistical substance to the reach/reliability claims.
- Direct, actionable protocol recommendations aimed at evaluation practitioners and policy audiences.
- Reuse of pre-existing cyber data is transparently flagged and isolated from the main crossed design.
- Carefully distinguishes "weak scaling under our protocol" from "the task is intrinsically compute-saturated."

## Limitations

- **Single scaffold (ReAct).** Bespoke scaffolds and elicitation effort can shift measured capability significantly (METR 2024, AISI 2024b). The HealthBench weak-scaling result in particular could reflect scaffold mismatch rather than intrinsic saturation.
- **Conservative protocol.** Adaptive branching, verifier-guided search, and hybrid serial–parallel allocation are not tested — the paper explicitly calls its results a *lower bound* on what broadly reproducible inference scaling can achieve.
- **FrontierMath is small (12 tasks)**, weakening task-level analyses and creating training-overlap risk for newer models.
- **Repetition guard uses an LLM judge** whose reliability is not calibrated; effective strictness likely varies across domains.
- **Provider coverage is limited to Anthropic + OpenAI** in the main suite, with 3 generations per family. The "frontier" claim does not extend to other providers.
- **Some model×benchmark cells are empty** in Table 1, so the "fully-crossed" claim is conditional on the five main benchmarks.
- **90-minute per-generation timeout** is a hard wall that may bound long-horizon trajectories before the 999-submission cap or the token cap.

## Relevance

与用户研究方向高度相关。本文正是测试时算力（inference compute scaling）对 LLM 能力评估影响的核心实证研究，提出的"算力—能力曲线 + 匹配预算代际比较"框架对模型选型、安全评估协议设计以及"老模型单点分数被新型号大预算反超"现象的解读都有直接参考价值；任务级 reach / reliability / efficiency 分解也适合作为分析自家模型行为的参考脚手架。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Does the cross-generational reach-and-reliability pattern replicate on open-weight models (e.g., Llama 4, DeepSeek) under the same protocol, and does the "newer models benefit less from parallel sampling" finding hold outside the Anthropic/OpenAI pair?
- How do these scaling patterns shift under *agentic* scaffolds (browser use, multi-step tool chains, code execution with persistent shells) compared to the single ReAct scaffold used here?
- For benchmarks that plateaued early (HealthBench, SWE-Bench Pro), is the plateau driven by a true capability ceiling or by the LLM-judge / test-harness saturating first?
- What is the minimum matched-budget range required to make cross-generation capability comparisons statistically and economically meaningful in a regulatory setting?
- Could a "protocol-aware" reporting standard (e.g., reporting a capability curve summary plus an elicitation budget declaration) be incorporated into the next generation of safety case frameworks?
