Inference compute scaling is real, but benchmark-dependent — FrontierMath climbs +11.7pp while SWE-Bench Pro barely budges.

How Inference Compute Shapes Frontier LLM Evaluation
Authors · Jessica McFadyen, Ole Jorgensen, Harry Coppock, Kevin Wei, Cozmin Ududec Date · 2026-06-16 Affiliation · UK AI Security Institute · Oxford · Harvard
inference-scaling LLM-evaluation test-time-compute benchmark-protocol frontier-models
Key Metric
+11.7pp on FrontierMath when the budget expands from 1M → 10M tokens; +9.3pp on HLE (64K → 5M), but only +0.3pp on SWE-Bench Pro (16M → 30M).
Why This Matters
Benchmark scores are protocol-dependent. Fixed-budget reports systematically understate frontier capability, and the underestimation grows as models improve — so single-number leaderboards mislead trend tracking.
Critical Edge
Main suite covers only Anthropic + OpenAI across 3 generations each — the "frontier" claim does not extend to Google, xAI, Meta, or DeepSeek.

Methodology

6 × 5 fully-crossed design: six frontier models (Opus 4 / 4.5 / 4.6 and GPT-5 / 5.2 / 5.4) on five main benchmarks, plus a separate cyber suite. One shared ReAct scaffold in Inspect AI, highest reasoning effort, 16,000 reasoning tokens per generation.

Budgets and protocol levers

  • Expanded token budgets 5M – 100M, one-to-three orders of magnitude above published defaults, chosen per task complexity.
  • Context compaction triggered at 130k running tokens using Inspect's default summary strategy.
  • Up to 999 resubmissions per trajectory, gated by an LLM-judge repetition guard (terminates after 3 semantically equivalent submissions).
  • Two feedback conditions: no feedback vs. oracle score feedback; 5 trajectories per (model × benchmark × condition × task) cell.

Benchmarks

  • Stateful: TerminalBench 2.0 (82), SWE-Bench Pro (100), FrontierMath (12, code-verified).
  • Stateless: HealthBench Hard (100, rubric-graded), HLE (100, MC filtered out, LLM-judged).
  • Cyber: Cyber CTFs (71), The Last Ones (1 long-horizon task, 32 milestones).

Where generational gains come from

  • Reach (unlock harder tasks): significant on all 6 benchmarks; largest on FrontierMath (+0.631), concentrated on harder tasks.
  • Reliability (stable solve rate): significant on 5 of 6; only HealthBench fails to improve.
  • Efficiency (tokens per solve): actually gets worse on 4 of 6 — newer models use more tokens per solve.

Intervention effectiveness

  • Repeated submission helps everywhere (1.11× – 1.70×); HLE gains +17.1pp at k90 ≈ 12.
  • Oracle feedback amplifies iteration most on HLE (+25.1 vs +9.1) and SWE-Bench Pro (+16.0 vs +12.7).
  • Parallel sampling (pass@1 → pass@10) lifts HealthBench +0.283, HLE +0.183; newer models benefit less (τ from −0.33 to −0.73).

Strengths

What works

  • Cross-domain coverage — terminal, SWE, math, medicine, cyber, expert knowledge.
  • Refuses to claim a one-size-fits-all scaling law; honest per-benchmark heterogeneity.
  • Mixed-effects regressions with generation × difficulty give statistical substance to reach / reliability claims.
  • Direct, actionable protocol recommendations aimed at evaluation practitioners and policy.
  • Cyber data reuse is transparently flagged and isolated from the main crossed design.

Limitations

  • Single scaffold (ReAct) — bespoke scaffolds can shift measured capability; HealthBench's weak scaling may reflect scaffold mismatch.
  • Conservative protocol — no adaptive branching, verifier-guided search, or hybrid serial–parallel allocation; results are explicitly a lower bound.
  • FrontierMath is small (12 tasks), weakening task-level analyses and risking training overlap.
  • Repetition guard uses an uncalibrated LLM judge; strictness likely varies by domain.
  • Main suite is Anthropic + OpenAI only, 3 generations per family.
  • Some model × benchmark cells are empty; "fully-crossed" is conditional on the five main benchmarks.
  • 90-minute per-generation timeout may bound long-horizon trajectories before the 999-submission cap.

Critical Assessment

Novelty

General claim (inference compute matters) is well established. The novelty is the cross-domain breadth under one framework plus the explicit protocol-level argument for matched-budget comparison. The reach / efficiency / reliability decomposition is a useful new framing.

Rigor

Strong: fully-crossed 6 × 5, 5 trajectories per cell, both feedback conditions, mixed-effects regressions, sensitivity analysis on a balanced panel. Modest caveats: only 12 FrontierMath tasks, τ analyses with 6 models are explicitly descriptive, and the LLM-judge repetition guard is itself uncalibrated.

Reproducibility

Token budgets, 130k compaction, 999-submission cap are concrete. Inspect AI is open source. Model snapshots listed (Table 1) with release dates; exact API endpoints and reasoning-effort parameter names are not always specified — a careful re-runner must match reasoning effort precisely.

Significance

If the policy argument lands, this could push safety evaluations toward reporting capability curves rather than single numbers — a finding that should change evaluation practice in regulatory contexts.

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Does the cross-generational reach-and-reliability pattern replicate on open-weight models (Llama 4, DeepSeek) under the same protocol, and does the "newer models benefit less from parallel sampling" finding hold outside the Anthropic / OpenAI pair?
  • How do these scaling patterns shift under agentic scaffolds (browser use, multi-step tool chains, persistent shells) compared to the single ReAct scaffold used here?
  • For benchmarks that plateaued early (HealthBench, SWE-Bench Pro), is the plateau driven by a true capability ceiling or by the LLM-judge / test-harness saturating first?
  • What is the minimum matched-budget range required to make cross-generation capability comparisons statistically and economically meaningful in a regulatory setting?
  • Could a protocol-aware reporting standard (capability curve + elicitation budget declaration) be incorporated into the next generation of safety case frameworks?
