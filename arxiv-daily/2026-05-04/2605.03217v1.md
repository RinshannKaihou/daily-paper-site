> **一句话总结：** 本文提出道德敏感度指数（MSI），通过七层递进的电车难题压力测试量化LLM在不同社会语境下的偏见强度，并结合五步机械可解释性管线（logit lens、注意力分析、激活修补、语义方向、OV电路重建）揭示了推理蒸馏会以U型曲线的方式重新引入已被大规模模型抑制的刑事偏见——即小模型有偏见、扩大规模消除偏见、蒸馏又把偏见带回来。

> **TL;DR:** This paper introduces the Moral Sensitivity Index (MSI), a composite metric (bias certainty + ambiguity + semantic entropy) evaluated across a 7-tier trolley-problem stress test on Claude, Qwen, Llama 3, and Gemini 1.5. It reveals model-specific "moral personalities" (e.g., Claude's step-function override at protected-class triggers, Gemini's structural skepticism of utilitarianism itself). A 5-step mechanistic interpretability pipeline applied to 6 models across 3 capability tiers uncovers a U-curve of criminal bias: SLMs are biased (87–100%), base instruction-tuned models suppress it (0.8–16%), and reasoning-distilled models reintroduce it (59–96%). Circuit-level analysis shows distillation reorganizes attention patterns, concentrates causal influence in specific heads, and compresses semantic representations around criminal identity.

## 核心贡献

- **道德敏感度指数（MSI）**：将LLM的偏见响应分解为偏见确定性（B）、模糊性（A）和语义熵（E）三个正交维度，通过OLS回归拟合系数，实现对不同模型"道德人格"的量化刻画。
- **七层递进伦理压力测试**：从纯数字基线到历史不公，逐层引入年龄、责任、种族/性别、社会经济地位等变量，精确定位每个模型的"道德拐点"。
- **U型偏见曲线**：首次系统性地发现推理蒸馏（DeepSeek-R1-Distill）会重新激活大规模模型已学会抑制的偏见模式，且蒸馏模型的偏见水平可达到甚至超过SLM。
- **五步机械可解释性管线**：按"偏好何时出现→在何处定位→是否因果→如何表征→如何表达"的逻辑顺序，将行为观察直接链接到层、注意力头和语义表示的计算电路。
- **跨阶段验证**：MSI行为分析中得分最高的刑事偏见场景恰好是机械分析中最容易触发偏见电路的条件，实现了行为-机制的双向验证。

## Key Contributions

- **Moral Sensitivity Index (MSI)**: A weighted linear composite of bias score, ambiguity rate, and semantic entropy that quantifies the intensity and abruptness of safety-alignment overrides as contextual complexity increases, moving beyond binary bias/not-bias evaluations.
- **Seven-tier ethical stress test**: A controlled trolley-problem dataset that systematically introduces numerical, age, responsibility, protected-identity, socioeconomic, historical-injustice, and combined systemic variables to isolate contextual triggers of alignment overrides.
- **Model-specific moral profiles**: Characterizes four major LLMs as having distinct alignment strategies — Claude's step-function override at protected-class triggers (0% unbiased at Tier 4), Qwen's category-first academic reasoning (peaks at Tier 5 via semantic entropy), Gemini's structural skepticism (rejects the utilitarian premise itself), and Llama 3's high-entropy variability.
- **U-curve of bias via distillation**: Demonstrates that SLMs exhibit strong criminal bias (87–100%), scaling to instruction-tuned base models suppresses it (0.8–16%), but reasoning distillation reintroduces it to SLM-like levels (59–96%), despite identical parameter counts and architectures.
- **Five-step mechanistic interpretability pipeline**: Logit lens → attention differential analysis → activation patching → semantic direction projection → OV circuit reconstruction, applied in causal order to trace where, how, and why label-driven overrides occur internally.

## Methodology

The paper operates in two stages: behavioral profiling and mechanistic validation.

**Behavioral Stage.** Four instruction-tuned LLMs (Claude 3.5, Qwen 3.5, Llama 3, Gemini 1.5) are evaluated on a seven-tier trolley-problem dataset. Each tier adds one layer of social context (numerical → age → responsibility → protected identities → socioeconomic status → historical injustice → combined). Responses are annotated as Biased/Unbiased/Ambiguous. The MSI is defined as MSI = αB + βA + γE, where coefficients are derived via OLS regression with tier level as the dependent variable. An everyday-bias dataset provides baseline sensitivity calibration.

**Mechanistic Stage.** Six models spanning three capability tiers are probed: SLMs (Qwen 2.5 4B, Llama 3.2 3B), base instruction-tuned (Qwen 2.5 7B, Llama 3.1 8B), and reasoning-distilled (DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B). The probe uses 50 criminal-vs-non-criminal forced-choice scenarios in a trolley format. The five-step pipeline proceeds: (1) logit lens projects hidden states through the unembedding matrix at every layer to track P(Criminal) evolution; (2) differential attention analysis identifies heads tracking criminal tokens; (3) activation patching ablates candidate heads to measure causal contribution; (4) semantic direction analysis projects concepts onto a valence axis; (5) OV circuit reconstruction reveals what tokens causally implicated heads promote.

## Results & Highlights

- **Claude** shows a step-function moral profile: bias rate jumps from 25% (Tier 3) to 75% (Tier 4, protected identities) to 100% (Tier 6), with MSI driven almost exclusively by the bias coefficient (α).
- **Qwen** exhibits a unique ambiguity-then-bias pattern: ambiguity peaks at 33.3% in Tiers 2–3 with 0% bias, then ambiguity drops to 0% and bias rises to 33.3% at Tier 5, driven by semantic entropy (γ). Highest type-token ratio among all models.
- **Gemini** begins with 37.5% bias at Tier 1 (numerical baseline), explicitly arguing that utilitarianism itself is biased. Peaks at 72.7% at Tier 5 (socioeconomic context), not Tier 4 (protected identities).
- **Llama 3** has the lowest bias sensitivity (50%) but highest semantic entropy (0.97), indicating highly variable, unpredictable responses.
- **U-curve of criminal bias**: SLMs show 87–100% criminal selection; base models suppress to 0.8–16.4%; distilled models revert to 58.6–95.7%. Distill-Qwen-7B assigns 95.7% normalized probability to the criminal option.
- **Mechanistic findings**: Distilled models commit to biased predictions earlier in the network (flip at L4–L13 vs. L8–L10 in base), concentrate causal influence in fewer heads, and exhibit "semantic compression" where neutral identities (Scientist, Citizen) are pulled toward criminal valence space.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty**: High. The combination of graduated behavioral profiling (MSI) with mechanistic circuit analysis is genuinely new. The U-curve finding about distillation reintroducing bias is both surprising and important. Prior work on bias treats outputs as binary; MSI's three-component decomposition is a meaningful advance.

**Rigor**: Moderate-to-high for the behavioral analysis; moderate for the mechanistic analysis. The n=50 sample size for mechanistic probing limits statistical power, and the authors acknowledge this. The forced-choice simplification of the trolley problem reduces real-world applicability. Coefficients for MSI are derived from the same data used for evaluation, creating a risk of overfitting to the specific tier structure. The claim that distillation "reactivates shallow statistical associations" is plausible but the paper does not directly test the mechanism of how distillation causes this — it only shows the effect.

**Reproducibility**: Moderate. Code is available. Models are accessed via public APIs. However, proprietary model APIs (Claude, Gemini) may change behavior over time, making exact replication difficult. The 50-prompt criminal-bias probe is included.

**Significance**: High for the AI safety and alignment community. The finding that reasoning distillation degrades safety-relevant internal representations has direct implications for deployment pipelines, especially given the EU AI Act's upcoming requirements. The paper makes a compelling case for post-distillation auditing.

**Red flags**: (1) The MSI formula MSI = αB + βA + γE is a simple linear combination fitted via OLS — there is no theoretical justification for why these three components should combine additively rather than, say, multiplicatively or via threshold functions. (2) The mechanistic analysis covers only Llama and Qwen families; generalization to other architectures is unknown. (3) The paper's title and framing suggest broad coverage of "moral sensitivity" but the mechanistic drill-down focuses exclusively on criminal bias, which is a narrow slice of the full tiered evaluation. (4) The everyday-bias dataset is mentioned but not rigorously integrated into the MSI framework.

## Strengths

- The seven-tier dataset is a clever experimental design that allows attribution of behavioral changes to specific contextual variables.
- The U-curve finding is policy-relevant and non-obvious — it challenges the assumption that distillation is behaviorally neutral.
- Model-specific moral profiles (Claude's step-function, Qwen's academic reasoning, Gemini's structural skepticism) are insightful and well-supported by examples.
- The five-step mechanistic pipeline is well-ordered and follows a logical causal chain from observation to intervention to representation.
- Clear writing with worked examples that bridge quantitative metrics and qualitative model behavior.

## Limitations

- n=50 for the mechanistic probe is small; observed patterns are suggestive rather than definitive.
- The mechanistic analysis reduces the full 7-tier MSI setting to a binary criminal/non-criminal forced choice, losing the richness of the behavioral framework.
- Only Llama and Qwen model families are examined mechanistically; coverage of other architectures (Mistral, Phi, etc.) is absent.
- The MSI linear combination lacks theoretical grounding; the additive assumption is untested.
- Proprietary model APIs are moving targets — results may not be stable over time.
- The paper does not investigate whether the distillation-induced bias reversion can be mitigated through targeted intervention (e.g., safety-aware distillation objectives), leaving the practical remedy unspecified despite flagging it as a research direction.

## Relevance

本文与LLM隐状态可解释性研究方向高度相关：其五步机械可解释性管线（logit lens、注意力分析、激活修补、语义方向投影、OV电路重建）直接涉及表征几何、电路分析和探测方法，且关于蒸馏如何改变安全相关内部表征的发现对理解模型可靠性具有重要参考价值。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- Can the U-curve of bias be observed in other distillation pipelines (e.g., GPT-4 → GPT-4-mini, or open-source teacher → student pairs beyond DeepSeek-R1)?
- Does safety-aware distillation (explicitly penalizing degradation of safety-relevant attention patterns) eliminate the U-curve, and what is the accuracy cost?
- How does the MSI framework generalize to non-English, non-Western moral frameworks where the trolley problem may not be culturally neutral?
- Are the specific attention heads identified as bias-driving heads consistent across different prompt formats, or are they artifacts of the forced-choice structure?
- Can activation steering or representation engineering be applied at the identified "flip layers" to dynamically control bias without retraining?
