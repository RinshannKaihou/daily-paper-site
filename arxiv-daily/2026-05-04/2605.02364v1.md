> **一句话总结：** InfoLaw 提出了一种信息论视角的缩放定律，通过将数据质量建模为信息密度、将重复训练建模为指数衰减的信息增益，实现对不同数据配方、模型规模和重复程度下 LLM 验证损失的准确预测（平均绝对误差 0.15%），并能自动搜索最优数据配比。

> **TL;DR:** InfoLaw replaces the traditional compute-centric scaling law with an "Information" metric that accounts for data quality (via per-bucket information density) and repetition (via exponential decay with logarithmic normalization). Fitted on 252M–1.2B models across 27 runs with diverse LayerMix recipes, it extrapolates to 7B/425B-token runs with 0.15% mean and 0.96% max absolute error in loss, and can search for optimal data recipes without additional training runs. Key finding: small models or small budgets should prioritize high-quality data; large models or large budgets benefit more from diversity.

## 核心贡献

- 将预训练过程建模为信息积累过程，提出 Information 指标，统一量化数据质量、重复次数、模型规模和训练 token 数对模型性能的影响。
- 设计指数衰减函数刻画重复数据带来的边际收益递减，并引入 log(K) 归一化项使得不同训练预算下的缩放行为可统一拟合。
- 证明不同数据配方（LayerMix 采样权重）下的实验点在 Information-Loss 平面上坍缩为一条统一的幂律曲线，而传统基于计算量的缩放定律无法做到这一点。
- 实现了对未见数据配方、更大模型规模（至 7B）和更高过训练程度下的可靠外推，并据此自动搜索最优数据配比，无需额外实验。

## Key Contributions

- Proposes InfoLaw, a data-aware scaling framework that models pretraining as information accumulation, replacing compute (C) with an Information metric that incorporates quality-weighted mixture data and repetition.
- Introduces an exponential decay model for information gain from repeated data exposure, with a logarithmic normalization term log(K) that unifies scaling behavior across token budgets.
- Demonstrates that diverse data recipes collapse onto a single power-law curve in the Information-Loss plane (L = α · info^{−β}), whereas traditional compute-based scaling laws produce scattered, non-predictive fits.
- Achieves 0.15% mean absolute error and 0.96% max absolute error in loss prediction on unseen recipes and scales up to 7B parameters and 425B tokens.
- Enables efficient data-recipe optimization: sampling 100k LayerMix configurations, computing Information for each, and selecting the minimum-loss recipe without additional training runs.
- Derives actionable guidance: small models/small budgets should prioritize quality; large models/large budgets should prioritize diversity.

## Methodology

The method centers on a **LayerMix** sampling framework and an **Information** metric:

1. **Data bucketing:** Documents are ranked by a merged quality score (FineWebEdu + DCLM classifiers) and partitioned into 6 quality buckets by percentile (0–5%, 5–20%, 20–40%, 40–60%, 60–80%, 80–100%). A LayerMix function H(w, K, S, B) controls the sampling proportion from each bucket, where w specifies target weights, K is total training tokens, and S is source corpus size.

2. **Information measurement:** For each quality bucket d, total information is I_d = f_d · M_d · log(K), where f_d = e^{−θ·d} is a parameterized quality density (higher-quality buckets get higher density), and M_d is the number of unique tokens from that bucket. The model's learning from repeated exposures is modeled as:
   - Per-exposure gain: I_i^{part}(t, λ(N), K) = I_i · λ(N) · e^{−λ(N)·t / log(K)}
   - Total accumulated: I_i^{total} = I_i · log(K) · (1 − e^{−λ(N)·R_d / log(K)})
   
   where λ(N) = a·ln(N) + b captures model capacity (non-embedding FLOPs/token), and R_d is the average repetition count for bucket d.

3. **Information-Loss power law:** L = α · info^{−β}, where info sums across all buckets. This yields a single unified curve across all mixture recipes and model scales.

4. **Fitting procedure:** Fit on 27 runs (9 model sizes from 252M to 1.2B × 3 LayerMix recipes HQ/MQ/LQ, each with 3.6× overtraining). Optimize θ (quality density) and λ(N) (learning rate) by minimizing Spearman correlation between predicted Information and actual validation loss. The loss is average perplexity over 5 downstream tasks (HellaSwag, ARC-E, ARC-C, MMLU, TriviaQA) converted from accuracy.

## Results & Highlights

- **Unified scaling curve:** All 27 fitting runs plus extrapolation runs collapse onto a single power-law line in the Information-Loss plane (Figure 3f), regardless of data recipe or model size.
- **Extrapolation to unseen recipes:** 25 held-out LayerMix configurations on 1.2B model achieve Pearson correlation of 0.76 between predicted and measured loss.
- **Extrapolation to larger models:** Predictions accurate for 1.5B–7B models on unseen MLQ and MHQ recipes, with 0.15% mean and 0.96% max absolute error across all unseen data points.
- **Cross-regime extrapolation:** When overtraining degree increases from m=3.6 to m=25, the scaling curve shifts in intercept but retains the same slope, and parameters fitted only on m=3.6 data predict m=25 behavior accurately (Figure 4).
- **Optimal recipe search:** On 2.5B model, the InfoLaw-searched optimal recipe achieves lower validation loss than all preset baselines (HQ, MQ, LQ).
- **Practical guidance:** Table 2 shows optimal LayerMix weights shift from quality-heavy to diversity-heavy as model size and training tokens increase. For a 7B model at 300B tokens, w_0 (top-5% quality) is 0.548; at 1000B tokens it drops to 0.395.
- **Generalization to RefinedWeb:** Quality density θ = 0.93 (vs 0.92 on primary data), max absolute error 0.36% on extrapolated MLQ configuration.

## Authors & Credibility

The authors are from an industry lab (likely ByteDance, based on author affiliations with prior work on QuadMix and related pretraining topics). Fengze Liu is a co-author of QuadMix (arXiv:2504.16511), a related data selection paper. The experimental infrastructure is substantial: 27 fitting runs + numerous extrapolation runs across 13 model sizes (252M–7.7B), trained on a 3.7T-token deduplicated Common Crawl corpus. The paper includes thorough appendices with ablation studies on the normalization term, alternative λ fits, and generalization to RefinedWeb.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty: Moderate.** The idea of modeling pretraining as information accumulation is conceptually clean, but the specific functional forms (exponential decay + log normalization) are empirically motivated rather than theoretically derived. The paper acknowledges this limitation. The core contribution is the practical unification of quality, repetition, and scale into a single predictive framework.

- **Rigor: Good but with gaps.** The fitting uses Spearman correlation as the optimization objective, which ensures monotonicity but not calibration. The 27 fitting runs span a narrow overtraining regime (m=3.6); the cross-regime validation (m=25) uses only a single anchor point (1.2B at 640B tokens). The quality bucketing is heuristic (6 fixed percentile buckets), and no ablation on bucket count or boundaries is provided. The λ(N) extrapolation to 7B relies on only 7 data points for the logarithmic fit.

- **Reproducibility: Moderate.** The algorithm is clearly specified (Algorithm 1), but the quality classifiers (FineWebEdu + DCLM) and the 3.7T-token corpus are not publicly released. The model architectures are detailed in Table 3, but training code and checkpoints are not available. The fitted parameters (θ=0.922, a=0.140, b=0.018, α=3.737, β=0.0441) are provided, which would allow reproduction if one has a comparable corpus.

- **Significance: High for practitioners.** The ability to predict loss for unseen data recipes without training is practically valuable for pretraining teams. The finding that optimal recipes shift from quality to diversity with scale is actionable. However, the framework's reliance on a specific quality scoring pipeline and bucketing scheme may limit generality.

- **Red flags:** (1) The evaluation uses only 5 benchmarks, all English, all relatively standard—no code generation, math, or multilingual tasks. (2) The validation loss ↔ downstream performance correlation (Figure 6) is shown but the linear relationship may not hold at very low loss regimes or for more complex tasks. (3) The "optimal recipe" search (100k random samples) is a relatively crude optimization method; gradient-based or Bayesian optimization might find better solutions.

## Strengths

- Conceptually elegant: the Information metaphor provides an intuitive and interpretable framework for understanding how quality and repetition interact.
- Strong empirical results: 0.15% mean absolute error across diverse extrapolation settings is impressive.
- Practical utility: the framework enables data-recipe optimization without additional training, which is highly valuable given the cost of LLM pretraining experiments.
- Actionable insights: the quality-vs-diversity trade-off across scales provides clear guidance for practitioners.
- Thorough appendices with ablations on normalization terms, alternative λ fits, and cross-dataset generalization.

## Limitations

- Quality bucketing is heuristic with no systematic ablation on bucket count or boundaries.
- Only 5 English benchmarks for evaluation; no coverage of code, math, or multilingual capabilities.
- Fitted on a narrow overtraining regime (m=3.6) with limited cross-regime validation (single m=25 anchor point).
- Theoretical justification for the specific functional forms (exponential decay, log normalization) is thin—they are empirically selected from three candidates.
- No theoretical explanation for why the overtrain degree m shifts the scaling curve's intercept.
- Corpus and quality classifiers are not publicly available, limiting reproducibility.

## Relevance

该论文直接关联 LLM 预训练中的数据工程问题——如何在不同计算预算下选择最优数据配比。对于关注 scaling laws 和数据质量对模型性能影响的读者而言，InfoLaw 提供了一个实用的预测工具和明确的"质量 vs 多样性"权衡指导。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does InfoLaw perform when the quality scoring uses a different pipeline (e.g., model-based perplexity filtering vs. classifier-based scoring)? Is the quality density function f_d sensitive to the scoring method?
- Can the framework be extended to domain-specific mixture optimization (e.g., balancing code, math, and natural language) by adding domain as an additional dimension to the bucketing scheme?
- What happens when the number of quality buckets is increased significantly (e.g., 20 or 50 buckets)? Does prediction accuracy improve, or does overfitting become a problem?
- How well does the Information metric correlate with performance on reasoning-heavy benchmarks (e.g., GSM8K, MATH, HumanEval) that were not included in the evaluation suite?
- Could the exponential decay model for repetition be replaced with a more theoretically grounded function (e.g., based on information theory or Bayesian learning curves)?
