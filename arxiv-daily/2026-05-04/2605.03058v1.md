> **一句话总结：** MechaRule 提出了一套将符号规则与 LLM 内部神经元因果关联的管线，通过对比层次消融（CHA）高效定位稀疏的"激动子"神经元，在算术和越狱任务上分别实现高达 71.1% 和 8.8% 的行为改变，并能为每个因果神经元生成可解释的 if-then 规则。

> **TL;DR:** MechaRule bridges symbolic rule extraction and mechanistic interpretability by (1) learning behavioral splitter rules that partition inputs into rule-matching vs. non-matching slices, (2) efficiently localizing sparse "agonist" neurons via contrastive hierarchical ablation (CHA) with confidence-guided pruning (Θ(k log N/k + k) interventions), and (3) anchoring interpretable if-then rules to each causal neuron. On Qwen2-7B and GPT-J, suppressing discovered agonists drops arithmetic accuracy by up to 71.1% and jailbreak success by up to 8.8%, while targeted interventions improve outcomes up to 20% (arithmetic) and 26% (jailbreaking). CHA recovers 96.8% of high-effect brute-force agonists. The key insight is that rule-aligned data splits are essential for finding selective causal neurons — spectral or random splits degrade substantially.

## 核心贡献

- 提出 MechaRule 管线，首次将符号规则提取与机制可解释性统一：既能产生人类可读的 if-then 规则，又能通过因果干预验证规则与神经元之间的关联
- 形式化定义了"激动子"（agonist）和"拮抗体"（antagonist）神经元，区分了规则选择性因果效应和广谱破坏性效应
- 设计了对比层次消融（CHA）算法，利用"溢顶"（overtopping）现象和置信度引导的保守剪枝，在稀疏假设下以 Θ(k log N/k + k) 次干预定位所有 k 个激动子
- 证明了在制度条件单调翻转函数和认证剪枝假设下，二叉层次搜索不会误剪包含真实激动子的分支（剪枝安全性定理）
- 在算术和越狱两个任务、三个模型上验证了管线有效性，发现 LLM 的任务行为由稀疏的、特征选择性的神经元群体实现

## Key Contributions

- Introduce **MechaRule**, the first pipeline that jointly produces symbolic rules grounded in causal circuitry: rules are both human-readable and validated by targeted neuron interventions
- Formalize **agonist** (rule-selective causal) vs. **antagonist** (broadly disruptive) neurons, separating strength from selectivity in causal effect measurement
- Design **Contrastive Hierarchical Ablation (CHA)**, a binary search over neuron groups with confidence-guided pruning (Clopper–Pearson bounds) that exploits the "overtopping" phenomenon — a few dominant neurons can mask weaker ones at coarse scales
- Prove a **pruning-safety theorem** (Appendix B): under regime-conditional monotonicity and certified bounds, no group containing a true τ-agonist is pruned, with probability ≥ 1 − α
- Demonstrate that **rule-aligned splits** are critical: learned behavioral splitters yield significantly more high-quality anchored rules than spectral-only splits, random coverage, or fake-rule controls

## Methodology

MechaRule is a four-stage pipeline:

1. **Behavioral Rule Extraction (Stage 1):** An LLM-based agent proposes discriminative predicates over inputs (e.g., "first operand divisible by 3") via contrastive inspection of successful vs. failed examples. These predicates, combined with seed features, feed into RuleSHAP (XGBoost + SHAP-weighted rule selection) to produce sparse if-then splitter rules r₀ that partition inputs into associated (D⁺) and unrelated (D⁻) slices, optimized for Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC).

2. **Search Space Reduction (Stage 2):** Spectral compression (PCA + k-center clustering) produces compact, representative evaluation subsets with matched controls (D̃⁺, D̃⁻). EAP-IG (integrated gradients on corrupted/clean pairs) ranks MLP-output write coordinates, retaining the top-M candidates for exact ablation (~43,008 coordinates in main runs).

3. **Causal Localization (Stage 3):** CHA recursively splits each layer's candidate set into halves, ablating each group and measuring slice-wise flip rates δ⁺(A) and δ⁻(A). A group is pruned only if its upper confidence bound on strength E(A) falls below threshold τ = 0.2. This exploits the overtopping abstraction: within a fixed baseline regime, effects are approximately monotone and saturating, so coarse-to-fine search is safe. The algorithm distinguishes agonists (high strength + high selectivity) from antagonists (high strength + low selectivity).

4. **Neuron-Anchored Rules (Stage 4):** For each localized singleton agonist j, RuleSHAP learns a Boolean rule rⱼ predicting when ablating j flips behavior on held-out data. Only neurons whose anchored rules achieve MCC ≥ 0.85 are reported as fully anchored.

Interventions target MLP-output write coordinates (the FFN contribution to the residual stream) during decode-time only (post-prefill), using mean-positional baselines.

## Results & Highlights

- **End-to-end (E0):** Qwen2-7B arithmetic yields 61 high-quality anchored neurons (MCC ≥ 0.85), whose union flips 71.1% of correct→incorrect examples and enables 21.1% incorrect→correct recovery. Jailbreaking yields 112 high-quality neurons with 8.8% harmful→safe flip coverage and 26.2% safe→harmful diagnostic coverage.
- **CHA recall (E2):** Across all completed comparisons, CHA recovers 559/890 (62.8%) brute-force agonists overall and **90/93 (96.8%)** in the strongest effect tier E(A) ∈ [0.5, 1.0]. Recall improves with effect size (Mann–Whitney U = 27.0, p = 0.017).
- **Split quality matters (E1):** Rule split + spectral coverage produces 631 high-quality agonists at t = 0.85, vs. 399 (spectral only), 242 (random coverage), and 179 (fake rule). Rule splits significantly outperform fake-rule controls (U = 217.5, p = 0.013) and random coverage (U = 286.0, p = 0.010).
- **Task specificity:** Only 8 neurons overlap between arithmetic and jailbreaking on Qwen2-7B (Jaccard = 0.034), suggesting task-specific mechanisms rather than globally fragile neurons.
- **Layer distribution:** Arithmetic agonists concentrate in early layers (m3, m4) and late layers (m26, m27), diverging from EAP-IG's late-layer bias — attribution scores and exact ablations highlight related but distinct objects.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty:** The core idea of anchoring symbolic rules to causally validated neurons is genuinely novel. Prior rule-extraction methods are ungrounded in circuitry; prior mechanistic work lacks symbolic outputs. The combination is non-trivial and well-executed. However, several components (RuleSHAP, EAP-IG, spectral compression) are existing methods composed into a pipeline — the novelty lies in the integration and the CHA algorithm.

**Rigor:** The formal framework is careful: agonist/antagonist taxonomy, regime-conditional analysis, separation of strength vs. selectivity, and the pruning-safety theorem (Appendix B) are well-defined. However, the paper honestly acknowledges that the empirical protocol uses a "simpler approximate pruning rule" rather than the theorem's full alpha-spending schedule, and the theorem's assumptions (A2, A3) may not always hold. The brute-force comparison is limited to single-circuit runs, not all circuits. Statistical tests are appropriately described as "descriptive" given few regime rows.

**Reproducibility:** Code is publicly available. Hyperparameters are extensively documented (Appendix J). The pipeline depends on several moving parts (LLM feature agent, XGBoost, SHAP, EAP-IG, TransformerLens), which may make exact reproduction sensitive to dependency versions. The 7B model experiments likely require significant GPU resources.

**Significance:** The paper makes a compelling case that rule-grounded localization is both feasible and useful. The ability to produce neuron-anchored rules that predict when interventions matter is a genuine advance for LLM interpretability. The practical impact is currently limited to structured tasks (arithmetic, jailbreaking with automated graders) and models up to 7B parameters.

**Red flags:**
- GPT-J arithmetic accuracy is only 2%, making the (1→0) flip coverage metric nearly vacuous — the paper acknowledges this but the headline "71.1%" only applies to Qwen2.
- The CHA recall of 62.8% overall means over a third of brute-force agonists are missed, though most misses are in the weakest effect tier.
- The pipeline is complex (4 stages, multiple sub-methods) and may be brittle on tasks where behavioral splitters are hard to learn.
- The overtopping hypothesis is regime-asymmetric: it holds mainly in the "richer" direction (e.g., suppressing correct arithmetic) and often fails in the opposite direction, limiting bidirectional applicability.

## Strengths

- Principled integration of symbolic rules with mechanistic interventions — fills a genuine gap in the literature
- Careful formalization with clear definitions, theorems, and honest discussion of assumption limitations
- Strong empirical results on the strongest-effect tier (96.8% recall) and substantial behavioral effects (71.1% accuracy drop)
- Comprehensive ablation studies (split quality, coverage method, CHA vs. brute-force, τ sensitivity, candidate budget sensitivity)
- Honest about limitations: acknowledges intervention semantics concerns, finite-sample noise, scope limitations, and the gap between the theorem and empirical protocol
- Task-specificity analysis (low Jaccard overlap) adds credibility that findings are not artifacts

## Limitations

- **Scope:** Only tested on two tasks (arithmetic, jailbreaking) with open-weight models ≤ 7B parameters. Generalization to larger models, other architectures, or more complex tasks is unknown.
- **Intervention basis:** Only MLP-output write coordinates are intervened on during decode-time. Attention-path mechanisms, MoE routing, multimodal circuits, and prefill effects are not captured.
- **Rule grounding:** Anchored rules are "supportive evidence" for an interpretable trigger, not proof that the underlying mechanism is uniquely symbolic. If the behavioral splitter r₀ entangles multiple mechanisms, selectivity degrades.
- **Computational cost:** The pipeline involves EAP-IG over large candidate sets (~43K–86K coordinates) followed by CHA, plus LLM-based feature proposal loops. Scaling to much larger models may be prohibitive.
- **Asymmetric regimes:** Overtopping is empirically regime-dependent — CHA is most effective when intervening against the learned behavior direction, and much less effective in the opposite direction.
- **Baseline choice:** Zero/mean/mean-positional ablation baselines are synthetic and can introduce distribution shift or LayerNorm-mediated side effects. Donor-grounded alternatives are more conservative.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How does CHA scale to models beyond 7B parameters (e.g., 70B+)? Does the sparse gating hypothesis (H1) still hold, or do behaviors become more distributed in larger models?
2. Can MechaRule be extended to intervention bases beyond MLP writes — e.g., attention head outputs, residual stream directions, or SAE latent features?
3. How robust are the discovered agonists to fine-tuning, RLHF, or safety training? Do the same neurons remain causally influential after alignment?
4. The paper shows agonists concentrate in early and late layers for arithmetic — is this layer distribution task-dependent, or does it reflect a general computational structure (early feature extraction + late readout)?
5. Can neuron-anchored rules be used proactively for runtime safety monitoring, e.g., detecting when a jailbreak-inducing neuron's trigger condition is met during generation?
