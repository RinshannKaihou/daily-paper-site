Trustworthy AI Suffers from Invariance Conflicts and Causality is The Solution

arXiv: 2605.02640v1

Date: 2026-05-04

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.02640

一句话总结： 本文将可信AI中公平性、鲁棒性、隐私和可解释性之间的矛盾重新诠释为不兼容的不变性需求冲突，论证了因果推理是理解和缓解这些权衡的统一框架，并提出显式和隐式两种因果集成路径。
TL;DR: A position paper arguing that trade-offs among fairness, robustness, privacy, explainability, and accuracy in AI arise from conflicting invariance requirements under different interventions on the data-generating process. Causal models provide a unifying framework by enabling selective invariance — blocking spurious or normatively unacceptable causal pathways while preserving legitimate ones. The paper covers both classical ML and foundation models, distinguishing explicit causal integration (SCMs, causal graphs) from implicit approaches (data augmentation, inductive biases), and offers a lifecycle perspective (pre-training, post-training, auditing) for integrating causality into large-scale systems.

Key Contributions

  • Invariance-conflict framing of trustworthy AI trade-offs: Reinterprets fairness (invariance to protected attributes), robustness (invariance to distribution shifts), privacy (invariance to data inclusion), and explainability (selective invariance under meaningful changes) as competing invariance requirements under different interventions on the data-generating process.
  • Selective invariance via causal structure: Shows that causal models resolve or soften trade-offs by distinguishing admissible from inadmissible causal pathways, enabling targeted constraints rather than blanket suppression of correlations.
  • Explicit vs. implicit causal integration: Defines two modes of incorporating causality — explicit (SCMs, causal graphs, path-specific constraints) and implicit (counterfactual data augmentation, inductive biases, multi-environment training) — and analyzes when each is appropriate.
  • Lifecycle perspective for foundation models: Maps causal integration to pre-training (distributional-level interventions), post-training/alignment (behavioral constraints), and auditing (retrospective causal probing), with concrete method references for each stage.
  • Multi-objective analysis: Demonstrates that causal models can simultaneously improve multiple trust dimensions because they are inherently more explainable, require less privacy noise (per Tople et al., 2020), and reduce reliance on spurious correlations that harm fairness and robustness.

Methodology

This is a position paper (not an empirical contribution) that builds its argument through:

  1. Formal reinterpretation: Each trustworthy AI objective is formalized as an invariance requirement under a specific class of admissible changes (interventions). Accuracy under the observational distribution is formalized as sensitivity to all predictive correlations, which directly conflicts with invariance demands.
  1. Pairwise trade-off analysis: The paper analyzes four key trade-offs (fairness–accuracy, privacy–utility, robustness–accuracy, explainability–performance) plus multi-objective interactions. For each, it identifies the statistical origin of the conflict, reformulates it as an invariance conflict, and shows how causal reasoning provides a resolution.
  1. Structural Causal Models (SCMs): The paper adopts Pearl's causal framework. A running hospital readmission example illustrates how a single causal graph can simultaneously enforce fairness (blocking A→Y and A→Z→Y), robustness (blocking spurious E→X→Y), privacy (path-specific obfuscation), and explainability (transparent pathway-based predictions).
  1. Integration taxonomy for foundation models: Reviews concrete methods across the FM lifecycle — causal representation learning and entity interventions at pre-training, causal RLHF and SCM-based regularization at post-training, mechanistic interpretability and activation patching at auditing.
  1. Discussion of limitations: Identifies conceptual challenges (potentially unresolvable tensions, concept superposition in FMs) and practical challenges (assumption/identifiability requirements, scalability of explicit methods, lack of causal data, computational overhead).

  • This is a conceptual/position paper — there are no experimental results. The contribution is theoretical framing and synthesis.
  • The paper draws on existing empirical evidence from the literature to support its claims (e.g., Tople et al., 2020 showing causal models need less privacy budget; Richens & Everitt, 2024 showing robust agents learn causal world models).
  • Provides a comprehensive appendix cataloguing methods for causal integration in both classical ML (CCML, IFL, disentangled VAEs, DML, causal discovery) and foundation models (causal representation learning, entity interventions, counterfactual data augmentation, SCM-based methods, causal RLHF, mechanistic interpretability).

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: The invariance-conflict framing is elegant and provides genuine insight — reinterpreting all trustworthy AI trade-offs through a single lens is valuable, even if individual pieces (causality for fairness, causality for robustness) are well-established. However, the paper's claims about causality resolving multi-objective trade-offs are stronger than what existing evidence supports. The paper acknowledges this indirectly but could be more upfront about the gap between the theoretical argument and empirical validation.
  • Rigor: The formal definitions (invariance under interventions, SCM formalization) are solid. The pairwise trade-off analyses are careful. However, some claims are stated more strongly than justified — e.g., "causal models are inherently explainable" is an aspiration rather than a proven property of learned causal representations.
  • Reproducibility: N/A for a position paper, though the formal definitions are precise enough to operationalize.
  • Significance: If the community adopts the invariance-conflict framing, this could shape how trustworthy AI research is structured. The lifecycle perspective for FM causal integration is practically useful. The paper serves as an excellent roadmap/reading guide for the intersection of causality and trustworthy AI.
  • Red flags: The paper risks preaching to the converted — researchers already convinced of causality's importance will find this compelling, but the argument may not persuade the "just scale" camp. The "Alternative Views" section (Section 6) is notably thin (half a page) and does not seriously engage with scaling-law arguments or non-causal approaches to multi-objective trustworthiness. Some proposed solutions (e.g., "path-specific obfuscation") remain speculative.

Strengths

  • Unified, elegant formal framework that connects disparate trustworthy AI literatures through a single invariance/intervention lens.
  • Comprehensive and well-organized — covers classical ML and foundation models with concrete method references.
  • The hospital readmission running example effectively illustrates how a single causal graph addresses all four trust dimensions simultaneously.
  • Honest about limitations: acknowledges that some tensions may be unresolvable, that concept superposition in FMs complicates causal reasoning, and that implicit methods offer weaker guarantees.

Limitations

  • No empirical validation — the paper argues theoretically and via literature citations but does not demonstrate the framework in practice.
  • The "Alternative Views" section is underdeveloped; the scaling-law perspective gets only a passing mention.
  • The distinction between "softening" and "resolving" trade-offs is sometimes blurred — the title promises "causality is the solution" but the body often concedes that trade-offs can only be softened.
  • Practical guidance on how to obtain the required causal structure in real-world FM deployments remains vague.
  • The paper is more synthesis/position than a concrete methodological advance; researchers already familiar with causality + trustworthy AI may find few surprises.

Relevance

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How would the invariance-conflict framework apply to emergent trust dimensions not covered here, such as truthfulness/hallucination control or alignment with human values?
  • Can implicit causal integration methods (e.g., counterfactual data augmentation) be rigorously shown to approximate the selective invariance guarantees of explicit SCMs, and under what conditions do they fail?
  • What benchmarks or evaluation protocols could jointly measure fairness, robustness, privacy, and explainability under interventional (not just observational) distributions?
  • How does concept superposition in foundation models fundamentally limit the granularity of causal interventions, and can sparse autoencoders or other disentanglement methods overcome this?
  • For the scaling-law camp: under what scaling regimes do implicitly causal behaviors (e.g., robust world models) emerge without explicit causal supervision, and how do they compare to explicitly causal approaches on multi-objective trust metrics?
