> **一句话总结：** 本文提出基于 Fisher 信息和局部表示几何的敏感性对齐框架 S-RAS，通过比较表示对微小扰动方向的平均可辨别性来补充传统的激活对齐方法（CKA/RSA/CCA），在神经网络层匹配、鲁棒训练区分和小鼠视觉皮层分析中展示了有效性。

> **TL;DR:** Standard representational similarity measures (CKA, RSA, CCA) compare activation point clouds but miss how representations differ in their local sensitivity to small input perturbations. This paper introduces the Spectral Riemannian Alignment Score (S-RAS), which summarizes each representation by its expected Fisher/pullback metric over a chosen perturbation-family subspace, compares these summaries using a log-spectral distance on SPD matrices, and provides a uniform multiplicative certificate over all lifted task values. Experiments show S-RAS recovers layer correspondence across independently trained CNNs (93.8% accuracy, near CKA's 94.4%), produces transferable class-conditional probes that separate model architectures on held-out images, distinguishes standard from robust (PGD/TRADES/MART) training regimes, and recovers stimulus-coordinate-dependent sensitivity structure in mouse visual cortex (outperforming CKA on cross-area retrieval).

## 核心贡献

- 提出了基于局部可解码信息的表示比较框架，聚焦于表示在指定刺激坐标子空间中对微小扰动的辨别能力，弥补了传统激活对齐方法（CKA、RSA、CCA）无法刻画局部敏感性的不足。
- 定义了投影期望 Fisher/pullback 算子作为二阶矩局部扰动任务族的最小完备数据集级摘要，并基于 SPD 流形上的仿射不变 Riemannian 度量提出 S-RAS 相似性度量，附带对所有提升任务值的一致乘性证书。
- 引入了增益/形状分解和类条件对比方向，可将数据集级敏感性对比外化为可迁移到未见图像和未见模型的有限扰动探针。
- 在人工网络（层匹配、架构区分、鲁棒训练区分）和生物系统（Allen Brain Observatory 小鼠视觉皮层）中验证了框架的有效性。

## Key Contributions

- Formalizes a **local perturbation-discrimination framework** for comparing neural representations, shifting focus from activation-point-cloud alignment to expected local discriminability over a specified stimulus-coordinate subspace.
- Identifies the **projected expected Fisher/pullback operator** as the minimal complete dataset-level summary for second-moment local discrimination tasks, with a rigorous proof that it uniquely determines all task values over the perturbation family.
- Proposes **S-RAS** (Spectral Riemannian Alignment Score), which compares regularized sensitivity summaries via the affine-invariant Riemannian metric on SPD matrices, yielding a dimension-normalized similarity score and a uniform multiplicative certificate (Theorem 1) bounding all lifted task values.
- Introduces **class-conditional contrast directions** (Proposition 2) that optimize between-group local quadratic sensitivity, producing finite perturbation probes transferable to held-out images and models — a distribution-level construction distinct from per-image eigen-distortion methods.
- Demonstrates that the framework **reveals controlled dissociations** between standard and robust training (ERM vs. PGD/TRADES/MART) and uncovers **stimulus-coordinate-family effects** in mouse visual cortex that activation-based methods miss.

## Methodology

The paper builds its framework in three stages:

1. **Local discriminability formalization.** Given a differentiable representation map $f: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^m$ and a stimulus parameterization $\psi$, the pullback metric $M_f(x) = J_f(x)^\top J_f(x)$ captures infinitesimal sensitivity. For a Gaussian discrimination task between nearby stimuli under noise $\Sigma$, the local Fisher information $I_{f,\psi}(s) = J_{h_f}(s)^\top \Sigma^{-1} J_{h_f}(s)$ governs discriminability via the quadratic form $d'^2(s, \eta) = \eta^\top I_{f,\psi}(s)\eta$.

2. **Dataset-level aggregation.** Fixing a coordinate family $P$ (orthonormal columns selecting a perturbation subspace) and averaging over the stimulus distribution yields the projected expected Fisher operator $\mathcal{F}_{f,\psi,P,\Sigma}$. This operator is proven (Proposition 1) to be the minimal complete summary: it determines all expected task values $T_{\psi,P,C_z}(f) = \text{Tr}(C_z \mathcal{F})$ for second-moment perturbation families, and equality of all task values implies equality of operators.

3. **SPD comparison via S-RAS.** Regularized summaries are lifted to the SPD manifold via $\tilde{A} = A + \varepsilon_{\text{reg}} \text{Tr}(A) I_k$. The affine-invariant Riemannian distance $d_{\text{AIRM}}(A,B) = \|\log(A^{-1/2}BA^{-1/2})\|_F$ is used, normalized by $\sqrt{k}$. Theorem 1 provides that if $d = d_{\text{AIRM}}(\tilde{A}, \tilde{B})$, then $e^{-d}\tilde{A} \preceq \tilde{B} \preceq e^d \tilde{A}$, giving uniform multiplicative control over all lifted task values.

The gain/shape decomposition separates total sensitivity ($\gamma = \text{Tr}(G)$) from directional allocation ($\hat{G} = G / \text{Tr}(G)$), and class-conditional contrasts $\Delta G^{(y)} = G^{(y)}_A - G^{(y)}_B$ yield shared contrast directions (top/bottom eigenvectors) that can be instantiated as finite image deformations.

## Results & Highlights

- **Layer matching:** S-RAS achieves 93.8% top-1 accuracy with whitened-PCA families on Tiny10 CNNs (K=768), nearly matching CKA's 94.4%. It substantially outperforms pointwise local-geometry baselines (pw-AIRM, MSA), validating the aggregation choice.
- **Transferable probes:** Class-conditional sensitivity contrasts yield finite perturbations that separate ResNet-18 from Small-ViT on held-out images with separation 1.10, versus 0.43 for random-contrast controls and 0.03 for label-permutation nulls (Wilcoxon $p \leq 2.72 \times 10^{-13}$).
- **Robust training dissociation:** Contrast-derived probes separate standard from PGD (0.99), TRADES (1.22), and MART (1.14) training on held-out images, while showing weaker PGD-vs-MART separation (0.28) — indicating the framework is selectively sensitive to meaningful training-regime differences.
- **Mouse visual cortex:** Full Fisher summary reaches 45.8% top-1 cross-area retrieval accuracy in Allen Brain Observatory data, significantly above CKA (28.8%), shape-only Fisher (25.9%), and naive full geometry (22.2%) after matched-count control. Matching depends on the stimulus-coordinate family: $\rho + \phi$ (0.500) and $\theta + \phi$ (0.486) outperform the full $\theta + \rho + \phi$ family (0.458), showing that adding coordinates can dilute the signal.
- **SPD certificate guarantee:** S-RAS provides a mathematically guaranteed uniform multiplicative bound over all lifted task values, unlike heuristic similarity scores.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** The contribution is substantial but incremental. The key insight — that activation alignment misses local sensitivity structure — has been noted before (e.g., Feather et al. 2025, Lipshutz et al. 2024). The main novelty lies in the clean formalization of second-moment task families, the minimal-completeness result (Proposition 1), and the SPD-based comparison with its certificate guarantee. The gain/shape decomposition and class-conditional contrast directions are elegant extensions.
- **Rigor:** The mathematical framework is well-grounded, with proper definitions, propositions, and proofs. The connection to Fisher information and population coding theory is appropriate. However, the framework is inherently limited to second-moment (quadratic) summaries and local (infinitesimal) perturbation analysis.
- **Reproducibility:** Code is publicly available. Experimental setups are described with sufficient detail (checkpoints, hyperparameters, K values, split protocols). The use of standard datasets (CIFAR-10, Allen Brain Observatory) aids reproducibility.
- **Significance:** The practical gains over strong baselines are modest in some settings (CKA still wins raw layer-matching accuracy; VISp-depth benchmark is essentially tied). The strongest contributions are conceptual (the formalization) and the biological application (where Fisher summaries genuinely outperform CKA). The probe-transfer experiments are the most compelling empirical contribution.
- **Red flags:** The paper is limited to second-order statistics and local linearization. The dependence on perturbation-family choice is acknowledged but raises questions about how to choose families in practice. The isotropic noise assumption ($\Sigma = \sigma^2 I$) in the Fisher formulation is a simplification for biological data where noise correlations matter. The comparison with pointwise baselines (pw-AIRM, MSA) uses a down-projected subset ($n=256$), which the authors acknowledge as a limitation rather than a comprehensive benchmark.

## Strengths

- Clean, principled mathematical framework with rigorous theoretical guarantees (uniform multiplicative certificate).
- The perturbation-family design makes the comparison question-specific and scientifically meaningful.
- Transferable probe experiments convincingly demonstrate that dataset-level sensitivity contrasts have practical predictive power beyond the discovery set.
- The biological application to Allen Brain Observatory data is a natural fit for the framework and shows genuine advantages over activation-based methods for cross-area retrieval.
- Gain/shape decomposition provides interpretable diagnostic tools beyond a single similarity score.

## Limitations

- Restricted to local (infinitesimal), second-moment analysis — cannot capture nonlinear finite-perturbation effects or higher-order sensitivity structure.
- Perturbation-family choice is a free parameter; no principled guidance is given for how to select families in novel settings.
- Practical advantages over strong activation baselines (CKA) are modest or absent in some benchmarks (raw layer matching, VISp-depth).
- Computational cost of Jacobian estimation scales with representation dimension and dataset size, though the paper uses finite-difference approximations.
- The isotropic noise assumption may limit applicability to biological systems where noise correlations are known to be structured and functionally relevant.

## 相关性

本文提出的表示几何分析框架与隐藏状态可解释性研究直接相关——通过 Fisher 信息和 pullback 度量刻画表示的局部敏感性结构，为理解不同模型/层如何编码信息提供了比传统激活对齐更精细的工具。对鲁棒训练（PGD/TRADES）的区分实验也触及了模型可靠性分析。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does the choice of perturbation family interact with the specific invariances learned by different architectures (e.g., convolutional vs. attention-based)?
- Can the framework be extended to handle structured or stimulus-dependent noise covariances $\Sigma(x)$, which are known to be important in biological population coding?
- What is the computational scaling behavior for large-scale representations (e.g., LLM hidden states with thousands of dimensions)?
- Could the class-conditional contrast probes be used as a diagnostic tool during training to detect when a model shifts its local evidence-use strategy?
- How do higher-order sensitivity summaries (beyond second moments) change the comparison, and when are they necessary?
