> **一句话总结：** 本文将 YES（Yet-achievably-Simple）训练认证框架从全连接网络扩展到 Transformer 语言模型，通过逐层"剥离"构建轻量参考解，发现训练中的模型在某些阶段甚至不如仅用 2 层 YES 构造的基线，揭示了量化微调下隐藏的优化低效问题。

> **TL;DR:** The authors extend the YES (Yet-achievably-Simple) training certification framework to decoder-only transformer LMs. By constructing lightweight layer-wise reference models that sequentially fit each transformer layer's intermediate representations, they produce provable baselines against which to judge training quality. Key finding: on a 3B-parameter OpenLLaMA model fine-tuned under 1-bit/ternary quantization, simple 2-layer or 5-layer YES baselines can match or outperform the fully trained model at various epochs, revealing that many intermediate layers are effectively redundant and that smooth loss curves can mask significant optimization inefficiency. The framework works as a data-driven, solver-agnostic diagnostic that disentangles poor optimization from quantization-induced distortion.

## 核心贡献

- 提出了一种适用于 Transformer 语言模型的逐层"剥离"（peeling）框架，通过在冻结训练模型的中间表示上逐层独立优化，构建轻量级参考解（YES 解），用于监控训练质量。
- 设计了多种层排列（permutation）策略，将 YES 层投影到训练模型的不同中间层输出上，以诊断哪些层对最终性能贡献最大、哪些层存在冗余。
- 在量化（1-bit 二值化、1.58-bit 三值化）微调场景下验证了方法的有效性，发现训练损失曲线的收敛可能掩盖显著的优化低效，YES 基线能揭示标准监控手段无法发现的优化瓶颈。
- 展示了 YES 解在测试集上具有与训练集竞争甚至更优的泛化性能，暗示 YES 基线可作为独立于验证损失的早停信号。

## Key Contributions

- Extends the YES training certification framework from fully connected ReLU networks to decoder-only transformer language models (LLaMA-style and GPT-2-style), enabling layer-wise training quality diagnosis for modern architectures.
- Introduces a permutation-based construction where each YES layer can target different intermediate outputs of the trained model, enabling systematic probing of layer importance and redundancy without retraining multiple ablated models.
- Demonstrates on OpenLLaMA-3B fine-tuned under 1-bit and ternary quantization that 2-layer or 5-layer YES baselines can match or exceed the fully trained model's performance at various training stages, exposing hidden optimization inefficiencies.
- Shows that YES solutions can generalize better than the main training solution on test data, suggesting the framework can serve as a principled early-stopping criterion independent of validation loss.
- Provides a data-driven argument for layer pruning or selective fine-tuning in quantized regimes by revealing that most intermediate layers contribute marginally to the final solution.

## Methodology

The YES (Yet-achievably-Simple) framework constructs lightweight reference models to serve as baselines for training quality certification:

1. **Cached Teacher Representations**: A trained transformer model is frozen, and per-layer hidden representations are extracted on a small cached subset of training data. These serve as fixed targets.

2. **Sequential Layer-wise Optimization**: A YES model with $L' \leq L$ layers is built. Each YES layer is independently optimized to match a selected intermediate representation of the trained model via regression (MSE). Once a layer is fitted, it is frozen and its output feeds the next layer. This ensures each layer is only locally optimized.

3. **Output Head Fitting**: After all YES transformer layers are fitted, a lightweight linear output head is trained on the final YES representation using standard cross-entropy loss, with all YES layers kept frozen.

4. **Permutation-based Target Selection**: Each YES layer can target the output of any deeper trained model layer (not just its corresponding layer). This produces multiple valid reference trajectories. For example, YES1 = [1,4] means the first YES layer targets layer 1 output and the second targets layer 4, effectively skipping intermediate layers.

5. **Comparison Protocol**: The YES solution's loss is compared against the trained model's loss. If the training trajectory fails to surpass the YES baseline, the optimizer is provably underperforming. The minimum across all permutations at each epoch forms the tightest bound.

6. **Quantization Settings**: The framework is tested under channel-wise 1-bit binarization and ternary (1.58-bit) quantization applied to MLP, gating, and output projection weights during fine-tuning.

## Results & Highlights

- **Small-scale LLaMA (4 layers, WikiText-2)**: With learning rate 2e-4, the training loss surpasses most YES baselines by epoch 15, but YES4=[1,4] (a 2-layer model targeting only layers 1 and 4) outperforms training, highlighting the dominant role of the first and last layers.
- **OpenLLaMA-3B with ternary quantization**: A 5-layer YES baseline with permutation YES1=[1,2,26,26,26] closely tracks the 26-layer trained model throughout training. Three of the five YES layers effectively project to the final layer output, suggesting most intermediate layers are redundant for this dataset.
- **OpenLLaMA-3B with 1-bit binarization (LR=1e-5)**: All 26-layer YES bounds closely track the training trajectory, with YES1 (projecting 23 layers to the final layer) emerging as the strongest bound. A reader examining only the training curve would conclude convergence, but YES bounds reveal better optimization paths exist.
- **Test generalization**: YES1 consistently achieves competitive or better test performance than the main training solution, demonstrating better generalization despite being a simpler construction.
- **Early stopping signal**: In several experiments, the training curve continues decreasing for hundreds of epochs after aligning with the strongest YES bound, suggesting YES bounds provide a meaningful early stopping criterion.

## Authors & Credibility

The authors are from the Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering at the University of Illinois Chicago. The work extends their prior OPT 2025 paper on YES training bounds from fully connected networks to transformer architectures. The progression from toy MNIST experiments to small-scale transformers to 3B-parameter OpenLLaMA fine-tuning demonstrates increasing experimental rigor. However, the experiments are limited to relatively small datasets (WikiText-2, WikiText-103) and the largest model is tested only under fine-tuning, not pre-training from scratch.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty**: Moderate. The core idea of layer-wise reference solutions was introduced in the authors' prior work (OPT 2025). The extension to transformers is meaningful but largely follows the same conceptual template. The permutation-based analysis of layer importance is a genuinely new contribution.
- **Rigor**: The experimental methodology is sound. The staged evaluation (toy → small LM → 3B model) is well designed. However, all experiments use WikiText variants, which are small relative to modern pre-training datasets. The framework's behavior during large-scale pre-training (not just fine-tuning) remains untested.
- **Reproducibility**: Good. The authors describe architectural details, hyperparameters, and quantization settings in detail. The YES construction itself is straightforward to implement. However, code is not referenced as available.
- **Significance**: The practical impact depends on whether practitioners adopt YES bounds as a monitoring tool. The observation that smooth loss curves can mask optimization inefficiency under quantization is important and actionable. The layer-redundancy findings could inform pruning strategies.
- **Red flags**: The YES construction requires computing intermediate representations of the trained model on a cached dataset, which adds overhead per epoch. The computational cost of evaluating all permutations grows combinatorially with depth. The paper does not benchmark this overhead or compare against simpler baselines like gradient norm monitoring. The generalization claims rest on small test sets. The framework is demonstrated only on fine-tuning, not on the more practically important setting of pre-training from scratch.

## Strengths

- Provides a principled, data-driven, and solver-agnostic framework for training quality certification — no modification to the training procedure is needed.
- Successfully scales from toy examples to 3B-parameter models, demonstrating broad applicability.
- The permutation analysis provides genuine insight into layer importance and redundancy without requiring multiple retraining runs.
- The finding that YES solutions generalize competitively is surprising and potentially useful as an early-stopping criterion.
- Clear and well-structured exposition that motivates the problem effectively.

## Limitations

- Only evaluated on fine-tuning small-to-medium datasets (WikiText-2, WikiText-103); behavior during large-scale pre-training is unknown.
- The computational overhead of computing YES bounds at each epoch checkpoint is not analyzed or benchmarked.
- Permutation search space grows combinatorially with model depth; the paper uses heuristics to select subsets but provides no principled guidance.
- The framework diagnoses problems but does not prescribe fixes — it tells you a layer is under-optimized but not how to improve it.
- No comparison against simpler training monitoring baselines (gradient norms, Hessian-based diagnostics, learning rate sensitivity).

## Relevance

本文与 LLM 训练可观测性、量化训练稳定性、以及训练质量认证高度相关。YES 基线提供了一种独立于损失曲线的诊断工具，直接对应了"监控训练运行、检测训练不稳定性、量化误差"等研究方向，且为量化微调场景下的优化瓶颈提供了可操作的检测方法。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does the YES framework behave during large-scale pre-training (not just fine-tuning) on datasets with billions of tokens?
- What is the computational overhead of computing YES bounds relative to a single training step, and can the construction be amortized or parallelized efficiently?
- Can YES bounds be used adaptively to guide learning rate scheduling or layer-wise learning rate assignment during training?
- How do the layer redundancy findings correlate with structured pruning methods — can YES-informed pruning match or exceed dedicated pruning algorithms?
- Does the permutation-based analysis change qualitatively for encoder-decoder models or non-autoregressive architectures?
