Dimensionality-Aware Anomaly Detection in Learned Representations of Self-Supervised Speech Models

arXiv: 2605.02715v1

Date: 2026-05-04

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.02715

一句话总结： 本文提出 GRIDS 框架，利用局部内在维度（LID）逐层分析 WavLM 和 wav2vec 2.0 在良性噪声和对抗攻击下的表征几何变化，发现对抗扰动在低 SNR 下显著抬升 LID 并在高层层持续，且 LID 升高与 WER 恶化正相关，基于 12 维 LID 特征的轻量分类器可实现 AUROC 0.78–1.00 的对抗/良性异常检测。
TL;DR: Proposes GRIDS, a framework that uses Local Intrinsic Dimensionality (LID) to analyze layer-wise representation geometry in WavLM and wav2vec 2.0 under benign noise and PGD adversarial attacks. Adversarial perturbations cause persistent early-layer LID elevation (especially PGD-MSE), and LID increases co-occur with WER degradation. A 12-dimensional LID feature vector fed to logistic regression achieves AUROC 0.78–1.00 for adversarial-vs-benign anomaly detection, enabling transcript-free robustness monitoring for self-supervised speech models.

核心贡献

  • 提出了 GRIDS（Geometric Robustness via Intrinsic Dimensionality in Speech）框架，首次将局部内在维度（LID）应用于自监督语音模型（S3Ms）的逐层表征几何分析。
  • 揭示了对抗扰动（PGD-MSE 和 PGD-CTC）在低 SNR 下显著抬升各层 LID，且在高层 SNR 下仍保持早期层的 LID 升高，而良性噪声则趋于收敛至干净语音的 LID 曲线。
  • 实证表明 LID 升高与 WER 恶化（ASR 性能下降）正相关，为无转录文本的鲁棒性监控提供了几何诊断工具。
  • 利用 12 维逐层 LID 特征向量训练轻量逻辑回归分类器，在对抗/良性二分类任务上实现 AUROC 0.78–1.00。

Key Contributions

  • Introduces GRIDS, the first framework applying Local Intrinsic Dimensionality (LID) to layer-wise representation geometry analysis in self-supervised speech models (WavLM, wav2vec 2.0).
  • Demonstrates that adversarial perturbations (PGD-MSE, PGD-CTC) cause the largest and most persistent LID elevation across layers at low SNR, while benign perturbations converge toward the clean LID profile as SNR increases.
  • Establishes a positive co-occurrence between LID elevation and WER increase, linking geometric deformation to downstream ASR degradation without requiring ground-truth transcripts.
  • Shows that a lightweight logistic regression classifier on 12-dimensional layer-wise LID features achieves AUROC 0.78–1.00 for adversarial-vs-benign anomaly detection across SNR levels.

Methodology

The GRIDS framework operates in three analysis modes applied to WavLM-Base and wav2vec 2.0-Base (both 12-layer, d=768):

  1. LID Estimation: For each transformer layer, frame-level embeddings are pooled across utterances within a condition (model, perturbation type, SNR). Local LID is estimated per frame using a Levina-Bickel MLE estimator based on kNN distances (k=50 primary), then aggregated via harmonic mean to produce a per-layer scalar. Features are standardized per layer-condition pair before kNN search.
  1. Perturbation Generation: Five perturbation types are compared under matched target-SNR conditions (0–40 dB): Gaussian noise, babble noise (Noizeus), competing-talker speech noise, PGD-MSE adversarial (maximizing final-layer representation distortion), and PGD-CTC adversarial (maximizing CTC loss for task-aligned transcription disruption). All use ℓ2-budget calibrated to the same per-utterance SNR.
  1. Anomaly Detection: Each utterance is represented by a 12-dimensional LID feature vector (one LID value per layer). A logistic regression classifier is trained to distinguish adversarial from pooled benign (Gaussian + babble + speech) inputs at matched SNR, evaluated with 5-fold grouped cross-validation (grouped by utterance ID to prevent leakage).

The evaluation uses 918 paired LibriSpeech test-clean utterances (5–10 s duration, 40 speakers), ensuring all perturbation types are computed on identical utterances for fair comparison.

Results & Highlights

  • Layer-wise LID trajectories: LID generally decreases from early to late layers (reflecting representational compression). Adversarial perturbations produce the largest upward shifts, with the clearest adversarial-benign separation in early transformer layers.
  • SNR-dependent divergence: At low SNR (0–10 dB), both adversarial and speech-like benign perturbations elevate LID substantially. At high SNR (30–40 dB), benign noise converges to the clean profile while adversarial inputs retain early-layer LID elevation — a useful relative marker.
  • PGD-MSE vs PGD-CTC: PGD-MSE consistently produces larger and more persistent LID elevation than PGD-CTC under the same SNR budget, as MSE directly targets representation distortion.
  • ∆LID–∆WER co-occurrence: At 0 dB, PGD-MSE induces ∆LID=16.03 with ∆WER=0.94 on WavLM. Gaussian noise remains low-impact (∆WER≤0.04). At high SNR, benign perturbations approach zero degradation while adversarial retains non-trivial ∆LID.
  • Anomaly detection: WavLM+MSE achieves near-perfect separation (AUROC 0.99, FPR@0.95=0.02 averaged across SNRs). wav2vec 2.0+MSE is the hardest configuration (AUROC 0.88). Separability decreases at high SNR partly because many adversarial examples fail to meaningfully degrade ASR (low attack success rate).
  • Cross-model consistency: Despite different pretraining objectives (WavLM uses denoising; wav2vec 2.0 uses contrastive learning on clean speech), the broad pattern of adversarial LID elevation is consistent across both models.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: High. This is the first application of LID to self-supervised speech model representations. The extension from single-vector image classifiers to variable-length frame-level embeddings with harmonic-mean aggregation is a non-trivial methodological contribution. The three-part analysis (geometry, ASR monitoring, anomaly detection) provides a comprehensive treatment.
  • Rigor: Strong experimental design — fully paired evaluation (918 identical utterances across all conditions), grouped cross-validation preventing utterance leakage, matched SNR budgets enabling fair benign-vs-adversarial comparison. The k-sensitivity analysis (Table 2) and the adaptive k-selection procedure demonstrate awareness of hyperparameter influence.
  • Reproducibility: Good but not complete. Architecture details, loss functions, and evaluation protocols are clearly specified. However, the adaptive k-selection procedure involves a "fixed fraction" threshold that is not explicitly stated. Code is not mentioned as publicly available. The use of specific pretrained model checkpoints from HuggingFace aids reproducibility.
  • Significance: The transcript-free monitoring angle is practically valuable — being able to detect adversarial inputs without decoding transcripts could be useful in deployment scenarios. However, the restriction to untargeted PGD attacks and 12-layer base models limits immediate real-world applicability.
  • Red flags: The anomaly detection evaluation pools three benign types as negatives against one adversarial type, which may inflate performance compared to a more challenging open-set scenario. The association between ∆LID and ∆WER is presented as co-occurrence rather than causal prediction — the paper is careful about this distinction, but readers should note that high LID does not always mean high WER (e.g., overlaid speech maximizes ∆WER at moderate ∆LID). The AUROC range 0.78–1.00 spans a wide gap; the lower end (0.78 at 40 dB CTC for wav2vec 2.0) indicates limited practical utility at high SNR.

Strengths

  • First systematic layer-wise LID analysis for S3Ms, filling a clear gap in the literature.
  • Careful experimental design with fully paired utterances and matched-SNR budgets ensures fair comparison.
  • The three-analysis structure (geometry → ASR monitoring → anomaly detection) provides a coherent narrative from understanding to application.
  • Honest about limitations — clearly states that elevated LID should not be treated as uniquely adversarial at low SNR.
  • The transcript-free monitoring concept is practically motivated and well-argued.

Limitations

  • Restricted to 12-layer base models (WavLM-Base, wav2vec 2.0-Base); behavior in larger models (e.g., WavLM-Large, 24+ layers) is unknown.
  • Only untargeted PGD attacks are considered; targeted adversarial attacks or other attack families (e.g., CW, DeepFool) may exhibit different LID signatures.
  • The adaptive k-selection procedure introduces a per-condition hyperparameter that complicates deployment — a fixed-k analysis shows qualitative stability but the full pipeline relies on the adaptive step.
  • At high SNR (40 dB), anomaly detection performance degrades substantially (AUROC as low as 0.78), precisely the regime where deployment monitoring would be most needed for subtle attacks.
  • The paper evaluates only on English read speech (LibriSpeech); generalization to conversational speech, other languages, or noisy recording conditions is untested.
  • The relationship between LID and WER is correlational (co-occurrence), not predictive — the paper cannot predict specific WER values from LID alone.

Relevance

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does LID-based anomaly detection perform on larger S3M architectures (e.g., WavLM-Large with 24 layers) — does the early-layer signal persist or get absorbed by deeper layers?
  • Can LID features be combined with global dimensionality measures (effective rank, PCA variance) for improved multi-scale robustness diagnostics?
  • Is the observed LID elevation pattern consistent across targeted adversarial attacks, or do attacks with specific acoustic targets produce qualitatively different geometric signatures?
  • How does the framework generalize to streaming/real-time scenarios where LID must be estimated from limited frame counts?
  • Could unsupervised LID-based monitoring be applied during S3M fine-tuning to detect representation collapse or overfitting to noise?
