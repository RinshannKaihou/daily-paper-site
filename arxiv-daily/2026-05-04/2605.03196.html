Geometric Deviation as an Unsupervised Pre-Generation Reliability Signal: Probing LLM Representations for Answerability

arXiv: 2605.03196v1

Date: 2026-05-04

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.03196

一句话总结： 通过测量LLM隐藏状态到可回答参考集质心的余弦距离，可以在生成之前无监督地检测不可回答的数学查询（ROC-AUC 0.78–0.84），但该信号在事实性查询中无效，且答案可答性几何信息主要编码在模型的前几层。
TL;DR: Proposes an unsupervised, pre-generation reliability signal based on cosine distance of LLM hidden states from an answerable reference centroid. Achieves ROC-AUC 0.78–0.84 for detecting unanswerable math prompts across three 7–8B instruction-tuned models (Llama 3.1, Qwen 2.5, Mistral 7B), outperforming both refusal-keyword detection and 5-sample self-consistency—while requiring zero labeled failure data and zero output sampling. The signal is form-conditional: strong for math/code, absent for factual prompts; peaks at early layers (2–5); and shows cross-model geometric consensus on which prompts are anomalous, despite divergent behavioral responses across models.

核心贡献

  • 提出了一种完全无监督的预生成可靠性信号：通过测量输入表示到可回答参考集质心的余弦距离来判断查询是否可回答，无需标注失败数据、无需微调、无需输出采样。
  • 在数学类查询中实现了强分离（ROC-AUC 0.78–0.84），优于拒绝关键词基线和五次采样的自一致性基线，且能捕获模型未明确拒绝但实际不可回答的情况。
  • 系统性地揭示了该信号的形式条件性（form-conditional）：在代码查询中效果较大但方差较高，在事实性查询中完全无效，为该方法的适用范围划定了清晰边界。
  • 逐层分析发现答案可答性几何信号在模型的前几层（第2–5层）最强，向输出层逐渐衰减，表明可答性信息在网络早期就已编码。
  • 发现跨模型几何共识现象：三个不同架构的模型在19个数学不可回答提示上表现出一致的几何偏离，但行为响应（拒绝 vs. 幻觉）因对齐训练不同而各异。

Key Contributions

  • Introduces a minimal unsupervised pre-generation reliability signal: cosine distance from the centroid of answerable-class representations, requiring no labeled failure data, no fine-tuning, and no output sampling.
  • Achieves strong separation on mathematical prompts (ROC-AUC 0.78–0.84 across Llama 3.1-8B, Qwen 2.5-7B, Mistral-7B-Instruct), outperforming refusal-keyword baseline and 5-sample self-consistency—including cases where models hallucinate without refusing.
  • Establishes principled boundaries on generalization: the signal is form-conditional, with large effects on code prompts but no reliable signal for factual prompts across all three models.
  • Layer-wise analysis reveals the answerability signal peaks at layers 2–5 and attenuates toward the output layer, suggesting answerability geometry is established before the final stages of generation.
  • Identifies cross-model geometric consensus on 19 of 50 MATH-U prompts across all three architectures, while behavioral responses (refuse vs. hallucinate) diverge—indicating alignment training shapes how models act on geometric information, not the information itself.

Methodology

The paper investigates whether representation geometry can signal answerability prior to generation. The core method is strikingly simple:

  1. Representation extraction: For each prompt, extract last-layer hidden states from instruction-tuned LLMs, apply mean pooling over tokens, and subtract the global mean vector (to remove anisotropy, per Godey et al., 2024).
  1. Reliability score: Compute cosine distance (1 - cos θ) from the prompt's representation to the centroid of answerable-class representations only (no unanswerable labels used). This own_dist serves as the reliability score.
  1. Matched-pair design: To isolate answerability from confounds, construct matched pairs where each unanswerable prompt shares domain, length, and syntactic form with an answerable prompt, differing only in the element causing unanswerability. Three prompt forms are studied: MATH (n=50 pairs), FACT (n=10 pairs), CODE (n=30 pairs).
  1. Models: Three architecturally distinct instruction-tuned models at 7–8B scale: Llama 3.1-8B-Instruct, Qwen 2.5-7B-Instruct, Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3. Scale is held constant to isolate architectural/alignment differences.
  1. Analysis pipeline: One-sided permutation tests (n=5000) for statistical significance, Cohen's d for effect size, ROC-AUC and F1 for classification. Compared against refusal-keyword baseline (single generation) and self-consistency baseline (5 samples).
  1. Layer-wise analysis: Extract mean-pooled hidden states at every layer for 20 MATH matched pairs, computing the answerability gap δ_l at each layer.

Results & Highlights

  • MATH form (strongest results): All three models show highly significant separation (p < 0.0001, Cohen's d = 1.12–1.41). Unanswerable math prompts consistently deviate from the answerable centroid with a gap of ≈ +0.37–+0.39. ROC-AUC: Llama 0.841, Qwen 0.782, Mistral 0.826.
  • Self-consistency fails on MATH: SC achieves only AUC 0.30–0.62 because instruction-tuned models hallucinate consistently (same wrong answer across 5 samples), yielding near-zero disagreement despite geometric anomaly.
  • FACT form (null result): No significant separation for any model (p = 0.36–0.57). Factual unanswerability is syntactically indistinguishable from answerable factual queries and does not disrupt the geometric cluster.
  • CODE form (partial): Large effect sizes (d = 1.01–1.31) across all models, but statistical significance is mixed (Qwen p = 0.008; Llama/Mistral p = 0.11–0.16) due to higher within-group variance.
  • Layer-wise profile: Signal peaks at layers 2–5 (δ ≈ 0.98–1.09) and decreases toward the last layer (δ ≈ 0.44–0.48). The attenuation is driven by answerable representations drifting toward unanswerable ones in deeper layers, not by the anomaly signal decaying.
  • Cross-model consensus: 19 of 50 MATH-U prompts are geometrically flagged as anomalous by all three models simultaneously, involving extremal/infinite mathematical objects.
  • Behavior divergence: Llama hallucinates on prompts where Qwen refuses, despite identical geometric deviation—alignment training modulates behavioral response, not the geometric signal itself.

Critical Assessment

  • Novelty: Moderate. The idea of probing representations for reliability signals is not new (PRISM, MHAD, Slobodkin et al.). The key novelty is the unsupervised formulation—cosine distance to an answerable centroid without any labeled failures—which is conceptually elegant and practically lightweight. However, the method is essentially a simple nearest-centroid classifier applied to hidden states, which is a well-known technique.
  • Rigor: Strong. The matched-pair design is a genuine methodological contribution that explicitly controls for surface form confounds. The use of permutation tests with centroid recomputation avoids null-hypothesis violations. Reporting both significance and effect sizes, alongside principled null results, is commendable. The layer-wise analysis adds depth.
  • Reproducibility: Good. Code and prompts are publicly available. All three models are open-source. The experimental setup is well-documented. However, the CODE experiments were run separately with different mean-centering contexts, making cross-form comparison harder to reproduce.
  • Significance: Moderate-to-high. The practical value is clear: a single-pass, pre-generation reliability signal that outperforms multi-sample baselines at zero additional inference cost. The form-conditional finding—especially the clean null result on FACT—is an important negative result that tempers overgeneralization. The early-layer finding could inform efficient monitoring architectures.
  • Red flags:
  • Sample sizes are modest (50/10/30 pairs). The FACT null result at n=10 is particularly underpowered—though the author correctly notes the MATH effect was already significant at n=10 in pilot experiments.
  • The method may conflate lexical oddity with structural unanswerability. Unanswerable math prompts often contain unusual tokens ("largest prime," "square root of -169"), and the paper acknowledges but does not fully control for perplexity.
  • Only 7–8B models tested; scaling behavior is unknown.
  • The refusal baseline is intentionally weak (keyword matching); comparison with semantic entropy or supervised probing (PRISM) is deferred.

Strengths

  • Elegant minimal approach: no labels, no fine-tuning, no output sampling—just cosine distance to a reference centroid.
  • Thorough experimental design with matched pairs that control for surface confounds.
  • Honest reporting of null results (FACT form) establishes principled boundaries.
  • Cross-model consensus analysis strengthens the claim that the signal reflects input structure rather than model-specific artifacts.
  • Early-layer finding has practical implications for efficient pre-generation monitoring.
  • Outperforms self-consistency (5× cost) on MATH, capturing confident hallucinations that output-variance methods miss entirely.

Limitations

  • Form-conditional: The signal is unreliable for factual/open-domain queries, limiting deployment to structured domains (math, code) with formal answerability constraints.
  • Modest sample sizes: FACT (n=10) and CODE (n=30) results carry high variance; larger benchmarks needed.
  • Lexical confound: Geometric deviation may partially reflect lexical unusualness rather than genuine structural unanswerability. Perplexity controls are acknowledged but not implemented.
  • Single probe choice: Only cosine distance to a mean-pooled last-layer centroid is tested. More sophisticated probes (PCA, learned hyperplanes, earlier-layer pooling) might recover the FACT signal or improve CODE significance.
  • Scale-limited: Only 7–8B models tested; behavior at larger scales (70B+, frontier models) is unknown.
  • Missing strong baselines: Full semantic entropy and supervised methods (PRISM, MHAD) are not compared; the SC baseline is a lightweight proxy, not the state of the art.
  • Single author annotation: Behavioral annotations are from one annotator with no inter-annotator agreement measured.

Relevance

Questions for Further Exploration

  • Would controlling for input perplexity eliminate the geometric separation, or does a genuine structural-unanswerability signal persist?
  • Can earlier-layer representations (layers 2–5) be used to build substantially stronger pre-generation classifiers than the last-layer results reported here?
  • How does the signal scale to 70B+ models and frontier models (GPT-4, Claude)? Does the form-conditional pattern persist?
  • Can the unsupervised centroid-distance approach be combined with supervised probing methods to achieve both breadth (across forms) and depth (within forms)?
  • Does the geometric consensus phenomenon extend to other structural violation types beyond math/code (e.g., logical contradictions, physically impossible scenarios)?
  • What happens when comparing base models vs. instruction-tuned variants of the same model family—does alignment training alter the geometric signal itself, or only the behavioral response?
