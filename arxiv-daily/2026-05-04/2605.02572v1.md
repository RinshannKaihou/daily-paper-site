> **一句话总结：** 本文通过受控实验（Sudoku、Rush Hour）系统揭示了任务步数（horizon length）本身是LLM智能体RL训练的根本瓶颈——即使推理复杂度不变，步数增长也会导致训练崩溃，而通过宏动作或子目标分解缩短有效步数可以稳定训练，并且短步数训练的模型能泛化到未见过的长步数任务（horizon generalization）。

> **TL;DR:** This ICML 2026 paper isolates "horizon length" (number of sequential actions needed to solve a task) as an independent training bottleneck for LLM agents. Through controlled experiments on Sudoku and Rush Hour where only goal distance varies while reasoning complexity stays fixed, the authors show that longer horizons cause RL training collapse due to exploration failure and noisy credit assignment—even when the model already possesses the underlying solving capability. The fix is "horizon reduction" via macro actions or subgoal decomposition, which stabilizes training. Crucially, models trained on short horizons generalize to unseen longer horizons ("horizon generalization"), enabling a curriculum-based training strategy.

## 核心贡献

- **首次系统性地将horizon length（任务步数）从推理复杂度中解耦出来**，证明仅增加步数就会导致RL训练不稳定和崩溃，即使底层推理能力完全相同。
- **提出"horizon reduction"作为核心训练原则**，通过宏动作（macro actions）或子目标分解（subgoal decomposition）缩短有效交互步数，显著稳定训练并提升长步数任务表现。
- **发现"horizon generalization"现象**：在短步数上训练的模型能有效泛化到未见过的更长的步数任务，且宏动作策略的泛化能力优于原子动作。
- **验证了结论的鲁棒性**：跨环境（Sudoku、Rush Hour、WebShop）、跨模型规模（1.7B、4B）、跨优化器（REINFORCE、GRPO-style）均成立。

## Key Contributions

- First systematic empirical study that decouples horizon length from reasoning complexity in LLM agent training, demonstrating that horizon length alone is a fundamental bottleneck.
- Identifies **horizon reduction** as a key training principle, showing that macro actions and subgoal decomposition stabilize RL training on long-horizon tasks.
- Uncovers **horizon generalization**: models trained on shorter horizons generalize to unseen longer horizons, with macro-action policies outperforming atomic-action baselines across all horizon lengths.
- Validates robustness across three environments (Sudoku, Rush Hour, WebShop), two model scales (1.7B, 4B), and two RL optimizers (REINFORCE, GRPO-style).

## Methodology

**Controlled experimental design.** The authors formalize three key concepts: *goal distance* d(s₀, g) (minimum atomic actions to reach the goal), *interaction budget* H_max (maximum allowed steps), and *effective horizon* h_π (actual steps taken). They construct controlled task suites using Sudoku and Rush Hour where goal distance varies (L1–L7, from 11 to 45 steps) while reasoning complexity is held constant (all puzzles solvable with basic techniques, verified by HoDoKu).

**Isolating horizon from solving complexity.** To ensure the model possesses sufficient solving capability regardless of horizon, they construct short-horizon proxy tasks (e.g., generate a complete Sudoku solution in one step). Only instances the model can solve in the short-horizon setting are retained, decoupling horizon effects from capability limitations.

**Training pipeline.** Qwen3-1.7B is first fine-tuned via SFT on expert trajectories (from GPT-5-mini), then trained with a stabilized off-policy REINFORCE variant using masked importance sampling and truncated importance sampling. Rewards are decomposed into trajectory-level (discounted return) and step-level (format + validity) components with batch normalization.

**Horizon reduction mechanisms:**
- *Macro actions:* Allow the policy to output multiple atomic actions per step, reducing the effective horizon.
- *Subgoal decomposition:* Break the global goal into verifiable subgoals (e.g., completing individual Sudoku subgrids), segmenting the trajectory with per-segment returns.

**Horizon generalization evaluation.** Models trained on L1–L4 (goal distance 11–30) are evaluated on unseen L5–L7 (goal distance 31–45) to test extrapolation. A curriculum strategy trains first on short horizons, then continues on longer ones.

## Results & Highlights

- **RL training collapses on long horizons:** On L3–L4 tasks, RL with atomic actions exhibits catastrophic instability and performance collapse, while L1–L2 training is stable and improves over SFT.
- **Macro actions stabilize long-horizon RL:** On L3–L4, macro actions prevent collapse and achieve substantially higher success rates than atomic actions across both Sudoku and Rush Hour.
- **Horizon is the bottleneck, not policy strength:** A controlled ablation restoring long horizons with macro-action policies re-introduces collapse, confirming effective horizon as the causal factor.
- **Flexible macro actions outperform fixed-length ones:** Policy-controlled variable-length macros consistently achieve the best performance, including on frontier models (GPT-5-mini, Gemini-3-Flash).
- **Subgoal decomposition rescues long-horizon training:** Dense reward with subgrid-level segmentation enables stable learning on L3–L4 where sparse-reward RL completely fails.
- **Robustness confirmed:** Results hold on WebShop (realistic web interaction), with a 4B model, and under GRPO-style optimization.
- **Horizon generalization works:** Models trained on L1–L4 generalize to L5–L7, and macro-action models outperform atomic-action models even when the latter have higher per-step accuracy—because fewer decision points means less error accumulation.
- **Curriculum learning leverages generalization:** Training first on short horizons (d ≤ 9), then continuing on longer ones (d = 10–12), yields large gains over direct long-horizon training.

## Critical Assessment

**Novelty (Moderate-High):** The key insight—decoupling horizon length from reasoning complexity—is simple but powerful and surprisingly underexplored. The "horizon generalization" phenomenon is a genuine empirical finding. However, the individual techniques (macro actions, subgoal decomposition) are well-known in classical RL literature, and the paper's contribution is more about framing and empirical validation than algorithmic innovation.

**Rigor (High):** The controlled experimental design is exemplary. Using HoDoKu to verify puzzle difficulty, filtering for solvability via short-horizon proxies, and the ablation that restores long horizons with macro-action policies are all strong methodological choices. The robustness checks across environments, scales, and optimizers add credibility.

**Reproducibility (Moderate):** The paper uses Qwen3-1.7B and GPT-5-mini for expert trajectory generation. While the controlled environments (Sudoku, Rush Hour) are straightforward to implement, the full training pipeline details are partially deferred to appendices. The use of proprietary frontier models for SFT data generation may limit exact reproducibility.

**Significance (High):** This paper provides a principled framework for thinking about a core challenge in LLM agent training. The findings have immediate practical implications for anyone training agents for multi-step tasks: prioritize horizon reduction before algorithmic sophistication.

**Red flags:** The environments are primarily puzzle-based (Sudoku, Rush Hour), which are far simpler than real-world agentic tasks. The WebShop experiment helps but is still relatively constrained. Whether these findings scale to truly complex, open-ended agent scenarios (e.g., software engineering, web automation with real browsers) remains an open question. The paper uses only 1.7B and 4B models; behavior at larger scales (70B+) is untested.

## Strengths

- Exceptionally clean experimental design that successfully isolates horizon length as an independent variable.
- Compelling ablation study showing that effective horizon—not policy representation—is the causal factor behind training collapse.
- The horizon generalization finding is practically useful and suggests a scalable training recipe.
- Clear, well-written paper with strong figures that communicate the key results effectively.
- Validates across multiple dimensions (environment, model scale, optimizer) rather than relying on a single setup.

## Limitations

- Environments are highly controlled and may not capture the full complexity of real-world agentic tasks (e.g., partial observability, stochastic environments, open-ended goals).
- Only small models (1.7B, 4B) are tested; it is unclear whether the same instability patterns hold at frontier model scales.
- Macro action design requires domain knowledge about appropriate action groupings, limiting automatic applicability.
- The paper does not address how to automatically discover effective macro actions or subgoal decompositions for arbitrary tasks.
- The Sudoku domain may overestimate the importance of horizon since error accumulation is deterministic and unforgiving in constraint-satisfaction puzzles.

## Relevance

本文与LLM智能体训练直接相关，系统揭示了长序列决策中步数本身带来的训练瓶颈。horizon reduction和horizon generalization的发现对设计RL训练pipeline（如工具使用、代码生成等多步任务）具有直接指导意义，提示我们在追求复杂算法之前应先关注任务的有效步数设计。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How do these findings transfer to non-deterministic environments with stochastic transitions and partial observability?
- Can macro actions be automatically discovered (e.g., via meta-learning or clustering of action trajectories) rather than hand-designed?
- Does horizon generalization hold when the reasoning complexity also increases, or only when it is held constant?
- How does the horizon bottleneck interact with context window length and in-context learning capabilities?
- Would process reward models (PRMs) combined with horizon reduction yield additive benefits, or do they largely address the same underlying issue?
