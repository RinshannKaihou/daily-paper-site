Distilling Long-CoT Reasoning through Collaborative Step-wise Multi-Teacher Decoding

arXiv: 2605.02290v1

Date: 2026-05-04

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.02290

一句话总结： CoRD 提出一种多教师协同逐步解码框架，通过困惑度评分和束搜索让异构大推理模型在每一步联合构建推理轨迹，从而蒸馏出接近甚至超越教师模型的小型推理模型。
TL;DR: CoRD reformulates Long-CoT reasoning distillation as step-wise collaborative decoding among heterogeneous teacher LRMs (QwQ-32B, R1-Qwen-32B, Phi4-Reasoning-Plus). At each reasoning step, teachers propose candidate steps scored by a meta-prover's predictive perplexity, with beam search preserving diverse high-potential trajectories. The resulting distilled student (R1-Qwen-32B) matches or exceeds all individual teachers on AIME24 (79.6%) and AIME25 (70.2%), outperforming curation (S1/LIMO-style) and post-hoc integration baselines by significant margins, while generalizing to out-of-domain (TaTQA) and open-ended (PubMedQA) tasks.

核心贡献

  • 将推理蒸馏重新定义为逐步协同解码过程：不同于传统的"生成后选择"（curation）范式，CoRD 让多个异构教师模型在每个推理步骤上协作生成和选择最佳步骤，使得任何单一教师无法独立产出的推理路径成为可能。
  • 提出基于困惑度的步骤选择机制（Predictive Perplexity Scoring）：使用一个元验证器（meta-prover）评估部分推理轨迹对正确答案的预测概率，作为选择候选步骤的信号，避免了 PRM 过早剪枝和 MCTS 的指数级搜索空间问题。
  • 引入提示引导的步骤分割（Prompt-guided Step Segmentation）：通过在 prompt 中嵌入 ### Step 标记，使不同教师模型的推理输出在粒度和语义层面保持一致，解决跨模型步骤对齐难题。
  • 集成束搜索（Beam Search）保留多样化推理路径：在逐步解码中维护 top-B 条候选轨迹，捕获局部看似次优但最终可能更优的推理路径（如策略性转向和自我纠正）。
  • 蒸馏出的 32B 学生模型在 AIME24/AIME25 上超越所有教师模型，证明了协同推理蒸馏不仅是简单模仿，而是能合成超越单一教师的推理能力。

Key Contributions

  • Reformulates reasoning distillation from post-hoc curation into step-wise collaborative decoding among heterogeneous teacher LRMs, enabling complementary reasoning strategies to be composed at each step.
  • Introduces predictive perplexity scoring via a meta-prover that estimates how well a partial reasoning trajectory predicts the ground-truth answer, providing a continuous, progress-aware selection signal.
  • Proposes prompt-guided step segmentation that inserts explicit ### Step markers into the prompt, ensuring consistent and semantically coherent step boundaries across diverse teacher models.
  • Integrates beam search into the decoding pipeline to maintain multiple high-potential reasoning trajectories in parallel, preserving strategic shifts and self-corrections that greedy decoding would discard.
  • Demonstrates that the 32B student model distilled via CoRD surpasses all individual teacher models on both AIME24 (79.6% vs. best teacher 78.9%) and AIME25 (70.2% vs. best teacher 67.9%).

Methodology

CoRD (Collaborative Reasoning Decoding) operates as a step-wise auto-regressive decoding process where reasoning steps serve as "tokens" and teacher-proposed steps form the "decoding vocabulary." The framework consists of three core components:

1. Prompt-guided Step Segmentation: The prompt embeds ### Step N. markers to guide teacher LRMs to structure their reasoning into clearly separated, semantically coherent steps. This ensures consistent granularity across heterogeneous teachers, unlike line-break or prefix-based segmentation which either lacks semantic coherence or produces inconsistent boundaries.

2. Predictive Perplexity Scoring: A separate model (the meta-prover, using QwQ-32B in the main experiments) evaluates candidate steps by computing the conditional probability of the ground-truth answer given the extended reasoning prefix. The predictive perplexity score quantifies how naturally the reasoning is expected to progress toward the correct solution, enabling early identification of promising paths without requiring full trajectories.

3. Beam Search Decoding: At each step, the top-B reasoning prefixes are extended with candidate steps from all K teachers, producing B×K proposals. The top-B extended trajectories with the highest predictive perplexity scores are retained. This balances exploration and exploitation, maintaining alternative reasoning paths at reasonable computational cost compared to MCTS.

The teacher pool consists of three heterogeneous LRMs: QwQ-32B, R1-Distil-Qwen-32B, and Phi4-Reasoning-Plus. Experiments use the LIMO-v1 dataset (817 samples) for distillation and evaluate on AIME24, AIME25, MATH500, TaTQA, and PubMedQA.

Results & Highlights

  • Student surpasses teachers: CoRD-Hetero with R1-Qwen-32B student achieves 79.6% on AIME24 and 70.2% on AIME25, exceeding all individual teachers (best teacher: 78.9% / 67.9%).
  • Consistent gains across model sizes: CoRD outperforms Curation and Integration baselines across all student sizes (7B/14B/32B). On AIME25 7B, CoRD-Hetero reaches 45.6% vs. Curation's 42.1% and Integration's 3.8%.
  • Integration baseline collapses: Post-hoc integration via GPT5o-mini severely degrades performance (7-12% on AIME), as it compresses Long-CoT into Short-CoT, destroying valuable supervision signals.
  • Reasoning quality metrics: CoRD-Hetero achieves 93.1% answer accuracy and 0.774 predictive perplexity vs. Curation's 84.8% / 0.652 and Integration's 91.2% / 0.223.
  • Teacher specialization emerges naturally: R1-Qwen-32B and QwQ-32B dominate early reasoning phases (problem formulation), while Phi4-Reasoning-Plus takes over in late phases (conclusion synthesis).
  • Outperforms S1 and LIMO datasets: CoRD's reasoning data consistently outperforms S1k-1.1, LIMO-v1, and LIMO-v2 curated datasets when training the same student model.
  • Generalizes beyond math: On TaTQA (table-based QA), CoRD-Hetero achieves 95.2% vs. Curation's 88.2%. On PubMedQA (open-ended biomedical QA), CoRD-Hetero reaches 91.8% vs. 88.4%.
  • Efficiency: CoRD runs in ~49% of MCTS wall-clock time with negligible meta-prover overhead.

Critical Assessment

Novelty (High): The reformulation of reasoning distillation as step-wise collaborative decoding is genuinely novel. While multi-teacher distillation and beam search are individually well-known, their combination in the specific context of Long-CoT reasoning — with predictive perplexity as a step-level signal — represents a meaningful conceptual advance over curation-based approaches (S1, LIMO).

Rigor (Good): The paper provides thorough component-wise ablations (step segmentation, selection criterion, decoding strategy), comparison against strong baselines (S1, LIMO-v1/v2), and analysis of collaboration dynamics (teacher specialization). Computational complexity analysis is provided. However, some concerns remain:

  • The meta-prover is always the strongest teacher (QwQ-32B), creating a potential circularity — the scoring model both selects steps and is one of the step proposers.
  • Evaluation is heavily focused on AIME benchmarks (monolingual math), with only limited out-of-domain testing (TaTQA, PubMedQA).
  • The Integration baseline uses GPT5o-mini, which may not be the strongest possible integrator, potentially exaggerating CoRD's advantage.

Reproducibility (Good): Code and datasets are promised on GitHub. The paper provides detailed hyperparameters, training configurations (in appendix), and uses publicly available model checkpoints. The meta-prover prompt template is included.

Significance (High): The finding that a 32B student can surpass all individual teachers through collaborative distillation is significant for the field. The framework's applicability beyond math (PubMedQA results) suggests broader impact.

Red Flags:

  • The paper uses GPT5o-mini as the integrator but does not disclose enough details about this model. Its catastrophic failure (7-12% on AIME) seems extreme and warrants scrutiny.
  • The homogeneous setup uses temperature variation alone, which may not provide sufficient diversity — this could make the heterogeneous results look more impressive by comparison.
  • Limited analysis of failure cases or when CoRD does not help.

Strengths

  • Clean, well-motivated reformulation of reasoning distillation as collaborative step-wise decoding
  • Strong empirical results with consistent improvements across model sizes, benchmarks, and settings
  • Thorough ablation study isolating the contribution of each component
  • Emergent teacher specialization is an interesting and well-analyzed finding
  • Lower computational cost than MCTS while producing higher-quality reasoning data
  • Generalizes to out-of-domain and open-ended tasks

Limitations

  • Evaluated primarily on English mathematical reasoning (AIME); multilingual and broader task generalization remains untested
  • Only SFT is used for distillation; combining with preference learning (DPO) is left to future work
  • The meta-prover selection is heuristic (choose the strongest teacher); no principled method for meta-prover selection is proposed
  • The approach requires multiple capable teacher models, which may not always be available
  • No analysis of how the number of teachers or teacher quality affects performance
  • Beam size is fixed at 4; sensitivity analysis to this hyperparameter is limited

Relevance

本文提出的 CoRD 框架涉及多 LRM 的协同推理与蒸馏，与 LLM 推理评估和输出一致性相关。虽然主要关注推理蒸馏而非训练稳定性或数值精度，但其多教师协作生成过程中对推理轨迹质量的评估机制（predictive perplexity）对理解推理输出一致性和可复现性有一定参考价值。

Questions for Further Exploration

  • How does CoRD perform when teachers have a larger quality gap (e.g., one very strong + two weak models)? Does the framework degrade gracefully or collapse?
  • Can the meta-prover be replaced with a smaller, specialized reward model to reduce computational overhead without sacrificing selection quality?
  • What happens when CoRD is applied to non-mathematical reasoning domains at scale (e.g., code generation, scientific reasoning, legal reasoning)?
  • How does the number of beam candidates (B) and teachers (K) scale in terms of both quality and compute? Is there a diminishing return threshold?
  • Can the emergent teacher specialization be leveraged to design more efficient routing strategies, where only the most relevant teacher generates candidates at each phase?
