> **一句话总结：** 本文从理论上严格证明了用参考策略采样的Boltzmann密度比加权的SFT（BOLT）可以精确等价于固定参考KL正则化RLVR的最优策略，并给出有限样本替换的完整误差分解和迭代刷新与KL策略镜像下降的等价关系。

> **TL;DR:** This paper derives the exact conditions under which static weighted SFT on precomputed reference rollouts can replace online KL-regularized RLVR. The key result: under reference sampling, only the prompt-normalized Boltzmann weight $\exp(r(x,y)/\beta)/Z(x)$ induces the same policy as the RLVR optimizer. The proposed BOLT method implements this as a two-phase procedure (precompute weights, then standard SFT), achieving competitive accuracy with GRPO while reducing training time by 75–85% and peak memory by 18–31%. A finite-sample theory decomposes the one-shot replacement gap into support, ESS, normalizer, generalization, and optimization errors, and shows that iterative BOLT with sampler refresh is equivalent to KL policy mirror descent.

## 核心贡献

- **识别了加权SFT的诱导策略身份**：证明了加权SFT损失函数实际上是在对采样器-权重乘积所诱导的策略做前向KL拟合，不同采样器和权重会拟合出完全不同的策略。
- **给出了目标匹配定律**：推导出在参考策略采样下，唯一能使加权SFT诱导策略等于KL正则化RLVR最优策略的权重是prompt归一化的Boltzmann密度比 $\exp(r(x,y)/\beta)/Z(x)$。
- **有限单次替换的完整误差分解**：将单次替换的误差严格分解为支撑集缺失代价、归一化估计、有效样本量方差、泛化误差和优化误差，解释了为什么更多SFT轮次无法修复覆盖不足。
- **建立了迭代BOLT与KL策略镜像下降的等价关系**：证明刷新采样器的迭代BOLT精确等价于KL-PMD，有限内部求解的误差作为精确镜像步骤的加性漂移进入。
- **提出BOLT方法**：两阶段流程（预计算权重 + 标准SFT），在GSM8K和MATH上以75–85%的时间节省和18–31%的内存节省达到或超过GRPO精度。

## Key Contributions

- Identifies the induced-target identity for weighted SFT: the sampler-weight product $q(y|x)w(x,y)$ uniquely determines the conditional policy being fit, making different weighting schemes comparable at the policy level rather than the optimizer level.
- Proves the target-matching law (Theorem 4): under reference sampling $q = \pi_\text{ref}$, the only weight that induces the fixed-reference KL-regularized RLVR Boltzmann target is the prompt-normalized density ratio $\exp(r(x,y)/\beta)/Z(x)$, up to prompt-level scaling.
- Provides a complete finite one-shot replacement theory (Theorem 9) decomposing the RLVR value gap into: exact support price $\beta\log(1/\pi^*(S_N|x))$, partition-function estimation error, ESS-limited variance, Rademacher generalization bounds, and empirical excess risk — each with distinct operational levers.
- Establishes that iterative BOLT with sampler refresh is equivalent to KL policy mirror descent (Theorem 11), with finite inner solves charged as additive drift from the exact mirror step (Proposition 13).
- Demonstrates empirically that BOLT achieves +1.44 to +3.60 points over the raw-reward Refit baseline on the same rollout data, confirming the target-matching prediction, while reducing training time by 75–85% vs. GRPO.

## Methodology

The paper develops a theory-driven replacement for online RLVR with three layers:

1. **Population-level target matching.** The fixed-reference KL-regularized RLVR objective has a closed-form Boltzmann optimizer $\pi^*(y|x) \propto \pi_\text{ref}(y|x)\exp(r(x,y)/\beta)$. Weighted SFT with sampler $q$ and weight $w$ induces policy $\tilde{\pi}_w \propto qw$. Setting $q = \pi_\text{ref}$ and equating $\tilde{\pi}_w = \pi^*$ forces $w = \exp(r(x,y)/\beta)/Z(x)$. Any other sampler-weight product leaves an irreducible RLVR value gap (Corollary 5).

2. **BOLT algorithm (Algorithm 1).** Phase 1: sample $N$ rollouts per prompt from $\pi_\text{ref}$, score with verifier, compute Monte Carlo normalizer $\hat{Z}_N(x)$, and store prompt-normalized Boltzmann weights. Phase 2: fit the fixed weighted-likelihood objective with standard multi-epoch SFT. No reference-model forward passes needed during optimization.

3. **Finite analysis.** The one-shot bound (Theorem 9) chains support restriction $\to$ normalizer estimation $\to$ empirical generalization $\to$ optimization residual, with a forward-to-reverse KL transfer condition certifying the RLVR value of the fitted policy. Iterative BOLT (Theorem 11) recovers KL policy mirror descent: $\pi_{\theta_K} \propto \pi_{\theta_0}\exp(Kr/\beta)$, with finite drift from exact mirror steps bounded per round.

## Results & Highlights

- **Target-weight effect (Table 2):** BOLT outperforms Refit (same rollout data, raw reward weights) by +1.44 on GSM8K and +3.60 on MATH with Qwen3-8B, confirming that the induced target matters more than the optimizer.
- **Efficiency vs. GRPO:** BOLT achieves 90.67% on GSM8K vs. GRPO's 90.01%, and 61.96% on MATH vs. GRPO's 61.37%, while reducing wall time by 85% (GSM8K) and 75% (MATH), and peak memory by 31% and 18%.
- **One-shot saturation:** Learning curves show one-shot BOLT plateaus after fixed reference rollouts are exhausted, consistent with the support-restriction prediction.
- **Refresh gains (Table 3):** Iterative BOLT reaches 1.68–3.38 points above the best non-BOLT checkpoint across Qwen3-0.6B, Qwen3-8B, and Qwen3.5-9B on GSM8K and HumanEval, consistent with KL-PMD coverage improvement.
- **Theoretical coverage frontier (Theorem 7):** For binary verifiers with pass probability $p$, achieving Boltzmann target mass $1-\eta$ requires $C_2(x) \geq (1-\eta)^2/p$, quantifying the temperature-coverage-variance tradeoff.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** The Boltzmann target itself is standard; the novelty lies in the *projection bridge* — proving that reference-sampled weighted SFT with density-ratio weights is the unique static replacement, and the clean error decomposition. The iterative-PMD connection is elegant but builds on well-known PMD results.
- **Rigor:** Exceptionally rigorous for an ML paper. Every claim has a proof. The finite-sample decomposition (Theorem 9) is the most complete analysis of offline RLVR replacement to date. The separation of support, ESS, normalizer, generalization, and optimization errors is genuinely insightful.
- **Reproducibility:** Algorithm 1 is clearly specified. However, experiments are single-run on Qwen models with one main temperature and limited rollout counts. No code release is mentioned. The absence of coverage/ESS/normalizer measurements in the experiments means the finite theory is only indirectly validated.
- **Significance:** The target-matching law provides a principled criterion for comparing weighted-SFT methods that previously seemed ad hoc. The efficiency gains are practically meaningful. However, the coverage limitation is severe — BOLT cannot discover solutions the reference policy never samples, which limits applicability to tasks where the base model already has reasonable pass rates.
- **Red flags:** (1) The empirical scope is narrow — single-run, limited model sizes, no $\beta$ or $N$ sweeps. (2) The paper's own discussion acknowledges missing direct coverage measurements, which are central to the theory. (3) The binary-verifier ESS frontier (Corollary 8) suggests that for hard problems with low reference pass rates, BOLT needs $\Omega(1/p)$ samples — potentially prohibitive. (4) The transfer condition $\kappa_\rho$ in Theorem 9 can be large for small $\beta$ or large reward ranges, weakening the certificate.

## Strengths

- Crystal-clear theoretical framework that makes precise what it means for weighted SFT to "replace" online RLVR at the policy level.
- The error decomposition is novel and operationally useful — it tells practitioners exactly which knob to turn (more samples, lower temperature, refresh sampler) for each type of error.
- The BOLT method is simple to implement: just change the weighting scheme on existing SFT infrastructure.
- Honest scoping of empirical claims as "projection evidence" rather than sweeping performance claims.
- Table 1 (induced targets of various methods) is a valuable conceptual contribution that clarifies the landscape.

## Limitations

- **Coverage bottleneck:** BOLT cannot create support that $\pi_\text{ref}$ never produces. For tasks where the base model has near-zero pass rates, the method is fundamentally limited without refresh.
- **Narrow experimental validation:** Single-run, one temperature, limited rollout counts, no $\beta$ or $N$ ablations, no direct measurement of the theoretical quantities (ESS, normalizer concentration, coverage).
- **Transfer condition dependency:** The forward-to-reverse KL transfer condition $\kappa_\rho$ can be vacuous for sharp Boltzmann targets (small $\beta$, large reward range), limiting the practical value of the RLVR value certificate.
- **Verifier dependence:** BOLT inherits the verifier's quality — imperfect verifiers shift the Boltzmann target, and the paper does not address verifier design.
- **Scalability unclear:** The Monte Carlo normalizer $\hat{Z}_N(x)$ requires evaluating all $N$ rollouts per prompt, and the theory suggests $N \sim 1/p$ for rare correct answers, which may be expensive for large prompt sets.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. How does the coverage frontier interact with chain-of-thought length? For long reasoning traces, the pass probability $p$ may be exponentially small in sequence length — does this make BOLT impractical for long-CoT tasks without aggressive refresh?
2. Can the Monte Carlo normalizer $\hat{Z}_N(x)$ be replaced with a learned estimator (e.g., a reward model or variational bound) to reduce the per-prompt rollout requirement?
3. The transfer condition $\kappa_\rho$ requires the fitted policy to stay in a density-ratio neighborhood of $\pi^*$. How often is this violated in practice for neural network policies, and what regularization could enforce it?
4. How does BOLT interact with reward hacking or brittle verifiers? The paper notes the target inherits verifier quality (Appendix G), but can the support-restriction analysis quantify the robustness penalty?
5. Could a hybrid approach — BOLT for covered prompts, online RLVR for uncovered ones — achieve the best of both regimes with a principled switching criterion based on $\hat{Z}_N(x)$ concentration?
