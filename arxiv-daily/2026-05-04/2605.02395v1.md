> **一句话总结：** 本文提出一个基于符号推理的可控、可验证的过程监督数据合成框架，通过模板感知的错误注入和符号重计算生成前缀无效但下游一致的错误推理链，用于训练过程奖励模型（PRM），在逻辑推理Best-of-8重排序和数学推理迁移任务上均取得提升。

> **TL;DR:** The authors build a symbolic-reasoning-based pipeline that constructs verified correct reasoning chains, injects template-aware errors at controlled positions, symbolically recomputes downstream steps, and verifies prefix non-derivability. The resulting paired correct/erroneous trajectories are translated into natural language for PRM training. On Llama-3.1-8B and Qwen-2.5-7B, the PRM improves Best-of-8 reranking on logical reasoning benchmarks (avg +6.3% and +4.8%) and transfers to mathematical reasoning on ProcessBench (F1 up to 19.2%). A key finding is that first-error localization remains substantially harder than overall step classification, even for frontier models like GPT-5 and Gemini-3.1.

## 核心贡献

- 提出了一个**可控且可验证**的过程监督数据合成框架，能够精确控制错误位置、错误类型和推理链一致性
- 设计了**模板感知的错误注入**机制，确保注入的错误与局部推理模式结构兼容，而非随机采样全局错误分布
- 通过**符号重计算**确保错误步骤之后的推理链与被破坏的状态一致，避免了简单的标签翻转
- 引入**前缀不可推导性检查**来验证注入的步骤确实是第一个无效步骤，过滤掉伪错误
- 构建了配对的正确/错误自然语言推理过程数据集（20K实例），支持PRM训练和细粒度过程评估

## Key Contributions

- A **controllable and verifiable** process supervision data synthesis framework that provides explicit control over error location, error type, and trajectory consistency
- **Template-aware error injection** that constrains perturbations by the rule template at the target step, ensuring structural compatibility with the local inference pattern
- **Symbolic recomputation** of all downstream steps under the corrupted state, producing trajectory-consistent erroneous chains rather than isolated label flips
- **Prefix non-derivability checking** that verifies the injected step is genuinely the first invalid step, filtering pseudo-errors where the conclusion remains derivable
- A paired 20K-instance dataset with aligned correct/erroneous natural-language trajectories, supporting both PRM training and fine-grained diagnostic evaluation

## Methodology

The framework operates in three stages: (1) **Correct chain synthesis** — starting from a target conclusion, it recursively expands intermediate goals using a rule template pool and verifies each step with a symbolic prover, retaining only truth-consistent structures. (2) **Error injection and symbolic recomputation** — given a verified correct chain, an intermediate step *k* is selected as the candidate first-error position. A template-aware perturbation alters the conclusion, misuses the rule direction, or corrupts the factual state. All downstream steps are then recomputed symbolically under the corrupted state. A prefix non-derivability check confirms the corrupted conclusion is not derivable from the original prefix; structural errors are verified against rule-direction, dependency-order, or acyclicity constraints instead. (3) **Natural-language translation** — both chains are converted to natural language using shared entity names, background stories, and predicate mappings, ensuring differences reflect reasoning discrepancies rather than surface variation. The step-aligned translation avoids label-leaking markers (e.g., "error", "mistake") in the PRM training input.

## Results & Highlights

- **Logical reasoning (Llama-3.1-8B):** Average Best-of-8 score improved from 0.528 (base) to 0.591 with the PRM, a +6.3 point gain. Notable improvements on folio (+5.0), pro_nomath (+8.6), and hans (+10.1).
- **Logical reasoning (Qwen-2.5-7B):** Average improved from 0.567 to 0.615, a +4.8 point gain. Strongest gains on temporal (+5.3), logicnli (+7.1), and hans (+7.0).
- **Transfer to math (ProcessBench):** The PRM trained on 20K logical reasoning instances achieved F1=19.2% average across GSM8K, MATH, Olympiad, and Omni-MATH, up from 4.1% with no PRM — a meaningful cross-domain transfer.
- **Step-level evaluation:** First-error localization accuracy (first_error_acc) is dramatically lower than overall step classification (all_step_acc) across all models. Even Gemini-3.1 scores 0.825 vs 0.941, and GPT-5 scores only 0.575 vs 0.866, highlighting that first-error localization is a substantially harder and underexplored problem.

## Authors & Credibility

The paper is authored by Yinghui Chi and Lucien Wang (corresponding author) from Jilin University. The work is methodologically transparent — full pipeline details, prompts, and error-type taxonomies are provided in appendices. Experiments use well-known open-source models (Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen-2.5-7B) and standard benchmarks. The comparison includes domain-adapted baselines and frontier proprietary models (GPT-4o, GPT-5, Kimi-K2.5, DeepSeek-V3.2, Gemini-3.1) for the step-level evaluation, lending credibility to the diagnostic claims. The authors provide their error-type distribution (11 types) and training data size (20K), supporting reproducibility.

## Critical Assessment

- **Novelty:** Moderate. The idea of using symbolic systems to generate verifiable reasoning data builds on ProverGen (Qi et al., 2025), and error injection for data augmentation is not new. The key novelty lies in the combined pipeline — template-aware error injection + symbolic recomputation + prefix non-derivability verification — which addresses a real gap in PRM data quality. The paired trajectory design with downstream consistency is a genuine contribution.
- **Rigor:** Reasonably strong. The symbolic verification step and non-derivability checking provide formal guarantees absent from Monte Carlo rollout methods. However, the paper does not compare against strong PRM baselines like PRM800K or Math-Shepherd on the same benchmarks, which makes absolute improvement claims harder to contextualize. The ProcessBench transfer experiment uses only F1 scores without reporting on the original ProcessBench leaderboard metrics.
- **Reproducibility:** High. The framework uses only symbolic provers and LLM-based translation with detailed prompts provided. Training data size (20K) is modest. However, no code release is mentioned.
- **Significance:** The diagnostic finding that first-error localization is much harder than step classification — even for frontier models — is a valuable empirical contribution. The framework's limitation to symbolic reasoning domains (logic) is significant: it is unclear how well the approach generalizes to domains without clean symbolic structure.
- **Red flags:** The absolute performance on ProcessBench (F1=19.2%) is low, suggesting limited practical utility for mathematical reasoning in its current form. The error diversity is bounded by predefined templates, which may not capture the full error distribution of open-ended LLM reasoning. The paper evaluates only Best-of-8 reranking and does not test in search-based or online settings.

## Strengths

- Provides formal verifiability guarantees (prefix non-derivability, symbolic recomputation) that are absent from rollout-based PRM data methods
- Clean separation of error types with template-aware injection enables controlled experiments on what makes PRM training effective
- The diagnostic evaluation exposing the gap between first-error localization and step classification is a valuable empirical finding
- Strong transparency — full prompts, error taxonomies, and pipeline details in appendices
- Demonstrates cross-domain transfer from symbolic/logical to mathematical reasoning

## Limitations

- Error diversity is constrained by predefined templates and may not cover the full range of errors in open-ended LLM reasoning
- The framework is built on symbolic reasoning structures, making it most naturally applicable to domains with relatively explicit logical or mathematical structure; extending to free-form reasoning is non-trivial
- Absolute performance on ProcessBench is modest (F1=19.2%), suggesting limited practical impact for mathematical reasoning in its current form
- Only Best-of-8 reranking is evaluated; performance in search-based decoding or online reward settings is unknown
- No comparison against established PRM baselines (e.g., PRM800K, Math-Shepherd) on the same evaluation protocol

## Relevance

本文提出了一种基于符号推理的可验证数据合成方法，与LLM推理评估和训练可靠性相关。其过程监督数据的可控性和可验证性设计思路，以及对推理过程中错误定位难度的实证分析，对理解LLM推理评估的瓶颈有参考价值，但与核心研究方向的直接关联度中等。

## Questions for Further Exploration

- How does the framework's performance compare to established PRM data (e.g., PRM800K, Math-Shepherd) on shared benchmarks under identical evaluation protocols?
- Can the template-aware error injection approach be extended to non-symbolic domains (e.g., commonsense reasoning, code generation) using approximate verification methods?
- What is the minimal set of error types needed for effective PRM training — would a smaller, more diverse set outperform the current 11-type taxonomy?
- How do PRMs trained on this data perform in search-based decoding (e.g., tree-of-thought, beam search) rather than just Best-of-K reranking?
- Does the observed gap between first-error localization and step classification narrow with scale, or is it a fundamental property of current architectures?
