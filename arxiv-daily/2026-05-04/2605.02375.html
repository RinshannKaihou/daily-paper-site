TL;DR: This paper provides a rigorous theoretical account of why RLVR with binary rewards causes diversity collapse. The author proves that binary rewards create a fundamental degeneracy in policy gradient methods — infinitely many distributions maximize expected reward. KL-control resolves this by selecting the filtered model (base model conditioned on validity) in the limit, but under model misspecification (the practical regime), decreasing the temperature β drives the policy toward concentrated distributions over a handful of valid outputs rather than the desired filtered model. The paper derives explicit formulas linking β to target validity rate and proposes that alternative divergences targeting the filtered model directly can avoid this failure mode.

Key Contributions

  • Identifies a fundamental degeneracy of binary rewards for policy gradient methods: the set of reward-maximizing distributions is infinite, with no distinguished element
  • Proves that KL-control selects the filtered model $p_* = a(\cdot \mid \mathcal{Y}_1)$ — the unique fully valid distribution closest to the base model in KL divergence — through a nontrivial asymmetry in the tilted distribution's convergence behavior
  • Shows that forward KL convergence to $p_$ works, but $p_$ cannot serve as a direct optimization target because $\mathrm{KL}(q \| p_*)$ is infinite for any full-support policy $q$
  • Under model misspecification, demonstrates that decreasing β drives the optimizer toward concentrated distributions over a shrinking set of valid outputs — the mechanism behind diversity collapse
  • Derives explicit formulas relating the hyperparameter β to a more interpretable target validity rate μ
  • Points to alternative divergences that target $p_*$ directly as a way to reward coverage of the filtered model's support rather than concentration on high-validity outputs

Methodology

The paper develops a theoretical framework analyzing RLVR with binary (verifiable) rewards. It proceeds as follows:

  1. Degeneracy analysis: Shows that for binary rewards $v(y) \in \{0, 1\}$, any distribution supported entirely on valid outputs maximizes expected reward, creating infinite solution degeneracy.
  1. KL-control resolution: Analyzes the tilted distribution $p_{[\beta]} \propto a(y) e^{v(y)/\beta}$ and proves it converges to the filtered model $p_*$ in forward KL as $\beta \to 0$, establishing this as the "natural" selection.
  1. Asymmetry result: Shows that while forward KL$(p_{[\beta]} \| q) \to$ forward KL$(p_ \| q)$, the reverse KL$(q \| p_)$ is infinite for full-support policies $q$, making $p_*$ an impossible direct optimization target for standard policy gradient methods.
  1. Misspecification analysis: Under model misspecification (the typical practical regime where the policy class cannot represent $p_*$), derives that the pressure to decrease β leads to concentration on fewer and fewer valid outputs rather than expanding coverage.
  1. Toy experiment: Illustrates the mechanism on an autoregressive experiment demonstrating how diversity collapses as β decreases under misspecification.
  1. β-to-μ mapping: Derives explicit formulas connecting the KL penalty temperature β to an interpretable target validity rate μ, giving practitioners a more intuitive knob to turn.

Results & Highlights

  • The infinite degeneracy of binary rewards is proven as a structural property, not an empirical observation
  • The filtered model $p_*$ is identified as the theoretically desirable target — the KL-closest fully valid distribution to the base model
  • Under misspecification, the paper proves that the standard KL-regularized objective drives policies toward concentration rather than coverage, explaining the empirically observed diversity collapse in RLVR-trained models
  • The β-to-μ formulas provide practitioners with concrete guidance on hyperparameter selection
  • The toy experiment confirms the theoretical prediction that decreasing β under misspecification leads to progressively fewer unique valid outputs
